Abstract (English)
This thesis investigates the possibilities of creating an LSTM network to make patient specific diagnosis
predictions based on medical records. The investigation revolves around the effect of including basic
data from the patients and using different ICD code mappings. It also investigates how the amount
of patients used for training and the size of their medical records affect the model performance. This
project is motivated by the good results similar studies previously have achieved [1].
The dataset used for the experiments is from the Copenhagen Male Study [2]. The dataset contains
basic data, hospitalization dates, discharge dates and the ICD codes associated with each hospitaliza-
tion of 5245 patients. The data was processed and recurrently fed into an LSTM network to train a
multi-label model to predict the diseases the patients will get at their next hospitalization.
By mapping the ICD codes to 269 disease categories and attempting to predict the patients’ last diag-
noses the following results were achieved: recall@10 = 58.3%, recall@20 = 70.0%, recall@30 = 76.7%,
AUC = 0.922 and #Avg. guess = 21.8.
Removing the basic data and only using the medical record as features, the results declined to: recall@10 =
45.2%, recall@20 = 61.9%, recall@30 = 71.0%, AUC = 0.899 and #Avg. guess = 27.9. The results are
promising and indicates that with more and better data it is possible to create an even better perform-
ing model. They also show the importance of including different sources of data like basic data in the
models.

Introduction
Machine learning has made its entry into businesses and societies all around the world. With a quick
search on the internet you can find millions of different articles about machine learning methods and
their applications. Applications that can have a huge impact on the present and the future like: Self-
driving cars [3], games [4], speech recognition and natural language processing [5]. A common concept
in all of these applications is the Deep Learning methods [6]. Deep learning has proven to be really
powerful and often yields better results than conventional machine learning algorithms [5].
Over time the deep learning results have improved and the algorithms have found new applications.
A sector where deep learning has an increasing impact is the medical sector. This is for instance the
case with IBM Watson Health - An AI that already today helps doctors diagnose and treat patients [7].
Watson is not the only player in this field. Other studies are working with deep learning as a tool to
diagnose patients and have achieved promising results [1][8].
1.1
Scope of this thesis
In this thesis we want to investigate if it is possible to predict the diseases a patient will get at their next
hospitalization. We will attempt to do this by using state of the art deep learning methods. Similar
work has been done in Doctor AI [1] and Deep patient [8] but with a different scope.
In the future we imagine that doctors will have access to a tool that uses basic data, medical records
and data regarding current symptoms, to predict diseases and guide the doctors towards the correct
diagnosis. Doctor AI is a step in that direction since it uses medical records to predict future diseases.
However, they do not include the basic data of the patients. The idea of using basic data is to get a
more complete picture of the patients. The basic data could include blood type, weight, height etc. and
in the future also genome data.
==PAGE:15==
2
Introduction
We will build a model using data from the Copenhagen Male Study, which contains basic data of
around 5000 patients combined with their medical records from the start of the 1970’s until 2014.
The patients’ diseases are represented as either ICD-8 or ICD-10 codes, which are codes from the
International Classification of Diseases system [9][10]. The model is not supposed to be able to stand
alone. It is meant as a tool to guide the doctors in the right direction. A patient can be diagnosed with
multiple diseases during one hospitalization. We aim to predict all the diseases a patient is going to be
diagnosed with at their next hospitalization, thus making this a multi-label problem [11]. The models
we chose to focus on for this project are different setups of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) and
Long Short Term Memory Networks (LSTM) trained on both basic data and medical records.
1.2
Motivation and goal
The work of this thesis is motivated by articles like Doctor AI that already have achieved promising
results when it comes to disease predictions. We want to replicate their methods on our dataset and
also expand and improve their techniques and knowledge.
The goal is to make a model that can predict the diseases associated with the patients’ next hospitaliza-
tion. Furthermore we want to investigate how good the model is both in general and when it is exposed
to different limitations. The goal is also to test the impact on the performance from different aspects
like model architecture and the amount of available data. We also test the performance for different
disease category granularities. We finally investigate the significance of including the patients’ basic
data together with their medical records.

Data description
In this chapter we will cover the data that is provided from the Copenhagen Male Study. The dataset
will be investigated in order to gain an understanding of the available data. Furthermore we will explain
how the data has been preprocessed to fit a format, we can use to achieve the goals described in Chapter
1.
2.1
Copenhagen Male Study
The available data was collected through a study called Copenhagen Male Study. The study began
in 1971 as a cardiovascular study. The main objective of the study was to predict whether a person
would get diagnosed with Ischaemic heart disease based on the persons physical activity and physical
fitness. All the participants in the study were men aged 40 to 59 years employed at large companies
in Copenhagen. The total amount of participants were 5245. The study received data from Danish
registers of morbidity and mortality [2].
The data was separated in two different datasets. One containing the basic data about the participants
and another containing their diagnoses as ICD codes and the corresponding hospitalization and discharge
dates. Different covariates like blood type was used in previous studies with the Copenhagen Male Study
data. However, for unknown reasons those covariates were not part of the datasets we had available.
2.2
International Classification of Disease
The diagnoses in the dataset are represented by their ICD codes. These codes refer to a disease clas-
sifications system called The international Classification of Diseases (ICD) that was first in-
troduced in 1948. The list has been revised several times since its creation and the 8th version (ICD-8)
were introduced in 1968 a few years before the Copenhagen Male Study was initiated. In 1994 the
medical institutions adopted the newest revision, ICD-10. For this reason the Copenhagen Male Study
==PAGE:19==
6

contains both ICD-8 [9] and ICD-10 [10] codes. ICD codes are the universal standard of identifying and
reporting diseases and health conditions for all clinical and research purposes [12]. This allows for:
• Easy storage, retrieval and analysis of health information for evidenced-based decision-making.
• Sharing and comparing health information between hospitals, regions, settings and countries.
• Data comparisons in the same location across different time periods.

1. Basic data: Dataset that includes basic biological and behavioral data about the participants.
It also includes some self reported diseases. A few irrelevant diagnosis covariates were discarded.
2. Diagnostic data: Dataset that contains all diagnoses for all participants. It also includes hospi-

2.4 Preprocessing
7
2.3.1
Basic data
This dataset contains basic data for 5245 participants collected at the start of the research study in
1971. It is collected through a questionnaire, an interview and an examination. The questionnaire can
be found in Appendix A and the names of all the original basic data covariates can be found in Appendix
B.
The questionnaire given to all the participants includes 84 multiple choice questions. The results were
translated into categorical integers. This means that the answers to a question like ’Were you unfit for
military service?’, that had the following possible answers: ’Yes’, ’No’ and ’Not conducted for military
service for other reasons’, were translated into 1, 2 and 3. The participants were also placed in different
social groups and assigned different social statuses. The basic data also contains information about the
participants body metrics. These values were not updated throughout the study and might therefore
not always be representative. A follow-up study was conducted in 1985, however the updated numbers
were not provided to us. The participants were finally asked if they previously had a heart attack or
diabetes for instance.
2.3.2
Diagnostic data
This dataset contains information about all diagnoses for all participants from year 1971 and onwards.
The diagnoses are represented as ICD-8 or ICD-10 codes. The dataset contains 158,503 entries, each
related to one diagnosis. Besides the ICD code for the given diagnosis, each entry includes the date of
hospitalization, the discharge data and an anonymous ID for the patient. The ICD codes has a high
degree of detail with up to seven characters. As the dataset contains dates for each diagnosis, it is
possible to get a complete picture of each patients disease history. We can also account for patients that
got the same diagnosis several times at different points in their life.
2.4
Preprocessing
Given the mathematical nature of machine learning algorithms we need to transform the content of the
datasets into numeric values. This requires some preprocessing as the provided datasets contain both
dates, categorical data and ICD-8/ICD-10 codes given as strings. We also need to get rid of the many
NaN values in the data. The following is a description of how we dealt with these issues.
==PAGE:21==
8

2.4.1
Basic data preprocessing
As previously described the answers to the questionnaire were translated into a numeric format. This
implies certain relations between the answers that might not be true. In the case of the military ser-
vice question, the answer 3 - ’Not conducted for military service for other reasons’ is closer to the
answer 2 - ’No’ than it is to the answer 1 - ’Yes’, which is not true. To accommodate this issue we
one-hot-encoded the questionnaire answers. One-hot-encoding transforms the data into a binary vector
with as many dimensions as there are possible outcomes. The true outcome, in this case the chosen
answer to the given question, was set to 1, and the rest of the outcomes in the vector were set to 0.
This removes the relation between the outcomes but it also increases the number of covariates in the
dataset by a factor corresponding to the average number of possible answers to the questions. We also
found that not all questions had an answer from all participants. To correct for this we added another
possible option to the one-hot vector. The extra parameter indicates that the question was not answered.
The basic data also includes important dates like date of birth. To transform these dates into numerical
values, we use a baseline date and compute the number of days since this date. We chose 1/1-1900 as
we know this date is before the first date in the dataset, and we thus avoid negative values. Another
approach could have been to translate the dates into days since birth. However, this approach would
remove information since it does not reflect the development over time when it comes to public health,
technology, medicine etc. The dates of hospitalization and discharge in the diagnostic dataset were
treated the same way as described above.
Covariates related to the participants death like day of death and cause of death were removed from
the dataset. When people are dead a disease prediction model is not relevant and these covariates are
thus useless.
The data for some of the participants were so inadequate that we decided to remove them from the
dataset. This was for done for two reasons:
1. If we found that the participant had very limited basic data.
2. If the participant had fewer than two recorded hospitalizations.
The reasoning behind 1. is that the basic data is of great importance in this project, so we could only
use participants that had it available.
The reasoning behind 2. is that there had to be at least one hospitalization that could be used as input

to the model and one hospitalization that could be used as the target. Unfortunately there were some
participants that only had 0 or 1 hospitalizations and were thus discarded from the dataset.
After the cleanup described above there were still a few NaN’s left in the basic data. These were either
set to 0 or to the mean depending on the covariate.
2.4.2
Mapping of ICD codes in the diagnostic dataset
The diagnostic dataset contained 5712 unique ICD codes represented as strings. From the beginning
we saw two issues with the ICD codes.
The first issue is that the ICD codes in the dataset have a high degree of detail. This means that many
very similar diseases gets different ICD codes because of small differences. The second issue is that in
1994 the ICD system changed from ICD-8 to ICD-10. The dataset therefore contains both ICD-8 and
ICD-10 codes, which means that the same disease can be represented in two different ways depending
on when it was diagnosed. This is an issue as it causes unnecessary complexity.
To deal with these issues, we decided to work with different ICD code mappings of different granularity.
As there did not exist a direct translation between the ICD-8 and ICD-10 codes, we had to create the
mappings ourselves. These mappings were created with the help of a doctor and a medical student.
We ended up handling the original 5712 unique ICD codes in three ways:
• We mapped the ICD codes into 269 disease categories using a coarse mapping.
• We also mapped the 5712 ICD codes into 3429 categories using a fine mapping.
• We finally used the pure ICD codes as they were, thus dealing with 5712 different diseases.
The mappings puts ICD codes for similar diseases in the same groups. This both reduces the complexity
of the problem and lets us bridge the gap between the ICD-8 and ICD-10 system.
The disease prediction models are meant as a tool for the doctors and should just serve as guidance. In
this setting it is often enough to tell the doctor that the patient might have pulmonary tuberculosis for
example. It is not needed to know which of the 70 subcategories of the disease the patient has. As long
as the doctor is guided in the right direction, the doctor can quickly find the right diagnosis. With this
in mind the 269 categories in the coarse mapping might be enough to help the doctor. A model that
can correctly predict pure ICD codes is of course preferred. However, with the available data that is
not possible to achieve. After the mapping the diseases were one-hot-encoded.

2.4.3
Normalization of the data
The preprocessed dataset contains many different covariates with different value ranges. As stated in
Chapter 1 the models we use for this project are based on neural networks. In neural networks the
input is combined linearly which means that, in theory, the network should be able to account for the
different scales with the weights and biases. However, in order to decrease training time we decided
to normalize the data, so the input features have a similar scale. We standardize the basic data by
removing the mean and dividing with the standard deviation. The same is done for the dates associated
with the hospitalizations. However, for those we only calculate the mean and standard deviation of
the training data and use those to normalize the test data. This is done to avoid information leaking
between training and test data [13].

Methods and Theory
As mentioned previously deep learning is the main focus of this project when it comes to the models.
However, to get a comparison we also train and test a random forest model.
This chapter covers the theory behind the different algorithms and explains the most important equa-
tions. To fully understand the strengths and weaknesses of this project, it is important to understand
the underlying algorithms. This chapter focuses on ”textbook theory” while Chapter 4 focuses on the
more practical aspects and shows how machine learning and deep learning has been used in other studies.
3.1
Reasons behind the model choices
The goal of this project is to build a model that can predict a future disease.
The time aspect is
therefore important and the model has to handle that well. As discussed in Doctor AI [1] recurrent
neural networks are currently the preferred model for handling longitudinal data. The article mentions
continuous-time Markov chain based models [18] and Hawkes processes [19] as alternatives that both
fall short compared to RNNs.
The LSTM is an evolution of the standard RNN and solves some of its issues [20].
The LSTM is
therefore a good model choice for this problem.
The following will first explain the theory behind
simple feedforward artificial neural networks, then move on to RNNs and in the end LSTMs.
Afterwards there is an explanation of classification and regression tree models with focus on random
forest.
Both LSTMs and random forests can handle multi-label classification which is what we do in this project.
3.2
Artificial Neural Networks (ANN)
An artificial neural network is a network of linked artificial neurons. In order to properly understand
neural networks we need to understand the concept of an artificial neuron [21].

When we want to use the neural network to predict a class or a value, we forward propagate the input
through the network as explained in the previous section. The output layer then outputs either the
probability distribution for the classes or other values in the case of regression. Since the initial weights
of the network are randomly chosen, they will not result in a great model. Therefore we need a way to
measure how wrong the model is and what weights should be changed to improve the model. We call
this process training of the network.

on majority voting of all the trees in the forest. Random forest has shown high performance and is thus
suitable for our analysis [13].
3.5.4
Multi-label and probability output random forest
The theory described above is related to single-label random forest. However, random forest can be
adapted to also handle multi-label problems which is the main focus of this project. The adaptation
from single-label to multi-label is done by the one-vs-all approach [26]. This means that one classifier
is fitted for each class against all the other classes. The input is thus passed through all the classifiers
to determine for each label, if the input should be associated with it or not.
Random forest can also be used to give probabilities for the input belonging to each class. This is
done by counting the fraction of observations with different classes in all the leafs of the trees, and also
counting how many of the trees in the forest that predicts a given label. The probabilities are then given
as the mean predicted class probabilities of the trees in the forest. The class probability of a single tree
is the fraction of samples of the same class in a leaf [27].
To compare the results of the random forest model to the results of the LSTM model the probabilities
are used, since the LSTM provides probabilities by design.

with autoencoders is to match the information in the input data but using fewer variables/dimensions.
This is similar to the idea of PCA for instance.
The autoencoders used are so-called denoising autoencoders. This means that the input data is cor-
rupted by changing random values to 0. This is done to avoid that the network simply learns an identity
of the input. By corrupting the input the network gets challenged and ends up with more robust fea-

5.1
Neural networks
The neural network models we trained all aimed to predict the diseases the patients would get at their
next hospitalization. The inputs to the models all had more or less the same structure: A sequence of
diseases combined with the relevant timestamps and the basic data about a patient. For each patient
the input data was stored as a matrix with a row for each hospitalization the patient had during the
study. The rows with the diseases that we wanted to predict were extracted as the test data. This was
often the last row, as we wanted to predict what happened at the last hospitalization. The remaining
rows in the matrix were recurrently fed into the RNN or LSTM network.
The diseases in the data could be represented either by their ICD-8 or ICD-10 codes, as a coarse map-
ping of the ICD codes to large disease groups or finally as a fine mapping of the ICD codes to smaller
disease groups. These mappings were explained in chapter 2.
The target of the model was the diseases the patients received at their next hospitalization. The target

at the top 10, 20 and 30 ranked diseases. The ranking is done based on the sigmoid scores
from the neural network. The recall is the same as the true positive rate (TPR). The recall
is high as long as the patients’ diseases occur in top 10, 20 and 30. The Recall@30 is for
instance 100% if a patient’s two next diseases occur at position 28 and 29.

6.6
Patient specific predictions
In the previous sections the models have been evaluated as the mean over all the tested patients. In
this section a few patients have been picked out to show how the model fares for specific patients. Their
results are shown in the two tables on the following pages. The first table shows patients with good
predictions, and the second table shows patients where the model did not do so well.
6.6.1
Good predictions
The model did well on many types of patients. Two of the main types are patients where the model
predicted diseases they have had many times previously and patients where the model predicts the most
common diseases. Both are two good result types as it shows that the model learned what the most
common diseases are, and that people often tend to get the same diseases more than once.
The patients in the table are a bit different. Patient 4849 is for instance diagnosed with lung cancer
even though he had not previously had that disease. Looking more into the patient shows that he was a
heavy smoker. This again shows how useful basic data can be when doing disease predictions. Patient
4849 also has a history of respiration problems which the model also might have used.
Patient 5211 is correctly predicted to have Chronic respiratory disease even though it is not a part of
==PAGE:78==
6.6 Patient specific predictions
65
his disease history. Patient 5211 is also a heavy smoker. In this case it can be assumed that the correct
diagnosis is mainly made based on the basic data. Patient 2380 has good predictions that also include
diseases that he has not previously had. The model is able to for instance link several diagnoses that
affect the heart together.
These results show that even with the limited amount of patients in the dataset, it was possible to make
a decent model. The highlighted patients show that the model has learned some important connections.
Both between the different diseases but also between the diseases and the basic data. The model is still
not perfect though. This can be seen in the next section.
6.6.2
Bad predictions
The patients with the worst predictions all have one thing in common: The true disease is a rare di-
agnosis in the dataset. The model thus have a hard time learning from the disease histories, as the
diagnoses rarely appear. The basic data was not helpful either in theses cases.
In the table with the poor predictions the true last disease is seen together with the disease history for
the patients. The patient specific AUC is also shown. These are all very low which translates to the
true diseases being at the bottom of the disease prediction list.
In the table it can for instance be seen that counselling is the true ”disease” for patient 2946. There can
be many reasons for a patient to get a code for counselling and it can therefore be difficult to predict.
As his last disease patient 3821 is diagnosed with mental retardation. This is also a rare diagnosis, and
since it is not part of the patients previous history, it makes sense that it is hard to predict. Mental re-
tardation is something that might be easier to correctly predict if genome information or family history

Discussion
This chapter will discuss the results and models of chapter 6. It will also include suggestions on how to
further improve on the model, and what the next steps in the process should be.
This project attempted to answer a few questions that will be discussed in the following:
1. Is it possible to build a model that can predict the next disease of a patient?
2. And if so, how good is it?
3. Is the best model the simple RNN or the more advanced LSTM?
4. Does it make any difference to include the basic data of the patients?
5. Does the model scale well with the amount of data it is trained on?
7.1
Performance of the model
The best final models can be seen in Table 6.17. Comparing these results to the baselines show that
the models can outperform the majority voting predictions by a satisfying margin. This alone answers
question one in the list above. It is indeed possible to make a predictive model that can learn from past
diseases and predict the next one.
In regard to how good they are, the results vary. As seen in chapter 6 the results are largely dependent
on which mapping is used to preprocess the data. The coarse mapped data proved to produce the best
performing model, which is not surprising as it deals with the least amount of categories.
The LSTM model trained on the coarse mapping was on average able to predict the correct disease at
guess 22. This is a decent result and shows that the model is able to capture connections in the data.
7.1.1
Results compared to Doctor AI
The recall values are similar to the ones of Doctor AI [1]. In Doctor AI 1183 disease categories are
used where the coarse mapping only has 269 disease categories. This means that Doctor AI has around

263,706 patients which is a lot more than was available during this project. Due to the different sized
datasets it is difficult to make any direct comparisons between Doctor AI and this project in terms of
performance. The patients in the Doctor AI article also had a lot more hospitalizations on average and
had more diseases diagnosed. This means that the substance in their data is more rich than the data

7.1.2
RNN vs. LSTM
Looking back at chapter 6 the results of the LSTM are always better than their RNN counterparts. An
LSTM model with a cell state of 0 is very similar to an RNN. It is therefore no surprise that the LSTM
outperforms the RNN. Our results show that the LSTM is at times only slightly better than the RNN.
However, it is safe to imagine that with a more complex dataset with larger medical records, the long
term memory of the LSTM will prove even more important than at this stage. Thus the LSTM is to be
preferred over the simple RNN when doing diagnosis predictions.
7.1.3
Basic data vs. no basic data.
The dataset used in this report is superior in terms of the Doctor AI data when it comes to basic data
about the patients. Using the basic data of the patients was one of the main objectives of this project.
The basic data is fed into the network together with the current diseases at each time step for each
patient. As seen in for instance Table 6.17 using the basic data improves the results. This is especially
the case for the coarse mapped LSTM model which is of main interest. The AUC fell from 0.922 to
0.899 when the basic data was removed. This is a large drop considering the general AUC range we
operate with. The difference was not as pronounced with the fine mapping, but the results were also in
this case better with the basic data.
This is a very promising result which suggests that with further research and more and better basic
data, it will be possible to make the disease prediction models even better.

The scalability of the model
Section 6.2.8 showed an improved performance as more patients were used to train the model. On figure

model could be even better than what it already is, if data from more patients were available. Larger

Overall the results with the basic data are very promising. Gathering the mentioned extra basic data

models and techniques that may or may not be able to even further improve the results.

7.4.1
Attention networks
As explained in chapter 3 the LSTM network is an extension of the simple RNN. LSTMs are superior
to RNNs when it comes to ”remembering” information from many time steps ago. This often makes
LSTMs better than RNNs which was also the case in this project. Attention networks might be able
to do even better than the LSTM [40].
The last state of an LSTM and RNN layer depends only directly on the previous state, which then again
depends on the previous state and so forth. Even though the LSTM has the cell state to effectively
capture information from the first input states, they might still be too far back in the sequence to have
the impact they should at the current state.
This is the problem attention networks solve. When producing an output they do not only consider
the previous hidden state, but a weighted combination of all the previous hidden states. The network
can then decide to pay attention to the states that are relevant for the current prediction. This is
for instance useful when doing translations, as sentences can often not be translated word for word.
Sometimes the last word in one language might correspond to the first word in another language. RNNs
and LSTMs would have trouble dealing with this problem. The attention network however, can just
decide to pay attention to the relevant word when it is needed [41]. Attention mechanisms have also
proven useful when dealing with images. They can for instance make accurate captions and are also
easier to interpret than other recurrent networks [42]. Attention networks can therefore in some cases
be superior to LSTMs and might be able to produce even better disease prediction models.
7.4.2
Embedding
As explained in chapter 5 the data samples were fed directly into the LSTM network. In the Doctor AI
article [1] they use a skip-gram model to learn a good representation of the data and use the weights
of this model as initial weights to a layer before the RNN. They call this layer the embedding layer. It
is used to find a lower dimensional representation of the input before the input is fed to the recurrent
cells. The skip-gram model were originally used as an efficient way of finding vector representations that
capture a large number of precise syntactic and semantic word relationships [29]. Since the hypothesis
of the Doctor AI project is to predict the next disease and medicine based on the previous diseases and
medicines used, this problem can be interpreted as the same kind of problem as predicting the next word
in a sentence. Another way of reducing the dimensionality of the input sample is to use an autoencoder.
An autoencoder is trained to copy the input through a hidden layer that is supposed to represent the
input as precisely as possible using fewer dimensions [43].
==PAGE:86==

73
The mappings used in this project can be compared to these embeddings. Other kinds of embeddings and
dimensionality reduction techniques could be used to maybe increase training speed and performance.
7.4.3
Other techniques to try
Several techniques exists for optimizing a neural network.
One way of optimizing a model that is
overfitting is through regularization. A popular regularization method is the L2 regularization that is
also known as weight decay. If a model is using weight decay the weights will decrease with a percent
of their value for each iteration. The decrease in the weights is determined by a parameter that is often
set such that the weights do not go towards zero too fast [44]. We see some overfitting when training
the model so it could be useful to utilize weight decay. Another popular regularization technique is a
dropout layer [45]. A layer with dropout will randomly choose some neurons and set their output to
zero. This technique has also proven useful for decreasing overfitting. As mentioned earlier dropout did
not improve our models’ performance. However, with more data and more complex networks dropout
might prove useful.
As explained in chapter 3 the use of mini-batches is an important part of training an artificial neural
network. We used mini-batches of size 1. This allows the model to adjust for changes in every single
data point. Using larger mini-batches could influence the training time and overfitting issue.
7.5
Interpretability of the model
One of the main issues with deep learning models is their limited interpretability. The networks often
consist of millions of weights connected in complex ways. We can observe that the model does its job
well, we just do not know why. This is also an issue with the prediction model created in this project.
In chapter 6 a few patients were highlighted and it was attempted to interpret their results. These
interpretations were mostly based on intuition and were not directly linked to the actual model.
Several ways of interpreting deep neural networks have been explored in the literature [46]. Heatmappings
are a way of looking at image classification networks and understanding what features of the image the
model deems important when doing the classification [47]. This is a very useful tool and makes the
model a lot more transparent.
At the current state of this projects prediction model, it is possible to help the doctors diagnose patients.
It is not possible based on the model to tell the patients why they got the given disease. Being able to
do that could be very useful.
Different techniques have been attempted to solve this problem. You can for instance look at specific

neurons and determine what input/output combination activates it [48]. However, as they conclude in
the mentioned article ”Interpretability of neural networks remains an important challenge in machine
learning”. So for now we have to accept that they work really well, but acknowledge that we do not
have a good way of interpreting them.
7.5.1
Interpretability of the results
For most of the results in chapter 6 the standard deviations are large. This means that the model is
sometimes very good at predicting and sometimes it is very poor. As mentioned above some of the
result interpretations were based on intuition. It would be very valuable to let a doctor look at the
results and help with the interpretations, and thus understand in what situations the model is actually
good. It is as mentioned still difficult to interpret the deep neural networks. Until better methods are
developed a more manual approach to the interpretation might let us learn when the model is useful
and when you can not use it.
7.6
Future work
As the experiments in this report revolves around a somewhat small and limited dataset, it would be
very interesting to do similar experiments on larger datasets with more rich basic data. The current
state of the model is not good enough for commercialization. Access to more and richer data is needed
in order to get closer to the point where doctors can use a model like this as an important part of their
job. To further increase the performance of the model, it would be interesting to also introduce other
types of data into the model. This could for instance be chat data where the patient provide information
about current symptoms. It could also be genome and family data as mentioned earlier. Combining all
different types of data into one single model is the final goal. This project is a step in the right direction,
and shows that it is possible to build a predictive disease model and that basic data is important, even
at the quality it is available here.

Conclusion
The attempt to use a deep learning LSTM model to make disease predictions showed successful.
By mapping ICD codes into 269 disease categories it was possible to achieve a recall@10 = 58.3%,
recall@20 = 70.0%, recall@30 = 76.7% and an AUC = 0.922 which corresponds to the model making a
correct prediction on average at the 22nd predicted disease. Removing the basic data and only using
the medical history the results declined to: recall@10 = 45.2%, recall@20 = 61.9%, recall@30 = 71.0%,
AUC = 0.899 and #Avg. guess = 27.9.
The analysis showed that basic data of the patients, as well as more patients are both important in order
to make a good model. We also learned that the results are highly dependent on the type of ICD code
mapping used. With more data it might be possible to operate with more granular disease categories.
The model performs best at the later stages in the patients’ life which is also caused by more data being
available at that point in time. The model performs well on common diseases but struggles with rare
diseases or short medical records.
Overall the results are promising and a step in the right direction. With further development and better
and more rich data, it may be possible to build a model that can predict diseases with a very high
accuracy. A model that in the future will help doctors make faster and more accurate diagnostics.
