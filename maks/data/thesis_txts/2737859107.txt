Abstract
In this thesis the solvent effects on the back-reaction of a molecular solar thermal (MOST)
energy storage molecule has been investigated in five different solvents. The solvents are
acetone, acetonitrile, dichloromethane, toluene and dimethylsulfoxide. In order to calculate
the reaction rate, transition state theory has been used. Two different levels of theory have
been applied to correct for the solvents effect on the dynamics of the reaction, and these
theories are Kramer’s theory and Grote-Hynes theory. Initially, the correction from Kramer’s
theory used a crude estimate of the molecules dimensions; however, results from this grossly
overestimated the effect of solvents. By performing molecular dynamics simulations, the
diffusion coefficient for the molecule in different solvents could be calculated, and the friction
could be calculated. This result was used to calculate the correction to the reaction rate
using Kramer’s theory. Similarly, to calculate the correction for Grote-Hynes theory, a set
of parameters already available were used. To further the investigation the time-dependent
friction was calculated by molecular dynamics simulation, which could be used to calculate
the solvent correction in Grote-Hynes theory. It was seen that dimethylsulfoxide had the
highest correction to the rate, and acetone had the lowest, a result that follows a trend with
viscosity. As toluene had the slowest reaction rate, and the second highest correction to the
rate it has been proposed that the ideal solvent for the reaction to take place in is toluene.

Introduction
Since the advent of the industrial revolution, there has been an increase in the energy con-
sumption of the world with growing population [1]. Therefore, novel and alternative sources
of energy are imperative to meet the demands of the general population. While fossil fuels
are an excellent source of energy, they contribute to vast amounts of greenhouse gases that
are detrimental to the world, and lead to climate change [2]. As a result there is a growing
concern about greenhouse gases, which has led to an increased interest in finding renew-
able sources of energy, that can mitigate the detrimental effects that conventional energy
has had on the climate [3]. While there already exists different types of renewable energy
such as solar energy and wind energy [4], exploiting chemistry may also lead to discoveries
of molecules that can aid in the production of clean energy. The manufacture and use of
these molecules, must however follow the principles of green chemistry. The field of green
chemistry follows 12 principles that constitutes identifying the risks and hazards of working
within chemistry, and strives to eliminate detrimental effects to the environment within the
workings of chemistry [5].
A core tenet of the 12 principles is to design processes that minimize energy consumption
[6]. Therefore not only safer syntheses are necessary in green chemistry, but using clean
and renewable types of energy are necessitated. Some of the green energy sources that have
garnered much interest is the field of photovoltaics, that is solar cells that can convert solar
radiation to renewable energy [7]. However, the efficiency of these technologies is low, require
large amounts of energy in the production, and has various problems such as insensitivity
towards moisture [8].
In this thesis a molecule is presented, which presents the possibility to store energy in a
metastable isomer when irradiated by visible light, and subsequently release the stored energy
when either heated or treated by a catalyst and revert back to its stable isomer [9]. This type
of molecule is known as a molecular solar thermal (MOST) energy storage system. Obviously,
this presents an interesting contribution to renewable energy sources, but without knowing
the amount of time the molecule is able to store energy, the interest becomes muddled. The
goal of this thesis has therefore been to investigate the dynamics of the back-reaction of
the molecule in a range of different solvents. In order to further investigate the effects of
solvents on the back-reaction, two different levels of theory have been applied with varying
degrees of complexity. These two theories are the Kramer’s theory [10] and the Grote-Hynes
theory [11]. In order to accurately investigate the effects of solvents, molecular dynamics
simulations have been performed, and compared with results from similar investigations in
other articles.
The first chapter provides an introduction to the different theories used in this thesis, which
includes an overview of transition state theory, density functional theory and molecular
dynamics. The second chapter presents the reaction that is of interest. The third chapter
describes the methods that have been used to gather the results, which are presented in
chapter four.
Chapter five presents a short discussion of the results of this thesis, and
chapter six and seven serves to present a conclusion and some ideas in case further work on
the results of this thesis are of interest.

1.1
Transition State Theory
Transition state theory, also called the activated-complex theory, is a theory developed to
understand the dynamics of reactions. The theory was first developed in 1935 by Henry
Eyring [12], and has extensively been used for bimolecular reactions [13]. RRKM theory
[14], named after Rice, Ramsperger, Kassel and Marcus is in principle transition state theory
applied to unimolecular reactions, rather than bimolecular reactions. The overall scheme for
a reaction investigated in RRKM theory is presented below:
Figure 1: Potential energy surface and energetics in RRKM theory
In figure 1, E0 represents the difference in energy between the zero-point energy of the
reactant, A, and the activated complex A‡. Throughout this thesis the terms "activated
complex" and "transition state" will be used to refer to the same species. The RRKM theory
has several associated assumptions, two of which are the same assumptions from transition
state theory. The assumptions are as follows: The reaction coordinate can be separated from
the other degrees of freedom, and classical dynamics are assumed at the reaction coordinate,
additionally no recrossings are allowed, and finally any way of partitioning the total energy
of the activated complex and the translational energy of the reaction coordinate has the
same probability.
The assumptions have several consequences for the calculation of the

rate constant. The first assumption that the reaction coordinate can be separated, implies
that the Hamiltonian of the transition state can be separated into a part corresponding
the Hamiltonian of the translational motion, and a Hamiltonian of the internal degrees
of freedom. The Hamiltonian of the transition state is defined as the total energy of the
transition state. The Hamiltonian of the translational motion corresponds to the motion
along the reaction coordinate, and is treated as free motion described by classical dynamics.
The second assumption, that classical dynamics are assumed at the reaction coordinate,
means that the average velocity is assumed to be constant, and moving in the positive
direction.
In transition state the theory movement in the positive direction, should be
understood as movement going only from the reactants to the products. This gives rise to
the third assumption, which is that no recrossings are allowed. As the average velocity is
only moving in the positive direction, any velocity moving towards the reactants is neglected;
therefore, no recrossings can occur. Lastly, that any way of partitioning the total energy
is equally probable implies that a thermal equilibrium has been reached, and that the rate
constant is independent of time [15].

1.3
Kramer’s Theory and Grote-Hynes Theory
One of the assumptions of RRKM theory implies that no recrossings are allowed, this as-
sumptions carries with it that the reaction moves only in the direction of the product, and
therefore no movement back towards the reactants occurs. It is possible that the theory
holds for the reaction rate in the gaseous phase; however, if a solvent is introduced, the
assumption of no recrossings does not theoretically hold up very well. Solvents have a di-
electric constant, and additionally introduce a friction. Intuitively the friction should hinder
the reaction somewhat as an increased friction would impose more forces on the system, and
in order to investigate what effect the solvent has on the dynamics of the reaction, several
theories have been proposed. The solvent correcting theories that have been investigated
in this thesis are Kramer’s theory [10] and the Grote-Hynes theory [11]. The theories treat
the effect of the solvent differently, with Kramer’s theory assuming a constant friction, and
Grote-Hynes theory assuming a time-dependent friction.
1.3.1

equation and the Fokker-Planck equation. Kramer’s theory is built upon the assumption of
Brownian motion in a particle and assumes that the friction imposed upon the particle from

1.4
Density Functional Theory
In order to optimize the geometry of the molecule and calculate its associated frequencies,
density functional theory (DFT) has been employed. DFT is an electronic structure method
that proposes that the ground state electronic energy can be explained by the electron
density [21] [22]. The theory was developed by Pierre Hohenberg and Walter Kohn [23][21].
The theory is built upon two theorems, the first theorem is that the energy is calculated
from a unique electron density, and the second theorem is that only the exact ground state
electron density can give the exact ground state energy. By applying these theorems functions
of functions, also called functionals, can be constructed that can determine the ground
state energy from the electron density. While traditional wave-function methods increase
in complexity as the number of electrons increase, DFT is independent of the number of
electrons, and thus makes it much easier to determine the ground state energy with increasing
complexity [21].

1.4.1
Functionals
As mentioned DFT is carried out using functionals.
There are many different function-
als, and a simplified view is that the more complex the functional the better the energy is
approximated using DFT. The different types of methods to construct functionals are the
Local Density Approximation (LDA), Generalized Gradient Approximation (GGA), Meta-
GGA methods, Hybrid-GGA methods, and double hybrid methods [21]. The various types
of functionals are often arranged in what is called the Jacob’s ladder, where each rung of
the ladder represent a type of method, and the higher the rung a more accurate description
of the energy can be obtained [26][27]. The lowest level is the LDA functionals, and the
highest the double hybrid GGA functionals. The functionals that are employed in this thesis
are all on the fourth rung of the ladder, and are the so called Hybrid-GGA functionals.
Hybrid-GGA functionals are an extension of GGA functionals, this means that they em-
ploy a combination of LDA, a gradient approximation term as well as a Hartree-Fock (HF)
exchange term [21]. These functionals often offer a large improvement to the calculation
of thermochemical properties, and description of the potential energy surface. One of the
most well known Hybrid-GGA functionals is the B3LYP functional which was developed by
Axel D. Becke and Chengteh Lee, Weitao Yang, and Robert G. Parr [28], [29]. However,
more recent advancements have been made to find even better fitting Hybrid-GGA func-
tionals. These functionals also include range-separation methods to account for long-range
and short-range interactions, which are not explicitly (or implicitly) defined in the classic
Hybrid-GGA functionals. Inclusion of range-separation methods can fix issues related with
overdelocalizing electron density as well as mitigating self-interaction errors. Additionally
dispersion correction can be added to many of the Hybrid-GGA functionals, and accounts
for some of the interactions missing in DFT calculations to provide an even more complete
picture of the potential energy surface[21].

1.6
Molecular Dynamics
In order to derive the mass-weighted friction, γ and the time-dependent friction, γ(τ), it is
not sufficient to only analyze DFT calculations, inherently these calculations are performed
ab initio, and do not posses any time element. The system must therefore be studied using
molecular simulation, to incorporate time propagation. There are different simulation tech-
niques that can be used, these are Monte Carlo (MC) simulations, which uses a probabilistic
determination to generate different configurations of the system[33], and there are Molecular
Dynamics (MD) simulations, which integrates the classic equations of motions to determine
the various properties that are of interest. In this thesis MD simulations have been used to
investigate the dynamics of the back-reaction. In order to calculate the forces and the system
dynamics, MD simulations uses force fields and various numerical integration schemes[34].
In this section the relevant theory of molecular dynamics is presented, and lastly a short
summary of the combined method for MD simulations is presented.

1.6.2
Ensembles
There are several steps involved in MD simulations.
The system must be relaxed such
that the structure does not explode, the system must be equilibrated in order to be run
in a particular ensemble or state, so the production of the properties can be calculated in
the final step[34]. Depending on what step is performed, different ensembles are used [34].
In MD simulations, the ensemble that is chosen constitutes what properties are kept fixed
during the simulation, and can be the number of particles, the volume of the simulation
box, the temperature, the pressure or the energy. The canonical ensemble keeps the number
of particles, the volume and the energy fixed, and is denoted NVT [35],[17]. It is therefore
useful to use during the equilibration step of the simulation, that is the point at which
the temperature is fixed to for example room temperature, and the energy is allowed to
minimize.
Another useful ensemble is the isothermal-isobaric ensemble, which keeps the
number of particles, the pressure and the temperature fixed and is also referred to as the NPT
ensemble [35][17]. The NVT ensemble uses thermostats to keep the energy fixed, while the
NPT ensemble keeps uses both thermostats and barostats to keep pressure and temperature
fixed during the simulation. Once the system has been equilibrated, it is then useful to
switch to the microcanonical ensemble. The microcanonical ensemble, also known as NVE,
keeps the energy fixed instead of the temperature. The energy is allowed to fluctuate around
the minimized energy of the system, and a more concise determination of the trajectories
can be made [35],[17].

Periodic Boundary Conditions
A real system which contains all atoms and molecules is very large, and thus it is close to
computationally impossible to calculate the properties of the real system. To mitigate this,
periodic boundary conditions are used in simulations to represent the real system [34],[35].
The amount of atoms in a simulation is usually in the order of a few thousands, and compared
to a real system this is only a microscopical representation of the real system. Implementing
periodic boundary conditions, can be thought as surrounding the simulated system by an
infinite number of copies of the simulated systems. The atoms in the simulated system are
allowed to move freely between the simulated system and its copies, and there are therefore
no hard walls, in order to ensure that the boundary does not influence the properties of the
system. A schematic illustration of periodic boundary conditions are presented below:

In order to solve the equations of motions it is necessary to calculate the potential energy,
from which also the forces can be calculated. In MD simulations, the potential energy is
calculated using what is known as a force field [34]. Depending on what type of simulation

calculate the energy associated with the bending motions of the molecule, the dihedral angles
are associated with the energy of torsion, and the Leonard-Jones and Coulomb potential is
a way of calculating the inter-molecular potential energy, that is the potential of interaction

bonded interactions are accounted for in a sum of a Leonard-Jones potential and Coulomb
potential [42].
1.6.7
Full Method
Combining the various elements of above, simulations with molecular dynamics can be per-
formed. It is imperative to make sure that initially the correct ensemble is chosen for the
equilibration run. This will ensure that the properties that are of interest can be correctly
calculated. In case dynamical properties are of interest, such as diffusion, the ensemble must
be chosen such that the energy has been minimized. The potential energy of the system
is calculated classically using molecular mechanics in what is known as force fields. Quan-
tum mechanical calculations provide a much larger degree of accuracy; however, the time
it takes to perform quantum mechanical calculations, compared with molecular mechanics
simulations is too large [34]. In order to keep the temperature and pressure constant, a
thermostat and a barostat must be used, of course depending on the ensemble that is used.

Once the system has been equilibrated, and it has been ensured that the different properties
that should be kept fixed are only fluctuating around a constant value, the production step
of the simulation can be engaged. In the production step a dynamic integrator, often of the
Verlet-type is chosen [34], which numerically solves Newton’s equations of motion. From
these equations of motion the trajectory of the system of interest can be extracted, and the
properties that are being investigated can be calculated.

3.2
Density Functional Theory
To optimize the structure and calculate the frequencies of the reactant and the transition
state, DFT calculations were performed. DFT calculations were additionally done for the
product, in order to calculate the energy storage capabilities. The Gaussian16 suite [49]
was employed to perform geometry optimizations and subsequent frequency calculations.
Three different functionals were used in order to compare energy storage capability, thermal
back-reaction (TBR) barrier, and the reaction rate . The functionals employed were the
CAM-B3LYP-D3 [50][51], M06-2X [52], and ωB97X-D [53] functionals. Additionally the
6-311G(d,p) [54] basis set was used, which is a Pople style basis set. The 6 indicates that
the core electrons are represented by six primitive Gaussian type orbitals (PGTO). The 311
indicates that it is a split valence basis set, that is the outer electrons are represented by
three, one and one PGTO’s.
PGTO’s can be written in the following way in Cartesian
coordinates [21][55]:
χζ,lx,ly,lz(x, y, z) = Nxlxylyzlz exp(−ζr2)
(3.2.1)
In equation 3.2.1 N represents a normalization factor, x, y, z represents Cartesian coordi-
nates, r is the distance to the nucleus, the sum of lx, ly, lz represents the type of orbital. ζ
determines the number of functions that should be used for the valence electrons, and should
therefore be increased with increasing complexity of the system investigated [21]. As is the
case with the basis set used in this thesis, the valence electrons are represented by a triple split
valence basis set, which is also known as triple zeta. The 6-311G(d,p) basis set also includes
polarization functions, this is denoted by the parenthesis (d,p). The polarization functions
can be on only non-hydrogen atoms or it can be on both hydrogen and non-hydrogen atoms,
Page 26 of 88
==PAGE:32==
3.2
Density Functional Theory
the d in the parenthesis indicates that a d-type polarization function are on non-hydrogen
atoms, and the p indicates a p-type polarization function on the hydrogen atom [21].
The CAM-B3LYP-D3 functional is an extension of the B3LYP functional, where CAM stands
for Coulomb attenuating method, and D3 stands for inclusion of dispersion correlation. The
Coulomb attenuating method is included in order to more accurately account for short and
long range interactions [50]. The dispersion correlation is accounted for using a common
DFT-D (Density functional theory with dispersion correction) scheme, but with modified
damping parameters for the dispersion correlation [51].
The ωB97X-D functional similarly introduces the long and short range interactions, as well
as dispersion correlation. The short range interactions are treated by an exchange functional,
while the long range interactions are treated by an HF exchange functional, and the ω is a
factor which controls the factor of long and short range interactions. Dispersion correlation
is calculated by inclusion of a potential energy term for dispersion in the potential energy for
DFT, and is a damping function of the interatomic distance as well as a dispersion coefficient
also accounted for with a DFT-D scheme [53].
While both the CAM-B3LYP-D3 and ωB97X-D functionals explicitly calculates the long
and short range interactions, and includes a contribution for the dispersion correlation in
the calculation of the energy, the last functional, M06-2X does not. The M06-2X functional
consists of many parameters, and in the construction of these parameters long and short
range interactions are accounted for, so while it does not explicitly define a a factor for these
interaction, it is built upon parameters where it is included. Unlike the CAM-B3LYP-D3 and
ωB97X-D functional, the M06-2X functional does not include any correction for dispersion,
although there is what is referred to as "dispersion-like" correction in one of the parameters
used in the construction of the functional[52].

3.2.1
Polarizable Continuum Model
There are different methods of solvating molecular structures computationally within Gaus-
sian16, and in this thesis a polarizable continuum model (PCM) was used, where the shape of
the solvent molecules is neglected, and instead the solvent is considered a contniuus medium
with a dielectric constant. The PCM method of modelling the solvent was originally pro-
posed by Miertus et. al. [58], but has subsequently been named the DPCM (Dielectric)PCM,
as other PCM like models have also emerged [59]. The specific PCM that was used in the
calculations is the integral equation formalism type, also denoted IEFPCM. IEFPCM was
first introduced in 1997 by Cancés et. al. [60], and has the advantage that it is also able
to accurately model anisotropic solvents, and not only isotropic solvents [60][59][61]. The
PCM methods uses Coulombs law to model the potential energy between to particles of
different charges, thus the potential is inversely proportional to the dielectric constant, and
with increasing dielectric constant the potential should decrease [15].

partition function was equal to one. In the Gaussian file it was identified that two potential
wells were accessible which meant that the vibrational partition function should be multiplied
by two.

the hindered rotor approximation, it is the rate that has been used when applying the var-

constant, but it is not certain whether this trend is also observed for the transmission co-

Discussion
Looking at the results from the MD simulations used to calculate the transmission coefficient
using either Kramer’s theory or Grote-Hynes theory, it appears that the MD simulations
represents more accurate transmission coefficients than the transmission coefficients found
by Kramer’s theory with an approximation for the hydrodynamic radius or by using the
analytical fit from [9] and there are several reasons why this must be the case.
A clear reason for the difference is that for each investigated solvent, the specific forces and
velocities have been calculated, and the results from these calculations have been used to
find solutions that are unique to the specific molecule in that solvent. Instead of relying
on the relatively simple Stoke’s law to calculate the friction on the molecule, the actual
friction imposed on the molecule in the solvent has been found, by finding the diffusion of
the molecule in solvent. Therefore no approximations to the hydrodynamic radius of the
molecule has been made. Assuming that the geometry of the molecule is either spherical or
a prolate spheroid leads to erroneous results, that grossly overestimates the solvent effect on
the dynamics of the back-reaction, as was seen when the transmission coefficient in Kramer’s
theory was first calculated. While it is possible to use Stoke’s law to find the friction, having
to find the hydrodynamic radius of the molecule without doing any assumptions to the shape
of the molecule seems to be much too time consuming, especially when relatively simple MD
simulations performed made it possible to find the diffusion and use that to directly compute
the friction.
Similarly, for the calculation of the Grote-Hynes transmission coefficient, the parameters
that are used in [9], are obtained from a different article by by Angulo et.
al [64].
In
this article different dye molecules were investigated, and the parameters necessary for the
analytical fit were found by performing spectroscopy and a simulation. An analytical fit for
the time-dependent friction which did not involve any oscillatory parts was proposed, where
it was assumed that the oscillations were small enough to be neglected. This assumption
was in the article supported by various experimental results using spectroscopic analysis. It
is possible that if the parameters proposed in the article were found similarly to the ones in
this thesis, then the parameters are not only dependent on the solvents but also the solute,
and the analytical fit is then not independent of what solute is investigated.
Obviously, the dye molecules are quite different to the photoswitches investigated by Hillers-
Bendtsen et. al. [9] and the photoswitch in this thesis, so using parameters that were fit
to different types of dye does not seem appropriate in the investigation of solvent effects
on the reaction rate for the photoswitches. The inclusion of the oscillatory behavior of the
time-dependent friction, is thus necessary in order to arrive at the most correct results for
the transmission coefficient. By comparing the half-life of the metastable conformer it is also
possible to illustrate the effect solvents has on the reaction.

of theory, are quite close to one - the upper limit - and the effect of the solvent on the
dynamics of the back-reaction is not as large as what was initially thought. Therefore, it is

Conclusion
The effect of different solvents on the back reaction of a photoswitch isomerization reaction
have been investigated. In order to elucidate the effect that solvents play on the reaction
dynamics, the reaction rate using standard RRKM theory has been compared with two
different solvent correcting theories. These theories are the well-known Kramer’s theory and
the Grote-Hynes theory.
To make an initial estimate of the solvent effect, the standard
result from Kramer’s theory was used, where the hydrodynamic radius of the molecule was
approximated to be a prolate spheroid, the friction was approximated using Stoke’s law,
and the transmission coefficient, that is the solvent correcting factor, could be calculated
and multiplied with the result for the reaction rate from RRKM. The result was used as an
initial approximation, but showed an overestimation of the solvent effect on the dynamics.
The investigation was then expanded by using an analytical fit to the friction, which could
be used to calculate the Grote-Hynes transmission coefficient. The analytical fit that was
initially used had been found from a simulation performed by Angulo et. al. [64]. The
Grote-Hynes solvent correction showed a much smaller effect on the reaction rate, albeit
not a negligible one. Additionally, from a simple comparison between the two correction
factors it was seen that Kramer’s theory represented a lower limit to the reaction rate. To
investigate the solvent effect for all the solvents, and not just the two solvents which had been
parameterized by Angulo et. al., that is acetonitrile and dimethylsulfoxide, an MD simulation
of the molecule investigated in this thesis was performed. From the MD simulation it was
possible to calculate the diffusion coefficient of the solute in different solvents, as well as the
time-dependent friction on the solute in different solvents.
These two results allowed for a better estimate of the transmission coefficient from Kramer’s
theory and Grote-Hynes theory. The transmission coefficient found for Kramer’s theory,
using MD simulations, showed a much smaller overestimation of the solvent effect, but it
was still smaller than the transmission coefficient found using Grote-Hynes theory.
The
transmission coefficient for Grote-Hynes theory was estimated using an analytical fit to
the time-dependent friction, and a numerical solution. The numerical solution displayed
a trend that followed the dielectric constant of the solvent, meaning that higher dielectric
constants had a smaller transmission coefficient. The analytical solution displayed a smaller
transmission coefficient with higher viscosity, a trend similar to the one observed for Kramer’s
theory.
After adjusting for the solvent effect on the reaction rate, the slowest reaction,
excluding the gas-phase reaction, was the one in toluene. As toluene has the second highest
viscosity, it was expected to have one of the smallest transmission coefficients, additionally
toluene has the smallest dielectric constant of the solvents investigated, which meant that the
reaction rate calculated was the slowest. The calculated half-life of the meta-stable isomer
in toluene was about 20 seconds, compared to the calculated half-life of the meta-stable
isomer in dimethylsulfoxide which was about 0.0045 seconds. As the molecule investigated
has been proposed as a photoswitch, that is a molecule that should be able to store energy
when irradiated by UV-visible light, it does not present itself as a suitable candidate if it
can be experimentally proven that the molecule has this incredibly short half-life, given the
range of solvents the back-reaction has been investigated in.

Further Work
While several theories have been applied to investigate the dynamics of the back-reaction,
there is much further work that could be done to enhance the conclusion of this thesis.
The molecule that has been investigated and proposed in this thesis, has been proposed
by Hillers-Bendtsen et. al. [46]. The transition state that has been used, is a guess for
the transition state, and in order to confirm that the transition state is in fact the actual
transition state it is insufficient to only look at the TBR and imaginary frequency, and
from this deduce that it is the associated transition state. Instead an Intrinsic Reaction
Coordinate (IRC) calculation should be performed in order to confirm it as the transition
state. Several attempts were made for the IRC calculation, however the calculation was never
able to converge. As mentioned, the transmission coefficient has been calculated using the
imaginary frequency and the time-dependent friction. In the derivation of the transmission
coefficient for Grote-Hynes theory, the forces of the reaction coordinate should be used to
calculate the time-dependent friction coefficient rather than the center of mass forces. By
performing normal-mode analysis, it would be possible to identify the reaction coordinate,
and calculate the forces of the reaction coordinate instead. This result could lead to an either
lower or a higher transmission coefficient; in any way a more precise estimate of the solvent
effects would be obtained. Additionally the analysis of the solvent effects on the molecule
using MD simulations, was only performed for the M06-2X functional, and not of the two
other functionals, the CAM-B3LYP-D3 and ωB97X-D functionals. By expanding the MD
simulation to also be carried out on the geometries from the calculations performed with
these functionals, greater statistical significance could be achieved and a better conclusion
on the transmission coefficient could be made. Ideally if it was possible to synthesize the
molecule, it would first of all prove that the molecule was not only theoretical, but various
spectroscopic experiments could be carried. Analogous to the experiments performed in [64],
the theoretically calculated reaction rate could be compared with experimental results and
prove the validity of the conclusions and results that have been presented in this thesis.
