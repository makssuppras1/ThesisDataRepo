Abstract
Electroluminescence (EL) imaging is a well-established PV diagnosis tool for indoor and night-
time PV module inspection, proven to be highly effective for assessing PV performance and
superior in diagnosis information to other characterization methods. Despite its potential, its
outdoor daylight applications are limited, mainly due to the solar irradiance at 1 sun being 4 to
5 orders of magnitude more intense than the EL signal coming from a PV panel forward biased
at Isc. Accounting for the low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), single image pair background (BG)
subtraction is insufficient to eliminate the sunlight noise embedded in the EL images and the
utilization of image processing techniques therefore appears to become necessary.
Currently, there is a lack of clear indications on how to optimize daylight EL to obtain useful
results. With the growing demand for outdoor daylight EL (dEL), the need to fill this knowledge
gap increases, calling for a deeper understanding of the interaction between the EL signal gen-
erated by the module and the noise produced by sunlight. Gaining insight into the EL emission
spectra of a module under dark conditions becomes essential in order to evaluate potential
correlations between its indoor and outdoor spectra, and thus dEL image.
This study encompasses comprehensive module- and cell-level EL image and spectra mis-
match studies of healthy and degraded PV samples of different cell technology types, manu-
facturing years, and datasheet electrical parameters. Three cell technology types are studied
(Al-BSF, PERC, and IBC), and modules of five different manufacturing years are used as the
selected samples.
Results clearly indicate newer modules lead to higher EL luminescence intensities, and when
comparing average EL emission spectra from samples with different solar cell technologies at
a current bias equivalent to the module’s Isc, the IBC module was found to have the highest
emission intensity. Meanwhile, the front and rear sides of PERC modules attained 14-21% and
10-16%, respectively, and the Al-BSF modules reached only 1-2% of the IBC peak lumines-
cence intensity, matching recent qualitative observations during outdoor luminescence imaging.
Additionally, the bifacial modules’ datasheet bifaciality percentages are verified in terms of EL
emission. Whilst the percentage decrease in EL peak intensity of a module or cell’s rear side,
compared to the front side, agrees with the datasheet bifaciality percentage at 100% Isc current
bias, this does not always hold at 10% Isc. When considering degraded PV samples, at both
module and cell levels, peak intensity variations were considerably more prominent than those
in healthy samples.
Regarding EL emission peak position, a maximum shift of 7.63 nm is observed between tech-
nologies, attributed to variations in their stabilization temperatures and their effect on the c-Si
band gap. Meanwhile, peak position shifts of a maximum of 3 nm can be detected between cells
within a module, resulting from temperature changes within a module during the measurement
process. Although small, these numbers are not considered negligible, as they are substantial
considering the small temperature variations to which the modules were exposed to.
Amongst the electrical parameters affecting the intensity of the EL signal, the cell Voc was shown
to have the most pronounced relationship, showing a clear linear correlation.
Deeper into the research, outdoor EL imaging and spectra analysis of one PV module per stud-
ied cell technology type is conducted. Daylight EL spectroscopy is subjected to the effects of
optical filters, and the EL image’s SNRKari is correlated against optical filter configurations and
camera exposure times. Modulated image sequence acquisition and FFT image processing
are used during the outdoor EL imaging.

highlight the already-known importance of constant solar irradiances for reduced-noise spectra
acquisition, and show a strong correlation between the indoor and outdoor experiments. As
expected, the higher the measured indoor EL emission (IBC > PERC > Al-BSF Mono-Si), the
more intense the daylight BG subtracted intensity, and the bigger the value of the EL image
quality marker. The IBC module under research provides SNRKari values above 4 (the minimum
level to provide high-quality EL images) under the no-filter scenario, and all filter scenarios, for
exposure times 1-5 ms, suggesting no optical filter is necessary for its daylight EL imaging. The
scenario which resulted in the highest SNRKari value for this module was the EL acquisition
at 3 ms exposure time using the BP50 optical filter. Additionally, the image quality indicator
demonstrates a subtle tendency to increase with longer exposure times rather than with filters
that block a larger portion of the solar signal.
Findings show that quantifying the EL signal does indicate successful daylight EL image ac-
quisition. By taking first steps into EL emission spectra and daylight imaging correlation, an
approach to indicating the need for SNR enhancement via evaluating the noise in outdoor EL
spectroscopy is suggested. This approach is shown to work for the IBC module no-filter case,
but additional statistics and EL spectra data at constant solar irradiances (clear-sky conditions)
are needed to verify the usefulness of the approach.
Building upon the results obtained in this thesis, it is recommended to use the quantified indoor
and outdoor EL emission results (expressed in absolute irradiance units), as a foundation to
develop a model to predict the performance of daylight EL imaging under different sunlight
irradiance conditions and provide insights into outdoor EL imaging optimal conditions, such as
the camera exposure time and suitable optical filter.

Introduction
1.1
Context
The use of photovoltaic (PV) solar power has experienced remarkable growth during the last
decades, driven by factors such as climate change concerns, the shift towards renewable en-
ergy sources, government policies [1] , and production and installation cost reductions [2]. The
International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) reported that the cost of solar PV globally
dropped by 82% between 2010 and 2019 [3]. Markus Fischer et al. [4], from the International
Technology Roadmap for Photovoltaic (ITRPV), predict a continued decline in the cost of PV
elements, projecting a promising cost reduction of 37% from 2020 to 2031 (refer to Figure 1.1).
Solar PV is expected to remain at the forefront of renewable energy expansion due to, amongst
others, the mentioned cost competitiveness. According to IRENA’s REmap analysis, solar PV
installations have the potential to six-fold over the next decade, reaching a global capacity of
2,840 GW by 2030 and 8,519 GW by 2050 (Figure 1.2) [5]. However, during their lifespan, PV
modules are subject to degradation factors that affect their performance and efficiency. With the
PV industry’s growth, it becomes crucial to develop reliable maintenance and diagnostic tools
to ensure module longevity and optimal performance.
Electroluminescence (EL) imaging is a well-established PV diagnosis tool for indoor and night-
time PV module inspection [6]. This non-destructive testing technique has been proven to be
highly effective for assessing the quality and performance of PV devices, making it superior in
diagnosis information to many other PV characterization methods. By subjecting a PV panel
to a forward bias voltage and analyzing the resulting light emission spectra, defects and mal-
functioning regions within the panel can be detected. Amongst others, these include solar cell
cracks, potential induced degradation (PID), and broken interconnections. Additionally, when
warranty claims necessitate module-level and cell-level imaging, this characterization method
allows for the examination of a PV module or cell’s internal structure, offering valuable informa-
tion regarding its performance and quality [6].
While nowadays, EL is widely used for PV cells and module diagnosis at laboratory and industry
levels, PV inspection companies have begun to capture EL images in the field, as it provides a
high cost and module integrity advantage by avoiding the need to remove modules from racks
and transport them to an indoor laboratory [7].
Despite the potential of EL imaging for PV diagnostics, its outdoor applications are limited due
to various challenges that restrict its use in utility-scale inspections, including the need for more
standardization in the measurement and analysis procedures [8]. Furthermore, the EL tech-
nique is typically utilized indoors or during nighttime hours because the luminescence signal
of crystalline silicon is much weaker than sunlight (low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)) [7], [8], [9].
For this reason, more intricate methods are needed in outdoor inspection. Moreover, utilizing
optical filters to mitigate sun noise and increase a module’s EL SNR, and increasing camera ex-
posure time to capture a higher EL signal are methods used in literature to enhance the quality
of dEL acquisition ([10], [11], [12], and [13], amongst others).

1.2
Background and Project Motivation
The motivation behind this study stems from the need to enhance the EL diagnostic technique
and characterization methodology employed for PV modules. Currently, there is a demand for
refined indoor laboratory-scale EL inspection, which calls for a deeper understanding of lumi-
nescence emission spectra from various PV module technologies, including Aluminium Back
Surface Field (Al-BSF), Passivated Emitter and Rear Contact (PERC), and Interdigitated Back
Contact (IBC) cells (refer to Figure 1.3 for these technologies’ indoor EL spectra). It is also of
interest to establish the relationship between the EL signal spectra and a PV module’s electrical

characteristics. The statistical data obtained from this research will serve as a dataset, providing
a valuable reference for future studies. At this point, it is worthwhile clarifying that ”EL spectra”
refers to the wavelength-resolved emission of light produced during electroluminescence, cap-
tured by a spectrometer. Having knowledge of how different PV cell technologies with different
electrical parameters emit EL is beneficial for the community, as it allows us to anticipate and
comprehend the observations when EL is used as a diagnostic tool.
Meanwhile, with the growing demand for outdoor daylight EL (dEL), it is essential to streamline
the process, making it easier, faster, and more reliable. Currently, there is a lack of clear indi-
cations on how to conduct effective daylight EL to obtain useful results. This leads to the need
for a deeper, comprehensive understanding of the interaction between the EL signal generated
by the module and the noise produced by sunlight (see Figure 1.4). This motivates this study
to provide a foundation and baseline to understand this relationship and to offer guidelines for
future modeling efforts that determine the appropriate settings for optical filters (i.e., how much
signal to filter away) and camera parameters (Figure 1.5).
Additionally, since the release of PERC technology cells in the market, the efficiencies of the
newer crystalline silicon (c-Si) technologies have been increasing considerably [14]. For this
reason, and considering the three technology types analyzed in this study are Al-BSF, PERC,
and IBC, the luminescence signal for the IBC and PERC samples is expected to be considerably
greater than that for the AL-BSF one, and the intensity for IBC greater than that for the PERC
module. However, the quantification of this irradiance intensity difference at module-level (Fig-
ure 1.3), and the implications of these technologies’ high EL intensities for daylight EL imaging
(Figure 1.4 and Figure 1.5) have not been yet quantified, representing a knowledge gap in the
PV community that is aimed to be addressed in this study.
The mentioned statistical information and outdoor EL imaging knowledge will help increase the
usefulness of the acquisition of EL diagnostic images during dark conditions and improve the
potential outcomes of this imaging during daylight conditions, enabling increased guidance for
PV fault detection.

1.3
Project Objectives
Overall, this thesis’ main objective is to conduct a solar cell EL spectra analysis and investigate
its implications for daylight luminescence imaging of PV modules.
Analogously to what is detailed in the Motivation Section 1.2, this study seeks to conduct com-
prehensive indoor investigations on PV modules to analyze and find correlations between their
EL imaging and spectral behavior and characteristics in relation to various PV cell technology
types (Al-BSF, PERC, and IBC, specifically), manufacturing years (2011, 2015, 2018, 2020, and
2023), and datasheet electrical parameters (cell open circuit voltage (Voc), cell current density
(Jsc), and efficiency). This analysis will thus improve the understanding of the underlying mech-
anisms of EL. As discussed in Section 1.2, the statistical information gathered will add to the
PV community’s existing body of knowledge, allowing researchers to foresee and comprehend
observations when EL is used as a diagnostic tool. For example, analyzing the EL image of a
module that had previously been characterized, and knowing that for a certain reason, its Voc
has decreased, can help us predict and understand the changes in EL intensity. This will enable
increased accuracy in PV fault detection and PV health-state identification.
In addition, by understanding the relationship between dEL emission spectra and sun noise,
the research aspires to establish a correlation between a module’s indoor and outdoor spectra.
By then comparing the discovered relationship with the findings obtained by investigating the
effects of optical filters and camera settings on dEL spectra acquisition (and the corresponding
EL image’s SNR), we aim to discover whether quantifying a module’s EL signal indoors indicates
success in daylight EL image acquisition.
In this context, we, therefore, intend to provide a baseline for future work to, by collecting ad-
ditional spectral data at different solar irradiances, develop a model that guides the acquisition
of outdoor EL measurements, providing insights into the necessary filters and exposure times

State of Art and Theoretical Background
When deployed outdoors, PV modules are subjected to continuous cycles of irradiation, temper-
ature, humidity, and mechanical stress, all of which can contribute to performance degradation
[15]. Defects and faults in PV systems can have varying impacts on power production, but they
consistently result in decreased performance, leading to reduced returns on investment [16].
Several characterization methods are used to detect and identify degradation modes in PV pan-
els, allowing for improved reliability, and cost-effectiveness of PV systems. While I-V (current-
voltage) curve acquisition can be used to measure a PV module’s electric properties directly,
there are imaging methods like visual inspection (VI), infrared thermography (IR-T), ultravio-
let fluorescence (UV-F), or luminescence techniques that can visualize materials and material
properties of a PV module [16].
A brief overview of common failure modes is presented in this chapter, focused on cell cracks
and Potential Induced Degradation (PID) since they are the two failure modes discussed in this
study. This is followed by a concise summary of the main characterization methods, briefly
commenting on Current-Voltage (I-V) curve characterization, as it is used in this project, and
giving particular emphasis on EL, its physical principles, applications, and previous research.
2.1
Degradation Modes (DM) in PV Modules
According to the Photovoltaic Power Systems Programme (PVPS) of the International Energy
Agency (IEA), PV failure is defined as any effect resulting in module power degradation that
normal operation cannot reverse or an event that poses a safety risk [16], [17], [18]. Despite its
use no longer being optimal, a degraded PV module may continue to produce electricity from
sunlight [19]. However, Wohlgemuth et al. [20] stated that when a PV module’s power falls
below 80% of its initial level, manufacturers deem the module to deteriorate.
The performance of the PV module can be affected by several factors, including irradiation,
temperature, humidity, and mechanical impact [20], [21]. Each of these different effects may
cause one or more types of module degradation, including corrosion, discoloration, delamina-
tion, cell breaking, and cracking [22]. It is interesting to highlight that deterioration modes are
only partially independent, making it sometimes challenging to distinguish between them when
inspecting a degraded module.
The IEA issued several reports dealing with failures of PV modules. An extensive overview of the
state of the art of common DMs can be found in the IEA PVPS Task 13 [18]. Filtering degradation
modes according to the PV module type, it can be extracted that the most predominant failures
in silicon wafer-based PV modules are cell cracks, PID, EVA discoloration, snail tracks, burn
marks, disconnected cell, string interconnect ribbons, and defective bypass diodes.
2.1.1
Cell Cracks in PV Modules
Cracks and microcracks, commonly found in PV modules or cells and formed in their semi-
conductor material, are often imperceptible to the naked eye. Particularly, c-Si cells are prone
to developing cracks due to their thin, brittle structure and crystalline nature [16]. Common
causes of crack formation include mechanical stresses during cell production, module man-
ufacturing, transportation, installation, and mechanical loads (e.g., wind, hail, or snow), and
thermal stresses and fluctuations during operation [23].

Depending on the size and position of the crack, the effects of a crack in solar cells can range
from having no impact on power production to completely shutting down the module. Köntges
et al [23], categorized the degree of cell breaks into three groups: Type A cracks, also known
as micro cracks, prevent cell parts from being disconnected; Type B, cracks that separate cell
parts, result in lower production but continued participation in power generation; and Type C
cracks, which entirely cut off some cell sections from participation in power generation.
Other degradation kinds like corrosion, encapsulant discoloration, and delamination typically
can occur due to breaks and cracks [15].
2.1.2
PID in PV Modules
PID is a condition characterized by the progressive performance deterioration of crystalline
silicon modules due to excessive voltage stress [24].
This DM occurs when there is a strong voltage difference between the solar cells and the
grounded PV module frame [25]. The potential difference in strings of PV cells (connected
in series) can reach several hundred volts [26] and to reduce the risk of electrical shock, all
metallic structures of modules are grounded. If the insulation between the structure and the ac-
tive layers is imperfect, leakage currents can be created [26]. This potential difference between
the solar cells and module frames in a functioning PV system is high at either side of the module
string [16].
Climate conditions like temperature and humidity accelerate the power loss due to PID, which
can also be caused by the use of incompatible materials in the building of the module [16].
Nevertheless, techniques such as applying reverse voltage during night time can be applied to
recover PID-affected PV modules [27].
2.2
Characterisation Methods (CM)
One of the critical challenges in the PV industry is to detect and diagnose degradation mecha-
nisms accurately and efficiently, defining its severity. As each available detection method offers
a unique insight into the module’s condition and performance, multiple methods simultaneously
can yield a more comprehensive understanding of the PV system’s state. A list of failures that
each characterization method may be used to identify is provided in Figure 2.2. It is of value to
consider that besides the detectability of a specific defect, the complexity of conducting a CM
is also essential.
Characterization methods can be divided into two categories: those that provide a direct mea-
surement of a module’s electrical properties (I-V curve acquisition) and those that provide visual
images of materials and material properties in PV modules (VI, IR-T, UV-F, and luminescence
techniques). As mentioned, different diagnostic methods are used to detect different degrada-
tion modes.

can be corrected to STC if the actual temperature and irradiance are recorded. On the negative

2.3
EL Imaging Technique
Luminescence imaging techniques (EL and PL) involve the measurement and analysis of light
emission from PV materials under two different excitation conditions. As EL is the characteriza-
tion technology studied in this project, it will be the main focus of this section. Nevertheless, the
physical mechanism behind PL and other characteristics of this technique are also explained,
in order to understand the differences between both luminescence techniques.
2.3.1
Physical Mechanism behind EL and PL
As briefly mentioned in Section 2.2.2, the primary distinction between the two luminescence
imaging techniques (EL and PL) is the element and process that triggers the light emission
from the semiconductor material. Whilst PL requires external photon excitation, EL relies on
the applied electrical bias to induce recombination and photon emission.
As a result of this PV module excitation, photons are emitted by Radiative Recombination
(RR), otherwise known as Band-to-Band recombination, of charge carriers in the semiconductor
material [28]. In this type of recombination mechanism, an electron from the conduction band
(CB) directly combines with a hole in the valence band (VB) and releases a photon. The energy
difference between the initial and final state of the electron is released in the process in the form
of a photon. As the emitted photon has an energy similar to the band gap, it results in limited
re-absorption, which allows it to exit the semiconductor material [29].
Besides radiative recombination, there are three other types of recombination that can occur
within a PV cell that are non-radiative (i.e., do not emit photons during the recombination pro-
cess). During these processes, the recombination of charge carriers (electrons and holes) re-
sults in the dissipation of energy as heat or energy given to another charge carrier, rather than
the emission of photons. The schematic sketches of the four types of recombination processes
in semiconductors are depicted in Figure 2.3. Diagram Figure 2.3 (A) illustrates RR, where the
purple arrow represents the movement of an electron, and thus the recombination of charge car-
riers (electron ”-” in the CB and a hole ”+” in the VB). The photon released through the process
is shown as a yellow arrow.
• Auger Recombination takes place when excess charge carriers exist in the system. It
involves the recombination of three charge carriers and the excess energy is transferred
to another carrier instead of being radiated as light [29]. In other words, and as shown in
Figure 2.3 (B), when an electron (”-” on the left-hand side of the diagram) and a hole re-
combine (purple downwards pointing arrow), rather than emitting the energy as heat or as
a photon, the energy is given to a third carrier, an electron in the conduction band (electron
”-” on the right-hand side of the diagram). This electron is excited (purple upwards pointing
arrow) but then thermalizes back down to the conduction band edge (orange arrow).
• Shockley-Read-Hall (SRH) Recombination occurs when an electron and a hole recom-
bine through defect states within the bandgap of the semiconductor material [29]. SRH is
schematized in Figure 2.3 (C), showing an electron being trapped (top left purple arrow)
by an energy state in the forbidden region (energy state between the CB and VB, shown
in the diagram as a white circle), introduced through defects in the crystal lattice. If a hole
moves up to the same energy state before the electron is thermally re-emitted into the CB,
the charge carriers recombine (shown by the bottom right purple arrow).
• Surface Recombination arises in surface defects such as imperfections or contaminants
[29]. These are represented as a white circle in Figure 2.3 (D). The purple arrow in this
diagram represents the recombination between two charge carriers.

EL Imaging Physical Principle and Excitation Source
EL uses an external direct current (DC) power source to stimulate radiative recombination in
the solar cells and cause the emission of photons with the energy of the band gap [16], [28].
When the PV module is subjected to a forward bias, several steps occur inside the PV cell that
lead to RR, and thus to photon emission in the form of the EL signal [31]. As the PV module is
subjected to a forward current bias, an electric field is created that allows the injection of charge
carriers, both electrons (injected from the n-type region) and holes (injected from the p-type
region), inside the semiconductor material. Figure 2.5 illustrates the process by representing,
as grey boxes next to the bands (shown in orange color), the connections between the power
supply and the module, either injecting electrons to the CB or extracting electrons from the VB,
leaving holes behind.
Driven by the electric field, these injected electrons move through the semiconductor material,
flowing from the negatively charged to the positively charged end of the device, encountering
positively charged holes in their way. When this happens, these electrons and holes recombine
(RR), releasing excess energy in the form of a photon (and a phonon) with the energy corre-
sponding to, approximately, the bandgap energy of the semiconductor material. Hence, this
causes the luminescence signal [32]. Depending on the bandgap, these emitted photons can
have wavelengths within the visible or near-infrared (NIR) range.
This process occurs in certain devices, such as LEDs (light-emitting diodes) and OLEDs (or-
ganic light-emitting diodes), where the electric current causes the emission of photons, and
is responsible for the light displays on electronic devices such as TVs, computer screens, or
smartphones.
Figure 2.4 represents the EL process by presenting the electrical band structure of an indirect
semiconductor (e.g., silicon) with its energy (E) on the y-axis and the crystal momentum vector
(k) on the x-axis. The two gray horizontal arrows represent the injection of electrons to the CB
and holes to the VB. These two charge carriers then recombine through RR (red arrow) releasing
a photon, represented by a black wavy arrow. The phonon aiding the process is shown as a
blue wavy line [30].

PL Spectroscopy Physical Principle and Excitation Source
In contrast to EL, PL is not triggered by an external electrical source, bur rather by the absorption
of light energy. In other words, PL involves the emission of photons by the semiconductor
material, occurring as the result of electronic excitation within the material in response to the
absorption of photons [34].
In essence, when the solar cell or module is illuminated by an external and homogenous light
source (e.g., the sun), the material absorbs this irradiance through a process called photo-
excitation, causing excess carriers to be generated, and thus the light of certain wavelengths to
be emitted [28]. According to the operating principle of a PV device, during the photo-excitation
process, when the energy of incident photons (hw) is greater than the semiconductor’s bandgap
energy (Eg), electrons (”-”) are energized through the absorption of the photon energy, and
are excited from the valence band (VB) to the conduction band (CB), leaving holes behind
(”+”) in the valence band (VB) [35]. When these electrons return to their original energy levels
and recombine with holes, their excess energy is released as photons [31]. While the band
transition of the electrons occurs without any change from momentum in direct semiconductors,
the condition of momentum (k) change must also be fulfilled in indirect semiconductors. This
change in momentum can be made by a suitable phonon (crystal lattice vibration) [36], [37].
Figure 2.6 represents this process analogously to Figure 2.4. The green arrow represents the
absorption of an incident photon (shown as a wavy line hwexe), which excites an electron, which
migrates to the CB (with the aid of the absorption/release of a phonon). This electron then loses
its energy and recombines with a hole in the VB, represented by a red arrow, releasing a photon
(shown as a wavy line hwPL) [30].

2.3.2
EL Applications in PV Fault Detection
Whilst the intensity of the light emitted from a PV module positively correlates to the incident
light (in PL) or to the forward current bias applied by the power source (in EL), the presence of
defects (such as impurities or cracks) in the semiconductor material can lead to non-uniformities
in the emitted light pattern, such as different shape spectra or lower intensity. By examining the
emitted light and its properties (through luminescence images and/or spectra, both explained in
this section), luminescence techniques can be used to identify and locate faults in PV modules,
enabling quality control and fault detection during production and after installation.
Since EL imaging technique was first introduced by Takashi Fuyuki et al., for the diagnosis of
c-Si solar cells in 2005 [28], it has rapidly advanced and is now extensively employed for non-
destructive assessment of PV cells, primarily to detect structural defects and evaluate electrical
property uniformity in c-Si cells.
Through Figure 2.2, Köntges et al. provides a list of the most significant fault types detectable
by EL and PL, and their expected representation in the resulting luminescence image [18].
Regarding the differences in defect detection capabilities of EL and PL, as illustrated in Fig-
ure 2.2, all of the studied PV module faults in that study that were detectable through PL (cell
cracks, PID, glass breakage, quick connector failure, junction box/bypass diode failure and light-
induced degradation) could also be identified using EL. However, EL is the only method capable
of detecting increased series resistance regions and the electrical changes caused by internal
circuitry discoloration. On the downside, since electrical excitation is required, EL imaging is
neither contactless nor quick to set up [38], whilst PL provides a straightforward, contactless
method.
EL (and PL) can be studied in both a quantitative and a qualitative way. Qualitative visual in-
vestigation of the resulting EL image allows us to easily recognize locations affected by faults

when this sample is subjected to the respective excitation source (described in Section 2.3.1).

an EL (or PL) camera, or an imaging system. This camera detects the photons emitted by the
semiconductor material and converts them into an electrical signal, which is then processed to
generate a visible image. The types of cameras required to capture these images are com-
mented on Section 2.3.4. The advantage of EL and PL images is the fact that defects can be
quickly identified and, most importantly, located in the sample, by taking just one image of the
whole cell or module.

EL Spectroscopy
As illustrated in Figure 2.5 and Figure 2.7, during EL and PL imaging, the emitted photons are
captured by an imaging device such as a camera, which allows the visualization of the EL signal
as an image. Another option is to use a spectrometer. This device records the emission signal
as an irradiance curve, representing the wavelength of the emitted photons (equivalent to the
energy of the released photon, thus, to the bandgap energy).
By examining the luminescent properties of the acquired spectra, such as the intensity, wave-
length, and spatial distribution of the emitted light, it is possible to identify, locate and assess
different types of faults, including cracks and degradation. They additionally allow for spatially
resolved analysis of optical and electrical properties of solar cells and modules [42], [43].
The curves observed in Figure 2.10 represent the luminescence spectra intensity acquired dur-
ing both EL and PL imaging. The luminescence emission peak for silicon-based solar cells at
ambient temperature is centered at, approximately, 1150 nm, as this is the specific wavelength
that corresponds to the energy difference between the VB and CB in silicon (bandgap energy)
[37], [44]. Using the equation: λ = hc/E, where λ is the wavelength, h is Planck’s constant
(6.62607015 × 10-34 Js), c is the speed of light in vacuum (2.998 × 108 m/s), E is the energy
bandgap, and knowing the energy bandgap of silicon is, approximately, 1.12 electronvolts (eV)
at room temperature, the 1150 nm wavelength can be calculated. In other words, the pho-
tons released during band-to-band RR have Silicon’s energy bandgap energy (1.12 eV) and
corresponding wavelength (1150 nm).
It can be seen in Figure 2.10 that, for the same material, the peak EL intensity tends to be
higher than the peak PL intensity. This intensity difference can be attributed to several factors,
including the following.
• The excitation mechanism itself, as different excitation mechanisms can lead to variations
in the population of excited states and the subsequent emission intensity [45].
• The excitation conditions such as the irradiance to which the module is exposed to (for
PL) or current density bias (for EL) can affect the emission intensity [45].
• The carrier injection efficiency [45].
In essence, as carriers are injected into the material through an external electrical bias during
EL excitation, this results in higher carrier densities and thus an increased probability of recom-
bination. This controlled injection of carriers leads to a higher luminescence intensity compared
to PL, as carrier excitation in this mechanism relies solely on the absorption of external pho-
tons, and these are limited by the available light source and the material’s absorption properties.
However, normalizing from 0-1 these spectra curves would result in the same spectra shape
for EL and PL of the same material [45].
Literature by Fei Yang et al. [46] studied the defect PL luminescence on multi-crystalline sil-
icon (mc-Si) wafers using an optical excitation source and an InGaAs camera with detection
sensitivity in the 0.9 to the 1-7 μm wavelength range. The recorded spectra (Figure 2.11) were
discovered to show a defect-band peak, besides the expected band-to-band peak. The camera
sensitivity covered the band-to-band emission at approximately 1.1 eV (around 1150 nm) and
the defect-related emission at approximately 0.8 eV (around 1550 nm). This defect-band peak
was attributed to high carrier recombination due to defects and impurities in mc-Si structures
[47]. To the best of our knowledge, the existing literature has not reported the presence of these
defect-band peaks in monocrystalline Si modules.

EL Image Acquisition, Processing, and Quality Metrics
Throughout the literature, various methods have been suggested for EL imaging, as the level of
background noise present during indoors and outdoors nigh-time and daylight varies notoriously.
Although the same fundamental principles are shared between these image acquisition and
processing techniques, different approaches to filter background light, separate the EL and
background (BG) signals, and enhance image quality can be used.
Night-time EL imaging has the advantage of low optical background, thus mitigating its impact
on the measurements [48]. Nonetheless, EL inspection during night-time is highly challenging
and presents various operational, logistical, and safety complications, including adverse en-
vironmental conditions, the use of light towers, and the complexity of navigating the plant to
polarize the modules [12]. The possibility of conducting outdoor daylight luminescence imaging
thus becomes highly attractive. However, it poses significant difficulties due to the need to im-
plement methods that effectively suppress background solar radiation and enable the extraction
of luminescence emitted by the cells [12].

disadvantages, seen in Figure 2.12.

Indoor or Outdoor Nigh-time EL Imaging
When EL images are acquired in dark conditions, either indoors in a laboratory with the lights
turned off, or outdoors during nighttime, single image pair acquisition and BG subtraction
can be used to capture and process the images [30].
This process consists in capturing an image without the EL signal (forward bias OFF) and sub-
tracting it from an identical image captured with the EL excitation ON. The first of these images
only captures the background noise (”BG”), whilst the second image (”EL+BG”) is expected to
contain the signal caused by the forward bias (EL signal) and the one proceeding from potential
background noise. The constant noise component between the two images can be successfully
removed by subtracting the images on a pixel-by-pixel basis, leaving the desired EL signal alone
(”EL+BL”-”BG”=”EL”). The dark grey curve, ”Dark IV”, in Figure 2.13 illustrates the section of a
PV module’s I-V curve that produces the signal when the module is under forward bias current
operating point during dark EL conditions (indoor or nighttime).

Outdoor Daylight EL Imaging
The light grey curve, ”IV curve at STC”, in Figure 2.13 illustrates the section of a PV module’s I-V

While capturing BG images for image pair subtraction is often necessary to correct indoor am-
bient light noise, it is strictly needed when performing daylight EL imaging (dEL) [49], [50].
However, when carrying out dEL, the single image pair BG subtraction is not enough to get
rid of the sunlight noise embedded in the images, as sunlight at 1 sun can be 4 to 5 orders of
magnitude more intense than the EL signal coming from a PV panel forward biased at Isc [9],
[51].
A potential alternative to capture the dEL images with increased SNR is using the batch image
acquisition and subtraction. This process involves taking several images with the EL signal
(forward bias signal ON), and then several without the EL signal (forward bias OFF) at the
same surrounding conditions, and then subtracting them as previously explained. This image
sequence can then be processed using a technique called averaging [49], [51], [52].
Although batch image acquisition can be accomplished in clear sky conditions [12], [45], it is
challenging in locations where the sky is usually cloudy [53]. In order to avoid this problem,
an alternative dEL image acquisition technique employed is modulated image sequence ac-
quisition, first mentioned by L. Stoicescu et al. [51] in 2014. This bias-switching approach
involves capturing images whilst biasing the module at a known and controlled frequency (ap-
plying a modulated electrical bias current), such that the corresponding EL signal emitted by the
module is also modulated at that frequency [53]. To do this, the polarization state of the solar
panels is altered, creating a pulsed EL signal [12]. In comparison with batch image acquisition,
this technique has the advantage of reducing the impact of fast changes in solar irradiance [49].
For both outdoor batch image and modulated image sequence acquisition, sequences of images
with the DC signal ON (PV module under direct polarization) and another with the DC signal OFF
(module under open circuit conditions) are captured, and the module/cell images sequentially
recorded are subtracted. As illustrated through the red curve in Figure 2.14, the images captured
during the ON-state consist of the EL emission, the ambient light (direct and reflected by the
module), and the PL emission excited by the sunlight. Meanwhile, the BG signal corresponding
to the OFF-state contains the ambient light (again, direct and reflected) and the PL emission.
This way, the difference between these two sets of images should provide the EL image.

(ON/OFF states, in red color). (ON/OFF states for PL images are depicted in blue). Source: [12].

For both batch image and modulated acquisition, the signal can be enhanced through averaging.
The image-averaging processing approach involves capturing multiple images of the same
position and then averaging the pixel values of corresponding pixels in each image. This method
can eliminate noise while retaining the underlying signal because noise is often random and
uncorrelated across multiple images [30]. In order to be able to average these images batched-
acquired for noise canceling and signal enhancement, the sequence should be acquired at the
same position and surrounding conditions with the PV panel biased (EL images) and not biased
(BG images).
Implementing PV module bias modulation introduces increased complexity to the equipment
used for dEL imaging in comparison to batch image acquisition, as it requires capabilities to
control the output of the power supply unit in a specific waveform and synchronization between
the signal and the camera. However, this last requisite can be avoided by employing the Fast
Fourier Transform (FFT) image processing technique [53].
The study conducted by dos Reis Benatto et al. [53] demonstrated that although SNR en-
hancement through averaging can be applied to both batch image acquisition and modulated
acquisition, this image processing method is limited in its effectiveness when the noise level is
high. In contrast, signal enhancement through FFT, only applied to the modulated acquisition,
is able to retrieve all the signal contained in a sequence of images.
EL Image Quality Metrics
When working with EL imaging, the quality of the image obtained is essential. As image sharp-
ness and noise [54] are the metrics used to analyze the EL image quality in this study, they are
the main focus of this section.
In first place, image sharpness relates to the visual quality, clarity, and distinctness of details
found in an image. This parameter is determined by the ability of an imaging system to repro-
duce fine details and edges accurately [30]. It is defined by the boundaries between zones of
different intensity [55]. While sharp images have well-defined edges and show finer details,
blurry ones lack a clear distinction between objects or features.
Meanwhile, image noise describes undesired random changes or disruptions in an image that
can be introduced during the image capture process due to sensor limitations, environmental
conditions, and electronic interference. Noise can manifest as speckles, graininess, or irregular
patterns in the captured images. As explained in the literature, [30], [56], one of the main noise
sources is background noise from luminescence sources other than the investigated sample.
Using appropriate image acquisition and processing techniques can help mitigate the influence
of noise in an image.
Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is a measure of noise, whilst SNR50 and SNRAV G are image qual-
ity parameters that quantify the quality of the signal by measuring the relative strength of the
desired signal compared to the level of noise present in an image. High SNR values indicate
clean and accurate representations of the image content [57]. As SNR EL image quality met-
rics, SNR50 is used in the literature, [40], for individual EL-BG image pairs, originally designed
to quantify the EL image quality acquired in dark conditions (indoor and nighttime). Meanwhile,
SNRAV G is used in the literature for image sequences [58].
As explained by dos Reis Benatto et al. [53], in the FFT image processing approach, the mod-
ulation frequency of the forward bias signal in the image sequence is analyzed in phase space.
By considering the EL signal in phase space, the SNR quantification can be approached based
on signal quality rather than image quality, unlike SNR50. This method enables the detection of
the presence and quality of a signal in the image series.

2.3.4
Camera Detectors for EL Imaging
When choosing a camera for testing materials, the primary specifications to consider are the
emission spectra of the material and the camera’s sensitivity in terms of its quantum efficiency
(QE) [9], [49]. In the EL imaging context, the QE describes the likelihood that an incident photon
will be transformed into electrical energy in the case of PV operation [59]. A higher QE in the
detector improves its sensitivity to the light being emitted, enabling more accurate imaging and
signal detection.
In cameras used for EL (and PL) applications, the detector within them is commonly Charged
Couple Device (CCD) or Complementary Metal Oxide Semiconductor (CMOS) [48].These can
be made of, InGaAs in the case of CMOS, or Si in both CCD and CMOS [31]. Different detectors
are used depending on the wavelengths at the emission peak of different solar cell technolo-
gies (e.g., 1150 nm for c-Si at ambient temperature [37] , and 1200 - 1400 nm for organic solar
cells [60]). In the case of c-Si, mainly Si-based CCD and InGaAs-based CMOS detectors are
employed, as they are the ones whose QE overlaps with c-Si’s emission spectra (refer to Fig-
ure 2.15).
Figure 2.15: Emission spectra from a range of solar cell technologies and the quantum efficiency of Si-based CCD
(”CCD” in the figure), Si-based CMOS (”sCMOS” in the figure), and InGaAs-based CMOS (”InGaAs” in the figure)
cameras. Source: [48].
While the Si-based CCD can achieve a high-resolution [48], [61], it presents a weak spectral
sensitivity in the EL emission range of c-Si solar cells (poor quantum efficiency beyond 100
nm). Consequently, it is only able to acquire a small portion of the silicon-based emission peak,
specifically the short-wavelength section [13], [50]. For this reason, luminescence imaging of
solar modules using Si-based cameras has to be conducted in a dark environment [12]. As
an additional drawback, Si-CCD’s long image exposure times (on the order of seconds) limit
inspection time [50].
Meanwhile, InGaAs-based detector cameras have an excellent spectral response to the Si
luminescence emission, detecting the emission entirely [62]. They, therefore, allow much faster

2.3.5
Optical Filters for Daylight EL Spectra and Image Acquisition
As described, image processing techniques, such as the ones described in Section 2.3.3, are
utilized to increase the SNR. Existing literature reports that to overcome the 4-5 orders of mag-
nitude more intense sunlight, optical “daylight filter(s)” can be placed in front of the spectrometer
probe or camera lens, to help further increase the signal [10], [51]. As described in the previ-
ous section 2.3.4, even when using InGaAs cameras, it is necessary to eliminate external light
sources other than the luminescence emission from the solar cell in order to conduct outdoor
daylight EL and PL of PV modules.
In [12], Guada et. al. advised using a set of appropriate filters for dEL, whose maximum trans-
mittance cover the spectral range of the luminescence emission of silicon. In their setup, they
made use of a short-wave infra-red (SWIR) band-pass filter, centered around 1160 nm with a
bandwidth of 150 nm and a transmittance close to 90%.

As shown in the Results chapter (Chapter 4), the module Al-BSF Mono-Si has cracks in multiple

spectra measurements were acquired of healthy points in each cell. This was no problem, as
cracks appear in specific locations.
Additionally, it is important to note that module PERC 2 was selected as it was initially considered
healthy. However, after characterizing it in the first experiment of this study, we detected it had
a 5% reduced performance. This raised suspicion that such a power loss level is considered
significant for a supposedly healthy module. Upon further investigation, we discovered that
other modules from the string from which PERC 2 had been dismounted displayed high PID
levels. PID typically occurs gradually throughout the string, with modules at the ends being more
susceptible due to higher potential. The positioning of the PERC 2 module was approximately in
the middle of the string, where the potential is comparatively lower during operation, suggesting
it should have been less affected by PID than other modules in the same string. The combination
of its position in the string and the observed 5% power loss provides enough evidence of the
early stages of PID in this module.
This is the reason why PERC 2 is included in the sample selection for experiments one, two,
and three as a healthy sample (Table 3.1) and experiment four as a faulty sample (Table 3.2).
In Al-BSF solar cell technology, screen-printed aluminum over the whole back surface (Al back
surface filed - Al-BSF) allows for lowering effective back surface recombination velocity [64]. As
a way of further improving the efficiency, instead of having a full Al back surface, PERC cells
can decrease rear recombination by inserting a patterned dielectric layer between silicon and
aluminum layers, so that only the aluminum comes in contact with a small portion of the cell area
[64]. Meanwhile, IBC cells are a type of rear contact solar cell that have the ability to prevent
shading losses by placing both contacts on the rear of the cell [64].

Experimental Setup for Indoor Experiments

EL Image Acquisition
The EL image tests were conducted in dark conditions (with the lights turned off) to ensure the
only source of light reaching the camera was from the PV module’s EL emission. This was

could disrupt and add noise to the EL images (and uncertainty to the EL spectra measurement)
reducing their quality and leading to inaccurate results. To ensure a steady state EL emission
before taking measurements, the PV modules were kept in constant bias until they reached a
stable temperature. The laboratory ambient temperature was also stable, controlled, and set to
25 ± 2°C.

EL Spectra Acquisition
The spectral data was acquired using a B&WTek Sol 1.7 InGaAs Spectrometer (Sol 1.7: 900
- 1700nm NIR Range TE Cooled InGaAs Array Spectrometer). The experimental setup used
during the acquisition of spectra measurements is illustrated in Figure 3.6, and the connection
diagram is shown in Figure 3.5.
Although also carried out in controlled laboratory conditions and controlled stable temperature,
EL spectra acquisition was able to be taken with the laboratory lights switched on. To capture
these measurements, an irradiance spectrum was first acquired with the module under no cur-
rent bias (EL signal OFF). The power supply was then turned on and after allowing time for
the module to be in steady state conditions, the EL spectra curves were measured (EL signal
ON). Subtracting these two spectra curves during the data processing phase was expected
to spectra only containing the module’s EL signal. This is due to the fact that, whilst the first
measurement recorded only the background noise (e.g., the signal generated by PL due to the
laboratory lights being turned on), the second one captured both that same noise plus the EL
emission from the module.
Figure 3.6: Experimental setup used in the measurement of spectra
For the same reasons as with the EL image acquisition, the cell spectra were also recorded at
both 100% and 10% of each of the PV module’s respective datasheet Isc.
During the module-level studies of healthy samples (Experiment 1 - Section 3.2.3, and Experi-
ment 2 - Section 3.2.4), the distance between the spectrometer probe and the module was fixed
at 5 cm. This distance was kept constant between measurements to avoid it being a potential
source of error between measurements. In the case of EL spectra acquisition for specific points
in a cell (Experiment 3 - Section 3.2.5, and Experiment 4 - Section 3.2.6), this probe-module
distance was reduced to 0 cm (direct contact between the two surfaces), in order to ensure the
measurement of specific point locations or defects.

Power Supply and Camera Settings
As theoretically explained in Section 2.3.3, a robust strategy to enhance the quality of the images
obtained at a high noise level during the day is modulated image sequence acquisition and
image processing using FFT [53]. This is the technique used in this study for the outdoor study’s
image acquisition and processing.
For the EL image acquisition, this was achieved in this study by applying a square function
generator in the PSU to modulate the power reaching the PV module, at a current of 100% Isc.
Meanwhile, spectra measurements were captured with the power supply on (PV module under
forward bias: EL signal) and off (PV module under no bias: background images for subtraction).
In this experiment, the power supply modulation is achieved by employing a rectangular function
generator with a frequency of 20Hz. This frequency corresponds to a period of 50 ms, resulting
in the power supply applying a forward bias current for 25 ms and not applying it for 25 ms. The
InGaAs sensor used in this experiment was susceptible to light intensity fluctuations. During
the experimental conditions, the sun intensity constantly varied depending on the time of the
day and the cloud cover. For this reason, capturing images at a faster rate was advantageous,
reducing the probability of the measured values rendering unreliable. The current bias wave-
forms applied to each of the three studied PV modules can be seen in Figure 3.28, Figure A.6,
and Figure A.7 for the IBC, Al-BSF Mono-Si, and PERC 1 modules, respectively.

The camera’s frame rate, expressed in frequency (Hz) or frames per second (fps), was config-
ured to be a multiple of the modulation frequency of the power supply for easier post-processing
and Fourier analysis of the images. The objective is to capture images at the highest possible
frame rate, as an increased number of images enhances the information available for analy-
sis. The camera utilized in this study had a maximum setting of 600 fps, which governed the
potential frame rate range. The camera’s exposure time of the camera is directly related to its
frame rate, indicating that while the power supply’s modulation frequency remained constant
throughout the experiment, altering the camera’s exposure time for each of the experiment’s
phases necessitated a corresponding adjustment to its frame rate.
In this experiment, the camera settings to be analyzed were rigidly established at exposure
times of 1 ms, 2 ms, 3 ms, 4 ms, and 5 ms for each filter configuration. The upper limit of
the exposure time was set at 5 ms, as exceeding this value would result in image saturation
due to the influence of sunlight noise, and a lower exposure time than 1 ms would result in
underexposure and low EL signal in the images.
It was required to capture either 8 or 16 images per period to acquire EL images. This was es-
sential due to utilizing the ”Analysis Shell” software for outdoor data processing, which employs
the FFT technique to eliminate sun noise and extract the EL signal.
Acquiring eight images per period (4 images captured under forward bias (EL signal) and four
as background images for subtraction) at a power supply modulation frequency of 20Hz meant
using a camera frame rate of 20 Hz*8 images per period = 160 Hz (or fps). This frame rate,
160 frames per second, can be used for a maximum exposure time of 1/160Hz = 6.25 ms. In
other words, this camera setting could be employed with 1 ms, 2 ms, 3 ms, 4 ms, and 5 ms.
Increasing the desired images per period to 16, and modulating the power supply at 20 Hz,
leads to a camera frame rate value of 20 Hz*16 images per period = 320 Hz (or fps) and thus a
maximum exposure time of 1/320Hz = 3.125 ms. Analogously, a 320 fps camera setting could
be used when working with 1 ms, 2 ms, and 3 ms.

Experiment 1 - Comparative Analysis of EL Spectra for PV Modules with

As a reminder, this study was conducted to investigate the effect of a PV module’s solar cell
technology types, manufacturing year, and datasheet electrical parameters on its EL imaging
and EL spectra intensity (in μW/cm2/nm).
During EL spectra acquisition using the spectrometer, relative intensities (no units) were recorded.
The spectral data was first dark subtracted (spectra acquired with the power supply OFF was
subtracted from that with EL excitation ON) and absolute irradiance values were subsequently
defined via calibration using an incandescent lamp, resulting in quantities with physical inten-
sity units. The incandescent lamp was calibrated with a reference exposure time of 248 ms.
Therefore, an additional correction was necessary to account for the difference in exposure
time. Taking raw measurements of module Al-BSF Mono-Si as an example, it can be observed
in Table 3.5 that the spectra measurements were obtained with an exposure time of 1000 ms
when a forward current of 100% Isc was applied. Hence, after the initial calibration step, Al-BSF
Mono-Si spectra metrics had to be multiplied by a factor of 248 ms/1000 ms. By following this
procedure, the obtained data represents the true absolute irradiance (μW/cm2/nm) and spectra
obtained from different modules and at different current bias intensities could be compared.
Graphs that include the individual spectra curves for all measured cells per module were initially
generated to process the data to conduct the desired and relevant analysis. As an example,
Figure 4.8 shows the spectra of all cells in the Al-BSF Multi-Si module, acquired at 100% Isc
current bias. Subsequently, an averaging data mathematical calculation was performed on
these curves to obtain each module’s average, maximum, and minimum irradiance spectra
curves. This process was conducted for both the front and rear sides of the module, if applicable,

To quantitatively compare the EL emission spectra for each of the five examined modules, four
main characteristics were extracted from each of their average curves of the calibrated lumines-
cence spectra: the spectra curve’s peak intensity, the peak position, the integrated area, and
the FWHM (Full Width Half Maximum). These values were calculated in Origin, via a mathemat-
ical integration calculation, and are graphically explained in Figure 4.4 (note that this spectra
curve does not correspond to any of the modules under investigation).
• Peak intensity: The highest intensity level reached by the spectra curve is referred to as
its ”peak intensity”, representing the curve’s amplitude. This parameter has a correlation
with the general electrical parameters for the measured location in the cell [28], [37].
• Peak position, or wavelength at peak intensity: The x-axis value at which the curve
reaches this highest intensity point corresponds to the ”wavelength at peak intensity”. It
details the precise wavelength where the highest intensity happens. The peak position
is related to the temperature dependency of the material bandgap and red shifts with the
higher temperature [37], [68].
• FWHM (Full Width Half Maximum): The FWHM provides information about the spectral
width or the spread of the curve, and it graphically corresponds to the width of the curve at
half of its maximum intensity. This parameter designates the range of wavelengths over
which the intensity is at least half of the peak intensity. A spectra’s FWHM is correlated
with carrier lifetime, where the narrower FWHM, the longer the lifetime [37].
• Integrated area: The spectra curve’s ”integrated area” is determined by an integration
calculation of the curve throughout the complete spectrum of wavelengths, and it repre-
sents the cumulative total of all the intensity values under the curve. In other words, the
overall energy or amount of light emitted within the designated wavelength range. This
spectra characteristic embodies peak intensity and FWHM into the curve area value.

As expected, the spectra’ shape varies significantly depending on the biasing conditions, and
three main observations can be identified. Module PERC 2’s average, maximum, and minimum
irradiance curves (Figure 4.5) serve as an example for all of these observations.
• The first observation is the considerable increase of peak intensity for EL measurements
acquired at 100% Isc in comparison to those obtained at 10% Isc. This refers to all the
average, maximum, and minimum curves. This difference can be attributed to the higher
carrier injection and, thus, current flowing through the cells when the module is biased at
100% Isc. This, in turn, results in a higher density of carriers in the semiconductor and
consequently enhances the probability of radiative recombination occurring and photons
being emitted. Higher photon emission leads to higher peak intensity in the EL spectrum.
The likelihood of radiative recombination events is additionally boosted due to the en-
hanced carrier mobility resulting from higher biasing conditions, as carrier transport to the
radiative recombination sites is improved. Lastly, the higher current bias intensifies the ex-
citation of defects within the module, increasing the probability of radiative recombination
at defect sites.
• Secondly, the curve broadness or width at increased bias levels (100% Isc) is signifi-
cantly greater than those at reduced current injection. As mentioned, increased charge
carrier injection augments the charge carrier density in the material. This can result in a
growth in non-radiative recombination processes, such as Auger and SRH recombination,
due to their physical processes explained in Section 2.3.1. As carriers recombine without
photon emission via these processes, the overall intensity of emitted light can be reduced,
leading to a broader distribution of the EL spectra.
Additionally, defects or non-uniformities within the PV module may become apparent at
higher biasing circumstances. This can result in a wider variety of EL spectra by intro-
ducing variations in cell carrier recombination processes. As a consequence, the effect of
these defects are more pronounced at 100% current bias, leading to a broader distribu-
tion of EL spectra. Moreover, thermal effects caused by increases in carrier injection can
contribute to the broader distribution of EL spectra at 100% Isc by impacting the recombi-
nation processes and rates, altering the distribution of energy levels and the population of
charge carriers.
• The variability or spread of the EL spectra across different cells in the module (spectra
”distribution” in terms of the difference between the maximum and minimum spectra of
cells in the module) observed when biasing the modules at 100% of their short-circuit
current (Isc) is considerably greater compared to that attributed to 10% Isc current bias.
This spectra distribution is illustrated in fig. 4.5 as the shaded area between the maximum
and minimum spectra.

affecting this spectra variability. The more intense these variations between cells are, the
broader the module’s EL spectra distribution (difference between maximum and minimum

As mentioned earlier, there is no observed correlation between the calculated FWHM values
of the average irradiance curves and the manufacturing year. However, in literature, this pa-
rameter is commonly associated with carrier lifetime, where a narrower FWHM indicates longer
carrier lifetimes [37]. According to this association, modules with higher power losses would
be generally expected to exhibit higher FWHM values. This, of course, depends on the type of
degradation mode, as cracks, for example, don’t lead to notoriously reduced local carrier life-
times. Surprisingly, results obtained from this experiment contradict this expected relationship.
When comparing the two PERC technology modules and considering their respective calcu-
lated power loss factors (2% for PERC 1 and 5% for PERC 2), the opposite trend is observed.
Specifically, PERC 2 demonstrates a lower FWHM compared to PERC 1, as depicted in Fig-
ure 4.13 and Figure 4.14. Similarly, between the two Al-BSF modules, the Multi-Si module,
which has a lower power loss than the Mono-Si module (as indicated in Table 4.2), exhibits a
higher FWHM, as illustrated in Figure 4.13. This last observation can be due to the fact that the
power loss in module Al-BSF Mono-Si was due to the present cracks.
While there is a theoretical possibility and previous research has demonstrated correlations
between peak intensity and all the electrical parameters in solar cells [28], [37] this module-
level analysis reveals a clear correlation only with the cell open circuit voltage.

In terms of the relationship between peak intensity and cell efficiency, a positive correlation was
anticipated based on the fact that, as stated by Fuyuki et al. [28], high efficiencies mean high
carrier diffusion lengths, which in turn mean high EL intensities. This suggests that higher cell
efficiency values would correspond to increased peak intensity. Upon examining the findings
illustrated in Figure 4.15, it becomes evident that although modules PERC 1, PERC 2, and
IBC possess similar cell efficiency values (20.64%, 21.34%, and 21.00%, respectively), their
respective peak intensities exhibit significant variations. Notably, the IBC module, who’s cell
efficiency falls within the range of cell efficiency values observed for PERC 1 and PERC 2,
demonstrates considerably higher peak intensity compared to the two PERC modules.

and the EL signal peak intensity (higher current density means higher carrier injection and thus
higher rates of radiative recombination and higher EL intensities), there appears to be no noto-
rious relationship. In line with this initial assumption, the results from Figure 4.15 validate the

FWHM Variability within a Module
The first observation regarding this parameter is that heatmaps showing FWHM variability within
a module generally show different patterns at 100% and 10% Isc, neither one of them resembling
the EL intensity seen in the EL images. No similarity can be seen either for the same surfaces
of bifacial modules at the same current bias conditions. The numeric FWHM distributions for
each module and current bias level are presented in Table 4.3.
Although it can be observed in the two PERC bifacial modules that FWHM has a higher variability
at 100% Isc bias than at a lower current bias, this conclusion cannot be generalized for the three
remaining modules.
As explained at the beginning of Section 4.1.2, a cell spectra’s FWHM is correlated with carrier
lifetime, where the narrower (smaller) the FWHM, the longer the lifetime. For this reason, mod-
ules with the highest FWHM variability within cells could indicate the presence of cell-located
degradation modes, which are expected to be represented as low-intensity cells in the module’s
EL image. This assumption (high EL intensity corresponds to low FWHM values or low EL in-
tensity corresponds to high FWHM values) holds in some cells, such as cell F6 in the Al-BSF
Mono-Si module, F10 in the PERC 1 module (front surface), and cells B1 and B2 in PERC 2
(front surface).
Contrary to what is observable in the EL images, cells that seem to be degraded such as F1, F2,
F3, and F4, in module PERC 1’s front surface, are in the range of the narrower FWHM, indicating
carrier substantial carrier lifetime and thus no major degradation defect. Meanwhile, cell B1 and
B7 of the same module shows one of the broadest FWHM values, indicating a reduced carrier
lifetime, whilst having a high EL emission intensity. This indicates that different degradation
mechanisms can be taking place in this last cell; one that keeps luminescence intensity but
decreases carrier lifetime. Other cells in the same situation (high EL emission intensity but high
FWHM value, or low EL emission but low FWHM value) are F5 in the Al-BSF Mono-Si module;
C2 in the Al-BSF Multi-Si module; B7 and B8 in module PERC 2’s front surface; and cells C1
and C2 in the IBC module. From this observation, it can be concluded that the EL image alone
is not enough to predict cell degradation modes.
Additionally, power loss can be linked to low carrier lifetime, thus suggesting power losses and
FWHM would have a positive correlation. Whilst module PERC 2 has the highest power loss
(see table 4.2), it presents the same FWHM variability as other modules (Al-BSF Multi-Si and
PERC 1), implying power loss can be caused by factors other than the carrier lifetime.
By observing the EL intensity mismatch, some trends could be found for PID-affected modules,
but in principle, a point measurement in the center of the cell is not a reliable method to evaluate
the health of the module, especially when cracks are involved (Al-BSF Mono-Si).
More sampling comparing new and older modules should be addressed to evaluate the ex-
pected cell EL intensity variation within the same module and if trends can be drawn towards
what is acceptable to assure a good quality PV module.

EL Imaging and SNR Values
The EL signal of interest is embedded in the qualitatively observed noise in the spectra curves
previously discussed. The power supply modulation is what allows us to eliminate this noise
and retrieve the EL signal, thus increasing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Image processing
can then help to further increase this SNR.
The EL images captured using this modulation technique were processed using a software
called ”Analysis Shell”, developed by DTU. In this software, images are processed via FFT
using sequences of 256 images, and 16 images per period, for the cases of 1, 2, and 3 ms,
and 8 images per period for the cases of 4 and 5 ms. Processed EL images and their SNR
were obtained from the software. Although little difference in quality can be observed by just
visually analyzing the EL images, the SNR is used as an image quality marker [53] to assess
the final processed images. The measurements (in mili amps) taken using the multimeter and
reference cell at the module’s plane of array (POA) allow us to know that the IBC EL images
were acquired at, approximately 611 W/m2 solar irradiance at the POA, while the Al-BSF Mono-
Si and the PERC 1 EL were captured at approximately 633 and 772 W/m2, respectively. These
values are calculated considering 126.82 mA as the conversion factor, which is how much the
reference cell measures at standard test conditions (STC). Even though the temperature at
the time of the EL image acquisition probably differed slightly from that at STC, these solar
irradiance values are still used as a reference.

First Steps in Correlating Outdoor EL Image Quality and EL Spectra Intensity
Referring back to Figure 4.40, we mentioned that the clear irradiance difference between the
EL ON and OFF spectra suggested that even without optical filters and signal enhancement
techniques, the EL signal of the IBC module should be detectable. Analogously, through Fig-
ure 4.41 we can hypothesize that when the outdoor measured EL spectra starts to be noisy and
undefined in comparison with the indoor spectra, we would start to need SNR enhancement (i.e.
FFT or image averaging and subtraction), but when this doesn’t occur, the EL signal should be
detectable without SNR enhancement, but with much simpler background subtraction. Con-
firming this hypothesis, a high-quality daylight EL image was obtained at 2 ms exposure time
using no optical filter - Figure 4.43.
Figure 4.44 shows two images from the IBC module, Figure 4.44 (a) with, and Figure 4.44 (b)
without EL signal, taken with an InGaAs camera without optical filters. Their profiles marked
with horizontal lines of the images are shown in Figure 4.44 (c).
In this particular instance, the demonstrated example highlights that the IBC module’s indoor
EL emission, with a peak intensity of, approximately, 70 µW/cm2/nm (refer back to Figure 4.6),
corresponds to a visible gray value difference of 1172.67 +/- 332.38 (Figure 4.44 (d)). This is
obtained by the image profile difference (BG subtraction). In other words, subtracting the blue
”IBC EL OFF” curve from the green ”IBC EL ON” curve pixel by pixel (as depicted in Figure 4.44
(c)). It is important to clarify that this calculated gray value difference serves as an indicator of
the embedded signal within the image difference, rather than an exact quantification of the EL
signal present in the BG subtracted EL image (EL ON minus EL OFF image - Figure 4.43).

solely relying on a single image pair subtraction. This is supported by the calculated SNRKari of
28.89, which significantly exceeds the minimum requirement of SNRKari > 4, stated by literature
[53] as the necessary minimum to ensure a high-quality image.

5
Summary, Conclusions, and Future Work
5.1
Summary
In this master’s thesis project, we conducted a comprehensive analysis of healthy and unhealthy
PV modules with the main objective of correlating EL image and spectra measurements, identi-
fying patterns, and establishing statistical trends in indoor laboratory and outdoor daylight condi-
tions. The main parameters for analyzing the indoor results were peak intensity, peak position,
integrated area, and FWHM. Meanwhile, SNR values, acquired for different camera exposure
times and optical filters were used to analyze outdoor results.
Two preliminary analyses were conducted, providing the basis for the subsequent experiments
conducted in the project. An InGaAs-based detector and a Si-based detector camera were
compared for EL image acquisition of the six utilized modules in this project. The InGaAs cam-
era outperformed the Si camera, delivering higher-quality images with shorter exposure times
(approximately two orders of magnitude lower). The repeatability and uncertainty of the spec-
trometer were also tested, revealing negligible errors in peak intensity, integrated area, and
FWHM measurements at varying distances between the spectrometer probe and the module.
Initially, five healthy PV modules representing different cell technologies and manufacturing
years were examined in the laboratory: two Al-BSF, two PERC, and one IBC PV module. The
parameters mentioned above (peak intensity, peak position, integrated area, and FWHM), av-
eraged over the entire module, provided statistics of the EL emission spectral parameters of
these modules, and were correlated to each module’s electrical parameters and manufacturing
year. Moreover, we performed a module-level analysis for each module by calculating the four
mentioned parameters for individual cells within the module. The results were presented as
heatmaps, allowing for a qualitative comparison with the module’s EL image. Quantitatively,
we investigated the extent of parameter variations within the module, pointing out the variation
in percentages of what can be expected in modules with no or little power loss for both high and
low current bias (100% and 10% Isc), and front and rear surfaces of bifacial modules.
Furthermore, we delved deeper into cell-level analysis by studying one representative cell from
each module. The four parameters were calculated for several points within each selected
cell. They were illustrated as contour plots and numeric statistical data, enabling us to evaluate
parameter variations at the cell level and compare the results with the previous module-level
analysis. The same analysis was conducted for unhealthy cells, allowing cell-level compari-
son between healthy and damaged cells. Representative cells with various crack patterns and
severity levels of cracks were chosen for the cracked module, while the PID module encom-
passed cells exhibiting different degrees of PID severity.
Last, an outdoor experiment was conducted, using one module from each cell technology. EL
spectra measurements were taken with the power supply turned on and off under various optical
filter configurations. Qualitative analysis of the spectra curves and subsequent subtraction of
the curves with and without EL excitation allowed us to isolate the EL signal of the module.
EL images were also captured using modulation of the power supply and image processing
software to enhance the signal-to-noise ratio. Different optical filter configurations and exposure
times were used to assess the image quality and determine the optimal settings for each module.

5.2
Conclusions
In the comparison between the average EL emission from modules with different solar cell
technologies, at a current bias equivalent to the module’s Isc, IBC cells were found to have
the highest emission intensity, the front side of PERC modules 14-21%, and Al-BSF reached
only 1-2% of the IBC peak luminescence intensity. This matched qualitative observations dur-
ing outdoor luminescence imaging found in the literature. A matching trend and very similar
percentages were observed with the modules’ integrated area results. The PV module’s man-
ufacturing year positively correlates with peak intensity and integrated area but not with the
module’s FWHM. Amongst the electrical parameters affecting the intensity of the EL signal, the
cell Voc was shown to have the most pronounced correlation.
A 0.7% maximum variation in peak position was measured within modules with different cell
technology types and is attributed to the differences in stabilization temperature between c-Si
technologies. Variations between cells in a module are maximum 0.28%, while those between
points in a cell showed a maximum of 0.57%. In these two last cases, the variations are ex-
plained by the red-shift caused by increased temperature, as module temperature may have
increased between spectra measurements. Normalized spectra curves match better at module-
than at cell-level, and also better than between different module samples.
Numerically, peak intensity and integrated area variabilities matched at both module and cell
levels, thus concluding to be interchangeable. The maximum peak intensity (and integrated
area) distribution within a module, at 100% Isc condition, was 73%, occurring in the Al-BSF
Multi-Si, due to its multi-crystalline irregular structure. Among the remaining healthy modules
(Al-BSF Mono-Si, PERC 1, and IBC modules), the average peak intensity (and integrated area)
distribution within a module was approximately 52%. At the same time, that at the cell level was
44%. Furthermore, average FWHM distributions at module and cell levels amongst healthy
samples were approximately 3.14% and 2.79%, respectively. At both module and cell levels,
peak intensity (and integrated area) tended to be more significant in the rear surfaces in bifacial
samples. Meanwhile, the opposite could be identified with FWHM variations. Lastly, while
variations in peak intensity and integrated area tend to be lower at 10% Isc compared to 100%
Isc bias at a module level, this trend could not be confirmed at the cell level. No noticeable trend
could be seen for FWHM values at 10% compared to 100% Isc, neither at module nor cell levels.
Cell-level results of degraded cells showed the same peak position variation as undamaged
samples (approximately 0.57%). On the other hand, peak intensity (and integrated area) and
FWHM variations were considerably more significant when defects were present.
Visually, while peak intensity and integrated area heatmaps at the module level and contour
plots at the cell level of healthy and unhealthy samples matched perfectly with each other, only
those at the module level correlated with their respective module’s EL image. Therefore, peak
intensity and integrated area measurements acquired at 10-20 points in a cell are insufficient to
represent the entire cell accurately. This is particularly true when the cell is affected by localized
defects like cracks. Adding on, even though a correlation between EL imaging and FWHM was
also expected, as increased carrier lifetime leads to a reduced FWHM value and would thus be
represented as brighter EL intensity in the image, it was not found either at module or cell level
(neither healthy nor unhealthy).
Finally, results from the outdoor EL experiment demonstrate the usefulness of quantifying the
EL signal in indicating successful daylight EL image acquisition. A clear correlation was found
between the indoor and outdoor experiment: as expected, the higher the measured indoor EL
emission (IBC > PERC > Al-BSF Mono-Si), the more significant the difference between spectra
captured with EL excitation (which includes PL embedded in the sunlight reflection and EL

caused by the power source), and without EL excitation (which only consists of the PL emission).
Therefore, the more precise and more accurate the background-subtracted irradiance curve
produced (a curve that should ideally only represent the module’s EL emission). However, this
is proven to only be accurate at relatively constant solar irradiances. As the desired EL signal
is embedded in the noise from the spectra, the power modulation (and image processing) are
proven to be necessary in most cases to eliminate the noise, retrieve the EL signal and enhance
the SNR value. A general trend is found where increasing the camera’s exposure time for
different optical filter configurations helps improve the image quality (quantified as the SNR).
As potential recommendations for outdoor settings, the IBC module under research pro-
vides SNRKari values above 4 (the minimum level to provide high-quality EL images) under
the no-filter scenario, and all filter scenarios, for exposure times 1-5 ms. The situation which
resulted in the highest SNRKari value for this module, 109.5, was the EL acquisition at 3 ms
exposure time using the BP50 optical filter. Furthermore, the PERC studied module presents
satisfactory signals for image extraction (SNRKari > 1) for all scenarios, however, high-quality
images (SNRKari values > 4) were only obtained when filters were used. BP50, 2 ms expo-
sure time provides the highest recorded SNRKari value (12.01). These outdoor settings would
however not be used as a final recommendation as the acquired SNRKari values showed great
variability, and would thus need to be re-evaluated. While no conclusive outdoor setting recom-
mendation can be provided either for the studied AL-BSF Mono-Si module, as the solar irradi-
ance variations were too high to provide insightful findings, it can be concluded that SNRKari
values higher than 1 (the minimum limit for a satisfactory EL image) were only obtained using
filters: BP50, LP950+SP1250, and LP1050+SP1250. Overall, filter BP50 seems to provide the
best quality results for all three modules.
An approach to predict the requirement of SNR enhancement through the differences in
noise difference between a module’s indoor and outdoor EL spectra is suggested. This
approach is shown to work for the IBC module no-filter case, but additional statistics and EL
spectra data at constant solar irradiances (clear-sky conditions) are needed to verify the use-
fulness of the approach.
In conclusion, this master’s thesis project successfully investigated the EL characteristics of
healthy and damaged PV modules through EL imaging and comprehensive statistics of the EL
emission spectral parameters. The findings provide valuable insights into PV cell-technology
type comparison, module-level, and cell-level mismatch analysis, highlighting the influence of
defects on the EL signal by statistically comparing healthy and damaged cells. Additionally,
outdoor EL analysis results give the baseline to understand the relationship between blocking
the sun and the signal, as using optical filters causes both. The already-acquired data in this
work has the potential to contribute to a future validation of the correlation between EL emission
spectra data and dEL image quality.
5.3
Future work
Regarding the indoor studies, further experiments with more extensive sampling for modeling
(increased number of PV technology types, increased number of modules of each technology,
and different samples of the same technology type but different manufacturing years) are re-
quired to establish the correlation between EL luminescence with a datasheet or STC electrical
parameters and with manufacturing year. Additionally, an increased number of fault types must
be examined to investigate if the relationship between peak position, peak intensity, integrated
area, and FWHM holds true for all cell defects or only for the ones tested in this master’s thesis.
Considering outdoor optical filter and exposure time analysis, to make further progress, it

==PAGE:111==
would be necessary to replicate the procedure with a larger sample size, including more sta-
tistical data and comparing the effects of diffuse and direct sunlight under various irradiance
conditions.
The findings derived from this outdoor analysis indicate several important considerations.
• Firstly, it is recommended to acquire additional dEL statistics with SNRKari calculations,
as the results show a very subtle tendency to increase with longer exposure times, but this
cannot be completely verified as the SNRKari results obtained in this study show slight
inconsistencies.
• Secondly, it is necessary to conduct outdoor EL spectra measurements under completely
stable sunlight conditions (100% clear sky) in order to establish a reliable correlation be-
tween the EL spectra captured outdoors and indoors. The ones acquired in this study
generally show high noise and variations between measurements to validate this relation-
ship.
• From the acquired results we expect that the approach previously suggested for predicting
the need for SNR enhancement (i.e. FFT), via noise difference between a module’s indoor
and outdoor spectra, should work for the IBC cases (BP25, BP50, LP950+SP1250, and
LP1050+SP1250), and for the PERC 1 no-filter case indicative results. In parallel, we
predict the BP50, LP950+SP1250, and LP1050+SP1250 will not need SNR enhancement
to provide a good-quality EL image, but the IBC BP25 and PERC 1 no-filter will. This
hypothesis should be verified with the already-acquired data in this work in order to assess
the potential usefulness and validity of the suggested approach.
• Furthermore, in order for this suggested ”SNR-requirement prediction approach”, it is cru-
cial to establish a conclusive relationship between sunlight-induced noise in the EL spectra
and its impact on the resulting images. This can be done considering the resulting image
profiles (”EL ON” and ”EL ON” gray values) and/or the SNRKari image quality marker.
These outcomes can be utilized in conjunction with other studies that also use the SNRKari
image quality metric (acquiring further statistics from many outdoor EL imaging tests with similar
conditions) to provide a more precise correlation between indoor EL spectra (quantified EL
emission in µW/cm2/nm) and outdoor EL imaging in terms of signal acquisition.
As a future direction, and building upon the results obtained in this thesis, as we have confirmed
there is a correlation between indoor and outdoor EL spectra, it would be recommended to use
the quantified indoor and outdoor EL emission results (expressed in absolute irradiance), as a
foundation to develop a model to predict the performance of daylight EL imaging under
varying sunlight irradiance conditions using the indoor and outdoor spectra. This model
can encompass aspects such as the estimation of optimal outdoor camera exposure times,
identifying suitable filters for varying sun irradiances, and determining the optimal number of
images required to acquire high-quality images, among other essential considerations. In this
way, we would be able to utilize indoor spectral data obtained from a specific module type and
simulate daylight EL acquisition for this module under different daylight conditions to be then
able to predict the outdoor EL imaging optimal conditions before doing the actual outdoor EL
imaging.
Conducting such investigations would be valuable and serve as a logical progression following
the completion of this thesis.
