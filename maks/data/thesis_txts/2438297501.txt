Abstract
Hydrofoils seem to have become an essential asset in the ﬁeld of monohull racing. In
the last edition of the Vendée Globe, 7 boats out of 29 were foil-assisted, while an ambitious
concept of a fully foiling monohull has been proposed by the challenger of the next edition of
the America’s Cup.
However, for surface-piercing hydrofoils, as the speed or the yaw angle increases, a detri-
mental physical phenomenon, ventilation, comes into play, whose consequences are enhanced
in the presence of cavitation or waves.
Numerical methods based on potential theory, such as Boundary Element Methods
(BEM), or the lifting line theory fail at modelling all these phenomena simultaneously, and
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) appears to be a good alternative to expensive towing
tank tests.
This thesis tests the ability of the CFD commercial software STAR-CCM+ to accurately
model the hysteretic behaviour of ventilation, in cavitating and non-cavitating conditions, in
calm sea or in presence of waves. Simulations are performed for still hydrofoil (static) and
yawing hydrofoil (dynamic).
First, a numerical set-up for the two ﬁnalist foils of the last Vendée Globe is validated.
Then, the transition to ventilation is investigated for a symmetric, surface-piercing strut, and
compared with experimental results. Finally, the same methodology is applied to the more
complex geometry of the Vendée Globe foils.
STAR-CCM+ proved itself able to capture the typical hysteresis loop of ventilation for
simple geometries, despite remaining uncertainties as regards the boundaries of the loop. In-
ﬂuence of cavitation in the ventilation process has been conﬁrmed, while the eﬀect of waves
requires further investigation, both numerical and experimental.
For complex geometries, dynamic simulations encountered convergence issues. Static sim-
ulations however suggest a hysteretic behaviour, that is likely to be achieved once divergence
problems will be overcome. Cavitation was not signiﬁcant to be relevant in the tested condi-
tions.
Keywords : CFD, VOF, Surface-piercing hydrofoil, Ventilation, Cavitation, Waves

Résumé
Les hydrofoils semblent s’être imposés comme un atout essentiel dans le domaine des
courses nautiques en monocoques. Dans la dernière édition du Vendée Glove, 7 bateaux sur
29 étaient assistés par des foils. Quant à la prochaine édition de la Coupe de l’America, Team
New Zealand a présenté un ambitieux concept de monocoque entièrement volant.
Cependant, dans le cas d’hydrofoils perçant la surface libre, lorsque la vitesse ou l’angle
de lacet augmente, des phénomènes physiques néfastes entrent en jeu. C’est le cas de la venti-
lation, phénomène intensiﬁé en présence de vagues ou de cavitation. Les méthodes numériques
telles que la Méthode des Éléments Limites, ou encore la théorie de la ligne portante ne sont
pas en mesure de modéliser tous ces phénomènes de manière simultannée, laissant comme
seule alternative la Mécanique des Fluides Numérique (MFN) pour modéliser la complexité du
problème.
Ce mémoire de ﬁn d’études se propose de tester la capacité du logiciel de MFN STAR-
CCM+ à modéliser avec précision le caractère hystérétique de la ventilation, en présence de
cavitation ou non, sur mer calme ou en présence de vagues. Les simulations sont réalisées à
la fois dans le cas d’un hydrofoil immobile à diﬀérents angles de lacet (statique) et dans le cas
d’un mouvement de rotation autour de l’axe vertical (dynamique). En premier lieu, un modèle
numérique est validé pour les deux foils ﬁnalistes du Vendée Globe. La transition entre les
diﬀérents régimes de ventilation est ensuite étudiée dans le cas simple d’un proﬁl symmétrique
placé verticalement à l’interface de la surface libre, pour lequel la comparaison avec des données
expérimentales est possible. Enﬁn, la même méthode est appliquée au cas de la géométrie plus
complexe des foils du Vendée Globe.
STAR-CCM+ s’est révélé capable de capturer la boucle d’hystérésis caractéristique d’un
hydrofoil ventilé dans le cas d’une géometrie simple, bien qu’une certaine incertitude persiste
sur les angles limites de cette boucle. L’inﬂuence de la cavitation sur la ventilation est con-
ﬁrmée, mais l’eﬀet des vagues demande de plus amples recherches, à la fois numériques et
expérimentales.
Pour les géométries plus complexes, cependant, la convergence des simulations dans le
cas dynamique s’est avérée problématique. Les résultats des simulations dans le cas statique
suggèrent toutefois la présence d’un comportement hystérétique. Le phénomène de cavitation
s’est avéré trop superﬁciel pour avoir une quelconque inﬂuence dans les conditions testées.

report of Philippine’s work.

Introduction
1906 marks the beginning of the hydrofoil revolution, when Enrico Forlanini successfully
"ﬂew" over the Maggiore Lake [1].
However, this revolutionary concept never really broke
into the market of commercial vessels, and only few isolated developments were conducted
for war vessel applications or fast ferries in enclosed water [1]. The main reason being the
cost of construction and maintenance of such vessels, since the hydrofoil surface has to remain
exceptionally smooth and clean to ensure good performances [2]. Additionally, Naval Architects
and Marine Engineers struggled to overcome the "50 knots barrier", a speed at which cavitation
is hardly avoidable. It’s not until supercavitating proﬁles - for which performances are optimised
in cavitating conditions, while rather poor in non-cavitating ones- have been developed that
hydrofoils gained popularity again [3, 4].
In the domain of sailing races however, where costs are scarcely a limiting factor - which
makes it a fabulous laboratory for research and development - the hydrofoil concept found
its way. Indeed, a signiﬁcant gain in speed is an obvious asset over other competitors, and
catamarans started to become equipped with hydrofoils in the 70’s.
For monohulls however, notwithstanding small moth dinghies, fastening hydrofoils comes
along with stability issues and the ﬁrst monohull exhibiting such appendages was not seen
until 2015, when Safran Sailing Team decided to test it on its IMOCA 60’. If the ﬁrst races in
sheltered water did not demonstrate the beneﬁts of the foils, the Transat Jacques Vabre 2015
showed their potential and 7 boats out of 29 were equipped with such appendages at the start
line of the last edition of the Vendée Globe, a circumavigation around the globe. Four of those
foiling yachts claimed the top ranks, as the lift gained represents a huge advantage to reduce
the hull resistance in water, around 10 to 15% faster than the traditional hulls for the IMOCA
series [5].
Those are, however, not yet entirely sustained by their hydrofoil, but assisted to unburden
the hull and reduce its drag, while reducing the heel angle, oﬀering more sail surface to the
wind [6].
For the upcoming America’s Cup, though, Team New Zealand introduced a concept of
a fully supported foiling yacht. Hydrofoils continue their progression, as the ﬁrst foiling boat
produced in series is about to be released on the market [7].
That said, the use of hydrofoils raises new engineering challenges.
At high speed or
yaw angles, a phenomenon of air suction along the span of the hydrofoil may occur, called
ventilation. If ventilated, the foil experiences a sudden lift reduction, which can have dramatic
consequences such as loss of manoeuvrability possibly leading to capsizing. Good prediction of
the ﬂow around a hydrofoil is therefore a necessity.
Numerical methods are extensively used to model the ﬂow around such appendages, from
Prandtl’s lifting line theory [8], to more advanced potential panel methods (BEM), including
more and more advanced features to take into account small aspect ratios [3], inﬂuence of the
free surface, cavitation [9, 10], or to model ventilation [11].
However, none of the above mentioned models are able neither to model the hydrofoil
dynamically, nor to investigate the interaction between ship motions, waves, cavitation, and
1
==PAGE:18==
ventilation. CFD, despite higher costs in terms of computational power, is in this way the only
numerical alternative to experimental full scale or model tests in towing tanks or hydrodynamic
tunnels.
Motivation
CFD is increasingly establishing itself as an engineering tool in naval consultancies [12],
especially in the ﬁeld of sailing races, where economic and thereby computational resources
are high. Even if lower cost computational methods are still used at the early design stage,
CFD has become essential during the design process. However, due to the extreme secrecy of
racing, no industrial studies or experimental data are available for real geometries. Only few
general results have been published, including the work of Harwood, who recently conducted an
extensive study of ventilation, comparing CFD results and experiments [13]. Young, Brizzolara
and Keller et al. constitute other examples [4, 14, 15].
However, no record has been found on numerical modelisation of the dynamic behaviour
of ventilation, and even less on the modelisation of ventilated surface-piercing struts in waves.
Nevertheless, waves have been proven to lower the cavitation inception angle [1] and is regularly
cited as a perturbation feature encouraging ventilation formation.
Unfortunately, the last
published study on this matter dates from 1973, while the physical understanding of ventilation
has stepped forward in the past 45 years.

Physics of foiling

1.5
Cavitation
Cavitation is an almost-adiabatic transformation resulting in a phase change from liquid
to gas. The phase change occurs when the local pressure falls below the vapour saturation
pressure of the ﬂuid at the given ﬂuid temperature (and salinity, as sea water is considered
here) [18, 19]. In the case where low pressure is due to a distortion of the pressure ﬁeld by
a ﬂowing liquid, such as a ﬂow around a propeller blade or a hydrofoil, it is referred to as
hydrodynamic cavitation [26].
To keep it simple, cavitation can be thought of as the adiabatic counterpart of boiling,
since boiling is an isobaric process [26].
Three main types of cavitation can be encountered:
• Bubble cavitation is a (cyclic) transient phenomenon where small gas bubbles are
formed and washed down along the ﬂow.
9
==PAGE:26==
• Sheet or Attached cavitation describes bigger cavities rather than isolated bubbles.
It can be a transient or fully established phenomenon. The former is mainly encountered
around hydrofoils and propeller blades, conferring to cavitation an instable nature. Su-
percavitation, as opposed to partial cavitation, is a type of sheet cavitation where
the length of the cavity Lc extends farther than the rear part of the considered object
(trailing edge in case of a hydrofoil or propeller blade).
• Vortex cavitation is encountered at the vicinity of the vortex line, where pressure
drops due to vorticity. This phenomenon can be seen in tip-vortices generated at the tip
of hydrofoils or propeller blades.
For the speciﬁc case of hydrofoils, the cavitation regime at a given Reynolds number Re
(or given speed) depends on two parameters: the angle of attack α of the proﬁle, and the
cavitation number σc. The cavitation number is deﬁned as [19]:
σc = p∞−pvs

For a given cavitation number, in the case of increasing angle of attack, typical regime
transition starts from bubble cavitation: separated bubbles form at the lowest pressure zones
and travel down the hydrofoil. As α keeps increasing, the bubbles coalesce into a stable cav-

is thereby achieved [26].
It is interesting to note that the pressure inside the cavity is not strictly equal to the
saturation vapour pressure. Indeed, a pressure gradient exists inside the cavity. For slender
cavities though, the pressure gradient is so small that assuming the pressure inside the cavity
is constant and equal to the saturation vapour pressure is a good approximation.
It is here that the interest of the VOF method to model the interface between diﬀerent
phases can be seen. Indeed, in case of bubble cavitation, the maximum cell size required to
resolve the bubble interface would be too small, while the VOF model, if not giving the actual
bubble shapes or boundaries, is still able to capture the presence of vapor bubbles by giving
the volume fraction of water vapour.

1.5.2
Half-Sommerfeld treatment
This treatment is not implemented in STAR-CCM+ but the simplicity of the approach
makes it easy to use. The software allows the user to set a minimum pressure under which the
solution is not permitted. The pressure is then bounded by this user-prescribed threshold.
The Gümbel’s condition (also called half-Sommerfeld Boundary condition) was initially
developed in the ﬁeld of tribology to model cavitation of lubricant around rotating shafts [28].
It states that the pressure cannot go below a prescribed pressure, here set as the saturation
vapour pressure.
This treatment does not necessitate the introduction of a third phase nor complicated
phase change models, computationally expensive due to the small time scales required. How-
ever, the model cannot account for bubble dynamics and therefore only applies to the case of
sheet cavitation.
Moreover, there are some drawbacks of using this method:
• the model assumes that the pressure inside the vapour cavity is strictly equal to pvs and
does not take any internal pressure gradient inside the cavity into account.
• the cavity still possesses the physical characteristics of the liquid, including density, or
viscosity.
1.6
Ventilation
As quickly mentioned in the introduction, ventilation describes the phenomenon of air
suction along the span of the hydrofoil at the vicinity of the free surface. As a consequence,
when ventilation occurs, the pressure side is open to the atmospheric pressure, and the hydrofoil
faces a dramatic lift reduction.
Physically, air is sucked in by the modiﬁed pressure distribution (local depression) around
the foil, due to the strut speed. Two conditions have been identiﬁed for the ventilation to occur
[29]:
• a favorable pressure gradient, meaning sub-atmospheric pressures in the regions likely to
ventilate.
• a ﬂow separation. In the absence of ﬂow separation, the air sucked-in along the strut
surface would be carried away along the ﬂow. It is worth noting that ﬂow separation may
be large-scale (such as stall) or small-scale (boundary layer separation).
In her paper, Casey Harwood et al. [30] gives a good overview of the state of the art of
ventilation physical phenomena, and gathers main experimental studies on the topic.

1.6.3
Transition mechanisms
Ventilation will occur if the conditions are such that the stall or bifurcation boundaries are
exceeded, or due to external disturbances. The ﬁrst mode of ventilation is called "spontaneous
ventilation" while the latter is referred to as "perturbation-induced ventilation".
In the following, stall/bifurcation should not be confused with inception/washout. The
inception angle is the angle at which the transition from FW to FV will occur, but not nec-
essarily equal to the stall angle. Conversely, the washout angle deﬁnes the angle at which the
ﬂow shifts from a FV to a FW regime.
Taking the example of a hydrofoil travelling at constant speed with an increasing angle
of attack, in the ﬁrst case, the ventilation inception angle is equal to the stall angle, while
it is expected to be lower in the second case. Similarly, for a hydrofoil travelling at constant
speed with decreasing angle of attack, the Fully Ventilated regime will be delayed until the
bifurcation angle is reached, while the washout angle might be diﬀerent in presence of external
disturbances.
Spontaneous ventilation
Several mechanisms can lead to spontaneous ventilation:
• Nose ventilation: ingress of air is made through the leading edge of the foil. The deﬂection
of the free surface on the pressure side creates a proximity between the separated region
attached to the leading edge (hatched in black in ﬁgure 1.12) and the ambient air. The
seal of water at the interface air/separated region can be broken by disturbing vortices
created at the leading edge of the foil, and then air rushes into the separated region,
eventually leading to a ventilated cavity.
Figure 1.12: Nose ventilation mechanism. Green lines and hatching: air, black hatching:
boundary layer separation, red arrows: ingress paths. Reproduced from [13]
• Tip-vortex induced ventilation: this mechanism describes the ingress of air through the
tip-vortex created at the deepest extremity of the foil, see ﬁgure 1.13. Unlike nose venti-
lation, for which the PV regime is sustained for a few seconds, this mode of ventilation
occurs over a short time lapse, leading to very sharp changes of the hydrodynamic loads.
This mode of ventilation is of course limited to hydrofoils of ﬁnite span, with a rather
16
==PAGE:33==
small aspect ratio, because the vortex needs not to be too long to be able to drive air
down to the foil. Tip-vortex ventilation was experimentally observed for angles of attack
larger than 14◦[13].
Figure 1.13: Tip-vortex ventilation mechanism, Green lines and hatching: air, black hatching:
boundary layer separation, red arrows: ingress paths. Reproduced from [13]
• Tail ventilation is a more complex mechanism observed only for high speeds and moderate
angles of attack. The characteristic depression of the free surface at the tail of the hydrofoil
can be the location for successively growing and collapsing disturbances, leaving residual
vortices that act as air paths towards the separated region.
According to Harwood [13], only tip-vortex ventilation leads to large hysteresis.
Perturbation-induced ventilation
Any perturbation can enhance any of the mechanisms presented above. In the following,
cavitation and waves are investigated as external pertubations.
Indeed, in the case of cavitation, the separated region is already ﬁlled with a gaseous
phase, at constant low pressure (≈pvs), creating a favorable pressure gradient for ventilation.
As soon as the water seal between air and vapour is broken, the cavity rapidly expands due to
the sudden pressure jump (from pvs to patm).
It is reckoned that waves have an inﬂuence on ventilation [1, 13, 29]. All authors agreed
on the fact that waves act as a perturbation and reduce the angle at which ventilation occurs
(the ventilation inception angle), by 2 to 4◦, using empirical correction factors. Unfortunately,
few records have been found of studies investigating the details of such a result. Kramer [33], in
the ﬁrst experimental study on wave inﬂuence on ventilation, assumes that the drifting orbital
particle paths create local velocity variations, which in turn leads to local changes in the angles
of attack. Consequently, instabilities grow, eventually speeding ventilation inception up. This
theory is also mentioned in [3].
Later on, McGregor [1] supports this theory but suggests that steepness and accelerations
are more relevant than orbital velocities, inﬂuencing the inception angle as follows:
• linear decay of the inception angle, with respect to the orbital velocity
• linear decay of the inception angle, with respect to the wave steepness
He also expands Kramer’s conclusions, stating that ventilation inception occurs at a crest
in head seas but that in following seas the most favourable point of ventilation varies, from
crest to trough in ﬂatter waves.

1.8
Wave damping/forcing
As mentioned above, simulating waves is a challenging problem in CFD. Since the ﬂuid do-
main is drastically reduced for obvious computational cost reasons, non-physical wave reﬂections
at boundaries can interfere with the solution. This concerns both prescribed waves to simulate
a sea state, or a system of waves created at the wake of a ﬂoating body or surface-piercing
strut. This issue is resolved by the built-in wave damping/forcing models in STAR-CCM+.
Often, the user is more interested in the solution at the vicinity of the considered body
than at the boundaries.
Forcing or damping applied at a boundary artiﬁcially drives the
solution towards a prescribed solution, which consequently no longer gives a physical result at
the vicinity of the considered boundary.
Wave damping or forcing is especially useful for simulations run over a long time range,
for the formed waves have time to propagate enough to reach the domain boundaries.
The aim of this approach is to reduce the computational cost by allowing a reduction of
the domain while still ensuring a physical solution, undisturbed by unwanted wave reﬂections.

2.1
Modelisation choices
Considering the secrecy of the racing industry, and the complexity of the physics involved
in the whole foiling boat system (since dynamic equilibrium depends on the canting keel, forces
acting of the sails, buoyancy of the hull, ballast dynamics, wind force and direction, sea state,
etc.), simplifying assumptions had to be made prior to launching the simulations. Indeed, in
the absence of input data such as hull, keel & foil 3D models, or reliable weight distribution, it
was decided to put the focus on the hydrofoil only.
Additionally, none of the six degrees of freedom are taken into account, since any move-
ment of the system would impact all the other components. For wave simulations it has been
considered allowing the hydrofoil to sway and roll, modelling the rest of the entire system as a
counteracting weight with a suitable center of gravity. But as the boat rolls, the center of buoy-
ancy of the hull changes, and the canting keel angle changes as well, increasing or decreasing
the component of the lift accordingly.
Great care was taken in the choice of the relevant physical values. The speed is taken
as ||U|| = 20kn, which is around the top speed reached by the foiling contestants of the last

Leeuwin and Cape Horn. Consequently, the route crosses the North Atlantic, South Atlantic,

Conducted simulations
The target of this thesis is to study ventilation for the hydrofoils of the last Vendée Globe.
However, the capacity of STAR-CCM+ to accurately represent the ventilation needs to be

checked prior to blindly trust the results on complex geometries alone. Consequently, the study
has been divided as follows:
• First, a numerical setup is developed for the two ﬁnalists foils of the last Vendée Globe.
In this context, a mesh convergence study is performed, by iteratively reﬁning the volume
mesh. In the absence of any experimental data for comparison, the setup is applied to
another geometry, reproducing the results extracted from a study taken as benchmark
[10]. This accounts for mesh validation.
• Second, the IMOCA hydrofoils are temporarily put aside and ventilation is investigated
in itself. This part mostly relies on the works of Harwood [13], and for this reason the
same geometry is used, namely a symmetric, bi-ogival vertical strut. The inﬂuence of the
angle of attack on ventilation is studied here, while the speed is kept constant. Two types
of simulations are run: a ﬁrst set of simulations (static) for which the hydrofoil is kept
still, for various angles of attack; and a second set (dynamic) in which the hydrofoil is set
in motion, with an increasing or decreasing yawing angle, depending on the phenomenon
considered (ventilation inception or washout, respectively). To avoid confusion, static/
dynamic terminology is preferred to steady/unsteady, since all the simulations are run
using implicit unsteady numerical scheme.
• Third, perturbation-induced ventilation is studied. The numerical setup for the bi-ogival
vertical strut is extended with the introduction of cavitation model or in presence of
waves. Two phenomena are investigated: the transition from fully cavitated hydrofoil to
a fully ventilated one by the breaking of the water "seal" between the vaporous cavity
and the free surface; and the mechanisms involved in ventilation inception in presence of
waves. Mostly dynamic analyses are performed in this part.
• Finally, what has been investigated in the two previous sections is applied to the IMOCA
geometry tested in section one. Considering the increased computational resources nec-
essary to run the calculations with this geometry, only one foil has been selected, namely
Banque Populaire.
In the following, no distinction is made between yawing angle and angle of attack, since
both are strictly identical for vertical struts. For the IMOCA geometries, each proﬁle section
experiences a diﬀerent angle of attack as the hydrofoil rotates, but the overall angle of attack
is deﬁned as the one at which the ﬂow strikes the vertical plane deﬁned by the foil line.
The inﬂuence of ventilation is assessed by the evolution of lift and drag values. Those
values are systematically monitored for each of the considered geometries. The reader shall
bear in mind that for the IMOCA hydrofoils, the lift corresponds to the vertical resulting force
while for vertical struts, the lift is orthogonal to the ﬂow speed in a horizontal plane.

Friedrich-Lewy condition). Physically, this condition may be seen as the insurance that a ﬂuid
particle does not travel more than one ﬂuid volume at a time per time step [43]. However, im-
plicit schemes tolerate larger values of the Courant number, and a value up to 10 is numerically
acceptable [44].
In the following simulations, the Courant number is monitored at the free surface, where
the largest disturbances are expected, generated by the presence of the surface-piercing body.

3.2
Spontaneous Ventilation
In the following, emphasis is given to the physics. As a consequence, the IMOCA geome-
tries are temporarily put aside to study a simpler geometry, extensively studied in the literature
[29, 1, 30, 13, 42, 46].
As previously stated, spontaneous ventilation occurs with an increase of angle of attack
or increasing speed.

tight manoeuvres at high speed, which is expected to be the case in races or regattas.

3.3
Perturbation-Induced Ventilation
In this part, external perturbations and their consequences on the ventilation inception
and washout angles are investigated. In the ﬁrst part, a cavitation model is introduced in the
numerical setup, while the second part focuses on the inﬂuence of monochromatic short waves.
All other parameters are kept constant, speed through Frh, and yawing speed Ω.
3.3.1
Cavitation-induced ventilation
As a ﬁrst attempt to model cavitation, the Schnerr-Sauer cavitation model is considered.
As a drawback, the time-step had to be dramatically reduced. In order to optimise the cal-
culation time, the highest time-step exhibiting convergence is chosen, namely 2.10−5s. This
value corresponds to the optimum for similar simulations conducted on cavitating propellers,
see [44].
First, static simulations were tested. According to [44], instead of running a simulation
with the cavitation model activated from the beginning, the introduction of cavitation after
having reached a fully-developed ﬂow, limits the time/number of time steps necessary to achieve
47
==PAGE:64==
convergence. Therefore, the simulation is run for a few seconds with the time step initially
chosen without cavitation (namely 0.01sec), stopped, and resumed with a smaller time step
after the introduction of the cavitation model.
Quickly, however, this modelisation of cavitation was abandonned. The required compu-
tational power to run a simulation was considered too big for an unsteady simulation with the
rotating region, aiming to last at least 30s (representing 1 500 000 iterations) to cover the span
of considered angles.
The half-Sommerfeld treatment was chosen instead, since only sheet cavitation is of in-
terest here. In the experimental study conducted by Harwood [13], cavitation was observed at
very low cavitation numbers (0,48 < σv < 1,5), where the ambient pressure was reduced up to
patm = 44mbar, reducing drastically the far ﬁeld pressure and getting closer to the saturation
vapour pressure.
The ﬂexibility of CFD makes it easy to reach physical conditions diﬃcult to achieve -
if not impossible - experimentally. Instead of lowering the ambient pressure, it was chosen
to artiﬁcially set the saturation vapour pressure to a value close to the atmospheric pressure,
reducing therefore the cavitation number without greatly varying the simulated set-up. It is
believed that in the ﬁeld of numerical simulation, the fewer parameters modiﬁed, the better,
especially for comparison purposes.
Aiming at a vapour cavity of reasonable length for angles of attack smaller than the
bifurcation angle, the new vapour saturation pressure was chosen. By looking at the pressure
distribution on the non-cavitating foil at various angles of attack, a good candidate appeared
to be pvs = 95000Pa. Consequently, the minimum allowable pressure in the Segregated Flow
model was set to pvs = 95000Pa, leading to a cavitation number lying within the following
boundaries: 0.49 < σc < 0.81 (the cavitation number depending on the far-ﬁeld pressure, it
necessarily depends on depth, see equation 1.16).
The cavity is observed by creating a pressure isosurface where the isopressure value is set
just above pvs, namely 95000,1Pa. Substantial cavitation is expected to occur around 8◦.
It should be emphasised that the minimum allowable pressure parameter is valid only in
case of compressible ﬂows [24], and therefore STAR-CCM+ has to be fooled by changing the
equation of state of the water phase. The latter is indeed changed from Constant Density to
User-Deﬁned Equation of State, where the compressible feature is activated while the physical
characteristics of the ﬂow remain the ones of an incompressible ﬂow. Density is kept constant
and equal to its previous value.
The simulation is directly conducted on a unsteady mode. Introduction of cavitation is
expected to lower the inception angle.

3.3.2
Wave-induced ventilation
Introduction of waves requires some major changes in the simulation setup. Indeed, waves
modelisation (including "simple" 5th-order monochromatic waves) can suﬀer frequency disper-
sion of amplitude damping while propagating on STAR-CCM+ [34]. Therefore, the guidelines
recommended by Perovič are followed:
• A pressure outlet at the top boundary is preferred to a velocity inlet.
• The implicit unsteady solver is extended to 2nd order instead of 1st.
• Wave damping at the inlet, outlet and side boundary is replaced by wave forcing.
The wave forcing coeﬃcient is taken as 20/T, where T is the wave period, obtained by
solving the 5th-order dispersion relation, given the wavelength λ.
The free surface reﬁnement zones are extended ahead of the hydrofoil, to catch the wave
elevation accurately.
The considered waves are 5th-order short waves of wave height H = 0.2m.
Two wave
periods have been considered: T = 0.5s and T = 0.25s. The water depth is set such that the
deep water condition is achieved for both frequencies (the deep water approximation feature
is not directly available for 5th-order waves in STAR-CCM+, but when the software detects a
deep water condition, a message is displayed in the output window). The wave length is given
by the dispersion relation (equation 1.22). The wave steepness s is kept track of, to be sure to
avoid breaking waves (empirical criteria is s = H/λ < 0.142 in deep water).

Back to IMOCA hydrofoils
What has been already been investigated in the previous sections is now applied to the
more complex geometry of the IMOCA hydrofoil. Only Banque Populaire is considered here.
The simulation setup used so far is extended to this case. The vertical bi-ogival strut is replaced
by the Banque Populaire hydrofoil, while the cylinder deﬁning the rotating region is adapted
to the new geometry.
Only non-cavitating and cavitating state simulations have been run, the waves phe-
nomenon requiring further investigation to be plainly understood, primarily to applying it
to a much more complex case.
It is unsure if this geometry would exhibit a large hysteretic behaviour, since the latter
is observed in case of tip-vortex ventilation only [13], and it is hard to say a priori if the
elbow is likely to create such a vortex. Ideally, a dynamic simulation should be run as in the
case of the vertical strut, at increasing and decreasing yaw angle, to be able to ﬁnd out which
mechanisms are involved in the ventilation process. Unfortunately, running such a simulation
was not achieved, accounting for the complexity of the geometry and the inﬂuence of the span
of the hydrofoil. The problem lies with the cylinder interface, where the motion might be too
important at each time step. Indeed, with a cylinder of a radius 6 times bigger than the one of
the simple strut (see table 2.6), a variation of 1◦.s−1 implies larger motions at a distance equal
to the cylinder radius from the rotation axis. Therefore, only static analyses are performed
from yawing angles ranging from 0 to 30◦.

Conclusion
The initial objective of this thesis was to model, through a commercial CFD software, the
mechanisms of ventilation inception and washout that may be troublesome for surface-piercing
hydrofoils, leading to drastic lift drop as soon as a yaw angle is applied. The thesis focuses on a
complex hydrofoil, inspired by the IMOCA 60’ race boats seen in the last edition of the Vendée
Globe, despite the impossibility to get access to the real geometry. Special emphasis is made
on modelling conditions as close as possible from reality, namely in the presence of waves and
cavitation.
First, a numerical setup was developed for this geometry, and, in absence of comparative
data, the same setup was tested for a simple vertical surface-piercing strut. Comparison with
experimental data served as validation. No particular issue was encountered at that stage.
Then, the ventilated regime and its associated transitions were investigated.
For this
purpose, the numerical setup was extended to the case of a rotating hydrofoil, increasing or
decreasing the yaw angle. Both static and dynamic analyses were conducted. The extended
setup was applied to an extensively studied geometry, for which experimental data are available
for comparison. Results are conclusive, since a hysteresis loop was indeed observed for both lift
and drag coeﬃcients, even though, for static simulation, the bi-stable region is only partially
identiﬁed by the software.
Next, the numerical setup was extended to more complex ﬂows, with the introduction of
cavitation model and waves.
Traditional cavitation modelisation through the Scherr-Sauer method ran into computa-
tional cost issues, since very low time-step values have to be chosen for the solver to achieve
convergence of the solution. Instead, a coarser model was chosen, the half-Sommerfeld treat-
ment, where no extra-computational power is required. As a drawback, the "vaporous" cavity
is not represented as a gaseous phase, but bears the same physical characteristics of the liquid.
As a consequence, the expected reduction of ventilation inception angle was not observed in
the case of dynamically yawing hydrofoil. Static simulations, however, exhibited a larger range
of value for which the hydrofoil ventilated than in non-cavitating conditions.
Regarding waves, only 5th-order monochromatic waves were modelled. Two diﬀerent fre-
quencies were tested, for the same wave height. The ﬁrst frequency -2Hz- did not provide
with signiﬁcant change compared to the still water case. For a higher frequency, however, the
hysteresis loop was reduced, and it seems that the introduction of waves triggers nose venti-
lation, leading to a persistent Partially Ventilated state after inception, until the stall angle is
reached. The reference experimental studies seem somewhat outdated to reach this conclusion
with certainty.
Finally, the vertical strut was traded back to the IMOCA hydrofoil. Only static analysis
was performed, since ﬂow continuity issues were encountered at the interface between the static
and rotating region. As expected, an overall performance reduction was observed at any yaw
angle, which is relieved as soon as the foil ventilates. Indeed, in this particular case, ventilation
has a positive impact: the air cavity is located under the tip, and thereby contributes in lifting
the foil out of the water, counteracting the negative eﬀect of the yaw. Cavitation, however, was
67
==PAGE:84==
not suﬃcient in the tested conditions to have any signiﬁcant impact.
To conclude, the goal has only been partially met. The ventilation hysteretic behaviour
was successfully demonstrated, but further investigation needs to be conducted to be able to
model complex geometries in a real sea state.
Further work
As a straightforward improvement, it would be interesting to combine the eﬀects of waves
and cavitation.
Regarding cavitation, it seems unavoidable to solve for the Schnerr-Sauer model, even if
computationally costly. The author truly believes that suﬃcient computational resources will
not be bounded in the near future with the progress and rapid increase of computer capacities.
Besides, the wave eﬀect on cavitation needs to be adressed in a more comprehensive
way, and parallel experimental investigation seems hardly avoidable.
Some of McGregor’s
observations did not have the chance to be veriﬁed numerically, and further studies should
go in that direction, by increasing the variety of frequencies and wave amplitudes tested. The
physical comprehension of the wave impact on ventilation would eventually allow for conducting
numerical simulations in irregular waves, which is hardly - and at great costs - accessible
experimentally. Indeed, with the fast development of computational capacities, it is expected
that CFD would be more systematically used, to eventually rely on model tests for veriﬁcation
only [47].
