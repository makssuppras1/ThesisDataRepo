Master Thesis: Interface passivation of CZTS
Abstract
Kesterite Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) is a promising semiconductor for thin ﬁlm photovoltaic. It is
indeed low cost together with earth abundant and a non-toxic alternative to other materials like
CIGS. However, despite all those advantages, CZTS solar cell performance is still low, limited by
a large open-circuit voltage (Voc) deﬁcit. A part of this deﬁcit is due to interface recombination
losses. The aim of this project is to test surface passivation strategies to overcome this previously
stated problem.
In this work, ultra thin alumina Al2O3 layers are used as passivation layer, deposited using
Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD). Two interfaces are passivated: the back interface between the
CZTS and back contact (Molybdenum) and the front interface between the p- and n-type layers.
As result of the back interface passivation, the Al2O3 layer improved the adhesion of the
CZTS on Molybdenum, preventing delamination during the annealing of CZTS. Moreover, the
optimal thickness found is between 11 and 16 ALD-cycles where the improvement reaches a
plateau. The mean improvement of the eﬃciency is 20-25%. This improvement is observed for
diﬀerent types of glasses and back contact recipes.
While the Fill Factor decreases with the alumina thickness due to a lower shunt resistance, the
open circuit voltage, the short circuit current increase of the optimal thickness. The Voc-deﬁcit
is reduced thanks to the eﬀect of passivation. The short-circuit current Isc is mainly improved
as more light is absorbed in the long-wavelength region corresponding to the bulk CZTS. The
MoS2 usually formed at the CZTS/Mo is modiﬁed by the introduction of the passivation layer.
The thicker the Al2O3 layer is, the thinner the MoS2 layer is and the lower the resistance series
Rs is.
At the end, the optimal thickness of Al2O3 layer is a compromise between a too thick layer
introducing too many defects in the crystal and a layer too thin that is not passivating the cell
as it could.
Concerning the front interface, the passivation of Al2O3 layer is found to be not conclusive.
The unpassivated cells performed better that the passivated ones but the experiments would
need to be continued with a broader range of thickness and better precursor to safely conclude
over the non-eﬃciency of the passivation by Al2O3.

Photovoltaics technology as a promising sustainable energy source
In 2015, the United Nations Member States adopted the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Develop-
ment. Divided into 17 Sustainable Development Goals, the SDGs, it aims at providing a blueprint
for a proper future and urges to take action against global poverty and climate change [1]. The 7th
goal is called "Aﬀordable and Clean Energy", it is focused on increasing the access to electricity
but also on the development of sustainable energy. Worldwide, more and more energy is produced
from renewable sources, see Figure 1. This development comes together with fundamental research
on technology to make them more eﬃcient and growing ﬁnancial ﬂows on the market. According to
the International Energy Agency (IEA), the share of renewable in energy consumption was 10.5%
in 2017 and is expected to keep growing [2]. Among this share, 9% is provided by photovoltaic
panels, see Figure 1.

The photovoltaic (PV) market is divided into several technologies. The most popular one is
crystalline mono or multi-Silicon (c-Si). It covers 95% of the PV production market [4]. The mono-
Silicon solar cells have a commercial eﬃciency of 20-24% while multicrystalline or polycrystalline
Silicon cells reach only 18-20% eﬃciency [4].
Another type of PV cells are the thin ﬁlm technologies. It includes cells like amorphous Silicon
(a-Si), Gallium Arsenide (GaAs), Cadmium Telluride (CdTe), Copper-Indium-(Gallium)-diSelenide
(CIGS and CIS) and Copper-Zinc-Tin-Sulﬁde (CZTS). Based on materials with a direct bandgap
and a higher absorption coeﬃcient, only thin layers are needed to make the devices. However,
there are barely reaching the eﬃciencies of c-Si cells and costs are still higher as the c-Si fabrication
processes are fully optimized [4].
Other types of solar cells are also in development like organic or perovskites solar cells. For
most of them, either the yields are low or the device are not stable in time [4]. Another technology
called tandem cell is a combination of several other types of cells in order to absorb more light,
reaching high eﬃciency. But with good performances comes higher prices.
During this thesis, the focus is set on the thin ﬁlm CZTS cells that will be both fabricated and
improved.

1.2
CZTS solar cells
One type of thin ﬁlm solar cells is the Cu2ZnSnS4 kesterite (or CZTS)-based solar cell. They are
considered a promising future photovoltaic devices as there are made with environmental-friendly
elements (at least the photoabsorber layer is) and their stability is high under operation. They have
potential for being less expensive to manufacture as their number of fabrication steps is lower and
require less energy [4]. The characteristics of the material are described in the following points:
• Cu2ZnSnS4 is a direct band gap semi-conductor. Its band gap is in the range of 1.48 to 1.63
eV, increasing with the [Sn]/[Cu] ratio [5].
• Its absorption coeﬃcient is high (>104 cm−1) [6].
• Its structure is a tetragonal crystal system quite complex because made of four elements
forming a quaternary I-II-IV-VI systems.
Some solars cells are also made with Selenium instead of Sulfur (CZTSe) or with an alloy of
Sulfur and Selenium (CZTSSe). CZTSe solar cells reach higher eﬃciency (see Table 1) but Selenium
is both toxic and not abundant on Earth. With a sulfur-selenium alloy, the band gap can be tuned
in the range 1.0–1.5 eV [7] so even higher eﬃciencies are reached (see Table 1).

The substrate is a soda lime glass. It is the support of the whole solar cell, from which each layers
will be built on. Glasses of 1mm (thin) and 3mm (thick) were used for the CZTS solar cells.
• The back contact
The back contact is collecting the holes. Diﬀerent elements where tested for the layer like In2S3,
ZnO, ZnSnO [7], even tungsten or gold [12]. However, the most common one is the Molybdenum
(Mo). It directly comes from the CIGS structure and despite chemical instability, it remains the
best one in terms of energy conversion eﬃciency [12]. This is the material used in the laboratory.
• The absorber layer
The absorber layer is a positively doped material. This layer is made of Cu2ZnSnS4 and gives
its name to the cell. There is several ways to make this layer. It can be vacuum processes: co-
sputtering, evaporation or pulsed laser deposition but it can also be non-vacuum processes like
spin-coating or spray-coating [13]. Its band gap is between 1.48eV (840nm) and 1.63eV (760nm) [5].
• The buﬀer layer
The buﬀer layer is a negatively doped layer. For the cells of this report, it is made of Cadmium
sulﬁde (CdS). Its bandgap is around 2.5eV (500nm) at 300K [14].
Like the Molybdenum, this
material is based on CIGS cells. Alternative and possibly non-toxic possibilities are investigated:
ZnOS, Cd1−xZnxS[15], In2S3 [16] and others.
• The resistive layer
The resistive layer is made of intrinsic Zinc Oxide (iZnO or RAZO). This layer is more resistive
than the TCO and therefore, the electrons are less able to move from the p-n junction to the top.
However, it is used to protect the cell from pin holes in the ﬁrst layers. Without RAZO, the path of
these pin holes is less resistive than the path to the TCO so electrons are more likely to go through
pin holes, lowering the eﬃciency. The RAZO layer prevents this situation by adding some resistivity
to the path. The bandgap of the AZO is around 3.4eV (365nm) at 300K [14].
The thickness and resistivity of the RAZO layer is therefore a balance between pin holes pas-
sivation and conductivity.
• The Transparent Conductive Oxide
The purpose of the Transparent Conductive Oxide (TCO) is to collect the charges created at
the p-n junction and carry them to the circuit so electricity can ﬂow. In the cells, Aluminium doped
Zinc oxide (Al:ZnO or AZO) is used for this layer.
TCO has two constrains. It must be transparent to light - ideally all wavelength but especially
the ones with an energy above the bandgap - in order to let the photons penetrate the bulk material
to the p-n junction. It should also be conductive to transport through the buﬀer layer the electrons
that are created at the p-n junction.

1.3
Thesis objectives
As presented in the previous subsection 1.2, the world record of eﬃciency of CZTS solar cells is
only 11.0% while CIGS-based cells already reach 23.3%. It was identify that one of the main reasons
for this limitation is a large open-circuit voltage (Voc) deﬁcit [17]. As a solution to this problem,
passivating the cells by inserting a layer at some critical interfaces could reduce the deﬁcit.
In this perspective, this Master thesis consists in the development of nanometer-scale passiva-
tion layers made of Aluminium Oxide Al2O3 (alumina) using atomic layer deposition (ALD) for the
interface passivation of CZTS thin ﬁlm solar cells. Indeed, the passivation properties of Al2O3 have
already be proven in the solar research [18].
Al2O3 passivation layers of diﬀerent thicknesses will be inserted between the CZTS and back
contact (Molybdenum) called "back interface" and between the two materials of the p-n junction
(CdS and CZTS) called "front interface". The thesis goal is to answer the following main research
questions:
• What is the thickness of Al2O3 that gives the highest performance? The atomic layer
deposition is a process allowing a great precision in the thickness of the material deposited as
a cycle of ALD corresponds to the deposition of a single layer. By trying diﬀerent thicknesses,
the objective is to test at which thickness the cells perform best.
• What is the physical eﬀect of the Al2O3 layer on the cells? Based on a literature
review (see section 2.1), the aim is to understand what is happening with the insertion of the
passivation layer. A whole set of characterization techniques is available to inspect more in
details the material to validate or disapprove the observation of previous teams.
This master project is part of the Photovoltaic Materials & Systems group at DTU Fotonik,
ranked among the world leaders in CZTS research. The work took place both in Risø and Lyngby
DTU campus, in collaboration with the cleanroom facilities of DTU Nanolab. The master thesis
counts for 30 ECTS for the Master of Science in Sustainable Energy and was conducted between
January and June 2021.

Theory
The theory section is divided into two subsections. The ﬁrst subsection 2.1 is devoted to the
theory on passivation layers, including a literature review of papers published on the same subject
as this thesis. The second part 2.2 develops the diﬀerent characterization methods used in the scope

2.1
Passivation layers
The passivation consists in making a material becoming "passive", that is to say, to protect him
so he became less aﬀected or corroded by its environment. The insertion of passivation layers is a
process known for some decades in the solar cells industry and is already done in the best performing
silicon solar cells. For instance, it is used in the current silicon record, the passivated emitter and
rear cells (PERC) reaching up to 26% eﬃciency [18]. Adding a passivation layers is expected to re-
duce the surface recombination velocity at the interfaces and to increase the performance of the solar
cell device by increasing the open-circuit voltage [17]. As the high-performing CZTSSe solar cells
are dominated by interface recombination [19], passivation could be relevant to cure this type of loss.
Usually, the passivation eﬀect is sorted into two categories: the chemical passivation and the
ﬁeld eﬀect passivation.
The chemical passivation is the reduction of interface defect density [20].
In a crystal
structure, a number of defects like dangling bonds creates deep defects in the band gap structure.
These defects are the place of Shockley Reed Hall recombination (non-radiative recombination).
The reduction of this defects by passivation (like the curing of dangling bonds by hydrogen or other
materials) leads to a reduction of surface recombination, see Figure 3 for an illustration of the
principle [21].
Figure 3: Chemical passivation principle
The ﬁeld eﬀect passivation is caused by the accumulation of charges in the passivation layer.
These charges are creating a ﬁeld whose eﬀect is to repeal the minority charge carrier (the electron
in case of CZTS) by Coulomb repulsion and force them to change direction while the majority
charge carrier concentration are attracted by the surface. As the concentration of minority charge
carrier is diminished, the probability of surface electron-hole pair recombination is reduced [20]. To
illustrate with an example, Figure 4 sketchs the eﬀect for a CZTS cell with an alumina passivation
layer. Al2O3 has a negative ﬁxed charge density [18]. When arriving at the back interface, the
holes created at the p-n junction are attracted by Al2O3 and passes through the layer to the back
contact. In the case of electrons (the minority carrier), there are repealed by the alumina layer and

2.1
Passivation layers
The eﬀects of the passivation layer on the performance of the device are complicated. Several
hypothesis and explanations are given in literature to explain the physical behaviour of solar cell
with alumina passivation, there are summarized in the following points.
• Reduction in rear surface recombination
The Al2O3 layer causes a chemical and ﬁeld eﬀect passivation as explained in the previous
section. The two phenomena leads to a a reduction in rear surface recombination velocity [19].
• MoS(e)2 interfacial layer
The back contact in Molybdenum (Mo) is a legacy from the CIGS structure from which the
CZTS where built. However, the CZTS/Mo interface is not as chemically stable as CIGS/Mo [22].
As the reaction between Molybdenum and CZTS(e) is energetically favorable, a thick MoS(e)2
interfacial layer is created. In the case of CIGS, the Molybdenum disulﬁde (diselenide) MoS(e)2
layer is important as it ensures a good adhesion between Mo and CIGS and an electrical quasi-ohmic
contact. A similar behaviour is often assumed for CZTS, even if it was not demonstrated yet [17].
However, if the MoS(e)2 is too thick, it will hinder holes transport and thus increase series resistance
(Rs), leading to the deterioration of the device performances [22].
The idea of introducing an Al2O3 intermediate layer is to reduce the thickness of the MoS(e)2
layer to its optimal behaviour. Several research groups tried to texture the alumina layer by some
nanopoints pattern to reduce the MoS(e)2 layer but still ensuring a contact between Mo and CZTS
[22].
• Sodium diﬀusion
In literature, some other eﬀects were shown. The alumina layer leads to a better homogeneity
and compactness for the CZTS absorber, reducing the number of voids [22]. Without the voids
which act like space barriers, sodium from the back side is more likely to diﬀuse to the absorber,
that is beneﬁcial for the cell [22].
However, in other experiments, it appears that the passivation layer tends to prevent the sodium
diﬀusion into the bulk [17].
• Secondary phases
Alumina could also aﬀect the formation of secondary phases like SnS and ZnS. These secondary
phase are believed to deteriorate the performance of the cell: ZnS by blocking the current ﬂow
(increase of Rs) and causing some dead areas and SnS by modifying the interface properties due to
its lower bandgap [19].

2.1
Passivation layers
As for the back interface passivation, many mechanisms occur with the introduction of the
alumina layer. Some are consequences of the insertion, other are presented as the cause of the
passivation. The following points are a synthesis of the various explanations given by researchers
about the passivation of CZTS/CdS interface.
• Reduction in heterojunction interface recombination
The most common eﬀect of passivation is the reduction of interface recombination leading to
the increase of Voc [27].
As explain in the previous section, passivation is divided into chemical and ﬁeld eﬀect passiva-
tion. According to the papers, ﬁeld eﬀect passivation is considered not the dominant eﬀect. Either
a thicker layer of Al2O3 would be require to see its eﬀect [21, 26] or simply because the alumina
is negatively charged so the eﬀect of repealing the electron created at the heterojunction would be
rather worsen the performance than improved it [25].
Therefore, the main eﬀect is chemical passivation: the curing of the dangling bonds eliminates
the deep level eﬀect in the band gap. Once these deep defects are gone, there are less sites for the
non-radiative combination to occur [21, 26].
Moreover, some charged point defects and Cu-Zn disorder at the CZTS surface leads to large
local potential ﬂuctuations. Treating the surface with Al2O3 would tend to passivated the charged
point defects and thus reducing the local potential ﬂuctuation [27].
The origin of this chemical passivation diﬀers from a paper to another. For Park et al. [26],
the hydrogen introduced during the ALD is the element curing the dangling bond in a similar way
that the alkaline elements passivate surface. These elements need a single covalence bond to satisfy
the octet rule so there are the best candidate for passivation (see Chemical passivation scheme
Figure 3). Indeed, in their experiment, the Al2O3 is etched during the deposition of CdS so only
the hydrogen remains at the surface. To test the hypothesis of a non-hydrogen passivation, Ojeda
et al. [21] are depositing the Al2O3 by thermal evaporation. Their results being satisfying, they
concluded that pure oxide is an eﬃcient passivation agent as well. For Cui et al. [27], a Cu-deﬁcient
layer is more likely to be formed thanks to the Al2O3. This layer with a high concentration of
Sodium and Oxygen would be at the origin of the passivation.
The eﬀect of TMA (a component of the atomic layer deposition) is also investigated. Cui et al.
[27] note an increase of the cell performance with only TMA in the ALD. They inferred that TMA
plays key role in passivation. On the opposite, Lee et al. [23] conclude that TMA plays no part in
the improvement of the device.
More than hydrogen, the segregation of sodium from bulk to the surface may also contribute
to the passivation [26, 27].
• Change in the band gap diagram
The Al2O3 is inserted at the p-n junction, the area in the cell where the band diagram is
important as it is at the origin of the charge carrier creation and migration.
In unpassivated devices, the large spike-like conduction band oﬀset in CZTS/CdS heterojunc-
tion is identiﬁed as one of the reason for large Voc-deﬁcit [21]. The conduction band oﬀset is even
larger in passivated device as alumina introduces an extremely large peak, creating a barrier that
can be overcome by electrons through tunneling, as the Al2O3 is thin [21]. Therefore, the alumina
layer should stay thin to avoid stopping the electrons but still chemically passivate the interface.

Moreover, according to ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy measurement from Cui et al [27],

separated so the interface recombination decreases.
• Passivation of pinholes and grain boundaries
For Lee et al. [23], the role of the Al2O3 is rather to insulate the unwanted conduction path-
ways like pinholes or blocking shunt. The ALD precursor penetrates into pinholes and the grain
boundaries of CZTS and increases the shunt resistance by the formation of an insulating Al2O3
layer. This eﬀect can be assimilated to the role of the RAZO layer.
• Prevention of intermixing of species at interface
In their paper, Kim et al. [17] notice a severe intermixing between CZTSe and CdS that is
blocked by the Al2O3 layer. As a consequence, the copper and cadmium do not inter-diﬀuse between
the p-n junction.
The mixing was considered beneﬁcial for CIGS as it is n-doping the Cd. Nevertheless, for the
CZTSSe, the mixing has been reported to have a negative inﬂuence on the performance [17].
• Widening of the depletion width
According Erkan’s team [25], one of the eﬀects of ALD-Al2O3 treatment is a wider depletion
width caused by a reduction in ﬁxed negative charge. Thanks to a capacitance-voltage proﬁle, they
could notice a depletion width of the passivated device of nearly 200 nm deeper tha the unpassivated
one.

2.2
Background on characterization methods
To characterise the samples, a set of techniques are selected for the project. The following
section presents the theory behind the characterization methods and their relevancy in the scope of
this study. The methods involved in the Thesis and their applications are:
• Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM): morphology of the surface, surface imaging including
cross section.
• Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy (EDX): chemical composition, stoichiometry.
• Raman spectroscopy: crystal phases.
• I-V Curve: electrical parameters including the eﬃciency η, the open-circuit voltage Voc, the
short circuit current Isc and the Fill Factor FF. With a mathematical ﬁtting, it is possible to
calculate as well the series and shunt resistances Rs Rsh, the ideality factor A and the dark
saturation current J0.
• External Quantum Eﬃciency (EQE): eﬃciency against the wavelength, it gives information
over the contribution of each type of losses and some parameters of the cells can be calculated
out from the EQE (band gap energy of all layers that actively absorb part of the light, short
circuit current density).
2.2.1
Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy or SEM is a characterization technique used to visualized the
surface of a sample at high magniﬁcation and oﬀers a 3D vision. SEM was mainly used in its
secondary electron mode (topography information) but other modes exist like the back scattered
electron mode (chemistry composition).
This method is based on an electron in-electron out interaction. A primary electron is send
at high energy to the surface of the sample. It impacts a weakly bonded electron of one atom of
the sample. This secondary electron is ejected to the detector where its energy is detected, giving
information on the surface.
The disadvantage of this technique is the need for a conductive material sample and the re-
quirement of a vacuum chamber - to avoid parasitic detection and to increase the mean free path
of the electrons. The requirement of a conductive material is fulﬁlled for semi-conductor samples.
A typical SEM cross section looks like the Figure 8. The thickness of the layers can be calculated
and compared from one sample to another. MoS2 layer is the slightly darker part of Molybdenum.
The passivation layers are too thin to be seen with SEM as there are smaller than 2 nm. The lateral
resolution of an SEM image is 5 to 10 nm [28].

2.2.2
Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy
Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy or EDX gives information on the chemical composition
of the surface (or even the bulk) and the stoichiometry.
A beam of electron is projected to the sample.
It can be the same as for SEM, the two
techniques are often coupled. The electron can eject an electron bonded to the atoms of the sample.
This ejected electron leaves a hole that is quickly recombined by an electron of higher energy. During
the recombination, a photon is emitted - whose energy is the diﬀerence of the two electron shells.
This diﬀerence is unique to each atom and electron shells. However, the diﬀerence of shells for two
elements can be very similar so overlapping of the emitted X-ray does not allow the distinction
between the two.
The lighter elements (from hydrogen to boron) can not be detected as their
transition energy is too low or not existing. For an incident beam energy of 15 keV (what is used
during the experiments), the volume of interaction is of the order of magnitude of the micrometer
(1-5 µm) [28].
For CZTS solar cells, EDX is quantitatively used to measure ratios of elements in the CZTS
layer and to check if the composition is good or if it should be adjusted. The theoretical composition
of the crystal is: Cu2ZnSnS4 so the stoichiometric ratio are Cu
Sn = 2 and Zn

4.1.2
Quality control of CZTS
The study of CZTS solar cells is a loop process. The cells are ﬁrst fabricated, there are then
characterized. All data are reported in the logbook. Depending on the performance of the cells, the
parameters of fabrication are adjusted. CZTS fabrication is a process with a low resilience, small
changes can easily disturb the process enough to leave the optimal region. Moreover, parameters
tend to drift from the ideal region to sub-optimal parameters with time, reducing the performance
of the solar cells.
SEM, EDX and Raman spectroscopy are performed to keep track of deviations and correct it.
• EDX
EDX gives the composition of the CZTS. It can be performed on CZTS precursor (before
annealing) or on annealed CZTS. For the same sample, the pre- and post-annealed compositions
tend to diﬀer as the annealing changes the chemical composition either by re-structuring the crystals
or by the addition of Sulfur and Tin. In our case, the Cu/Sn increases after annealing (between
5% and 10% of relative change) and the Zn/Sn decreases after annealing (between 5% and 20% of
relative change).
Unfortunately, the results given by EDX are not exact as the CZTS layer is too thin (400-
600 nm) compared to the depth of inspection (1-2 µm). The ratios of interest (Cu/Sn and Zn/Sn)
tend to be higher for thinner layer than what they truly are. To give an idea, CZTS has been
sputtered on Silicon during 40 min and 15 min and then the EDX ratio are calculated and presented
in Table 13. As no Silicon is detected for 40 min, it can be infer that the 40 min ratios are close to

4.1.3
Evolution of fabrication parameters
Even thought it is not the main objective of the project, the conditions of fabrication
were adjusted along the time of the project. The modiﬁcations are done in order to get better
performances.
• Delamination
Delamination is a mode of failure where a material fractures into layers, see Figure 24. De-
lamination happens after the annealing or during the ammonium hydrosulﬁde etching before CdS.
In our case, delamination is the CZTS layer that does not adhere to the back contact any more so
the ﬁlm is separated from the cell. Either the entire cell (see Figure 24) or only a part of it can be
delaminated. It limits the progress as a part of the material is wasted.
Delamination was prevented by adapting the annealing parameters (from long time (45 min)
low temperature (575°C) to shorter time (15 min) high temperature (585°C)) or the sputtering
composition (see next point). It is more frequent for thin substrate (1 mm).
Figure 24: Example of a delamination after annealing - the left samples are thick and the right
delaminated one is thin
• Zinc content adjustment
The targets power of CZTS sputtering were shifted from high Zinc content to a lower Zinc
content, closer to idea theoretical conditions. The explored range of Zn/Sn is depicted in Figure
25 where the explored range is shown. Nevertheless, lower Zinc content samples were more prone
to delamination. As it is discuss later in the result, alumina at the back protects the samples from
delamination. Therefore, with the Al2O3, a broader range of composition could be explored.

Due to overlapped peaks, some elements are diﬃcult to distinguish. The Molybdenum and
Sulfur have their main peaks at the same energy (2.307 keV for Kα of S and 2.293 keV for Lα of
Mo). It is therefore not possible to know the diﬀerence between Molybdenum and MoS2. For seeing
the diﬀerence, other techniques like Raman should be used.
In literature, a change in the Sodium diﬀusion is spotted as an eﬀect of passivation [17, 22].
Unfortunately, it is impossible to learn about Na diﬀusion as Na main peak is overlapping
with Zn in EDX (1.041 keV for Kα of Na and 1.012 keV for Lα of Zn).

4.2.4
Conclusion for the back interface passivation
After the fabrication of many batches, there is enough statistics to conclude over the improve-
ment due to the insertion of a passivation layer between the Molybdenum constituting
the back contact and the absorber CZTS layer. The same phenomenon has been observed
over diﬀerent substrates, leading almost all the time to a higher eﬃciency for the passivated samples
(Fig 30). The improvement is maximal for a plateau around 10 to 15 ALD-cycles (corresponding
to 1.2 to 1.7 nm) of alumina thickness (Fig 28). Beyond that point, the performance degrades and
the passivated cells are worst for more than 20 ALD-cycles (around 2.3 nm).
Another important diﬀerence with the passivated sample is the improvement in adhesion of the
CZTS to the Mo (Fig 27 & 46). This eﬀect is especially seen for thin substrates that are delaminating
more frequently. The Al2O3 layer also aﬀects the back contact by reducing the thickness of the MoS2
that is normally formed at the Mo/CZTS interface. For high thicknesses (>25 ALD-cycles), no MoS2
is formed anymore (Fig 45 & 44). It is possible that the reduction of the MoS2 positively impacts
the eﬃciency as a too thick MoS2 layer could hinder the holes transports [22].
Nevertheless, many properties of the cell are not inﬂuenced by the passivation layer. The alu-
mina layer does not impacts the formation of ZnS at the back of the absorber layer (Fig 47) not
the band gap (Table 18). The front surface of CZTS (Fig 37 & 38) is similar with or without alumina.
While the ﬁll factor keeps degrading with the thickness, the Voc and Jsc reach a maximum at the
10-15 cycles plateau. The improvement of Voc is believed to be due to the chemical passivation. The
improvement of Jsc is linked with a higher absorption in the CZTS region (Fig 33a). Unfortunately,
the eﬀect of the alumina layer on the Sodium diﬀusion could not be inspected as Na is overlapping
on Zn using EDX.

During this work, the back interface of the CZTS cells was inspected. It was the ﬁrst time it

5
Conclusion
During the thesis, the potential of passivation by deposition of Aluminium Oxide (Al2O3) layer
by Atomic Layer Deposition in CZTS thin ﬁlm solar cells was investigated.
On the one hand, the results for the passivation at the back contact/CZTS interface (or
back interface) are quite encouraging: the adhesion is improved, removing risks of delamination and
the eﬃciency is slightly improved for a thickness around 10-15 ALD-cyles. This change could be
included on a systematic way to the fabrication process as it is beneﬁcial.
On the other hand, the results for the passivation at the CZTS/CdS interface (or front inter-
face) implies that the Al2O3 layer has a negative impact on the performance of the cell. The devices
without the Al2O3 layer are performing better than with it. Its use is therefore not recommended,
unless a more complete study gives a diﬀerent conclusion.
A detailed study has been carried out to understand the passivation mechanism in the case of
the back interface. First, the increase of the Voc for the 10-15 ALD-cycles plateau (corresponding
to a thickness of 1.2 to 1.7 nm) is a direct eﬀect of the passivation role played by the Al2O3 layer.
The improvement of the Jsc is due to a better absorption of the CZTS bulk layer as seen on the
EQE curves. The collection of carrier is also improved as the EQE of the sample with Al2O3 is less
bias-depend than without it. The ﬁll factor degradation induced by the thickness of the Al2O3 layer
can be explain by the decrease of the shunt resistance - likely to be due to the insertion of defects
from Al2O3 in the CZTS crystal lattice. An important eﬀect of the Al2O3 is the role it plays in
the formation of the MoS2, usually between the back contact of Molybdenum and the CZTS. The
thicker the Al2O3 layer is, the thinner the MoS2 layer is. At the optimal thickness, the thickness of
MoS2 and Al2O3 are certainly optimized to hinder the less as possible the hole transport but still
ensure a good contact between Mo and CZTS.
Further study may complete the comprehension by using some other characterisations tech-
niques:
1. a SIMS or Atom Probe Tomography depth proﬁle - with a resolution that enable to distinguish
Sodium from Zinc and also to know more about the distribution of Al in the cell (as the EDX
mapping is giving some confusing results) 2. a Photoluminescence spectrum, either mapping of the
surface or a time-resolved photoluminescence - in order to see if the signal is increased or not with
passivation.
The front interface study was less extensive that the one of the back interface. It would be
relevant to continue the work with better precursors as even the reference performances are low.
Some suggestions to explore further the front passivation:
1. Try again the series above 30 cycles as it seems there is a recovery of the reference performance
in the 2 ﬁrst series.
2. Try a lower ALD deposition temperature - it is possible that a too high temperature is damaging
the CZTS layer.
3. Try diﬀerent substrates. As all the substrate are not behaving equally, it is possible that an
improvement can be seen on some of them and not on others.
4. Try a diﬀerent passivation layer (like ZnS), maybe with a diﬀerent deposition technique that
does not involve high temperature.
5. Characterized more in detail a reference against a passivated sample to gain some understating
about the mechanisms at stake.
