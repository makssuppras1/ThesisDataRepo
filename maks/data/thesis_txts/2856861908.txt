Abstract  
The Greenlandic lifestyle is deeply connected to nature, with recreational activities in the 
backcountry playing a significant role in daily life. Recreational cabins are an integral part of this 
culture, sometimes self-built using prefabricated modules and components imported from milder 
climates which face significant challenges in Greenland’s High Arctic conditions. The intense sun, 
strong winds, extreme temperatures, and drifting snow place immense demands on the building 
envelope, often compromising its integrity. Additionally, the remoteness of building sites, 
combined with limited skilled labour and challenging logistics, often delays construction by months 
or even seasons. Furthermore, the unique Arctic lifestyle presents spatial demands that are rarely 
met by designs originating from more temperate regions. 
This research investigates the design flaws of existing cabins through site visits and interviews with 
local stakeholders, including hunters, Arctic Circle Trail participants, and users of snowmobiles, all-
terrain vehicles (ATVs) and dog sleds. An extensive literature review complements this fieldwork, 
analysing the state-of-the-art in prefabricated cabin designs and technologies, including innovative 
waste-handling solutions. 
The outcome of this project is a conceptual design for a recreational cabin tailored to Greenland’s 
Arctic conditions. The design addresses critical issues such as climate resilience, lifestyle 
requirements, infrastructure limitations and terrain challenges. The emphasis is placed on 
prefabrication, modularity, material selection, transportability, buildability, and architectural 
integration. This concept offers a practical, sustainable, and culturally relevant cabin design that can 
be easily used by municipalities, local communities, and individuals to meet the unique demands of 
Arctic life.

1. 
Introduction 
1.1 
Background and Context 
Greenlandic lifestyle is deeply rooted in their Inuit heritage, with a strong connection to nature and 
a resilient spirit honed by the harsh Arctic environment [1]. Traditional practices like hunting, 
fishing, and dog sledding are still cherished and integral to their culture, as I observed during my 
stay in Greenland for six months to study how to build in extreme environments with DTU. While I 
primarily stayed in Sisimiut on Greenland's West coast, as shown on the map below, I also visited 
Ilulissat, Kangerlussuaq, Attu, Kangaatsiaq, Aasiaat, Qasigiannguit, and the abandoned settlement 
of Sarfannguit. 
 
Figure 1 - Map of Greenland [4] 
 
Cabins, huts or "hytter" in Danish, are an important part of life for many Greenlandic people for 
hunting, fishing and dog sledding, and for the international community for mainly hiking [2]. 
During my stay in Greenland I observed that people retreat to their private cabins on weekends or 
holidays to enjoy peace and connect with nature, while the international hiking community mostly 
relied on public community cabins [2] conveniently scattered near towns across the landscape. 
These cabins, usually located in remote areas that can only be reached by hiking, All Terrain 
Vehicles (ATV), snowmobiles, or dog sleds, represent a simple and self-sufficient Greenlandic way 
of life [3]. These cabins generally do not have modern amenities like electricity, drainage or 
running water, mainly due to the remoteness and the difficulty to connect to the available 
infrastructure, which is generally limited to the settlements and towns.

1.2 
Problem Statement 
The design of most existing cabins in Greenland reflects a longstanding influence from Danish 
architecture, seen in features such as rectangular layouts, open-gable roofs, and timber construction 
[5]. While these elements have become common in Greenlandic cabins, they are not ideally suited 
for Arctic conditions. Rectangular forms with open-gable roofs have a higher surface area compared 
to more compact shapes such as domes, leading to greater heat loss, which is an obvious 
inefficiency in the Arctic climate [6]. Additionally, timber, while abundant in Denmark, is scarce in 
Greenland, making it a less sustainable building material choice for local construction [7]. 
 
Figure 2 - Typical Public Cabin from the Arctic Circle Trail 
To maximise the efficiency of building in Greenland due to shorter construction windows and 
unavailability of materials and workforce, housing in Greenland in the 1950’s shifted more toward 
prefabricated ‘type houses’ commonly used across the country [6]. A similar prefabricated approach 
has also been applied to private and public cabin constructions where some owners opt for self-built 
options using prefabricated cabin modules and components imported from milder climates [8] 
which could bring however the following climatic; logistical and construction; and design and 
functionality challenges. 
1.2.1 
Climatic Challenges 
Extreme temperatures  
Prefabricated modules and components from milder climates may not be equipped to handle the 
extreme cold of the Arctic, which in the coastal regions range from -8°C in the temperate south to   
==PAGE:10==
 
  DTU

-30°C in the north [9]. Materials used may become brittle, lose their insulating or sealing properties, 
or fail to prevent heat loss, leading to inefficient energy use and discomfort for occupants.  
Strong winds  
Structures designed for milder climates may not be able to efficiently handle the high winds and 
heavy snow loads common in the Arctic produced by katabatic winds and Arctic cyclones [9]. This 
may result in structural instability, damage to the building envelope, and potential safety hazards. 
Rectangular shapes would also not be ideal for perpendicular forces. 
Snow and Ice Accumulation 
Drifting snow and ice buildup may exert additional loads on roofs and walls. Prefabricated elements 
not designed for heavy snow loads might collapse or experience leakage issues as the snow melts 
and refreezes, compromising the building's integrity

Permafrost  
The shifting and thawing of permafrost may cause uneven settling of foundations, leading to 
structural damage. Prefabricated modules and components may not be designed to accommodate 
these ground conditions.

Arctic sun  
The extended daylight hours in summer, the higher levels of UV radiation due to thinner ozone 
layer [10], and intense sunlight due to reflection of sunlight off snow and ice may cause materials to 
degrade faster.

1.2.2 
Logistical and construction challenges 
Remoteness  
Transporting prefabricated modules and components to remote locations in the Arctic may be 
challenging and expensive. The limited infrastructure and accessibility of many sites may also 
complicate construction. 
Limited workforce and skills  
The availability of skilled labour in remote Arctic communities may be more limited [7], making 
the assembly and construction of prefabricated cabins more challenging. 
Short construction season  
The short Arctic construction season may further complicate and delay building projects, increasing 
costs and potential for weather-related disruptions. 
1.2.3 
Design and functionality challenges 
Space requirements  
Arctic lifestyles and activities may require different spatial configurations than those found in 
standard prefabricated cabin designs. For example, there might be a need for larger storage areas for 
equipment and supplies or dedicated spaces for drying gear [6]. 
Energy and waste management 
Arctic conditions require efficient energy and waste management solutions due to the lack of 
infrastructure [11]. Cabins designed for milder climates might not include adequate provisions for 
renewable energy systems or waste-handling solutions suitable for remote Arctic locations. 
Cultural appropriateness  
Prefabricated cabin designs from milder climates may not reflect the cultural preferences and 
traditions of Arctic communities, leading to dissatisfaction and a sense of disconnect from the built 
environment [8]. 
1.3

Following the perceived challenges of using imported prefabricated cabin designs from milder 
climates, the research aim and objective is to provide the concept design of a recreational cabin 
specifically suited to the Arctic, addressing the unique needs and challenges of the region. The 
objectives of this study will be the following.

 Assess Current Design and Usage Challenges

• Identify the impact of environmental factors on cabin durability and functionality.

hunters, recreational trail users, and other groups who frequent these cabins, to understand 
usage patterns and specific space needs. 
• Explore how Greenlandic lifestyle requirements, including seasonal activities and 
backcountry practices, influence space needs and cabin functionality.

2. 
Literature Review 
2.1 
Overview of the Greenlandic Climate and Environment 
Greenland is defined by its extreme and diverse Arctic climate, shaped by its geographic location, 
vast ice sheet, and unique topography. Its environment is characterised by considerable variations in 
temperature, precipitation, and wind patterns, which differ significantly between coastal regions and 
the interior. These conditions interact with dynamic processes such as ocean circulation, glacial 
melting and atmospheric feedbacks influencing not only Greenland’s ecosystems and weather but 
also its role in the global climate system. 
 
Figure 5 - Schematic of some of the key Climatic Features and Mechanisms in the Arctic [9] 
General Geographical Description 
Greenland, the largest island in the world, is comprised of numerous mountainous islands almost 
entirely covered with a permanent ice cap up to 3,000 m thick [12]. It spans 24 degrees of latitude 
(2,670 km), is 1,200 km across at its widest point, and covers an area of 2,166,086 km2. Along the 
Greenland coast, outlet glaciers flow from the ice sheet to the sea, which periodically break off, or 
“calve”, forming icebergs.

Extreme Temperatures 
Greenland experiences some of the most extreme temperatures in the world due to its Arctic 
location and the vast expanse of the Greenland ice sheet, which covers approximately 80% of its 
surface. Temperatures vary significantly depending on geographical location, elevation, and 
proximity to the coast, where coastal areas generally exhibit milder temperatures compared to the 
interior.  
Winter temperatures along the coasts can range from -8°C in the relatively temperate south to as 
low as -30°C in the colder northern regions, and during summer, coastal temperatures may rise to 
between 5°C and 10°C in the south. In contrast, the central ice sheet often records temperatures 
below -50°C during winter, and even in the peak of summer, temperatures rarely exceed -10°C [9]. 
The interplay between Greenland’s extreme temperatures and its ice sheet creates a complex 
climate system, where the cold temperatures in the interior support the accumulation of ice and 
snow, while warmer conditions along the coasts can accelerate glacial melting, particularly during 
the summer months. This temperature gradient between the ice sheet and the coastal areas also 
drives significant atmospheric dynamics, including katabatic winds that further shape the region’s 
environment [9].

Wind 
Greenland's unique topography and climatic conditions contribute to the prevalence of high winds, 
particularly along its coasts and ice sheet margins. One of the most notable wind phenomena is the 
katabatic wind, which occurs as cold, dense air descends from the central Greenland ice sheet 
toward lower elevations. These winds are particularly intense during the winter months, when the 
temperature difference between the ice sheet and surrounding regions is most pronounced. [9]. 
In addition to katabatic winds, Greenland's coastal regions are influenced by Arctic cyclones and 
large-scale atmospheric circulation patterns, such as the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO), a 
climate phenomenon characterised by irregular fluctuation of atmospheric pressure over the North 
Atlantic Ocean [14]. These cyclones bring stormy conditions, enhancing wind speeds and creating 
hazardous weather [9]. 
 
Figure 8 - The North Atlantic Oscillation [14] 
Precipitation 
The Arctic receives relatively low total annual precipitation, generally less than 500 mm and 
typically ranging between 200 and 400 mm, with precipitation being higher along the Arctic coast. 
==PAGE:16==
 
  DTU

Since cold air holds less moisture, the frequency of precipitation may be high, but the overall 
intensity remains low, which explains why snow accumulation during winter is relatively modest 
across much of the Arctic. The driest regions, where precipitation levels are approximately 140 mm 
per year, are located in eastern Siberia, northern Canada, and Greenland; however, as one moves 
toward the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, total precipitation gradually increases to exceed 600 mm 
per year. [12] 
 
Figure 9 - Distribution of precipitation (mm/y) in the Arctic [12] 
Intense Sun Exposure 
Despite its Arctic location and cold climate, Greenland experiences intense sun exposure during the 
summer months due to extended daylight hours and the high reflectivity of its ice-covered surface. 
The phenomenon of the midnight sun, where the sun remains visible for 24 hours a day in northern 
areas, amplifies the exposure to solar radiation. Clear skies common during summer further enhance 
the intensity of sunlight, particularly in the interior and coastal regions where snow and ice reflect 
solar energy back into the atmosphere [9].

Drifting Snow 
Drifting snow is a defining feature of Greenland's climate, significantly influenced by its persistent 
high winds and vast snow-covered terrain. Strong katabatic winds descending from the Greenland 
Ice Sheet and coastal storm systems frequently redistribute snow, creating snowdrifts and altering 
surface conditions. This process is particularly prominent in winter, when winds are strongest, and 
the landscape is dominated by fresh snowfall [9]. 
Snowdrifts accumulate in depressions and other sheltered areas, significantly affecting snowpack 
depth and density while posing challenges for human activities and infrastructure. These drifts can 
obstruct transportation routes, such as roads and airstrips in coastal settlements, and create logistical 
difficulties in research areas on the ice sheet by burying equipment [9]. 
2.2 
Arctic Infrastructure and Society 
2.2.1 
Kalaallit Nunaat (Greenland) and the Inuit 
To understand the housing and cabin situation in Greenland, it is necessary to understand the history 
and culture of the Inuit. 
The term "Inuit" is a modern ethnonym referring to the descendants of peoples who archaeologists 
believe lived in and around the Bering Strait approximately 2,000 years ago. Evidence suggests that 
these ancestors migrated across the northern coastal regions of North America and into Greenland 
during the 13th century, as shown in the figure below, likely replacing or absorbing earlier 
inhabitants. By the time of European exploration and the subsequent colonisation of the Americas, 
Inuit ancestors were well-established in their traditional homelands, now known as Inuit Nunangat 
in Canada and Inuit Nunaat in Greenland [15]. 
 
Figure 11 - Migration from Bering Strait Through Canada to Greenland [16] 
Historically, Inuit societies were organised into small groups, usually comprising a few dozen to a 
few hundred individuals, tied to specific regions of land, water, or ice. These groups identified 
themselves with a primary place by adding the suffix -miut, meaning "the people of." While each 
==PAGE:18==
 
  DTU 
Prefabricated Cabin for Greenland 
  December 2024        
MSc Thesis 
Page 12
 
group maintained distinct regional identities, they shared common cultural practices, beliefs, and 
spoke dialects of a single language, collectively referred to as Inuktut. The word "Inuit" itself means 
"people" in Inuktut, with its singular form being "Inuk." Following colonisation, the term took on 
an additional meaning, uniting Inuktut speakers and their descendants as a single people. In 
Greenland, the Inuit refer to themselves as Kalaallit (singular Kalaaleq). [15] 
 
 
Figure 12 - Celebrations on Greenland's National Day on the 21st of June in Sisimiut 
Today, melting permafrost presents significant challenges to modern infrastructure across the 
Arctic, threatening homes, airport runways, and public buildings. The Greenlandic people, in 
particular, have sought remedies to colonial legacies by advocating for greater autonomy through 
Home Rule, with many aiming for full independence. However, Greenlandic people now face 
complex questions about balancing traditional knowledge with modern scientific approaches. For 
example, efforts to manage wildlife sustainability using techno-scientific standards often conflict 
with the practical, localised knowledge of hunters and fishers.  
 
Figure 13 - Some Houses in Sisimiut Have Settling Issues, Probably Due to Melting Permafrost 
2.2.2 
Greenland Political Setting 
Greenland achieved Home Rule in 1979, followed by a greater degree of autonomy with the 
establishment of Self Rule in 2009. A pivotal development came in January 2010, when ownership

of subsurface resources was officially transferred from Denmark to Greenland. Since then, much of 
the national discourse has centred on how Greenland’s economic future is linked to the extraction of 
minerals and hydrocarbons. [17] 
For several years, democratically elected Greenlandic parliaments and governments have 
emphasised that oil, gas, and mineral exploitation should play a key role in contributing to the 
sustainable development of Greenlandic society. This strategy is part of a broader effort to reduce 
the country’s dependence on the annual block grant it receives from Denmark. 
However, these resource-centred policies often overlook local community perspectives on the 
interconnected relationships between humans, the environment, and the more-than-human world. In 
northwest Greenland, for example, many residents view themselves and animals as sharing 
interconnected spaces and ecologies, rather than existing in separate or mutually exclusive realms. 
This contrast highlights the complexities of balancing national economic aspirations with deeply 
rooted cultural and ecological values. 
2.2.3 
Relationship Greenland and Denmark 
Greenland, with a history of human habitation stretching back 4,000 years to the first Arctic settlers, 
has evolved into a young democracy, having gained legislative power only in 1979. Despite this 
progress, Greenland remains technically tied to Denmark as part of the Kingdom, operating under a 
foreign parliament and constitution [3]. 
Early History (982 - 1721) 
Greenland was initially inhabited by Norse Vikings led by Erik the Red in 982. These settlements, 
primarily in southern Greenland, persisted for several centuries but ultimately declined and 
disappeared by the late 15th century due to factors such as climate change, disease, and economic 
challenges.   
Greenland fell under Norwegian rule in 1261, and subsequently became part of the united Danish-
Norwegian kingdom in 1536. This period marked the beginning of Scandinavian influence on 
Greenland, shaping its political and cultural landscape [18]. 
Early Contact and Colonisation (1721-1953) 
In 1721, Denmark-Norway, under the leadership of missionary Hans Egede, established a colony in 
Greenland. The primary objectives of this colonisation were to Christianise the Inuit population and 
establish a profitable trade network [19]. 
Following the dissolution of the Denmark-Norway union in 1814, Denmark retained control over 
Greenland. This period marked the consolidation of Danish authority over the island and the 
integration of Greenland into the Danish colonial system. 
In 1953, Greenland's status was elevated from a colony to an integral part of the Kingdom of 
Denmark. This change granted Greenlanders Danish citizenship and representation in the Danish 
Parliament, but it also subjected Greenland to Danish laws and policies.  
Home Rule and Autonomy (1979-2009) 
In 1979, Greenland was granted Home Rule, transferring competencies and responsibilities from 
Denmark to Greenland. This allowed Greenland to govern its internal affairs, such as education, 
health, and social welfare, while Denmark retained control over foreign affairs, defence, and 
monetary policy, as seen in the figure below.  
In 1985, Greenland voted to leave the European Economic Community (EEC), a predecessor of the 
European Union (EU). This decision was primarily driven by concerns over the impact of EU 
fishing policies on Greenland's fisheries, a crucial sector of its economy.

Self-Government (2009-Present) 
The implementation of the Self-Government Act in 2009 marked a significant milestone in 
Greenland's journey towards self-determination. This act granted Greenland greater control over its 
natural resources, including minerals and fisheries, and provided it with the legal framework to 
pursue independence if it so chooses.  
Since the implementation of self-government, discussions about full independence have intensified. 
Greenland's vast natural resources, particularly its mineral deposits, have fuelled aspirations for 
economic autonomy and political sovereignty. However, the island remains heavily reliant on 
financial support from Denmark, which complicates its path towards complete independence. 
Denmark currently supports Greenland with an annual block grant of DKK 3.9 billion 
(approximately EUR 520 million), which represents around 20% of Greenland's GDP and over half 
of its public budget [20].

2.2.4 
Planning in the Arctic 
After becoming an official part of Denmark in 1953, economic development in Greenland was led 
by the Danish-established Greenland Technical Organisation (GTO), which prioritised 
infrastructure improvement.  
While efficient, the GTO operated without democratic principles, with planning decisions 
centralised in Copenhagen under Danish authorities. This left the Greenlandic people with minimal 
involvement in shaping their future or influencing building design. This technocratic and centralised 
approach included significant investments in modernisation but paid limited attention to local 
needs, traditions and aspirations [3].  
One example of this centralised decision making was the closure of Qullissat, a major town, in 
1968, further fuelling the Greenlandic people’s demand for political influence. This shift ultimately 
contributed to the transfer of national spatial planning responsibilities from Denmark to Greenland 
in 1981 as part of the Greenland Home Rule, marking a step toward increased autonomy. 
2.2.5 
Interviewing in Greenland 
Inuit philosophy places a strong emphasis on personal opinions and integrity, which are considered 
inviolable. Inuit culture discourages direct confrontation or argumentative behaviour; disagreements 
are not addressed head-on, as is common in the Western world, where discussion and persuasion 
often involve direct challenges. Instead, if a disagreement arises, an external factor is introduced to 
resolve the situation. For example, rather than directly denying a child’s request for a sweet, a

are more indirect and egalitarian. Greenlandic people often speak softly, reflecting their cultural

control while being respectful and adaptive. It is advisable to begin with descriptive questions, such 
as “Could you tell me what your work consists of?” and move to contrast questions to explore 
deeper meanings. This culturally sensitive approach can help create effective and respectful 
communication [3]. 
2.2.6 
Sustainability 
The Inuit believe that today's scientific community tends to overlook the significance of indigenous 
knowledge, which is deeply rooted in indigenous culture and carries its own unique worldview and 
priorities. For instance, when discussing biodiversity, the external perspective often emphasises 
protection and conservation. However, the Inuit see the need to protect and conserve biodiversity in 
a way that allows them to continue using it, as these animals are essential for their sustenance. As a 
coastal hunting people, their culture is intertwined with the environment and ecosystem. The Inuit 
maintain a relationship with these animals, not with the aim of shielding them from everyone, but as 
part of their way of life. This cultural difference can sometimes be difficult to bridge.  
Local resource users view scientific experts as being out of touch with reality and belonging to a 
different, external social group. As a result, when the Greenlandic government relies too heavily on 
these scientists, it is seen as becoming disconnected from its own people. 
The essential political discussions around implementing management strategies for sustainable use 
also require addressing social categories and the related user rights [21]. 
2.3 
Vernacular Architecture 
Vernacular architecture refers to a style of building that is deeply rooted in local traditions, culture, 
and the environmental conditions of a specific region. It relies on the use of indigenous materials 
and construction techniques tailored to meet the needs of the people within a particular area, often 
without the involvement of formally trained architects [22].   
In Greenland, traditional structures were built using locally available materials such as stone, turf, 
and driftwood. These materials are not only abundant in the harsh Arctic landscape but also 
essential for creating durable, insulated homes capable of withstanding extreme weather conditions 
[23]. 
Adaptation to the environment is a key feature of Greenlandic vernacular architecture. The designs 
prioritise minimal exposure to freezing temperatures and maximise insulation, ensuring warmth and 
survivability in one of the planet’s coldest climates. This practical approach is reflected in the 
compact and efficient construction of traditional Greenlandic homes, which are often designed to 
merge with the landscape, offering protection from the elements.

the use of durable materials and robust construction methods. Geographical isolation compounds 
these challenges, as most materials must be imported, making prefabrication a practical solution to 
reduce construction time and dependency on limited specialised local labour.

2.7 
Waste Management 
2.7.1 
Waste Management in Greenland 
Greenland faces unique challenges in waste management due to its sparse population, harsh Arctic 
climate, and limited infrastructure. Annually, approximately 50,000 tons of waste are generated, 
predominantly from residential, commercial, and imported packaging materials. Current practices 
include landfilling, incineration, and limited recycling of metals and hazardous waste, often 
exported abroad [11] 
Key challenges include the lack of advanced sorting systems, limited flue gas cleaning in 
incineration plants, and uncontrolled emissions of gas and leachate from landfills, posing significant 
environmental risks. The vast distances between settlements and extreme weather conditions 
complicate waste collection and recycling logistics. 
For unconnected areas or cabins which aren’t connected to the waste drainage system, toilet waste 
is usually collected in plastic bags from a “honey-bucket” toilet.  Based on the latest waste action 
plan, waste would then need to be transported to central processing facilities, such as incinerators or 
designated landfills, for proper handling, instead of disposing near the cabin [42] 
 
Figure 48 - Honey Bucket Example 
Managing human waste in remote areas without access to traditional drainage systems requires 
innovative solutions. In the following points an overview of various toilet systems suitable for such 
settings is presented. 
2.7.2

Composting toilets decompose human waste into compost through aerobic processes, sometimes 
with the help of additional organic materials such as sawdust or straw. In cold climates, maintaining 
adequate temperatures is crucial for effective composting. Strategies include installing units indoors 
or using heating elements to sustain microbial activity during colder periods. 
For example the Loveable Loo [43] is a simple compost toilet of a 4.7 litre bucket housed within a 
wooden box and topped with a standard toilet seat. After each use, waste is covered with materials 
like sawdust to control odour and prepare for composting. This design emphasises simplicity and 
cost-effectiveness, making it accessible for DIY construction

2.8.1

The traditional methods of constructing buildings with brickwork, monolithic, and prefabricated 
reinforced concrete are inefficient in the Arctic due to challenges such as water freezing in masonry 
and concrete mixtures at low temperatures. Also, the difficulty of transporting heavy prefabricated 
concrete structures over long distances presents a challenge in the Arctic, which is why SIPs are a 
good practical alternative for Arctic constructions [46]. 
Structural Insulated Panels (SIPs) are used for walls, floors, and roofs and offer superior insulation 
and air tightness compared to traditional construction methods. SIPs are lightweight, quick to 
assemble, and enhance building energy-efficiency, making them ideal for cold climates.

the composite panel include wood and thermoplastics reinforced with fibres, which also work well 
under tensile strength under temperatures of -20°C, -40°C and -60°C [34]. Steel could be used due 
to its high mechanical properties and low cost, however the panels would then be heavy and prone 
to cold brittleness. Thermal insulation is achieved through ceramic fibres or rigid foam such as 
polystyrene, while fire resistance is ensured by fireproof composites capable of withstanding 
temperatures up to 1200°C [47]. 
 
Figure 55 - SIPs Being Used for a Residential Building [46] 
To assess the durability of fibre-reinforced thermoplastics, a low-velocity impact performance of 
fibre-reinforced composite sandwich structures was assessed. Carbon fibre reinforced polymer 
(CFRP), glass fibre reinforced polymer (GFRP), and carbon-glass fibre hybrids were assessed under 
room and Arctic-like low-temperature conditions. Testing revealed that GFRP composites exhibit 
the highest damage tolerance and the least energy absorption, while CFRP composites showed 
brittle fractures and reduced impact resistance, especially at -70°C. Hybrid composites harnessed 
the strengths of both fibres but suffered extensive delamination due to material incompatibilities 
[47].

Polyurethane (PPU), particularly in its sprayed form, offers exceptional thermal insulation and

sacrificing performance. It has a higher upfront cost, however its efficiency and reduced need for 
supplementary materials make it cost-competitive in the long term, despite logistical challenges 
posed by reliance on imported equipment and specialised application expertise. 
2.8.3

Expanded polystyrene, used by companies like Japan Dome House as seen before, is a lightweight 
yet robust material renowned for its excellent insulation properties and energy efficiency. Enhanced 
through treatments for strength, flame retardancy, and UV protection, it ensures long-term 
reliability while its chemical composition, consisting only of hydrogen and carbon, makes it safe for 
occupants and is environmentally friendly, releasing no harmful substances throughout its lifecycle, 
including during incineration. Its exceptional airtightness minimises energy loss, significantly 
reducing heating costs and making it a good choice for sustainable construction.

Introduction 
This research aims to develop a design proposal for recreational cabins tailored to the unique 
environmental, cultural, and functional demands of the Arctic, specifically in Greenland, by 
employing a mixed-methods approach to address the challenges and limitations of existing designs.  
By integrating site visits, stakeholder feedback, and a review of technical literature, the research is

shaping cabin use, ensuring the proposed design is practical, user-centred, and informed by real-
world data.

4.3 
Cultural and Functional Requirements 
4.3.1 
Local Hunters, Snowmobile, Dog Sled and ATV Users 
Hunting is an important practice locals carry out throughout the year, usually with snowmobiles or 
dog sleds in winter, and ATVs when the snow has melted.  
Local hunters do not usually use the public cabins when hunting, but do use their personal cabins. 
In spring and summer, some also use a cotton tent in an “owned” tent and hunting area that has been 
passed down from generation to generation. Such areas and traditions are unwritten, but are 
understood and followed by local hunters, which in peak caribou hunting season bring all the 
supplies they need to sustain themselves for up to two months in these tented areas [1]. 
 
Figure 64 - Modern Tent Area Used by a Local Hunter [49] 
Although locals typically do not use the public cabins for overnight stays, they often rely on them as 
rest stops or storage points during their journeys between Sisimiut and Kangerlussuaq. For example, 
at the Canoe Centre cabin, travellers frequently arrive by snowmobile or ATV to store supplies in 
the adjacent outdoor storage space, which are invaluable for dog-sledding trips where carrying 
heavy loads is impractical. The journey itself varies significantly depending on the mode of 
transport, taking approximately 5 hours by snowmobile, 6-8 hours by ATV, and 3 days by dog sled, 
with the outdoor storage space particularly useful for parking dog sleds during these extended trips. 
4.3.2 
Tourism Growth and Hiker Demographics 
The Arctic Circle Trail (ACT) has become a prominent attraction for international tourists, drawing 
an estimated 1,200 hikers annually, predominantly during the summer months. However, local 
participation remains minimal, with domestic hikers accounting for only about 10% of users. This 
disparity is attributed to cultural differences, as recreational hiking is less common among 
Greenlandic people. The growing popularity of the ACT has not been matched by corresponding 
investments in infrastructure and maintenance, leading to significant challenges in managing the 
trail [3]. 
The surge in summer hikers has exacerbated maintenance issues, including poorly managed toilets 
that lead to odour problems and garbage left behind by some tourists unfamiliar with local norms. 
While winter usage of the trail is less frequent, it is steadily increasing, introducing complexities to 
maintaining the trail year-round. Despite its significance as a tourism draw, the ACT receives little 
attention or funding compared to local events like the Arctic Circle Race, which primarily engage 
the domestic population.

5. 
Design Proposal 
5.1 
Introduction to Design Concept 
I believe the future of cabin design in Greenland requires a departure from the colonialist traditions 
of rectangular Danish structures, embracing instead the vernacular ingenuity and cultural heritage of 
Inuit architecture. This vision moves beyond the idea of a simple shelter to create an immersive 
experience that celebrates resilience, sustainability, and harmony with the natural world. The design 
concept draws heavily on the principles of Inuit dwelling types, particularly the igloo and earth-
covered structures, which embody thermal efficiency, adaptability, and a profound respect for the 
environment. 
This cabin concept reimagines the relationship between architecture and its surroundings. Its form, 
defined by smooth, aerodynamic curves, is designed to withstand the harsh Arctic winds and shed 
snow naturally, echoing the practical brilliance of Inuit design. The cabin’s footprint remains 
minimal, respecting the natural landscape and avoiding the scars of overdevelopment.  
This innovative design concept culminates in a structure known simply as The Dome. More than a 
cabin, The Dome is a statement – a reimagining of Greenlandic architecture that rejects colonial 
influences and reclaims cultural and environmental harmony. It serves as both a refuge and a 
landmark, a place where timeless wisdom meets modern innovation to create a living testament to 
Greenland’s heritage and future. 
 
Figure 76 - The Dome Concept 
5.1.1 
Why a dome?  
The dome shape offers one of the best natural structures for resisting high winds, which is crucial 
for a location like Greenland where the weather can be extremely harsh. A flat surface would create 
resistance, leading to structural strain, but the curved nature of the dome minimises this by allowing 
the wind to flow smoothly around the structure, reducing pressure points.  
==PAGE:60==
 
  DTU 
Prefabricated Cabin for Greenland 
  December 2024        
MSc Thesis 
Page 54
 
 
Figure 77 - The Dome Sketch - Wind 
Another key advantage of the dome shape is its ability to shed snow efficiently. In Arctic regions, 
snow accumulation is a major issue, as it can add significant weight to a building’s roof and create 
potential structural damage. With a dome, snow naturally slides off rather than accumulating, 
reducing the need for constant maintenance and reinforcement. With a circular layout, this also 
creates scour zones around The Dome which prevents snow to accumulate. 
 
Figure 78 - The Dome Sketch - Snow 
Furthermore, dome structures are inherently thermally efficient, enclosing more volume with less 
surface area. This geometry reduces heat loss and makes it easier to maintain warmth inside the 
cabin with minimal energy consumption, which is particularly important in cold, remote 
environments where resources are limited. The air in contact with The Dome cools and falls to floor 
level, which is then heated by either the occupants or a fuel heater, depending on the requirements 
of the Cabin.

Flexibility and Distinct Neighbourhoods 
In the new cabin design, experimentation and creativity is encouraged as an open process for the

Massing 
Dome buildings are not a new thing. In fact, for thousands of years the Inuit have used such shapes 
to build their dwellings for the reasons described in previous points. In The Dome concept we will 
be combining the efficient shape of a dome with equally geometric panels to reduce the absolute 
amount of types of pieces that are needed.

shapes composed of flat polygonal faces. Among these, Platonic solids stand out as a unique 
category. Platonic solids are a special class of three-dimensional geometric shapes where all faces 
are identical regular polygons, the same number of faces meet at each vertex, and all are highly 
symmetrical.

5.4 
Architectural and Spatial Design 
The architectural and spatial design of the cabin has been carefully developed to harmonise 
functionality, adaptability, and resilience in the harsh Greenlandic environment. Inspired by the 
efficiency and simplicity of a dome structure, the cabin’s layout prioritises modularity and resource 
optimisation while addressing the unique needs of Greenlandic lifestyles for all types of users 
including hikers, ATV, snowmobile and dog-sled users. This section delves into the cabin’s 
architectural layout, exploring how its spatial organisation, room functionality, and design strategies 
can fulfil the requirements of life in Greenland. 
5.4.1 
Entrance Passage 
The entrance area of the dome serves as a transitional space, connecting the exterior to the interior 
through a reduced-height passage reminiscent of design principles seen in modern igloos and 
traditional vernacular architecture. The reasons for having a reduced-height entrance are twofold: 
firstly, it simplifies construction by aligning with the triangular panel geometry, which becomes 
harder to manage as the opening height increases, and secondly, it reduces the warm air pooling at 
high level inside the dome to escape when the door is opened.

5.5.1 
Overview of the Building Envelope Design 
The building envelope of the geodesic dome is engineered to deliver superior insulation, 
weatherproofing, and durability, ensuring it withstands Greenland's extreme Arctic environment. 
The envelope will consist of triangular Structural Insulated Panels (SIPs) that integrate insulation, 
structural strength, and ease of assembly. These panels are designed to interconnect seamlessly, 
forming an airtight and highly thermally efficient shell.

edges, as the panels meet at specific angles. The bevelled edges ensure a precise, gap-free fit 
between adjacent panels, accommodating the dome’s curved shape. The required bevel angle is 
determined by the dihedral angle between two adjoining panels, illustrated below as the angle

5.5.2 
Weatherproofing the SIPs 
The geodesic dome’s Structural Insulated Panels (SIPs) would be weatherproofed through a 
combination of durable cladding, protective membranes, and effective sealing techniques tailored to 
Greenland’s Arctic climate. External cladding made from GFRP or asphalt shingles would provide 
the first line of defence against moisture, wind, UV radiation, and physical damage. GFRP, being 
lightweight, non-corrosive, and UV-stable, is especially suitable for long-term exposure in extreme 
conditions. These materials are securely attached to the SIPs with concealed fasteners to prevent 
water infiltration and minimise thermal bridging. 
Figure 114 - Asphalt Roof Shingles Examples [54] [55] 
To enhance protection, a waterproof membrane is applied directly to the SIPs. Rubberised asphalt 
or butyl-based self-adhering membranes would form a seamless barrier against water and air 
infiltration, while breathable membranes allow vapor to escape, preventing condensation within the 
panels. Special attention should be given to edges and joints to ensure comprehensive coverage and 
moisture protection. 
Sealing the panel joints and edges is critical to achieving a weatherproof envelope. High-
performance polyurethane or silicone sealants are to be applied to all seams, providing flexibility 
and durability in sub-zero conditions. For additional protection, EPDM or butyl tape are to be 
installed at the joints, ensuring they remain airtight and watertight under wind and snow loads.  
The geodesic design of the dome itself contributes to its weatherproofing as the triangular geometry 
encourages natural runoff of snow and ice, while the smooth cladding surface prevents adhesion, 
reducing the risk of moisture infiltration or ice dams. Airtightness is further maintained by sealing 
SIP seams with adhesive-backed vapor barrier tapes, and a blower door test during construction 
should be carried out to ensure no air leaks compromise the building envelope. 
Finally, UV-resistant and hydrophobic coatings applied to the cladding protect it from sunlight 
degradation and ice adhesion, preserving its weatherproofing capabilities over time while reducing 
maintenance needs.

5.5.4 
Structural Stability 
The geodesic dome’s shape is inherently stable, efficiently distributing loads across its surface and 
minimising stress concentrations. This design is ideal for: 
Snow Load: The triangular geometry allows snow to slide off naturally, preventing excessive 
accumulation. The SIPs are engineered to withstand the weight of heavy snow through their high 
compressive strength. 
Wind Resistance: The aerodynamic shape of the dome minimises wind pressure, reducing the risk 
of structural damage in high winds. 
Foundation Adaptability: For uneven or frozen terrain, the dome is supported on adjustable raised 
platform system. These foundations are minimally invasive, ensuring stability without disrupting 
the permafrost layer. 
5.5.5 
Substructure 
The substructure for the geodesic dome cabin is designed to elevate the timber floor approximately 
one meter above ground level, ensuring that the structure remains clear of melted snow and 
allowing wind to pass beneath it to prevent snow accumulation. This elevation minimises direct 
contact with the cold ground, providing natural thermal insulation and preventing issues such as 
moisture-related decay, structural damage or water pooling around the cabin. Additionally, the 
raised design protects against permafrost melting by reducing heat transfer into the ground, 
preserving stability in Arctic conditions.

Spring offers the optimal window for material transport, as snowmobiles can operate effectively on 
the melting snow without sinking, allowing components to reach the site in time for summer 
construction. If needed, materials can be stored on-site until the build begins.

5.7.3 
Modularity and Flexibility 
Each dome unit is designed to function as an independent section of the whole, accommodating 
specific functions such as sleeping areas, kitchens, or storage spaces. Thanks to this modularity, the 
design allows for easy customisation, enabling additional domes to be added or removed without 
compromising the overall structure. Also, this approach supports phased construction where 
sections of the cabin can be built independently when resources or time are limited, making it very 
practical for remote or challenging locations. 
Given there are only four types of panels using the same connectors, this will streamline assembly, 
ensuring seamless integration between domes even in challenging Arctic conditions. This design 
also allows for disassembly, allowing individual panels to be replaced or upgraded without 
disrupting the entire structure.  
This feature is particularly beneficial for long-term use in remote locations, where repairs and 
modifications must be simple and efficient. By embracing modularity, the cabin achieves a high 
degree of flexibility, ensuring it can be tailored to meet the evolving demands of its users and 
environment. 
The modular approach will create a series of benefits that will continue to be fine-tuned throughout 
the detailed design process. The base materials of the building and the modules should be fine-tuned 
to: 
• Minimise material use and associated CO2 emissions 
• Maximise performance for both the facade and structure  
• Increase potential for disassembly and reuse 
• Maximise quality assurance and precise material tracking 
• Reduce material waste and lower on-site cost 
• Maximise performance in terms of air leakage and insulation 
To maximise the degree of modularity, the detailed design should: 
• Minimise the number of different components and joints 
• Use as few types of materials as possible 
• Build inseparable parts within the same material 
• Use mechanical joints and provide easy access to joints 
• Separate different building parts with varied service life

6. 
Conclusion 
Recreational cabins are integral to the Greenlandic lifestyle, supporting the active, nature-connected 
traditions of local communities. While the cabins currently in use often fulfil their purpose, some 
are found in disrepair, highlighting opportunities for improvement in design and functionality. One 
of the most pressing issues is toilet waste management, where the failure to properly maintain waste 
systems frequently results in unsanitary and unbearable conditions. Addressing such challenges is 
essential to enhance the usability and sustainability of cabins in Greenland’s remote and extreme 
environment. 
This thesis proposes a shift in approach toward cabin design, introducing a dome-shaped structure 
that addresses the unique demands of Arctic life: The Dome. While rectangular cabins are 
functional, they lack the efficiency and adaptability that a dome design can provide. A dome-shaped 
cabin is quicker to construct, easier to transport in prefabricated panels, and more straightforward to 
repair. Its inherent structural sturdiness allows it to better resist the Arctic's strong winds and heavy 
snow loads. The Dome also offers a fresh and innovative aesthetic that is likely to appeal to both 
local and international users, standing out from the uniformity of existing rectangular cabins. 
More importantly, The Dome design draws inspiration from the vernacular architecture of the Inuit, 
who have relied on similar forms for thousands of years. These traditional structures embody 
principles of resilience, efficiency, and harmony with the environment, offering lessons that modern 
cabin designs must respect and integrate. The widespread adoption of rectangular, colonial-style 
cabins over the past century has often ignored the wisdom of Inuit architectural traditions, 
replicating forms that are neither culturally attuned nor environmentally optimised. This thesis 
advocates for reclaiming Inuit-inspired architecture, not merely as a nod to the past but as a 
pathway to creating designs that are truly suited to Arctic conditions and lifestyles. 
6.1 
Limitations and Recommendations 
This thesis presents a conceptual design for a dome-shaped recreational cabin tailored to 
Greenland’s Arctic environment, but it is important to acknowledge its limitations. While the 
proposed design serves as a foundation, emphasising prefabrication, modularity, transportability, 
and cultural relevance, moving from concept to construction will require a detailed design that 
addresses technical and regulatory requirements. 
A complete design must ensure compliance with structural, fire, life-safety, and thermal standards, 
while also detailing materials, finishes, framing, bracing, and methods for assembly. Particular 
attention should be given to the connection points between panels to ensure they are secure and 
resilient against Arctic conditions. Additionally, it will be necessary to determine whether a 
secondary structure is required to support the panels during erection, which would influence both 
the construction process and overall design. Comprehensive documentation, including 
specifications, drawings, technical reports and equipment schedules will also be essential. 
Furthermore, installation, maintenance, and replacement strategies must be developed to guarantee 
the cabin’s durability and functionality over time. 
This thesis offers a vision for more efficient, sustainable, and culturally aligned Arctic cabins. Its 
success will depend on advancing the concept into a detailed and actionable design that thoroughly 
addresses these practical considerations and meets the unique challenges of Greenland’s 
environment.
