Abstract
The growing concern of the environment has led to an increased share of renewable energy sources in the energy systems.
Wind turbine power generation is today competing with the traditional energy supplies due to several advantages such as
availability, inexhaustibility and sustainability. As a result, the interest of identifying suitable locations for the installment
of wind turbines has increased. Although the technical potentials can be identiﬁed, challenges such as social acceptance
has caused multiple projects being rejected. The present study aims to examine the socio-technical potential of onshore
wind in Europe and the associated costs. Socio-technical potential is deﬁned as the potential using scenicness data as a
measure of public acceptance through visual impact, in this study. The potential is calculated by identifying the available
area with a geographical information system, landuse information and exclusion of aesthetic areas. Based on four diﬀerent
scenarios, technical, low scenicness exclusion, medium scenicness exclusion and high scenicness exlusion, the impact of
removing scenic landscape areas has been investigated. The calculated technical potential is 67 PWh. By excluding scenic
areas the potential is reduced by 15%, 53% and 66% for the low scenicness exclusion, medium scenicness exclusion and
high scenicness exclusion scenarios, respectively. The generation cost ranges from 0.025-0.125 e/kWh.
1
Introduction
In accordance with the Paris Agreement [1] having the
goal of reducing the greenhouse gas emission and keep
the global average temperature to well below 2 ℃,
decarbonisation of the energy supply is important to
achieve the target. The worlds primary energy supply
is still today highly depended on fossil fuels. In 2018,
the amount of the worlds primary energy supply was
of 79% fossil fuel, namely 16% Coal, 35% oil and 28%
natural gas [2]. The utilisation of renewable energy in
the electricity sector is growing, especially from solar
and wind sources. Their investment cost reduction and
zero fuel cost increases the attractiveness [3]. For 2050,
the European Council has set the ambitious target of
becoming climate-neutral and according to one of their
calculated decarbonisation scenarios for 2050 made by
the European Commission, wind energy can account
for more than 50% of the total power production in
EU [4, 5].
Figure 1 illustrates the total installed wind power
capacity in Europe over the past ten years. This rep-
resents a signiﬁcant increase of 10.3% in average per
year. As illustrated, the installed capacity in Europe
in 2009 was 77 GW. In 2019, the capacity had increased
to 205 GW, accounting for 15% of EU’s total electric-
ity demand.
Although the interest in oﬀshore wind
has increased, three quarters of the new installed ca-
pacity in 2019 in Europe were onshore, mainly due to
high development costs for oﬀshore wind [6]. Although
the increased share of renewable energy in the energy
systems is important for decarbonisation, it includes
stability problems for the electrical grid. Indeed, stor-
age systems and interconnection between countries will
play an important part in the future.
Based on the high growth and wind energy being a sus-
tainable energy source, it can be assumed that onshore
wind energy will play a signiﬁcant role in achieving the
goals for 2050.
However, deployment of these large-scale infrastruc-
tures on land are often met by opposition, mainly from
the local communities where the construction is placed.

In Germany, the country with the largest amount of

A study by Pedersen et al.

Although noise is a common concern,

impact of visual changes to a place or a landscape can
have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the attitudes towards
wind farms and is one of the main problems for wind
turbine siting [7, 11, 13].
The concept NIMBY (not in my backyard) has been a
highly discussed term over the recent years. NIMBY
is often used in situations where a local community re-
spond negatively to measures that are considered pos-
itive at a national level, but which nevertheless reduce
the quality of the local environment. The construction
of e.g. wind power may be considered necessary to re-
duce greenhouse gas emissions, but few want such fa-
cilities in their vicinity [13]. Indeed, considering these
social barriers and key stakeholders at planning could
lead to a more accepted system development. How-
ever, a consequence of a high amount of NIMBY atti-
tudes could lead to the environmental goals not being
reached.
In light of the aforementioned goals of wind energy
in Europe and social barriers, this study aims to
calculate the socio-technical potential in Europe 1. In
order to achieve this, a deﬁnition of the term socio-
technical potential is stated, followed by a deﬁnition
of exclusion areas. Lastly, to consider social barriers,
scenicness data from Great Britain is investigated
in order to identify how aesthetic a landscape is.
The webpage called Scenic-Or-Not [14] is used where
rating of diﬀerent geotagged photographs around
England, Wales and Scotland is gathered. The paper
is structured as follows:
in Section 2, a literature
review is given followed by a description of data and
methodology in Section 3. In Section 4 the results are
presented and later discussed in Section 5. Lastly, in
section Section 6, the conclusion of the study is stated.

social acceptance of renewable energy by three main
aspects being socio-political, community and market
acceptance. The socio-political acceptance is usually

scenic areas.
Overall, it can be noted that the visual concerns are
the main factor in rejection of wind turbine projects. It
aﬀects both the community acceptance and the social
acceptance as the landscape is aﬀected by the installa-
tions and that the regular view eﬀects the rejection by
the population [7].

Socio-technical potential
The term ”potential” in wind energy is usually deﬁned
in four categories, namely, theoretical potential, geo-
graphical potential, technical potential and economic
potential [24].

Geographical potential is the ﬁrst reduction of the

considered. Hence, the geographical potential is the
available areas for wind power generation (km2).
Technical potential is the amount of energy that can
be generated by the installed wind turbines in the
suitable areas under technical constraints (kWh/year).
Economic potential is the potential that can be realised
economically depending on which potential that is
investigated. It also depends on the perspective taken
i.e from a private investor’s or society’s perspective
and can be aﬀected if policy support of renewables are
considered (kWh/year) [24, 25].
The term socio-technical potential is at the time
of writing not well deﬁned, although the interest
of the social barriers and acceptance for wind tur-
bines has increased.
As mentioned in Section 2.1,
the visual concerns are a main factor for social
acceptance.
Hence in this study,
Socio-technical
potential is the potential using scenicness data as a
measure of public acceptance through visual impact.
Diﬀerent areas are excluded depending on the how
scenic they are and the threshold selected (kWh/year).

3.1
Geographical potential
To identify suitable areas for onshore wind, Corine
Land Cover (CLC-2018) of the EEA is used as the
basis. The raster data divides the European area in
44 classes with a resolution of 100m x 100m. It has a
complete coverage although it suﬀers from limitations
on the accuracy such as single buildings and small vil-
lages [30]. The unsuitable areas are excluded using Ge-
ographic information system software (ArcMap 10.7).
In Table 7 the diﬀerent functions used in the software
throughout the study are described. There are no uni-
form regulation and the planning acceptance and leg-
islative restrictions are highly region speciﬁc. Hence,
the distance for exclusion zones is assumed based on
previous studies [25, 26, 31] and are stated in Ap-
pendix E. To account for the already existing turbines,
World database (2015) from The Wind Power is used
[32]. The dataset contains the position, the number
of turbines, hub height and the total nominal power.
To account for the area of the turbines or wind farms,
an approximation of the rotor diameter is calculated.
From a database of turbines provided by Mckenna et
al. [26], a multi linear regression based on the nominal
power, hub height and rotor diameter is determined to
ﬁnd the rotor diameter for the existing turbines. Fol-
lowing, an assumption is made for the required space of
these turbines being eight rotor diameters in the main
wind direction and ﬁve rotor diameters in secondary
wind direction.
Creating a rhombus area and limit-
ing the wake eﬀect [31]. Nationally designated areas
such as national parks and nature reserves provided
by the CDDA (common database on designated ar-
eas) are included [33]. Some parts of Finland, Ireland
and Estonia and all of Turkey is not included in this
dataset and is therefore not considered in this study.
To account for protected areas of valuable species and
habitats, NATURA 2000 are also included [34]. Ad-
ditionally, this layer do not include Iceland, Norway,
Turkey, Switzerland, Serbia, Bosnia and Herzegovina,
Montenegro, Kosovo, Albania and Macedonia. These
are not considered. Both layers are extracted with a
buﬀer distance of 200 m. Due to technical and ﬂuid-
mechanical reasons, areas with a slope greater than 20◦
is removed [31]. After removing the unsuitable areas,
the resulting geographical potential contains of poly-
gons with landuse categories.
3.2
Technical potential
In the second step, the available areas and wind speed
data are overlayed. The wind speed is extracted from
the Global Wind Atlas (GWA) containing microscale
wind climate data with a resolution of 250m x 250m
[35]. The data is downscaled from large-scale wind cli-
mate data provided by atmospheric re-analysis data
(ERA5 dataset from the European Centre for Medium
Range Weather Forecast (ECMWF) from 2008-2017),
containing means of hourly wind speed.
The wind
speed extracted is at 100 m above sea level. If mul-
tiple values of wind speed are within the same polygon
of available land, the average wind speed is calculated.
The resulting areas are then classiﬁed based on the
landuse category and wind speed. In this study 11 dif-
ferent turbines with the range from 2.3-3.0 MW are
used. The data is collected from Mckenna et al. [31]
where the total turbine investment is based on the re-
quired material inputs and their associated costs. The
power curve for each turbine are extracted from the
web page The Wind Power [32]. The data for these
turbines can be seen in Appendix A where the turbine
density is based on the same spacing as described in
Section 3.1. The resulting turbine density is between
4.38-9.26 MW/km2. In order to select the most suit-
able turbine for each polygon, two diﬀerent optimisa-
tion methods have been investigated; the lowest lev-
elised cost (LCOE) and the highest production. Us-
ing the optimisation of LCOE makes the potentials
economically assessed. For each combination of wind
speed from 4.25 m/s to 14 m/s, in 0.25 m/s intervals,
and landuse category, the most suitable turbine is se-
lected based on LCOE or production. These calcula-
tions are done using MATLAB. Firstly, the wind speed
at 100 m above ground is corrected to hub height and
adjusted for the roughness length corresponding to the
landuse category, for every turbine using Equation (1)

3.3
Socio-technical potential
After the technical potential is calculated, the socio-
technical potential is determined by how scenic the dif-
ferent areas are. This is obtained by a dataset created
in Great Britain called Scenic-Or-Not [14]. Scenic-Or-
Not gathers geotagged photographs of Great Britain
The dataset contains 212,212 images (January, 2020)
with a total number of 1,536,054 votes and covers 95%
of Great Britain. Each image represents 1 km2. In this
analysis, only photos that are rated at least 3 times are
used. The rating goes from 1 to 10 where 1 indicates
”not scenic” and 10 indicates ”very scenic”. The num-
ber of votes, the fraction of votes and the cumulative
values with ratings between 1 and 10 is given in Ta-
ble 2. For the diﬀerent rates it can be noted that a
low fraction of the votes are 7 or higher. The scenic-
ness data are joined with landuse categories for Great
Britain in order to ﬁnd a correlation between votes
and landuse. This gives every image a category based
on their location.
As a result, the number of cate-
gories of landuse is reduced to 35. The categories not
included are: 212 (Permanently irrigated land), 213
(Rice ﬁeld), 221 (Vineyard), 223 (Olive groves), 241
(Annual crops associated with permanent crops), 244
(Agro-foresty areas), 323 (Sclerophylous vegetation),
335 (Glaciers and perpetual snow) and 422 (Salines).
Hence, these categories can not be investigated of how
scenic they are and are not excluded when calculating
the socio-technical potential.
Based on the landuse
category for each photo with the given average vote,
the mean, mode and median of each category is cal-
culated. Figure 3 illustrates the mean scenicness vote
for each landuse category, these results are used fur-
ther in the investigation. It can be noted that Urban
Fabric is given a low scenicness rate. This conﬁrms the
study by Seresine et al. [18] that areas with manmade
structures are rated as less scenic. In contrast, Water
Bodies has a high scenic rate. Bare Rocks is rated as
the most scenic landuse category and Airport as the
lowest.
In this study, four diﬀerent scenarios are investigated
in order to analyse the eﬀect on the potential of how
scenic an area is. The diﬀerent scenarios are based on
the percentile or quantile of the mean scenicness rat-
ings which is stated in Table 3. The potential for each
scenario is determined by removing categories with a
mean rate higher than or equal to the given scenicness
value.

Results
In Appendix F the results of the potentials for each
country in Europe are presented including the available
area as a fraction of the total land area, the potential
generation density (GWh/km2) and the average cost
potential. The optimisation method for the displayed
results is the turbine selection with the lowest levelised
cost. The main result is a socio-technical potential of
between 22-57 PWh with the associated costs rang-

of between 24-62% of the total area. The calculated
technical potential is 67 PWh with the available area
of 69% of the total area. A great variation of the ab-
solute generation potential between the countries can
be noted. For the low scenicness exclusion scenario,
the geographical potential is reduced by 7% and has a
small eﬀect on the calculated socio-technical potential
in total and for each country. The medium scenicness
exclusion scenario however, reduces the available area
by 52%. This is also reﬂected in the socio-technical
potential causing a high reduction. The last scenario
(high scenicness exclusion) is almost one third of the
technical potential and results in a socio-technical po-
tential of 22 PWh. The relative results such as avail-
able area and average potential per area are quite
consistent where countries with extremely high avail-
able land area results in a high generation per area.

and 8% for the medium scenic-

6
Conclusion
In this study, the socio-technical potential for Europe
has been determined (i.e. EU28, Norway, Switzerland
and Turkey). The socio-technical potential is deﬁned
as the potential using scenicness data as a measure of
public acceptance through visual impact. The method-
ology is based on allocation of the best suited wind tur-
bines based on the wind speed and the roughness of the
surface, by minimising the generation costs. Scenic-or-
Not data has been investigated and used as an approx-
imation for public acceptance of wind turbines. The
main result is that a socio-technical potential for Eu-
rope is ranging from approximately 22-57 PWh with
the available area between 1-3.2 million km2. The as-
sociated costs is 0.0249-0.1235 e/kWh. The largest po-
tential with the associated costs can be found in France
with the socio-technical potential of 3500 TWh. For
the turbine selection, four diﬀerent turbines were se-
lected based on the lowest generation method and only
two turbines for the highest generation method. Look-
ing at the diﬀerent optimisation methods, choosing the
turbine with the highest production resulted in higher
generation and costs.
In comparison with other studies, the determined
technical potential of 67 PWh for Europe is quite high
and is reﬂected by the large body of available area.
However, comparing the socio-technical potential cal-
culated by Enevoldsen et al. [27] of 138 PWh is ex-
tremely higher.
A sensitivity analysis showed that changes in the
mean scenicness rate for landuse categories can vary
the potential greatly, depending on the thresholds in-
vestigated. With the scenario of high scenicness ex-
clusion no variation was obtained. However, for the
medium scenicness exclusion scenario, the reduction in
potential was of 13%. In addition, the sensitivity anal-
ysis of UK showed the diﬀerence in using the average of
Scenic-or-Not votes compared to the calculated mean
scenicness votes. For two of the investigated scenarios,
the potentials had a small variation. Furthermore, the
calculated mean scenicness rate includes a higher po-
tential compared to Scenic-or-Not for the investigated
areas.
The main uncertainties for the study is the applied
methodology using scenicness rates as a measure of
public acceptance.
Other weaknesses is the method
of overlaying the wind speed, and missing of protected
