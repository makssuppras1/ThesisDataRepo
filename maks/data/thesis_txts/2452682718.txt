Abstract
Proper waste management (and proper sanitation) are as essential to the economy and society
as a whole as the provision of potable water, shelter, food, energy, transport and communica-
tions are [Wilson (2015)]. As waste composition data are used in different context regarding
waste management planning and environmental assessments, it is important to expand the
current knowledge within physico-chemical waste compositions in order to better handle
various types of parameters and elements depending on material waste fractions [Götze et al.
(2016)].
As waste composition studies are time consuming and usually difﬁcult to compare with
each other, this study chose to further the research conducted by [Götze et al. (2016)] via a
meta-analysis of waste composition data, by updating a waste database (signiﬁcant amount
of waste composition data collected by DTU Environment), conducting a database summary
of overall waste data sets, applying descriptive statistics and creating Pearson correlation
matrices to derive trends and correlations among different parameters.
This waste composition study combined and consulted literature data from different sources
in order to update the current waste database. This study found 19 publications meeting
criteria, which resulted in 1.151 data entries whereof most originated from Asia. Of the
1.151 data entries, 63% were dry weight, 17% were wet weight and 20% were unknown (?).
Database summary revealed that overall, 116 publications, meeting the criteria for literature
selection, has been reviewed by the combined studies, which has resulted in 13.248 data
entries whereof most originated from Europe. Of the 13.248 data entries, 79% were dry
weight, 2% were wet weight and 19% were unknown (?). This study conducted descriptive
statistical analysis of the reported physico-chemical waste composition data in order to de-
rive data distribution and value ranges for physical parameters and element concentrations.
Based on 13.248 individual data entries, median values and percentiles for 28 parameters in
11 individual waste fractions are presented and discussed. Finally, this study successfully
applied Pearson’s correlation matrix to evaluate linear correlation between two variables
(parameters / elements) and thus detect potential indicator elements / parameters; explore
the origin of the elements / parameters; identify the waste fractions of elements / parameters
with similar properties and present uncorrelated elements / parameters.
Detailed data sets are attached to this study in the hope that further analysis and new
application of the data are investigated.
Keywords: Waste management; Municipal solid waste (MSW); Descriptive statistics; Physico-
chemical compositions; Meta-Analysis; Heavy metals; Energy content; Heating value; Nutrients and
Salts, Waste Material Fraction; Pearson Correlation Matrix; Organic waste; In-organic waste

Dansk Resumé
Ordentlig affaldshåndtering (og ordentlig sanitetsforhold) er som helhed lige så vigtige for
økonomien og samfundet som adgang til rent drikkevand, husly, mad, energi, transport og
kommunikation er [Wilson (2015)]. Da data om affaldssammensætning anvendes i forskellige
sammenhænge med hensyn til affaldshåndteringsplanlægning og miljøvurderinger, er det
vigtigt at ekspandere nuværende viden inden for fysisk-kemiske affaldssammensætninger
for bedre at kunne håndtere forskellige typer af parametre og elementer afhængig af materi-
aleaffaldsfraktioner [Götze et al. (2016)].
Da affaldssammensætningsstudier er tidskrævende og normalt er vanskelige at sammen-
ligne med hinanden, valgte dette studie at undersøge og videreføre forskningen udført af
[Götze et al. (2016)], ved at foretage en meta-analyse af affaldssammensætningsdata ved at
opdatere en affaldsdatabase (betydelige mængder af affaldssammensætningsdata indsamet
af DTU Environment), udførelse af en databaseoversigt af de samlede affaldsdatasæt, an-
vende beskrivende statistikker og lave Pearson-korrelationsmatricer til at udlede tendenser
og korrelationer mellem forskellige parametre.
Dette affaldssammensætningsstudie kombinerede og konsulterede literaturdata fra forskel-
lige kilder og baggrunde for at opdatere den nuværende affaldsdatabase. Dette studie fandt
19 udgivelser som mødte de opsatte kriterier, hvilket resulterede i 1.151 dataindtastninger,
hvoraf de ﬂeste stammede fra Asien. Af de 1.151 dataindtastninger var 63% tør vægt, 17%
våd vægt og 20% var ubekendt (?). Database opsummeringen viser at der nu i alt indgår
116 udgivelser, som møder kriterierne for literaturudvælgelsen, der er blevet anmeldt af
de kombinerede studier. Dette har resulteret i 13.248 dataindtastninger, hvoraf de ﬂeste
stammer fra Europa. Af de 13.248 dataindtastninger var 79% tør vægt, 2% våd vægt og 19%
er ubekendt (?). Dette studie udførte beskrivende statistiske analyser af de rapporterede
data om fysisk-kemisk affaldssammensætning for at udlede datadistribution og værdier for
fysiske parametre og elementkoncentrationer. Medianværdier og percentiler for 28 parametre
i 11 Individuelle affaldsfraktioner, baseret på 13.248 individuelle dataindtastninger, blev
præsenteret og diskuteret. Slutteligt, så lykkedes det dette studie at benytte Pearsons ko-
rrelationsmatrice for at evaluere lineære korrelationer mellem two variable (parametre /
elementer) og herved opdage potentielle indikator elementer / parametre; udforske ophav af
elementer / parametre; identiﬁcere affaldsfraktioner af elementer / parametre med lignende
egenskaber og præsentere ikke-korrelerede elementer / parametre.
Detaljeret datasæts er vedhæftet dette studie med håb om at yderligere analyse og brug-
barhed af disse data bliver undersøgt i fremtiden.
Nøgleord: Waste management; Municipal solid waste (MSW); Descriptive statistics; Physico-
chemical compositions; Meta-Analysis; Heavy metals; Energy content; Heating value; Nutrients and
Salts, Waste Material Fraction; Pearson Correlation Matrix; Organic waste; In-organic waste

Introduction
Waste management, or rather solid waste management, is not a new topic but is as old as
the human civilization itself, however it has only been viewed as an engineering discipline
for roughly one century [Christensen (2011)]. Where classical waste management dealt with
public cleansing of cities, modern waste management, spurred on by industrialization and
urbanization, deals with newly introduced materials and chemicals that severely affects
the types and composition of waste, making proper handling of waste more complex and
expensive [Christensen (2011)]. The increasing global population highlighted by Figure 1.1
adds further interest and complexity to modern waste management
Figure 1.1: Projected world population until 2100, source: the ﬁgure is adapted from [United
Nations (2019)].
If we deﬁne waste as a "left-over, a redundant product or material of no or marginal value for the
owner and which the owner wants to discard" [Christensen (2011)], an increase in population
yields more owners of left-overs, redundant products or materials with little to no value,
effectively creating more waste locally and globally. Regardless of the fact that the global
population growth rate is declining, we, as humans, are still going to increase in population
size for quite some time, meaning that we are creating more and more waste, rendering
modern waste management one of the most important and urgent practices to focus on. This
statement is further solidiﬁed by ensuring proper sanitation and solid waste management is
just as important to the economy and society as a whole as the provision of potable water,
shelter, food, energy, transport and communication are [Wilson (2015)], who states that en-
suring proper sanitation and solid waste management is just as important to the economy
and society as a whole as the provision of potable water, shelter, food, energy, transport and
communication are.
The recent ﬁndings by [Götze et al. (2016)] suggests that in order to properly manage waste
one must understand the physical and chemical compositions of waste, trends and corre-
lations as state-of-the-art environmental assessment of waste management systems rely on
data of the physico-chemical composition of individual material fractions making up the
speciﬁc waste. [Götze et al. (2016)].

2.2.1
Waste Management
The engineering discipline of (solid) waste management is relatively new (roughly one century
old), but the term and principles of sound waste disposal is as old as the human civilization
[Christensen (2011)]. Studies have shown that the soil, in and outside the cities, conceals old
waste that can be traced back to the Stone Age, of which the old waste contains middens
and remnants of tools; In the middens, remains of bones and shellﬁsh, such as mussels and
oysters, were found. Looking even closer, the remnants of identiﬁed tools indicates that
hunting and ﬁshing were sources to obtain food. Often the middens were discarded in a pile
near coastal areas or at the settlements. Furthermore, the humans were more likely living as
nomads, due to the dependency of the hunting and ﬁshing, and hereby forced to move from
area to area to search for food.[Barles (2014)]
Another example revolves around the industrial revolution, which took place in several
places after 1770. During this period the agriculture was made more efﬁcient by mechanical
means (which has now been replaced by newer and better technology). However, at the start
of the 19th century, many people still lived in the countryside as farmers. The produced
waste from these farms contained farm animal feces as well as human feces, which were
discarded to the middens and subsequently used as fertilizer on the cultivated soil. This
stands as a stark contrast to the habits in urban areas (cities) during the same period in time:
Here, people often lived in small apartments (several lived under the same roof). The waste
generated from these apartments were human feces and rotten food such as vegetables, fruit,
ﬁsh remains etc., where the food were either discarded onto the street or on a landﬁll often
located on the outskirts of the city. The human feces were handled by digging holes in the
backyards (the holes were also used as latrines). When the holes were ﬁlled up, the feces
were either covered up with soil or removed with a bucket and sold to the peasants with the
purpose of reusing the feces as fertilizer.
23
==PAGE:27==
During the 19th century, the railways were created, and more people moved from the
countryside to the city (urbanization). The people found work in factories, which notoriously
contributed to more waste on both the streets and in the canals of the city, causing contamina-
tion of bacteria in the drinking water. At the same time, the untreated waste at the landﬁlls
took up more and more space due to the population growth in the cities. The contaminated
water and untreated waste at the landﬁlls caused public health issues, which resulted in the
spread of intestinal infection Cholera, due to the pathogens transported and delivered by
rats, ﬂies and seagulls that either drank the water, ate the waste or used it as a nest. The
infection caused a high death rate. Furthermore, the accumulation of waste at the landﬁlls
also caused unpleasant odours, that was spread by the wind. Lucklily for the cities and their
inhabitants, the health conditions for humans changed signiﬁcantly in the late 1800s with the
introduction and implementation of sewers [Barles (2014)].
These examples, besides attesting to the longevity of waste management, highlights both the
potential beneﬁts and potential harm proper waste management (or lack thereof) can lead to.
The literature reveals several deﬁnitions of waste. This study abides by the waste deﬁni-
tion provided by [Christensen (2011)]: "waste is a left-over, a redundant product or material of no
or marginal value for the owner and which the owner wants to discard.", effectively meaning that
when a product / material / object / item becomes worthless for the owner, the item will
more likely be thrown out and become waste. However, waste is relative and varies from
owner to owner. For instance, what might be viewed as waste for one owner might be the
most prized possession of another owner. More speciﬁcally, according to [Christensen (2011)],
what is considered waste depends on several factors:
Time: Products that are rare and difﬁcult to replace during trying times affecting
product scarcity (e.g. war time, trade embargoes) are often repaired by the owner, of
which time and effort is spent.
(i)
Location: Products containing food residues are often discarded in Urban areas, and
becoming waste, while in the countryside the residues would more likely be used for
animal feeding highlighting the fact that location plays a role determining a product as
waste.
(ii)
State: The state or condition of a product also plays a factor in considering waste. If the
product is in a bad condition but repairs are affordable, then the product is unlikely
to be discarded. On the other hand, if the product is old, expensive to repair, or even
beyond repair, then the product will likely be discarded.
(iii)
Income level: Literature studies have shown that there is a correlation between income
level and discard rate of products; the higher the income level a consumer has, the
more products will be discarded i.e. more waste is produced. The reason for this
phenomenon is due to easy availability of newer / better products accessible by the
higher income level. In addition, the ﬁnancial crisis in 2008 showed that the tendency
to buy more products and subsequently discard more products switched as the general
income level fell [Det Europæiske Miljøagentur (2016)].
(iv)

==PAGE:28==
Personal preferences: Products that are part of a collection or has a sentimental value
for the owner, might not end up as waste, which highlights that personal preference plays
a factor in determining waste.
(v)
While it is difﬁcult to determine what products, items or objects might become waste, solid
waste is easier to deﬁne as this type of waste mostly consists of waste in the solid phase
[Christensen (2011)] - solid waste may also include sewage sludge derived from waste water
treatment plants and / or chemical waste derived from the industry - both in the liquid phase.
Given the history of trying to deﬁne waste, various initiatives have been launched in or-
der to better understand it. For instance, the European Commission has developed a waste
framework directive [Directive 2008/98/EC (2008)] that describes when materials become
waste and how to manage it in such a way so it does not affect human health or the surround-
ing environment negatively including water, soil, air, plants and / or animals.

2.2.2
Waste Characterization
In order for the authorities to assess the fate of the waste, it is necessary to characterize the
waste by examining the physical and chemical composition of the waste materials and their
performances when they are exposed to different conditions. According to [Allesch and
Brunner (2014)], the following 3 approaches to analyze the waste characterization are:
1. Analyze the waste directly i.e. the chemical elements for each waste material are
examined.
2. Analyze the waste product, of which the waste materials (residues) comes from a
treatment plant and are to be analyzed and further utilized to determine the chemical
composition of the particular waste before treatment.
3. Analyze the composition of products / materials in waste by considering estimates
obtained from national / regional statistics.
Analysis of waste is necessary since waste can consist of many different materials (and el-
ements) that may react differently depending on exposure. Furthermore, the examination
of waste materials helps us understand when appropriate technology is to be used, which
further improves waste management initiatives.

• Landﬁlls with biocovers (that captures the gas)

2.2.3
Physical Composition analysis
Information regarding physical characteristics of MSW are useful for the municipalities when
making decisions about which technology to apply to dispose of waste. When character-
izing the physical composition of solid waste, the following characteristics / analysis are
considered: picking analysis, particle size distribution, moisture content and waste densities, which
according to [Christensen (2011)] are the most important characteristics. Other physical
characteristics of MSW are: optical properties, shape of the components, magnetic- and
electric properties and colour.

and Christensen (2011)].

1. Picking analysis: Involves manually sorting of waste materials of which the material
fraction can be identiﬁed.
2. Particle size distribution: This analysis evaluates whether or not the waste material
is suitable to be, for instance, sorted in mechanical separators, shredded and/or com-
promised mechanically due to the particle size. In addition, this analysis also cover
the determination of the particle weight - Figure 2.7 illustrates typical value ranges for
particle sizes of different waste fractions. The particle distribution inﬂuences waste
packing densities - the smaller particles, the higher packing density, and less water /
bacterial movement through the waste. A solution to obtain smaller particles could be
shredding of the waste, which also would ensure a more homogeneous composition
[Christensen (2011)].
3. Moisture content: expresses the wet content in waste. The greater the moisture content,
the heavier the weight of the waste. Waste containing high content of moisture is not
suitable for incineration due to the high concentration of wet content since incineration
of wet materials requires more energy than it produces, which is caused by evaporation
of the wet content. To determine the moisture content, the waste is weighted before
and after drying, often occurring between 77-105°C until constant weight arrives.
Besides water, at 105°C, VFA, mercury and some organic compounds (also called VOCs)
might volatilize [Lagerkvist and Christensen (2011)]. Moisture is a volatile property
allowing for rapid changes, which should be considered if it is to be determined in a
characterization. Essentially, this means that moisture is able to move from a particular
material fraction to another fraction. In addition, the moisture content is an important
factor when investigating the possibility of composting and in leachate calculations.
4. Waste densities: Expresses the mass per unit volume ([kg/m3]). The densities consist
of bulk density and material density, where bulk density expresses the wet weight
part in the waste compressed in a known volume, and the material density, also called
the speciﬁc density, expresses the density for the given material without voids. The
densities are practically used to design treatment facilities, sorting facilities and to
design landﬁlls. The typically ranges of bulk and material densities for selected waste
materials are shown in Table 2.1 and 2.2, respectively.

2.2.4
Chemical Composition Analysis
When investigating the waste behavior it is essential to examine the chemical properties
therein. According to literature [Lagerkvist and Christensen (2011)] the most important
parameters concerning chemical characteristics are: pH (and alkalinity), organic matter,
inorganics, LHV / HHV and organic pollutants (such as AOX, PAH, PCB, pesticides etc.).
pH is used to control leaching of waste. The alkanity expresses proportionality between
pH and alkaline, meaning that the more alkaline the waste is, the higher the pH of the
waste. Under conditions where the waste is subjected to temperature increase, the pH drops
drastically, resulting in waste fraction materials containing insoluble metal ions (at neutral
pH-value) dissolved in the leachate.
The organic matter in waste can be described as the waste materials containing carbon
compounds (except for CO2, carbonates, cyanides an carbides), of which the C-atoms are
covalently bound to other elements (often N, O and H). When expressing the organic matter
in municipal waste, food waste, kitchen waste and other organics (e.g. sewage sludge) are
referred to. When determining the organic matter content in waste, different approaches are
available, of which the organics can be expressed as volatile solids (VS), total organic carbon
(TOC) or chemical oxygen demand (COD). However, when describing the organics in solid
waste VS is often referred to as seen in Figure 2.8).
Figure 2.8: Illustration of typical waste composition
VS deﬁnes the solids that volatilize by ignition at 550°C. The remaining part after the ignition
is called the ash content. TOC is applied when the total amount of organic carbon of the
waste is determined by CO2 produced by the inorganics in waste. TOC is determined by
thermal heating of the waste sample and then measured via the produced CO2. Finally, COD
is applied when the content of organics is determined by chemical oxidation. It should be
noted that other important groups of organic compounds exist, such as: VFA’s (butyrate, ac-
etate, propionate, etc.), lipids (fats), proteins, carbohydrates (sugars), natural ﬁbres, synthetic
30
==PAGE:34==
organic materials (which are plastics) and non-combustibles (inerts), however, the analy-
ses of these compounds are very demanding and not precise according to [Christensen (2011)].
• The sources of lipids are mainly from food waste containing fats and cooking oil. Lipids
are considered to be suitable products for energy recovery technologies due to high
caloriﬁc values (≃38MJ/kgTS ). Lipids behavior in solid waste is that, when the
surrounding temperatures increase, the lipids transform from solid to liquid phase.
Due to the property of lipids with low solubility in water, the decomposition process in
waste occurs slowly [Rao and HarshaKota (2007)].
• The sources of proteins are mainly from food- and garden waste, which accounts for
5-10% of the dry part of solid waste [Rao and HarshaKota (2007)]. The behavior of
proteins in food waste is that they will break down and form amino acids. However,
cases where unpleasant odours are present, indicates that amines are produced during
the decomposition.
• The main sources of carbohydrates are waste obtained from food and gardening waste.
Carbohydrates consist of sugars and polymers of sugars, the latter being starch and cellu-
lose (polymer of glucose). Furthermore, carbohydrates are deﬁned as ready biodegrad-
able, of which CH4, H2O and CO2 are produced. Furthermore, it is not recommended
to expose waste containing carbohydrate content for too long in the open air as the
waste will attract rats and ﬂies [Christensen (2011)].
• Natural ﬁbers are containing cellulose and lignin (a complex polymer). The main
material fraction of natural ﬁbers are gardening waste, paper and cardboard and food
waste. The issues with natural ﬁbers are that both cellulose and lignin are resistant to
biodegrade. However, natural ﬁbers are suitable for incineration due to high content of
cellulose, which represent a high caloriﬁc values, e.g. paper consists of 100% cellulose
has a caloriﬁc value (for over-dried paper between) 12MJ/kgTS and 18MJ/kgTS,
according to the literature [Rao and HarshaKota (2007)].
• Synthetic organic materials (plastics) covers PE, PS, PVC, PU, LDPE, HDPE, PP and PET.
According to literature, plastics account for approximately 5-7% of the total amount of
solid waste, which is considered high due to the issues regarding the material [Rao and
HarshaKota (2007)]. One of the issues with plastics is that plastics do not biodegrade
and therefore need to be handled in a proper way. Since plastics have a high caloriﬁc
value (approximately 32MJ/kgTS [Rao and HarshaKota (2007)]), a suitable solution
is to apply the incineration technology under controlled conditions. However, it is
recommended to remove the PVC before the incineration process, since incinerated
PVC will result in acid gas and dioxin production. An acid gas release can result in acid
rain and thereby contribute to forest death. Where dioxins are toxic chlorine-containing
substances can cause health damage, such as cancer, on humans and animals.
• The non combustibles covers glass, metals, ceramics, dust, dirt, ashes, as well as
construction and demolition waste - basically waste materials that are not suitable to

==PAGE:35==
incinerate due to very low caloriﬁc values, which indicates that more energy is used
than produced (glass is −0.3 −0.1MJ/kgTS [Lagerkvist and Christensen (2011)].
Inorganics contain different elements / substances, where the important elements are con-
sidered heavy metals, nutrients and salts. For heavy metals, Cd, Cu, Ni, Cr, Hg, Zn, Fe and
Pb are considered elements in addition to toxic metal Al and metalloid Si - these elements are
characterized as sensitive element during extraction of element to the given medium in gas or
liquid phase). The important nutrients in inorganics are compounds containing Nitrogen (N),
phosphate (P), sulfur (S) and potassium (K). Where the usual important elements of salts are:
sulfate (SO2−

(Mg).
LHV / HHV expresses the lower heating value (LHV) and the higher heating value (HHV)
in terms of [MJ/kgTS]. These parameters are important when assessing whether waste
materials are suitable to be treated at a thermal treatment plant or a biological treatment plant.
The heating values are determined with a bomb calorimeter; a sample of the waste material
is combusted with presence of oxygen, after which the temperature in the calorimeter is
increased and the released energy of the combustion process is determined. The difference
between LHV and HHV is that HHV also includes the released heat of water condensed in
the calorimeter, which is not the case for LHV.
2.2.5
Applied Statistic
When dealing with the large data-sets that commonly occur in meta-analysis of waste,
statistics are applied to asses not only quality of the given data-set, but also if trends and
interactions can be found [Götze et al. (2016)], [Boldring (2009)]. The quality of a data set
can be assessed by investigating the mean, spread and standard deviation of a given data
set. To this end box plots are often applied in the context of waste management [Götze
et al. (2016)]. Finding trends and interactions in a large data set is more difﬁcult, but a
good initial approach is to look at the data covariance in a form where e.g. concentrations
of an always large magnitude do not overshadow concentrations of a smaller magnitude.
Pearson correlation coefﬁcient often serves as this data covariance measure in the context of
meta-analysis of waste management [Götze et al. (2016)].

4.4
Pearson Correlation Matrix
This section presents the results of evaluating the linear correlation between two variables
(parameters/elements) by applying Pearson correlation coefﬁcient, which has a value (degree
of linear relation) between +1 and -1 also called the strength between two variables. +1 refers
to a total positive linear correlation and -1 to a total negative, where 0 refers to a non-linear or
no correlation.
The Pearson correlation matrix was calculated not only for every specie from all fractions,
but also for each specie in each individual fraction. A few select ﬁgures of interest are shown
in this section, while the remaining ﬁgures can be seen in Appendix E. Due to the size of
the data-set, a color map was used to illustrate the correlation coefﬁcient visually, instead of
using a large table with numbers which quickly becomes confusing and unmanageable.
According to [Boldring (2009)] the correlation is characterized as very weak at −0.2 ≤rxy ≤
0.2, weak at −0.5 ≤rxy < −0.2 and 0.2 < rxy ≤0.5, medium at −0.7 ≤rxy < −0.5 and
0.5 < rxy ≤0.7, high at −0.9 ≤rxy < −0.7 and 0.7 < rxy ≤0.9, and last, the correlation is
characterized very high at ±1.
A general trend that could be observed in all the data-sets was that no one specie was
positively or negatively correlated to all other species. However there were cases in which
one or more species in each fraction (including the global data-set) had a combination of
either a strong positive correlation or strong negative correlation to all species in the given
data-set. This trends shows up in the correlation matrix as a blue and yellow line that
penetrates through the entire matrix. These parameters are of immense importance in the
data-set as measuring this one component allows one to predict whether all other species
will have a high (if positive correlation) or low (if negative correlation) concentration in the
given fraction. For most of the fractions sporadic correlation between the elements could
be found, but for a select few fractions these correlations were localised in one or more of
the four subgroups (high tech application elements, Heavy metals/toxic elements, Energy
related parameters, Nutrients & CHNO-elements).

Conclusion & Recommendation For Future Work
This chapter summarized the meta-analysis of waste composition data study presented in
this thesis and elaborates on the ﬁndings expressed as recommendations for future work in
order to keep expanding our knowledge within the topic of waste management and waste
composition data.
5.1
Conclusion of Results
This section provides the conclusion of the results achieved during the execution of the thesis
4 research objectives spurred on by the 11 pre-deﬁned fractions (Mixed organics, Food waste,
Gardening waste, Paper & Cardboard, Composites, Plastic, Combustibles, Metal, Glass, Inert and
Mixed waste).
5.1.1
Update and revision of database
• 19 publications meeting the criteria for literature selection were reviewed, which re-
sulted in 1.151 data entries.
• Of the 1.151 data entries, 63% were dry weight, 17% were wet weight and 20% were
unknown (?)
• Data availability by region revealed that the majority of database entries originated
from Asia. No database entries were from Africa, Americas (South) and Middle East.
Finally, Asia and Australia provided information rich publications which saw a higher
number of suitable data entries than the number of articles.
• There are still roughly 700 publications which need to be reviewed before the waste
database is completely up-to-date.
5.1.2
Database summary of overall waste data sets
• Overall, 116 publications meeting the criteria for literature selection has been reviewed
by the combined studies of [Götze et al. (2016)] and this study, which resulted in 13.248
data entries.
• Of the 13.248 data entries, 79% were dry weight, 2% were wet weight and 19% were
unknown (?). This study and its data entries, compared to overall number of data
entries, found a relatively large portion of data entries to be wet weight (17% vs. 2%
overall), a marginal higher portion to be unknown (?) (20% vs. 19% overall), and a
lower portion of data entries to be dry weight (63% vs. 79% overall).
• Overall data availability of heavy metals and toxic elements is spearheaded by Cd, Pb
and Zn, which highest material waste compositions are combustibles and food waste.
• Overall data availability of nutrients and salt-elements have CHNO in the top 5 (key
components in organic waste). Rounding out top 5 is S (prominent nutrient). Both
62
==PAGE:66==
CHNO-elements and nutrients have highest compositions of combustibles and food waste
material waste compositions.
• Overall data availability of energy related parameters is spearheaded by Ash and HHV,
which highest material waste compositions is combustibles. Common for all energy
related parameters is that material waste fractions metal, glass and inert are in small
numbers as they are not suited for incineration.
• Overall data availability by region revealed that the majority of database entries origi-
nated from Europe followed by Asia. Europe also provided information rich publica-
tions as their ratio of database entries to number of publications are higher (number of
data entries > number of publications).
• The updated waste database now consists of data entries from all regions [Africa,
America (North), America (South), Asia, Europe, Middle East, Australia as well as
Review and (?)].
• There are still roughly 700 publications which need to be reviewed before the waste
database is completely up-to-date.
5.1.3
Descriptive Statistics
• Based on 13.248 individual data entries, median values and percentiles for 28 parameters
in 11 individual waste fractions were presented and discussed
• Data ranges and applied medians for heavy metals and toxic elements revealed the Fe
and Al are detected in lots of materials.
• Data ranges and applied medians for nutrients and salt-elements revealed that C is
included in all organic matter as opposed to Metal, Glass and Inerts, which are not
prominent in this category.
• Data ranges and applied medians for energy related elements revealed that C is included
in all organic matter as opposed to Metal, Glass and Inerts, which are not prominent in
this category.
• Data ranges and applied medians for energy related elements revealed that the highest
median value of heating values are obtained from plastics, making plastics suitable for
energy recovery with the exception of PVC (dioxin production). In addition, the highest
medians for ash content were obtained from glass, inert and metal fractions.

5.2
Recommendations For Future Work
This thesis has delivered new knowledge regarding the physico-chemical composition of
waste, but there is still more to be done within this ﬁeld of waste management research. In
particular, it is suggested that:
• Review the remaining publications in literature, found by using keywords ["municipal
solid waste", "waste characterization", "waste composition", "waste analysis", "waste manage-
ment", "heavy metals", "nutrients" and/or "energy content"] and update the waste database
in order to get the complete overview.
• Investigate the suitable data entries from pool of publications seek to associate the data
entries with speciﬁc regions as well as countries and cities to get a holistic view of the
data entry segmentation.
• Data regarding food waste and other organics are more speciﬁcally provided such as
information on VFA’s and other important compounds are listed. It is important that
these organics are further investigated.
• Critically examine the Pearson Correlation Matrix. For instance, are there non-linear
trends? In addition, Pearson Correlation Matrix only tells us that variables are correlated,
but not if they are in fact really linked in a particular fraction or if components in the
fraction interacts with measurements. It could be interesting to acquire data, where
way of measuring and data itself could be compared.
• Apply more statistics and correlations in the form of p-tests and t-tests in order to evaluate
the soundness of the descriptive statistics as well as the Pearson Correlation Matrices.
The overall goal for future work should be to increase the knowledge and understanding
within physico-chemical waste compositions, which will help make state-of-the-art environ-
mental assessment of waste management systems more sophisticated, more effective and
perhaps more efﬁcient - Increase of knowledge and understanding within physico-chemical
will help determine which technologies are suitable to get rid of speciﬁc waste types. Im-
proved waste management systems and how we as a society become more sustainable, will
64
==PAGE:68==
beneﬁt greatly from such research, which is ultimately great for the environment, the econ-
omy and public health. As stated by [Wilson (2015)]: "... sound waste management is much
more than merely desirable. Sound waste management is absolutely essential" - This thesis and
the work conducted by [Götze et al. (2016)] as well as DTU’s Department of Environmental
Engineering in general contributes to sounds waste management, and must continue to do
so as dynamics and complexity of production and consumerism will continue to challenge
the goal of global sustainability.
