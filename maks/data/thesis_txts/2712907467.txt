Abstract
An optimization method based on spare parts inventory stock for a hypothetical wind farm
was created using Monte Carlo simulation approach using Python. The components sim-
ulated were given failure rate data and random numbers generated determined the status
of the part. The failures are said to follow a Poisson distribution. Key output parame-
ters such as turbine availability, component stock levels, downtime costs and inventory
holding costs were gathered after the simulation. The output data was assessed and
safety stock levels were altered till an optimal solution was found by attempting to solve
for the lowest total downtime. Time to repair was implemented into the simulation to add
complexity to the simulation model and legitimatise the simulations further. The results
showed an optimal value can be achieved by assuming various input parameters how-
ever, the uncertainty due to the assumptions are also acknowledged throughout.

Introduction
The Earths climate has been changing throughout history. While most of these changes
were due to the variations in Earth’s orbit and solar energy in the past; there is no denying
that since the mid-20th century, the current warming trends are to be attributed to human
activity. Human activity, through the need for the development of societies, are predicted
to impact the climate which will bring about adversities to living beings and our environ-
ments if left unchecked. With this realization, the world has been urged to transition from
fossil fuels and push toward employing cleaner sources of energy to reduce greenhouse
gas emissions and mitigate these consequences.
While many renewable energy technologies have been developed and improved over
time, the only way to completely substitute fossil fuel dependency would be through inte-
grating a healthy mix of renewable energy technologies which can efficiently provide en-
ergy where required. What this would mean for each of the renewable technologies would
be to bring them to a competitive pricing point where developers are willing to invest into
these technologies over fossil fuels. Ultimately it would be to improve the technologies as
such where the Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) is at a competitive standard.
In terms of wind energy, onshore wind has undergone considerable development while
being able to bring the LCOE to a point where it can attract energy developers to step into
the wind energy industry and benefit from it [1]. While it is a great step for wind research,
this also means that there will be significant land use in the process. Offshore wind on
the other hand, has been proven to work well and can be implemented to a larger scale
with the potential to generate higher amounts of electricity; and while building on sea is
difficult, it does mitigate the potential land use risks than onshore wind seems to face.
Wind farm profits are calculated by taking the income generated from the electricity sales
and subtracting the investment plus the Operation and Maintenance (O&M) costs. Build-
ing a wind farm on sea presents challenges in both these costs; While the investment
costs are important to identify, this thesis will look into the reliability aspects of offshore
wind farms with the intention of improving the O&M costs offshore turbines face.
1.1
Offshore wind energy
Offshore wind has seen considerable growth over the recent decades and showing no
signs of slowing as the industry sets its sights to develop larger, more efficient turbines as
well as increase the size of wind farms to reduce overall unit costs. As of 2019, offshore
wind has accounted for about 10% of global new wind power installations as it extends into
new markets and is expected to reach more than 20% of total wind installations by 2025
[2]. This growth can be attributed to factors such as higher wind potential in the offshore
environment, technology maturity, improvements in wind farm planning and installation
technologies [3]. Offshore wind also resolves problems faced by onshore projects which
include noise, physical restrictions and aesthetics to the surrounding landscape.
With all the benefits offshore wind has to offer, it still has a significantly higher LCOE as
compared to other renewable sources such as onshore wind or solar photovoltaics (PVs)
[4]. This is mainly due to the accessibility challenges faced when building on the sea which
requires specialised equipment and materials. The turbines are built several kilometers
away from shore and each turbine must undergo structural considerations regarding wind
and wave loads, water depths, turbulence, moisture, grid connections, etc.

Moreover, experienced offshore wind developers can look to solve the challenge of build-
ing on the sea by tackling the logistical hurdles as the industry currently spends an esti-
mated 25-30% of the total life cycle costs in in O&M expenditures [5]. Transporting these
turbines and its parts for installation and maintenance to the site requires special transport
such as vessels designed to carry the large blades, equipment, and skilled technicians
capable of operating in the offshore environment; all of which contribute to the high ex-
penses undergone by these projects.
1.2
Offshore wind operations and maintenance
Offshore wind farm developers will look to use concise strategies when estimating the
Operational Expenditures (OPEX) for the lifetime of their energy plant more than onshore
as the accessibility for maintenance is restricted due to the need for specialized equip-
ment and appropriate weather conditions. Onshore wind farms typically have availability
around 95-99%; in comparison with offshore, which is around the range of 60-70% [6].
The availability of a farm is a good measure to assess the performance as it gives an es-
timate on how long the farm is producing energy during its lifetime and improper planning
for logistical maintenance for offshore projects contribute to the availability of the plant
and O&M costs.
Offshore turbines are prone to relatively higher failures during their life time as they are
placed in rough marine conditions; and during unfavourable weather (such as winter sea-
sons), the whole wind farm may be inaccessible if a component fails and requires repairs.
The turbine could be off for longer than it would need to be which would ultimately increase
the running costs for the turbine. Even during favourable conditions, O&M tasks become
costly offshore as it is influenced by factors such as distance to shore, turbine reliability,
and maintenance strategies; as offshore farms require special equipment to conduct such
activities which are generally not available at short notice.
To operate an offshore wind farm and reduce the O&M costs associated with it, developers
must ideally plan maintenance depending on the site using data from appropriate systems
such as Supervisory Control, or Signal Conditioning, and Data Acquisition (SCADA) and
condition monitoring as well as other tools such as remote monitoring using drones, boats,
or helicopters to predict appropriate maintenance cycles and turbine failures. Proper O&M
planning and logistics management is key for reducing the high operational costs for off-
shore wind farms, and while novel methods are being investigated, there is no doubt that
data, and the accessibility to it, is the key to creating accurate models which can work in
various locations.

The work in this paper is conducted through independent study and hence the model
that is developed will rely on data gathered from studies conducted in the logistics and
reliability management. The literature survey was carried out to explore current O&M
systems as well as reliability characteristics for offshore wind.

1.3.2
Review papers
Dao [13] composed a review paper that compiled publicly available reliability data for both
onshore and offshore wind turbines and investigates the impacts of reliability on the cost
of energy. They compared onshore and offshore wind turbine reliability to find sources
of uncertainty, as well as gathered wind turbine failure rates and downtime which were
broken down by their respective sub-assemblies. The significant variations in both failure
rates and down times were concluded to be from contributing factors such as volume of
data, location, duration of collection, and turbine power ratings all which have an effect in
the reliability uncertainty. It was also concluded that data sources with low failure rates
are dominant.
A review paper by Shafiee [14] explores research on current and future O&M logistics
in offshore wind energy by developing a classification framework which divides decision
making into three echelons in the maintenance organization realm and categorized in time
frames such as long-term (strategic), mid-term (tactical), and short-term (operational) de-
cisions. The strategic echelon includes decisions that can be taken initially by developers
to reduce O&M costs in the long term such as designing wind farms for reliability by se-
lecting appropriate wind farm locations, set ups, and also research in selecting proper
maintenance strategies. The tactical echelon covers research conducted involving spare
parts inventory management, and maintenance support organization; all of which include
decisions that can be made between once a year or once in five years. Finally the op-
erational echelon covers research that refers to day-to-day decisions that are required in
O&M management which include maintenance scheduling tasks, maintenance vessels
routing and maintenance performance measuring systems.
They were able to conclude that, amongst the three reviewed categories, the areas that
were most researched were wind maintenance strategies, maintenance support organi-
sation, and the maintenance scheduling tasks, while also being able to uncover the areas
of least research. Moreover, they found that areas related maintenance, repair and oper-
ation issues have received less attention and that the long-term (strategic) decisions have
the most effect on O&M which influences the profitability of the offshore wind projects.
Seyr [15] reviews offshore wind farm O&M with a focus on decision support models for the
scheduling of maintenance. Relevant literature is displayed for influential factors pertain-
ing to offshore wind farm O&M scheduling which includes accessibility, reliability, costs,
weather, failures, damage, and more.

Zhang [16] conducts a review of 148 papers regarding spare parts inventory manage-

methodologies.
1.3.3
Research literature
Lindqvist [17] conducted research similar to the work in this paper. They model spare part
logistics for wind turbines and analyse discrete strategies to compare profitability using
a spare parts optimization software (OPUS10) to find optimal stock levels and reorder
sizes. In terms of spare parts, they were able to find that profits can be seen using a
central warehouse for reordering and storage, and large savings can be made using an
optimized spare part strategy even though the costs related to spare part investment,
reordering, and storage constitute of a small proportion in life support costs. It was also
mentioned that the lead times due to crane ships could affect the stock sizes for critical
components and that pooling of spare parts contribute positively to cost efficiency rather
than local storage and handling.
A dissertation by Von Tursten [18] evaluates O&M logistics concepts using event-discrete
simulations to investigate a system and cost for different situations. Some of the interest-
ing findings were that onshore based logistics concepts are economically superior when
an offshore plant is close to shore and the offshore concepts usually lead to higher avail-
ability for the offshore wind power plant but the increased costs for the additional equip-
ment reduces the profitability. They also find that for offshore plants 100 km away from
the base station and have 90 turbines that the offshore based concepts are more cost
efficient than the onshore.
Pan [19] looks at spare parts optimization based on Monte Carlo simulations using a fuzzy
multi-objective optimization method to evaluate the operational availability and spare parts
support costs of the equipment. They were able to prove the proposed method which
focused on two indicators, the operational availability and the spare parts support costs.
A thesis paper by Chris [20] looks at hypothetical 100 units of 5MW wind turbines to
understand the economics of locating a floating offshore wind farm. They examine various
maintenance strategies but most notable was the use of a Monte Carlo simulation using
failure rates and component costs to estimate costs for offshore wind turbine inventory
which has influenced the work in this paper
While literature on logistics management strategies are many, some notable papers give
valuable insights. Van bussel [21] [22] shows that the costs for some EU offshore wind
farms vary from 18% to 23% which is typically much higher than the usual 12% for on-
shore projects. Tusar [23] shows that reducing downtime is one of the goals of proper
maintenance strategies and that determining an appropriate strategy is most common.

2
Problem statement
Offshore wind farms consist of a large number of turbines depending on the location and
project scope. A wind turbine is a system that comprises of various sub-systems. In
general, these sub-systems consist of sub-assemblies which are made of various com-
ponents. An overview of how a wind turbine system can be deconstructed is presented
in Figure 2.1. The structure presented is used to find relevant failure information on sub-
assembly that can be used to conduct an analysis. Whether a component fails or a part,
they all contribute to the reduction of reliability of the system as a whole. In other words, as
one part fails, the component attached to that part starts breaking down which eventually
leads to the sub assembly failing and ultimately the system itself.
Figure 2.1: Wind turbine structure [13]
When a component fails in an offshore wind turbine, the farms have to find distinctive
solutions to fixing particular issues as it requires special transport vessels, technicians,
and proper maintenance timing to name a few. Costs for maintaining are higher in an
offshore environment due to accessibility constraints generally.
Moreover, when a failure occurs and the component is required to be replaced, a spare
part is required. When a part is needed, it has to be ordered from the supplier which
usually has a lead time that varies depending on circumstances such as location from
the Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) and OEM availability. During this time, if the
spare part is required and not in inventory storage, the turbine will be down for longer than
anticipated which leads to operational downtime.
On the other hand, turbine developers must also look into storing these spare parts (such
as a warehouse to hold stocks) which are also a cost driver for the company that is oper-
ating and maintaining these wind farms as they consist of inventory holding costs.
If spare part inventories are not managed appropriately, it would lead to the wind turbine

It is therefore crucial to find an optimum solution to balance the cost of holding inventory

2.1
Objective
The aim of this thesis is to simulate an offshore wind farm using data gathered from various
literature. Spare part stocks will be modelled for a particular wind farm under certain
assumptions and failure rates. Moreover, this thesis will attempt to find optimum spare
parts inventory to keep in stock while considering the holding costs and service levels.
The modelling and optimization will be done using PYTHON 3.8. Relevant information
will be presented in chapter 4. The main objectives to fulfill by the end of the thesis would
be:
• How to model a wind farm using failure rate data
• How different spare parts inventory affect the service level based on the failure rate

Wind turbine capacity factor
The capacity factor is a ratio of the actual electrical energy produced versus the theoret-
ical maximum electricity output over a time period. In terms of wind power, it is a helpful
indicator of the actual production relative to the production that is possible. It can be com-
puted over a time scale of a year which can aid in calculating the Annual Energy Produc-
tion (AEP) of a turbine. Computing it over months can give valuable insights to seasonal
effects of the farm however, it would require wind climate data to estimate correctly.
Normally, the AEP for a turbine is specific to a site location and the wind environment.
Since this thesis has no specific turbine data or wind climate to get a value for the AEP,
an estimate for an 8 MW turbine will be made using the capacity factor.
Offshore wind farm capacity factors are available and data from offshore wind farms in UK
[32], Denmark [33], Belgium [34] and Germany [35] were evaluated to find the capacity
factors considering all the farms lie approximately close to 38% to 42%.
A capacity factor of 40% was assumed for the modelling in this research after investigating
the data.

4.2
Monte Carlo approach
A Monte Carlo approach was taken to model the component failures in the wind farm for a
time period of 50 years. This approach relies on a repeated sampling of random numbers
to obtain a solution to a deterministic problem. It is generally used to solve problems that
have a probabilistic interpretation.
18

==PAGE:26==
For the simulation model, the wind turbine consisted the sub-assemblies integrated with
their failure rates from Table 4.1. Once the simulation is set to run, it begins on the first day
and first turbine which generates a random number for each of the parts. If the random
number is greater than the failure rate for the specific part then the simulation generates
a new random number for the next part until all the parts have been checked against the
random number; it then moves to the next turbine and does the same. In contrast, if the
random number is less than the failure rate for the particular part, then the part is consid-
ered to have encountered a failure, and the entire turbine is turned ”Off”, which prevents
the other components from failing due to the random numbers not being generated for
that particular turbine.
The simulation consists of two main turbine states, ”On” or ”Off”, and the structure is
described by their respective modules
4.2.1
On module
The ”On” module or ”Working” state of the turbine is defined weather the random number
is above the failure rate for the individual component. Once it is known that the random
number value is above the failure rate value, then the simulation progresses to the next
component within the turbine. Once all the components are checked with a different ran-
dom number for each, then the simulation moves to the next turbine and does the same;
until all 100 turbines are checked for failures. Once that is completed, it moves to the next
day until 50 years have passed for all 100 turbines.
4.2.2
Off module
If the random number is compared with the failure rate of a component and the result is
less than the failure rate for that component, then the part is considered to have failed
and the turbine is set to ”Off”. Once that specific turbine is turned off, no random numbers
will be generated for the components in that turbine and the part that failed is set to the
”Failed” state.
4.2.2.1
Failed state
Once the part is set to the failed state, an order is placed for that component, which will
arrive after the lead time, and the simulation runs a check on the current stock levels for
the components. If there is a spare part available, it then removes a part from the stock
list for that specific component sets the status to ”Repairing”. If there is no part in the
stock, then the turbine is set to an ”Out of stock” state.
4.2.2.2
Repairing state
Once a part is removed from the stock list, the turbine is considered to be ”Repairing”
where the failed component is set to repair for the duration of time that is defined in Ta-
ble 4.3. The turbine is off during the time of repair and is turned back on after the repair
is complete.
4.2.2.3
Out of stock state
The out of stock state is realised when there is no stock available in inventory for that
component and the turbine is kept off until a stock is replenished. As a failure has occurred
that set the part to ”Out of stock”, an order is placed for a new part at the same time; once
the stock is back, then the turbine is set to a repairing state.

dependently from one another and the probability that an event occurs in a given length
of time does not change through time.

4.8
Time to repair
Finally, the time to repair data was incorporated into the simulation to predict what the
increased costs would be due to repairs as well as out of stocks. Time to repair data was
used from Table 4.3 and presents a hypothetical which attempts to make the model more
realistic. The scenario poses the question; what would happen if we assume a continuous
review policy with a failure based maintenance strategy?
The time to repair was incorporated to see how the availability of a turbine will change if
this hypothetical scenario is in place, since realistically, spare parts are not immediately
being replaced; especially in an offshore environment.
Incorporating the time to repair attempts to push the model further, but few more as-
sumptions must be made, such as the O&M team constantly having access to special
equipment5 to replace the parts and that the time to repair values that are assumed6 are
valid for the purposes of this thesis.
It would be beneficial to take into account the time to repair as failed parts generally do
require some time to be repaired or replaced in our case.
For the computations done by the simulation model, the total downtime cost is estimated
by including the cost of repairs as well. When a part has failed and the turbine is off, there
is a stock check that takes place. If a stock is available, then the stock is used and a
new part order is placed. The crew then go to replace the failed part with the stock for
the duration of the repair time and that would mean that the turbine is off for that time.
Furthermore, if the part is out of stock, the turbine will be off until the stock returns and
the stock is replenished then the turbine will be placed in a repairing state.
During the time of these events occurring, the simulation keeps track of the number of
days the turbine is off for including the repair time; then, the total downtime of repairs and
out of stock days are accumulated at the end and then averaged over multiple iterations.

Discussion
The simulation model created is used to find the optimal balance between downtime costs
and inventory holding costs. After generating the results. It is found that the optimal level
of spare parts that can be stored in inventory would be the number of spares part stocks
defined for Set number 3 (from Table 6.1).
Among all the simulations modelled, one thing is clear which is that the uncertainties need
to be recognized. While an optimal solution was found; dealing with spare parts inventory
requires a vast array of validated data so that spare parts inventory management can be
handled well. For all the assumptions made, there are always more considerations that
can be done to further define a well organized inventory management system.
The simulation model is still at its infancy, however, the analytical results from the Poisson
process and Mean time to failure do coincide with the results of the simulation. The failures
do indeed follow a Poisson distribution and the simulation model is taken a few steps
further by adding different failure states such as out of stock as well as repairing states. All
of which improve the model, nevertheless, it should be acknowledged that the model relies
on many specific assumptions made through various literature that have been referenced.
Therefore, it makes this simulation model particularly constrained to its assumptions.
All the key output parameters that were generated depended heavily on the input data and
assumptions utilized. The failure rate data was collected from multiple research papers
and have their own respective assumptions stated. The failure rates by far have the
biggest influence on the simulation model while the component costs and wind turbine
selection influence the optimization output cost parameters.
In reality, component costs are different depending on the manufacturer which make it
challenging to create a generalized model. While all the assumptions stated were refer-
enced to their sources, there is still a vast amount of data required to bring the model
closer to a realistic solution such as lead time variability, actual wind climate data for a
specific wind turbine, reorder and storage costs which can further have an influence on
the optimal inventory quantities.
When it came to the implementing the time to repair, it was assumed that access to main-
tenance vehicles such as crane ships and helicopters was readily available but the costs
of utilizing such equipment and respecting their lead times was not accounted for. Acces-
sibility and seasonal variations around the offshore wind farm was not taken into account
either; all of which contributes to the downtime costs as well as the inventory management
policies.
The model imagines a continuous review policy where individual turbines in the farm were
assessed daily for failures and a failure based maintenance strategy was used which
assumed that maintenance operations occurred as a failure was encountered such as
replacing the turbine part in our case.
The sub-assemblies used do not fully make up a turbine but instead, constitutes of some
main components from the rotor, drive train, electrical system and a few more.
The differences in costs for components must also be considered as turbine manufactur-
ers supply a vast array of parts which do have different costs all of which make it chal-
lenging to create a generalized model.

The model created assumes that the once the component has failed, it is being replaced
by an item kept in storage which is not the case in reality. In truth, the failed part will most
likely be sent for repairs and will be repaired to a certain quality level.
What this simulation model does provide is in fact a prediction of the component failures
considering the assumptions stated. It tracks the spare parts inventory stock demand as
well as the time between the failures which can aid in producing a robust spare part main-
tenance strategy to find a balance between optimal stock levels and downtime costs. Be-
ing able to forecast the demand for various spare parts with precise accuracy is important
when it comes to spare part inventory strategies. Spare parts must be treated different to
inventory management supply chains as they depend on factors such as random failures,
failure rates and failure rate changes as the materials wear out through time. In addition,
a well made model should accommodate additional inputs to describe different offshore
wind situations.

Conclusion
The simulation model was created was to find optimal spare part inventory stocks. It was
done through a number of assumptions that were stated throughout the report. These
assumptions are critical to understanding how the model works as it is created to optimize
spare part stocks for a specific system.
Data on offshore parts and logistic operations are scarce and the assumptions made were
to create a model that is able to take input from general reliability data for wind turbine
components and carry out an optimization strategy where the inventory holding costs are
compared with the downtime costs.
This thesis poses a variety of solutions from how to create a simulation model to accom-
modate for failure rates for multiple turbines as well as important variables required to
optimize spare part stock levels. The optimal number of spare parts inventory required to
reduce the cost as much as possible was found. The simulation model shows what the
main factors affecting the costs of downtime as well as the inventory holding costs.
When attempting to find an optimal solution, the service level for the specific part was
used to determine the performance of that part to check how much of the demand that
part is able to meet. Adjusting the stock values based on the service level was found to
be useful when attempting to reduce downtime costs as the parts that failed most often
required the most spare part stocks. The component costs were also a big factor which
affected the inventory optimization as some components costed more than the others
and failed as often. Finding the right balance included running multiple simulations with
different sets of stock levels while looking at keeping the service level for the components
generally around 90%.
The time to repair was implemented in the model as well and brings about nearly identical
data as to without the time to repair; however, the total cost of the system is significantly
higher as each turbine is off for a larger amount of time, therefore leading to less income
being generated.
There can always be more reliability data that can be added to improve the model and
bring it to a realistic fidelity, however, after running numerous amounts of simulations and
comparing them with the literature provide prior; the data that is outlined in this thesis is
proved to confidently plan the most profitable strategies for spare parts for a wind farm.
