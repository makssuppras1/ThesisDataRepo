Abstract
In this study, three different scientific questions are investigated.
Firstly an im-
plementation of the direct method from direct strapdown airborne gravimetry is
developed and verified. Secondly, the basis of understanding quantum gravimetry
and the need for hybridization with a classical gravimeter is investigated, and a
prototype hybridization method is implemented. Thirdly an exploit into the possi-
bilities of using Kalman-based filters, both as optimal lowpass filters within airborne
gravimetry and as a basis for hybridization of quantum gravimetry measurements
with the direct strapdown gravimetry measurements.
A case study performed by
National Oﬀice for Aerospace Studies and Research (ONERA) in the Bay of Biscay,
France, 2019 is used. The survey consists of five reference profiles, flown between two
waypoints. Using a traditionally used Butterworth filter, the direct method gravity
disturbance results show great stability with an Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)
of 0.69 [mGal] at an Full-Width-Half-Maximum (FWHM) of 110 [s], At a spatial
resolution of λFWHM = 10.6 ± 0.9 [km]. The hybridization of the raw atomic sig-
nal from the Gravimètre Interférométrique de Recherche à Atomes Froids, Embar-
quable (GIRAFE) quantum gravimeter and the classical gravimeter yielded an ac-
celeration estimate with a significant bias and trend. GIRAFE gravity disturbance
estimates provided by ONERA were therefore utilized in the rest of the project. A
total of three different Kalman based filters was implemented: A lowpass filter, a
lowpass filter with the introduction of tie values for drift correction, and a lowpass
filter with the introduction of GIRAFE gravity disturbance estimates, to correct the
relative measurements from the direct method. Promising results were shown for all
three implementations. The Kalman lowpass filter yields an RMSE of 0.78 [mGal] at
a spatial resolution of 9.58 ± 0.9 [km] but with a high-frequency noise on the profiles.
It has been shown that further tuning of the parameters used in the Kalman filters
is necessary.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In Airborne gravimetry [Forsberg and Olesen, 2010], three different methods can be
utilized for measuring the Earth’s gravity field [Becker, 2016]:
• Traditional mechanical spring gravimeters, mounted on a gyro-stabilized plat-
form. [Forsberg and Olesen, 2010]
• Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) in a strapdown configuration. [Johann et al.,
2019]
• The use of the newer quantum gravimeter systems, mounted on a gyro-stabilized
platform, with the addition of a traditional mechanical spring gravimeter. [Bidel
et al., 2013a]
For all three, the Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) positioning is equally
important, to determine the kinematic accelerations of the plane/helicopter [Jekeli
and Garcia, 1997]. This study aims to tackle three different problems, regarding the
two last methods.
Strapdown gravimetry covers the geodetic technique where an IMU is used in a strap-
down configuration, meaning that the IMU is bolted directly to the chassis of the
plane/helicopter [Jensen and Forsberg, 2018]. This is highly beneficial compared to
the traditional method, in regards to the weight and volume of the system, as no
gyro-stabilized platform is needed [Becker, 2016, C. L. Glennie et al., 2000]. Using
strapdown gravimetry two different methods can be used to evaluate the gravity dis-
turbance, the indirect method and the direct method.
In the indirect method or
”one-step approach”, the gravity disturbance is estimated as a state in an Extended
Kalman Filter structure, using the specific force measurements from the IMU and
positioning information from the GNSS. This method is proven to work, but the
result is highly dependent on the extensive fine-tuning of the filter [Tim Enzlberger,
2018, Bidel et al., 2023]. The direct method or ”cascade approach”, consist of several
consecutive processing steps. Where the gravity disturbance essentially is computed
directly from the difference between the kinematic accelerations of the plane and the
specific force measurements of the IMU [Johann et al., 2019, Becker, 2016, Kwon and
Jekeli, 2001]. The first aim of the study, is an implementation of the direct method
and validation of the results, as on par with the state-of-the-art methods.
Traditionally all gravimeters, used in airborne gravimetry, be it strapdown IMU’s or

stabilized spring gravimeters, has been relative [Forsberg and Olesen, 2010]. This
essentially means that the measurements need to be corrected for the drift and pos-
sible bias of the gravimeter. This is done by tying the measurements to a known
fix-point gravity value, where the known value, could be an absolute gravity measure-
ment taken before and after the specific survey. Absolute gravity measurements, is
however an extensive operation, which takes a long time and uses a lot of equipment.
[Torge and Müller, 2012, Bidel et al., 2023]. A relative gravimeter could also be used,
but this would only correct for the drift of the gravimeter over time. Within the
last few years, quantum gravimeters based on cold atom wave interferometry have
emerged [Bidel et al., 2013a, P. R. Berman, 1997]. This newer technology shows
the potential for absolute gravity measurements at a much higher sampling rate than
traditional absolute gravimeters [C. Freier et al., 2016, Ménoret et al., 2018]. Further-
more, quantum gravimeters have been used in airborne gravimetry settings [Bidel
et al., 2023, Huang et al., 2023].
This essentially enables the possibility for per-
forming absolute airborne gravity measurements, which could be hugely beneficial
for geological mapping, geophysical exploration, or geodesy [Tim Enzlberger, 2018].
In this study, the quantum gravimeter GIRAFE developed by the French aerospace
lab ONERA is investigated. This is done both to understand the basics of quantum
gravimetry and to later implement a hybridization of the absolute quantum gravity
measurements with the relative direct method estimates.
In airborne gravimetry and especially strapdown airborne gravimetry, a significant
signal-to-noise value is present [Becker, 2016]. The last part of this study aims to
exploit the use of Kalman based filters, in relation to lowpass filters and to hybridize
the direct method results with quantum gravimeter measurements. Depending on
the design of the Kalman filter, different measurement updates can be introduced to
correct or adjust the direct method estimates. This, in theory, means that the direct
method results and GIRAFE results can be combined, to provide a higher quality re-
sult than the estimates themselves. Furthermore, the GIRAFE measurements could
be used to adjust the relative IMU estimates to absolute gravity [Jensen and Forsberg,
2018, Brown and Hwang, 2012].
The overall structure of the report is divided into three different subsets. This means
that each chapter is divided into three different parts: a direct method part, a quan-
tum gravimetry part, and a Kalman filter design part. This ensures that each element
of the report is clear and can be compared in the discussion and conclusion at the
end of the report.

2.3
The Signal To Noise Problem - A Need for Optimal
Filtering
In airborne gravimetry, a well-known problem is the significant observation noise
[Tim Enzlberger, 2018 Becker, 2016, Forsberg and Olesen, 2010]. This leads to a
significant signal-to-noise ratio in the final gravity disturbance estimates. Tradition-
ally filters like a Gaussian or Butterworth type filter are used to lowpass filter the
gravity disturbance estimates [Jensen, 2022, Forsberg and Olesen, 2010, Forsberg et
al., 2001]. In this study, the possible use of filters based on the Kalman structure is
implemented and tested, as Kalman filtering is based in the core principle of optimal
filtering. In addition, the Kalman filter structure also allows for the use of tie/bias
value determination directly in the filtering. In theory, a better evaluation for the
gravity disturbance is obtained when incorporating external gravity measurements,
such as the GIRAFE observations, to update the direct strapdown estimates. This is
further explained in section [3.4].

CHAPTER 3
Methodology
In this chapter, a thorough description of the theory and the implementation thereof
is found. The first part is regarding the computation of gravity disturbance through
direct strapdown airborne gravimetry using the direct method. The second part is
an overview of the hybridization method used by ONERA to constrain the cosine
dependency in the quantum gravimeter output. The last part is regarding the imple-
mentation and theory behind the use of Kalman filters to both lowpass filter gravity
disturbance estimates from the direct method and hybridizing the direct method with
the results from ONERA’s Quantum Gravimeter GIRAFE
3.1
Gravity Disturbance Definition δg
Assuming that the shape of the Earth could be described perfectly as an ellipsoidal
sphere with a homogeneous mass distribution, then the gravity at any point P (gP ) on
the surface or above/below it, could be described by the normal gravity γP . [Torge
and Müller, 2012]. As Earth does not have a homogeneous mass distribution, an
additional measure for gravity is to be defined. The disturbing gravity vector δgP ,
which accounts for all the inhomogeneous structures in the Earth.
This in turn
means that the gravity measured at any point P (gP ), can be defined by: [Hofmann-
Wellenhof and Moritz, 2005, Tim Enzlberger, 2018].
gP = γP + δgP
⇔
δgP = gP −γP
(3.1)
By neglecting the small difference in angle between the gravity g and normal gravity
γ (deflection of the vertical), then the gravity disturbance at point P is defined by
the magnitude of the quantities. [Torge and Müller, 2012, Hofmann-Wellenhof and
Moritz, 2005]
δgP = ∥gP ∥−∥γP ∥= gP −γP
(3.2)
By using GNSS the geodetic coordinates needed for determining the normal grav-
ity (geodetic latitude and ellipsoidal height h) can be determined, a more detailed
description of this can be found in Section 3.2.3.2. In the field of physical geodesy,
the gravity anomaly is traditionally the chosen quantity when working with gravity

Quantum Gravimetry and Hybridization Method.
In this section, the basic principle behind the GIRAFE gravimeter is explained, along
with a description of the hybridization method between the quantum gravimeter and a
classical gravimeter, in order to constrain the acceleration measurement, as explained
in section 2.2. The hybridization method here is based upon the work done by: [Bidel
et al., 2023, Bonnin et al., 2022, Bidel et al., 2020, Bidel et al., 2013a]. A theoretical
description of the physics and quantum mechanics behind the GIRAFE quantum
gravimeter is determined not to be included in the scope of this work, instead, the
focus in this section, is on the information the user get from the instrument. For
a theoretical walk-through of the quantum gravimeter technology see [Peters et al.,

3.3.1
Quantum Gravimetry - Basic Principle
The quantum gravimeter GIRAFE utilizes the same technology as for all Cold Atom
Interferometry (CAI) gravimeters [Peters et al., 2001, Bidel et al., 2023, P.
R.
Berman, 1997, Titterton et al., 2004]. The test mass, from which the accelerations are
deduced, consists of a gas of cold atoms (87Rb), which is kept in a trap, by methods
of laser cooling and trapping [Bidel et al., 2013a, Metcalf and Var der Straten, 1999].
The GIRAFE instrument uses a Mach-Zehnder type atom interferometer [Bidel et al.,
2013a, Bidel et al., 2023], which utilizes three equally spaced Raman laser pulses re-
sulting in Raman transitions between two stable states of the cold atoms. The output
from the Quantum gravimeter is given as the proportion of atoms being in the state
F = 2 compared to F = 1. Which is determined using fluorescence detection of the
test mass [Bernard et al., 2022].
A schematic illustration of a quantum gravimeter setup is seen in Figure 3.4.

5.3.1.3
Tuning of Kalman Filter Hyperparameters
As explained in Section 3.4, the Kalman filter structure is dependent on a set of
hyperparameters, which is defined by the user. These include: The standard deviation
of the measure used for measurement updates in the filter. The standard deviation of
the measures that are being evaluated. And the along-track correlation parameter. To
optimize these values, a sequential method has been used. This is done by ”guessing”
the three values, and thereafter optimizing them one at a time. A range for each value
is determined, and the filter in question is iteratively compiled over that range, the
RMSE value from the reference profiles at each iteration is saved, and a minimum can
then be computed. Having determined an optimized value for a specific parameter,
then this is now fixed to that value, before running the Filter over a new set of ranges
for the next parameter. The results for the three optimizations can be seen in Figure
5.20 below.

6.1
Direct Strapdown - Direct Method
The first part of the discussion is regarding the implementation of the direct method
to derive gravity disturbance from a direct strapdown setting. The main focus is on
the verification and performance of the direct method. The discussion is based on
the methodology presented in section 3.2 and the results presented in section 5.1.
6.1.1
RMSE of Lowpassed Gravity Disturbance Profiles
To verify the performance of the implementation, a measure for the repeatability of the
method was introduced. The computation of the RMSE value of reference profiles, is
a common method in the literature, for validating airborne gravimetry methods. The
base implementation of the direct method as described in Figure 2.1, is missing the
last step. As explained, in airborne gravimetry the choice of lowpass filtering method
is essential to the final quality of the product. In this project a 3rd order multipass
Butterworth filter is used to lowpass filter the gravity disturbance results from the
direct method. This filter is a new implementation of a traditionally used filter at
DTU SPACE, and the results would give a good indication of the performance of this
specific implementation. In Figure 5.3 the results of the reference profiles analysis
are shown for the survey. The used FWHM of 110 [s] results in an RMSE = 0.69
[mGal] with a full wavelength spatial resolution of ≈10.6 ± 0.9 [km]. This is on par
with results shown in [Tim Enzlberger, 2018 and Johann et al., 2019, Bidel et al.,
2023] where the indirect method has been used in the same setting of a reference
profile analysis. An offset is observed between the reference profiles in Figure 5.3, the
magnitude of the offset between the profiles is below 4-5 [mGal]. The same offset is
seen throughout the reference profile results shown in the study, no major emphasis
is however put on the offset, as the RMSE value is still low. A plausible cause for
the offset could be the difference in the flight height between lines. This should not
be a problem, as the results are given as gravity disturbance. This means that if
==PAGE:84==

the error is caused by this, a local difference in the gravity gradient could be the
cause. Another cause could be that the gravity disturbance results shown here, are
not tidal corrected or bias corrected. A possible time-dependent offset is, therefore,
a plausibility [Pertsev, 2007].
6.1.2
Restrictions From the Geometry of the Case Study
The validation of the method is strictly constrained by the geometry of the case survey.
The case survey as shown in Figure 4.1, only consists of the five reference profiles and
two different airports. The problem with constraining the choice of FWHM value
for the lowpass filter is illustrated in Figure 5.7 and 5.8. Where all cases of FWHM
values in Figure 5.8 result in approximately the same RMSE value. To determine
feasible values of FWHM, external information is therefore needed in the analysis. In
this project the EGM2008 gravity disturbance model, provided by ICGEM is used
to one: Compare the gravity disturbance profiles, and two: To derive a constrained
FWHM value based on an RMSE analysis from the estimated gravity disturbance,
and the model estimates. The results from this is seen in Section 5.1.3 and Figure
5.9. A constrained FWHM value is determined at ≈115 [s] resulting in a RMSE
= 1.32 [mGal] between the reference profiles and the EGM2008 gravity disturbance
estimates. In Figure 5.5 it was found that the model and estimates deviates outside
the reference profiles. This indicates that the direct method is not as well performing
in a high-energy environment as when the plane is flying as stable as possible. It has
to be noted that the model EGM2008, works fine as a representation for the gravity
disturbance profile, over open ocean, but over land, the model is not suﬀicient.
Furthermore as a product of the geometry of the survey, and a lack of absolute gravity
measurements at the start and end of the survey. It has not been possible to adjust
the relative measurements from the IMU properly. An adjustment from the relative
measurements to de-trended relative measurements could be possible if the survey

introduced, if an adjustment is introduced, based upon the stationary measurements

6.1.3
Importance of Correct Attitude Controls Sampling Rate
During the implementation process, the importance of the attitude control frequency
extracted from the GNSS/INS solution became evident. During the early stages of
the implementation, high-frequency deviations in the final lowpassed filtered gravity
disturbance profiles were found. These problems arise when using attitude controls
with a frequency of less than the frequency of the IMU sampling rate, in this project
a 300 [Hz] sampling rate is used for the IMU. In the first implementations of the
method, the attitude controls were extracted from Inertial Explorer at a frequency
matching the GNSS sampling rate (1 [Hz]). Given that the IMU is in a strapdown
configuration, it is most likely that changes in the pitch and roll of the plane at high
frequencies induce an unknown bias on the vertical acceleration measurement, which
is not taken into account when using low-frequency attitude controls. It was found
that by introducing attitude controls at frequencies matching the sampling rate of
the IMU, the problem was resolved.
6.1.4
Direct Vs. Indirect Method
In direct strapdown airborne gravimetry, two different ways of determining the gravity
disturbance can be used. The direct method used in this project, and the indirect
method. Where the direct method can be described as a cascade of different sequential
implementations, the indirect method utilizes an extension in the Extended Kalman
Filter framework, where the gravity disturbance and associated biases are determined
as error states directly in the filter. The indirect method is also commonly known
as the ”one-step approach” compared to the many steps used in the direct method.
However, the performance of the indirect method is highly dependent on the precise
tuning of the Kalman filter parameters, leading to the method being more diﬀicult
to use than the direct approach. In this study, the first objective was to implement
the direct method, with a performance that is on par with the quality of the indirect
method. The reference profile analysis used, showed that the implementation shown
in this project can produce gravity disturbance profiles with a high enough precision
and repeatability to qualify for this.

6.3.2
Tuning of Parameters
As explained in Section 3.4 a set of parameters can be tuned to optimize the perfor-
mance of the Kalman implementations. The three main parameters are the standard
deviation on the measurement update, the expected standard deviation on the gravity
disturbance profile, and the along-track correlation of the gravity disturbance. By ad-
justing one or more of these parameters significantly different results can be produced.
This also means that to obtain the best possible result, a tuning of the three parame-
ters is needed. Shown in Figure 5.20, are the results of the tuning process. The tuning
process is a very simple implementation where each parameter is tuned independently
from the others, and a start guess for the three parameters is implemented based on
values used in implementations of the indirect method [Jensen, Nielsen, et al., 2019].
Due to the simplicity of the solution compared to the complexity of the problem, this
solution will cause induced errors, as the parameters will be determined wrongly. A
more stable way of solving the optimization problem would be to optimize all three
parameters at once and use this multi-space to determine a common minimum. It
was decided in the process to use a simple implementation due to the possible gain vs.
time spent on the problem. Only small improvements were observed before and after
the tuning process, and no difference was seen in the high-frequency noise, on the
reference profiles. A further optimization of the parameters might solve the problem,
but it was decided to be out of the time frame for this project.

gravity measurements is performed, using tie points at the start and end of the
survey, this linear correction is based on a least squares solution. Now by introducing

6.3.4.2
Introduction of Unknown Bias
Due to missing information on the test setup in the ATR-42 plane, an unknown bias
is introduced in the Kalman based hybridization of the GIRAFE gravity disturbance
estimates and the direct method estimates. In the direct method the leverarm be-
tween the GNSS antenna and the IMU was introduced. To derive gravity disturbance
estimates from GIRAFE the leverarm from the GNSS to the GIRAFE gravimeter is
introduced. But the leverarm between the IMU and the GIRAFE gravimeter is not
known. By this, an unknown (small) bias is introduced in the Kalman filter results.
Furthermore, as the GIRAFE instrument is gyro-stabilized, there might be a discrep-
ancy between the downward direction of the two instruments, even with the correction
of the frames in the direct method.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This project was essentially divided into three sub-projects. An implementation of
the direct method to derive gravity disturbance from a direct strapdown setting. An
investigation of the hybridization and basic theory behind the GIRAFE quantum
gravimeter, and the hybridization method to derive absolute gravity measurements.
Lastly an exploit into the possibilities of using the Kalman filter framework in re-
lation to the direct method and the possible hybridization with GIRAFE absolute
measurements.
The first main goal of the project was the implementation of the direct method in
direct strapdown airborne gravimetry. A verification method was introduced based
on repeated reference profiles and the results shown in Section 5.1. With the introduc-
tion of a small pre-filtering and evaluating rotations based on the 300 [Hz] attitude
controls, repeatability of the gravity disturbance estimates over the reference profiles,
was determined with a very low RMSE of 0.69 [mGal] using a traditional used Butter-
worth filter with a FWHM of 110 [s]. The stability of the gravity disturbance results
were shown to be significantly more stable in high energy environments with the in-
troduction of the pre-filtering compared to an implementation without. The impact
of the pre-filtering is as explained, a small unknown induced bias in the spatial res-
olution computation for the results. The gravity disturbance estimates were further
compared to the EGM2008 model estimates, where the overall trend was observed to
be the same, but with an expected offset (of ≈40 [mGal]), due to the relative mea-
surements from the IMU. A small offset between the reference profiles was shown,
with the cause of this discussed in Section 6, but with no clear hypothesis for the
root problem (The same offset is also observed in the GIRAFE quantum gravimeter
estimations). These results show that the method is implemented successfully and
can deliver results on par with similar verification tests using the indirect method.
In the second part of the project, the basic theory behind quantum gravimetry was in-
vestigated. A test implementation of the hybridization between the quantum gravime-
ter and the associated classical gravimeter was tested to constrain the output from
the GIRAFE gravimeter. Data from the case survey was provided by ONERA along
with their final gravity disturbance results both non-filtered and lowpass filtered at
three different FWHM’s values. A method for deriving gravity disturbance from the
GIRAFE measurements was implemented, and the provided gravity disturbance es-
timates were used as a reference solution.
This can be seen in Section 5.2.
The

comparison of the implemented hybridization method and the ONERA provided re-
sults, showed a clear trend and bias in the residuals in the range of some 100 [mGal].
Furthermore, by comparing the RMSE of the reference profiles, it was determined
that the implemented method for deriving gravity disturbance is significantly less
stable with changing FWHM value, compared to the ONERA’s solution. For further
use of the GIRAFE gravity disturbance estimates, it was therefore decided that the
provided results were used. As the main goal for this part of the project, was to gain
knowledge about the workings of quantum gravimetry, and not necessarily implement
a fully working hybridization method, no more time was used on the subject.
The last main goal of the project was an exploit into the possibilities of using the
Kalman filter structure to combine the results from the direct method with the new
quantum gravity measurements. In total three different Kalman based filters were
implemented in the project, based upon the Extended Kalman Filter, using the error
states implementation instead of the total states. This essentially results in modelling
of the error on the gravity disturbance results, such that the Kalman filters ”act” like
a traditional lowpass filter. The main goal for the first implementation was to show
that a Kalman based lowpass filter, could provide a result similar to the tradition-
ally used Butterworth filter. Furthermore, this basic implementation should act as
the basis for the more complex implementations. The results were promising, as the
overall structures of the gravity disturbance signal were recovered by the Kalman
lowpass filter. However as discussed, some high-frequency noise with low amplitude
also propagated through the filtering. A tuning of the parameters for the filter was
performed, using a simple method, due to a benefit vs. time spent on problem anal-
ysis. A small difference in the high-frequency noise was observed after the tuning
of the parameters. The lowpass Kalman filter implementation yielded a RMSE of
0.78 [mGal] at a spatial resolution computed to 9.58 ± 0.9 [Km]. This is on par with
the results shown using the Butterworth lowpass filter, but with the additional noise
considered, a more thorough tuning of the Kalman filter is needed.
Building upon the Kalman based lowpass filter, tie values were introduced as mea-
surement updates to the filter. This meant the addition of a bias term in the error
state vector, which was used to model the drift of the IMU derived gravity distur-
bance estimates. The implementation showed the possibility of introducing tie values
directly in the lowpass filtering of the gravity disturbance estimates, but as discussed,
a discrepancy between the start and end location for the case survey means that the
relative measurements of the IMU can not directly be used as tie values. The test
implementation shown here, gave a similar result to the Kalman lowpass filter, with
a RMSE of 0.79 [mGal] at the same spatial resolution. The bias term is intended to
be modelled as a linear trend, which is also observed to be true in the results.
The last Kalman filter implementation is a further expansion of the use of measure-
ment updates. Instead of tie values, gravity disturbance estimates from the GIRAFE
quantum gravimeter is used as measurement updates to the filter structure. This
essentially means that the direct strapdown results are corrected from relative to ab-

This short section includes short descriptions of suggestions for a continuation of

Regarding the direct method, an implementation of deriving the horizontal compo-

different survey, preferably a grid survey would give more opportunities to investigate
the stability of the implementation, through a cross-over point analysis. Furthermore,

deriving gravity disturbance estimates from GIRAFE measurements, needs some fine-
