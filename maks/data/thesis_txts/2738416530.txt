Abstract
To enhance the process of automating airplane deicing. This thesis presents a machine­
learning approach for generating complete and contextualized 3D representations of air­
planes from partial LiDAR scans by predicting the entire geometry of an aircraft and provid­
ing semantic information about its different parts, such as wings, body, tail, and turbines.
The proposed solution integrates deep learning techniques to streamline the aircraft scan­
ning part of the autonomous deicing process, thus improving its efficiency.
This study investigates two primary approaches to semantic point cloud completion: a
sequential model using separate networks for point cloud completion and segmentation
(PoinTr+Next), and a unified model that combines both tasks within a single network
(PoinTrSeg). I modified the PoinTr architecture by incorporating skip connections to en­
hance completion performance and introduced a novel segmentation head to facilitate
simultaneous completion and segmentation. I evaluated these models using synthetic
data from the ShapeNet and ShapeNetPart datasets and real­world airplane scans.
The results indicate that the PoinTrSeg model achieves comparable completion quality
to the sequential approach while significantly improving segmentation accuracy.
The
PoinTrSeg model also demonstrates a 35% faster inference time, making it more suit­
able for real­time applications. However, both models exhibit limitations when applied
to real­world data, highlighting the challenge of bridging the gap between synthetic and
real­world environments.
This thesis contributes to the field by demonstrating the feasibility and advantages of
integrating point cloud completion and segmentation into a single model, enhancing per­
formance and efficiency. It provides a foundation for further research and development
in real­time, robust 3D point cloud processing for various applications.

Introduction
This thesis explores the capabilities of Artificial Inteligence (AI) to generate complete and
contextualized 3D representations of objects given their partial view. More specifically,
given a partial 3D scan of an airplane, this project proposes to use machine learning to
predict its entire geometry while giving it semantic information about its different parts,
such as wings, body, tail, and turbine.
This chapter will overview the problem that motivated this project and its goals. Finally, I
will outline the structure of this thesis.
1.1
Problem Formulation ­ Autonomous Airplane Deicing
The aviation industry is crucial in modern society, facilitating global travel, commerce,
and connectivity. Both passenger and cargo air traffic have shown steady growth over
recent years, with figures nearing pre­pandemic levels and expected to continue rising in
the future [1]. As the number of flights grows, ensuring that aircraft can operate safely in
various weather conditions becomes increasingly vital.
Airplane flight conditions are extremely sensitive to surface irregularities. These irregu­
larities can severely impact flight performance by disturbing the smooth airflow through
the plane’s surface, adding more weight to the aircraft, interfering with control surfaces
such as flight spoilers and wing flaps, or coming loose during flight, which can damage
the engines and the airframe.
One cause for these irregularities is the accumulation of frost, ice, or snow in the plane.
These can even occur when the ambient temperature is above freezing via a process
known as cold soaking. In cold soaking, the fuel inside the aircraft is so low that it can
lower the wing’s temperature to below the freezing point of water. When water comes in
contact with it from either condensation or rain, it will create a frost layer in the airframe.
Several aircraft accidents have been attributed to icing­related issues where, in most of
these, the frost was responsible for the airplane to stall [2]. These accidents can be fatal,
so removing all frost from the aircraft before the take­off via ground deicing is extremely
important.
There are several methods of deicing an aircraft, such as using hot water or infrared
heating. Still, most commonly, a deicing fluid based on propylene glycol or ethylene glycol,
which freezes at a lower temperature than water, is sprayed into the aircraft just before
take­off. This process involves piloting a specially designed truck to the side of the airplane
and manually spraying the deicing fluid in the ice­affected zones.
Since this is a very season­specific job and the deicing vehicle has some distinctive con­
trols, new operators must be trained yearly to do this work. Because of that, companies
have been trying to develop a system that allows trucks to deice airplanes autonomously.
The Vestergaard company, together with IPU and DTU, are developing a platform for
autonomously deicing trucks.
The autonomous deicing process can be divided into three parts (Fig. 1.1):
• Navigation: where the autonomous truck navigates the airport apron, avoids colli­
sions, and approaches the airplane with the best angle to spray it.

• Spraying: where the best movement of the truck’s arm and spray nozzle is calcu­

This thesis will focus on the scanning step of the pipeline.
1.1.1
Airplane scanning
In the current approach for scanning the airplane, the truck has to rotate a couple of
degrees side­to­side so that the Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) scan can capture
many points of the aircraft. A Simultaneous localization and Mapping (SLAM) [3] algorithm
is used while the truck moves to create an accurate and denoised aircraft mapping. The
generated point cloud is then aligned and matched to different aircraft types from a pre­
defined database of 3D airplanes [4]. With this alignment, the system knows the location
of the aircraft and the position of the area that needs to be spayed.
This approach has some drawbacks: scanning the airplane is time­consuming, and mov­
ing the truck very close to the aircraft always comes with the risk of collision; the positioning
of the sensor can impact the mapping. For instance, if the sensor is behind the truck’s
arm, there will always be a blind spot in the scanning, or if the sensor ends up too close
to the aircraft, there might be some spots missing in the scans. All of this can impact
the alignment of the database’s aircraft, which is very sensitive to noise and poor initial
scans. Also, this approach requires a database of 3D airplane models that will need to
be frequently updated in case new aircraft models are introduced, making the algorithm’s
matching step slower.
Therefore, I propose to utilize a deep learning system to reconstruct the aircraft, given the
partial view obtained from the truck. The system would also give semantic information
about the generated points, substituting the need for matching and aligning the aircraft
type. This could make the system faster at recognizing and understanding the aircraft it
is looking at and less prone to errors in the case of a poor scan. Figure 1.2 shows how
this system’s input and output would look.

1.2
Research Questions
During the implementation of the proposed solution, the following research questions will
be discussed:
• How can deep learning be utilized to complete partial point clouds? This ques­
tion examines the methodologies for effectively inputting and outputting point clouds
in neural networks. It seeks to identify which architectures are best suited for this
task and the specific challenges involved in processing partial point clouds.
• How can semantic information be integrated into the solution? This question
investigates whether a separate model is required for segmentation or whether a
single model can simultaneously output segmented and completed point clouds. It
explores the potential advantages of integrating these tasks and whether incorpo­
rating a segmentation loss improves the overall completion performance.
• How well does a model train on synthetic data perform on real­world data?
This question evaluates the generalizability of models trained exclusively on syn­
thetic data. It seeks to determine if these models are usable in real­world applica­
tions and whether including real­world data in the training set enhances performance
and robustness.

Related Work
Point clouds possess unique characteristics, such as being unordered and having varied
sizes, which make them challenging to work with using neural networks compared to other
types of data with intuitive tensor representations. Despite these challenges, several
works have addressed these issues and developed various methods for applying deep
learning to point clouds.
2.1
Classification and Segmentation
Classification involves taking the entire point cloud as input and producing a single output
indicating the object the point cloud represents, such as identifying whether it represents
an airplane or a chair. On the other hand, segmentation aims to classify each point in
the point cloud individually. For example, in the case of an airplane, segmentation would
identify which points represent the wings, turbines, tail, etc. Figure 2.1 shows the input
and output of both a classification and a segmentation network.
Figure 2.1: Representation of point cloud classification and segmentation networks input
and outputs.
One of the first architectures specifically designed to handle point clouds was PointNet
[5], which respects the unordered nature of point clouds and is flexible with their size by
using shared Multi­Layer Perceptron (MLP) and a max pooling layer to achieve a global
feature representation. PointNet revolutionized point cloud processing by introducing a
method that directly operates on raw point sets, without requiring voxelization or other
forms of preprocessing that could lead to information loss. However, PointNet’s architec­
ture struggled to capture local structures due to its global feature extraction approach.
To address this limitation, PointNet++ [6] was developed as an enhancement, utilizing
multiple PointNet networks at different scales within the point cloud to learn hierarchical
features and accommodate non­uniform densities. In other words, it applies PointNet
recursively on nested partitions of the input point set, capturing fine geometric details
more effectively.
More recently, PointNeXt [7] further iterated on the PointNet++ architecture by improving
training strategies and introducing new layers, significantly enhancing its performance.
PointNeXt incorporated state­of­the­art techniques such as residual connections and ad­
vanced normalization methods, improving accuracy and robustness in classification and
segmentation tasks.

The Point Cloud Transformer (PCT) [8] introduces the novel transformer architecture to
the problem of point clouds. It uses attention layers that enable the input to gain infor­
mation within itself to capture the point cloud’s local and global context more efficiently.
The transformer was initially designed for natural language processing, but it has proven
effective in handling point cloud data by leveraging self­attention mechanisms to model
the relationships between points. This allows PCT to dynamically focus on different parts
of the point cloud, improving its ability to capture complex structures.
Similarly, SPoTr [9] designed a global cross­attention mechanism for point clouds that im­
proves the scalability of the network by only needing to compute the attention of a small
subset of self­positioning points. This reduces the computational complexity and memory
usage, making it feasible to process larger point clouds. SPoTr’s innovative approach to
attention computation enables it to maintain high performance even with limited compu­
tational resources.
Another approach to using point clouds in deep learning is to use Graph Neural Network
(GNN) [10]. This method treats the points as nodes in a graph, creating edges based
on specific heuristics, such as Euclidean distance. The graph is usually represented as
an adjacency matrix to be used by a neural network. GNNs, such as Dynamic Graph
CNN (DGCNN) [11], dynamically update the graph structure during training to better cap­
ture local geometric features and relationships between points. DGCNN introduced edge
convolution operations that adaptively learn the neighborhood structure, allowing the net­
work to focus on meaningful geometric patterns.
AGCN [12] uses another approach to GNNs, which employs graph convolution within an
adversarial network to enhance point cloud segmentation. The AGCN framework consists
of a generator, which produces segmentations using attention­based graph convolutions
to capture local and global features, and a discriminator, which evaluates the quality of
these segmentations. This adversarial setup encourages the generator to improve its
outputs, leading to more accurate and robust segmentations, particularly in noisy or in­
complete data scenarios.
Other notable works in this domain include KPConv [13], which introduced kernel point
convolutions to perform convolution operations directly on point clouds. KPConv demon­
strated significant improvements in classification and segmentation tasks by leveraging
deformable kernels that adapt to the local geometry of the point cloud.
Furthermore, recent advancements like RandLA­Net [14] have focused on improving the
efficiency of point cloud processing. RandLA­Net utilizes random point sampling and
local feature aggregation to reduce computational complexity while maintaining high ac­
curacy. Its lightweight architecture makes it suitable for real­time applications and large­
scale point cloud datasets.
In summary, point cloud classification and segmentation have seen significant advance­
ments driven by advancements in neural network architectures and the increasing avail­
ability of data and computing power. These developments have led to substantial im­
provements in accuracy and performance over the years, enabling more effective and
efficient processing of complex 3D data for various applications.
For this work, I used PointNext as the main architecture for doing part segmentation for
its state­of­the­art results, availability, and ease of use of the code1. For this reason, the

2.1.1
PointNext
PointNext builds upon the work of PointNet++, which is a hierarchical network that applies
the PointNet model recursively at different scales. It works similarly to a Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN), where it extracts local features from small neighborhoods and
further groups the results into larger ones to produce higher­level features, allowing the
model to capture the local geometries of the point cloud effectively. Figure 2.2 shows the
model’s architecture. The local feature learner, PointNet, will be explained more in­depth
in Section 3.2
Figure 2.2: Architecture of the PointNet++ Network. Image taken from [6].
One of the critical improvements of PointNext is the introduction of the Inverted Resid­
ual MLP (InvResMLP). It consists of separable MLPs and a residual connection between
the input and output of the block to alleviate the vanishing gradients problem. These are
appended after every Set Abstraction layer to deepen the network without substantially
increasing the model’s complexity. The model also unifies the encoder part for classifica­
tion and segmentation tasks and introduces a symmetric decoder to obtain segmentation.
Figure 2.3 shows the architecture of PointNext and its differences from PointNet++.
Figure 2.3: Architecture of the PointNext Network. Image taken from [7].

2.2
Point Cloud Completion
Point cloud completion estimates the entire object or scene from its partial view. This
process reconstructs missing parts to form a complete model. This process is crucial
in many areas, such as 3D reconstruction, robotics, and augmented reality applications,
where obtaining a full representation from partial data is essential. Figure 2.4 illustrates
the input and output of a point cloud completion network.
Figure 2.4: Representation of the point cloud completion network’s input and output.
One of the earliest and most famous neural network architectures to tackle this problem
was the Point Completion Network (PCN) [15]. It consists of an encoder­decoder type
of architecture with a structure similar to PointNet, and the decoder consists of an MLP
to generate a coarse output point cloud. It then initializes a 2D grid, which the network
learns how to fold around the coarse point cloud into the desired 3D shape, effectively
filling in missing parts.
The transformer architecture is also being used in the completion problem. PointAttn [16]
designs two novel blocks: the Self­Feature Augment block, which leverages self­attention,
and the Geometric Details Perception block, which uses cross­attention to enhance the
detail in the generated point cloud. Similarly, PoinTr [17] and its successor AdaPoinTr
[18] also leverage the self­attention layer. Still, it integrates k­Nearest Neighbors (kNN)
inside the transformer block to capture geometric relations in the point cloud, significantly
improving the completion results.
The SA_Net [19] utilizes a similar strategy as PCN by folding 2D grids into the completed
object but makes this hierarchically. In other words, it has multiple folding layers running
sequentially. Skip connections are added between the encoder and the folding blocks,
allowing for better information flow and reconstruction accuracy.
More sophisticated methods have recently emerged to tackle dense 3D point cloud com­
pletion. For instance, RFNet [20] employs a Recurrent Forward Network comprising three
modules: the Recurrent Feature Extraction (RFE) module, the Forward Dense Comple­
tion (FDC) module, and the Raw Shape Protection (RSP) module. This approach itera­
tively refines the point cloud, preserving trustworthy information by symmetrization and
patch­wise upsampling, achieving high­fidelity dense point clouds.
ViPC [21] is another notable method that completes partial point clouds using complemen­
tary information from additional single­view images. This multi­modal approach leverages
8

visual cues to enhance the completion process, addressing occlusion challenges and
complex structures.
The comprehensive review by Fei et al. [22] highlights various state­of­the­art methods
in point cloud completion, including point­based, view­based, convolution­based, graph­
based, generative model­based, and transformer­based approaches. This review pro­
vides a detailed comparison of these methods and discusses their performance on differ­
ent datasets, offering valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of each approach.
For this work, I used PoinTr as the main architecture to complete the partial point clouds
for its state­of­the­art results, availability, and ease of use of the code2. For this reason,
the following subsection will explain how it works in more depth.
2.2.1
PoinTr
PoinTr is a model that reformulates the process of point cloud completion as a set­to­set
translation problem. It adopts transformers in an encoder­decoder architecture, which
makes it easier for the model to learn global and local structural knowledge. The input
point cloud is represented as a set of unordered groups of points with position embeddings
converted into a sequence of point proxies representing local regions of the point cloud
by computing the furthest point sample (FPS) to locate a fixed number of point centers
and extracting the feature of the point centers from the input point cloud. These are used
as input for the transformer blocks. The overall architecture of the model can be seen in
Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Architecture of the PoinTr Network. Image taken from [17].
This model primarily aims to leverage transformers’ impressive sequence­to­sequence
generation ability for the point cloud completion problem. PoinTr models a set­to­set
translation task as an analogy to language translation, where instead of translating a se­
quence of words into another sequence, it aims to translate a set of points into another
set of points. This is done by generating the missing point proxies of the point cloud given
the point proxies of the partial input cloud. So, the network is modeled as:
V = ME(F), H = MD(Q, V)
(2.1)
where ME and MD are the encoder and the decoder respectively, V are the output fea­
tures of the encoder, H are the predicted point proxies, and Q are the dynamic queries of
the decoder. These dynamic queries serve as an initial estimate for the predicted proxies.
They are dynamically created by passing the encoder output by a query generator mod­
ule. More specifically, the decoder output V goes through a linear projection followed by
a max pooling to generate a global feature of the object. Then, this is linearly projected

Point cloud semantic completion is a combination of both previous methods. It estimates

One of the prominent networks for SSC is the Semantic Point Completion Network (SPC­

cess point clouds. The Observed Point Encoder (OP­Encoder) extracts features from
observed points, while the Observed to Occluded Point Decoder (O2OP­Decoder) maps

processing power. To enhance performance, the Image­Point Fused SPCNet (IPF­SPCNet)

JS3C­Net [27] is another approach that jointly learns semantic segmentation and scene

enhancing the overall feature representation and improving the performance of both tasks.
SCPNet [28] presents a solution with a multi­path design to maintain sparsity and avoid

semantic knowledge from a multi­frame teacher model to a single­frame student model,
achieving high­quality semantic and geometric representations.
CasFusionNet [29] adopts a cascaded approach with multiple levels of global completion,
semantic segmentation, and local refinement. Each level consists of a global completion
module (GCM), a semantic segmentation module (SSM), and a local refinement module
(LRM). This coarse­to­fine strategy progressively refines the completion and segmenta­
tion results, providing detailed and accurate reconstructions by leveraging dense feature
fusion across the network.
The S3CNet [25] efficiently processes large­scale outdoor scenes by leveraging sparse
tensors and dynamic voxel fusion. This network uses sparse convolutional layers built
on the Minkowski Engine to handle high­dimensional spaces efficiently. Dynamic voxel
fusion densifies reconstructed scenes using 2D semantic Bird’s Eye View (BEV) maps,
enabling real­time processing in complex environments by focusing on significant areas
and reducing unnecessary computations.

Technical Background
In this chapter, I will explain some technical concepts, primarily related to deep learning,

3.1
Deep Learning
Machine­learning­powered solutions are becoming increasingly present in our everyday
lives. These systems are used in various applications, from identifying objects in images
to social media recommendation algorithms and AI assistants. Deep learning is a subset
of machine learning that focuses on neural networks with multiple layers, where the deep
comes in the name. It is a representation­learning method where raw data is fed to the
model, and it automatically finds a type of representation that can be used for the task
at hand. These layers usually comprise simple non­linear modules that each transform
the input into a more complex, higher­level representation. With this in mind, composing
various modules in sequence can make the network learn complex functions.
The key aspect of deep learning is that humans do not design these feature layers; they
are learned from data using a general­purpose learning procedure. Most commonly, this
is done through supervised learning, where, during training, for a certain input, the model’s
output is compared to what we would want the model’s result to be, what is called ground
truth. This comparison is made via an objective function, also called a loss function, that
measures the distance between the model’s output and the desired result. The machine
then uses this distance to adjust its internal parameters in a direction that would reduce
its error.
The network’s weights are adjustable parameters that define the function computed by
the neural network. For instance, if you have a model with only one parameter and no
non­linearities, it would function as a linear equation in the form of:
ax = y
(3.1)
where x is the input, y is the output, and a is the network parameter, also called weight,
which will be adjusted during training to fit the training data. A typical deep­learning model
may have hundreds of millions of these parameters, with the most recent language models
having up to trillions of parameters.
Deep learning models typically use stochastic gradient descent (SGD) [30], an algorithm
that adjusts the model’s weights so that the network actually learns from training. It works
by taking an average of the objective function, which can be seen as a landscape in a
high­dimensional space of weight values, and using backpropagation [31] to compute the
gradient vector that indicates how to adjust each weight to increase the error. Taking the
negative of this gradient vector will indicate the direction of the steepest descent in the
landscape in relation to the current position. By doing this iteratively, the model will learn
the best parameters for accurately performing the task for which it was trained.
There are many ways of building a neural network, but most commonly, it will be a junction
of existing modules or layers well­established in machine learning. Next, I will explain
some of the layers this project uses in more detail.

Transformers

Point clouds in Deep Learning
Until now, I have only discussed structured data, such as images and audio, as input
for these deep­learning models. However, point clouds are unstructured, meaning they
do not have a regular grid structure and are order invariant. Because of that, they don’t
have a natural representation that deep­learning models can easily exploit. Furthermore,
another challenge of working with point clouds is their density variations, where, in certain
areas, the density of points is significantly higher than in others. This is especially true
for LiDAR scans, where closer objects will have a higher density of points than farther
objects.

One approach to making point clouds easy to use in deep learning is to divide the space
into small cubes called voxels, which can be considered 3D pixels. This makes it pos­
sible to use 3D CNNs that can easily capture spatial information. One problem with this
approach is the high memory requirement for fine voxel resolutions. Also, when turning
the space into voxels, depending on the resolution, a lot of information can be lost, or
most of the voxels will carry no information because they do not have any points inside
them. For these reasons, approaching point clouds as voxel grids is not computationally
and memory efficient.
Another approach is to turn the point cloud into a graph, with the points as the nodes and
the edges representing the spatial relationships between them. This makes it possible to
apply convolution over each point’s k­nearest neighbors, effectively capturing local struc­
tures in the point cloud. It is also permutation invariant, which is perfect for point clouds.
Even though it has shown great promise, this approach is computationally intense.
One of the most famous approaches was pioneered by PointNet [5], where the raw 3D
coordinates can be directly input into the model. This is done by passing all points through
the same MLP, called shared MLP, which will create a feature vector for every point, re­
sulting in a tensor of shape (np, nf), where np is the number of input points, and nf is the
feature vector’s size. In practice, the shared MLP is simply a 1D convolution where the
(1x3) sized kernel slides in the dimension of the points. After that, a max pooling oper­
ation is calculated so that only the nf dimension is left, creating a global feature vector
independent of the number of input points. Figure 3.6 shows the architecture of PointNet,
which illustrates how these operations are used to classify or segment a point cloud. This
is a simple but effective strategy, although it can struggle to capture local information from
the point cloud.
In the case of Transformers, one of the approaches above is usually used to create a
feature vector of the point cloud, which can then be used as input to the attention layer.

goes through a series of shared MLPs, where it generates the segmentation value for
each point.

6
Conclusion
This thesis explores the use of machine learning to generate complete and contextualized
3D representations of airplanes given a partial view from a LiDAR scanner. The proposed
solution aims to enhance the autonomous deicing process by providing the system with
precise spatial and contextual information about the aircraft to be de­iced.
This study compared two approaches: the first utilized two neural networks, one for com­
pleting the point cloud and the other for segmenting it; the second combined completion
and segmentation into a single neural network. Both approaches were evaluated using
synthetic data and real­world partial scans of airplanes.
The key achievements of this thesis are summarized as follows:
Firstly, this thesis analyzed the addition of skip connections in PoinTr’s encoder­decoder
architecture, which improved the base model’s performance from 1.09 to 0.9 average
Chamfer Distance on object completion. However, this increase was mainly achieved
because of the increase in the query vector from 96 to 128.
Secondly, this thesis introduced a novel segmentation head, which can be integrated with
a point­completion model. This was achieved by leveraging the global feature calculated
from the encoder­decoder part of the network, which encodes valuable information for
segmentation. The k­nearest neighbors (KNN) algorithm was also introduced to generate
ground truth for the segmentation of the completed cloud, allowing for the use of any
segmentation loss function.
Thirdly, this thesis conducted quantitative and qualitative comparisons between the two
approaches to semantic completion. It was found that completing and segmenting in a
single model had minimal impact on the quality of the object’s reconstruction but signifi­
cantly improved segmentation quality. Additionally, a runtime comparison revealed that
the single model is 35% faster, making it more suitable for real­time applications.
Finally, this thesis evaluated both approaches on real­world airplane scans. The model
demonstrated decent completion when the input partial cloud had high aircraft coverage,
representing nearly half of the airplane. However, performance was subpar when the
input cloud had low coverage. This can be attributed to the exclusive training on synthetic
data, which makes the models less precise when applied to real­world data with higher
variance.
In conclusion, this project demonstrated the technical feasibility of merging point cloud
completion and segmentation into a single deep learning model. This approach produced
significant improvements in the segmentation of reconstructed point clouds while also
being faster than using two separate networks, making it more suitable for on­device
applications.

For future work, several directions can be pursued:
• Investigate ways to reduce memory usage and processing demands associated with
the segmentation loss.
