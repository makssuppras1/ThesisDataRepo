Abstract 
To protect the environment, the CO2 emissions from the building industry, as one of the main 
contributors to global pollution, ought to be reduced. Research and development have focused 
on alternatives to cement use by investigating traditional ways of building, with earth 
constructions at the center of this discussion. One form called adobe bricks are traditional 
unbaked clay bricks, which are known for their low embodied energy, thermal efficiency, and 
recyclability. The aim of this study seeks to further improve the durability of the material using 
compaction and calcium-based stabilization of the clay.  
 
The yellow clay used is defined as a low-plasticity and low organic-matter Clay Loam. Its 
characteristics are analyzed through geotechnical investigations such as particle density, 
organic matter, pH, grain size distribution, liquid and plastic limit, and mineral composition. 
With this information, optimal compaction of the clay is investigated. This is obtained by the 
addition of 25% of sand by weight and a water content close to the plastic limit of the clay of 
0,28 water to binder ratio. These proportions result in a strong unstabilized and air-dried earth 
material showing load-bearing potential for indoor and protected outdoor use.  
 
Three different chemical stabilization techniques are studied with variable proportions of water 
content and calcium-based agents: wood-ash, calcium carbonate, and gypsum. The chemical 
reactions of the stabilizations are discussed through mineral investigation with XRD scans. It 
is concluded that the wood-ash and gypsum agents agglomerate within the clay matrix when 
hydrated and do not react with the low reactive clay structure. The mechanical characteristics 
of the stabilized samples are analyzed for shrinkage, density, compression strength, 
hygrothermal performance and water weathering. The wood-ash stabilizer formed ettringite 
when hydrated, which distressed the clay matrix and resulted in compromising the sample 
characteristics analyzed. The calcium carbonate stabilized samples showed minor reactions 
resulting in a similar material behavior as the unstabilized clay samples. The hydrated gypsum 
created strong agglomerates within the clay matrix, which improved all tested characteristics 
and resulted in a strong and durable stabilized earth material with load-bearing capacity and 
outdoor use potential. Additional to improving the soils characteristics, stabilizing clays with 
agents like gypsum are proven to be a way to standardize and reduce the inconsistencies in 
soils for potential use in the building industry.

1. Introduction 
Environmental protection and sustainable development are, now more than ever, of global 
importance. The construction industry is currently one of the major contributors to the 
degradation of the environment. It is contributing to approximately 30% of the global CO2 
emissions and has concrete as one of the most used building materials. [39] This realization 
leads to a lot of research outputs around sustainable and circular new building techniques. [1] 
 
This analysis looks back at one of the oldest construction materials known to humans, 
traditional earth constructions, to be inspired by the ancient building techniques using simple 
resources that are directly derived from nature: raw soil, straw, water and wood. The aim is to 
improve on these materials through research and development by achieving more stable 
versions that fulfill the contemporary building requirements. This paper will analyze the 
characteristics of adobe bricks and potential improvements of its properties by mechanical and 
chemical stabilization of the soil. The focus is thus primarily on the matrix of the clay and the 
experimental research will not include fibers or straw. 
 
Adobe bricks are unbaked earth bricks, which are sustainably very interesting due to their low 
embodied energy, thermal efficiency, and recyclability. However, the unbaked earth material 
has some weaknesses regarding its moisture absorption, weathering, shrinkage cracking and 
unstable structure. To counteract these disadvantages, earth constructions are often stabilized 
by the addition of a stabilizing agent or by a mechanical compaction technique. Incorporating 
a calcium-based stabilizing admixture to the soils has been used since thousands of years, with 
lime as one of the oldest agents known. [40] These techniques are used to benefit the durability 
by increasing the compressive strength, decreasing the porosity and weathering, and control 
the swell and shrinkage. [6,11,39] 
 
Currently, the most commonly used stabilizing agents are cements or modified cementitious 
materials for its lime content and additional pozzolan reactants. [40]  It has been proven that 
the production of cement contributes to around 8% of the global CO2 emissions. [2] Moreover, 
production of cement also releases significant amounts of waste product named cement kiln 
dust (CKD), which contributes to respiratory and pollution health risks. [3] Therefore, state of 
the art research is focusing on new building materials with alternatives to cement use.

In this thesis, an attempt to replace the use of cement in clays is investigated by the addition of 
different calcium-based agents, and the benefits of the binders in earth bricks are studied.

commercial name used when it is excavated from a clayey earth layer. To portray the real 
definition of the soil and determine its characteristics, the experimental work starts with a

section.

2. Background 
2.1. Earth Constructions 
Going back to traditional ways of constructing, earth was used as the main source for building 
materials. The good cohesion in the soil allows for forming of the desired shape because of the 
presence of clay acting as a natural binder. [4]  This research focusses on the experimental 
improvement of a particular unfired earth construction method: Adobe bricks.   
 
Adobe bricks are historically sun-baked earth bricks made by molding malleable mud, most 
often with the addition of straw. [4]  Adobes are consisting of two primary materials each 
providing their purpose; soil and natural fiber. Both raw materials are usually sourced locally 
where the construction itself is built. Because the bricks are sun-dried (or air-dried) instead of 
oven-baked, the carbon footprint of processing these traditional bricks are close to zero. [6, 11] 
 
There has been various research made on adobe bricks and possibilities to improve them to 
increase their durability. Interesting findings that have already been published will be used as 
background research conducting this thesis research. The following sections and tables 
summarize important considerations about the raw soil properties, fibers, stabilization, 
molding, drying and post-treatment from existing research on earth constructions. 
2.2. Soil 
The soil or mud excavated out of the ground, is commonly categorized into different groups 
depending on the texture of the soil. Texture expresses the grain size distribution of the soil 
littparticles, and this gives the soil different characteristics when used in any application. 
Minerals have been divided into the following main grain fractions, Gravel (20-2mm), Sand 
(2-0,06mm), Silt (0,06-0,002mm), and Clay (<0,002mm), going from the largest to the smallest 
particle size respectively. The presence of clay in soil is of the utmost importance to bring 
cohesiveness and plasticity to the material, it will thus act as the binding agent of the matrix. [4]  
==PAGE:13==
 
4 
 
Figure 1:  Soil grain size distribution depicted on triangular grid, according to the US Soil Taxinomy. 
The beneficial plastic properties of clay are due to its different chemical composition and 
physical properties compared to the other grains. The very small grain size and elongated shape 
gives a greater contact surface increasing the contact with films of water linking to other grains. 
This cohesiveness is also due to the clay mineral’s complex hexagonal molecular structure. 
These molecular particles create strong bonds with polarized water molecules which then gives 
the cohesive property to the material. [4] 
 
To get the desired soil consistency and thus the desired properties of the brick and durability 
when dried, an ideal grain size distribution is preferable. This means that adequate quantities 
of sand, silt and clay are needed in the adobe mixture. A characteristic of clay is that it has 
important volumetric expansion in presence of water and shrinkage when drying. Since a 
construction is exposed to cycles of dry and humid climates, it is important to limit the amount 
of clay in the mixture to avoid disserving the durability of adobes due to volume fluctuation. [9] 
On the other hand, clay and to some extent, silt act as the binder of the soil. Thus, a necessary 
amount of clay and silt is needed to create a matrix that will bind the present sand particles 
together. The amount of gravel should be minimal as its larger particles cannot be bound by 
the clay and silt matrix due to their heavier weight. There remains a relatively wide range in 
acceptance of the grain sizes because originally, adobes are made from locally available soil 
resources which varies drastically from one location to another. [11]

2.3. Natural Fibers 
The addition of straw as a natural fiber into the material can bring some important benefits to 
adobe as a construction material. The primary reason for adding fibers into the soils, is that it 
reduces the shrinkage of the bricks when drying, it also improves the ductility when exposed 
to tensile stresses. [40] Adding a natural fiber also reduces the density and thus the weight of 
the material, this can be an advantage when thinking of building construction without 
mechanical help as the elements are usually large. Depending on the fiber ratio and its 
properties, a lower density of the material can result in a lower compressive strength, however 
if the raw material is very brittle and the natural or synthetic fibers are strong, the compressive 
strength can also be improved. [4]  
==PAGE:16==
 
7 
 
The most commonly used natural fiber in adobes is straw. Conveniently, straw is the most 
widely available biomass in Denmark, and retails at a cheap price. Thus, adding the straw can 
lower the material cost of the construction on top of the other advantages previously named. It 
is important to acknowledge that the use of straw can bring a few disadvantages as retention of 
water, rotting, a lower compressive strength and density depending on the straw ratio and more 
difficult brick molding. [5]  
 
It is also interesting to look at the availability of straw in Denmark, the actual resources of the 
country this material is investigated in. Currently, in Denmark, approximately 50% of straw 
from wheat, barley and other cereals are “left on field” in the agricultural biomass industry. 
Having a possibility to reuse this agricultural waste into the building industry shows several 
benefits from an economical and environmental point of view. [14]

2.4. Soil Stabilization 
To benefit the use of soil in practice, soils are commonly stabilized. Stabilization, referred to 
as a chemical process by adding a reacting agent, is performed on soil with the goal to improve 
the materials engineering properties as strength, reduced swell, durability and setting time. 
 [32]   It is common to use cement as a stabilizer in construction materials of soil. It can react 
in different ways with clay and sand generating different mineral structures. When the cement 
reacts with the sandy skeleton it creates a cement matrix during the curing phase which 
increases the compressive strength of the material significantly, while reducing the plasticity 
index. These results are certainly dependent on the grain size distribution as well as the amount 
of cement added. Generally, desired results are obtained withing a range of 4-12% of cement 
added to the soil. [45] It is also important to note that the ideal grain size distribution may 
differ when working with cement as a stabilizer. As for example, since the molecular bonds 
generated by cements are stronger than by clay, addition of gravel may increase the 
compressive strength. [4]  
 
Although the advantages of using cement as a stabilizer are undeniable, the goal of this analysis 
is to replace this additive by a more eco-responsible material. More sustainable solutions can 
be seen as biomaterials, industrial by-products, waste or recycled materials with the focus on 
circular use. Bio-ashes are formed from the combustion of biofuels, which are materials 
producing energy into power and heating by combustion. These fuels originate from biomass 
which are directly subtracted from nature; ex. straw, wood. [12]

longer and it may take place over months or years.” [32]

Ettringite and thaumasite formation is inevitable when 
working with soils which consist of high sulphate contents, 
as it is the case for high-clay soils. The degree of ettringite 
formation depends strongly on the availability of ions to react 
into the mineral. In contrast with ordinary cements, the ion 
availability is lower in soils, it thus takes a longer curing time 
and is sensitive to the climate the material reactions are 
taking place. Ettringite formation takes place in two phases, 
the first and non-damaging formation takes place 
instantaneously when the soil is mixed with the stabilizer and 
the second formation takes place during the curing time when 
the clay-matrix is already formed. This second mineral 
formation phase can lead to a deterioration of the material. 
To avoid the extent of these disadvantages, it is important to 
mix the clayey soils well in water in order to homogenize the 
distribution of the formed ettringite and thaumasite minerals.

2.5. Molding 
Uniform molding and good compaction are primordial for a good brick quality. Here, the water 
content of the soil is largely of importance. The presence of water helps mold the soil more 
easily but also plays a major role in determining the properties of the soil, depending on where 
it is molecularly present. It is important to know that water can be present at different molecular 
levels and this is related to the grain size distribution of the soil and especially to the types of 
minerals present. [4] Moreover, water content in the brick mixture has an important role in the 
compactibility of the bricks when molded and compressed. When the soil is well compacted 
during the molding, the properties of the dried brick will be improved. Properties improvements 
suggested are mechanical strength, but also reducing the shrinkage and swelling fluctuations.

homogenized by turning the cylinder upside down for 60 seconds. Then the measuring tool is

When the desired soil composition is homogenously mixed, the samples are molded into oiled 
forms of dimensions; 50x50x50mm that are made from hard plastic which avoids corrosion 
and improves the ease of demolding. The molding differs from sample to sample depending on 
the water content and thus the material’s plasticity of the mixture. The soil is hand compacted 
in three layers when low water content is present, and the material is poured when very high 
water contents are present in the soil. 
 
Two curing methods are used comparatively in this research. The first “dry curing” method 
consists of air drying the samples at ambient climate for the mentioned time. This climate was 
measured with average values for a temperature of 25,1±1 °C and a relative humidity of 
31,6±3 %. The second curing method is the “humid curing” where the samples are cured 
wrapped in plastic foil and placed in a climatic chamber at a temperature of 20°C and relative 
humidity of 85 %rh for the mentioned time. Both curing methods are applied on the 
unstabilized reference samples as a mean of comparison. The stabilized soils are treaded with 
the “humid curing” method in order to keep the desired hydration for the chemical reactions to 
take place.

To interrupt further hydration of the samples after the desired curing time, the samples are 
treated with a double solvent exchange. This method is commonly used to stop cement 
hydration by letting the water present evaporate quickly out of the material without altering the 
cement’s matrix. [23,24,25] From the prepared paste samples, 4-6 gram is subtracted out of 
the middle of the tube. This paste is immersed in isopropanol, with a ratio of 16,667 mL per 
gram soil. This substance is stirred vigorously for 30 seconds and then left for 5 minutes. The 
excess isopropanol is then poured out and the process is repeated once more with the same 
amounts. When the solution has rested for 5 minutes, it is then vacuum filtrated until dry. The 
soil residue is then soaked into diethyl with a ratio of 3,333 mL per gram soil. This mixture is 
again stirred for 30 seconds and left for 5 minutes. The solution is then vacuum filtrated until 
dry and the residue is moved to a glass plate. The soil is then placed in a ventilated oven at 
40°C for 8 minutes. The dry material is then crushed and sieved over a 63 𝜇𝑚 mesh and stored 
into sealed plastic containers.  The next day, the powders are compacted into metal molds ready 
for the XRD test.

5.3.4. Water Weathering  
The immersion test shows the very different behaviors of each material when exposed to direct 
water. The pure SL-1 soil, as predicted, but also the EW-1 material dissolved their outside layer 
fast. The highest erosion is observed in EW-1 where an average of 33,4% mass loss is 
measured. The undesirable EW-1 behavior is not assumed to be due to the addition of CaCO3, 
because this mineral is practically insoluble. However, it is suspected to be due to the higher 
clay content in that soil, which is more soluble in water. [41]  
 
The wood-ash stabilized samples lost the least mass of all samples. With increased wood-ash 
content a better performance in water is measured, proven with the SL-WA-2 sample which 
lost as little as 0,1% mass. A hypothesis for this effect is that when the wood-ash stabilized 
samples come back in contact with water, the wood-ash absorbs some water and develops 
further reactions while hydrated instead of dissolving. This can be the reason that the SL-WA-
2 samples with the highest WA content lost the least mass.  Furthermore, the gypsum stabilized 
samples, lost an average of 4,6% mass, which it did not affect the outer texture a lot, however 
the corners of the samples eroded the most by the water. This suggests that gypsum is less 
soluble than the clay and that it this way improved the stabilized sample against weathering.

6. Discussion  
6.1. Soil Characterization 
The decisive result of the soil characterization is to obtain optimum compaction and particle 
bonding. This way achieving the highest strength with little shrinkage and low porosity to 
ultimately obtain a highly durable material. 
 
Looking at the grain size distribution, for high strength by particle bonding a high clay 
percentage is needed, whereas coarser materials are needed to avoid shrinkage. [5] 
Furthermore, finer particles characterized by a higher surface area increases the electrostatic 
attraction and thus the possibility for chemical reactions in stabilized soils to occur, which 
increases strength development possibilities. [20] The SL soil characterized as a Clay Loam is 
considered to have the right distribution to attain a good balance between fine and coarse grain 
sizes with 27% clays, 40% silts and 33% sand. 
 
For adobes, the ideal liquid limit is suggested between 30 and 50% with a corresponding 
plasticity index between 15 and 30%. This means that the VG clay situates at the low 
recommended limits which are suitable for adobes and is identified as a low-plasticity clay. [4] 
The water content required to achieve the desired material consistency for molding, and thus 
also optimal compaction is strongly dependent on the PL and LL of the clay. As a general 
matter the water content for compaction must be between the plastic limit and the liquid limit 
of the soil material. [34] When wanting to compact the material in the most advantageous way, 
to improve strength and reduce shrinkage deformations, the water content must be lower, thus 
closest to the plastic index. The PL of the VG clay was measured at 18,8% and the LL at 34,0%. 
These values explain the good properties for the reference samples at 0,28w/b which is 
approximately 20%bw water content. An additional consideration for the stabilized samples is 
that the water needed to achieve the right consistency for molding is much higher. This is due 
to the addition of a percentage of very small ash particles that require a higher water to binder 
ratio.  
 
Moreover, in pure soils, the high presence of organic matter is characterized by a higher 
porosity in the dried material which reduces the strength and the compactibility. [17]  Overall, 
a lower organic matter is desired for adobe application. The SL soil showed the lowest loss of 
==PAGE:68==
 
59 
ignition with 2,4%, compared to the VG and EW soil. This is because it is mixed with sand, 
which has a very low LoI and low content of organic matter. 
 
For all the reasons above, is why the SL soil was chosen to be most suitable soil in the stabilized 
experiments. The SL soil also ultimately showed good characteristics in strength, shrinkage, 
hygrothermal performance and surface appearance, with a good compaction and without 
chemical stabilization.

which affected the samples in different ways. The hydrated wood-ash in the WA stabilized 
samples formed ettringite in between the clay matrix. Having an overall view of the results, it 
is noticed that the formed ettringite deteriorated the clay quality due to pressure on the clay 
structure, which resulted in undesired material properties. For the gypsum stabilized samples, 
the hydrated gypsum agglomerated when mixed with water and quickly created strong bonds 
between the clay structure. The hydrated gypsum benefitted the results for all the sample 
properties and decreased the setting time when molded, which suggests that the hydrated 
gypsum did not distress the clay bonds. Finally, the calcium carbonate stabilized samples 
simply increased in calcite content but did not show improvements in most properties, showing 
similar strength and density as the unstabilized samples. 
 
The lack of reaction between the stabilizer and the clay, can be due to the lack of available 
water, undesired curing conditions or too high organic matter, however it is very likely to be 
due to the clay’s mineralogical properties. [17]  Clays are generally known to have a low 
reactivity due to its crystalline microstructure which makes stabilization more difficult. The 
microstructure is composed of an arrangement of lamellas with clay minerals including 
tetrahedral silicate and octahedral aluminate which is generally electrically negative. The 
adjacent layers are linked together by strong hydrogen and cation bonds making a very stable 
and neutral structure. [38] 
 
Figure 48: Structure of the three most common clay minerals. [5] 
                          
 
Figure 49: Lamellar structure of clay minerals. [5] 
To improve the reactivity of clays, this rigid microstructure is transformed into an amorphous 
structure. This can be done by different methods, using an acid, thermal or mechanical 
==PAGE:70==
 
61 
treatment. The thermal treatment is widely used and called calcination of clay. Here the 
material is heated up from 400°C till 900°C and undergoes dehydration, dihydroxylation, and 
high temperature reactions. This thermal treatment results in a collapse of the crystal structure, 
thus a more amorphous structure which is more reactive. The mechanical treatment is an 
alternative that avoids involving chemicals or high temperatures. It is based on intense grinding 
of the clay between two solid surfaces to break up and alter the microstructure. [38]

to acknowledge that the raw yellow clay used in this analysis is a very strong material on its 
own when it is only mechanically stabilized, with the right water content and molding method.  
It is proven with the reference experiments where compressive strengths of 4,5 MPa were 
achieved.

additive. [5] However, from the discussion on stabilization in Section 6.2, it is concluded that 
the compressive strength of the final material is the result of the strength of the bonds that the 
stabilizers create themselves, and how much they distress the clay bonds by exerting pressure 
on them. [20,22] The most critical results were analyzed on the wood-ash stabilized samples 
as the lowest compressive strength and density was observed. The pure hydrated wood-ash 
samples showed a gain in strength after 28 days of curing, for which Nina Sigvardsen observed 
more strength gain up till 90 days of curing. [13] On the wood-ash stabilized clay samples, the 
opposite is observed with strength and density loss over a longer curing time. It can thus be 
assumed that the ettringite formed by the wood-ash majorly interrupts the clay matrix and is 
also much weaker than the clay bonds on a short curing time.  
 
Important to note is that, from comparing literature and the experimental studies, ettringite 
formation does not always cause distress in a soil’s molecular composition. The extent of the 
material’s deterioration is dependent on where the ettringite agglomerates form and in which 
direction they expand, disturbing the surrounding matrix. [20,22]  In the case of the wood-ash 
stabilized clay, the ettringite formation did deteriorate the material. An additional hypothesis 
is made knowing that ettringite starts decomposing from 50°C with a peak around 100°C. [13] 
After the experimental samples with wood-ash are humid cured, they are oven dried at 50°C 
for 24 hours. The assumption of the drastic strength loss can be that the ettringite formed in the 
==PAGE:72==
 
63 
wood-ash samples distressed the clay bonds during the humid curing time. When the samples 
are then oven dried for 24 hours, the strength that is built by the ettringite is then reduced due 
to the decomposition of some of the ettringite bonds at this temperature. As a result, it leaves 
the material with a very low strength and density and a high porosity. 
 
The strength of the bonds created by the calcium sulphate hydrates in the gypsum stabilized 
samples showed to build quick, which decreased the setting time, and reached high results after 
28-day curing time without distressing the clay matrix. A lower density is observed in the 
samples, which is due to an increase of the optimum water content while compacting and 
molding.  
 
Generally, the addition of calcium carbonate into a clayey soil is known to increase the 
compressive strength of fired bricks by 40-50% with rising proportion of CaCO3 and reduce 
the energy demand of heating by 30-50%. [42] The improved strength of the material is usually 
caused by the calcium carbonate that makes the clay particles agglomerate, by the carbon acting 
as a cementing agent when heated to approximately 900°C. This is because when heating up 
CaCO3, CO2 is released and creates CaO. When CaO reacts with water it forms Ca(OH)2, which 
is one of the reaction products in an ordinary Portland cement. [15,42] This theory is not 
observed in the unbaked soil bricks of the experiments as the high temperatures are not reached 
for these reactions to occur. No improvement in either strength, density or other properties was 
noticed in the experimental samples. Adding this to the reality that the additive was mixed into 
the soil at least 2 months prior to the molding of the samples, done by Egernsund-
Wienerberger©.

6.4. Durability 
The durability of each material in this report is measured in regards of permeability and the 
weathering of the experimental samples. These are discussed in this section.  
 
There is an important discussion regarding permeability of earth constructions, which often is 
understood as breathability. Permeability describes the “flow through” of water vapor in 
constructions. Clay is known to be a permeable material because of its high capillarity. That 
means that there is no need for an additional vapor barrier in wall constructions composed of 
clay.  Because of this property, clay can store a high amount of water vapor and release it back 
without damage to the construction itself. [18] As a consequence of this phenomenon, but also 
depending on the type of clay minerals, it leads to important swelling and shrinkage cycles 
depending on the local climate of the construction and its exposure to humidity, which can be 
an important disadvantage. Another negative effect of this is that during winter, with higher 
dampness, there is more humidity in the internal environment which leads to a higher heat loss 
to the colder exterior. [6]

6.5. Reflection 
There are a lot of clear benefits to using earth as a building material from an environmental, 
cost efficient and toxicity point of view. However, there are also crucial disadvantages when 
wanting to introduce the bricks as a standardized construction material, in terms of production 
and use that cannot be overlooked. This section reflects on some general issues that require to 
be considered. 
• Drying time of the bricks 
The pure soil requires a significant drying time which is higher with increasing amounts of 
clay. This is a current issue with unbaked bricks for large scale production, as the wet bricks 
are not stable enough immediately after molding. The addition of a stabilizing agent as gypsum, 
did reduce the setting time significantly, providing a good solution to this issue.

• Inconsistency in soils 
A global standardization is very difficult since the material is based on locally sourced soils, 
which will significantly reduce the transportation cost and correlated emissions. Each soil is 
very specific, and its mineral particles and organic matter are very dependent on the climate, 
topography, parent material, organisms and time. This leads to majorly different soils 
depending on the area. [44] Geotechnical classification of the soil and mechanical testing will 
be necessary before constructing with the material collected. However, stabilizing the clay can 
reduce some inconsistencies as agents like gypsum are standardized materials. 
• Labor intensive  
Like most raw materials, excavating clay is very labor intensive and requires digging large 
holes deep in the ground. Regarding production of earth bricks, uniformization of the clay, 
homogenously mixing additives and fibers and finally compactly molding the material into its 
shape can require intense labor. Having a mechanical manufacturing system is beneficial in 
large scale production. However, on a smaller single house scale, with the right techniques it 
is feasibly done by handcraft. 
• Availability 
When thinking of producing earth bricks on a larger scale in building constructions, an 
important amount of clay is needed. Looking at its availability in Denmark, it might seem 
abundant because of the country’s soil characteristics. But suitable clay is only found in some 
specific areas and needs to be excavated in the specific clayey earth layer situated four to nine 
meters underground. Having such deep holes can have an important impact on the landscape, 
and therefor needs to be refilled. Danish legislation and surrounding neighborhood’s 
complaints can potentially make the excavation of the desired clay amounts very complex. This 
needs to be considered because, to build sustainably, the raw materials should also be sourced 
as locally as possible.

6.6. Future Work 
The subject of this thesis gives a brought overview of the many research potentials on 
improving earth building materials. There remains a lot of more specific focus areas to test on 
clay stabilization and long-term results to analyze in order to provide a global idea for new and 
improved sustainable earth building materials. Some additional research suggestions are made 
in this section that could further improve the brick’s mechanical properties.  
 
As was described from the literature research, adobes originally contain an addition of natural 
fibers which can potentially bring some mechanical improvements. Looking into adobe 
properties by adding small fibers can be a way to reach an increase of the flexural strength as 
the compressive strength of the clay from Valby, Helsigne is already considerably high. Special 
attention must then be paid at the interaction between the stabilizing agent and fibers that are 
added.   
 
Using a mechanical compaction method would elevate considerably the quality of the bricks. 
When the material is compressed by a machine it leaves less room for imperfections, resulting 
in a more uniform surface, more homogeneous material distribution and a higher density of the 
material. In the testing process of the material, having a more homogenous material leaves less 
room for error and more consistent results. When thinking of general brick production, it also 
would considerably decrease the manufacturing time. 
 
==PAGE:80==
 
71 
Reconsidering the stabilization and the lack of reactivity of the clay which was discussed in 
Section 6.2. Looking to generate chemical interaction between the clay and the stabilizing agent 
could result in interesting developments. These reactions can be improved by altering the clay’s 
mineral structure from crystalline to amorphous. This can be done by using an acid, thermal or 
mechanical treatment on the clay. 
 
To bring a broader idea of the outcomes that each stabilizing method brings, working with 
different clay profiles should be analyzed. Using the same stabilizing techniques on a weaker 
clay can be a way to have a more precise idea of the mechanical impact it brings. As the soil 
was already strong, positive effects of the stabilizers did not result in a large strength gain, as 
shown in the case of the gypsum. It can be interesting to see these effects on a softer clay with 
a lower strength, as there might be a greater increase of strength. If in this case there is further 
investigation with wood-ash as a stabilizer for clay, simply humid curing the samples over a 
longer period of time, and not oven drying them afterwards could potentially bring some 
benefits in strength. By avoiding too high temperatures after curing and considering a longer 
curing time, the wood-ash can fully harden without deteriorating any of the formed ettringite. 
These new bonds created could bring an improvement to a weaker clay.

7. Conclusions 
The conclusions of all the experimental results indicate that the Valby yellow clay used is of 
high quality and the geotechnical properties are adequate with the addition of 25% sand by 
weight. By simply using the appropriate mechanical stabilization, the material can result in 
desirable characteristics for unbaked bricks, complying to the standards of a load-bearing earth 
brick for indoors or protected outdoor use. Mechanical stabilization is based on the right water 
content, which is close to the plastic limit of the clay, a homogeneous mixture, and the right 
molding method with manual or mechanical compaction.  
 
The addition of wood-ash resulted in compromising most characteristics of the dried material. 
The final texture of the samples was poor with high porosity and an increased number of fine 
cracks. The hydrated wood-ash formed ettringite agglomerates within the clay matrix, which 
significantly reduced the strength and density of the dried samples. The reactions distressed the 
clay more with increasing proportions of water and ash. Due to the high capillarity of the ash, 
a fast water absorption is observed, which makes molding and compaction more complex 
leading to a less homogeneous sample. A clear improvement of the material is noticed in the 
water weathering of the material, with visually no effect on the surface in direct water contact. 
Incorporating gypsum greatly improved the material’s plasticity, resulting in a smoother 
surface, less cracks and lower visible porosity. It also decreased the drying time, which can be 
very useful for the mass production of bricks. The clay stabilization with gypsum increased the 
compression strength and reduced the shrinkage, water sorption and weathering. The improved 
characteristics give the new material the potential to comply to the standards of a load-bearing 
earth brick for outdoor use. The addition of calcium carbonate had a minor influence on the 
sample’s properties, showing similar behavior as the unstabilized samples. This stabilization 
technique is not suited for unbaked bricks as no high temperatures are reached for 
improvements through chemical reactions with calcite. 
 
Finally, the addition of suitable stabilizers such as gypsum, can help correct several flaws 
observed in unbaked clay bricks as a low-strength building material. Together with mechanical 
stabilization, it can help standardize adobes as a quality building material, by reducing the 
inconsistencies between different clays and soils.
