Abstract
The growing trend towards electric vehicles (EVs) in the mobility sector has lead to new
opportunities and technologies. One of these new opportunities is the utilisation of EVs to
provide power back to the grid using VehicleôÙtoôÙEverything (V2X) capable Electric VehiôÙ
cle Supply Equipment (EVSE). These EVSEs allow for bidirectional power transfer (BPT)
which has many useôÙcases. This thesis presents an experimental characterisation and
assessment of stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart V2X equipment for EVs. Research herein evaluates varôÙ
ious performance parameters as well as wider compatibility of a prototype EVSE develôÙ
oped by the french power electronics manufacturer Watt & Well (W&W). Key parameters
studied include efficiency, response and power ramp rates, systemically tested using real
production and prototype EVs as well as emulators.
The study demonstrated high compatibility of the W&W EVSE, with significant perforôÙ
mance improvements over previous generations. Most notably was achieving BPT using
a real CCS2 EV through the recently released ISO 15118ôÙ20 standard, likely making it one
of the first times such a session has been successfully realised independently of direct
collaboration between EV and EVSE manufactures.
Despite the advancements made, the study also identifies several barriers and challenges
hindering broader adoption of BPT technologies. These include lacking implementation of
the open ISO 15118ôÙ20 standards and compliance with other various relevant standards
and codes to name but a few.
The thesis evaluates these parameters, compatibility, advancements and barriers to highôÙ
light the potential of BPT technologies to better integrate EVs into the electrical grid as
active participants, providing services beneficial to both owners and various actors in the
energy sector. The thesis concludes that although significant progress has been made,
there still exists barriers that need to be overcome before all benefits of V2X and BPT
technologies can be fully utilised to enhance the electrical grid and promote a more susôÙ
tainable energy landscape.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The transportation sector is currently undergoing a significant shift from fossil fuelôÙbased
propulsion technologies to a less carbon intensive and more sustainable electrically driven
future. Due to increasing environmental concerns, and with recent impressive technologôÙ
ical advances in battery energy storage and electric driveôÙtrains, there has been a resurôÙ
gence in the interest of electric vehicles (EV) leading to the development of modern day
EVs [1]. It is these modern EVs that are, in part, crucial for reducing greenhouse gas
emissions and addressing climate change in the transport sector [2].
Widespread adoption is ongoing and presents significant challenges for the owners and
operators of private and commercial vehicles. Challenges include concerns about vehicle
range, lack of charging infrastructure, capital expenses, and even political opposition in
some markets [1]. A further concern is the impact on the electrical grid. EV charging
infrastructure requires a considerable upgrade to the grid as the power for charging EVs
originates mostly from centralised power plants, putting a strain on existing power lines,
substations and other components of the grid [3, 4].
However, one of the opportunities of EVs is the fact that it is a battery on wheels. To
utilise this, a currently developing technology is VehicleôÙtoôÙEverything (V2X). V2X refers
to the bidirectional flow of electricity and information between EVs and energy systems
like loads, homes, buildings or even electrical grids [5]. As the term V2X includes comôÙ
munication, a different term referring only to the transfer of electricity is utilisedëƒ bidirecôÙ
tional power transfer (BPT). BPT has been standardised and utilised with the Japanese
CHAdeMO connector and protocol, but it is a quickly vanishing protocol in the European
market where the Combined Charging System (CCS) has become the mandated conôÙ
nector for Direct Current (DC) power transfer [6, 7]. The main focus in this thesis is on
implementation through CCS, and as such, refers to this unless specified otherwise.
As a single vehicle unit, an EV can be used as small scale energy storage for home backup
during outages, as a costôÙsaving measure in places with variable electricity prices, or as
storage for excess photovoltaic electricity production for homes with solar panels just to
name a few of potential possibilities [8]. As an aggregate of many coordinated vehicle
units, EVs can facilitate better integration of nonôÙdispatchable renewable energy sources
(RES) by acting as largeôÙscale energy storage with fast acting dispatchable electricity
[9, 10]. This fastôÙacting dispatchable electricity also allows for EVs to provide ancillary
services such as frequency services [11], demand response [12], peak shaving [13] and
several others [14], that overall reinforces the stability and robustness of the grid [11, 15].
Thus, by enabling this bidirectional power flow and providing these services, the EV owner
could lower the overall cost of ownership by either of these selling or saving useôÙcases.
To provide a structured power flow between EVs and the grid, Electric Vehicle Supply
Equipment (EVSE) is used. These are what colloquially are referred to as charge points.
The control and communications between EV and EVSE is standardised and, for the most
part, implemented through international standards IECôÙ61851 and ISOôÙ15118 [16, 17].
These standards are intended to ensure interoperability between the many various EV
and EVSE models on the market. Understanding the hardware and software requirements
and protocols necessary for structured power flow is therefore paramount to the further
penetration of EVs to the market, and especially for proper and more widespread adoption
of V2X functionality.

1.2
Objectives
This thesis aims to explore the various V2X useôÙcases, including VehicleôÙtoôÙGrid (V2G),
VehicleôÙtoôÙHome (V2H), and VehicleôÙtoôÙLoad (V2L). Each useôÙcase can provide distincôÙ
tive benefits for grid stability, home energy management, and onôÙtheôÙgo mobile power
solutions, respectively. Defining and analysing these useôÙcases, the thesis will provide
a broader understanding of the practical applications and potential advantages of V2X
technologies.
Furthermore, the current implementation and adoption of V2X functionality is investigated,
which is severely limited for commercially available EV and EVSEs. Currently only a few
auto manufacturers allow for BPT on production vehicle models and few EVSE models
are available with the capability. Those few models presently available have the capability
limited and mainly through proprietary implementations. An example of this is VolkswaôÙ
gen (VW) ID 77 kWh series vehicles, which have BPT functionality, but only in Germany
and only with the Hager Energy Management System EVSE [18]. VW has furthermore
implemented a soft limit, restricting BPT to 4,000 hours or 10,000 kWh, whichever comes
first. Such restrictions indicate a different concern for wider BPT adoptionëƒ the impact
on battery degradation when the battery is utilised outside the definitions of the battery
warranty. While research suggests that battery degradation caused by low power BPT
is minor, it is a pervasive concern of ordinary EV owners [19, 20]. Furthermore, no stanôÙ
dardised system exists for determining BPTãs impact on battery warranty. Standardised
implementation and determinations of functionality value and impact are thus barriers that
need to be overcome to increase adoption.
Another key objective of the thesis is examining the technical requirements and commuôÙ
nication protocols crucial for successful implementation of V2X technologies. This exôÙ
amination includes an analysis of the relevant standards, mainly ISOôÙ15118 with focus
on ISOôÙ15118ôÙ2 and ôÙ20, as this is the standard most relevant for power transfer and
communications between EV and EVSE.
Finally, and the main topic of this thesis, is an inôÙdepth experimental characterisation of
a prototype stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart BPT capable EVSE produced by Watt & Well (W&W). PerôÙ
formance parameters such as efficiency, ramp and response time, current ripple, and
others, are determined experimentally through use of real EVs and EV emulators. Test
scenarios are created to conduct systematic testing of these various parameters and data
is gathered through direct measurement of electrical components. Additionally, various
EVs are tested against the EVSE to determine compatibility with the standards and to
gather information on the current state of adoption on the vehicle side. These aspects
are analysed and used to compare with previous generations of BPT EVSEs to pinpoint
advancements made but also to identify potential improvements for future development.
The results and findings of this thesis aim to contribute information on the ongoing develôÙ
opment of V2X systems and to provide insights and analyses of stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart equipment
to the further advancement of V2X technology.

Overview of V2X Technology
2.1.1
V2G ôÙ Definition, Importance & Use Case
VehicleôÙtoôÙGrid refers specifically to the power flow from the EV battery into the connected
electrical grid. This process is referred to as ãdischargingã an EV. By leveraging the enôÙ
ergy storage capacity of EV batteries, V2G functionality offers the possibility of providing
ancillary services to the grid enhancing grid stability as well as potentially supporting wider
integration of renewable energy sources. V2G, and other V2X, technologiesã importance
lies in the potential to transform EVs from ordinary passive consumers of electric power
into active participants in the wider energy system [21].
Key benefits include:
Grid Stabilisation
Ancillary services such as frequency regulation, voltage support and operating reserves
are essential to maintaining the balance between electricity supply and demand. This balôÙ
ance ensures the reliability of the grid preventing power outages. EVs connected to the
grid through an EVSE, and able to discharge power, can act very quickly as there is no
need to e.g. start a turbine [22]. An example hereof is V2G enabled EV and EVSEs that
provide frequency regulation by charging or discharging based on necessity, maintaining
the relevant grid frequency (50 Hz in Europe). They are able to provide this as a rapid reôÙ
sponse to fluctuations, often near instantaneous depending on control mechanisms [23].
This could e.g. be in the case where nonôÙinsignificant generation capacity goes offline
leading to missing supply of power. This imbalance would decrease the frequency which,
if it drops too low, could lead to cascading failure. A different example if an area has
a blackout e.g. if a transmission line disconnects. Demand would instantaneously drop
significantly leading to excess supply thereby increasing the frequency [24].
While a single EV controlled individually likely cannot provide sufficient amounts of power
for any meaningful impact, EVs in aggregate can. This could be done through large scale
Charging System Operators controlling the EVSEs, either with a significant number of
private vehicles or, perhaps easier, with fleet vehicles where several vehicles are located
within the same local geographic area e.g. a large parking lot [5]. Also bus or truck depots
would be prime locations for larger deployment of V2G capabilities.
Renewable Energy Integration
V2G can facilitate further integration of nonôÙdispatchable RES, such as wind or photoôÙ
voltaic solar, storing excess energy generation in periods of high production/low demand
and supplying the energy back to the grid when demand exceeds production. This could
help smooth out the negative high variability concerns of renewable energy generation.
This is particularly important in places where there is little or no availability of pumped
hydro storage, a dispatchable RES [21].
Environmental Impact
By the aboveôÙmentioned benefits, V2G could reduce need for expensive fossilôÙfuel based
peak demand power generation technologies like gas turbines.

Economic Benefits
EV owners and utility companies could experience an economic benefit from V2G impleôÙ
mentation. EV owners could engage in energy arbitrage by selling excess energy back
to the grid for monetary or other economic benefits in expensive hours while purchasing
when prices are low [25]. This would naturally lead to peak shaving where demand would
decrease in these expensive hours causing utility companies to have less need to own
and operate expensive peak demand power generators.
Grid operators would also see savings when there is less reliance on expensive power in
times of peak demand [26]. Furthermore, as electric infrastructure by necessity must be
dimensioned to handle peak power flow, including cases where power has to be routed
through different lines in the cases of larger disconnects plus safety margins, peak shavôÙ
ing would decrease strain on electric infrastructure and could potentially defer some costly
grid upgrades [27].
These uses can provide utility and benefits for individuals, companies and society as a
whole, especially in locations with high energy variability e.g. from RES or where the enôÙ
ergy infrastructure may be underôÙdeveloped and easily overwhelmed.
2.1.2
V2H ôÙ Definition, Importance & Use Case
VehicleôÙtoôÙHome (V2H) is the discharging of EV batteries specifically for use within a
home. This capability can turn an EV into a backup power source as well as a tool for
optimising home electricity usage. Through use of a transfer switch and often a Home
Energy Management System (HEMS), coordinated power flow between EV, home and
grid, energy use can be optimised based on availability, demand and cost. This further
underlines V2Hãs significance through the added energy independence and resilience the
functionality provides homes [27, 28].
Several benefits and useôÙcases exist, chief among which include:
Renewable Energy Storage and Utilisation
Ordinarily homes with their own production of renewable energy, most commonly phoôÙ
tovoltaic solar, may charge their EVs at times of peak production to optimise usage and
lower cost of purchasing electricity from the grid [29]. However, with V2H this stored
energyãs use is not limited to EV usage but can instead be used during low production
hours, evening out the homeãs renewably sourced energy consumption. This reduces deôÙ
pendence of fossil fuelôÙbased power generation from the grid, thus lowering the homeãs
carbon footprint [21].
Peak Load Shifting
As an extension of the optimised renewable energy storage and utilisation, V2H also enôÙ
ables peak load shifting where homes utilise the EV battery for power by discharging
when demand and prices are highest and charging when cheapest. This can lead to not
insignificant cost savings on electricity bills. Furthermore, it may reduce strain on the grid,
especially when more widespread within the distribution grid [30].
Backup Power Supply
One of the most critical uses of V2H capability is in providing backup power during grid
outages. Especially in emergency situations like natural disasters, where the grid may
be down for extended time periods, V2H can supply reliable electricity to essential apôÙ

2.1.3
V2L ôÙ Definition, Importance & Use Case
VehicleôÙtoôÙLoad capability allows EVs to directly power external electrical devices and
loads. While the principle of V2L is not new and can be done using even the 12V battery of
ICE vehicles, making available the highôÙvoltage battery pack of EVs opens up many more
possibilities of this technology given the significantly higher capacity and power output.
V2L essentially transforms an EV into a mobile power source, able to provide electricity
wherever and whenever it is needed [32]. While the previously mentioned functionalities,
V2G and V2H, most commonly require an EVSE to invert the batteryãs DC power into
useful Alternating Current (AC) power, V2L typically uses an inverter built directly into the
vehicle itself. This functionality is usually available either through standard power outlets
available within the vehicle or through an adaptor plugged into the AC charging port of
the vehicle. This is especially useful in scenarios where traditional power sourcing is
unavailable or impractical.
Importance of V2L technology is emphasised by the flexibility it provides and wide range
of applications such as:
Construction and Industrial Applications
Using a V2L capable EV, power tools and equipment at e.g. construction sites, may be
used even where no grid power is available. This capability supports activities requiring
reliable and portable power and is especially true for new development sites or remote
locations where power lines may not have been installed yet. These scenarios often use
generators which are noisy, require fossil fuels to operate and can be relatively expensive
to maintain. In contrast, an EV with V2L functionality would require little to no extra effort
to use outside the ordinary vehicle maintenance and costs.
Mobile Business Operations
Businesses that require mobility or are temporary such as food trucks, food stands at fesôÙ
tivals, mobile workshops and so forth, are oft dependent on generators or small external
batteries to operate. Instead, V2L allows for kitchen appliances, tools, lightning and other
necessary equipment to be powered directly from the vehicle within or attached to where
business is conducted. Enabling mobile businesses to operate independently of reliance
on external power sources reduces operational constraints and adds flexibility while inôÙ
creasing sustainability.
Recreational Use
Besides the practical and business useôÙcases mentioned above, V2L is also potentially
beneficial for personal and recreational use. This could be providing power for outdoor
gatherings or camping in the wild, enhancing the utility of EVs for ordinary everyday perôÙ
sonal use.

Backup Power Supply
Similar to V2H, V2L can be used as an emergency backup power supply. However, as
the power scope is usually smaller with V2L, instead of being able to provide power for
a whole home unit, the use case is more limited to only the most necessary devices and
require them being connected directly to the EV. Nonetheless, overall installation is easier
as no EVSE is necessary, thus increasing emergency utility.
V2L can thus significantly enhance the versatility of EVs as a convenient and flexible moôÙ
bile power source. The technology also benefit from the ease of use and implementation,
avoiding most technical and regulatory requirements that making it more feasible in the
short term compared to other V2X technologies.
2.1.4
V2ôÙOther ôÙ Definition, Importance & Use Case
Beyond the more known V2X applications mentioned above, there are several other poôÙ
tential uses of BPT that further extend the capabilities of EVs. These other V2X uses
further enable EV interaction and integration into the energy ecosystem.
V2B
VehicleôÙtoôÙBuilding (V2B) is a larger scale implementation similar to V2H, often intended
to describe BPT use in commercial buildings. Integrating with a buildingãs energy manôÙ
agement system, energy usage can be optimised for cost and, perhaps more importantly,
for operational resilience such that a power outage does not fully stop work from beôÙ
ing conducted. Critical V2B uses could include extra hospital backup power, currently
accomplished using batteries and generators. Through the aggregate nature of this imôÙ
plementation, the building may also contribute to some of the mentioned V2G useôÙcases.
V2V
VehicleôÙtoôÙVehicle (V2V) is the transfer of energy between vehicles. This capability can be
useful for roadside assistance if an EV runs out of power with no charging available. While
no proper framework for direct DCôÙDC coupling of highôÙvoltage batteries currently exists,
indirect V2V is already possible using V2L, albeit slow. Through use of an intermediary
BPT capable EVSE V2V is also possible, as will be briefly touched upon in Chapter 3 [33].
As adoption of EVs continue to grow and infrastructure expands, the use of V2X technoloôÙ
gies can have a considerable positive effect on the overall energy ecosystem significantly
enhancing the flexibility, efficiency, resilience and sustainability of both small and large
energy ecosystems [34, 35]. Therefore, there is a dependence on compatible and reliôÙ
able equipment that enables these functionalities. This equipment must operate efficiently
while responding and acting quickly, which is why testing and properly characterising such
equipment is important.

2.3.1
ISO 15118
The purpose of ISO 15118 is to provide a standardised framework for EVôÙEVSE comôÙ
munication. This ensures that relevant stakeholders in the eôÙmobility sector e.g. vehicle
manufacturers and charging infrastructure providers, can develop interoperable systems.
By defining a standardised communication protocol, ISO 15118 facilitates seamless interôÙ
action between EVs and EVSEs and promotes cohesiveness in the charging experience
for users [39]. This furthers reliability and ultimately contributes to the advance of the eôÙ
mobility transition. This section will briefly go through the most relevant parts and topics
of ISO 15118 and its bearing on this thesis.
2.3.2
ISO 15118ôÙ1
Part 1 General Information and Use Case Definition is the foundational document of the
ISO 15118 standard. It provides general information and use cases for V2G communicaôÙ
tions and serves as the baseline for understanding the standard which ensures all relevant
parties from vehicle manufacturers to energy utility providers have a unified understanding
of V2X systems [40].
2.3.3
ISO 15118ôÙ2
Part 2 Network and Application Protocol Requirements establishes the standardised base
V2G communication protocol requirements for energy transfer from EVSE to EV, known
as charging or GridôÙtoôÙVehicle (G2V) energy transfer [17]. These requirements are funôÙ
damental for reliable information exchange between EV and EVSE ensuring the energy
transfer can be completed safe and effectively. Part 2 describes the various states and

transitions managing the active communication between EV and EVSE used for initiation,
ongoing data exchange throughout, and termination of an active session, including monôÙ

specific requirements for BPT, it does establish the foundational functionalities which are
expanded upon in Part 20 to allow for more advanced features like BPT.
A fundamental element of Part 2 is the initial negotiation on which protocol to use for the
session. The base level EVôÙEVSE connection is described in IEC 61581 and is outside the
scope of this thesis [16]. However, some High Level Communications (HLC) elements
are necessary for the understanding of how sessions work and are therefore included

Once base level communications have been established through PLC, the EV will send inôÙ
formation on which protocols it supports. These could be DIN SPEC 70121, ISO 15118ôÙ2
or ôÙ20. This is done by sending a message supportedAppProtocolReq to which the EVSE
will respond with a message choosing which protocol to move forward with, based on its
own list of supported protocols. Each protocol has a VersionNumberMajor and a VersionôÙ
NumberMinor [17]. The VersionNumberMajor has to match for a session to move forward,
however the VersionNumberMinor describes additional functionality, and the session will
use the lowest minor number agreed upon, with additional functionalities being ignored
[17]. As an example, if the EV supports ISO15118 version 2.2, but the EVSE only supports
ISO 15118 version 2.1, functionality included in VersionNumberMinor=2 will be ignored.
This is of particular importance for custom implementations of the standard as developôÙ

here is support for BPT capable EnergyTransferModeTypes which are not included in ISO
15118ôÙ2. This is how Volkswagen has implemented BPT support through ISO 15118ôÙ2
for their ID 77 kWh series vehicles and similarly how the DrossOne V2G project has imôÙ
plemented support in Fiat 500e test vehicles using DIN SPEC 70121 [18, 42]. Both these,
and most other BPT CCS implementations currently available thus require specific EVSEs
that support the same custom minor versions of a protocol, while remaining compatible
with ordinary charging infrastructure.
Another important element of ISO 15118ôÙ2 is the ChargeParameterDiscovery messages,
where the EV and EVSE will communicate, among others, defined charging voltage, curôÙ
rent and power limits. These ensure that neither will attempt to transfer electricity outside
the technical capacity of the other. It is worth noting that if these limits change during an
active session e.g. if either is derated due to overheating or restriction of available power
to the EVSE, new limits are not communicated through the ChargeParameterDiscovery.
Instead, dynamic charge limits are communicated to which the EV and EVSE will adhere.
Neither can go beyond what was agreed upon in the initial ChargeParameterDiscovery
messages however, even if the dynamic limits are increased beyond original limits [17].
In practice this has little effect, but for the testing conducted for this thesis, this meant
that every session needed to start with defined limits that would encompass all relevant
electrical parameters required for a specific test session.
Lastly of main relevancy for this thesis is CurrentDemand Request and Response mesôÙ
sages exchanged during an active session. The EV will continuously send a request
for specific current and voltage target to match the batteryãs charging power profile and
the EVSE will respond with the ongoing current and voltage provided. This continuing
exchange constitutes the charging loop and will adhere to both the hard ChargeParameôÙ

ISO 15118ôÙ20
Part 20 2nd Generation Network Layer and Application Layer Requirements is an exôÙ
panded replacement for ISO 15118ôÙ2 [43]. It is based on Part 2 but introduces and enôÙ
ables additional advanced functionalities like more sophisticated security measures, wireôÙ
less power transfer (WPT) and, of highest relevancy, BPT.
EVs and EVSEs supporting ISO 15118ôÙ20 initiate base level communications the same
way as ôÙ2. However, with the additional functionalities now available, the HLC afterwards
differs slightly. This means that there is no backwards compatibility between the two proôÙ
tocols [44].
Additional key communication components include:
ãÂ Electrical parameter limits for discharge in the ChargeParameterDiscovery and dyôÙ
namic charge/discharge limit messages
ãÂ Allowance for CurrentDemand Request/Response messages with negative current
and power values
ãÂ BPT capable EnergyTransferModeTypes
ãÂ Request/Response messages allowing for a change in power flow direction during

Furthermore, Part 20 introduces the concept of Dynamic Mode which allows for control
of power flow to be managed by the EVSE instead of the EV as is the case in Scheduled
Mode, the only mode available in Part 2.
As the name indicates, Scheduled Mode is designed for scenarios where energy transfer,
in either direction, follows a defined schedule. In this mode, the EV is in control of the
session which is useful for ensuring the EV has specific amounts of energy available at
scheduled times to be used for the required transportation needs. This mode is thus optiôÙ
mal where timing is known in advance and can be used for broader energy management
strategies like renewable energy storage, peak load shifting or energy arbitrage.
Dynamic Mode is designed for flexible and quick responsive energy transfer scenarios
with realôÙtime changes to parameters based on conditions. Dynamic Mode has the EVSE
in control of the session, which is useful as the EVSE will be able to dynamically adjust
power flow based on external requests made by e.g. utility companies or from direct meaôÙ
surements on the connected grid. Ancillary services requiring fast realôÙtime responses like
frequency regulation are therefore best performed in Dynamic Mode. Transportation purôÙ
pose safeguards are also implemented in Dynamic Mode, such that the operator still can
utilise the EV for its primary purpose: transportation. These safeguards include ability to
define a minimum SoC threshold below which discharging will not occur and a target SoC
which the EV/EVSE will attempt to maintain while providing services. A departure time
setting is also implemented so that priority can be given to avoid excessive discharge and
thus reach target SoC near expected departure time. Developers can use these features
to develop algorithms that forecast vehicle usage and grid demands, thus optimising the
utility of V2X services.
As such, both modes have appropriate uses relevant to the conduction of V2X applicaôÙ
tions.

3.3.2
Test 2 ôÙ Auxiliary Consumption
Test 2 was defined to test the EVSE auxiliary consumption. The components herein are
mainly contactor engagements, BMPU cooling and EVI operation.
Data was collected using the Chauvin Arnoux PEL106.
The test protocols were designed to test the EVSE in various configurations. Each test
lasted for 5 minutes, and was done by manual control through the GUI. First the passive
consumption was tested with and without any contactors engaged. Then the EVSE was
tested with 1 BMPU charging and discharging through CHAdeMO at maximum power.
Last was tested with 1 BMPU charging through CCS2. As time did not allow for these tests
during the stay at ElaadNL, no discharging test could be conducted for CCS2. However,
as the auxiliary consumption of the BMPU should be equal whether converting power for
CHAdeMO or CCS, the main difference between the auxiliary consumption should lie only
in which EVI board was used, which should be the same whether in charge or discharge
mode. Furthermore, it was intended to test with two BMPUs, but failure of BMPU 2 during
this segment of testing made this not possible.

5.1
Results
The results found in this thesis provide an extensive insight into BPT vehicle compatibility
and the performance characteristics of the W&W EVSE V2X Prototype with particular
focus on the BMPU.
5.1.1
Vehicle Compatibility
A significant amount of time was spent researching and sourcing various CCS2 EVs with
the purpose of exploring implementation of various communication protocols. The primary
purpose was to identify any EVs that might support BPT.
To explore this, vehicles were tested for their supported protocols. The Priority/SupportôÙ
edAppProtocol list from Table 4.5 was gathered from the W&W EVSE logs for testing sesôÙ
sions completed with the respective vehicles. The most significant result here is Priority 3
of the Confidential EV with prototype software: ãurn:iso:std:iso:15118:ôÙ20:DC ISO20_DCã.
This line is communicated from the EV to the EVSE and shows its support for ISO 15118ôÙ
20. Given its priority listing of 3 meant it was necessary to disable DIN SPEC 70121 and
ISO 15118ôÙ2 functionality from the W&W EVSE, so as to force the EV into communicating
using ISO 15118ôÙ20. This led to successful establishment of communication using ISO
15118ôÙ20 and successful energy transfer in both directions. The success of this test may
be one of the first times ISO 15118ôÙ20 BPT has been completed independently. Most, if
not all, tests of this type have been completed by close collaboration between the EVSE
and EV manufacturers. While both EVSE and EV are prototypes, this result validates that
a standardised implementation is feasible, and might become available soon.
Additionally, while not further relevant for this thesis, it is interesting to note the ConfidenôÙ
tial EVãs developing support for AC V2G, shown as Priority 4.
Early research indicated that a promising candidate could have been the VW ID.4 77 kWh,
of which two were tested. However, as shown in Table 4.5, neither had support for ISO
15118ôÙ20, and therefore could not perform BPT with the W&W EVSE. Given VW stateôÙ
ments of BPT support for this vehicle series, implementation must be through proprietary
implementations of ISO 15118ôÙ2 or DIN SPEC 70121. These vehicles, and the other veôÙ
hicles listed in the table, were therefore not usable for electrical characterisation of the
W&W EVSE.
The vehicle compatibility test further underscores the necessity for EV manufacturers to
adopt support for ISO 15118ôÙ20 if they wish to facilitate proper implementation of BPT
and allow their EVs the ability to provide V2X services. However, a reason for the lack
of support might be the recent release of the standard, with ISO 15118ôÙ20 only having
been released in 2022. Implementation takes time, and as shown previously, several
manufacturers have announced upcoming support already.
Further study could therefore be in expanding the scope, testing compatibility with newer
models, perhaps even EV models released in late 2024. This could provide more context
for the status of ISO 15118ôÙ20 implementation. It might also be relevant to investigate
which EV manufacturers intend or, maybe more importantly, do not intend to provide supôÙ
port for BPT. Identifying and understanding motivation behind support could shed light on
which technical or regulatory barriers might need to be overcome. This could assist in the
furtherance of V2X implementation.

5.1.2
Efficiency
Efficiency is one of the most important performance characteristics to any energy transôÙ
fer system, and as such, manufacturers of V2X power electronics must make sure that
their products have high efficiencies if they wish to be part of the developing V2X marôÙ
ket. Therefore, considerable effort was put into this part of the study, determining power
consumption of all the various elements that make up the EVSE.
The BMPU is the most important component as it is where conversion takes place and
is where most efficiency gains are made. However, minimising auxiliary consumption is
also important, particularly for lower power systems. This is because there are some base
elements of the auxiliary consumption like EV/EVSE communications, EVSE communiôÙ
cations to Charging Point Operators and other ongoing passive operations that have fixed
power consumption not scaling with EVSE power levels. These are what are shown in
Figure 4.7a. As such, this part of the auxiliary consumption amounts to a bigger part of
the total EVSE consumption for lower power system operations.
Other auxiliary consumption like cooling and, in this case, BMPU contactor configuration,
scale with power levels although still remain only a small part of total consumption. BMPU
contactor impact is evident in the different power consumption between Test 2_0_1 and
2_0_2, where 2 contactors are engaged, configured for CHAdeMO using BMPU 1. As
shown in Figure 4.7, there is an average difference of ã¥30 W, or about 15 W per contacôÙ
tor.
Furthermore, the impact of EV/EVSE communications, BMPU cooling and other required
passive operations for CHAdeMO sessions are shown in Figure 4.8. These account for
an additional average ã¥78ôÙ84 W. The difference seen in between Figure 4.8a and FigôÙ
ure 4.8b are likely due to the slightly higher power level available for discharge (11 kW
discharge vs 10.5 kW charge), which would require more cooling.
It is also notable from Figure 4.1 that auxiliary consumption tends to be higher when power
flow is higher, although the correlation is not exact, and is determined by the EVSE itself
to remain within thermal limits. Auxiliary power consumption appears to be slightly less
for CCS sessions, which might be explained by the prototype nature of the EVSE, in parôÙ
ticular as EVI A, used for CCS, is a specifically developed board for the EVSE while EVI
B, used for CHAdeMO, is a RPi board with custom firmware but not developed specifically
for the task.
Unfortunately, BMPU 2 broke before Test 2 was conducted, and as such, auxiliary conôÙ
sumption in parallel, series or two simultaneous sessions could not be measured. These
results would however be interesting as they could provide further insight into the speôÙ
cific division of auxiliary consumption between the relevant elements and could be part
of further testing beyond this thesis. Testing with two BMPUs in parallel and series could
provide information on how much of the power is required for cooling and how much power
is for the various other processes like communication, especially since cooling requireôÙ
ments should be less in series than in parallel given the lower amount of heat generated
by less current being needed for same power output.
BMPU conversion efficiency remains, however, where most losses occur. It is evident
from all Figures in Section 4.3.1 that conversion efficiency is higher for AC to DC converôÙ
sion than from DC to AC, higher towards higher power levels, and approaches zero when
nearing zero power output. This tracks with previous research in the field by Zecchino et
al. [47].
It was not possible to determine the exact reason for this, but one possible reason might
be component efficiency of the various elements used in the conversion. Furthermore,
AC to DC is a relatively simple conversion whereas DC to AC requires capability to genôÙ

view of the electrical characteristics. With more time, sampling periods could have been
significantly longer, thus allowing measurement variability to be minimal.
Furthermore, most of these impossible efficiency points were found within the area where
DC current ripple was highest. This makes those current measurements extremely unstaôÙ
ble, and in combination with the short sampling time and relatively low sampling frequency,
a series of measurements made at unfortunate moments within the sampling time could
lead to an average that is higher (or lower), than was actually present.
Lastly, but of high significance, is the use of fixed DC voltage levels for all efficiency tests
but CHAdeMO, due to the HIOKI sensor range mistake. Voltage and current are intrinôÙ
sically correlated, and by using measured currents but defined voltages, the calculated
DC power is inherently flawed, if only slightly. This is apparent from Figure 4.4, where
it is evident that no efficiency points are above 100%. It would therefore be appropriôÙ

sampling periods and perhaps more accurate measurement equipment.

likely due to two thingsëƒ lower voltage requiring more current for the same power output
thus generating more waste heat, and that testing was limited to only 10 kW per the
manufacturers request therefore not operating in the optimal efficiency areas.
An important parameter when characterising efficiency is heat and therefore temperature.
Temperature was not included in the measurements or considerations because proper
monitoring of this would have required the BMPU be opened to apply the equipment on
the proper components. The EVSE has temperature monitoring sensors, but accessing
and logging these values requires direct CAN bus interfacing. Both of these tasks were
outside the scope of this thesis but should be considered in further studies. It might have
been possible to add temperature sensors for the airôÙcooling outlet of the BMPUs, but the
nature of how testing was conducted the measurements would have been of little value
and inclusion in data analysis would have required immense effort to correct for situations
where tests were conducted in quick succession, with small breaks, or on different days.
The only comment to provide then is that all tests were conducted indoors and at room
temperature.

5.1.3
Response, Ramp & Maximum Activation Time
Understanding the response, ramp and maximum activation time of the EVSE is crucial
as these directly influence which V2X applications the W&W EVSE might reliably be used
for. These times might not have high impact on uses such as optimised renewable enôÙ
ergy utilisation or energy arbitrage, where power flow remains relatively constant over the
relevant period, or other scenarios where sessions are generally operated in Scheduled
Mode. However, for V2G ancillary services such as frequency regulation, or utilisation
for emergency backup power where seamless switching between grid and EV provided
power is necessary, response time has to be swift to be effective.
Response Time is defined as the time it takes from when a command is sent to the EVSE
until a response is provided, seen as when ramping starts. It is evident from all tables
presented in Section 4.3.3 that average response time is < 1 s when power flow direction
is unchanged. In this case, the EVSE has to execute the Limitations Payload command,
changing the current setôÙpoint accordingly. However, when power flow direction changes,
response times go slightly above 1 s. The increase in response time for these situations
makes sense, as the EVSE has to execute an additional commandëƒ the Power Direction
Payload, thereby changing direction. This holds true for all tests when going from 0 kW to
max discharge, and from max discharge to max charge, both scenarios where power flow
direction is changed. However, the results also clearly indicate that the response time
going from max charge to max discharge, the last row of each table, response time is
significantly faster. This behaviour is abnormal, and theoretically should behave similar to
the reverse transition from the row above. When looking at the example from Figure 3.14,
this is also apparent, as the last two blue marker lines, indicating the response time for
this last transition, are nearly overlapping. A speculative reason might be that the switch
from charge to discharge mode is quicker than the opposite, but then a similarly quick
response time should be present for the 0 kW to max discharge transition, which clearly is
not the case. Some unknown interaction could be at play here, and it should be possible
to investigate this further by sniffing the CAN communication. However, the behaviour
remains unexplained.
Ramp Time is the time difference from when a response is provided until the EVSE
reaches the current setôÙpoint. Ramp time therefore has an inherent correlation to the
power difference of each transitionëƒ larger jumps in power levels take longer time. It is
therefore more prudent to consider the Power Ramp Rate, which are the power differôÙ
ences divided by the ramp time. Significant variability is present in the ramp rates across
the various test configurations. Particularly noteworthy is the slow ramp rate of only 9.7
kW/s from 0 to 10.5 kW using CHAdeMO and a Nissan Leaf, presented in Table 4.6. This
is less than a third of the ramp rate for the same transition using a similar configuration
with CCS2 and the CDS, from Table 4.7. The other transition ramp rates for these two
tests follow the same trends and remain relatively close. The results are also consistent
across all three repetitions, as can be seen from the actual measurements presented in
Appendix C.1, so the reason for this variability remains unclear. The BMPU specification
sheet lists a maximum ramp rate of up to 30 kW/s, which was actually achieved for the
CCS2 0 to 10.5 kW transition [58]. However, when actually implementing the EVSE for a
V2X useôÙcase, consideration should be made that this maximum ramp rate is only achievôÙ
able under specific circumstances. Additionally, W&W states in the BMPU specification
sheet that the BMPU is capable of switching power direction and go to full power in less
than 750 ms. Results do not immediately support that claim, as the fastest of such transiôÙ
tions was found to be 1067 ms (Test_1_7_1 for power transition ôÙ11 to 10.5 kW, Rep 3 of
Appendix C.1. However, this is likely because these tests were conducted using the GUI

API. Direct BMPU control by interfacing through CANopen would likely allow for faster
times. Furthermore, there is a clear increase in ramp rate with increased voltage. It is

Maximum Activation Time is the time difference from command issuance to completed
power ramp, or simply the sum of response and ramp time. Understanding hereof can be
important when implementing the EVSE in V2X applications, as some useôÙcases might
require full activation within a certain time frame to properly provide the expected service.
However, in such scenarios, consideration should also be made to the control method.
Most V2X applications would be controlled remotely e.g. a utility sending a command
request to the EVSE. The values found and calculated for this thesis are all from local
control. Therefore, in practice, knowledge of how the EVI receives and executes remote
commands needs to be investigated.

of 0 kW. This bears relevancy as real V2X sessions might just as well be changing power
flow direction or power setôÙpoints while power is already flowing. Time did not allow for
testing these but it might be worth further study.

5.2
Experimental Setup & Design Flaws
The experimental setup used for the study in this thesis required a comprehensive arôÙ
rangement of equipment to ensure reliable data gathering and testing of the relevant
functionalities. Despite best attempts some design flaws and issues are worth discusôÙ
sion to evaluate and look for how it could be improved in the future.
A chief issue within the measurement equipment setup was data logging inaccuracy and
synchronisation, which were noted for all nonôÙoscilloscope DC measurement equipment.
The Chauvin Arnoux L452 Data Loggers consistently dropped measurement points when
set to measure in a ôÝ 1 V data range. This had huge impact on the gathered data and
made it unusable for data analysis. These L452s were then set to measure in a ôÝ 10
V data range, where points were not dropped anymore. However, the granularity of the
measured values was then only available in integers (1 A or V increments) which was not
precise enough for proper characterisation. This necessitated the addition of the HIOKI
LR8430ôÙ20 Data Logger, which could both measure more precisely and with a higher
sampling frequency. Unfortunately, introduction of the HIOKI brough two new flawsëƒ one
by mistake and one inherent.
The flaw by mistake was incorrect setting of the HIOKI sensor range for the voltage meaôÙ
surements. The differential probe, bringing the voltage down into a measurable range
was set to a 1/1000 division, so that 400 V input would have a 0.4 V output. However,
the HIOKI channel used to measure voltage was set to measure ôÝ 10 V. This caused the
input to always be in the lowest levels of the sensor range where inaccuracy is signifiôÙ
cantly higher. This mistake was found during data analysis, as testing with the CDS has
been utilising fixed voltages with very little variation. The measured voltages varied by
up to ôÝ 20 V, and the baseline was also several volts over the actually defined voltage.
This is what led to the determination of using the fixed voltages to calculate DC power
when characterising efficiency. The only exception here is for the CHAdeMO efficiency
tests which, as described earlier, were tested with the correct sensor range, and where
measured voltage stayed within the estimated correct values.
The inherent flaw was that the HIOKI was not synchronised with the Chauvin Arnoux
measurement equipment, especially problematic given the Chauvin Arnoux equipmentãs
inconsistent internal clocks. This could be fixed postôÙtest during data analysis using the
command signal from the RPi. L452s were synchronised with the PEL104 and PEL 106,
measuring AC properties, and using the RPi command signal, the HIOKI could be synôÙ
chronise to the L452, thereby synchronising to the PELs.
A recommendation for the measurement equipment setup is therefore to develop and emôÙ
ploy a more integrated data gathering system. The system should preferably be able to
handle both AC and DC input while sampling at a high frequency and with high precision.
This could significantly streamline both the data gathering and analysis processes.

A different issue relates to the RPi command signal. The scripts created to execute the
test protocols were designed in a way that once an API response call from the EVSE was
observed, the command signal would be sent. However, this means that the EVSE reôÙ
ceives a command and only once the command has been accepted, sends a signal back
to the RPi which then issues the command signal. This means that there is an, albeit
small, time lag between EVSE command receival and command signal. This matters not
for efficiency tests as they are an average over a sampling period that begins significantly
after the command has been executed. Nor does it matter for ramp times as these are
measured by when a ramp starts and ends, and thus do not use the command signal.
However, the response time measurements are based on the command signal. ThereôÙ
fore, this lag should have been considered when measuring response times. Given that
the lag time was not measured and appears relatively inconsistent, additional testing of
response time is merited. If utilising similar protocols as used for the testing in this thesis,
the structure of the script should be adapted such that the command signal to the meaôÙ
surement equipment is sent at the same time the command payload is sent to the EVSE.
Thus, response time would include the time it takes for the EVSE to receive and accept
the command(s). This approach would more closely resemble how the EVSE would be
controlled when used for V2X applications.
Another important aspect of studies such as this, is testing under realôÙworld conditions.
Due to the lack of BPT compatible CCS2 EVs currently available, testing was primarily
done using the CDS emulator. Future research should attempt to include more EVs so
that testing can be done for realôÙworld scenarios, preferably spanning a larger range of
conditions or different battery voltage architectures. This could add insights into restricôÙ
tions or opportunities that testing with only emulators or prototype vehicles cannot.
5.3
Overcoming Barriers for Adoption
With the results of the experimental study, it appears clear that there are EVs and EVSEs
ready or nearing market readiness for V2X implementation. However, V2X technologies
still face several barriers that need to be overcome for the facilitation of widespread adopôÙ
tion.
While standardisation of V2X and BPT implementation now exists, implementation of this
has been slow. Using the open ISO 15118ôÙ20 standard has so far been hindered mainly
by being released only recently and with the production cycle in the automotive sector, this
is understandable. However, even proprietary implementations have been far between.
This indicates that other barriers exist for further adoption.
One of these barriers could be the lack of a proper regulatory framework and particuôÙ
larly a standardised way of evaluating the impact of V2X use on a vehicleãs value [19,
20]. Albeit small, V2X use does have an impact on battery degradation. Therefore, EV
manufacturers need tools to address the impact such use may have on their obligation
within a battery warranty. This impact, however, is unlikely to be equal between battery
chemistries, battery capacities and EV models, leading to EV manufacturers themselves
imposing restrictions on BPT use, as evidenced by VWãs 4,000 hour / 10,000 kWh limit.
Furthermore, another barrier is among EV owners, where this same concern for battery
degradation or other wear on their vehicle exists [64]. Therefore, awareness of both posôÙ
itive and negatives of V2X use should be raised such that myths might be busted and
concerns alleviated, allowing owners to decide for themselves whether the benefits are
worth it.

Beyond these, another consideration, although not further touched upon in this thesis, is
compliance with local grid codes. Grid codes exist to ensure grid stability, safety and interôÙ
operability of various systems integrating in the grid. This means that any system wishing
to participate must comply with the local grid code to ensure safe and efficient power
transfer. Grid codes can vary between different grids and as such the power delivered
must be at least up the quality specification of whichever grid is currently being engaged.
For DC BPT, compliance lies with the EVSE as this is where AC power is converted. This
means that BPT EVSEs need to be designed for compliance in all markets where they are
sold and may be installed. It also requires a way for them to be setup such that a specific
EVSE complies with the grid it is connected to. This should be manageable as DC EVSEs
rarely move. However, more complicated is AC BPT. In this case it is the EV that creates
the AC power and therefore needs to be compliant. As EVs are mobile, and may be used
across many different grid code locations, the EV has to know which grid code is relevant
for current engagement [65]. This could be done through EV geoôÙlocation or from HLC
with the EVSE. The inverter also has to be capable of adjusting the AC output ensuring
compliance, if AC BPT is to be allowed. These reasons might therefore also explain why
some BPT systems have been limited location wise. VW with the Hager HEMS allows for
DC BPT, but initially only available in Germany, and the upcoming Renault 5 with AC BPT
has this capability only using the Mobilize EVSE and only in France to start with. These
market restrictions highlight a further barrier to adoption.

Conclusion
The experimental study conducted within the scope of this thesis has provided several
valuable insights into the interoperability and electrical characterisation of the W&W V2X
EVSE Prototype. This assessment focused on various performance parameters, compatôÙ
ibility with a range of production and prototype EVs. The thesis also explored numerous
V2X useôÙcases and the current state of V2X implementation in society.
The most important key finding is the successful ISO 15118ôÙ20 BPT session using the
EVSE with a confidential prototype EV. This is likely one of the first times an independent
study has managed to conduct such sessions through nonôÙproprietary means. Beyond the
test with the prototype vehicle, compatibility with a wide range of vehicles and emulators
was tested successfully. This shows the versatility of the EVSE, and further illustrates the
importance of implementing open standards where interoperability is paramount, as it is
in the field of EV infrastructure.
Additional key findings are the performance parameters of the EVSE.
Efficiency tests show that the equipment has improved slightly over previous generations.
However, discharging still remains more inefficient than charging, and further improveôÙ
ments might be worth exploring.
Response time and ramp rates show that significant improvements have been in this field.
With response rates often under one second, and average ramp rates twice or thrice as
fast as previous generations, the W&W EVSE has shown to be clearly superior and may
very well be ready to provide relevant V2X services requiring quick responses to even
large power change requests.
DC current ripple was also evaluated to ensure that the power quality was compliant with
the IEC 61851ôÙ23 standard. However, results here clearly indicate that the EVSE is not
compliant. Therefore, this parameter should be investigated further in order to properly
ascertain whether these results were due to a flawed test protocol, or if the EVSE needs
to be developed further to solve this issue.
The findings presented in this thesis highlight the potential of V2X technologies and asôÙ
sesses the readiness and current implementation hereof.
The successful compatibility tests and the electrical characterisation of the W&W V2X
EVSE Prototype demonstrate how this technology can effectively integrate EVs into acôÙ
tive participants in the electrical grid, providing various services benefiting the individual
and society as a whole.
Nonetheless, several barriers need solving in order to facilitate a broader adoption of V2X
technologies. Open standards promoting V2X and BPT are now available and ready to be
implemented, but such processes takes time. Even proprietary implementations are rare
indicating that several other concerns need to be alleviated before proper realisation of the
capabilities might be fully utilised. Frameworks to standardise the impact of BPT on EV
manufacturers and owners should be explored, such that the concerns of these might be
lessened. Awareness of the benefits of BPT should also be raised, as it has a great potenôÙ
tial to improve the electrical infrastructure. Compliance with grid codes also needs to be
addressed, so that owners may utilise the full functionality of their EV or EVSEs wherever
they may be. Overcoming these barriers requires a multifaceted approach throughout the
entire development chain, from regulatory bodies and manufacturers and all the way to
the owners and operators of EVs

Work to address these barriers should continue such that V2X BPT integration soon may
