Abstract
The Plane Wave Model (PWM) has been shown to be eﬀective in reconstructing arbitrary, low fre-
quency, source-free sound ﬁelds which respect the homogeneous Helmholtz equation. This method
reconstructs a sound ﬁeld with a superposition of plane wave functions whose amplitudes are esti-
mated using array measurements. In practice, it requires the acoustic source used to generate the
sound ﬁeld to be outside the reconstructed domain, making it hard to accurately reconstruct the
sound ﬁeld in an entire room unless the source is placed near the boundaries.
This work presents a method that makes it possible to reconstruct an arbitrary low frequency sound
ﬁeld containing an acoustic source in the interior the reconstruction domain.
This enables more
accurate sound ﬁeld reconstructions in entire arbitrary enclosures (e.g. a room with unknown boundary
conditions) regardless of source position.
The method makes use of the Dirichlet Green’s function to decompose the sound ﬁeld into its re-
verberant and direct source contributions. The direct part of the sound ﬁeld is then modeled with
the free-ﬁeld Green’s function of the source, and the reverberant part (which is a solution to the
homogeneous Helmholtz equation) is reconstructed using the PWM. The full solution is obtained by
superimposing the two parts. The method shows a signiﬁcant improvement in reconstruction quality
at lower frequencies and in the regions closer to the source, where the direct source contribution is
strong. Since the reverberant part of a sound ﬁeld becomes weaker for more heavily damped rooms,
the method also improves reconstructions in these conditions.
Validation of the proposed method is performed through numerical simulations, which serve as well to
study the inﬂuence of several variable parameters. Finally, a set of experimental tests is also conducted
for validation. A comparative analysis between the proposed method and the classic PWM is carried
out throughout both the numerical and experimental tests.

Introduction & Goal
An accurate reconstruction of the sound ﬁeld in an room can provide useful information for numerous
purposes. The immediate solution requires knowledge of the Green’s function of the room in question.
In practice, however, room boundaries are not ideal, and so the Green’s function of a room is not easy to
formulate. A way to circumvent this requirement is to decompose the sound ﬁeld into a superposition of
elementary wave functions, such as plane or spherical waves, which respect the homogeneous Helmholtz
equation.
The sound ﬁeld can then be reconstructed by estimating the coeﬃcients of those wave
functions.
Wang et al. [13] compared the representation of a sound ﬁeld in a cavity using both a Spherical Wave
Model (SWM) and a Plane Wave Model (PWM). The PWM was shown to be superior at extend-
ing the reconstruction volume to large cavities due to its non-decaying propagation characteristics.
Verburg [12] studied the performance of the PWM when using both Least Squares (with Tikhonov
regularization) and Compressive Sensing, two diﬀerent solutions to estimate the wave coeﬃcients. The
study determined that both solutions perform similarly. Ergo, this work will focus entirely on using
a plane wave basis for the sound ﬁeld decomposition, and on using the Least Squares solution with
Tikhonov regularization to solve for the wave coeﬃcients.
These methods, however, are designed for source-free volumes, obeying the homogeneous Helmholtz
equation.
This thesis proposes a method valid for sound ﬁelds which respect the heterogeneous
Helmholtz equation, in order to deal with domains containing an acoustic source inside the recon-
struction volume. This is done by ﬁrst decomposing the sound ﬁeld into its reverberant and direct
source contributions. The book Fourier Acoustics [14] calls the sum of these two parts the Dirich-
let Green’s function (or Neumann Green’s function, depending on the boundary conditions). This
technique is found in literature in multiple contexts: The book Fourier Acoustics [14] develops these
special forms of the Green’s function in order to overcome the need to specify both the pressure and
velocity on a HIE1 surface to determine the pressure on its interior. In Computational Ocean Acoustics
[8], the concept is introduced and utilized in the context of underwater acoustics and half-space prob-
lems. Chardonnay et al. [4] used the technique in the context of applying blind a de-reverberation
pre-processing step for source localization algorithms. The method of eliminating the reverberant
contribution is based on the Vekua theory, taking advantage of the diﬀerent convergence rates of the
two parts that compose the full solution (direct and reverberant).
The beneﬁts of this technique have yet to be fully explored in the context of room acoustics and
sound ﬁeld reconstructions.
As such, this thesis will focus on exploring the potential advantages
and improvements of applying this technique to the PWM for sound ﬁeld reconstructions of entire
rooms. Chapter 1 covers the theoretical concepts necessary to formulate and understand the proposed
sound ﬁeld reconstruction method. Chapter 2 focuses on an analysis based on numerical simulations,
in which the proposed method is tested, analyzed, and benchmarked against the classical PWM for
several case-studies. Finally, chapter 3 aims at presenting and analyzing the experimental results
carried out to validate the numerical simulations.

Chapter 1
Theory
This theoretical section aims to explore all the aspects related to the method proposed in this work
– the Heterogeneous Plane Wave Model (HPWM). It is structured in a way that allows the more
experienced reader to skip sections 1.1 to 1.4, if considered basic, as they are self-contained and serve
as fundamental building blocks and reference material for section 1.5.
Section 1.1 shows that the physical boundary of an ideal room is equivalent to an arbitrary virtual
boundary measured inside it. As such, equivalent solutions for the Helmholtz equation can be found
with either boundary. An analogous demonstration for the homogeneous case can be found in app. D.
Section 1.2 tackles the deﬁnition of the Green’s function in free-ﬁeld, which is a critical part of this
work’s method infrastructure.
Section 1.3 presents the idea of a plane wave expansion. With it, one can decompose a sound ﬁeld in
a sum of such waves that, together, form an orthonormal basis for L2. This idea will play a major
role in the sound ﬁeld reconstruction method.
Section 1.4 discusses the decomposition of the considered partial diﬀerential equation’s solution into
a homogeneous and a heterogeneous part. This idea, combined with the one from section 1.3 will be
paramount to the success of the proposed method.
Section 1.5 compares and describes both the classical Plane Wave Model and the proposed Heteroge-
neous Plane Wave Model.

1.3
Plane Wave Expansion
1.3.1
On the Sum
The idea of vector spaces and vector basis is powerful and ubiquitous. A set of vectors that, together,
can span a space of vectors are called the eigenvectors of that space. For example, two non-parallel
vectors can span the whole R2 and, thus, compose a basis for R2.
At the same time, it is possible to treat functions as vectors of inﬁnite dimension. Consequently, a set
of non-parallel vectors of this kind can, then, span a space of functions. This abstraction allows for
the decomposition of complicated functions into a sum of simpler ones. One common example is the
Fourier transform, whereby one can describe a function as an inﬁnite sum of complex exponentials.
There, the exponentials are referred to as the eigenfunctions, i.e., functions that, together, span a
space of functions.
In the context of acoustics, a pressure ﬁeld, which is a function of space, can also be described using
diﬀerent a basis. Here, the focus is kept at the basis composed by complex exponentials of the form
f(k, x) = ejk·x
(1.8)
where k is the wavenumber which carries information about the frequency of the wave and the medium
in which it travels.
Because the functions in eq. (1.8) are physically interpreted as plane waves,
acousticians usually call plane wave expansion the decomposition of sound ﬁelds as a sum of functions
of the form in eq. (1.8). In essence, one is describing a non-trivial sound ﬁeld as a sum of trivial sound
ﬁelds.
The equation below deﬁnes the plane wave expansion used in this work.

1.5.1
Plane Wave Model (PWM)
The main assumption behind the PWM is that any given homogeneous sound ﬁeld can be expressed
as a sum of plane waves as pointed out in [2] and [13]. This method can be described in individual
steps as listed below.
Step A consists of choosing a number of microphones, an array geometry and position, and a set of
possible plane waves. The positioning of the array of microphones constitutes the group of points xm
deﬁned in section 1.3.2. At the same time, the chosen plane waves build km from the same section.
With those two sets in hand, E can be built.
Step B, which is the one that requires more care, is to excite the domain with a frequency sweep and
sample the sound ﬁeld with the microphone array chosen in Step A. In this step, one acquires the data
for p which makes the equation
p = Ea
from section 1.3.2 have only one unknown.
In Step C, the data obtained is fed into the solver ([6]) for the minimization problem exposed in
section 1.3.2. The output of the solver is the set of coeﬃcients corresponding to plane waves coming
from diﬀerent directions - a.
The last step is to combine a with km and build, in this way, the reconstruction to the sampled sound
ﬁeld in Step B.
It is crucial to mention that, as this method aims to reconstruct homogeneous sound ﬁelds, it requires
no information about the source or its Green’s function. However, this also means that the source used
to excite the sound ﬁeld can not be inside the domain of reconstruction if good results are desired, as
this reconstruction method cannot predict a source singularity inside the reconstructed domain.
1.5.2
Heterogeneous Plane Wave Model (HPWM)
The main assumptions of the hereby proposed HPWM is that the sound ﬁeld is heterogeneous and
that the source(s) can be modeled. This method can be described in individual steps as listed below.
The ﬁrst and second steps mimic the ones in section 1.5.1 where the initial parameter choice is made
and the transducer data is acquired from the domain.
Step C is where the methodology starts to diﬀer signiﬁcantly. First, p will be normalized by jωρQ,
eﬀectively being transformed into the domain’s sampled Green’s function G(x, xs).
Secondly, as
==PAGE:14==

14
proposed in section 1.4, this result is decomposed into forced and homogeneous parts
G(x, xs) = g(x, xs) + h(x, xs)
where g(x, xs) is assumed to be known. As the target is to ﬁnd h(x, xs), p will be replaced by it, thus
the problem becomes
h(x, xs) = Ea
and the solver will ﬁnd a such as to approximate h(x, xs).
Step D corresponds to the last step in section 1.5.1. Here, a and km will be combined in order to
build the homogeneous part of the Dirichlet Green’s function h(x, xs).
The last step of the HPWM is to combine the result of Step D with the known g(x, xs) to ﬁnd G(x, xs)
and, if necessary, de-normalize it back into sound pressure values.
In essence, this method decomposes the sound ﬁeld into a direct part (forced solution) and a rever-
berant part (homogeneous solution). This decomposition allows the source to be placed inside the
reconstructed domain. Moreover, the plane wave expansion is used to approximate only the homo-
geneous solution h, eliminating the direct inﬂuence of the source from the problem. However, this
requires an accurate model of the source, as well as knowledge of the source’s position relative to the
observation points. In contrast, the PWM described in section 1.5.1 requires no knowledge regarding
the source whatsoever.
1.5.3
Error Sources
There are two main sources of uncertainty to be concerned with mathematically. The ﬁrst is the
approximation error of the solution to the homogeneous Helmholtz equation. This error is known
to converge exponentially in l2 when using either Fourier-Bessel functions or plane waves as the
construction basis ([4]). The second is the approximation error of the source modeling.
Both the PWM and HPWM are aﬀected by the former. The homogeneous solution terms are repre-
sented by p and h(x, xs) in sections 1.5.1 and 1.5.2, respectively. However, only the HPWM is aﬀected
by the latter and that is indicated by the term g(x, xs), which needs to be either known analytically
– as in the ideal cases – or approximated. The robustness of the method to errors in source modeling
is explored in section 2.7.
Even though it might seem that the HPWM is more prone to error sources than the PWM and, thus,
would perform worse, that is not the case. The uncertainties added due to modeling the source are
not high enough to hinder the improvement it brings. This is conﬁrmed throughout chapters 2 and 3.
Moreover, the additional source of error in the HPWM is a result of the segmentation of the full
solution into two parts, meaning that each part is simply prone to a diﬀerent type of uncertainty.

Chapter 2
Numerical Simulations
All reference sound ﬁelds used in the numerical simulations are generated using the ﬁnite-element based
software COMSOL and are subsequently imported into MATLAB. To better simulate a real-life scenario,
all generated sound ﬁelds are contaminated with Gaussian white noise with 40 dB SPL of average
and approximately 3 dB of standard deviation. It is important to stress that, in some situations,
the noise ﬂoor can have a non-white distribution. This fact was ignored, however, to simplify the
cross-frequency performance analysis of the method in question1.
Both reconstruction models (PWM and HPWM) are implemented in MATLAB. After the reference sound
ﬁeld is imported from COMSOL to MATLAB, it is normalized to the Green’s function domain. Based on
the pre-deﬁned array properties, a discrete set of M values is used as the observations which are fed
to the reconstruction methods. The HPWM then subtracts the direct source contribution from these
observations before using them to solve for the plane wave coeﬃcients using [6]. All simulations use
a discrete set of N = 1024 plane waves, whose directions are deﬁned around the surface of a sphere.
After obtaining the reverberant solution as a ﬁnite sum of plane waves, the direct source contribution
is added back to the solution.

2.3
Inﬂuence of Room Boundary Conditions
This section focuses on examining the inﬂuence of the boundary conditions of the room on the quality
of the reconstructions. To do this, diﬀerent absorption coeﬃcients are attributed to the boundaries of
the domain. For all cases, the HPWM reconstructions are compared to the PWM, which will serve as
a benchmark.
Because the HPWM decomposes the solution into direct and reverberant parts, it can be predicted
that the results will improve signiﬁcantly as the reverberant part of the solution becomes weaker.
Given a perfect model of the source, it is trivial to foresee that, if there is no reverberant energy (total
absorption), the HPWM would provide a perfect reconstruction for all frequencies. Note, however,
that the model of the source used in these simulations is not perfect, as the simulated references and
source model are generated using diﬀerent methods.
The reference sound ﬁelds used for the observations are generated with COMSOL, whilst the source
model used in the HPWM is the analytical monopole Green’s function (eq. (1.7c)). This is a good
practice to avoid the “inverse crime” of using the same theoretical ingredients for the simulation and
the subsequent inversion [15]. Otherwise, the simulation for the scenario of total absorption would
become completely trivial. Nonetheless, the source model used in this section can be considered to
have a very high degree of accuracy. The robustness of the method to erroneous source modeling will
be examined later in section 2.7.

understand how they aﬀect the pressure ﬁeld itself. Figure 2.3 shows a cross-section of the absolute

reverberant sound ﬁeld is eﬀectively decreasing as the absorption increases. As such, rigid boundaries
result in a more reverberant sound ﬁeld and damped boundaries result in a less reverberant sound
ﬁeld.

Another observation can be made regarding the inﬂuence of diﬀerent boundary conditions. As stated
in the beginning of this section, rigid boundaries result in a reverberant sound ﬁeld contribution with
a higher dynamic range due to stronger reﬂections. Because the sound ﬁeld has to be sampled with a
limited set of observations in order to be reconstructed, having a higher dynamic range of pressures
makes it harder to capture a good representation of such a sound ﬁeld. On the other hand, it is
easier to capture a good representation of a low dynamic range sound ﬁeld using the same limited set
of observations, resulting in more accurate reconstructions. This will be analyzed in more detail in
section 2.5 where the inﬂuence of the array position is discussed.

otherwise.
This absorption coeﬃcient was chosen to emphasize the relative improvements of the
HPWM, as well as to reduce the variations/instability of the results for visual clarity, whilst keeping
the absorption low enough not to be unpractical and unrealistic.

2.7
Robustness
Modeling the source is at the core of the HPWM. As such, it is important to study how the performance
of the method changes when the source is poorly modeled.
To do that, two sets of simulations are run in which the source parameters - position and volume
velocity - fed to the method do not match the ones used to generate the reference sound ﬁeld. The
reference sound ﬁeld is generated in the same 5 × 3 × 3 m3 room, with a single point-source placed in
(x, y, z) = (1, 1, 1) with constant volume velocity Q = 5 × 10−3 m3s−1.
Because these errors directly aﬀect the direct part of the sound ﬁeld, which decays with distance, it
can be expected that the inﬂuence they have on the results also decays with distance. Therefore, each
set of results is shown for two diﬀerent cross-sections normal to the x-axis: one closer to the source
at x = 1 m, and one further away at x = 4 m. As such, the MAC frequency responses used in this
section correspond to single horizontal slices of the MAC ﬁgures used up to this point. This analysis
also allows diﬀerent simulations to be plotted in the same ﬁgure.

A quick comparison of the two ﬁgures conﬁrms that further away from the source, where the direct
source contribution is weaker, the source position errors degrade the results to a much smaller degree.
However, there is a region in ﬁg. 2.23(b) with larger deviations around 70-125 Hz. These deviations
could be related to the existence of nodal planes 25 cm away from the actual source positions for the
low-order modes of order 2 (in any dimension). The natural frequencies of many of these modes fall
within that frequency range and these nodal planes are close enough to the source to be within the
range of the erroneous estimations. However, further investigation would be required to verify this
hypothesis. Nevertheless, this hypothesis would in fact result in higher deviations further away from
the source due to the fact that it would mostly inﬂuence the reverberant part of the sound ﬁeld, which

mismatch increases. The drop in quality is noticeable, even for a 1 cm mismatch, and stays approxi-
mately constant up until 350 Hz. However, this performance drop changes only very slightly between
errors of 1 and 5 cm. In practice, when estimating the source position with modern measurement
equipment, realistic errors can be expected to be smaller than 5 cm. Nevertheless, even with precise
measurements of the source position, errors of smaller magnitude can always be expected in practice
due to the fact that the acoustic center of the equivalent point source can change with frequency and
other factors[3]. Even so, errors of up to 20 cm still provide better results than the PWM across the
entire frequency range of interest.

3.1.1
Equipment & Infrastructure
All the experiments were conducted in a 3.29 × 4.38 × 2.96 m3 ﬂanking transmission room1. A set of
porous and perforated absorbers with unknown properties were used to dampen the room in one of
the two scenarios tested.
The energy source used was a DYNAUDIO BM6 loudspeaker connected to a loudspeaker ampliﬁer.
It was placed at (2.40, 0.89, 1.49) in the used reference system, as depicted in ﬁg. 3.1. This source
was chosen for its approximately ﬂat frequency response and omni-directional characteristics in the
frequency range between 50 and 500 Hz, which facilitate the source modeling. A single B&K Type
4191 Free-Field microphone was used as the measurement device and it was attached to the moving
arm of a Universal Robots UR5 robot to make the transducer positioning more accurate.
Finally, the ampliﬁer powering the microphone was the B&K Nexus ampliﬁer which was connected to
the input of a National Instruments NI–4431 sound card. The output of the sound card was connected
to the loudspeaker ampliﬁer.

3.1.2
Microphone Arrays
Two types of arrays are used throughout the experiments. The ﬁrst is a 66-microphone array where 63
are arranged in a open spherical conﬁguration, as in the numerical simulations2, and 3 are randomly
placed in the interior of the sphere to improve the odds of solution stability ([5]). The main reasons for
this choice are the will to test the method with a compact array (i.e., not distributed across the entire
room) and to parallel the array conﬁguration used in the numerical simulations. The spherical array
used here is the same size as the one used in the numerical simulations (V ≈1 m3, r = 0.62 m). It is
approximately centered in the room, with the UR5 robot positioned as in ﬁg. 3.1, as a consequence of
what was observed in section 2.5 (especially ﬁg. 2.16).
The second type is a 305-microphone planar array, used solely for reference ﬁeld measurement purposes.
The samples are distributed in a uniform square grid with a distance between adjacent samples of
10 cm, and cover an area of a circle with a radius of 1 m. This allows for a good resolution in the
considered range of 50 to 500 Hz. Two sets of measurements with this array in diﬀerent positions are
combined in order to extend the area of the reference plane to a total of approximately 6 m2.

3.1.3
Q Estimation
One of the requirements for the proposed HPWM method to work is an accurate enough model of
the acoustic source. However, it is outside the scope of this work to optimize and assess the process
of estimating Q. To save time and keep the focus (which is to assess the potential improvement of
the HPWM over the PWM), the procedure used to estimate the DYNAUDIO’s loudspeaker volume
velocity was highly empirical. Even though it will be concisely described below, it should not be taken
as a canonical way of estimating a source’s volume velocity.
First, the loudspeaker itself was considered to be a monopole, which is an arguably reasonable as-
sumption in the frequency range considered.
Consequently, its volume velocity can be calculated
using
|ˆp(x)| = ωρ|Q|
4π|x|
(3.1)
which is the classical expression for a monopole’s pressure ﬁeld in free-ﬁeld.
This expression, coupled with sound ﬁeld measurements which provide values for p and |x| at diﬀerent
frequencies, can be used to make a coarse estimation of the volume velocity of the source. In this case,
an average of the absolute value of the pressure for all positions was used, together with the distance
between the source and the center of the spherical array. It should be noted that it was shown in
section 2.7.2 that the HPWM is relatively robust to volume velocity errors, and it will be shown in
sections 3.2 and 3.3 that the results indeed conﬁrm that assessment.

of the source can also be found.
Unfortunately, the Q values obtained with this alternative were not realistic and produced results that
are not worth showing. The fact that the room is not perfectly rectangular nor rigid surely played a
major role in the failure.

3.1.4
Procedure
MATLAB was used to control the UR5 robot and feed the loudspeaker ampliﬁer with the excitation
signal. The frequency sweep was generated and played through the loudspeaker for each of the 66
microphone positions. The sweep response was then recorded for all positions. Background noise
measurements were conducted to ensure the SNR was suﬃcient across the entire frequency range of
interest. The recorded data, together with the estimated volume velocity, was fed into the MATLAB
algorithm that implements both the HPWM and the PWM, enabling the sound ﬁeld reconstruction.
In order to assess the quality of both methods, two of the planar arrays were used to measure the
sound ﬁeld created by the sweep at the plane z = 1.5 m. These measurements were considered the true
ﬁeld at those positions. The comparison was mathematically calculated using the MAC, as described
in section 2.1.1, and qualitatively judged visually.

Conclusion
Sound ﬁeld reconstructions using the proposed HPWM are deeply analyzed and compared to the
conventional PWM in this thesis. The focus is on low frequency sound ﬁelds within a rectangular
room with an acoustic source inside the domain. The HPWM is shown to be superior to the PWM
by means of both numerical and experimental results when using a multitude of room and array
conﬁgurations.
The method shows a signiﬁcant improvement in reconstruction quality at lower frequencies, due to
the direct modeling of the source, and in the regions closer to the source, where the direct source
contribution is strong. The HPWM is also demonstrated to improve the reconstruction quality even
further for damped rooms, where the reverberant part of the solution becomes weaker. As such, it is
demonstrated that the HPWM can improve reconstructions signiﬁcantly, even when the source is not
in the interior of the domain. It is also shown that, if a certain low-frequency mode is not excited due
to source position, and the array only captures the weak reverberant ﬁeld, the HPWM is still able to
reconstruct the entire sound ﬁeld accurately.
Furthermore, this work shows that the HPWM can be extended to include multiple sources inside the
domain, and that the relative improvement over the conventional PWM scales with the number of
sources modeled accurately.
The HPWM does, however, require information regarding the source in order to eﬀectively model
it. Regarding source position errors, the robustness analysis concluded that errors above 20 cm are
required to underperform the conventional PWM. In practice, simple measurements of source position
with modern equipment should be far below that threshold. However, they are always prone to errors
of smaller magnitude due to the fact that the acoustic center of the equivalent point source can change
with frequency and other factors. A way to improve the method in this regard would be to follow a
more thorough method of acoustic center estimation, such as the methods suggested in [3].
The volume velocity of the point source is also required.
The robustness analysis concluded that
amplitude errors always guarantee improved results (compared to the PWM) so long as the amplitude
is not heavily overestimated. Accurately estimating this parameter, however, is a diﬃcult task in
practice. Although a more precise estimation of this parameter could be done with measurements of
the vibrational velocity U of the diaphragm of the loudspeaker used as the acoustic source, this method
cannot be integrated within the HPWM in any way. What could be done to improve the volume
velocity estimation using the available array measurements would be to pre-process the recordings
with a de-reverberation algorithm in order to make the use of the free-ﬁeld point source equation
more appropriate when estimating Q. The de-reverberation method proposed in [4] could work, as it
does not require any knowledge regarding the domain’s boundary conditions. However, that method
does require the array measurements to enclose the source, severely limiting the possibilities.
Further work could be done to try and integrate source position and volume velocity estimation
algorithms within the HPWM. Given the availability of array measurements, algorithms for source
localization and source amplitude estimation (potentially via de-reverberation) could be implemented
within the HPWM to reduce its dependency of a priori information regarding the source.

Also related to estimation errors, the results suggest that for frequencies above the studied range (above
500 Hz) and if the distance from the source is high enough, using the PWM might be preferable due
to the inevitable errors introduced when modeling the source.
The experimental validation was limited due to two main factors. The ﬁrst of which is the limited
reference plane, which did not contain measurements in the vicinity of the source position for practical
reasons.
This made it impossible to quantitatively verify the low frequency improvement in the
reconstructions around the source, as seen in the numerical results.
Nevertheless, the qualitative
analysis of the results was suﬃcient to observe this improvement intuitively.
The second factor that limited the experimental validation was the need for a more robust choice of
interior array points. While the numerical simulations did not require these interior samples to achieve
good results, the experimental reconstructions behaved diﬀerently. A set of three random interior
points was used, which, despite making a diﬀerence, were insuﬃcient to obtain results that aligned
with the numerical simulations for the higher frequencies. Nevertheless, the two diﬀerent experiments
(with and without damping) were suﬃcient to verify the assessment made in the numerical simulations
regarding superior HPWM reconstructions for weaker reverberant conditions.
