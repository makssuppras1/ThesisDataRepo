Abstract
A new type of commercial hybrid solar panels are hitting the market. These panels are cooled with a ﬂowing liquid,
and are therefore called Photo Voltaic Thermal (PVT) panels. The cooling can give two positive effects. The heated
coolant can potentially be utilized for domestic hot water and/or space heating. Furthermore, the cooling can also
increase the electric efﬁciency of the panel.
The aim of the project was to ﬁnd the temperature coefﬁcient for the power production, open circuit voltage and
short circuit current. The goal was also to ﬁnd the expected temperature of the liquid exiting a PVT panel, and
what this heated liquid can be used for. Finally, the goal was to answer how PVT panels can become technically
and economically feasible. Doing a literature study on the topic, as well as performing experiments where energy
production of a PVT panel was examined was done in order to investigate this.
Four experiments were performed in an attempt to answer the questions mentioned. First of all, a short circuit
experiment and an open circuit experiment were performed to get a characterisation of the PV part of the PVT panel,
and to see how the insolation and temperature affect the current and voltage. These experiments show that the open
circuit voltage in a PV cell is highly dependent upon temperature. The experiments showed a decrease in open circuit
voltage of 12 % with a temperature increase of 22 ◦C. The results also indicate that the short circuit current is mostly
dependent upon insolation, and not sensitive to changes in temperature.
An experiment was also performed in order to investigate the electrical performance of the PVT panel, and how
the electrical power output is changed when the PV cells are being cooled or not. The experiments show that the
electrical power output is improved when the PV cells are cooled down. The results from the experiment performed,
show a reduction in power production of 8 %, when the cooling of the PV cells is turned off.
The experiment performed to investigate the thermal performance of the PVT panel indicate that the thermal power
output is lower when electricity is produced, compared to when a panel solely works as a solar collector. The results
in this project shows a reduction in thermal output of 9 % when the PV cells produce electric power, compared to
when they are disconnected. The thermal power comes from the temperature increase in the water running trough
the PVT panel. This temperature increase is maximum 3.49 ◦C with the PVT panel and ﬂow rate level used in this
project.
The results explained above indicate a voltage temperature coefﬁcient of 0.5 % per K, and a temperature coefﬁcient for
the power production of 0.7 % per K. From the results obtained in this project, it does not seem like the temperature
of the panel has an effect on the short circuit current. In order to give an answer to whether or not there is a value in
taking PVT panels into use, additional research should be performed. It is necessary to perform further research to
ﬁnd out what the value of the lukewarm water exiting the PVT panels is. Such a project is already under planning
at DTU Electro in collaboration with several partners, and an application for funding of the research has been
submitted.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter will provide a brief introduction to the problem researched in this project, and
give a broader understanding to how this problem is connected to the overall problems that
society is facing today. A problem formulation will also be presented in this chapter, as well as
a brief introduction to how the problem might be solved.
1.1
Background
Global warming is one of the biggest challenges our society is facing over the next decades.
There is a strong scientiﬁc consensus that the modern society’s emission of greenhouse gasses
contributes strongly to the changes in the climate. [4] The IPPCs Fifth Assessment Report
concludes, with a 95 % certainty that:
"the human inﬂuence on the climate system is clear and is evident from the increasing greenhouse gas
concentrations in the atmosphere, positive radiative forcing, observed warming, and understanding of the
climate system." [5]
In the same report, the IPPC deﬁnes mitigation as a human intervention to reduce the sources
or enhance the sinks of greenhouse gases. [5] One way to reduce sources of greenhouse gases is
to utilize more renewable energy as opposed to fossil energy. Some of the obvious renewable
energy technologies are solar panels, as in PV panels or solar collectors.
The global solar industry has experienced an explosive growth over the last years, and is one
of the renewable energy technologies beneﬁting from widespread social acceptance. Further
research on solar energy can lead to further growth and diversiﬁcation in applications. [6]
Of the radiation hitting a PV panel, only a small part of the energy is converted into electricity
[7]. Most of the energy is generated to heat. In addition, heat is generated from the electrical
losses in the PV panels. A sensible thought is to utilize all the thermal energy available as well.
Conventional PV panels are only cooled by means of ambient air. However, a new type of
commercial hybrid solar panels, referred to as PVT panels, is hitting the market. This new type
of solar panel is cooled with a liquid coolant. The heated water could potentially be utilized for
e.g. domestic hot water or space heating.
In addition to being able to utilize the heat, cooling the PV laminate with a ﬂowing coolant can
increase the electrical efﬁciency, as the efﬁciency of a PV panel is dependent on temperature.
Theoretically, the efﬁciency of a PV panel is, depending on the technology, varying with a
coefﬁcient of 0.5% per degree Celsius [8].

There are many studies published on the topic of PVT panels recently. One of them is from
The Institute of Sustainable Energy Technology at University of Nottingham. They did a study
where different PVT technologies were assessed. The study concludes that liquid cooling is
preferable to air-cooling. The reason is that liquid cooling gives a more efﬁcient utilization
of the energy capture. Additionally, liquid cooling gives a more homogeneous temperature
on the surface of the PV module. The study also goes into detail describing how a PV cell is
dependent on temperature. [7]
"Performance evaluation of new type hybrid photovoltaic/thermal solar collector by exper-
imental study" [9] is also one of the many studies on PVT published lately. A new type of
PVT module is tested for electrical and thermal efﬁciency. This new PVT module use liquid
cooling where the pipes are placed in graphite. The results showed that the PVT module had
a maximum electric power output of 79 W while a PV panel with the same technology had
a maximum power output of 70 W at the same time under the same conditions. This can be
linked to the fact that the back of the PV panel had a temperature up to 16.74 ◦C higher then
the PVT panel. The Study also experienced that the thermal efﬁciency of the PVT panel was
better with low water temperatures, of around 20 ◦C. [9]
A study on PVT panels was performed at DTU in collaboration with a Danish producer of PVT
panels, Racell, in 2014. This study focused on the thermal performance of PVT panels, and
looked at the best operating temperature in order to get the most thermal energy out of the
panel. The conclusion was in the end similar to the other studies mentioned above, that a low
system operating temperature is important for a high yearly energy output. The report also
states that hot water preheating, ventilation air preheating, pool heating or connection to the
cold side of a heat pump are possible applications for the thermal energy output from the PVT
panel. [10]

This section will go into depth of how a photo voltaic cell works, how many cells are connected

2.1.1
Semiconductors
A solid material can be an insulating or a leading material, depending on the arrangements
of atoms. Some solids have a their atoms arranged in speciﬁc patterns. Such a solid is called
a "crystalline solid". The atoms in a crystal are held together in a speciﬁc pattern by "bonds".
There are ﬁve different bonds that can hold the atoms in a crystalline solid together; ionic,
covalent, metallic, hydrogen and molecular. [1]
Crystalline solids with metallic bonds are good conductors as they have ’free’ electrons. Solids
with covalent bonds however, are generally electrical insulators. Bonds are in some cases formed,
by two or more of these idealized forms of bonds, and between insulating and conducting
materials, there is a group of solids called "semiconductors". These do not have a free electron,
but at room temperature they show a modest electrical conductivity, which increases with
temperature. [1]
There are many semiconductor crystals, but silicon and germanium are the most important
ones. The two dimensional lattice structure of silicon is showed in ﬁgure 2.1. As can be seen
from the ﬁgure, each atom has four nearest neighbours. A silicon atom has 14 electrons, and 4
in the outermost orbit. These four electrons are called "valance-electrons" which form "covalent
bonds" with the four neighbouring atoms. [1]
In chemically pure silicon which contains no foreign atoms, this ideal situation exist at 0 ◦K, and
the crystal behaves like an insulator since no free electrons are available. When the temperature
of the crystalline silicon is raised, or light hits the surface, the valance bonds brake, and the
valance electron becomes a free electron leaving behind a "hole" as showed in ﬁgure 2.2. The
hole is an incomplete covalent bond. [1]
It takes energy equal to "band energy", Eg, a characteristic parameter of a semiconductor, to
separate an electron from the bond and make it a free electron. For silicon, Eg is equal to 1.1 eV
at room temperature. When a hole already exists in a bond, it is easy for an electron belonging

2.1.2
Photovoltaic Cells
Photovoltaic cells are cells made of semiconductor materials that use a p-n junction to convert
energy present in both direct and diffuse solar radiation to direct current (DC) electricity. A p-n
junction is the interface between a p-type and n-type semiconductor. The usual way to create
such a junction is to dope a slice of crystalline semiconductor with donor type atoms at one
side, and acceptor type atoms on the other. [1]
The junction region is depleted of moving charges, because the holes near the junction of the

p-region disappears due to electrons that have diffused across the junction. This region is
called "depletion region" or "space charge region", and has a width of about a few tenths of a
micrometre. On each side of this region, charge carriers exist in the holes in the p-region and
electrons in the n-region. As the charge in these regions are not uniform, an electric ﬁeld and a
contact potential difference is created. This represents a potential energy barrier. [1] [8]
As light falls on the photovoltaic cell, the semiconducting material absorbs the light photons
of certain wavelengths. Electrons and holes are put in motion and diffuse to the p-n junction
where there is as strong electric ﬁeld as explained above. However, the electrons and holes
cannot move through this electric ﬁeld, and produce an electric current through the electric

There are many semiconductor technologies available, but the silicon material in any form is
best suited for the fabrication of PV cells, due to low costs, and the fact that it is the most
abundant element available on earth. [1] Some semiconductor technologies using silicone are
explained in more detail below.
Mono-Crystalline Silicone
Mono-crystalline cells are ﬁrst generation PV cells. The semiconductor is extensively researched,
and is understood well compared to other semiconducting materials used in solar cells. The
most common way to grow pure silicon into a crystalline block is the principle of Czochralski
8
==PAGE:23==
MARTE S. MOKLEIV • MASTER THESIS • CHAPTER 2. THEORY
process. This process involves putting a single seed of crystalline silicon in contact with a melt
of high purity silicon. The atoms of the molten silicon adapt to the pattern of the seed and as
it cools, it solidiﬁes into a single crystal structure. The crystal is then sliced into thin slices,
and textured to improve solar energy absorption. The thin slices of silicon are made into p-n
junctions by doping the wafers at high temperature. Boron and phosphorous are the most
common dopants, although gallium is a more promising dopant for p-type silicon wafers then
boron. This is because boron is proven to cause a defect giving a drop in efﬁciency of about 1-2
% over time due to a light-induced degradation in the cell. [1]
Silicon materials have been thoroughly studied, and mono-crystalline photovoltaic cells are
looked at as a reliable source of electric power. Efﬁciencies of 14 - 17 % are common. The
highest efﬁciency veriﬁed for mono-crystalline PV cells is over 24 %.[1]
The disadvantage with mono-crystalline silicone is that the cost is very high. One of the reasons
for this is that a lot of good crystalline material is lost as sawdust when cutting the solid
crystal blocks into thin slices. Mono-crystalline Silicone PV panels used to dominate the PV
market, but after 2000, the market share was reduced to around 40 % due to competition from
poly-crystalline silicone cells.
Poly-Crystalline Silicone
Poly-crystalline silicone is produced in a casting process where molten silicone is poured into a
rectangular mould and cooled into a silicon block with large columnar grains. This block is then
cut into "bricks" where the edges on all sides are removed because they are low quality regions.
The process of cutting and processing the brick of poly-crystalline silicone, and the waste from
the process is much like the one for mono-crystalline silicon. The casting process is however
cheaper and less sophisticated than the Czochralski process used for mono-crystalline silicone.
This is the biggest reason why poly-crystalline silicone is 20 % cheaper then mono-crystalline
silicone. [1]
The advantages with poly-crystalline silicon technology are lower capital costs, and a higher
throughput for producers. This technology also has a lower sensitivity to the quality of the raw
material, and the rectangular or square shape of the cells gives a higher packing density of cells
in a PV module then if mono-crystalline silicone is used. [1]
PV modules made out of poly-crystalline silicone, generally have an efﬁciency lower then that
of a mono-crystalline PV module. The average efﬁciency of commercial poly-crystalline silicon
cells was 12.6 % in 2010 [1]. The best poly-crystalline silicone cells are reported to have an
efﬁciency of 18.6 %. [1].
A new production method for polycrystalline silicon has been under development over the last
years, and is called "Edge-deﬁned Film-fed Growth" (EFG). A laser can be used to cut these
silicon blocks into thin slices. This reduces the waste dramatically, and is the most economical
and environmentally friendly wafer production process. There are however challenges related
to the temperature control in the process as a necessary temperature stabilisation of ± 1 ◦C is
difﬁcult to achieve. Another production method that has an aim of reducing silicone waste is
the String Ribbon (SR) technology. [1]
Amorphous Silicone
Unlike mono-crystalline and poly-crystalline silicone, amorphous silicone is prepared as a thin
ﬁlm of silicone with an amorphous surface having no lattice order. The ﬁlm is placed on a large
ﬂat plate of glass, stainless steel or polymer. Cadmium telluride (CdTe) and copper indium
gallium di-selenite (CIGS) are other materials used for thin ﬁlm PV cells.[1] [8].

Doping shortens the lifetime of minority carriers in an amorphous silicone cell severely, hence
decreasing the efﬁciency. A p-i-n structure is thus used for the cell formation, so that the solar
light is absorbed in the un-doped i-region and also generates electron-hole pairs in the same
layer. The efﬁciency of a cell with such a structure is over 12 %, but it will be decreased to 5 %
in a matter of months. By using several of these p-i-n structures with different energy band gap
i-layers, the solar spectrum is absorbed more efﬁciently, and the cell stability is improved. [1]
The amorphous silicone does however contain structural defects on the surface. These structural
defects cause the thin ﬁlm silicone technology to have a lower efﬁciency than the mono- and
poly-crystalline technologies. The highest stabilized efﬁciency is just over 8 % for a single-
junction module, just over 9.5 % for a double-junction module, and just over 10 % for a
triple-junction module. [1]
Although the thin ﬁlm technology have a lower efﬁciency, it is scalable, and the production can
be in an automated process, which leads to much lower production costs. A big advantage is
also that the thin ﬁlm PV panels produce energy under low light conditions with reasonable
conversion efﬁciency. Other advantages with the thin ﬁlm technology are that it is lightweight
and ﬂexible. This enables integration of PV panels into building structures. [8] [1]
The thin-ﬁlm technology brought a revolution to solar cell manufacturing, because the materials
are so cost effective. This low cost has made thin-ﬁlm PV modules very popular over the last
years despite the low efﬁciency. [1]

An I-V diagram can explain a PV panel‘s characteristic. Tests to ﬁnd a panels I-V curve are

power production, current and voltage at the maximum power point and the ﬁll factor. From
this information an I-V curve like the one showed in ﬁgure 2.6 can be found. In the I-V diagram,
the maximum current is the short circuit current, and it decreases as the voltage in the panel
increases. The panels’ current reaches zero when the voltage is at its maximum, the open circuit
voltage. [1]
The equivalent circuit of a photovoltaic cell is displayed in ﬁgure 2.7, where Iph is the current

of the junction are shorted. The short circuit current is thus a function of cell design, and is
proportional to photon ﬂux. If the load is an open circuit, the corresponding voltage is the open
circuit voltage. [1]

Notice however, that the I-V curve will change with changing conditions, thus the maximum

2.1.5
Connection of PV cells
A DC voltage of around 0.55 V is generated across a typical photovoltaic cell, regardless of the
design. However, a panel usually consists of more then one cell in order to achieve a higher
capacity. A higher voltage level is then obtained by connecting the cells in series.[8]
However, a problem appears, when connecting the cells in series. When cells are connected in
series, the current is never higher then the current in the cell producing the least in a panel. If
any of the cells then have a small shadow over it or a physical crack, then the production in the
entire panel will be limited. Placing some bypass diodes in the panel solves this problem. If a
shadow then falls over a cell, the current will bypass this area of the panel, so that that some
of the cells in the panel still can produce power, and the entire panel is not shut down. The
conﬁguration of where diodes are placed varies in each panel design. [1]
A PV installation often require more power then what can be delivered by one panel. In this
case, several panels are connected in large units called arrays. The modules in the array can be
connected together in both series and parallel, depending on the voltage and current level that
is desirable as input to the inverters. [1]
2.1.6
Solar Inverter
The solar inverter has two purposes. One is to convert the electricity from DC to AC. An
important task for the inverter is also to control the voltage in the panel so that it is operating
at the point in the I-V curve giving the maximum power production at all times. This is called
MPP (maximum power production) tracking. With recent advances in solid state switching
devices, inverters are becoming very efﬁcient, and an efﬁciency of about 97 % is common. [8]
MPP trackers, also called charge controllers, makes sure the voltage level in the panels always
are at the maximum power production point in the I-V curve. Such a controller usually is a
DC-DC converter, which keeps the output voltage constant, and varies the input voltage with
the maximum power point. [1]
The output power from the MPP tracker is the input to a full bridge-switching conﬁguration,
which inverts the voltage and current from DC to AC. Based on the application of the power
from the solar array, the inverter can give a square wave, quasi-square wave, or a pure sine
wave. A number of devices like power transistors, thyristors, MOSFETS and IGBTs, can be used
for the design of power stages in inverter technologies, depending on the output power. The
important characteristics when choosing an inverter for a PV installation are reliability and
efﬁciency. Different inverter concepts can be used in connection with a PV system, and some of
them are explained below.[1]
A module inverter is connecting a single PV module to the grid. A disadvantage of this
arrangement is however, that the inverter efﬁciencies decrease with smaller installations. [1]
A string inverter connects a string of PV panels to one inverter, and they usually have a capacity
to invert power between 500 W and 2.5 kW. [1]

2.2
Thermal Solar Systems
The vast majority of thermal solar systems produce energy to heat domestic hot water because
the demand of domestic hot water is relatively stable throughout the year, even with changing
seasons. This way, the size of the collector area can be optimized such that the increased solar
availability in summer can be used efﬁciently. [3]
Energy from solar collectors can also be used for space heating. Increasing efﬁciency on storage
technology, and increased use of heating systems at low temperatures enable solar energy to
be used for space heating. A challenge when using solar energy for space heating is that the
production is at its peak in the period where heating is not necessary. [3]
However, there are some systems that combine heating of domestic water with space heating
in transitional periods. This type of system is common in Denmark, Sweden and Germany. If
a building is build as a low-energy or passive house, solar collectors mounted on the roof of
a building can provide 50-100 % of the buildings energy need for space heating and potable
water. Normally insulated houses can have 20-50 % of the heat consumption covered by solar
energy. [3]

2.2.1
Solar Collectors
A solar collector is the driving force in a thermal solar energy system. The collector gathers
energy from the suns insolation. This energy is transferred to the heat transfer ﬂuid circulating
in the solar collector. There are many types of solar collectors, but the most common ones are
ﬂat plate collectors and vacuum tube collectors. These are described in more detail below.
Flat plate collectors have a market share of 85-90 % for systems that heat potable water with
solar energy. The ﬂat plate collector has a simple, and robust structure. They are protected
against heat loss with insulation material like rock wool, and are covered up with hardened
highly transparent glass that is designed to let most of the light trough instead of it being
reﬂected. The casing and the back are often in a metal like aluminium, zinc or stainless steel.

2.3
PVT Panels
As explained in 2.1.3, cooling down PV modules so that they have a homogeneous low
temperature can enhance the power output. This can be done with natural air-cooling, forced
air-cooling, or liquid cooling. The more energy efﬁcient way would be to utilize the heat
removed from the PV module. This waste heat recovery means that with removing the problem
18
==PAGE:33==

of warm PV modules, you can get a waste heat supply to e.g. space heating. This is the concept
of PVT panels. When the waste heat is utilized one can reach conversion efﬁciencies up to 70 %.
Kern and Russel were the ﬁrst ones to address PVT panels in 1978. [7] [13] They state that PVT
panels are interesting because they cover the function of both a PV panel and a solar collector
in one panel, which saves space. However, even though one PVT panel produce more energy
then one PV panel alone, or one solar collector alone, it does not offer as much energy as both
of those panels together. They also argue that since the investment of a PVT panel is greater
then the investment for a PV panel or a solar collector, one needs to analyse weather or not the
beneﬁts of a PVT panel can match the greater investment. [13]
There are many ways of extracting heat in PVT panels, included air-based PVT and liquid based
PVT. Liquid based PVT is preferable to air based PVT, as the temperature in the PV cells is
more homogeneous with the use of liquid cooling. Water/coolants also have a higher speciﬁc
heat capacity then air, giving a higher performance. Although some refrigerants that can go
through a phase change at relatively low temperature have been adopted in some systems,
water is the most common ﬂuid used in PVT panels. [7]
The traditional temperature that is used in district heating networks today is 80 ◦C as a supply
temperature, and 40 ◦C as return temperature. [14] PVT panels will not, however, be able to
deliver temperatures in this range. According to [10], a supply temperature between 20 ◦C and
30 ◦C is preferable in order to achieve a good thermal output. Hence, there is a limitation to
what the thermal energy from PVT panels can be applied for.
The application areas for the thermal energy from a PVT panel is limited due to the low
temperature, but according to [10], possible areas of use include ﬂoor heating and heating of
domestic water in connection with a heat pump.
Floor heating requires lower temperatures than radiators. According to [15], the temperature
of ﬂoor when being heated should be between 19 ◦C and 29 ◦C. Using hot water from district
heating will not be suitable, as the temperature is too high. PVT panels might however be a
very good supplier of energy for ﬂoor heating as the output temperature is expected to be in
this range.
The optimal temperature of domestic hot water is 55 ◦C. [16] A water to water heat pump
could be used to warm up domestic hot water up to this temperature by using the heated water
exiting the PVT panel as an energy source. Such a system can, according to [17], give a COP of
the thermal system of 6.7.

Conversion Efﬁciency
In order to check the performance of a PVT panel, [9] calculate the conversion efﬁciency. The
conversion efﬁciency is the sum of the efﬁciency of the PV part of the panel, as well as the
efﬁciency of the thermal part of the panel. This is expressed in equation 2.8.
ηPVT = ηpv + ηth
(2.8)
The conversion efﬁciency can however be misguiding since electrical and thermal energy cannot
be directly compared. To solve this, [9] suggest to calculate Primary Energy Saving efﬁciency.
As displayed in equation 2.9, the only difference from the conversion efﬁciency is that the
efﬁciency of the PV part of the panel is divided by a factor, ηp, with a value of 0.38. This factor
represents the conversion efﬁciency of a conventional thermal power plant, which is normally
just below 40 %. [18] [9]

The intention of this chapter is to explain the experiments performed in order to answer the
projects problem formulation. The purpose of the experiments, what the different experiments
are expected to give an answer to and the experimental set up is explained.
3.1
Purpose of experiment
The purpose of the experiments performed in this project is to ﬁnd out if liquid cooling enhances
the performance of PV panels, by calculating the panels‘ temperature coefﬁcient from the result
of the measurements. Furthermore, the hope is that the experiment will verify what the output
temperature can be expected to be, and how much thermal energy that can be expected from a
PVT panel at different input temperatures.

A SMA Sunny Boy inverter was connected to the PVT panel. The inverter was then connected

inverter, and the grid voltage is at a good level. The SMA Sunny Boy inverter has a maximum

Meterological Measurements
In order to verify the measurements done, and to perform calculations, it is important to have

3.3
Conducting Experiments
Different experiments are performed to answer different parts of the problem formulation. The
different experiments performed are explained in more detail in this section.
3.3.1
Characterisation of Panel
Characterisation of the panels used for the experiments is important in order to ﬁnd information
on the behaviour of the PV cells. This experiment was also performed with the intention of
ﬁnding the temperature coefﬁcient of the short circuit current and the open circuit voltage. Two
experiments were performed in order to answer this.
For the ﬁrst experiment, the PVT panel was connected in a short circuit over an hour. This
was done in attempt to ﬁnd a relationship between the short circuit current, insolation and
temperature.
The second experiment performed in order to characterise the PVT panel was an open circuit
experiment. The panel was connected in an open circuit over several hours. For some of the
time, water was running trough the PVT panel and cooling it, and some of the time the cooling
was turned off. This was done to test how the open circuit voltage behaved with the PVT panels
temperature varying. This experiment was performed in order to ﬁnd a relationship between
open circuit voltage, temperature and insolation.
3.3.2
Electrical Performance
On a clear day, the PVT panel was operated as intended, with PV cells connected to the grid
and water ﬂowing trough the absorber. In the middle of the day, the water circulation was
turned off for a period. The goal was for this period to show how the photovoltaic cells operate
differently when being cooled or not, and to ﬁnd out how much performance is dropped.
3.3.3
Thermal Performance
The fourth experiment performed, also had the PVT panel running as normal most of the day.
However, the PV cells were disconnected for a period in the middle of the day. This experiment
was performed in order to see how using the panel to produce electricity would affect the
thermal energy extracted from the panel.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The results presented in chapter 4 and their validity will be discussed in this chapter. This
chapter will also try to answer the question raised in the problem formulation.
5.1
Characterisation
The ﬁrst results presented in chapter 4 were the results of the short circuit and open circuit
tests performed on the PVT panel. The short circuit current seems to behave in line with the
solar insolation, and seems unaffected by small variations in temperature. This corresponds
well with section 2.1.3, stating that PV cells short circuit current is not that dependent upon
temperature, but is very dependent on the insolation. However, the variations in the insolation
during the exercise were small, and ideally, a new experiment should have been performed
with bigger variations in insolation and temperature in order to make solid conclusion.
The 12 % drop in open circuit voltage as the temperature in the PVT panels rise to 55 ◦C corre-
sponds well with what is mentioned in 2.1.3 about the open circuit voltage being dependent on
temperature. However, the 12 % voltage drop is still lower then the ﬁndings in [8], where it is
stated that the drop in open circuit voltage can be up to 14 % in the middle of the day if the
temperature of the panel exceeds 43 ◦C.
The absolute temperature coefﬁcient for the open circuit voltage was found to be -0.2 V per K,
and the relative temperature coefﬁcient -0.5% per K. However, there are many uncertain factors
in these experiments, and there is thus an uncertainty related to these ﬁgures.
5.2
Electrical Performance
The experiment explained in 3.3.2 was performed in order to see how much the electrical
performance of the photo voltaic cells can be improved when being cooled. The results showed
that the power production decreased as the cooling was stopped. The cooling was turned
off in the afternoon, when the power production already was supposed to decrease, but the
slope of the electricity production clearly change, and decrease faster, as the temperature in
the PVT panel starts to increase. The power production actually decreases with 8 % in only 24
minutes. There is however uncertainty related to this number as it is hard to say how much of
the decrease in power is from reduced solar insolation, and how much is from a higher panel
temperature.

The thermal power had a peak as water started ﬂowing trough the panel again. This is probably
because of the high temperature difference between the PVT panel and the water now entering.
As the cooling is turned back on, the response in the power production is an increase of 2.5
% in only 3 minutes. An increase of 2.5 % at 5 pm when the insolation is low and decreasing,
is a strong indication that the power production is increased as the PV cells are cooled. The
temperature coefﬁcient for the electrical power production is found to be -0.7 % per K.
That the insolation during the day was stable and without ﬂuctuations, and is not the reason for
the drop in voltage, which again builds under the conclusion that the electrical power output is
increased by cooling the PV cells.
5.3
Thermal Performance
The experiment explained in section 3.3.3 is performed in order to ﬁnd out more about how the
thermal power output is affected by the electric power production in the PV cells.
When the PV cells are reconnected to the inverter after being disconnected for half an hour, the
electric power increases to 200 W in a matter of seconds. However, it takes 12 minutes for the
thermal power to react, and decrease with 9 % . This is due to thermal inertia. Merriam-Webster
deﬁne thermal inertia like this:
"the degree of slowness with which the temperature of a body approaches that of its surroundings and
which is dependent upon its absorptivity, its speciﬁc heat, its thermal conductivity, its dimensions, and
other factors"
This means the dimensions of the PVT panel, the thermal conductivity, the speciﬁc heat and
absorptivity causes a delay to when the thermal power output increases or decreases as the
panel is introduced to a higher or lower energy level.
As for the temperature increase of the water running trough the PVT panel, the maximum
temperature difference was 3.49 ◦C in the experiments performed in this project. In a PVT
panel, the aim is to have a low uniform temperature on the surface of the panel in order to avoid
a limitation of power production because some cell are warm. The low temperature difference
achieved in the experiment is therefore reasonable. In comparison, if the panel is only run as a
solar collector, the temperature throughout the panel would not have to be uniform. The ﬂow
of water trough the panel could thus be lower, resulting in a higher temperature difference in
the panel and a higher thermal power output.
5.4
Value of Cooling PV Panels
The technical feasibility of PVT panels is dependent upon what is the wanted output. If the
demand is high for hot water, but the electricity is considered a bi-product, a solar collector
might be a better idea as it can give a higher temperature difference of the water running
through the panel and thus provide higher energy output. However, if the main demand is
electricity, but lukewarm water delivered by a PVT panel could be utilised for something useful,
PVT panels can be a very good idea. It can be a good idea because a higher power output can
be achieved by cooling the PV cells, and because energy savings can be made for e.g. domestic
hot water or space heating.
The experiments performed in this project were executed in the summer. The value of cooling
the PV cells in winter might not be as high as the ambient temperature will contribute to cool
down the panel as well. PVT panels are not constructed in the same way as solar collectors

and are not as protected from heat loss to the surroundings. It might actually be difﬁcult to
utilise the energy before it is loosed to the surroundings. Additional research should therefore
be initiated, looking in to the performance of PVT panels in the winter months.
In order to conclude whether or not PVT panels are a good idea, further research should also
be done on areas of use for the lukewarm water in the PVT panel. This is important because
the increase in electrical power produced is not alone enough to cover for the extra cost of the
PVT panel, compared to a PV panel. According to [20], the investment is 10 % more expensive
then an individual PV panel and solar collector, but that manufacturers hope the price will
decrease as volume builds. Although the investment is higher, the payback time can be shorter,
according to [21].
The experiments performed in this project did not have cooling on the water tank. This caused
the inlet temperature of the water to increase over the course of the day. Experiments should be
performed where the thermal energy is utilised. This will give results on how the PVT panel
works with more constant inlet temperature, and give answers to how valuable the thermal
energy is. Such a project is already under planning at DTU Elektro in collaboration with DTU
BYG, DTU MEK, Metro Therm and Racell Saphire Technologies, and an application for funding
of the research has been submitted.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
A characterisation of the PVT panel examined in this project was performed experimentally.
These experiments show that the open circuit voltage in a photo voltaic cell is highly dependent
upon temperature, and can in fact decrease with 12 % with a temperature increase of 21.8
◦C, resulting in a temperature coefﬁcient of -0.5 % per K. The results also indicate that the
short circuit current is mostly dependent upon insolation, and not sensitive to changes in
temperature.
The experiments in this project show that the electrical power output from PV cells are improved
when cooled down. The results from the experiment performed in this project, show a reduction
in power production in 8 %, when the cooling of the PV cells is turned off, with a temperature
coefﬁcient of -0.7 % per K.
The experiments performed to investigate the thermal performance indicate that the thermal
power output is lower when electricity is produced, compared to when a panel solely works as
a solar collector. The results in this project shows a reduction in thermal output of 9 % when
the PV cells produce electric power, compared to when they are disconnected. The thermal
power comes from the temperature increase in the water running trough the PVT panel. This
temperature increase is maximum 3.49 ◦C with the ﬂow rate used in this project.
In order to give an answer to whether or not there is a value in taking PVT panels into use,
additional research should be carried out. It is necessary to perform further research to ﬁnd
out what the value of the lukewarm water exiting the PVT panels is. Such a project is already
under planning at DTU Electro in collaboration with several partners, and an application for
funding of the research has been submitted.
