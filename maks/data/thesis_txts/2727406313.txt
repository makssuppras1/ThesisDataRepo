Abstract
This project is based on a test rig located at the Technical University of Denmark.
The test rig uses active magnetic bearings to levitate and stabilize a rotor, controlled
by an PID position controller. The rotor can be rotated by an external motor con-
nected with a flexible coupling.
A mathematical model of the system is developed and a simulation of the model is
created. An experimental frequency response function of the system is performed
which is compared with a frequency response function of the simulated model. The
simulated system is adjusted with minor scaling factors, in order to improve simi-
larity between the systems.
On basis of the model, a sliding mode controller is designed in order to increase
the robustness of the controller. The design, includes parametric uncertainty of the
coupling forces and the inertia of the rotor due to assumptions of constant diam-
eter. Together with the controller, a 1st and a 2nd order surface is designed. The
designed controller with the corresponding surfaces are simulated and yields good
results for both surfaces. Following good performance in simulation, the controllers
are implemented on the test rig, however here the performance is not satisfactorily.
Some modifications is applied but without acceptable results.
Thus, a super twisting sliding mode controller is designed with knowledge of the cou-
pling uncertainty. The controller is also simulated and afterwards implemented on
the test rig, were for both surfaces, the response is acceptable in case of step changes
in both simulation and on the test rig. The designs are tested when the rotor is
spinning in the range of 40-160 Hz, and is finally compared with the PID controller
performance. Here it’s seen that the STSMC with the 1st order surface results in the
best performance, in all rotational frequencies. Besides the performance, other pa-
rameters of the controllers such as design complexity, tuning parameters and power
consumption is compared, were clearly the PID controller is more simple and has a
lower power consumption when rotating.

Introduction
Active magnetic bearings (AMB) has a wide range of advantage including; little need
for maintenance, no need for lubrication, low friction, and a long life time. The per-
formance of the system is barely affected by extreme temperatures and pressures,
making them well fitted for harsh environments. However, due to the highly non-
linear nature of the electromagnetic forces, these bearing types can be difficult to
control. Further, they are expensive and requires more energy to operate, than con-
ventional bearings. Thus the choice of bearing types requires a lot of consideration,
which is not in the scope of this project.
This project aims to investigate the use of non-linear control on a highly nonlin-
ear system such as an AMB system. The project is based on the test rig seen in
figure 1.1, which is a test rig located on DTU, in the basement of building 414.
The test rig includes two AMB’s, used to levitate the rotor, which lies horizontally
in the middle. The rotor is connected to a flexible coupling that limits the axial
movement. The coupling is further connected to a motor, used to spin the rotor at
desired angular velocities.
Figure 1.1: Active Magnetic Bearing system
The test rig features a linear PID-controller that stabilizes the system. Linear con-
trollers can be extremely powerful and due to their ease to implement they are widely
used in the industry. However, since almost no systems are truly linear, the degree of
non-linearity is decisive of controller performance outside of the linearization point.
Linear controllers are tuned and designed to work in the point of linearization, and
if the system is pushed away from this point of operation the performance of the
controller decreases. Depending on the degree of non-linearity the performance can
decrease quite a lot, to the point where the system becomes unstable. This is where
nonlinear controllers excels, by including the systems nonlinearities in the controller
design, performance is not decreased by leaving a specific point of operation. The
downside of nonlinear control, is that it’s often much more difficult to both design,
tune and implement. The goal of this project, is to show that what is gained in per-
formance will over-shine the increased difficulties that comes with this type of design.

In relation to the UN World Goals, AMB’s are not widely spread but its use is
growing. We hope to show that by choosing a nonlinear design their performance
and robustness is increased, making AMB’s a much more desirable choice when
it comes to bearings. We believe that Goal 9.4 fits well, in terms of retrofitting
industries since AMB’s has no need for oil or lubrication and doesn’t need to be
changed/maintained as often as classic bearing systems
In order to investigate the benefits of nonlinear controllers, a mathematical model
of the test rig will be developed (section 2). This model is necessary to design a
nonlinear controller, and the precision of the model will be decisive for the perfor-
mance of the controller. The model will also be the foundation of a simulation that
will be build in Simulink. This simulation will be used to evaluate and tune the
initial controller designs, before implementation on the test rig. In order to ease the
transition from simulation to implementation, quite a lot of time will be put into
making the simulation comprehensive and as realistic as possible. In order investi-
gate the precision of the simulation, it will be validated with experiments from the
test rig (section 3). With knowledge of the system and the simulation as a tool, the
nonlinear controllers can be designed (section 4, section 6). Lastly, the performance
of the PID and the nonlinear controllers will be compared (section 5, section 7).
Simulations, plots and calculations throughout the project will be done in MAT-
LAB, and all files are available in a zip file as an appendix to the digital hand in.

Modelling and System Description
The motor connected to the rotor, can generate torque which accelerates the rotor to
ad angular velocity up to 10.000 (RPM) (≈166 (Hz)) [1]. The coupling is designed
to transfer torque from the motor to the rotor and to minimize translation in the
axial direction. The rotor position in the other two directions are controlled through
the two AMB’s, which are mounted with an equal distance to the rotors center of
mass. The test rig has been adjusted and tested a lot during the years in difference
projects, and therefore the complete system is relatively complicated with a lot of
functionalities, which is outside the scope of this project. There is installed multiple
hall sensors, which measure the currents in the bearings and the position of the
rotor, which are used as feedback to the controller.
Each element of the test rig will be descried further in the following subsections
and the mathematical models of each element will be elaborated. All models are
described in the local reference frame. When combining the models, the system
is described in a global reference frame with the rods center of mass as origin. A
global assumption for the model is that all subsystems are modeled as time-invariant
system.
2.1
Current Dynamics of Bearings
In order to evaluate the current dynamics in the bearings, some simplifying assump-
tions are made in order to limit the complexity of the model. The reasoning for each
of the assumptions will be elaborated through the analysis. The assumptions are:
• Neglecting permeability in iron core
• Neglecting flux leakage and fringing in the air gap
• Isolation between the stator elements ensures that eddy currents will not ap-
pear in the length direction Therefore forces generated in this direction is
neglected
• Neglecting magnetization of iron core

create a magnetic field that affects the rotor. By doing so, a desired forces can act
on the rotor based on the currents sent to the bearings. The expression for the

3.2
Frequency Response Functions
The idea behind the FRF is to analyze the measured input and output by perform-
ing a Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT). The FFT converts the signals from the
time domain into the frequency domain, where the amplitude of the signals in the
different frequencies appears. The specific frequency has a corresponding output in
the Fourier spectrum. The combination of all frequencies with the corresponding
amplitude is the FFT of the signal. The advantage of this method is that the am-
plitude of the Fourier transformed signal is large, when the signal is periodic. It is
known that resonance is periodic, and therefore the frequency where the resonance
is present is clearly visible in the frequency domain.
Since the measured signals are digital, the sampling frequency these measurements
are collected at, dictates an upper limit of how wide a spectrum the FFT can be
done at. In order to compensate for this, one can apply a window function, which
multiplies the signals with a weight function, which force the signal to be zero out-
side the sampling period.
There exist multiple methods to design the windows,
however in this analysis, the Hanning window is used (calculated with the in-build
MATLAB function hann()).
The FRF can be determine in two ways [6].

Implementation of SMC
Most of the ground work regarding implementing the test rig has been done already,
such as installing sensors/ actuators, and calibration of them. All the signals from
the test rig is connected to Data Acquisition system (DAQ) and the signals are fil-
tered by a digital filter. The DAQ is connected to a PC through dSpace-software,
which is connected to an already existing Simulink model with multiple purposes.
The setup has two DAQ’s which runs with two different sampling rates. All data
in this project, is gathered from only one of the DAQ’s, and it is sampled with a
sampling time of 8.3 · 10−5 seconds. Due to this very fast sampling, the process can
be assumed to be real-time. By using dSpace, the system can be operated through
a Graphical User Interface (GUI). For the existing system, a GUI is already imple-
mented. The test rig is fully functionally with a PID-controller implemented and
several years of research has been done on the system already. A considerably num-
ber of hours is put into understanding how the previous implementations was done,
and how signals enters and leaves the control software. Since the system has been
used for a number of projects a lot of data is gathered, however only the relevant
data for this project will be described.
This means, that what the implementation is mostly concerning are the controllers
designed in the project. However, important differences between the test rig and the
theoretical model are found, and this section is dedicated to describe the differences,
how to convert between the two systems and most importantly, the different con-
trollers performance. The GUI is likewise updated in order to operate the system
with the new controllers and to visualize important data from the system, while it
is running.
Since the position of the rotor is measured with hall sensors, these will be influ-
enced by the magnetic/electric difference in the rotor material. This phenomena is
known as electrical runout[10]. In order to compensate for this, the system has a
function to compensate for this[1]. During start up of the system, the rotor should
rotate at least one full rotation, since this will "calibrate" the sensors. This fea-
ture improves the position measurements but is not part of the simulation since the
electrical runout isn’t modeled.

controllers react towards an unknown disturbance, and the FRF shows a transfer
function from controller to the displacement of the rotor.
The configurations of the test are the same as the experiments described in sec-

that can increase the coherence by changing the resolution in the frequency domain,
and thus decreasing noises influence. A minimum resolution of around 1 (Hz) is de-
sired. The consequence of increasing the amount of blocks (and thereby decreasing
the window size) is visualized in appendix in figure N.3 and figure N.4. It shows that
no matter the window size, the same pattern in the coherence is observed. Thus it

7.2.4
Complexity Analysis
Besides the downside of the increased control current, STSMC design is also a more
complicated controller with multiple design parameters. The design of the PID is,
well known and standardized method of designing controllers. STSMC designs are
complex and includes both different surface design and many parameters to tune.
STSMC also (depending on surface design) require information about derivatives of
the states, which are often not measured. Either, one can estimate these or measure
them, but more sensors will increase the cost of the system.

8
Discussion
With this project limited to a five months time period, obviously not everything
can be investigated as thoroughly as one might would have wished for. This section
seeks to discuss what could have been done differently, more precise and what further
work could include. For this discussion, five subjects have been chosen to be the
most important and is given a section each.
Uncertainty
As mentioned, SMC and STSMC is a powerful controller in terms of handling system
uncertainty and producing robust designs with a high performance.
During the
design of both the SMC and STSMC, it was assumed that the mechanical features
of the couplings and the diameter of the rotor was uncertain. While this is true,
it turned out that the influence in the scope of the uncertainty is very small. The
bearings electrical features and their ability to generate force was however assumed
to be perfectly known. In retrospect, this should have been prioritised higher when
modelling, and included in design of the controllers, since this is probably a much
more influential uncertainty.
Another contribution to the uncertainty of the system is unbalanced mass in the
rotor which is a normal undesired aspect of rotating machines. As further work,
it could be interesting to derive a method to estimate the unbalances and include
them in the controller design. If it is possible to estimate these, better disturbance
rejection might be obtained.
Force scaling
The bearings are neither perfectly circular nor generates equal forces in the different
directions with equal inputs. It was attempted to include some of the differences
between the bearings, by introducing the force scaling factor. This is however a
very simple and linear scaling method, which is difficult to obtain perfect results
with. As seen in the different FRF’s presented, when the theoretical model fits in
low frequency regions, it might not in higher, and vice versa. A more precise way of
modelling this, could have been done by making each actuator individual, e.g. the
distances s0 or the electrical resistances R.
Variable η
One of the big challenges in the SMC and STSMC design is to determine good gains.
When using constant gains, it is a difficult task to determine the magnitude of the
disturbance. If the gains are over-estimated, the controller is too conservative which
will effect performance, and if the estimation is too low one will risk that the system
becomes unstable.
When using the variable gain, the disturbance is measured and thus there is no risk
of over/under estimating this. However, the calculation of η, was in this project
still done offline and based on estimations with worst-case situations in mind. This
74
==PAGE:83==
Gustav Stæhr Hassager & Nikki Christoffersen
s144966
s145016
constant was used to guarantee a time to reach criteria for the SMC, but was only
valid in the case of constant gain. The constant was still used, since it gave a estimate
of what this value could be in the case of variable gain. Since acceptable performance
was never achieved with the SMC, further work could include investigating how to
implement a variable η.
The STSMC was implemented with good results. It was however done without any
time to reach criteria, meaning that the performance was obtained by simply tuning
multiplayer gains until a fast enough controller was obtained. This is quite time
consuming, and it’s not very intuitive. Further work, could include investigating if
the time to reach criteria could be implemented on the STSMC solution, preferably
as a variable.
Estimation
Since the surfaces is based on the derivative of the states, a simple lowpass filter
is designed in order to estimate these. The designed filters shows that in order to
reduce the noise, the trade off is the ability to estimate the dynamics, where the
cutoff is most dominant for the acceleration and jerk. This probably have an effect on
the performance of the controller while the rotor is spinning, since of course the rotor
is not in steady state. If the error between the estimate and the true value is large,
this directly impact the controllers ability to cancel elements of the dynamics. In
order to achieve a better estimation of the derivatives, there exist multiple solutions.
One could design a Kalman Filter, which is a linear quadratic estimator. It is off
most importance, to evaluate such a filters performance, when the system is not
at the linearization point, since this is one of the clear disadvantageous of linear
estimators. Another possibility could be to design an Sliding Mode Observer. This
a nonlinear estimator, based on the same principles of the SMC.
Current Control
Lastly, the PI controller for the current has not been thoroughly investigated, as it
was designed and implemented prior to this project. This controllers sets a limit
for how fast the control currents can change, and is thus a limiting factor of the
bandwidth of the complete closed loop system. If this controller could be made
faster, or changed to be a nonlinear controller, maybe some of the performance of
the STSMC could increase.
Further, the stability of the entire closed loop system could have been studied on a
higher theoretically level. In order to prove higher stability properties, it is neces-
sary to investigate the exact properties of the current controller as well, instead of
just assuming GAS.

Conclusion
This project aims to investigate the use of nonlinear controllers for highly nonlinear
systems. A realistic simulation environment is developed in Simulink, based on a
mathematical model of the system. A set of experiments are carried out, in order
to obtain an experimental FRF of the test rig. The mathematical model and sim-
ulation is validated via comparisons of the test rigs FRF and the simulated FRF.
Based on this comparison, fine tuning of the mathematical model is done to further
increase the accuracy of the model.
With a good model and a simulation environment with a satisfying precision, two
position controllers based on the SMC approach are designed. THe difference be-
tween the two controllers are the surface design. One is designed with a 1st order
surface and one is designed with a 2nd order surface. Both controllers shows good
performance in simulation, with similar time responses. The SMC with a 2nd order
surface has less oscillatory control currents which is desirable when implementing.
Both designs are implemented on the test rig, but it’s found that the good results
from the simulation are not possible to obtain. The chatter is too dominating in
order to obtain good results, and in an attempt to reduce the chatter, the switching
mechanism demotes the controller to have a behaviour similar to a proportional
controller.
Due to the poor results from implementing the SMC, the design is expanded with
an Super Twisting-algorithm. The STSMC shows, as seen with the SMC, very good
results in simulation with respect to time response, but with much smoother control
currents. This is again true for both the surfaces. When implementing the new
designs, very good results are seen, similar to the simulation results. This controller
is compared with the linear PID-controller, with a simple test. The controllers are
evaluated on their ability to do step responses and reject mass unbalanced distur-
bances while rotating. In both tests the STSMC’s shows good performance. The 1st
order surface provides the best results of all the controllers, with the 2nd order sur-
face providing similar results as the PID. The STSMC (both surfaces) is, in opposite
to the PID controller, able to handle instantaneous step changes in the reference.
The performance of the controller is also analyzed with respect to FRF’s. It is how-
ever not directly comparable with the responses seen in the rotational experiments,
since the rotational performance is affected by unbalanced mass, and the FRF yields
information about the complete system. It is seen that the STSMC has increased
the stiffness overall and made the system more damped in most frequencies.
The increased performance of the STSMC controller, also has some disadvantages in
relation to the PID. The STSMC is a more complicated design, and require multiple
tuning parameters. Besides the increased complexity, the required control currents
is higher. Overall the trade-off between increased control performance and the cost
of this (in case of energy and complexity) is a key parameter to have in mind when
deciding for a control solution for similar systems.
