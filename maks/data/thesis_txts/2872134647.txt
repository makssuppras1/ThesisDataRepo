Abstract
This thesis presents a methodology for reverse engineering the aerodynamic design of
wind turbine blades based on known operational performance curves, including power,
thrust, pitch, and rotational speed. Using Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory as a
foundation, this work develops simplified models to approximate aerodynamic parameters
such as lift coefficient distribution, blade solidity, and glide ratio.
The reverse engineering approach is used to build a Python tool and is validated on the
DTU 10MW Reference Wind Turbine and then applied to the IEA 10MW and 15MW refer­
ence turbines to verify its robustness. The results demonstrate that the method provides
a good approximation of the thrust coefficient and blade solidity, particularly for high tip­
speed ratios where approximations are more realistic. However, discrepancies in the
power coefficient and glide ratio highlight the limitations of assuming constant drag­to­lift
ratios and some other approximations on the integrals. Thus, there is a need for more
sophisticated high­load corrections and integral hypotheses.
Despite these limitations, the developed tool offers a fast and handy method for a first
blade design without the need for detailed aerodynamic data. Future improvements could
include more advanced integration models and adding aeroelasticity to refine the obtained
design.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In a world facing accelerated climate change, the transition to renewable energy is no
longer a choice, it is a necessity.
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change
(IPCC), in its Sixth Assessment Report (AR6), highlights the critical need to drastically
reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 to keep global warming below 1.5°C above
pre­industrial levels [1]. While this limit is set globally, some regions of the world have
already surpassed the 1.5°C limit, which is worrying for the future, according to the latest
IPCC report. Since electricity generation remains one of the largest contributors to CO2
emissions globally, increasing renewable energy sources in the global electricity mix, par­
ticularly wind and solar, has become a cornerstone strategy for climate mitigation.
In alignment with these environmental objectives, the European Union has adopted am­
bitious policies, including the European Green Deal and the Fit for 55 package, which aim
to reduce net greenhouse gas emissions by at least 55% by 2030 and achieve climate
neutrality by 2050 [2]. Wind energy, both onshore and offshore, is expected to play a key
role in achieving these targets. According to WindEurope, the EU expects to have an
installed capacity of over 590 GW of wind energy generation by 2040 [3], which requires
both massive investment and continuous technological innovation.
Apart from climate change, the war in Ukraine has shown an urgent geopolitical reality:
energy is not only an environmental concern, but also a matter of national security and
sovereignty. The European countries’ past dependence on Russian fossil fuels exposed
serious weaknesses in energy supply chains, forcing governments to change their energy
strategies [4]. In this context, domestic renewable energy sources, especially wind and
solar, are being positioned as key assets for energy independence, resilience, and price
stability. This shift has introduced a new dynamic to the wind industry, making accelerated
deployment not only an ecological choice but also a geopolitical necessity.
One of the main trends in this rapid transformation is the race for larger and more powerful
wind turbines. The most recent commercialized machines now exceed 15 MW in rated
power and 250 meters in rotor diameter, numbers that were inconceivable just twenty
years ago. However, this scaling­up brings new difficulties. Designing blades for such
massive turbines requires not only advancements in material science and structural me­
chanics but also highly adaptive aerodynamic designs that respond to site­specific wind
conditions, energy targets, and structural constraints.
Unlike the beginning of wind development, when standardized designs were deployed
broadly, today’s project developers and manufacturers must tailor blade geometries to
maximize efficiency under local wind conditions, comply with noise and load limitations,
and optimize for annual energy production (AEP). This necessity has led to dynamic blade
design, which requires multiple redesign iterations based on, for example, desired maxi­
mum loads.
This report explores a reverse­engineering approach to blade design, starting from known
operational curves such as power and thrust curves, to evaluate the aerodynamic geom­
etry of the blade that would generate such performance. By exploiting Blade Element
Momentum (BEM) theory and making simplifying assumptions, it is aimed to change the

1.2
Thesis outline
The goal of this thesis is to model and implement a tool to give a good approximation of
the blade design of a wind turbine. This work will focus on blade design using reverse
engineering techniques. The main hypothesis is that reverse engineering, despite the
high complexity of blade design, can be used starting from power, thrust, rotor speed,
and pitch curves, to reach a valid design that is realistic and matches those curves.
Chapter 1: In the first chapter, a quick context and introduction are given to highlight the
importance of wind energy and wind turbine blade design. The problem is identified, and
a quick description of the content of the thesis report is given.
Chapter 2: In the second chapter, a Literature review is conducted on blade design for
wind turbines, and, in a second part, on the application of reverse engineering to wind
turbine design and propellers.

reach the final equations

2
Literature Review
This chapter provides an overview of the state­of­the­art in wind turbine blade design.
Firstly, the standard approach for wind turbine design will be described and presented.
In a second part, the notion of reverse engineering applied to wind turbine and propeller
design will be presented, and the pros and cons of this method will be discussed.
2.1
Classical Approach of Wind Turbine Design
In this section, the standard approach to wind turbine blade design will be discussed to
gain a deeper understanding of the entire process.
2.1.1
Design Phases
In the classical wind turbine design approach, the main objectives are to achieve optimal
power output while maintaining a robust design that can withstand high loads over time.
The design process is well described in the work of Christian Bak [5]. Classical wind tur­
bine design is typically divided into two main phases. The first one is a preliminary design
where the overall sizing is defined, including the rotor diameter, tower height, and rated
power. Then, a second time, a detailed design is created which focuses more on aerody­
namic and structural components such as the blade shape and the material distribution.
This second step is the one that takes the most time in the design because there is a need
to iterate through the whole process, which requires many simulations to assess the de­
sign. It is also where there is a quite accurate estimation of the loads that the turbine will
be subject to and the power output of the designed turbine.
Those two phases are often optimized separately using simplified models in the prelimi­
nary phase and refined using high­fidelity simulations during the detailed phase [6].
2.1.2
Aerodynamic Design Tools
The Blade Element Momentum (BEM) method is the most commonly used aerodynamic
tool for wind turbines’ rotor evaluation. It has been used and enhanced in the work of
Glauert [7], Prandt and Betz [8]. It combines momentum theory with blade element the­
ory with an assumption of radial independence to estimate spanwise aerodynamic loads
[9]. It can be coupled with some model corrections concerning tip losses (e.g., Prandtl’s
correction), high loads, yaw and tilt misalignment, etc.
Alternatively, some more advanced tools which include potential flow methods such as
vortex particle method and potential panel method, and mesh­based computational fluid
dynamics (CFD) solvers (Navier­Stokes) can be employed, as discussed in the work of
Hansen and Martin Otto Laver [9].
2.1.3
Structural and aeroelastic Modeling
Structural design traditionally uses Finite Element Models (FEM) for detailed stress, de­
formation, and modal analysis, Modal­based models for faster simulations while keeping
acceptable accuracy, and coupled aeroelastic solvers [9] combining aerodynamics, struc­
tural dynamics and control systems. FEM can range from simple analytical models to
beam model alongside with cross sectional properties or to mesh­based solvers. These
models have a large range of computational complexity and accuracy. Consequently,
there is a tradeoff when selecting one model.

This modelling is included in Time­domain aeroelastic simulation tools, such as Open­
FAST [10] and HAWC2 [11], which are used to evaluate wind turbine behavior under
realistic and turbulent wind conditions.
2.1.4
Design Criteria
The design of wind turbines is governed by a wide range of technical, environmental, and
economic constraints [12].
The design criteria are defined through international standards, such as the IEC 61400­1
standard, which defines a comprehensive set of Design Load Cases (DLCs) to simulate
the environmental and operational conditions that a wind turbine can face through its
operational life.
Regarding economic criteria, the Cost of Energy (CoE) is used as an optimization metric.
CoE accounts for capital expenditures (CAPEX), operational expenditures (OPEX), and
energy production over the system’s lifetime. It is often used as the merit figure in multi­
disciplinary optimization frameworks. The design process aims to find a balance between
high performance and low manufacturing, maintenance, and installation costs.
Structural and Performance Constraints are also considered in the design process. They
ensure that the turbine does not exceed material stress or strain limits, avoids resonance,
maintains tip clearance and deflection bounds under load, complies with manufacturing
constraints (e.g, maximum chord, etc.), and finally limits noise emission and visual impact
in regulated environments.
2.2
Implementation of Reverse Engineering
2.2.1
Concept of Reverse Engineering
Reverse engineering is a design concept in which one attempts to get to the initial design,
getting as much information as possible out of the more or less limited input that one has.
Depending on the system considered and the technology used, the knowledge gained
through this process can fix issues or explain the functioning of something.
2.2.2
Reverse Engineering in Aerodynamic Design
Blade Geometry Reconstruction
For blade geometry reconstruction, 3D scanning technologies can be used. For instance,
a study on the Enercon­40/500 (E40) wind turbine blade utilized 3D scanning to create a
detailed CAD model, enabling further analysis and potential repurposing of the blade in
various applications [13]. Once this is done, the airfoil profile can be estimated using the
cross­section.
In a study made by Ghinea et al., the blade geometry is reconstructed and redesigned
to improve aerodynamic efficiency. The performance is evaluated through simulation and
comparative analysis with the original profile [14].
2.2.3
Structural Design and Material Identification
Reverse engineering also allows identification of composite layups and materials, spar
cap and shear web configurations, and structural model calibration.
Blades often use carbon fiber­reinforced polymers for their strength­to­weight advantages.
In the report by Abbas Khan [15], the Ista Breeze i500 blade was reverse­engineered us­
ing 3D scanning and SolidWorks, then manufactured using composite layup techniques.
Structural performance was validated using FEA (Finite Element Analysis) and strain
gauge testing.

2.2.4
Reverse Engineering as a Design Strategy
Reverse Engineering is not only used for reproducing designs but also to enhance them.
It can be aerodynamic optimization, weight reduction, or noise reduction. The Parametric
3D Blade Geometry Modeling Tool used in turbomachinery (e.g., GE 1.5 MW wind turbine)
exemplifies this approach. It offers efficient blade modeling while using a reduced set of
aerodynamic and geometric parameters, which are suitable for optimization and redesign.
[16]
2.3
Conclusion on the literature review
In wind turbine design, classical design relies on forward modeling and optimization, while
reverse engineering enables the enhancement of existing designs. Combined, they cre­
ate a powerful design loop enabling high­performance, cost­effective wind turbines.

3
Methodology and design choices
In this chapter, the methodology employed throughout the thesis is described and jus­
tified, providing the reader with a clear understanding of the theoretical framework and
assumptions used.
3.1
Description of a wind turbine
A wind turbine is a complex rotating object that extracts the kinetic energy from the wind
and converts it into mechanical energy via the rotation of its shaft, and then into electrical
energy through an electrical converter.
3.1.1
Wind turbine type
Wind turbines can be classified into several designs depending on their axis of rotation and
intended operating conditions. The two main categories are the vertical­axis wind turbine
(VAWT) and the horizontal­axis wind turbine (HAWT). In this report, the wind turbine type
studied is the HAWT configuration, as it is the most common design globally and the
standard for large­scale wind turbine farms.
Figure 3.1 illustrates various hypothetical wind turbine designs influenced by different dis­
ciplinary optimizations, highlighting the necessity of a multidisciplinary perspective for ef­
ficient turbine design.[17]
Figure 3.1: Dream Turbines: A Multidisciplinary View of Wind Turbine Design. (artist:
Rick Hinrichs)[17]
3.1.2
Wind turbine blades
The focus of this work is on the aerodynamic design of the blades. Other components,
such as the controller or the drivetrain, are assumed to be known.

The blade of a wind turbine can be described by its span and is often divided into 2 2­
dimensional airfoils along the blade span for a better understanding. A 2­D airfoil is a
section of the blade, normal to the blade span as presented in Figure 3.2. Each airfoil is
described by its chord, its thickness, and its position along the blade span.
Figure 3.2: Basic concept of an airfoil [18] [19]
Blades are flexible objects subject to aeroelasticity effects, including bending and torsional
deformations. These effects can create undamped vibrational modes of the blade, called
resonance. It can be very destructive for the blade and needs to be accounted for in the
design process.
For the sake of this report and to simplify the modelling, the blades will be considered as
rigid, so flexibility will not be investigated.
3.2
Aerodynamic rotor modelling
In this report, the rotor aerodynamics are modelled using the BEM (Blade Element Mo­

To describe the one­dimensional (1­D) momentum theory, detailed in Bak’s work [5], three
simplifications are made. Firstly, it is assumed that the air flowing through the wind tur­

Secondly, the flow is supposed to be steady, meaning that the direction and the value of
the wind speed are constant. Lastly, the rotor is considered to be a perfect disc, meaning
that it is composed of an infinite number of blades, leading to uniform loading.

6
Conclusion and future work
6.1
Conclusion
This thesis presented the development of a reverse engineering methodology to approx­
imate the aerodynamic design of wind turbine blades starting from known performance
curves: power, thrust, pitch, and rotational speed. The methodology was based on the
Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory and simplified through a set of physically reason­
able approximations to extract key aerodynamic parameters, including the lift coefficient
ratio, blade solidity, and glide ratio.
Initially, the method was developed using the well­documented DTU 10MW Reference
Wind Turbine, demonstrating promising results in thrust coefficients and a reasonable es­
timation of blade solidity. The methodology was then applied to the IEA 10MW and 15MW
reference turbines to further assess its robustness. For both designs, the reconstructed
thrust coefficient CT matched well with the reference data.
However, some discrepancies appeared, particularly for the power coefficient CP and the
glide ratio estimations, suggesting that the simple assumptions regarding drag­to­lift ratio
distribution ( Cd
Cl ) may not capture the full aerodynamic behavior of wind turbines. Addition­
ally, limitations in the high induction region (where a > 1/3) highlight the need for more
advanced corrections beyond the basic BEM theory to properly model flow separation
and wake expansion phenomena.
Despite these limitations, the developed reverse engineering tool provides a useful and
computationally efficient method for estimating blade design parameters linked to the
thrust coefficient from operational performance data, making it a valuable starting point for
early­stage design or retrofit studies where limited aerodynamic information is available.
6.2
Future Work
Building upon the findings of this thesis, several promising directions for future work can
be identified.
Firstly, the Aerodynamic Modeling could be enhanced by introducing high­load correc­
tions, such as Glauert’s correction, to improve the accuracy and robustness of thrust and
power predictions, particularly at low tip­speed ratios.
Then, a blade Glide Ratio Modeling dependent on the radius could be investigated, possi­
bly based on airfoil­specific performance data or machine learning regression on existing
datasets.
The next step would be to extend the methodology to include blade flexibility and aeroe­
lastic coupling, especially important for next­generation large rotors where aeroelastic
deformation can have significant effects on performance and loads.
It is important to keep in mind the structural design in order to couple the aerodynamic
reverse engineering with a structural model, to ensure that the resulting blade designs not
only match aerodynamic targets but also respect structural constraints, like stress limits
and resonance avoidance.
Once finalized, the reverse engineering tool could be turned into an optimization loop
where, starting from initial guesses, an optimized blade design is generated to minimize
