Abstract
In the urgency of climate change, and the need for every country to participate in the
prevention effort. This thesis goal is to study how to build a sustainable strategy for de¬≠
veloping countries. For this purpose, backcasting is applied conjointly to Madagascar and
Ecuador to build climate mitigation of the energy system strategy for 2050. The approach
followed is qualitative and based on the GHG characterisation of a midpoint in 2035 and
the endpoint. This study is based on online available documents and insights from ex¬≠
perts in sustainable development in the considered regions. The goal is set to reduce the
annual GHG emission by 80% compared to the BAU to fulfil the target of limiting the rise
of temperature to 1.5¬∞C under the constraints of a 3% annual GDP growth for both coun¬≠
tries and the forecasted population growth. After an examination of the current situation
of Ecuador and Madagascar, the pathways are created by examination of the consump¬≠
tion and the production of energy and the possible solution to reduce GHG emissions.
The pathways consist of renewable and sustainable energy production and more efficient
ways of consumption. Overall, building a sustainable strategy using backcasting in devel¬≠
oping countries, at least in the climate mitigation of the energy system, is possible. The
identified difficulties are the economic pressure, the available energy resource and the
social situation of the countries.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The Sixth Assessment Report of the IPCC [1] urges countries to act against climate
change, and points out an objective, limiting climate change to 1.5¬∞C to reduce the impact
of climate change to a minimum. Under such a goal, the involvement in the fight of every
country is needed, and international commitment has been made in the Paris Agreement
[2] and reaffirmed more recently at the COP26 [3]. But not every country is equal in this
fight, developing countries have more than just environmental issues they have important
economic and social problems, such as poverty, corruption, or equality. For now, the
development of the more advanced country has been achieved through industrialisation
and the use of fossil fuels, and it is still the path followed nowadays (e.g. UK, Germany,
Japan, China). Their possibility to be more environmentally friendly or the way they vision
their way to be more sustainable is through their position of being already developed and
having the means to pursue these visions for instance with heavy infrastructures in green
energies and established policy framework and institution to create energy system, taxes,
etc. But if the world is to fight against climate change, this path of development is ques¬≠
tionable. Therefore, this Master Thesis is motivated by answering the following question:
how can a sustainable strategy to fight against climate change take form in a developing
country?
1.2
Research approach
To try to answer this question, this Thesis will compare the cases of Madagascar and
Ecuador with the use of the backcasting methodology through the scope of the energy
system and climate change mitigation. The aim would be to create CO2 reduction sce¬≠
narios.
To try to encompass the diversity of developing countries, Madagascar and Ecuador are
chosen to be studied. They are two developing countries at two different stages of de¬≠
velopment. Ecuador, ranked 86th in HDI in 2019 [4], is more developed than Madagas¬≠
car, which is among the least developed country, ranked 164th in HDI in 2019 [4]. The
two countries are different, they belong to two separate geopolitical and cultural areas,
Ecuador being from Northern South America and Madagascar from Southern Africa, and
their size in areas and populations are not comparable. However, both countries present
important similarities. Both present high richness and diversity in natural resources, they
are biodiversity hot spots, they present similar geography and climate patterns, they are
submitted to a similar threat to climate change, and they both have a similar centralised
political system. Furthermore, they face similar challenges, though at different stages:
Poverty, corruption, weak institutions, criminality, and deforestation to name a few. There¬≠
fore with these similarities and differences, a comparison might allow us to draw the par¬≠
ticularity of building a sustainable strategy for developing countries.
The underlining meaning of sustainability that influences this study is as follows. The clas¬≠
sical meaning for sustainable development is the ‚Äùdevelopment which meets the needs
of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own
needs‚Äù as stated in the Brundtland report, Our Common Future [5]. This concept has his¬≠
torically been understood as meeting the three pillars of sustainability, environment, social
and economics [6]. Sometimes this concept has been reduced to an environmental issue

Methodology
2.1
Backcasting
Backcasting is a future planning technique which begins by considering what future we
want to achieve is a method that, given a desired goal in the future, works backwards in
order to draw out a pathway from the present, as it is defined by Robinson [8]. The notion
was introduced by the latter to analyse future energy options [9], according to [10]. This
process of backcasting is more of a creative process to draw out ideas than a demonstra¬≠
tion of scientific results as stated by Dreborg [11]. The backcasting method has mainly
been used in energy cases, for instance in Sweden [12, 13], but it has also been used
in various domains such as the transport system in the Netherlands [10]. And fitted to
this study, backcasting has been found suitable for the environmental challenges, ‚Äùthis
approach can be applied to find ways to prevent the failure of trends and testing the
possibility of new paths along which the sustainable development is obtainable‚Äù [14]. The
methods have been used to cover issues related to sustainability and different levels such
as regions, companies, and social¬≠technical systems [15]: in the Netherlands for sustain¬≠
able developments [16] or in Sweden for the sustainability of companies [17]. Hence the
use of this method appears to be appropriate in our case to draw out a sustainable strategy
in the context of climate change. The previously cited cases mostly concern developed
country but backcasting have previously been applied in developing countries like our
case, for instance, a use case exists in Machakos County in Kenya for the development
of a clean cooking strategy [18]. Other use cases for a whole sustainable strategy exist
like in the case of a village in Nepal [19]. Therefore such a method has been proved to
be an appropriate tool in different regions of the world. Furthermore, the method has also
been applied at the scale of a whole country, so similar to the scale of this study, such as
in the case of participatory backcasting for low carbon energy system in Russia [20], or a
backcasting for better water governance in South Africa [21]. However, even though, the
method is used in a comparative analysis to evaluate the impact of this method in sus¬≠
tainable strategy like in Ghaffar Ali et al. in Asia [22], this study just compared different
backcasting analysis to evaluate its tool, there is no known study using the method as a
comparative approach.
As previously stated, this study will focus on applying the backcasting methodology con¬≠
jointly to Madagascar and Ecuador as a comparative analysis. Because of the vastness of
the subject and the limited time, the scope of this study is limited to climate mitigation and
the energy system of both countries. The year of reference for the current situation is 2019
it is chosen as a compromise between being as close to the present and the availability
of data. Henceforth, all numerical data will be representatives of this reference year, but
when data are not available, the most recent data available is chosen. The target year is
chosen to be 2050 as it is a common target year for a longer term strategy.
The backcasting framework here is an adaptation of Robinson‚Äôs paper [8] summarised in
Figure 2.1, and explained as followed.

2.2
Documentation and sources
This study is conducted remotely from both countries and is mainly based on available
literature and information available online. The choice of the literature examined is made
with the principle of specificity. The documents must be as recent as possible and cor¬≠
respond the most to the country. It is worth noting that the temporal specificity is often
hard to fulfil as recent scientific public are sometimes hard to find, especially in the case
of Madagascar. Recent scientific literature is markedly more developed in Ecuador than
in Madagascar. The criteria of ‚Äùcorresponding to the country‚Äù mean that the documenta¬≠
tion should be about the country the regions explored must be the most similar, i.e. the
closer geographically, culturally, or economically (developing country). The exception is
made for more creative solutions in the pathway generation. The basis of the documenta¬≠
tion of this study, is composed of the known relevant document in terms of climate change
which are the Intended Nationally Determined Contributions (INDC) [23, 24] and the Tech¬≠
nological Need Assessment (TNA) [25, 26] of both countries. Further documentation is
searched through official sources of Ecuador and Madagascar, the UN, and the World
Bank, through search engines specialised in scientific publications (Google Scholar, DTU
find it), and a simple search engine (Google).
In addition, to increase the specificity of the study, interviews of experts around the sub¬≠
ject. The interviewees were selected because of their expertise in sustainability and cli¬≠
mate change in the studied countries. The interviews are relatively loose and follows the
Guideline: Conducting Interviews [27] and adaptation of Strategies for Qualitative Inter¬≠
views [28]. The interview duration was set to 15 min, though more 30 min in practice, a
set of 4 fixed questions have been asked to each interviewee:
‚Ä¢ How is the concept of sustainability perceived in your region?
‚Ä¢ What are the most important objectives for more sustainability?
‚Ä¢ What is the current state?
‚Ä¢ What could be done to achieve more sustainability?
The use of the method of the snowball effect is used to find more interviewees but that
was not carried out with much success. This consist of finding new interviewees on the
recommendation of the previous interviewee.
In total three experts were interviewed :

2.3.2
Pathways characterisation
The pathways and the BAU are characterised at three different points, the 2019 situation,
the mid¬≠term point in 2035, and the end¬≠point 2050. They are characterised by their
energy consumption by source and the associated GHG emissions.
In the 2019 situation, energy consumption is determined by the available data.
The energy consumption follows the consumption defined by the scenarios.
The energy consumption of 2035 and 2050 characterisation are determined by the energy
mix and the efficiencies that comply with the scenario generation, the constraint of CO2
reduction and energy potentials based on modifications of the BAU.
The emission of the pathways generation follows the Greenhouse Protocol methodology
[29] and is calculated based on the energy consumption by energy source multiplied by the
GHG emission factor (EF) of that energy source. They are expressed in CO2 equivalent
over 100 years. This constitutes an estimation in order of magnitude.

3
Vision
In the objective of limiting climate, the best scenario is limiting the increase of temperature
to 1.5¬∞C, this can only be done with a 66% of probability reaching global climate neutrality
by 2050, according to the IPCC sixth report [1]. Furthermore, the report, underlines that
the peak of CO2 emissions should happen within 3 years [1]. Now the question is how
this goal of 1.5¬∞C can be translated to both studied countries and their energy systems.
By signing the Paris accord and publishing their INDC, Ecuador and Madagascar have
fixed their own goals regarding climate change. Ecuador‚Äôs vision regarding climate change
is to stay well below 2¬∞C and as close as possible to 1.5¬∞C, increase resilience and de¬≠
crease vulnerability to the adverse effects of climate change in priority mitigation and
adaptation sectors [23]. For 2025, according to their conditional first NDC, the goal is to
reduce the emission from the BAU by 20% which is to say to keep their emission of GHC
under a 9% increase from 2010 to 2025 and decrease the emissions of the land use, land¬≠
use change and forestry (LULUCF) sector by 20% compared to 2008 for 2025. According
to the ELENA model [32], these goals are insufficient for well below 2¬∞C targets. There is
no further long¬≠term plan for Ecuador.
Similarly to Ecuador, Madagascar‚Äôs vision regarding climate change is to stay well below
2¬∞C and as close as possible to 1.5¬∞C [24]. Madagascar‚Äôs goal is to curb its CO2 emis¬≠
sions compared to BAU and to increase its absorption via the LULUCF sector. Concretely,
for 2030, in terms of CO2 emissions Madagascar‚Äôs goal is to reduce its direct emissions
by 14% compared to BAU and increased the absorption of C02 by 32%, this would mean
a net reduction of 10% of CO2 compared to 2010 (2010 projection from 2000). According
to the NDC, this would leave Madagascar CO2 negative of ¬≠70 MtCO2e in 2030, repre¬≠
senting a ¬≠10% CO2 emissions compared to 2010. There is no academic evaluation on
whether these targets would fulfil the temperature goal, but it fulfils both sub¬≠target of CO2
neutrality and reaching the peak of emission. Like Ecuador, there is no longer¬≠term plan.
For this study, it is important that for Madagascar and Ecuador the time horizon for the
strategies is aligned and since climate change demands a long¬≠term plan, the time hori¬≠
zon is fixed to 2050, like most climate change strategies. Furthermore, for comparative
purposes, the pressure to reduce GHG emissions must be the same for both countries.
Hence a similar goal expressed in GHG reduction compared to the business as usual sce¬≠
nario should fulfil that condition. Hence for both countries in this study, the constraints are
the two exogenous parameters of the population and the growth trends for both countries
for 2050, as well as the reduction of the annual GHG emissions.
For Ecuador, the ELENA study [32] suggest a 65% reduction of the emission by 2050
from 2015 with the implementation of reforestation, leading to a stable emission of CO2
per year that would fulfil the 1.5¬∞C goals. Now by adapting to the current data, that would
represent a reduction of around 70% compared to 2018 the last emissions data available.
Compared to a business as usual scenario of 3%GDP following the population projec¬≠
tion (Section 5.1), this would mean around an 80% reduction compared to the BAU. The
energy system does not represent all the emissions of the country and the potential of
GHG reduction is different in each sector, with some sectors like forestry able to be GHG
negative. The assumption for the goal is a reduction of the annual GHG emissions of 80%
by 2050 compared to the BAU of the energy system allow to fulfil the 1.5¬∞c target.

4.1
Ecuador
4.1.1
Socio¬≠Political Situation
Ecuador exists as a country since its separation from Gran Colombia in 1830. It‚Äôs a unitary
presidential republic with a multi¬≠party system. The executive is composed of a president
and his cabinet. The legislative is assured by the National Assembly. And the judiciary is
assured by the Supreme court. After a tumultuous political scene, stability in the country
has been brought back by President Rafael Correa a democratic socialist (PAIS Alliance)
from 2007 to 2017. During his term, with socialist reform the poverty has diminished
from 36.5% to 22.5% for the period 2006 to 2016, the GDP has increased as well as
the standards of living, and inequalities have diminished. Currently, the country is led by
President Guillermo Lasso of the conservative party CREO and follows the policy of his
predecessor President Len√≠n Moreno (PAIS Alliance) in implementing more liberal political
and economic reforms [33, 34, 35, 36].
In 2008, Ecuador has recognised the rights of Nature in its constitution, cementing the no¬≠
tion of ‚ÄúBuen vivir‚Äù from the Quechua culture in the country. It recognises that ‚ÄúPachamama‚Äù,
or Mother Earth has the right to exist, to maintain and regenerate its cycles, structure, func¬≠
tions and evolutionary processes. Furthermore, from 2008, Ecuador‚Äôs law recognises the
rights of education, health, food, social security, water, the protection the natural and
cultural assets, leaving a clean environment, ecologically stable, free of pollution and in

6.2
Discussion
The pathways for Ecuador and Madagascar have achieved the reduction of 80% emis¬≠
sions in 2050 compared to the BAU under the constraints of economic growth and popu¬≠
lation growth, corresponding to a reduction of 50% and 60% compared to 2019 for Mada¬≠
gascar and Ecuador respectively. However, this is just focused on the energy system and
remains incomplete in the overall climate mitigation problem. In the question of sustain¬≠
ability in general, to reach the goal of 1.5¬∞C, global CO2 neutrality is to be reached by
2050 [1]. In this study, only the question of the reduction of GHG emission reduction has
been treated, however, neutrality can only be reached with CO2 absorption. in this thesis,
the fulfilment of the 1.5¬∞C target has been formulated on the condition of CO2 absorption
through reforestation. Hence, the question of CO2 absorption through carbon capture and
storage technologies, and reforestation needs to be answered. From the perspective of
the GHG budget, the objective at 66% probability to limit the global mean temperature at
1.5¬∞C set a carbon budget based on the 2019 population set GHG budget of 900 MTCO2e
for Ecuador and 1400 for Madagascar [1]. Hence the energy system of Ecuador, through
the proposed pathway uses the whole budget, while Madagascar‚Äôs pathway uses 60% of
that budget. Knowing that the energy system of both countries represents around 30%
of the current GHG emissions, the possibility of fulfilling the 1.5¬∞C targets is questioned.
But as previously pointed out, the pathways possibly have a rippled effect on agriculture
and waste emissions by reusing their waste. And the sector of Agriculture and Forestry
can capture CO2. All in all, with 80% of CO2 emission reduction in the energy sector, the
goal of 1.5¬∞C can be reached on the condition of mitigation and GHG absorption in other
sectors.
When comparing Madagascar to Ecuador in this study, a few points can be outlined. The
starting point of both countries is different. Ecuador has a more developed energy sys¬≠
tem. Where Madagascar has to fulfil more basic needs in the energy system such as clean
cooking and access to electricity, Ecuador only has to improve the quality of its system.
Ecuador‚Äôs economy is already more developed, hence resulting in more economic pres¬≠
sure of the change on Madagascar than on Ecuador, as pointed out in the economic as¬≠
sessment. Furthermore, it seems that Ecuador has access to more controllable sources of
renewable energy which are hydropower and geothermal energy, though in the end, both
countries can produce enough renewable energy to fulfil the need of a growing economy
and a growing population, they can produce even more energy if needed. Hydropower
is both one of the biggest potentials in energy production with Geothermal in second for
Ecuador and Solar for Madagascar. They achieve the target GHG reductions, while for
Madagascar it is enough to consume renewable energy except for the transport system,
where only efforts in more efficiency are needed, Ecuador has to go the extra mile of
electrifying its transport system.

For both countries, the pathway allows more sustainability but their impact is only limited to

Conclusion
The application of backcasting at a country level on Madagascar and Ecuador has al¬≠
lowed us to outline two sustainable strategies aiming at climate mitigation for both coun¬≠
tries. These strategies outline how the consumption and the production of energy can
be modified to allow a transition to a more sustainable energy system. This transition
relies on a more efficient energy system relying more on renewable energies. Applying
at the country level is limited in that being able to fulfil the country level strategy requires
project planning at a different lower scale, for instance, improving the transport system in
Antananarivo requires a strategy in itself. Overall, backcasting at a country level allows
drawing directive lines for other strategies to be created.
Applying, backcasting jointly on Madagascar has resulted in the creation of similar path¬≠
ways everywhere it was possible. Indeed, if an option is a good possibility for Ecuador,
then if applicable, it is applied to Madagascar and reverse. Contrastingly, the divergence
of the pathways is highlighted, putting forward the differences between the two countries.
Constructing, the pathways are limited by the available literature and the monitoring of
the countries. The difficulty to realise the pathways is determined by the economic situa¬≠
tion, the available natural resources, and the barrier is the social state of the country. But
overall, sustainable strategies in developing countries at least in the climate mitigation of
the energy system are possible.
To go further, it is important to develop the research and the data collection in Ecuador
and especially in Madagascar. Research in climate mitigation in the energy system but
also other systems such as agriculture, forest management, or waste management. In
regards to climate change, developing strategies in the other sector is needed, as well in
climate adaptation and geoengineering for GHG capture. And for sustainability in general,
and all these strategies to be even realised, a plan for solving the major social problem
like poverty, hunger, corruption, and weak institutions is needed.
