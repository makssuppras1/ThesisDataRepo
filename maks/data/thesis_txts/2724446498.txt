Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Clean room is an engineered space, where a very low concentration of airborne 
particulates is maintained. It is well-isolated, well-controlled from contamination, and 
actively cleansed. Such rooms are needed for scientific research and industrial 
production for all nanoscale processes, such as pharmaceutical and semiconductor 
manufacturing. The cleanroom is designed to keep low concentration level of dust, 
airborne organisms, vaporized particles, as well as from whatever material is being 
handled inside [1]. 
There are a few requirements that should be maintained in a clean room, such as 
temperature, relative humidity, pressure, air velocity, cleanliness, etc. It needs to be 
stated that the key performance in terms of cleanliness is represented by the level of 
particle concentration [2][3]. Moreover, airborne particulate content, a metric of air 
quality, can be classified as viable or non-viable contaminants and may originate from 
humans (e.g. skin cells) or from areas of production (e.g. opening packaging materials 
and operating centrifuges or vortexes). These contaminants have been implicated in 
adverse effects on patient health, justifying the need for monitoring airborne 
particulate concentrations [4]. 
Generally, non-viable particles such as dust, fibers, and other debris can provide a 
surface for bacteria to attach to and survive on. As a result, efforts are typically made 
to minimize the presence of non-viable particles by use of HEPA (High Efficiency 
Particulate Air) filters and controlled air flow. However, it is still possible for viable 
bacteria to be present in the air, and for these bacteria to attach to non-viable particles 
and be transported throughout the cleanroom. 
Hence, EU GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) Annex 1 provides specific guidance 
on the manufacture of sterile medicinal product. It regulates the limits for airborne 
particulate concentration in a cleanroom operating and not operating (Fig. 1-1). 
 
Fig. 1-1 Limits for airborne particulate concentration in EU GMP Annex 1 [5]

The US Federal Standard 209E formed ISO 14644 Standards to regulate airborne 
particulate cleanliness classes in cleanrooms and clean Zones [3]. It provides 
classification of particle populations that are within the specified lower threshold 
particle size ranging from 0.1 µm to 5 µm (Fig. 1-2) in ISO 14644 - Part 1. 
 
 
Fig. 1-2 Limits for airborne particulate concentration in ISO 14644 Part 1 [3] 
Furthermore, Xu and Zhou [2] introduced that the flow state in the cleanroom belongs 
to the turbulent flow. Cleanrooms can be classified as: cleanrooms with non- 
unidirectional flow, i.e. mixed flow (Fig. 1-3), cleanrooms with unidirectional flow 
(Fig. 1-4 ), and cleanrooms with radial flow, also called vector flow (Fig. 1-5). Of those, 
non-unidirectional flow and unidirectional cleanrooms are most commonly used.

In non-unidirectional flow cleanrooms, where low particle concentration is needed in 
the whole room but not only in the region below the air inlet, the clean air should play 
the role of dilution sufficiently after leaving the air inlet, and more space before the 
working area is diluted. So, in this case the used air inlet should have the enough 
capability of dilution [2]. 
On the other side, for unidirectional flow cleanrooms, the cross-sectional area almost 
remains the same along the flow passage from the air supply outlet and return air 
grille in the cleanroom. Besides, with the uniform effect by the plenum chamber and 
HEPA filter, velocity at the cross section is comparatively uniform. In the working 
area, the streamlines are parallel and unidirectional, and vortex does not exist. 
Therefore, the self-purification ability in UDAF cleanroom is very good. The highest 
cleanliness level can thus be achieved [2]. 
 
 
1.2 Literature review 
The effect of only few factors on air cleanliness in the cleanroom have been studied. 
The results may be summarized into three main aspects [6]: 
1. The inlet/outlet openings arrangement and the ventilation airflow rate 
significantly affect the indoor airflow field [6][7][8][9][10][11]. 
2. The contaminant concentration through the room highly depends on the 
contaminant source location [6][11][12]. 
3. The location of obstacles may considerably affect the airflow pattern and the 
resultant contaminant distribution [6][13][14]. 
Yunus Emre Cetin [15] reported that the best performance with regard to the air 
change efficiency is achieved for the case of the inlet/outlet openings located on the 
same wall. Any inlet/outlet formation that prevent a proper mixing and cause a bypass 
of the supplied air result lowest ventilation performances. Whereas, it only considered 
the ventilation efficiency in the whole room rather than specific clean zone. Besides, 
the supply air grilles in the study were rectangle openings without diffuser plates. 
==PAGE:10==
4 / 98 
 
In Pavol’s study [16], it recommended the optimal position of the exhaust diffuser 
was always supposed to be in the lowest possible part, to effectively remove 0.5 μm 
particles from the clean zone (above the operating table) in an operating room. 
Nevertheless, the recommendations were based on CFD simulations without 
experimental validation. In addition, the supply air inlets were rectangle openings, 
excluding diffuser plates. 
Yun-Chun Tung [17] pinpointed that the locally balanced flow rates of the supply and 
return air (ceiling-return ventilation arrangement) had the better effect on the removal 
of particles (< 10 μm) in the empty cleanroom. In their case study, the CFD modelling 
was under iso-thermal conditions, ignoring the energy effects, such as the supply air 
temperature, heating source, and tools’ surface temperature and height. 
Yang and Hao [18] carried out the field test of pharmaceutical cleanroom cleanliness 
subject to multiple disturbance factors. The measured cleanrooms were equipped with 
perforated diffusers. A notable over-specification of cleanliness in the operational 
state was found. The particle concentrations was considerably lower than the required 
Class B specification; In the at-rest state, the particle concentration was two orders of 
magnitude lower than required in the standards even when the supply air volume was 
reduced by half. This article provided an overview of the practical status of 
contamination in the cleanrooms with perforated diffusers. Nonetheless, it focused on 
cleanrooms with perforated air supply diffuser but not on performance of swirl type 
diffuser. 
The above literature review reveals that the cleanliness in a cleanroom strongly 
depends on the indoor airflow pattern. However, there is no consensus on which 
ventilation strategy is more effective in the removal of contaminants. 
Moreover, some studies signify a lower position of the exhaust opening while others 
proposed ceiling mounted exhausts. Besides, few articles deeply investigated the 
effect of swirl and perforated diffuser in terms of air cleanliness in cleanrooms. 
Regarding to the shortcomings and conflicts in the cited literature outlined above, the 
purpose of this report was to conduct a comprehensive study about the influence of 
the type and location of air supply/exhaust diffusers on contaminant distribution and 
air cleanliness, both experimentally and numerically. In the experimental part, air 
velocity and temperature measurements were conducted. Experiments were 
performed in an existing cleanroom classified with ISO 7 at rest and ISO 8 in 
operation. The collected experimental results were used in the validation of the 
numerical approach. It is expected that the outcomes of this investigation will 
contribute to more efficient ventilation strategies as presenting profound information 
regarding to the effect on pharmaceutical cleanrooms. 
Here are the hypothesis based on previous studies: 
• Swirl diffuser may provide good air mixing pattern and thus dilute particles; 
• Perforated (low impulse) diffuser may provide good cleanliness within its 
supply air stream. This performance can be used to designate the potential 
clean zone in cleanrooms; 
==PAGE:11==
5 / 98 
 
• Lower level exhaust provides the holistic air mixing in the whole room, 
especially when combined with ceiling mounted supply diffuser. 
• Exhaust air location at higher room level could be more effective to remove 
fine particles concentrated at ceiling level caused by buoyancy effect. 
 
 
 
 
2 
Objectives 
The main objective of the study was to examine the effect of different supply/exhaust 
diffuser configuration and their type on the particle concentration distribution in a 
cleanroom ISO class 8. 
The specific objectives of the study were: 
1. To develop a numerical model for predicting airflow characteristics and particle 
concentration distribution in a cleanroom; 
2. To carry out validation of the numerical model against data obtained during 
physical experiments; 
3. To identify which configuration (low-level or high-level exhaust) provides better 
ventilation effectiveness to remove particles from the cleanroom; 
4. To identify which type of supply diffusers (swirl or low impulse) provides better 
ventilation effectiveness to remove particles from the cleanroom

3 
Methodology and Numerical Procedure 
In this study, field measurements were conducted in an existing cleanroom to acquire 
temperature and velocity information. The measuring data were used for the CFD 
boundary conditions, as well as a baseline to validate the developed and used 
numerical model based on Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) analysis. 
The validated CFD set-up and the boundary conditions were then applied to build a 
series of numerical modelling with the aim to investigate the effectiveness of various 
ventilation strategies for dilution and reduction of concentration of particles generated 
in the simulated clean room. The strategies included adjusting the exhaust grill 
location and use of supply air diffusers of different type. As a result, the optimal 
ventilation strategy was proposed accordingly 
Ansys Fluent (2021R1) was adopted during the CFD simulations.

3.3 Numerical Details and Boundary Conditions 
The numerical models were implemented by employing the commercial program of 
ANSYS FLUENT (version 21.1). 
3.3.1 
Effect of gravity 
The airflow in the clean room was influenced by the natural convection flow 
generated by the operator and lights’ heat emission, thus, the effect of gravity was 
enabled. The gravity was set to be -9.81 m/s2 for the Y axis. 
3.3.2 Energy equation 
The energy equation was enabled. The energy equation should be solved since the 
natural driven flows are impacted by the temperature differences in the room. 
==PAGE:23==
17 / 98 
 
3.3.3 Selection of Turbulence model 
The airflow pattern and temperature distribution in the room is governed by the 
conservation laws of mass, momentum and energy. 
The airflow in the clean room is considered to be turbulent, therefore an appropriate 
turbulence model is enabled. 
The flow is considered to be steady-state, incompressible and turbulent. Previous 
studies have shown that the Renormalization group (RNG) k-ε turbulence model 
produced good validated results for indoor airflow, temperature, and contaminant 
distributions [24][25]. Thus, the RNG k-ε turbulence model was selected for the 
cases. 
Full buoyancy effect was enabled for the calculation of the dissipation rate (ϵ). 
Enhanced wall functions were used to calculate the transport variables near wall [26]. 
All other specifications of the RNG k-ϵ model were kept as default [26]. 
3.3.4 Selection of Radiation 
The Surface-to-Surface radiation model was used for the simulations. This model 
accounts for the radiation exchange in a room of gray-diffuse surfaces. The “Surface 
to Surface” model assumes that any absorption, emission, or scattering of radiation 
can be ignored; therefore, only ’surface-to-surface’ radiation need be considered for 
analysis [26]. 
Thus, “Surface to Surface” radiation was enabled for the simulations as a practical 
condition.

diffusion of particles emitted from the operator. It was switched on after accessing the 
steady-state convergence considering the calculating load and computing efficiency. 
Accordingly, it was turned into transient-state firstly. The interaction with continuous 
phase and unsteady particle tracking were both enabled under Discrete Phase Model.

3.3.6 Solver Set-Up 
The coupling between pressure and velocity was solved using the SIMPLE algorithm 
[25][26]. 
3.3.7 Solution methods and controls 
Second order upwind scheme was selected for discretization of momentum, turbulent 
dissipation rate, turbulence kinetic energy and energy. Second order upwind scheme 
is considered to be more precise than first order scheme therefore it was selected for 
this simulation [11][31]. 
PRESTO! scheme was selected for discretization of the pressure equation since it is 
recommended when there is buoyancy driven flow [25]. For discretization of the 
gradient it was selected Green Gauss Node-based, which is recommended when it is 
used tetrahedron mesh [25]. 
The Under-Relaxation factors of 0.3 and 0.7 were used for pressure and momentum 
respectively for the first 1500 iterations, then adjusted to 0.2 and 0.4 respectively and 
the simulation continued with 500 steps. At last, they were changed to 0.2 and 0.2 for 
==PAGE:26==
20 / 98 
 
another 500 iterations. In order to provide optimum convergence a factor of 0.95 was 
set for the energy [25]. All other relaxation factors for the rest of the variables were 
kept as the default values in Fluent 2021. 
3.3.8 Materials 
Since the objective was to study the airflow in the clean room, the properties of the air 
were modified. A ‘Boussinesq’ model was chosen to model buoyancy driven force as 
it provides fast convergence for most natural convection flows when there are small 
temperature variations [25]. Since mixing air distribution in the room was simulated, 
the air temperature differences in the room were expected to be within 0 K. Air 
density was set to 1.19 kg/m3 at room air temperature of 20 ⁰C and 1 atm. Thermal 
expansion coefficient of air was set with 0.00335 K−1 [25].

3.4.3 Residual convergence 
The convergence process in Fluent is monitored by the residual history of different 
variables such as x-, y- and z- air velocities, energy, turbulent kinetic energy (k), 
turbulent dissipation rate (ε) and continuity. The solution will converge when all the 
residual falls below certain criteria. All the residuals criteria were set to 0.001 except 
the energy which was set to 10−6. After the iterations were finished the residuals were 
stable and decreased to the acceptable levels [24][25][26]. 
In Fig. 3-23, it can be seen that the residuals experienced three stages during 
convergence. At the beginning, the under-relaxation factors for pressure and 
momentum were set with 0.3 and 0.7 respectively. Afterwards they were adjusted to 
0.2 and 0.4, and the factor of 0.95 was set to energy for optimal convergence. At last, 
all residuals except ‘continuity’ fell below the desired criteria with the factor of 0.2 
and 0.2 for pressure and momentum. Nevertheless, due to the fact that the monitored 
temperature wouldn’t change with more iteration and the fact that both mass and heat 
conservation were fulfilled, the solution was considered convergent. The under- 
relaxation factor value was referred to DTU FLUENT tutorial [25].

The errors in CFD application are mainly caused by: (1) Physical/ Geometry 
modelling error, (2) Iterative convergence error, (3) Discretization error and (4) 
Computer round-off error [37]. The iterative convergence error has been improved by 
implementing grid independence and the round-off error has been improved by 
enabling “double-precision” in Fluent Launcher [26] during the study. Furthermore,

by Celik [35]. Lastly, the physical/ geometry modelling error will be validated with 
on-site measurement, see section 4.2. 
As a result, for simulated values, the upper and lower limits of the error bars are the 
steady-state results plus and minus the discretization error, respectively [36].

Discussion 
According to the result, in the existing room layout and with the present drug vessel, 
all three cases satisfied the EU and global allowed limits for number of particles with 
size 0.5 µm. The diffuser configuration in case B (swirl diffuser with ceiling exhausts) 
provided the highest cleanliness at the critical area within 10 cm above the tank 
surface (Fig. 4-20). However, in case B the particle concentration in the area between 
the source (the dummy) and the tank (Fig. 4-22, points tank-Sp and tank-Wsp) was 
higher than in case A (swirl diffuser with low level air exhausts). This outcome is due 
to the different airflow distribution in the cleanroom caused by the change of the 
exhaust location. In case B, the particles were not spread around the room like in case 
A, but travelled almost directly towards the door (Fig. 4-27). Case A manage to 
provide the highest dilution of particles in the area around the source. This means that 
if the tank is moved closer to the source, case A configuration might provide better 
cleanliness at the critical area (i.e., surface of the tank) than case B. Distance between 
the source and the critical area is another parameter that needs to be considered in 
future investigations. 
Case A and C provided on average similar cleanliness at the critical area. The results 
show that at the sampling points below the low impulse diffusers the particle 
concentration was greatly reduced. It could be expected that the low-impulse diffuser 
(used in case C) would have provided high efficiency in terms of particle removal at 
the critical area if it was located exactly above the tank. The low-impulse diffuser is 
designed to deliver downward laminar flow, which almost does not mix with room 
contaminated air and thus provide clean air directly to the target/critical area. 
However, this means that the operator in the cleanroom must make sure the critical 
area is placed exactly under the diffuser, which limits the flexibility of the room. 
Furthermore, regarding case A diffuser configuration (swirl diffuser with low level air 
exhausts), the particle concentration at any of 75 monitoring points was two orders of 
magnitude lower than the standard requirements for class C and D. For case B and C 
(low impulse diffuser with low level air exhausts), the particle concentration at any of 
75 monitoring points was one order of magnitude lower than the standard criteria. 
Case A can provide good air mixing in the whole room, which enables to dilute the 
space uniformly. Case B can form and maintain an air shortcut in the room, and thus 
provide lower dilution rate of particles. In case C, the low impulse diffuser can 
provide extraordinary cleanliness at the area below its air stream. However, there is a 
discrepancy of cleanliness within the diffuser’s air stream and outside its air stream 
and this difference can exceed 95%. 
Therefore, in future designs with an unknown dimension/ position of the drug vessel, 
case A is recommended when the position of contamination source is dynamic. And it 
can perform good air mixing and thus extensively dilute the entire room. Case B is 
recommended with a fixed position of the operator. It should be noted that in case B 
there was a short circuit of the supplied air, which in general is not recommended for 
ventilation design. In this way, the air shortcut can be taken advantage of and it’s 
necessary to place the vessel (or the required clean zone) on the other side against the 
==PAGE:79==
71 / 98 
 
contaminant source. In case C, the super clean space beneath diffusers should be 
utilized. Thus, it’s a good idea to place the vessel opening right within the air stream. 
Whereas, case C is least recommended in non-UDAF (unidirectional airflow) 
cleanroom because the area of diffuser is small, and it will be difficult to locate the 
opening precisely if the vessel is not fixed in daily operation. What’s more, the 
consequence of not locating the opening properly will be dangerous since the 
contamination outside the air stream is 20 times of the clean air.

Conclusion 
Generally, a numeric model was created and validated with experimental data for 
prediction of airflow and particle concentration spread in a pharmaceutical 
cleanroom. 
In conclusion, in terms of airborne particles removal in the cleanroom classified with 
ISO 7/8, it is best to place the exhaust grilles at the ceiling when the clean outdoor air 
is supplied via ceiling swirl diffusers and the position of contaminant source is fixed 
and away from the critical area with at least 1.6 m. 
Overall, this study strengthens the current design practice to place the exhaust grilles 
at a lower level when swirl supply diffusers are used and mounted at the ceiling in 
cleanrooms classified with ISO 7/8. And it is necessary to designate the clean zone in 
the opposite side of the room against the contaminant source. 
The ventilation strategy of using low impulse supply air diffusers at the ceiling with 
low level exhaust grilles can also be applied in ISO 7/8 cleanroom. However, it is 
vital to place the clean zone strictly under the supply air stream. Otherwise, 
cleanliness at the critical area might be highly deteriorated. 
 
 
Limitations: 
In this study, even though a detailed field measurement and numerical investigation 
was carried out, there were still some limitations. 
1. 
The results of the current study are limited to only one type and position of 
contaminant source. 
2. 
3. 
The k-ε turbulence model was selected to simulate indoor airflow in this 
study. It works well in case of airflow with high turbulence and velocity. 
However, it works less well when the air velocity and turbulence are low as in 
the case with the low impulse diffusers. 
4. 
Due to the lack of space reservation for the door gap during the geometry 
design, the particle concentration was not able to be tracked within the door 
gap. Therefore, the index of Contaminant Removal Effectiveness from the 
door gap was not calculated. 
5. 
The reduction of the air change rate in the room to 20 ACH as recommended 
by some guidelines for cleanrooms ISO7/8 was not further investigated. In this 
way, potential energy savings can be obtained. 
6. 
The study investigated only one particle size 0.5 µm, however in reality there 
are various particle size emitted from people’s body or expelled particles.
