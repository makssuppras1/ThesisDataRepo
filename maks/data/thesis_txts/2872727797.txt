Abstract
This thesis investigates how an e-learning platform can be designed to address the lack
of integrated, interactive digital tools in Danish upper secondary education, reduce
teachers’ workload, and support student engagement and differentiated learning. The
research was motivated by challenges exposed during the COVID-19 pandemic, which
highlighted how existing platforms like MaCom’s Lectio, used by 90% of Danish upper
secondary schools, primarily serve administrative functions and offer limited support
for interactive learning activities and active student participation. As a result, many
students lost motivation and fell behind during online teaching. Although teachers
recognize the value of quizzes, flashcards, and other interactive content, they often
lack suﬀicient preparation time to develop such materials themselves. When they do
try to incorporate these elements, they are forced to rely on external tools, leading to
fragmented learning experiences and raising concerns about data privacy, consistency,
and added complexity. To address these issues, the thesis also explores how emerg-
ing technologies such as artificial intelligence and gamification can be responsibly
integrated to engage and motivate students, without adding to teachers’ workload.
To address these gaps, the project combined a literature review, iterative pro-
totype development, and qualitative and quantitative feedback from teachers and
students. In-depth interviews with key stakeholders were conducted throughout all
stages of development to maintain a user-centered design process. Insights from these
interviews were used to create detailed personas representing different types of users,
as well as user stories and journey maps to visualize typical workflows and pain points.
These tools guided design decisions and ensured that the prototype aligned with the
practical realities of classroom use. Usability testing, supported by a System Usabil-
ity Scale (SUS) survey, was used to evaluate the application’s success in meeting
real classroom needs. Data was collected from a total of 38 participants from five
organizations, with most participants contributing at multiple stages of the iterative
development process.
The findings show that interactive content, especially quizzes and flashcards that
promote active recall, is highly valued by both teachers and students but is often
too time-consuming to create manually. Automatic generation of such content us-
ing AI proved to be a promising solution, helping teachers save preparation time
while maintaining pedagogical quality. Gamification elements like instant feedback,
progress tracking, and digital badges were found to motivate students when designed
to encourage self-improvement rather than competition. The final prototype brought

This master’s thesis is written as part of the Master of Science in Engineering (Com-
puter Science & Engineering) program at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU).

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, the digital transformation of education has accelerated rapidly, re-
shaping how teachers plan lessons and how students engage with course material. In
Denmark, upper secondary education increasingly relies on digital tools to support
teaching both inside and outside the classroom. However, while many schools use
digital learning management systems, such as MaCom’s Lectio which is used by 90%
of Danish general upper secondary programs [28], these systems primarily handle
administrative tasks like timetabling and grade reporting and offer limited support
for interactive learning activities and active student participation [3]. As a result,
teachers often limit their use of such features or resort to using external websites and
third-party apps to create quizzes, flashcards, or discussion boards. While these tools
can help increase student engagement, relying on multiple external platforms creates
fragmented learning experiences and can raise concerns about data privacy and the
consistency of teaching materials.
At the same time, research shows that student engagement in digital learning
environments can drop if the materials do not actively involve students or if teachers
lack the time to create high-quality content that supports different learning styles [4,
11]. Many teachers face significant time constraints, which makes it challenging to
prepare engaging learning materials and learn how to effectively integrate new digital
tools into their teaching. This gap can result in passive learning, where students
simply read uploaded materials without meaningful practice, interaction, or feedback,
ultimately harming learning outcomes.
The COVID-19 pandemic further exposed these limitations. Many Danish teach-
ers struggled to adapt their teaching methods to fully digital environments, often
defaulting to lecture-based approaches that lacked interaction and differentiation [4].
This underscored the need for more supportive digital tools that can help teachers
maintain student engagement and keep track of progress, even when direct supervi-
sion is limited.
Given these challenges, this thesis investigates how an e-learning platform can be
designed to help overcome the key obstacles faced by teachers and students in Dan-
ish upper secondary education. The core goal was to analyze current shortcomings
in digital teaching tools and explore practical ways to support teachers in creating
interactive and engaging learning activities within a platform tailored for classroom
use.
This project focuses specifically on upper secondary education, because this
level combines students’ high digital competence with a need for engaging, flexible

learning tools that support exam preparation and self-study. Although higher levels
of education in Denmark already make extensive use of asynchronous teaching and
self-directed learning platforms, these practices are less utilized in upper secondary
programs, where maintaining student motivation and active participation still relies
heavily on teacher-led activities inside the classroom [4]. Additionally, teachers in

Through literature review, prototype development, and iterative user feedback
from both educators and students, the project aims to identify features and design
principles that can enhance digital learning without increasing teacher workload. The
following chapters provide an overview of related research on e-learning and gamifica-
tion, describe the design and development process of the prototype platform, present
findings from user testing, and discuss the implications for future classroom integra-
tion and continued development.
1.1
Problem statement
This can be summarized in the following problem statement:
How can a tailored e-learning platform be designed to address the lack
of integrated, interactive learning tools in Danish upper secondary
education and reduce teachers’ workload while supporting student
engagement and diverse learning needs?
1.1.1
Research Questions
To explore the problem outlined in the previous section, this thesis is guided by a
set of sub-questions that address both the user-centered and technical aspects of the
proposed solution. These sub-questions aim to investigate the needs and challenges
of teachers and students, as well as the practical considerations involved in designing
and implementing the e-learning platform.
User-Centered Focus
• What are the main limitations of existing digital learning platforms (e.g., Lectio)
in supporting interactive teaching and active student participation in Danish
upper secondary schools?
• What features and functionalities do teachers and students consider most valu-
able in an e-learning platform for learning enhancement?

• What were the main challenges faced by Danish upper secondary schools during

Technical and Design Focus
• How can incorporating emerging technologies and strategies like artificial intel-

1.2
Delimitation
This thesis focuses on designing and prototyping an e-learning platform that helps
Danish upper secondary school teachers maximize engagement and learning outcomes.
The project is centered around technical feasibility and user-centered design, drawing
from our background in computer science. Therefore, it does not address broader
social or infrastructural challenges such as access to devices, internet connectivity, or
institutional readiness for digital transformation. Likewise, policy changes, national
implementation strategies, and long-term adoption planning fall outside the scope of
this work.
While pedagogical insights from teachers and relevant literature inform the plat-
form’s functionality, the thesis does not engage in comprehensive pedagogical theory
building or curriculum design. The integration work is scoped to Lectio, and does
not include integration with other educational systems or LMS platforms. Addition-
ally, the platform is developed as a prototype, not a production-ready solution. For
example, the selection and refinement of the underlying language model used for quiz
generation is treated as a proof of concept. Final decisions in this area would be made
by MaCom in a real deployment scenario.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
In this chapter, peer-reviewed articles, books, and conference papers will be studied
with the purpose of gaining a foundational understanding of e-learning methods and
tools currently used in Danish upper secondary education programs and other vo-
cational schools. This includes an extensive research of the benefits and drawbacks
discovered in introducing digital elements into the classroom, as well as the challenges
that have contributed to the lack of digital incorporation in upper secondary schools
compared to other Danish education programs. The review considers primarily Dan-
ish literature to understand the current state of e-learning in Denmark and relevant
international literature for more general e-learning theory and considerations, such
as artificial intelligence, gamification and privacy.
2.1
E-learning
A review of the existing literature highlights that digital learning materials can im-
prove education by providing opportunities for individual learning, assisting dialogue,
and encouraging active student participation [1], but for e-learning to be effective, it
must go beyond static content and incorporate interactive elements that stimulate
critical thinking and engagement [4]. Despite these advantages, the full integration
of e-learning tools in Danish upper secondary schools remains limited.
A report
analyzing online education in Denmark during the COVID-19 pandemic found that
many teachers reverted to traditional lecture-based methods, reducing opportunities
for interaction and active student participation [4]. This highlights the challenges of
incorporating digital teaching methods into established pedagogical practices.
Danish upper secondary school students rank among the most digitally competent
in the world, and most students entering secondary education have already accumu-
lated 10-11 years of experience in the use of daily digital technology [3]. Although this
digital fluency reduces the need for extensive technical training, it does not eliminate
the challenges associated with digital learning environments. One of the key chal-
lenges is ensuring that students remain focused in online or hybrid learning settings,
as studies have shown that students are easily distracted when working on digital
platforms, often multitasking with unrelated activities [3].

2.1.1
Teaching methods
The choice of teaching methods remains a debated topic, and educators often seek
guidance on the most effective approaches. However, research does not support the
idea that a single method is universally superior, reinforcing the importance of ped-
agogical freedom in selecting teaching strategies [3]. Digital tools designed for the
classroom should therefore seek to support a wide range of teaching and learning
methods rather than enforce a certain method. Teaching methods should also sup-
port the diverse learning styles of students, which will be examined in sections 2.2
and 2.6.
The pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital tools in education, often with-
out adequate preparation or support for teachers. This sudden shift exposed the need
for flexible, research-informed platforms that can support diverse teaching methods
without requiring educators to rely on trial-and-error [3]. In response, educational
technology must now aim to offer intuitive, evidence-based solutions that align with
pedagogical goals while minimizing the need for technical expertise or extensive prepa-
ration.
2.2
Educational Differentiation in E-Learning
Educational differentiation involves adapting teaching methods, materials, and assess-
ments to accommodate diverse student needs, and in e-learning, this presents both
challenges and opportunities. Differentiation is essential in e-learning to ensure that
all students, regardless of their abilities or learning styles, can engage effectively with
course materials.
The transition to emergency remote teaching in Denmark high-
lighted significant challenges in adapting instruction to digital formats [4]. The lack
of differentiation widened the gap between self-directed learners and those requiring
structured support, worsening the existing inequalities. Teachers reported diﬀiculties
in providing personalized assistance, particularly for students who needed additional
guidance [4]. An analysis of Danish upper secondary schools found that many edu-
cators lacked the training and resources to effectively tailor instruction in a digital
setting [3]. The reliance on standardized online materials often failed to accommo-
date the diverse needs of students, contributing to disengagement and lower academic
performance [7].
In addition to varying academic abilities and levels of independence, students
differ in their preferred learning styles. Some learners benefit from visual materials
such as diagrams and videos, while others respond better to textual explanations,
audio, or hands-on interactive content [24]. E-learning platforms can support these
differences by offering content in multiple formats and enabling students to choose
how they engage with material. Supporting different learning styles not only increases
accessibility but can also improve motivation and retention, making digital learning
more inclusive and effective for a broader range of learners. E-learning tools should
therefore be designed with flexibility in mind, allowing teachers to adapt materials

and assessments to fit individual student needs, and enabling students to learn in
ways that reflect their personal strengths and preferences [24].
2.3
Feedback in E-Learning
Providing timely and effective feedback on student work is a critical component of
meaningful education, and digital platforms can facilitate feedback loops that enhance
learning outcomes [3]. Studies highlight the importance of feedback in maintaining
student engagement and ensuring comprehension, particularly in remote learning en-
vironments [29].
One of the primary benefits of digital feedback is its ability to
be immediate and personalized, and structured digital learning environments allow
teachers to track student progress and provide targeted feedback based on perfor-
mance data.
However, challenges remain in ensuring that digital feedback is both meaningful
and actionable. In some cases, students struggle to interpret automated feedback,
particularly when it lacks contextual explanations [29]. To mitigate this, educators
are encouraged to incorporate a mix of automated and teacher-provided feedback to
maintain clarity and motivation.
Moreover, effective feedback mechanisms should
include opportunities for students to reflect on and act upon the feedback received.
Studies emphasize the need for feedback to be interactive, allowing students to seek
clarification and engage in self-assessment [1]. Incorporating peer feedback through
digital tools can also enhance collaborative learning and critical thinking skills.
2.4
Existing E-Learning Tools and Their Effectiveness
Different digital platforms have been employed in Danish upper secondary educa-
tion, each with varying levels of success. Lectio remains the primary administrative
tool but lacks strong pedagogical functionality [1, 16]. Microsoft Teams and Google
Suite gained widespread adoption in recent years, and both have been widely used
for virtual learning, with Google Suite proving particularly effective for real-time
collaboration.
While Moodle remains in use at several institutions, its broader adoption has been
constrained by its complexity and lack of integration with Lectio. Praxis Online, a
simplified and Moodle-based alternative, is more commonly used in vocational edu-
cation due to its structured and accessible course planning features [1, 16]. These
findings suggest that while comprehensive LMS platforms exist, user-friendliness and
adaptability are key factors influencing adoption. Educators’ experiences with these
platforms will be further investigated through interviews and a comparative analy-
sis in chapter 4 with the purpose of gaining further insight into the strengths and
weaknesses of existing e-learning tools.

2.5
Gamification & Engagement Strategies
The integration of game-like elements into educational settings (gamification) has
emerged as a potentially useful tool to enhance student engagement, motivation, and
learning outcomes. In an online context, gamification is particularly valuable, since
it is more diﬀicult for teachers to manage students directly [36]. Studies have shown
that when applied effectively, gamification can help to transform traditional learn-
ing environments into interactive and immersive experiences, leading to increased
participation and deeper understanding of educational content.
2.5.1
Core Elements of Gamification in Education
Common game-based elements frequently used in educational contexts include [36,
31]:
• Points and Badges: Rewarding students for their achievements helps motivate
them and give a sense of progress.
• Leaderboards: Introducing competitive elements can drive engagement by
encouraging students to participate more actively.
• Instant Feedback: Providing immediate responses to student actions helps
reinforce learning and allows for timely corrections.
• Progress bars: Progress bars can function as a simple form of immediate
feedback on student progress, providing a sense of accomplishment.
Each gamification element has its own potential benefits and drawbacks depending
on the education context and how they are implemented. A comprehensive literature
review of 119 papers about the relationship between gamification and education [8],
found that gamification is a rapidly growing phenomenom and that integrating these
mechanics into an education significantly increased student satisfaction and participa-
tion. The research indicated that students were more likely to engage in discussions,
complete assignments, and actively interact with digital learning materials when game
elements were incorporated [8]. Points and badges in particular showed significant
positive correlation to engagement [31].
2.5.2
Gamification and AI-Driven Engagement
Advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) have significantly contributed to the ef-
fectiveness of gamified learning environments by enabling dynamic and personalized
content delivery. One study from 2023 [5] investigated the use of automatic question
generation within a Massive Open Online Course (MOOC) through a custom Moo-
dle plugin. This AI-powered tool generated personalized multiple-choice questions
based on learners’ prior performance, adapting diﬀiculty levels in real-time.

2.5.3
Challenges and Considerations in Gamification
While gamification has been shown to enhance engagement and motivation in e-
learning, it also introduces several challenges that must be carefully addressed to
maximize its effectiveness [31]. Without proper implementation, gamified elements
may lead to unintended consequences, such as student disengagement, misalignment
with learning objectives, or an overreliance on extrinsic motivation rather than intrin-
sic interest in learning.
One significant concern is the overemphasis on competition. Leaderboards and
competitive game mechanics can be powerful motivators, but they may also discour-
age students who struggle to keep up with high-performing peers. Excessive compe-
tition can lead to frustration, anxiety, and disengagement, particularly for students
who rank lower. A way to mitigate this issue is by incorporating collaborative gamifi-
cation elements, such as team-based challenges, where students work together toward
a shared goal. Personalized progress tracking instead of public leaderboards can al-
low students to monitor their own growth without feeling pressure from peer rankings.
Additionally, focusing on self-improvement metrics, where students compete against
their past performance rather than classmates, can help maintain motivation without
creating unnecessary stress [31, 39].
Another challenge is ensuring that game elements support, rather than distract
from, learning objectives.
If poorly designed, gamification may cause students to
prioritize earning rewards over mastering the subject matter. This is closely linked
to the tension between extrinsic and intrinsic motivation. Extrinsic motivators, such
as points, badges, or ranks, can boost short-term engagement but risk shifting the
student’s focus away from the learning itself [12]. In contrast, intrinsic motivation is
driven by curiosity, mastery, and personal relevance. For gamification to have lasting
==PAGE:22==
10
2 Literature Review
impact, it must support intrinsic motivation by making learning feel meaningful,
enjoyable, and self-directed. For instance, providing students with choice, autonomy,
and feedback tied directly to their understanding rather than just rewards can create
deeper engagement. Adaptive learning techniques, where game elements dynamically
adjust to the student’s skill level, further reinforce mastery over simple participation,
ensuring that educational value remains central to the experience [31].
A final challenge lies in the technical and pedagogical expertise required to suc-
cessfully implement gamification. Many educators may lack the necessary time or
experience to integrate gamified elements effectively into their curricula. Without
adequate support, gamification may be applied inconsistently or in ways that do not
enhance learning. User-friendly gamification tools should be developed to lower the
technical barriers for implementation and allow teachers to align game mechanics
with their pedagogical intentions [26].
2.6
Artificial Intelligence in Education
Recent developments in artificial intelligence have allowed it to play an increasingly
large role in education, offering new opportunities to enhance student engagement,
personalize learning experiences, and optimize educational outcomes. This section
explores how AI has so far been integrated into e-learning, its benefits, and the chal-
lenges it presents, with a focus on its application in modern education technologies.
Personalized Learning through AI
One of the most significant contributions of AI to e-learning is its ability to de-
liver personalized learning experiences. Traditional e-learning systems often adopt a
one-size-fits-all approach, which can fail to meet the diverse needs of learners. AI-
powered systems, however, analyze vast amounts of data such as student performance,
learning pace, and interaction patterns to tailor content and recommendations to in-
dividual learners [20, 19]. For instance, adaptive learning platforms like Knewton
and DreamBox use AI algorithms to adjust the diﬀiculty of exercises and provide
real-time feedback based on student performance. Such systems can significantly im-
prove learning outcomes by ensuring that students receive content that matches their
current knowledge level and learning style.
Enhancing Engagement and Motivation
AI is also being used to enhance student engagement and motivation in e-learning
environments. Chatbots and virtual assistants can make learning more fun and in-
teractive, and adaptive learning technologies can help students stay engaged by pre-
senting material at their level of understanding [19]. AI can analyze large amounts of
data and provide insights into student performance, allowing teachers to better un-
derstand their students and tailor their instruction accordingly, while also reducing
==PAGE:23==
2.6 Artificial Intelligence in Education

teacher workload. This can lead to improved learning outcomes and better student
performance.
Challenges and Ethical Considerations
Despite its potential, the integration of AI in e-learning is not without challenges.
One of the primary concerns is data privacy and security. Collecting and analyzing
student data raises ethical questions about how this information is stored, used, and
protected. Ensuring compliance with regulations such as the General Data Protection
Regulation (GDPR) is essential to maintain trust and protect student privacy [19].
A major concern identified in the 2021 article Privacy and E-Learning: A Pending
Task [2] is that the widespread outsourcing of educational platforms and services to
third-party providers has shifted student data away from the control of educational
institutions.
This trend increases the risk of data breaches, unauthorized surveil-
lance, and misuse of student data for secondary purposes like behavioral profiling or
commercial exploitation. The rise of cloud-based learning management systems and
software-as-a-service models has led to a loss of institutional control over learner data
and increased reliance on vendors whose data practices may lack transparency and
accountability.
The paper also highlights how learning analytics and other data-driven approaches
in education can unintentionally contribute to surveillance practices. While these
technologies promise personalized learning and improved pedagogy, they also carry
the risk of reinforcing power imbalances between institutions and students, partic-
ularly when learners are unaware of the extent and implications of the data being
collected about them.
To mitigate these risks, the article proposes several measures: implementing strong
data encryption, applying differential privacy techniques, using alias systems to pro-
tect student identity, and enforcing privacy-by-design principles in educational soft-
ware. These steps, alongside greater transparency and stakeholder involvement, are
essential to ensuring that AI and e-learning tools do not compromise student auton-
omy and trust.
Another challenge is the potential for bias in AI algorithms. If the data used to
train AI systems is biased, the resulting algorithms may perpetuate or even exacer-
bate existing inequalities. For example, biased algorithms could disadvantage certain
student groups, leading to unfair outcomes [19]. Additionally, AI systems must be
transparent about their decision-making for students and teachers to trust their deci-
sions, particularly regarding grading and feedback. AI-based education systems must
also be designed with accessibility in mind, ensuring that all students, including those
with disabilities, can access and use the technology.

3.1
Research design
This study follows a User-Centered Design (UCD) approach to ensure that the de-
velopment of the digital learning platform is driven by user needs and feedback [10].
UCD emphasizes an iterative process where users (students and teachers) are actively
involved in shaping the solution through continuous testing and refinement.
To structure and guide this user-centered process, the project applies the Double
Diamond framework [9], which provides a structured approach to creative problem-
solving. While UCD defines the mindset of continuous user involvement, the Double
Diamond offers a process model that organizes the project into four phases: Discover,
Define, Develop, and Deliver. The first two phases focus on exploring and synthesizing
user needs, while the other two guide solution development through prototyping and
refinement based on user feedback.
During the prototyping phase, the Build-Measure-Learn (BML) cycle is employed
to validate design choices and evaluate the usability of the prototype through iterative
testing [6]. This combination of UCD principles, the Double Diamond structure, and
BML iterations ensures a methodical design process centered around real user needs.
This cycle follows a structured process where an initial version of the solution is built,
tested with users, and improved based on their feedback.
By combining User-Centered Design, Double Diamond, and Build-Measure-Learn,
the project has a structured yet flexible approach to platform development, where
qualitative research, iterative prototyping, and usability testing play a central role.
The following sections provide a detailed explanation of each framework and how they
have been applied in the study.

When designing the prototype, it was important to understand the user interactions,

3.3.1
Personas
To guide the design of the e-learning platform, we used the method of persona creation,
a common tool in user-centered design.
Personas are fictional but research-based
user profiles that help synthesize data and articulate user needs, behaviors, and goals.
They are especially useful in digital product development, where understanding a
diverse user base is critical.
The personas were developed based on qualitative data collected through the
interviews. Each persona represents a synthesis of multiple real user traits and reflects
a distinct attitude toward digital learning. In line with best practices, both primary
and contrast personas were defined. The primary personas represent the main target
group, while the contrast persona is used to validate assumptions and ensure wider
accessibility to the platform [22].
3.3.2
User story map
To identify the core features of the application, we applied the User Story Mapping
(USM) technique [32]. This method helped structure the development of the platform
according to user needs by visualizing activities and tasks from the perspective of
users, in this case, both teachers and students.
A preliminary map was sketched based on assumptions and early informal observa-
tions. This initial version focused on outlining the typical digital teaching flow, from
preparation to evaluation. We then refined and iterated on this map using qualitative

3.4
Testing Strategy
To ensure that the developed platform aligns with user expectations and supports
intuitive interactions, three complementary testing methods: A/B testing, usability
testing, and unit testing were employed. While A/B and usability testing are widely
used in iterative design processes and are especially suitable for evaluating early-stage
prototypes in educational technology contexts, unit testing was used internally during
development to verify the functionality of individual components and ensure a stable
foundation for user-facing features [14, 37].
3.4.1
A-B testing
A/B testing was used to evaluate specific UI decisions and interaction flows. In this
method, users were shown two different versions of a particular feature or screen,
for example, two variations of the quiz interface or progress tracking dashboard. By
observing user preferences and behaviors between the two variants, the goal was
to identify which design better supports clarity, ease of use, and user engagement.
A/B testing is especially suited to situations where minor design differences (e.g.
button placement, information hierarchy) can have significant impacts on usability or
motivation, both of which are crucial in a learning platform targeting students and
teachers.
3.4.2
Usability testing
To evaluate the usability of the prototype in a structured and standardized way, we
used the System Usability Scale (SUS). SUS is one of the most widely adopted tools
for measuring perceived usability and has been validated across a broad range of
==PAGE:32==
20
3 Methodology
domains and systems. It consists of 10 statements rated on a 5-point Likert scale,
alternating between positive and negative phrasing to reduce response bias [25]. The
survey can be seen in appendix A.
One of the primary reasons for using SUS in this project was its ability to provide
a quick assessment while still producing reliable and interpretable results. As most of
the project was based on qualitative data (e.g. interviews and observational feedback),
the SUS survey introduced a structured quantitative dimension that complemented
our findings well. This made it particularly well-suited for this setting, where par-
ticipants only had limited time to engage with the prototype during scheduled test
sessions.
3.4.3
Unit Testing
Unit testing is a fundamental practice in software development that involves verifying
individual units of code in isolation to ensure they function as intended.
In this
project, unit testing was instrumental in maintaining the reliability and stability of
both the backend and frontend components of the learning platform throughout the
development lifecycle.
A test-driven development (TDD) approach was adopted to guide implementation.
Each unit test targeted a specific function or component, isolating it from the rest of
the system to produce clear and focused feedback. To enhance development eﬀiciency,
tests were automated and integrated into the continuous development workflow.
The backend, developed in Python using the Flask framework, was tested using
pytest along with Flask-specific tools. These tests evaluated API endpoints, busi-
ness logic, data processing, and error handling. The frontend, built with React and
JavaScript, employed Jest and the React Testing Library to validate component ren-
dering, user interaction handling, state management, and backend integration via
mocked API calls.
Testing efforts prioritized functionality central to the user experience, including
quiz generation and student progress tracking. Descriptive test names and a well-
structured test suite contributed to traceability, linking each test to specific user
stories or system requirements.

4.1
Requirements Discovery
The purpose of this section is to define the requirements of the developed e-learning
platform based on initial information gathering interviews. Insights from these in-
terviews were analyzed to identify common goals, needs, and pain points, which
were then synthesized into user personas representing the platform’s primary user
types. These personas along with a comparative analysis of existing LMSs served as
a foundation for defining functional and non-functional requirements, ensuring that
the system addressed real-world challenges. Based on these requirements, user story
maps were created to break down key tasks and features into user-centered flows.
Finally, a user journey map was developed to visualize how users interact with the
platform over time, highlighting moments of engagement, friction, and opportunity
for improvement.
4.1.1
Personas
In this project, we have developed three personas based on the initial interviews; two
teachers and one student. One of the teachers, along with the student (Sofie and
Frederik), represents the primary target group for our platform. The second teacher
(Erik) serves as a contrast persona, included to challenge our assumptions and confirm
that our design choices are truly aligned with the intended users. This helps validate
that the platform is built for the right audience [22].
Sofie, a Danish upper secondary school teacher from Gentofte Gymnasium, rep-
resents the modern, tech-enthusiastic educator. With a background in Didactics and
Technology, she actively seeks digital tools that promote student-centered learning.
Sofie embraces experimentation, often trying out new platforms, and values sharing
ideas with colleagues. For her, technology is a means to enhance student autonomy

4.1.2
Comparative analysis
A comparative analysis was conducted of different Learning Management Systems
(LMS) commonly used in Danish upper secondary education. The purpose was not
to identify the “best” platform, but to assess how existing systems support interactive
and gamified learning. This comparison served as a tool to highlight patterns and
gaps that could inform the design of our own e-learning platform.
The systems included - Moodle, Lectio, Praxis Online, Microsoft Teams, Google
Suite, and Kahoot - were selected based on their widespread use across the institutions
we interviewed. Although Kahoot is not an LMS, it was included due to its popularity
in classrooms for gamified engagement.

nical review of each platform, but also on qualitative insights drawn directly from
interviews with teachers and students (see Section 3.2). The ratings reflect recurring
themes and perceptions expressed during these interviews, such as Moodle’s flexibil-

4.1.3
Requirements
Based on the initial interviews, the defined personas and the comparative analysis of
LMSs, the following requirements were identified to satisfy the needs of end users.
4.1.3.1
Functional Requirements
• Integration with Lectio: The application should be designed for seamless
integration with MaCom’s existing Lectio platform. This ensures compatibility
with current infrastructure, facilitates potential full-scale adoption, and sup-
ports the consolidation of digital teaching tools, reducing the number of sepa-
rate platforms that teachers and students must learn and manage. Integration
should also reduce teacher workload by enabling direct access to subject and stu-
dent data from Lectio, eliminating the need for manual setup and streamlining
the creation of modules and activities.
• AI-Generated Interactive Content: The platform must support automatic
generation of interactive learning activities, such as multiple-choice quizzes and
flashcards. This feature is intended to reduce teacher workload and promote
active recall among students, a learning technique that involves retrieving in-
formation from memory to strengthen retention and understanding. Teachers
should also be able to review and edit generated content before publishing it to
ensure quality and relevance. The purpose of the interactive content should be
learning and retention rather than grading.
• Gamification of Learning Activities: To increase student engagement and
motivation, the system should include basic gamification elements in connection
with interactive activities. These may include features such as instant feedback,
visual score summaries, completion tracking, badges, avatars, or progress levels.
The selection and design of gamification elements will be guided by continuous

feedback from both teachers and students to ensure they are implemented in a
responsible and motivating way that aligns with user preferences and reviewed
literature. The specific gamification features incorporated in the final product
and their justifications are explained in section 4.6.4.
• Manual Addition of Learning Material and Activities: Teachers must
be able to manually add learning materials, such as PDF files, external links
(e.g., to articles or videos), and custom-created quizzes or flashcards to a specific
module. These materials should be easy to upload, organize, and edit directly
within the module interface. The goal is to support flexible teaching workflows
and ensure that teachers retain full control over the structure and content of
each module, even without using AI generation.
• Student Activity and Performance Tracking: The platform must allow
teachers to track individual student engagement and performance, particularly
for quizzes and other interactive activities. This includes viewing quiz results,
completion rates, and timestamps of activity interactions. The purpose is to
help teachers monitor progress and ensure that students remain active and en-

learning.
• Forum for Asynchronous Communication: Each subject should include
an integrated forum where students and teachers can engage in asynchronous
discussions related to the learning content. The forum must support text posts,
threaded replies, and file attachments to facilitate sharing of questions, expla-
nations, and supporting materials. Teachers should have moderation privileges,
including the ability to delete posts. This feature is intended to support com-
munication during and outside of classroom hours, offering a more structured
alternative to Lectio’s existing messaging system, allowing students to seek help
both from the teacher and other students.
4.1.3.2
Non-Functional requirements
• Data Privacy and Security: The platform must adhere to strong data pri-
vacy and security standards in accordance with GDPR and institutional policies
[2]. All personal data, such as student activity, quiz results, and discussion posts,
must be stored securely and access-controlled to prevent unauthorized access.
To further protect user data, all AI-powered features (e.g., automatic quiz gen-
eration) must rely on locally hosted language models (LLMs), ensuring that no
educational content or student data is sent to external servers or third-party
APIs. Additionally, the system should avoid unnecessary data retention and
provide clear separation between classroom features and administrative data
(e.g., grades) to minimize the risk of accidental disclosure.
• User Interface Simplicity and Classroom Focus: The platform’s user in-
terface should be clean, intuitive, and focused exclusively on features relevant

to classroom learning. For students, learning material must be organized in
a way that minimizes distractions and allows quick access to relevant content.
For teachers, it should be easy to manage modules and to add, remove, or edit
activities with minimal effort. To maintain simplicity and ensure compliance
with data privacy and institutional policies, the platform should exclude broader
administrative features found in Lectio, such as grade reporting or assignment
submission, which fall outside the classroom use case. This addresses the pri-
mary issues expressed by teachers and students with platforms like Moodle and
Lectio, namely the complexity of setup for teachers and the excessive number

• Performance and Compatibility: The platform should be lightweight and
responsive, ensuring fast load times and smooth interaction across devices, even
on school-provided or lower-end hardware. It must be compatible with com-

top and tablet usage without requiring installation. Performance must remain
acceptable under typical classroom conditions, including multiple students ac-

• Scalability: The platform must be designed with scalability in mind to support
potential deployment across multiple Danish upper secondary schools, including
both STX, HTX, HHX, HF and vocational institutions. The system architecture
should allow for the smooth handling of increased numbers of users, modules,
and concurrent quiz activity without significant performance degradation. This
includes the ability to scale both data storage and server-side processing as
needed for broader institutional adoption. While server-side processing in the
final deployed product will be managed by MaCom, the prototype should be
developed using a scalable architecture to ensure a smooth transition to full-
scale implementation.

The map illustrates the typical steps a teacher follows when attempting to moni-

begins with the teacher’s motivation: the desire to track student performance and

they can either manually create quizzes or flashcards, or use the built-in AI-powered

Once the content and associated exer-
cises are prepared, the teacher shares the material with students, who engage with it
asynchronously.
As students begin interacting with the module, the platform passively tracks their
progression, monitoring opened activities, completed quizzes, and time spent on tasks.

overview. The outcomes from these tools inform further action:
• If the quiz results or engagement metrics indicate good performance, the teacher
may continue the module without changes.
• If poor results or low engagement are detected, the teacher may adjust their
teaching strategy, either by modifying content delivery or reaching out directly
to the student in question.
In this way, the user journey map illustrates how the platform closes the loop
between content creation, student interaction, and pedagogical decision-making, al-
lowing teachers to adopt a more data-informed and responsive teaching style. The
journey map was iteratively updated based on feedback from teacher interviews and
workshops and serves as a reference point for validating both feature design and user
experience throughout the development process.

concept. To achieve this, a low-fidelity prototype was created in Figma, guided by the

4.2.1
Design

tem. When a user is redirected from Lectio, they arrive at a streamlined platform
that displays only the relevant materials for a specific module (lesson). The user is
presented with a sidebar that lists subject topics, each containing its corresponding
modules. These modules serve as the core content area of the platform and can be
expanded or collapsed. Inside each module, activities are listed with icons indicating
whether they have been opened or not. At the top of each module, a progress bar
visually shows how many activities have been completed.

4.2.2
User Feedback and Results
To evaluate the initial prototype, four semi-structured interviews were conducted
with teachers from Danish upper secondary schools.
These sessions explored the
participants’ current use of e-learning tools and their impressions of the concept
presented in the Figma prototype.
4.2.2.1
Main features feedback
A key finding was that interactive elements, particularly quizzes and flashcards, are
considered highly valuable by both teachers and students. However, teachers consis-
tently highlighted that creating such materials manually is time, consuming and often
unrealistic given their existing workload. As a result, the feature they found most
compelling was the system’s ability to automatically generate interactive learning
content using AI. Based on this feedback, it was decided that AI-powered generation
of quizzes and flashcards would become the core functionality of the platform, with
all other features designed to support and enhance this primary purpose.
This decision is further supported by recent research and media coverage. For
instance, a recent Weekendavisen article from May 2025 [18] emphasizes the impor-
tance of the active recall method, where learners repeatedly retrieve knowledge from
memory, as significantly more effective than passive review strategies. Quizzes and
flashcards are especially suited for this approach, and in one referenced study, stu-
dents performed 25% better on topics where quizzes had been used. Despite this
pedagogical advantage, a national study by EVA [11] found that 73% of Danish up-
per secondary teachers cite lack of time as a key barrier to integrating digital learning
tools. This aligns with the feedback from the interviews and underscores the relevance
of AI in alleviating this challenge by automating content generation, a task AI mod-
els are particularly well-suited for due to their strength in summarization and text
transformation.
Additionally, the inclusion of ways to track student performance and participa-
tion was seen as a productive way to counteract the negative aspects of e-learning
and asynchronous teaching, this being keeping students engaged with the material
even without direct teacher supervision. The inclusion of a forum feature was also
appreciated, since Lectio currently has no such property, causing teachers to use ex-
ternal platforms like Padlet. This would make it easier for students and teachers to
communicate during asynchronous teaching both during and outside school lessons.
Regarding introducing gamification into the solution, primarily through quiz taking,
the teachers expressed that most students love gamified elements in tools like Kahoot,
which gets students excited to participate. However, it was recommended to focus
on personal competition and improvement rather than competition between peers to
avoid unnecessary stress.

New features feedback
Finally, in terms of specific feedback on the design of the first iteration, teachers sug-

Furthermore, it would provide a clear framework for leveraging organized content
during AI-powered quiz and flashcard generation, thereby streamlining preparation
and enhancing student engagement through interactive learning tools.

Iteration 2
The goal of this second prototype iteration was to demonstrate and evaluate the AI
quiz generation process in practice, using real input data and observing how well
it aligned with teacher needs and expectations, and to begin shaping the actual
structure and logic of the final platform, allowing early usability testing of features
like activity organization, student tracking, and quiz workflows based on the previous

dynamic or technical functionality. Based on teacher feedback, the most compelling
feature of the concept was the AI-powered quiz generation. Since this functionality
involves complex backend logic and real-time interaction, it could not be meaning-
fully represented or evaluated through static wireframes alone. Therefore, the second
iteration was implemented directly in code to validate the technical feasibility of
AI-powered quiz generation, test how well the automatic quiz functionality could in-
tegrate with structured subject material and provide a more realistic foundation for
gathering user feedback, especially on interactive elements like answering, tracking
progress, and generating content. Since this requires the implementation of multiple
large features, this iteration includes the majority of the development process. How
these features were implemented is covered in section 4.6.

4.3.1
Design Changes
This design of the base platform follows a similar design from iteration 1, but a
number of changes have been made based on user feedback, particularly regarding
the quiz/flashcard generation and taking, which has been developed from an idea into
a concrete implementation.
Starting with platform design changes, instead of accessing a module by clicking
the module in Lectio, the application now opens with a subject selector, and select-
ing a subject routes directly to the module of the current date and time slot. The
structure of the subject material has been adapted to user needs by organizing it
around modules, minimizing the number of clicks to find relevant material. The plat-
form sidebar now contains modules instead of topics, defaulting to the module of the
current date. The selected module content, primarily learning material, now fills all
of the main view, limiting distractions in the interface. For similar reasons, forums
have been moved inside modules to concentrate information where it is most required.
As in iteration 1, the platform continues to track whether a student has opened an
activity or completed a module. However, this functionality has now been made more
visible and engaging through a simple form of gamification, using green and yellow
indicators to signal full or partial completion.

4.3.2
Evaluation Method
To evaluate the second prototype and gather feedback from technical perspectives, a
focus group interview with eight Lectio developers at MaCom was conducted. The
purpose of this session was to evaluate the technical feasibility of our concept, explore
potential integration with the existing Lectio infrastructure, and gather feedback from
a developer perspective on the use of AI in an educational platform context. Follow-
ing this, we conducted four moderated usability tests at Gentofte Gymnasium, two
with pairs of teachers and two with pairs of students. In these sessions, participants
were asked to navigate the prototype, describe what they saw and understood, follow-
ing a think-aloud protocol, and respond to follow-up questions about usability and
functionality. The purpose was to assess how intuitively users could interact with the
platform and whether the features aligned with their expectations and workflows.
Furthermore, A/B testing was used to evaluate a key UI design decision: whether
the sidebar should display individual modules or grouped topics (called ”Forløb” in
Lectio and the prototype). Participants were presented with two interface variations
and asked to complete equivalent navigation tasks in both.
This method allowed
us to observe user preferences and interaction patterns in a controlled comparison,

4.3.3
User Feedback and Results
The focus group interview with MaCom developers primarily served an exploratory
role, helping to assess the technical viability of the prototype and gather perspec-
tives on AI use in educational platforms. While much of the discussion centered on
broader applications of AI beyond our current scope, such as feedback generation or
predictive analytics, these insights were valuable in confirming the relevance of our
chosen approach. The developers’ interest in automated content generation validated
our focus on AI-driven quiz and flashcard creation, while the breadth of suggestions
provided inspiration for potential future features. Moreover, the session helped clar-
ify the project’s scope by distinguishing between feasible short-term functionality and
longer-term possibilities. The interview also provided an opportunity to discuss how
the data used in the platform should be organized to integrate with Lectio’s database.
The moderated usability tests conducted with both teachers and students pro-
vided valuable insight into how the prototype was perceived in terms of usability,
functionality, and relevance to real-world classroom needs. Across both groups, the
platform’s design was consistently described as intuitive and easy to navigate, re-
quiring no external explanation or guidance. Participants were able to explore and
understand the interface independently, which validates the core structure and user
experience of the current iteration.
The feature that received the strongest positive response was the automatic gener-
ation of interactive content, which corroborates the findings of the previous iteration,
and the decision to focus on this as the primary driver of the product. Teachers

4.5
Software Architecture and Design Patterns
The finished learning platform follows a client-server architecture with clear sepa-
ration of concerns between the presentation layer and business logic. This design
facilitates scalability, maintainability, and independent evolution of the application’s
components. The system is structured around several key architectural patterns that
support its educational functionality while maintaining flexibility for future enhance-
ment, which this section describes.
4.5.1
Frontend Development
The user interface is built with React, a popular JavaScript framework that enables
interactive and dynamic web applications. Bootstrap provides the visual design frame-
work, ensuring the platform looks professional and works seamlessly on multiple de-
vices and browsers. The frontend handles all user interactions, from taking quizzes
to viewing learning materials and progress tracking.
4.5.2
Backend Services
The server-side functionality is powered by Python with the Flask web framework,
chosen for its simplicity and robust ecosystem for educational applications. The back-
end manages all data processing, file uploads, and API endpoints that connect the
frontend to the underlying services. Its core capabilities include document processing
to extract text from uploaded materials, and audio transcription for converting multi-
media content into usable text. The backend also handles quiz generation by running
algorithms that create relevant questions based on the provided learning materials.
In addition, it supports progress tracking and implements badge systems that help
keep students motivated and engaged throughout their learning activities.
4.5.3
Data Storage Strategy
The application employs a file-based JSON storage system rather than a traditional
database. For full release deployment, a dedicated database system would typically
be recommended. However, in this case, the final product is intended to integrate
with MaCom’s existing infrastructure, which already includes a proprietary database
solution.
Rather than developing a redundant database layer, this project uses a
JSON-based storage format that mirrors the structure and data types used by MaCom.
This decision ensures compatibility and simplifies future integration, as the JSON
data can be easily ported into MaCom’s system without significant transformation or
migration efforts.

While this approach has limitations for high-volume applications, it offers several
advantages for the current educational context. It provides simplicity, as there is no
need for a complex database setup. It also ensures portability, allowing easy deploy-
ment without introducing additional database dependencies. The structure of the

4.5.4
Client-Server Architecture
The application follows a traditional client-server model with a RESTful API interface.
The server, built with Flask, exposes a collection of endpoints that the React client
consumes. This separation offers several advantages for development and deployment.
It enables independent development, allowing frontend and backend teams to work
concurrently with minimal dependencies.
It provides deployment flexibility, since
the client and server can be deployed separately and scaled according to different
needs. It also ensures technology isolation, meaning that changes to the client or
server implementation do not affect the other component as long as the API contract
remains consistent.
The API is organized around resources such as quizzes, activities, modules, and
students rather than actions, in accordance with RESTful principles. This resource-
oriented design simplifies both the understanding and future extension of the API.

4.6.1
Lectio Integration
Integration with Lectio is a key consideration in the design of the platform, as minimiz-
ing the number of digital tools used in the classroom is essential to ensure a seamless
teaching and learning experience. However, due to the complexity of Lectio’s inter-
nal systems and lack of understanding of its backend during the development of this
prototype, a full technical integration has not been implemented.
Instead, the prototype has been designed to be data-compatible with Lectio, mak-
ing future integration by MaCom’s development team straightforward. This strat-
egy avoids the need for significant reverse engineering or detailed understanding of
Lectio’s codebase during prototyping, while still allowing the prototype to function
independently for development and testing purposes. To align the prototype with
Lectio’s data ecosystem and future integration possibilities, the following design con-
siderations were made:
• Students and module structures are represented using identifiers and fields that
resemble Lectio’s existing conventions, by organizing the platform in topics, con-
taining modules containing activities. This makes it easy to port data between
the systems.
• Learning materials in the prototype (e.g., PDFs and links) are stored using a
simplified data structure that mirrors the way course material is organized in
Lectio.
• Data used or generated by the platform, such as quiz results are stored per
student as a percentage of correct answers, consistent with the output format
used in Lectio’s own quiz system.
Furthermore, user authentication has not been implemented in the prototype. In
a full deployment, this responsibility would fall under Lectio’s existing authentication
infrastructure, which handles login and access control for both teachers and students.
Keeping authentication out of the prototype allowed for faster iteration and testing
without compromising compatibility with Lectio’s production environment.
In addition to data compatibility, the prototype has been developed with mod-
ularity and extensibility in mind to further ease future integration.
The backend
logic is decoupled from the interface and structured in a way that supports poten-
tial API exposure. Authentication is abstracted to allow straightforward replacement
with Lectio’s existing login system, and file handling is standardized to ensure con-
sistency. These architectural decisions aim to reduce integration effort for MaCom’s
development team should the platform be adopted beyond the prototype phase.
4.6.2
AI-powered quiz and flashcard generation
The application leverages artificial intelligence, specifically implemented through Large
Language Models (LLMs), to automatically create educational content in the form
==PAGE:62==
50
4 Results
of quizzes and flashcards. Both quiz and flashcard generation follow the same funda-
mental process:
• User Request Initiation: The process begins when a teacher selects content
and requests quiz or flashcard generation through the user interface. The system
captures specific parameters such as question type, number of questions, or topic
focus to customize the generated content.
• Content Extraction: The system then accesses and processes the selected
learning material, whether it’s a document, video transcript, or text passage.
For documents, this involves reading and analyzing the text; for videos, the
system first converts speech to text before processing.
• AI Processing: The extracted content is sent to a LLM along with specific
instructions about the desired educational output. The AI analyzes the mate-
rial for key concepts, important details, and relationships between ideas before
generating appropriate questions or flashcards.
• Response Formatting: Once the AI generates the content, the system struc-
tures it into a standardized format. For quizzes, this includes organizing ques-
tions, multiple-choice options, and correct answers. For flashcards, this involves
creating front/back pairs with concise information.
• Delivery to User Interface: The formatted educational materials are sent
back to the frontend application, where they appear in an intuitive interface for
teachers to review, edit if necessary, and assign to students.
This approach ensures that educational materials are consistently relevant to the
source content while saving educators valuable time. Teachers maintain control over
the final content by being able to review and modify the AI-generated materials before
making them available to students.
4.6.3
Large Language Model Selection
To power the quiz generation functionality, we utilized locally run LLMs through the
open-source framework Ollama. This approach was chosen primarily to ensure full
data privacy, as no user content is ever sent to external APIs or cloud services. Instead,
the models are executed directly on a local machine, ensuring that all processing
remains entirely offline.
When the program is initiated, the selected LLM is loaded into memory and
processes input data, such as PDF content and prompts, during the session. Once
the session ends, the memory is cleared automatically, and no session data is kept
unless explicitly saved by the application (e.g., by writing the generated quiz to a file
or saving the quiz to a module). This ensures a stateless architecture in which each
run is isolated, and the model has no memory of prior interactions. Quiz content is
generated in real time based solely on the input content and prompt.

4.6.4
Gamification Implementation
Our implementation of gamification was done by insights from user interviews as well
as existing literature on motivational design in digital learning environments. Rather
than applying gamification as a blanket feature set, we selected specific elements,
such as progress bars, badges, and celebratory feedback, that directly addressed the
needs and preferences expressed by both students and teachers. Each feature was
implemented with a focus on supporting motivation, emotional engagement, and pro-
gression visibility within the quiz-based learning experience [31]
Progress Bar
One of the gamification elements we implemented in the e-learning platform is the
progress bar, which dynamically tracks student progress through a module. The bar
is updated each time a student accesses and completes an activity, such as videos,
PDFs, or quizzes. This visual element is shown to both the student as individual
feedback, and the teacher as class-level progress analytics.

Why it was implemented:
The decision to integrate a progress bar was informed
by our user interviews with both students and teachers.
Students expressed that
progress bars helped them gain a sense of progress and idea of how they were doing
particularly in modules with mane activities. Teachers also highlighted the value of
such visual feedback, particularly when trying to monitor student engagement with
asynchronous activities like homework or group assignments.
Literature support:
Our choice is strongly validated by recent empirical research.
In a pilot study titled “From Zero to Hero: When a Simple Line Can Make All the
Difference” [30] conducted a case-control experiment with 21 students. The group
that had access to a progress bar reported a significantly better overview of the
course scope and described a stronger sense of accomplishment and motivation to
complete tasks. The authors conclude that progress bars, despite their simplicity,
hold potential to enhance the learning experience by acting as feedback mechanisms
that promote persistence and goal orientation [30].
This aligns with theoretical models like the Self-Determination Theory (SDT),
where elements such as progress bars create a sense of competence by showing learners
tangible signs of their improvement [13]. In this way, progress bars act not just as
navigation aids, but also as motivational triggers that reinforce effort, especially in
longer, self-directed modules.
Badges
To support learner motivation and engagement, a digital badge system was imple-
mented in the platform. This feature was designed to recognize and celebrate mean-
ingful learning milestones and quiz accomplishments. The badge interface consists of
two sections: ”My Badges”, which displays the badges already earned by the student;
”Available Badges”, showing which badges remain to be unlocked. A screenshot of
available quiz badges can be seen in figure 4.19.
The badge system was introduced with a focus on quiz-related achievements. For
example, students can earn badges for completing their first quiz, achieving a per-
fect score, or completing multiple quizzes with high scores. These small moments
of recognition are surfaced visually within the platform, creating an environment
that celebrates consistent effort. Additional badges are awarded for exploration (e.g.,
completing quizzes across different modules) or perseverance (e.g., retrying diﬀicult
quizzes multiple times). These examples illustrate just some of the possible badge
ideas, in practice the system could easily be expanded with many more badges to
reward different types of learning behaviors and milestones.

5.1
System Usability Scale Analysis
To evaluate the usability of the final prototype, the SUS score was calculated based
on the conducted survey. The system usability scale ranges from 0 to 100, and the
average score obtained for the final prototype was 83.6 based on responses from a
total of 20 participants, 10 students and 10 teachers. Looking more closely at the
individual responses, the lowest received score was 60, while the highest was 100 with
a standard deviation of 11.7 across all responses. This is significantly lower than most
SUS standard deviations, which typically range from 16.8 to 22.5 [35], which suggests
that respondents faced very similar experiences navigating the platform. This was
likely because the user groups of teachers and students respectively are more homo-
geneous than most other user bases, and because the prototype was designed in close
cooperation with the intended users. Familiarity with the existing learning manage-
ment system Lectio and other external e-learning tools also likely made the system
easier and faster to learn to navigate.

With a standard deviation of 11.7 and a sample size of 20, we can estimate the
system’s perceived usability with a confidence level of 95% and a margin of error
of roughly ±5 SUS points [35] (see appendix B for calculations). This means that
the true average SUS score for the system can be said with 95% confidence to lie
between 78.6 and 88.6. A sample size of 20 was deemed suﬀicient because it allows

margin of error, given the relatively low observed standard deviation of 11.7.

satisfactory. NPS classifies user sentiment as Promoter, Passive, or Detractor based
on the score’s ability to drive user recommendation [33].
The mean SUS score of 83.6 for the prototype places the system in the A grade cat-
egory, which corresponds to the 90–95th percentile, meaning usability is rated higher
than 90% of scores in the database, which corresponds to an ”Excellent” usability.
This score falls into the promoter range of Net Promoter Score (NPS) interpretation,
indicating that users would likely recommend the platform to others. Specifically, a
SUS score of 83.6 statistically correlates to an NPS of approximately 45% [34], mean-
ing the prototype could be expected to have 45% more promoters than detractors.
However, it is important to note that this mapping between SUS and NPS is based
on empirical correlations and not a direct measurement, and therefore the resulting
NPS estimate should be interpreted as an approximate indicator rather than an exact
score.

SUS survey conclusion
Taken together, the SUS results strongly suggest that the implemented prototype is
perceived as highly usable by both students and teachers. The feedback aligns with
previous qualitative findings, where participants described the system as intuitive
and simple to navigate.
The high SUS score also confirms that design decisions
such as organizing material by modules and minimizing navigation clicks successfully

At the same time, the analysis revealed a consistent pattern where both teachers
and users involved in co-creation rated the platform more positively. This overlap
introduces a potential bias that limits the ability to isolate whether positive feedback
was driven by involvement, user role, or both. While all groups fell into the “accept-

all users.

These factors should be kept in mind when interpreting the

Data Privacy and Security.
The system has been designed with strong privacy
protections, aligning with GDPR and common institutional policies. All AI-powered
features, including automatic quiz generation, rely exclusively on locally hosted lan-
guage models. No student data or educational content is sent to external APIs or
cloud-based services, eliminating third-party exposure. Additionally, sensitive admin-
istrative features, such as grades and assignment submissions, have been deliberately
excluded to reduce the risk of accidental data leaks and to support classroom-specific
use cases only.
User Interface Simplicity and Classroom Focus.
The prototype’s interface
was developed with simplicity and ease of use as guiding principles. Feedback from
teachers and students confirmed that the platform is intuitive and can be navigated
without prior instruction.
The learning material is clearly organized by modules
and all essential classroom activities, such as quizzes, flashcards, file uploads, and

activities with a few clicks, and the platform avoids including broader administrative
functions that often overcomplicate tools like Lectio and Moodle.
This focus on

features outside the scope of daily teaching. The success in keeping the prototype as
user friendly as possible is validated by the achieved SUS score of 83.6, which confirms
that the intended users find it easy to learn and navigate.
Performance and Compatibility.
The system is designed as a lightweight web
application that performs well on school-provided hardware and older devices.
It

the application to support growing numbers of students, modules, and concurrent

5.3
Limitations
While the project successfully demonstrated the potential of integrating AI-generated
interactive content and gamification into a dedicated e-learning platform for Danish
upper secondary education, several limitations should be noted when interpreting the
results.
First, the technical capabilities of the prototype were constrained by the limited
availability of suitable locally hosted LLMs and by the computing power accessible
during development and testing. This means that the AI-driven quiz and flashcard
generation relied on relatively small models, which do not fully reflect the speed
and quality achievable with more advanced models or larger server infrastructure.
This limited capacity influenced both the scope and the perceived performance of
the prototype during user testing, meaning that the full potential of AI-supported
content generation could not be demonstrated in practice. If more resources had been
==PAGE:79==
5.3 Limitations
67
available, more testing of the quiz generation could have been done, and additional
use of AI could have been tried such as other types of interactive content and AI-
generated feedback on quizzes.
Secondly, the prototype was not tested in a live classroom environment with real-
time student use under authentic teaching conditions. While the usability, features,
and interface were evaluated through simulated scenarios and user walkthroughs, the
platform’s actual impact on student learning outcomes, motivation, and knowledge
retention was not measured in a real educational setting. This means that it remains
unverified whether the AI-generated quizzes, flashcards, and gamification elements
would deliver measurable improvements in learning effectiveness over longer periods
of use.
Additionally, while the project included multiple rounds of qualitative and quan-
titative user feedback, the sample size, especially for interviews, remained modest
with potential for bias. Many participants were recruited through existing contacts,
and the timing of data collection partly coincided with the exam period and holidays,
which limited access to a broader and more diverse pool of teachers and students. As
a result, the findings may not fully represent the wide variation in needs and practices
across all Danish upper secondary schools.
Together, these limitations highlight the need for further testing in authentic
classroom contexts, exploration with more powerful AI models, and expanded user
studies to validate and refine the platform’s design and functionality for broader
deployment.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore how an e-learning platform can be designed to address
the lack of integrated, interactive digital tools in Danish upper secondary education,
reduce teachers’ workload, and support student engagement and differentiated learn-
ing. Through a combination of literature review, iterative prototype development,
and qualitative and quantitative user feedback, the project has shed light on both
the pedagogical needs of teachers and students and the technical and design consid-
erations required to address these needs effectively.
The research confirmed that current platforms like Lectio are primarily designed
for administrative tasks, such as scheduling, attendance, and grade reporting, and
offer limited functionality for supporting interactive teaching and active student par-
ticipation. This lack of pedagogical support became especially evident during the
COVID-19 pandemic, when Danish upper secondary schools were forced to transition
to fully online teaching. Many teachers struggled to maintain student engagement
and differentiate instruction because they lacked integrated digital tools for creating
interactive activities. As a result, they often had to rely on a patchwork of external
tools and third-party websites to set up quizzes, flashcards, and discussion boards.
While this helped cover immediate needs for interaction, it created a fragmented
learning experience, added extra work for teachers to manage multiple platforms,
and raised concerns about data privacy and inconsistent learning materials.
A key finding from the literature, interviews, and testing is that teachers and
students highly value interactive content, especially quizzes and flashcards that pro-
mote active recall, a proven method for strengthening knowledge retention. However,
the time required to create such materials manually remains a significant barrier
for teachers, limiting their ability to maintain engagement in asynchronous or fully
digital teaching scenarios. The thesis shows that an e-learning platform specifically
designed to integrate AI-driven automatic generation of interactive content directly
within the classroom environment can effectively overcome this challenge. By con-
solidating these features into one secure platform, instead of requiring teachers to
use multiple disconnected tools, the solution addresses the gaps exposed during the
pandemic and meets the need for more engaging, flexible, and time-eﬀicient digital
teaching methods in Danish upper secondary schools.
The project also explored the use of gamification elements, which were found to in-
crease student motivation and engagement when implemented carefully. Specifically,
features such as instant feedback on quiz answers, visible progress indicators, and dig-

ital badges for task completion were well received by students, who noted that these
elements made learning feel more interactive and rewarding. These results support
the idea that clear and immediate recognition of progress can help sustain attention
and encourage repeated use. However, it is essential to use gamification responsibly
to ensure that game-like features do not overshadow actual learning goals or create
unhealthy competition among students. This means prioritizing designs that encour-
age personal progress and self-improvement rather than constant peer comparison,
providing options for students to choose their own pace, and integrating gamified ele-
ments that directly reinforce the subject matter rather than distracting from it. This
aligns with previous research but also highlights that thoughtful, student-centered
implementation is crucial to balance extrinsic motivation with meaningful, intrinsic
learning outcomes.
Similarly, artificial intelligence presents promising opportunities for supporting
teachers and enhancing learning in ways that would otherwise be too time-consuming
or impractical. In this project, AI was primarily used for automatic generation of
quizzes and flashcards, but future applications could include adaptive learning paths,
personalized feedback, or real-time identification of students who need extra help.
These possibilities underline the potential for AI to reduce teacher workload while
also offering students a more tailored and engaging learning experience. However,
to ensure AI is used ethically in education, it is critical that systems are transpar-
ent about how student data is processed, that sensitive information remains securely
stored ideally through locally hosted open-source models, and that teachers and stu-
dents retain clear control over what data is collected and how it informs learning ac-
tivities. Keeping privacy, explainability, and fairness at the forefront will help build
trust and ensure that AI complements, rather than replaces, pedagogical judgment
and student autonomy.
Usability testing showed that the platform’s user interface and user experience
met the goal of being intuitive and time-eﬀicient for teachers and students.
The
qualitative interviews as well as the achieved System Usability Scale (SUS) score
support this, although the sample size and participant selection should be considered
when interpreting the results. The SUS score was well above the commonly accepted
threshold for good usability, indicating that users found the platform clear, easy to
navigate, and practical for classroom use. According to standard SUS interpretation,
this suggests that the platform’s interface is perceived as excellent by most users,
increasing the likelihood that teachers and students would adopt it willingly in a real
educational setting. The feedback also emphasized that an effective e-learning tool
should have a clean and uncluttered interface, with a clear structure that makes it
easy to find and access learning materials and activities with as few clicks as possible.
Unnecessary features and menus should be avoided to reduce cognitive load and
prevent confusion, especially for students who switch frequently between different
digital tools. For teachers, it should be straightforward to create, edit, and organize
modules and activities without needing extensive technical training.
Overall, the
findings underline that simplicity and focus on essential classroom functions are key
to encouraging adoption and daily use in busy school environments.

full-scale implementation. This section first outlines key recommendations for future
actions and implications for continued research, and then describes concrete areas of
future work on the prototype itself. Together, these next steps aim to expand the
system’s capabilities, increase its educational impact, and further align it with the
needs of students and teachers in Danish upper secondary schools.
6.1.1
Implications for Future Research
Implications of AI in education – benefits, risks, and teacher perspectives.
As AI tools become increasingly integrated into education, future research should ex-
amine their broader implications. This includes potential risks such as overreliance,
lack of transparency, bias in generated content, and reduced teacher autonomy, as
well as benefits like increased eﬀiciency, personalization, and workload reduction. Re-
search is needed to ensure that AI is implemented in ways that support, rather than
undermine, student learning and teacher roles. This includes developing pedagogically
sound guidelines, safeguards against misuse, and ethical frameworks for responsible
deployment.
More research on impact of gamification on learning outcomes.
Gamified
elements like progress tracking, streaks, and rewards were central to the developed
prototype and were informed by existing literature on educational gamification. How-
ever, given the importance of these features in the platform’s design, further research
is needed to evaluate their actual impact on learning outcomes, engagement, and
student motivation in practice.
Rather than proposing entirely new gamification
mechanisms, future studies should examine how existing elements function across dif-
ferent student demographics and learning contexts, including potential unintended
consequences.

Finally, the educational impact of
the platform should be empirically tested. Future studies could examine whether the

Next Steps for the Prototype
Full integration with Lectio.
Although the prototype was developed to function
independently, full integration with MaCom’s Lectio platform remains a critical goal
for long-term adoption. This would allow teachers to import subject data directly
from Lectio, such as class rosters, course schedules, and uploaded materials, stream-
lining setup and reducing the administrative burden. Furthermore, quiz results and
activity tracking data could be exported back into Lectio, consolidating student per-
formance information within a single system. Achieving full integration would require
collaboration with MaCom developers and alignment with Lectio’s data architecture
and security protocols. As an initial step toward this integration, it is recommended
to initiate a limited pilot program in close collaboration with Lectio’s development
team. This pilot would serve to identify potential integration points, assess API com-
patibility, and evaluate compliance with relevant data security and privacy standards.
Insights from the pilot could inform the design of a scalable solution and facilitate a
smoother transition toward full integration.
While the current prototype relies on a locally hosted language model running
on a developer machine, a full-scale deployment would require a robust and secure
infrastructure for AI-powered quiz and flashcard generation. Crucially, the current
platform’s design keeps generation on the teacher’s side only, meaning students do not
directly trigger heavy AI workloads. This significantly reduces the required compute
resources compared to real-time, student-facing AI systems.
For nationwide use across Danish upper secondary schools, covering thousands
of teachers, a cloud-based infrastructure would likely be suﬀicient to handle peak
demand. While running the system on-premises could, in theory, offer more direct
control, it is unlikely to be feasible for MaCom given its size and limited IT capacity
(10–15 developers/IT staff). Maintaining and securing an internal GPU cluster would
require significant expertise and operational overhead. Therefore, the recommended
approach is to deploy the system via a trusted EU-based cloud provider with strong
GDPR compliance. By ensuring all data processing remains within EU borders, using
encryption, and avoiding the inclusion of personal student data in AI tasks, this setup
can meet both scalability and data protection requirements.
