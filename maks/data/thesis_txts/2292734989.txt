INTRODUCTION 
 
In the age of global warming where our use of energy is inching our world closer and 
closer to an inhospitable home for our future generations, every stone is turned to re-
duce the energy that is superfluously dissipated in inefficient equipment. In this thesis 
the stone getting turned is audio amplifiers. There has been made big advances in the 
efficiencies of audio amplifiers in the last decades with the introduction of switch mode 
power amplifiers. And there may still be some improvements to gain by assimilating the 
powersupply with the amplifier in the design process.  
 
As the main part of audio amplifiers in the world, most of the time are played at levels 
far below the advertised power levels of the amplifier, advertised at excess of a couple 
of hundreds watts RMS output of a sine signal, an optimization of the dimensioning of 
the power supply would be cost beneficial and aid in energy efficiency. 
In case of a switch mode power supply, SMPS, for a CLASS D amplifier many compo-
nents can be dimensioned with smaller values and hence cost less, and also be made 
more compact. Energy efficiency can be optimized by regulating the output voltage 
from the SMPS to track the output level of the music from the amplifier. Other optimi-
zations as zero voltage switching and synchronous switching will all aid in the quest for 
energy efficiency.

1.1 
Crest factor 
 
The crest factor is a measure of the relation between the peaks and the RMS value of a 
waveform. This is important to have in mind when having to do with music which in 
nature is dynamic signals. With DC or a square wave having a crest factor of 0 dB and a 
sine a crest factor of 3 dB, music can have varying crest factors from very low to 25 dB 
and above. This has been investigated in various literatures, e.g. [1] [2] .

1.2 
Replay Gain  
 
As not every music track sounds equally load, people are accustomed to manually 
change the volume of the amplification when tracks are either to low or too high. A 
60 dB a-weighted SPL was for example found to be the average preferred listening level 
==PAGE:16==

for public domain television while the preferred listening level for portable audio play-
ers was found to be 85 dB a-wighted SPL [2]. 
To counter the differences in sound pressure levels of different kinds of music, and 
make them sound equally loud a technique has be implemented in some media players 
and some networks streamers called Replay Gain.  
 
Replay Gain consist of an algorithm to scan tracks and give them an attenuation value 
that the player can either conform to or not.  
When Replay Gain scans the music, it makes use of the knowledge about human hear-
ing. As we do not experience all frequencies in our hearing spectrum equally loud this is 
taken into account when scanning the music. 
On Figure 1-4 our hearing equal loudness contours are shown. It can be seen that our 
ears are most sensitive to the frequencies between 1 and 4 kHz, and that the frequency 
sensitivity is dependent on the SPL. 
 
 
Figure 1-4: Equal Loudness contours showing the level of a comparison tone required to 
match the perceived loudness of 1 kHz standard tone presented at different levels [4].  
 
To account for these characteristics in our hearing the music is filtered by an inverted 
approximation of the of the equal loudness curve, blue curve in Figure 1-5. A 10th order 
IIR filter approximation, red graph, is at the higher frequencies designed by MATLABs 
“yulewalk” function, while the lower frequency attenuation is achieved with a 2nd order 
Butterworth high pass filter. The resulting red curve is used by Replay Gain.  
The filter coefficients for 44.1 kHz and 48 kHz sample rates can be found on [5]. 
After filtering, the signal is chopped into 50 ms pieces and for each block the RMS val-
ue is calculated. The RMS values are then sorted into numerical order and the RMS 
levels that occurs most often would be indicated as loudness level

POWER SUPPLY DESIGN 
2.1 
Active Clamp Forward Operating Principle 
 
The Active clamp forward converter is a buck converter isolated with a transformer, 
where the magnetizing energy is actively clamped by a switch to hinder the transformer 
in getting saturated. Actively clamping the magnetisation energy allows, in comparison 
to using a tertiary reset winding, to lower voltage stresses on FETs and diodes, and is 
more efficient than using RCD or zener clamps [11]. 
 
The choice of using an active clamp forward converter in this project is based on some 
of the advantages it has; 
 
• Possibility to have duty cycles higher than 0.5 (though with a penalty in high 
voltage stresses) 
• Zero Voltage Switching of the main switch  
• Isolation of input and output by introducing a transformer.  
• Synchronous rectification 
 
Of course it also has some disadvantages that have to be kept in mind when designing 
• Additional MOSFET required for Active Reset 
• Complex gate drive circuitry 
• Critical that maximum duty cycle is clamped 
• Requires advanced PWM control technique 
 
Most of these disadvantages are however simple to mitigate as Integrated Circuits are 
available, where these concerns have been dealt with. In this thesis the integrated high 
performance active clamp/reset PWM Controller circuit, NCP1562A [12] will be uti-
lized. It readily has in-built programmable solutions for clamping the duty cycle, 2 
MOSFET gate drivers with overlap delay control and other advanced circuit protection 
features.

The switching steps of the converter waveforms are illustrated in Figure 2-2 and are as 
follows:  
 
• t0-t1: Here the main switch, Qm, is turned on while the clamp switch is off. The 
primary current, IP, that is the sum of the transformer magnetizing current and 
the reflected secondary currents, ramps up through the main switch and the 
transformer. A current also flows in the secondary side through the forward rec-
tifier diode, DREC. 
• t1-t2: The main switch turns off and the voltage Vds ramps up as the magnetizing 
current charges the drain capacitance of the switch, Cdrain. 
• t2-t3: The drain voltage reaches the clamp capacitor voltage at t2, while the pri-
mary current charges both the drain capacitance and the clamp capacitor Cclamp 
through the body diode of the clamp switch, Qclamp. 
• t3-t4: In this time interval Qclamp can turn on under Zero Voltage conditions, and 
once it turns on, the current will flow through its channel. Qclamp has to be turned 
on before the primary current changes direction at t4 as there otherwise would 
not be a path for the current to return.  
• t4-t5: After the current has reached zero at t4 the current now reverses and starts 
discharging Cclamp thereby also decreasing the drain voltage. The magnetizing 
current continues to build up until t5 where the active clamp switch is turned off. 
• t5-t6: Qclamp is turned off as fast as possible to force the magnetizing current to 
discharge the drain capacitance. If this is not possible current would still be 
flowing through Qclamp. As the drain capacitance is discharged the drain voltage 
decreases to a level determined by the inductive energy, EL, stored in the mag-
netizing and leakage inductances. If there is not enough inductive energy to 
completely discharge the drain capacitance ZVS will not be achieved.  
A way of mitigating this problem can be seen in the magnetizing energy equa-
tion (2.1), showing that for a given voltage and on time, where the product of 
LMAG and IMAG is constant, a doubling of IMAG would result in ½ LMAG. Thus to 
increase the inductive energy one could reduce LMAG.

2.8.2 
Cycle Skip 
 
As the NCP1562 PWM controller can disable the converter when a continuous overcur-
rent condition exist, this pin on the controller is used to set the time for how long time in 
overcurrent mode will be allowed, before disabling the converter.  
As measurements show, Figure 4-7, that at least 500 µs of time is needed in current lim-
iting mode to be able to charge the DC capacitor on the amplifier without the converter 
disabling, a 27 nF capacitor is placed at this pin to ground, resulting in 750 µs of cycle 
by cycle current limiting, before disabling the converter. 
 
2.8.3 
Soft Start/Soft Stop  
 
By slowly starting up the converter by ramping up the dutyratio the stresses on the con-
verter can be reduced. And also the clamp capacitor in the forward topology needs to be 
discharged while powering down the converter, as the otherwise high voltages in the 
capacitor can damage the converter. So a controlled turn off of the converter is also 
needed.  
These problems are solved in the NCP1562 controller by setting the charge/discharge 
time of the voltage in this pin by placing a capacitor to ground.  
By placing a 6.8 nF capacitor here, a soft start time of 2 ms is set, which will make it 
possible for the converter to enable again in time, if a overcurrent cycle skip event oc-
curs. 
 
2.8.4 
Overlap Delay 
 
The overlap time delay between switching the main switch and the clamp switch is set 
at this pin. By testing and looking for Zero Voltage Switching this value has been set by 
placing a 80 kΩ resistor to 140 ns leading and 190 ns trailing delay [12]. 
 
2.8.5 
Switching frequency and max duty ratio 
 
The switching frequency is set at 500 kHz and the maximum duty ratio allowable is set 
to around 0.6 by use of the graphs from the data sheet [12]. Resulting in Rt  = 12 kΩ and 
Ct = 240 pF.

TRACKING CIRCUIT 
 
The power supply has to adjust its voltage according to the amplitude of the incoming 
music signal amplitude. To do this, a reference is needed, that envelopes the maximum 
amplitude values of the signal. Using this reference in the SMPS regulation loop would 
ensure that the amplifier would have the required voltage available to reproduce the 
amplified audio signal peaks.  
This could in theory be realized by directly using the audio signal as the reference, cre-
ating an audio waveform generating power supply.  This however requires the power 
supply to both have fast rise and fast fall times, which requires low capacitive loads. As 
the amplifier in question has two 10 mF input capacitors this would not be possible. An 
investigation of using small capacitors or even removing the capacitors to do fast track-
ing could be done in the future. An example could be a closely integrated amplifier and 
SMPS board, where the supply capacitors are moved to the input of the power supply. 
 
In this thesis the method of getting the enveloping voltage will be achieved by utilizing 
a peak/hold circuit, where the effort would be focused on getting fast rise times. In this 
way the supply voltage has to ramp up really fast, and slowly decrease until a new high 
peak signal requires the high voltage. The benefit of this is also, that the input capacitors 
would be stressed less, as they would not need to charge and discharge with the music. 
They will though experience more stress than if only supplied by DC, especially when 
going from 5 V to 21 V.

As it is a power supply to a stereo amplifier both channels has to be taken into account, 
when the peak amplitude for the voltage has to be found. Above, Figure 3-1, the steps in 
finding the highest peak between the two channels and holding the value, is shown. 
 
At first the two channels are separately rectified. This is done, as an audio signal is al-
ternating, and could have peaks that are higher at the negative period also. By rectifying 
the signal a positive value of the highest peak will be available to hold. 
 
The two rectified signals are hereafter fed into a holding capacitor, where the higher of 
the two channels will dominate. This signal is then conditioned before reaching the error 
amplifier opamp. The conditioning has to be done, so that the minimum voltage does 
not go below 5 V, when no or low volumes of music is playing, and that the voltage can 
reach 21 V when the input signal is at its maximum. 
 
A DSP(Analog Devices EVAL-ADAU1701) [16] is placed in the signal path between 
the power supply and the amplifier to act as a delay line. This is needed as there is delay 
throughout the SMPS including the peak/hold circuit which will hinder the first peaks of 
a signal to have the required voltage in time. The supply capacitors of the amplifier 
worsens this, as they are of high value and require time to charge.  The audiossignal 
from the soundcard used has peak voltage level of 1.05 Vp.

a precision rectifier [17], [18], [19], [20]. They all have different characteristics and 
complexities. But they also all have their own limitations. The one chosen in this thesis 
is a rather simple High Speed Precision Rectifier from [18] and [17]. This design has 
been chosen as it shows good performance at 20 kHz, with slight distortion of the lead-
ing edge of the rectified output waveform resulting from the slew of U1. It also has high 
input impedance, which is desired, as not to affect the split audio signal.

4.2.2 
Supply voltage rise time 
 
One of the important parameters men wanting to envelope the playing audio signal as 
closely as possible, is to make the sure the voltage needed to play the peaks of a piece of 
music is readily available. If the rise time of the voltage is long, a long delay has to be 
introduced in the signal path of the music for the voltage to be ready in time. The longer 
time the supply is delivering high voltages the less the benefit of tracking the music will 
be with efficiency in mind. 
The rise time of the voltage in this design has been limited in part by the big 1000 µF 
DC capacitors on the amplifier, current available and the maximum voltage possible to 
deliver with the duty ratio clamped to a maximum of 0.6. 
In Figure 4-7, it is measured how fast the voltage rises together with the current sup-
plied to make this happen.  As the current rises really fast to begin with, so does the low 
voltage until the current limiting of the SMPS kicks in at around 40 A. After being in 
current limiting mode for about 400 µs the current slowly decreases limited by the dif-
ference between the voltage and the highest available duty ratio of SMPS.

4.4 
Setup of delay on DSP 
 
As the supply voltage to the amplifier takes time to ramp up when needed, partly be-
cause of the two 1000 µF input DC caps that have to be charged, a delay in the audio-
path has to be introduced. This will ensure, that the voltage is available for the high 
voltage requirements peaks in the audio in time, and thereby avoid clipping of the sig-
nal. As measurements show that the worst case rampup time of the voltage is around 2 –
 4 ms a delay time of 5 ms is chosen to be implemented. 
To implement the delay an evaluation board containing the ADAU1701 DSP was used. 
This board has an easy graphical programming interface inclusiver the in- and ouputs 
needed in the project.

As mentioned, the programming of the DSP was done graphically as can be seen in Fig-

which are the left and right channel of the input signal, and into a signal inverter func-

and the non-inverted signal is wanted. The signal is then put into the delay function 
blocks, where 500 sample places have been reserved. The amount of samples the DSP 
has to delay is calculated to 240 samples for 5 ms of delay time as the sampling fre-
quency is 48 kHz.

4.5.4.4 
Loudness equalized 250 mW into 4 Ω resistor 
 
These measurements, in combination with the next measurements are made to get an 
idea of the improvements in energy loss and efficiency when the music/signals are 
loudness normalized i.e. made to sound equally loud for a human listener. As speakers 
have their sensitivity given as dB at 1 m with 1 kHz, by playing a 1 kHz tone at 250 
mW and 500 mW into a resistor, can easily equate to how loud the music would be 
playing with different speakers when their sensitivities are known.  
In case of a speak with at sensitivity of 90 dB for 1 W @ 1m, driving this speaker with 
500 mW would result in 87 dBSPL at a distance of 1 m and 81 dBSPL at a distance of 
2m. Driving the same speaker with 250 mW would result in 84 dBSPL at 1 m and 78

CONCLUSION 
In this project the SMPS-Class D Amplifier combination is investigated in the pursuit of 
efficiency improvements and thereby also improvements in the footprint minimization 
of amplifier power supply design.  
 
A SMPS has been designed, that can track the peak voltages of an audio signal.  
 
It has been found that large relative improvements in efficiency of both the SMPS and 
Class D Amplifier are achievable by automatically varying the voltage required by the 
amplifier to reproduce an unclipped audio signal. Improvements are observed through-
out the loading range with exception of playing a signal with crest factor of 3 dB at full 
volume.  
 
Although implementing this kind of power tracking systems could globally save tons of 
CO2 a power saving difference of 1.5 W does not seem so big when trying to market the 
product to the public. One would have to consider the complexness of the system and 
hold it up against the cost and savings of integrating a powersupply with an amplifier. 
Another way of selling the idea could be in portable/mobile equipment, where power 
efficiency is directly connected with how long you can use your product before the bat-
tery is depleted.  
 
Some improvements that could be done and tested with this system is listed below: 
• Try with lower DC supply capacitors to be able to track and adjust the voltage 
faster. 
• Integrate SMPS with amplifier to totally remove DC supply capacitors of ampli-
fier and envelope the music signal with a bandwidth over 20 kHz.  
• Smaller transformer and inductor for reduction of size.
