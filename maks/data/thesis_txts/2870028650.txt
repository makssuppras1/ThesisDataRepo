Abstract
Cerebral palsy (CP), a non­progressive neurological disorder affecting approximately 2­3
per 1,000 live births worldwide, results in lifelong motor impairments, including spasticity,
reduced Range of Motion (RoM), and challenges in daily activities, necessitating inten­
sive, long­term rehabilitation. Conventional clinic­based therapy for children with CP is
often prohibitively expensive, geographically inaccessible, particularly in low­resource or
rural settings, and characterized by monotony, leading to low adherence and substantial
emotional and financial burdens on families. This thesis presents a proof­of­concept for
a low­cost, home­based rehabilitation system designed to address these challenges, in­
tegrating smartphone­based pose estimation using Google ML Kit, which tracks 33 key
body landmarks in real­time, with a Shape Robotics Spin mobile robot for autonomous
positioning, and Tailscale for secure, low­latency data transfer. Over a six­month pe­
riod, the system was developed and experimentally evaluated with six university students
performing hammer curl exercises, achieving a repetition counting accuracy of 61.66%
(SD = 26.86%), an average elbow RoM of 176.36° (SD = 9.23°), and strong user accep­
tance scores (expectation = 5.0, usefulness = 4.33 on a 5­point Likert scale). Despite
these promising results, limitations such as occlusion­related errors, static robot position­
ing, and the non­representative healthy cohort (versus CP children) highlight the need
for technical refinements, including dynamic repositioning algorithms inspired by Pepper­
Pose’s adaptive design (Wang et al., 2024), and clinical validation with CP patients. The
findings establish a scalable, affordable foundation (11,500 DKK total hardware cost) for
CP rehabilitation, leveraging neuroplasticity through repetitive, engaging exercises, and
propose future directions—such as integrating depth sensors/LiDAR and expanding ex­
ercise scope—to enhance therapeutic efficacy and accessibility, contributing significantly
to equitable healthcare solutions for global CP populations.

Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement
Cerebral palsy (CP) is a complex neurological condition that affects approximately 2 to 3
per 1,000 live births globally, making it one of the most common causes of childhood mo­
tor disability [1][2][3]. This non­progressive complex neurological condition encompasses
a group of disorders primarily characterized by permanent motor impairments. These im­
pairments can range from mild to severe and often coexist with sensory, cognitive, and
communication deficits.The impact of CP extends beyond the physical limitations expe­
rienced by children. They face significant challenges in performing everyday tasks, par­
ticularly due to impairments in motor functions such as reaching, grasping, and walking
[1][4]. These challenges not only impact the quality of life for the children but also place a
substantial emotional and financial burden on their families. The need for prolonged and
resource­intensive care often stretches family resources to their limits, especially during
times like pandemic, or in general in low resource countries, highlighting the urgent need
for more accessible and effective rehabilitation solutions [5] [6] .
Rehabilitation plays a pivotal role in improving motor functions and promoting indepen­
dence among children with CP. Current rehabilitation strategies emphasize high­intensity,
repetitive, and task­specific training. These approaches are designed to leverage the prin­
ciples of neuroplasticity – the brain’s capacity to reorganize itself by forming new neural
connections. Neuroplasticity is particularly crucial for motor learning in children with CP,
as it offers the potential for significant improvements in function over time[1][7]
However, conventional therapy, typically provided in clinical settings, faces several limi­
tations that hinder its effectiveness and accessibility:
• High costs associated with frequent clinic visits and specialized equipment.
• Geographic inaccessibility for many families, particularly those in rural or under­
served areas.
• Limited availability of trained professionals, leading to long wait times and infrequent
sessions.
• The monotonous nature of traditional therapy, which can cause children to lose in­
terest and engagement over time.
These limitations not only affect the consistency and intensity of therapy but also its overall
effectiveness in promoting motor skill development and functional independence.
In response to these challenges, home­based daily rehabilitation exercises have emerged
as an alternative or supplementary approach to traditional therapy. These programs are
designed to provide therapeutic exercises within the child’s home environment, offering
several potential advantages[5]:
• Increased consistency in treatment, as therapy can be integrated into daily routines.
• Potential for higher training intensities, as exercises can be performed more fre­
quently.

• Reduced logistical burdens associated with clinic visits, thus greater convenience
for families.
Studies have demonstrated that well­designed home­based exercise routines can lead
to significant improvements in motor skills and functional independence [8]. However,
several challenges remain in implementing effective home­based rehabilitation:
• Heavy reliance on parental involvement, which can lead to caregiver fatigue and
decreased adherence over time.
• Lack of advanced technological tools to provide precise feedback and real­time
monitoring.
• Difficulty in creating engaging experiences for children that maintain their interest
and motivation over extended periods.
Recent technological advancements, particularly in the fields of robotics and computer vi­
sion, offer promising solutions to these challenges. Socially assistive robotics (SAR) and
pose estimation technologies have shown significant potential in pediatric rehabilitation.[9]
These innovations not only provide precise, task­specific training but also introduce inter­
active and gamified elements that enhance children’s motivation and engagement.
Despite their promise, the integration of these technologies into scalable, low­cost sys­
tems suitable for at­home use remains an under explored area. Addressing this gap is
essential to making high­quality rehabilitation accessible to a broader population of chil­
dren with CP and their families.
This study tackles these challenges by introducing an affordable and innovative assistive
solution designed to support children with cerebral palsy (CP) in their rehabilitation jour­
ney. The proposed system leverages the patient’s personal smartphone, in combination
with autonomous mobile robots and pose estimation, to facilitate targeted limb­specific
strengthening and training exercises. By integrating these components, the solution aims
to enhance the effectiveness of rehabilitation routines while maintaining accessibility and
ease of use for both patients and caregivers.
1.2
Research Motivation
The motivation for this research arises from the intersection of two pressing needs: the ur­
gent demand for accessible rehabilitation solutions for children with CP and the untapped
potential of emerging technologies to address this demand. The integration of mobile
robotics and monocular cameras in rehabilitation systems offers a compelling opportunity
to bridge this gap. Several factors underpin this motivation­
1.2.1
Technological Feasibility
Advancements in mobile robotics and computer vision have reached a stage where real­
time human motion tracking can be achieved using affordable and widely available de­
vices. Monocular camera­based pose estimation systems[10], such as those enabled
by Google ML Kit[11], provide a cost­effective means of capturing detailed motion data.
Similarly, mobile robots equipped with adaptive algorithms can respond dynamically to a
child’s movements, ensuring precise and personalized therapy delivery. These technolo­
gies lay the groundwork for creating robust, scalable rehabilitation systems.

1.2.2
Advantages of Home­Based Therapy
Home­based therapy addresses several limitations of clinic­based interventions. By shift­
ing rehabilitation to the home environment, families can integrate therapy into daily rou­
tines, reduce travel­related stress and costs, and achieve higher training intensities [12].
Research indicates that home­based programs not only improve motor outcomes but also
empower families by involving them more actively in the therapeutic process. However,
the success of such programs depends on the availability of tools that are both user­
friendly and effective in delivering precise, engaging therapy.[5]
1.2.3
Child Engagement and Motivation
Maintaining a child’s motivation is a critical factor in the success of any rehabilitation pro­
gram. Traditional therapies often struggle to sustain engagement, particularly in younger
children. Gamification and interactive technologies have been shown to address this is­
sue effectively. Socially assistive robots, for instance, use human­robot interaction to
create engaging, personalized therapy experiences. Integrating such technologies into
home­based systems can transform rehabilitation from a monotonous chore into an en­
joyable activity, thereby enhancing adherence and outcomes.[13]
1.2.4
Accessibility and Affordability
The global burden of CP is disproportionately higher in low­ and middle­income coun­
tries, where access to specialized rehabilitation services is often limited. Even in devel­
oped countries such as USA, medical costs for children with cerebral palsy are nearly ten
times higher than the cost of caring for a child without a disability [14] . A study done
in 2019 [15], shows that for children with CP, average annualized Medicaid costs were
$22,383 (~160,000 DKK) in 2013­2015, which has only increased over the past decade
due to inflation. Another study for Australia showed that families affected by cerebral palsy
face substantial annual out­of­pocket costs averaging $4,460AUD(~20000DKK) with an
interquartile range of $11,966 (~53515.20 DKK), with expenses doubling for those with
more severe impairments (GMFCS III­V) compared to milder cases, despite the existence
of the National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS) in Australia [16]. Developing afford­
able, portable, and scalable solutions is therefore crucial. The use of monocular cameras
and mobile robots offers a promising approach to democratizing access to advanced re­
habilitation technologies. By leveraging widely available hardware, such as smartphones,
this research aims to create solutions that are both cost­effective and impactful [3]
These motivations collectively highlight the potential of integrating robotics and computer
vision into home­based rehabilitation systems. By addressing existing gaps and leverag­
ing emerging technologies, this research seeks to contribute to the broader goal of making
high­quality rehabilitation accessible to all.

for at­home rehabilitation that integrates a smartphone­based pose estimation system
with a mobile robot. This system addresses the limitations of existing approaches by
combining Google ML Kit pose detection, autonomous robot positioning, and real­time
exercise analysis for upper limb rehabilitation exercises. The specific objectives are as
follows:

1.3.1
System Development
The primary objective is to design and implement an integrated rehabilitation system that
leverages modern technological capabilities. At its core, the system utilizes a smartphone­
based pose estimation system powered by Google ML Kit, capable of tracking 33 key body
landmarks in real­time with high precision. This is complemented by a Shape Robotics
Spin mobile robot, engineered for autonomous positioning to maintain optimal viewing an­
gles during exercise sessions. The integration is facilitated through a secure data transfer
mechanism using Tailscale, ensuring reliable real­time communication between compo­
nents.
1.3.2
Exercise Analysis Implementation
A crucial objective of this research is to develop and validate comprehensive quantitative
metrics for rehabilitation and robot assessment. The system implements real­time track­
ing of elbow joint angles during hammer curl exercises, providing precise measurements
of movement execution. This is enhanced by an automated repetition counting system
that utilizes carefully calibrated joint angle thresholds to ensure accuracy. The implemen­
tation includes Range of Motion (RoM) measurements for upper limb exercises, coupled
with detailed exercise speed analysis for movement quality assessment.
1.3.3
Technical Validation
The technical validation objective focuses on demonstrating the system’s capabilities
across multiple dimensions. This includes rigorous validation of pose estimation accu­
racy specifically for upper limb tracking, ensuring reliable movement detection and analy­
sis. The assessment extends to evaluating the effectiveness of robot repositioning during
exercise execution, confirming the system’s ability to maintain optimal viewing angles
autonomously. Comprehensive testing of the real­time data transfer reliability between
system components ensures seamless operation, while detailed evaluation of exercise
detection and counting accuracy validates the system’s practical utility.
1.3.4
Usability Assessment
The final objective centers on evaluating the system’s practical applicability in real­world
scenarios. This assessment utilizes a carefully modified 5­point Likert scale questionnaire
to gather structured feedback on user acceptance [17][18][19]. The evaluation framework
encompasses six key dimensions: acceptability, usefulness, expectation, trust, prefer­
ence, and concern, providing a comprehensive understanding of user interaction with the
system. The assessment also includes collection and analysis of qualitative feedback
regarding system operation and user experience, offering insights into potential improve­
ments and refinements for future iterations.
Through the achievement of these objectives, this research aims to validate both the
technical feasibility and user acceptance of an integrated smartphone­robot rehabilita­
tion system. This validation will establish a solid foundation for the future development
of accessible at­home rehabilitation solutions for children with cerebral palsy, potentially
transforming how rehabilitation services are delivered in home environments. Therefore,
the research question of this master thesis is­
”Can a low­cost integration of smartphone­based pose estimation and a mobile
robot provide an effective and accessible rehabilitation solution for children with
cerebral palsy in home settings?”

1.4
Scope and Limitations
This research represents an initial step toward addressing the broader challenges of home
based rehabilitation for children with CP. While the study focuses on developing a basic
proof­of­concept system, it is essential to acknowledge its scope and limitations:
1.4.1
Scope
• Target Population: The system is designed for children with CP who experience motor
impairments, particularly those affecting the upper and lower limbs.
• Technological Integration: The research explores the integration of monocular cam­
eras and mobile robots, utilizing existing platforms such as Google ML Kit for pose esti­
mation.
• Environmental Context: The system is tested in controlled, home­like environments
to simulate real­world conditions and evaluate feasibility.
• Key Metrics: Metrics such as system accuracy, usability and engagement are used to
evaluate the proof­of­concept.
1.4.2
Limitations
• Technical Constraints: As a proof of concept, the system has limited capability to han­
dle complex motor impairments or diverse home environments. Future iterations will need
to address these challenges.
• Sample Size: The study involves a small sample size with university students rather
than the intended pediatric population with cerebral palsy , which may limit the generaliz­
ability of its findings.
• Parental Involvement: The system requires active parental participation( based on the
type/severity of cerebral palsy and age of the child), which may pose challenges for fam­
ilies with limited time or resources.
• Exercise Scope: The system focuses on a specific set of rehabilitation exercise, ex­
cluding more complex or diverse motor tasks.
• Hardware Limitations: The reliance on monocular cameras may affect pose estimation
accuracy compared to systems employing depth sensors or stereo cameras.The fixed po­
sition of the monocular camera on the robot also limits the range of view for the robot.
• Sedentary Lifestyle: Most people with CP spend 76­99% of waking hours being seden­
tary Only 2­7% of time is spent in moderate to vigorous activities. [12]
By defining these parameters, this research establishes a clear framework for evaluating
the proposed system while identifying areas for future improvement. The ultimate goal is
to demonstrate the potential of integrating robotics and computer vision into home based
rehabilitation, paving the way for more comprehensive and accessible solutions in the
future.

2.1
Cerebral Palsy and Rehabilitation Needs
2.1.1
Medical Context and Rehabilitation Challenges
Cerebral palsy (CP) is one of the most prevalent childhood motor disabilities, affecting an
estimated 17 million individuals worldwide. This lifelong condition is the result of damage
to the developing brain, leading to impairments in body movement, muscle coordination,
and, in many cases, cognitive and sensory functions [20][13] [12]. One of the most dis­
abling features of CP is gait impairment, which is clinically characterized by reduced walk­
ing speed, endurance, and step length, along with inefficient motor control [20]. These
impairments hinder independence and significantly affect the quality of life for affected
children and their families.
The neurobiological basis of CP varies depending on the type and severity of brain dam­
age. Abnormalities such as periventricular white matter injury, basal ganglia damage,
and cortical malformations are commonly observed in individuals with CP [7]. Recent
neuroimaging studies suggest that early intervention can improve motor outcomes by
leveraging neuroplasticity, particularly in the first two years of life [7]. However, despite
the potential benefits of early rehabilitation, access to specialized therapy remains a global
challenge due to financial, geographical, and logistical constraints. Additionally, dispar­
ities in healthcare accessibility across different socioeconomic backgrounds exacerbate
the challenge, limiting the availability of high­quality rehabilitation programs for underpriv­
ileged children [13].
The rehabilitation of children with CP typically involves a multidisciplinary approach, in­
cluding physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech­language therapy, among
others. The primary goals of rehabilitation are to improve motor function, enhance inde­
pendence in activities of daily living, and promote overall well­being [20]. Achieving these
goals requires an understanding of the diverse needs and challenges faced by children
with CP and their families.
2.1.2
Importance of Repetitive and Engaging Exercises for Children
Rehabilitation for CP focuses on repetitive and engaging exercises that enhance motor
learning through neuroplasticity [7].The central premise behind task­oriented rehabilita­
tion is that frequent and intensive practice leads to improved functional outcomes. Studies
indicate that repetition­based training programs help reinforce neuroplasticity by strength­
ening neural pathways associated with movement execution [20].
A randomized controlled trial found that children who underwent repetitive locomotor train­
ing with an electromechanical gait trainer showed significant improvements in walking
speed, endurance, hip kinematics, and step length [20]. These findings emphasize the
role of task­specific training and the potential benefits of assistive technology in enhanc­
ing rehabilitation outcomes. Additionally, maintaining motivation in pediatric rehabili­
tation is a recognized challenge. Children with CP often experience difficulty adhering
to traditional rehabilitation programs due to monotony and lack of engagement. Research
indicates that incorporating gamification, interactive systems, and real­time feedback into
rehabilitation protocols can improve adherence and overall outcomes [21]
Further advancements in interactive rehabilitation systems have shown that using multi­
media stimulation and EEG­based concentration tracking can enhance engagement lev­

tasks based on cognitive engagement levels represents a major step forward in tailoring

movements, improving both gross and fine motor functions [22].
The importance of repetitive and engaging exercises in pediatric rehabilitation cannot

2.2.1
Current Technologies
The field of rehabilitation technology has seen considerable advancements, particularly in
home­based settings. Wearable motion capture systems, exoskeletons, and virtual reality
(VR) have emerged as promising solutions for motor rehabilitation. These technologies
enable remote monitoring of motor functions and provide real­time feedback, thereby re­
ducing dependence on in­person therapy sessions [13]
Wearable Motion Capture Systems
Wearable motion capture systems utilize inertial measurement units (IMUs) and gyro­
scopes to track body movements. These systems are typically worn on joints and limbs
to capture kinematic data which is then transmitted to a mobile device or computer for
analysis. Studies have shown that wearable sensors can accurately monitor joint angles
and movement trajectories, facilitating detailed movement assessments for motor reha­
bilitation. Additionally, they provide real­time feedback, enhancing the self­management
of rehabilitation exercises.
Despite their accuracy, wearable systems have limitations, including discomfort during
prolonged use and calibration issues due to sensor drift. Furthermore, they require precise
placement on the body, and improper alignment can significantly reduce data accuracy.
As a result, researchers are exploring sensor fusion techniques to improve robustness
and minimize errors. [13] [23]
Exoskeletons in Rehabilitation
Exoskeletons are robotic devices worn on the body to assist or resist joint movements,
providing targeted motor training. They are particularly effective for gait training and up­
per limb rehabilitation. Exoskeletons can deliver highly controlled, repetitive movement
patterns, promoting neuroplasticity and enhancing motor learning in patients with CP. Re­
search indicates that robotic exoskeletons improve muscle coordination and joint align­
ment during repetitive training sessions.
However, exoskeletons have several limitations, including high costs[24], limited adapt­
ability to individual patient needs, and restricted mobility due to their bulk and weight.
Additionally, the lack of cognitive engagement and social interaction can reduce patient’s
enthusiasm to rehabilitation protocols. Recent advancements are focusing on lightweight
exoskeletons with adaptive control algorithms to enhance patient experience and thera­
peutic outcomes. [13] [23]
Virtual Reality (VR) in Rehabilitation
VR has gained traction as an immersive rehabilitation tool that enhances engagement
and motivation through gamified experiences. VR­based rehabilitation systems use 3D
simulations to create virtual environments where patients can practice motor tasks in a
safe and controlled setting. These systems provide real­time feedback on movement ac­
curacy, enabling patients to self­correct their postures and improve motor skills. Addition­
ally, VR has been shown to reduce anxiety and pain perception by providing a distractive
and enjoyable experience.
A significant advantage of VR is its adaptability, allowing therapists to customize exer­
cise routines based on patient progress and rehabilitation goals. However, cybersickness
(nausea and disorientation caused by VR headsets) remains a challenge, particularly for
pediatric patients, not to mention the difficulty in putting on the headset by themselves.
Furthermore, VR systems require expensive hardware and technical expertise for setup
and maintenance, limiting their accessibility in low­resource settings.[23][13]
Augmented Reality (AR) in Rehabilitation
AR superimposes digital information onto the real­world environment, offering an inter­
active experience that enhances engagement during rehabilitation exercises. Unlike VR,

deployment of these systems in clinical practice.[13] [8]
2.2.2
Advantages and Limitations of These Solutions
Despite their potential, current rehabilitation technologies face limitations that hinder widespread
adoption. VR and motion capture systems, while effective, often require specialized hard­
ware and software, which can be costly and inaccessible to many families . Additionally,
many robotic­assisted rehabilitation devices, such as exoskeletons, are expensive and
require significant technical expertise for operation [13] [23].
Another critical limitation is the adaptability of these technologies to home environments.
Wearables and motion capture systems often require the supervision of therapists, which
reduces their feasibility for fully independent use [13]. Exoskeletons, while beneficial in
controlled environments, may not be suitable for pediatric CP patients due to challenges
in customization and mobility. Given these limitations, there is a pressing need for af­
fordable, user­friendly, and scalable rehabilitation technologies that can be seamlessly
integrated into home settings.
The successful adoption of rehabilitation technologies in home­based settings requires
careful consideration of factors such as cost, ease of use, and adaptability to individual
needs. Overcoming these limitations is crucial to ensuring equitable access to high­quality
rehabilitation services and empowering families to actively participate in the recovery pro­
cess without stress.

The proposed system for home­based rehabilitation integrates a monocular camera,
a mobile robot, and a pose estimation framework to enhance rehabilitation for chil­

analysis of motor function and provides detailed movement assessment without the
need for additional expensive hardware.
• Mobile Robot: The system includes a self­adjusting robot equipped with Blue­

the patient’s movements. It can reposition itself based on detected occlusions and
thresholds, ensuring that all relevant joints remain within the camera’s field of view.
• Processing Unit: The computer serves as the central processing hub, running
the pose estimation data processing code and transmitting control signals to the

3.1.2
Data Flow and Processing Pipeline
1. Frame Capture and Preprocessing →The smartphone captures video input of the
subject in real time, with the Google Pixel 8’s camera system operating at 1920x1080
resolution and 30 frames per second to ensure high­quality input for pose detection.
Before pose detection begins, the image needs to be processed. This typically takes
5­10ms on modern devices. The system implements rate limiting that varies based
on the robot’s state :
­ During exercise state: Minimum delay of 0.1 seconds (maximum 10 FPS)
­ Other states: Minimum delay of 0.5 seconds (maximum 2 FPS)
The system implements adaptive sampling frequencies, modulating data acquisition
rates according to activity context. During exercise intervals, the framework utilizes
enhanced temporal resolution (maximum 10 FPS) to adequately capture biome­
chanical dynamics, while employing reduced sampling (maximum 2 FPS) during
non­exercise states to optimize computational efficiency. Strategically allocating
computational resources based on analytical relevance, the system maintains suffi­
cient kinematic detail during critical rehabilitation monitoring phases while mitigating
potential processing bottlenecks that could compromise real­time performance.
2. Pose Detection and Landmark Extraction →The Google ML Kit Pose Estimation
API processes the input, identifying and tracking key body landmarks. The extracted
data is sent to the computer at 30 frames per second to determine whether adjust­
ments are needed for optimal positioning.
3. Robot Position Adjustment→If critical landmarks show confidence scores below
0.9 or are only partially visible, the Shape Robot moves backward until all landmarks
are clearly visible. When all required data points can be seen, the robot automati­
cally goes into the Right Hammer Curl exercise mode and moves to a position that
has been experimentally chosen for this study.
4. Joint Angle Calculation →Using vector mathematics, the system computes the
elbow joint angle between the upper arm (shoulder­to­elbow) and forearm (elbow­
to­wrist) vectors.
5. Exercise State Detection→The system monitors the elbow angle transitions be­
tween carefully calibrated thresholds (extension: 160° and flexion: 35°) to accu­
rately detect and count exercise repetitions while maintaining proper form require­
ments.
Each stage in the pipeline is designed to operate within specific time constraints to

3.7
Data Protection and GDPR Compliance
Privacy­by­Design Approach:
This research implements a privacy­by­design approach
in accordance with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) framework. Data pro­
tection principles were integrated into the system architecture from the initial design phase
rather than added as an afterthought. This approach ensures that privacy considerations
are fundamental to the system rather than superficial additions.[33]
The system architecture specifically addresses GDPR requirements through several key
measures:
1. Data Minimization: The pose estimation system collects only skeletal landmark
data necessary for exercise analysis, avoiding the storage of raw video footage. This
approach minimizes personal data processing in accordance with Article 5(1)(c) of
GDPR, which requires personal data to be ”adequate, relevant and limited to what
is necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed.”
2. Local Processing: The primary processing of visual data occurs locally on the
participant’s smartphone, with only abstracted landmark coordinates transmitted for
analysis. This reduces privacy risks associated with transferring potentially identifi­
able visual information.
3. Secure Data Transmission: All data transmission between system components
occurs via the Tailscale VPN implementation, ensuring end­to­end encryption of all
rehabilitation data. This fulfills the GDPR requirement for appropriate technical mea­
sures to ensure data security (Article 32).
Patient­Owned Device Strategy :
A significant privacy­enhancing feature of our sys­
tem is its utilization of the patient’s own smartphone for data capture and initial processing:
1. Enhanced Data Control: By leveraging the patient’s personal device rather than
requiring a separate dedicated camera system, users maintain greater control over
their personal data. They can directly access, review, and if necessary, delete the
application and associated data.
2. Reduced Third­Party Access: The use of personal devices eliminates the need to
install external recording equipment in the home environment, reducing concerns
about continuous monitoring or unauthorized access to recording devices.
3. Familiar Privacy Settings: Patients are typically already familiar with the privacy
settings and capabilities of their own devices, increasing comfort and trust in the
system. This familiarity with device security features enhances user confidence in
data protection.
4. Device­Level Security: Modern smartphones incorporate robust security features
such as biometric authentication, device encryption, and secure enclaves, provid­
ing additional layers of protection that would be costly to implement in dedicated
hardware.
This approach was validated by our participant feedback (mean score = 4.0, SD = 0.632
as shown in Table 5.5), indicating that users felt more comfortable with privacy aspects
when using their own devices rather than built­in cameras on the robot.

5.3
Discussion and Contextualization
The proposed home­based rehabilitation system, integrating smartphone­based pose es­
timation with a mobile robot, represents a launching step toward accessible and engaging
therapy for children with cerebral palsy (CP). The quantitative results—61.66% repetition
counting accuracy, an average Range of Motion (RoM) of 176.36°, and variable repeti­
tion speeds—coupled with strong user acceptance (e.g., mean expectation score of 5.0)
provide a mixed picture of technical feasibility and practical potential. While the system
demonstrates promise as a low­cost, scalable solution, its performance reveals critical
areas for improvement, particularly when contextualized within the landscape of reha­
bilitation technologies and the unique needs of CP patients. This subsection critically
analyzes these findings, benchmarks them against existing literature, and explores their
implications for pediatric rehabilitation, caregiver support, and future technological devel­
opment.
Technical Performance in Context:
The system’s repetition counting accuracy of 61.66%
is a modest achievement for a proof­of­concept but falls short of benchmarks established
by more advanced rehabilitation technologies. For instance, Zhao et al. [10] achieved
85% accuracy in classifying gross motor function in CP children using monocular video
with STGCN(refer to Section 2.4), a method that leverages temporal dependencies to
enhance motion tracking. Similarly, wearable motion capture systems with inertial mea­
surement units (IMUs), as reviewed by Aqel et al. [13], routinely exceed 90% accuracy
in detecting repetitive exercises, benefiting from direct sensor placement on the body.
Our system’s lower accuracy stems primarily from its reliance on 2D pose estimation via
Google ML Kit, which struggles with occlusions and lacks depth data. This limitation was
particularly evident in participants P1 and P3, where false negatives dominated due to
suboptimal robot positioning and partial landmark visibility.
In contrast, the system’s RoM tracking (average 176.36°, SD = 9.23°) aligns closely with
healthy adult norms (approximately 180°), validating the precision of the joint angle cal­
culation algorithm (Section 3.4.1). However, this metric’s relevance to CP rehabilitation is
nuanced due to the experiment’s sample population. Beretta et al. [4] reported baseline

==PAGE:52==
RoM values of 90­120° in CP children with upper limb impairments, with robotic therapy
yielding gains of 10­15° over weeks. Our healthy cohort’s near­maximal RoM reflects the
absence of motor deficits but underscores a disconnect: the system must be recalibrated
to detect smaller, therapeutically significant ranges typical of CP patients. Variability in
RoM consistency (e.g., P4’s SD = 15.30° vs. P1’s 6.33°) further suggests sensitivity
to viewing angles and participant fatigue, issues compounded by the monocular cam­
era’s limitations compared to multi­camera or depth­sensing systems (e.g., Kinect, used
in Mousavi et al. [22]).
Repetition speed (average 26.47s/set) offers additional insights but are hampered by in­
complete data due to missed repetitions. The observed trends—P5’s acceleration (22.24s
to 13.07s) and P4’s slowing (38.41s to 21.90s)—mirror fatigue patterns in healthy adults
but lack the granularity needed for CP­specific analysis. Our system’s reliance on ac­
curate pose detection for timing exercise repetitions creates a feedback loop: that com­
promises speed calculations and fatigue assessment. The repetition counting algorithm
(Section 3.4.2) relies on accurate joint angle detection (e.g., elbow flexion/extension) to
increment counts, but missed repetitions—due to occlusions or low landmark visibility
(<0.90, Section 4.1.1) or exercise speed —truncate the data used for timing, as seen in
incomplete sets like P1’s single set (Section 5.1.3). This truncation skews speed mea­
surements (e.g., 26.47s/set average, Section 5.1.3), reducing the reliability of the fatigue
index ( Section 3.4.3) by limiting the ability to detect RoM degradation or movement con­
sistency, critical for CP rehabilitation. This issue, aggravated by static robot position­
ing, highlights the need for improvements like dynamic repositioning, as noted in Chapter
6, to enhance pose detection accuracy and support fatigue assessment. Compared to
exoskeleton­based systems [20], which provide controlled resistance and precise timing,
our lightweight approach trades precision for affordability but requires algorithmic robust­
ness to match clinical utility.
Benchmarking Against Rehabilitation Technologies:
When placed alongside exist­
ing home­based rehabilitation solutions, the system occupies a unique niche but reveals
trade­offs. Wearable motion capture systems (Arntz et al.[23]) offer high accuracy (>90%)
and detailed kinematic data but are costly and require precise sensor placement, limiting
accessibility for home use. Exoskeletons, while effective for gait and upper limb training
(e.g., 15­20% gait speed improvement in Smania et al.[20]), are way more expensive and
bulky, rendering them impractical for widespread adoption. Virtual reality (VR) systems,
such as those in Mousavi et al.[22], achieve engagement through gamification but face
challenges like cybersickness and hardware costs, issues our system avoids by leverag­
ing ubiquitous smartphones.
Socially assistive robots (SARs) that are designed to interact with and support people of­
fer a useful point of comparison. Research by Blankenship et al. [35] found that these
robots can help children with Cerebral Palsy stay more engaged in their rehabilitation Our
system’s qualitative results (e.g., usefulness = 4.33, trust = 4.33) echo these findings,
suggesting that the Shape Robotics Spin robot’s presence enhances motivation, a critical
factor given CP children’s aversion to monotonous therapy [21]. However, unlike SARs
with advanced interaction, our robot’s static positioning during exercises limits real­time
adaptability, a gap reflected in the 61.66% accuracy. Integrating dynamic repositioning,
similar to SLAM­enabled robots, could bridge this divide, aligning our system more closely
with SAR benchmarks. The reason we did not integrate SLAM­enabled dynamic reposi­
tioning in our system due to its computational and cost constraints, which are inconsis­

Interdisciplinary Significance:
Beyond rehabilitation, this work intersects with robotics,
computer vision, and human­computer interaction (HCI). In robotics, the system’s inte­
gration of pose estimation with autonomous positioning contributes to the growing field of
assistive mobile robots, though its static behavior during exercises lags behind adaptive
systems (e.g., PepperPose [19]). In computer vision, the reliance on monocular pose esti­
mation highlights both the potential and limits of low­cost 2D tracking, pushing the case for
hybrid solutions (e.g., 2D + depth fusion) in resource­constrained settings. From an HCI
perspective, the high trust (4.33) and privacy comfort (4.0) validate a patient­owned de­
vice strategy, offering a model for privacy­conscious design in healthcare technologies—a
priority under GDPR [33].
The system’s scalability also has socioeconomic implications. With CP prevalence at 2­
3 per 1,000 live births [3] and disproportionate burden in low­income regions, affordable
tools like ours could democratize access, aligning with WHO’s call for assistive technology
equity [2]. However, achieving this requires overcoming technical hurdles (e.g., accuracy,
adaptability) and ensuring usability across diverse home environments, from cramped
urban apartments to rural settings with uneven terrain.
Critical Reflection and Broader Impact:
Reflecting on the results, the system’s mod­
erate performance underscores a classic engineering trade­off: cost vs. capability. By
prioritizing affordability and simplicity—hallmarks of scalability—it sacrifices the precision
of clinical­grade systems, a deliberate choice for a proof­of­concept but a barrier to imme­
diate adoption. The 61.66% accuracy, while below therapeutic thresholds, is not a failure
but a baseline, revealing the feasibility of integrating off­the­shelf components (Google ML
Kit, Shape Robotics Spin) into a novel framework. This aligns with the work of Jatesik­
tat et al. [8], who advocate autonomous modeling of repetitive movement as a stepping
stone to refined systems.
The broader impact lies in its potential to shift rehabilitation paradigms. If scaled and
refined, it could reduce the annual cost of conventional therapy by thousands of dollars
[14][15][16] [5], empower families, and leverage neuroplasticity through consistent home
practice [7]). Yet, without addressing occlusion,positioning, expanding exercise scope,
and validating with CP patients, it remains a prototype rather than a solution. The qualita­

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Summary of Contributions
This thesis presents a proof­of­concept system for home­based rehabilitation, integrating
smartphone­based pose estimation (Google ML Kit) with a mobile robot (Shape Robotics
Spin) to support upper limb exercises for children with cerebral palsy (CP). Over six
months, the system was developed and tested with six university students performing
hammer curl exercises, yielding an average repetition counting accuracy of 61.66%, a
mean Range of Motion (RoM) of 176.36° (SD = 9.23°), and promising user acceptance
scores (e.g., expectation = 5.0, usefulness = 4.33 on a 5­point Likert scale). These re­
sults demonstrate the feasibility of combining low­cost, off­the­shelf technologies into an
accessible rehabilitation framework, addressing key challenges in conventional CP ther­
apy: cost, geographic inaccessibility, and engagement.
The system’s primary contribution lies in its affordability and scalability potential. By lever­
aging a Google Pixel 8 and a Spin robot—far less costly or bulky than exoskeletons or
VR setups— it offers a viable alternative for families burdened by the high annual cost
of clinic­based therapy (Fortin­Bédard et al., 2023). Qualitative feedback underscores its
motivational appeal, mirroring the engagement benefits of socially assistive robots (SARs)
like those in Blankenship and Bodine [35]. Similar to PepperPose (Wang et al.[19]), which
uses the Pepper robot to enhance human­robot interaction through precise pose estima­
tion, our system employs a robotic companion to sustain user interest, though with a focus
on therapeutic rather than social outcomes. The privacy­conscious design, utilizing per­
sonal smartphones over built­in cameras (mean privacy comfort = 4.0), further aligns with
ethical standards, paralleling PepperPose’s emphasis on user trust in interactive settings.
Technically, the system advances the application of monocular pose estimation in reha­
bilitation by adapting Google ML Kit’s 33­landmark tracking to monitor exercise execu­
tion in real­time, a feat comparable to PepperPose’s full­body pose estimation with Me­
diapipe BlazePose. While PepperPose achieves higher accuracy (>90% in controlled
settings) using sophisticated hardware and deep learning, our system prioritizes cost­
effectiveness, achieving moderate success with simpler tools. This trade­off positions
our work as a stepping stone toward democratizing rehabilitation technology, particularly
in low­resource contexts where CP prevalence is high (McIntyre et al.[3]).
6.1.1 Limitations and Critical Reflection:
Despite these contributions, the system’s
limitations temper its immediate applicability. The 61.66% repetition counting accuracy,
while a reasonable baseline, falls below clinical benchmarks (e.g., >80% in Zhao et al.,
2023) and PepperPose’s reported precision, reflecting challenges with occlusion and
static robot positioning. False negatives (average 11.5 per participant) highlight the monoc­
ular camera’s vulnerability to obscured landmarks, a constraint less pronounced in Pep­
perPose, which benefits from Pepper’s height­adjustable design and multi­angle visibility.
Testing with healthy university students rather than CP children—due to ethical and logisti­
cal constraints—further limits ecological validity, as CP­specific motor impairments (e.g.,
spasticity, reduced RoM) were not evaluated. This mirrors PepperPose’s initial testing
with healthy adults, though its social focus mitigates the need for therapeutic specificity.
The single­exercise scope (hammer curls) and reliance on parental oversight also cur­
tail the system’s versatility and autonomy, echoing a broader challenge in home­based
Home Based Rehabilitation using Pose Estimation and Mobile Robot

rehabilitation (Fortin­Bédard et al.[5]). Fatigue detection, while conceptually sound, was
undermined by incomplete set data, a byproduct of accuracy issues. Compared to Pep­
perPose, which integrates real­time feedback and adaptive robot behavior, our system’s
static Spin robot lacks the dynamism to adjust viewing angles(of the robot or the camera)
during exercises, aggravating pose detection errors.
Critically, the system embodies a classic engineering dilemma: balancing cost, acces­
sibility, performance and use of resources available. Its modest accuracy aligns with
Jatesiktat et al. [8], who view autonomous movement modeling as an iterative process,
yet it lags behind PepperPose’s seamless human­robot synergy. This suggests that while
the system lays a foundation for innovation, it requires significant refinement to rival es­
tablished rehabilitation technologies like IMUs (>90% accuracy) or SARs (effect sizes
0.5­0.7). Nonetheless, its user acceptance and low­cost framework offer a compelling
proof­of­concept, with potential to transform CP therapy if scalability challenges are ad­
dressed.
6.1.2 Future Directions:
Building on these findings, future work should focus on en­
hancing technical performance, validating with the target population, and expanding ther­
apeutic scope, drawing inspiration from PepperPose’s iterative development. Specific
directions include:
1. Validation with CP Children:
• Conduct trials with pediatric CP patients to assess efficacy under real­world
motor impairments (e.g., RoM <120°, spastic movements). This parallels Pep­
perPose’s eventual adaptation to diverse user groups, ensuring therapeutic
relevance. Adjust angle thresholds (e.g., flexion <90°) and validate against
clinical outcomes (e.g., Gross Motor Function Measure scores).
2. Dynamic Robot Repositioning:
• Integrate Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) or adaptive position­
ing algorithms into the Spin robot, akin to PepperPose’s real­time adjustments.
This would mitigate occlusion (e.g., P1’s 20 false negatives) by enabling the
robot to reposition dynamically during exercises, potentially boosting accuracy
to >80­90%. Explore lightweight depth sensors (e.g., Intel RealSense or stereo
vision methods) to complement 2D pose estimation.
3. Expanded Exercise Repertoire:
• Broaden the system to support multiple exercises (e.g., shoulder abduction, leg
extensions), mirroring PepperPose’s full­body tracking capability. This requires
recalibrating landmark detection and state machines for diverse movements,
enhancing versatility for comprehensive CP rehabilitation.
4. Enhanced User Engagement:
• Incorporate gamification and verbal feedback, inspired by PepperPose’s inter­
active cues (e.g., “Great job!”), to leverage the perfect expectation score (5.0).
Develop a child­friendly interface with visual rewards (e.g., points, animations)
or Digital Twin to sustain motivation, addressing CP therapy’s monotony chal­
lenge [21].
5. Reduced Caregiver Burden:

• Automate setup and monitoring (e.g., voice­guided instructions, auto­calibration,
easier set­up) to minimize parental involvement, a limitation not addressed in
PepperPose but critical for home use. Integrate remote therapist oversight via
Tailscale, enabling real­time adjustments and reducing family workload.
6. Benchmarking and Clinical Integration:
• Compare the system against clinical standards (e.g., physiotherapy session
outcomes, IMU­based tools) and PepperPose’s metrics to quantify therapeutic
impact (e.g., motor skill gains over 8 weeks). Pursue longitudinal studies to
assess neuroplasticity benefits [7], positioning the system as a complement to
professional care.
7. Scalability and Cost Optimization:
• Refine hardware/software integration to lower costs further (e.g., using older
smartphones) and test in diverse environments (urban vs. rural), aligning with
WHO’s assistive technology equity goals [2].Explore better options for robot
positioning and robot understanding/ learning.
8. Motorised Mounting Mechanism:
• By implementing a motorized mounting mechanism for the mobile device, the
system can dynamically enhance its visual capture capabilities and interactive
potential, thereby expanding the range of observable movement and improving
the overall tracking precision of rehabilitation exercises.
6.2
Vision:
If these enhancements are realized, the system could redefine home­based CP rehabilita­
tion by offering a scalable, engaging, and affordable alternative to conventional methods.
Its parallels with PepperPose—using robotics to bridge human needs and technology—
suggest a shared trajectory toward interactive assistive systems, though our focus on
CP therapy targets a critical healthcare gap. By reducing annual therapy costs, increas­
ing treatment consistency, and empowering families, it aligns with global health priorities
(WHO[2]) and could serve the 17 million CP individuals worldwide [3]. Future iterations
might integrate with telehealth platforms, creating a hybrid model where robots and thera­
pists collaborate, a vision PepperPose hints at in social contexts but remains unexplored
in rehabilitation.
In conclusion, this project establishes a foundation for low­cost, robot­assisted rehabilita­
tion, with user enthusiasm and technical feasibility as its cornerstones. While its current
performance reflects prototype­stage limitations, its alignment with PepperPose’s interac­
tive ethos and its unique affordability, positions it as a catalyst for future innovation. With
targeted refinements, it has the potential to transform how CP rehabilitation is delivered,
making high­quality therapy an everyday reality in homes worldwide.
