Summary 
Offshore wind power industry today is developing rapidly and the installed capacity is taking grand 
dimensions. In 2015 alone, the annual installed capacity in Europe not only exceeded its predecessor, 
but doubled the amount. As of now, majority of offshore developments, as many as 69%, are located 
in the North Sea whereas only 13% of the wind parks are installed in the Baltic Sea. 
Looking from the perspective of an independent investor, this phenomenon might give an impression 
as if North Sea has competitive advantages over Baltic Sea and is more attractive location to invest in. 
That triggers series of other questions regarding the cost differences of an offshore wind farms in the 
North Sea and in the Baltic Sea, which have not been studied in detail previously. 
The goal of this thesis was to investigate the cost differences in the Baltic Sea and in the North Sea by 
comparing two scenarios and carrying out private economic analysis. Here, the key findings are 
highlighted: 
 Elements with highest cost fluctuation 
The cost elements with highest cost fluctuation depending on the site were identified to be foundation, 
installation, export cable, scheduled and unscheduled maintenance logistics. 
 Dependencies 
The main reasons causing the cost fluctuation for elements are the distance to the shore, water depth, 
seabed peculiarities, weather window. 
 Total lifetime cost 
Total lifetime cost for the Baltic Sea was found to be 59% lower than for the North Sea scenario.  
 Levelized cost of electricity 
Levelized cost of electricity for the Baltic Sea was found to be 72 EUR/MWh lower than for the North 
Sea. This however is highly sensitive to capacity factor changes. 
 Sum of required support 
Sum of required support in the Baltic Sea was found to be 1.4 billion euros less than for the North Sea 
scenario, this however is highly dependent on the country the wind farm is installed and herewith the 
electricity prices. 
Based on these findings, it was concluded, that it is more feasible to build an offshore wind farm to the 
Baltic Sea than to the North Sea. However, the findings represent fictional scenarios. Each case is highly 
sensitive to a number of uncertain parameters subject to location, which should be considered prior 
making the final investment decision.

1. Introduction 
Over the last 40 years, wind industry has developed rapidly. Within this time, wind power has become 
more mature and the annual installed capacity has risen exponentially (see Figure 1). By the end of 
2015, the cumulative installed capacity for offshore wind power in Europe exceeded 11 GW (see Figure 
2), this amount produces 40.6 TWh in a normal wind year, enough to cover 1.5% of the EU’s total 
electricity consumption (European Wind Energy Association, 2016).  
Wind energy in particular is attractive for investors for its reliable technology, very low operational 
costs, a constant electricity generation price and a great natural potential (Ernest & Young, 2015). 
Onshore wind farms have proven their reliability and can now produce electricity competitively 
compared to fossil-fuel fired power plants, offshore wind farms, however, are lagging behind and have 
much higher generation cost than other renewable energy technologies (Irena, 2012). 
At the same time, it needs to be bore in mind that the land available for wind harvesting is restricted 
and the wind conditions are weaker, whereas offshore wind resources are abundant with stronger and 
more consistent wind and the available space is larger (U.S. Department of Energy, 2016). European 
Commission describes offshore wind industry as “the energy of the future”, hence it is fair to say that 
the future of wind farms can be seen out in the sea (EWEA, 2012).

1.1 Offshore wind development in Europe 
The world’s first offshore wind farm was installed in 1991 in Vindeby in Denmark. The park consisted 
of 11 450kW stall controlled wind turbines, located 2 km from the coast (S.B.O.W.E. Regions, n.d.). 
Three years later, the next wind farm was installed, nevertheless, the success story of offshore wind 
power did not start. The technology for going offshore was still emerging and it was way more 
expensive than its onshore brother. 
From 2000s the offshore wind energy has started increasing rapidly. When in 2000 the installed 
capacity was 35.6 MW, then by the end of 2015 it was already over 11 GW (European Wind Energy 
Association, 2016). 
Even though the installations have grown greatly, it is reported that the cost of energy is still not 
competitive with other energy production methods and is still higher than onshore energy cost (Ernest 
& Young, 2015). Hence the main focus in offshore wind industry right now is to find the way to cut 
costs. The main opportunities identified for cost reduction include the following:

1.2 The North Sea & the Baltic Sea 
According to European Environmental Agency, Europe’s total wind energy potential is almost 20 times 
higher than projected energy demand in 2020 (EEA, 2009). 40% of the potential is identified to be 
offshore, nevertheless, different restrictions such as shipping lanes, gas and oil platforms, Natura 2000 
areas etc. reduce the offshore potential more than 90% (EEA, 2009). The largest offshore potential can 
be found from the North Sea and the Baltic Sea. Figure 3 illustrates the enormous potential that 
European waters within 10 – 30 km from the coast hold, justifying many wind farms installed to the 
North Sea. At the same time, the Baltic Sea has a huge potential, but still has only 13% of the total 
offshore installations in Europe (European Wind Energy Association, 2016).

1.3 Nelja Energia and Estonian market 
Nelja Energia is a company developing renewable energy industry in Estonia. The company is owned 
by Estonian Investors (23%) and Vardar Eurus (77%) from Norway. The name, Nelja Energia, refers to 
4 different renewable energy types the company deals with – wind energy, hydropower, solar power 
and biomass, however they are also involved in other renewable energy related areas such as 
hydrogen, gas, etc. (4Energia, 2015). 
As of 2014, Nelja Energia owns 15 wind farms in the Baltic States with a total installed capacity of 222.7 
MW (4Energia, 2015). 11 of them with a capacity of 143.8 MW (4Energia, 2015) are installed in Estonia. 
This makes 4Energia the biggest wind farm owner in Estonia. 
Currently Nelja Energia is developing a 700-1100 MW offshore wind farm in Estonia nearby the shore 
of the island Hiiumaa. Wind farm with that size is too large for Estonian consumption, specially taking 
into account old conventional power plants still in operation and the country’s large oil shale mines. 
In order to achieve the European Union Renewables Directive (2009/28/EC) targets, the National 
Renewable Energy Action Plan (NREAP) for Estonia sets the following targets for 2020:

1.4 Problem statement 
As of now, Estonia has achieved its 2013/2014 interim targets and the overall renewable energy share 
has already exceeded the 25% target, accounting for 25.6% from final energy demand (Eufores, 2014; 
European Commission, 2015b). The resources for renewable energy are there, but the demand for 
green energy has decrased, and as a consequence, subsidies have decreased. In 2013 the new 
Electricity Market Act with new support schemes was approved. This states that the amount of 
electricity that will receive support should not exceed the levels stipulated in the NREAP and 600 
GWh/yr cap for wind power should be maintained (Riigikogu, 2015). As of 2014, wind power already 
accounted for 604 GWh (see Figure 8).

1.4.1 Research question 
This thesis will investigate, whether the assumption made by Nelja Energia holds by answering the 
main research question: 
Under which conditions it is more feasible to build and offshore wind farm to the Baltic Sea than to 
the North Sea?  
With this question, the following queries will be addressed: 
What is the total lifetime cost of a wind farm in the Baltic Sea and in the North Sea? 
What is the levelized cost of electricity in the Baltic Sea and in the North Sea? 
What is the required support in the Baltic Sea wind farm and in the North Sea wind farm? 
1.5 Report outline 
The report is structured as follows: 
Chapter 2 explains the methodology used throughout the work and the choices and limitations made. 
In Chapter 3 the costs of an offshore wind farm will be broken down in detail to Capital Expenditure 
(CAPEX) and Operation and Maintenance (OPEX). 
In Chapter 4 the key elements causing the highest cost flux depending on the site will be identified and 
the cost scenarios will be created. 
In Chapter 5 the private-economic analysis of chosen scenarios will be carried out, the total lifetime 
cost and the unit cost of the wind farm will be found and the required support level will be determined. 
Chapter 6 carries out sensitivity analysis on different parameters that can change the feasibility of a 
typical wind farm either in the Baltic Sea or in the North Sea location. 
Chapter 7 conducts comparative discussion about the feasibility of an offshore wind farm in the Baltic 
Sea and in the North Sea, draws conclusions about the work done and gives recommendations for 
future work.

2. Methodology 
This chapter presents the methodology and techniques used in order to answer the posed research 
question. 
The subjects of this study are offshore wind farms in the Baltic Sea and the North Sea and the cost 
differences implied by the location. 
2.1 Cost elements of an offshore wind farm 
First part of the study uses qualitative research to break down the costs for a typical offshore wind 
farm. A typical wind turbine consists of more than 8000 different components, hence it is important 
to note, that simplifications were made and the breakdown for this work is rather high level. 
The cost breakdown presented in this work is mainly inspired by UK-based technical consultancy 
company, BVG Associates reports “Value breakdown for the offshore wind sector” for the British 
Government Renewables Advisory board and “A Guide to an Offshore Wind Farm” for The Crown 
Estate as well as it is consulted with representatives from Siemens. 
Descriptive methods were used to characterize identified cost elements and the share of a certain 
element was found. 
2.2 Site dependent cost fluctuation 
After the identification procedure, a small number of components were chosen for further 
investigation. These were the elements that were identified to have the highest cost fluctuation 
depending on the location and its parameters change e.g. water depth and distance to the shore. Those 
elements were the reason why the cost of an offshore wind farm for different locations was changing. 
These elements were inspected and analysed more in depth to identify the reasons for the location-
dependent cost difference. In this process, the experts from the industry were consulted. It is 
important to note, that the data is not publicly available and it is extremely difficult to get the actual 
figures. Various companies were approached such as DONG Energy, Siemens, Vattenfall, Vestas MHI, 
Nelja Energia. Only few of them were willing to share their knowledge, namely Vattenfall, Siemens and 
Nelja Energia, who also had to draw a boundary line at certain point, when the information requested 
became too sensitive and it could have harmed the companies competitive advantage later. 
The insight from the companies was incorporated into the analysis by first contrasting and validating 
the available data with the industrial expertize and then analysing the results of this work and their 
veraciousness.

2.2.1.2 Operational expenditure (OPEX) 
In order to determine regular shares of operations and maintenance element, interviews in Vattenfall 
were used. 
To determine the cost magnitude for operations and maintenance elements, cost data per MWh from 
different wind farms were collected (DONG Energy A/S, 2008; Risø, n.d.; Windpower Monthly, 2010) 
as well as average value for operations and maintenance (O&M) cost per MWh (Pieterman, 2013).

OPEX, largely dependent on the distance to the shore was then plotted. Based on data inspection, a 
linear interpolation was found to be the best fit to determine the cost development for O&M. The 
shares for different elements were identified during the interviews in Vattenfall. 
The results were then added to the different scenarios and the analysis were carried out. 
It is important to note, that this work uses simplified version of O&M cost development. Maintenance 
costs are highly dependent on weather window, but in this work they are not modelled separately due

3. Cost elements of an offshore wind farm 
Wind farms in general are very capital intensive. When comparing offshore wind farm investment costs 
per one MW with onshore wind farm investment cost per MW, it can be seen, that the offshore wind 
farms can be still 50% more expensive compared to its onshore siblings (Morthorst & Kitzing, 2015). 
This, nevertheless, is expected to change in forthcoming years as the offshore technology is still 
considered as emerging technology that has not reached its maturity yet and hence the cost is still 
rather high (The Crown Estate, 2012). 
In a typical wind farm, upfront investments take up ca 75% of the total lifetime cost (Morthorst & 
Kitzing, 2015). This is highly different from a typical electricity generation technology, where the initial 
investments take only approximately 40% of their total lifetime cost (Morthorst & Kitzing, 2015). 
In order to get a good overview and an understanding of different cost components in an offshore 
wind farm, the components of capital expenditures (CAPEX) and operations and maintenance 
expenditures (OPEX) were split into different categories. 
The following chapter explains the components in detail and tries to identify the dependencies.

In this work, the investment costs were broken down into four different categories including the 
following components: 
1. Development and consent 
2. Turbine

3.1.1 Development and consent 
Building an offshore wind farm is a long and complicated process. Development and consent 
procedures are crucial in sense of moving forward from the concept level to an actual step towards 
the construction of a wind farm. Development starts several years before the actual construction. 
3.1.1.1 Environmental analysis 
Environmental analysis such as 
- 
environmental survey 
- 
sea bed survey 
- 
met station survey 
are important to conduct well in advance in order to find out, whether it is allowed and feasible to 
build a wind farm to the desired location. 
Installed met mast will allow assessing the wind resources site specifically and will give an accurate 
data to compare the projected wind speeds and wave heights by general programs for the whole North 
Sea or Baltic Sea. The cost of a met mast depends on the sea depth and distance to the shore, with the 
cost rising the deeper the sea and the further the distance from the shore (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Sea bed survey consists of two major parts – firstly, geophysical survey of bathymetry and sea bed 
features. Secondly, geotechnical surveys of the sea bed characteristics (The Crown Estate, 2010). Sea 
bed survey is an important component of the development process as it helps a number of processes 
including the foundation design which has crucial effect on cost. It also plays an important role in 
designing a wind farm layout which in turn will affect the annual power output as well as it minimizes 
the risk during installation activities (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Environmental survey is crucial due to its possibly great effect on several fields, such as birds, bats, 
marine mammals, fish, zoobenthos, and macrophytes (OSPAR Commission, 2008). Well-conducted and 
approved environmental impact assessment will assure the smooth construction of the wind farm 
from the side of environmental authorities or brings the focus to the problematic spot at the right 
time. 
The cost of environmental surveys depends a bit on the location of the wind farm, in extent the 
distance from the shore and the water depth. For example, it is not possible to use the same vessel for 
==PAGE:30==
19 
 
conducting surveys near-shore and far-shore surveys due to health and safety reasons. Hence this cost 
varies depending on the location of the wind farm (Carbon Trust, 2010). Nevertheless, the share of this 
element is negligible when considering the whole wind farm costs.  
3.1.1.2 Development Services 
Development services include different elements, such as 
- 
front-end engineering and design 
- 
project management 
- 
consulting fees 
- 
other services. 
Front end engineering and design studies are carried out in order to address the areas of technical 
uncertainty and develop the concept of the wind farm in advance of contracting (The Crown Estate, 
2010). The main parameters such as wind farm layout, grid connection, turbine size and foundation 
type are decided considering economic viability. The output of these studies is later used by 
construction management team to implement the wind farm (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Project management has an important role on developing an offshore wind farm. It provides a 
framework helping to set feasible yet ambitious goals and governing to accomplish them. Developing 
an offshore wind farm is a complex process and having highly skilled and experienced team members 
helps to ensure that the project is delivered on time and within budget. 
In project management (PM), the location of the wind farm is not expected to have a great influence 
on the cost of the wind farm, however, a wind farm capacity does. PM costs for a small wind farm and 
for a big wind farm are the same, cause the competences needs to be there, all functions are needed 
and the structure is the same. Hence the MW cost for a big wind farm from PM perspective is 
decreasing. (Michaluk, 2016) 
3.1.2 Turbine 
An offshore wind turbine consists of three main elements such as rotor, nacelle and tower, these 
elements can be further subdivided. 
The turbine converts the kinetic energy extracted from wind into electrical energy which can then be 
used in industry and regular households. 
 
==PAGE:31==
20 
 
3.1.2.1 Rotor 
The rotor extracts kinetic energy from the air and converts it into mechanical power which can then 
be converted to electricity (U.S. Department of Energy, 2015a). 
The rotor contains of 
- 
blades 
- 
hub 
- 
blade bearings 
- 
fasteners. 
The blades capture the energy in the wind. They are subject to the external loading, which includes 
the flapwise and edgewise bending loads, gravitational loads, inertia forces, loads due to pitch 
acceleration as well as torsional loading (L. Mishnaevsky Jr, 2011). Nowadays light and durable 
composite materials are found to be the best to satisfy the list of requirements needed for a wind 
turbine blade, such as high strength, fatigue resistance and stiffness (L. Mishnaevsky Jr, 2011). 
The key parameters that define blade shape are chord (length of airfoil cross-section), thickness of 
airfoil cross-section, twist (angular rotation of airfoil) airfoil shape and position of aerodynamic centre 
and these parameters are optimized during the blade design (The Crown Estate, 2010).  
Depending on site conditions - wind class and turbulence - different types of blades are used. This in 
turn can influence the price of a blade. However, if the site uses the same kind of turbine, there are no 
differences in the cost of a blade (Michaluk, 2016) and this work, it is assumed to be the same. 
Rotor hub’s task is to connect the blades to the main shaft. It is expected that the cost is not depending 
on site dependent, hence we assume the same cost for a hub in both, the North Sea and in the Baltic 
Sea. 
Blade bearings the main bearings have to absorb the forces and bending moments coming from the 
rotor and have to protect the gear from the resulting deformations and shock loads (Eolotec, 2015). It 
is not expected to have any cost differences according to the region in this section. 
Fasteners (either bolts or studs) are used in critical bolted joints such as connecting rotor to the main 
shaft (The Crown Estate, 2010). It is assumed, that the cost of fasteners will be the same regardless 
the site location. 
 
==PAGE:32==
21 
 
3.1.2.2 Nacelle 
The nacelle supports the rotor and converts the rotational energy from the rotor into three phase AC 
electrical energy (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Nacelle consist of several elements such as 
- 
main shaft 
- 
gearbox 
- 
generator 
- 
yaw system 
- 
controller 
- 
pitch system 
- 
power take-off 
- 
brake system 
- 
nacelle housing 
- 
main frame 
- 
cooling system. 
The main shaft (low speed shaft) carries the torque from the rotor to the gearbox which is supported 
at the rotor end by the main shaft bearing and at the other end either by the gearbox or separately 
mounted bearing (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
The gearbox connects the low-speed shaft to the high-speed shaft and converts the rotor torque at a 
speed of 5-15 rpm to a speed of up to around 1500rpm for efficient conversion to electrical energy by 
the generator (The Crown Estate, 2010). The gear box is a costly and heavy part of the wind turbine 
and engineers are exploring direct-drive generators that operate at lower rotational speeds and don’t 
need gear boxes (U.S. Department of Energy, 2015b). A gearbox for a 5 MW wind turbine may be up 
to 65 tonnes (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
In case of using low-speed direct-drive generators instead of gearboxes and high-speed generators, it 
must be noted, that the generators will be more expensive and heavier, but the technology is 
developing rapidly (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
The generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy. The efficiency of a generator is 
critical, especially at a part load, because most of the time, a wind turbine generates 20%-80% of rated 
power in low-to-medium wind speeds (The Crown Estate, 2010). For this element, it is not expected to 
have any differences in the cost, depending on the location. 
==PAGE:33==
22 
 
Yaw system contains of yaw bearing, yaw motor and yaw break. Yaw system orients the nacelle to the 
wind direction during operations (The Crown Estate, 2010). For a large offshore wind farm, the yaw 
system usually consists of 6-8 geared electric motors, hydraulic breaks, yaw bearing connecting the 
nacelle & tower and sensors measuring the position of the nacelle (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Controller consists of a number of computers, which monitor the conditions of the wind turbine 
continuously, and collects statistics on its operation. It controls a great amount of valves, hydraulic 
pumps, switches and motors in the wind turbine. (Danish Wind Enery Association, 2003) 
Controller provides supervisory control and active power and load control in order to optimize wind 
turbine life and revenue generation, whilst meeting externally imposed requirements (The Crown 
Estate, 2010). 
Controller provides data to the Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition system (SCADA) and also uses 
the input from SCADA. Key control parameters are rotor speed, output power and the pitch angle of 
each blade (The Crown Estate, 2010). According to the information received from the controller, the 
wind turbine will either start working when the wind speed is proper or stop working in case of high 
wind speed when the strong wind may damage the turbine. Controller plays an important role in a 
well-functioning wind turbine. 
The pitch system adjusts the pitch angle of the blades in order to control turbines power output, 
minimize loads and either start or stop the turbine as needed at the moment (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Depending on wind conditions on site, the pitch angle might change rather quickly, up to few degrees 
per second in medium-to-high winds and or very little in low wind sites (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Nevertheless, it is not foreseen any site dependent cost differences for pitch system cost. 
Power take-off receives electrical energy from the generator and adjusts voltage and frequency for 
onward transfer to the wind farm distribution system (The Crown Estate, 2010). It consists of 
converter, transformer, switchgear and cables. 
Brake system stops the rotor in emergencies, it can be either hydraulic, mechanical or electrical (U.S. 
Department of Energy, 2015b). 
Nacelle housing covers and protects the key components of the wind turbine including the gearbox, 
generator, controller etc (Danish Wind Enery Association, 2003). As well as protecting from the 
weather, frequently it supports lightning and other auxiliary supplies. Some nacelles are large enough 
for helicopter to land on it. Nowadays trend in an offshore wind industry promotes every turbine’s 
own helihoist platforms, which enables service technicians with their tools to be airlifted to the turbine 
==PAGE:34==
23 
 
even with bad weather (Hansen, 2015). This is useful in case of long distances to the shore and short 
weather windows that do not allow servicing by boats. 
Main frame holds the yaw system, main shaft and gearbox. It is generally made of cast steel (Sharpley, 
2015). 
Cooling system assures the right temperature for key components such as generators and converters 
that tend to heat up and thereby become less effective (State of Green, 2015). 
For these elements in nacelle, there are no big cost differences foreseen depending on the Baltic Sea 
or the North Sea (Michaluk, 2016), hence the elements are considered as non-fluctuating elements 
and the cost will be kept the same throughout the work. 
3.1.2.3 Tower 
The last and probably the most robust element of the turbine is the tower. It is usually made out of 
tubular steel structure and it supports the nacelle as well as provides the access to the nacelle and 
houses electrical and control equipment (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Towers are typically uniformly tapered. Fatigue, extreme loading and natural frequency requirements 
and avoidance of buckling drive the design of a tower. (The Crown Estate, 2010) 
The cost of the tower can be very labile as it’s mostly made out of steel and steel prices are known to 
be volatile. Rising commodity prices in 2006-2008 drove increased wind power costs, with the price of 
steel tripling between 2005 and its peak in mid- 2008 (IRENA, 2012). As of today, the steel prices are 
back at the level of early 2000s, meaning they are extremely low (Steel Benchmarker, 2015). However, 
this can change within a day. 
Towers and blades are considered to be the most expensive components of the turbine in an offshore 
wind farm, accounting roughly half the total turbine cost, hence the predicted cost reduction in these 
processes by 5.4% and 4.5% respectively (Clean Energy Pipeline, 2014) is much awaited either through 
economies of scale, technological innovation or increasing deployment of higher capacity turbines. The 
cost of a tower may vary depending on the site, but this goes always hand in hand with the foundation 
hence the price change is accounted on foundation cost change(Besnard, 2016; Michaluk, 2016). 
3.1.3 Balance of plant 
Balance of plant includes all the components of the wind farm that do not belong to the turbine. These 
are components such as foundation, cables, offshore substation and onshore substation. 
 
==PAGE:35==
24 
 
3.1.3.1 Foundation 
Foundation provides the support for the wind turbine. It consists of 
- 
transition piece 
- 
foundation structure. 
Transition piece is providing the connection between the foundation and the tower, it is made out of 
steel (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Foundation structure is an interface between the seabed and the transition piece.  
Depending in the site and water depth, offshore wind farms use different types of foundations. 
Currently in Europe, 5 types of foundations have been used – by the end of 2014, there were 78.8% of 
monopiles, 10.4% gravity foundations, 4.7% jackets, 4.1% tripods and 1.9% tripile foundations installed 
in European waters (EWEA, 2015). 
As of today, monopiles, made out of steel, are by far the most commonly used offshore wind turbine 
foundations. Monopiles are driven into the seabed by hammers (Malhotra, 2010). The design of this 
foundation is relatively simple, but they are currently limited by the depth of the sea and peculiarity 
of the sea bed. To date, Rambøll has monopiles installed in depth of 32 meters, but they believe there 
is still some untapped potential for monopiles and is hence designing a new XL monopile which could 
cope with water depths such as 60 meters (Daubney, 2013). Rambøll consultants believe there is large 
cost-saving potential to optimize the design of monopile (Wind Energy Update, 2015). 
Gravity based foundations are made from re-enforced concrete and are suitable for waters with depth 
up to 30 meters. As the name implies, it resists the overturning loads by its own gravity. They are 
typically used at sites, where installation of piles to the seabed is difficult doe to hard rock ledge or on 
competent soil sites in relatively shallow waters (Malhotra, 2010). 
Jacket foundations are made of steel and can be used in water depths up to 45 meters. In 
manufacturing process, many single steel beams need to be welded together. (EON - Climate & 
Renewables, 2012) 
 Tripod foundations are made out of steel and can be used with a maximum water depth of 35 meters. 
The manufacturing process for tripods is extensive and all different pieces have to be welded together. 
They are considered to be still in the development phase. (EON - Climate & Renewables, 2012) 
Today’s offshore turbines are located in water depths, that current foundations can accommodate. 
Nevertheless, the depths are expected to increase and technology must catch up and create new 
solutions for deeper water. Thus floating foundations are being developed and prototypes are being 
==PAGE:36==
25 
 
tested already (Rambøll, n.d.). This on the other hand requires again great investments for research 
and development. 
The cost of foundation may vary greatly depending on its type which in turn is dependent on the 
location it is installed. It is expected that future North Sea developments include deeper waters, hence 
the popular monopiles must be replaced with other types of foundations which expectedly will 
increase the CAPEX cost significantly. In shallower waters such as typical Baltic Sea location, the well-
known and cheaper foundations can be used. In order to get better overview, this will be further 
discussed in next chapter.

3.1.3.2 Cables 
Cables deliver power output from the wind turbines to the shore. Two types of cables can be 
distinguished 
- 
inter array cable (collection system) 
- 
export cable (transmission system). 
Inter array cable connects the turbines to the offshore substation. These cables are typically rated 33-
36 kV with the mass of 20kg/m (The Crown Estate, 2010) and are not considered as significant drivers 
for increasing the cost.  
Export cable is connecting the offshore and onshore substations. The further is the wind farm located 
from the shore, the more export cables are needed and the longer the cable must be. Export cables 
are considered to be very expensive and sensitive for distance increase, hence this element will be 
further discussed in the next chapter.  
 
 
==PAGE:37==

3.1.3.3 Offshore substation 
Offshore substations are used in order to reduce electrical losses by increasing voltage before 
exporting the power to shore. They are usually delivered as one element of a contract to connect the 
offshore wind farm to the grid. Some wind farms might have more than one substation to increase the 
security of export (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Offshore substation is an expensive element, however, its’ cost is not very sensitive neither to the 
location nor to the size of a wind farm. It’s a one-time investment not depending that much on the site 
(Besnard, 2016; Campos, 201AD). The cost of its foundation and installation will increase with the 
distance and the depth, but the share of it is not as significant as some other components share. 
3.1.4 Installation and Commissioning 
This paragraph includes all installation and commissioning activities for turbine and balance of plant, 
both sea based and land based. 
This section is divided into foundation installation, turbine installation, electrical installation, offshore 
substation installation. 
3.1.4.1 Foundation installation 
Foundation installation includes transportation to the site and putting the foundation to the position. 
Installation process for foundations may vary according to the type of foundation used and the seabed 
peculiarities. 
For example, monopiles are driven into the sea bed using a hammer or anvil system. 
For larger, 5MW and bigger turbines, jackets or tripods in steel or concrete gravity foundations are 
being used. Jacket and tripod foundation pin piles are driven into sea bed and then the foundation is 
lowered onto the pile heads and grouted to the position. 
Concrete gravity foundation weighs way more and must be floated to the position before being sunk. 
Offshore substation foundations can be installed similarly to turbine foundations, but it must be noted, 
that they are significantly larger. (The Crown Estate, 2010) 
Foundation installation (as all installation activities) is highly dependent on weather, distance and 
depth, hence it is expected to have differences in North Sea and Baltic Sea installation costs. This will 
be further discussed in next chapter. 
==PAGE:38==
27 
 
3.1.4.2 Turbine installation 
Turbine installation includes transporting the turbine components – tower, nacelle, blades and 
auxiliary systems - to the site and installing the turbine on the foundation. Again, installation methods 
may vary and the degree of turbines pre-assembly can be different. For example, one configuration 
might be, where the turbine is in six separate pieces - 2 pieces of tower, nacelle with hub and 3 blades 
separately. At the same time, there is another configuration with only three pieces – tower in one 
piece, “bunny ear”1 and a third blade. This method was considered to be the lowest cost option for 
installation, but according to some manufacturers, the components experience unexpected loads 
during the transportation and it can damage the blades (Maples, Saur, & Hand, 2013). 
But as the market develops, more innovative solutions are likely to emerge (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
Even though it is possible to gather the knowledge and learn from the best about the installation 
methods, unpredicted weather has a great influence on turbine installation cost. Hence it will be more 
closely discussed in next chapter. 
3.1.4.3 Electrical installation (export and array cable) 
Electrical installation includes export and inter array cable laying. 
Export cables are laid in as long sections as possible, up to 70km in order to avoid subsea connections. 
Cables are typically buried 1.5-3 meters below sea bed in order to avoid disturbance from fishing 
vessels or ship anchors. Export cable-laying is seen as a significant constraint due to few vessels 
available and limited number of companies with expertise. (The Crown Estate, 2010) 
Inter array cables may have several different lay-outs depending on the calculations and decision 
made by the developer. Cables may be laid in a spider arrangement with a small number of turbines 
connected to a single cable, or in a series of chains with many turbines on each cable (The Crown 
Estate, 2010). 
In order to carry out cable installations, special cable laying vessels, trenching vessels, cable ploughs 
are needed. These vessels are expensive and as already mentioned, there is not that many companies 
with expertise to carry out the work, hence low competition and high bargaining power from the 
supplier. 
As there is a great cost difference foreseen for this installation activity, it will be further discussed in 
the next chapter.

3.1.4.4 Offshore substation installation 
Offshore substation is floated out from the port on a barge, which is usually equipped with a heavy lift 
crane. Special vessels and cranes are needed and a positive weather window, which allows to carry out 
the installation smoothly. 
The installation process will be further discussed in the following chapter. 
3.1.4.5 Construction port 
For an offshore wind farm, it is important to have a construction port that meets the requirements to 
accommodate the pre-assembly of a turbine and other activities related to the wind farm construction. 
The port requires a lot land for wind farm components as well as a special quay that could 
accommodate service vessels (The Crown Estate, 2010). 
3.2 Operations and Maintenance (OPEX) 
Operations and maintenance function is to provide support during the lifetime operations and to 
ensure the optimum output of the wind farm. 
3.2.1 Operations 
Operational support is provided all year round including turbine and environment monitoring activities 
as well as responding to unexpected turbine faults, plan scheduled maintenance and manage customer 
and supplier interaction (The Crown Estate, 2010). Operational support includes wind farm monitoring, 
administration and other back office tasks. 
It is not foreseen any great cost differences for operations activities depending on the site. 
3.2.2 Maintenance 
Maintenance provides service and repairs and is usually subdivided into two sections 
- 
preventive (scheduled) 
- 
corrective (unscheduled). 
Preventive maintenance includes proactive repair or replacement of known wear components. It can 
be based either on routine inspection or condition monitoring system information (GL Garrad Hassan, 
2013). Preventive maintenance can be scheduled in advance, so that the production loss would be 
minimal and there would be favourable weather window to carry out the service. Preventive 
maintenance can be subdivided into material cost and logistics cost. Material cost is expected to be 
the same in different locations, however, the logistics is expected dependant on the site and will be 
further discussed in next chapter (Campos, 201AD). 
==PAGE:40==
29 
 
Corrective maintenance means the repair or replacement of failed or damaged components (GL 
Garrad Hassan, 2013). Corrective maintenance cannot be scheduled before the failure has happened, 
hence immediate accessibility cannot be guaranteed unless the turbine has a helihoist. This in turn will 
result in unpredictable turbine downtime and lost production. Turbines with helihoist that are 
experiencing a small failure, can be repaired faster, but failure that requires a vessel for a repair still 
highly depends on weather and the distance. 
Corrective maintenance can be subdivided for materials and logistics. In materials, there is no cost 
difference depending on the site, however in logistics, great cost differences for the North Sea and 
Baltic Sea locations are seen(Campos, 201AD). 
If the wind turbines in the North Sea are not equipped with helihoist, the repair time may increase 
tremendously. In case they are, turbines with helihoist that are experiencing a small failure, can be 
repaired faster, but failure that requires a vessel for a repair is still highly depends on weather and the 
distance. 
Future North Sea offshore wind farm turbines are all expected to have helihoist platforms in order to 
address this issue, fix the damages quickly and obtain possibly higher power output. If the weather 
would not be favourable for services by boat, then helicopters can fly with winds up to 20 m/s. Given 
that constraint, the positive weather window is much bigger(Hansen, 2015).

-

-

-

This chapter ratiocinates the chosen components in depth, explains their importance and finds out the

2. 
4.1.1 Foundation 
In an offshore wind farm, foundation is seen as a component with the highest cost flux. The cost of 
foundation is mainly driven by 
- 
Water depth 
- 
Seabed peculiarities  
In some wind farms, 2-3 different types of foundations can be used. As of now, 79 % of foundations 
installed in the North Sea and in the Baltic Sea are monopiles (EWEA, 2015). 
Foundation cost may vary greatly depending on its location. Firstly, the seabed peculiarities - in 
different areas the layer of soil is different, it might be sandy bottom, rocky outcrops, soft silt, stiff clay, 
mud etc. this all influences the foundation type and the amount of costly material needed. There also 
might be some unexploded ordnances (UXO) from World War II and then the seabed needs to be 
cleared and prepared first. 
In case of soft, muddy bottom, a monopile needs to be longer and drilled deeper to the seabed in order 
to ensure the fixity. 
Secondly, deeper water requires more materials to be used and hence also drives up the cost. 
==PAGE:42==
31 
 
In this work the share of 18% of base case CAPEX is used as a base share for foundation as advised by 
the reports (Carbon Trust, 2010; GL Garrad Hassan, 2013) and the future shares for different scenarios 
will later be computed. 
4.1.2 Installation 
Installation is seen as an element with high cost fluctuation depending on the location as well. 
Installation activities are dependent on 
- 
Distance to the shore/base harbour 
- 
Water depth 
- 
Positive weather window 
- 
Seabed particularities 
- 
Foundation type 
Positive weather window is very important for carrying out installation activities. That means a certain 
maximum wave height and wind speed. As of today, small boats cannot operate beyond a 1.5 meter 
wave height and wind speed of 10m/s and large jack-up vessels cannot operate beyond a 2.5 meter 
wave height and wind speed up to 20 meters (Garrett, 2011). 
It is known, that Baltic Sea experiences lower waves and wind speeds than the North Sea(EON - Climate 
& Renewables, 2012; “NORSEWinD,” n.d.), hence the positive weather window for North Sea is lower 
than for the Baltic Sea. Weather is irrespective of human behaviour, but nowadays pretty well 
predictable. Nevertheless, the installation time can suffer in it. 
Another important factor is construction port availability. Either the closest port that can 
accommodate the needs or another port shall be redesigned for wind farm port needs. This needs to 
be well considered during the development phase, but it has an influence to the final cost. 
The distance to the construction port plays an important role in installation costs. This can either 
increase or decrease the travel time (lost working time), fuel costs, vessel and other equipment rental 
period and crew hiring costs. 
Greater distance is also more sensitive to weather impacts. In a long distance installation site, the 
travel time to and off the site might be eventually longer than effective working time itself, if the 
weather window is short. In a near-shore site, even a short weather window gives longer effective 
working time, as the transit time decreases. Also, a near shore site experiences less extreme wind and 
wave conditions. 
==PAGE:43==
32 
 
Installing an offshore wind farm requires great number of different service vessels. For foundations 
and cable installation, the vessels needs are dependent on the seabed particularities, foundation type 
and the depth of the water. Deep water large turbines require bigger jack-up vessels which are very 
expensive and difficult to hire (Michaluk, 2016). 
The installation equipment can be either purchased or rented, but the availability can be very low and 
the cost very high. 
In present work it is assumed, that the importance of the distance to the shore and water depth are 
weighed equally. 
In present work, the base share for installation is considered to be 26% as recommended by the 
literature (Carbon Trust, 2010; The Crown Estate, 2010) and the future values in different scenarios 
will be computed. 
4.1.3 Export cables 
The cost of an export cable is mostly driven by 
- 
Distance to the shore. 
Export cable is very expensive and the further is the distance from the shore, the higher will be the 
cost of the cable 
In this work, 9% is used as a base share for the export cable as advised by Vattenfall (Besnard, 2016). 
Future shares for different scenarios will be computed later. 
4.1.4 Scheduled maintenance logistics 
From operations and maintenance costs, scheduled maintenance logistics was identified as a site 
dependent cost flux element. This is mainly driven by 
- 
Distance to the shore. 
Even though scheduled maintenance can be planned in advance, it still requires to hire expensive 
vessels, fuel, crew. This all is depending on the location. 
In this work, for scheduled maintenance logistics is considered to be 8.5% as advised by Vattenfall 
(Campos, 201AD). 
4.1.5 Unscheduled maintenance logistics  
Second element from OPEX that was identified as a high cost flux element depending on site was 
unscheduled maintenance logistics. This is mainly dependant on 
==PAGE:44==
33 
 
- 
Distance to the shore 
- 
Weather window 
- 
Nature of the repair 
In addition to the distance dependency, the weather constraint now plays great role as well as the 
nature of the break down and vessel availability (in most cases). 
When a minor break down occurs and vessel is not required on site, a helicopter can do the service if 
a helihoist is there. Helicopter has less weather restrictions than vessel (it is only constraint by the wind 
speed higher than 20 m/s), hence the positive weather window for a helicopter is larger than for a 
vessel. Helicopter rent can be more expensive, but it may trade off with the lost power and 
consequently lost revenue. 
On the other hand, if a major break down occurs, a vessel is needed. Depending on the nature of a 
break down, the type and size and thereby the cost will be determined. If a turbine experiences a major 
failure, such as gearbox break down, large cranes are needed to take down the gearbox and transport 
it to the land. 
If for scheduled maintenance it was possible to plan the maintenance well advance, hire the vessels, 
find suitable weather window and so forth, then for unscheduled maintenance it is not possible. It 
might happen, that necessary vessels are not available at the moment. Or when the vessels arrive, the 
weather is not suitable for a repair anymore and the vessel has to wait in the port for few days until 
the maintenance can start. The whole time, the rent for this extremely expensive equipment is 
accumulating. As an example from Vattenfall, a repair might take 1-2 days, but the vessel can be hired 
for a week – waiting for the weather to be able to go offshore. A large vessel rent is 0.3-0.4 million 
euros per day (Campos, 201AD; Michaluk, 2016) which is seen as a large cost depending on the site. 
In this work, for unscheduled maintenance logistics is considered to be 34% as advised by Vattenfall 
(Campos, 201AD).

5. Private economic valuation 
In order to evaluate the feasibility of a project, a private economic valuation for different scenarios is 
conducted. This also includes finding the key parameters for determining the cost of the project such 
as total lifetime cost and levelized cost of electricity. 
5.1 Cash flow model 
The model is designed to conduct a private-economic valuation and present the result. 
5.1.1 Control section 
At first, the user can choose between 4 countries, where the wind farm is located at – Estonia, 
Denmark, Germany or United Kingdom. This choice determines the corporate tax rate and the power 
price differences.  
Secondly, a scenario can be chosen. This determines the differences in capital expenditures and 
operational expenditures. 
It is important to note, that in this work, Denmark is used as an example when comparing the different 
scenarios. This choice is made due to its location – with an access to the Baltic Sea and to the North 
Sea and in order to keep the scenarios comparable. 
5.1.2 Inputs 
For different scenarios, different inputs for CAPEX and OPEX were used. Other parameters were kept 
the same comparing the Baltic Sea and the North Sea, unless for sensitivity analysis. 
Discount rate of 6.5% is used (Brückmann, 2015). DiaCore suggests the rate between 5% - 6.5% for 
Denmark on financing renewables and 6.5% was decided to use. This on the other hand is quite 
conservative, because according to this assessment, Denmark (as well as France, Germany and 
Belgium) has rather low discount rate comparing with other European countries. Hence the sensitivity 
analysis will later vary this parameter. Corporate tax rate in Denmark in 2016 is 22% and power price 
in 2015 46,3 EUR/MWh. As an example, a wind farm capacity was chosen to be 500 MW representing 
larger developments in the future. 
It can be noted, that the capacity factor of 40% is used at first for calculations for both, Baltic Sea and 
North Sea case. This might seem like an underestimation for North Sea case, taking into consideration 
that the North Sea wind farm is located far out in the sea and wind conditions are expected to be much 
better there. Hence, this should not be considered as a final and firm result and an observant eye 
should be kept on sensitivity analysis which is carried out on capacity factor.

6. Sensitivity analysis 
In this work, several assumptions are used that might change in time or depending on the location. In 
order to understand the importance of these factors, the sensitivity analysis for different parameters 
was carried out. 
6.1 Sensitivity to capacity factor 
The most important thing for a power plant is the electricity production, which generates the positive 
cash flows and revenues. Hence, the higher the capacity factor, the more profitable is the project. 
Offshore wind farms are known to have higher capacity factor than onshore wind farms, nevertheless, 
this still varies greatly depending on the site and turbines. In this work, capacity factor of 40% was used 
as a base for calculations. Capacity factor is much dependent on site characteristics. Power production 
depends on the site wind speeds - the better wind conditions, the higher are the chances to reach 
greater power coefficient and the rated power which in turn will improve greatly the capacity factor. 
On the other hand, the higher are the wind speeds at the location, the lower is the accessibility in case 
of an unforeseen break down and corrective maintenance needs to be carried out. Restricting wind 
speed and wave heights decrease the availability which in turn has negative impact on the capacity 
factor. 
As previously seen from Figure 6, the mean wind speed in the North Sea is greater, than in the Baltic 
Sea, specially further out in the sea, which was also a chosen case for this work. Thus it can be assumed, 
that the capacity factor for North Sea case is actually higher than 40% as assumed in the main 
calculation.  
The sensitivity analysis for capacity factor was conducted in a range of 35% - 50% which corresponds 
to currently existing offshore capacity factors. 
Figure 21 illustrates how the TLTC increases while the capacity factor increases - due to a higher power 
production OPEX cost increase, which is dependent on produced electricity. 
==PAGE:60==

Figure 21 TLTC sensitivity to capacity factor change. 
Figure 22 represents the LCoE sensitivity to capacity factor. It can be seen, that LCoE is highly 
dependent on capacity factor. The higher is the capacity factor, the more power is being produced and 
the utilization is higher. The higher is the utilization factor of the power plant, the lower the unit cost 
of electricity becomes and large investments become reasonable. From this figure, it can be seen that

capacity factor 35%-50%, which is 28 EUR difference for MWh. Hence it can be said, that capacity factor 
has great influence on LCoE.

7. Discussion and Conclusions 
Undoubtedly, offshore wind energy technology in general is very expensive. In addition, the cost of a 
wind farm depends on several different factors that have a high influence on cost elements and thus 
on overall cost. 
The elements that are the most sensitive to the change of location are foundation, export cable, 
installation, and corrective and preventative maintenance logistics. The cost of these elements differs 
greatly depending on its location. Factors, such as water depth, distance from the shore, seabed 
peculiarities, wind speed and wave height are crucial to determine the cost fluctuation for these 
elements. 
In this work, three different offshore wind farms with different locations were compared and the 
results were drawn accordingly. 
Foundation, that is mainly dependent on seabed peculiarities (sandy bottom, rocky outcrops, soft silt, 
stiff clay, mud etc.) and on water depth, is the most cost sensitive element from fluctuating 
components. Its cost can vary between 14% and 27% of the total CAPEX cost depending on its location. 
Installation, which is driven by both, the distance to the shore and the water depth, varies between 
23% and 31%. This is lower degree of variation than for foundation, but as installation activities are 
rather expensive, then even a small change affects the total cost more. 
Export cable experiences a slight cost increase in relations to the distance to the shore, but the share 
in the total CAPEX remains in the range of 8% to 9%. 
The logistics of preventative maintenance is mainly driven by the distance to the shore whereas the 
logistics of corrective maintenance additionally depends greatly on positive weather window and the 
accessibility of the wind farm. 
In a typical offshore wind farm in the Baltic Sea with the water depth of 10 meters and distance to the 
shore 11 kilometres, the total CAPEX was calculated to be 2.83 mEUR/MW, at the same time a future 
offshore wind farm in the North Sea with the distance to the shore 50 kilometres and the water depth 
of 45 meters, the CAPEX per MW installed was calculated to be 4.61 mEUR which is 63% more 
expensive than the Baltic Sea location. 
TLTC for the typical Baltic Sea location is 2.02 billion euros, while the cost for the North Sea location 
was found to be 3.42 billion euros, making this offshore wind farm 59% more expensive than the Baltic 
Sea.  
==PAGE:64==
53 
 
LCoE for the Baltic Sea with the capacity factor of 40% was found to be 105 EUR/MWh while the same 
figure for the North Sea was 177 EUR/MWh. This is 72 euros more expensive per MWh which is caused 
by the location specific cost increase. Even though it seems like a great difference, this range fits well 
with the suggested values from previous analysis, suggesting the offshore wind LCoE varies between 
119 – 194 EUR/MWh (Kost et al., 2013). Having that said, it is very important to mention the 
significance of the capacity factor and the simplification which was done in this work. Capacity factor 
that depends on several variables, plays the key role on power production and hereafter on LCoE. It 
might be the case that North Sea location has higher capacity factor than 40% due to higher wind 
speeds and more energy captured from the wind. When increasing the capacity factor for North Sea 
to 50%, then the LCoE decreases from previous 177EUR/MWh to 150 EUR/MWh which is more 
competitive than in the case of capacity factor being 40%. 
Required support for the Baltic Sea scenario was found to be 704 million euros over the lifetime, 
whereas for the North Sea, the same figure was 2107 million euros. This is a great difference, but now 
it is very important to note, that this amount depends greatly on other external factors such as the 
choice of a country and herewith electricity prices related to it. LCoE however, will remain similar. 
Also, it is crucial to note, that the cost differences are much dependent on site specific conditions, 
regardless whether they are located in the North Sea or in the Baltic Sea. It should be stressed, that in 
this work, the typical developments for the North Sea and the Baltic were considered. But at the same 
time, the North Sea may experience the same conditions as here described as Baltic Sea conditions 
and the other way around. One should not be misled by the title of scenarios, but also closely 
investigate the site specifications. Nevertheless, when considering the most likely characteristics of 
future developments and a large number of parks, it is likely that the average parameters are in the 
range of the here analysed scenarios, making this investigation a valuable contribution to the 
discussion where offshore wind parks could be developed in the future. 
As of today, offshore wind energy remains to be very expensive compared to the other technologies, 
but this is due to its relatively young age and emerging stage where the technology has not reached 
its maturity yet. However, in the future, the cost is expected to decrease due to economies of scale, 
technological innovation and improvements in logistic infrastructure. This in turn will have an influence 
on the cost differences depending on different sites, but this will be work for future investigation. 
As seen from results, the offshore wind farms do require external support in order to be feasible, hence 
the renewable energy policies and security by governments needs to be in place to encourage future 
developments. 
==PAGE:65==
54 
 
By finding the TLTC, LCoE and required support for the Baltic Sea and the North Sea, the main research 
question can be answered. 
Based on presented scenarios, it is more feasible to build and offshore wind farm to the Baltic Sea than 
to the North Sea as the TLTC, LCoE and required support is lower than for the North Sea scenario and 
whereas Baltic Sea requires 36 EUR/MWh support the North Sea wind farm would require as much as 
109 EUR/MWh. Hence it can be concluded that the it is more feasible to build an offshore wind farm 
to the Baltic Sea with support of 36 EUR/MWh than to the North Sea with the same amount of support. 
Future work 
After conducting this study, several areas have been discovered, that could be investigated further or 
be more detailed in order to get even more accurate results. 
In this work, many simplifications were done and often only distance and depth was used as a 
parameter when calculating the components cost. This was due to lack of time and data. Nevertheless, 
there is a possibility to introduce more parameters to the equation and add weights for parameters 
which would lead to more accurate cost estimations and better planning. 
Moreover, external conditions, such as electricity prices for each country could be investigated and 
implemented to get more accurate answers from country specific perspective. 
Another suggestion that was not included in this work is about investigating the society’s cost of 
electricity, which additionally for system cost also investigates social effects, employment effects, 
geopolitical risks, environmental costs etc. which would also decrease the cost of electricity and makes 
it more competitive on the market.
