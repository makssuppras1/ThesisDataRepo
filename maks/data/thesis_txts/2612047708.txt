Abstract
A project driven by students of Technical University of Denmark, DTU Roadrunner,
is ambition to build up a fuel eﬀicient Ecocar with autonomous driving function and
win the Shell Eco Marathon Autonomous UrbanConcept competition. This competition
consists challenge for self-driving vehicle race on a track.
This thesis addresses the challenge of optimal path planning on the race circuit for DTU
RoadRunner project. Path planning of a racetrack with and without barriers on the
sides have been investigated and implemented in previous projects but its primary focus
was to find the center line of the track. The center path is further used to determined
optimal path in terms of minimum lap time and shortest distance via motion planning
based on optimization methods. The track data process and vehicle dynamic model
development are done and they are as inputs for the the trajectory optimization. Once
the optimal path is obtained the trajectory tracking models are developed to mimic the
vehicle’s hardware behaviors to follow the optimal path. The solutions were tested on
various tracks to create a robust method for DTU Ecocar in future competitions.
Keywords
Self-driving vehicle, Autonomous vehicle, Minimum time trajectory, Shortest distance
path, Optimal path, Optimization, Direct method, Trajectory tracking.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Autonomous driving cars are one of the most popular topics in recent years on both
research and industry. Apart from universities, many technical unicorns such as Waymo,
Nuro, WeRide and Tesla have released their own on road tests of full or high levels
self driving cars instead of developing prototype.
These developments inspire more
widespread access to the autonomous driving technologies.
1.1
Shell Eco-marathon
Shell Eco-marathon (SEM) is a world-wide student engineering competition on fuel
eﬀiciency hosted and sponsored by Shell [She20].
The goal of the competition is to
design and build the vehicle that is able to run a certain number of laps on the race
track and achieve as high energy eﬀiciency as possible. There are two different vehicle
types for competition, Urban Concept and Prototype. The Urban Concept requires the
car to be similar to the road car features such as assembling four wheels. On the other
hand, the Prototype is focus on lightweight and in usually three wheels are suﬀicient.
Regarding to energy category teams can choose to compete in internal combustion engine
(gasoline, diesel and ethanol), battery electric and hydrogen fuel cell. Figure 1.1 shows
all the teams participated in 2019 Shell Eco-marathon event.
Figure 1.1: Teams photo of Shell Eco-marathon 2019.

DTU Roadrunner
DTU Roadrunner is a project run by students of Technical University of Denmark. The

Self-Driving Challenge
In 2018 the Shell Eco-marathon introduced a new category to autonomous driving chal-
lenges that requests vehicle to run autonomously one or more laps on the track without
interfere by the driver. To complete the requirements of self-driving challenge DTU
Ecocar was upgraded its hardware and software platform to expand its autonomous
capabilities [Høj17]. Normally the autonomous robot includes functions of perception,
localization, navigation and control [SNS11] and parts of these functions have been im-
plemented on DTU Ecocar via previous projects [Top18] [Lar18] [Mad20] [Chr20]. Due
to these implementation currently the DTU Ecocar is capable to drive autonomously on
a racetrack or on rural areas.

1.4
Problem statement
The aim of this project is to improve the DTU Ecocar’s self-driving performance. After
reviewing the previous works and tests it is found that the prior navigation solely found
the center line of the track and it certainly could be further extended. Therefore the
work of this project will be focus on the motion planning which gives a better path
planning and the problem statement and goals are described as:
”This project will apply the motion planning to achieve smooth and optimal path
tracking, in order to give the DTU Roadrunner Ecocar more intelligence and enhance
its eﬀiciency in autonomous mode.”
As stated, the problem is cataloged in the scope of motion planning and it can be
resolved through several project objectives:
• Acquire the data of track and process them to computational required format.
• Model the vehicle dynamics since it needs to be taken into account to ensure the
feasibility of the computational path.
• Identify the definition of ”optimal path” and calculate the optimal path for the
known track. Keep in mind that the calculation method should work on other
tracks as well.
• Recognize the driving behaviors of the Ecocar and build the model to simulate
these behaviors into optimal path tracking.

CHAPTER 2
Analysis and Design
The motion planning is an important part of navigation and it finds a feasible and
optimal route for the vehicle to follow autonomously.
The state of the art motion
planning methods and their applications will be reviewed firstly. The coming project
analysis will consider the project objectives together with introduced motion planning
methods and choose the solutions that can achieve project goals and conquer the previous
limitations.
2.1
Motion Planning Overview
The goal of motion planning for self-driving vehicle is to compute a safe and dynamical
feasible trajectory. Depending on the context of feasible, the motion planning takes
obstacles information into account and offers a collision free trajectory meanwhile meets
vehicle physical limitation and some other targets such as traversing time and passenger
comfort. Typically the output of motion planning is passed to vehicle controllers and
the controllers generate a set of input signals to follow the given path.
Oftentimes the motion planning problem is described as path or trajectory planning
and tracking. In the context of trajectory planning it usually finds a solution from current
vehicle configuration to target configuration without prescribing the velocity profile or
how to follow the goal path. On the other hand, the trajectory tracking considers the
vehicle configuration changes over time [Pad+16].
Motion planning techniques in autonomous car has been study for the last decades
and amounts of them have been applied on the realistic cases of self-driving vehicle.
According to [Pad+16] and [Gon+15] the motion planning techniques can be classified
as graph search, sampling based and optimization based.
2.1.1
Graph Search Planner
In graph search approach the state space of vehicle is typically discretized and repre-
sented by a graph like occupancy or lattice. Denote the graph as G = (V, E) where V is
a set of vertices belong to configuration space and E are edges. From the view of path
planning if the vehicle moves from state A to target state B and this can be implemented
by searching different vertices belong to V on a graph. Consequently a feasible path for

the vehicle can be transformed to the connection of edge segments. There are sets of
graph search algorithms have been applied on autonomous driving cars and here three
common strategies are discussed.
2.1.1.1
Dijkstra
Dijkstra is graph search method similar to breath first search and its aim is to find put
the shortest path on a graph which is typical discritized cell grid. The implementation
of Dijkstra is described in [LaV06] and [SNS11]. The strength of Dijkstra algorithm
is it suits for global path search and it regularly returns the shortest path. However,
Dijkstra does not include heuristic function that leads it is slow from the point of view
of computation cost. Besides, the resulted path is discrete and does not take account
vehicle constraints so it is not applicable for real time situations.
2.1.1.2
A-star
A-star (A∗) algorithm is an extension of Dijkstra with a heuristic function that gives
direction to nodes are closer to the target while Dijkstra explores all the potential nodes
only. The heuristic function includes the cost of the path from start vertex to current
node and the cost approximates the path from current node to the goal. So the A*
is to find out a path to the goal with the lowest cost. The cost is usually measured
by distance. The optimality of A* heavily depends on the determination of heuristic
function.
There are several improved algorithms based on A* in mobile robotics application.
Standford University applied hybrid A* on its automated vehicle in DARPA Urban
Challenge [Mon+08] and Dave et al. [FHL08] implemented Anytime D* (AD*) on their
vehicle and won the DARPA Urban Challenge in 2007.
2.1.1.3
State lattice
In state lattice method the edges of a graph represent the vehicle continuous motion and
the vertices are different possible states. The path searching in state lattice planning
can be viewed connecting between any two discretized states if there is a feasible path
containing all features. The state discretization and cost function determines the best
path between nodes are critical and they transforms the state lattice planning problem to
a sequential decision process [PK05]. [ZS09] presents state lattice planning was applied
on dynamic on-road scenarios considering time and speed dimensions.
2.1.2
Sample Based Planner
The basic idea of sample based planner is to sample the configuration or state space
randomly and incrementally connect the reachable nodes inside the graph. The search
==PAGE:23==
2.1 Motion Planning Overview
7
is stop if a node is close enough to the goal and the desire path is obtained by tracing the
edges from the end configuration to the start configuration. The most popular sample
based algorithms in robotics are probabilistic roadmap method (PRM) and rapidly-
exploring random tree (RRT) and the RRT has been tested a lot on self driving vehicles
[Pad+16].
2.1.2.1
Probabilistic Roadmap Method (PRM)
The probabilistic roadmap method (PRM) is to sample nodes randomly in the configura-
tion space and test them to check if they are in the free space. After determining the free
space nodes a planner is used to connect these configuration to other free configuration
and a graph search method is applied to search a path from start node to target node.
More specifically there are two stages for the PRM method.
Firstly a roadmap
graph is created where generates random configuration and it connects to neighbors.
The connections are continuously added until the roadmap is dense enough. The next
is query phase that connects the start and target configuration and the path is obtained
by a graph search algorithm such as A∗[Kav+96].
2.1.2.2
Rapidly-Exploring Random Tree (RRT)
Similar to the PRM, the rapidly exploring random tree (RRT) is firstly to generate
nodes randomly in the configuration space but it connects nearest free vertex at each
iteration and a verification is set up to check if the vertex and the edge are collision free.
In the initial version of this method, the iteration is terminated once the expanding tree
includes a node in the goal region [KF11].
In [KF11] improvements of RRT are described and an optimized version named RRT*
was developed. In short, two key features are added in RRT*. First RRT* memories
the travelled distance of each node to its parent node and it refers to cost. If a lower
cost is found then the proximal node will be replaced cheaper node. The second feature
is to rewire the tree if the newly added node reduces the cost.
2.1.3
Optimization Based Planner
The optimization typically minimizes or maximizes a cost function meanwhile subjects to
various constraints such as vehicle dynamics. In motion planning the optimization based
planner is used for trajectory planning in dynamic scenarios or considering dynamic
constraints and the solution of it is a trajectory [Pad+16].
Interpolation is one popular technique in optimization planner that allows the found
paths become smooth.
Several types of curves like clothoid curves, polynomial and
Bezier curve are chosen for path smoothing and curve generation [Pad+16]. The trajec-
tory resulted from optimization method with interpolation are ensured its feasibility on
vehicle driving, compared with the discretized path generated by graph search.

Optimization planner can be used to compute trajectory complied with vehicle model
[Zie+14]. For the autonomous race car, [Chr+19] and [Cas00] implemented a time min-

2.3
Project Analysis
One of the primary targets for DTU Ecocar is to participate in Shell Eco Marathon
autonomous competition.
Unlike the complicated road environment for general au-
tonomous driving the race competition has its specific driving area without obstacles.
The obstacles are static and only available on the sides of the track therefore the mo-
tion planning only needs to identify the feasible running area and find out a feasibility
path. In addition, for a race there is usually a specific index such as shortest lap time
or minimum energy eﬀiciency to set up a target to measure the design.
2.3.1
Previous Limitation
The previous navigation algorithm is able to handle autonomous running challenge but
there are limitations. The first restriction of it is the implemented Voronoi diagram
method is capable to find the middle line of the track only. From the point of view of
safety, center line is a good option because it avoids collision between vehicle and track
sides. However from the perspective of race where requires the minimum lap time, the
middle line is not the best choice.
The optimal race line typically is identified as a trajectory that the vehicle drives
to achieve the best lap time. Geometrically the track can be viewed as composition of
straight lines and corners. On the straight lines the least driving time can be obtained
through keeping maximum speed as long as possible. On the other hand at the corners
it expects to maintain the maximum speed as well but this not always can be achieved
==PAGE:28==
12
2 Analysis and Design
because there are constraints limit the maximum allowed speed. Typically in a race
the corner driving strategies often link to the question how to choose the apex properly.
The apex of a corner is identified as inside tangential point that is closest to the vehicle.
There exists numbers of corner driving strategies and the most popular three of them
are summarised in [Mad20]:
• Shortest Path: It specifies a series of points which allows the vehicle travels the
as least distance as possible at the corner. For a corner of the track the shortest
path is easy to know which is the path as close to the inner track as possible and
it is shown in Figure 2.5 (a).
• Geometric Apex: Theoretically the optimal race path at a given corner is the
geometric line takes the widest possible arc through a corner. The demonstration
of it is shown in Figure 2.5 (b), the line begins on the track outer and reaches the
apex in the middle of the inner corner then finally finishes again back to the track
outer. This strategy is characterized by large curvature radius and allows the car
to pass the curve at high speed.
• Late Apex: It is typically applied on professional racing vehicle with high propul-
sion when it turns at a corner following a long straight section. The process of late
apex is described in Figure 2.5 (c) where indicates the vehicle delays the turning
point to shorten the entering radius and lengthen the exiting radius. The late
apex allows requires slower speed to enter the corner but is compensates for earlier
acceleration when exiting the corner to straightway.
Obviously the center path of a track fulfills none of these strategies.
The other
limitation of current navigation is that it is local path planning only. In general the
local path planning cooperates with global path planning that the global planning finds
out an overall optimal path on an entire map and local planning schemes to follow global
optimal path based on the real time sensors inputs. If the global planning was absent
the local planning can only find the regional optimal path due the limit range of sensors.
Therefore it is essential to include global path planning for the Ecocar to provide the
optimal trajectory offline and then it is used to guide the local path planning.
2.3.2
Requirements and Proposed solution
As stated this project aims to expand and improve navigation capabilities of DTU Ecocar
through including motion planning techniques to find out an optimal trajectory. In some
cases the fastest lap time is given by the shortest traversing distance. This is based on
the assumption that the car can maintain high speed at the corners following shortest
path but this is not always true. At the professional racing the least time trajectory
is often obtained by the one which maintains the highest possible acceleration in the
correct direction [Cas00] and this means to use proper corners driving strategies. So the
optimal trajectory here can be seen as minimum lap time or minimum driving distance.

3.4
Track Data Post-process
The raw data of the track are potentially obtained either by sampling or experimentation
which only contains a limited amount of variables. It can not provide the numerical
quantity for a intermediate value of the variables. Moreover if a constant step on distance
i.e. ds is chosen for optimization the raw track data probably can not provide exactly
the same value of ds. For these reasons we need to interpolate the original track data.
There are many interpolation skills such as Newton methods, polynomial function
and Lagrange form. Since the interpolation methods are not the main task of this project
so their detail investigation and comparison will not be presented here. Considering the
complexity and computational cost of them the spline function is used for track data
interpolation. The spline interpolation uses piece wise polynomial functions to insert a
set of points with each polynomial for two connection segments. The implementation
of spline function is available in Python’s SciPy library and the spline calculation of a
track is implemented in [Ale20].
The London track after interpolation at distance of 1m between each of two points
is seen in Figure 3.7. The interpolated track smoothed the raw track and reduced the
number of points from 1941 to 940 which is the center length of acquired London track
data.

The indirect and direct methods for solving the optimal control problem are reviewed in
above sections. Indirect methods mainly depends on Pontryagin’s Minimum Principle
while direct methods tries to solve the optimization problem by discretizing continuous
problem and using mathematical programming techniques.
In this project the direct method will be used since the indirect solving process is
complicated if the constraints are complicated where it is true in this project, the indi-
rect methods need to use specially designed algorithms or the use of penalty function to
include constraints in objective function. In contrast the strategy to deal with the con-
straints in direct methods is considered to be ideal from the view of numerical eﬀiciency
and accuracy [Cas00].
There are several software programs such as GPOPS-II and CasADi to solve con-
strained optimization problem using direct method. As this project is aim to solve the
minimum time problem rather than compare the performance of various solvers so the
results of different solvers are not going to test. Furthermore the CasADi is an open
source software but GPOPS-II requires license fee, therefore CasADi is chosen. The user
guideline of CasADi is available at [Cas20].

5.4
Optimal Trajectory
The optimal trajectory and vehicle dynamics influence on each other. The availability of
control inputs affect the vehicle to select a trajectory closer to time minimum or shortest
path solutions.
==PAGE:57==

The solution of time minimum optimization depends on the solved control inputs
u. Once the control inputs are not satisfied or partly not satisfied then the resulted
trajectory is potentially not the least time any more.
Consider the case of vehicle
passing the corner following the time minimum trajectory, it should be at its maximum
allowed speed which is determined by found inputs and physical limits. If the inputs are
violated and the vehicle can not reach its maximum allowed speed at the corner, then the
optimal path is to turn sharply closer to minimum distance path to avoid unnecessary
driving distance. One way to further optimize trajectory is a linear combination of time
minimum trajectory Ptime and shortest distance trajectory Pdist. Therefore the optimal
trajectory Popt problem can be formulated as.
Popt = η · Ptime + (1 −η) · Pdist
(5.50)
Where η is a weight parameter between these two trajectories and it should be
0 ≤η ≤1.
Depends on the capabilities of vehicle actuators, the optimal trajectory Popt gives
more flexibility to vehicle to drive close to minimum time or minimum distance path by
determining η. If the control inputs found by time minimum optimization can be fulfilled
η can be chosen as 0, which totally driving on the minimum time trajectory and this
gives the best lap time. Otherwise, Popt including shortest path can avoid unessential
driving distance at the corner that could give the better lap time as well.
5.5
Partial Conclusion
The minimum time problem was identified as an optimal control problem where the
cost function is the least lap time and meanwhile subjects to dynamic equations devel-
oped in Chapter 4 and various constraints considering maximum allowed speed, steering
angle and its turning speed limit, effort constraints, Kamm’s circle and collision free
constraints. Once the problem was formulated the indirect and direct methods were
investigated and direct collocation method with Lagrange polynomials was determined
to solve the formulated minimum time problem.
Regarding to the shortest path it was formulated geometrically as a quadratic prob-
lem where minimized the square of distance in each segment and summed them up in the
end to obtained the overall shortest trajectory of the track. Consider that the possibility
that the actuators can not execute the found control inputs that leads the theoretical
minimum time trajectory is not the least path actually, an optimal trajectory combining
paths found by optimal control and quadratic problem was introduced that still could
give least lap time.

6.1.2
Braking Model
The other task of longitudinal control is regulate deceleration i.e. braking torque. The
current navigation algorithm applies a so called ”Fast-Slow” strategy where defines two
sets of engine turns on speed von and off speed voff for fast mode and slow mode [Lar18].
The fast mode defines higher von and voff than slow mode and it is ideally used on
the straight sections and aims to achieve higher average velocity. While the corner is
detected the speed control is switched to slow mode where its von is defined as the speed
ensures the vehicle can pass a 90◦turn. Braking is applied either switching between fast
and slow mode or the speed exceeding maximum allowed speed.
However, in the discussion of the report [Lar18] the authors recommended to omit
the Fast-Slow strategy as it switches to slow mode when the straight section has small
turns or small bumps and it led to a mechanical error in 2018 London competition.
Additionally, from the point of view on fuel eﬀiciency the braking should be used as less
as possible to save the fuel consumption. So the braking is applied only in the case of
velocity over the maximum speed.

7.1.3.1
Tyre-road adhesion
The tyre-road adhesion is often identified as static friction and describes the slide or slip
of the vehicle. The value of it usually is close to 1 if the road condition is dry enough.
Taken Equation (5.18) in section 5.2.3 into account where describes the transmittable
horizontal force that the car can transmit to prevent skid, in other words, the turning
radius depends on the how much static friction effort can be utilized if maximum speed
is determined.
The optimal path contains time minimum route where assumes the road surface is
dry enough and friction coeﬀicient µ is constant for the whole track. But this is not
always true in realistic e.g. some parts of the track are at wet condition that causes low
friction coeﬀicient. Therefore it is worth to investigating how the friction coeﬀicient will
impact on the optimal path. Normally the friction coeﬀicient µ can achieve 0.7 to 1 on
the dry track surface while it could reduce to 0.4 or even as low as to 0.1 depends on
how wet the road is. Here the values µ = 0.9, µ = 0.4 and µ = 0.1 are chosen to verify
the impact of µ on the optimal path.
The different optimal trajectories results based on various tyre-road adhesion µ values
are shown in Figures 7.6. These results suggest the lower µ value the vehicle turns at
larger radius. From Figures 7.6(a), 7.6(c) and 7.6(e) it can see that in the case of µ = 0.1,
as the red dash lines show, the vehicle starts to turn earlier than other µ values before
entering the corner and it requires the most radius to pass the corners in the whole
turning process. On the other hand when µ = 0.9 it allows the vehicle to drive closer to
inner track to achieve smaller turning radius. This is described quantitatively in Figures
7.6(b), 7.6(d) and 7.6(f). The sign of y axle in these figures indicates turning direction
and it should be neglected when mapping the curvature to curve radius. In the situation
µ = 0.9 it always reaches the lowest curvature that means smallest radius while µ = 0.1
is constantly at the highest curvature in these sections so it needs largest radius. This
phenomena is consistent with the Kamm’s circle mentioned in section 5.2.3.4 as well.
Apart from maximum allowed turning radius another factor influenced by tyre-road
adhesion µ is the maximum allowed speed at the corners.

Partial Conclusion
The test results of trajectory optimization and tracking were presented. Specifically the
results of minimum time trajectory, minimum distance path and the optimal path on
their combination were illustrated on London track and RRC track. The comparison
between them and center path was discussed and it can be concluded that the paths
after optimization are better on the corner driving and more reasonable than center path.

The optimization algorithms were further tested on an man-made track and the result
was quite satisfied therefore it supports the developed algorithms are robust to work on
other tracks.

how they influence the result of optimal trajectory. The result on various adhesion co-
eﬀicients indicate the lower value of adhesion requires more turning radius but allows
lower maximum speed to turn, in other words, the adhesion changes the layout of opti-
mization paths. In addition, the lower steering wheel speed needs more space to turn as
well because it can not reach the expected steering angle in time.

independently to show they are close to the hardware performance. After this the tests
on entire showed the developed models can track the optimal trajectory correctly. Both
lap time and energy eﬀiciency on Ecocar are influenced by the selection of engine turn on
and off speeds and they are trade-off each other. If the lap time was more priority than
fuel eﬀiciency both engine on and off speeds should be set higher as possible. On the
other hand if fuel consumption was the main concern then the engine on speed should
be at low value and off speed is at high value. If the speed limit is changed due to the
adhesion the engine turn on and off speed need to be planned to avoid exceeding the
maximum allowed speed.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
8.1
Approach
In this project the goal is to build methods to develop offline global optimal trajec-
tory based on information from rough map and further compute the steering angle and
velocity profiles according to the optimization results.
The previous navigation algorithm is a real time planning that extracts the center
line of a track as a reference path based on the obstacles or lane edges information. But
theoretically the center line is not the optimal trajectory either in time or in distance.
To obtain the optimal trajectory for a known track firstly the information of the
track requires to be represented numerically. The track data are processed to model as
center line coordinates and its distances to both sides along the track. Apart from the
track model the vehicle dynamics are needed as well. Typically the vehicle dynamics
are analyzed according to the forces of each tyre on xyz dimensions and together with
other parameters such as steering wheel angle, to derive the joint forces on the whole
vehicle and its dynamic representations.
Once the track data and vehicle model are developed the time minimum trajectory is
constructed as optimal control and applied numerical optimization to obtain the optimal
path and its control inputs. On the other hand the shortest path is formulated via track’s
geometry feature and obtained from the solution of a quadratic minimized problem. The
optimized trajectory is combined with time minimum and distance minimum path and
fed to the path tracking models where output the velocity and steering angle profiles.
The approach presents in this project to compute the offline optimal trajectory is a
general method independent on track layouts and vehicle types. In future if the Ecocar
competes on tracks other than London track and Roskilde racetrack or it is upgraded
to capable to drive at higher speed, the simulation design here will be still applicable.
The path tracking models are the mathematically models of engine, steering wheel
and vehicle states update. They were compared with the on-site test data to verity their
outputs. However, since the on-site test data varied due to the specific driving strategies
and environments of tracks it is diﬀicult to mimic entirely the behaviors of the hardware
especially the engine.
The result accuracy of optimization and trajectory tracking depend on the parameters
of vehicle model therefore it is important to determine the these parameters before

8.2
Results
The design of the optimization algorithm resulted in achievement of the project goals.
The computed optimal trajectory considers together with time minimum strategy and
shortest distance strategy. Because the time minimum trajectory is no longer the least
lap time if the solved control inputs are violated therefore the shortest distance path is
taken into account for compensation. The result is more optimal than center path both
on the perspectives of traversing distance and corner driving strategies.
In addition, the optimal path allows the vehicle to drive straightly on the sections
that are not straight but they are wide enough to pass through straightly. This is can not
be achieved by driving on the center line since it follows the path layout change. Apart
from two race tracks the test result on an artificial track proves the optimization works
not only limit on the London and RRC tracks. Moreover two factors affect the results
of optimization were investigated. One is the tyre road adhesion that limits the vehicle
turning performance at the corners and the discussion reveals the lower value of tyre
road adhesion the larger turning radius is needed but meanwhile the lower maximum
speed at corner is allowed. The other factor is the steering wheel speed that if the speed
of steering wheel is not fast enough the car will deviate from optimal trajectory since
the steering wheel can not turn to expected angle in time.
Apart from demonstrated results the implemented trajectory optimization can also
compute the optimal trajectories in below cases:
• The track is reversed
• Arbitrary lap numbers
• Speed higher than 25km/h, see the Figure 10 in Appendix B.
Regarding to trajectory tracking the models developed of it achieves project target as
well. Although the engine model is not capable to entirely mimic the hardware behaviors,
the outputs of it are still close to the on-site test data. The path tracking results on the
whole tracks shows the feasibility of the developed models despite of the tracking path
slightly deviates from reference path at partial corners sections. The overall performance
of optimal trajectory computation and trajectory tracking are robust as the test results
demonstrated.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusions
This project has expanded the current autonomous vehicle platform of DTU Dynmo Eco-
car with new navigation capabilities. The Ecocar system integrated with this project’s
solution now can robustly plan the optimal trajectory offline for various racetracks.
In details, the solution has formulated the minimum time trajectory problem into a
optimal control problem where the vehicle dynamic model and other physical constraints
are taken into account. The direct method was applied to solve the optimal control
problem numerically. Regarding to the distance minimum trajectory problem it was
considered as geometrical problem without vehicle dynamic model and formulated it as
a quadratic problem. The optimal trajectory was obtained via combining time minimum
trajectory and shortest distance trajectory.
Once the optimal path is obtained the
trajectory tracking model was developed to simulate the vehicle performance and follow
the optimized trajectory.
The results from the optimal trajectories and path tracking are satisfactory, and
the conclusion is that the stated goals and requirements in section 1.4 and 2.3.2 are
accomplished.
• The target of providing an offline smooth and optimal path is clearly resolved.
Optimal trajectory in terms of minimum time and shortest distance have been
researched, investigated and implemented.
• Tests were conducted on London track and RRC race circuit, the results and
analysis were satisfied and they were clearly improved than center path.
• More than London race circuit and RRC racetrack, the implemented optimization
method is robust to work on other tracks as well. An artificial track was tested
and satisfied optimal trajectory results were obtained.
• Engine, steering and states update models have been developed based on the limi-
tations of the Ecocar platform to simulate the performance of piratical hardware.
• The trajectory tracking was implemented and it has been verified that the models
worked on SEM 2018 London track and RRC track.
As promised at the beginning of the project, the outputs from this thesis will expand
the DTU Ecocar path planning capabilities to have a global trajectory offline instead

CHAPTER 10
Future Work
Consider Track Gradient
It is common that the racetrack includes gradients and it is not total flat. Actually the
London track and RRC track include slopes in some sections but unfortunately these
information are lacking . The slope of a track directly influences the longitudinal joint
force which has impact on the acceleration. Therefore it is necessary to considering
more on the gradients in the vehicle model design in future if the slope information are
available.
Adhesion Pre-Estimation
The calculation of optimization in this thesis assumes the friction coeﬀicient µ is a
constant however the values of µ on a track could vary e.g. some parts of it is wet and
the rest are dry. As discussed the µ value has significant influences on the trajectory
optimization. So it is worth to estimating the variable values of µ before optimization.
Dynamic Weight Factor
Currently the optimal path is obtained by a linear combination of time minimum tra-
jectory and shortest distance trajectory where the weight factor is a constant. It can
be further improved through introducing the dynamic weight factor that considers the
current states such as speed and then decides a path to be closer to time minimum
trajectory or shortest path to further get better lap time.
Model Improvements
The models for trajectory tracking can be further improved by adding the sensor noise
and more precise vehicle resistance model to simulate the practical hardware performance
better.
==PAGE:90==
74
10 Future Work
Local Motion Planning
So far the optimal path computation and trajectory tracking are the parts of global
motion planning. The next step is local motion planning where refers to the optimized
trajectory including steering and velocity profiles and combining with sensor inputs to
control the vehicle to follow the reference trajectory.
Hardware Upgrade
The control principle of Ecocar engine i.e.
fully switch on and off only is the main
shortcoming to limit the optimality of Ecocar, compared it with general autonomous
vehicle. If the propulsion could upgrade to output the torque continuously it can achieve
more control states and has more possibilities to become optimal.
