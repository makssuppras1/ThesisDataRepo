A B S T R A C T
Current solutions for substructures for offshore wind turbines are excessively
expensive at sites where the water depth exceeds 40 −50m, making offshore
wind non-competitive at those depths. With an increasing focus on climate
change and a growing political desire to accelerate the transition from energy
sources based on fossil fuels to renewable energy sources, exploiting a larger
portion of the accessible offshore areas for harvesting wind power may be of
great importance.
In this report the NREL 5 MW offshore wind turbine equipped with guy
wires is optimized using the Matlab-function fmincon at a site with a water
depth of 60m. The cost of the concept substructure is then evaluated includ-
ing the cost of spiral strand guy wires and drag embedded anchors, but not
including installation, transportation or maintenance, and it is found that it is
signiﬁcantly cheaper than conventional substructures at that depth.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
While the world is realizing that the majority of the current sources of energy
are exhaustible and that exhausting them has climatic consequences, the de-
velopment of technology to harvest renewable energy sources progresses at a
continuously increasing rate.
Though the potential for renewable energy is largest for solar energy, wind
energy represents a signiﬁcant potential, especially considering that the peak
periods of solar and wind rarely coincide. Due to these circumstances, the
further development of wind energy must be deemed important.
1.1
offshore wind
Within wind energy a distinction is made between onshore and offshore wind
energy. Historically, the most accessible and thus most utilized part of the re-
source is onshore wind energy. In some parts of the world, the expansion of
onshore wind energy are coming to a halt due to increased turbine size and
limited free space. As a consequence, developers have begun looking towards
the sea, where the wind resource is generally better, but the expenses are big-
ger. In Tab. 1.1 a brief summary of the pros and cons of offshore wind energy
can be seen.
pros
cons
Generally more wind.
Low roughness means less turbulence,
which leads to smaller RNA loads.
No neighbours.
Installations and foundations are rela-
tively more expensive.
Grid connection is expensive, and is sel-

Table 1.1: Pros and cons of offshore wind energy compared to onshore wind en-
ergy.
Whether or not to invest in an offshore wind farm often comes down to
the predicted economy of the project. It is a major investment which needs
to have a reasonable probability of repaying itself. Particularly the cost of the
foundation can be an issue because it increases dramatically as the water gets
deeper. At waters deeper than 30m, new solutions are being investigated and
at waters deeper than 60m, no full-scale non-ﬂoating wind turbines have ever
been installed.

O F F S H O R E W I N D T U R B I N E S
With an increasing interest in expanding wind energy offshore, many different
concepts of substructures for offshore wind turbines have been considered. Of
those concepts, only a good handful have been made into full scale prototypes,
and even fewer are being installed on a regular basis.
2.1
existing concepts
In the following, some of the currently used offshore wind turbine foundations
are discussed.
2.1.1
Gravity foundations
Onshore wind turbines are in most cases installed with a gravity based foun-
dation. A gravity based foundation relies on the mass of the foundation to
prevent the turbine from falling over [3].

Vindeby Offshore Wind Farm, the ﬁrst offshore wind farm ever installed,

and development and reﬁnement of different types of gravity foundations is
still ongoing.
Before installing a gravity based foundation, it is usually necessary to pre-
pare the seabed, by removing the soft upper layers and laying out gravel. The
foundation must be protected from scouring.
For deeper waters than 25m, gravity based foundations for modern sized
turbines become extremely heavy, i. e. impossible to lift and very difﬁcult to
handle.

2.1.2
Monopile foundations
Most offshore wind turbines today are installed on a monopile. Simply put, a
monopile is a large steel cylinder that is hammered or vibrated into the seabed
without any seabed preparation. To correct the verticality of the monopile,
which is difﬁcult to control during hammering, a transition piece is added us-
ing a type of grouting. In addition to guaranteeing reasonable levelling of the
wind turbine, the transition piece is equipped with boat landing, ladder, plat-
form and whatever may be needed for maintenance purposes. An illustration
of a monopile can be seen in Fig. 2.2a.
At deeper waters, the monopile needs to be incredibly large to have sufﬁ-
cient stiffness to support a wind turbine properly. This makes it too heavy and
expensive compared to the current alternative at deeper sites – the jacket.
2.1.3
Jacket foundations
A jacket foundation is a type of lattice structure as illustrated in Fig. 2.2b. Usu-
ally a tower or mast can be made the lightest using a lattice tower because
it is able to reduce the internal forces to tension and compression, of which
steel can withstand a lot. A few examples are The Eiffel Tower, The Fuhrlän-
der Wind Turbine in Laasow, Germany (which currently has the tallest wind
turbine tower in the world), and almost every TV-mast or power pylon erected.
A major advantage of the general lattice tower is that the aero- or hydro-
dynamic forces are signiﬁcantly reduced compared to a cylinder structure of
similar stiffness [4]. This is because the projected area, as well as the cross
section area of a lattice structure is much smaller than for a cylinder.
2.1.4
Floating platforms
In acceptance of the fact that most of the area of the world seas, and a very
large ratio of the nearshore sea areas of e. g. eastern Asia and North America,
have a water depth of more than 100m, concepts for ﬂoating wind turbines are
being investigated with an increasing interest. Though a ﬂoating wind turbine
per deﬁnition does not have contact with the seabed, it is anchored using either
catenary or taut cables depending on the method of stabilization.
In Fig. 2.3 two different concepts are illustrated. The ﬁrst one in Fig. 2.3a is
a spar buoy concept which is stabilized only by the restoring force of the sub-
structure, which then needs to be massive. The second illustration in Fig. 2.3b
is of a tension leg platform, which means the vertical cables, or tendons, are
in tension. For the tendons to be in constant tension, the buoyancy force of the
ﬂoater must exceed the weight of the turbine and ﬂoater combined. Though
the cost of the mooring system is signiﬁcant in a ﬂoating system stabilized by
mooring lines, the production cost of the substructure can be reduced greatly
compared to buoyancy stabilized ﬂoating systems [5].

2.2
guyed concepts
In many cases when constructing tall structures such as TV-masts, meteorology-
masts or similar, guy wires are attached. The purpose of the wires is to reduce
the moment throughout the structure by transforming it into tension in the
wires. That way, the area moment of inertia of the structure, i. e. the stiffness,
does not need to be as large and less material can be used. The vertical force
in the structure will increase because the guy wires usually have a downwards
angle, and a certain pretension. Though the vertical force induces stress in the
structure, the beneﬁt of the reduction of moment far outweighs the increased
stress from vertical force.
Historically, even wind turbines have often been equipped with guy wires.
For two reasons in particular, this is no longer the case. Firstly, the increased
rotor diameter forces the hub into heights where the increase in wind speed
per meter is very small compared to the increase in material used for the
tower1. The second reason is aesthetics, which is also why lattice towers have
generally been replaced by tubular towers.
In Fig. 2.4 a guyed offshore wind turbine is illustrated with two types of
support – clamped and simple. The difference between the support models
is described in Section 3.2. Though the structural advantages of the guyed
turbine concept are numerous, some disadvantages are also introduced with
the guy wires. From [6] the disadvantages can be listed as:

M O D E L S
This report does not feature an experimental study, but a study based on simu-
lations. For the simulations, mathematical representations of reality are needed
of which some are brieﬂy described in this chapter.
3.1
hawc2
To simulate the wind turbine behaviour under the various environmental con-
ditions, the aeroelastic code HAWC2 v. 11.8 is used. Developed at Risø, Den-
mark, HAWC2 handles structure parts, as e. g. the tower and monopile, as
separate bodies divided into Timoschenko beam elements [7]. The bodies are
attached to each other by a set of constraints.
HAWC2 uses a ﬁxed time step for the simulation which allows the user
to know the exact length of the signal which is sometimes an advantage –
especially for the postprocessing of the result time series.
HAWC2 is able to calculate aerodynamic forces using extended blade ele-
ment momentum theory and hydrodynamic forces using Morison’s equation
as described in the following.

3.2
support model
Two different support models are considered in this report, a clamped- and
a simple support. They differ from each other by their boundary conditions.
The clamped support does not allow any rotation of the beam, or monopile
in this case, at the root, and acts with a moment to ensure that. The simple
support allows the monopile to rotate at the root, and cannot contribute with
any moment to keep the structure in place. The physical interpretation of the
clamped support is a regular monopile, which extends down into the seabed.
The simple support can be achieved in the real world by an articulate joint as
described in [10]. An illustration of each support along with some examples
of the moment distribution along the height of the structure derived from [11]
is shown in Fig. 3.1.

O P T I M I Z AT I O N P R O C E D U R E
The primary concern when designing a wind turbine is to ensure the preven-
tion of failure. Further, for economic and competitive purposes, it is important
to use as little excess material as possible, as long as that does not lead to
excessively complex geometries.
4.1
general approach
The purpose of the optimization procedure is to minimize the material used
while ensuring, within a reasonable probability, that failure does not occur
within the lifetime of the wind turbine. Failure can be caused by several dif-
ferent speciﬁc events or by fatigue – the growth of cracks due to alternating
stress. These events are described in the IEC-standard [14].

4.4
load evaluation
According to the IEC 61400-1 standard [16], there are four types of ultimate
limit state analysis that need to be considered. They are:
• Ultimate strength.
• Fatigue failure.
• Stability (Buckling etc.).
• Critical deﬂection (Mechanical interference between blade and tower etc.).
Here, fatigue and stability will be evaluated, as stability is considered more
critical than ultimate strength. The reason for this is that the structure investi-
gated is a large thin-walled cylinder, which is more prone to buckling than e.g.
a thick-walled cylinder.

4.5
mass optimization
The cost of a relatively simple offshore structure, such as a monopile, is directly
related to its weight, since the manufacturing complexity increases insigniﬁ-
cantly with the scale of the structure. Hence, to reduce the cost of a monopile
==PAGE:34==
22
optimization procedure
the weight needs to be reduced. The difﬁculty of minimizing the weight is that
the response of the structure changes as the geometry is altered. This means
that it is necessary to re-simulate and re-analyse the altered structure with
all the load cases which is a time-consuming task. Further, the optimization
procedure needs to consider a lot of variables – one for each node radius and
thickness adds up to 34 variables for the entire tower and monopile. Assum-
ing that only a single processor is available, the computation time is counted
in months, rather than minutes or hours. For a project of this scale, that is
unacceptable, which means a different approach must be considered.
4.5.1
fmincon-function
The tool used to minimize the mass of the monopile is a Matlab function
called fmincon which is a nonlinear programming solver. Within a lower and
upper bound, lb and ub, fmincon minimizes the function f (x) while making
sure that a set of linear and nonlinear constraints are satisﬁed. For the pur-
pose of this optimization, no linear constraints are necessary, but the nonlinear
constraints are deﬁned as a function c of x, that must satisfy

for all entries of c
(4.21)
The variable x can be a vector or matrix – each element, xi, representing a
variable. The lower and upper bounds have the same size as x and x must
then fulﬁl
lb ≤x ≤ub
(4.22)
4.5.1.1
Variables
The variable x of the function f contains the geometric properties of the tower
and monopile, used to determine the mass of it, i. e. x = {R1, R2, . . . , Rn, t1, t2, . . . , tn}
with R being radius, t being thickness, and n being the number of nodes in the
tower and monopile. The z-values of the nodes are left constant.
4.5.1.2

Apart from geometric considerations such as a linear conical shape of the tower
or cylindrical shape of the monopile, the only constraint that x strictly needs
to fulﬁl is that the structure may not fail during any of the load cases. As ex-

exceed the buckling strength, and that the damage due to fatigue may not ex-
ceed 1. With the utilization, U, from extreme or ultimate loads as a function of

4.6.2
Load simulations
Ideally, all load cases should be run with every change in geometry, but to
reduce optimization time, a few assumptions can be made:
• For the ultimate or extreme load cases, the single simulation from the
relevant design load cases, that inﬂicts the most severe stresses in the
structure can represent all the ultimate or extreme load cases – DLC 1.1
and DLC 2.3.
• The fatigue load cases can be represented by the single most representable
simulation from the fatigue load case – DLC 1.2.
• The above determined representative simulations are the same regardless
of changes in geometry.
Locating the representative ultimate or extreme simulation is straight forward.
The one that introduces the most critical load in any point along the structure
is the most representable. However, as the geometry is changed, there is a
chance that another simulation will introduce higher loads. Most likely, the
difference will be insigniﬁcant.

There are three signiﬁcant differences between Step I and Step II:

the dynamic response of the altered geometry is not considered. Hence,
Step II needs to be run more than once to guarantee structural integrity.

7.3.1
Minimum mass veriﬁcation
The purpose of the optimization was to minimize the materials used for the
substructure. However, with the procedure described in this report there are
several pitfalls for the optimization. Firstly, fmincon is satisﬁed with locating
a local minimum and does not necessarily explore the entire solution space.

E C O N O M I C P E R S P E C T I V E
Though the mass of the substructure is an important factor in determining
the cost, other parameters have signiﬁcant inﬂuence and should be estimated.
These parameters include the complexity of manufacturing, the support type,
and the requirements for the mooring system.
In the following, only the costs associated with materials and manufacturing
are considered. Transportation, installation, and maintenance are not.
8.1
substructure
Using the same approach to calculate the costs of the substructure as in [5], the
complexity of manufacturing is introduced as a variable. The complexity is a
measure of the difﬁculties of manufacturing a certain geometry. It relates the
material cost with the manufacturing so that manufacturing cost equals the
material cost multiplied by the complexity. For a monopile, such as the ones
used in this study, a complexity factor of 100% is used. This may be slightly
conservative for a regular monopile, but considering that an attachment point
for the guy wires needs to be added it is regarded reasonable. Hence, for the
case of two guy wires, the complexity is increased to 150%.

C O N C L U S I O N
With the Matlab minimization-function fmincon at the centre, a variety of
different mass optimizations of a guyed offshore wind turbine substructure
have been performed.
The starting guess for the optimization has been decided by the boundary
conditions and simpliﬁcations, but is based on the geometry deﬁned in Tab. 4.1
from [15]. That means that the outer radius has started at 2.7m, except when
the upper boundary is deﬁned as something else, and the shell thickness has
started at 40mm. The environmental conditions of the simulations are found
in [13] which is based on measurements of wind and waves from an offshore
site in the North Sea relatively near the coast of the Netherlands.
The optimization was split into two different steps, Step I and Step II. Step I
investigated the dynamic response of the turbine for DLC 2.3 and used those
results for the optimization. Step II did not consider the dynamic response,
which meant that multiple iterations of Step II was necessary to ensure the
structural integrity of the design. Load cases 1.1, 1.2, and 2.3 were all simulated
and evaluated for that purpose.
The optimizations were performed for three different guy attachment levels,
27.1, 20.0, and 10.0 meters above MSL, C0, Cz20, and Cz10, respectively, and
showed that the smallest substructure mass was possible with a guy attach-
ment level of 27.1 meters above MSL, assuming that the lowest possible mass
was found for Cz20.
For Cz10, the necessary mass of the substructure was signiﬁcantly increased
compared to the other two. The ultimate load of DLC 2.3 was more decisive
for the geometry of the substructure than with lower guy attachment level. For
Cz20 and Cz10 only the geometry at the monopile bottom and 10 meters above
was decided by the fatigue loads. For C0 fatigue loads were design driving
from the seabed and 20 meters up and from MSL and 20 meters downwards.
Two types of support models were used – a clamped support (as a mathemat-
ical representation of a monopile lowered deep into the seabed), and a simple
support (representing a spherical joint at the monopile base) – the latter was
only optimized with a guy attachment level of 27.1m. The result of the simple
support optimization, S0, showed that fatigue damage is a major issue when
the monopile is free to rotate around the bottom. Part of this issue may be
caused by the fact that the second tower mode eigenfrequency is signiﬁcantly
lower than the ditto of the clamped support structure – 0.552 −0.612Hz against
0.910 −0.917Hz for the initial geometry – and even falls into the 3P range of
frequencies.
Both types of support are an idealization of reality as the monopile is never
actually clamped nor free to rotate. Part of the issues of the simple support
53
==PAGE:66==
54
conclusion
may be resolved by adding friction to the rotation or including soil conditions
in the simulations.
An optimization denoted as C1.2 where Step I simulated and evaluated both
DLC 1.1 and 2.3 was done to check if including the fatigue design load case
would improve the design compared to only using DLC 2.3. This optimization
found an infeasible solution after computing for 9 days, and was not repeated.
Finally, an optimization with a second set of guy wires, ﬁxed to the same
anchors but attached to the tower 10 meters below MSL was done. Not surpris-
ingly, the second set of guy wires helped reduce the mass of the substructure
drastically, as very little loads are exerted on the lower part of the substruc-
ture. Thus this conﬁguration proved to have the lowest mass of all. However,
due to the extra complexity of the structure, the extra cables, and the stronger
anchors it did not have the lowest cost.
It was shown that the minima found in Step I was not global minima outside
Step I, though they may have been global minima under those particular con-
straints within Step I. Thus, the conclusion is that using DLC 2.3 as a basis for
ﬁnding the optimum monopile diameter is not adequate.
economics
Considering the economics of each optimization, the mass of the substructure
plays a signiﬁcant role, but generally the cost of the substructure including
manufacturing is of similar magnitude as the cost of the anchors and cables
combined. The advantage of the simple support substructures consist in them
not needing to reach deep into the seabed. To reﬂect this advantage, the mass
of the other substructures was modiﬁed to include 25 meters of in-seabed cylin-
der, with a shell thickness of 40mm.
The complexity-factor was introduced to relate the cost of materials with
the cost of manufacturing, and a conservative complexity factor of 100% was
chosen for the substructure with a single set of guys. For the substructure with
two guys, C2guy, a complexity factor of 150% was chosen.
Based on information from a manufacturer, the spiral strand costs were set
to €250 per meter which means that the cable expenses of each substructure
ranges from €93 −206,000.
The type of anchor chosen was a Stevmanta VLA based on considerations
from another study. From the manufacturer website, the necessary area of the
anchors was found to be 18m2 and a cost of €110,000 for such anchors was
provided by the manufacturer.
Without considering transportation, installation, maintenance, grid connection
or similar, the cheapest substructure proved to be C1.2, with a price tag about
1.5% smaller than the nearest competitor.
Adding another set of guy wires proved very efﬁcient in reducing the loads
in the structure, but the increased expenses for cables and anchors was sig-

discussion
The masses of the substructures found are suspiciously low, which is mainly
due to the high stiffness being obtained by very low shell thicknesses and
large diameters. In theory, increasing the ratio of diameter over thickness is
the most material-efﬁcient way to increase stiffness of the structure, but tak-
ing other considerations such as resistance to buckling into account, the best
approach becomes more complicated. In this report buckling stress due to
bending and axial force has been properly evaluated according to [17], but
shear forces have generally not been considered. Further, no extra thickness is
added to accommodate for the material prone to corrode over the course of
the turbine lifetime. In the case of fatigue, the slope of the end of the SN-curve
takes corrosion into account, but it may be that further correcting measures
should have been taken. This deﬁciency may be signiﬁcant in terms of mass of
structure, and should be further investigated.
For a water depth of 60m, in terms of materials and manufacturing, the guyed
turbine concept is signiﬁcantly cheaper compared to more conventional sup-
port structures. This along with its versatility makes the concept very interest-
ing for further studies.
suggestions for future work
In retrospect, a lot of things could have been done differently or improved. Be-
low is a list of suggestions for investigations or alterations of the optimization
procedure:
• Because of the very theoretical nature of the simple support as imple-
mented in this work, the soil conditions should be implemented as springs
in HAWC2. Probably, the optimum design of the structure has neither a
fully clamped support (as that requires a lot of mass below the seabed)
nor a simple support (because that introduces a lot of fatigue to the struc-
ture), but something in between.
• A generic algorithm or similar should be implemented to guarantee lo-
cating a global minimum. By running simulations parallel on a cluster,
the computation time should not increase signiﬁcantly.
• With an appropriate support – a compromise between clamped and sim-
ple – the loads across the substructure can probably be tuned to be more
evenly distributed
• If the loads are more evenly distributed as suggested above, perhaps a
lattice structure is suitable as a replacement for the monopile, as less
wave forces would act on it – see Fig. 9.1 on.
==PAGE:68==
56
conclusion
• The transition from guy wire to tower should distribute the tension from
the guy wires in the tower in the most suitable manner to reduce stresses
in the tower. Those stresses should be considered for the tower design.
• Other load cases, such as transportation or icing should be considered.
• In the optimization of the substructure with two sets of guy wires, the
diameter of wires was kept at 130mm. It could be investigated if a smaller
diameter will sufﬁce making it a little cheaper.
• Different water depths should be investigated – both shallower and deeper
waters.
