Summary
This thesis covers the process of designing autodocking control of a small ASV. First a
6-DOF mathematical model of the ASV is made from prior knowledge on the system.
This model is then simplified to a 3-DOF model that will be used for purpose of de-
signing a controller. The controller is designed as a adaptive backstepping controller.
Apart from the controller multiple auxiliary system will made in order to make a
complete docking system for the ASV. The auxiliary systems include a look-ahead
guidance system, control allocation and reference filtering.
The complete docking system is tested in simulation. The simulations include envi-
ronmental disturbances to the system. The result of the simulations is found to show
promising behaviour of ASV when controlled by the docking system.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
The automation of ships is not anything new. For many years captains has been
using highly autonomous autopilot systems at sea. Systems as such are present in all
modern maritime vessels. In the 21st century increasingly more autonomous systems
has been developed. So much so that the International Maritime Organization (IMO)
made interim guidelines for Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS) in 2019.
The IMO has more recently started to integrate autonomous ships in their regulations.
The automation of maritime vessels has become a new frontier in the application of
industry 4.0. In recent years many maritime companies has been looking into an
autonomous solution to maritime operations. Some examples of are.
вАҐ Falco Autonomous car ferry. A collaboration between Rolls-Royce and FinFer-
ries www.finferries.fi/en/news/press-releases/finferries-falco-worlds-first-fully-autonomous-
ferry.html
вАҐ SailDrone Autonomous wind- and solar powered boat. Used for maritime data
collection. www.saildrone.com
вАҐ Vaiaro Passenger ferry in Kiel by CAPTN.www.captn.sh
вАҐ Mayflower 400 Autonomous research vessel by ProMare and IBM www.mas400.com
вАҐ Yara birkeland Autonomous container ship by Yara. www.yara.com
вАҐ Aurora Robotized inflatable life raft by Radar MMS www.radar-mms.com
In order for a vessel to be fully autonomous all functions of the ship must be done
autonomous. This means that the vessel must not require any crew either onboard
nor ashore for it to operate. However, the full automation of maritime vessels does
not come without multiple challenges. The foremost important challenge is to avoid
collisions with other vessels and structures as well as groundings. In order to do this
multiple systems for perception and localisation must be made. Extensive research
has been done on the topic of making autonomous maritime systems safe and reliable.
Examples of research consortiums that are currently working on fully autonomous
maritime vessels are

Motivation
One of the major challenges in making an autonomous vessel is the docking proce-

introduces a risk of collision with structures thus requiring precise navigation and

for the autonomous ferry in Limfjorden. The intention is to have the ferry dock and

1.3
Scope
The scope of this project is to design a system that makes autonomous docking possi-
ble as well as reliable. The system must be robust enough to overcome disturbances
from weather.
Making a docking system that works for any ship is of course not feasible. There-
fore this project will be limited to just a single vessel. Initially the plan was to design
a docking system for the passenger ferry in Aalborg. This was however not possible
as the ferry-project is not yet at a stage where the ferry can be modelled. Modelling
the ferry requires extensive knowledge on the dynamics of the vessel. A few exam-
ples are where the center of gravity and center of floatation are located, and how
hydrodynamic forces influence the vessel. Without such knowledge it is not possible
to obtain a good mathematical model of the vessel. This is why it was necessary to
design a docking system for a vessel for which the required knowledge is readily avail-
able. A small twin-hull research vessel called the Otter was deemed a good candidate.
As many parameters of the Otter can be obtain from [PF09]. The otter is, like the
Limfjorden ferry, a twin-hull vessel. Such vessels have two hulls (hence twin-hull) con-
nected by a structure above sea level. Some well know examples of twin-hull vessels
are the Hobie Cat and common high speed ferries.
The vessel that will be used in this project will have the same hull as the Otter, but
will be fitted with azimuth thrusters.
Throughout the rest of this document this
hypothetical vessel will be referred to as the ASV (Autonomous Surface Vessel).

2.2
Assumptions
In order to model the ASV some general assumptions must be made. These are:
The vessel is a rigid body
This assumes that no flexing in the hull will occur. Flexing is mainly seen in large
vessels. Due to the light weight and small size of the ASV flexing will certainly be
negligible.
The earth is flat and not spinning
This is a necessary assumption if the NED-frame is assumed to be a inertial frame.
Earths rotation and curvature mainly effect vessels traveling long distances. The ef-
fect of earths rotation and curvature when sailing 550m across Limfjorden are very
small.
The roll, pitch and heave of the ASV is almost zero at all times
This assumption is a prerequisite for neglecting the dynamics in these modes as it is
done in order to simplify the 6-DOF model to a 3-DOF model.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
5.1
Conclusion
The main goal of this project was to design a automatic docking system for a small
ASV. This was done by using an adaptive backstepping controller, as well as aux-
iliary systems for control allocation, course generation and reference filtering. The
adaptive backstepping controller showed promising robustness to uncertainties in hy-
drodynamic coeпђАicient and weather conditions. The inclusion of the guidance system
together with a reference filter was found to improve the performance of the docking
system. A guidance system was made using a look-ahead guidance scheme. This
system was capable of maneuvering the vessel both forwards while docking and in
reverse when departing. All though the results are promising there is still a long way
to a final autonomous solution. Many problems was found during the project, some
of which has yet to be solved.
5.2
Future Improvements
5.2.1
Estimating the relative velocity ќљr
The main problem with using the relative velocity ќљr in the controller is that this
velocity vector is diпђАicult to estimate. A GPS system will only provide information
on the velocity and position relative to the ground. In order to estimate this velocity,
the vessel will have to be fitted with a sensor called a log, which measures speed
through water. Alternatively an estimate of ќљr could be calculated from an estimate
of ќљ and an estimate of the current.
5.2.2
More actuate model
As discussed in 2.3.5 the correctness of the current model of the hydrodynamic was
found to be questionable. As a future improvement to the overall model, the hydrody-
namic model must be revised. The most outstanding issue is the difference in the step
response of the ASV when actuated only in surge and when actuated only in sway.
Apart from this it would be interesting to investigate performance of the adaptive

5.2.3
Resolving thruster singularity
The current design of the thrusters introduces a singularity. A force in sway only
can be made by a propeller turned to either ќЊ = ѕА

the thrusters.
5.2.4
Including thruster dynamics in the design of the controller
Extending the adaptive position controller to include the dynamics of the thrusters as
in [FB97], could eliminate the need for both a reference filter and control allocation.
Doing this a third step in the adaptive backstepping control design is needed where
