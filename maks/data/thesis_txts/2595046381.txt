Abstract
The use of glass in structural applications has increased in recent years, mainly using
laminated and tempered glass, since they offer a higher resistance to loads. Because
of this, it would make sense to adopt a solution incorporating both (laminated tem-
pered glass). What sometimes gets overlooked is the effect that the failure of one (or
more) of the glass layers can have on the entire structure, since the fragments would
not disperse but remain in the original position thanks to the inter-layer.
This project aims to determine the stiffness of “loosely confined” failed tempered glass.
This is relevant at an industry level for multiple reasons. Particularly, in large pieces
of laminated glass, sometimes neighboring glass fails as a consequence of the failure
of another layer. This could create a cascade effect in the laminated structure, with
progressive failure of the layers.
In order to obtain this solution, the focus is initially aimed at understanding the
behaviour of a single tampered glass fragment. To do this, an analytical model is
established as a basis to describe this behaviour. Successively, a similar FEM model
is created, first in 2D and then in 3D, to have a more realistic set up to describe the
real object.
Once all the important variables have been studied, the interest is to apply these
characteristics to a real case describing the failure of laminated tempered glass. In
the end, a framework will be set into place to theoretically analyse these types of
problems.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Glass
The Oxford English Dictionary [1] defines glass as “a hard, brittle substance, typi-
cally transparent or translucent, made by fusing sand with soda and lime and cooled
rapidly. It is used to make windows, drinking containers, and other articles”. From
this definition, it is immediately possible to distinguish some important character-
istics, which are the reason why this material is becoming increasingly utilised in
constructions.
The glass most often used in the structural field is soda-lime-silica glass, obtained, as
mentioned above, through fusion and cooling without crystallization. This last detail
is characteristic for glass, as it is an amorphous material, i.e. it consists of an irregular
network of silicon and oxygen atoms, with the presence of some alkaline parts.
The glass commonly used for windows and similar applications is also known as an-
nealed glass.
Figure 1.1: Example of glass use in buildings [2].
From an aesthetic standpoint, the use of glass is closely connected to the fact that
it is transparent, therefore permitting a view through the material. The more classic
use of glass has been in windows, but nowadays it has developed as a substitute for
==PAGE:18==
2
1 Introduction
whole sections of a building, such as walls or the roofing, maintaining the original aim
of allowing a view through these sections and increasing the amount of light that can
access the building.
Furthermore, glass has great chemical resistance to a lot of aggressive substances,
which makes it one of the most durable materials in constructions. [3]
One of the biggest downside for the use of glass in structures is the fact that it is
a brittle material. This means that, if the elastic deformation reaches its limit, the
glass will break. This usually corresponds to a relatively small actual deformation
and means that this material does not possess any kind of plastic behaviour.
In order to increase the strength of structural glass, different solutions are adopted,
such as the use of tempered glass or laminated glass.
1.1.1
Tempered Glass
The cause for failure in a glass is mainly connected to the imperfections that this
material presents on its surfaces. Even when a glass surface seems to be perfectly
smooth to the naked eye, it in fact hides some microscopic flaws.
During bending, these cracks are opened due to tensile stress and start growing, until
breakage is reached. Tempering of glass provides a residual stress distribution in the
glass which is tensile in the center of the glass and compressive on the surface. As
long as the tensile stress from bending is lower than the compressive residual stress,
the flaws on the glass surface will not open.
Figure 1.2: Effect of residual stress in tempered glass [3].
==PAGE:19==
1.1 Glass
3
The thermal tempering process, which is the one most commonly used for structural
glass, consists in heating float glass to approximately 620◦C −675◦C and then cooling
rapidly with jets of cold air. This process will cause the surfaces of the glass to cool
faster than the interior. Once the surfaces reach a temperature below the transition
temperature for glass, which is around 525◦C, they will solidify.
Following this, the interior cools. The thermal shrinkage is resisted by the already
solid surfaces, leading to the parabolic residual stress field characteristic of tempered
glass. It should be noted that the residual compressive stress at the surface is twice
as big as the tensile stress at the center of the glass.
1.1.2
Laminated Glass
Another method used to increase the strength of a glass structure is to use lamina-
tion. This technique consists of bonding multiple glass panels through the use of an
interlayer.
Figure 1.3: Example of Laminated Glass [4].
The most commonly used interlayer material is the viscoelastic material polyvinyl
butyral (PVB). The nominal thickness of a PVB layer is 0.38 mm, but it is possible
==PAGE:20==
4
1 Introduction
to combine multiple foils.
The benefit from the use of laminated glass can be seen especially in its post-breakage
behaviour. The presence of the interlayer allows the structure to maintain a certain
structural capacity, which can vary depending on the material used.
1.1.3
Combining Tempered and Laminated Glass
From a theoretical point of view, the use of laminated tempered glass would seem
to offer the best possible conditions since it combines the characteristics previously
described, such as very high resistance to breakage. This kind of combination also
presents some downsides to its use, which can be further increased compared to how
they affect the single elements.
For example, fragments of tempered glass tend to deform. In a laminated tempered
glass structure, the breakage of one glass ply will cause it to expand. Part of this
effect will be transferred through the interlayers to the other glass panels, with a risk
of a cascading failure of the whole structure.
1.2
Finite Element Analysis
The Finite Element Method, in short FEM, is a numerical procedure used in the
solution of engineering problems.
Usually these problems present complicated ge-
ometries, loadings or material properties, which would make it difficult to solve with
classic analytical solutions. [5]
Furthermore, the Finite Element Analysis is usually performed by computers because
of the number of equations that are into play at the same time.
One of the basic concepts connected with FEM is discretization, which allows to
model objects with a series of finite elements. A higher number of elements would
also increase the accuracy of calculations.
All the elements are interconnected through node points.
Figure 1.4: Example of Finite Element discretization of an object (adapted from
[6]).

1.2.2
Non-linear FEM
Linearity in Finite Element problems is usually an approximation.
Instead, real
problems in structural mechanics present non-linear behaviour. This happens when
the stiffness matrix or the load vectors have a dependency on the displacement.
Non-linear problems can mainly be connected to three aspects:
• Material non-linearity
• Geometric non-linearity
• Boundary non-linearity
The first is connected to material properties changing during the displacement, such
as what happens with elasto-plastic or visco-elastic materials.
The second, Geometric non-linearity refers to cases in which the kinematic quantities
do not have a linear relation.
Finally, in this work, we will mainly deal with boundary non-linearity, more specifi-
cally connected to contact. These type of problems are considered as non-linear since
the contact itself changes some of the boundary conditions during the displacement of
the models. To give an example, we imagine a round element between two boundary
elements. when moving one of the boundary elements closer to the other, the force
coming from the middle element will not evolve linearly, instead, it will increase at
a higher rate with the displacements due to a larger contact boundary between the
elements.
The FEM used in this work will partially resemble the example just described.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Through this work, an overview of the behaviour of “loosely confined” failed laminated
tempered glass has been achieved. This understanding was obtained through a study
of the behaviour of single fragments of tempered glass. From this, it was possible to
define some of the characteristics that the confined glass would possess after failure.
Of particular importance is the estimation of the stiffness.
Afterwards, the behaviour of the stiffness was analysed for different settings, such
as a variation in the residual stress of glass or a change in the expected sizes of the
fragments.
The knowledge acquired until this point was then converted into a higher scale, by
looking at a complete glass panel. After understanding the characteristics that were
transferred to the larger model, a simple set up was obtained to determine if the
failure of adjacent glass sheets in a laminated structure. By using this model, it is
also possible to estimate a minimum thickness necessary for the adjacent glasses to
resist breakage, based on the strength of the glass used.
6.1
Further Work
Now that a basis has been set in place for the analysis of laminated tempered glass,
further developments can be made.
Firstly, improvements could be made to the Analytical Model to refine it. An ex-
ample that could get the results even closer to the ones coming from the 2D Finite
Element simulations is to include some friction between the different parts in which
the fragment was divided. This way the element would stop behaving as a series of
small parts, instead move closer to a real element.
Furthermore, since the Analytical Model at the moment is based on a unidimensional
behaviour, it could be interesting to develop it into a 2D analytical model. This would
mean, for example, including the influence the displacement has on the contact area
in both directions.
Looking at the 3D model, the use of squared glass fragments has been a simplifica-
tion of the real behaviour of tempered glass at breakage. Since the shape can vary,
a higher amount of different model could be created. The focus should be on the
hexagonal shape, as it is often considered as the most likely shape for tempered glass
==PAGE:66==

fragments [15].
Since certain theoretical results have been obtained in this paper, a natural step
would be to set up some experimental work. This would allow a comparison between
the theoretical results expected and the real-life case. Alongside this, a study can be
conducted more in-depth for example in how the use of different types of interlayers
might impact the overall behaviour of the structure.
