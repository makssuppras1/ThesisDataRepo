Abstract 
 
Cloudburst management has received massive attention during the last years due to the 
historical rain events in 2011 and 2014, and a number of other extreme rain events. 
Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD) play a major part in order to avoid comprehen-
sive damages caused by the massive water volumes. WSUD is divided into four different 
typologies including green boulevards, retention boulevards, retention spaces and 
cloudburst boulevards This thesis aims to perform an analysis of the MIKE Urban model 
for the water catchment of Bispebjerg, Ryparken and Dyssegård (BRD). The aim of the 
analyze is to identify whether it is possible obtain reliable results for observed dis-
charge for retention boulevards modelled by standard MOUSE elements in MIKE Urban. 
 
Investigations of different established scenarios for the MIKE Urban model for the BRD 
catchment, revealed significant model technical problems when the cloudburst projects 
are modelled by elements which are not designed for the particular purpose. Hence, 
this thesis emphasis the importance of comprehensive analysis, and quality checks of a 
model before it can be used with a satisfying outcome.  
 
Investigations of the obtained water balances for the different established scenarios of 
the MIKE Urban model for the BRD catchment revealed problems with water generated 
in empty parts of the system. The source of the generated water is the designed cross 
section shape (CRS) profiles used for the cloudburst and retention boulevards. The CRS 
profiles are designed with a partially horizontal bottom, which is generating the water 
in the periods where no water is observed in the system. This is due to fact that MIKE 
Urban generates a minor amount of water in the lowest point of the link in order to be 
able to run.  
 
Investigations of three tested modelling methodologies of retention boulevards re-
vealed significant problems with utilization of fictive basin volume during peak flow. 
The problem is observed for modelling methodologies based on an initial loss modelled 
as basins in MIKE Urban. This is a result of the used dhiapp.INI file which extends the 
basin geometry. This result in massive fictive volume above the implemented basins, 
where the water can be stored temporally. The investigations of the three tested mod-
elling methodologies revealed also significant problems if the user defined basin geom-
etry is not defined up to the crest level of the weir. The basin is here not connected 
correctly to the node, resulting in that the weir only is working in one direction. Hence, 
water stored in the basin and fictive basin volume is not led back to the system when 
excess capacity is observed after the peak.  
 
Investigations of the effects of varying extent of retention boulevards within the system 
revealed deviating results for one of the investigated cloudburst projects due to a model 
technical error. The nodes for the investigated retention boulevard were here modelled 
with a standard MOUSE Classic(Engelund) for Outlet head loss with a diameter of one 
meter. Hence, the nodes provide thereby a throttling effect for the modelled retention 
boulevard with a width of 13 meters. The throttling effect neutralize thereby the retain-
ing effect for the modelled retention boulevard.

Dansk sammenfatning 
 
Skybrudssikring har opnået stor opmærksomhed i løbet af de sidste år grundet de hi-
storiske skybrud, der blev observeret tilbage i 2011 og 2014, samt en række af andre 
ekstreme regnhændelser. Lokal Afledning af Regnvand (LAR) indgår her som et af de 
primære redskaber til at undgå de omfattende skader forårsaget af de massive vand-
mængder. LAR er her opdelt i fire forskellige løsningstypologier, herunder grønne veje, 
forsinkelsesveje, forsinkelsespladser og skybrudsveje. Denne afhandling har til formål 
at udføre en analyse af MIKE Urban modellen for vandoplandet, Bispebjerg Ryparken 
og Dyssegård (BRD). Analysen har til formål at undersøge, hvorvidt det er muligt at 
opnå pålidelige resultater for observeret afstrømning fra forsinkelsesveje, der er mo-
delleret med standard MOUSE elementer i MIKE Urban. 
 
Undersøgelser, af forskellige opstillede scenarier af MIKE Urban modellen for BRD op-
landet, afslørede store modeltekniske problemer, når de planlagte skybrudsprojekter 
modelleres med standard MOUSE elementer. Denne afhandling understreger derfor 
vigtigheden af omfattende analyser, og kvalitetskontrol af en model, før den kan anven-
des med et tilfredsstillende resultat. 
 
Undersøgelser, af de opnåede vandbalancer for de opstillede scenarier af MIKE Urban 
modellen for BRD oplandet, afslørede problemer med vand genereret i tomme dele af 
systemet. Kilden til den genererede vand, er de designede tværsnits profiler, som an-
vendes for de implementerede skybruds-og forsinkelsesveje i modellen. Tværsnitspro-
filerne er udformet med en delvist vandret bund, hvor der generes en mindre mængde 
vand i de perioder, hvor der ikke opleves vand i systemet. Dette skyldes, at MIKE Urban 
genererer en mindre mængde vand i det laveste punkt af systemets ledninger for at 
være i stand til at køre sine beregninger. 
 
Undersøgelser af tre testede modelleringsmetoder af forsinkelsesveje afslørede store 
problemer med udnyttelse af fiktivt bassin volumen under peak flow. Problemet obser-
veres for modelleringsmetoder, som er baseret på et initial tab, der modelleres som 
bassiner i MIKE Urban. Dette er et resultat af den anvendte dhiapp.INI fil som forlænger 
bassingeometrien. Dette resulterer i et stort fiktivt bassin volumen over de implemen-
terede bassiner, hvor vandet kan opbevares midlertidigt under peak flow. Undersøgel-
serne af de tre testede modelmetoder afslørede også store problemer, hvis den bruger 
definerede bassingeometri ikke er defineret op til topkoten af overløbet. Bassinet er her 
ikke tilsluttet korrekt til systemknuden, hvilket resulterer i, at overløbet kun fungerer i 
en retning. Derved kan vand lagres i bassinet og i det fiktive bassinvolumen, men kan 
derved ikke ledes tilbage til systemet, når overskydende kapacitet observeres efter 
peak flow. 
 
Undersøgelser af udbredelsen af forsinkelsesveje returnerede afvigende resultater for 
et af de undersøgte skybrudprojekter grundet en modelteknisk fejl. Knuderne for den 
undersøgte forsinkelsesvej blev her modelleret med en standard MOUSE Classic(Enge-
lund) for Oulet head loss med en diameter på en meter. Derfor giver knudepunkterne 
en droslende effekt for den modellerede forsinkelsesvej med en bredde på 13 meter. 
Den droslende effekt neutraliserer hermed den forsinkende effekt af den modellerede 
forsinkelsesvej.

1 Introduction  
Denmark has experienced a number of extreme rain events during the last couple of 
years. Especially the historical extreme rain events back in 2011 and 2014, caused ma-
jor infrastructural problems (Copenhagen City, 2015a). Studies of the climate changes 
indicate that the frequency of extreme rain events will increase in the future (Christoph, 
et al., 2006). When major volumes of rain fall in Copenhagen, the main parts of the city 
are affected by the stormwater flowing across the different parts of the city regardless 
of municipal borders and boundaries between neighborhoods (Copenhagen City, 
2015b). Hence, cloudburst management is an area that needs immediate action in order 
to avoid damage caused by the massive water volumes. Over the next few years, Copen-
hagen is going to be protected against extreme rain events, where Water Sensitive Ur-
ban Design (WSUD) appears to be a central element in the climate adaption process 
(Copenhagen City, 2015a). 
 
As a part of the cloudburst management plan, Copenhagen has been divided into seven 
water catchment areas. Each water catchment is again divided into a certain number of 
cloudburst branches. This thesis investigates the water catchment area of Bispebjerg, 
Ryparken and Dyssegård (BRD) which contain 16 cloudburst branches and 65 cloud-
burst projects. Hydraulic calculations have identified the natural waterways through 
the BRD catchment, which form the basis for the cloudburst branches. Based on the 
natural waterways within the branches a number of project boulevards have been iden-
tified. Cloudburst projects will be implemented along the project boulevards to ensure 
that the stormwater is removed safely from an area (Copenhagen City, 2015b). 
 
The planned cloudburst projects are based on four types of solution typologies: cloud-
burst boulevards, retention boulevards, retention spaces and green boulevards. The 
four types of solution typologies have varying hydraulic characteristics. Green boule-
vards are designed to remove and retain water locally, and will typically be located up-
stream. Cloudburst boulevards are designed to discharge water in an efficient way, and 
will typically be located downstream. Retention boulevards and retention spaces are 
designed to retain and store water. The retention typologies will typically be imple-
mented between the green boulevards and the cloudburst boulevards (Copenhagen 
==PAGE:15==
 
2 
 
City, 2015b). A combination of the four types of WSUD typologies can decrease the pres-
sure in the sewer system during peak flow.  
 
The 65 planned cloudburst projects for the BRD catchment have been implemented in 
an extraction of HOFOR’s status model 2016. The model is for this thesis used to inves-
tigate the improved performance of the already existing system after the implementa-
tion of the planned projects. A hydraulic model is also an important tool in order to 
analyze the effects of the selected modelling methodology of the four types of cloud-
burst typologies. A hydraulic model is likewise an important tool in order to investigate 
how varying extent of the four types of cloudburst typologies is able to affect the ob-
served runoff and discharge processes within the water catchment area. (Copenhagen 
City, 2011). A hydraulic model can also be used for the investigations of the system per-
formance during rain events with varying characteristics, including velocity, size, and 
direction (Seo & Schmidt, 2013). 
 
1.1 Aims and objectives 
Computed discharge is determined by two factors in MIKE Urban, including the charac-
teristics of the used rain input and the configuration of the modelled water catchment 
(Seo & Schmidt, 2013). This thesis aims to perform an analysis of the MIKE Urban model 
for the BRD catchment in order to identify whether it is possible to obtain reliable re-
sults for observed discharge for retention boulevards modelled by standard elements 
in MIKE Urban. The impacts of changes in the configuration of the modelled water 
catchment will be investigated, by testing the BRD model with three different modeling 
methodologies of retention boulevards, and varying extent of deployment. The impacts 
of the characteristics of the used rain input will be investigated by testing the BRD 
model with standard Chicago Design Storm (CDS) and moving rain with different mov-
ing patterns.  The thesis will focus on the following underlying objectives: 
 
 
 
 
Primary focus on how the implementation of retention boulevards is able to 
improve the hydraulic behavior of the already existing system. The properties 
of cloudburst boulevards, green boulevards and retention spaces will be taken 
into consideration in the overall evaluation. 
 
 
Three modelling methodologies of retention boulevards in MIKE Urban will be 
tested. The three modelling methods will include; (1) Cross Section Shape 
(CRS) profiles with overflow to basins without throttling, (2) CRS profiles with 
varying Manning number, and (3) CRS profiles with overflow to basins with 
throttling. 
 
 
Investigation and understanding of the effects of the three modelling methods 
described above, with focus on the hydraulic performance.

 
Test of various values for the Manning number in modelling methodology 2 (15 
– 45 m1/3/s), in order to identify the effects of increasing surface roughness. 
 
 
Testing the rainfall-runoff model with varying extent of retention boulevards – 
in order to identify the importance of the extent of retention boulevards within 
a system. This will be tested with modelling methodology 2.  
 
 
Test the rainfall-runoff model with moving CDS upstream and downstream of 
the catchment with different velocities of 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0 m/s - in order to iden-
tify the effects of the velocity and direction of the moving rain.  
 
 
 
 
This thesis will primarily test the hydraulic performance of the implemented retention 
boulevards within the BRD catchment. Hence, factors such as possible improved water 
quality and recreational values will not be taken into account in the overall evaluation 
of the implemented retention boulevards. Factors such as project economy, legislation 
and operational cost are not a part of the overall scope of this thesis either, and will 
likewise not be taken into account in the overall evaluation of the retention boulevards.

2 Background 
This section is a description of the background for the thesis including an introduction 
to the established Copenhagen climate adaption plan and Copenhagen cloudburst man-
agement plan. Subsequently there will be an introduction to Water Sensitive Urban De-
sign (WSUD). Finally, there will be a description of the characteristics of the specific 
case area, including a description of the planned water tunnel called the Big Y. 
2.1 Rain events in the future 
Studies indicate that the increase in atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations may 
increase the frequency of extreme rain events (Christoph, et al., 2006). Based on this 
fact, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has established a Special 
Report on Emissions Scenarios (SRES) where various greenhouse gas emissions sce-
narios are described (IPCC, 2000). The city of Copenhagen has used IPCC’s A2 scenario 
as the basis for assessing the future climate impacts. This scenario assumes that the 
temperature will increase with 3 degrees during the 21st century (Copenhagen City, 
2011). 
 
The Meteorological Institute of Denmark (DMI) predicts that the effects of climate sce-
nario A2 will cause changes of the observed rain events. It is predicted that scenario A2 
will cause an increase in the amount of rain during the winter months with 25 - 55% in 
2100, while the amount of rain during the summer months is expected to decrease with 
0 - 40%. The intensity of the observed rain events is expected increase (Copenhagen 
City, 2011). 
 
The intensity of the extreme rain events is expected to increase with 20 - 50%, where 
the intensity of the most frequent incidents will increase the most, and the intensity of 
the least frequent incidents will increase the least. Changes in the observed rain events 
have a significant impact on the runoff generation and the load on the sewer system and 
streams. The intensity of the rain with a return period of 10 years will increase with 
30% in 2100 according to climate scenario A2. This means that sewer systems must be 
==PAGE:18==
 
5 
 
able to handle 30% extra during maximum situations. That is, if the sewer system is 
going to fulfill the same requirements and the same function as today (Copenhagen City, 
2011). 
 
The intensity of a 100-year rain is expected to increase with 40% in 2100 according to 
climate scenario A2. This means that the amount of runoff will increase significantly in 
2100 compared to today. The increase in intensity means that rain with a certain inten-
sity will occur more frequently in the future. For example, a 1-hour rain, which today 
occurs once every 50 years, will occur once every 10 years in 2110 (Copenhagen City, 
2011). 
2.2 Climate adaption plans in Denmark 
The municipality of Copenhagen has established a climate change adaption plan accord-
ing to the agreement between the Local Government of Denmark and the Government 
in 2012 (Klimatilpasning, 2015). The aim of the plan is to adapt the expected changes 
in the future climate. The level of climate adaption depends on various factors, such as 
distance to the ocean, main type of land use and amount of infrastructure (Copenhagen 
City, 2011) Topics of the climate change adaption plans of Copenhagen will be pre-
sented in the following sections. 
2.2.1 
Copenhagen climate adaption plan 
The city of Copenhagen established a climate chance adaption plan in 2011 in order to 
adapt to the changes in the climate. The plan defines the various challenges Copenhagen 
will face in the future. Furthermore, it specifies solutions that seem appropriate for the 
various areas in Copenhagen based on current knowledge. The main objective of the 
plan is first of all to prevent bad investments and to obtain synergy between the climate 
adaption and other ongoing urban planning projects. The planned adaption solutions 
should furthermore be flexible in relation to changes in the expectations for the future 
climate (Copenhagen City, 2011).  
 
In 2009, five initiatives were defined as essential for the further work with the climate 
adaptation of Copenhagen. Including, (1) Development of green and blue methods to 
discharge stormwater during extreme rain events, (2) Establishment of green solutions 
in order to reduce flood risk, (3) Increased use of passive cooling systems of buildings, 
(4) Coastal protection, and (5) Develop a comprehensive adaption strategy. It is not 
possible to ensure Copenhagen completely against climatic related accidents, either 
technically nor economically.  However, there may be a number of cloudburst projects 
implemented in order to prevent damages from the major volumes of rain fall on Co-
penhagen or to reduce the extent of the damages. There is a major difference between 
the costs of flooding of a historical building and an apartment building. This difference 
must be considered so that the buildings with highest historical and societal value have 
the highest priority (Copenhagen City, 2011).  
 
==PAGE:19==
 
6 
 
The municipality of Copenhagen has established a plan consisting of three levels. The 
first level aims to reduce the likelihood of flooding or to prevent it completely. The first 
level includes the establishment of dikes, local management of stormwater and local 
adaption of the sewer capacity. If actions at level one are implemented efficiently, ac-
tions on level two and three will not be conducted. The aim of the second level is to 
reduce the extent of the flooding. The second level includes alert systems of extreme 
rain events, establishment of waterproof basements, and climate adaption of public ar-
eas. If actions at level two are implemented efficiently, actions at level three will not be 
conducted. The aim of the third level is to reduce the vulnerability to flooding by per-
forming actions that makes is cheaper and easier to repair the damages after flooding. 
(Copenhagen City, 2011). 
2.2.2 
Copenhagen cloudburst management plan 
Based on the climate adaption plan, the city of Copenhagen established a cloudburst 
management plan in 2012. The plan recommends that Copenhagen must be protected 
to a service level, where the city only experiences flooding from a rain event with a re-
turn period of 100 years. This service level means that private persons in the worst-
case scenario are going to accept a water level of 10 cm at the boundary between public 
and private areas. Cloudburst boulevards are not covered by this service level. This 
means that some areas of the boulevards are going to experience a water level above 
10 cm. For cloudburst boulevards, the service level is replaced by a principle, describing 
that a cloudburst project must not result in an increased level of risk of damage 
(Copenhagen City, 2012). 
 
Two types of solutions are taken into account when problems with flooding are consid-
ered. The first type of solution includes decoupling of stormwater, either by separate 
sewer systems or by WSUD, see Section 2.3 for further information about WSUD. The 
plan consists of 300 WSUD projects that include surface and pipe solutions. The surface 
solutions will contribute to the green and blue environment in the city. The second type 
of solution is also known as “Plan B” where stormwater is led towards areas where it 
does not have any or little effect. (Copenhagen City, 2011). 
 
It is not possible to implement all of the projects at the same time. Instead, the Cloud-
burst management plan has a time horizon of at least 20 years. Hence, there is a need 
for a prioritization (Copenhagen City, 2011), which can be seen from the following four 
points: 
 
 
1. High risk areas 
As a part of the Climate Adaption Plan a risk assessment has been conducted. The 
results are presented by risk maps, indicating where the flood risk is highest (in 
DKK), hence where cloudburst adaption has the greatest impact.  
 
2. Areas where solutions are easy to implement 
Areas, where stormwater easily can be led to places where it does not make any 
damage. These are areas close to the harbor or lakes at a certain size. Projects that 
==PAGE:20==
 
7 
 
protect these areas against flooding have been conducted since 2012. The projects 
include openings in the quayside where the stormwater easily can be led to the sea. 
 
3. Areas with already ongoing projects 
The cost connected to WSUD projects can usually be reduced, if the projects are 
conducted in connection with other renovation and construction projects. 
 
4. Areas with synergy effect  
It is possible to obtain synergy effects by combining WSUD projects with other ini-
tiatives like the water plans for a municipality.  
 
 
 
Based on the four points above, different areas within the BRD catchment have been 
selected for an early implementation of cloudburst projects. For example, areas with 
already planned urban renewal projects, and the new bus lane at Tagensvej which is 
planned to be a part of a larger cloudburst project (Ziersen, 2017).  
 
2.3 Water Sensitive Urban Design 
In the natural water cycle, rainwater will evaporate to the atmosphere, be absorbed by 
vegetation or infiltrate into the ground (Melbourne Water, 2017). The urban develop-
ment has changed these processes dramatically by an increased amount of impervious 
areas. The amount of generated runoff has thereby increased significantly during the 
urbanization. This has changed the timing, velocity and volume of the discharge (Paul 
& Meyer, 2001). WSUD represent a green technology within the cloudburst manage-
ment that aims to decrease the amount of runoff from urbanized areas by imitating the 
natural water cycle (Melbourne Water, 2016). WSUDs have various objectives includ-
ing (Fletcher, et al., 2014):  
 
 
 
Manage the water cycle in a safe and effective way, with focus stream flow 
and groundwater, together with waterway erosion and flooding. 
 
 
Reduce the runoff and peak ﬂow from urban areas by implementing local re-
tention elements and minimizing the amount of impervious areas.  
 
 
Maintain and, if it is possible, improve the quality of the stormwater, consid-
ering sedimentation, protection of surrounding vegetation, and minimization 
of pollutants transportation towards surface – and groundwater. 
 
 
Encourage water conservation and minimizing the import of potable water 
supply, through the harvesting of stormwater and the recycling of 
wastewater, and reductions in irrigation requirements.

3 Theory 
This section is a description of the theory used for the thesis including a theoretical 
description of to the two tested rain inputs for the established rainfall-runoff models; 
here standard Chicago Design Storms (CDS) and rain with different moving patterns. 
Afterwards there will be a description of how the natural runoff processes can be af-
fected by urbanization, and WSUD. Subsequently, there will a description of the rainfall-
runoff model used for the investigations of the effects of moving rain and changes in the 
physiographic properties of the investigated catchment. Finally, there will be a descrip-
tion of the three investigated cloudburst projects, here BIR5.4, BIR8.2 and BIR7.4. 
3.1 Rain   
The shape of the generated hydrographs in the stablished rainfall-runoff models is 
partly determined by the characteristics of the used rain series. The following sections 
will describe two types of rain inputs, including standard CDS and moving rain. 
3.1.1 CDS 
CDS are commonly used as rain input for rainfall-runoff models in order to investigate 
the performance of the sewer system (Winther, et al., 2011). The shape of a CDS can be 
seen from Figure 3.1.2. A CDS is based on an intensity-duration-frequency (IDF) curve, 
which is based of statistical analysis of single independent rain events. The independ-
ency is normally defined as a minimum time between the rain events. For each rain 
event, the maximum rain volumes for different durations are evaluated. The observed 
volumes from rain events with a certain duration are ranked, and the statistical distri-
bution function is thereafter evaluated. This results in a function for each investigated 
duration. Curves of the average rain intensity can then be drawn as a function of the 
duration. This is the IDF curves, see Figure 3.1.1. Each curve is based on data from dif-
ferent rain event as the different durations are treated separately. Hence, design storms 
based on IDF curves contain data from different real rain events (Arnell, 1978).  
 
==PAGE:24==
 
11 
 
 
 
Figure 3.1.1 Obtained intensity-duration-frequency (IDF) curves for Göteborg (Sweden) 1929 – 
1971 (Arnell, 1978) 
 
Design storms developed from a IDF curves are characterized by the fact that the aver-
age intensities for various durations follow an IDF curve. The most frequent way of de-
veloping a design storm is to assume that the peak intensity is observed in the middle 
of the rain event, and then distribute the rest of the rain symmetrically on each side of 
the peak, see Figure 3.1.2 (Arnell, 1978).  The duration of the used CDS must be selected 
based on the time it takes the stormwater to flow from the most distant point of the 
catchment to the system outlet. This is also referred to as time of concentration, tc. This 
is done to ensure that the sewer system can handle a CDS, when the entire catchment 
contributes at same time (Butler & Davies, 2011).

3.1.2 Moving rain 
Rain series with different moving patterns may result in significant variations in the 
hydrological response of the rainfall-runoff model. Variations may also be observed 
from rain events with the same amount of rain during a specific period (Seo & Schmidt, 
2013). The effects of moving rain events are expected to be more pronounced as the 
area of the catchment increases (Seo & Schmidt, 2013). The BRD catchment covers an 
area of 29.1 km2 (Copenhagen City, 2013), hence moving rain must be taken into ac-
count, when the system performance is evaluated. Previously performed studies of 
moving rain indicate an interval in which the same rainfall duration returns a higher 
peak response for a moving rain compared with CDS. The increased peak response from 
a moving rain is among others dependent on the relative rainstorm velocity, size, and 
direction (Seo & Schmidt, 2013). This thesis focuses on the effects of the velocity and 
the direction of the moving storm. A downstream moving rain with a velocity corre-
sponding to the average velocity of the sewer system is considered as worst-case-sce-
nario, as the peak of the rain will then occur at the same time as the peak flow for the 
sewer system (Danielsen Sørup, 2016). 
3.2 Surface runoff 
The shape of a generated hydrograph is, besides the characteristics of the rain, deter-
mined by the configuration of the water catchment. This section will describe the hy-
drological processes connected to the transformation of stormwater to a generated hy-
drograph.  This section will furthermore describe how the hydrological processes can 
be affected by urbanization and the implementation of WSUD.  
3.2.1 
Natural surface runoff processes 
The natural water cycle will be described in the following sections. The natural water 
cycle includes humidification, storage in depressions and interceptions, infiltration, and 
evaporation. Rain is termed as stormwater as soon as the rain hits the ground surface. 
The essential hydrological processes when stormwater is converted into rainfall runoff 
is illustrated by Figure 3.2.1. 
 
Initial loss 
Humidification, storage in depressions and interception results in an overall loss. This 
loss is typically referred to as the initial loss for the system.  Hence this loss occurs in 
the beginning of the rain event, see Figure 3.2.2. The variation of this loss is between 
0.5 mm and 5 mm. The initial loss is lowest at roofs and newly laid asphalt surfaces 
without depressions. The initial loss is highest at horizontal surfaces in park areas 
(Winther, et al., 2011) 
==PAGE:26==
 
13 
 
 
 
Figure 3.2.1  Essential hydrological processes when stormwater is converted into rainfall runoﬀ 
(Hesselberg Foged, et al., 2016) 
 
 
Infiltration 
Infiltration represents the process of rainfall passing through the ground surface into 
the pores of the soil. The infiltration capacity of a soil is defined as the rate at which 
water infiltrates into it. The magnitude depends on factors including soil type, structure 
and compaction, initial moisture content and surface cover. The infiltration rate tends 
to be high initially, but decreases exponentially to a final quasi-steady rate when the 
upper soil zone becomes saturated (Winther, et al., 2011), see Figure 3.2.2. 
 
Evaporation 
The evaporation has a magnitude of 0.1 µm s-1 during a single rain event (Winther, et 
al., 2011). This thesis will only simulate CDS rains with a duration of four hours giving 
an effective evaporation 1.44 mm, hence evaporation will not be included, or com-
mented further. 
 
Overland flow 
The effective intensity is what remains when the combined lost intensity is subtracted 
from the rain intensity, see Figure 3.2.2. The effective intensity is then converted into a 
hydrograph by a hydraulic process called surface routing, or overland flow. This pro-
cess includes a movement of the runoff across the surface of a sub-catchment towards 
the nearest manhole of the sewer system (Butler & Davies, 2011).  
 
==PAGE:27==
 
14 
 
This process is typically represented by a time-area-method, or a kinematic wave equa-
tion in computation programs. This established rainfall-runoff model for the BRD catch-
ment uses the time-area-method, as described in Section 3.4.1. The network model is 
normally more detailed compared to the surface model. This is because it is more im-
portant to determine the runoff volume from a certain rain event than determining the 
hydraulic processes. It is furthermore impossible to determine the geometrical catch-
ment parameters for all the sub-catchments with an accuracy justifying the use of a de-
tailed rainfall-runoff model (Winther, et al., 2011). 
 
The geometry of the connected sub-catchment has a significant impact on the shape of 
the hydrograph. It is characteristic that the effective intensity is negative in the period 
after the rain event and until the surface runoff ends. This is a result of infiltration and 
evaporation from the water membrane that is formed during the rain event (Winther, 
et al., 2011).

(Winther, et al., 2011) 
3.2.2 
Surface runoff after urbanization 
The increase in impervious areas leads to greater volumes of generated surface runoff 
due to the limited possibility of infiltration, see Section 3.2 for further information. 
When the amount of impervious surface cover (ISC) increases with 10 – 20%, the 
amount of runoff increases to the double, and when ISC increase with 35 – 50% the 
amount of runoff increases threefold, and when ISC increase with 75 – 100% the 
amount of surface runoff increases more than fivefold, this is illustrated by Figure 3.2.3 
(Paul & Meyer, 2001). The effective intensity is thereby greater in urbanized areas, as a 
result of decreased initial loss and infiltration loss, see Figure 3.2.2 
 
==PAGE:28==
 
15 
 
 
 
Figure 3.2.3 Illustration of the changes in the hydrologic process by increasing impervious areas 
in urbanized areas (Paul & Meyer, 2001) 
 
Surface runoff has a higher velocity in urbanized areas than it has in unurbanized areas, 
hence the lag time between the center of the rain event and the center of the runoff 
hydrograph is shortened in urbanized areas compared to unurbanized areas, see Figure 
3.2.4. The decreased lag time may result in higher peak discharge. The flood discharge 
from day-to-day events are effected more by urbanization compared to flood discharge 
from extreme events. This is due to the fact, that an unurbanized area acts as an imper-
vious area during extreme rain events, due to increased soil moisture (Paul & Meyer, 
2001).

3.2.3 
WSUD effects on surface runoff 
WSUD aims to decrease, and delay the peak of runoff from urbanized areas by imitating 
the natural water cycle (Melbourne Water, 2017). WSUDs can be diverted into three 
groups of WSUD typologies with various hydraulic characteristics. The three types of 
WSUD typologies are normally implemented by a standard WSUD strategy where: the 
first group is designed to infiltrate and retain water locally, these will typically be lo-
cated upstream within the cloudburst branch. The second group is designed to dis-
charge water in an efficient way, this group will typically be located downstream. The 
third group is designed to retain and store water temporarily. This group will typically 
be implemented between the infiltrating and discharging elements. A combination of 
the three types of WSUD typologies can thereby decrease the pressure in the sewer sys-
tem during peak flow (Ziersen, 2017). Examples of the three types of WSUD typologies 
are seen from Table 3.2.1. It is furthermore seen from Table 3.2.1 that the WSUD ele-
ments go from green to grey down through the WSUD strategy.

This thesis focuses on retention boulevards as cloudburst typology. A retention boule-
vard is defined as a boulevard with retaining and storing elements. This is obtained by 
implementing retention elements into the boulevards. A retaining element could be a 
rain garden, green swales, or similar elements (Copenhagen City, 2015a) This thesis 
will not focus on the specific implementation methodology of a retention boulevards, 
but how a retention boulevard can affect the configuration of the water catchment. 
 
A retention boulevard aims to fulfill all five objectives described in 2.3. This thesis will 
mainly focus on how a retention boulevard is able to handle the water balance in an 
effective and safe way, with focus on flooding, and how a retention boulevard is able to 
reduce the runoff and peak ﬂow from urban areas. A retention boulevard may further-
more delay the peak of the hydrograph. The hydraulic function of a retention boulevard 
is not to remove stormwater, hence the same amount of water must approximately pass 
==PAGE:30==
 
17 
 
through the retention boulevard over time (Copenhagen City, 2015a). However, a mi-
nor part of the stormwater may be stored in the green elements. A decrease and a delay 
of the peak can decrease the dimensioning of the planned pipe solutions within the sys-
tem, which is preferable in an economical perspective (Ziersen, 2017). The decreased 
and delayed peak may result in a longer tail of the hydrograph, which may be a disad-
vantage for the treatment plant downstream. A longer tail of the hydrograph will de-
crease the efficiency of biological processes at the treatment plant (Breinholt, 2017). 
An inversely proportional tendency between the extent of retention boulevards and ob-
served max discharge is expected to be observed (Ziersen, 2017). 
 
The retention of stormwater is the greatest hydrological service that retention boule-
vards provide. Depending on the selected implementation methodology of the retention 
boulevard, much of the stormwater may be captured in depression or vegetation of the 
rain garden or green swales and eventually evaporates or is released back to the sewer 
system, when excess capacity is observed (Ziersen, 2017). Water is allowed to be stored 
in basins and depressions for 24 hours according to the agreed service (Gabriel, 2015).  
Hence, WSUD may work as an increased initial loss of the system. The effective intensity 
may thereby be decreased by the introduction of retention boulevards, see Section 
3.2.1.  
 
An increased surface roughness of a retention boulevard may also affect the runoff 
characteristics. An increased roughness of the surface decreases the flow velocity, and 
will thereby delay the observed runoff from the sub-catchment (Paul & Meyer, 2001). 
Hence, the lag time between the center of the rain event and the center of the runoff 
hydrograph may be increased. Retention boulevards based on an increased surface 
roughness may thereby improve the hydraulic performance of the system. Retention 
boulevards effect on the shape of a generated hydrograph are illustrated by Figure

3.4 Rainfall-runoff models 
A rainfall-runoff model is established for the BRD catchment in MIKE Urban in order to 
investigate the effects of the planed cloudburst projects. The BRD model is established 
for decision-making process purposes. However, it is important to consider that the ob-
tained results from a MIKE Urban models, rarely give an exact picture of the real system.  
This is a result of uncertainties, and errors, introduced in the modelling process. Hence, 
it is thereby important to evaluate the obtained results critically (Warmink, et al., 
2010). 
 
A MIKE Urban model is a rainfall-runoff model which is diverted into two independent 
simulations, including a runoff model which output woks as input for the network 
model. The principle of the surface model is to convert a rain into inflows for the nodes 
(manholes) for the sub-catchments in the system. The network model computes the 
flow in the system of pipes using the inflows from the various nodes as load for the 
network.  
==PAGE:34==
 
21 
 
3.4.1 
MIKE URBAN - runoff models 
MIKE Urban provides four different runoff models, including; Model A (Time/area 
method), Model B (Non-linear Reservoir method), Model C (Linear Reservoir method), 
and UHM (Unit Hydrograph model). Model C has two sub-variants, including; Model C1 
(Dutch runoff model) and Model C2 (French runoff model). The four types of models 
may result in different simulated runoff for the same catchment in subsequent setups. 
This is due to that each model uses different sets of data and parameters, as well as 
different concepts for runoff computation (DHI, 2016a).  
 
Model A is used as runoff model for the BRD model. The surface runoff is computed by 
the “Time-Area” method in MOUSE Runoff Model. The amount of generated runoff is 
determined by the initial loss, a continuous hydrological loss and the size of the con-
tributing area (sub-catchment). The shape of the generated hydrograph is among other 
determined by the concentration time, tc and the time-area curve. These parameters 
represent and describe the reaction speed of the catchment and the shape of the catch-
ment (DHI, 2016a). The full theory and computation scheme of the runoff model is be-
yond the scope of this thesis, for further information see (DHI, 2016a). 
3.4.2 
MIKE URBAN - network model 
When the runoff from sub-catchments is computed, the runoff is assigned to a node 
within the network in order to connect the generated runoff to the network. MIKE UR-
BAN provides three ways to compute the pipe flow, including the dynamic wave ap-
proach, the diffusive wave approach and the kinematic wave approach.  
 
 
The dynamic wave approach uses the full momentum equation, which includes 
forces of acceleration. The dynamic wave approach allows thereby correct sim-
ulations of backflow and fast transients. The dynamic wav approach must be 
used when change in inertia of the water volumes is important over time and 
space. This is the when the observed bed slope is small and the forces of the 
bed resistance are considered to be small (DHI, 2016b).  
 
 
The diffusive wave approach uses the force of gravity, bed friction, and the 
terms of hydrostatic gradient for the momentum equation. The diffusive wave 
approach does not take the terms of inertia into account and is thereby appro-
priate for analyses of backflow in cases where the forces of wall resistance and 
link bed and dominate (DHI, 2016b).   
 
 
The kinematic wave approach calculates the flow on the assumption of a bal-
ance between the forces of gravity and friction. Hence, the kinematic wave ap-
proach is not able to simulate effects from backflow(DHI, 2016b). 
 
The dynamic wave description is recommended to be used in all cases except where it 
can be shown that either the diffusive or kinematic descriptions are adequate. Hence 
the dynamic wave approach is used for this thesis. The diffusive and kinematic wave 
==PAGE:35==
 
22 
 
approximations are simplifications of the full dynamic descriptions. They are imple-
mented to offer improved computational efficiency. The overall theory and computa-
tion scheme of the network model are not a part of the overall scope of this thesis, for 
further information see (DHI, 2016b).

4.1.1 
CDS   
A CDS with a return period of 100 years and a safety factor of 1.4 is used as rain input 
for the established models. The safety factor of 1.4 is based on IPCC’s A2 scenario, see 
Section 2.1. The synthetic CDS are generated with the Excel spreadsheet Regional CDS 
Ver_4 0_ENG (Gregersen, et al., 2014). Based on the coordinates of the location, the 
yearly average amount of rain is set to 665 mm and the climate grid average value is set 
to 25.5 mm/day. The time step value is set to 1 minute, and the asymmetry coefficient 
is set to 0.5. Some of the models were not able to run with a CDS with a time step value 
of 1 minutes due to the observed peak of 90.1 µm/s. A CDS is therefore generated with 
a time step length of 5 minutes, in order to decrease the peak intensity. The peak inten-
sity is observed to be 66.3 µm/s for the CDS with a time step value of 5 minutes. The 
graphs of the generated CDSes are available in Appendix B, and the generation of the 
time series (dfs0.file) is described in detail in Appendix C.

4.1.2 
Moving rain 
This section is a step-by-step description, how to implement a moving rain in MIKE Ur-
ban. The moving rain is for this thesis represented as a CDS with different movement 
patterns. The CDS will be moving upstream and downstream along the catchment with 
different velocities Three velocities of the CDS are tested, including 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0 m/s. 
The average flow velocity of the system is assumed to be 1.0 m/s. This assumption is 
based on investigations of the flow velocity in Lersøledningen. Hence a downstream 
moving rain with a velocity of 1.0 m/s is assumed to be worst case scenario for the sys-
tem, see Section 3.1.2 for further information about the expected effects of the velocity 
of the moving rain. The velocity of 0.5 m/s are thereby representing a situation where 
the CDS is moving with a velocity corresponding to the half of the worst-case velocity. 
And the velocity of 2.0 m/s are representing a situation where the CDS is moving with 
a velocity corresponding to the double of the worst-case velocity. The three velocities 
are tested in order to identify how the system performance is affected by moving rain 
with different velocities. The velocity of the moving rain is determined by two factors 
in MIKE Urban; including the distance between the rain gauges and the delay of the 
generated rain series.  
 
Initially, a straight line of rain gauges is implemented in the model. The line is located 
along the south branch of the water tunnel (the ‘Big Y’), and is illustrated as the yellow 
dashed line at Figure 4.1.1 for illustration. The rain gauges are located with constant 
distance in order to simulate a constant velocity of the CDS. The specific number of rain 
gauges depends on the velocity of the tested moving rain.  
 
The CDS, used for the moving rain, are generated with a time step value of 1 minute (see 
Section 4.1.1), hence the CDS can be delayed with 1, 2, 3…. n minutes. In order simulate 
a moving rain with a velocity of 1.0 m/s, the rain gauges are located with a constant 
distance of 60 meters, and the generated series are delayed with n+1 minutes. The rain 
gauges are located with a constant distance of 120 m and the rain gauges are delayed 
with n+1 minutes in order to obtain a velocity 0.5 m/s. To obtain a velocity of 2.0 m/s 
the rain gauges are located with a constant distance of 60 meters, and the rain series 
are delayed with n+2 minutes.

changes in the network model. The retention boulevards are implemented by well-
known components in MIKE Urban such as basins and links with a cross section shape 
(CRS) profile. It must be noted that these components are not designed in order to il-
lustrate cloudburst typologies. Hence, unexpected complications are expected in the 
modelling process.  
 
The model is established on the assumption, that stormwater will only be observed at 
ground level, when the capacity of the existing sewer system is exceeded. Hence, the 
generated runoff from the sub-catchments is assigned to one of the nodes within the 
original network model.  When the capacity of the system is exceeded, water is lead the 
to the implemented cloudburst projects. This sections is a conceptual description of the 
three investigated modelling methodologies of retention boulevards.   
4.2.1 
Methodology 1 – CRS profiles with overflow to basin without throttling 
Retention boulevards are here modelled as cloudburst boulevards with overflow to ba-
sins. Hence, the retaining element for modelling methodology 1 is based on an initial 
loss. The cloudburst boulevards are modelled in MIKE Urban as links with an open CRS 
profile under pipes and channels. The CRS profiles are defined under CRS, and the CRS 
type is set as X-Z open. All CRS profiles are designed with sidewalks of 1.5 meters on 
each side of the boulevard. The sidewalks are elevated 20 cm from the boulevard, and 
the middle of the boulevard elevated 25 cm from the bottom of the curbside, see Figure 
4.2.2 for a conceptual illustration of the CRS profile. The width of the CRS profiles de-
pends on the specific retention boulevard. The cloudburst boulevards are modelled 
with a Manning number at 45 m1/3/s, as a Manning number of 45 m1/3/s is assumed to 
simulate a normal surface with no retention (Ziersen, 2017). The Manning number for 
the cloudburst boulevards is specified under Materials as material type ‘Skybrudsvej’. 
In this way, it is possible to refer to this specific material type every time a cloudburst 
boulevard is modelled.  
 
The overflow from the cloudburst to the basin occurs by an implemented weir. MOUSE 
provides two different methods for the computation of free overflows for weirs. This 
model uses the flow computation based on a standard rectangular overflow weir for-
mula with user-specified discharge coefficient, which is set to default value of 0.403 for 
all weirs within the model (DHI, 2016b). The standard overflow formula for a rectan-
gular notch is described by Equation 3.

4.3.1 BRD model 
Model BRDmodel_PLAN_edit_v2 is a numerical rainfall-runoff model (MIKE 1D) cover-
ing the BRD catchment. The model receives water from the water catchment area of 
Copenhagen west and Frederiksberg west, Ladegårdsåen and Fredriksberg east and 
Vesterbro, Nørrebro, Østerbro, Inner City. The model also receives water from the mu-
nicipality of Gentofte and Gladsaxe. An overview of the structure of Model BRD-
model_PLAN_ edit_v2 can be seen from Table 4.3.1. And an illustration of the MIKE 
model of the BRD catchment can be seen from Figure 4.3.1 
 
The retention boulevards are implemented in the model by modelling methodology 1, 
see Section 4.2.1. The model contains furthermore a number of fictive cloudburst boule-
vards. A fictive cloudburst boulevard is one that naturally carries stormwater towards 
retention spaces or a pipe solutions. A fictive cloudburst boulevard is modelled as a 
general cloudburst boulevard described in Section 4.2.1.  
 
The model is set up for a 100-year rain, this is done by increasing the reduction to 1.0, 
illustrating that all areas of the catchment is contributing to the generated runoff. Be-
fore starting runoff computation, the runoff model type is set to Time/Area curve, and 
the fixed time step is set to 60 seconds under Runoff Parameters. When the runoff com-
putation is successfully ended, the runoff computation is used as network load input. 
The runoff computation is run with Dynamic wave as model type, and the minimum is 
set to 1 second under Network Parameters in order to manage possible instability of 
the model due to the size and complexity of the model. All network simulations for the 
BRD model are run for 24 hours in order to investigate the full length of the tail of the 
obtained hydrographs.

4.5.1 Calibration process for the test model 
A set of calibration criterions are established for the ‘trial-and-error’ process described 
in Section 4.3.2. The calibration criterions will indicate how precise the test model 
(TESTmodel_PLAN_edit_v2) reflects the hydraulic dynamics observed in the full BRD 
model (BRDmodel_PLAN_edit_v2).  
 
Quantitative and qualitative criterions are specified for the calibration process. The 
quantitative criterions include an assessment of the average error and the spread of the 
residuals. The average error must be close to zero, and can be determined by Mean Er-
ror or Mean Absolute Error. The spread of the residuals is determined by the uncer-
tainty in the observation data. The qualitative criterions are given by following; (1) The 
obtained values from the test model must be realistic, (2) The residuals must be dis-
tributed in a reasonable way in time and place, (3) The observed hydraulic dynamics 
from the full BRD model must be reflected by the test model.

model (BRDmodel_PLAN_edit_v2) ψBRD, and the results from the test model (TEST-

5 Results and discussion 
Obtained results for the scenarios of the two base models described in Section 4.4 will 
be analyzed and discussed in the following sections. The obtained results will be inves-
tigated at local and regional scale in order to investigate the effects of changes in the 
configuration of the water catchment, and variations in the tested rain input. The three 
tested modelling methodologies, and the effects of varying Manning number for model-

retention boulevards will be investigated both at local and regional scale. Finally, the

pected complications, and model technical errors, the analysis and discussion will pri-
marily focus on the observed pitfalls when cloudburst typologies are modelled with el-
ements which are not designed for this purpose. The unexpected complications with 
the model were revealed as a continuous process. Hence, some obtained results will be 
discussed as false positive in the preliminary investigations.

6 Conclusion 
Based on the performed investigations of the established scenarios for the MIKE Urban 
model of the BRD catchment, this thesis concludes that unexpected model technical er-
rors within the model affects the obtained results in a way where it is difficult to answer 
the stated sub-objectives for the thesis. However, some consistent tendencies are ob-
served for the obtained results, where: 
 
 
Investigations of varying Manning number for modelling methodology 2 indi-
cate that no significant changes are observed for the obtained hydrograph by 
changing the selected Manning number. The highest observed reduction for 
max discharge is observed for project BIR8.2, where the max discharge is de-
creased with 16% and the time of observed discharge is delayed with five 
minutes. These results are obtained when the retention boulevard is modelled 
with a Manning number of 15 m1/3/s compared to 45 m1/3/s showing no reten-
tion.  
 
 
Investigations of varying extent of retention boulevards indicated an inversely 
proportional tendency between the extent of retention boulevards and ob-
served max discharge at local scale. The highest observed reduction for max 
discharge is observed for project BIR8.2, when all project boulevards are mod-
elled as retention boulevards. The max discharge is here decreased with 
56.14% and the time of observed discharge is delayed with 17 minutes. It is 
thereby concluded that the extent of retention boulevards has the highest im-
pacts of the system performance at regional scale. 
 
 
Investigations of the effects of varying extent of retention boulevards at re-
gional scale indicated no significant improvements of the hydraulic perfor-
mance, even when all project boulevards are modelled as retention boulevards.  
 
 
Investigations of the moving rain revealed that the velocity of a rain moving 
upstream has a significant impact on the time where the backflow dominates 
the system, as a result of an increased lag time before the upstream system is 
contributing at a level where the direction of the link discharge turns positive. 
Investigations of a rain moving upstream indicates an interval in which the 
same rainfall duration returns a lower peak response for a moving rain com-
pared with a standard CDS. The highest observed max discharge is occurring 
for a moving rain downstream with a velocity of 1.0 m/s. corresponding to the 
assumed average velocity of the system. The investigations of rain moving 
downstream indicate, in contrary, an interval in which the same rainfall dura-
tion returns a higher peak response compared to a standard CDS. It is thereby 
concluded that variations in the velocity and direction of the moving rain have 
the highest impacts of the system performance at regional scale. 
 
==PAGE:78==
 
65 
 
This thesis concludes that the implementation of cloudburst typologies in MIKE Urban 
by standard MOUSE elements is a working area with many challenges and opportuni-
ties for improvement. The unexpected model technical errors were revealed as a con-
tinuous process, where a technical error was revealed by one performed investigation 
but not by another. This means that obtained results from a MIKE Urban model may be 
analyzed false-positive. This emphasizes the importance of comprehensive analysis and 
quality checks of a model before it can be used with a satisfying outcome. This thesis 
identified four sources of technical errors which affect the obtained results in a signifi-
cant way, where: 
 
 
Investigations of the water balances for the established test model revealed 
problems with water generated in empty parts of the system. The CRS profiles 
for the cloudburst and retention boulevards are designed with a partially hor-
izontal bottom, which is generating the water due to fact that MIKE Urban gen-
erates a minor amount of water in the lowest point of the link, when no water 
is observed, in order to be able to run. The problem can be avoided by design-
ing the CRS profile with a small fictive ditch in the bottom. By introducing the 
new CRS profiles for the established test model, it was possible to reduce the 
generation of water in empty parts with 88.1%. The same problem is also iden-
tified for the full BRD model, but is less visible for this model due to the size of 
the handled water volumes.  
 
 
Investigations of the effects of varying extent of retention boulevards within 
the system revealed deviating results for project BIR5.4 due to a model tech-
nical error. The nodes for the investigated retention boulevard were here mod-
elled with a standard MOUSE Classic(Engelund) for Outlet head loss with a di-
ameter of 1 meter. Hence, the nodes provide a throttling effect for the modelled 
retention boulevard with a width of 13 meters. The throttling effect neutralize 
thereby the retaining effect for the modelled retention boulevard. 
 
 
Investigations of the three tested modelling typologies revealed significant 
problems with utilization of fictive basin volume during peak flow for model-
ling methodology 1 and 3. This is a result of the used dhiapp.INI file which ex-
tends the basin geometry upwards. This result in massive fictive volume above 
the implemented basins, where the water can be stored temporally. 3561 m3 
of water are stored in the fictive volume during the peak flow situation for pro-
ject BIR8.2 in model BRDmodel_PLAN _edit_v2_2 where only 300 m3 of basin 
volume is implemented. This result in a misleading performance of the system. 
 
 
Investigations of the three tested modelling methodologies revealed significant 
problems if the user defined basin geometry is not defined up to the crest level 
of the weir. The basin is in this case not connected correctly to the node, result-
ing in that the weir only is working in one direction. An investigation of the link 
and weir discharges throughout the branches for model modelling methodol-
ogy 1 revealed that 1930.92 m3 of water ends up in basin BIR8_3_0014 and 
1174.78 m3 of water ends up in basin BIR8_3_0017. The water is not released 
back to the system, which is a problem as the performance of the system be-
comes misleading.

8.3 Appendix C 
Model technical descriptions of the generation of dfs.0 files in MIKE Urban and technical 
description of the establishment of the test model.  
 
 
Generation of dfs0 files in MIKE Urban 
 
The generated CDS are then converted into dfs0 files in MIKE Urban under Time Series. 
Initially, a new time series is created. Under TS Group Properties the Time Type is de-
fined as Equidistant_Calender, the Time Unit is set to seconds and the Start Time is set 
to 15-09-2016 00:00:00. The number of Time Steps depends of the given length of the 
applied CDS. The Time step value must be defined with same value as the selected time 
step in the excel spreadsheet Regional CDS Ver_4 0_ENG. The generated CDS is then 
inserted by selecting the column Intensity in the Excel spreadsheet. The unit of the rain 
series is specified under Item Properties. Rainfall intensity is selected as type and mu-
m/sec is selected as unit. When all parameters are given, the time series is saved with 
a proper name.  
 
 
  
Creation of test model in MIKE Urban 
 
The test model is created by selecting the elements connected to project BIR5.4. The 
selection is then exported by the import/export function in MIKE URBAN. And the se-
lection is backed up as a simple XML file. A new project is created, called BRD-
model_PLAN_TEST_edit_v2. The coordinate system for the new project is set to 
ETRS_1989_UTM_Zone_32 under edit coordinate system. The simple XML file is then 
imported into the new project by the import/export function. The control of the model 
is not transferred by the import/export function hence the control is implemented man-
ually.
