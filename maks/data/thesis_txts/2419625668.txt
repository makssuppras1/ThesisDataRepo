Abstract
The aim of this thesis has been to investigate polarization properties of supercontinuum laser.
One of the fastest acting nonlinear eﬀects in supercontinuum generation is Modulational In-
stability. This is where most of the generation supercontinuum happens early on in the ﬁber.
It’s polarization counter part Polarization Modulational Instability is like wise a very fast
acting and important eﬀect in supercontinuum generation and slightly more circumstantial
to investigate. Therefore in the pursuit of understanding Supercontinuum lasers polarization
properties and investigation of PMI has been the main focus of the master thesis.
The procedure for doing this was to ﬁrst understand the equation that governs supercontin-
uum generation with multiple polarizations, the so called multi-mode Generalized non-linear
Schrödinger equation. Therefore this was derived from ﬁrst Maxwell’s equations, and with
intermediate know equations in-between being the NLSE and GNLSE.
When this derivation was done, ﬁrst order perturbation analysis was preformed on the
MM-GNLSE in order to derive an expression for Polarization Modulation Instability, and to
understand it. This led to predictions for the PMI spectra and the gain in diﬀerent photonics
crystal ﬁbers (PCF).
The diﬀerent optical ﬁbers gain spectrum was then investigated though simulations of the
MM-GNLSE. Experimental work was also done in order to try and verify the predicted PMI
spectra. In this experiment work done in both the normal and anormal dispersion regime, in
order to get predicted MI and PMI curves. Simulation were also done in the hope to better
explain what was observed in the experiments.

1.1
Introduction
A Supercontinuum (SC) is light that has been heavily broadened in its spectrum. This makes
it possible to generate "white" light pulses from monochromatic1 picoseconds pulse lasers. A
common method of accomplishing this is via a powerful pump laser propagating in material
with strong non-linear eﬀects. Optical ﬁbers of the type Photonic Crystal Fibers (PCF) can
especially generate high non-linear parameters by concentrating the intensity in very small
cores that would not be possible in conventional optical ﬁbers. PCF employ periodic mi-
crostructures in ﬁbers to guide light rather than traditional internal reﬂection from a change
of refractive index between a core and a cladding.
Even though Supercontinuum Generation (SCG) has been observed as far back as 1970
[Alfano & Shapiro, 1970], it is only became common after 2000 to use PCF’s to generate SCG
[Alfano, 2016, p. 120]. In an experiment in 2009 it was shown SCG from the ultraviolet and
up to 6.28 µm wavelength by pumping a 2 cm ﬁber ﬂuoride ﬁber with a ≈180 femto second
laser[Qin et al., 2009]. It is still a very heavily researched ﬁeld in photonics, and there are
still new discoveries made inside this ﬁeld. Often the polarization is neglected when doing
work of the nature of either, analytically, numerical and experimental work. It is usually
disproportionately more diﬃcult to account for polarization in a system than it is worth. But
many natural phenomenon are heavily dependent on the polarization and this can be used
to the advantage of the inventive scientist or engineer. That is why a study of polarization
properties in non-linear ﬁbres is important.
This thesis will be a study of polarization properties of supercontinuum lasers and on
how the supercontinuum is generated in the early stages of the ﬁbers, here speciﬁcally the
phenomenon know as the Polarization Modulation Instability (PMI) is of interest. PMI has
been studied often in the literature, but often it is of bifringent ﬁbers, and with circular polar-
ized light [Tonello et al., 2007] [Haelterman, 1994] [Agrawal, 2013, Chapter 6.]. Of particular

1.1.1
Structure of thesis
This thesis will start at Maxwells equations and then though a theory section, go though the
derivation diﬀerent levels of complexity of the envelope equation in an optical ﬁber. This
will correspond to ﬁrst the Non Linear Schrödinger Equation (NLSE). Then the delayed ma-
terial response function is taken into account, and this leads to the Generalized Non linear
Schrödinger Equation (GNLSE). This is then taken further and goes into Multi Mode Gener-
alized Non Linear Schrödinger Equation (MM-GNLSE). This equation is very important for
the further since it is the foundation for the derivations on Polarization Modulation Instability
(PMI) as well as numerical simulations. The PMI gain is then derived for the full MM-GNLSE
and an analysis of the predicted PMI spectra for diﬀerent ﬁbers and pump frequencies are
shown as well in this analysis section. This then concludes the theory part of this thesis.
The sections following the analysis section will be dedicated to numerical work, where
ﬁrst an quick explanation of the method used as well as some investigation the validity of the
solver. Also the results of simulations with too low a tolerances is shown here. Then some
simulations will be done to investigate further the PMI spectra, and how the predictions from
the analysis correspond to the simulations. As numerical simulations are a big part of the
other sections, this section is not taken too far in the numerical analysis.
This is then followed by an experimental section. The experimental section covers two
diﬀerent experiments, that are to look at two diﬀerent situations where Modulation Instability
(MI) as well as PMI is expected to occur. The ﬁrst experiment is to look at a 10 meter ﬁber
pumped with non polarized laser light in the normal dispersion regime. Here the expectations
are that while MI should occur it is not expected to be visible in the end of the ﬁber. There-
fore simulation are made in the hope of showing this, while using the spectra at the end of
the ﬁber as conformation that the simulations are valid. The second experiment is a 30 cm
ﬁber that is pumped the abnormal dispersion regime with a polarized ﬁltered source. Here
PMI is expected to occur, and the results are supplemented with the analytical predictions
and numerical simulation for the setup.
This thesis is then ended in a conclusion and an outlook.

Chapter 2

When considering electromagnetic wave propagation in any material, the properties are de-
ﬁned though Maxwell’s equations. However when considering propagation through optical
ﬁbers a simpliﬁcation can be made. The invariance1 along the propagation axis of the ﬁber,
makes it possible to solve the wave equation via separation of variables. This gives rise to in-
variant mode functions, and moves pulse propagation to an envelope function instead. These
envelop function take the form of a Non-Linear Schroedinger Equation (NLSE). When taking
into account a delayed time response in the media of propagation and when expanding this
into multiple modes of electromagnetic solutions to the wave equation this gives the Multi-
Mode Generalized Nonlinear Schrödinger Equation (MM-GNLSE).
Therefore this equation is the foundation for all further analytical derivations and numer-
ical work done in this thesis. Other sources have derived this equation, most noticeably to
this thesis is the result of Polleti et al. [Poletti & Horak, 2008, eq. (6)]. However since it
was not possible for the author of this thesis to accounting for all the terms a priori, in the
aforementioned article, and because it was of such importance to the thesis it was decided
that a derivation was necessary.
The decision for a full derivation was two fold. First it would validate the result in Polleti
et al. Secondly it reveal the necessary assumption for deriving such an equation, as well as
give an opportunities to discuss the relevant properties of the individual terms of the equation.
Therefore the procedure in chapter is to start with Maxwell’s equations, to derive the wave
equation.
Then proceed to derive the envelope equation, which gives the linear eﬀect of
the NLSE. In the linear eﬀects section, chromatic dispersion as well as attenuation will be
discussed. Then by assuming no delayed time response of the medium, the NLSE will be
derived. Here will be discussed Self phase modulation and solitons. When then including
delayed time response, the GNLSE will be derived, and this will lead into a discussion of the

2.2.1
Photonic crystal ﬁbers
Photonic crystal ﬁbers (PCF) are optical ﬁbers that guide electromagnetic waves via a photnic
crystal like micrometer structures transverse to the propagation. They are usually constructed
of a single material with surrounded by periodic structure of "holes" of air. However many vari-
ations of this concept exist ( see for example liquid ﬁlled PCF’s to tune [Haakestad et al., 2005]
to tune the photonic bandgap). The ﬁbers of interests in this thesis is constricted to silica
ﬁbers with air holes. Before the actual discussion of PCF’s, a touch up on photonic crystal is
in order.
Photonic crystals are composite materials that use periodic structures of dielectrics to
construct artiﬁcial band gaps that are not inherent to the individual materials used. The
repeating structure is reminiscent of crystals, hence the name photonic crystals, and in fact
some of the mathematics usually used in crystals can be used in the calculations (for example
Bloch waves). Even fairly simply crystal structures can very quickly become troublesome to
calculate analytically, but they can be solved numerically in ﬁnite element solvers such as
Comsol. This is what was done get the dispersion curves, and overlap integrals of the modes,
however not as part of this thesis. The Comsol simulations used in this thesis was provided by
Rasmus D. Engelsholm for the PCF’s that will be referred to as the red and white ﬁbers, and
by Ivan Bravo Gonzalo for the ﬁber that is referred to as the Anormal Dispersion (ANDI) ﬁber.
The PCF’s are usually deﬁned by two parameters: the Pitch Λ and the hole diameter d,
where it is usually the ﬁst parameter and the ration between the them that referred to, d/Λ.
The ﬁbers referred to though in this thesis has the parameters: Red ﬁber Λ = 3.3µm, d/Λ = 0.52,
the white ﬁber Λ = 2.6µm, d/Λ = 0.52, and the ANDI ﬁber Λ = 1.44µm, d/Λ = 0.39.

2.2.2
Chromatic dispersion and ZDW
Chromatic dispersion is the phenomenon when light at diﬀerent colors/wavelengths travel at
diﬀerent propagation speeds. When solving the wave equation (2.11), it becomes apparent
that in vacuum all light travel at the speed of light, c, and there is no chromatic dispersion.
However when propagating though dielectrics the material responds to the electric ﬁeld, and
the response is dependent on the frequency of the applied ﬁeld. In bulk material in results in
a frequency dependent refractive index, n(ω). However in ﬁbers the chromatic dispersion is not
only dependent on the material, but also on the geometry of the ﬁber. Since the propagating
modes change their shape in the ﬁber with frequency, it is natural to assume that their propa-
gation constant also is dependent on the frequency of the light. When at low frequencies, the
wavelength is large and at some points comparable to the ﬁber diameter2, and here the ﬁber
geometry is the dominant to the frequency dependence of the propagation. This is referred
to as waveguide dispersion. At high frequencies the electromagnetic ﬁeld is inﬂuenced by the
material properties, since in this regime the dielectric constant is quite frequency dependent,
and this is referred to as the material dispersion.
Both are combined when ﬁnding the propagation constant though the eigenvalue via Com-
sol. Since the refractive index so often is tied to the material properties of dielectrics, to avoid
confusion, a new parameter is deﬁned as the eﬀective refractive index, neff, via the relation

Delayed Raman response is known to stem from the fact that the there is a delayed response
in the medium from the nuclei of the materiel. The delayed Raman response is usually deﬁnes

of the material that the wave ins propagating in. In silica the fractional Raman contribution

Analysis of gain spectrum
This section will be dedicated to an analysis of the gain spectra derived in the previous sec-
tionl. Having derived the equations for PMI in case of a analytical where only one of the
modes are pumped, it becomes apparent that if only the dispersion up to β2 is included, there
can only be MI in the normal dispersion regime, and only PMI in the anormal dispersion
regime. However it is not always a good idea to approximate the dispersion to only second
order, speciﬁcally for supercontinuum generation.
When including the higher order dispersion terms, it is possible for there to be MI in the
anormal region if β4 > 0, and it would also be possible for there to be PMI in the normal
regime if β4 < 0. These eﬀect are often contributed to four wave mixing, but it is clearly the
same eﬀect as MI/PMI. When this occurs, the gain spectra does not grow up slowly from
the pump wave, but rather gives gain spectra that seam to grow up in bands farther away.
They will always be symmetric in the frequency domain around the pump frequency, to abide
by the law of conservation of energy. It is two photons that recombine with the pump laser
frequency, or close around it of the pulse has dispersed, to give a up- and downward stoke
shifted contribution. When plotting this in the wavelength domain however the band does
not look even, which is also expected, but it needed a mentioning. This comes from how the
wavelength and frequency domain is related as inversely proportional.
An other observation is that with the increase of power, the width of the MI and PMI
spectrum widen. The width of the gain spectrum has a squareroot dependent on power, so
when the power is doubled the width of the gain spectrum is doubled. Selected MI and PMI
spectra has been chosen in ﬁgure 3.2.

Comsol simulations.
Comsol is commercial Finite Element method (FEM) solving software that can simulate
a wide range of physical systems without the user needing to write their own code for the
task. Comsol has the capabilities to calculate the mode proﬁles and dispersion curves via
doing Finite Element method on the ﬁber geometry and solving the linear part of eq. (2.10).
Comsol will therefore return a solution of the type
˜E(r, ω) = F(x, y, ω)eu(ω)z
(4.1)
Here Comsol returns the ﬁeld mode functions F(x, y, ω) and the eigenvalue u(ω) that act as
a solution to Maxwell’s linear wave equation. The returned values are both complex, where
the complex and real part of the eigenvalue respectively represents the dispersion and the
conﬁnement loss of the attenuation, β(ω) and α(ω), which can be used to calculate the eﬀective
refractive index neff(ω) = (β(ω) −iα(ω)/2)/k0.

4.1.1
Interaction picture and Dormand-Prince Runge-Kutta method
Because of the non linearity of the GNLSE it can only be solved analytically for very speciﬁc
cases, and because of the non linearity there is no guaranty that a found solution is unique.
Usually a numerical integration is therefore required. A common method of solving it is the
split step method, having been used since 1973 [Zakharov, 1972]. The split-step method is a
approximative integration scheme that solves the the linear and nonlinear part of the equation
separately for small steps at the time. This will give a dominant error that is proportional to
the square in the steep size [Agrawal, 2013, p. 48]. This convergence is not comparable with
other methods[Christensen, 2017], and therefore it is not the one used in this solver.
The numerical integration method used is the Dormand-Prince Runge-Kutta interaction
picture method, abbreviated DPIP. The interaction picture represents a method of solving the
GNLSE via transforming the ﬁeld into the interaction picture, and thereby eliminating the
linear term, before solving the equation. This is reminiscent of what was done in the section
of the NLSE where we went out of the interaction picture in order to get the linear part out
explicitly. Now we simply need to do it in reverse, so therefore if we deﬁne the MM-GNLSE

Experimental veriﬁcation is what fundamentally separates pure mathematics and physics. In
mathematics it is the axioms and lemmas that deﬁne the mathematical reality for which all
derivations there upon hold. It simply states that if a system fulﬁlls the stated axioms and
lemmas, then it is true that the results apply. There is therefore no way to falsify mathematical
construction, as long as the derivations are done properly.
Physics on the other hand does have a speciﬁc rule set deﬁned by the natural world. And
even though we do not always understand all the rules, they are there and they are well
deﬁned. Therefore any physical theory must accurately predict observations in nature or it is
simply not physics.
That is why experimental veriﬁcation for any theory or simulation is of utmost impor-
tance. Even though experimental work has not been the focus of this thesis, the opportunity
was seized in two instances, to try and gather some data related to PMI.
The ﬁrst experiment was done in collaboration with Kyei Kwarkye at DTU Photonics and

discussed below in details, neither MI or PMI were observed at the end of this ﬁber. And as
the ﬁber was spliced, it was not possible control the polarization in the input.

exiting the ﬁber the polarization is ﬁlters. This then gave the optimal conditions to observe
PMI. Some eﬀect that in this thesis are concluded to be PMI was observed, and it will be
discussed and analyzed the the next sections.

Chapter 6.
Conclusion
Chapter 6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to investigate polarization properties of supercontinuum lasers.
In order to do that the main focus was on understanding Modulational Instability (MI) and
Polarization Modulation Instability (PMI). In order to do that the multimode Generalized
Nonlinear Schrödinger Equation was derived, and many of the properties of the MM-GNLSE
was discussed along the way.
With the MM-GNLSE derived and a better understanding of how it worked, this was then
used to make a simpliﬁed two polarization equation, via some approximations of the overlap
integrals. This was then used to develeop an analytical ﬁrst order perturbation theory on a
continuous wave solution, known as PMI. The PMI spectra were investigated and character-
ized. PMI could only occur in the anormal dispersion regime when higher order dispersion
terms were neglected, but could appear in the normal dispersion regime when they were in-
cluded. The same but opposite for the MI, where it was found in the normal dispersion regime
but could also occur in the anormal regime with higher order of dispersion include in the gain
spectra.
This was then simulated, and the ﬁndings for the PMI spectra was conﬁrmed via simu-
lations. In total simulation over three diﬀerent ﬁbers in at many diﬀerent wavelengths was
done to characterize the spectrum, both in the ﬁbers normal and anormal regime.
Two experiments were done. The ﬁrst was a search for MI in a 10 meter ﬁber knows as
the "Red" ﬁber. The prediction was that there would be MI in the ﬁber, but that it not be
found at the end of the 10 meter ﬁber because at that point the spectra would broaden to
much. A shorter ﬁber was not an option. The second experiment was a 30 cm ﬁber know
as the ANDI ﬁber, because of its anormal dispersion. By sending in linearly polarized light
it was possible to generate PMI here and the spectra was compared with simulations. The
observed spectra and the simulated spectra, showed similarity in their general spectrum and
behaviour, but they where shifted in an odd manner in respect to each other. Still this was
seen as an experiment conﬁrmation of the PMI predictions.

Conclusion
6.1
Outlook
Polarization properties of supercontinuum lasers is a very broad subject, and it was not pos-
sible to cover all the diﬀerent subject in a single thesis. There are many more phenomenon to
study, and in respect to what a direct continuation of this thesis would be is summarized here.
One of the most interesting things to look for in Polarization Modulation Instability is
how it is aﬀected by birefringence. That is properly also why it is so well researched. However
most of the research was done on circular polarization modes instead of linear modes. The
reason properly being that when the polarization is circular the birefringence becomes very
easy to to manage.
An other interesting property that would have been interesting would be to look at the how
having multiple pumps would eﬀect the PMI and in general supercontinuum spectra. And on
this note if it would be possible to calculate PMI for diﬀerent spectra than a monochromatic
continuous wave solution.
Since it was found that the only Q of the over lap integrals that could transfer energy
between the modes were, in the two polarization case, Q(2)
xxyy and Q(2)
yyxx for respectively trans-
ferring energy from mode y to mode x and for the other from mode x to mode y. This could
be used to deﬁne Comsol simulations to speciﬁcally look for variations in the Photonic crystal
ﬁbers structure that would minimize these overlap integral, while keeping the rest high, in a
computer learning environment. That way it might be possible to develop ﬁbers that would
maintain polarization and generate supercontinuum.
