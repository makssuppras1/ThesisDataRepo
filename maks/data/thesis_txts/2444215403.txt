Abstract
AISI 304 stainless steel is widely used in production due to their good formatbility and
corrosion properties. The austenitic stucture in AISI 304 steel will partly transform to strain-
induced martensite when plastic deformation is applied. The initial state of the material is
important since it will determine the properties of the deformed samples after production.
The eﬀect of deformation on mechanical and corrosion properties are investiged through this
paper. Two diﬀerent batches of AISI 304 is investigated in order to understand the inﬂuence
of chemical composition and grain size.
This report clarify diﬀerences in material properties before and after deformation inﬂuences
by grain size and chemical composition. Future research is mentioned in order to understand
the inﬂuence of deformation even further.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This report investigates the material properties of 304 stainless steel used in the production
of electrocardiography (ECG) studs at AH metal solutions. The studs are a part of elec-
trocardiography electrodes, where the main function for the stud is to ensure good contact
between the cables and the sin sensing part. In electrocardiography, the ECG electrodes are
used to measure signals from the heart. The heart beats in an electrophysiologic pattern, it
is possible for the ECG electrodes to measure these electrical changes in the skin and these
measurements are used to investigate cardiac problems. Figure 1.1 shows an illustration of
the diﬀerent elements in the ECG electrode and a real ECG electrode.
(a)
(b)
Figure 1.1: (a) Diﬀerent elements in the ECG electrode (b) ECG electrode [1]
The ECG electrode consists of diﬀerent elements, as seen in ﬁgure 1.1(a). In the middle of
the electrode is an acrylonitrile-butadiene-styrene (ABS) stud, Ag/AgCl usually covers this
stud. This ABS stud covered with Ag/AgCl will be referred to as the Ag/AgCl stud in this
report. On top of the Ag/AgCl stud is an American Iron and Steel Institute (AISI) 304 also
known as EN 1.4301, stud that is produced by deep drawing. Between these two studs, a
polymer seal is placed, the task for the polymer seal is to make sure the two studs are not in
contact with each other. Underneath the Ag/AgCl stud is a gel with 3 % wt NaCl.
Various problems about the material properties have arrived in connection to the storage of
the ECG electrodes. When the electrodes are ready for the packaging, they are vacuum packed
in bags and then they are transported to all over the world. The problem arises primarily in

countries with higher storage temperatures, where the electrodes are seen to corrode within
the two year service time. The corrosion mechanism that has been seen on the electrodes
are mainly stress corrosion cracking and pitting. The corrosion products produced on the
electrodes will make an increase in the impedance and thereby decrease the performance of
the electrode. Furthermore, corrosion appearance on a MedTech product as ECG electrodes
is unwanted. As there are high demands for the mechanical requirements and also on the
corrosion properties of the ﬁnal product. At the same time, an essential factor for AH metal
is to keep the production price low for the studs, as they are producing several million per year.
The AISI 304 stud is manufactured by deep drawing from a 0.2 mm thin sheet. AH metal
produces a lot of diﬀerent studs for these ECG electrodes, in this project the model 8109 is
used for the investigations. The diﬀerent model numbers relate to diﬀerent stamping processes
as tooling, and small geometric changes occur between the diﬀerent models. AH metal has
experienced that during the production of these studs, the quality of the product can vary
from batch to batch. They have experienced that material batches within the chemical
composition speciﬁcations for a 304 stainless steel performs mechanically diﬀerent than other
batches of AISI 304 that they are using. The diﬀerences in performance can be seen by the
studs showing cracks after the production by deep drawing (CRACKS PARALLEL WITH
TOP AND BOTTOM OF STUDS. EJ AT FORVEKSLE MED SCC). These cracks are
time-consuming and expensive for the company since the quality of studs with cracks are
not accepted. The deep drawing process for producing the 8109 stud consists of 9 diﬀerent
deep drawing steps followed by two clamping on the neck of the stud in order to create the
mushroom shape. The diﬀerent positions on the stud that will be referred to in this report
can be seen in ﬁgure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: Description of stud geometry
The project aims to identify the critical material properties that cause the failure of ECG
studs model 8109 after production with deep drawing. The AISI 304 is delivered to AH metal
on 750 kg sheet coils with a thickness of 0.2 mm. The investigations will be made on an
incoming AISI 304 raw material sheet coil batch that showed cracks during deep drawing
and will be compared to an AISI 304 coil that did not crack during production. The process
parameters are kept constant for the production with these two materials, so the diﬀerence in
performance is only aﬀected by the material and not by production parameters. The samples
that will be investigated is the raw material coil in order to understand the condition of

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
The following chapter will give a short review of the theory used in this project in order to
understand the analysis and all this will be used in the discussion later in this report.
The initial material for the studs are 0.2 mm thin strips of 304 austenitic stainless steel
delivered from Otelinox or Ferrinox on coils with a width of 64 mm, a weight of 750 kg, and
a prize around 75000 DKK. The thin strips can be produced by hot rolling until the ﬁnal
thickness. To release the internal stresses that are produced by the thickness reduction, the
strips are solution annealed. The annealing process is a recrystallising of the grains [5]. In
this case, the solution annealing performed by the manufacturer is with a temperature of
1100 ◦C in a H2 atmosphere, followed by convection cooling. The treatment time is not
speciﬁed by AH metal. Annealing of austenitic stainless steels are normally performed within
the temperatures 1000 to 1150 ◦C and the treatment time is maximum 1 hour [6]. The
annealing process aﬀects the material properties of the raw coils that are received at AH
metal. Kain et al. showed that by solution annealing of AISI 304 SS the ASTM grain size
decreased, meaning that the average grain size increased for the samples [7].
Stainless steel is known for the good corrosion properties compared with other types of
conventional steel. The good corrosion properties are obtained with a chromium content
of at least 11 wt.%. Even though most stainless steels have a chromium content of around
18 wt.%. The high content of chromium protects the steel against corrosion due to the
formation of a thin chromium oxide (CrxOy) layer on the surface, that passivates the metal.
If this transparent oxide layer gets broken either by a chemical or mechanical attack, it will
regenerate itself in oxidative environments such as water or atmosphere. Additional elements
can be added in order to improve the corrosion properties, and this could, for example, be
molybdenum or nickel. Stainless steel exists in three diﬀerent main classes, dependent on
the crystal structure of the metal. The three classes are martensitic, ferritic and austenitic.
The three classes diﬀer in their crystal structures and thereby also in their general material
properties[8]. The austenitic stainless steel is the 300-series, in this project the AISI 304 also
known as EN 1.4301 is investigated. The austenitic phase consists of an FCC crystal structure,
which is possible to create when the nickel content is at least 8 wt.%. The austenitic stainless
steel is one of the most used stainless steel [9] since the austenitic phase is characterised by
good formability, high strength and good corrosion resistance [10]. In order to obtain the
austenitic structure at room temperature, it is necessary to add elements that will stabilise
the austenitic phase structure [11]. The elements C, N, Cu, Mn, and Ni, will stabilise the
austenitic structure [12].

2.1
Deformation and material properties
The austenitic (γ) stainless steel is metastable at room temperature, and by plastic de-
formation, the martensitic structure will be formed. This process is called strain-induced
martensite. Material properties change during this phase transformation since the properties
of the martensitic structure diﬀer from the austenitic. The changes in material properties
by the eﬀect of cold rolling has been investigated. It was shown that the formation of
strain-induced martensite increased the hardness of the material, the tensile strength, and
yield strength [13]. The amount of strain-induced martensite transformed is aﬀected by
temperature, the chemical composition, stacking fault energy (SFE) and the amount and
rate of deformation [14]. Austenitic stainless steels generally have a low SFE compared
to other stainless steels [9]. By modelling and experimental data, it has been seen that
increases in the strain rate and decreasing in the austenitic grain size reduces the amount of
strain-induced martensite. Furthermore, it was seen that lower deformation temperatures
reduced the content of martensitic structure [15]. Di Schino et al. showed that by decreasing
the grain size the ductility of the material decreased and the yield strength increased [16].
2.1.1
Strain induced martensite - SIM
Thermodynamically the formation of martensite in the austenitic structure could happen
at room temperature, but the driving force that is needed means that it will not happens
spontaneously. The formation of martensite can, therefore, happen by the loading of an
external force, that could, for example, be by deep drawing. Strain-induced martensite can
induce two diﬀerent kinds of martensite in the austenitic structure. A hexagonal close-packed
structure (HCP), ϵ or a body-centered cubic structure (BCC), α′ [17]. Diﬀerent authors have
shown two diﬀerent transformation sequences for this phase transformation. By deformation
created by plastic deformation the transformation sequence γ →ϵ →α′ has been seen. It
was stated that the volume fraction of ϵ martensite increased in the beginning with small
deformations until it reached its maximum value, then the amount decreased again. Then
the amount of α′ martensite started to increase by the increase in plastic strain [18]. It
has also been seen that the transformation γ →α′ can occur due to dislocation reactions
[19]. The α′ is nucleated at shear bands intersections [20] [21] [22] where the ϵ is nucleated
at overlapping stacking faults [21]. Lebedev et al. showed that the chemical composition
of the steel inﬂuenced the amount of phase transformation occurring, they also concluded
that the deformation caused by tension would prefer to transform to α′, and the opposite
when the deformation is created by compression the ϵ martensite is preferred [23]. The two
diﬀerent forms of martensite that can be formed during deformation have diﬀerent mechanical
properties. The α’ has increased strength compared to the austenitic structure. The phase
transformation from austenite to martensite will induce changes in the volume of the structure.
The γ →ϵ transformation will create a decrease in the volume of 0.81 % while the γ →
α′ transformation creates an increase in the volume of 2.57 % [14]. The volume expansion
of the martensitic α′ structure will create residual tensile stresses in the austenitic matrix
[24]. These residual stresses will decrease the resistance against stress corrosion cracking
[25]. The ϵ phase has some similar mechanical properties with the austenitic phase, and this

2.2
Corrosion
As described the corrosion resistance of stainless steel relies on the formation of an oxide
ﬁlm. Therefore it is important that this ﬁlm is uniform and the surface is clean and not too
rough in order to establish the best possible oxide ﬁlm. As soon as the oxide ﬁlm is broken
or hindered to form the steel can be exposed to local corrosion. Especially the resistance to
stress corrosion cracking is low if the Ni content is increased to more than 8-9 wt.% these
properties increases [40]. Previously the corrosion properties of the ϵ martensite and the
γ - austenite phase have been investigated in order to compare these two. The corrosion
properties of α’ phase martensite is lower than for the austenitic Γ phase. This means that
the corrosion rate for this martensite is much higher[41] [42].

2.2.1
Pitting corrosion
Pitting corrosion is a local corrosion attack happening when the passive ﬁlm is destroyed,
either mechanical or by chemical attack. In chloride environments pitting occurs in the areas
with a local inhomogeneity that will break down the passivating ﬁlm locally [43]. Pits will
create many local anode/cathode relationships were the corrosion would initiate. The pit will
become the anodic part, where the surrounding material will act as the cathode. Since the
anodic/cathodic ratio can be relatively large, this form of corrosion is hazardous. Another
dangerous factor is that the pits are growing inwards in the material, this means that the
corrosion sites can be hard to detect from the outside even though the corrosion attack is
severe underneath the surface[44]. Pitting attacks can be divided into three development
steps, the initiation of the pit, metastable propagation and ﬁnally stable propagation of the
pit. The initiations of the are when the passive oxide layer breaks down locally. Metastable
propagation is when the pit still is small and still able to repassivate. When the repassivation
is no longer possible to obtain the pit will be in stable propagation [45] [46]. The mechanism
for the increase in corrosion rate for pitting corrosion inside the pit is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1
and explained by the following text and equations.

2.2.2
Pitting resistance equivalent
The pitting resistance equivalent number (PREN) can be used to compare stainless steel types
in order to get an idea of their resistance to pitting corrosion. The properties of stainless steel
to resist pitting are mostly determined by the chemical composition of the metal. Therefore
the PREN value is calculated by the elements that contribute to the resistance, see equation
2.8. In order to improve the properties against pitting corrosion, the amount of these elements
is increased. For austenitic stainless steel, x = 30. Increase in PREN value indicates an
increase in corrosion resistance against pitting [48].
PREN = %Cr + 3.3(%Mo + 0.5%W) + x%N
(2.8)
Additions of Mn and Mo has shown an inﬂuence on the pitting corrosion resistant in chloride
environments [49]. From the equation, it can be seen the importance of Mo content since it
is times 3.3 for calculating the PREN value. The eﬀect of Mo was investigated by Ilevbare
and Burtstein [49] and it showed that an increase in Mo content is decreasing the nucleation
and metastable pitting both in amount and sizes of pits. This leading to a decrease in
stable pits and thereby an increase in the corrosion properties against pitting corrosion. The
content of Mn is important since manganese sulﬁde inclusions (MnS) can form and lead to
precursor cites for the pitting initiation. Increase in Mo content and decrease in Mn content
is increasing the pitting resistance [50].
2.2.3
Stress Corrosion Cracking
The combination of residual stresses and a corrosive environment can cause stress corrosion
cracking (SCC). This type of corrosion can be seen as cracks propagating transgranular or
intergranular in the material. Stainless steel, especially the AISI 304 are at risk of stress
corrosion cracking in chloride environments. Pitting is often seen as the precursor of stress
corrosion cracking and happens when the chloride environment locally breaks down the
protective chromium oxide on the surface, or if the material has damage and therefore the
oxide layer will not get created as normal. The failure as a result of stress corrosion cracking
can occur suddenly, and without any signs, therefore it is seen as a critical form of corrosion.
The eﬀect of stress on corrosion properties for SCC has been investigated, showing that

2.3
Magnetic materials
The austenite (FCC) crystal structure is paramagnetic where the (BCC) structure formed by
strain-induced martensite is ferromagnetic. Since no other magnetic phases can be formed in
austenitic SS by cold working or heat treatment the amount of magnetisation indicates the
amount of martensite formed during deformation [7]. Therefore it is interesting to understand
magnetic materials further in these investigations. The magnetic properties can be measured
by a hysteresis loop, an example of this can be seen on ﬁgure 2.3. Magnetic materials can be
divided into soft- and hard magnets. The diﬀerence can be seen as the size of the coercive
ﬁeld, which is the width of the loop (−Hc too HC). Soft magnets have a narrow width, where
the width of the hysteresis for the hard magnets are approximately 105 times larger. Hard
magnets are characterised by being permanent magnets, meaning that when the magnetic
ﬁeld is removed, the material will still be magnetised. On the other hand soft magnets will
more easily become magnetised, and afterwards, the magnetisation will also disappear [53].
Figure 2.3: Examples of hysteresis loops - to the left a hard magnet,to the right a soft magnet
In a hysteresis loop the magnetic moment is measured, it is created due to a magnetic moment
created on the atomic scale in the material. A movement of charge results in an electric
current, when this current is running in a loop, a magnetic moment is created. Electrons are
charged, and they move around the orbit. This gives the atom a magnetic moment. If the
atom contains two electrons in each orbit, for example, helium the moment from one of the
electrons will cancel the moment of the other electron. When the atom has three electrons in
orbit instead of two, two of the electrons will cancel each other, and the last electron will
then create a net moment in the atom. When the atom has a net moment, it means that
they have a magnetic dipole, and the materials were the overall net moment is cancelled
is non-magnetic [53]. The conﬁguration of the atomic dipole illustrates diﬀerent magnetic
behaviours.
Previous investigations have shown that the saturation magnetisation is increasing with
an increase in the volume percent of α′ martensite. In these investigations, the martensite
was induced by rolling sheets of AISI 304 with diﬀerent reduction thicknesses. They also
showed a correlation between hardness and the amount of deformation. The more reduction
in thickness they created the harder the material became, this was due to the martensitic
structure that was formed in the austenitic phase [27].

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
In this chapter the results presented will be discussed according to the literature that was
presented in the previous chapters.
According to the theoretically calculated values for the stability of the austenitic phase and
the literature [30] is was seen that the chemical composition giving the lowest Md temperature
and the highest SFE was the ﬁne grained material. The Md temperature is taking the
grain size into account and states that smaller grains will give a decrease in the amount
of martensitic transformation [29]. From the low Md temperature and the high SFE, it
would be expected that the ﬁne grained material would obtain the lowest volume fraction of
strain-induced martensite by deformation. The chemical composition of the two AISI 304
batches only diﬀers on minimal amounts of elements.
Hardness of the raw coil materials was measured, and the results showed that the highest
hardness measured on the rolling surface was found in the ﬁne grained material, according to
table 4.6. This correlates with the Hall-Petch relationship from equation 2.5 and the literature
[35]. It was seen that by an increase in the amount of grain boundaries the hardness increased.
5.1
Strain-induced martensite
The diﬀerences in grain size between the two diﬀerent AISI 304 means according to the
literature that the coarse grained material should be expected to have the highest ductility
[13]. The longer elongation obtained by tensile tests of the coarse grained material before
breakage means that an increase in grain size increase in the ductility of the material.
By measuring the mass magnetisation of the tensile test samples it has been seen that the
amount of strain-induced martensite α′ increases by elongation of the tensile test strips.
This was also expected according to the literature that has shown exponentially increase in
volume fraction of martensitic α′ with deformation [17]. This statement was also shown by
XRD were the amount of strain-induced martensite α′ was measured on tensile test strips
deformed 2 mm and 10 mm. It was seen that the intensity of the BCC peaks increased
by deformation, and at the same time the intensity of the FCC peaks decreased during
deformation. Which also was expected since the formation of martensite is happening within
the austenitic grains, so the transformation is happening at the expense of the austenitic
structure and therefore the decrease in peak intensity. Only the martensitic α′ was detected
by XRD, the ϵ martensite could not be seen in the XRD analysis. Measurements on tensile

The VSM measurements on the studs illustrates the amount of martensitic structure that
is created in the studs during production with deep drawing. It was measured that mass
magnetisation of the ﬁne grained studs is in average 13.7 mm2/kg. By comparing these
measurements with the results from the tensile test strips deformed until break it can be seen
that the mass magnetisation obtained in the ﬁne grained stud is close to the maximum critical
value for mass magnetisation that causes the break in the tensile test strip. For the coarse
grained sample the mass magnetisation measured in the studs are in average 19.1 mm2/kg,
it can be seen that this is not as close to the maximum critical value for the break as for
the ﬁne grained material. This shows that the amount of martensite created in the coarse
grained studs during production is lower than the amount of martensite obtained in the
tensile test strips deformed until the break. Since the ﬁne grained material during production
is very close to obtaining the amount of martensite that will fracture the sample, this could
be the reason why AH metal has seen cracks on some of their batches when producing the studs.
The VSM experiments are measuring the mass magnetisation of the whole sample at once.
This means that the mass magnetisation can be higher in the areas where the volume fraction
of strain-induced martensite α′ is increased. This could give local areas were the mass
magnetisation in the stud is even closer to the maximum critical values for the break of the
strain-induced martensite α′.
It has been seen by hardness measurements on the cross-section of the studs that the hardness
varies across the samples, meaning that the strain-induced martensite placed where the defor-
mation is highest. Literature [34] [13] shows an increase in volume fraction of strain-induced
martensite α′ will give increase in hardness. From ﬁgure 4.16 it can be seen that the area
of the stud that is most aﬀected by the strain-induced martensite α′ is the neck, where the
measured hardness was highest. It was seen that the obtained hardness in the stud was highest

5.2
Corrosion properties
The corrosion investigations of the initial raw coil material illustrate that the coarse grained
material has superior corrosion properties since the corrosion potential is highest and the
corrosion current is lower than for the ﬁne grained material. This must be expected since
the coarse grained material has a lower area of grain boundaries which are known to being
energy areas where corrosion can initiate.
The polarisation performed on the microelectrochemical setup on tensile test specimens
showed that by deformation of 2 mm the corrosion properties decreased from the initial
state. Experiments conducted on the ﬁne grained material showed that by an increase in
deformation the corrosion potential decreased and the current increased as well, which means
that by higher deformation the corrosion properties decreased. SEM images of the area
exposed to polarisation reveal one large pit on all deformed samples after the polarisation
test. In some cases, the pit was placed in the middle of the exposed area, but in most
cases, the pit showed a round imprint that indicated the shape of the silicone foam that has
been used to keep the exposed area constant. It seems like crevice corrosion has happened
between the working electrode and the foam. This could be the reason why the results are
not showing a more clear distinction between the diﬀerent elongation levels. What should
be remembered from these results are the small amount of deformation that is needed to
decrease the corrosion properties from the initial state.
Experiments for stress corrosion cracking properties of the samples shows that ﬁne grained
material has the best properties against SCC. When comparing this results with the amount
of martensite measured by VSM it shows that the material with the highest volume fraction
of martensite is performing worst against SCC. Since the transformation of α′ martensite
induces an increase in volume, which will strain the surrounding grains. A higher amount
of martensite can then result in a higher amount of strain in the surrounding structure. A
higher amount of strain will increase the number of cracks. From this, it was seen that the
increase in strain-induced martensite would decrease the corrosion properties against stress
corrosion cracking.
The properties for pitting corrosion for the samples show that the coarse grained material
performs best since the reduction in weight is highest in the ﬁne grained material. This can
be explained by the higher amount of grain boundary present in a material with ﬁner grains.
Grain boundaries are often sites for nucleations of pits, and therefore a higher amount of
grain boundaries will increase the number of pits.

5 Discussion
The samples that have been annealed shows diﬀerent mechanical properties, corrosion
properties and microstructures. Even though the raw coil material should have been annealed
as well before the delivery at AH metal, ﬁgure 4.2 are showing a large diﬀerence between the
microstructure of the raw coil materials and the annealed materials even though they should
have been treated by the same process. It is also seen that the microstructure obtained
after annealing are more equal between the ﬁne and coarse grained material. This could
indicate that the annealing process can be used to control the grain sizes of the diﬀerent
batches. If that is the case, then the chemical composition and stability of austenite are
less critical in regards to the mechanical properties of the studs since it was seen in the
literature that grain size has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence of the amount of strain-induced martensite
created by deformation. When investigating the corrosion performance on both the studs
and the raw coil annealed material it can be seen that by annealing the studs and coils the
pitting breakdown is decreased and the samples are therefore more exposed against pitting
corrosion. The corrosion potential is increased for the annealed stud samples. In general, no
improvements in corrosion properties were shown by the annealing of the samples.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This work has investigated the inﬂuence of deformation in two AISI 304 batches where the
deformation will introduce strain-induced martensite α′ in the structure. The two diﬀerent
batches of AISI 304 have small diﬀerences in the chemical composition, but a diﬀerence in
grain size of the microstructure is observed. The corrosion properties of the AISI 304 and
how these changes due to the strain-induced martensite α′ is investigated. Corrosion test for
electrochemical polarisation, pitting corrosion and stress cracking corrosion is investigated.
The concluding points are the following:
• The elemental composition and microstructure of stainless steel sheet batches were
investigated, and the results show that the elemental compositions are very similar;
however a diﬀerence in grain size was found. By annealing the samples, the uniform
microstructure was created for the two diﬀerent AISI 304 batches.
• The mechanical properties of stainless steel sheets were investigated, showing that
coarse grained material has a higher ductility than the ﬁne grained material. Increase
in deformation will give an increase in the strain-induced martensite α′ resulting in
higher hardness and lower ductility. Increase in grain size will also provide an increase
in the amount of strain-induced martensite. The two AISI 304 batches showed diﬀerent
critical values for the amount of strain-induced martensite that could be introduced
before the break in tensile tests, which can explain why AH metal are experiencing
cracks in some batches during the production of studs.
• The corrosion properties were investigated by electrochemical polarisation in 3.5 wt.%
NaCl, pitting test and stress corrosion test. Results show that only a small amount of
deformation lowers the corrosion resistance. Polarisation experiments show decreased
corrosion potential and lower pitting potential for deformed samples. Crevice corrosion
has inﬂuenced the results obtained in the polarisation of the tensile test strips. Therefore
the best setup that can be used is the Avesta cell since it prevents crevice corrosion.
Annealing experiments carried out did not show an improvement of corrosion properties.
The ﬁne grained stainless steel resulted in a higher material loss in pitting test, whereas
the most cracks after stress corrosion test were seen for the coarse grained material.
