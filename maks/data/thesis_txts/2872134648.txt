Summary (English)
This thesis presents a comparative study of open/source cyber ranges, aiming to provide use/
ful insights into their use for cybersecurity education and training. While commercial solutions
offer extensive features, their adoption is limited by cost and infrastructure requirements.
Open/source cyber ranges offer a promising alternative, but there is a lack of organized
documentation on their capabilities, limitations, and deployment practicality.
The study analyzes thirteen open/source cyber ranges based on criteria including scalability,
user management, documentation quality, ease of deployment, training capabilities, and
compliance with open/source principles. It combines qualitative evaluation with structured
comparison matrices to highlight each platform’s strengths and weaknesses.
A practical case study on deploying KYPO Lite Cyber Range Platform using Google Cloud
Platform and Microsoft Azure explores setup challenges and the importance of good docu/
mentation.
A key outcome is a decision model to assist instructors and developers in selecting suitable
cyber ranges based on their needs and constraints. Platforms such as Open Cyber Range
and KYPO CRP show broad capabilities for complex setups, while StratoCyberLab and Docker
Security Playground offer lightweight solutions for resource/limited environments.
The thesis concludes that although open/source cyber ranges vary in maturity and usability,
they can still be effective educational tools in suitable settings. Future work should include
comparison with commercial solutions and public test environments for hands/on evaluation.

Resumé (Danish)
Denne afhandling præsenterer et komparativt studie af open/source cyber ranges med
henblik på at give nyttig indsigt i deres anvendelse til uddannelse of træning i cybersikker/
heds. Hvor kommercielle løsninger ofte tilbyder omfattende funktionalitet, begrænses deres
anvendelse af høje omkostninger og krav til infrastruktur. Open/source cyber ranges udgør
et lovende alternativ, men der mangler samlet dokumentation om deres funktioner, begræn/
sninger og praktiske anvendelighed.
Studiet analyserer tretten open/source cyber ranges baseret på kriterier som skalerbarhed,
brugerhåndtering, dokumentationskvalitet, installationsvenlighed, træningsfunktionalitet
samt overholdelse af gode principper for open/source. Det kombinerer kvalitativ evaluering
med strukturerede sammenligningsmatricer for at fremhæve styrker og svagheder ved hver
platform.
Et praktisk casestudie af opsætning af KYPO Lite Cyber Range Platform ved brug af Google
Cloud Platform og Microsoft Azure, fremhæver potentielle udfordringer i opsætningen og
betydningen af god dokumentation.
Et væsentligt resultat er en beslutningsmodel, der kan hjælpe undervisere og udviklere med
at vælge en passende cyber range i forhold til deres behov og begrænsninger. Platforme som
Open Cyber Range og KYPO CRP viser bred funktionalitet til komplekse opsætninger, mens
StratoCyberLab og Docker Security Playground tilbyder lettere løsninger velegnet til miljøer
med begrænsede ressourcer.
Afhandlingen konkluderer, at selvom open/source cyber ranges varierer i modenhed og
anvendelighed, kan de stadig fungere som effektive undervisningsværktøjer i velegnede
situationer. Fremtidigt arbejde bør inkludere sammenligning med kommercielle løsninger og
etablering af offentligt tilgængelige testmiljøer for praktisk evaluering.

Readers guide
User management In the context of the cyber ranges the user management is the support
for at least administrative users and low/permission users such as the cyber range
trainees.
Testbed A controlled virtual environment used to simulate real/world cybersecurity systems
and scenarios primarily meant for testing the system.
Cyber range A virtualized platform for simulating cyber attacks and defenses, primarily
meant for training and education of people.
Infrastructure as Code (IaC) A practice of managing computing infrastructure using config/
uration files rather than manual processes.
Scenario Definition Language (SDL) A structured notation (typically YAML or JSON) used to
define cyber training scenarios including networks, users, events, and configurations.
OpenStack An open/source cloud computing platform that enables deployment and man/
agement of virtual infrastructure.
Ansible An open/source automation tool used for software provisioning and configuration
via agentless scripts in YAML format.
Red team exercise A security training exercise simulating offensive tactics like penetration
testing or exploitation.
Blue team exercise A security training exercise simulating defensive operations such as
detection, monitoring, and incident response.
FAIR-USE4OS A set of open/source evaluation principles combining FAIR (Findable, Accessi/
ble, Interoperable, Reusable) with USE (User/centered, Sustainable, Equitable).
Virtual machine (VM) A software/based emulation of a computer system that runs an oper/
ating system and applications independently.
Nested virtualization The ability of a virtual machine to itself host virtual machines, used in
complex cyber range setups.
Low hardware requirements Indicates a cyber range can be run on modest hardware (e.g.,
standard laptop) without significant delay or resource usage.

1 Introduction
The world of cybersecurity is evolving in both threat domains and complexity, with a constant
in/flow of emerging technologies adding new attack vectors to the virtual world. Further
increasing frequency of cyber attacks also highlight the need for effective and specialized
training environments that can prepare students, employees, and cybersecurity professionals
for real world scenarios. Cyber ranges, are specialized virtual environments that can simulate
realistic cyber threats and provide tailored cybersecurity exercises according to changing
requirements. While many commercial cyber ranges offer extensive functionalities, their
accessibility can be limited due to significant constraints in regards to cost and infrastructure
for many teams or organizations. As a result, an increasing interest has been directed towards
open/source cyber ranges as low/risk cost/effective alternatives.
Despite the growing popularity of open/source cyber range solutions, comprehensive
comparative analyses are scarce. To better the entry into the world of open/source
ranges what is needed are comparisons of educational effectiveness, operational strengths,
limitations, and setup complexities. The lack of comparative information poses a challenge
to organizations and learning environments seeking to make informed decisions on tools for
cybersecurity education.
This thesis addresses the issues by providing a comparative analysis of a selection of
the most prominent and developed open/source cyber ranges available today. Key criteria
are identified for evaluation, such as scalability, user management, integration capabilities,
scenario customization, documentation quality, hardware requirements, ease of deployment,
and more. It is also considered how well each range actually performs as an open/source
software (OSS) solution. The study provides a clear objective overview of the each of the
platform’s capabilities for core project features, development and management, as well as
training capabilities. Additionally, this study will explore technical considerations and practical
deployment of cyber ranges.
Ultimately, this research seeks to empower educators, instructors and teachers, by providing
detailed insights into the realm of open/source cyber ranges. This facilitates better
informed decisions for selection of solutions for deploying optimal training environments for
cybersecurity education.
1.1 The problem
Cyber ranges has become a more discussed tool for learning cyber security recent years as
considered in Jawad et al. [11]. Jawad et al. consider the number of study papers published
in the recent years compared to previous years. However, a problem is that there is lacking
information on the use of open/source cyber ranges for use in education. To make this
plausible the following should be considered:
• The current situation of open/source cyber ranges / Definition and the overview of cyber
ranges
• The strengths and weaknesses of the current open/source cyber ranges
• The considerations for setup / Time, hardware requirements, costs

of systems in an isolated and safe environment is secondary in this study.

1.4 Related work
Some studies have been used as an important basis for constructing this technical study

recommended to use the checklist from ECSO in addition to this thesis to cover all aspects.
This thesis will not go into depth on terminology and taxonomy for cyber ranges. However,
although the topics discussed are not complicated an general understanding on cyber ranges
can improve the interpretation. The studies used as a basis for this definition of terminology
and taxonomy are from both Yamin et al. [14] and Jawad et al. [11]. Both are systematic

Jawad et al. proposes an updated base taxonomy and an overview of articles separated by
domains. Jawad et al. also considers more meta information of other studies impact on cyber
range taxonomy, and it considers research papers up to and including 2024.
1.5 Scenario definition language (SDL)
The concept of infrastructure as code (IaC) is a well known good practice as it ensures
consistency across deployments, and enables for better reusability. Scenario definition
languages for cyber ranges work in a similar way and now serve as a standardized method
for defining, configuring and managing cybersecurity exercises. SDLs allows instructors to
specify the topology and configuration for training scenarios with virtual machines, networks,
services, vulnerabilities, and more. By using SDLs it ensures both consistency and reusability
for scenarios. It also makes it easier to share scenario setups with others enabling them to
reuse and reproduce scenarios and exercises.
Several of the open/source cyber ranges leverage SDL for custom scenario creation. Most
commonly the popular more human readable notations are used such as YAML or JSON, for
their simplicity and readability. By using SDL, instructors and administrators can automate
setup, deploy complex configurations, and manage varying scenarios. Additionally, SDL
promotes community contribution and knowledge sharing which can be leveraged especially
effectively for open/source ranges.

1.6 Tooling for cyber range setup
Multiple open/source cyber ranges leverage other open/source tools to optimize and
simplify development and use of the platform. Benefits obtained from good tools can be
automation, virtualization, containerization, configuration management, and more. Multiple
of the compared open/source cyber ranges rely on OpenStack and Ansible to simplify and
optimize deployment of scenarios and enhance usability.
1.6.1 OpenStack
OpenStack is an open/source cloud computing platform which can provide IaaS. This enables
users to deploy and manage virtual machines, networks, and storage, using a modular
architecture. It is especially beneficial for cyber range setups due to its scalability, flexibility
and tools for management, which allow developers to provision complex virtual environments
for cybersecurity training [15]. With its popularity it also allows for knowledge sharing and
community support for troubleshooting.
1.6.2 Ansible
Ansible is an open/source automation tool that is widely used for configuration, application
deployment, and task automation. It uses YAML notation to reduce the complexity of cyber
range deployment and maintenance. With an agentless design, it does not require any
additional software on the nodes it manages in a setup [16]. It has an extensive module library,
and an active community, which makes it a good choice for optimizing working with cyber
ranges [17].
1.7 Open-source in the context of cyber ranges
Open/source is an important aspect to cover in relation to cyber ranges. The cyber ranges
which will be compared can all be argued to be open/source. However, for clarification it seems
important to specify what is meant by open/source for the cyber ranges in this thesis.
A book chapter of Salahuddin et al. [18] discusses open/source as having become a buzzword
outside of open/source software (OSS). OSS can be said to be dependent on the licensing, only
being open/source if the licensing allow for continues use, modification, and distribution of
the software. However, even this is a bit ambiguous as it is not clear if use allows for ability to
run the entire project. Look at just the licensing for the source code, might not mean that the
cyber range should be considered open/source if other virtual resources are needed for setup.
A consideration is that “open/source” cyber ranges might require resources such as hardware
or third party tools for hosting, making it more than just software needed for actual use. It will
be assumed that limited use due to hardware constraints is not considered in the evaluation
for if a cyber range is open/source. Limitations due to needed external tooling or frameworks
which is commercialized will be considered as the cyber range not being open/source.
It will also not be considered strictly open/source if essential part of the project is unavailable
without explicit contact to request access. This includes source code, or architecture
definitions such as virtual images or environments. Even if the most of the project is publicly
available and is using open/practices it is not considered open/source unless the deployment
of complete range seems plausible with the public resources and repositories, excluding
documentation which is another aspect for evaluation.

2.1.1 EDU Range
EDU Range is an open‑source cyber range platform specifically designed for cybersecurity
education. It aims to provide a cost‑effective, accessible, and adaptable environment for
academic institutions, educators, and students to engage in hands‑on cybersecurity training.
By offering interactive lab exercises and simulated scenarios, EDU Range enables learners to
apply theoretical knowledge in practical settings.
Development and Support: The EDU Range project is maintained as open‑source with its
GitHub repository, which hosts a Flask‑based implementation of the platform. Although it
is driven by community contributions, EDU Range is supported by academic and research
institutions that prioritize innovative approaches to cybersecurity education. It has many
contributors, however, the most recent commits on the original edurange/flask repository are
from November 2023 [20]. In the organisation there is another project, edurange3, which is an
overhaul of the original, which leverages newer faster technologies like server side rendering
with React, scenario containerization, new API, and more [21]. Edurange3 does not have much
interaction on github yet, but the it was updated may 2025, so it could be the new repository
to follow along for EDU Range updates.
The project’s architecture emphasizes a lightweight, modular design that facilitates easy
deployment—whether on cloud servers or local machines. It is well suited for environments
with varying technical infrastructures. Detailed documentation, including installation guides
and usage instructions, is available both on the official EDU Range website [22] and within its
GitHub repository [20].
Key Features:
• Educational focus and strong academic existence: EDU Range is tailored to meet the
unique needs of the cybersecurity education market. Its design prioritizes usability and
accessibility, allowing educators to deploy training scenarios without high upfront costs.
As it is one of the older ranges multiple studies have been performed on EDU Range and
research papers are available in different databases. Most of the research papers focus
more on the cybersecurity skill level study than the platform itself.
• Simulated OS for UI: EDURange WebOS is a web/based operating system inspired by
Ubuntu and implemented using Next.js. It is works fully in the web environment, and allow
for a high level of realism for the users solving the exercises. It leverages Kubernetes for
containerized instanses of a users exercise [1]. In figure Figure  2, WebOS can be seen
running metasploitable in the background with the IU for solving the exercises in the
foreground.

2.1.2 KYPO Cyber Range Platform (KYPO CRP)

training and research. It is managed by the Institute of Computer Science at Masaryk
University (ICS MUNI), and is as old as EDU Range from 2013. It has now been discontinued

manner. It is aimed at both academic and professional training scenarios, providing a
robust foundation for features for learning and tracking progress statistics. Its source code
and documentation is hosted on GitLab [24]. Developed by a team of researchers at ICS
MUNI, the platform benefits from continuous updates and community contributions. This
academic backing ensures that KYPO Cyber Range remains current with emerging cyber
threats and educational needs. Documentation is very limited on gitlab, but more extensive

The CRACK Multidomain Cyber Range (CRACK MCR) is an open/source cyber range platform
developed by the University of Genoa’s Computer Security Laboratory (CSEC Lab). As its
name suggests, CRACK MCR is designed to support multi/domain cybersecurity scenarios,

The range can interface with a “Ship/in/the/Loop” simulator to incorporate realistic shipboard

The most recent big research of CRACK MCR at Genoa was led by Enrico Russo, who in
2020 introduced the CRACK framework to simplify cyber range scenario creation [26]. It is on
his github that CRACK is currently hosted¹⁰. The range infrastructure leverages and extends
this framework, as well as a lightweight digital twin environment (LiDiTe), to build complex
scenarios across multiple domains [12]. Backing for the project comes from academic and
industry collaboration in Italy. The University of Genoa’s CSEC Lab hosts the cyber range and
continues to develop new features (e.g., integration of emerging IoT/OT devices and advanced
attack automation via tools like Caldera). CRACK MCR is disseminated under an open/source
license to encourage use by other researchers and educators. Documentation and support
are provided through published papers, technical reports, and the project’s repositories.
MaCySTE had a commit last year, however CRACK on Russo’s Github has not had a commit in

• Multi/domain scenario integration: CRACK MCR enables the creation of cyber exercises

traditional IT systems (enterprise networks, servers and workstations) alongside OT

simulation of cloud services and even mobile infrastructure. In addition, CRACK MCR
provides built/in SOC elements, like simulating a Security Operations Center view, so
blue team participants can practice using monitoring dashboards and analytics during an
exercise. This multi/domain capability is particularly important for training in sectors like
maritime, energy, or smart cities in interconnected, realistic complex cases.

2.1.4 Docker Security Playground
Docker Security Playground (DSP) is an open/source framework that leverages
containerization to create interactive cybersecurity training scenarios. The platform uses
Docker containers as lightweight virtual machines, allowing users to build complex network
topologies with both vulnerable systems and attacker tools, on a single host or modest
hardware [12]. By taking a microservices/based approach, DSP enables the orchestration
of multi/container “laboratories” that simulate real/world networks (complete with routers,
servers, client machines, etc.) in which various security scenarios can be carried out [28]. A
web UI lets instructors and students design and launch scenarios without deep knowledge of
Docker commands. DSP is suited for academic courses, allowing students to practice skills like

2.1.8 CyberRangePi
CyberRangePi was created as a proof/of/concept by developers at the United States Military
Academy West Point, to drastically reduce the cost of cyber ranges for education. The project
was first presented in 2020 (SIGITE conference) [9], and its code has since been available on
GitHub with no licensing specified [32]. The range require low hardware resources, and as
the name sugests, it should be able to run on Raspberry Pi devices. It has not been updated
since April 2019, however, it does not require ongoing updates to work. its core scenario uses
a deliberately vulnerable web app specific CTF exercises on docker that remains useful for
teaching [9], [33], [34]. This vulnerable web app, called Damn Vulnerable Web Application
(DVWA), is still used to teach now, and contains the most well known CTF/type exercises in one
platform simulating a website from a docker container running with Cyber Range Pi. While it
is easy and low/cost it does not support custom scenarios [9].
Key Features:
• Very low cost hardware: The range is built on a cluster of Raspberry Pi 3B+ single/board
computers, chosen for their low price and power consumption [9]. The reference design
uses 4–5 Raspberry Pis networked together, plus a network switch as can be seen on
Figure 10. This could probably be done even cheaper now, years later, as computers have
advanced significantly since.

services (e.g., DVWA’s web interface) through a browser, as if they were attackers on the

2.1.9 CyRIS
CyRIS (Cyber Range Instantiation System) was developed by the Japan Advanced Institute
of Science and Technology (JAIST). The tool was first released in 2016 and presented in
academic conferences as an open/source solution to automate cyber range creation [35].
The formulation about it is a bit specific, when calling it an instantiation system, but it can
be regarded as a cyber range like the other competitors. The original development team
concluded its work around 2021, but CyRIS remains actively maintained [36]. The maintained
project has been moved to a new Gituhub repository, which has had less interaction with
stars, forks, and watches, making it more difficult to find [37]. It is also worth noting that the
project is maintained, but not further developed in regards to features [37]. CyRIS does not
provide a GUI for the users, and require users use commands [35].
CyRIS is freely available for anyone to use or modify. It has been used in together with
the CyTrONE framework (which provides a higher/level interface and learning mangement
system integration) [35]. The documentation is in academic papers or on the Gitbub. While it
does contain a full user guide, it is not the most user friendly compared to competitors [36].
Research publications show that new users can still can adopt the system while it does not
seem simple. Overall, CyRIS is well/supported through scholarly publications and remains one
of the more transparent and tested open/source cyber ranges [38].
Key Features:
• SDL for range provisioning and configuration: CyRIS automatically prepares cyber range
environments using YAML as the SDL syntax. This includes range configuration (hosts,
network settings, software to install, actions to execute) essentially an abstract recipe for the
exercise. Given this description and a set of base VM images, CyRIS orchestrates the entire
setup. This includes, provisioning VMs and configuring their OS and network, installing
required tools or vulnerable software, and applying any scenario/specific customizations
[35], [36]. While some of the competitor open/source cyber ranges rely on third party tools
to do some of the work, if you want to manage it all in a contained project CyRIS works well.
• Live traffic generation: Beyond just deploying machines, CyRIS can inject scenario content
during exercises to make the it more realistic. It supports launching incident events, such
as running an exploit or a malware sample on a host, or replaying attack traffic, to emulate
a live threat for realism [35].
• Clone management for scaling for more user: To support events with many participants (e.g.
a class or competition), CyRIS includes a cloning capability [36]. A single instance of a VM
(for example, a target server) can be prepared, and then it can be specified in SDL to create
N clones of it. CyRIS will handle duplicating the base image and customizing identifiers
(hostnames, IPs) for each clone. This is useful for giving each student or team an identical
isolated environment to work in. This is good for making sure they do not break the exercise
for eachother while playing around. Clones can be spawned from one base image in an

Origins and Evolution: The Role of DETERLab
DETERLab was an influential cyber range platform established in the early 2000s by a
consortium of academic and research institutions. It provided a controlled, virtualized
environment for simulating cyber/attacks and defensive operations, pioneering methods
for cybersecurity experimentation and training. DETERLab’s innovative approach made it
a cornerstone for research in cyber defense strategies; however, as cybersecurity threats
evolved, so did the demands on simulation platforms.
The decision to replace DETERLab is described in [50], and it was based on the fact that over
time, DETERLab began to show limitations in the following key areas:
• Scalability: The architecture was not designed to efficiently handle a large number of
concurrent users or complex, multi/domain scenarios.
• Technological Obsolescence: DETERLab’s reliance on older virtualization technologies made
it less adaptable to modern containerization and orchestration methods.
• Usability and Integration: The platform’s user interfaces and integration capabilities were
not in step with current best practices, making it challenging to integrate with external
systems or scale for enterprise/level training needs.
These limitations motivated the development of SPHERE, a platform conceived to overcome
the shortcomings of DETERLab by embracing modern IT practices and technologies. SPHERE
not only inherits the foundational concepts of DETERLab but also reimagines them with
enhanced performance, ease of deployment, and improved integration capabilities, making
it a more suitable solution for contemporary cybersecurity training and research.

3 Practical considerations
3.1 Case study: KYPO Lite CRP
As a measure to evaluate the practicality of setting up to work with a range a case study
was performed. One of the medium complexity cyber ranges was picked for this study,
namely KYPO CRP, which has a Lite project for deployment purposes that can be deployed on
obtainable virtual hardware. The case study was to explore potential uncertainties, difficulties
and divergencies from the instruction for setup of KYPO Lite Cyber Range Platform (KYPO Lite
CRP) [53]. KYPO Lite CRP is a setup of the KYPO platform which does not support multiple users
accessing the sandbox environments with topology for hosted exercises. That is completely
fine as what is to be tested is the setup of the cyber range platform with while following the
given instructions, and seeing if anything is missing.
To test this authentically the study was evaluated using two different cloud providers:
• Google Cloud Platform as cloud provider, which is the one the project infrastructure defined
for using Terraform [53].
• Microsoft Azure with a VM supporting nested virtualization.
This should work to test both that the range is cloud/agnostic, but also how it is to setup using
a different service/provider than the developers used.

3.1.3 Conclusion of case study
Even though KYPO CRP had relatively much documentation compared to the other ranges,
the deployment instructions for the KYPO Lite CRP project was proven to be lacking. It is also
possible the setup was slightly outdated compared to the cloud providers versions of internal
tools such as Vagrant. With enough time and DevOps knowledge it definitely seems possible
to set it up, but it is by no means as optimal as it could have been with better documentation.
This is likely to hold for multiple of the medium to high complexity cyber ranges.
This results shows that one of the most important parts for making open/source ranges more
practical is improved documentation and active communities.

3.3 Costs and resources
An important consideration is the monetary cost of using the cyber ranges. Even if the range
is open/source you will need to have it running somewhere to use it, which means you will
incur costs. This can be minimized if running a classroom or training session where trainees
are using a simpler range like StratoCyberLab or Docker Cyber Playground, which is hosted
on their local machines. This would mean the hardware is already owned and the cost would
come down to insignificant electricity and wear and tear of computers.
However, if using one of the more complex and feature rich ranges with user management
then the server would need to be hosted in some way. The cheaper and more difficult option
would probably to host it on/premise with owned physical hardware. This would require an
initial buy in of the hardware, which would run and incur some electricity costs. The other

4 Future work
Changes are happening rapidly for open/source cyber ranges and comparisons could change
a lot given a few years. For the simpler, cheaper ranges StratoCyberLab seems to be under
very active development, and future features could include AI to simulate live interaction
in exercises for dynamic scenarios. While is still very viable, it has recently KYPO CRP been
discontinued. Maybe the successor CyberRangeCZ²⁷ will be made open/source in the future
[23].
4.1 Comparison to commercial solutions
It would provide a more complete comprehension of the capabilities and limitations of the
overall state of open/source cyber ranges with a comparison against commercial solutions. It
would be interesting to weigh up the features of open/source and commercial to see if open/
source ranges are at a state where they can compete with more costly solutions.
A commercial comparison could also serve to contribute an impartial idea of the benefit it
brings to use a commercial range versus the cost. As there are many commercial cyber ranges
multiple would need to be analyzed to get an idea of the general state they are in.
4.2 Ideal comparison of training conditions
As the requirements for the cyber ranges depend on the training needed, it would make for
a better comparison if an example of the cyber range from the trainees’ perspective could
be provided. This could be a simulated environment with a simple training scenario for each
cyber range. It would be ideal if a running instance of each cyber range was hosted with
corresponding default scenarios demonstrating their training capabilities and user interface
for the trainee.
It could be the proprietors of each range but as they are open source it is not certain they
would shoulder the hosting cost. Therefore, it might be more realistic for a third party, maybe
government funded, to host each range in a their separate isolated virtual environment for
users to test out.
To save money on hosting the cyber ranges’ virtual resources could maybe be allocated on
a request basis from a button on a website. Then it would take some time to start up, but it
would reduce costs significantly.

5 Conclusion
This technical study has systematically evaluated the prominent open/source cyber ranges,
and highlighted their strengths and limitations in a context of practical use for cybersecurity
training and education. The analysis reveals that there is a substantial variation in training
capabilities, hardware requirements and deployment complexity among the compared
platforms. StratoCyberLab and Docker Security Playground were found as optimal solutions
for environments with limited hardware resources or less complex training needs. While Open
Cyber Range and KYPO CRP demonstrated good functionality all around, seeming suitable
for more extensive and scalable training requirements.
The practical case study, examining KYPO Lite CRP’s deployment, underscored the importance
of comprehensive documentation and ease of setup, highlighting challenges that users might
face in real/world implementations. This further reinforces the significance of selecting cyber
ranges that align closely with institutional resources and technical competencies.
Additionally, the study also emphasizes the necessity for documentation quality, community
support and frequent updates, which significantly influence the longevity and adaptability of
cyber ranges. Platforms such as StratoCyberLab showcase active development and promising
features such as integrated AI assistance. This further improves their potential educational
value. Conversely, platforms that lacked regular updates or comprehensive documentation
demonstrated clear limitations in practical adoption. In the case study it was even shown that
one of the ranges with more documentation, KYPO CRP, was deemed lacking in deployment
instructions for their KYPO Lite CRP made for testing local deployment. This suggests the
importance of sustainability, quality documentation, and continued development in open/
source initiatives.
In conclusion, open/source cyber ranges provide significant educational value, and can
serve as cost effective platforms for developing cybersecurity skills. Future research
directions include extending comparative analyses to commercial solutions and establishing
standardized environments for direct, hands/on comparative evaluations. Such solutions
could further solidify the role of open/source cyber ranges as important tools for improving
cybersecurity education.
