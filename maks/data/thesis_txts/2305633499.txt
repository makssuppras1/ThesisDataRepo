Summary (max 2000 characters): 
In the coming years many offshore wind farms will reach the end of their 
planned project lifetime. Experience in decommissioning these farms is 
limited since the offshore wind industry is relatively young. Needless to say, 
prolonging the operational time would reduce the cost of energy (CoE) and 
thus make offshore wind energy more competitive when compared to other 
energy sources. 
This thesis investigates a potential lifetime extension of an offshore wind 
turbine monopile foundation by reinforcing it with a tripod frame structure. 
Hereby the focus is on the fatigue limit state. The 5 MW NREL reference wind 
turbine and a monopile design from the UpWind project are used as a base. 
The soil and metocean conditions are adopted from the North Sea K13 
shallow reference site with a water depth of 25 m. The fatigue life is assessed 
by time simulations performed in the aero-hydro-servo-elastic code HAWC2. 
A set of sensitivity studies on tripod design parameters are performed to 
determine favorable solutions. Finally, the novel concept's profitability is 
assessed and compared to a conventional monopile. 
The results show that the novel concept is able to reduce the CoE in the 
future. Further studies are necessary to optimize the design and precisely 
state the profitability

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The oﬀshore wind industry has seen heavy growth in the last few years.
Many wind farms are already installed or under construction and even more
are in the planning stage. Usually such farms have a design life of 20-25
years. Because the oﬀshore wind industry is a fairly young industry, most
wind farms are still in operation. However, several projects will reach their
intended design lifetime in the next few years. Operators must decide what
actions to take when the end of life is reached. Three technical scenarios are
possible [26]:
• Decommissioning
• Repowering
• Life extension
Decommissioning
Decommissioning a wind farm would means terminating its operation. The
target is to return the site to the condition before the wind farm was con-
structed. This means, every component such as turbines, foundations and
electrical infrastructure must be dismantled and transported to land. The
turbines are disassembled by their components: blades, nacelle and tower.
Next, the foundation is taken apart and split into smaller sections to facili-
tate handling. Then the parts are lifted onto transport vessels and shipped
to land. From there trucks will transfer the scrap to their ﬁnal treatment

Repowering
Repowering an oﬀshore wind farm entails rebuilding or replacing the existing
turbines. The aim is to enhance the rated power by replacing the turbines
with modern, larger or more eﬃcient ones, or by changing the rotor and the
nacelle while reusing the tower. Following three options are possible [26]:
• Reusing the tower with a new lower capacity turbine
• Reusing the tower with a new higher capacity turbine
• Replacing the old tower and turbine with a new tower and a higher
capacity turbine
For all three cases an additional 20-25 years service life is pursued.
For
the case where the tower is reused it is necessary to reassess the structural
integrity. When the turbine capacity is increased the electrical grid may
have to be upgraded too. Additional, higher wake losses due to larger rotor
diameters can impact the overall performance of the wind farm.
Life extension
Life extension is believed to be the most proﬁtable option [26]. It implies
keeping the turbine in operation beyond its initial design lifetime. This can
include that single worn out components have to be replaced. Extending
the lifetime can be achieved by various means.
The most common is a
combination of inspections, maintenance and condition monitoring coupled
with a reassessment of the structural integrity. As an example, in [20] it was
shown that the reassessment based on measurements of the fundamental
natural frequency and wave loads resulted in a life extension of 47 years. In
general the actual life extension is very often dependent on the site speciﬁc
conditions.
1.1
Motivation
The oﬀshore wind industry is constantly trying to reduce the cost of energy
(CoE) to make the renewable energy produced oﬀshore more competitive
with traditional sources. The matter of how to continue after the end of life
has received more attention in the last years. Repowering and life exten-
sion can yield more proﬁt and thereby contribute to lower the CoE. Special

1.2
Project scope
The presented work examines a novel concept to extend the lifetime of a
monopile foundation by reinforcing it with a tripod frame structure after a
certain time of operation, see Figure 1.1. The main focus in this thesis is to
evaluate the potential of additional gained lifetime with respect to fatigue
damage that arises in the support structure. This study makes use of exist-
ing reference models for a baseline design that allows benchmarking. The
novel concept is compared to the classical monopile with respect to natural
frequencies, bending moments and resulting fatigue life. Further, diﬀerent
structural tripod layouts are investigated by geometrical and environmental
parameter sensitivity studies.
In order to evaluate the feasibility of the new concept the introduced end of
life scenarios are compared with an economical analysis.
The aim is to evaluate the added value of the novel concept rather than to
ﬁnd an optimal design. The ﬁndings should enable one to compare diﬀerent

1.3
Thesis outline
This report is comprised of theoretical, descriptive and evaluative sections.
Since the project progression of this thesis relies on foregoing ﬁndings, the
report structure is divided in 5 main chapters as follows:
• Background (Chapter 2)
• Phase I (Chapter 3)
• Phase II (Chapter 4)
• Phase III (Chapter 5)
• Conclusions (Chapter 6)
Chapter 2 provides a selection of theoretical background that is important
in the modeling process of an oﬀshore wind turbine. The reader is introduced
to the characteristics and mathematical descriptions of site speciﬁc wind,
wave and soil conditions. Next, a brief description of the load modeling
from these environmental conditions is given. Further, the response of a
turbine subjected to these loads, and the phenomena natural frequency as
well as damping, and their inﬂuence on fatigue, is shortly addressed. The
last section focuses on the general consideration in the design process and
explains the procedure of the fatigue limit state analysis.
Chapter 3 introduces the reference wind turbine, reference monopile and
reference site. After the model set up in HAWC2 is stated in detail. Then
the design load cases are mentioned followed by a natural frequency and
fatigue limit state analysis. Phase I ﬁnishes with a ﬁnal monopile fatigue
design that allows one to benchmark the results of the following study.
Chapter 4 tackles the novel concept. In the beginning a small introduction
is given concerning the tripod itself. Then the integration into the existing
HAWC2 model is mentioned. The basic behavior under loading is analyzed,
and a preliminary design of the tripod is presented for the further investiga-
tion. Geometrical and environmental parameters are examined with respect
to their sensitivity to natural frequencies and fatigue damage.
Chapter 5 investigates and compares the proﬁtability of the end of life
scenario, life extension and repowering. This is realized by conducting dif-
ferent case studies. Variable parameters are the tripod layout, the moment
of reinforcement and the maximum operational time of the wind turbine.
Chapter 6 provides a conclusion of the ﬁndings of the conducted study and
discusses recommendations for future work.

Literature often uses diﬀerent terms and deﬁnition to describe the system
of an oﬀshore wind turbine (OWT). The beginning of this document is ded-
icated to deﬁning the terminology used throughout this study. The purpose

Background
This chapter provides a selection of background knowledge. The presented
theory aims to improve the understanding of the modeling process, analysis
and results presented later on. In the beginning the mathematical descrip-
tion and modeling methodology of environmental conditions such as wind,
wave and soil is presented. Then, a short explanation of how an OWT will
responded to these conditions is given. Finally, an overview of usually con-
sidered design situations is pointed out. Particular attention will be paid on
the fatigue limit state.
2.1
Wind
Wind is a result of temperature diﬀerences that occur due to unequal warm-
ing of air by the sun. The temperature diﬀerences result in pressure diﬀer-
ences and air starts to stream to counterbalance these diﬀerences. The highly
variable nature of wind results in time, spatial and directional changes. Fig-
ure 2.1 shows a wind speed spectrum over a wide range of frequencies. A
spectral gap can be observed between the 10 minutes and 1 hour period.
Even though turbulence is present, the mean wind speed is constant in this
region. Therefore, it is common practice to use the mean wind speed from
this range as input for further mathematical modeling. Usually site speciﬁc
long term measurements are performed. The wind speed and wind direction
is logged in the above mentioned 10 minute to 1 hour periods. Statistics
and mathematical models are used to model the wind variety accurate and

In pile foundation design, it is common practice to represent the soil by
nonlinear springs as shown in Figure 2.11. A pile driven into the seabed

stiﬀness depends on the soil properties, the pile dimension and the loading.

soil proﬁle and the corresponding soil properties are obtained by geological
studies, geophysical surveys, and geotechnical investigations [34]. Primarily,
one can distinguish between ﬁne-grained cohesive soil (clay and slit) and

describes the soil characteristics. The main parameters needed to setup the

response amplitudes will decrease and thus damage the structure less. The
following are damping mechanisms active on an oﬀshore wind turbine:
• Aerodynamic damping
• Hydrodynamic damping
• Material damping

Aerodynamic damping occurs during operation. The aerodynamic forces are
changed by the velocity of the vibrating structure. This will add damping
to the system. The hydrodynamic damping is also caused by the oscillation
of the structure. This is usually accounted for by the inclusion of relative
velocities in the Morison equation [14]. The aerodynamic and hydrodynamic
damping is accounted for when using and aero-hydro-servo-elastic code. Ma-
terial damping is caused by internal friction of the steel and is well addressed
in the literature. Soil damping is the most complex and unexplored. It is
caused by internal and geometrical eﬀects. Recent studies [35],[22] showed
that present soil damping stated in recommendations and standards are
underestimated.
2.5
Design consideration
The aim during the design process is to evaluate a ﬁnal design that with-
stands all occurring lifetime loads, while simultaneously being reliable and
cost eﬀective.
The veriﬁcation is performed according to diﬀerent stan-
dards and guidelines. A set of design situations also called design load cases
(DLC) is deﬁned with the corresponding site speciﬁc data. For each DLC the
==PAGE:38==
2.5. DESIGN CONSIDERATION
25
structures response is evaluated. This can for instance be achieved by time
domain simulations with an aero-hydro-servo-elastic code such as HAWC2.
The resulting response is then checked for diﬀerent limit states. Usually
following limit states are analyzed:
• Ultimate limit state (ULS)
• Serviceability limit state (SLS)
• Accidental limit state (ALS)
• Fatigue limit state (FLS)
In the ULS the structure is analyzed and design for extreme events. The
highest expected loading during the design lifetime is evaluated as a combi-
nation of the environmental conditions and operational states. Whereas in
the SLS and ALS, the maximum allowable deﬂection of the structure and
the impact of unintended loads is examined. The FLS veriﬁes that the struc-
ture will withstand the occurring cyclic loads during its lifetime. Fatigue
loads are often design drivers for oﬀshore wind turbine support structures
[37] [35][36]. Additionally, a potential lifetime extension of an oﬀshore wind
turbine foundation seems to be dependent on the fatigue rather then on
extreme loads. Therefore, the focus in this study will be on the FLS.

Figure 3.4 shows the work ﬂow of the input generation for HAWC2 of the

bodies. Each single body is described by nodes in their local coordinate
system. The bodies are then geometrically oriented, linked to each other
and if appropriate constrained in the global coordinate system. The cross
sectional data along each members length is generated in a separate input

the ae and pc ﬁle, refer to [33]. The wind turbine controller is linked via
DLL’s (Dynamic Link Library). It should be noted that the entire HAWC2
NREL 5 MW wind turbine model was adopted from the HAWC2 website
[2]. The monopile was incorporated in the model. The NREL controller
was replaced by the DTU controller used in the UpWind study in order to
achieve compliance between the models.
In [11] two diﬀerent soil proﬁles are available. One is denoted as hard and
the other as soft. Both proﬁles result in a rather stiﬀfoundation. Therefore
the soft proﬁle was selected for the further study since it is believed to be
more realistic. The axial and lateral nonlinear soil springs are generated as
described in Section 2.3. The resulting force displacement curves are shown
in Figure 3.5. The springs are evaluated every meter and linked to the cor-
responding nodes along the pile.
Wind and wave inputs are depending on the speciﬁc load case in question.
In general, wind modeling is mainly achieved by specifying parameters such
as wind speed, Vw and turbulence intensity, TI. The turbulent wind time
series is then generated by the Mann model over a speciﬁed grid, refer to
Figure 2.4. Needed wave parameters are mainly water depth, wave height,
wave period and an appropriated wave theory.
The hydrodynamic coeﬃcients for the monopile and transition piece are
adopted from [11]. Hydrodynamic calculation points are speciﬁed at every
node of the submerged member. Additionally, 7 extra calculation points
between the other points are applied in order to achieve more accuracy.
Bolted ﬂanges at the tower and water masses inside the ﬂooded members are
represented by lumped masses at the corresponding nodal positions. Details
on the ﬂange masses and their elevations can be found in [11].
Diﬀerent additional models and correction factors for example wind shear,
tower shadow, dynamic stall and wheeler stretching are applied in the HAWC2
main input ﬁle. For more details refer to the HAWC2 manual [33]. The
HAWC2 ﬁles can be found in Appendix C.
Finally, the desired output is deﬁned. Since the focus in this study, will be
on the substructure most of the output concentrates on these parts. How-
ever, it is reasonable to observe the entire system’s behavior by specifying
additional outputs.

3.7
Conclusion
Phase I presented the development of a monopile reference model needed
in the following chapter. The initial UpWind monopile design had to be
adjusted to meet the fatigue limit requirements. This resulted in a heavier
design. The total weight of monopile including transition piece increased by
about 10 %. As a result an increase of the ﬁrst natural bending frequency
could be observed. However the natural frequencies still lay in the permit-
ted turbine frequency range. The maximum damage reach a value of 0.90
below the mudline and at the top of the conical section at an elevation of
-5.4 mMSL. The damage curve below the mudline follows the overturning
moment. At the pile toe the utilization is more or less zero and starts to
increase rapidly towards the mudline. Just below the mudline the maximum
is reached and the damage starts to drop again. A low utilization can be
observed from the mudline towards the ﬁrst conical section. At the conical
section the curve increases again due to the high stress concentration fac-
tors. Around the mean sea level the damage drops rapidly again. This is
due to the large wall thickness assumed for the grouted connection in this
region.
The evaluated ﬁnal fatigue design does not represent a complete or optimized
solution. Nevertheless, the resulting values for the fatigue damage, mass and
the natural frequencies are considered suﬃcient for the purpose of this study.

4.3
Basic behavior
In the beginning of a design process a simple static model can give a better
understanding of the basic behavior of the structure. It is useful to know
how loads are transferred throughout the structure and to identify load sit-
uations and locations where maximum stress will occur. Hence a simple
beam model is developed in Ansys. The loading due to the wind turbine is
represented by an overturning moment and a shear force at the interface,
see Figure 4.3a. The connection to the seabed is simpliﬁed by ﬁxed supports
at the mudline.
The ﬁrst thing one would like to know when reinforcing a monopile with a
tripod is the change of the overturning moment along the monopile. Figure
4.4 shows the normalized overturning moment for a conventional monopile
and for a monopile that is reinforced by a tripod. Notice, that the moment
is signiﬁcantly reduced for the latter. The moment drops at the elevation
where the pile sleeve is connected to the monopile. After it remains more
or less constant until below the mudline where it decreases towards the pile
toe.
This indicates that the loading is transferred to the tripod frame structure.
A further examination of the Ansys model will provide better insight. The
resulting total displacement, bending moment and axial force are shown in
Figure 4.3. Looking at the displacement shows that the maximum defor-
mation occurs at the monopile top where the loading is applied. Then it
decreases towards the pile sleeve. The tripod itself behaves very stiﬀand
barely displaces. The total bending moment in Figure 4.3c follows the distri-
bution of the displacement and that of Figure 4.4. Finally, Figure 4.3d shows
that the diagonal braces and the legs are loaded axial. Thereby, the brace
aligned with the load is loaded in compression, whereas the front braces are
loaded in tension.
In summary, note that the load of the turbine is primarily transferred
through the diagonal braces over the legs to the subpiles. Unlike the monopile
the subpiles will transfer axial loads via shaft friction and end bearing to
the soil. The mudbraces are utilized very little when the tripod is installed
but will carry some load during transportation and ensure stability during
installation.
Next, the inﬂuence of the load direction is investigated. In this
manner a worth case loading direction can be determined and considered in
the design analysis. Therefore, the structure is rotated in 10 degrees steps
while the load direction is ﬁxed. Then the normalized maximum combined
stress is plotted over the rotation angle, see Figure 4.5. It can be seen that
the load pattern repeats itself every 60 degrees. This is due to the symme-
try of the tripod. The highest load is induced when one diagonal brace is
leeward aligned with the wind turbine. In this position the brace is mainly
axial loaded.

4.5
Sensitivity studies
The resulting lifetime extension is a function of various parameters. As an
example, the time when the tripod is installed plays an important role. For
instance, a monopile that is 15 years in operation has consumed more of its
fatigue life than one that is only 10 years in service. Hence, a longer lifetime
extension with the tripod component is expected for the latter.
When adding the tripod, three additional piles are anchored in the seabed.
This will likely add additional soil damping to the system and have a pos-
itive impact on the fatigue. On the other hand, the geometrical layout of
the tripod will have an impact on the fatigue damage magnitude of the
diﬀerent members. Therefore, it is crucial to investigate diﬀerent concepts
and gain information about the arrangement of the individual members and
their impact on the fatigue life. As an example, the height of the pile sleeve
will inﬂuence the damage rise at the monopile. Positioning the sleeve at a
higher elevation may reduce the damage at the monopile, but at the same
time result in a heavier design. Another example is the choice of the diago-
nal brace angle. It is interesting to know how the utilization changes in the
monopile or tripod members when the brace angle is varied.
In the following a sensitivity study of diﬀerent design parameters is per-
formed. The aim is to ﬁnd most favorable conﬁgurations of the tripod and
investigate the inﬂuence of additional soil damping. The following parame-
ters are considered:
• Pile sleeve height
• Diagonal brace inclination

4.6
Conclusions
The performed sensitivity studies emphasized how the 1st natural bending
frequencies and the fatigue utilization along the support structure alter to
a number of design parameters. It should be noted that:
• A higher pile sleeve arrangement increase the 1st natural bending fre-
quencies, reduces fatigue in the monopile but increase the relative
fatigue in the tower.
• A brace angle of 60 degrees is found to be optimum with respect to
fatigue design. This arrangement decreases the fatigue in the support
structure from the mudline towards the tower top and decreases the
utilization at the diagonal brace ends. No clear trend of the relation
between the brace angle and 1st natural bending frequencies was found.
• Additional soil damping reduces the fatigue utilization in the support
structure while the 1st natural bending frequencies do not signiﬁcantly
change.
• An earlier reinforcement results in a longer life extension of the support
structure.
An optimal design is highly dependent on the intended end of life scenario.
Aiming a life extension for some years will not require a maximum fatigue
reduction. Therefore, a lower pile sleeve height might be more favorable since
this will save material. If however, the concept of repowering is pursued, it
is tempting to make available more fatigue life.

Phase III: Economic analysis
The goal of Phase III is to evaluate if the novel concept can help to reduce
the cost of energy (CoE) and generate a higher proﬁt than a conventional
monopile project. The previously performed sensitivity study indicates that,
dependent on the tripod conﬁguration and reinforcement scenario a wide
range of additional lifetime is achievable. Thus, two end of life scenarios are
studied and examined for their proﬁtability:
• Life extension
• Repowering
The analysis to be undertaken will compare the end of life scenarios to
the conventional monopile project with a 20 years lifetime from Phase I.
In the beginning of this chapter the methodology of the economic analysis
is introduced. After this, the concept of life extension is investigated by
performing case studies of diﬀerent tripod conﬁguration and reinforcement
scenarios. Finally, concept of repowering is examined. Two cases will be
considered: A higher capacity RNA with the initial tower and a higher
capacity RNA with a new tower.
5.1
Economic analysis
One can assume that an existing RNA can operate for some additional years
past its lifetime. However, it is unlikely that the turbine will survive the

entire additional lifetime provided by the tripod. It follows that the RNA
has to be replaced at some point. Consequently, more money needs to be
invested, indicating that the longest extension might not necessarily be the
most cost eﬃcient one. This emphasizes the need for an economic evaluation
of individual concepts to compare them and determine an optimal solution.
In corporate ﬁnance the net present value (NPV) and the internal rate of
return (IRR) are commonly used to evaluate the proﬁtability and to com-

investment is proﬁtable. Further, the project with the highest value is most
likely the most lucrative.
The NPV and IRR are not well-suited for analyses with unequal lifetimes.
Therefore, equivalent annual annuity (EAA) is chosen in this study. The
EAA considers the diﬀerent project lifetimes by determining a constant an-
nual cash ﬂow over the project lifespan. The present value of these cash
ﬂows is exactly equal to the project’s NPV.
The methodology of the economic analysis is detailed in Appendix B.

5.3
Repowering
Repowering an existing oﬀshore site appears to be a challenging task. A
higher capacity RNA will generate higher loads. For instance, the linear
scale of the bending moment follows a cubic law (s3) [30]. Consequently,
the increased loads need to be carried by the substructure. Whether an
already utilized substructure that was designed for lower loads can endure
these increased loads is highly questionable.
It is without doubt, that the novel concept can enhance such an intention
from a technical perspective. However, it needs to be evaluated if the concept
can guarantee a typical lifetime of 20-25 years for a higher capacity RNA to
be proﬁtable.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
The presented study investigated how the lifetime of an oﬀshore wind turbine
monopile foundation can be extended by reinforcing it with a tripod frame
structure after a certain time of operation. The aim was to conclude the
proﬁtability of this concept.
A monopile reference design was needed to
benchmark and compare the results obtained later on.
The study made use of existing reference models, namely the NREL 5 MW
baseline wind turbine and the UpWind monopile design. The metocean and
soil data was adopted from the UpWind study which based the design on the
K13 shallow water site. The pile foundation was represented by nonlinear
axial and lateral springs generated on the base of the available soil data. The
study focused on the fatigue limit state. Thus, time domain simulations of a
reduced set of load cases were performed in HAWC2. The results were post-
processed in accordance to GL [25]. Reproducing the exact fatigue reference
design was unsuccessful, and thus the initial geometry was adjusted to meet
the design criteria. The ﬁnal monopile design provided the fatigue damage
along the elevation of the support structure.
Next, the behavior of the monopile with the additional novel concept was
examined. An analysis to explore the basic behavior of the novel concept
during loading was conducted, followed by a preliminary structural design.
Afterwards sensitivity studies of diﬀerent layouts and design parameters
were performed with HAWC2. The aim was to ﬁnd a favorable design with
respect to natural frequencies, fatigue damage and thus a maximum of life
extension. It was determined that in order to select a optimal design an
75

6.1. FUTURE WORK
economic assessment of diﬀerent end of life scenarios was necessary.
The last chapter addressed this issue. The end of life scenarios, life extension
and repowering were investigated and compared to a conventional monopile
project.
During the life extension analysis, case studies were performed with vari-
ous tripod layouts combined with diﬀerent moments of reinforcements and
RNA operational periods. The results were compared with respect to the
economic parameter EAA. It was concluded that the novel concept provides
highly ﬂexible solutions that can achieve a signiﬁcantly higher EAA than
the monopile. A high pile sleeve arrangement, an early reinforcement period
and a long RNA service lifetime emerged to be most beneﬁcial to increase
the EAA.
In order to investigated the concept of repowering a model of a higher ca-
pacity RNA was required. The new RNA was obtained by downscaling the
DTU 10 MW RWT to a 6 MW capacity. The tripod conﬁguration was ad-
justed such that it lead to a maximum lifetime enhancement. Two scenarios
were explored. Once the initial tower was reused and once the initial tower
was replaced by a new tower designed for a 6 MW capacity. The results
of the analysis showed that the EAA did not substantially increase when
the tower was reused. Whereas, exchanging the tower involved a moderate
increase of EAA compared to the conventional monopile.
The results in the previously presented chapters led to the conclusion that
the novel concept is able to extend the life of a monopile foundation while
being more proﬁtable compared to a conventional monopile project. How-
ever, the conclusions throughout this report pointed out that further studies
are required for a better assessment.
6.1
Future work
The following provides some recommendations on how the present study
should be continued.
The proﬁtability of the novel concept should be reassessed by considering
that the conventional monopile is decommissioned after 20 years and a new
higher capacity OWT is installed.
Moreover, it is advisable to extend the fatigue limit state analysis.
Ad-
ditional load cases with wind misalignment, wave directionality and fault
condition should be evaluated. After an operational modal analysis can be
performed to provide an insight into whether resonance phenomena will oc-
cur in the OWT rotational interval.
Stress concentrations at the joints and the manufacturing feasibility should

be included in the fatigue evaluation. The limit state analysis should be ex-
tended by verifying the structural integrity against ultimate, serviceability
and accidental limit states. Furthermore, a foundation pile length assess-
ment should be conducted to evaluate the optimal length of the subpiles.
The added soil damping should be evaluated properly. Consequently a struc-
tural optimization of the tripod members could be carried out. In addition,
a detailed analysis of the tripod joints by a ﬁnite element analysis is recom-
mended.
Next, a feasibility study should be performed which investigates how the
installation process and the connection between monopile and tripod could
be realized and optimized.
Finally, the application of the novel concept in combination with other life
extension provisions should be investigated and evaluated with respect to
the proﬁtability.

This section provides a short description of the economic calculation that was
