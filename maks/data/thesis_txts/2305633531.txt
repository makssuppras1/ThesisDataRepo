Abstract  
In previous work it was found that the ionic liquid [BMIM]Cl has a catalytic effect on 
glucose’s mutarotation. This thesis addresses this phenomenon as it has not been 
investigated further before. In this thesis the activation energy for the mutarotation was 
analysed in four different ionic liquids and studied by both in-situ ATR-FTIR spectroscopy 
and ab initio calculations.  
The vibrational frequencies were obtained in the ab initio study and used to identify the 
characteristic bands related to the mutarotation. Two characteristic bands at ∼1062 cm-1 
and ∼1076 cm-1 associated with the α − and β anomer respectively was found.  
 
The reaction rate of α-D-glucopyranose in four different ionic liquids were measured in-situ 
by ATR-FTIR spectroscopy using the band for the formation of the β anomer. Precise 
activation energies were obtained by using the initial rate in the calculation of the Arrhenius 
equation. The activation energy for the mutarotation in [BMIM]Cl was determined to be 
101.21 ±1.09 KJ/mol. The same method was used for the determination of the activation 
energies in the three other ionic liquids; [BMIM]Br, [BMIM]Cl  [BMIM]OAc, and [BMIM]Cl  
[BMIM]CO2CF3 respectively. The activation energies were determined to be 102.16 ±4.71 
KJ/mol, 67.16 ±4.31 KJ/mol and 57.77 ±4.72 KJ/mol respectively.

Introduction 
Carbohydrates have a great impact on biological processes such as production of energy, 
molecular recognition processes, glucoconjugate antibiotics, and cell attachment (Schnupf 
et al. 2010). To date, D-glucopyranose is one of the most widely studied monosaccharides. 
Even though glucose has been studied extensively there are still subjects to be revealed. One 
of them is the kinetics of mutarotation in ionic liquids (Schnupf et al. 2010). Mutarotation 
has been discussed for 170 years since Dubrunfaut described it for the first time in 1846 
[Dubrunfaut, 1846]. Scientist today still discuss the reaction mechanism and the 
phenomenon related to the anomeric effect.  
To study mutarotation in ionic liquids, it is crucial to understand the physical and chemical 
properties related to glucose and carbohydrates in general. The conformation of glucose in 
gas phase is a C1 chair conformation with four hydroxyl groups and one hydroxymethyl 
group. Glucose is often found in two different anomeric forms, α- and β-D-glucopyranose. α 
and β differ in the orientation of the hydroxyl group at the anomeric centre (Le Barc et al. 
2001). 
The kinetics of mutarotation in an aqueous solution are well described but studies of the 
kinetics in ionic liquid have just reached the surface. A. J. Kunov-Kruse has observed that 
ionic liquids have a catalytic effect on mutarotation in the study of biomass conversion 
(Kunov-kruse 2013). This observation has not been stated before and therefore it is 
interesting to study this effect further. 
Previous kinetic studies of glucose in aqueous solution have been conducted with 
polemetric and NMR measurements. The rate of the mutarotation has been determined, the 
geometry of the ∝ and 𝛽 anomer are specified, and the abundance at equilibrium is 
determined with high accuracy.  These studies in aqueous solution give the equilibrium 
ratio of 36 % α-D-Glucopyranose and 64 % β-D-Glucopyranose but also a small amount ~ 1 
% of the open chain species (Polavarapu & Ewig 1992), Lowry, 1929]. Furthermore, the 
NMR data for aqueous solutions indicates that the ∝ and 𝛽 anomer exist in three different 
conformations; Gauche-Gauche (GG), Gauche-Trans (GT), and Trans-Gauche (TG) as 
illustrated below in Figure 1. The most abundant conformer in aqueous solution is the GG 
conformation for both ∝ and 𝛽, but an almost equivalent amount of GT is also present at

Throughout this thesis, the kinetics of mutarotation in four different ionic systems are 
studied in-situ using Attenuated Total Reflectance Fourier Transformation Infrared 
Spectroscopy (ATR-FTIR) method to analyse the influence of different anions on the 
activation energy. The aim is to specify the different activation energies and to qualify the 
properties that are related to the change of anion in the ionic liquid.  The four different ionic 
liquid systems represent different properties related to structure and hydrogen basicity 
strength. Both parameters are subject to investigation in relation to the rate of 
mutarotation.  
Also, quantum mechanical calculations are carried out in this thesis to gain in-depth 
knowledge of geometries, potential energy surfaces, transition for different conformers, 
reaction paths, and so forth. Ab initio studies are used as a guide for experimental study of 
an unknown field. The vibrational frequencies are obtained and used to qualify which bands 
in the infrared spectrum that relate to the mutarotation and which relate to the ionic liquid. 
The calculated infrared spectra are a great tool to investigate overlapping bands and to

clarify if the chosen medium is suitable for the system of interest. Furthermore, the ab initio 
calculations are used to analyse the effects of the anions in the mechanism of mutarotation.

studies of glucose in gas and aqueous phase respectively [Ref].

The Anomeric Effect 
In the early studies of carbohydrates it was clear that the classic conformational analysis 
known from the cyclohexane could not explain the preference for a polar substituent like 
OH at the anomeric centre to obtain the axial position rather than the less hindered 
equatorial position. Today it is still one of the most discussed phenomena in carbohydrate 
chemistry.  
The anomeric centre is critical for the reactivity of carbohydrates since it is where the ring 
opening occurs exposing the important functional carbonyl group. This reaction is called 
mutarotation and will be discussed in the section Mutarotation. As molecular conformations 
are closely related to their physical properties and chemical reactivity, it is essential to 
understand the physical origin of the anomeric effect.  
Figure 2 below illustrates carbohydrate’s preference for the axial position at the anomeric 
centre instead of the less hindered equatorial position.  
Figure 2.  In glucopyranose the OR group at the anomeric position, marked on the figures, prefers the 
axial position rather than the less hindered equatorial position known from the classic conformational 
analysis (Clayden et al. 2012). 
 
 
In the scope of this thesis the anomeric effect is not analysed to gain any conclusion, but the 
phenomenon is enlightened and a review of the recent research is done.  
In 1955 J.T. Edward published a study of carbohydrates where he discussed the unusual 
preference for polar substituents such as OH to be axial instead of equatorial at the anomeric 
centre.  This phenomenon was later studied and termed the anomeric effect by Lemieux 
(1958) (Edward John 1993), (Praly & Lemieux 1987). 
The anomeric effect was first discovered in carbohydrates but now it is recognized in a more 
general term denoted the generalized anomeric effect by Atona (1969). The original 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
definition of the anomeric effect is applicable for pyranoses, but the generalized anomeric 
effect is observed in both cyclic and acyclic molecules having two or more heteroatoms 
linked to the Canomeric tetrahedral centre.  The conformation of this class of compounds

The two models have each their strengths and weaknesses in explaining the conformational

In previous studies of the anomeric effect, it was evident that preference for a polar group 
to be axial decreased in polar solvents. Furthermore, the studies showed geometrical 
differences associated with the anomeric effect, namely the increase of the exocyclic and the 
decrease of the endocyclic C-X bond length as well as widening of the X-C-X bond angle in 
axial conformation (Salzner & Schleyer 1994). Both theories have their justification in 
experimental studies. The hyperconjugative model gives a well-defined explanation of the 
geometrical changes seen in x-ray crystallographic data, where the electrostatic model is 
mostly endorsed by the observation that the magnitude of the anomeric effect decrease 
when the solvent’s dielectric constant increases. Though, for the electrostatic model the 
dipole interaction is unable to explain the geometrical changes and the hyperconjugative 
model is unable to explain the solvent dependency.

Solvation Effect 
The increase in the stability of the equatorial position relatively to the axial is one of the 
most significant results of the solvation effect imposed on glucopyranose structures. The 
strong interaction between the solvent molecules and the lone pairs of the anomeric oxygen 
atom in β-glucopyranose surpasses the combined contributions of the same interactions in 
α anomer (where the lone pairs of the anomeric oxygen are hindered by the rest of the 
molecule) and an anomeric effect which makes the α anomer more abundant in gas phase 
(Karabulut & Leszczynski 2013); (Ha et al. 1991). In the analysis of glucopyranose in 
aqueous solution the ratio of α and β at equilibrium is 36:64, favouring the β conformation. 
The equilibrium ratio suggests that there is a significant force positioning hydroxyl in the 
axial position.  
The change of stability from α to β in solution suggests that the solvation effect is stronger 
than the anomeric effect.  Experimental observations have showed a reduction of the 
anomeric effect in more polar solvents. Salzner has made the same observations by 
theoretically investigations (Salzner & Schleyer 1994). Furthermore, experimental NMR 
studies of glucopyranose in aqueous solution reveal three different conformations of each 
anomer, Gauche/Gauche (GG), Gauche/Trans (GT), and Trans/Gauche (TG) with respect to 
the position of the hydroxymethyl group and the hydroxyl group at the anomeric centre. 
NMR studies predict an overall preference for β and GG and GT conformation to be most 
abundant for both the α and β anomer and only a small amount of the TG conformation. 
However, Ha and Salzer’s delicate ab initio simulations of glucopyranose in a simulated 
solution phase and gas phase have revealed an interesting change [Ref]. In gas phase the 
most stable anomer is the α TG due to the enhanced anomeric effect and intramolecular 
hydrogen bonding stabilization (Salzner & Schleyer 1994). The anomeric effect in terms of 
intramolecular electrostatic forces is the driving force for the α conformer in non-polar 
solutions and in gas phase, whereas free energy simulations with an empirical force field 
show that a substantial intramolecular solute-solvent interaction, mainly electrostatic,

between the sugar and the solvent favours the β anomer of D-glucopyranose (Tvaroska & 
Carver 1998) (Ha et al. 1991). 
In search for answers scientists have debated the amomeric effect within the last 50 years 
on the basis of experimental studies with x-ray crystallography and NMR spectroscopy and 
theoretical ab initio studies. Some have experimented with peptide sensor to confirm 
and/or dismiss theories, and where others have relied on theoretical studies with 
environment simulations and gas phase results. Scientists even disagree about the ab initio 
studies. Some argument that Density Functional Theory (DFT) studies interpreted with 
Natural Bond Orbital (NBO) analysis confirm the hyperconjugative model (Cocinero et al. 
2011), and others have used valence bond theory to confirm the electrostatic model (Wang

Mutarotation 
The mutarotation is a phenomenon closely related to the anomeric effect since it is at the 
anomeric centre the reaction occurs and opens the ring. As stated in the above section the 
solvent has a great influence on the anomeric effect and the abundance of the different 
conformations, and hence the solvent is expected to have a significant influence on the 
mutarotation.  
More than 170 years ago, researchers found that sugars existed as cyclic hemiacetals or as 
hemiketals. The French chemist A. P. Dubrunfaut was the first to describe the time 
dependent optical rotation change of sugar in freshly prepared solution, and the 
mutarotation as a phenomenon was discovered [Dubrunfaut, A. P. Compt. Rend., 1846, 23, 
38; (Horton & Wałaszek 1982); (Lewis et al. 2006).  
The mutarotation of glucose is important to understand due to the chemical reaction 
occurring in living organisms and for industrial purposes. During the mutarotation the 
functional group of the anomeric hemiacetal is exposed. This functional group is of special 
interest for many biological processes [(Brönsted & Guggenheim 1927); Lowry; (Lewis et 
al. 2006). Following the work by Dubrunfaut, Brønsted and Lowry, scientists have worked 
to define the reaction mechanism of acids and bases on glucose in solution, and thereby to 
define the mechanism of the mutarotation to gain a profound understanding of the kinetics.  
==PAGE:22==

Scientists have analysed the specific rotation in aqueous solution by polarmetric 
measurements, NMR, and gas-liquid chromatographic measurements (Hudson & Dale 
1917); (Nelson & Beegle 1919); (HYVÖNEN et al. 1977c)(HYVÖNEN et al. 
1977b)(HYVÖNEN et al. 1977a). With high accuracy Nelson and Beegle specified the 
equilibrium the specific rotation of 𝛼 to 𝛽 to be 52.5°  in aqueous solution, this is further 
proved by Isbell & Pigman in 1937 (Pigman & Isbell 1937). This was the beginning of several 
years of research of the mutarotation.  
Different theories have been proposed for the mechanism behind the mutarotation. Lowry 
(1927) proposed a trimolecular reaction mechanism with a hydrated aldehyde as an 
intermediate. This reaction pathway was later dismissed due to inconsistency with 
experimental data (Silva et al. 2006). A reaction where water is added to the lactone oxygen 
as an oxonium hydrate instead of forming a hydrated aldehyde intermediate have also been 
proposed (Silva et al. 2006) 
Today the most cited reaction mechanism involves the formation of the free aldehyde form 
where the predominant anomer in the solution is converted to the other anomer through 
the open chain conformation to reach equilibrium. The ∝ and 𝛽 anomers have similar

Ionic Liquids 
Ionic liquids have mainly been studied due to their excellent properties of being a solvent 
for drugs and other molecules with low solubility in more common solvents. The special 
characteristics of ionic liquids that distinguish them from other solvents are their ability to 
be a ‘designer’ solvent.  Due to their variety of cations and anions it is possible to mix the 
ions to obtain the desired properties of the ionic liquid [Gou, 2007]. Another important 
feature is that the ionic liquids do not have a significant vapour pressure which makes them 
perfect for in-situ studies due to the constant volume over time, and hence constant 
concentration.  
Three parameters are used to define the properties of ionic liquids, electrostatic (polarity), 
hydrogen bond basicity, and Van der Waals (dispersion). The solubility in ionic liquids is 
found to depend mostly on the anionic part rather than the cationic part. The hydrogen bond 
basicity varies significantly for anions but varies little for cation alternation [Gou, 2007].  
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
The hydrogen bond basicity is predicted to have a significant influence on the rate of 
mutarotation. This expectation is due to the properties found for ionic liquid as a solvent. It 
is found that the hydrogen bond basicity, and hence the properties of the anion, is the 
strongest force for the solubility of flavonoids. The mutarotation is promoted by reaction 
with an anion. The hydrogen bond basicity might influence the anion ability to reach 
reaction site faster.  
The acetate has the highest hydrogen bond basicity of 39.05 and is expected to promote the 
lowest activation energy followed by chloride (36.63), bromide (29.64), and 
trifluoroacetate (20.39).   
For the ab initio study of the mutarotation and the activation energies in this thesis, three 
of the anions above are analysed. The ab initio calculations might explain whether the 
mutarotation is promoted by the anions hydrogen basicity as predicted, or if it is an 
unknown factor that promotes the mutarotation.  
This study in ionic liquids is a new area of investigation, and therefore, it is difficult to 
predict the outcome of the analysis. The theories described above have only been analysed 
in relation to solubility.

Normal Modes  
Normal modes is the vibration modes in a molecule. The number of Normal modes depends 
on the symmetry of the molecule.  If it is assumed that every bond between the atoms in the 
molecule is purely harmonic, when the complex system as a molecule could be broken down 
to the description of a sum of non-interaction harmonic oscillators called normal modes. In 
the harmonic apprximation each normal mode has a related frequency unaffected by the 
other modes in the molecule. Though this assumption is not accomplished in real molecules. 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
Bonds in a real molecule are not harmonic and it results in weakly dependency of normal 
modes affecting the vibrations and frequencies. A phenomenon called molecular vibrations, 
which is a combination of the fundamental frequencies associated with normal modes. 
[Milan, 2012] 
It is possible to predict the number of normal modes in a, theoretical, infrared spectrum. 
Each molecule has three translational degrees of freedom. This results, in 3N degrees of 
freedom for a molecule containing N atoms. Three of these are translations and are not of 
interest for the modes in a molecule and for a non-linear molecule, as glucopyranose, three 
rotational degrees of freedom also exist. The rotational and translational parameters do 
however not contribute to the internal vibrations of a molecule resulting in 3N-6 degrees of 
freedom. Glucopyranose have therefore (3 ∙24) −6 = 66 normal modes. In molecules 
containing symmetry, some of the vibrations might be degenerated resulting in a spectrum 
with fewer modes than predicted. Since glucopyranose is in the symmetry group C1 it has 
no symmetry apart from the identity operator, and all modes should be represented in the 
theoretical spectrum (Engel 2010). 
For the experimental spectra, these modes can be hard to distinguish from each other due

 Ab initio Study 
“The underlying physical laws necessary for the mathematical theory of a large 
part of physics and the whole of chemistry are thus completely known, and the 
difficulty is only that the exact application of these laws leads to equations much 
too complicated to be soluble. “  
- 
P. A. M. Dirac, 1929 
 
As Dirac stated in 1929 the mathematical foundation behind chemistry is complete but the 
difficulties lie in solving the Schrödinger equation exact.  
The ab initio calculations is determined with Hartree-Fock (HF) and Møller-Plesset 
perturbation of second order (MP2) methods. The Hartree-Fock and Møller-Plesset 
perturbation is an approximate solution of the Schrödinger equation. The orbitals are 
expanded on a set of basis functions.  By adding large basis sets the approximation gets close 
to the real energy obtained through experiments. For the quantum mechanical calculations 
the accuracy is restricted by the computational cost (Hehre et al. 1986).Therefore small 
basis set is often used to gain simple knowledge. For more detailed analysis in relation to 
experimental studies larger basis sets are used. For molecules or systems as glucose the 
electron correlation is important to include to calculate a more precise energy. The MP2 
includes the electron correlation in the calculations. Ab initio calculations are performed on 
a gas phase system and on single molecules which complicates the comparison with 
experimental data.  
In the following calculations the HF method is used to determine the geometrical 
information for the different glucose isomers. After limiting the number of isomers of 
interest, more accurate calculations are performed with the MP2 method.  
Both HF and MP2 calculations are performed using the 6-31G* basis set. In general, it is 
necessary to employ split-valence basis set with inclusion of polarization functions and to 
correct for electron correlation (Hehre 2010). This basis set describes the core electrons by 
six gauss function contracted to one, and for the valence orbitals the basis set is split into 
two functions where the first is three gauss functions contracted into one and one primitive 
Gaussian function. ‘*’ denotes that the basis set contains a group of d polarization functions.  
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
The Møller-Plesset perturbation theory is based upon the assumption that a complicated 
system can be solved by using a simple system where the wave functions and energies are 
known. Furthermore, it is assumed that the changes in the wave function and energies 
change evenly with the perturbation, and the changes in both can be described by expanding 
the energies and the wavefunctions. For the Møller-Plesset perturbation of second order, 
the correction energies can be obtained. For more accurate energies the MP4 method can 
be applied which is of the fourth order in the perturbation (Hehre 2010); (Engel 2010).1  
The software program SPARTAN ‘14 by Wavefunction Inc. is used for all the ab initio 
calculations and based on molecular orbital theory [Hehre (SPARTAN), 2014]. A systematic 
analysis of possible conformers of both α and β glucose molecules are determined by full 
geometry optimisation at the HF/6-31G* level of theory. Second-order Møller-Plesset 
perturbation (MP2) method is used to include electron correlation with 6-31G* basis set, 
the perturbation is also performed on all α and β conformations used for further studies.  
For more advanced calculations such as determination of the transition states, only the MP2 
method is used since Hartree-Fock overestimates the relative energies of the transition 
structure (Hehre et al. 1986). The vibrational frequencies are predicted by the MP2/6-31G* 
method.  
The HF method contains a known systematic error due to the neglect of electron correlation, 
resulting in overestimation of the vibrational frequencies. Therefore, it is usual to scale the 
predicted frequencies at the Hartree-Fock method by an empirical factor of 0.8929. This 
scale factor have been demonstrated to produce very good correlation with experimental 
data (Araujo-Andrade et al. 2005); (Foresman 1996). For the vibrational frequencies 
calculated with MP2 the empirical scale factor of 0.9427 is used (Foresman 1996). 
The great number of isomers is one of the most influential issue complicating carbohydrate 
studies and hence ab initio studies. The energies of each isomer have to be calculated to find 
the lowest energy conformation for further investigation.  
Previous quantum mechanical calculations of the lowest energy conformation in the gas 
phase reveal that the αTG, αGG, and βGG conformation, respectively, have the lowest energy 
of all the conformers (Jebber et al. 1996). For the experimental studies in aqueous solution 
the abundance of the conformers is significantly different from that derived in calculations

ab initio study of glucose both αTG and αGG conformers are investigated. For the analysis 
of the β conformer, only the GG conformer is analysed since the preferences are the same in

ATR-FTIR Spectroscopy  
Fundamental of Spectroscopy  
Spectroscopy is a widely used technique to identify a given molecules properties, from 
functional groups to kinetics. Spectroscopy is based upon the transition between energy 
stages when a molecule is exposed for light. The attenuation of the incident radiation 
resulting from absorption of radiation is measures as function of the incident wavelength. 
Absorption spectra consist of different bands related to the transition between two energy 
levels.  
The transition between energy levels induced by a photon is described as  
 
ℎ𝜈= |𝐸2 −𝐸1| 
(eq. 14) 
 
Where ℎ is the Planck constant, 𝜈 is the frequency and E2 and E1 is the energies respectively.  
The selection rule for vibrations to occur is that the bond, must contain a dipole moment.  
To analyse and observe the mutarotation in-situ it is beneficial to study the vibrational 
frequencies related to the anomeric centre beforehand. It is expected that the two different 
anomers will have a slightly change in their vibrational frequencies related to the 
mutarotation due to the axial and equatorial position of the OH group.  
The vibrational frequency depends mainly of the two vibrating atoms on each of a bond and 
less on atoms further away. This gives the well-known group frequency, which makes 
infrared spectroscopy highly suitable for characterizing molecules functional groups (Engel 
2010). 
==PAGE:64==

FTIR – Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy  
Fourier Transformed Infrared spectroscopy is the mostly used method. It is a mathematical 
approach where the interferograms is transformed from the time domain to frequency 
domain. First an interferogram has to be obtained. A beam is emitted from the light source, 
through a sample and the incident wave reaches a beam splitter, which splits it into two 
separate beams. Each beam is reflected into mirrors. One of the beams is reflected into a 
stationary mirror where the second is reflected into a mobile mirror. Due to the difference 
in distance, the two beams reach the detector at different times. The intensity varies with 
distance as the mobile mirror is moved towards the beam splitter, which results in a signal. 
The signal is measured by the detector called interferogram as a result of the interference 
of the two waves (Engel 2010). 
The data measured in the interferogram cannot be directly analysed for the characteristic 
absorption frequencies, by using Fourier transform techniques, the interferogram can be 
converted from a time domain to a frequency domain which result in the IR-spectrum 
normally known.

Attenuated Total Reflectance – Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy. This technique 
enables the monitoring of individual species in –situ, and based upon the principle on

The Principles of Attenuated Total Reflectance Spectroscopy 
The principles in Attenuated Total Reflectance Spectrocopy do in many ways resemble the 
usual transmission spectroscopy, and with some mathematical manipulations it is possible 
to obtain a spectrum almost identical to a transmission spectrum. For obtaining a useful 
transmission spectrum, a time consuming and difficult sample preparation is needed. In 
transmission spectroscopy a thin film of the sample is crucial otherwise the sample will 
absorb all the light and hence impossible to gain any information of the samples vibrations 
(Holm & Palik 1978); Milan 2012].    
Organic compounds with weak absorption are difficult to analyse with the transmission 
spectroscopy. The initial work of Jacques Fahrenfort (Fahrenfort 1961) and N.J. Harrick 
(Harrick 1967) describes the essence of the concept behind attenuated total reflection 
spectroscopy and with this method it is possible to study liquid samples and reactions in 
liquid, pastes and solids without the problems connected to the sample preparation to 
obtain a thin film (Fahrenfort 1961); (Harrick 1967). 
The principle of ATR relies on using a prism in which a sample is placed. An infrared beam 
is emitted from the light source through the prism in which the beam is attenuated through 
the sample of interest back through the prism and through the FTIR system described 
above.  
By using a prism with a high refracting index the wave can leave the prisms and penetrates 
the sample with a penetration depth. The sample absorbs the energy transferred from the 
incident light and thereby decreasing the intensity of the reflected light – the light is then 
attenuated.  
The reflection of light through a prism with a high refracting index is based on the theory of 
radiation propagation in an object with a refractive index 𝜂1undergoes total internal 
reflection at an interface of an adjacent medium of lower refractive index, 𝜂2. The reflected 
wave through the prism and hence the sample is termed evanescent. The refractive index of

and must exceeds a critical angle 𝜃𝑐 [Mirabella, 1993; (Fahrenfort 1961).

In-situ Investigation of the Mutarotation of D-glucopyranose’s  
From previous studies by A. J. Kunov-Kruse it is known that [BMIM]Cl decelerate the 
mutaroration at room temperature compared to glucose in aqueous solution (Kunov-kruse 
2013). To gain more knowledge about mutarotation in ionic liquids a study is set up. Four 
different 
ionic 
liquids 
are 
chosen 
due 
to 
their 
different 
properties.  
The reason behind testing this group of ionic liquids is partially to see why some of the ionic 
liquids almost inhibit the mutarotation and some have catalytic effect on the rotation, and 
partially for the ease of making suitable computer models. 
Some of the properties related to the different ionic liquids is the charge distribution and 
the hydrogen bond basicity. [BMIM]Cl and [BMIM]Br both have spherical negative charge 
distribution where [BMIM]OAc and [BMIM]CF3CO2 have a more localized distribution of the 
negative charge. The localized charge might be easier to get to reach the reaction site 
compared to the spherical distributed charge. For the bond basicity the anions is in 
following order OAc > Cl >Br  > CF3CO2. The hydrogen basicity have been illustrated to 
increase solubility and that the hydrogen basicity is primarily dependent on the anion, 
which is the key factor in the promoting ring opening [Guo, 2007].  
β-D-glucopyranose is used as reference for the mutarotation to α-D-glucopyranose.  
It is found that the speed of the mutarotation in [BMIM]OAc and [BMIM]CF3CO2 is too fast 
to be able to record the mutarotation in-situ (Kunov-kruse 2013). Furthermore it have been 
showed that the [BMIM]Cl slows the mutarotation (Kunov-kruse 2013). To control the 
speed of the mutarotation [BMIM]Cl, [BMIM]OAc or [BMIM]CF3CO2 is mixed.  
Preparing the Ionic Liquids and Ionic Liquid/glucose solution  
In the preparation of the ionic liquids, the most important treatment is to remove water 
from the sample. Ionic liquid purchased from Sigma-Aldrichis is 97-99% pure, but it is fairly 
hydrophilic and absorbs water from the environment quickly. Therefore it is necessary to 
dry each sample before use and seal the dry sample.  The ionic liquid is evaporated under 
vacuum at approximately 353K with magnetic stirring for 1 hour. The ionic liquid is tested 
for the water content by recording a spectrum to ensure a reasonably pure sample. The 
reason for removing water from the ionic liquid is to prevent water to promote the 
mutarotation and thereby providing misleading kinetic information.  
==PAGE:69==

Also, ∝−D −glucopyranose does contains a small amount of water and, again, to reduce 
the influence of water in the measurements of the mutarotation, the ∝−D −glucopyranose 
is dried under vacuum for 1 hour at approximately 353K.  
In the first sample preparation of [BMIM]Br, the sample crystallized within few minutes. 
The crystals were in small flakes, they look fully homogeneous, they melt easily at 353K, and 
hence it was chosen to analyse the sample. If the sample indeed is fully homogenized the 
concentration profile should remain the same throughout the Arrhenius study. The benefit 
of having a homogeneous crystalized sample is that it remains stable at room temperature 
without the need of being frosen and kept at dry ice which is related to a high risk of sample

In-situ ATR-FTIR Spectroscopy  
All the in-situ ATR-FTIR experiments were carried out using Thermo Scientific Nicolet iS50 
FT-IR with Specac Golden Gate ™ ATR cell and the Specac High Temperature Golden Gate 
Controller™for accurate temperature control. As explained previously, the light beam from 
the IR spectrometer in the ATR cell is focused through precisely positioned mirrors into the 
diamond. The exposed area of the diamond, represent the sampling spot for recording of 
the IR spectrum. The direct contact between the sample and the diamond is one of the main 
principles in Attenuated Total Reflectance spectroscopy. The evanescent wave passes 
through the diamond to the sample and back through the diamond to be collected at the 
detector. 
Correction of the ATR spectra   
One of the advantages of using ATR-FTIR instead of ordinary transmission spectroscopy is 
the simple sample preparation such as making thin film. This is due to the principle of the 
penetration depth, in which the attenuated wave does not depend on sample thickness. 
[Bruker.com/optics, 2011]   This phenomenon is described in the previous section ATR-
FTIR Spectroscopy.  
OMNIC™ is a data collecting software program from Thermo Scientific. With OMNIC™ it is 
possible to achieve a real time monitoring of the spectrum of the sample on the ATR crystal. 
The real time view of the spectra allows for an immediate analysis of the sample. In 
OMNIC™, a sophisticated algorithm corrects the position and intensity of the absorption 
band in an ATR-spectrum to match it with a transmission spectrum of the same sample 
[Bruker.com/optics, 2011; Omnic™ Spectra Software, 2012]     
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
A disadvantages of the algorithm is that, at times, it is difficult to determine the exact 
refractive index of the sample which is needed. For more common substances the refractive 
index can be found in literature, but for less studied substances as ionic liquids in a mixture 
this information can be difficult to obtain. A refractive index of D-glucopyranose is 
estimated to 1.5 in all ionic liquids (Kunov-kruse 2013). 
ATR-FTIR is a sensitive system and therefore some complications such as drifting of the 
spectra during a series can occur. Issues such as drifting are corrected manually by 
assuming zero absorbance at 4000 cm-1.  
Background spectra 
As illustrated in the section Theoretical Infrared Spectra do the ionic liquids have smaller 
bands in the same area as the characteristic bands of  as glucose hence the bands observed 
is a combination of that of the ionic liquid and that of the glucose. A difference spectrum 
enables removal of the contribution from the ionic liquid and leaves only the contribution 
related to the glucopyranose (Kunov-kruse 2013). 
Difference spectra are obtained by recording a background spectrum for the ionic liquid 
during each experiment. The background is subtracted from each recorded spectrum in the 
series in the deconvolution. As a result only the contribution from the glucose is obtained, 
thus it is possible to analyse the mutarotation in-situ. This is done under the assumption 
that the background is stable during the entire in-situ study.  
This correlates well for most of the experiments. However, it is not confirmed for 
[BMIM]OAc. The significant bands for acetate change position during the experiment. The 
bands shift further down in the spectra, which indicates a weakened interaction between 
the imidazolium ion and the acetate. The suggested theory behind the observation is that 
the hydroxyl groups in glucose are interfering with the bonding interaction between the 
imidazolium ion and the acetate. To verify this postulate an experiment is set up.  
First and foremost, it is important to analyse if it is indeed the hydroxyl groups in glucose 
that interferes with the interactions in the ionic liquid and thereby changes the acetate 
bands in the background for [BMIM]OAc.  
Water is used to simulate the possible hydrogen interactions equally to glucose. For glucose 
it is assumed that each hydroxyl group contributes with one hydrogen interaction. For 
water the assumption is one water molecule is equal to one hydrogen interaction.  Hence 
==PAGE:73==
73 
 
six water molecules are set to be equal to one glucose molecule. In the recorded ATR 
spectrum for water in [BMIM]OAc a similar shift in the acetate bands is observed.  It is 
demonstrated that glucose changes the intermolecular bonding interactions and confirms 
the previous stated postulate.  
For further analysis of the mutarotation in [BMIM]OAc, a background corresponding to the 
shift during the experiment is needed. 
Background estimation for [𝑩𝑴𝑰𝑴]𝑶𝑨𝒄∙ 𝑯𝟐𝑶  
The reaction mixture for the simulation should be similar to the solution for the experiment 
of glucose in [BMIM]OAc. To justify it the assumption of the ratio six water molecules to one 
glucose molecule is a close approximation of the reaction mixture of the experiment, a range 
of concentrations is set up. For simplicity, the range is chosen going from half the expected 
concentration and up to the double of the expected concentration in four steps.  
For each addition of water an ATR spectra is recorded at 303K. The analysis confirms that 
six water molecules equal one glucose molecule, and the recorded spectra with a water 
content of 200µL equal to 10 wt% α-D-glucopyranose is used as background for the further 
analysis.  
Deconvolution 
Deconvolution of the recorded spectra is a time consuming task, and consequently, A. J. 
Kunov-Kruse developed a script to handle the great amount of manual work in 2013. The 
script is based on a linear combination of Gauss functions for estimation of the area under 
the band. The script establishing the Gauss function describing glucopyranose is used as 
initial guess of approximation for the recorded spectra (Kunov-kruse 2013). 
Gauss functions are a rough approximation of infrared bands. Compared with Gauss 
functions, infrared band have a broader top but narrower tails. It is also common to use 
Lorentz functions as approximation to infrared bands. For Lorentz functions the opposite 
of the Gauss functions is valid. The Lorentz function is narrower in the top but broader at 
the tails. For the purpose of this analysis, only the area of the band is essential and hence 
the area under a gauss function is in a good resembles of the real band area.  
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
A more accurate estimation of an infrared band is to use Voigt function, which is a 
convolution of Gauss and Lorentz functions but the mathematical formulation is more 
complicated and have no significant improvement of the estimation of the area.  
Each Gauss function can be described by three parameters; amplitude, centre position, and 
width of the curve. For the background fitting (BG), a sum of fourteen Gauss function is 
found to be suitable for the description of the bands (Kunov-kruse 2013).

Even though the script for the deconvolution reduces the amount of manual work, a few 
manual steps are required. One of this is the correction of the baseline. The gas flow is 
mounted manually on the ATR cell to ensure an inert environment, which creates a 
disturbance in the baseline. The pressure applied to mount the gas varies due to human 
hand and changing temperature. To eliminate this disturbance a background spectrum of 
the ionic liquid is recorded without the inert gas flow. The same is done with the reaction 
sample. It should be mentioned, that a minor error might occur due to the exposure of the

sample to air and moisture. The background is subtracted from the reaction spectra, hence

the recorded in-situ study is stable. E.g. for [BMIM]Cl ∙α −D −glucopyranose it is observed 
that there is no change associated with the 950cm−1 position. After the subtraction it is 
evident that this position should be corrected with 0.0016 amplitude units for all recorded

pressure on the ATR cell. For [BMIM]Br ∙α −D −glucopyranose an disturbance from the 
apparatus is causing a significant drifting in the recordings. A manual correction is applied 
by assuming zero absorbance at 4000cm−1and correction factors ranging from 0.0078 to 
0.0089 amplitude units are used at 408K.  
In the deconvolution of the recorded spectra an output containing the area of each band of 
interest is achieved. In this study it is only the area of the bands in the range 
from 940 cm−1 to 1140cm−1 which is used for the determination of the initial reaction rate, 
and hence, in the determination of the activation energies.

mutarotation.  
 
As described in the section Ionic Liquid and Ab initio Calculations it is expected that the 
hydrogen basicity is the most influenced factor on the mutarotation. For the activation 
energies measured in the in-situ ATR-FTIR this is not the case. The activation energies 
indicate a structural preferences. Concerning the acetates (acetate and trifluoroacetate), 
both have a more localized charge distribution which might be the strongest force for 
mutarotation.  
Deviations and Experimental Errors 
During the experiments different observations were made that could enhance the accuracy 
of experiments and limit experimental errors.  
When the ionic liquids were bought at Sigma-Aldrich the purity was around 99 %, though, 
a significant amount of water was detected and hence was all the elements were dried at 
approximate 80 C for 1 h before use, including α-D-glucopyranose. In the in-situ study it 
was clear that water imposes a major influence on the measurements.  
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
Though the temperature was controlled with Specac High Temperature Golden Gate 
Controller™ it was difficult to obtain a constant temperature at 25 ℃.  
Complete homogeneity in the sample has been assumed. The majority of the samples did 
crystalized over time. The exact time of crystallization is unknown and it is possible that 
some of the measurements were made on small crystals. Though, the concentration was 
observed during each measurement ensuring no significant change in concentration.   
A disadvantages of working with liquids was the possibility of ionic liquid leaking down the 
sides of the diamond crystal, and hence, contributing to the measured spectra. Therefore, it 
was important to be careful when applying the sample on the crystal.  
The estimation of the refractive index used to correct the ATR spectrum was set to be the 
same for all the measurements. To improve the measurements and limit the deviations, the 
refractive index should be determined for each system. The mathematical approximations 
of the bands in the deconvolution may have introduced a minor deviation compared to the 
true activation energies. 
Discussion  
The ab initio investigation is purely in gas phase whereas all the in-situ measurements are 
in a liquid phase. The analysed reaction path for the mutarotation in gas phase is promoted 
by just one anion. It has previously been stated that at least eight to nine molecules are 
needed to simulate the experimental mutarotation in a solution (Suzuki & Sota 2003). Even 
though it is not possible to compare the results obtain from the ab initio investigation with 
the results in the in-situ measurements, the ab initio investigations can be used as a 
guideline for what to expect in the in-situ experiments. Furthermore, the ab initio study 
gives a great fundamental knowledge of the mechanisms involved in mutarotation and the 
kinetics.  
The vibrational frequencies determined in the ab initio study have been useful in the 
characterization of bands in the experimental obtained spectra.  
In the in-situ study, a large group of samples was dismissed due to poor dissolution of 
glucose and crystallization of the sample over time. Furthermore, it was observed in many 
==PAGE:87==
87 
 
of the measurements that the sample contained a significant amount of water. The 
consequence was to make new samples and new measurements. 
Experience in sample preparation and in using ATR-FTIR can eliminate waste of samples 
and time. 
Even though many of the measurement have been repeated and improved a couple of times, 
the obtained activation energies should be interpret carefully. To ensure statistically 
reliable results the in-situ measurements have to be reproduced.  
The results in the in-situ study indicate that a lower activation energy is obtained for the 
ionic liquids with a localized charge distribution rather than depend on the hydrogen bond 
basicity as first assumed. To fully understand the influence of the ionic liquids an extended 
study have to be made.  
Another interesting approach would be to make a fully homogeneous solid sample. A solid 
sample is more stable and the problems with recrystallization could be eliminated. One 
solid sample was analysed in this thesis, but unfortunately it was not homogenous. The 
analyse was purely of curiosity and the sample was not produced for the purpose. 
Therefore, the possibility for making a homogeneous solid sample should not be dismissed 
on basis of this analysis.  
Conclusion  
The aim of this thesis was to measure the activation energy for α-D-glucopyranose’s 
mutarotation in four different ionic liquids by using in-situ ATR-FTIR spectroscopy and by 
assuming that the initial rate could be described by Arrhenius equation. Glucose’s 
mutarotation was also to be investigated ab initio. Furthermore, the ab initio study was used 
as a guide for the analysis of the bands associated with the mutarotation in the in-situ study.  
In the ab initio investigation of the mutarotation it was found that the mutarotation goes 
through one of two reaction mechanisms, a SN2 mechanism resulting in the break of the 
anomeric stereogenic centre and the endocyclic oxygen, or a mechanism deprotonating the 
anomeric OH group and protonating the endocyclic oxygen which promotes the break of the 
bond between the anomeric stereogenic centre and the endocyclic oxygen.  
==PAGE:88==
88 
 
For the chloride-, acetate-, and trifluoroacetate-anion the mutarotation is promoted by the 
SN2 mechanism while hydroxide deprotonates the anomeric OH group. The hydroxide ion 
is basic and will pulls in the acidic hydrogen. In an aqueous environment would the 
hydroxide be stabilized by two or more water molecules and the simulation with water 
cluster changes the hydroxide ion reaction into the SN2 mechanism.  
The vibrational frequencies obtained in the ab initio study was a great tool to predict the 
bands obtained in the in-situ study. Two characteristic infrared bands related to the 
stretching of the anomeric bonds, and hence the mutarotation, were identified at ∼1062cm-
1 and ∼1076 cm-1 associated with the α − and β anomer respectively.  
The reaction rate of α-D-glucopyranose in four different ionic liquid was measured in-situ 
by ATR-FTIR spectroscopy using the band of the β anomer formation. The activation 
energies were determined by plotting the initial rates in relation to the Arrhenius equations. 
The activation energy for the mutarotation in [BMIM]Cl was determined to be 101.21 ±1.09 
KJ/mol. The same method was used for the determination of the activation energies in the 
three other ionic liquids; [BMIM]Br, [BMIM]Cl  [BMIM]OAc, and [BMIM]Cl  [BMIM]CO2CF3 
respectively. The activation energies were determined to be 102.16 ±4.71 KJ/mol, 67.16 
±4.31 KJ/mol and 57.77 ±4.72 KJ/mol respectively. 
The activation energies were also determined ab initio. Though, instead of bromide the 
hydroxide ion was analysed.  
From the analysed reaction profiles of the αTG-D-glucopyranose and αGG-D-glucopyranose 
to the βGG-D-glucopyranose the activation energies were determined with MP2/6-31G* 
level of theory. The activation energies were determined as follows;

The ab initio determination of the activation energies indicated that the hydrogen bond

mutarotation.  
The in-situ study indicated that the strongest force is a structural mechanism. It was clear 
that acetate-like structures accelerate the mutarotation where the spherical anion 
deaccelerates the mutarotation.  
However, a definitive conclusion cannot be made from this study alone. The experimental 
study has to be recreated to ensure that the validity of the findings, and more anions could 
be included in the ab initio study for a better simulation of the experimental set-up.
