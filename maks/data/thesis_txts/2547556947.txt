Abstract 
Due to rising global temperatures, warming of permafrost is occurring at an alarming rate and with 
dire effects. In mountainous regions, its degradation in bedrock is altering landscapes and ecosystems, 
as well as directly impacting human infrastructure and safety by increasing the risk of natural hazards 
such as rock falls and landslides. Numerous events of this sort have been recorded in the past decades 
wherein the melting of permafrost has been attributed as a leading trigger. In the European Alps 
especially, much infrastructure – mainly for tourism and recreation, but also for energy production – 
is located near steep permafrost-rich slopes and bedrock. This is also the case at the Zugspitze mountain 
in Germany, where permafrost has been discovered in the steep rock walls below the mountain’s crest. 
Over the years, an increasing number of structures have been built in this area, including cables cars, 
restaurants and huts, which together host an estimated half a million tourists a year. Consequently, the 
importance of monitoring this permafrost has grown, much of which is performed inside an excavated 
tunnel beneath the summit – the “Kammstollen”. For over a decade, researchers at the Technical 
University of Munich (TUM) have been carrying out monthly monitoring of the tunnel’s walls using 
geophysical surveying techniques such as electrical resistivity tomography (ERT) and relative micro-
gravimetry. These, together with direct temperature measurements, have enabled them to image the 
subsurface. This study introduces an additional geoelectrical method – time-domain induced 
polarization (TDIP) – as a novel approach for characterizing bedrock permafrost. An experimental 
setup was implemented to effectively measure the natural IP response (chargeability) of the rock and 
thus obtain information on the water/ice content. During a period of four months, multiple 
measurements were taken along two transects of the tunnel, each with different conditions in terms of 
the rock’s temperature and saturation. With the aim of developing a precise and efficient methodology, 
a number of factors for data acquisition were tested and examined: (ⅰ) cable types, (ⅱ) electrode arrays, 
(ⅲ) tunnel conditions, (ⅳ) transmitter settings. The raw data was inspected and then inverted using 
RES2DInv to create 2D cross-sectional tomograms. The results of the research found that: (ⅰ) 
unshielded multicore cables are most efficient without compromising on data quality; (ⅱ) Wenner array 
is most consistent and least sensitive to electromagnetic coupling effects; (ⅲ) there are clear differences 
in the rock conditions of the two transects, seen by the highly varied chargeability values; (ⅳ) factors 
such as the length of the IP windows for capturing the voltage decay, as well as the minimum injected 
current and its acquisition time, are all vital for achieving good results. Moreover, a clear correlation 
was observed between the ERT and IP results from the same transect. The quality of the data was 
assessed via statistical analyses, that verified the importance of taking measurements multiple times to 
attain reliable results. The study confirmed that an IP response can be detected and has established the 
methodological groundwork for future monitoring. In order to implement long-term surveying using 
this technique, further research will be required to understand the relation between the measured 
chargeability values and the presence/absence of water/ice in the rock.

1 Introduction 
 Motivation 
1.1.1 Mountain permafrost 
The term permafrost is defined as lithospheric material that remains at, or below, a temperature of 
0℃ for at least two consecutive years (Permafrost Subcommittee NCR Canada, 1988). In the 
northern hemisphere it underlies 24% of the Earth’s land surface (Zhang et al., 2008), primarily in 
the polar and circumpolar regions, but also in alpine regions at both high and low latitudes. 
Permafrost exists in different forms such as vegetated soil, rock glaciers, glacier deposits, or in 
steep bedrock. The depth from the surface at which seasonal freezing and thawing occurs is defined 
as the active layer and has a typical thickness of 0.5-8 m (Gruber & Heaberli, 2008). Daily freeze-
thaw cycles occur as well. In the Swiss Alps for example, such daily cycles can reach 20-30 cm 
depth in bedrock (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008), while annual freeze-thaw cycles can reach depths 
on a magnitude of several meters (Krautblatter and Hauck, 2007). Perhaps the most distinctive 
characteristic of mountain permafrost is its very high spatial variability and discontinuity, even 
over short distances. This is influenced by various surface and near-surface features and properties, 
namely topography (elevation, slope, aspect, roughness), material composition, and snow cover. 
These affect surface micro-climatology – with ground temperatures varying by many degrees 
within a given region – and as a consequence, the distribution of permafrost. Acquiring data on 
mountain permafrost is a challenging task as its occurrence is often sparse and spotted only in 
areas where infrastructure has been constructed. Being a thermal condition, and located beneath 
an active later, it is essentially ‘invisible’ – making it very difficult to detect both remotely and at 
the ground surface. Moreover, based on its definition, at least two years of measurements are 
necessary in order to reliably detect and identify the permafrost table. Deeper investigations can 
also be made to determine the presence or absence of permafrost. However, these can be costly 
and time consuming, and disturb the thermal conditions if executed by drilling. For all of the above 
reasons, the research of mountain permafrost entails numerous challenges for the scientific 
community (Gruber & Heaberli, 2009). Many studies have attempted to create regional permafrost 
distribution models for the European Alps; yet they cannot be compiled into one map as they are 
mostly calibrated for a specific region, use different methods, and exclude large areas (Boeckli et 
al., 2012; Kenner et al., 2019). Boeckli et al (2012) were the first to develop a uniform statistical 
model to estimate the permafrost distribution for the entire region. They generated The Alpine 
Permafrost Index Map (APIM) which assigned an index value ranging from 0 to 1 for the 
likelihood of permafrost occurrence, and concluded that for an index value ≥ 0.5 permafrost can 
be found in 3% of the European Alps. Additional studies in which permafrost distribution has been

1.1.2 
Permafrost degradation and implications 
According to the IPCC fifth assessment report (AR5): “permafrost temperatures have increased in 
most regions since the early 1980s in response to increased surface temperature and changing snow 
cover” (IPCC, 2014), referring to both high-latitude and high-elevation regions. Moreover, the 
report attributes climatic warming as a leading cause of permafrost degradation, that will very 
likely continue into the 21st century with increasing global temperatures. This has been established 
based on various studies and field observations, many of which originated from the EU-funded 
Permafrost and Climate in Europe (PACE) Project. The results of this project can be found in the 
extensive work of Harris et al., 2009 who provide a thorough overview of the current status of 
permafrost in Europe, as well as paleoclimate reconstructions for assessing historical trends of 
permafrost appearance and disappearance. A decade later, Biskaborn et al (2019) compiled a 
global data set of permafrost temperature time series from the Global Terrestrial Network for 
Permafrost (GTN-P) in which they further demonstrated the extent of permafrost warming in the 
European Alps. Data from both researches will play a crucial role in future monitoring of 
permafrost distribution as time series become longer.   
The degradation of permafrost results in a deeper active layer with the receding of the permafrost 
table, as well as of the base of the layer – albeit at a much slower rate. Nevertheless, with time this 
could lead to the disappearance of permafrost altogether. Magnin et al (2015) found that in the 
Mont Blanc massif (French Alps), narrow peaks below 3850 m a.s.l might experience a complete 
loss of permafrost by the end of the 21st century. In many places, permafrost exists close to its 
melting point (within 1-2 ℃), making it particularly sensitive to warming temperatures. That said, 
colder permafrost actually warms more rapidly than that around its melting point. Similarly, 
bedrock tends to warm faster than glacial debris or soil due to its lower ice content (IPCC SP, 
2019).  
The impacts of melting permafrost are vast. In arctic and sub-arctic regions, it is accelerating 
climate change by exposing organic matter stored in previously frozen soils, causing them to 
decompose and thereby release massive amounts of methane, a strong greenhouse gas (Schuur et 
al., 2015). In alpine regions, it is altering the evolution of landscapes and ecosystems and affecting 
human safety and infrastructure by increasing the risk of natural hazards such as rock falls, debris 
flows, soil creep, and ground subsidence. While the time-scale of the effects indeed varies between 
these regions, the significance of their implications is unquestionable.

1.1.3 Effects on slope stability of steep rock walls   
Warming-induced permafrost degradation in rock walls is already showing in high-mountain areas 
(Gruber & Haeberli, 2007). Due to an absence of insulation – namely snow, vegetation, or soil – 
the interaction between atmospheric and ground temperatures is very strong on steep bedrock 
slopes (Harris et al., 2009). Three principal factors govern the long-term stability of icy rock walls 
in high mountains: geology, topography, and ice conditions, with changes in the latter being the 
most rapid (Haeberli et al., 2017). While equivocal ideas on the relation between permafrost and 
slope stability have existed for a long time, it has only been in the past few decades that more 
advanced theories and observations have come about (Gruber & Haeberli, 2007; Krautblatter et 
al., 2013; Deline et al., 2015). A rock/ice-mechanical model developed by Krautblatter et al (2013) 
considered the relation between shear forces and shear resistance of frozen rocks, so as to gain a 
better understanding of the mechanical destabilization processes that take place in warming 
(permafrost-rich) rocks. They noted that the change of rock temperature – primarily in the range 
of -5 to 0℃ – greatly alters the mechanical properties of water-saturated rock and ice-filled joints. 
Such changes can lead to destabilization of the rock, especially on steep slopes, and are considered 
to significantly contribute to rock-slope failures (Verleysdonk et al., 2011; Krautblatter et al., 
2013). Their model further showed that rock-mechanical properties tend to become more important 
for higher normal stress and greater depths, i.e. high-magnitude failures; while ice-mechanical 
properties become dominant after the initial deformation accelerates and are more substantial for 
shallower depths and smaller magnitude failures.   
Because of the high energy potential stored in steep environments, and the possibility of compound 
events occurring, the outcomes of failure events can at times be devastating (Deline et al., 2015). 
An example of such a disastrous event is the Kolka-Karmadon ice-rock avalanche on September 
2002 in the Caucasus which claimed 125 lives (Haeberli et al., 2004). Yet not all mass movements 
– whether caused by permafrost degradation or in general – are on this scale, but rather occur at a 
wide range of magnitudes and frequencies. Over the last decades, an increasing number of 
hazardous rockfalls and rockslides of various magnitudes have occurred in alpine regions 
(Krautblatter et al., 2010). This is especially true for the summer of 2003 in which the European 
Alps experienced extremely hot and dry weather that led to considerably deeper active layers of 
the permafrost, and in turn, to many rockfall events of steep bedrock detachments. Most of these 
events were triggered without heavy rainfall or seismic activity and exposed large areas of ice in 
the detachment zones (Schiermeier, 2003; Gruber et al., 2004b); ice that is believed to have acted 
as a cementing product for discontinuities along these (failure) zones (Harris et al., 2009). That 
said, analyzing the cause of each individual failure, or attributing it directly to warming permafrost, 
is still a difficult task with high uncertainties.

In the European Alps, much infrastructure is built directly beneath or near steep bedrock

and magnitude.

   Research questions & aim of thesis 
The aim of this thesis is to use the time-domain induced polarization technique to acquire 
information on the water/ice content in the rock walls of the Zugspitze mountain in Bavaria, 
Germany. To our knowledge, this geophysical approach for permafrost characterization in steep 
bedrock walls is novel and has not yet been attempted. The work consisted of monthly to bi-
monthly field work, as well as extensive literature review and data analysis.  
As such, the following questions are addressed: 
• 
Can time-domain induced polarization be used at the Kammstollen to obtain information 
on frozen/unfrozen and/or wet/dry rock? 
• 
What IP set-up configuration is most effective for that in terms of array, cable type and IP 
parameters? 
• 
Can a correlation be found between results from ERT and IP measurements?

2 Study Site 
 Geographical and geological setting 
This study focuses on permafrost and ice-filled joints in the steep rock walls of the Zugspitze (2963 
m a.s.l). The Zugspitze is part of the Wetterstein range in the Northern Limestone Alps at the 
German-Austrian border and is the highest mountain in Germany. The upper part of the mountain 
is composed of a thick (~800 m) limestone massif belonging to the Triassic Wetterstein formation 
that is stratified in beds tens of meters thick (Miller, 1961). Its lower parts consist primarily of 
limestones and dolostones of the Partnach, Reifling, Steinalm and Gutenstein formations, 
intercalated with marlstones and marls (Haas et al., 2014). The mountain’s crest is oriented mainly 
east-west with steep slopes facing north at an inclination of 60°-70° and south at ~35°. Due to a 
fault line, the bedrock near the summit is highly weathered and crevassed (Körner and Ulrich, 
1965).  
 Permafrost investigations and conditions  
Since the Miocene, karst dissolution has created a subsurface drainage system in the bedrock of 
the Zugspitze that is occasionally blocked by permafrost above 2500 m a.s.l (Krautblatter et al., 
2010), with the majority located near the mountain’s crest in both bedrock and regolith (Gude et 
al., 2005). Its distribution highly depends on topography (slope inclination and elevation) and 
shading effects (slope aspect), with the south face being permafrost free due to the higher exposure 
to solar radiation.  
First evidence of permafrost at the Zugspitze was documented during the construction of the 
Eibsee-Zugstpize cable car in 1961, when frozen loam and ice lenses up to 10 cm thick were found 
in the crevasses at the foundation level (Ulrich & King, 1993). Later in the 1990s, during 
excavations for a new cable car from the base of the south slope (Zugspitzplatt) to the summit, 
layer joints filled with ice down to a depth of 3 m were exposed. In 2007, the 
Bayerische Landesamt für Umwelt (LfU) (Bavarian State Office for the Environment) drilled two 
boreholes across the summit crest reaching a length of 44.5 m. The holes are equipped with 27 
thermal sensors and are used to monitor the temperature of the rock and permafrost in the center 
of the summit on an hourly basis. Their results clearly show seasonal changes within the mountain;

3 State of the Art 
 Geophysical characterization of permafrost 
A major problem in estimating the response of permafrost to climatic changes is the need for, and 
lack of, 3-dimensional information on the composition, structure and water content of the 
subsurface. The detection and characterization of permafrost essentially depends on the differences 
of the frozen material from its surrounding non-cryotic material. Since different geophysical 
properties change according to water content and phase,ground-based geophysical methods can be 
effective for obtaining this information, with temperature and ice content most commonly being 
the main properties of interest. Moreover, most of the measurements are relatively inexpensive, 
non-invasive and easy to repeat, making them convenient for long-term monitoring. To date, three 
primary geophysical parameters have been used for making this differentiation: (ⅰ) electrical 
resistivity, (ⅱ) dielectric permittivity, and (ⅲ) seismic wave velocity (Harris et al., 2009). In this 
study, an additional parameter is examined for gaining information on the subsurface properties: 
chargeability, also termed the induced polarization response.

 Induced Polarization (IP) 
3.2.1 
Theoretical background 
Induced polarization (IP) is a technique widely used in applied geophysics. While originally 
developed for detecting and characterizing high-polarizable mineral deposits, nowadays it is also 
used for near subsurface studies in relatively low-polarizable environments. Over the past few 
decades, considerable instrumental and methodological developments have been made for IP field 
measurements, increasing both its usage and its range of applications. This will be further 
elaborated on in subchapter 3.3.   
IP can be performed either in time-domain (TDIP) or in frequency domain. The frequency domain 
method is also referred to as spectral IP (SIP) in which multiple frequencies are used. In general, 
SIP is more often performed in the lab, while TDIP is preferred for field applications due to its 
low cost and the fact that most resistivity meters can easily perform these measurements. Indeed, 
in this work, only the TDIP method has been used and hereafter when the term IP is used, it is 
referring to TDIP. 
IP is measured by injecting a current through a material and observing changes in the residual 
voltage with either time or varying frequencies. When the current is first turned on, there is a build-
up of ionic charges which leads to an increase in the measured potential, known also as 
overvoltage. In TDIP, when the initial current is turned off, the primary potential (V0) rapidly 
drops to a secondary potential (Vs).  This can then be measured at discrete time intervals (tn), also 
termed IP windows or gates, until it eventually diminishes asymptotically (see  
Figure 6). The changing voltage of the IP decay, also referred to as the IP response or discharge 
curve, can be described by the following equation:

The IP phenomenon is caused by two main mechanisms: the membrane polarization and the 
electrode polarization. The former is mostly due to clays within the rock, whereas the latter occurs 
where electronically conductive minerals in the rock come into contact with (electrolyte-rich) 
water and the current flows partially through both.  
Membrane polarization tends to take place in rocks, especially those with small pore spaces 
(Milsom J, 2003), which contain clays or fibrous minerals together with an electrolyte fluid such 
as groundwater. These negatively charged clay particles cover the pore walls and edges, attracting 
positive ions within the electrolyte conductor that ‘build up’ on them. This creates layers of 
opposing ionic charges known as “electric double layers”. When an electric field is applied, the 
arrangement is distorted; negative ions accumulate on one side of the pore throat and positive ones 
accumulate on the other. Eventually the pore passage is blocked by a barrier of ions, hence they

are no longer mobile. Once the electrical field is removed, the ions diffuse and return to their 
equilibrium positions, creating an internal current around the polarized material. This is essentially 
what is responsible for the voltages measured in the IP survey.  
 
Figure 7. Demonstration of membrane polarization mechanism (taken from “Geophysics for Practicing

With electrode polarization, the mechanism is similar to that of membrane polarization, only the 
cause is different. It occurs when metallic material, such as sulphide minerals, is present within 
the rock and comes into contact with an electrolyte fluid such that the current flows through both.  
When subjected to an electric field, these metallic particles become electrically charged and attract 
nearby ions of opposite sign creating electric double layers which block the pore passage. Each of 
these layers develops an electric dipole moment, i.e. an overvoltage, which increases the IP within 
the rock. As soon as the primary current is interrupted, the residual voltage decays while the ions 
diffuse and return to their equilibrium state. While the degree of electrode polarization naturally 
depends on the concentration of metals within the rock, it has been found that in fact the surface 
area of the metallic material plays a larger role (Milsom J, 2003). Hence, disseminated 
mineralization, rather than massive mineralization, results in a higher polarization (Telford et al., 
1990).  
The main measurable difference between membrane polarization and electrode polarization is the 
magnitude of the response, with the latter having significantly larger IP effects (Sumner, 1976). 
That said, clays are much more abundant in rocks than sulphides, and therefore membrane 
polarization is more often the source of observed IP effects (Milsom J, 2003).  
In addition to the two aforementioned mechanisms, the IP also depends on the salinity of the pore 
fluid; i.e. the concentration of ions within the pore water. The higher the salinity, the greater the 
potential build-up of ionic charges is, and hence the chargeability of the rock. Moreover, the 
connectivity and complexity of the pore spaces, also called tortuosity, contributes to the 
chargeability. With an increase in tortuosity, the ions are less capable of moving through the rock’s 
pores, such that when an external electric field is applied, they accumulate faster and in larger 
quantities. This in turn increases the IP effect and the chargeability of the rock.

3.2.2 A brief history of IP and its application in mineral exploration 
The first field observations of the IP phenomenon date back to around 1912 and are attributed to 
the French geoscientist Conrad Schlumberger, who together with his brother Emile, are considered 
pioneers in the area of geophysics and well logging (Telford et al., 1990). Following a monograph 
published in 1920 by C. Schlumberger in which he described the IP phenomenon, he was recruited 
in 1929 by the USSR to carry out well logging investigations in the oil fields of Azerbaijan and 
Chechnya. Throughout the 1930’s, the method was further developed and applied in the USSR, 
mainly for hydrocarbon and ore exploration, and in 1939 Schlumberger’s method for borehole 
logging using IP was patented in the USA (Seigel et al., 2007). Later during World War II, the US 
navy – which was most likely unaware of the developments taking place in the USSR (Telford et 
al., 1990) – independently developed the IP technique for the purpose of detecting mines. After 
the war and throughout the 1950’s, extensive research and investigations on the applications of the 
IP method were pursued by various universities, governmental agencies and mining corporations 
across North America (Sumner, 1976). While most surveys were being carried out for mineral 
exploration, some also looked into using the method for groundwater exploration (e.g. Vacquier 
1957). By the beginning of the 1960’s, the use of the IP phenomenon for subsurface surveying had 
gained popularity worldwide, with equipment becoming more readily available and field results 
more reliable. Since then, IP – specifically in the time-domain – has become recognized as a 
simple, relatively cheap, and efficient geoelectrical technique for mineral exploration.  
3.2.3 Environmental field applications of IP  
In recent years, new and more reliable geophysical methods for mineral exploration have been 
developed and are nowadays generally preferred over IP surveys (Milsom J, 2003). At the same 
time, the recognition of IP as a promising tool in hydrogeology, civil engineering and for 
environmental applications has increased considerably, along with improvements in automatic 
data acquisition and data interpretation (Aristodemou & Thomas-Betts, 2000). Some examples of 
environmental field investigations using IP are given in this section. Additional geophysical 
exploration methods – such as electromagnetism, seismology or gravimetry – are not elaborated 
on here.  
One useful application of IP is in the delineation of landfills. Carlson et al (1999) showed that 
buried waste produces a measurable IP effect compared to its geologic background, even when the 
waste does not contain metallic material. They also confirmed that by using multi-channel 
receivers and a 2D model inversion, the IP method is in fact advantageous over other geophysical 
methods which had been used until then, such as radar or conductivity, both in terms of reliability 
and the depth of investigation. 
==PAGE:26==

In many cases, landfills – whether active or inactive – can lead to contamination of groundwater 
or soils by hazardous substances, also referred to as leachate plumes. It is important to understand 
the extent of these plumes, especially for remediation efforts. 
A common way of investigating the spreading of contaminants in the subsurface is by drilling a 
network of monitoring wells in the vicinity of a site. However, in addition to being costly to 
construct and maintain, the wells limit to point measurements alone, which provide only partial 
knowledge of the extent of contamination (Mepaiyeda et al., 2019). Injecting tracers to monitor 
the plumes is yet another method, but entails the risk of further contaminating the groundwater 
(Dahlin et al., 2010). Non-invasive geoelectrical methods have been found to be fast, cheap and 
suitable for characterizing leachate plumes over an entire area (source). Resistivity is one 
parameter that can be measured for this purpose as the organic pollutants, found in most leachates, 
increase the number of ions in the fluid, resulting in an overall reduction in resistivity of the 
groundwater (Aristodemou & Thomas-Betts, 2000). That said, the resistivity of materials may vary 
according to changes in the fluid content, making it at times difficult to distinguish between 
different materials with comparable resistivity values (Dahlin et al., 2010). Over the past two 
decades, an approach that combines IP and DC resistivity measurements has been extensively 
researched to overcome this ambiguity and uncertainty. As an example, Dahlin et al (2010) carried 
out a DC-IP survey to delineate the extent of buried waste at two different landfills, as well as map 
associated contaminant leakages. Taking multi-channel measurements and inverting the data with 
2D and 3D modelling software, they were able to create high resolution tomograms depicting the 
investigated waste deposits and the migration of leachate plumes. Further studies using a similar 
approach include those of Leroux et al., 2010, Gazoty et al., 2012, and Mepaiyeda et al., 2019, to 
name a few. They were all able to show that complementing resistivity values with chargeability 
values from TDIP measurements can significantly improve the quality of the investigation’s 
results, making DC-IP surveys a powerful tool.  
Lately, geoscientists have been searching for new methods to monitor the changes in permafrost 
active layer dynamics and permafrost degradation caused by global warming. Also for this 
purpose, coupled DC-IP surveying has been found to be effective. Doetsch et al (2015) 
demonstrated the use of this technique to quantify thaw-freeze dynamics in unconsolidated 
sediments at a site in western Greenland. They set up a sophisticated remote-controlled time-lapse 
DC-IP monitoring system that allowed them to take and adjust measurements for a duration of 
seven months, which covered both freezing and thawing periods. They then inverted the acquired 
data; once for DC resistivity values alone, and then combined with IP chargeability values. Their 
results clearly showed that during the freezing period the ground’s resistivity increased, while the 
(normalized) chargeability decreased; the latter a result of less unfrozen water and thus a higher 
==PAGE:27==
 
17

Master’s Thesis 
ion concentration in the pore-water. Their study concluded that a combined DC-IP approach can 
reliably be used to non-intrusively monitor the freezing processes of permafrost at a scale of tens 
to hundreds of meters.  
As discussed in Chapter 1, another consequence of climate change is geomorphological changes 
in high mountain areas due to the thawing of ice. Duvillard et al (2018) were the first to carry out 
a 3D survey combining DC and IP measurements to characterize a rock glacier. They chose a 
section of the Thorens rock glacier, based in the French Alps. This site was interesting as it is 
located in a permafrost zone that is undergoing significant degradation, directly affecting the 
stability of the rock glacier, and has also been anthropologically disturbed by the construction of 
a ski resort. The composition of the rock glacier is highly heterogenous, made up of a complex 
mixture of porous material and embedded metallic particles. To consider the different cases of 
interest – ice-rich material, presence or absence of graphite in the rock, and the presence of clay 
minerals – they used a versatile petrophysical model to model the rock glacier. In their results, 
they presented 2D cross-sections and 3D tomograms of the study site, based on electrical 
conductivity and chargeability values. Additionally, they performed a series of laboratory 
experiments to better understand these values. While their work highlighted some shortcomings in 
the interpretation of ERT in an environment with such poorly mineralized pore water, it 
demonstrated the usefulness of coupling the method with IP measurements for characterizing 
Alpine rock glaciers.

3.3 Technical aspects 
When carrying out IP surveys, there are various technical aspects to consider which depend on the 
site and the aim of the investigation. In addition, as IP measurements are relatively easily disturbed 
(Dahlin and Leroux, 2012), one must gain an understanding of the surroundings in which the 
survey is performed. As the work of this thesis is the first of its kind in the Zugspitze Kammstollen, 
such factors need to be examined carefully, so as to lay the foundations for future IP measurements. 
This also entails much trial and error regarding the use of different equipment – namely the type 
of cable – as well as the choice of electrode array. The following section gives an overview of 
these technical considerations.  
3.3.1 Electromagnetic (EM) coupling 
A difficulty that often arises in IP surveys is electromagnetic (EM) coupling, which includes both 
capacitive and inductive coupling. This is especially true when using multicore cables, which today 
are generally preferred over classical single-electrode cables, as they allow for faster and easier 
data acquisition while maintaining sufficient sensitivity and resolution (Dahlin and Zhou, 2006). 
Capacitive coupling is defined by Dahlin and Leroux (2012) as “current leaks from high-potential 
surfaces or conductors to low-potential surfaces or conductors”, and is the main coupling effect 
considered in this work. It takes place primarily between: (ⅰ) transmitter (current) and receiver 
(potential) cables, (ⅱ) rock and cables, (ⅲ) two receiving (potential) cables (Radic, 2004). For 
multicore cables, the first interaction is most problematic as the current and potential wires are 
placed together and cannot be separated. In general, capacitive coupling has been found to increase 
with higher electrode resistances (Nielsen, 2006), longer cables and longer arrays. Inductive 
coupling is another element of EM coupling and is dependent on the geometry of the wires and on 
the material investigated. However, in TDIP measurements it is usually regarded as negligible and 
can be corrected during the modelling and inversion process (Nielsen and Baumgartner, 2006), 
and is therefore not accounted for here.  
3.3.2 Background potentials 
Background potentials refer to potentials measured by two electrodes, even when no current is 
being transmitted. These can occur for multiple reasons, such as a potential difference in the 
subsurface, natural electrode polarization, or by polarization of previously charged electrodes 
(Olsson, 2016). Stainless steel electrodes, such as those installed in the Kammstollen, are 
particularly affected by the latter. When a current is transmitted through them and they are charged 
up, the residual potential (after current cut-off) can take up to tens of minutes to completely decay. 
Hence, when repeated measurements are taken on the same electrodes, a false (superimposed) 
potential can be recorded, sometimes magnitudes higher than the direct potential (Dahlin, 2001). 
==PAGE:29==

Thus, repeated measurements in the same conditions and with the same equipment can help deduce 
the extent of these background potentials. 
3.3.3 Negative chargeabilities 
Obtaining negative chargeability values in TDIP surveys is usually considered a sign of bad data 
quality. While they may also be a consequence of the distribution of chargeable material in the 
ground, such as zones with only a thin chargeable layer at the surface, for this study site the former 
is assumed. The tendency of negative IP data also differs according to the electrode array used, 
whereby they are more likely to appear when measuring in Dipole-Dipole (Dahlin et. al, 2015). In 
this work, the negative chargeability values were recorded and used as an indicator for the data 
quality, but finally removed during the data analysis stage.  
3.3.4 Cables 
Two ways for reducing capacitive coupling are (ⅰ) to use shielded cables and (ⅱ) to separate current 
and potential wires. As mentioned previously, multicore (also referred to as multi-electrode) cables 
are highly efficient, yet they lack both these features. For this reason, two additional types of cables 
were used for measurements in this work, so as to better understand the magnitude of these effects 
at the study site. Further details on the different types of cables are provided in the Materials and 
Methods chapter.   
3.3.5 Arrays 
When conducting geoelectrical investigations of the subsurface, a number of electrode arrays 
(configurations) with different geometries are available, each of which has a different depth of 
penetration and spatial resolution, as well as limitations and advantages. The choice of array 
depends on the purpose of the survey, the geology, the depth of investigation, and the amount of 
information desired (Coggon, 1973). In this work, two common arrays were chosen: Wenner and 
Dipole-Dipole (henceforth, sometimes referred to in this work as DD). Wenner is very widely used 
and considered by some as the “standard” array which others are often compared to (Milsom J, 
2003). Dipole-Dipole was once popular for IP measurements as when using single-electrode 
cables, it is possible to separate the current and potential wires and thus reduce the EM coupling

1) Acquisition delay – refers to the time period between turning on the current transmission and 
the signal integration for the measurement. This allows for the rock to become completely 
charged, and if set too low, can reduce data quality.  
2) Acquisition time – the length of ERT signal integration for each part of the measuring cycle. 
In general, the higher this value, the longer the applied current is and, in turn, the better the 
data quality.  
3) Minimum current – sets the lowest current that can be transmitted per measurement. To allow 
the instrument to perform measurements, especially in areas of high ground resistance, this 
value should be set as low as possible.    
The output voltage for current injection was always set to maximum (600 V) to assure the highest 
signal-to-noise ratio, or signal level, for each measurement. The chargeabilities were measured in 
twenty time windows varying from 20 to 80 ms, after a 7.5 ms delay from the time the current was 
cut off. These numbers were chosen based on trial-and-error and were found to yield full IP curves 
for the measurements.  
4.2.5 Electrodes and contact resistances 
The electrodes used are a mix of 10x100mm and 10x120mm stainless steel screws that were 
previously drilled into the bedrock of the Kammstollen for other geophysical surveys. During the 
field work, it was noticeable that many of them were either loose or extremely rusty, and there was 
a concern that this may influence the data quality. Therefore, before the work carried out between 
February 17th-19th, 2020, a total of fifteen electrodes were replaced – five in the side tunnel and 
ten along the main tunnel transect (Figure 16). Battery grease was applied to improve the contact 
between the rock and the electrodes. 
Before each measurement, particular attention was paid to the electrode contact resistances as high 
resistances can lead to bad data. These were reduced as much as possible in order to maximize the 
current flow and the amplitude of the received signal, and to avoid current leakages. The “Focus 
One” electrode test of the Terrameter LS, which measures the contact resistance of each electrode 
against all the other electrodes, was performed prior to every measurement. This allowed to 
monitor the contact resistance of each individual electrode and thus to identify any with 
unacceptably high values (e.g. > 150 kΩ). To achieve better contact, salt water was sprayed 
between the rock and the electrodes (ABEM, 2016).

 Reliability of measurements 
One method for assessing the reliability of the measurement procedure is by manually repeating 
measurements multiple times in an identical configuration and examining how consistent the 
results are. This was first performed using multicore cables in both the main tunnel (Project 11) 
and side tunnel (Project 3). Two measurements were taken consecutively, and a third one the 
following morning (approximately 10 hours later).  
An additional analysis of measurement reliability was conducted on the measurements described 
earlier in section 4.2.3 (Projects 17,18,19). Here, for each test, measurements were repeated 
consecutively four times in 30-60 s intervals.  
It should be noted that the Terrameter LS can be set to automatically repeat cycles of current 
injections per measurement (up to four times in a row),  also known as stacks. The difference in 
this case being that the operator only receives one value, which is essentially an average of those 
measurements. Still, to evaluate the quality of the data acquired, a coefficient of variation (CV) – 
estimated by the instrument – can be inspected. This coefficient is equivalent to the standard 
deviation between the stacks, divided by the mean value for a data point, and is considered a 
paramount means of assessing the data quality (Gazoty et al., 2013). The number of stacks was set 
to range between 2 to 4.

inverted along with the IP values, are not included in this work.

6 Discussion 
 Cable type comparison 
It is noticeable from Figures Figure 18-Figure 20 that although the unshielded cables show slightly 
higher chargeability values, the results are overall quite similar. In all three images the 
chargeability of the subsurface is relatively low for the first meter or so, barely exceeding 10 
mV/V. It then increases gradually with depth, reaching maximum values of 15-20 mV/V by two 
meters below the surface. This is particularly the case for the right section of the profiles, which is 
the area closer to the side tunnel entrance. The reason for this could be that the rock in this area is 
more frozen and saturated than lower down the main tunnel (left section of profiles). This 
correlates very well with the ERT results that show this transect crosses from an unfrozen zone to 
a frozen one (see Figure 10). It is important to pay attention to the fact that the depths of 
investigation are not identical for all three tomograms, and hence the values seen must be compared 
according to the depth and not by their location on the profile.    
As mentioned before, the coefficient of variation gives an indication about the quality of the 
measurements, whereby the lower the number, the better the quality. Figure 21 displays these for 
all three types of cables; with the lowest mean coefficient of 9% for the unshielded multicore 
cables, and the highest of 17% for the shielded single-electrode cables. While all three values are 
relatively low, it nonetheless gives an idea about the quality of data acquired using each cable type. 
 Electrode array comparison 
On the contrary, there are clear differences in the results obtained when measuring in Wenner 
(Figure 22) and Dipole-Dipole (Figure 23) arrays. While both figures exhibit higher values in the 
right parts of the profiles, those in Figure 23 are significantly larger. The image also presents an 
additional zone of high values in the bottom left part of the profile, which may be caused by the 
higher noise-to-signal ratio, typical of Dipole-Dipole arrays.  
The significantly higher number of negative chargeability values recorded when measuring in 
Dipole-Dipole than in Wenner (see Table 10) further indicates a possibility of noisy data. While it 
is not always the case when measuring in this array, it is apparent that in the conditions of the 
Kammstollen, it is indeed not favorable.

 Tunnel comparison 
6.3.1 Main tunnel vs. side tunnel 
Results from the two tunnels show that the side tunnel exhibits higher chargeability throughout. 
This could be due to the wetter conditions of the rock, which plays an important role in the degree 
of the IP phenomena. In the deeper zones, values reach 60 mV/V – considerably higher than any 
recorded in the main tunnel. These values appear mainly in the right corner of the profile, which 
represents the far end of the tunnel, nearest to the exit to the mountain side. This area is perhaps 
the most frozen of both the transects, with very high electrode contact resistances (as seen in Figure 
25), and hence potentially poor data quality. Additionally, it is seen that Figure 26 displays a 
somewhat smooth pattern in which the chargeability increases gradually with depth, while Figure 
27 is blotchier and has no clear trend. Both images show that the shallow zone (down to one meter) 
has relatively low values, yet Figure 27 also exhibits some patches of high values – for example, 
around x=53m – that could be an outcome of coupling effects.  
A few observations can be made from the results of the electrode contact resistance tests (Figure 
24 and Figure 25). In the main tunnel, the values are distributed randomly between the electrodes, 
with a maximum value of 42 kΩ and a mean of 10.7 kΩ. In the side tunnel, the values are generally 
much higher and increase substantially for the last electrodes. Again, this is likely due to the large 
presence of ice at the far end of the tunnel.  
6.3.2 ERT vs. IP 
While the comparison of DC resistivity (ERT) and IP tomograms in Figure 28 is strictly a visual 
one, as a definite connection between the resistivity and chargeability of the rock has not yet been 
determined, the profiles clearly show a well-correlated pattern. Both the resistivity and the 
chargeability values gradually increase from the left side of the profile to the right side. In the case 
of the chargeability the increase occurs vertically (with depth), with the resistivity it is more 
horizontal. Nevertheless, the similar pattern obtained from the IP method further confirms the 
knowledge (gained from ERT) that this transect indeed crosses from an area of unfrozen rock to 
frozen rock.

6.3.3 Geological consideration - series of four-electrode tests 
The series of  “4E tests” were performed to understand the influence of clay material in the bedrock 
on the IP phenomena. The evaluation was done by: 
1) Extracting IP sums for the three transects (see Table 11), giving an idea of the chargeability 
of the rock. 
2) Analyzing the decay curves of the individual measurements (FiguresFigure 29-Figure 31), 
helping to identify noisy data.  
Moreover, four consecutive measurements were taken to eliminate any noisy results and thus 
ensure a higher reliability (elaborated in section 6.4.2) .  
The first test (4E_FR), carried out in frozen rock at the upper part of the main tunnel, was used as 
a reference for the other two tests. The results are presented in Figure 29 in which three of the four 
measurements are consistent, with overlapping asymptotic curves. The curves steeply fall in the 
first three IP windows, pointing at a rapid reduction in chargeability of the rock, and then steadily 
flatten out until the last window at 0.8075 s. The mean electrode contact resistance in this transect 
was 121 kΩ. This is similar to the resistances measured in the side tunnel, providing further 
verification of the frozen conditions.   
The second test (4E_CJ) was also conducted in frozen bedrock at the upper part of the main tunnel, 
but with a clay-filled joint running along the middle of the transect. While the chargeability values 
in the initial windows are higher here, the decay curves are shaped similarly to those of the first 
test. The electrode contact resistance was the highest, with a mean value of 199 kΩ. 
The third test (4E_CF) was conducted in pure clay material within fractured bedrock. There, the 
chargeability values are significantly lower than the other two tests. Also, the curves are smoother, 
illustrating a slower discharge over time. All curves are more or less consistent, with three of them 
entirely overlapping. The electrode contact resistance recorded in this transect was the lowest of 
all transects in both tunnels, averaging at a mere 3 kΩ. 
To better examine the differences between the three transects, averaged decay curves for each test 
are shown in Figure 32. The chargeability values  are plotted on a logarithmic scale for 
visualization purposes. In addition, mean IP sums are shown in Table 11. Agreeable with the 
observations from the curves, it can be seen that the value is highest for the area of frozen bedrock 
with a clay-filled joint in the middle, followed by the purely frozen bedrock. This seems to indicate 
that the presence of clay material in frozen rock increases the IP effect. Still, the fact that the 
chargeability of the clay material in the 4E_CF test is so low is somewhat conflicting. It could be 
argued that the contact between the installed electrodes and the clay material was too poor, but as

 Reliability of measurements and data quality  
6.4.1 Repeated measurements – multicore cables 
The thrice-repeated measurements in Projects 3 and 11 were carried out in order to check for 
potential discrepancies in the data quality of the results (based on the coefficients of variation).  
Ideally, repeated measurements should be performed in small time intervals (on the order of 
seconds to minutes) to ensure that the conditions have not changed. This was the case for the first 
two measurements in each project, yet due to logistical limitations, the third measurements were 
only performed approximately 10 hours later. Furthermore, it should be noted that the electrodes 
had already been charged the same day during ERT measurements. The results reveal only minor 
discrepancies in the data quality, mainly in the side tunnel where the CV improved by 3% between 
the first measurement and the following two (Figure 33). In the main tunnel, the differences are 
even more subtle with only a 1% change between each measurement (Figure 34). When comparing 
the average CVs for each project/tunnel (Figure 35), a slightly lower value is depicted for Project 
3. However, this should be regarded with caution as the number of values for this project is half 
of those in Project 11, and also excludes two large outliers.     
6.4.2 Four-electrode tests 
The series of four-electrode tests comprised of repeated measurements in short time intervals, 
which made it possible to assess the differences in data quality between them. Figure 36 shows the 
coefficients of variation of all the measurements conducted in the three tests. It can be seen that 
for all three, the coefficient is highest in the first measurement, with values ranging between 18% 
- 38%. The coefficients then decrease in the following measurements, whereby: for the 4E_FR and 
4E_CJ tests, the values are lowest in the fourth and final measurement (2% and 7%, respectively), 
and for the 4E_CF test, it is lowest in the third measurement (2%). This reveals a significant 
improvement between the first measurement and the third/fourth one. A possible cause for this 
could be due to the fact that the electrodes used were brand new and had never previously been 
actively polarized. While no literature on this was found, the research group of Prof. Andreas 
Kemna at University Bonn have observed similar effects and hypothesize that this may be 
explained by a short-term electrical equilibrium (personal communication with Jonas Limbrock, 
17.04.2020).

7 Conclusions 
The conclusions of this thesis consist primarily of methodological recommendations for carrying 
out IP surveys at the Kammstollen (Zugspitze). They have been made based on numerous 
observations from field work, as well as extensive literature review. Little can be concluded about 
the presence of permafrost solely from the measured chargeability values. Any assumptions made 
on the relationship between the two were derived from ERT measurements and temperature data 
loggers.  
Three types of cables were used for the IP measurements in this work, with the aim of determining 
which is most suitable for the study site. They were evaluated based on two main factors: 
1) The quality and reliability of the data – namely, the susceptibility to noise and 
coupling effects. 
2) The time and effort required to perform the measurements. 
The chargeability tomograms created for the three cable types were found to be profoundly similar. 
The coefficient of variations were all relatively low as well, yet the lowest was obtained for the 
multicore cables. Lastly, in terms of the time and effort required for performing the measurements, 
the use of multicore cables was by far the most practical. When using these, a series of 
measurements along the main tunnel transect was completed relatively easily in 30 minutes, while 
with the single-electrode cables this work was tedious and took up to 3-4 hours. For all the 
aforementioned reasons, it is apparent that using multicore cables is most recommended.  
Moreover, the IP measurements were carried out in two electrode array configurations – Wenner 
and Dipole-Dipole – using both multicore and single-electrode cables. Yet as with the latter it was 
possible to separate the current and potential wires, and thus reduce coupling effects, the results of 
those were used for assessing the two arrays. The tomograms showed very clear differences: in the 
Wenner array, the image was smooth and similar to the results using multicore cables, while in the 
Dipole-Dipole array, the image was far noisier. Furthermore, in Dipole-Dipole a significantly 
larger number of negative chargeability values were measured than in Wenner – an additional sign 
of noisy data. Hence, it seems that for the Kammstollen measuring in Wenner is favorable. 
The different conditions between the two tunnels were also evident in the results of the IP 
measurements. The rock walls of the main tunnel transect, which from ERT data are known to be 
mostly unfrozen, exhibited much lower chargeability than those of the frozen side tunnel. The 
results seen in the tomograms were also consistent with those from ERT images, where both 
showed a transition zone from lower to higher values along the transect. The frozen conditions in 
the side tunnel led to extremely high contact resistance of the electrodes, possibly enhancing 
existing coupling effects and consequently reducing the quality and reliability of the data.  
==PAGE:61==

In addition, the effect of clay-filled fractures present in the rock walls of the main tunnel was 
investigated in a series of tests. Through them, it was observed that although the chargeability of 
the clay material alone is close to non-existent, when such fractures cross an electrode transect, 
they increase the measured chargeability of the rock.  
Finally, the reliability of data acquisition was checked by repeating measurement multiple times 
and inspecting the coefficients of variation between measurement cycles. The results from the 
four-electrode tests were equivocal and showed that large differences are found between the first 
and successive measurements. Thereupon it is advised to perform measurements multiple times in 
order to eliminate bad data and obtain truly reliable results. 
Still, the assessment of data quality cannot be entirely based upon the coefficient of variation and 
other differences, such as the signal levels of the measurements and the IP values themselves, 
should be taken into consideration.    
To conclude, this work demonstrated that the time-domain induced polarization technique is 
capable of detecting differences in the rock conditions of the Kammstollen. The set-up 
configuration for carrying out these measurements has been developed and refined such that the 
next steps towards long-term IP surveying can be taken. Recommendations for future work are 
discussed in the next and final chapter.

8 Outlook and future work 
The ultimate goal this study worked towards is creating a long-term time-series consisting of both 
ERT and IP measurements, thus providing a better understanding of warming-induced changes to 
the permafrost at the Zugspitze. Due to the restricted time span of the thesis, as well as the many 
logistical matters involved for carrying out field work, only an initial experimental setup could be 
completed within this project. Nonetheless, many important observations were made that provide 
a sound basis for future research on both the methodology and implementation of IP surveys in the 
tunnel. This research could entail the following: 
3) Lab experiments – performing IP measurements on Wetterstein rock samples 
from the Zugspitze. By measuring the chargeability of the rock during freeze-
thaw cycles, temperature-chargeability calibration curves can be obtained. 
These would provide a valuable insight on the relationship between the IP 
values measured and the presence/absence of ice in the rock – currently the 
most crucial factor missing.  
4) Further refinement of the methodology – e.g. measuring in a multiple-gradient 
array, as described by Dhalin & Zhou (2006).  
5) Implementing a coupled DCIP technique as suggested by Olsson (2016), with 
a 100% duty cycle and longer on-time injection to increase the signal strength. 
6) Eventually extending the measurements to the upper section of the main tunnel, 
which in parts has similar conditions to the side tunnel. By doing so, a 
comparison of frozen rock conditions will be made and any discrepancies in the 
IP results can be attributed to other sources.  
7) Setting up an automatic data acquisition system – this would contribute both to 
the data reliability (as emphasized in this work) and, in the long run, to creating 
a denser time-series.
