Abstract
In recent years, process mining has increasingly expanded to unstructured data sources,
such as video recordings of human activities. However, extracting structured and seman­
tically meaningful event logs from such visual data remains a fundamental challenge.
This thesis proposes a modular, self­supervised abstraction pipeline that transforms fine­
grained, timestamped micro­actions into high­level, interpretable events suitable for pro­
cess discovery. Using the Assembly101 dataset as a case study, I train a contrastive
BERT­based Transformer model to encode symbolic action sequences into semantic em­
beddings. These embeddings are clustered using UMAP for dimensionality reduction and
HDBSCAN for density­based grouping. Semantic labels are then assigned using a large
language model (LLM), producing abstract event logs.
The pipeline is evaluated both quantitatively—via clustering metrics and segment­level
F1­scores—and qualitatively—via process discovery using procedural, declarative, and
frequency­based methods.
Experiments across both monocular and multi­view video
inputs confirm the pipeline’s robustness to perspective variability. Notably, abstraction
accuracy—measured by segment­level F1­score—improves from 0.47 with raw symbolic
input to 0.77 after full semantic abstraction, indicating a substantial enhancement in inter­
pretability and structural clarity.
Overall, this work contributes a generalizable framework for video­based process abstrac­
tion and underscores key limitations of applying traditional process mining techniques to
high­frequency, sensor­derived action data.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Process mining is a data­driven technique for analyzing, monitoring, and improving real­
world processes based on event data . Traditionally, it relies on structured event logs
extracted from enterprise information systems, such as Enterprise Resource Planning
(ERP) and Customer Relationship Management (CRM) platforms. These systems record
transactional activities with well­defined case identifiers, activity labels, and timestamps,
thereby enabling control­flow discovery and conformance checking [1]. Over the past two
decades, process mining has become an essential tool in Business Process Management
(BPM), with applications across domains including finance, healthcare, and manufactur­
ing [2]. However, this reliance on structured logs significantly constrains its applicability.
Many real­world processes, particularly those occurring in physical environments, do not
generate system­based event traces, resulting in substantial blind spots in end­to­end
process analysis [3].
To overcome these limitations, recent research has investigated the use of unstructured
data sources—such as video, audio, and sensor streams—as alternative inputs for pro­
cess mining. These modalities provide richer and more direct representations of real­
world activities, especially in contexts where structured logs are unavailable [4]. Among
these, video data is particularly promising due to its capacity to capture fine­grained visual
cues, spatial interactions, and contextual dynamics, which are essential for reconstructing
activity sequences [5].
Although substantial progress has been made in computer vision for video analysis, most
conventional models are not tailored to process­level semantics. Action recognition archi­
tectures such as I3D and TSM classify short video segments into predefined categories
with reasonable accuracy [6], and object detectors like YOLO are widely employed to lo­
calize relevant regions in surveillance and industrial scenarios [7]. However, these mod­
els are generally optimized for isolated classification tasks and do not capture temporal
or hierarchical dependencies among actions. Moreover, their recognition accuracy often
falls short in practical applications due to motion blur, occlusion, or variations in camera
perspectives—particularly in uncontrolled environments.
Even when low­level actions are successfully detected, they often prove too fine­grained
and frequent to support process­level reasoning directly. In video­based contexts, actions
such as “reach,” “grasp,” or “move” may occur frequently but lack standalone process
significance. To enable meaningful process discovery, such atomic actions must be ag­
gregated into semantically coherent, higher­level events, such as “assemble component”
or “perform inspection”. Without this semantic abstraction, the resulting event logs are
typically too fragmented or verbose to support effective analysis. Recent studies highlight
the necessity of context­aware transformations that restructure raw action streams using
temporal, spatial, and domain­specific information [8].
Semantic abstraction has thus emerged as a critical step in bridging the gap between
low­level visual observations and high­level process understanding. Aggregating atomic
actions into structured events aligned with process­level activities enhances both inter­
pretability and analytical utility. Semantics­aware event transformation techniques aim to
enrich or restructure action sequences through the integration of contextual knowledge,
thereby improving their alignment with real­world tasks.

To facilitate such transformations, Transformer­based models offer promising capabilities
due to their strength in modeling sequential dependencies in long input streams. While
originally developed for natural language processing, Transformers have been success­
fully applied to vision tasks involving temporal reasoning and contextual understanding
[9]. These capabilities suggest potential for process­oriented applications, where encod­
ing fine­grained visual events into semantically rich latent representations may support
abstraction tasks such as clustering and activity inference [10]. Although prior work has
explored Transformer­based abstraction in business process logs, their application to un­
structured visual data remains an open and under­explored direction.
Complementing this, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong capa­
bilities in interpreting structured and perceptual data and in generating semantically rich
descriptions. In video understanding, LLMs have been utilized to summarize temporal
sequences, infer causal relations, and generate process­level annotations [11]. Given
action­level embeddings or cluster prototypes, LLMs can be prompted to produce inter­
pretable event labels, facilitating the automated construction of abstracted event logs.
Their pretrained knowledge and in­context reasoning capabilities make them particu­
larly well­suited for enhancing the semantic depth and interpretability of process mining
pipelines.
1.2
Problem Definition
The central objective of this work is to address the challenge of discovering meaning­
ful process models from unstructured, fine­grained visual data streams. In contrast to
traditional process mining pipelines that rely on structured system logs, the input in this
setting consists solely of raw video footage—without the support of annotations, workflow
models, or explicit event traces.
Among various domains of visual human activity, manual assembly tasks offer a particu­
larly suitable testbed for this challenge. These tasks involve complex sequences of object
manipulation, often captured in multi­camera video setups, and have attracted increasing
attention in both process mining and activity recognition communities [12]–[16]. The com­
bination of procedural complexity, physical interaction, and repetitive yet flexible behavior
makes manual assembly an ideal candidate for evaluating semantic abstraction pipelines.
The overarching goal is to construct a generalizable pipeline that transforms low­level
action sequences into abstract event logs suitable for downstream process discovery.
Although the empirical evaluation focuses on human assembly videos, the approach
is intended to be applicable to a broad range of scenarios in which unstructured, high­
frequency sensor or visual data constitutes the only available input.
This problem setting introduces several key challenges:
• Low­level granularity and the need for abstraction: Recognized actions typi­
cally comprise dense, symbolic micro­movements (e.g., “reach,” “grasp”) that lack
standalone semantic meaning. Such fine­grained outputs must be semantically ag­
gregated into higher­level process events, which requires robust and interpretable
abstraction mechanisms.
• Lack of reference models and high variability: Manual processes often do not
adhere to predefined workflows. Variations in task sequence, execution style, and
product configuration pose significant challenges for model­free discovery, particu­
larly in the absence of process templates.

• Semantic ambiguity in visual behavior: Visually similar actions may correspond
to different process steps, while visually distinct gestures may fulfill the same ab­
stract function. This semantic ambiguity complicates the mapping from perception­
level inputs to process­level understanding.
• Learning­based abstraction and generalization limitations: Mapping low­level
actions to abstract events commonly relies on learning­based techniques such as
clustering and representation learning. These techniques are highly sensitive to
embedding quality, data distribution, and noise, and frequently struggle to generalize
across task variants or execution contexts.
• Error propagation and robustness requirements: Recognition errors occurring
in early stages of the pipeline can propagate through subsequent abstraction and
modeling stages, adversely affecting process discovery outcomes. Thus, the overall
approach must be resilient to noise and uncertainty inherent in perceptual inputs.
1.3
Research Objectives and Research Questions
The primary aim of this work is to develop a generalizable abstraction framework for trans­
forming fine­grained action sequences—extracted from raw video data—into structured
and semantically meaningful event logs suitable for process mining. The focus is not on
video recognition per se, but on bridging the semantic gap between low­level visual be­
havior and high­level process semantics. Human assembly processes are selected as a
representative use case to evaluate the proposed pipeline under realistic, unstructured,
and multi­variant conditions.
Specific Objectives
To support this aim, the following specific objectives are formulated:
• Encode fine­grained action sequences into semantically meaningful latent repre­
sentations;
• Group these representations into abstract temporal segments that correspond to
high­level activities;
• Employ Large Language Models (LLMs) to assign interpretable semantic labels to
the resulting segments;
• Evaluate the quality and utility of the abstracted event logs using standard process
mining techniques.
Research Questions
• RQ1: Representation Learning How can fine­grained action sequences be en­
coded into semantically expressive embeddings that generalize across variations in
task execution?
• RQ2: Semantic Abstraction To what extent can clustering and LLM­based label­
ing generate consistent and interpretable high­level events from fine­grained visual
input?
• RQ3: Process Discovery Utility Do abstracted event logs improve the quality and
interpretability of process models derived from highly unstructured video­based in­
put?
• RQ4: Input Robustness How does camera configuration (e.g., monocular vs. multi­
view) influence representation learning outcomes and the fidelity of subsequent pro­
cess discovery?

1.4
Contributions
This thesis proposes a modular, unsupervised abstraction pipeline that transforms fine­
grained action sequences extracted from raw video data into structured, semantically
meaningful event logs suitable for process mining.
• Contextualized representation learning: A Transformer­based encoder is trained
to model temporal dependencies across micro­action sequences and generate se­
mantically expressive embeddings.
• Unsupervised clustering: The learned embeddings are grouped into abstract event
segments using density­based clustering techniques, enabling temporal aggrega­
tion without ground­truth supervision.
• Semantic labeling with LLMs: Large Language Models (LLMs) are employed to
assign interpretable process­level labels to the clustered segments, producing ab­
stracted event logs aligned with human­understandable activities.
The pipeline is evaluated on a manual assembly dataset and demonstrates robustness
to perceptual noise, offering a novel pathway for extending process discovery techniques
to domains where only unstructured visual data is available, such as manual assembly or
human­robot collaboration scenarios.
1.5
Thesis Structure
The thesis is organized as follows:
• Chapter 2: Related Work Reviews existing literature on process mining, event
abstraction methods, and the application of computer vision and large language
models in behavior and workflow analysis.
• Chapter 3: Methodology Presents the architecture of the proposed abstraction
pipeline, including contextual representation learning, clustering, and semantic la­
beling.
• Chapter 4: Experimental Setup Describes the dataset, preprocessing procedures,
embedding model training, and clustering and abstraction.
• Chapter 5: Abstraction Evaluation Assesses the quality of the learned embed­
dings and abstraction results using both quantitative metrics and qualitative visual­
izations.
• Chapter 6: Process Discovery Applies multiple process mining algorithms to the
abstracted event logs and evaluates their effectiveness in reconstructing interpretable
and consistent models.
• Chapter 7: Generalization across Visual Perspectives Evaluates how the ab­
straction pipeline generalizes to predicted symbolic input from single­view and multi­
view video, highlighting robustness to noise and viewpoint diversity.
• Chapter 8: Discussion and Conclusion Summarizes the main findings, reflects
on limitations, revisits the research questions, and suggests directions for future
work.
This structure reflects the logical progression from motivation and design to implementa­
tion, evaluation, and reflection.

2
Related Work
2.1
Literature Scope and Structure
To support the development of the proposed framework, relevant work was reviewed
across four key areas: video­based process mining, visual event extraction, event ab­
straction, and the emerging use of Large Language Models (LLMs) in process mining.
This concept­driven review spans research from process mining, computer vision, and
language modeling, focusing primarily on literature published between 2020 and 2024.
• Video­based Process Mining: Studies exploring how video and other unstructured
data sources can be incorporated into process modeling and analysis, often through
spatial heuristics or rule­based pipelines.
• Event Extraction in Vision: Techniques for detecting and segmenting atomic ac­
tions or interactions from video input, including object detection, pose estimation,
and temporal action recognition.
• Event Abstraction: Approaches for aggregating low­level actions into high­level
activities, ranging from rule­based systems to clustering and representation learning
methods.
• LLMs in Process Mining: Recent efforts to integrate large language models into
various stages of the process mining pipeline, such as event interpretation, trace
summarization, user­query answering, and—most relevant to this work—semantic
labeling of clustered events.
In this work, LLMs are primarily leveraged to semantically label clustered action segments.
However, their broader potential in facilitating human­understandable process insights
highlights a growing research trend toward language­guided process analytics.
2.2
Event Extraction in Vision­Based Settings
Event extraction from video constitutes the foundation of visual process mining. It aims to
detect and temporally localize primitive actions, object manipulations, or human gestures
that correspond to real­world process steps. Three primary vision­based strategies are
prevalent in the literature: object­centric detection, pose­based analysis, and temporal
action segmentation.
2.2.1
Object Detection and Tracking
Object detection models such as YOLO are widely used to localize entities of interest—
e.g., people, tools, or components—within individual frames. These detections are sub­
sequently linked over time using tracking algorithms like DeepSORT [17], which enhance
identity preservation via deep appearance embeddings. This facilitates the construction
of coherent object or agent trajectories, even in the presence of occlusion or varying view­
points.
Tracking plays a critical role in process­oriented applications, as it enables the consis­
tent grouping of frame­level detections into case­level traces. Such traces are essential
for downstream abstraction and process discovery. For instance, Melfsen et al. [18] an­
alyze pig behavior using zone­based tracking, while Sandberg and Oliveira [19] extract
aviation­related events by combining detection outputs with rule­based logic. Although

these pipelines are highly efficient for streaming scenarios, they depend heavily on anno­
tated training data and exhibit sensitivity to environmental variation. Furthermore, without
additional abstraction mechanisms, their outputs remain low­level and fragmented.
2.2.2
Pose Estimation and Skeleton­Based Analysis
Pose estimation models predict skeletal keypoints over time for articulated entities—both
human and animal—providing a compact and robust representation of motion. Compared
to raw RGB input, pose­based features are background­invariant, privacy­preserving, and
well­suited for analyzing physical activities in industrial, medical, or behavioral contexts.
Prominent systems include OpenPose [20] and AlphaPose [21].
However, pose data alone lacks semantic grounding: it captures how something moves,
but not what is being manipulated. To enable meaningful event inference, pose estimation
is often combined with object detection or hand–object contact estimation. For example,
the IKEA ASM dataset [22] employs joint modeling of pose, object presence, and hand–
object interaction to identify fine­grained assembly steps. In this context, pose information
serves as a complementary modality that provides structural cues to support multi­modal
abstraction, though it is rarely sufficient in isolation.
2.2.3
Action Recognition and Temporal Segmentation
Action recognition models classify short video clips into behavior categories such as “pick
up” or “assemble,” based on motion dynamics and visual appearance.
Unlike pose­
based approaches, these models operate directly on raw video input and are capable
of capturing global spatiotemporal patterns. Temporal segmentation frameworks—such
as TSM [6], Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) [23], and Transformer­based archi­
tectures like ActionFormer [24]—divide continuous video streams into meaningful action
units by modeling inter­frame dependencies.
These models are valuable for identifying candidate event boundaries, yet they face chal­
lenges such as ambiguous transitions, overlapping actions, and limited generalizability
across domains. Within the process mining context, they are typically used as front­end
mechanisms for proposing initial event segments. However, their outputs generally re­
quire further semantic abstraction to generate structured, interpretable event logs suitable
for downstream analysis.
2.3
Event Abstraction Techniques
Event abstraction refers to the process of transforming low­level events—typically ex­
tracted from vision­based or sensor­rich data—into higher­level, semantically meaningful
activities that align with human interpretation and process logic. Existing techniques can
be broadly categorized into rule­based, clustering­based, representation learning, and
large language model (LLM)­assisted strategies [25]. This section introduces represen­
tative works in each category, with an emphasis on recent developments relevant to the
present study.
2.3.1
Rule­based Mapping
Early approaches in visual process mining often relied on spatial rules or Area of Interest
(AOI) detection to convert raw signals into structured event logs. For example, Kratsch
et al. [26] applied manually defined rules and complex event processing (CEP) logic to
cooking videos, while Chen et al. [27] combined object detection and AOI segmentation
to generate video­aligned process models in kitchen environments. Melfsen et al. [18]
used object­zone mapping and tracking to describe pig behavior sequences from video
data, illustrating rule­based spatial abstraction in agricultural contexts. Similarly, Sand­
berg and Daniel [19] proposed a streaming pipeline for extracting event logs from airport

videos using object­based detection and rule heuristics. While interpretable and relatively
straightforward to implement, these approaches are typically domain­specific and struggle
to generalize to complex workflows or support semantic abstraction.
2.3.2
Clustering for Abstraction
A growing body of work applies unsupervised clustering techniques to group similar low­
level events into abstracted behavioral units. The Video­to­Model pipeline [12] segments
hand trajectories using motion­based clustering in assembly scenarios. Rebmann et al. [8]
introduce a semantics­aware transformation technique that clusters event sequences
while preserving temporal and contextual consistency. Gavrić et al. [13] propose a multi­
modal abstraction pipeline in which video and sensor modalities are embedded and clus­
tered before label generation via LLM prompting. These approaches demonstrate the
flexibility and scalability of clustering­based abstraction, particularly when applied to un­
structured or heterogeneous input data.
2.3.3
Representation Learning
Embedding­based techniques have been increasingly adopted to learn semantic repre­
sentations of event data. Pfeiffer [28] design a Transformer­based framework to capture
behavioral context from business logs, enhancing clustering performance and model in­
terpretability. Bettouche [29] proposes a Transformer­driven semantic clustering model
in which learned embeddings are grouped using PCA [30] and HDBSCAN [31], demon­
strating robustness to log noise and improved downstream process discovery. Rebmann
et al. [32] present a multi­perspective abstraction method that integrates temporal, control­
flow, and resource views using a CNN­based encoder, thereby enabling semantic group­
ing across multiple dimensions. Compared to rule­based systems, representation learn­
ing methods reduce the need for manual feature engineering and generalize more effec­
tively to novel domains.
2.3.4
LLM­assisted Semantic Labeling
Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable generalization capabilities
across diverse domains, including text summarization, code generation, and multimodal
understanding. Their capacity to extract semantic structure from noisy or unstructured
inputs makes them a natural fit for integration into process mining pipelines, particularly
in contexts where traditional log­based techniques fall short.
Recent studies have explored the use of LLMs to support various process mining tasks
beyond conventional log analysis. Shirali et al. [33] apply LLMs to integrate and abstract
noisy event logs sourced from IoT devices, showing how semantic coherence can be
recovered in complex, heterogeneous environments. Gavrić et al. [13] incorporate GPT­
based components into multimodal abstraction pipelines, using visual and symbolic em­
beddings as prompts to extract human­readable descriptions of clustered activities. Berti
[34] further demonstrate that carefully engineered prompts allow LLMs to consistently
generate high­quality labels for clustered events, without requiring manually curated on­
tologies or rule sets.
These developments highlight a broader shift toward LLM­assisted abstraction frame­
works, where generative models are employed to bridge the gap between raw behavior
traces and structured, interpretable event representations. Within this paradigm, seman­
tic labeling of clustered event segments emerges as a key application: by prompting the
LLM with representative sequences, human­interpretable subprocess names can be as­
signed in a scalable and consistent manner.

2.4
Research Gap
Although substantial progress has been made in both vision­based event extraction and
semantic abstraction from structured logs, several important gaps remain at their inter­
section:
• Lack of semantic abstraction in vision­based pipelines. Most video­based pro­
cess mining studies concentrate on rule­driven event extraction using spatial heuris­
tics such as area­of­interest (AOI) detection or object­zone tracking. For instance,
the “Video­to­Model” approach [12] segments actions by mapping hand positions
to predefined material bins, while Melfsen et al. [18] infer pig behaviors based on
spatial heatmaps. As illustrated in Figure 2.1, these pipelines identify actions within
fixed spatial zones but do not abstract them into semantically coherent process­level
tasks.
• Disconnection between visual signals and process semantics. Transformer­
based embedding and clustering methods have demonstrated strong performance
on business process logs [28], [29], [32]. However, these approaches typically as­
sume structured symbolic input and are seldom applied to unstructured, noisy visual
data such as hand trajectories or egocentric video streams.
• Absence of end­to­end abstraction frameworks from raw video. While recent
efforts have explored LLM­assisted cluster labeling [34], they frequently operate on
preprocessed or pre­structured embeddings and rely on significant manual inter­
vention. Fully integrated pipelines that begin with raw video and yield interpretable
event logs remain underdeveloped.
• Limited benchmarks for high­granularity, real­world tasks. Existing studies on
visual abstraction are often confined to scripted environments, such as cooking [35]
or staged assembly tasks [22]. As a result, they may not generalize effectively to
industrial domains involving subtle, overlapping actions and varying viewpoints.

2.5
Positioning of This Work
The literature review highlights a clear gap: prior research predominantly focuses ei­
ther on low­level event extraction from video using rule­based or area­of­interest (AOI)
heuristics, or on semantic abstraction techniques applied to structured symbolic action
logs. While AOI­driven video approaches are effective in specific settings, they often lack
semantic generalization and interpretability. On the other hand, representation learning­
based abstraction has demonstrated promising results for structured event logs, but these
techniques are seldom adapted to the noisy and unstructured nature of visual data streams.
This work positions itself at the intersection of these two domains. It explores whether
recent advances in representation learning and large language model­based semantic
labeling can be effectively extended to enable semantic process mining directly from raw
video input. Departing from reliance on spatial heuristics or manual mappings, the pro­
posed method adopts an integrated, learning­based abstraction strategy. This strategy is
designed to process noisy, fine­grained visual input and is evaluated in terms of its abil­
ity to generate interpretable event logs suitable for downstream process discovery and
conformance analysis.
In addressing this methodological gap, the present work introduces an end­to­end ab­
straction framework that transforms unstructured video streams into structured, semanti­
cally meaningful process representations. The proposed pipeline bridges the divide be­
tween computer vision­based event detection and process mining­oriented abstraction,
offering a unified solution for video­driven process understanding.

Methodology
3.1
Overview of Abstraction Pipeline Design
Figure 3.1: Overview of the proposed learning­based abstraction pipeline.
Video data captured from human­performed tasks, such as manual assembly, typically
exhibits fine temporal granularity and substantial semantic ambiguity. Although contem­
porary computer vision models are capable of extracting low­level action primitives (e.g.,
pick up screwdriver), these sequences are often noisy, fragmented, and structurally in­
consistent. Consequently, the direct application of process mining techniques to such
raw data remains infeasible. To address this challenge, a modular pipeline is proposed to
abstract fine­grained symbolic action sequences into semantically meaningful high­level
events, with human assembly tasks serving as the primary use case.
The abstraction process begins with symbolic micro­actions extracted from multi­view
video recordings of toy assembly procedures. These action sequences are segmented
into overlapping windows using a fixed­size sliding window approach (parameter speci­
fications are provided in Chapter4). Each windowed segment is subsequently encoded
using a Transformer­based model trained with a contrastive learning objective, which en­
courages the separation of semantically distinct contexts in the embedding space.
The resulting segment embeddings are then projected into a lower­dimensional mani­
fold using UMAP [36], followed by clustering via HDBSCAN [31], a density­based al­
gorithm well suited for nonlinear and noisy datasets. Key hyperparameters for UMAP
(e.g., n_neighbors, min_dist) and HDBSCAN (e.g., min_cluster_size) were empiri­
cally tuned in preliminary experiments; the final settings are detailed in Chapter4.
To obtain human­interpretable abstractions, representative segments from each cluster
are selected and used to prompt a large language model (LLM), which assigns concise
semantic labels. A post­processing step is then applied to merge semantically redundant
clusters based on LLM­based similarity estimation and manual verification. The resulting
abstract event sequences are formatted into structured logs, which serve as input for
downstream process discovery and conformance analysis.

3.3
Event Abstraction
The objective of this stage is to transform low­level symbolic micro­action sequences
into higher­level, semantically meaningful events suitable for process discovery. This
is achieved through a three­step pipeline:
• Contextual representation learning: A contrastively trained Transformer encoder
generates contextual embeddings for symbolic micro­action windows.
• Unsupervised clustering: Segment embeddings are grouped into latent behav­
ioral categories using dimensionality reduction and clustering techniques.
• Semantic labeling: A large language model (LLM) assigns interpretable labels to
the discovered clusters.

The following subsections describe each component in detail.
3.3.1
Representation Learning
To capture the local temporal context of symbolic micro­action windows, this work em­
ploys a Transformer­based encoder trained using contrastive learning objectives. Instead
of treating symbolic events as generic tokens, we follow recent advances in process
representation learning that emphasize learning domain­specific embeddings for event
logs [28], [37].
The encoder adopted in this study is based on the bert-base-uncased model from the
Hugging Face Transformers library [38], initialized with pretrained weights and fine­tuned
on symbolic micro­action sequences. As suggested by prior research on process repre­
sentation learning [28], generic pretraining objectives such as Masked Language Model­
14
From Video to Process: Semantic Event Abstraction via Representation Learning and LLMs
==PAGE:23==
ing (MLM)—which randomly masks input tokens and trains the model to predict them—
often exhibit limited discriminative capacity in domains characterized by repetitive and
compact vocabularies, such as symbolic action logs derived from video. Experimental
results in this work confirmed that embeddings trained solely with MLM lacked sufficient
semantic separation for downstream clustering tasks. A more detailed analysis and com­
parison of MLM­based and contrastive learning objectives is provided in Chapter8.
To address this limitation, a contrastive representation learning strategy was adopted,
in line with the broader trend in event embedding research [37]. Positive sample pairs
are constructed using temporally adjacent or augmented micro­action segments from the
same case, while negatives are sampled from unrelated cases. This training is guided
by the InfoNCE (Information Noise Contrastive Estimation) loss, which encourages em­
beddings of positive pairs to be similar while pushing negative pairs apart. It operates by
contrasting each positive pair against multiple negative examples within the same batch,
promoting semantic separability in the learned representation space.
The resulting encoder maps each action window to a fixed­dimensional vector suitable
for unsupervised clustering. Compared to traditional one­hot or statistical encodings, this
embedding­based abstraction pipeline enables more scalable and flexible modeling of
complex visual processes.
3.3.2
Clustering
The symbol micro­action sequences are abstracted into advanced semantic activities,
using a two­step unsupervised abstraction process that includes dimensionality reduction
followed by clustering. This section outlines the design principles and theoretical consid­
erations of the selected technology.
Dimensionality Reduction.
To project high­dimensional segment embeddings into a
space amenable to clustering, two dimensionality reduction methods were considered:
• Principal Component Analysis (PCA) [30]: A linear technique that projects data
onto orthogonal axes of maximal variance. While efficient and interpretable, PCA
only captures global variance and may distort local neighborhood relationships,
which are critical for semantic embeddings.
• Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) [36]: A non­linear man­
ifold learning technique that preserves both local and global data structure. UMAP
constructs a high­dimensional graph representing local relationships and optimizes
a low­dimensional layout with similar topological properties. Its ability to maintain
semantic continuity makes it better suited for capturing fine­grained behavior dis­
tinctions in action embeddings.
Other techniques such as t­SNE [39] were excluded due to their instability under repeated
runs, inability to preserve global structure, and poor performance when used as a pre­
cursor to clustering [40]. In our context, we prioritize methods that provide consistent
structure and preserve both micro­ and macro­level relationships between segments.
Clustering Algorithms.
Then evaluated three unsupervised clustering algorithms to
group embedded action segments into abstract event categories:
• K­Means: A centroid­based method that partitions data into k isotropic clusters by
minimizing intra­cluster variance. It is computationally efficient but assumes convex
cluster shapes and requires the number of clusters to be specified in advance.
From Video to Process: Semantic Event Abstraction via Representation Learning and LLMs
15
==PAGE:24==
• Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM): A probabilistic model that represents the data
as a mixture of Gaussian distributions. GMM allows soft cluster assignments but
still relies on assumptions about Gaussianity and requires choosing the number of
components beforehand.
• HDBSCAN [31]: A hierarchical, density­based clustering algorithm that extends DB­
SCAN by enabling variable density regions and robust cluster selection. HDBSCAN
does not require predefining the number of clusters and can label outliers as noise,
which is particularly useful for real­world noisy embeddings derived from human­
performed actions.
Five combinations of dimensionality reduction and clustering benchmarked were selected
: PCA+KMeans, PCA+GMM, UMAP+KMeans, UMAP+GMM, and UMAP+HDBSCAN.
The combination of UMAP and HDBSCAN was ultimately selected due to its superior
performance in terms of cluster compactness, semantic coherence, and noise tolerance.
Quantitative and qualitative evaluations of all configurations are presented in Chapter5.
3.3.3
Semantic Labeling via LLM
Once clustering is complete, a GPT­based language model is used to generate concise
semantic labels for each cluster. Representative windows which are centroidal examples
are selected and formatted into prompts with task­specific instructions. To handle over­
lapping semantics (e.g., ”assemble wheel” vs. ”install wheel”), a secondary abstraction
step computes label similarity using LLM embeddings, with candidate merges manually
reviewed to finalize the event vocabulary. This produces structured, high­level event se­
quences, which serve as the abstracted event log for subsequent process discovery.
3.4
Process Discovery
3.4.1
Abstract Event Log Generation
Following semantic labeling, each trace consists of overlapping micro­action windows,
each associated with a cluster identifier and a corresponding high­level event label.
To obtain a clean and structured abstract event log, a greedy segmentation strategy is
applied to select one representative event at a time and skip overlapping or redundant
segments.
The process starts by scanning from the beginning of each trace using a sliding window
approach. At each position, a fixed­size window is considered—using lengths of 10, 15,
or 20 micro­actions. For the segments within the window, their embeddings are aver­
aged and compared to cluster prototypes. The most similar cluster is selected as the
representative abstract event.
The window then shifts forward by the same number of micro­actions (stride = window
size), ensuring that selected events do not overlap. This continues until the end of the
trace.
Each abstract event is assigned the timestamp of the first micro­action in its window. The
final output for each case is a sequence of time­ordered abstract events, recorded in the
standard process mining format with:
• Case ID — A unique identifier corresponding to each video case.
• Event Name — The abstract label assigned to the corresponding cluster (e.g., as­
semble wheel).
• Timestamp — The start time of the earliest segment within the matched window.

3.4.2
Process Discovery
To extract interpretable process models from the abstracted event logs, three complemen­
tary discovery strategies are employed: declarative modeling via DCR graphs, procedural
modeling using the Inductive Miner, and frequency­based modeling with the Disco tool.
These approaches represent distinct paradigms of process modeling—each offering dif­
ferent strengths in terms of formal expressiveness, behavioral flexibility, and interpretabil­
ity.
Declarative Modeling.
Dynamic Condition Response (DCR) graphs [41] are used to
capture constraint­based behavior representations. Declarative models are particularly
well­suited to human­performed tasks characterized by high variability and partial order
execution. The abstract event logs are analyzed using the dcr4py library [42], which sup­
ports the automatic derivation of relations such as condition, response, milestone, and
exclude based on temporal ordering. The resulting models are further explored in the
DCR Designer tool. Declarative modeling enables the representation of soft constraints
and optional behaviors, making it suitable for environments where rigid control­flow as­
sumptions may not hold.
Procedural Modeling.
Procedural models aim to reconstruct explicit control­flow struc­
tures from event logs. Among various procedural discovery algorithms, the Inductive
Miner (IM) and its filtering variant (IMf) are selected, both implemented in the PM4Py li­
brary [43]. Compared to older methods such as the Alpha Miner [44], which suffers from
low robustness to noise and poor handling of concurrency, the Inductive Miner guarantees
soundness of the resulting Petri nets and can handle noise and infrequent behavior more
effectively. The Heuristics Miner [45] is another alternative, but it lacks formal guarantees
and often produces overfitted or unsound models in low­support settings. The choice
of IM/IMf strikes a balance between formal correctness, simplicity, and real­world appli­
cability. Noise thresholds are tuned to control model granularity and suppress spurious
behavior patterns in the abstract event logs. The resulting Petri nets are evaluated using
established quality metrics including fitness, precision, generalization, and simplicity.
Frequency­Based Modeling.
To provide an interpretable, statistical overview of behav­
ior without enforcing execution semantics, the Disco tool is used [46]. Disco generates
directly­follows graphs (DFGs) enriched with frequency and performance statistics, which
are particularly helpful in exploratory settings. Although such models do not support for­
mal analysis or simulation, they offer high visual interpretability and are often preferred
by analysts for identifying core routines, optional paths, or rare deviations. This method
complements the formal nature of procedural and declarative models by emphasizing ob­
servable trends over theoretical completeness.
Rationale for Combined Strategy.
The selection of these three strategies ensures
methodological diversity across formalisms. While procedural models offer structure and
analyzability, they are rigid and less effective in capturing flexible or partially ordered be­
havior. Declarative models address this by modeling constraints rather than sequences,
but may lack interpretability for novice users. Frequency­based models offer immediate

visual feedback, though they sacrifice formal rigor. This triad provides a comprehensive
lens through which to evaluate the abstraction pipeline’s capacity to support diverse ana­
lytical needs—ranging from formal model discovery to practical process insight.
All models are derived from the same abstracted event logs, enabling direct comparison
in Chapter6 and Chapter8 in terms of structural complexity, semantic alignment, and user
interpretability.

Experimental Setup
4.1
Overview
This chapter presents the experimental setup used to train the abstraction model and
evaluate its effectiveness in supporting process discovery, using high­quality symbolic
annotations as input. All experiments in this stage are conducted exclusively on the of­
ficial fine­grained action labels provided by the Assembly101 dataset [14]. Predictions
from vision­based recognition models and multi­view fusion mechanisms are deliberately
excluded and will be addressed separately in Chapter7.
The chapter is organized into two main parts:
• The first part details the training procedure of a Transformer­based embedding model,
which learns contextual representations from symbolic action sequences. A pre­
training corpus is constructed from annotated traces and segmented to support both
masked language modeling and contrastive learning. The resulting embeddings are
projected using UMAP and clustered with HDBSCAN to yield semantically coher­
ent event segments. These clusters are then labeled using a large language model
(LLM) to form abstract activity types.
• The second part applies the full abstraction pipeline to the annotated sequences
to generate structured event logs composed of high­level semantic events. These
abstracted logs are used as input to process discovery methods, including Inductive
Miner and DCR graph generation, allowing for a controlled evaluation of how well
the abstraction supports interpretable and structured model reconstruction.
4.2
Model Training and Abstraction
4.2.1
Training Data Preparation
To train the embedding model for event abstraction, a symbolic corpus is constructed
from the official fine­grained annotations of the Assembly101 dataset. Each disassembly–
assembly instance is identified by a unique case_id and annotated as a sequence of
verb–object action labels (e.g., pick screwdriver, unscrew bolt).
For each case, the action labels are grouped by case_id and concatenated into a single
line using the [SEP] delimiter, preserving the original temporal order. The resulting corpus
comprises one line per case, formatted as follows:
pick screwdriver [SEP] reach screw [SEP] put down screwdriver
To accommodate BERT’s maximum token length of 512, a dynamic sliding window strat­
egy is applied to split longer sequences into token­compliant segments. Each line is first
tokenized using the BERT tokenizer, and the window is greedily extended until approach­
ing the limit. A fixed overlap of 15 actions is maintained between adjacent windows to
preserve local temporal continuity. Actions that cause token overflow are skipped. The
final corpus consists of valid training segments suitable for masked language modeling
(MLM) and contrastive learning.
4.2.2
Transformer Embedding Model Training
The embedding model adopts the bert-base-uncased architecture and is fine­tuned us­
ing a hybrid objective that combines masked language modeling (MLM) and contrastive
learning. For the MLM component, 15% of input tokens are randomly masked, following

4.2.4

To assign interpretable names to the discovered activity clusters, a large language model
(LLM) is employed. Each cluster is represented by its medoid—the symbolic window
whose embedding is closest to the cluster centroid.
These representative windows are converted into natural language prompts and submit­

To ensure reproducibility, the temperature parameter is set to 0. Initial outputs are often
overly specific or redundant (e.g., “install wheel”, “attach wheel”, “assemble wheel”). To
address this, a secondary abstraction step is performed: similar labels are grouped by
semantic similarity, and a generalized label is derived by re­prompting the LLM, e.g.:
Given these similar labels: install wheel, attach wheel, assemble
wheel, provide a more general name that captures their common meaning.
This two­stage procedure results in a compact and domain­consistent vocabulary of high­
level activities. The finalized labels are then used to replace corresponding symbolic
windows in each case, forming the abstracted event log.

4.3.2
Abstract Event Log Construction
To construct abstract event logs, symbolic traces are segmented and encoded using the
trained Transformer. For each position in the sequence, multiple candidate windows (e.g.,
lengths 10, 15, and 20) are extracted and embedded. Each embedding is compared
to cluster centroids via cosine similarity, and the most similar cluster is selected. The
corresponding abstract label is assigned to the window.
A greedy segmentation strategy is used to minimize redundancy: after each assignment,
the window advances by the selected length minus a fixed overlap (3 actions). If the
remaining tail of the sequence is too short for standard windows but exceeds a minimum
threshold (e.g., 5 actions), a fallback abstraction is applied.
To avoid trivial loops, adjacent identical labels are merged. The final trace is a sequence
of high­level events, each linked to the set of micro­actions it summarizes. Abstract logs
are saved in CSV format with the following fields:
• case_id — Unique identifier for the disassembly–assembly instance.
• abstracted_action — Assigned high­level label from the LLM.
• Timestamp — The start timestamp for abstracted sequence.

Quantitative Evaluation
Three standard internal clustering metrics were used. These metrics collectively capture
cluster compactness, separation, and overall structure, providing a robust basis for eval­
uating clustering performance [50].
• Silhouette Score (↑): This metric quantifies how well each data point fits within its
assigned cluster compared to other clusters. It is defined as the normalized differ­
ence between the average intra­cluster distance and the nearest­cluster distance.
A higher silhouette score indicates that clusters are both cohesive (points are close
to each other within the same cluster) and well­separated from other clusters.
• Calinski–Harabasz Index (↑): Also known as the Variance Ratio Criterion, this met­
ric evaluates the ratio of between­cluster dispersion to within­cluster dispersion. A
higher value suggests that clusters are dense and well separated. It is sensitive to
the number of clusters and works well when cluster shapes are convex.
• Davies–Bouldin Index (↓): This index measures the average similarity between
each cluster and its most similar cluster, where similarity is defined as the ratio of
within­cluster scatter to between­cluster separation. Lower values indicate better
clustering performance, with well­separated and compact clusters.

6.5
Summary and Comparative Reflection
This chapter evaluated three contrasting approaches to process discovery—declarative
modeling via DCR graphs, procedural modeling via the Inductive Miner, and frequency­
based visualization via Disco—applied to the same abstracted event log derived from a
semi­structured toy assembly process. Each method was analyzed in terms of its mod­
eling assumptions, structural expressiveness, interpretability, and alignment with the be­
havioral characteristics of the domain.
Declarative modeling with DCR graphs was unable to extract meaningful constraints, as
the input log lacked consistent causal, conditional, or exclusive relationships. Although
such dependencies may have existed in the original fine­grained action sequences, they
were eliminated during the abstraction process to improve tractability. This illustrates a
trade­off between semantic richness and modelability: while abstraction facilitates analy­
sis, it can obscure the low­level dependencies that constraint­based models depend on.
Procedural modeling using the Inductive Miner yielded technically sound Petri nets with
near­perfect fitness, but consistently low precision and limited interpretability. The discov­
ered models overgeneralized due to the high variability and absence of dominant control­
flow patterns in the data. While this reflects the operational flexibility of real­world assem­
bly tasks, it limits the analytical value of procedural discovery in such domains. Moreover,
attempts to apply the Inductive Miner directly to the unabstracted log were infeasible due
to excessive event vocabulary size and trace­level sparsity, again emphasizing the trade­
off between granularity and model fidelity.
In contrast, Disco’s frequency­based approach provided a cognitively aligned and inter­
pretable representation of both routine and exceptional behavior. By avoiding rigid struc­
tural assumptions, it highlighted dominant paths, optional branches, and trace­level devi­
ations. This enabled analysts to reason about commonalities and exceptions in a manner
consistent with human cognitive models of process understanding. For the toy assembly
process, this approach proved the most effective in revealing actionable patterns.
Taken together, these findings suggest that traditional control­flow modeling techniques
may be ill­suited for domains characterized by contextual flexibility, human judgment, and
physical interaction constraints. In such settings, exploratory tools that tolerate structural
variation and emphasize interpretability—such as frequency­based visualization—can of­
fer more practical insights.
Looking ahead, hybrid strategies that combine the semantic abstraction power of large
language models with the flexibility of frequency­based modeling may provide a promis­
ing direction. For instance, cluster­based abstraction could be coupled with frequency­

structural rigor. More broadly, this comparative study underscores the importance of align­

7.5
Reflection and Limitations
The evaluation presented in this chapter shows that the proposed abstraction pipeline
can substantially improve the semantic quality of event logs generated from vision­based
symbolic predictions. In both single­view and multi­view settings, applying abstraction
significantly boosts the F1­score. Among the two, multi­view input yields both higher
symbolic prediction accuracy and better abstraction results, with the highest F1­score
achieved in this configuration. This suggests that the pipeline is able to take advantage
of complementary observations across different camera views to generate more coherent
and semantically meaningful event logs.
Despite these encouraging results, several limitations remain. First, the symbolic pre­
dictions used as input are often noisy and temporally inconsistent. Second, the evalu­
ation is based on strict segment­wise matching with reference annotations. While this
provides a clear and reproducible way to measure performance, it does not account for
partial matches or semantic similarity between predicted and ground­truth segments. For
instance, two labels that are conceptually close but phrased differently are still treated
as mismatches. This could lead to an underestimation of the actual alignment between
predicted and reference event logs.
To better reflect semantic similarity, future work could consider using softer evaluation
metrics. One option is the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD), which measures how much
effort is needed to transform one distribution into another. Adapting this to compare ab­
stracted event sequences could help account for both label similarity and timing differ­
ences.
Finally, the current evaluation is limited to a subset of the Assembly101 dataset with
ground­truth annotations. While this setting allows for controlled testing, it does not fully
reflect the challenges of applying the pipeline to real­world data. In practice, inputs may
vary more in terms of environment, camera placement, and task complexity. Future work
should therefore include tests on less constrained datasets to better assess the general­
ization ability of the proposed method.

The pipeline proposed in this thesis operates on symbolic action sequences extracted

the input data exhibits extreme granularity, temporal fragmentation, and substantial inter­
instance variability.
Although such micro­actions may display local regularities—such as tool grasping typi­
cally preceding usage—these patterns are weakly expressed and highly context­dependent,
making it difficult for process discovery algorithms to recover consistent structural pat­
terns. In the absence of abstraction, the inherent noise and variability of raw sequences
render them intractable for downstream modeling or interpretability.

8.3
Evolution of Embedding Strategy: From Masked
Modeling to Contrastive Learning
The initial embedding strategy adopted in this study utilized masked language model­
ing (MLM) as the primary pretraining objective for the Transformer encoder. Inspired by
the success of BERT in language understanding tasks, this approach involved randomly
masking tokens within symbolic action sequences and training the model to reconstruct
them from contextual cues. The intended outcome was the learning of contextualized em­
beddings that capture short­range dependencies between micro­actions in physical task
executions.
However, the MLM­based approach proved insufficient for the specific demands of event
abstraction in this domain. Action tokens such as “lift,” “grab,” or “rotate” are semantically
under­specified and often occur within highly compressed sequences. Moreover, identical

action labels may appear in semantically distinct contexts—e.g., “assemble first wheel”
versus “assemble second wheel.” While such cases can be reasonably grouped under a
shared activity category (e.g., wheel assembly), many other actions lack such clear task­
level consistency. As a result, the clustering process yielded a small number of coherent
groups but assigned a large portion of segments to noise. This indicates that the em­
beddings learned under the MLM objective failed to adequately capture context­sensitive
semantics, with clusters often dominated by surface­level lexical similarities rather than
deeper functional structure.
To address these limitations, the embedding strategy was revised to combine masked
language modeling (MLM) with contrastive learning as a hybrid training paradigm. While
MLM supports local contextualization by reconstructing masked tokens, contrastive learn­
ing enhances global semantic structure by encouraging the encoder to separate semanti­
cally dissimilar segments and bring similar ones closer in the embedding space. Positive
and negative segment pairs were sampled both within and across cases, allowing the
model to learn task­level boundaries beyond token­level co­occurrence. Empirical results
confirmed that this combined approach yielded more coherent clusters, reduced noise,
and better alignment with meaningful behavioral patterns compared to using MLM alone.
This transition—from token­level MLM to segment­level contrastive learning—proved es­
sential to the success of the abstraction pipeline. It underscores the importance of align­
ing pretraining strategies with the structural characteristics of symbolic video­derived data
and with the requirements of downstream modeling tasks such as event abstraction and
clustering.

8.5
Limitations
Although the proposed pipeline demonstrates promising performance in symbolic abstrac­
tion and process modeling, several limitations remain regarding data availability, repre­
sentation strategy, label validation, and cross­domain applicability. These issues are dis­
cussed in detail below.
• Limited data volume for contrastive embedding training. The contrastive repre­
sentation learning module was trained on 206 annotated cases from Assembly101.
Contrastive methods rely on diverse positive and negative pairs to structure the em­
bedding space effectively. The limited number of training instances may hinder the
model’s ability to capture the full range of assembly strategies and object interac­
tions, thereby reducing generalizability. Expanding the training set could improve
contextual representation and enhance semantic cluster separability.
• Restricted exploration of representation learning architectures. The current
implementation employs a BERT­based Transformer encoder optimized for sym­
bolic sequences.
However, BERT is not tailored for modeling temporally struc­
tured or action­oriented data.
Recent developments in lightweight and domain­
adapted encoders—such as DistilBERT, TinyBERT, TimeSFormer, and other tem­
poral architectures—were not evaluated. These alternatives may provide improved
abstraction quality, reduced model complexity, or greater efficiency, particularly in
resource­constrained settings.
• Fixed­size sliding window segmentation may disrupt semantic continuity. The
embedding model operates on fixed­length input windows during training, which
simplifies preprocessing but disregards natural variation in action duration and event
boundaries. This rigid segmentation may split semantically connected actions—
such as preparatory motions or transitional gestures—across separate windows,
weakening long­range contextual encoding. More adaptive segmentation techniques,
such as attention­based chunking or dynamic windowing, could better preserve se­
mantic coherence during embedding.
• LLM­generated labels lack systematic validation. Cluster labeling was performed
using a large language model (LLM) based on the assumption that the clusters re­
flect coherent semantic structure and that the LLM can generate accurate labels.
Although this approach enabled efficient abstraction with minimal manual interven­
tion, it lacks formal validation. Only a subset of clusters underwent manual review,
and no inter­annotator agreement or domain­specific evaluation metric was applied.
This weak supervision introduces a risk of semantically ambiguous or imprecise la­
bels in the final abstract event log.
• Absence of cross­domain and cross­dataset evaluation. All experiments were
conducted using the Assembly101 dataset, which focuses on manual toy assembly
in a fixed­rig camera environment. While the pipeline showed strong performance in
this domain, its generalizability to other domains remains untested. Potential bench­
marks such as IKEA ASM, EPIC­Kitchens, or Breakfast Actions present complex,
visually unstructured activities and could serve as valuable testbeds for assessing
robustness. Evaluating the pipeline across diverse datasets is essential to demon­
strate its broader applicability in real­world scenarios.

8.6
Future Work
To extend the findings of this thesis and address the identified limitations, several research
directions are proposed. These include enhancing evaluation robustness, improving rep­
resentation learning strategies, designing context­sensitive segmentation models, and
validating the pipeline across broader application domains.
• Developing more flexible evaluation metrics. The current evaluation relies on
strict position­wise or segment­wise matching to compute precision, recall, and F1­
score. While reproducible, such metrics may penalize outputs that are semantically
similar but temporally misaligned. Future work should explore distance­based met­
rics such as Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) or edit distance with semantic alignment.
These approaches can better account for minor shifts in event order or timing, en­
abling more realistic assessments of abstraction quality. Additionally, incorporating
ranking­based comparisons or confidence­weighted matching could further enrich
the evaluation protocol.
• Systematic analysis of embedding architectures and training strategies. The
current embedding module uses a BERT­based encoder trained with contrastive
loss and fixed hyperparameters. However, the broader design space for symbolic
action embedding remains underexplored. Future studies should investigate al­
ternative architectures (e.g., GRU, Bi­LSTM, DistilBERT, TimeSFormer), training
objectives (e.g., triplet loss, InfoNCE, supervised contrastive learning), and hyper­
parameter settings (e.g., batch size, learning rate, sampling strategy). Such com­
parative analyses could inform best practices for pretraining in symbolic or visual
domains and improve downstream abstraction performance.
• Training a domain­specific segmentation model. The pipeline currently seg­
ments input using fixed­length sliding windows, which may misalign with natural task
boundaries. A promising direction is to develop a dedicated segmentation model
that identifies soft or hard boundaries based on temporal context. Techniques such
as sequence tagging (e.g., CRF or Transformer­based models), boundary detection
networks, or self­supervised change­point detection could be employed. Aligning
segmentation with task­relevant transitions would yield more coherent behavioral
units and enhance cluster purity and label consistency.
• Integrating temporal dynamics into process discovery. While the current ab­
straction focuses on symbolic structure, it does not explicitly consider temporal at­
tributes such as duration or inter­event intervals. Incorporating such information
during embedding or process discovery may enrich the resulting models. For ex­
ample, time­aware embeddings or temporal process models (e.g., time­augmented
Petri nets, temporal DCR graphs, or probabilistic workflows) could distinguish be­
tween fluent executions and interruptions, offering a more faithful representation of
real­world activity dynamics.
• Generalizing the pipeline across datasets and domains. All experiments in this
thesis were conducted on Assembly101, a dataset centered on toy assembly tasks.
To assess generalizability, the proposed pipeline should be applied to other domains
such as IKEA ASM, EPIC­Kitchens, or sports analytics datasets (e.g., SoccerNet).
These environments present different behavioral structures, interaction patterns,
and camera configurations. Cross­domain evaluation would reveal the pipeline’s
adaptability and highlight opportunities for domain­specific adjustments, such as
prompt tuning or view­dependent segmentation heuristics.

8.7
Conclusion
This thesis presented a modular and unsupervised abstraction pipeline designed to trans­
form fine­grained action sequences extracted from video data into process­minable ab­
stract event logs. To address the challenges posed by unstructured symbolic input from
multi­view recordings, the proposed method combined Transformer­based contrastive
representation learning, density­based clustering, and large language models (LLMs) for
semantic labeling. This framework enabled the discovery of high­level behavioral struc­
tures from low­level micro­actions without relying on manual annotations or predefined
task boundaries.
Experiments conducted on the Assembly101 dataset demonstrated that the proposed ap­
proach generalizes well to both single­view and multi­view configurations. In both cases,
the abstraction process substantially improved the interpretability and structural clarity of
the resulting event logs. For example, the segment­level F1­score increased from 0.45 to
0.75 in the single­view setting and from 0.47 to 0.77 in the multi­view setting, highlighting
the effectiveness of the learned representations and clustering strategies in filtering noisy
symbolic inputs.
Although the method was evaluated within the domain of manual assembly, the underlying
principles are broadly applicable. The pipeline integrates techniques from computer vi­
sion, sequence modeling, and process mining, offering a potential template for analyzing
unstructured behavioral data in other contexts.
Several challenges remain and point to directions for future work. These include improv­
ing the handling of temporal granularity, exploring adaptive segmentation strategies, and
validating the consistency and reliability of LLM­generated labels. Further research may
also investigate the application of this pipeline to more diverse and less controlled video
datasets.
In summary, this work contributes a reproducible and extensible approach for bridging the
gap between low­level perceptual data and high­level process analysis. By enabling pro­
cess mining from raw video input, it opens up new possibilities for understanding human
activity in complex, sensor­rich environments.
