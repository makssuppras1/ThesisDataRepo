Abstract 
Due to climate change it is expected that precipitation will increase in Denmark. Together with im-
permeable urban areas, the runoff from those are expected to increase as well. The drainage system 
in Denmark are common sewers and do not have the expected capacity to cope with the increased 
runoff. If the problem is not addressed an increase in overflow frequencies and amount is going to 
happen. Separation of the drainage system is thought as a solution for resolving the problem. Nature 
Based Solutions (NBS) can be used to improve the natural water balance by increasing the infiltration 
and evaporation. LAR-potential is a tool that assess the impact of different types of NBS has been 
developed. The tool is now under further development in order to make it interactive. 
The purpose of this project was to automate simulations in SWMM that produced performance indi-
cators for a bio-retention cell which are going to be a part of the interactive tool. The performance 
indicator consists of the annual water balance together with the return period for overflow and the 
effective volume. Initially input values for a bio-retention cell in SWMM were found based on sen-
sitivity analysis and literature studies. SWMM was to be initiated through Python via a package called 
PySWMM. Results from both SWMM directly and via Python showed no difference. The perfor-
mance indicators were calculated based on SWMM’s simulations outputs (annual water balance) to-
gether with time series analysis. A python script was developed that initiated simulations in SWMM 
and at each time step extracted the overflow time series from the bio-retention cell. This was used for 
determining the return period for overflow and effective volume. Further, the script iterated through 
different conditions for a bio-retention cell. The conditions included the area ratio between the catch-
ment and the NBS itself and the native soil’s hydraulic conductivity. At each change the performance 
indicators were calculated and stored in a database. 
Based on the performance indicators in the database an example of how the interactive tool could be 
used to prevent impervious urban areas to contribute to overflow were given.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
 
The continuous increase in population and in the number and size of urban regions across the world, 
pose great challenges for sustainable development - especially in the urban environment. By 2050 the 
urban population is expected to double in urban areas (United Nations 2017). Cities will face enor-
mous challenges related to climate threats which includes more extreme weather. Land development 
activities currently increases the imperviousness of the land surface which decreases the potential for 
infiltration thus increasing the runoff. Further, increased imperviousness decreases the time for runoff 
to peak and a shorter time of concentration (Maliva 2019).  
Over the several past decades global warming has been linked to have changed the hydrological cycle. 
This includes changes in precipitation patterns, higher intensities and more extreme rain events. It is 
most likely the northern part of the world which would receive the greatest impact (Bates, B. 
Kundzewicz, Z. Wu 2008). It has been concluded that Denmark in last 20 years has experienced more 
extreme rain events and an increase in the frequency of these (Arnbjerg-Nielsen 2006). Ultimately 
this is causing more occurrences of flooding in the urban areas as the drainage systems is designed 
on historical data and climate changes were not considered. 
All municipalities should have a save plan for disposal of wastewater according to the Danish Envi-
ronmental Protection Act. As a result, the municipality of Copenhagen came with a plan, 
Spildevandplan, for managing all sort of wastewater. Wastewater management is an important factor 
when it comes to the aquatic environment, urban development, recreational activities and climate 
adaptation (Københavns Kommune 2018). The plan ensures that the requirements of the Danish En-
vironmental Protection Act are meet. Further, the plan ensures that wastewater is only expected on 
the surface once every 10th year and every 5th year for catchments with separate drainage. During a 
100-year event a maximum of 10 cm of wastewater is allowed in the public space. Separating the 
drainage system is one of the methods used to meet the requirements. The current state of the drainage 
system is in good condition but lack the capacity as more rain is expected. Separating the rain leaves 
more capacity to the existing drainage system and avoid expansion of pipes and conduits.    
Given the increase in urbanization worldwide, more extreme weather and the impact of urban storm-
water the management of urban drainage is a critically important challenge (Fletcher et al. 2015). 
Nature Based Solutions (NBS), can be employed to restore urban water systems back to pre-devel-
oped conditions. They are designed to be sustainable urban water systems that have multiple benefits, 
such as stormwater pollution management and frequent flood mitigation. This could be a paramount 
solution for urban city development. 
In Denmark detailed hydrological and hydraulic models are used in the planning of the urban water 
system. These models are too complex in the sense of implementing NBS in urban areas. Working 
with NBS as a solution in an early planning and design phase requires clear communication and 
cooperation between water and non-water professionals. The accuracy of the solution can therefore 
be down prioritized in order to work on a solution that can be employed fast (Lerer, Vester, et al. 
2015).

There is a need to develop a tool that is readily adaptable across professional disciplines regarding 
climate adaption in the urban area. A tool has been developed by (Lerer, Sørup, et al. 2015) that meet 
the requirements of the utility companies in the early planning and design phase of employment of 
NBS in urban areas. The tool allows the user to get the assessment of the hydrological impact of an 
NBS in a given area. The area ratio between the catchment and the NBS, together with impervious 
area of the catchment and the hydraulic conductivity of its native soil are needed for input to the tool. 
The impact is then found through a lookup table which consist of return period for overflow and the 
annual water balance. The use of lookup tables enables instantaneous results of the impact and com-
pletely frees the user for managing the rainfall (Lerer, Sørup, et al. 2015).  
 
The team behind the tool has been given a grant from the MUDP and a new project has been initiated 
that will further develop the tool. SCALGO is one of the main actors in this project and has developed 
a flood risk platform, SCALGO Live, used for flood screening. The idea is to incorporate the tool in 
the already existing platform and will give the tool an interactive interface. This allows the user to 
draw multiple NBS on a catchment and immediately see the result of those. The tool is expected to 
be available on the market by the end of 2020. 
 
1.2 Problem statement 
The purpose of this master’s thesis is to get a detailed understanding on the impact on surface runoff 
of nature-based solutions (NBS) when they are being implemented in impervious urban areas. Ini-
tially, one type of NBS, a bio-retention cell, is going to be investigated and tested. Hydrological 
modelling is necessary for the purpose of this project. Using the United States Environmental Protec-
tion Agency’s Storm Water Management Model (SWMM) allows long-term simulations for surface 
and subsurface hydrology. SWMM contains the opportunity to add NBS to catchments which enables 
the investigation of their impact on urban catchments. Further, the purpose is to create a script that 
will automatically initiate simulations in SWMM and iteratively changes the conditions for a bio-
retention cell. These conditions are the area ratio between the NBS and the catchment together with 
the hydraulic conductivity of the native soil. The script initiates SWMM via a Python extension called 
PySWMM and it has to be adaptable for other types of NBS. This will generate sufficient amount of 
data for NBS and their impact in urban areas. The data will eventually be stored in an SQLite database 
that will be used by the incorporated tool on the platform of SCALGO Live.  
Following objectives must be solved in this thesis: 
• Understand the processes within a bio-retention cell. 
• Determine reliable input values for a bio-retention cell. 
• Making sure that simulation outputs from SWMM can be replicated through Python. 
• Creating a script that automatically iterates through different conditions for NBS.

2 Theory 
2.1 Nature Based Solutions (NBS) 
In the case of urban drainage management, a common use of terminology has been difficult to estab-
lish since dealing with urban water issues in different parts of the world allows for local interpreta-
tions of similar concepts thus needing to name it something new. For example, low impact develop-
ment (LID) which is mostly used in North American and is also what SWMM uses. LID tries to 
minimize the cost of stormwater management and does that by using the site layout and integrated 
control measures in order to establish a natural hydrology (Fletcher et al. 2015). In Australia the term 
water sensitive urban design (WSUD) is used and tries to manage the water balance and maintain/im-
prove water quality. Sustainable urban drainage systems (SUDS) is used as the term in the UK and 
aims to achieve a more sustainable way of draining stormwater compared to conventional solutions. 
LAR is a Danish term and is an abbreviation of the words “lokal afledning af regnvand” which is 
translated to local diversion of stormwater.  
All of the above names and definitions of solutions dealing with stormwater are more or less trying 
to achieve the same; reducing the risk of flooding. This point is at least what this project is focusing 
on. More precisely reducing the risk of flooding be the means of Nature Based Solutions (NBS). In 
this project, NBS is used for all solutions that manage stormwater. Though when addressing storm-
water solutions in SWMM the native term LID is used. 
2.2 Modelling tools 
Storm Water Management Model (SWMM) is a widely used for its ability to model and analyze 
advanced urban water processes. It was developed back in 1971 by the United States Environmental 
Protection Agency (Rossman 2015). It has undergone various major upgrades since its release and 
currently SWMM 5.1 is their latest version, which is a completely re-written built. This current ver-
sion allows engineers to represent combinations of green infrastructure practices as LID controls to 
determine their effectiveness in managing runoff. Some of these practices can also provide significant 
pollutant reduction benefits. Though, SWMM only models reduction in runoff flow volume, at the 
moment (US EPA 2019). 
SWMM is used in this project firstly because of the ability to implement LID modules to the catch-
ment. Secondly SWMM is interfaceable with Python through a package called PySWMM. This pack-
age allows for control algorithms to be developed exclusively in PySWMM which further allows 
combinations of functions and object as well as storing hydraulic data.  
 
2.3 Rain data – Kløvermarken  
The data comes from historical rainfall series which is based on rainfall recording from station 
Kløvermarken in Amager, Denmark. The rain series covers approximately 36 years of data, stretching 
from 07.01.1979 to 09.12.2015. This data was obtained by using a tipping bucket rain gauge with 1-
minute recordings. The data is compressed to 10-minute timesteps. The 36 years of rainfall makes it 
possible to test how well the NBS performs in different conditions. The rain series contains more 
events with low return periods but still includes some extreme events, see Figure 1.

2.4 Bio-retention cells 
There is a variety of NBS, and this thesis will narrow it down to only focus on bio-retention cells, as 
the elements within roughly can represent other types of NBS  A bio-retention cell can be configured 
in many ways, but the main purpose of such units is to collect water coming upstream from a catch-
ment which the bio-retention cell is treating and retain it for a period of time. It usually consists of 
three different layers which includes a surface layer, a soil layer and a storage layer. The type of bio-
retention cell in this thesis will have all these layers and configured with a drain in the bottom of the 
storage layer. The storage layer also has the possibility to exfiltrate water through the bottom. It is 
worth mentioning that the bottom of the drain is aligned with the bottom of the storage layer. See 
Figure 2 for an illustration of this specific type of bio-retention cell. The type used in this thesis is 
called type A. Type B, C and D are all variations of A and will be presented below.

Type A is the type of bio-retention cell that will be used as reference in this thesis. This type includes 
all the mass transfer components showed on the figure above. The other types are simple variations 
of Type A where a component has been removed or changed. Type B has no exfiltration through the 
bottom. Type C has no drain. Type D has no exfiltration thorough the bottom, but the drain has been 
elevated thus creating an offset. There will be performed simulations for all four types and the per-
formance indicators for these will be available in the database. 
The surface layer is usually occupied with some degree of vegetation and acts like a depression which 
volume is defined by the berm height and the amount vegetation present within the depression. Water 
in the surface layer is stored and the residence time is determined by the rate of evaporation and 
infiltration to the soil layer. If the run-on to the bio-retention cell exceeds the rate of evaporation and 
infiltration the water level within the unit will rise and eventually the unit will be overflown. 
The soil layer consists of some sort of engineered media. These types of media are designed for the 
purpose of improving the infiltration- and water-capacity and treating of incoming water. The soil

layer receives the infiltrated water from the surface layer and loses water through evaporation and 
percolation into the storage layer below it. 
The storage layer is designed to store as much water as possible why materials are chosen to obtain a 
high porosity. In Copenhagen plastic cassettes is preferred to use a storage layer in bio-retention cells. 
The storage layer receives percolated water from the soil above it and loses water via infiltration into 
the underlying native soil, by an outflow throughout an underdrain system and evaporation.

2.4.2 Hydrological processes  
The hydrological processes SWMM assumes is governing in a bio retention unit is based on following 
assumptions: 
1. Throughout the depth of the bio-retention unit the cross-sectional area of the unit remains con-
stant.  
2. Flow through the unit is one-dimensional in the vertical direction.  
3. Infiltration from the top layer into the soil is uniformly distributed.  
4. The moisture content does not change throughout the soil layer.  
5. The adhesive forces between the water and soil causing surface tension are negligible. 
 
From the assumptions the bio-retention unit can be modelled by solving a set of simple flow conti-
nuity equations. Each equation describes changes in the water content in a layer over time and are 
expressed as the difference between the inflow and the outflow water flux. The equations are listed

2.5 Uncertainties of NBS modelling in SWMM 
Computational modelling allows the user to model a variety of different scenarios before a site is 
developed. Rainfall‐runoff modelling is an example of simulations that are run to determine areas 
that are likely to flood as a result of a given storm event. Programs such as SWMM have created 
Nature Based Solutions (NBS) add‐ons that can model the role of different devices in reducing runoff 
(Charlesworth and Booth 2016). 
However, there are some limitations associated with modelling, especially when it comes to the 
model’s output and its relationship to the real world. A model is only as good as the data used to 
construct it and if the data lacks the quality, the output will suffer from it. There are also specific 
uncertainties surrounding modelling NBS. Since the solutions are nature based, it could be imagined 
that some sort of vegetation will be used. The type and amount of vegetation may vary over the chosen 
area, which is typically complex to model and can produce variable outputs (Sañudo-fontaneda et al. 
2018). Beside vegetation, many NBS contains engineered media which is crucial when it comes to 
reducing flow rates, retaining runoff for evapotranspiration and delaying discharges (Liu and 
Fassman-Beck 2017). The composition, depth and saturated hydraulic conductivity for NBS differs 
from type to type and can be the limiting factor when modelled.  
Based on sensitivity analysis the most influential parameters can be found and addressed in this thesis. 
The influential parameters are the ones that impact the output of the model most significantly. A one-
at-a-time sensitivity analysis were performed by (Schiolborg and Lindbladh 2016) in order to test the 
influence of 15 input parameters for a bio-retention cell. The analysis changed one parameter at a 
time to a lower and upper bound and calculated the impact on evaporation, infiltration and overflow 
compared to a base model. The analysis found that the area ratio between the NBS and catchment 
together with the hydraulic conductivity of the native soil to be the two most influential parameters 
(Schiolborg and Lindbladh 2016). This is an expected result, and this is what the thesis will address. 
One of the purposes was to create a database that users could use to look up the impact of an NBS 
based on the area ratio and the native soils hydraulic conductivity. More importantly is the height of 
the soil and storage layer together with the field capacity of the soil, that also showed to have a high 
impact on the result. The one-at-a-time sensitivity analysis is a fast way of identifying influential 
parameters but it does not give possible interaction between parameters (Saltelli et al. 2000). For this 
reason, two Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis were performed as well. The first analysis took the 5 
most influential parameters (area ratio, hydraulic conductivity of the native soil, height of the soil 
layer, height of the storage layer and the field capacity) and tested them. The first Monte Carlo sen-
sitivity analysis found the same influential parameters as the one-at-a-time analysis. It could not con-
clude whether the rest of the parameters were not influential. A second Monte Carlo sensitivity anal-
ysis were then performed. The second test held the two most influential parameters from the first test 
constant. The result from that test can be seen on Figure 4.

From the figure it seems that the field capacity is influential for the evaporation. It can be seen that,

been found by (Fassman-beck and Saleh 2019). They showed that the field capacity has the most 
significant influence on performance of a bio-retention cell. This conclusion was determined on sim-

4 Method 
As mentioned in the problem statement one of the objectives was to produce a database which in-
cluded the performance indicators for a bio-retention cell. These indicators consist of the water bal-
ance, return period for overflow and the effective volume. The water balance includes how much 
water is being infiltrated, evaporated, drained and overflowed from the bio-retention cell and the 
governing equations for the water balance was presented in theory section. The determination of the 
return period for overflow and the effective volume will be described in the following sections. 
4.1 Process flow chart 
On Figure 6 the working process can be seen and is meant as an overview of how the different com-
ponents are communicating in order to generate the performance indicators for the bio-retention cell. 
 
Figure 6 – Process flow chart of how SWMM is operated through PySWMM to perform these long-term simulations that are necessary 
to identify the impact of a given NBS. A Python script is incorporated in this process which is extracting the overflow volume from

The process starts by defining the catchments properties and inputting the necessary rain and evapo-
ration data in SWMM. The rain series is also loaded into the Python script and this is done simply to 
save computation time. The simulation is then initiated by PySWMM and ready to be iterated through. 
The catchment properties, rain data and evaporation data are held constant throughout the automated 
simulations. 
==PAGE:25==
Page 20 
 
Before the simulation starts the area ratio, imperviousness of the catchment and the hydraulic con-
ductivity of the native soil is changed via the input file. This is done by PySWMM. The simulation 
is then started and at each timestep the water balance is calculated and appended by SWMM. The 
water balance at the end of the simulation is then extracted by PySWMM and inserted into the data-
base via the Python script. Simultaneously with the water balance in being appended, PySWMM is 
stepping into the ongoing simulation and extracting the overflow volume from the bio-retention cell. 
Together with the loaded rain data, the return period and effective volume is being calculated and 
inserted into the database.  Finally, the area ratio and the hydraulic conductivity is inserted into the 
data base. This completes the process of: 
1. Changing input files. 
2. Initiating a simulation. 
3. Extracting time series for overflow from the bio-retention cell. 
4. Calculating the necessary performance indicators for a bio-retention cell with a specific area 
ratio which is in a native soil with a specific hydraulic conductivity. 
5. Inserting the results into the database. 
The performance indicators for the bio-retention cell has been based on the entire period that the rain 
series span over, which means that one simulation and its results only account for one entry to the 
database. Once the simulation is complete, the Python script alters the input file to the SWMM model, 
and the above steps are repeated. The alteration iterates through different area ratios (between the 
bio-retention cell and the catchment) and the native soils hydraulic conductivity. This would theoret-
ically give a complete understanding of a bio-retention cell’s impact in urban areas. The final script 
and explanation of the script can be found in the appendix.

4.3 Determination of performance indicators 
PySWMM do not inherently have all the functions necessary to populate the database with the wanted 
performance indicators. As showed in the above section the mass-balance from the bio-retention cell 
can directly be extracted and used as input to the database. The following two sections will present 
the calculation for the return period and the effective volume respectively. 
4.3.1 Return period determination 
The return period for overflow in the bio-retention cell is one of the parameters that is not directly 
generated from the built-in functions from PySWMM. The determination of the return period is then 
calculated by time series analysis of the rain and the overflow from the LID. An example can be seen 
on Figure 7.  
 
Figure 7 - Rain depth (blue solid line) and overflow volume from NBS (black dashed line) over time.  
On Figure 7 the overflow volume from the bio-retention cell is illustrated as a result of a rain event. 
The start of an overflow event is defined as the time index where the overflow from the bio-retention 
cell is no longer zero. The end of the overflow is then when the flow from the bio-retention cell is 
zero again. This way of defining overflow events will in practice result in more events than expected. 
Figure 8 is an example on why this is the cases. 
==PAGE:27==
Page 22 
 
 
Figure 8 - Rain depth (blue solid line) and overflow volume from NBS (black dashed line) over time. Three different overflow events 
can be seen. 
If one were to determine the number of overflow events on the above figure the number would be 3 
within the same day when using the definition described above. The definition works throughout the 
whole time period of 36 years and is able to distinguish between overflow events, but realistically, it 
does not make that much sense when it stands on its own. In this example it can be seen that the 
overflow volume turns zero and in the very next timestep a new overflow event is detected. This is 
also the case for the third overflow event in the example. This example would likely not have three 
separate overflow events in reality and would most likely be considered as one single overflow event. 
In order to accommodate for this over estimation of event a condition is added to the existing defini-
tion of determining overflow events: If there is less than 144 timestep, equivalent to 24 hours, between

4.3.2 Effective volume determination 
The other parameter needed to be calculated in order to populate the database is the effective volume. 
The effective volume is defined as the available storage within, in this case, the bio-retention cell at 
the beginning of a rain event. Effectively, this would be equivalent to the accumulated rain adjacent 
to the time where the LID is beginning to overflow. If the same two timeseries from the return period 
estimation is taken into consideration again then the effective volume can be expressed as seen on 
Figure 9.

Figure 9 - Rain depth (blue solid line) and overflow volume from NBS (black dashed line) over time. The effective volume is marked 
over the rain depth (dashed red) and the area under is marked (solid red). 
A rain event is causing the LID to overflow, and these were the cases where the effective volume 
could be determined as this is where the bio-retention cell is performing up to its maximum potential 
at that given time. The light red area surrounded by a red dashed line is the accumulated rain up to 
the point of where the bio-retention cell starts to overflow. This area is defined as the effective volume 
for this specific case.  
The determination of the effective volume is starting from the point over where the bio-retention cell 
starts to overflow and then a backwards summation of the rain is done. The summation continues 
until there is more than an hour between rain events which is illustrated on Figure 10. 
 
Figure 10 - Rain depth (blue solid line) and overflow volume from NBS (black dashed line) over time. The effective volume is marked 
over the rain depth (dashed red) and the area under is marked (solid red). 
On the example above the summation exceeds a tiny dry period as it is less than an hour and thus 
including the following rain after the dry period. This backwards summation of rain from the point 
of overflow until a dry period is longer than an hour is done throughout the entire time series. The 
accumulated rain (effective volume) is event depended and therefore have some variations. In some 
cases, the bio-retention cell has been partially filled by a rain event prior to the event causing overflow

fective volume is determined with a lower and upper bound. The overall effective volume is deter-
mined by taking the median of the distribution. The lower bound is the minimum value whereas the 
upper bound is the maximum value for that simulation. This determination will be discussed in the

7 Conclusion 
In this project the hydrological impact of a bio-retention cell configured with a drain was assessed. 
The bio-retention cell was chosen as its different layers can represent other types of NBS. The impact 
the bio-retention cell has on the catchment consist of some performance indicators. The performance 
indicators include the yearly water balance of the bio-retention cell, the return period for overflow 
and the effective volume. The governing equations within the bio-retention cell were identified and 
influential parameters where found via various sensitivity analysis. The most influential parameters 
were the area ratio, the native soil hydraulic conductivity and the soil layer field capacity. Based on 
the knowledge of the governing equations and the influential parameters a final set of parameters 
were determined.     
The performance indicators were determined by a combination of results directly generated from 
SWMM and time series analysis in Python. Code were developed in Python that could initiate simu-
lations in SWMM via an extension called PySWMM. This initiation showed to be successful and the 
output (water balance for the bio-retention cell) directly from SWMM were no different from what 
was produced via PySWMM. The code also extracted the overflow (time series) from the bio-reten-
tion cell at each timestep, which was used to determine the return period for overflow and the effective 
volume. 
An automated script was developed, based on the code. The script did as followed: 
1. Changed the input files to SWMM.  
2. Initiated a simulation in SWMM. 
3. Extracted time series from SWMM. 
4. Calculated the return period for overflow and effective volume. 
5. Inserted the performance indicators into a database. 
The script iteratively changed the area ratio changing the size of the bio-retention cell. Based on the 
size of the bio-retention cell the imperviousness of the catchment changed accordingly. The native 
soil’s hydraulic conductivity was also changed iteratively. The above 5 steps were repeated after each 
change. 
A final database with the performance indicators was used to give an example on how impervious 
areas could improve the natural water balance. The example showed that Svendborggade contributed 
to overflow Løgstørgade during a rain event that had a rain depth of 13 mm. A bio-retention cell was 
installed in Svendborggade that made up 7% of the catchment and the hydraulic conductivity of the 
soil were 3.6 mm/hr. These conditions resulted in significant increase in infiltration. According to the 
performance indicators in the database Svendborggade will not contribute to overflow Løgstørgade 
when a rain event with 13 mm of rain is happening.

9.2 Code structure 
The main code can be seen in the next section. 
The first part of the script is about importing modules and functions necessary for initiating simula-
tions in SWMM and calculate the performance indicators. Line 7-26. 
1. Some standard Python modules are imported: numpy, pandas, os and statistics. 
2. From PySWMM are the modules: Simulation, LidControls, LidGroups and Subcatchment 
imported. 
3. Three functions are imported fill_zero, group_sequnce and create_connection. They will be 
explained later. 
Next part is the last part before the simulation are initiated. Line 29-47. 
1. Rain data is imported from dat. file and converted to a DataFrame. 
2. The name of the database is defined, and a connection is established. 
3. A list of the hydraulic conductivities that will be iterated through is defined. 
4. A list defines the area of the bio-retention cell. This list will also be iterated through. 
The loop of the two list are set up. Before the script steps in to the simulation the modules are needed 
to be initiated. Further some values have to be changed. Line 53-76. 
1. The four modules imported from PySWMM are initiated. 
2. The native soil’s hydraulic conductivity is changed. 
3. The imperviousness of the catchment are changes accordingly to the size of the bio-retention 
cell. 
4. The list with the overflow from the bio-retention cell is initiated. 
The area of the bio-retention cell is changed. The initiation of the simulation and calculation of the 
performance indicators are ready to begin. Line 79-86. 
1. Changing area of bio-retention cell. 
2. Setting timestep of simulation to 10 minutes. 
3. Stepping into the simulation. 
4. Appending overflow from bio-retention cell. 
After the simulation is done the water balance can be extracted. Line 93-99. 
The timeseries analysis is the next part of the script. First are the return period for overflow deter-
mined. Line 103-122. 
1. The overflow from the bio-retention cell is converted into a DataFrame. 
2. A new Dataframe with zeroes and ones. It is zero when no overflow is happening zero and 
one when overflow is happening. 
3. The function fill_zero is called. It replaces zeroes with ones if there are less than 144 consec-
utive zeros. This is equivalent to 24 hours. 
4. Checks where the data is not zero. 
5. Counts the consecutive occurrences of ones. 
6. Return period is calculated.

Next part is to calculate the effective volume. Line 125-152. 
1. Finds each index for the overflow event. 
2. The start of the overflow event is found be the function group_sequence. It returns the first 
value of the consecutive overflow event from the line before.  
3. A new DataFrame called is based on the rain. Fill_zero is called again and a place holder value 
is inserted if there is less than an hour between to rain events. 
4. A list is initiated. The list is the start of a rain event causing overflow. 
5. Looks backwards in the rain series from the overflow start and finds the first zero. This is then 
defined as the start of the rain event. 
6. A list called rain_sum is the sum of rain between the start of the rain event and the start of the 
overflow event.  
7. The effective volume is then calculated. 
 
The final part of the script is the SQLite database insertion. Line 157-169.
