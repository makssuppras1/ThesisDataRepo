I. IntroducƟon 
 
A. Background and moƟvaƟon 
 
The transiƟon to a more sustainable and decentralized energy system needs reliable and cost-eﬀecƟve 
distribuƟon grid monitoring. In the future years, the demand level and dynamics of electric distribuƟon 
networks will considerably increase due to the growing number of distributed energy sources, such as electric 
vehicles and heat pumps.  
Consequently, monitoring assets in the medium- and low-voltage power systems is essenƟal to prevent 
operaƟonal issues such as excess and poor power quality. However, tradiƟonal Supervisory Control and Data 
AcquisiƟon (SCADA) soluƟons are challenging to implement economically and technically in a large 
distribuƟon network. 
This master's thesis focuses on creaƟng a system for collecƟng and analyzing data from power consumpƟons 
using the Raspberry Pi 4 unit and esƟmaƟng the voltage magnitude intervals. The project is based on the 
previously developed state esƟmaƟon algorithm, which esƟmates the boundary and operaƟng point of the 
enƟre feeder. The objecƟve of this project is to design and implement an algorithm capable of collecƟng and 
processing data on power consumpƟon and evaluaƟng its adaptability based on the designed system.

In this study, the proposed method and algorithm focus on esƟmaƟng the expected load on the system based 
on minimum and maximum consumpƟon data rather than considering the worst-case current esƟmaƟons 
presented in the state esƟmaƟon published. Then, uƟlizing this consumpƟon data as input, the algorithm 
calculates the voltage magnitude intervals within the power system. This approach provides a comprehensive 
understanding of the voltage behavior under typical operaƟng condiƟons, which can help monitor and 
maintain system stability and performance.

II. Literature Review 
A. Overview of state esƟmaƟon methods 
 
TradiƟonally, the local networks that deliver electricity from the transmission system to end-users, including 
residenƟal, commercial, and industrial customers, have been designed to facilitate a one-way passage of 
electricity from centralized power plants to consumers. However, the increasing integraƟon of renewable 
energy sources such as photovoltaic (PV) panels and wind turbines and the growing deployment of distributed 
energy resources (DERs) such as electric vehicles (EVs), heat pumps, and energy storage systems are altering 
the character of distribuƟon grids. 
IntegraƟon of DERs poses several diﬃculƟes for distribuƟon grids: 
First, the intermitent nature of renewable energy sources can result in supply ﬂuctuaƟons, which can cause 
voltage and frequency ﬂuctuaƟons in the grid. 
The increased deployment of DERs can cause distribuƟon equipment and infrastructure to become 
overloaded, resulƟng in disrupƟons and power quality issues. 
The integraƟon of DERs causes issues with grid protecƟon and power system stability. 
DistribuƟon grid operators are employing sophisƟcated technologies and novel operaƟonal strategies to 
resolve these challenges. For instance, sensors and monitoring systems can enable grid operators to monitor 
and control the distribuƟon network more eﬀecƟvely.

C. CriƟcal analysis and gaps 
 
One potenƟal limitaƟon of the proposed method and algorithm is the assumpƟon of known load point 
informaƟon. Although it is assumed to be known through an overview of large consuming units, such as EVs 
and heat pumps, and general residenƟal consumpƟon limited by fuses, it may not always be accurate.   
The paper needs to provide detailed informaƟon on the tesƟng and validaƟng of the proposed algorithm in 
real-world scenarios, which leaves gaps in understanding how the system would perform under diﬀerent 
operaƟon condiƟons and in various distribuƟon networks.  
A potenƟal weakness in the proposed algorithm could also add complexity to the esƟmaƟon process and 
increase the computaƟonal burden. In addiƟon, the opƟmizaƟon problem's assumpƟons, and simpliﬁcaƟons, 
as well as uncertainƟes in the line impedance matrix and current magnitudes, may also impact the accuracy 
of the esƟmated current angle intervals. Therefore, further research and development may be needed to 
improve the accuracy and eﬃciency of the current angle interval esƟmaƟon process.  
In this study, the minimum and maximum consumpƟon data is used as input to esƟmate the expected load 
on the system, which can, in turn, be used to esƟmate the voltage magnitude interval at diﬀerent buses in 
the system.

III. Methodology 
 
A. Research design and approach 
The primary objecƟve of this project is to develop and implement an algorithm that collects and analyzes 
data related to the power system, especially the radial distribuƟon system. The research design employed a 
simulaƟon and implementaƟon approach involving a case study and a Raspberry Pi unit using Python. 
The backward-forward sweep (BFS) method is used in Python to calculate the power ﬂow for the 33-bus case. 
A 24-hour dataset with a 15-minute interval is used for simulaƟon to analyze how the system reacts. Using 
this approach, the study could test scenarios and determine their eﬀects on the system's performance. 
 
Power ﬂow in a radial distribuƟon system  
 
The analysis of distribuƟon systems using Newton -Raphson and fast decoupled power ﬂow methods is 
limited due to several factors such as [1]:  
 
• 
High R/X raƟo of feeders: The high resistance to reactance raƟo in distribuƟon systems results in slow 
convergence or even divergence of the Newton-Raphson method. 
• 
Weakly meshed distribuƟon systems: DistribuƟon systems oŌen have a radial or weakly meshed 
topology, which can cause the Newton-Raphson method to converge to a local soluƟon rather than 
the global one. 
• 
Unbalanced systems: DistribuƟon systems are oŌen characterized by unbalanced loads and 
unbalanced network parameters, which can lead to many variables being solved in the Newton-
Raphson method. 
  
Due to these factors, alternaƟve power ﬂow methods have been proposed in this work, including the 
Backward-Forward sweep(BFS) method, which can provide accurate soluƟons for radial distribuƟon power 
systems. 
The Backward forward sweep power ﬂow algorithm is a method used for solving power ﬂow problems in 
radial distribuƟon systems. It is parƟcularly suitable for distribuƟon systems due to its simplicity. The 
algorithm is based on the principle of ﬂow from the root bus to the terminal buses and the back to root bus.

B. MQTT data communicaƟon with energydata.dk 
 
This secƟon describes MQTT and how it is used with the Raspberry Pi 4 module for test and simulaƟon 
purposes.  
MQTT (Message Queuing Telemetry Transport) is a lightweight messaging protocol that is designed for use 
in IoT (Internet of Things) environments [3].  
MQTT is used for sending and receiving messages between devices over a network. MQTT works on a 
publish-subscribe model, where devices can publish messages to speciﬁc topics, and other devices can 
subscribe to those topics to receive the messages.  
There are serval key concepts to understand in MQTT, including:  
A. Publish and subscribe.  
B. Messages  
C. Topics  
D. Broker  
In A, there are two primary acƟons a device can take. The ﬁrst is to publish a message on a speciﬁc topic, 
while the second is to subscribe to a topic to receive messages sent to that topic. 
In B, devices exchange informaƟon in the form of messages, which can be a command or data such as 
sensor readings. 
In C, topics help to specify where messages should be sent or received. They form strings with forward 
slashes (/) separaƟng the levels. 
In this project, a topic is created for consumpƟon data : 
topic = 'OsamaMQTT/data/consumpƟon1' 
In this project, the MQTT is used to send and receive data related to energy consumpƟon in a 33-bust test 
feeder. The Raspberry Pi 4-unit acts as a subscriber to receive the data published by the sensors and as a 
publisher to send the collected data to the Energydata.dk database. The data being exchanged includes 
informaƟon on power consumpƟon and the minimum and maximum power levels. 
The broker in D is a key concept in the MQTT system. It serves as a hub for receiving all messages sent by 
devices and ﬁltering them based on topics. It then determines which clients are interested in a speciﬁc 
message and publishes it to them. In this project, the broker is the MQTT broker provided by Energydata.dk 
==PAGE:23==
21 
 
In addiƟon to the above, QoS (Quality of Service) is a component used in MQTT. QoS is responsible for 
ensuring the message delivery process's reliability. MQTT oﬀers three diﬀerent levels of QoS for message 
delivery: QoS 0, QoS 1, and QoS 2[7]. It's important to note that you can set diﬀerent QoS levels for 
publishing and subscribing to messages. This ﬂexibility allows you to customize your messaging system 
according to your speciﬁc needs and requirements. 
• 
At QoS 0, messages are sent without any acknowledgement from the broker, making it the fastest 
but least reliable QoS level.  
• 
At QoS 1, messages are sent with acknowledgement from the broker, ensuring that messages are 
delivered at least once, but this comes at the cost of higher network traﬃc.  
• 
Finally, at QoS 2, messages are sent with a handshake between the sender and receiver, resulƟng in 
the most reliable but also most network-intensive message delivery(4 message).
