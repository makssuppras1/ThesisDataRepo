Abstract
1DTU Health Technology, 2Center for Basic Metabolic Research
Master in Bioinformatics and Systems Biology
Polygenic Risk Scores in Phenotype Modelling
by Nanna Møller Barnkob1
Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) and alcoholic liver disease (ALD) are
two highly prevalent liver diseases believed to be caused by the interplay between
environment and genetic background of an individual. While several single-nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNPs) associated with the development of disease have been identi-
ﬁed, the use of genetic information to discriminate severity or predict disease progres-
sion is yet to be demonstrated.
In this thesis, the genetic data from a combined cohort of N = 1177 individuals at-
risk for NAFLD or ALD were utilized in the form of polygenic risk scores (PRS). These
PRSs were successfully calculated for a range of phenotypes directly or indirectly
associated with liver disease such as liver enzyme levels, anthropometric features,
serum lipid levels, as well as Kleiner ﬁbrosis score and NAFLD activity score. To
understand the role of PRSs in discriminating stages of steatosis and ﬁbrosis, the
PRSs were combined with clinical data as input features for the two machine learning
algorithms, random forest and logistic regression.
Comparing models with and without PRSs showed that a basic age and gender
model using both types of models for steatosis could improve the discrimination of
severe steatosis from an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (ROC
AUC) of 0.55 (± 0.05) to 0.61 (± 0.01), whereas a random forest model predicting
presence of ﬁbrosis improved only marginally from 0.577 (± 0.035) to 0.583 (± 0.031).
This suggests that while PRSs may not provide additional information when discrim-
inating ﬁbrosis, the quantiﬁcation of genetic total liability towards a high BMI may
improve severe steatosis discrimination signiﬁcantly in the absence of more informa-
tion. In a clinical setting, this result could possibly help health-care practitioners
initiate lifestyle interventions for individuals with particularly high genetic risk in
order to prevent development of NAFLD and ALD.

Introduction
Motivation
Environment and genetics together inﬂuence the phenotype of an individual, but to
what degree the genetics determine what we observe is often diﬃcult to quantify.
The two liver diseases alcoholic liver disease (ALD) and non-alcoholic liver disease
(NAFLD) are both examples of phenotypes hypothesized to be caused by the interplay
between environment and genetic liability. These two diseases, with almost indistin-
guishable pathology, account for the vast majority of chronic liver disease worldwide
and are consuming an increasing proportion of healthcare resources (Boyle, Masson,
and Anstee, 2018). Being able to diagnose early and quantify risk of further compli-
cations may be crucial to preventing development towards (non)-alcoholic steatohep-
atitis (ASH/NASH), ﬁbrosis, cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).
In this thesis, the genetic contribution to development of liver disease is quan-
tiﬁed through the use of the polygenic risk score (PRS). PRS calculation is a novel
data-driven method based on the numerous genetic associations quantiﬁed by genome-
wide association studies (GWAS), especially aimed at predicting traits that are highly
polygenic and thus in contrast to traits caused by one or a few signiﬁcant variations
such as Mendelian diseases. By genotyping an individual, it is now possible to cre-
ate an overall genetic score based on the common variation in the genome and place
individuals into risk groups based on this. This is in perfect accordance with the
growing focus on precision medicine in Danish healthcare. Knowing the genetic risk
classiﬁcation of an individual may help health care professionals direct recommenda-
tions on lifestyle interventions more eﬀectively or make better decisions on choice of
medication. The performance of PRSs remain to be fully elucidated, and is currently
dependent on large sample size and quality GWAS summary statistics. Accordingly,
the methodology behind PRS calculations is a ﬁeld in rapid development.
Machine learning and its applications have in the recent years received grow-
ing attention ranging from the research community to the general public. Machine
learning comprises algorithms that allow a computer to learn from data and is most
commonly seen as a subset of artiﬁcial intelligence, although these two terms are fre-
quently used interchangeably. Machine learning models have played a central role in
what can be known as computer-aided diagnostics, where health care professionals
may be assisted in decision making by automated processing of data.
In this thesis, the genetic background of an individual based on their PRS is
combined with clinical measurements in a machine learning model aimed at predicting
the presence of ﬁbrosis and steatosis in the liver, a task known as classical binary
classiﬁcation. While the focus of machine learning is often on its ability to discriminate
between outcomes, understanding the patterns learnt by the model may also reveal
risk factors and phenotypes relevant to ﬁbrosis prediction. Machine learning models,
such as the ones developed in this thesis, may one day become a tool which can aid
==PAGE:14==
xiv
in early liver disease diagnostics, hereby ultimately improve the quality of life for
numerous people by preventing cirrhosis and HCC.
Aims
The overall goal of this project is to assess the value of PRSs in predicting liver disease
outcomes.
Speciﬁcally, the aims can be partitioned as follows:
• To calculate PRSs for a range of relevant phenotypes for a mixed cohort of
individuals susceptible to liver disease by implementing a bioinformatic PRS
pipeline
• To build machine learning-based prediction models for liver disease utilizing
the calculated PRSs, clinical measurements, lifestyle and demography of an
individual
• To assess how PRSs may improve liver disease prediction models by comparing
feature importances, and discuss to what extent they may be incorporated in
clinical patient assessment

Chapter 1
Background
Central to the aims of this thesis is fatty liver disease and its genetic etiology. In this
chapter, the central pathology in liver disease is introduced, as well as concepts in
large-scale population genetics including GWAS and PRS.
1.1
Alcoholic Liver Disease and Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver
Disease
The liver diseases NAFLD and ALD are two conditions with large overlap in pathology,
which may share the designation fatty liver disease (FLD). Both diseases aﬀect the
liver, which is responsible for a large number of vital functions in the body including
metabolism, ﬁltering of the blood and synthesis of important proteins.
NAFLD is a common liver disease found in 17-33% of the Western adult popula-
tion (Loomba and Sanyal, 2013). The condition is deﬁned by an abnormal retention
of fat in the liver known as steatosis, and is diagnosed by a fatty acid content in
the hepatocytes greater than 5%, in individuals who consume little to no alcohol.
Some regard NAFLD as the hepatic manifestation of metabolic syndrome (MetS),
deﬁned by obesity, high blood pressure, high blood sugar (commonly caused by early
increasing insulin resistance), high serum triglycerides, and low serum high-density
lipoprotein (HDL). Thus it follows that the increasing prevalence of obesity and MetS
is also leading to an increasing prevalence of NAFLD. Steatosis is additionally an
independent risk factor for developing both type 2 diabetes (T2D) and cardiovascular
disease (CVD) (Targher et al., 2016).
ALD shares much of the same pathology, although this diagnosis comprises both
alcoholic pure steatosis and alcoholic steatohepatitis.
Simple steatosis in ALD is
mostly caused by disturbed liver metabolism, and may be benign and normalize with
abstinence (Thiele et al., 2018).
The common representation of liver disease progression is seen in Figure 1.1.
NAFLD may be symptom free, and is generally not severe, but approximately 10-
30% of cases progress to the more serious NASH (Fernando et al., 2019).
NASH
diﬀers from simple steatosis by the presence of hepatocyte death, inﬂammation and
various degrees of ﬁbrosis (Sircana et al., 2019). Because of remodelling of extracellular
matrix, ﬁbrosis makes the liver appear stiﬀ, thus ﬁbrosis is also referred to as liver
stiﬀness. The progression from simple steatosis to NASH or ﬁbrosis is often explained
by a "two-hit hypothesis" (C P Day and James, 1998), where the ﬁrst hit comprises
excess fat accumulation, and the second hit is driven by genetic or epigenetic factors,
microbiome, diet, adipokines or advanced glycation end products (AGEs) (Fernando
et al., 2019). Fibrosis characterizes the gradual build-up of scar tissue in the liver,
whereas cirrhosis describes the replacement of normal functioning hepatic tissue with
scar tissue and may lead to liver failure, requiring liver transplantation.

1.1.1
Development of liver ﬁbrosis
Fibrosis in the liver is characterized by extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling initi-
ated by inﬂammation and is associated with overexpression of ECM components in all
ﬁbrosis stages. The most abundant ECM proteins include the ﬁbrillar type I and III
collagens in early ﬁbrosis, and type IV collagen and the non-collagen elastin, which
is especially abundant in cirrhosis (Baiocchini et al., 2016)). The ECM changes were
previously regarded as a passive consequence of liver injury, but is now considered a
biologically active system, and ﬁbrosis and cirrhosis are in some literature regarded as
reversible (or bidirectional) if hepatic inﬂammation is successfully removed (Pellicoro,
Ramachandran, and Iredale, 2012, Karsdal et al., 2015).
The development of ﬁbrosis is driven by hepatic myoﬁbroblasts which are only
found in an injured liver (Iwaisako, Brenner, and Kisseleva, 2012). The progenitors
of myoﬁbroblasts are primarily hepatic stellate cells (HSCs) and portal ﬁbroblasts.
HSCs are resident in the liver, and exhibit a quiescent phenotype in healthy livers,
but in response to activation signals transdiﬀerentiate to cells that are able to produce
excessive amount of ECM proteins (Fernando et al., 2019). HSCs are activated by

signals from apoptopic bodies from injured hepatocytes, acetaldehyde, lipid peroxida-
tion products and reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Gandhi, 2017). These signals can be
initiated by immune cells residing in the liver such as the macrophages Kupﬀer cells.
Kupﬀer cells both phagocytose apoptotic bodies and secrete cytokines, for instance
in response to gut-derived lipopolysaccharide (LPS) recognized through their Toll-like
receptor 4 (TLR4) and CD14, exemplifying the role of the gut-liver-axis. Bile acids
from the gut may also promote inﬂammation (Heymann and Tacke, 2016). Together,
this can lead to a release of ROS, eventually causing increased HSC activation and
ﬁbrogenesis.
Finally, GWASs has helped uncover variations in MERTK associated with ﬁbro-
sis. This gene encodes the TAM family protein MerTK which is expressed by Kupﬀer
cells. MerTK can promote ﬁbrosis by secretion of TGF-β1, which activates HSCs.
Whenever HSCs are activated, they secrete retinol, which is cleaved to retinoic acid.
Because retinoic acid induces cleavage of MerTK, leading to its inactivation, it follows
that when the majority of HSCs are activated, MerTK can become constitutively ac-
tive (Cai et al., 2020). In conclusion, if these inﬂammatory signals are sustained, the
liver is left in a state of chronic hepatic inﬂammation which leads to formation of scar
tissue, loss of tissue architecture and ultimately organ failure (Pellicoro, Ramachan-
dran, and Iredale, 2012).
1.1.2
Genetics of liver disease
Through genetic studies, several variants, which individually have modest eﬀects on
the phenotype have been identiﬁed. A few variants of special interest are introduced
here.
PNPLA3 I148M
One variant residing in PNPLA3 (patatin-like phospholipase domain–containing 3)
has been robustly identiﬁed across several GWAS of the whole spectrum of liver dam-
age related to NAFLD, from simple steatosis to NASH, ﬁbrosis, and HCC (Trépo and
Valenti, 2020). The variant has also been identiﬁed in both adults and children, in
several ethnicities, and is associated both with disease susceptibility and progression
for ALD and NAFLD (Toshikuni, Tsutsumi, and Arisawa, 2014). The variant is iden-

148 (I148M) (Namjou et al., 2019). While its function is not completely understood
in detail, it is hypothesized to be loss-of-function (Pirazzi et al., 2014).
PNPLA3 is highly expressed in HSCs. It is repressed when retinol is abundant,
resulting in storage of retinyl palmitate in lipid droplets, and is induced under retinol
depletion, promoting the release of retinol by hydrolysis of retinyl palmitate to retinol
and palmitic acid (Pirazzi et al., 2014). As a response to insulin, PNPLA3 is upreg-
ulated, and promotes intracellular lipid droplet remodeling. The same responses are
not observed for the variant PNPLA3 I148M (Pirazzi et al., 2014).

1.1.3
Liver status assessment
The liver is examined using a long variety of measurements with diﬀerent degrees of
invasiveness. Only the ones used in this thesis are introduced here, and other tests
include Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) and the Enhanced Liver Fibrosis
test (ELF) (Xie et al., 2014).
Blood markers for liver disease
Enzymes in plasma are widely used as indicators of liver disease. Speciﬁcally, the
enzymes of interest are alanine transaminase (ALT), alkaline phosphatase (ALK), γ-
glutamyl transferase (GGT), and aspartate transaminase (AST). ALT is a marker of
damage to liver cells and may also be increased in obese individuals. ALP is a marker
of biliary obstruction, and GGT levels are highly sensitive to alcohol intake (Mueller,
2013). A GWAS investigating ALT, GGT and AST variants by Chambers et al., 2011
located genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs in genes encoding biliary transporters, genes
involved in carbohydrate and lipid metabolism, accumulation of hepatic triglycerides,
including PNLPLA3 described in the previous section, inﬂammation and immunity.
AST is also found in the liver and often used in ratio with ALT (denoted the AST/ALT
ratio).

Transient Elastography
While biopsy is considered gold-standard, this process is highly invasive. It is a source
of both large discomfort for patients and risk of complications. In some cases, a biopsy
might not be feasible. Transient elastography (TE) is an alternative for detecting liver
ﬁbrosis and cirrhosis based on ultrasound and low-frequency elastic waves which are
propagated through the liver. Brieﬂy explained, the velocity of the elastic waves is a
measure of the liver tissue stiﬀness and is higher in a ﬁbrotic liver (Jung and Kim,
2012). TE is measured in kPa, and diﬀerent cut-oﬀs are set to discriminate between

Gholami-Fesharaki, 2016). TE is most commonly performed using FibroScan R⃝. The
non-invasive nature of TE makes it useful for monitoring disease progression (Thiele
et al., 2018).

1.2.1
Basic concepts
Allele frequency is the relative frequency of which an allele is observed in a population,
and is calculated from counting the number of occurrences of the speciﬁc allele divided
by the number of copies. The most frequent allele in a population is known as the
major allele and the less frequent as the minor. The minor allele is often considered
the eﬀect allele, denoted A1, which is assumed to be contributing towards the studied
phenotype. The number of eﬀect alleles is the reference point for counting the allelic
dosage, which may be 0, 1 or 2. Since a lack of consensus has created confusion on
denotation, the nomenclature followed in this thesis is shown in Table 1.3.

The observed frequency of the minor allele is denoted MAF. In more traditional
statistical genetics, we may denote the dominant allele frequency as p and recessive

The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium
The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE) is a mathematical property describing the
expected frequency of alleles in a population formulated by Hardy and Weinberg in
the beginning of the 20th century.
Overall, the Hardy-Weinberg principle builds on the following assumptions:
==PAGE:24==
8

• There is no selection in a population from generation to generation making
some phenotypes more likely to reproduce (also known as random mating)
• There are no mutations between generations giving selective advantages
• The population, n, is large
Additional assumptions will not be discussed in detail here.
In conclusion, the allele frequency is assumed to be stable. From this, the Hardy-
Weinberg equation can be derived which describes the relationship between the prob-
ability of ﬁnding a homozygote dominant (p2), heterozygote (2pq), or homozygote
recessive genotype (q2), in a population:
(p + q)2 = p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1
(1.1)
Large deviation from this simple principle is a commonly used test for population
stratiﬁcation, selection and inbreeding and in quality control of raw genetic data, as
departure can be a sign of technical error (Turner et al., 2011).
Hardy–Weinberg can also be used to deﬁne the expected number of heterozygotes
in a population based on allele frequency. High heterozygosity is associated with high
genetic variability, whereas low heterozygosity can be a sign of population bottlenecks
or inbreeding. Inbreeding will cause an increase in homozygosity for all genes.
1.2.2
Linkage Disequilibrium
Linkage disequilibirum (LD) is deﬁned as the non-random association of alleles at
diﬀerent loci (Slatkin, 2008). Statistically, we see LD as the allelic frequency of one
allele is linked to the frequency of another, which violates completely random recom-
bination. The observation of alleles A and B together may be known as a haplotype
and the frequency, pa,b as the haplotype frequency.
LD is an important basis for imputation and assumes that LD structure is similar
in subpopulations, where it has been shown that LD is organized in distinct haplotype
blocks (Zhang et al., 2002). LD has revealed several evolutionary ﬁndings, but must
be taken into account when calculating PRSs, because the eﬀect of markers that are in
LD with a true disease mutation might be overestimated as explained in the following
section.
A simple measure of the LD between two variants is the coeﬃcient of linkage dis-
equilibrium Dab = pab −papb where pa and pb refer to the respective allele frequencies
alone. This measures the diﬀerence between the frequency of the two alleles observed
together and the frequency under assumption that they are independent. By dividing
Dab with its theoretical maximum, Dmax (given by min(pA(1 −pB), pB(1 −pA))), we
can obtain D′ (Slatkin, 2008).

denote both the broad or the narrow-sense variability, which is the total or restricted to
additive eﬀect of the alleles, respectively. The total heritability is commonly denoted
H2, but is more seldom used in practice.

1.3
Genome-Wide Association Studies
GWAS is a widely applied type of genetic study aimed towards identifying associa-
tions between genomic markers and observed phenotypic traits. As of August 2020,
the GWAS catalogue by EMBL-EBI, which is a collection of the ﬁndings of individ-
ual studies, contains 4,554 publications and 185,864 signiﬁcant genetic associations
(Burdett et al., n.d.).
The main results of a GWAS in PRS context are the summary statistics. Sum-
mary statistics are a set of genetic variations identiﬁed by chromosomal coordinates
or ID (such as RS ID from dbSNP (Sherry et al., 2001)) and their corresponding
eﬀect size. The eﬀect size is given in (log)-odds ratio in case/control studies, or as
==PAGE:26==
10
Chapter 1. Background
a signed β coeﬃcient from a linear regression. Summary statistics must also include
a signiﬁcance level (p-value) and standard deviation, the sample size included in the
estimation, and will often also include an imputation score and the minor allele fre-
quency in the sampled population. Summary statistics are also referred to as the base
data in the context of PRS calculations.
As GWAS is a research ﬁeld of its own, this section will only focus on providing
the necessary background motivating the methodology in PRS calculations.
1.3.1
Technological basis
The rapid development in the ﬁeld of GWAS and population genetics has been enabled
by the advent of low-cost sequence technology most notably through high-throughput
SNP genotyping arrays. Genotyping arrays contain a range of pre-determined probes
between 500,000 to 5 million SNPs or short indels.
The most widely used arrays
are produced by Illumina R⃝or Aﬀymetrix R⃝. Especially knowledge of LD haplotype
block structure has been useful in selecting the number of markers, or "tag SNPs",
necessary for an association study (Zhang et al., 2002). The use of pre-selected SNPs
means that genotyping arrays are limited in detection of novel and rare variants, which
may instead be investigated by sequencing DNA directly using WGS or whole-exome
sequencing (WES).
Post-processing sequencing data is computationally expensive, because it requires
more complex bioinformatic steps, including aligning reads to a reference genome and
variant calling, but as both software and hardware is evolving, WES and WGS provide
an alternative to arrays, which may inﬂuence the future of GWAS.
1.3.2
Association tests in GWAS
The most common model used in GWAS is the additive genetic model, which assumes
that the heterozygote phenotype is intermediate between the homozygote for the
minor and major allele. The genetic model is rarely known prior, but can sometimes
be estimated based on biological knowledge. This means that the additive model will
in general not take into account heterozygote advantage or disadvantage, but rather
model the eﬀect of the number of alleles as a monotonic, uniform trend.

Before tests are performed, usually some quality control is applied. This involves
ﬁltering on MAF, because these SNPs are more susceptible to genotyping errors and

the power to detect these is extremely low (Turner et al., 2011). Filtering on impu-

Continuous traits are usually tested using a generalized linear model (GLM) such
as Analysis of Variance (ANOVA). These test assume that the trait is normally dis-
tributed, which may be achieved through a transformation, that the trait variance
within each group is the same, and that the groups are independent. Dichotomous
traits are primarily tested via logistic regression or a contingency table.
The lin-
ear models are usually preferred, because they are more ﬂexible and thus allow for
incorporating covariates (or possibly confounders) into the model.
The p-value of the marker is calculated from a test statistic, usually following a
χ2-distribution, but it is subject to multiple testing. A Bonferroni-corrected threshold
(calculated by p/m where m is the number of tests) is commonly set to 5 · 10−8 for
genome-wide signiﬁcance. The genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs are usually visualized
using a Manhattan plot as seen in Figure 1.3, where the peaks resemble very small
p-values.
Test statistics may often be inﬂated because markers are in LD with disease-
causing markers. This phenomenon is shown in Figure 1.4 and needs to be taken
into account when calculating PRS. This ﬁgure shows how several markers close to
the true disease loci, PNPLA3 I148M, are also signiﬁcantly associated with NAFLD

of a locus with several high-LD SNPs will be over-estimated.
Some test statistic inﬂation is to be expected under polygenic inheritance (Yang,
Weedon, et al., 2011), but often principal component analysis (PCA) is used to ac-
count for confounding by population stratiﬁcation and cryptic relatedness. Population
stratiﬁcation occurs when unobserved subpopulations diﬀer systematically in both ge-
netics and phenotype, and may cause spurious associations (Turner et al., 2011). Any
number from three to twenty principal components are often selected and added to
the linear model, and this is commonly also adopted in PRS calculations.
1.4
Polygenic Risk Scores
Each individual may carry a number of alleles that either decrease or increase trait or
disease risk (Visscher et al., 2017). Combining these genetic contributions scattered
across the genome in one score is known as the PRS, which may also be seen as a
weighted sum of the number of risk- or protective alleles. The PRS oﬀers a data-
driven alternative to the classical statistical approach of using only contributions of
genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs. This score can be denoted the canonical genetic risk
score (cGRS)), and explain only a limited part of the total variance of a phenotype,
which is captured in h2
GWS (Visscher et al., 2017). PRSs are optimized for predictive
performance by parameter selection in training data set, lending a number of principles
from machine learning.

cancer. In the study by Khera et al., 2018, the PRS based model adjusted for age,

1.4.2
Correction for linkage disequilibrium
As explained in Section 1.2.2 and seen in Figure 1.4, many SNPs are often observed
together. This is both the basis of imputation, but also implies that markers in LD
with true disease-causing mutations essentially carry much of the same information in
terms of eﬀect size. The goal in LD correction is to avoid overestimating the eﬀect of
correlated SNPs in a particular locus. This can be done by shrinking the β-estimates
according to LD pattern, or by simply removing SNPs in high LD with others. This
is the basis of the two methods worked with in this thesis, Bayesian β-shrinkage and
clumping followed by thresholding (C+T).
Because both of these methods are dependent on parameter optimization, a train-
ing and a validation data set are required for PRS calculation to avoid overﬁtting.
During training, the parameters are ﬁtted to the genetic data to optimize prediction
power of a phenotype. The performance can then be estimated without bias using an
independent validation data set.
Bayesian beta-shrinkage
LDpred is based on approximating the posterior mean eﬀect from GWAS summary
statistic, conditioned on LD information and a genetic architecture prior, denoted
E[β|˜β, D], where ˜β is the least-squares estimate of the eﬀect size given with the input
summary statistics. The posterior is estimated based on Markov Chain Monte Carlo
(MCMC), making it computationally expensive because of the large number of iter-
ations to ensure convergence. The LD estimation is based on a reference panel, or
on part of the genotype data. For the mathematical details, see Vilhjálmsson et al.,
2015.
The hyper parameter selected during training is the fraction of causal variants
which can be a proxy for polygenicity of the trait. An LD radius also needs to be
speciﬁed which is often heuristically set to the number of SNPs divided by 3,000.
β-shrinkage yields a new set of corrected summary statistics with the same number
of variants a the input list. C+T will yield a thinned and truncated list of variants to
include in the PRS calculation.
Pruning, clumping and thresholding
Pruning and clumping are two highly similar methods for LD correction, and the terms
are often used interchangeably. Both methods are aimed at removing SNPs that are
==PAGE:30==
14
Chapter 1. Background
correlated above a given threshold and within a given range of one representative SNP
(known as the index SNP).
The two methods diﬀer primarily by the ordering of SNPs. In pruning, SNPs
are ordered by genomic coordinates whereas SNPs in clumping are sorted by some
statistic, for instance p-value (in increasing order) or MAF in the target population
(in decreasing order). Because of the use of p- or MAF-values, clumping can also
be known as informed pruning. For pruning, the correlation between a SNP and the
following ones within a range or a number of SNPs is calculated. If the correlation is
large, the SNP with higher MAF is kept and the other removed. Using the remaining
SNP, the same process is repeated. This means that while "walking" through the
genome, all SNPs except one may ultimately be removed from the set if SNPs are
continuously correlated and within range.
Ordering by p-value in clumping is an
attempt to keep SNPs with the strongest signals, and follows a similar procedure as
pruning, but ensures that the most signiﬁcant SNP in the chromosomal window is
never removed.
The correlation between SNPs in LD is mostly based on r2 (Equation 1.2) and
chromosomal window in base pairs. This yields two hyper parameters that may be
optimized for PRS calculation using training data but often standard parameters are
used (r2 = 0.1 and 250kb clumping window)
The clumping-based PRS training requires a ﬁnal step, thresholding, which ﬁne-
tunes the ratio between signal and noise. In this step, a range of signiﬁcance thresholds
for the p-value are applied to determine the required statistical signiﬁcance that allow
a SNP to be included.
The threshold value which yields the best performance is
selected. Thresholding is performed after clumping and will cause more SNPs to be
removed, unless the optimal threshold is 1. The selected threshold is analogous to the
fraction of causal variants in β-shrinkage.
Other methods
In combination, the C+T method has three notable hyper parameters: correlation
threshold, r2, chromosomal window (cw) and the threshold, t. Additionally, the im-
putation score, info, for a SNP may be included as a ﬁltering parameter. In a recent
publication by Privé et al., 2019, PRSs calculated based on a grid of these parameters
were combined (stacked) in a linear model using penalized logistic regression. Prac-
tically, instead of having one vector of PRS scores, the PRS is represented as matrix
of dimensions N × K where K = kr2 × kcw × kt × kinfo and each k is the number
of possible parameter values for r2, cw, t and info. The authors showed that the
performance of this method was consistently higher than β shrinkage and C+T on
independent validation data, but it is also a highly computationally intensive proce-
dure. A ﬁnal method used frequently in literature uses penalized regression but for
correcting summary statistics (Mak et al., 2017).

2.2.1
Classiﬁcation models
The two model types, random forest and logistic regression, were selected based on
their interpretability and complexity.
The models are based on diﬀerent underly-
ing algorithms and assumptions, and thus provide an opportunity for comparisons
between the two, and ﬁnding a superior model for this type of data. Both model
types are robust in settings with a large dimensionality compared to number of data
points through diﬀerent strategies. Random forest has the ability to learn cryptic and
non-linear relationships between features, whereas logistic regression is linear in the
parameters.
For the ﬁnal prediction models, logistic regression was used with penalization (also
known as regularization), where the size of weights, βp, for each parameter is shrunk
to reduce over-ﬁtting and ultimately the variance of the model. The β-values were
restricted using the ℓ2 (quadratic) norm. The strength of regularization is controlled
using the parameter λ.
Logistic regression was also used without penalization as the β-values is an esti-
mator of the log odds ratio for a unit increase in x. This approach was applied when
estimating the eﬀect of PNPLA3 I148M variant dosage and ﬁnd the odds ratio of
diﬀerent liver disease outcomes by exponentiating the log odds ratio.
Model training and validation
Both model types were evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation which may be reviewed
in Section A.2.4. Balanced class weight was used because of a high degree of class
imbalance. The random forest models were trained using a ﬁxed number of decision
trees (estimators) of 500 which was selected as a trade-oﬀbetween computational
load and ﬁtting suﬃcient estimators. For the penalized logistic regression model, an
additional inner layer of cross-validation was introduced to select the regularization
strength, λ. A range of 20 λ-values tested were tested from 10−5 to 105, where a large
value is strong regularization.

This means that all multivariate imputation models were ﬁtted on the training data
only, and the models were then applied in the testing data set to impute the missing
values seen here.
2.2.3
Inclusion of genetic data
The models were trained with and without using genetic information in three formats:
1. PRS as a score given in Equation 2.1,
2. PRS-based risk groups: low, medium and high, deﬁned by their deviation from
a standardized normal distribution
3. Single variants as introduced in Section 1.1.

The groups low and high are below or above one standard deviation from the
mean of zero, meaning that the vast majority of people fail in the medium group.
This was done to understand if the eﬀect of being at the tails of the distribution was
more predictive than the actual PRS value.
The single variants were extracted after QC of the genetic data, and were included
in the model represented by their dosage. In this case, we do not use β values from any
summary statistics, but instead allow the machine learning models to ﬁt the weights
on training data. I included single variants primarily to compare what additional
knowledge we may gain from aggregating information across the genome in the PRS.
Additionally, the eﬀect of PNPLA3 I148M was investigated alone (by its dosage)
to replicate the ﬁndings in GWAS, and to understand whether the eﬀect of the allele
can be observed both using biopsy-veriﬁed outcomes and using the proxy-derived
ﬁbrosis and steatosis incidences to validate the usage. This was done using logistic
regression.

Target cohorts
All subjects included in the models were from two diﬀerent studies, GALAXY and
SIPHON. Combined, N = 1177 data points with both phenotype data and genetic
data were available. The data collection process for GALAXY has ended whereas
SIPHON is still on-going. Data were collected at Odense University Hospital, De-

2.3.1
GALAXY
GALAXY will be referred to as GALA for simplicity. GALA is a cohort study focused
on ALD containing in total 463 ALD cases, to which a healthy control (denoted
HP) group of 154 participants was recruited based on age and gender matching the
case distributions.
Cases are deﬁned as at-risk for ALD by alcohol use above 21
units/week for men and 14 units/week for women. One of the primary goals of this
study was to assess how TE can be used to screen for ALD-related ﬁbrosis in high
risk populations. Primarily participants with high TE (> 6 kPa cut-oﬀused) were
recruited for follow-up analysis including liver biopsy, testing with non-invasive ﬁbrosis
markers, and follow-up one year after inclusion. Some participants with < 6 kPA
were also biopsied, and biopsy data is available for in total 328 individuals in the
data set. There is no biopsy data for the healthy population for ethical reasons. The
alcohol-overusing participants with biopsy-proven healthy livers (F0 ﬁbrosis score)
work as active controls (healthy at-risk participants with false-positive liver stiﬀness
measurement by TE), and the participants with low TE and biopsy-veriﬁed healthy
livers may function as conﬁrmation of true negatives.
2.3.2
SIPHON
The SIPHON study is titled "Non-invasive screening for liver ﬁbrosis in high-risk pop-
ulations", also focused on collecting data using TE as a tool for assessing liver stiﬀness.
SIPHON include both participants with risk of NAFLD, ALD and background popula-
tion. High risk for NAFLD is deﬁned as T2D and/or MetS as described in Section 1.1.
The background population is selected by random invitations to residents in Funen,
aged 40-75. When data collection has ended, SIPHON will comprise approximately

3.1.2
Deriving optimal thresholds for proxy measures
The association of the genetic data with the severity of steatosis and ﬁbrosis was as-
sessed using the non-invasive proxies, CAP and TE, respectively. In order to optimize
their predictive power for the true outcomes, ROC AUC was used to derive optimal
cut-oﬀthresholds for discriminating stages using the GALA-ALD cohort, for which
we have gold-standard biopsy data. The thresholds, AUC and number of cases out
of the 328 available biopsy data points are summarized in Table 3.2. The conﬁdence
intervals were derived by bootstrapping 1000 data sets with sample size of 1000 from
GALA-ALD.
Binary outcome groups were created based on increasing the decision boundaries
for steatosis and ﬁbrosis severity. To clarify, steatosis > 0 refers to discriminating
between no steatosis (S0) and steatosis of any severity (S1-S3), steatosis > 1 refers
to identifying moderate to severe steatosis (S2 and S3). Similarly, fibrosis > 0 refers
to translates to predicting any ﬁbrosis (F1-4).
Ultimately fibrosis > 3 refers to
identifying cirrhosis (F4).
While TE has a consistently high AUC for ﬁbrosis prediction, especially as sever-
ity increases, CAP is not nearly as predictive and performs better when discriminating
between S0 and S1-S3, although these diﬀerences are not signiﬁcant. The C.I. for dis-
criminating between no and any ﬁbrosis is quite narrow, indicating a low variance and
consistency in the predictor, but the interval is relatively large for CAP in all three
cases. This especially applies to the case of steatosis > 1, where the C.I. overlaps the
boundaries for both steatosis > 0 and steatosis > 2.
Because of the limited sample size for GALA-ALD, the proxy-based outcomes
are used as an approximation to the true state of the liver in the remaining analyses.
Based on these results, using TE as a proxy for ﬁbrosis is reliable, but this cannot be
said with the same conﬁdence for CAP.

In summary, all except two PRSs predict their target trait with signiﬁcant corre-
lation, although the signal is weak for most of the phenotypes. These results together
indicate that the PRSs may have some but limited explanatory power in the machine
learning models, but that the signal might indeed be cryptic or non-linear.

Imputation of lifestyle features
Largely because of the data collection process, the data suﬀers from a number of errors
and unrealistic values. Additionally, some questions are only presented for a subset of
the test subjects, for instance concerning alcohol habits. In order to still include these
features in the combined model, some measures are taken to get reliable estimates of
these values.
• Alcohol consumption: Several outlying and unrealistic values are found in
the amount of alcohol consumed daily or weekly. For 12 of the observations,
the daily consumption is found to be exactly 7 times the weekly. For these,
we assume that the two columns were shifted, and we shift them back. For 24
remaining observations, the daily consumption remains higher than the weekly
and these are removed.
– The upper limit for alcohol consumption per day is heuristically set to 50
units/day because alcoholics may possibly tolerate a very large intake due
to several elevated liver enzymes. All observations above are considered
errors.
– In the ALD data set values are removed based on the cut-oﬀ: daily:1
weekly: 1 and max_alc: 57. In the SIPHON dat set, none are removed.
• Duration of alcohol overuse: alc_years is transformed to groups deﬁned
by the intervals: < 1 year, 1 ≤years< 5, 5 ≤years < 10 years and > 10 years to
reduce the impact of extreme outliers. If SIPHON subjects do not have overuse
(which also indicates no overuse the last 5 years), alc_years is set to 0 (group
< 1 year).
• Smoking: Smoking status is encoded as never, previous or current by renaming
according to Tables C.3 and C.4 in Appendix C. If subjects never smoked,
smokeyears and smokevolume are set to 0.

Chapter 4
Discussion
The overall aims of this thesis were to generate PRS for a range of phenotypes relevant
to liver disease which was largely achieved. Utilizing them in machine learning models
combined with clinical data showed that the PRSs only supplied a limited amount of
additional information in speciﬁc scenarios and only for discriminating steatosis. The
features that were the main drivers of prediction were identiﬁed, which was largely
dominated by clinical blood markers. The main and intermediate results are discussed
in the following section.
4.1
PRS calculations
PRSs for an array of phenotypes were successfully calculated, and all except for ALK
and WHRadjBMI were signiﬁcantly correlated with the phenotype they aimed to
measure. While the correlation was weak for most phenotypes except for BMI, trends
between PRS and phenotype could nevertheless be shown using genetic decile groups
and scatter plots. The highest R2
PRS obtained was for BMI, and the estimate of 12.4%
is consistent with what has been previously reported (Paré, Mao, and Deng, 2017),
and with performance obtained in our group (14.3%, Hansen group, CBMR). As BMI
is not assumed to be signiﬁcantly more heritable than other phenotypes, the high
performance of the BMI PRS may rather be attributable to two main points: the
summary statistics are based on an unprecedented large sample size of approximately
700,000 individuals, and the training cohort included 10,000 individuals from UKBB.
While the summary statistics is of higher quality because eﬀect sizes are more accu-
rately estimated, the large training cohort may have ensured better generalizability.
4.1.1
Relationship with heritability
Although one might hypothesize that the traits with high SNP-based heritability
would be easier to generate a PRS with high predictive power for, there is no clear
trend between R2
PRS and h2
snp. The h2
snp may be viewed as an upper limit for the
R2
PRS, because of the uncertainty in β’s from external summary statistics.
This
indicates that we could also quantify the performance of the PRS in reference to h2
snp,
and present the BMI PRS as capturing 63.57% of the narrow-sense heritability. This
percentage is signiﬁcantly lower for the other phenotypes.
We may see that the liver enzymes with lower h2
snp are diﬃcult to create PRSs
for, but the order of h2
snp and R2
PRS diﬀers. The ALK phenotype is suggestively the
most heritable, but has the least predictive PRS. This might be highly related to the
fact that the selected threshold for ALK yielded a PRS based only on 178 SNPs from
a threshold of 10−4, selected based on the unrelated Inter99 cohort. By the design
of the data-driven PRS calculations, the parameters will in all cases be ﬁtted to the
training data, which may not yield results that are generalizable in the validation set
==PAGE:64==
48
Chapter 4. Discussion
(in our case, the combined data set). The same applies to the PRSs for ALT and
GGT which were suggestively heritable estimated from summary statistics, and also
yielded PRSs identical to the canonical risk scores. The selected threshold for both,
10−8, suggests that the genetic background deﬁning liver enzyme concentrations is
not highly polygenic as the other traits. While this could also be an indication that
the etiologies for higher ALT and GGT concentrations are mostly somatic, this idea
is partly contradicted by the high heritability estimated from the summary statistics
alone. The idea of understanding whether the etiology for liver enzymes is somatic or
genetic could possibly be investigated using Mendelian randomization.
Use of summary statistics
The major limitation of the PRS is their basis, the summary statistics. Especially for
the NAS and Kleiner score, the GWAS behind the summary statistics was of extremely
limited sample size of less that 300. This made their calculation and the use of them in
the models an experiment likely to fail, which also makes the suggestive importance
of these two as features in the model surprising. Additionally, while the NAS and
Kleiner score features may rather be considered as categorical, it is here treated as
an ordinal variable, because of the limited examples for each class. Ordinal encoding
suggests a monotonic relationship between the range from 0 to 3 or 4, and the actual
severity of steatosis and ﬁbrosis, respectively. Concluding that these two PRSs have
high utility at this stage would be bold.
The heritability of ﬁbrosis and steatosis
has previously been estimated to be 0.52 (95% C.I, 0.31–0.73) and 0.5 (95% C.I.,
0.28–0.72) Loomba, Schork, et al., 2015, which poorly matches our estimates. Here
we may note that the h2

a presentation, the authors of LDSC (Bulik-Sullivan et al., 2015) argue that LDSC is
not suitable for sample size below 3.000, indicating that also the Kleiner estimate is
unreliable in spite of low variance.
The very limited sample size for these rare phenotypes is a limitation that may
be overcome in the future, as GWAS quality and sample size grows and several large
meta-analyses are initiated for more phenotypes.
Technical limitations
PRSice is a light-weight tool which is several orders of magnitude faster than LDpred
because of its simple underlying algorithm. Hereby, it is highly convenient for quickly
testing associations. For developing a more ﬁne-tuned PRS, it is possible that LDpred
may be able to achieve higher predictive performance. Currently, there is not con-
sensus on the superior performance of LDpred in literature (Privé et al., 2019, Paré,
Mao, and Deng, 2017), but it cannot be ruled out that calculating the same scores
using LDpred may improve predictive power of the PRS. Especially for the scores that
performed the best in their close-to canonical form (GGT, ALK and ALT), LDpred
may account better for polygenicity. Although it may improve prediction, this may
only be feasible for phenotypes were training data is abundant, as it has been esti-
mated in our group that 1000 samples are required for β-shrinkage and at least 300 for
selecting the fraction of causal variants. Nevertheless, benchmarking the performance
of methods for the same trait would be a useful contribution to the ﬁeld of PRS.
PRSs were calculated using two data sets, a training set for ﬁnding parameters
(threshold or fraction of causal variants), and a validation set for estimating perfor-
mance (R2
PRS), but independence could not be ensured for all phenotypes because of
limited data. In this case, the performance could have been estimated without bias

One factor that may account for limited performance is that only the intersection
of SNPs from summary statistics and target data are included in estimating the PRS.
How much this impacts performance could possibly be quantiﬁed with a range of
experiments masking SNPs at random, but to address this issue may also require
a number of bioinformatic tools that could synchronize the summary statistics and
target cohort to a larger extend. In this context, some may argue that the QC pipeline
employed for the target data, which ﬁlters out primarily low MAF SNPs, may be an
unnecessarily conservative precaution that might even impact performance negatively.
The main argument for this would be that the overlap in SNPs is reduced even further,
and the GWAS SNPS, if it is documented, have already been subject to QC.
Using MAF ﬁltering, we may exclude SNPs that are rare in our population but
yet has a signiﬁcant contribution towards the phenotype. Although removing SNPs
out of HWE is also a way of reducing the eﬀect of genotyping errors, it may also
cause true associations to be removed from the set of SNPs included (Turner et al.,
2011). Both of these ﬁlters represent yet another trade-oﬀbetween signal and noise.
It is possible that PRS calculation methods may in the future be developed towards
including information on both MAF, HWE and impuation score instead of the strict
removal of SNPs presented here.
This could perhaps be achieved using Bayesian
methods where observed MAF, HWE, and INFO could be used in a prior probability
of the SNP being a true association.
4.2
Machine learning models
The model types used in this thesis were random forest and penalized logistic regres-
sion, which are both robust in settings with a large number of features compared
to data points. The two models are built on quite distinct theory and methodology
(decision trees and modelling of log-odds), and thus presented a good opportunity
for comparison. Here we learned that the diﬀerence in performance between the two
model types was not signiﬁcant, but they did provide diﬀerent important features.
The marginally higher performance in random forest may be attributable to ability to
learn non-linear og higher-order relationships, but the feature dependencies this may
yield remains to be explored.
The models were selected based on interpretability, but several other models could
be utilized, among others gradient boosting (GB) and the artiﬁcial neural network
(ANN). GB was not selected because it is also based on CART trees, and the ANN was

Diagnostic accuracy of CAP and TE
This thesis was focused on binary classiﬁcation with target outcomes created by
thresholding the proxies TE and CAP, based on cut-oﬀs derived from the histologi-
cal data from the biopsies of the GALA-ALD data set. The results of thresholding
revealed that CAP is only a decent predictor of steatosis, whereas TE can predict ﬁ-
brosis with a consistently high ROC AUC, similar to what was presented in Hashemi,
Alavian, and Gholami-Fesharaki, 2016.
Thiele et al., 2018 presented for steatosis > 0 an AUC of 0.77 (0.71-0.83,
steatosis > 1, AUC of 0.78 (0.72-0.83), and steatosis > 2, an AUC of 0.82 (0.75-
0.88) with 95% conﬁdence intervals given in parenthesis. The CAP thresholds were
estimated using leave-one-out cross validation.
In comparison, the estimates found in this thesis were, in order of increasing
severity, AUC of 0.823 (0.8-0.85), 0.799 (0.78-0.82) and 0.797 (0.77-0.82). Contradict-
ing the ﬁndings in Thiele et al., 2018, the performance did not increase with severity,
which possibly could be attributable to the few examples of S3 (37/328). Overall, the
estimates found here correspond well to what was presented by Thiele et al., 2018,
which also includes the large conﬁdence intervals.
In spite of this preliminary ﬁnding, the models were based on predicting CAP-
based steatosis. This means that the models should not be viewed as discriminatory
models for steatosis directly, or at least used with caution. How this may be translated
to a clinical setting is diﬃcult to assess.
The continuous nature of CAP and TE would also make it possible to approach
the classiﬁcation problem as a regression problem instead, focused on predicting the
actual proxy value. One limitation here would be the ability to predict extreme values,
which is not captured in the class labels. Similar models could be used, including
random forest based on regression trees, or linear regression using ℓ2-penalization of
weights (known as LASSO).

Use of clinical data
In order to merge the data sets, several features had to be omitted or recoded as they
were only available in one data set or phrased diﬀerently. The missing features was

the analyses to one data set. This, on the other hand, would suﬀer under the limited

was a feature available in GALA-ALD, could have been a means of correcting this, it

be handled using more advanced methods, for instance by using PCA components for
correlated features, which was originally not done in order to keep interpretability of
the models simple.
4.2.2
Performance of the models
The performance of the models using blood markers only discriminating any TE-based
ﬁbrosis were 0.824 (± 0.026 and 0.801 (± 0.018) using logistic regression and random
forest, respectively. The performances for the same outcome using all features were
0.84 (± 0.022) and 0.811 (± 0.025). These performance metrics quantify the ability
to detect early stage ﬁbrosis where interventions may be most eﬀective.
In a statistical setting, the parsimonious blood marker model would quickly be
selected by metrics that penalize by the number of parameters such as Akaike informa-
tion criterium (AIC). In a machine learning-setting, the model with highest predictive
performance on independent data is selected, regardless of model complexity, when
employing methods that explicitly account for high dimensionality.
The comparison of models aimed at predicting increasing severity of ﬁbrosis sug-
gests this tasks becomes easier with severity, as ROC AUC increases for each cut-oﬀ.
Simultaneously, increasing the severity decreases the prevalence of positive examples
from 0.29 to 0.19, 0.8 and 0.8 for fibrosis > 0 to fibrosis > 3, thus increasing the
class imbalance. The same increase in performance was not seen if looking at the
precision-recall AUC. This is due to the increasing class imbalance which creates a
lower bound for performance, meaning that the relative increase in mean performance
is in fact signiﬁcant. This indicates that the models may indeed perform better for
diagnosis of severe ﬁbrosis.

4.2.3
Models including genetics
Through my experiments with adding genetics-based features, I was not able to im-
prove prediction of ﬁbrosis, neither using PRS or single variants. This was in spite
of the preliminary signiﬁcant association of PNPLA3 I18M with ﬁbrosis. While the
models should be able to account for insigniﬁcant eﬀects, introducing the remaining
variants may have mainly introduced noise. Understanding which of these variants
may have been important in the models, if any, is a task for future work, while a more
interesting approach would be to utilize the genetic data in a GWAS study.
Most of the PRSs calculated did not show signiﬁcant contribution to predicting
liver ﬁbrosis when adding these to baseline models (gender and age), neither as risk
groups or as individual scores, although this was hypothesized to be the scenario where
the PRSs could especially demonstrate their utility. No increase in mean AUC were
observed, while the variance did increase. While the BMI and Kleiner score PRSs
were identiﬁed as important features in the parameter-rich random forest models,
they could not provide additional information compared to the other features.
The baseline models consisting of age and gender predicting steatosis based on
CAP did improve signiﬁcantly from including PRSs, mainly attributable to the BMI
PRS, but also partly to the NAS PRS. As obesity is a known important risk factor for
NAFLD (Anstee, Daly, and Christopher P Day, 2011), the overlap in genetic suscep-
tibility is a good example of how combining several PRSs calculated for underlying
known risk factors in a model may possibly predict total genetic liability. While this
result alone is positive, it should be noted that they are inherently limited by the
proxy CAP’s low predictive power for steatosis.

4.3
Application in the clinic
The research presented in this thesis aimed to quantify how genetics and speciﬁcally
PRS could contribute to clinical risk assessment or categorization of individuals at high
risk to liver ﬁbrosis and steatosis. While literature often focuses on quantifying risk in
diﬀerent genetic risk groups, PRS performance was in this thesis directly measured by
building models with and without PRS and comparing their performance. This would
be an indication of when PRSs could supplement phenotype measurements available
in diﬀerent clinical scenarios.
Here it was shown that the performance of the models including PRS did not im-
prove the models signiﬁcantly from the models without, and the PRS features would
primarily be described as noise, contributing to the increasing variance in performance.
It was shown that adding single signiﬁcant variants of interest did not improve pre-
diction either. The selected phenotypes were a large mix of direct measures of disease
risk (NAS and Kleiner), whereas the others were phenotypes signiﬁcantly associated
with liver disease, as shown in the models.
In spite of the limited discriminatory power presented in this thesis, PRSs may
become useful in clinical practice in the future following several central steps. First,
growing GWAS sample size will result in more well-estimated summary statistics.
Secondly, the increasing use of sequencing technology may uncover rare variants that
contribute with additive eﬀects that are currently missed due to the use of genotyping
chips and haplotype-based imputation.
Sequencing technology may also elucidate
the gap in missing heritability. Finally, more sophisticated methodology for creating
predictive PRSs may also highly move the clinical utility forward (Lewis and Vassos,
2020).
Further works
Several additional projects have been already been introduced in this discussion. Fur-
thermore, as suggested by Janssens, 2019, the researchers working with PRS should
continue to explore modelling strategies that take into account the underlying biol-
ogy and not just mathematical properties or unexplainable gains in performance. An
idea which remains to be explored is to select genetic contributions based on cell-type
speciﬁc expression (for example quantiﬁed via single-cell RNA-seq) in order to reduce
the amount of noise contributed from irrelevant SNPs. In the context of liver disease,

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this thesis, I calculated individual PRSs for participants susceptible to fatty liver
disease for a range of traits relevant to steatosis and ﬁbrosis development. All except
two of the resulting PRSs explained a signiﬁcant amount of the variance of the trait
they sought to predict, although the signal was relatively weak. Nevertheless, this
demonstrated that PRS can be used to create individual scores that in the future may
be play an important role in precision medicine.
Furthermore, I merged and prepared clinical data from three data sets, SIPHON,
GALAXY-HP, and GALAXY-ALD, in order to construct machine learning models,
based on random forest and logistic regression. The GALAXY-ALD cohort was used
for deriving thresholds for the proxies, CAP and TE, to approximate biopsy-veriﬁed
stages of liver steatosis and ﬁbrosis, respectively. Here I showed that TE is a conﬁdent
predictor of ﬁbrosis, whereas the performance of CAP is less convincing. The diﬀerence
in use of biopsy data or proxies was then demonstrated by PNPLA3 I148M, which
has previously been associated with ﬁbrosis development.
The models based on clinical data were trained to discriminate proxy-ﬁbrosis and
steatosis based on blood markers alone or all available features. These results showed
that the much more parsimonious model for ﬁbrosis performed approximately as well
as the full model, contrary to the feature-rich steatosis model, which signiﬁcantly
outperformed the blood marker model.
The clinical data were also combined with PRSs to investigate how these could
improve diagnosis. Here, I found that PRSs could not signiﬁcantly improve classi-
ﬁcation models of ﬁbrosis (ROC AUC increased marginally from 0.577 (± 0.035) to
0.583 (± 0.031) predicting any ﬁbrosis), but could improve a simple model based on
age and gender of steatosis from 0.55 (± 0.05) to 0.61 (± 0.01) for the most severe
state of steatosis. The use of PRSs were compared with use of eleven genome-wide
signiﬁcant variants identiﬁed in liver disease which could not improve performance in
either of the scenarios.
The ﬁndings suggest that the use of PRSs in a clinical setting for identifying
ﬁbrosis is premature, because the PRSs cannot supply signiﬁcant additional informa-
tion neither for a simple baseline model or when only anthropometric features are
available. In contrary, the genetic liability for a high BMI could possibly guide rec-
ommendations for high-risk individuals to initiate lifestyle-interventions in order to
prevent the development of steatosis.

A.1.1
LD score regression for heritability
LDSC is a method developed for examining the relationship between test statistics
and LD, aimed at distinguishing inﬂated test statistics from confounding bias (mostly

contrast, an elevated test statistic due to confounding will not correlate well with LD,
and will be a sign of genomic inﬂation as described in Section 1.3.2.

between a SNP of interest and the k SNPs that reside within a 1 centimorgan (cM)

Bootstrap aggregating (bagging)
Bagging is an ensemble approach based on training several classiﬁers on bootstrapped
data sets. Intuitively, this can improve classiﬁcation performance through the "wisdom
of the crowds", where the outcome is predicted based on an ensemble of classiﬁers
instead of a single one. An observation will be classiﬁed according to majority voting
as resembled in the Figure A.2. Bootstrapping denotes a commonly used statistical
method where a sample is drawn with replacement from the full dataset. The idea is
that the empirical distribution of the sample will mimic the distribution it is drawn
from. For each bootstrapped data set, a decision tree is trained. This yields a hyper
parameter that may be optimized.
It can be shown that the variance of the ensemble model is reduced to the correla-
tion between the trees (intuitively, trees trained on samples from the same distribution,
will be highly similar, especially if certain features are much more predictive than oth-
ers). This means that to reduce the variance of the model, the decision trees should
be decorrelated. This is implemented by iteratively randomly subsampling features
from the total feature space such that the set of available features for optimal classi-
ﬁcation will diﬀer for each split. This results in a the desirable property that which
features are selected for splitting the data can be a measure of feature importance
that is comparable with the size of β estimates of the logistic regression model. Just
like the regularization techniques, this can be regarded as a feature selection step.
A.2.4
Cross validation
Cross validation (CV) is a method characterized by splitting the data in subsets
and using diﬀerent combinations of this data for training, testing and validation. In
all cases, training and testing is not performed on the same data, as this leads to
overestimation of performance. In this work, we use k-fold CV (as opposed to leave-
one-out or the hold-out method) where training is performed on k −1 parts of the
data and testing on the last.
CV can be used for both model performance estimation and model ﬁtting. For
performance evaluation, we wish to measure the generalization error of the model,
i.e. how well the model will perform on average when presented with new, unseen
data. Model ﬁtting includes hyper parameter optimization and/or feature selection.
In general, if any decision-making regarding the model is made, either nested CV or
two-layer CV is required to asses the model performance without bias.
