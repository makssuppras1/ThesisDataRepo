Abstract
This Master Thesis carries out one of the latest ways of solving the Liner Shipping Network
Design Problem (LSNDP) with transit time restrictions, namely a two-tier stage algorithm.
The LSNDP deals with optimising the network design for liner shipping routes. This problem
is known to be NP-hard [12] and like many industry problems, the number of constraints are
numerous. Therefore, we have taken up the simplified problem, in order to look into the general
feasibility of this procedure. Our model includes the transit time constraints, the transshipment
costs and the possibility of rejecting demand. This will thus allow us to compare our results
with Brouer et Al. (2015) [3].
In collaboration with Maersk, there is a shared interest/curiosity in, if small alterations made
to an already deployed network, could further optimise the network, with regards to lowering
the forfeiture of containers. Thus being able to provide global transportation options to all
customers from all ports, even if some container transports do not carry an individual profit.
To be more precise, the forfeiture has been minimised through the total cost, as each forfeited
container is associated with a great penalty cost. Furthermore, for minimising the total cost of
the network, the focus has also been on the most important constraints, the rotation’s capacity,
weekly frequency, the commodity’s transit time and demand. These - as the names suggest -
include making sure that the assigned vessel classes do not carry more containers than their
capacities allow, that the vessel classes follow a weekly frequency in their rotations and that the
deliveries come in time given a set of origin and destination pairs.
For the practical aspect of this thesis, our algorithm is modelled around the data given in
LinerLib, which is introduced in Broer et al. (2014a)A Base Integer Programming Model and
Benchmark Suite for Liner-Shipping Network Design [2]. The solution data set found in LinerLib
consists of seven different regions, where two variants of each region exist, resulting in a total of
three variants for each region. Additionally, the data set provides relevant information regarding
ports, distances, vessel classes and demand from customers. For each variant, there exists a
public solution, presented in Brouer et al. (2014b)[4], which is also accessible at the github
repository. These publicized solutions will act as our initial networks. Here, we do note that the
problem solved in Brouer et al. (2014b) does not include the transit time restrictions. However,
our two-tier algorithm starts with a network (the upper tier) and then solves the flow problem on
it afterwards (which contains the time restrictions). This means that the solutions from Brouer
et al. (2014b) are only used as an initial network, not a way of comparing results.
In order to design an improved network - which is the first stage of the algorithm -, we need
to know how much commodity is fulfilled. This leads us to the second stage handled in this
algorithm: the Time Constrained Multi-Commodity Flow (TCMCF) problem. This problem
has been shown to be NP-hard [8] [13].
The TCMCF problem can be formulated either w.r.t. paths or edges. The path-formulation
(versus the edge-formulation) does not only help lower the computational time - due to e.g.
fewer variables - but also enables us to address the transit time restrictions in an easier manner,
why we chose it.
In order to solve the path-formulated TCMCF problem, we will be using
a Column Generation approach such as in Karsten et al. (2015) [13] for example. This will
result in a restricted master problem (RMP) and its respective subproblem to be solved. That
is, respectively, the TCMCF problem for a set of feasible paths and the problem of finding all
feasible paths. The latter falls under the category Resource Constrained Shortest Path (RCSP)
problems, with the resources being transit time (constrained) and reduced cost (unconstrained).
These are interconnected, since we need the dual variables from the RMP, in order to compute
the reduced cost, needed to find feasible paths.
The set of feasible paths needed to reach optimality is found using an A-Star Label Setting

==PAGE:4==
algorithm. Here, we find all the feasible paths (in terms of transit time) which are needed to solve
the TCMCF optimally for all commodities (hence the name of the problem: Multi-Commodity
Flow). The A-Star Label Setting algorithm consists in finding the shortest feasible paths (w.r.t.
reduced cost) and using those to determine the best shortest path for each commodity, thus
solving the given RCSP problem.
The loop between the RMP and the RCSP subproblem
continues until no more feasible paths are needed to be found, in order to reach an optimal
solution to our TCMCF.
Since we wish to minimize the amount of forfeiture (as well as the total cost), once the TCMCF
is solved, we scrutinize the reasons behind the forfeitures. We have categorized those reasons
into three areas entitled Ports, Transit Time and Capacity. These correspond to a port (either
origin, destination or both) missing from the network, the transit time being too low or, lastly,
that used vessel classes are saturated and thus cannot carry the remaining demand. We create a
list of the forfeited demands, sorted w.r.t. ’impact’, meaning that the demands which can yield
most revenue are to be dealt with first. Given the reason, we will be changing the network in
order to potentially solve this forfeiture with the use of different destroy/repair functions, that
we have created. Using a scoring system, our metaheuristic, which is a variation of an Adaptive
Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) [17], will choose which functions to use next in order to
solve the given forfeiture. If this change in network is accepted we create a new priority forfeiture
list. This way, we gain feedback from the TCMCF not only by calculating the network’s total
cost, but also by addressing the reasons behind the forfeiture. These loops are run until a given
maximum computational time is reached, if the best solution is not changing for a given number
of iterations or if no more changes can be done to the network to handle the given forfeiture
problems.
The results of our project indicate that such a method can indeed solve the LSNDP with transit
time restrictions. Our objectives were to lower the total forfeiture through small changes in the
network, chosen with the help of specific forfeiture flow feedback. Our model seems to satisfy
these objectives for all of the instances we have tested on. This is promising for companies
which are willing to change only small attributes in their rotations in order to further satisfy
their customers’ needs.
More specifically, we notice that all improved networks have a overall increased vessel speed.
Some instances manage to increase the total number of ports in the network and some lower the
number of used vessels.

Introduction
The maritime transport industry is one of the most environmentally friendly modes of trans-
portation, taking care of almost 90% of the global trade [6]. In 2019 it was estimated that more
than 14 billion $ was annually entangled in the industry 1. Needless to say, even a small reduc-
tion in cost can result in a high amount of money saved. A big cost for the shipping companies is
the fuel consumption. Thus if one can design faster, smarter and/or shorter shipping rotations,
the company could save millions whilst also reducing their CO2 emission.
This leads us to the Liner Shipping Network Design Problem (LSNDP), which consists in best
planning the periodic sailing routes for vessels that need to ship large quantities of cargo from
a set of origin ports to their corresponding destination ports. The design of the network is
often complex - in fact strongly NP-hard [2] - as multiple variables need to be considered, espe-
cially for large shipping companies. In this thesis, we will solve the Time Constrained LSNDP
(TCLSNDP). This one includes the most important constraints: fulfilling demand, adhering
to the transit time restrictions, respecting the vessel classes’ capacities and accounting to the
weekly frequency on the operated routes. Some other constraints that were disregarded were,
for instance, the cabotage laws and maximum number of transshipments. Our implementation
accepts butterfly nodes but does not undertake more complex rotation structures.
This thesis was made as a collaboration with Maersk. For both partners there is a joint interest in
researching and developing strategies to alter current networks, to better customers and business
relations. Thus, we want to implement smaller changes which decrease the total forfeiture for a
given network.
This implementation could enable transporting cargo which individually is less profitable and
thus leading to a costlier solution. Hence, the objective of this master thesis is, to give Maersk
a tool, which does not only give valuable insight into the reasons behind the high forfeiture,
but also presents a new solution (the network and the container flow through it) resulting in
less forfeiture. Thus, we expect this flow gain to increase and for this to possibly outweigh the
potential increase in network cost. This would, evidently, entail a decrease in total cost.
LinerLib is a benchmarking data set introduced along with A Base Integer Programming Model
and Benchmark Suite for Liner-Shipping Network Design [2] and contains data for the modelling
of real-life like services and network for liner shipping. Note that it is not actual data from Maersk
but "based on real-life data from the largest global liner shipping company, Maersk Line, and
supplemented by data from several industry and public stakeholders" [2].In Section 3 the data
set is fully explored. The Github repository for the data set is accompanied with publicized
solutions introduced with A matheuristic for the liner shipping network design problem [4].
These solutions will for this thesis act as the initial networks, for which we wish to train and
test our model on.
In the literature there exists promising algorithms to solve this problem, most notably the two
tier algorithms, which split the problem into two interconnected parts; the network design and
the container flow [6]. This is the approach we have chosen in this thesis. More specifically,
we follow a service-first flow-second approach [8]. This consists in solving the upper tier first,
namely designing the network, followed by solving the lower tier, namely the container flow
through that network.
Given that we do not design the initial network from scratch ourselves, we will start by handling
the lower tier of the problem with the given initial network. This lower tier will be expressed as a
Time Constrained Multi-Commodity Flow (TCMCF) problem. It will inform which commodities
are forfeited and why.
This feedback will be used to modify the network accordingly, thus

returning to the upper tier of the LSDNP algorithm. These modifications will be executed by a
series of destroy and repair functions. After the network has been somewhat modified, the next
forfeited commodity can be tended to. This loop continues until the termination criteria are
met. The Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) metaheuristic embraces these nested
operations. Being an adaptive metaheuristic, it will learn with the iterations and through a
scoring system, which destroy and repair functions are best to utilize depending on the reason
behind the given commodity’s forfeiture.
The TCMCF, being a NP-hard problem, will be solved using Column Generation such as is
done in Karsten et al. (2017)[12]. This consists in dividing the problem into two problems,
namely the Restricted Master Problem (RMP) and the Resource Constrained Shortest Path
(RSCSP) Sub-Problem. The RMP will find the optimal number of containers to be sent on a
set of feasible origin-to-destination paths. Using the dual variables from the RMP, solving the
RSCP sub-problem will output the feasible paths that are needed to reach optimality. It is in the
RCSP problem that the transit time restrictions come into play. The RSCP sub-problem will
be solved using a label setting algorithm as is done in Karsten et al. (2015)[13]. Specifically, we
have chosen to implement an A-Star label setting algorithm inspired by Tai-Yu Ma (2014)[16].
The loop between the RMP and the RSCP problem continues until there are no more feasible
paths that are needed to be considered in order to solve the TCMCF problem optimally.
The TCMCF will inform which commodities are forfeited and for what reason.
These are
comprised into three categories: the network does not include the origin and/or destination
port, the transit time is too low or that there is not enough capacity to carry all the cargo.
Given the reason for forfeiture, the ALNS chooses how to destroy and repair the network. These
heuristics are then scored for their performance, which will affect their probability of getting
picked in a later iteration of the ALNS. The performance is solely based on minimizing the total
cost. However, forfeiting containers will have a negative effect on the total cost (on the flow cost
to be more exact) due to the attached penalty. For this thesis the penalty has been chosen to be
substantially larger than in Brouer et al. (2014b)[4]. This was chosen in order to accommodate
the objective of the thesis, in short reducing forfeiture.
This adaptive metaheuristic will run many iterations in order to explore the solution space
further.
This could allow for both escaping the local minimum and then reaching a new -
hopefully global - minimum.
We will marginally modify the ALNS such that its heuristics are destroy/repair function paired
together.
These pairs will not - in the bigger instances - reach a big degree of destruction.
Instead, it will settle with smaller degrees of destruction, in order to accommodate the objective
of this thesis of not fundamentally altering the networks.
In spite of the many smaller network modifications, we expect our program to improve the initial
solutions from Brouer et al. (2014b). We expect our solutions to increase the number of demand
fulfilled whilst lowering the total cost of the solution.

Literature Review
In order to go about this Time Constrained LSDNP in an efficient manner, we have drawn
inspiration from both recent and classical literature. These have not only helped us in time
of need, but also shed a light on the state-of-the-art methods, common do’s and dont’s and
generally the context and theory behind the problems and applied methods.
2.1
Context
When diving into this interesting but complex problem that is the Time Constrained LSNDP,
two papers were particularly useful. These were essential in our understanding of the general
context of the problem also including the common ways of solving it.
Erik Orm Hellsten’s PHD thesis [8] not only skillfully contextualized the LSNDP, but especially
nicely outlined the different popular methods used to solve it. He starts off with an introduction
to liner shipping, even including a description of the LinerLib data set, which we have used in
this project as well. For what concerns the data set, we, however, mostly relied on the paper
associated with it, namely Brouer et al. (2014a)[2]. The PHD thesis gives a thorough overview
of the types of models and algorithms used to solve the LSNDP. Here, different strategies on how
to design two-tier algorithms are presented. The service-first and flow-second algorithms and
the backbone flow are examples of aforementioned two-tier algorithms. In short, the service-first
and flow-second algorithms consist in designing a network (set of services, in this thesis called
rotations), followed by finding the flow through that network. One then gains feedback from the
flow solution and can redesign the rotations accordingly. The backbone flow algorithms, on the
other hand, start by running the flow on a fully connected graph and given that flow feedback
(maximizing the backbone flow) designs the network accordingly. Essentially the backbone flow
follows the reverse order of the tasks dealt with in the service-first and flow-second approach.
As mentioned previously, for what regards the details of the available data, we relied more on
Brouer et al.
(2014a).
Here, everything from the business, the network, the infrastructure,
the demand in the liner shipping world, down to the corresponding limitations in the Liner
Lib data set are thoroughly explained. Moreover, this paper presents a reference model and
benchmark suite for the LSNDP. In their model they use a Tabu search metaheuristic in order
to iteratively change the network (along with solving the according MCF problem), inspired by
the Tabu-search based model in Alvarez (2009)[23]. The Mixed Integer Program (MIP) neigh-
borhood used in the Tabu search is, however, a more advanced version of the one seen in the
paper Alvarez (2009), as it takes additional rules into account and generates rotations of higher
quality. Their model parallelises the sub-problems, allowing a batch of different sub-problems to
be run simultaneously, thus resulting in many different solutions. This permits them to choose
the network change which results in the best improvement in objective value (by running the
MCF on the network).
Although they successfully manage to extend the model, they conclude that there is still high
need for and potential in future works, in order to find better solutions as well as taking more
commercial requirements into account. After sparring with network designers from Maersk Line,
they concluded that the rotations their program found "resemble real-world rotations, and that
the overall network fulfills most of the relevant properties" [2].
Survey papers are common for staying updated in the current trends of a certain problem solving,
whilst also describing the problem in a holistic manner. Such a paper exists for the LSNDP [6].
This gives an updated overview of the problem, the typical variations studied, data sets used

(Liner Lib), models and lastly the performance of different popular algorithms.
2.2
Multi-Commodity Flow problem
The first problem met in our two-tier algorithm is the TCMCF problem. In order to solve this,
we have taken inspiration from various papers, among which Karsten et al. (2015) [13] and
Tai-Yu Ma (2014) [16] were particularly useful.
In Karsten et al. (2015), they not only present an algorithm for solving the TCMCF problem but
also prove why including the transit time restrictions are so important in a business perspective.
In fact, they conducted experiments, by "taking the networks produced by a LSND heuristic
and comparing the estimated revenue with and without including the time constraint in the
cargo flow calculations. The results show a substantial difference, and we therefore recommend
that future LSND algorithms should include the transit time constraint if possible" [13].
In this same paper, they focus on solving the TCMCF problem. They explain the difference
between the edge and path-formulated MCF and justify choosing the path-flow formulation. It
was shown to be more efficient when dealing with bigger (and thus more realistic) instances,
since the number of edges will grow faster than the number of paths. Furthermore, the path-
formulation facilitates the addition of transit time constraints. These constraints are included in
the definition of a feasible path. This results in dividing the TCMCF problem into two distinct
problems. They solve the Master Problem using delayed column generation and resolve the Re-
source Constrained Shortest Path (RSCP) sub-problem by employing a label setting algorithm.
Additionally, they present graph-contraction techniques and different approaches to setting up
transshipment edges and nodes in the network, which would result in an improvement in com-
putational time.
They conclude that including the transit time constraints is both a must and possibility when
solving the LSNDP (and thus the MCF problem). In fact, as mentioned previously, the difference
in profit between the results with and without transit time constraints included in their calcu-
lations are too substantial to be disregarded. Additionally, as the transit time constraints are
added, the solution space is correspondingly narrowed down. They did also see improvement in
computational speed when contracting the graphs and structuring the transshipment differently.
Their use of the label setting algorithm to solve the RCSP sub-problem in Karsten et al. (2015)
prompted us to further research such efficient algorithms. This lead us to An A-Star label-setting
algorithm for multimodal resource constrained shortest path problem by Tai-Yu Ma[16]. This pa-
per goes into depth in solving RCSP problems with the use of an A-Star label-setting algorithm.
This algorithm has shown itself efficient in solving RCSP problems for realistic multimodal net-
works. It follows a strategy of discarding labels that one can assure will not result in a feasible
path. This check is done using shortest path algorithms in order to gain upper bounds for both
the transit time resource and the reduced cost of a future path. In our implementation, we
have made use of shortest path algorithms, Dijkstra’s for the time resource and Bellman-Ford’s
for the reduced costs. If these bounds are not in accordance with the restrictions (under the
allowed maximum transit time and a negative reduced cost) we can already dispose of this label.
Furthermore, the dominance check in their algorithm includes sorting the labels w.r.t. reduced
cost, which will accelerate the additional label discard process.
We supplemented our understanding of the RCSP problem by turning to Irnich et al. (2006)
[10]. In this paper, label-setting algorithms are introduced and explained but with a bigger
focus on the theory it stems from. Other general theoretical insight for the Column Generation
approach to solve the TCMFC problem - even including the RCSP problem - was gained through
5
==PAGE:12==
the book Shortest Path Problems with Resource Constraints by Desaulniers et al. [7].
2.3
Two Tier Structure: Combining the Network Design and MCF to solve
the LSNDP
This project also focuses on the two-tier structure of the LSNDP with transit time constraints
and how to implement it in order to solve the problem. There exists many ways of handling
this problem. We have thus taken inspiration in several papers tackling the problem in different
manners.
In Mikkel Johansen’s master thesis [11], the potential benefit of alternative fuels is investigated
through the solution of the LSNDP using an Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) ap-
proach. We are using the same data as is used in Johansen’s thesis, namely LinerLib [2]. Mikkel
Johansen didn’t choose the same two-tier structure as we have, but, instead, implemented a
backbone flow.
In additional contrast to our approach, he disregarded the transit time con-
straints. This means that he starts with a MCF problem and thereafter creates a network based
upon his flow solution. The first iteration consists in solving the MCF assuming that there are
edges between all ports; the network is then created in order to maximize the backbone flow. He
sets the MCF up as a path formulation and solves it using Column Generation and Djikstra’s
algorithm for the shortest path subproblem. For the generation of rotations, he uses a heuristic
approach with a serial insertion procedure, i.e. he iteratively inserts edges present in the paths
found with the highest amount of backbone flow.
After finishing the initial network, the metaheuristic (ALNS) approach commences. Here, three
destroy and four repair methods are created. It is important to add that the flow is used as
feedback in order to direct the reparation of the destroyed solution, i.e. the flow is included
inside the repair functions.
His thesis was of great inspiration to us, but papers such as Brouer et al. (2015)[3] were also very
inspirational, as they included the transit time constraint. However, in Brouer et al. (2015),
they differed from using an ALNS metaheuristic and instead a LNS approach using an Integer
Program (IP) as its heuristic. The IP is modelled such that it maximises the estimated profit
increase from removing and inserting port calls, accounting for the other changes this implies.
Here, they also soften the transit time constraints, by adding a proportional penalty if a com-
modity neglects its transit time (i.e. is delayed). Their initial solution is found by using a greedy
knapsack based construction heuristic.
An other paper that has also studied using a two-tier algorithm to solve the LSNDP is Alvarez
(2009) [23], which was very successful and cited for their Tabu Search implementation. Agarwal
et al. (2008) [1] presents three different methods of solving the LSNDP; a Greedy Heuristic,
Column Generation and, especially, Benders Decomposition approach.

The latter sheds a light on the fundamental ideas behind the ALNS. To stay up to date, we read

Data
The LSNDP represents the problem of optimising liner shipping routes by searching for the
network that results in the lowest total cost. The number of rules and constraints one must
adhere to makes it a challenging assignment. In fact, we look to fully optimize the handling
of tons of cargo from and to different ports (also known as commodities). Each commodity
comes with specific information such as demand, maximum allowed transit time and revenue.
The demand denotes the number of containers that need to be shipped.
The transit times
signify the maximal time (in weeks) the travel must take, for the container to get from origin
to destination. Lastly, the revenue indicates the price the client is prepared to pay per forty
feet container equivalent unit (FFE)2. Designing the network to accommodate for all of the
commodities gives rise to some complex network structures. Additionally, for each region or
country that a vessel must sail through, specific rules can be imposed upon the vessel, better
known as cabotage rules. These rules are typically restricting which companies (depending on
their nationality) are allowed to sail in which territories. This complicates knowing which ships
and subcontractors, the planners can send into the different countries.
The practical aspect of this master thesis is built upon the LinerLib data set. It is a benchmark
suite for liner shipping network design problems, and was published alongside A Base Integer
Programming Model and Benchmark Suite for Liner-Shipping Network Design [2]. The data set
is a collaboration between Maersk and the authors of Brouer et al. (2014a) [2]. It has been
created to further the research for Liner shipping networks, given the huge monetary potential
there is for even small savings as well as the mathematical interest of solving this NP-hard
problem [2]. The data set was last reviewed in January 20113, and thus is not updated with
the latest trends. In the last decade the global supply chain has increased and recently with the
impact of Covid19, there has been a huge increase in shipping demand prices, as can be seen
for the years 2020 and 2021 on the Shanghai Containerized Freight Index4. However, we still
believe this data set to be relevant for this master thesis, since it includes all the data, we wish
to take into account in our implementation.

which have a net gain. Thus, if it is too expensive to transport cargo, compared to the revenue
gain, it would not select it. In other words, when the penalty is low it makes one lean towards
only transporting profitable demand, whereas a higher penalty puts a value to a lost business
opportunity.
There is can thus be a trade-off between fulfilling the clients’ wishes and pursuing total net gain.
Given that we have set ourselves to find a solution with less forfeiture and have thus put the
clients’ needs first, we have chosen to link a large penalty with container forfeiture.
This results in the program’s objective being to minimize the total cost of the network, by
focusing on sending as much cargo through as possible. This is in accordance to Maersk’s wishes
of maintaining a good and trusting relationship with their clients.

Network
The network is divided into three components; Edges, Nodes and Rotations, which all are
implemented as mutable structs in Julia (as seen in Section 13). With our current im-
plementation, our network can handle the removal and addition of nodes, edges and butterfly
nodes, which will be explained later. It also updates the speed (and consequently duration) and
numbers of vessels assigned to a rotation which experiences change. We have chosen to model
the network, such that the transit time restrictions in our MCF, can be dealt with relatively
easily. The network was thus inspired by the complete network structure presented in The time
constrained multi-commodity network flow problem and its application to liner shipping network
design [13]. The small alteration is that for each unique port in a rotation we have modelled
two nodes, as can be seen in Figure 1 and Figure 2.
Known limitations to our implementation include the insertion of new butterfly nodes and the
creation of new rotations, as these features have not been needed for the development of our
heuristics.
5.1
Nodes
During the process of creating a stable network, we realised that we needed an excessive number
of nodes. For our network we have prioritized four different kinds of network nodes; Arrival,
Departure, Source and Sink. The Arrival and Departure nodes are paired together and for each
rotation using a port, a pair is created and associated with that port. Furthermore, for each
commodity’s origin port, a Source node is associated to facilitate the commodity-based flow
calculations. That is, for each commodity a Source and Sink node are defined. Analogously,
each commodity’s destination port has an associated Sink node.
In Figure 1 an example portraying the aforementioned nodes can be seen.

5.2
Edges
To accompany the various types of nodes and especially for modelling transshipment between
the rotations, we needed additional kinds of edges. In total we have created six different types
of edges; Forfeited, Voyage, Transshipment, Internal, Load and Unload. An example of the use
of Transshipment edges can be seen in Figure 2. The remaining types of edges can also be seen
in Figure 3.
For cargo which cannot be transported (due to any constraint), we send it on the Forfeited
edges. The forfeited edge is modelled as a direct edge from the commodity’s source node to its
sink node (as can be seen in Figure 2).
The transit time for such an edge is always sat to be the maximum time allowed for that
commodity, since we want this to be a ’feasible’ last resort for the flow.
The associated cost of a forfeited edge is equal a forfeiture penalty price. Indeed, not sending
cargo through doesn’t cost anything in terms of typical flow and network cost. However, as
mentioned in Section 3 we want to associate a monetary loss with forfeiting containers (thus
using a forfeited edge).
These edges are not included in any rotations, since the forfeited edges are not linked with any
rotations. Thus they do not include an upper bound (capacity) limit.
Lastly, the distance of such an edge is, evidently, equal zero, as it will simply not leave the

supplier.
An example of the use of Forfeited edges can be seen in Figure 3.
The Voyage edges go from a Departure node to the subsequent Arrival node.
Both nodes belong to the same rotation, and the Voyage edge is thus affiliated to that rotation.
The edge has a distance, extracted from the LinerLib data set [2], equal to the shortest possible
distance between two ports. We note that, in our implementation, we will always choose the
shortest distance between two ports, no matter the canal cost.
Furthermore, the edge has a duration that is calculated by the average speed for that rotation,
and a cost per container (in FFE) which is taken from the associated port call cost.
An example of the use of Voyage edges can be seen in Figure 1, Figure 2 and Figure 3.
Each Arrival and Departure node is connected via an Internal edge.
The time for this edge has been sat to equal one day in accordance with historical data [2].
There is no cost nor distance associated with this edge.
This edge is, evidently, associated with its nodes’ shared rotation.
The use of Internal edges are illustrated in Figure 1, Figure 2 and Figure 3.
For each port, all of the source nodes in that port are connected to all of the departure nodes in
that port via Load edges.
Theses edges also have a time sat to equal one day, in accordance to [2].
Furthermore, the edge has a cost equal to the load cost for the associated port (per FFE).
This edge also contains information about the rotation it loads to through a rotation ID.
Like with the Internal edges, the Load edges represent a distance of zero nautical miles.
An example of the use of Load edges can be seen in Figure 1 and Figure 3.
For the Unloading edges, the structure is similar, but the edges start at departure nodes and
end at sink nodes.
The time is sat to equal zero days instead of one day, as they have already spent one day in the
internal edge.
Its cost is, similarly to the Load edge, equal the unload cost for the associated port (per FFE).
The distance sailed on this edge is also equal zero nautical miles.
This edge type also contains information about the rotation it unloads from through a rotation
ID.
The Unload edge is shown in the same figures as for the Load edge.
Lastly, we have the Transshipment edges. These are edges going from each Arrival node in the
port to all the other Departure nodes part of a different rotation.
These edges have a time of three days as given in [2], the cost is taken as the transportation

6
Multi-Commodity Flow Problem
As mentioned in the introduction, we are interested in solving the LSNDP with transit time
restrictions. We are going to design our version of an ALNS metaheuristic, which will follow a
two-tier approach. More exactly, a service-first flow-second approach as it is called in [8]. As
the name suggests this means that we start by designing rotations followed by finding the flow
corresponding to trying to fulfill the demand on that network. Thereafter, we redesign (make
some changes to) the network in order to accommodate the flow’s feedback and find the new
flow and so on.
In our case, we start with a given network (instead of designing one from scratch ourselves). As
mentioned earlier, this initial network comes from the solutions introduced in [4] and which are
accessible at the Github repository. This leads us now to the problem corresponding to finding
the flow on that network.
Finding the flow through a network, consists in minimizing the cost of sending demand through
the network (taking revenue into account too) without violating a specific set of constraints.

Although these edges also arrive to their respective destinations, forfeited containers clearly do
not generate revenue.

The capacity constraints (3) guarantee that we do not overload the vessels. More precisely, the
sum of all containers sharing the same edge on their way to their respective destinations should
not exceed the assigned vessel’s capacity uij.
Lastly, we do not allow to send a negative number of containers but do allow fractions of
containers, i.e. the domain of our decision variable is relaxed compared to the model in Section 4
and thus not restrained to being integer. We allow this as, in practice, the commodities tend to
be in the thousands and thus a fraction can be perceived as negligible.
Furthermore, in this problem, we work with fixed demands, i.e. we have not modelled our
demands with uncertainty. This will thus result in a gap between the model’s demands and the
ones in practice, since in reality, the demands are not exactly known in advance. This means that
the potential rounding errors followed by the acceptance of fractions of containers (which are
minor given the large number of containers) will anyway be overshadowed by this gap in demand
[13]. Additionally, in Liner Shipping Cargo Allocation with Repositioning of Empty Containers
[5], given their experimental results, they found the "gap between the integer solution found by
rounding and the optimal LP solution [to be] within 0.01%".
As will be noticed, none of these constraints include a restriction on transit time. Indeed, the
inclusion of this is strenuous when formulating the MFC with regards to edges. Instead, we
chose to use the path-formulation.
6.1
Path-Formulated Master Problem
The inclusion of transit time restrictions turn this MFC problem into a NP-hard one as proven
in [13]. Being so, an efficient method of going about such problems is through a Column Gen-
eration approach as is also done in [13].
Formulating the MFC master problem with a path-formulation not only facilitates the inclusion
of transit time constraints but also results in the definition of fewer variables (and thus a compu-
tational acceleration). This formulation consists in defining our variables for each feasible path
(for each commodity) and not each edge. This will clearly result in the definition of fewer vari-
ables. This formulation will also facilitate including the transit time constraints, since a transit
time is defined per commodity and can thus be included in the definition of a feasible path. We
thus define a feasible path as a set of consecutive edges starting at the origin and ending at the
destination of the given commodity, that together adhere to the transit time constraint of that
commodity. The path-formulation of the MCF looks as follows.

There are two types of paths: the forfeited paths and the non-forfeited paths. The non-forfeited
path designates a set of consecutive edges that containers can take to get from their commodity’s
origin to destination.
The cost affiliated to this type of path is the sum of its edges’ costs
subtracting the revenue associated with the path’s commodity.
The forfeited paths, however, do not include revenue. Indeed, a forfeited path is defined as
a path consisting of its commodity’s forfeited edge (Section 5.2), which symbolizes that the
containers sent on that path are forfeited. In other words, this means that the customer will not
receive these containers. Consequently, the supplier will not receive revenue. The cost attached
to forfeited paths corresponds to a penalty for forfeiting this cargo.
The demand constraints (6) hold the same purpose as in the edge-formulation, namely that all
containers for each commodities are considered. The dual variable associated to a such constraint
is called πk.
The capacity constraints (7), once again, verify that no vessel takes on more containers than its
capacity allows. Note that for the forfeited paths, the capacity has no upper bound, as there
is no limit to how many containers can be forfeited - in fact they all can. The dual variable of
the capacity constraint is denoted by λe and expresses a guess of how much the objective value
could get reduced by if more capacity was available on the corresponding edge.
Lastly, the decision variables are defined per path - again, resulting in considerably fewer decision
variables compared to the edge-formulation - and no negative number of containers can be sent
on a path.
6.2
Column Generation
As mentioned in the definition of a feasible path, the transit time restriction is insinuated instead
of explicitly formulated as a constraint in the master problem; this would be strenuous. Seeing
that the MCF including transit time restriction is a NP hard problem [13], an efficient manner
of going about it is by Column Generation. Indeed, using the path-formulated MCF, we can
clearly divide the problem into a Restricted Master Problem (solving (5)-(8)) and a sub-problem,
which consists in finding new feasible paths to add to the set of paths that are needed to reach
optimality.
The dual variables from the Restricted Master Problem (RMP) will play an important role in
finding the new subset of feasible paths, which will contribute to lead to optimality. In fact, the
dual variables are included in the calculation of the reduced costs. Thus, feasible paths with
negative reduced costs belong in the set of feasible paths that will lead to an optimal MCF
solution. Every time a new feasible path j - that can lead to optimality - is found by the sub-
problem, we add a corresponding reduced cost column to our RMP in terms of the new feasible
path to be included in Ωk (with k being j’s commodity) and thus an additional decision variable,
xj, in the RMP. Evidently, we continue to generate new columns until we have all feasible paths
necessary to reach optimality. Then, we, per definition, have solved that MCF problem with
transit time restrictions to optimality.
An overview of the Column Generation approach is illustrated through a diagram in Figure 6.

problem. In fact, when solving our sub-problem, we will only be considering non-forfeited paths.
Therefore, it is important to initially add all forfeited paths to the set of path which will help
reach optimality. This way, the RMP will always have the possibility of forfeiting cargo.
Note that the time spent on a forfeited path is equal to the maximum transit time of the
corresponding commodity. This is due to the fact that we need to have the option of forfeiting
cargo, but do not wish to choose that option unless strictly necessary. This is why, the transit
time is the highest possible and the cost of such a path is raised with a penalty. We are, thus,
making these paths as unattractive as possible, whilst keeping them feasible. Lastly, we remind
that since they have no upper bound on their capacity and do - on paper - originate in and
arrive at, respectively, the right origin and destination port, they fulfill all criteria for them to
be categorized as feasible paths.

This means, that we find ourselves in a Resource Constrained Shortest Path (RCSP) problem.
Indeed, the distance of a path is its reduced cost (also called an unconstrained resource) and
the constrained resources it needs is the time it takes to travel on it.
From [9] we found that an efficient way of solving this RSCP problem is by using a label setting
algorithm. An example of a very well-known shortest path algorithms that uses a label setting
approach is Dijkstra’s shortest path algorithm. In short, a label setting algorithm consists in
exploring the different ways of getting from origin to destination in a graph, with the objective of
finding the shortest way, by making use of labels. These labels store little information (to lighten
the computational memory and later accelerate the time) such as what node it has reached in
the graph and its label predecessor. This way when a label reaches the destination node, one
can backtrack using the labels’ predecessor until reaching the origin node and thus finding the
set of nodes, which result in the shortest distance from destination to origin. This is simply
reversed in order to get the shortest way from origin to destination.
Inspired by [16], we have chosen to implement an A-Star Label Setting algorithm in order to
solve our RSCP sub-problem (that is sub-problem in our MCF problem, see Figure 6). Our
implementation of the A-Star Label Setting algorithm is outlined in algorithm 1. The algo-
rithm 1 finds the shortest paths, i.e. the cheapest paths w.r.t. reduced cost, which have sufficient
resources, i.e. do not violate the corresponding commodity’s transit time constraint.

only once, namely before solving the first RMP - saving both computational time and memory.

that the optimal solution to the MCF will be using all of the paths in Ω. But, instead, that they
all were essential in the finding of the paths which will be used in the optimal solution.

the shortest possible time to get from commodity k’s source to sink node. Then it simply needs

the A-Star algorithm for that commodity and thus a waste of computational time and memory.
Therefore, we only look for new feasible paths for - i.e. run the A-Star Label Setting algorithm
on - the commodities in K′.
The A-Star Label Setting algorithm starts off by creating a label at the commodity’s source
node. A label consists of some information; a unique label ID, the node it has reached, the time
28
==PAGE:35==
it has taken to reach it, the temporary reduced cost associated with the label’s corresponding
incomplete path and, lastly, the label’s predecessor (in form of a label ID).
As with all label setting algorithms, we do not store the set of edges corresponding to the label’s
incomplete path, since this would take up too much computational memory and time. We will,
however, still need to store this incomplete path’s temporary reduced cost and transit times with
its associated label. Instead of storing the set of edges, storing the label’s predecessor’s label ID,
will , at the end, allow us to find the sequence of nodes found by this algorithm, which constitutes
the shortest path from origin to destination. This is clear to be less time consuming than storing
all edges corresponding to all incomplete paths (labels) met throughout this algorithm.
The first label created will thus contain the following information: ID 1, the source node, and
zeros for the remaining values - note that no label will have an ID of zero.
Once the first label is created, we insert its unique ID into the queue, Q. Additionally, we are
going to store this label in a set of labels called Lsourcek, which corresponds to the set of all labels
having node sourcek. Generally, Ln denotes the set of all the labels at node n. This set will
be useful for tracking all the labels in the different nodes and sort them in terms of temporary
reduced cost. This is done in the Dominance Check (algorithm 2), which is a part of the A-Star
Label Setting algorithm (algorithm 1) and will be explained in Section 6.3.1.
As we move on in the algorithm 1, we choose a label to extend from. This is done by finding
the label with the lowest temporary reduced cost in the queue. Choosing the one with lowest
temporary reduced cost will guarantee us to find the best feasible path. As mentioned earlier,
the path with lowest reduced cost (if it is strictly negative) will be chosen as the new feasible
path for its commodity, k, thus equal Ωk, which will be added to Ω. Given that πk is the same
for all paths in commodity k (as it is defined per commodity) and the relation shown in (11),
then we can conclude that choosing the label with the lowest temporary reduced cost guarantees
finding the path with lowest reduced cost. This is because we are running the algorithm per
commodity. In other words, we are searching for the best new feasible path for each commodity-
if such exists in the given network.
Note that when we have picked the label from the queue, this label is then removed from the
queue, such that we don’t pick it again.
Then, we test the potential of creating all the new labels using all the outgoing edges from the
current label’s node. We test these one by one. The testing is done by calculating the upper
bounds for the potentially new label’s incomplete path’s time and temporary reduced cost.
Indeed, for what regards the time metric, we add the time associated to the current label,
currentTime, with the time it takes to use the new outgoing edge, e, denoted by te. We also
add the shortest time it takes to get from e’s end node, i to the commodity’s sink node. This is
done by using the aforementioned R, to be more exact, the value Ri,sinkk. If
currentTime + te + Ri,sinkk ≤maxTTk,
(12)
with maxTTk denoting the maximum allowed transit time for the commodity, then we can
proceed with checking for reduced cost. Otherwise, we can already conclude that continuing
this incomplete path will always lead to an infeasible (complete) path (due to violation of the

6.3.1
Dominance Check
Checking for dominance is an additional step for discarding useless labels and thus increasing
computational speed (and lowering computational memory). This time, it is done by comparing
all the labels that are at the same node and evaluating if some are dominated by others. For
a label to dominate an other label, it needs to have strictly lower temporary reduced cost and
strictly lower transit time than the - thus dominated - label. This makes sense as the dominating
label, would thus suggest that there is a way of getting to node n (an incomplete path if
n ̸= sinkk, otherwise complete path) which is both strictly cheaper (w.r.t. temporary reduced
cost) and quicker (w.r.t. transit time) than the path the dominated label represents. Then it
would simply be inefficient and useless to keep the dominated label (corresponding to a slower
and more expensive path). On the other hand, if the path associated with a label has lower (or
equal) temporary reduced cost but greater (or equal) transit time than an other path (affiliated
with an other label), then neither dominate the other. As we cannot justify the removal of any
of those two labels, we keep them both. This is also why, we can have two labels with the same
temporary reduced cost and transit time in our set Ln.
Our implementation of the Dominance Check-part of the algorithm is outlined in algorithm 2.

Metaheuristics
Metaheuristics such as Simulated Annealing, Tabu Search, Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search
and many more have in the past decades shown themselves to be a very popular approach,
especially when it comes to very complex, large, real-life problems. In fact, they have proven
themselves efficient to solve the LSNDP (with and without transit time restrictions) [23] [4]
[3]. As the name suggests, these methods rely on heuristics and are not exact methods. In
fact, contrarily to exact methods (such as branch-and-bound for mixed integer problems for
example), metaheuristics do not strive for finding the optimal solution. Instead, they aim to
find a near-optimal solution, a ’very good’ solution.
When exact methods cannot reach the optimal solution of these complex, very large real-life
problems in a satisfactory time - if at all - the metaheuristic framework comes to the rescue.
This is for example the case with the Vehicle Routing Problem, where the metaheuristic called
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) was shown to a state-of-the-art approach for
solving the problem [22] [17].
The fact that one can get ’very good’ results relatively fast (compared to the exact methods,
which only look for the optimal solution) is what has attracted a lot of companies to implement
these methods. In fact, as with the LSNDP, we are more interested in getting good results fast,
rather than optimal results only available for very small instances (up to ten ports as mentioned
in [4]) or with slow time and big gaps.
Another aspect that is both intriguing but can also be perceived as cumbersome is the flexibility
associated with the metaheuristical approach. It does not put any restrictions on how to for-
mulate the objective functions or constraints but instead allows the designer to customize the
algorithm in order to accommodate the specifics of one’s problem. However, this also means
that to get good results, the design needs to be modelled very specifically to the problem at
hand.
For those reasons, metaheuristics have also been a very popular method for solving the LSNDP.
In fact the following papers [23] [4] [3] are examples of proof that it is a respected method for
solving the LSNDP (also including transit time restrictions).
We also found that metaheuristics were the best option for our study answering to making small
changes to a given network, with the objective of reducing forfeiture whilst - to a certain degree
- accepting a corresponding higher network cost. In fact, here, metaheuristics fell perfectly in
our scheme. It is a large, complex real-life optimization problem, with a goal that is not set in
stone. What is meant is that we wish to reduce forfeiture but somewhat accept a corresponding
higher network cost.
Furthermore, since another wish is to make small changes to the network in accordance with
the TCMCF’s feedback, the metaheuristical approach seems perfect. With such method, we can
design our framework and customize it such that it can adhere to these wishes. As a matter of
fact, we wanted to create a variety of ways to change the network depending on the TCMCF’s
feedback. That is why we picked an adaptive metaheuristics. This means that it learns (why
the name adaptive) throughout the procedure, which ways lead to better solutions.
More specifically, we chose to use the Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS). This one
has been shown to be a state-of-the-art approach for solving the Vehicle Routing Problem, but
even just generally a beloved method for large, complex real-life problems such as the LSNDP
[22].
The ALNS (from David Pisinger and Stefan Røpke in [17]) - as the name suggests - is an adap-
tive version of the Large Neighborhood Search (LNS). The LNS is also a metaheuristic that
has shown itself very useful in various transportation and scheduling problems [18] [22]. Large
34
==PAGE:41==
neighborhood search methods consist in having a set of heuristics which allow to explore large
neighborhoods. These heuristics are called respectively destroy and repair functions (always
containing some sort of stochastic element). The idea with the LNS is to gradually improve
the solution by destroying and repairing it. Every time a solution is accepted, the destroy and
repair moves are made on that new solution and so on until the algorithm is terminated due to
time and then outputs the best solution found.
Like most other metaheuristic, a solution is not only accepted if it has a better objective value.
Indeed, worse solutions can also be accepted and this is, in fact, a very important part of the
metaheuristic approach.
In metaheuristics an analogy of a hillclimber is frequently used. In our case, we are in a mini-
mization and not maximization problem and thus we will call it the valley descender. If we find
ourselves in a chain of mountains (and thus valleys) - where altitude represents the objective
value of the solution - then we wish to find the lowest valley of them all (the global minimum).
Our large neighborhood searches (simulated by our well-designed heuristics) manifest themselves
by how far the valley descender can see before choosing what direction to take (per iteration).
If we were not accepting any worse solutions, i.e. the descender was never climbing in order to
explore the chain of mountains (the solution space), then we would most probably never leave
the valley we started in and thus be stuck in the same local minimum.
In more heuristical terms, this means that we need to accept worse solutions from time to time
in order to explore the solution space more and, in turn, hopefully find a different and better
minimum.
Therefore, the ALNS makes use of the fellow metaheuristic’s, Simulated Annealing (SA), accep-
tance criteria. With the help of two parameters, the temperature, T, and the decay rate, α, we
start by accepting relatively worse solutions and slowly (dictated by the decay rate) decrease
the probability of accepting as worse solutions. Mathematically, the SA probability is defined
as
p = e−∆
T
with ∆denoting the difference in objective value between the current and the worse solution
and T the temperature parameter. As mentioned before, T is decreased for every iteration with
a factor of 0 < α < 1.
The presence of (potentially many) parameters play a big role in the aforementioned flexibility
associated with metaheuristics.
Given that the search for better solutions is not as systematic as exact methods, metaheuristics
rely on running many iterations. Due to that, they entail that the heuristics are computationally
quick - thus often very simple - steps, such that there is a possibility of running thousands of
iterations within hours or at most days (depending on the problem at hand).
The Adaptive version of the LNS, namely the ALNS, thus consists in defining multiple destroy
and repair functions, in order to create different and consequently varied neighborhood searches.
It adapts by scoring the heuristics w.r.t. performance and regularly reassess which heuristics
should be utilized more than others.
This spoke to us, as we wish to make a variety of small (and thus simple and quick) changes
to the network depending on the TCMCF’s feedback. To be more precise, we will scrutinize
the TCMCF’s feedback: where, why and how much of a commodity is forfeited and tweak the
network accordingly.
This tactic aligns with our objective of decreasing forfeiture, maybe even accepting a corre-
sponding increase in network cost. Therefore, we chose to use the ALNS framework to host our
service-first flow-second [8] approach.
We will now outline the setup of our metaheuristic and how it is a variation of a classical ALNS.

7.1
Feedback from TCMCF to Network Design
One of the main goals with this thesis was to use feedback from the TCMCF and thus tweak
the network accordingly. Given our simplified version of the LSNDP, we divided the reasons for
forfeiture into three categories:
• Ports: the origin and/or destination port are/is missing from the network. Clearly, this
makes it impossible for the commodity to be fulfilled but instead entails full forfeiture.
• Transit Time: the transit time is too low, given the rotations, there is no way to get from
origin to destination satisfying the current transit time restriction.
• Capacity: with the current rotations, the vessels cannot carry the necessary containers in
order to fulfill the commodities’ demand.
7.1.1
Reason for Forfeiture
These reasons for forfeiture will shed a light on what kind of transformation the network would
need to undergo, in order to lower the forfeiture of a given commodity.
Although we have
separated these reasons into three exclusive categories, it is worth mentioning that more than
one of these reasons could be behind the forfeiture of a commodity. To be more precise, one
could picture a commodity being (fully or partially) forfeited due to both the transit time but
also the capacities being too low. Nevertheless, for simplicity, we stick with these three exclusive
reasons. Note that the issue that could be met with this assumption, is that we try and solve
a forfeited commodity by using a Transit Time heuristic, when in fact, it could have been more
valuable to use a Capacity heuristic instead.
Ports
Ports missing is the easiest of the three reasons to detect. Here, we simply check if the origin
and destination ports are present in the current network. If one of them (or both) are not, then
we can safely state it to be the reason for the commodity’s full forfeiture.
Note here that if the ports are missing, neither of the other two options can be included in the
reason for forfeiture, considering that there is - given the current network and thus rotations
- no feasible non-forfeiture path from origin to destination and consequently no way of telling
whether the capacity or the transit time is too low.
Transit Time
The transit time is the next forfeiture reason that is inspected. As mentioned in Section 6, given
the current network under examination, the shortest paths for each commodity are computed
just before the TCMCF using a Dijkstra shortest path algorithm.
As explained in Section 6, the graph on which we run the Dijkstra algorithm is the network
without any forfeited edges. When computing the shortest distances from origin to destination,
we can quickly realize whether such a path complies to the transit time. If it cannot - i.e. the
shortest distance takes longer to travel than allowed by the transit time restriction -, then we
know that given the network’s rotations, there is no path that can adhere to the transit time.
Capacity
Since we only have three different reasons for forfeiture, if the two previous reasons were not the
source for a given commodity’s forfeiture, then we assign that commodity the capacity reason.
One of the reasons why we do not further investigate whether the capacity is an issue when the
transit time was shown to be a cause for forfeiture, is because it is quite strenuous. Indeed,
solving the transit time issue does not entail that there is also enough capacity available for the
paths in question. However, we cannot, with certainty, say which edges should increase their
36
==PAGE:43==
capacity, in order to transport the forfeited commodity, before we have solved the transit time
issue.
If a commodity is using a forfeited path, this implies that there was no possible way of getting
the forfeited amount of containers from the origin to destination. This is due to the high penalty
associated with forfeiting cargo that we have implied as discussed in Section 3. However, there
is still a theoretical possibility that the commodities are being forfeited due to the cost of
shipping them being too high. Being that we have raised the penalty substantially, in practice
the possibility is in essence zero, but more importantly if it were to be the case, then the cost
for sending this cargo might just not be worth paying. This is a constant trade-off that is met
in these kinds of problems: fulfilling demand versus the cost of fulfilling those demands.
Given that there are so many different paths, which include different rotations, it is almost
impossible to say on what edges (and thus rotations) the capacity problem lies.
Since we only have three different reasons for forfeiture, if the two previous reasons were not the
source for a given commodity’s forfeiture, then we assign that commodity the capacity reason.

7.2
Destroy & Repair Functions
Depending on the reason for forfeiture, we will be having a series of destroy/repair functions -
also called heuristics - to choose from.
The program’s general strategy consists in focusing on one forfeited commodity at a time and
tweak the network accordingly. We have, therefore, set up the priority list that way, as explained
in Section 7.1.2. This tactic is a little different to the one affiliated to the typical Adaptive Large
Neighborhood Search (ALNS) [17]. Usually the algorithm figures out where and how it makes
sense to destroy and repair the solution. Our variation of the ALNS consists in running one
iteration of an LNS inside the adaptive metaheuristic and score this destroy/repair pair according
to its performance. This is different to the original ALNS, as this algorithm scores the destroys
and repairs separately and mixes and matches.
We will further explain the functioning of our algorithm in Section 7.3. Indeed, the original
ALNS will, in a nutshell, try and escape from the local minimum by making relatively random
changes and with time (iterations are meant here) have more and more insight to make more
educated guesses. In our variation, the flow from random to more educated changes is kept.
However, we have framed the search by constraining what functions to choose depending on the
forfeiture reason. This is a consequence to the simplification of the forfeiture reasons.
We have deemed it necessary to pair up destroy and repair functions, given the strategy we are
leading with and of how the program is implemented.
During the development and implementation of our heuristics the focus has been on simplicity
and speed since we want the heuristics to be utilized multiple times during the program and
to follow our objective of making small changes to the initial network. This can be seen in the
rather simple but also effective heuristics that we have developed. In total, we have developed
ten different heuristics; the five first (RandomAddPort, ClosestAddPort, SmallestRotAddPort,
RandomReplace and ClosestReplace) are tailored to when the reason for forfeiture is ’Ports’,
the next three (RandomSpeedIncrease, AnalysisSpeedIncrease and RemoveRandomPortSpeedIn-
crease) are connected to ’Transit Time’, and the last two (SwitchRandomFleets and SwitchFleets)
are there to help when ’Capacity’ is given as the reason for forfeiture. All the heuristics have
some stochastic elements associated with them. The stochastic elements give the network the
flexibility to create new networks that differ from previous iterations. This way a heuristic can
create larger neighborhoods and thus explore the solution space further.
7.2.1
Destroy/Repair Functions against Missing Ports
If the reason for forfeiture is given as ’Ports’ - meaning that the port is not detectable in the
network - we wish to insert it.
For that, we have five different heuristics, which one could
further divide into two groups. The first group includes RandomAddPort, ClosestAddPort, and
SmallestRotAddPort. This group all share the same fundamental logic, that is to simply add a
new port into a current rotation. They differ in the way they choose which rotation to insert
the new port into, which we will specify for each heuristic.
The second group includes the rest, that is RandomReplace and ClosestReplace.
Instead of
targeting a rotation for which to insert the new port, these heuristics target all the ports within
a (given) radius of the new port. From here an "old" port is selected and replaced with the new
port. These heuristics are particularly useful when we want to insert a new port, but cannot
because there aren’t enough vessels available, for that rotation, to cover the potential extended
38
==PAGE:45==
distance. In that case, replacing ports instead of only adding a new port will shorten the new
rotation distance and, thus, less vessels may be needed. The *Replace heuristics add additional
flexibility to the program. The disadvantage is that we might remove a more rewarding ports
from the rotation compared to the new port which we insert.
In order to measure if a new port can be inserted/replaced into a new rotation, we calculate the
maximum distance, max distance, that the current rotation can cover when the vessel’s speed
is increased to maximum and we utilize all available vessels (vessels already assigned for that
rotation and potentially spare vessels we have of that class). If the new potential distance for
inserting/replacing the new port is below the new calculated max distance, we can safely say
that the new port will not invalidate the rotation if inserted. Note that when we adjust the
rotations, we want to use the smallest amount of available vessels within the given speed limits,
such that more vessels are freed up for other rotations.
The distance used for measuring if a new port can be inserted is always set to the smallest
available, i.e. if a distance using a canal is smaller, then we select that distance and update the
rotations with the corresponding canal cost.
All of the heuristics have a list of steps they can take. For each step, checks are conducted in
order to assess whether this step will result in a feasible solution or not. If it does not, the next
step on the list is tested out (through these checks). If the list is exhausted, the heuristic could
not fulfill its task given the parameters and current network and will instead notify the algorithm
that it failed. When the ALNS is told that the heuristic failed its task, the new solution will,
evidently, be the exact same solution as the one before the use of the heuristic. Thus, instead of
assessing whether this new (but exact same) solution is accepted or best, we move directly on
to the next forfeited commodity in the priority list (as explained in Section 7.1.2).
RandomAddPort
The RandomAddPort heuristic does indeed what the name suggests. Firstly a list of suitable
places to insert the port is generated. The list is created via running through all of the port
pairs for all of the rotations, generating a line between these port-pairs and if that line is within
a given radius from the new ports placement, it is added to the list. This radius is dependent
on a parameter called rotDist.
After the list has been created, we simply draw a random index from the list, corresponding to
which rotation (and between which ports) we wish to insert the new port in. If this insertion of
the port in this position passes the max distance check it will be added between those two ports
in that rotation. If it doesn’t we pick an other index from the list of considerable rotations (and
more specifically positions). If we exhaust the list, we output that no changes could be made
in order to add this port, given the parameter rotDist and the current network. In this case,
we move on with the algorithm, choosing the next forfeited commodity to tend to (as explained
earlier in this section and in Section 7.1.2).
The advantage of this function is entirely in the stochastic element. We allow the program to
insert a new port in a random rotation and thus the heuristic creates a neighborhood to search
in.
ClosestAddPort
The logic of the ClosestAddPort heuristic uses a lot of the same functionality as RandomAddPort.
However, they differ in the way they choose what rotation to add the port in.
Where the
RandomAddPort-function chooses the rotation at random, the ClosestAddPort simply selects
the one port-pair which is the closest to the new port. We remind that the list of considered
rotations to be added to is identical to the one computed for RandomAddPort.
Selecting the closest distance is a way for the new port to disrupt the rotation the least, given
that the added distance the rotation must traverse is kept to a minimum. The closest rotation
is chosen using the Soft Max function (16) as was done with the priority list (this time in
39
==PAGE:46==
reverse, since the shorter the distance, the better). This way we keep a stochastic element to
our heuristic. If it cannot add it in that rotation, it chooses an other position from the list and
so on until it either has exhausted the list or has successfully added the new port somewhere in
the network.
SmallestRotAddPort
The initial process for the SmallestRotAddPort is again similar to the two previous functions.
However, in this function we simply sort the list of possible rotations by their size, that is how
many ports are a part of the rotation.
We reason that such a heuristic levels the playing field, by opting for the program to favor
building smaller rotations up instead of making big rotations even bigger. And generally it
offers an other neighborhood to the search in the solution space.
RandomReplace
The RandomReplace heuristic does again exactly what the name suggests. Firstly we find a
suitable list of all the other ports which lie inside a considered distance radius. This radius is
dependent on the parameter portDist. The procedure is the same as the list created for the
closest rotations, here, it is just with one port.
A feasibility check is carried out, ensuring that the rotation associated with the potential port
remains feasible, given the availability of vessels and their speed limits. From the list we ran-
domly choose which port we want to replace with a new port.
The reason behind such a function lies in the flexibility that it adds to the program, in terms
of a new neighborhood search. Again opting the program to further explore the solution space.
Generally, heuristics replacing ports define different neighborhoods to those that simply insert
them.
We note that if a butterfly port is set to be replaced, the new inserted port will become a
butterfly port too.
ClosestReplace
This function is very similar to the RandomReplace function. The difference is that it will choose
the closest rotation - in the same way as is done in ClosestAddPort, using the Soft Max function
(16). And such as RandomReplace, it will look to replace the new port with the chosen port
from the list.
We note that if a butterfly port is set to be replaced, the new inserted port will become a
butterfly port too.
7.2.2
Destroy/Repair Functions against Low Transit Time
As mentioned earlier we also created heuristics which are in place for dealing with transit time
restrictions. Once the reason for forfeiture has been identified as ’transit time’, we have identified
two ways of solving such an issue. Either we can alter the transit time limitations, such that
we allow a bigger transit time, or we can alter the rotations in such a way that the speed of the
rotation is increased. The latter will potentially allow the paths from origin to destination to
have a faster duration.
The three heuristics which we have developed for this reason of forfeiture all deal with increasing
the speed of a rotation. Again, some functionalities are shared between the different heuristics.
A rotation’s speed is only increased if it is deemed possible to increase the speed enough, such
that it meets transit time restrictions for the given commodity, without exceeding the vessel’s
maximum speed. If that is the case we increase the speed to a minimum of what is needed, but
with chance of choosing an even higher speed, though not higher than the vessel’s maximum
speed. This will add an additional stochastic element to all of these heuristics. If it is not the
case, as described earlier, the checks are carried out for the next rotation to be considered, until
the list is exhausted or that a rotation’s speed has been changed.
40
==PAGE:47==
RandomSpeedIncrease
The first heuristic RandomSpeedIncrease generates a list of all the rotations, shuffles the list,
thus introducing the stochastic element to the heuristic. It goes through all of the rotations in
the list. If, as mentioned before, it is possible to increase the speed such that the demand of the
commodity in question can flow through, we then increase the speed to at least the minimum of
what is needed. In fact, the new speed is chosen randomly in the feasible interval now defined,
as explained earlier in this section.
Again, such a function introduces an element of randomness, both due to the shuffling in the
list and the stochastically chosen new speed. This is helpful to potentially escape local minima
and explore a broader solution space.
AnalysisSpeedIncrease
The heuristic AnalysisSpeedIncrease differs from RandomSpeedIncrease in the way they create
the list of rotations. While RandomSpeedIncrease included all of the rotations and chose one at
random, AnalysisSpeedIncrease only selects the rotations which are featured on the shortest path
for the commodity in question. We remind that since we are in a transit time caused forfeited
commodity, the shortest path of that commodity takes longer than the maximum transit time
allowed. The list of rotations is then sorted based on the distances the shortest path travels
on. In other words, the rotation which the shortest path travels the most on (distance) will
thus be ranked highest on the list. With such a heuristic we are more inclined to finding a
rotation computationally faster for which we can increase the speed, although generating the
list is computationally slower than shuffling the - already known - list of rotations.
RemoveRandomPortSpeedIncrease
The final heuristic RemoveRandomPortSpeedIncrease differs in many ways from the two previous
ones. Whilst the criteria for accepting a speed increase remain the same and the rotation list
is built in the same ways as AnalysisSpeedIncrease, we here instead consider the possibility of
deleting a port from the rotation. The potential port is found at random once a rotation is
selected, if the removal of the port with the speed increase can pass the duration check, we both
remove the port from the rotation and consequently increase the speed of that rotation in the
same manner as before. If it does not pass this check, we pick a new rotation (and a random
port from that rotation).
This is the only heuristic where we delete a port from the rotation, without replacing it with
another port. This adds to the flexibility of the program, because we here can shorten some
of the rotations and, thus, potentially change the shape of the rotations in a different manner,
especially combined with the port inserting heuristics.
The penalty of this heuristic comes in form of the increased complexity which adds expensive
running time.
7.2.3
Destroy/Repair Functions against Low Capacity
Lastly we have heuristics in place for when the capacity limit is the reason for forfeiture. The
two heuristics SwitchRandomFleets and SwitchFleets both build on the same core logic for de-
termining if it is possible for two rotations to switch their vessel class. The heuristics attempt
to switch the vessel classes between two distinct rotations. The lists are thus similar to the ones
for transit time heuristics: lists of rotation IDs.
We check that the new vessel class’ draft can fit into all the ports of a rotation. Additionally, we
check if there are enough vessels to cover the new duration Lastly, we check that the rotation
can still function given a speed between the new vessel’s mininum and maximum speed. The
above checks are carried out for both vessel classes and rotations involved in the switch. With
these heuristics we hope to utilize the available vessel class better - both in terms of allowing
more containers to be sent through and if vessels can be spared for later use.
41
==PAGE:48==
SwitchRandomFleets
For the SwitchRandomFleets we, given a rotation, randomly go through all of the other rota-
tions and choose the first rotation, such that the switch between the two rotations’ vessels is
compatible. Again, we introduce additional stochastic elements, which adds the same benefits
as noted before.
SwitchFleets
Where SwitchRandomFleets chooses another rotation randomly, we here create a sorted list of
rotations to choose from. The metric for sorting the list is the utilization of flow on the rotation
versus how much capacity the rotation actually uses. In other words, the sorting is based on
how efficient the rotations are given their vessel class. This is a heuristic that looks to fully
optimize the usage of each vessel.

7.3.1

large neighborhoods. The neighborhood is implicitly defined by the destroy and repair functions.
Usually, the destroy methods are very stochastic and with a significant degree of destruction,

we wish to run many iterations of the metaheuristic.

7.3.2
Termination Criteria
The termination criteria for algorithm 5, are based on the number of iterations run, how many
times we have met the same best solution and the priority list. To be more exact, if the maximum
total number of iterations or maximum times to stay on the same best solution are reached, maxIt
and maxSameBest, the termination criterion is met and, consequently, the algorithm stops and
outputs its last best found solution. The last way, the termination criterion is met is if the
priority list is empty (remember that every time a priority is chosen from the priority list, it is
withdrawn from it too).
In our case, we have instances of very different sizes and have thus chosen the maximum total
number of iterations depending to the instance. For the number of times we keep the same
solution as best, we drew inspiration from [19]. Here, they choose this number to be a hundredth
of maxIt.
7.3.3
Acceptance Criteria
The acceptance criteria - just like the termination criteria - are based on what is used in the vast
majority of the most used metaheuristics nowadays. This consists of the Simulated Annealing
(SA) acceptance criteria.
In short, they take the difference in objective value between our
new and previous solution, and calculate a probability accordingly (along with the temperature
parameter). If the new solution is better, i.e. its objective value is strictly lower (since we are in
a minimization problem), this solution is instantaneously accepted; otherwise, it is chosen with
the SA probability, defined as
p = e−∆
T ,
(17)
with ∆denoting the difference in objective value between the new and the previous solution
(∆> 0, since the new solution is worse and consequently has a lower objective value than the
previous solution) and T denoting the temperature parameter.
With ∆being strictly positive (since we only need to compute (17) when the new solution is
worse than the previous) and T strictly positive, we have a strictly negative exponent yielding
a strictly positive probability. Additionally, as T is going towards zero, p is also going towards
zero (for a fixed ∆). In other words, the lower the temperature, the lower the probability of
accepting a worse solution (for a fixed difference in objective value).
Similarly, the worse network the new solution yields (i.e. the bigger the ∆), the lower the
probability of accepting that solution. This is self-explanatory.
The idea with choosing a worse solution was described in the start of Section 7. The idea is
to accept worse solutions (especially in the start) in order to explore the solution space further
and hopefully find a better minimum. Since the heuristics are simple (they all do unornamented
moves) and computationally quick, we are not changing the network a lot per iteration. However,
one can imagine there needing a certain level of disruption in order to experience a noticeable
change in the solution - escaping local minimum -, especially when starting with good initial
solutions as is done in this thesis. This is why some worse solutions need be accepted, such that
the network can undergo bigger changes.

The higher the α (0 < α < 1), the less the temperature degrades over time and thus still accepts

cost. This is due to the idea that we wish to focus on leaving the local minimum in the start
of the run; afterwards, we focus the attention on getting to the minimum in the valley we are
situated in.
In other words, at the end of the algorithm, T will have decreased and thus the probability of
accepting worse solutions will too. This will sensitize the algorithm at the end to focus (more)
on reaching its local minimum, whereas the start allows more search of new valleys.

Tuning process
As one might have noticed, there are many parameters involved in the implementation of this
ALNS. The general idea with parameters is that they have an influence on the performance of the
algorithm (otherwise they would be fixed). Optimally, we would be tuning all these parameters
to try and reach the optimal model for each instance. Indeed, usually one has many data sets
that look alike and can thus divide these into tuning and testing sets. The tuning sets will be
used to find the better combination of parameter values, that will then be used to be tested on
the testing data sets to gain the final results of one’s model’s performance.
There is a big and important variation in optimal parameter values across problems and even
instances. Not only are the right values important for getting the best results possible, but some
parameter values have a direct impact on the algorithm’s procedure, e.g. rewarding accepting
worse solutions more than accepting a solution based on its better objective value, or to what
degree we accept a worse solution to name a few. Seeing that conducting exhausting tuning would
entail tuning all the parameters - which will here be outlined - this would linger substantially.
Given our limited time, we had to prioritize the tuning of some parameters and fix the rest with
the help of literature (especially [17], [22], [18], [19] and [20]), logic and experience.

8.2.4
Forfeiture Penalty
Another parameter that is central to our algorithm, but that doesn’t appear as explicitly, is the
penalty for forfeiting commodities. Indeed, in the objective function of our TCMCF (5), we
subtract the revenue to the cost for each path used (multiplied by the number of containers sent
on that path). There are two types of paths: the forfeited paths and the non-forfeited ones.
When we are sending containers from their origin to their destination port through the network’s
rotations, we use non-forfeited paths. A cost is attached to such a path. This consists in all the
running costs which are met through that journey on the different vessels and dependent on the
number of containers sent on that path. The revenue is also container based and is, evidently,
commodity-related. The forfeited paths, on the other hand, represent when there are no feasible
way for some containers to be sent from origin to destination and, consequently, are forfeited.
Unmistakably, the revenue associated with a forfeited path is non-existent, since the client have
not received those demanded containers. Analogously, the cost of not sending these containers
will not have any running costs. Nevertheless, we wish to penalize forfeiting cargo, as we and
Maersk are interested in trying to minimise the forfeiture of containers.
This leads us to our penalty parameter. In [2], they use a penalty of 1, 000 US dollars per
forfeited container. We chose to amplify the former penalty with a factor 45. Not only will
this push the MCF to send as many containers through as possible (unless the cost minus
the revenue amounts to more than 45, 000 US dollars, which is hard to believe it ever will),
but it will, in turn, also raise the total price of the flow (compared to the network) and thus
dominate in the total cost of a solution. This is all in accordance with our primary desire of
satisfying as much demand as possible. The penalty of 45, 000$ has been found suitable to our
objective in the preliminary testing of our program. Indeed if one is to look at the Baltic region
with 22 commodities and a total demand (commodity multiplied demand) of 4, 904 containers,
multiplying the total containers with the penalty results in 4, 904·45, 000$ = 220, 680, 000.00$ or
2.21 · 108. The corresponding solution, as taking from the LinerLib, for that region is 3.05 · 106.
Thus at even 10% forfeiture the penalty of forfeiting would still dominate the objective.
8.2.5
Scoring
When we are assigning the weights of the function pairs, we are making use of two sets of
parameters. γ, the reaction parameter, controls how much the weights react to the function
pair’s performance in the segment (see Section 7.3.5). If γ is approaching zero, the weights will
not change that much according to their averaged success in the segment. In fact, seeing that the
weights all start equal (since we have no prior knowledge on the function pairs’ performances),
if γ approaches zero, the weights will only slightly change after each segment, meaning that we
will stay fairly close to equal weights. If, on the other hand, γ approaches one, then the opposite
occurs. That is, we will be weighing the function pairs’ success in the segment very strongly
and detach more from its performances across past segments. If γ = 0.5, one could see it as
weighing the past performances equally to the current performance (in this segment).
We drew inspiration from [18] and chose γ = 0.1. This is in accordance with our opinion of not
letting the weights fluctuate too much (γ close to one) but instead keeping them close to equal
in the initial segments.
There is also the question of how many iterations a segment includes. In the same paper, their
segments consist of 100 iterations. Given that some of our instances (World Small) are massive
49
==PAGE:56==
and we, consequently, cannot run thousands of iterations, but only hundreds, we have chosen to
decrease the number of iterations in a segment to ten instead of 100. Given that we only have
ten destroy/repair function pairs and at most five per reason for forfeiture, we are aware that
this success mean per segment is done over a very small set of scores. The smaller the segment,
the more often we update the weights (and in consequence the probabilities). This means that
the individual performances (a function pair’s performance in one iteration) will most probably
have more impact on the overall sum, compared to when there are many more iterations in one
segment.
The next crucial parameter, for what concerns the scoring and choosing the destroy/repair
function pairs is, evidently, the scores. As explained and can be seen in Table 9, the performance
of a function pair is divided into four categories and scored with the according score. All the
scores are distinct. After browsing through recent literature, we have met different kinds of
scoring. The difference lies in the δi values. One big difference lies in the relation δ1 > δ2 > δ3 >
δ4. This seems logical: a function pair yielding a best solution yet gets a better score than if it
yields a "just" better solution, which in turn is better scored than when a solution is accepted
although it has a worse objective value; lastly, the worse solution - which is not accepted - is
graded the worst. Now, there is another popular kind of scoring dictionary, namely where the
relation goes δ1 > δ3 > δ2 > δ4. This one might seem counter-intuitive, but also makes sense.
As a matter of fact, here the function pair yielding a worse solution, which is accepted gets a
greater score than the function yielding a solution, which is accepted due to it having a better
objective value. Here, we remind that a worse solution is accepted as a function of the difference
in objective value (17), i.e. for a given temperature, the probability of choosing a worse solution
decreases with the increase of the worse solution’s objective value (we are in a minimization
problem and a high objective value is thus worse than a low objective value). This will reward
the function pairs which help navigate the search space better and try and escape the local
minima.
We have gotten inspired by this same paper [18] (and other papers) and have created four
different scoring dictionaries. Their scoring dictionary looked as following
δ1 = 33
δ2 = 9
δ3 = 13
δ4 = 0.
This kind of dictionary - as previously introduced - rewards accepting worse solution more than
accepting better solutions.
As mentioned before, this paper has segments of 100 iterations.
Being that we have lowered it to consisting of ten iterations instead, we chose to scale them
accordingly, dividing the scores by ten (just like the number of iterations). We wanted to keep
integers and thus came up with the scoring dictionary
δ1 = 4
δ2 = 1
δ3 = 2
δ4 = 0.
We also wanted some scoring, with more polarized scores (as well as rewarding with respect to
their objective value). Gaining inspiration from other papers and especially our courses at DTU
we came up with the following scoring dictionary

relation to how we calculate our cost.

10.2
Forfeiture
For each variant of the regions we manage to reduced the amount of forfeited cargo. For the
larger regions, i.e. Pacific and World Small, the average improvement in forfeiture is 24.06%
(Low) and 29.12% (Medium) for pacific and 3.28% (High) and 8.53% (Low) for world small.
For almost all of the other regions, we have a forfeiture percentage above 40%. This observ-
able difference could be due to the fact, that the larger instances have more rotations and our
heuristics will thus not have the same degree of destruction compared to on the smaller regions.
Indeed, as the heuristics are not set to destroy (and repair) more depending on the instance sizes,
the larger instances will suffer a smaller destruction degree. This would lead to the heuristics
creating smaller neighborhoods searches as the instances grow in size. Thus yielding a more
restricted exploration of the solution space.

This hypothesis seems to be in accordance of our results, as the forfeiture difference seems to be
decreasing as the instance size grows.
10.3
Segments
It Section 8.2.5 we discussed choice of how many iterations a segment should consist of. In
this thesis we chose a segment to equal ten iterations. This was deemed necessary given the
limitations on how many iterations we could run the World Large region. This however limited
the smaller regions, as in the same time they could reach over ten of thousand iterations. The
small regions could have benefit in a larger segment size, as the scoring for each functions would
be more balanced.
This does not necessarily mean that it will cause a worse solution, simply a slightly different
yield of this scoring method (Section 7.3.5) as the point of it is to compute the average, given
that the heuristics have been chosen many times, such that the average is more reliable.
Here we note that originally, the probability was computed after each iteration, meaning that
the heuristic in question was scored immediately, given its performance [17]. This shows that
it is not ’wrong’ to score in a different manner, which follows the customization of the general
metaheuristical approaches. Our way of scoring, meaning setting the segments to equal only
ten iterations, is simply a method finding itself between the original and the newest scoring
methods.
Lastly, we note that given that the reaction parameter is so low (γ = 0.1) that the average of
their performances, will no matter what, not have a big influence on their new weights.
10.4
Results
For all the best solutions gathered from the different regions variations, we see an average speed
increase throughout their networks. It is likely that the speed increases is a substantial factor
for improving the networks.
For the bigger regions we are prone to not utilize the maximum number of vessels available per
class, which is unfortunate as there could be a potential for greater solutions which the using
the unused vessels. Unfortunately, the program is not suitable for creating its own rotations and
thus, we are limited to make due with the number of rotations given in the initial solution.
10.5
General LSNDP
The LSNDP is known to be a complex problem, as it doesn’t only comprehend many different
elements (a rotation consists of ports and a vessel class, which have adjustable speeds) but
also different problems (the MCF with transit time and the Network Design Problem). Being
a realistic problem - contrarily to the more theoretical problems - many rules and constraints
can be added.
Thus, simplification is needed.
This simplification lies in concentrating on
the most important constraints, namely the demand fulfillment, the transit time compliance
and the capacity respect.
We, for example, turned away from the cabotage rules.
This is,
evidently, directly linked to the reasons for forfeiture; if more constraints are added, there will
be more possible reasons for forfeiture. Here, we note that if many more rules were included
in the problem, we would probably leave the priority list/reason for forfeiture tactic, since it
would be too complicated to locate, where the problem lies. However, this simplification of the
problem is only a limitation in solving the whole realistic problem, otherwise it is just scaling
the problem, such that it can be solved (and which would result in significant results for the
receiving company).

Data

10.6.1
Bunker Fuel Price
The cost of bunker fuel, as is calculated right now, does not factor in the container-load. However,
it would seem to make sense that a watercraft (such as other vehicles) uses more fuel if it is
carrying a heavier load. Indeed, as the vessel is heavier, the draft of the ship increases and should
therefore use more fuel. The draft of a vessel is defined as "the distance between the waterline
and the deepest point of the boat" 5. However, this is not taken into account in LinerLib - as
mentioned in [2] -, and consequently neither in our implementation when computing the bunker
fuel consumption. This will, in turn, make loading more cargo on the vessel cheaper than what
it in reality is. We could, thus, envisage our program pushing on loading the vessel as much
as possible. One could then further argue, that this simplification, or limitation, might even
be for the best. Although, it is not representing as accurate prices, it pushes the program to
use the vessel classes at our disposal to its fullest, which would be a logical tactic. In fact, it
would seem mindless - also with an environmental perspective - to use more vessels, purely due
to bunker fuel costs. Yes, it would entail using more fuel, which is not environmentally friendly,
but compared to employing a whole new additional vessel, it should be more considerate.
10.6.2
Instances
As mentioned in Section 3, we have access to seven different regions, which lead to dissimilar
instances also in terms of size. Each region holds three instances (varying the TC rates as well
as vessel quantity). This sums up to 7 · 3 = 21 different datasets that, on paper, can be used.
This is not a great number of datasets, especially seeing that we cannot use some regions as
tuning and testing on others, being their dissimilarity. In consequence, we have to tune and test
with a total of three datasets available per region.
The two biggest of these seven instances, Europe Asia and World Large, were simply too massive
for our algorithms to handle and we were then left with 5 · 3 = 15 instances to work with in
total. This is very few.

10.7.3
Rotation Structures
One of the ideas behind the butterfly nodes is supposedly to be an intermittent load-holding
spot in the rotation for the vessels. In other words, the vessel can leave some cargo on the
port, resume its expedition and load off of some containers by delivering demand to the next
ports, before returning to the butterfly node and pick up the left cargo (now that the vessel has
space for it again). Unfortunately, since our program exclusively follows an origin-to-destination
mindset (in terms of transporting cargo), we did not embrace the possibility of temporarily
leaving containers in the butterfly nodes in order to allow more containers travelling on that
rotation. The same idea goes for the complex structure, simply to a bigger degree.
Our program does handle butterfly nodes, although they do lose some of their purpose with this
implementation. However, since our program designs networks (by continuously tweaking on
already drawn networks), the inclusion of butterfly nodes doesn’t necessarily stop at handling
them, but could also comprehend creating them.
Heuristics
Indeed, our program can read a network with butterfly nodes - which was useful since some of the
Liner Lib initial solutions were composed of those. However, it cannot create new butterfly nodes.
Seeing that we have two heuristics, which replace two ports (Section 7.2.1), these can replace the
ports and maintain the structure. In fact, the replace functions remove a chosen port from one
of its rotations and inserts the missing port instead of the original port (leading to the exclusion
of the original port in the network). This way, if our initial solution contains m butterfly nodes,
then our final solution will unequivocally/undeniably contain at most m butterfly nodes (it can
contain less if some of those special nodes were deleted by RemoveRandomPortSpeedIncrease).
In short, this means that we can only have butterfly nodes in the rotations, which included them
to start with (the initial solution).
The same goes for complex structures.
In actual fact, we do not have any function, that creates new rotations. This is a perceptible
limit to our program. Although we have five port heuristics, that insert at most two ports to
the network’s rotations and one transit time function pair, that removes a port, we have no
function pair that creates new rotations. This implies that if we start with a network with n
rotations, our best solution will assuredly also only contain n rotations. This is due to the fact
that our RemoveRandomPortSpeedIncrease function never deletes rotations but instead fails to
accomplish its task and the algorithm continues accordingly.

Indeed, if the rotation it has to delete a port from only connects two distinct ports, it doesn’t,
but instead outputs that it failed to fulfill its duty. One could have implemented port inserting
functions, which split existing rotations - as long as those rotations have at least three ports
- into two distinct rotations (connected through a transshipment port), where the new port is
added to one of these two rotations. One could also implement a function, which simply creates
a new two-ports-rotation, which would most probably be colonized with the use of SmallestRo-
tAddPort or the other port insertion functions. More about these in Section 12. Not creating
new rotations limits the easiness of changing the network drastically and thus attempt to leave
the local minimum too.
In actual fact, generally, our functions don’t make big changes per iteration. They only look
at one rotation at a time. Compared to the usual destroy functions out there, which typically
destroy e.g. 15% [17] of the solution, changing an aspect of one rotation is not as significant for
big instance such as World Small, that contain over 30 rotations, which would make 3%. Here,
we do note that the port insertion heuristics, can add up to two ports per use. This gives an
upper bound on the destruction degree for the World Small instances of around 6%. However,
for the smallest instance (Baltic), these changes total to destroying over 30% of the solution (or
even 60%) and we notice better results for the smaller instances that, in consequence, experience
higher degree of destruction. That said, we are more interested in the larger instances and this
does in consequence become an important limitation.
Although we don’t destroy the solutions as much as usually done, we also rely on RemoveRan-
domPortSpeedIncrease to help open up the network. The latter is a heuristic which is assigned
to solve the low transit time problems. However, it will also help further open up the network,
for the heuristics designated to insert port(s). This further demonstrates the complexity of the
problem - even the simplified version; there are many aspects in a rotation (ports, vessel classes,
which in turn have capacities and speeds assigned) and thus many ways of destroying and re-
pairing.
10.7.4
Customizing the ALNS
As mentioned many times throughout this thesis, we have chosen to slightly alter the ALNS
algorithm, such that it stays an adaptive version of the LNS, but it is manifested differently.
Given that nobody - that we could find - has modified the well-known ALNS this way, limitations
are bound to be met.
As explained in Section 7.3, we adapted the ALNS to our problem. Our objective was to revise
the network according to the - updated - feedback from the flow. Additionally, we wished to
generally decrease forfeiture by making smaller changes to a given network.
This led us to
categorize each forfeiture and modify the network by tending at the forfeited commodities one
at a time. This differs from the usual way the ALNS operates on the other problems. Generally,
the ALNS runs for many iterations with the purpose of starting with more drastic changes to
the solution and in hopes of finishing with a much better solution due to the destroy functions’
high degree of destruction combined with the efficient and smart repair functions. The usual
ALNS relies a lot on the many iterations. Since, we are attending the commodities one by one
and trying to solve their forfeiture reason immediately, one could say that we are expecting the
network to become better - in terms of that commodity at least - instantaneously. This said, we
also rely on the many iterations, for the algorithm to correct the forfeitures the previous changes
have caused. This meant, nevertheless, that our heuristics would not necessarily have as wide
of a target on the solution as they usually do.

10.7.5
Tuning
Lastly, we repeat (from Section 8) the limitations of our tuning. These are all caused by our
limited period. Optimally, we would have tuned the grand majority of the parameters with an
abundant tuning and testing data set and multiple runs and take the average. Instead, we had
to fix the majority of the parameter values, stick with the one tuning instance and only run the
tuning once.
Fixing a value to parameters without tuning is dangerous, as the literature behind the inspiration
of the values is not tackling the same problem and especially not in the same way. Otherwise,
the parameters are fixed from educated guesses or wishes for how the algorithm should work,
e.g. increasing the forfeiture penalty enormously in order to push the program to forfeit as
few commodities as possible. This will clearly add to the list of assumptions and thus pull the
algorithms in assumed directions. One could perceive each parameter as dragging the algorithm’s
mechanism towards a direction. Since many haven’t been tuned, we have assumed to know (or
prioritized) what directions we wish the program to take. For example, we fixed the reaction
parameter γ = 0.1, this one will give the success rate of a function pair low impact (more
exactly 10%) on the function pair’s weight (which directly relates to the probability of choosing
this function pair later on).
As a prioritization had to be carried out, we chose to focus on the areas: Scoring and Heuristics
(Section 8.2.5 and Section 8.2.6). Here, we further prioritized, by choosing to concentrate on
the scoring dictionaries (only creating a small palette) and the port inserting heuristics. We
considered these as the most impactful in their corresponding areas.
Additionally, we started by tuning the different scoring dictionaries and selected the scoring
dictionaries accordingly. Next, we carried on with the port heuristic tuning, only using the
selected scoring dictionary. Again, one would have seen it more thorough, if we would have run
all the combinations. We do underline that it is practically impossible to run all the different
combinations of all different parameters without running some kind of forward or backward
selection.

Conclusion
In this thesis, we have shown a way of solving the LSNDP with transit time constraints using
a slightly different ALNS approach. We solve the LSNDP with transit time constraints using
a service-first flow-second two-tier algorithm. This one consists in starting with a given (good)
network (here we used the solutions from [4]). We then make small alterations to the network
(for every iteration where the change is accepted) with respect to the feedback from the TCMCF
solution (solved using a Column Generation approach). The feedback was categorized into three
distinct forfeiture reasons. Heuristics were then designed to accommodate the different reasons
with the purpose of solving the individual commodity’s forfeiture.
The metaheuristical approach let us customize our model in order to accommodate our goal.
Indeed, we have wished to investigate the possibility of improving a given initial network’s total
forfeiture (even accepting a potential increase in network cost) by making small alterations to
the starting network.
Examining the results from Section 9 we can draw the conclusion that we indeed achieve im-
provements to our main objectives. Nearly all of the solutions (except Mediterranean) show
improvement to the initial network. For the Mediterranean region, however, the changes to the
network cost outweigh the flow improvements. Given the uncertainty regarding the numerical
results, as discussed in Section 10, we cannot conclude how great of a gain we accomplish in a
monetary aspect. However, the goal was to prioritise a minimal forfeiture even if it sometimes
resulted in a worse cost for that network as long as there is a total net profit. In that regard we
do see the results as positive, given the sizeable reductions in the forfeiture for the best solutions.
Additionally, we wished to improve those networks by only making small changes to it. This is
also achieved. As we can see in the results, all the improved networks (for all of the different
regions) have higher speeds and some regions show to use fewer vessels or additional ports in the
network. These are, for a company, changes that are easier to implement compared to changing
the rotations completely.
The LNS results described in Section 9.6 are again a testimony that many of the heuristics
on their own seemingly improve the initial network. Again we cannot trust the actual figures,
because of how the cost is very likely to be miscalculated in some step. Some of them, however,
did not seem to improve the network alone. This is unfortunate but we did expect some of the
heuristics to mostly work in combination with others.
For many of the larger regions we are not able to increase the number of ports in the network.
We do, however, instead, manage to reduce the total number of vessels deployed. Typically it is
more efficient, with regards to the cost, to have fewer vessels but them sailing faster.
The above conclusions are a testimony to the idea that creating small changes to a good initial
network - when modelled as is done in this thesis - can be enough to better the forfeiture and
perhaps even the total objective cost.
Additionally, the implementation of the Column Generation (using an A-Star algorithm for the
RCSP sub-problem) approach to solve the TCMCF has been successful. This was necessary in
order to output a flow feedback, which the Network Design part of the problem needed. The
method of small alterations to an initial network has not been explored before in this regard.
As a collaboration with Maersk, it is optimistic to see that indeed, it can be possible improve
an already deployed network. Even more so when the improvements seem easier to implement
and propose more satisfactory outcome when in comes to fulfilling the customers’ needs.

Future Work
In Section 10.4, we highlighted the biggest limitations of our program. The majority of them were
caused by lack of time. In this section, we will be presenting what could have been experimented
with, had we had more time, but also what other directions, we could have taken to tackle this

12.1
If Time had Permitted
As was outlined in Section 10.4, our biggest limitation - as often is the case - was the time
ceiling. If we had had more time, we would have created new function pairs, but, especially,
carried out a thorough tuning process, in order to find the optimal parameter values, as well as
running the bigger instances for a longer time.
We would have conducted a grid search for the tuning for example (other methods could be
random search or early stopping), which would have allowed us to better the tuning process.
Additionally, we would have not only run more iterations for the bigger instances (Pacific and
World Small), but we would have generally run the programs more times. We did ten runs for
the testing but had to stick with one run in the tuning process. This decreases the credibility
of our tuning conclusions (compared to running multiple times and taking the average as was
done in the testing).
The limited tuning and testing datasets are, nonetheless, still an issue that prolonged time
wouldn’t have solved.
12.2
MCF
Such computational time limitations could be relieved by increasing the efficiency of the MCF.
Although we have taken many precautions and have created a very efficient MCF algorithm,
there is still space for further improvements.
12.2.1
Current MCF Shortcuts
We added elements to the algorithms in order to lower the shortest path calculations. These
techniques are further explained in Section 6.
In fact, in the A∗Label Setting algorithm, algorithm 1, we pre-examine the commodities, by
excluding the commodities that are found not to be able to reach their destination within their
demanded transit time. This is done by running the shortest path Dijkstra algorithm and finding
the shortest paths in the network (excluding the forfeited edges). If the shortest path is longer
than the approved transit time, then we know for a fact that this commodity cannot be fulfilled
without violating the transit time restrictions. This already reduces the set of commodities, that
needs to be run in the algorithm 1.
Next, we, constantly, check that the current labels, still have the potential of leading to a path
that adheres to the transit time restriction, through a Dijkstra algorithm, and yielding negative
reduced cost, using a Bellman-Ford algorithm (since the latter allows for negative weights). This
further reduces the shortest path calculations, as we don’t pursue useless labels.
In the Label Dominance Check, outlined in algorithm 2, we, also reduce computational time by
ordering the labels in lowest reduced cost, yielding a faster search mechanism, but also deleting
the labels that are dominated, to, once again, reduce the number of labels to be pursued. This
Dominance Check is, in itself, evidently, time-consuming. Therefore, it is important to place
it in the algorithm, where it can have the greatest effect, meaning where there is potential of
removing more labels at a time, as can be done in algorithm 2.
Lastly, we terminate algorithm 1 - for a given commodity -, when the first label reaches the
75
==PAGE:82==
commodity’s sink node. This way we find the most optimal shortest path for each commodity
(if it exists) and don’t look for all feasible paths in one run.
12.2.2
Tips for MCF Computational Speed
Nevertheless, there are additional measures that can be taken, in order to reduce the compu-
tational time. In [13], they further reduce the shortest path calculations by running the label
setting per origin node, rather than it being commodity based. This is quicker as there are more
commodities than origin nodes; many commodities can depart from the same port. However, the
way we have setup the network entailed that we create source and sink nodes per commodity,
as explained in Section 5.3.1. This means that with our implementation of the network, we
wouldn’t benefit from this label setting by source node approach.
As mentioned previously, the Dominance Check can be placed wherever in the A∗algorithm,
but we wish to make the most efficient use of it and this is why we chose to place it right before
the creation of a new label.
There are also other methods that can be used to solve the Resource Constrained Shortest
Path Problem (RCSPP). For instance, they introduce the Dynamic Programming Heuristics in
[]irnich2005a among other routines. This heuristic is based on the label setting and dynamic
programming techniques but is created to accelerate the computation. In [15] they make use
of the forced early stop rule, which consists in exiting the dynamic program when enough (pre-
defined numbers) negative reduced cost columns and labels have been created. Although this
method is faster, it risks to miss out on some negative reduced paths. This is a trade-off that
need be considered before implementing this method.
12.3
Different Setup
We could have chosen different ways of solving the LSNDP. Indeed, there are many different
approaches to solving this complex problem, from small to more fundamental changes.
12.3.1
Small Changes
Destroy/Repair Functions
The first thing that jumps into mind is the creation of other destroy/repair functions. Without
inundating with new function ideas, we would like to highlight a couple of attributes, which we
think the program could benefit from.
As mentioned previously, we would have liked the possibility of creating more rotations, in order
to further open the network up. This could be implemented in port inserting function pairs; the
latter could split a rotation with at least three ports, into two distinct but connected rotations,
where one of the two now also included the before missing port. This is one of many ways the
issue could be addressed.
For the low transit time, we could have created a function which changed the speed on more
than one rotation at a time. To be more exact, it would use the shortest path (from Dijkstra),
which we already have computed for our AnalysisSpeedIncrease and increase the speed on all
the rotations that that path uses - to the extent possible. This would distribute the increase in
speed on more rotations and thus make it more probable to work and leaving the rotations closer
to their design speed (where they start), where the fuel is cheaper. It would also, supposedly,
also have a broader impact as it would accelerate more rotations, rather than accelerating one
rotation to a greater extent.
We could also envisage tackling the low transit time without changing the rotations’ speed.
76
==PAGE:83==
Indeed, maybe reversing the order of the port calls in the rotation could cause lower distance
travelled on the rotation for some commodities, which in turn means saved time.
Generally, we could also spend additional time on optimising the implementations of our de-
stroy/repair functions, since computational speed is important in metaheuristics, to allow many
iterations to be run and thus potentially more changes.
Rotation Structures
Beyond bettering the destroy/repair functions, we would also fix the butterfly node. We would
have allowed the vessel classes to temporarily store some cargo on the butterfly node, which
they would recover on their way back (reaching the butterfly node a second time before having
completed the rotation’s cycle).
We would also tweak the destroy and repair functions for them to even be able to create their
own butterfly nodes.
We would also build on the implementation, in order for it to accept other complex rotation
structures.
12.3.2
Greater Level of Destruction
One could also have pictured creating more damage to the network when destroying it. Instead
of at most removing one port, one could have removed many and created different repair func-
tions, which would try and reinsert all the removed ports but starting with the missing port.
This would have followed the more classical destroy and repair functions affiliated to the ALNS
metaheuristic. This could be very interesting for the capacity problems as well. Here, we could
remove many vessels from different rotations and reassigned them with prioritizing fulfilling the
commodity which capacity problem we are attending. Although these seem tempting, we do
observe that the repair process is generally complicated, as distance, duration, speed and vessel
type and availability need to coincide in order to create a feasible rotation. This is also behind
our one-change approach.
If so, we could experiment between picking the destruction level randomly from an interval de-
fined with regard to the instance as David Pisinger and Stefan Røpke[17] would or follow Shaw’s
approach which consists in increasing the level of destruction with the iterations.
If we were to change this, we would probably withdraw from our variation to the ALNS and
return to the more classical version.
12.3.3
Extensive Changes
Erik Orm Hellsten’s PHD thesis[8] could serve as an encyclopedia on the LSNDP. He inspires
many different ways of tackling this problem. Among many tings, he dives into the two general
approaches for a two-stage algorithm for the LSNDP. They are "service-first and flow-second
algorithms" - this is the category our algorithm falls into - and the "backbone flow algorithms"
(such as was utilized in Mikkel Lassen Johansen’s master thesis [11]). The latter consists in
starting with solving the MCF in order to tweak (initially create) the network. This is reversing
the order we have followed. We started with a solution in the form of a network, whereas the
backbone flow algorithms creates a full graph (all the nodes are connected) and solve the MCF
to create rotations.
Within these two categories, there exists many - if not infinite - ways of designing algorithms.
77
==PAGE:84==
In Karsten et al.[12] they address the Time Constrained Liner Shipping Network Design Problem,
like we do, but instead of creating a vast set of destroy/repair functions, they modelled an IP,
which they solve in order to choose where and how to destroy (and repair) the solution - in
terms of removing and adding port calls. The model looks to maximize "the estimated profit
increase obtained from removing and inserting port calls, accounting for the estimated change of
revenue, transshipment cost, port call cost and vessel class cost". This IP includes soft transit
time constraints, which allows the program to send cargo to their destination with delay in
exchange of a (proportional) penalty, that is added to the objective value. The other constraints
handle the matter of vessel assignment (to maintain weekly frequency) with the port call changes
and also accounting for vessel availability.
This acceptance of sending cargo with a delay is an aspect that we have missed in our implemen-
tation. This situation is very realistic and simply forfeiting the cargo because of a short delay
would be rash. We justified our management of this problem through the important wish of
sending as much through as possible. If we had included the revenue, it would have desensitized
the program to forfeiture.
12.4
Solving LSNDP to Optimality?
For what concerns the Network Design problem, we could have used alternatives to metaheuris-
tics. Instead, we could have applied Decomposition algorithms. This could have been Benders as
is suggested in [1], since this one is suited for problems with many continuous variables in mixed
integer linear programming with linking constraints (meaning that it is difficult to separate into
subproblems). As long as the difference between the upper and lower bound of the objective
value is noticeable, more optimal cuts can be added to the Benders master problem, thus cre-
ating more constraints. Although this, theoretically speaking, is an efficient way of solving the
problem at hand (with potential of solving it to optimality), it is often disregarded for the initial
complexity of expressing the problem but also since it has to solve an integer master problem for
each iteration. McDaniel and Devine (1977) modified the method, in order to solve more linear
programs and fewer integer programs, which showed itself successful. However, this technique
is still not commonly used in the real world.
The optimality cuts (the generated constraints) stem from the dual solutions of the MCF. A
diagram of the procedure can be seen in Figure 7. Along with the MCF procedure - present in
Figure 7 -, which, itself, has been presented in a diagram (Figure 6), it resembles a Russian doll.
Note that this MCF is still solved in the Column Generation setting.

Solving the problem to optimality using Benders Decomposition is very time-consuming and is
therefore not used. If a heuristic was employed in order to decrease the time, we would risk a

12.5
Expanding the Problem
12.5.1
Added Constraints
As mentioned throughout the whole thesis, the LSNDP is a simplification of the actual real-world
problem. This means that one can always add constraints to the problem, which can somewhat
complicate the problem. However, we, here, mention two constraints which would have been
next to get integrated in our model.
The first is the cabotage rules. Some countries do not allow cargo from certain other countries
to be transported within their borders, they have then established cabotage laws. Clearly, this
is a legal and important restriction that the company need take into account when designing
the network and more importantly when choosing the paths for each commodity.
Another constraint lies in the number of transshipment which we allow on a path from origin to
destination. Indeed, if a commodity goes through many different rotations, it is more susceptible
to experience delays. Evidently, there is more chance of delays when unloading and loading
happens - on the transshipment port - rather than when simply sailing on the ocean. Some
models, therefore, include a maximum number of transshipment per commodity.
79
==PAGE:86==
These added constraints would be implemented in the A∗Label Setting - incorporated in the
definition of a feasible path.
These would, in consequence, build on the reasons for forfeiture.
12.5.2
An Other Perspective
One could also look into the LSNDP from new perspective. Our fellow DTU student investigated
this problem but with an environmental view on things. He is the author behind Decarbonizing
Liner Shipping by Applying Energing Technologies and Route Optimization [11]. As the name
suggests, he simulates mobilizing vessel classes with different kinds of fuel on the usual routes
with the objective of investigating more CO2 neutral solutions in the liner shipping world. This
is an exciting area as we, nowadays, are very oriented towards climate friendly solutions, but
might not be ready to lose liner shipping completely, since it is the cheapest mode of transporting
cargo and thus the solution most companies choose.
The pressing wish for the liner shipping companies to be more environmentally friendly is even
mentioned in [2], which shows the relevance of this study. In fact, we quote from [2]: "Global
carriers such as Maersk Line put great effort into creating a network design of low bunker
consumption to meet a demand for environmentally friendly transportation of goods and improve
the economy of the network".
12.5.3
Company Implementation
Finally, it would have been compelling to adapt our program, or even create a flexible program
in order for it to be easily implemented in the company and business setting. This doesn’t only
include interface, it also means allowing the program to include specific constraints without
great effort. One could imagine commodity-based restrictive rules (such as cabotage laws). But
it could also be that the company orders a rotation to look a certain way, relax some constraints
or demand a certain response to a very specific situations.
