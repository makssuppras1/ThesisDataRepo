Abstract
Bacteriophages (phages) are viruses that infect bacteria and are present in al­
most every environment on Earth. They exhibit two distinct lifecycles ­ lytic and
lysogenic, one involving the bacterial cell lysis after releasing new virions, with the
other connected with integration into the bacterial genome as a prophage. Within
marine environments, bacteriophages take part in the process of carbon cycling
through viral shunt, as well as modulating the metabolism of bacterial hosts by in­
corporating auxiliary metabolic genes (AMGs) or, on occasion, biosynthetic gene
clusters (BGCs). This project aimed to identify phages within metagenomes de­
rived from Danish marine sediment, while also trying to establish a workflow able
to identify and analyse both known and novel bacteriophages found in this environ­
ment. Through a comparative analysis of state­of­the­art bioinformatics programs
used for bacteriophage discovery, an optimal program was chosen to increase the
chances of phage discovery in marine sediments. The identified phage genomes
were later analysed using several command­line programs to evaluate the quality
of predictions, investigate their phylogeny using a network­based approach, fol­
lowed by the identification of AMGs and BGCs, of relevant function to the marine
bacteria.

Hypothesis and Aim
Background
Bacteriophages, or phages, are viruses that infect bacteria and are present in
all sorts of habitats. In marine environments, bacteriophages are responsible for
nearly half of bacterial deaths. Bacteria killed via phage predation contribute to the
marine carbon cycle through the process of viral shunt, which allows dissolved or­
ganic matter to be recycled by other microorganisms and not higher trophic­level
organisms. Bacteriophages play a big role in shaping the diversity and evolution­
ary dynamics of bacterial communities. They alter the metabolism of the host by
incorporating auxiliary metabolic genes (AMGs) into its genome. On rare occa­
sions, phage­encoded biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) can be introduced into
host genomes as well. While this is a way of harnessing the molecular machinery
of the host for phage replication, it can also convey a competitive advantage to
the bacterium, thus influencing the composition of microbial communities. In ad­
dition, phage virulence and host resistance to phage infections evolve alongside
each other, further highlighting the impact of phage­host interactions on microbial
ecosystems.
Problem
Despite their importance, numerous bacteriophages remain unidentified in envi­
ronmental samples, due to the lack of reference genomes for marine sediment
phages in available databases. On top of that, navigating the existing bioinfor­
matic programs which employ various approaches to phage discovery, is chal­
lenging. Furthermore, a comprehensive downstream analysis is required to iden­
tify the genomic makeup and infer the taxonomy of results, which proves difficult
due to the nature of highly mosaic bacteriophage genomes.
Hypothesis
Multiple bioinformatic programs for phage discovery have been developed over
the years. They employ sequence­based, gene­based, or hybrid approaches and
offer a number of functionalities. However, their pitfalls are performance issues,
no active maintenance or inherent weaknesses and biases. In a similar manner,
multiple tools for subsequent analysis can be used, which combined, reinforce the
2
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
==PAGE:10==
need for a standardised workflow which could be applied to identifying phages in
marine sediment metagenomes, and their further investigation.
Aim
This project focused on discovering phages within metagenomic data derived
from Danish marine sediment samples. The samples were already sequenced
using Oxford Nanopore long­read sequencing, followed by assembly and bin­
ning of reads using the seabed­symphony pipeline. Through comparative analy­
sis of state­of­the­art bioinformatic software used for bacteriophage identification,
an optimal tool was selected for this data set. The identified phage genomes
were then analysed using several command­line programs to evaluate the quality
of predictions, investigate their phylogeny using a network­based approach, fol­
lowed by the identification of AMGs and BGCs, relevant to the marine bacterial
communities. The ultimate goal was to come up with a workflow, able to identify
and analyse both known and novel bacteriophages found within marine sediment
metagenomes.

Introduction
2.1
Bacteriophages
Being the most widespread biological agents on planet Earth, [1] [2] bacterio­
phages, or phages, infect bacteria across all known environments [3]. This diver­
sity in hosts is also reflected in the variety of phage morphologies, shapes, and
sizes. Generally, phages have single­ or double­stranded DNA or RNA genomes
enclosed in a capsid, which can be polyhedral, attached to a tail, or filamentous
[4]. The majority of known phages are members of the Caudoviricetes class, with
double­stranded DNA genomes and a tail [4]. Bacteriophage genome sizes vary
greatly as well, ranging from ∼2.5 kb to even more than 700 kb, as reported by
some studies [4] [5]. However, an average phage genome falls between 30 kb
and 50 kb [6].
Alongside high variability in size, phages exhibit little sequence similarity between
one another [6]. This diversity can be observed even for phages that target the
same host [4]. Still, phages share some common genetic signatures ­ hallmark
genes encoding proteins related with structural elements like tail and capsid, or
phage lifecycle and infection mechanisms, display a level of conservation [6] [7]
[8]. In addition, bacteriophage genomes exhibit a high degree of mosaicism, [2]
meaning that different genomic regions have distinct evolutionary histories, stem­
ming from horizontal gene transfer (HGT) events [2] [4]. HGT is one of the drivers
behind bacteriophage evolution [2]. Another factor is phage­host relationships,
which shape the diversity and evolutionary dynamics of both parties.
2.1.1
Life cycles
Just like other viruses, phages require a host to replicate. They infect a host bac­
terium by adsorbing to its cell surface, which involves a crucial interaction between
phage Receptor Binding Proteins and bacterial receptors and evading the defence
mechanisms of the host [9] [10]. Phages then inject their genetic material into a
bacterial cell and harness the molecular machinery of the host to produce new
virions. This process occurs through two major cycles: lytic and lysogenic cycle.
(Figure 2.1) Lytic cycle ultimately leads to bacterial cell lysis and release of new
phages into the environment, in search of new hosts to infect [11] [12]. In some
cases, phages can also enter a chronic cycle, which consists of replication without
4

==PAGE:12==
the death of the host [2] [11]. Chronic cycle was observed predominantly in fila­
mentous phages from Inoviridae family [2]. In lysogenic (temperate) cycle, phages
integrate their genetic information into the host genome and exist as prophages,
or sometimes extrachromosomal elements, which are replicated alongside the
bacterial chromosome [11] [12]. They remain dormant, and while not producing
new phages, they are still being passed down to future generations of bacterial
cells [2]. However, in response to the changing environmental conditions that can
threaten the survival of the host, a prophage can excise itself from the bacterial
genome and initiate the lytic cycle [11].

teriophage can enter either lytic or lysogenic cycle. In the lytic cycle, phage DNA
is replicated by the host (3), new phage particles are assembled (4), and released
through lysis (5). In lysogeny, phages integrate into the bacterial chromosome (6),
being passively replicated and inherited as the host cell divides (7­8). Lysogenic
cycle continues until specific triggers activate the lytic cycle (9). Figure created

Prophages integrate into the genome of the host via the means of site­specific re­
combination, where bacteriophage integrases recognise attachment sites within
the genomes of both the bacterium (attB site) and the phage (attP site) [12] [13].
This results in prophage insertion into the genome, where it is flanked by direct
repeats of bacterial DNA. These flanking regions are important for the reverse
process of prophage excision from the bacterial chromosome [13]. The triggers
for bacteriophages to switch between lytic and lysogenic cycles remain unclear,
though stress and growth rates of bacteria, [11] as well as host abundance and
environmental stressors are believed to play a role, [2] with lysogeny serving
as a protection mechanism to phages during low host abundance. Additionally,

the lysogenic cycle is more common during times of phage abundance, whereas
phage scarcity promotes the lytic cycle [2].
2.1.2
Lysogenic conversion
Prophages integrated into bacterial chromosomes can protect their hosts from in­
fections by similar phages [14]. They can also modulate host phenotype through
the expression of phage­encoded genes that are originally derived from bacteria,
through a process called lysogenic conversion [2] [15]. Bacteria can obtain new
beneficial traits from prophages carrying auxiliary metabolic genes (AMGs) [2] or
sometimes even biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) [12]. Even though they origi­
nate from bacterial hosts, phage AMGs are suggested to have evolved separately,
being more closely related to each other, rather than bacterial genes [11]. Through
influencing host metabolism, bacteriophages also promote their own replication
[2].
Auxiliary metabolic genes
Numerous AMGs were found in phage genomes, including those of marine phages
[11] [14]. These auxiliary genes can enhance the functionalities of the host by en­
coding virulence factors or toxins that allow their host to colonise new ecological
niches and promote its fitness and survival. For example, Corynebacterium diph­
theriae becomes pathogenic after an infection with a phage harbouring tox gene
encoding diphtheria toxin [16]. In marine phages, expression of AMGs related to
nutrient cycling, carbon metabolism, or photosynthesis has been reported [2] [11].
Some genes were also tied to nutrient availability and temperature conditions spe­
cific to a given zone of the marine environment. Kelly et al., 2013 reported that a
higher prevalence of AMGs related to phosphate uptake was observed in phages
infecting cyanobacteria from phosphorus­limited marine environments. Expres­
sion of these genes supports host survival in nutrient­poor conditions, while facili­
tating the replication of phages, which requires phosphorus. This further signifies
the influence of bacteriophages on bacterial communities and their adaptation to
changing environments.
Biosynthetic gene clusters
Phage­encoded BGCs occur very rarely ­ Dragoš et al., 2021 screened over 10
thousand phage genomes available in Bacterial and Viral Bioinformatics Resource
Center database (formerly PATRIC) [17] for BGCs, using antiSMASH [18]. The
study found that only 0.07% of genomes contained BGCs, still, it also reported on
sublancin, a bacteriocin encoded by SPβ prophage, found in Bacillus subtilis. This
illustrates that, while infrequent, bacteriophages can equip bacteria with BGCs re­
sponsible for e.g. bacteriocin synthesis, which provides a competitive advantage

to hosts over other bacteria.
2.1.3
Phage­host interactions
In marine environments, nearly 50% of bacterial deaths can be attributed to phage
predation, [11] which further highlights bacteriophages’ role in shaping bacterial
populations. In addition, phages and their bacterial hosts evolve alongside each
other, participating in a so­called evolutionary arms race, where bacteria develop
mechanisms to resist phage infections and phages evolve countermeasures to
overcome these defences [2] [9]. Bacteria protect themselves against phage in­
fections by mechanisms like biofilm formation and altering surface receptors to
prevent adsorption, as well as restriction­modification and CRISPR­Cas9 sys­
tems that can cleave integrated prophages post­infection [14]. Bacteria have been
found to contain defence islands grouping phage­resistance genes, and recipro­
cally, the presence of antidefence islands was reported for some bacteriophage
genomes [2]. It was also suggested that phages exchange genes associated with
their counter­defence mechanisms between each other and even other mobile
genetic elements (MGEs) through the means of recombination. [9] This gene
transfer can occur during co­infections, when multiple phages attack a common
host and interact within its cell [2]. As a result of coevolution, bacteria grow more
resistant against known bacteriophages rather than future phage infections, while
phages improve their infectivity towards past hosts rather than new ones [2]. Still,
there exist both strain­specific phages and phages capable of infecting a broader
range of hosts [11]. While a smaller range of more closely related bacterial hosts
has been reported for marine phages, [11], changes in host range are dynamic
and a subject of phage adaptation and evolution [2].
2.1.4
Bioinformatic phage discovery tools
Throughout the years, multiple bioinformatic programs have been developed for
the purpose of phage discovery. They utilise feature­based (gene­based), alignment­
free sequence­based, or hybrid approaches to predict phage sequences in metage­
nomic data [19].
Gene­based methods
Tools like Phigaro [20], or PHASTER [21], rely on protein homology by comparing
the input data against amino acid sequences or protein annotations from Hidden
Markov Models (HMMs), found in phage­specific databases. Regions abundant
in phage­hallmark genes are identified through sequence alignments. Hence,
marker­based methods are constrained by the choice of databases searched, as
well as the abundance and variety of phage genomes available within them [19].

==PAGE:15==
Sequence­based methods
Identification using solely nucleotide sequences involves recognising specific se­
quence features in the input data, based on which, machine or deep learning
models make predictions on whether the input is likely of bacteriophage origin
[22]. Sequence markers include, among others, gene frequency and the distance
between genes used by Marvel [23], local changes of the AT/GC skew stemming
from prophage insertions employed in PhiSpy [13], or viral k­mer frequencies from
VirFinder [19] [24]. Although sequence­based methods are alignment­free, their
accuracy still relies on databases to some degree, since publicly available se­
quence data is most often used as training data for the prediction models [19].
Hybrid methods
In addition, there exist tools like geNomad [22], VIBRANT [8], or VirSorter2 [25]
that incorporate both feature­based and machine­learning­based predictions [19].
By combining the strong points of each method, hybrid tools improve the accuracy
of phage discovery, both when identifying well­characterised phage genomes as
well as novel sequences not found in databases [22].
The choice of a phage discovery program is closely connected with the goal of the
analysis, data specifics like the sample’s origin and composition, or even the level
of computational expertise of the end­user [19]. Other factors to consider include
the overall performance and speed of computations that should not be overlooked,
especially when analysing large data sets. In addition, state­of­the­art phage dis­
covery tools are not perfect ­ performance biases introduced with the training data
sets have been reported [19]. Another challenge is no active maintenance of the
programs, resulting in tools not being up­to­date in terms of the databases used
for phage identification, as well as newer versions of tool dependencies, leading
to errors or inadequate analysis. This all highlights the need for benchmarking
of phage discovery tools, so that the optimal one can be selected, improving the
odds of finding novel phages in marine sediment metagenomes.
2.2
Viruses and marine ecosystems
2.2.1
Marine carbon cycle and viral shunt
Bacteria are the backbone of marine ecosystems, serving both as the base of
the marine food web and as key oxygen producers. It has been suggested that
approximately 50% of the oxygen found in the atmosphere comes from photo­
synthetic bacteria from the marine microbial loop [26]. In marine environments,
bacterial mortality can be attributed to either predation by single­celled eukaryotic
8
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
==PAGE:16==
grazers or bacteriophage lysis [11]. Both processes lead to the release of car­
bon and nutrients, which can be recycled by other organisms. However, through
grazing, carbon is transported up the food chain to higher­trophic­level organisms.
Resulting cellular detritus and waste contribute to the particulate organic matter
(POM), which can then be sequestered through the biological pump ­ a key pro­
cess of carbon cycling in marine ecosystems [11] [27]. On the other hand, bacte­
ria killed through phage predation contribute to dissolved organic matter (DOM),
which is recycled by other microorganisms in the microbial loop rather than higher
trophic­level organisms, via the process of viral shunt [11]. This not only supports
microbial communities but also fuels oxygen production through microbial photo­
synthesis.
The process of phage lysis contributes to the carbon and nutrient cycling, while
sustaining the marine microbial loop. This establishes bacteriophages as key ele­
ments of the marine ecosystems, having far­reaching environmental implications.
2.2.2
Marine sediment
Marine sediments are deposits formed by inorganic and organic matter that accu­
mulate on the seafloor. Their composition varies depending on depth, geographic
location, climate, and human activity [28] [29].
Ecological importance
Marine sediments are the habitat of benthos, which consists of diverse organisms
like bacteria, algae, plants, and animals living within the sediment and on its sur­
face [30]. The organisms present in marine sediment participate in key processes
of carbon and nutrient cycling, breaking down organic matter and waste products,
which can be then transferred back to higher­level consumers. They are also a
key component of the marine food web and contribute to the oxygenation of the
sediment, facilitated by bioturbation from burrowing animal movements within the
sediment [31] [32]. Microbial activity also takes place at the sediment­water inter­
face (marine microbiology), however, it is the sediment and not the water column
above it that is the hot spot for microbial activity [33]. Dead organic matter that
reaches the seafloor can either be broken down by sediment bacteria and returned
back to the carbon cycle in the form of CO2, or stored in the sediment, thus remov­
ing it from the carbon cycle (carbon burial). This balance is of great importance
to the global carbon levels [33]. The crucial role that marine sediment plays only
reinforces the idea that this element of the marine environment deserves to be
studied deeper.

Bacterial communities
Marine sediments are the home to diverse microbial communities of great biosyn­
thetic potential, which remains largely untapped [34] [35]. In a comprehensive
study of marine sediment microbial diversity, Hoshino et al., 2020 performed a
taxonomic analysis of 299 marine sediment samples from all over the world. It
revealed that sediment has a unique bacterial community, distinct from the one
found in seawater. It also showed that the composition of bacterial communities
differs depending on oxygen availability, with members of Firmicutes and Pro­
teobacteria dominating in the oxic sediment, and Atribacteria, Chloroflexi, and
Planctomycetes phyla in the anoxic sediment.
Musat et al., 2006 found that
Bacteroidetes, Planctomycetes, and Proteobacteria of Deltaproteobacteria and
Gammaproteobacteria classes, are the most commonly found bacteria in the anal­
ysed Wadden Sea sediment. In their analysis of Danish marine sediment, Kjer­
sgaard Bech et al., 2020 reported that marine sediments from this area were
dominated by Proteobacteria of the Rhodobacterales and Pelagibacterales order,
Bacteroidetes of the Flavobacteriales order, Actinomycetales. They also found a
substantial fraction of unknown, unclassified bacteria, further highlighting the ex­
isting gap in knowledge on marine sediment microbial diversity. Bridging this gap
is crucial for understanding marine ecosystems, the process of nutrient cycling,
and the potential discovery of new bioactive compounds.
2.3
Metagenomes and metagenomics
A metagenome is a collection of all genetic material from all organisms present
in an environmental sample, which can be studied through the means of metage­
nomics. Metagenomic analysis provides information on both the diversity and
function of the genetic content present in the sample [36]. Of note, metagenomic
analysis does not require prior isolation and culturing of bacterial isolates. Thus,
it more accurately captures microbial diversity and community dynamics, allowing
for the study of the uncultured majority of organisms present in the environment
[37].
2.3.1
Sequencing
To study metagenomes, DNA is first extracted from the sample and used to pre­
pare a library, which is then subjected to sequencing, through which the nu­
cleic acid sequence can be determined. It is crucial to quality check the DNA
post­extraction, to ensure an adequate amount of high­quality DNA is acces­
sible for sequencing [36]. During library prep, DNA is fragmented and ligated
10
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
==PAGE:18==
with adapters and barcodes, which enable sequencing and sample identification
post­sequencing, respectively. Numerous sequencing technologies are currently
available, offering both short and long reads as the output, with the choice of se­
quencing method depending on the analysis objectives. In the case of this project,
Oxford Nanopore long­read sequencing was performed [38]. In this technology,
the DNA strand is passed through a nanopore with an electric current applied.
The sequence is determined by analysing the disruptions in the current, charac­
teristic for each nucleotide base, in a process called basecalling [38]. Long­read
sequencing technology was a conscious decision, since longer reads provide for
less fragmented assemblies and are better­equipped for handling repetitive re­
gions, like BGCs or flanking regions surrounding MGEs like prophages.
2.3.2
Post­sequencing
Data straight from the sequencer is a collection of raw reads, which after adapter
removal and trimming of low­quality regions, can be assembled into longer con­
tigs (contiguous fragments), based on overlaps between them. Assembling reads
without a reference genome, known as de novo assembly, is commonly used in
metagenomics, since the data often contains a mix of various organisms. A com­
mon approach to de novo assembly relies on de Bruijn graphs and exact matches
between k­mers, which are substrings of sequencing reads of length k. De Bruijn
graphs consist of nodes of unique (k­1)­mers and directed edges connecting them
in the form of k­mers. A genome is reconstructed by following the edges of the
graph, visiting each edge only once, which is referred to as walking an Eulerian
path. Mapping to a reference genome can be used when analysing a metage­
nomic sample of known composition, with reference genomes readily available in
databases [36].
To determine which metagenomic sequences come from which organisms, genome
binning can be performed. This process groups contigs into bins based on ge­
netic similarity, with each bin representing a distinct genome [36]. Bins are qual­
ity checked for genome completeness and contamination to only leave the high­
quality ones. Dereplication can also be performed, which involves removing re­
dundant, identical or near­identical sequences from the bins. Genomes recon­
structed from metagenomic assemblies are called metagenome­assembled genomes
(MAGs) and can be used for further analysis and annotations.

2.4
Seabed­symphony pipeline
Seabed­symphony pipeline was developed by Felipe Vaz Peres [39] for the pur­
pose of marine sediment metagenomes analysis. The pipeline includes 15 steps
that allow for processing raw sequencing reads, their assembly and binning, fol­
lowed by subsequent analysis.
2.4.1
Pre­processing of reads, steps 0­4
The first step of the pipeline is checking the quality of the raw reads with NanoPlot
v.1.42.0 [40] and SeqKit v.2.8.0 [41]. This analysis generates various statistics
like the number of sequences, total bases sequenced, read length distribution,
accompanied by several plots for an easier assessment of raw data quality. After
the initial quality check, reads are quality­filtered using Filtlong v.0.2.1 [42], which
takes into account the length and identity % of each read, to only retain the top
90% of reads longer than 1 kb. Post filtering, adapters and barcode primers are
removed from the reads with Porechop v.0.2.4 [43] [44]. Next, the quality of reads
post­trimming is checked once again with NanoPlot and SeqKit.
2.4.2
Metagenomic assembly, steps 5­8
After pre­processing, metagenome assembly is performed using metaFlye v.2.9.2
long­read assembler [45]. In contrast to other assembly programs, metaFlye does
not rely on de Bruijn graphs but uses repeat graphs instead, which use approxi­
mate sequence matches, making them better equipped for handling long­reads,
which are more error­prone and contain long stretches of repetitive sequences.
In a repeat graph, edges represent genomic sequences, which can be unique or
repetitive, whereas nodes are the overlaps between them, marking the bound­
aries of repetitive sequences [45]. A genome is reconstructed by following the
edges of the graph, so that each unique edge is visited only once [45] [46].
Metagenomic contigs resulting from the assembly step can be then classified as
either eukaryotic or prokaryotic with Whokaryote v.1.1.2 [47]. To make predictions,
Whokaryote combines two classifiers: a random forest algorithm, which takes into
account gene length, gene density, or the distance between genes, and Tiara ­
a deep­learning classifier based on k­mer frequency profiles [48]. Sequences
predicted as prokaryotic are then extracted from the assembly data using a custom
script extractContigsFromWhokaryote.py [39] and can be subjected to genome
binning.
12
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
==PAGE:20==
2.4.3
Binning, steps 9­11
To bin sequences likely originating from the same or related organisms, four dif­
ferent binning programs are used in the pipeline: MetaBAT2 v.2.15 [49], MaxBin2
v.2.2.7 [50], CONCOCT v.1.1.0 [51], and GraphMB v.0.2.5 [52]. Prior to binning,
assembly contigs are indexed and mapped back to reads. This process creates
a Sequence Alignment/Map file (SAM), which, after conversion to a binary BAM
file, can be used to generate a depth file with the average read coverage for each
contig. In seabed­symphony, MetaBat2 is used to generate the depth file through
jgi_summarize_ba­m_contig_depths script. In terms of the binning algorithms,
MetaBat2 generates a pairwise distance matrix based on contig abundance and
tetranucleotide frequency, and uses graph­based clustering for binning contigs
[49]. MaxBin2 takes into account the same features as MetaBat2, but uses a
probabilistic model and single­copy marker genes during binning [50] [52]. CON­
COCT uses coverage across multiple samples, nucleotide composition, and k­mer
frequencies to perform unsupervised binning [51]. Lastly, GraphMB leverages
assembly graphs apart from contig features when binning with a graph neural
networks algorithm [52].
Binning predictions from all four programs are used as input for DAS Tool [53],
which finds an optimal set of non­redundant bins, which are then quality­assessed
with CheckM v.1.2.2 [54].
2.4.4
Phylogeny and functional analysis, steps 12­15
Sea­bed symphony pipeline performs taxonomic classification and functional an­
notations of MAGs, using GTDB­TK v.2.3.2 [55] and Bakta v.1.9.3 [56], respec­
tively. Genomes are also analysed for the presence of BGCs with AntiSMASH
v.7.1.0 [18] and BiG­SCAPE v.1.1.9 [57].
The FASTA sequences of metagenomic assemblies and MAGs generated at steps
5 and 10 of the pipeline, respectively, can be used for further downstream analysis
for the purpose of phage discovery with geNomad.
2.5
geNomad pipeline
geNomad is a program that enables the identification of MGEs, such as viruses
and plasmids, in genomic, metagenomic, and metatranscriptomic data [22]. It
consists of a series of modules that can be run separately or sequentially when
executing the entire pipeline in end­to­end mode.
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes

==PAGE:21==
2.5.1
Annotate
The first step of the pipeline is predicting genes in the input FASTA sequences us­
ing Pyrodigal­gv v0.3.2 [22]. Predicted genes are then compared against a data
set of ∼228 thousand marker protein profiles, specific to viruses, plasmids, and
chromosomes, with MMseqs2 v14.7e284 [58]. This step produces gene annota­
tions and lays the groundwork for taxonomic assignment of sequences to Inter­
national Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses classification system of viral taxa in
the final step of the pipeline.
2.5.2
Find­proviruses
To identify potential prophages, geNomad searches for regions within the se­
quences where genes labelled with viral markers are surrounded by host­specific
genes. A conditional random field machine learning model is used to calculate and
assign a probability score to each gene, of how likely it belongs to a prophage.
Regions with a high number of likely prophage genes form ”provirus islands”. Is­
lands close to each other within the sequence are merged to prevent splitting a
single prophage. Finally, total viral marker enrichment scores for these islands are
calculated and their boundaries refined by checking for any neighbouring tRNAs
or integrases, which are often found flanking prophages, marking their integration
sites.
2.5.3
Classification
geNomad is a hybrid tool, combining both gene­based and alignment­free models
to classify sequence’s origin as virus, plasmid, or chromosome.
Marker­classification
Marker­classification module makes predictions based on the markers assigned
to genes in the annotation step. Each marker in geNomad’s marker data set has
a virus, plasmid, and chromosome score assigned, which describe its specificity
to each of the three groups. These scores are used alongside other genomic
features such as gene density and specific sequence motifs, by an ensemble
of classification trees. This model scores each sequence on how likely it is to
represent a chromosome, plasmid, or virus.
NN­classification
Neural­networks­based classification module is used for assigning viral, plasmid,
or chromosome category directly based on input nucleotide sequences. This is
especially useful for sequences with few or no markers assigned to their genes.
In this process, sequences are first split into 4­mers, which presence at a given
position within the sequence is represented in the form of a binary matrix. Matri­
ces are then encoded and fed to an IGLOO neural network classification model
14
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
==PAGE:22==
[59]. During encoding, sequence feature maps are generated, which place similar
sequences closer together, while retaining relationships between non­contiguous
sequence regions due to random slicing by the IGLOO architecture.
Aggregated­classification
The final step is combining the results of both classification models into a single
classification of the sequence as either virus, plasmid, or chromosome. Scores
from the two models represent two branches, which are then weighted by an atten­
tion mechanism. If the majority of genes in the sequence have markers assigned,
the marker branch contribution has more weight to the overall classification. Con­
versely, when few genes have markers assigned to them, the neural­network­
based classification is weighted more.
2.5.4
Score­calibration (optional)
Classification scores from the previous step do not reflect actual probabilities of a
sequence being a virus, a plasmid, or a chromosome until score calibration is per­
formed. This process takes into account the estimated composition of the sample,
based on the observed frequencies of viruses, plasmids, and chromosomes in the
initial classification. This is then used to adjust the scores so that they reflect the
true underlying probabilities for each of the fractions. Ultimately, score calibration
improves classification and produces estimated probability and false discovery
rate values.
2.5.5
Summary
Post­classification results are subjected to filtering based on a number of ad­
justable thresholds for score, marker enrichment, or number of hallmark genes in
the predicted sequences, in order to output more reliable results. Multiple output
files, from FASTA sequences, gene annotations to summary tables, are generated
in this final step and can be used for further analysis.

4.1.1
Tool specifics
All of the phage discovery tools selected for benchmarking were published in re­
cent years, with the oldest being Phigaro, dating back to 2020. Unfortunately,
only a handful of programs appear to be actively maintained, based on the activ­
ity in their respective GitHub repositories, including addressing of issues raised
by various end users (Table 4.1).
Installation
All programs but one were installed using the Conda package manager, with the
exception being PhageBoost.
The successful installation and configuration of
PhageBoost necessitated cloning the entire GitHub repository and debugging one
of the scripts. The remaining programs had a considerably smoother installation
process. geNomad, VIBRANT, and VirSorter2 required a separate download of a
necessary database as part of the setup.
Runtime
The 6 programs varied considerably in terms of runtime and the actual CPU time
spent on computations. The fastest tools in the lineup were geNomad and Phage­
Boost, which completed their runs in a matter of minutes, followed by Phigaro
which took ∼20 minutes to produce the output. The remaining programs were
subpar in comparison, taking up to 2 hours to complete or not exiting properly
despite generating results, like in the case of DeepVirFinder or VirSorter2.
Output and extra functionalities
After a successful run, most state­of­the­art phage discovery tools generate a list
of viral hits identified within metagenomes, alongside their extracted sequences
and genomic coordinates for the integrated prophages found. Some programs
include tool­specific or probability scores to evaluate the predictions. In addition,
tools offer several diverse features, including taxonomic classification and func­
tional annotations.
Phigaro generates annotated prophage genome maps, offering the most visual
and user­friendly interface for data interpretation. It also marks potential trans­
poson insertion regions within prophages. The remaining tools output files in a
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
27
==PAGE:35==
tab­delimited (tsv) or text (txt) format. VIBRANT assesses the quality and com­
pleteness of the viral genomes found, along with AMG and metabolic pathway
analysis. geNomad identifies not only viral but also plasmid sequences in metage­
nomic data and provides gene and protein annotations for both. Moreover, geNo­
mad and Phigaro perform taxonomic assignment of the viral sequences identified,
which is useful for further downstream analysis.
Prophages and free phages detection

5
Discussion
5.1
Benchmarking revealed geNomad as the
optimal phage­discovery tool
There exist multiple bioinformatic programs for viral sequence identification in
metagenomic data, which can be used for phage discovery. These tools em­
ploy different algorithms and approaches to identifying phage sequences, they
also differ in speed and functionalities. To increase the chances of finding bac­
teriophages in the marine sediment it was necessary to evaluate some of the
available programs, in order to make an informed decision on which one to use in
this project. During evaluation, the purpose for benchmarking was reinforced by
the fact that some of the programs are not actively maintained or updated, which
not only makes it challenging to install and use them, but also casts doubt over
their accuracy. This was the case for DeepVirFinder, PhageBoost and VIBRANT.
While all programs exhibited variation in their prophage predictions from the test
bacterial genomes, determining the ground truth in terms of the expected num­
ber of prophages in each bacterial species is challenging. Still, since lysogenic
phages enhance bacterial virulence, pathogenic bacteria can be expected to carry
more prophages [73]. This aligns with the findings of geNomad and VirSorter2 for
Escherichia coli and Salmonella enterica from the test set of bacterial genomes.
Benchmarking consistently identified geNomad and VirSorter2 as the top perform­
ers in identifying prophage sequences. This aligns with the results of Bai et al.,
2025, where strong performance on actual metagenomic data was observed for
both tools. However, due to the fastest runtime, a wide range of functionalities
and being the most recent program out of the selection, geNomad was ultimately
selected as the designated phage discovery tool for this project. Additionally,
this program leverages both gene­based and neural­network­based classifiers
for making predictions, which allows for the identification of both documented, as
well as novel bacteriophages. The ability to identify both characterised and novel
bacteriophages is essential to unravelling the marine sediment virome, given the
underrepresentation of marine sediment phage reference genomes in available
databases. GeNomad is also one of the few tools that are well­documented on
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
51
==PAGE:59==
GitHub [74] and its own wiki page [75], making it more transparent and credible,
while, at the same time, user­friendly.
That being said, the benchmarking performed is limited to six phage discovery
tools, which might not capture the full picture of state­of­the­art programs for
phage identification. However, the programs selected in this project were unveiled
through a comprehensive literature review, including other published evaluations
of bacteriophage identification tools [76], [77]. This benchmarking process also
assumes the use of a single program for phage identification, whereas in theory,
a multi­tool approach could be implemented, combining the strengths of different
phage discovery programs and consolidating the results later on. An example of a
similar workflow is the What the Phage pipeline [78], which combines a number of
phage discovery programs run in parallel, with subsequent downstream analysis.
However, this particular workflow does not include geNomad as one of the phage
prediction tools, in addition, its command­line version relies on Nextflow [79] and
Singularity [80] workflow managers, which can be challenging to navigate for less
experienced users.
5.2
Metagenomic data quality across barcodes
Marine sediment metagenomes were sequenced using Oxford Nanopore long­
read sequencing. The choice of sequencing technology was informed by the na­
ture of bacteriophage genomes, which contain regions rich in repeats stemming
from flanking regions surrounding prophages, as well as possible BGCs within
the sequences. Not only that, longer sequencing reads improve the contiguity of
metagenomic assemblies [81], and allow for capturing the entire phage genome
in a single read, without it being fragmented across multiple reads [82]. All of this
combined, improves the chances of accurately capturing the marine sediment vi­
ral communities and increase the likelihood of finding novel phages [82].
The sequencing quality varied between the four barcodes analysed, which is to
be expected considering that each barcode represents a sample that was sub­
jected to different methods of DNA extraction. Barcodes 2, 4 and 5 averaged
around 2.5 million reads, with the exception being barcode 3 accumulated less
than half of that many reads. Barcodes 4 and 2 were found to have the highest
sum length of sequencing reads, however these two barcodes include an addition
of 1% Pseudomonas protegens DTU 9.1. The considerably large genome size of
Pseudomonas likely affected the final amount of reads in these barcodes [43] [83].
Moreover, barcode 4 yielded the best quality assembly, having the highest N50
values, as well as the largest contig size. These statistics point to assembly from
52
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
==PAGE:60==
barcode 4 having the longest contigs, which increases the likelihood of identifying
bacteriophage genomes of a higher degree of completeness from this barcode.
This aligns with the subsequent phage discovery results (Figure 4.3), with barcode
4 having the highest median length of the viral sequences identified.
The relationship between sequencing depth (the total number of bases sequenced)
and the number of identified phage genomes appears to be linear (Figure 4.2),
whereas in actuality, the graph will plateau at a certain depth, where it is no longer
possible to identify any more genomes within the data. Being limited to only 4 data
points representing each of the barcodes, the true relation between the two met­
rics is challenging to accurately capture.
Having to analyse more sequencing data from the marine sediment sample stud­
ied in this project, a more complete picture of the diversity of the bacteriophage
communities within it would emerge. Limiting the sample pretreatment to a single,
optimal DNA method would also reduce the variability across different barcodes.
5.3
Phages identification in marine sediment
metagenomes with geNomad
In total 247 non­integrated phages and 62 prophages were identified in the ma­
rine sediment metagenome analysed with geNomad. The majority of bacterio­
phage sequences fell on the shorter end of the spectrum (4 ­ 17 kb), with a cou­
ple of longer sequences between 30 and 70 kb, and the longest reaching up to
nearly 110 kb, likely corresponding to near­complete or complete phage genomes.
Overall, long sequences tended to accumulate more viral marker genes, and se­
quences assigned higher virus scores were observed to be less likely to be false
positives, both indicating higher confidence in their viral origin.
During this analysis, no thresholds for phage identification were applied, as apply­
ing a strict filtering would minimise the size of an already limited number of results
and limit the chances of finding novel phages. A drawback of this approach is the
inclusion of 16 results with FDR above 0.05, which could be false positives. On
the other hand, sequences of different origin can likely be filtered out during the
downstream analysis, due to their lack of genomic similarities to the true phage
genomes.
One of the drawbacks of geNomad reported by Bai et al., 2025 is relatively worse
performance on sequences under 3 kb, in contrast to greater sequence lengths.
Camargo et al., 2023 identified the weakness of geNomad in the context of as­
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
53
==PAGE:61==
signing the taxonomy to sequences shorter than 3 kb. Contigs from metagenomic
assemblies < 3 kb are present in data from all barcodes, which could have effec­
tively influenced bacteriophage predictions. However, Camargo et al., 2023 also
demonstrated that implementing the score calibration of geNomad, which was ap­
plied in this analysis, benefits the classification of shorter viral sequences. More­
over, since complete bacteriophage genomes are typically longer than 3 kb [6],
the impact of this limitation is likely minimised in the context of this project.
Moreover, it is important to mention that geNomad does not solely identify bac­
teriophages but is designed for finding viral sequences. Therefore, a thorough
inspection of the results and potential filtering of eukaryotic viruses is necessary
if phage discovery is the objective of the analysis. For this purpose, taxonomy
assignments performed by geNomad or database searches in case of the unclas­
sified results can be leveraged.
5.4
Impact of overlapping predictions across
barcodes
The metagenomic data used in this project originated from the same marine sed­
iment sample that was subjected to different preparation methods, each repre­
sented by a unique barcode. Having in mind the subsequent phylogenetic anal­
ysis of the identified bacteriophage sequences, it was important to acknowledge
the potential for cross­barcode overlap or redundancy in predictions. The initial
approach to identifying overlaps through an all­vs­all BLAST search was found
suboptimal for bacteriophage sequences. This is because BLAST alignments
penalise gaps and don’t take into account gene rearrangements and inversions,
which result from site­specific recombination occurring in phage genomes [84].
Therefore, a more robust approach to overlaps was taken, using the Viral Cluster
assignments from vConTACT2, as it infers genomic similarity based on the pro­
teins shared and not a direct alignment of bases, which is better suited for the
highly mosaic bacteriophage genomes.
Using this method, several overlaps between barcodes were identified, with 7
VCs, corresponding to 51 phage sequences, being shared between all four bar­
codes. In addition, the highest overlap between a pair of barcodes was observed
for barcodes 2 and 4. Initially, this intersection was thought to correspond to VCs
specific to Pseudomonas phages, since samples from these barcodes were en­
riched with Pseudomonas protegens DTU 9.1. However, when verified with the
network graphs, only 2 sequences from this intersection were found to cluster with
54

==PAGE:62==
the known Pseudomonas bacteriophages.
However, it is important to note that while the overlap patterns were explored in
the two Venn Diagrams in Section 4.4, they cannot be compared directly. This is
because not all identified phage sequences were included in the phylogenetic net­
works created by vConTACT2. Furthermore, some sequences were considered
as outliers, due to insufficient shared protein content, to belong to a VC.
Nevertheless, the presence of these overlaps was deemed not a major concern for
the scope of this project, since it would not prevent from effectively capturing the
diversity of bacteriophages within the marine sediment, ensuring a comprehensive
picture of the virome.
5.5
Host contamination could represent auxiliary
metabolic genes
Quality control of the identified bacteriophage sequences revealed 19 instances
of possible host contamination. Despite this, these sequences were retained in
the subsequent analysis, as the suspected contamination may instead represent
the presence of AMGs of bacterial origin within phage genomes.
5.6
Phylogenetic characterisation of marine
sediment phages
Marine sediment bacteriophage genomes exhibited a degree of genomic similar­
ity to the reference phages from the ProkaryoticViralRefSeq94­Merged database
used by vConTACT2. However, this overlap was insufficient for the sequences
to belong to the same VCs as the references, rendering it challenging to assign
taxonomy to the identified phage genomes. While the majority of sediment se­
quences exhibited more similarity between each other, rather than the database,
a single genome clustered with the characterised bacteriophages associated with
Mycobacterium, and several grouping with Pseudomonas and Salmonella phages.
This could be ecologically relevant, as members of Mycobacterium genus, such
as Mycobacterium marinum ­ a fish pathogen, are found in marine environments
[85]. In addition, both Pseudomonas and Salmonella can be found in marine sed­
iments as well [86] [87], not to mention the addition of Pseudomonas protegens
DTU 9.1 to the sediment sample preparation. Furthermore, some identified phage
genomes shared a few connections with bacteriophages infecting bacteria of the

55
==PAGE:63==
Rhodobacterales order (Dinoroseobacter, Roseobacter, Rhodobacter, Paracoc­
cus). This aligns well with the predominant bacterial communities in Danish ma­
rine sediments, as characterised by Kjersgaard Bech et al., 2020.
Unlike geNomad, which classified most sequences only to the class level (Cau­
doviricetes), vConTACT2 clustering allows for making predictions about the pu­
tative taxonomic family assignments of the identified bacteriophages. This high­
lights the potential in leveraging vConTACT2 networks for achieving more spe­
cific taxonomic classifications of bacteriophage genomes from the marine sedi­
ment metagenomes. In the network graph from vConTACT2, sediment genomes
identified in this project clustered with bacteriophages of the Myoviridae, Podoviri­
dae, and Siphoviridae families, which were all previously observed in the marine
ecosystem of the North Sea, as reported by Wichels, et al., 1998. Still, the ma­
jority of connections between genomes exhibited rather low edge weights, with
insufficient evidence to make confident taxonomic assignments.
Moreover, two larger clusters (Group A, Group B in Figure 4.7) were distinct from
all reference phages and comprised solely of the identified sediment genomes,
sharing a high degree of genomic similarities. In the network graph, which ex­
plores only the local phylogeny of the identified phages (Figure 4.8), these clus­
ters were found to contain multiple longer genomes. These findings suggest that
the aforementioned groupings could represent novel bacteriophages. Therefore,
these sequences should be further examined to explore this hypothesis.
Bacteriophages present in the marine sediment analysed by Breitbart et al., 2004
were found predominantly as integrated prophages. The local phylogeny net­
work revealed a number of connections between the non­integrated phages and
prophages, within the same VC. This further illustrates the presence of the same
phages in different lifecycles within the sediment, both of which serve distinct roles
in controlling marine bacterial populations and participating in marine carbon cy­
cling by driving viral shunt [11].
Lastly, a limitation of the phylogenetic analysis performed on the identified bacte­
riophages in this project lies in the fact that marine sediment reference genomes
are scarce in the available databases. This makes it more challenging to accu­
rately assign taxonomy to the phages found experimentally. In order to improve
the classification potential, a manual curation of a database composed primarily
of the available marine sediment bacteriophage genomes could be implemented
in the network generation process by vConTACT2.

No biosynthetic gene clusters were found
No instances of BGCs were found within the identified marine sediment phage

5.8
Auxiliary metabolic genes of marine
environment relevance were identified in
phage genomes
Screening the identified phage sequences for AMGs was performed based on
gene annotations provided by geNomad and Pharokka. As a result, 26 phage
genomes containing putative AMGs or morons of potential significance to marine
bacteria were investigated in more detail. Morons in the context of bacteriophages
refer to phage­encoded genes that enhance the fitness of their bacterial hosts, but
are of no use to the phage itself [88]
Several putative AMGs identified by Pharokka were connected to host survival
and adjusting to environmental changes. ABC transporters are ATP­dependent
transport proteins that facilitate nutrient uptake in various bacteria. Their key role
in environmental adaptation and competition for nutrients in the ocean ecosys­
tems was reported for SAR11 bacteria (Pelagibacterales order), while also pro­
viding new insights into the process of marine carbon cycling [89]. Moreover,
toxin­antitoxin systems, present among the functional annotations, refer to sys­
tems of genes encoding a toxin and a corresponding antitoxin. These systems
were reported in coastal Synechococcus strains, aiding bacteria in surviving ad­
verse environmental conditions by inducing their dormancy, via translation inhibi­
tion [90]. A similar role was observed for cation efflux proteins, which help maintain
bacterial homeostasis and remove numerous cations, including heavy metal ions
exhibiting antimicrobial properties, from bacterial cells [91]. Furthermore, poten­
tial AMGs related to carbon metabolism were found among the annotations from
Pharokka. These included genes tied to porphyrin biosynthesis ­ a precursor for
the synthesis of heme and chlorophyll, not only enhancing the metabolic capac­
ities of the host for energy production but also having key implications for the
biogeochemical cycling in marine environments [92].
The candidate AMGs from geNomad were related to energy production as well
as virulence. Cyanase is an enzyme responsible for the breakdown of cyanate
Phage discovery in sediment metagenomes
57
==PAGE:65==
into ammonium and CO2.
In marine microorganisms, cyanate plays a crucial
role in photosynthetic assimilation of organic nitrogen, and its subsequent rem­
ineralisation by the action of nitrifying bacteria in the deep ocean.
Moreover,
cyanase­encoding genes were found to be frequently disseminated through hori­
zontal gene transfer [93], which could point to the involvement of bacteriophages.
Apart from nitrogen cycling, several potential AMGs tied to carbon utilisation were
found among the results. Pectin present in the cell walls of seaweed and seagrass
was reported in the literature as a carbon source for some marine bacteria [94].
Thus, bacteria possessing genes encoding pectin and pectate lyases could effec­
tively break pectin down and utilise it as an energy source. Another substrate for
marine sediment bacteria is taurine, which acts as an important source of energy
and several sulfur compounds resulting from taurine degradation. Hence, taurine
transport systems are prevalent in marine bacterial genomes [95]. Finally, gene
annotations associated with type VII secretion point to important protein secretion
complexes found in Mycobacteria, with mycosin protease facilitating their proper
functioning. These systems are crucial for the transport and release of virulence
factors [96].
These findings demonstrate the strong relevance of the identified AMG arsenal to
the promotion of fitness and survival of bacteria in marine sediments. This further
signifies the ability of bacteriophages to influence bacterial communities and their
adaptation to changing environments, presenting them as key elements of the
marine ecosystem.
To further explore the potential of bacteriophages in modulating the metabolism of
their hosts, future perspectives could include screening the bacterial communities
within the analysed marine sediment for the presence of the metabolic genes iden­
tified in bacteriophages from the same environment. This could provide broader
insights into the phage­host interactions within marine sediments.

This would also necessitate transforming the phage dis­

Conclusions
Benchmarking of a selection of state­of­the­art phage discovery tools revealed
geNomad as the optimal program that was used for phage discovery in the Danish
marine sediment sample. It was selected due to its hybrid approach to phage
identification, ability to identify both non­integrated phages and prophages and
additional functionalities and user­friendliness. Discovering both prophages and
lytic phages is important, because of their roles in moderating the diversity and
evolutionary dynamics of marine bacterial communities, while also participating in
marine carbon cycling through viral shunt.
In terms of geNomad predictions, longer bacteriophage sequences also tended
to have higher marker enrichment scores, while the FDR of predictions lowered
whenever higher virus scores were assigned to the sequences.
Network­based phylogeny analysis revealed several sediment bacteriophages ex­
hibiting a level of genomic similarity with known phage genomes, infecting bac­
teria present in marine sediments. However, this was not enough to confidently
classify these sequences into bacteriophage families. Conversely, two distinct
clusters consisted solely of the identified sediment phages with a high degree of
similarity, representing possible candidates for being novel phages.
While no BGCs were observed, several putative AMGs were found within the iden­
tified bacteriophage genomes. These AMGs could improve bacterial fitness and
adaptation to environmental changes, as well as utilising novel nutrient sources
and increased virulence. Thus, highlighting the role bacteriophages play in shap­
ing bacterial communities within the marine ecosystem.
