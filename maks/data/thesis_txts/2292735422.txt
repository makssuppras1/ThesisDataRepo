Title: Accurate and Fast Prediction of Wind Turbine
Performance in Wind Farm for Layout Optimization
Author: Dimitrios Kompolias
Department: DTU Wind Energy
Abstract:
This project aims to develop a fast and reliable wind farm layout opti-
mization tool. Therefore, the prediction of wind turbine performance
in wind farm is analyzed. The determination of performance is based
on the power production, as well as the fatigue of wind farm. Un-
doubtedly, the power production of a wind turbine is inﬂuenced by
the wakes, so an engineering wake model is developed and evaluated,
which follows a Gaussian distribution and is turbulence dependent.
Regarding fatigue/loads, HAWC2 is used to assess the aeroelastic re-
sponse of a stand-alone wind turbine based on the IEC 61400-1 ed.3
standard, as well as for a turbine that is in the wake of an upstream
turbine based on the Dynamic Wake Meandering mechanism. Due to
the fact that, Horns Rev oﬀshore wind farm studied in this project
consists of 80 × V80-2.0 MW wind turbines, an upscaling approach
of the NREL WindPACT 1.5 MW is used instead. In addition, the
optimization of an oﬀshore wind farm layout is a multidisciplinary
problem, which includes various variables. In this study due to the
limited time, as well as the quite long computational time, some sim-
pliﬁed cases of Horns Rev wind farm are examined in order to op-
timize the location of wind turbines. Concerning this, a previously
developed algorithm is used (RS-Random search algorithm), which
starts from an initial feasible layout and then iteratively improves it
by taking into account the objective function. The objective function
of this study is to maximize the total power output and minimize the
fatigue of a speciﬁc wind farm.
Keywords: wake models, oﬀshore wind farm design, power model-
ing, fatigue/loads, layout optimization

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the research that is presented in this MSc. thesis and it
outlines the motivation of investigating the wind turbine performance in wind farm
for layout optimization. Therefore, an overview of the current situation regarding the
development of wind energy will be given.
1.1
General aspects and motivation
It is a fact that energy is vital for humanity. Energy growth is linked to well-being
and prosperity across the world. The major source of energy comes from fossil fuels.
However, nowadays we are facing the problem of increasing price of oil and climate
change, so it is imperative to develop and exploit renewable sources of energy. Over
the past years there has been a signiﬁcant development of the energy that is produced
from renewable energy sources.
In Figure 1.1 it is illustrated the installed power
generating capacity per year (MW) for all sources of energy and the renewable shape
of its year. More speciﬁcally, in 2000, new renewable power capacity installations
amounted a mere 3.6 GW. Since 2010, annual renewable capacity additions have
been about ten times higher than in 2000 (34.6 GW). Overall, 412.7 GW of new
power capacity has been installed in the EU since 2000. Of this 29.4 % has been wind
power, 56.2 % renewables and 91.1 % renewables and gas combined [13].
The net growth since 2000 of wind power (116.8 GW), gas (101.3 GW) and solar PV
(87.9 GW) was at the expense of fuel oil (down 25.3 GW), coal (down 24.7 GW)
and nuclear (down 13.2 GW) [13].
The installed capacity of the other renewable
technologies have also been increased but not with the same percentage. In Figure
1.2 it can be seen the net electricity generating installations in the EU from 2000
to 2014. It is obvious that EU’s power sector continues to move away from fuel oil,
coal, nuclear and gas, whereas the wind seems to be the most dominant source of
generating electricity compared to the others.

Moreover, regarding wind energy sector it is evident that there is a growing trend in
size and capacity of wind turbines. Thus, this trend promotes more oﬀshore wind
projects in the market compared to the onshore ones. Indeed, major disadvantages of
onshore wind farms can be tackled by choosing oﬀshore conditions. Firstly, oﬀshore
sites can meet the demand of the market (larger size of wind turbines), since there
are continuous areas that wind farms can be placed. The larger size of wind turbines
is linked with higher wind speeds at hub height, so this enhances more the develop-
ment of oﬀshore projects. Another important factor is that because the roughness
length is lower, less turbulence is generated on oﬀshore sites, which results to better
performance of wind turbine based on power production and fatigue damage.

On the other hand, there are also some considerable drawbacks related to oﬀshore
wind farms. The high cost per kWh of produced energy is one of the most signiﬁcant.
This cost is related to the more expensive foundations as well as the integration into
the electrical network. What is more, the cost can be notably increased due to the
worst weather conditions that usually might be occurred in an oﬀshore site.
Therefore, to maintain the growing trend of oﬀshore wind energy industry, it is es-
sential to minimize the eﬀect of the already mentioned drawbacks. Concerning this,
diﬀerent kind of investigations is carried out in order to optimize oﬀshore wind farm
layout. By optimizing the arrangement between wind turbines, better performance
for each of them can be achieved, as a result the overall cost of energy to be decreased.
1.2
Problem statement
Inevitably, oﬀshore wind farm layout is a key factor for the viability of the project.
Due to the fact that it determines the length of the inter-array cables, it is related
directly to the cost of energy of the project. In addition, the electrical losses between
arrays, as well as the losses due to the wake eﬀects are some of the most important
factors that can be determined by the arrangement of wind turbines. Therefore, it
is crucial that turbines are located in a wind farm with such a way that provide the
highest eﬃciency in the system. However, the complexity of the design space and the
discontinuities in the search space caused by the wake eﬀects, make it hard to optimize
analytically. Thus, in order to solve the optimization problem one of the issues that
should be resolved is to generate a wake model that is fast and accurate enough to

layout can be deﬁned. To do this, a random search (RS) algorithm is implemented,
which starts from an initial feasible layout and the improves the layout iteratively in
the feasible solution space [15].
1.3
Background knowledge
The optimization of layout of wind farms is a highly complex process because of the
multi-disciplinary nature and the trend for larger size and higher capacity wind farms.
Due to the multidisciplinary eﬀort that is required, some objective functions should be
maximized and/or minimized. These functions might include power production, cost,
fatigue, while meeting various constraints (e.g WF boundary, noise, etc.). Concerning
this, diﬀerent methods have been proposed to tackle the issue of complexity.
The ﬁrst study in wind farm layout optimization carried out from Mosetti et al.
[29].
An area of 2.0 km by 2.0 km was used, which was further divided into a
10 by 10 grid of square cells. The total size of each cell was corresponding to ﬁve
turbine diameters, whereas the turbines could be placed only in the centre of the grid.
Regarding wake modeling, Mosetti et al. [29] implemented the N.O. Jensen model
[22], which considers a linear wake expansion of the wake, while Genetic Algorithms
[19] were used for optimization. Further, Kusiak and Song [24] proposed a model with
only constraints of the minimum distance between wind turbines and the wind farm
boundary. Based on these parameters, the position of turbines is allowed to reside
anywhere within wind farm. Moreover, Rethore et al. [32] investigated optimization
by taking into account also a cost model into the objective function. This model is
focused on testing methods that optimizes the proﬁt of oﬀshore wind farms based on
a balance of the annual energy production, the electrical grid costs, the foundation
cost and the fatigue of wind turbine components. What is more, Saavedra-Morreno
et al. [33] suggested a model that instead of using a statistical distribution of wind
speed of directions, a spatial distribution of the wind ﬁeld is considered.
On the algorithmic side of the problem, diﬀerent approaches have been investigated
such as Monte Carlo [28], Genetic Algorithms [29], Random Search [15] and Particle
Swarm Optimization [36]. More speciﬁcally, the Monte Carlo algorithm tends to be
used when it is infeasible to compute a result with a deterministic algorithm, how-

2.1
Analytical Wake Models
It is a fact that wind turbines operate in the atmospheric boundary-layer (ABL) and
they experience diﬀerent wind speeds, wind shears and turbulence levels. As a part
of the atmosphere, ABL is the one that is closest to the ground, where turbulence
motion is dominated. Based on this, two key characteristics of turbine wakes are
aﬀected: (a) the velocity deﬁcit, which is directly associated with power losses in
wind farms, and (b) the wake turbulence, which aﬀects the ﬂow-induced dynamic
loads on downwind turbines [37].
In Figure 2.1 it is illustrated the eﬀect of wakes in wind turbines. As it can be seen,
the wake propagation can cause various eﬀects in the downstream turbines. These
eﬀects are linked to wind turbine performance, such as power production and fatigue
damage.
Due to the fact that there is a growing trend in the number and size of installed wind
farms around the world, wakes of wind turbines have been an essential topic of study.
As many wind turbines in wind farms are exposed to wind velocities that are lower

turbine lifetime. In section 2.2 it is proved that the most accurate models are the

In this section the parameters of the wind turbine controller are calculated based on

order to obtain reliable results. The gains of the controller of each region of operation

minimum and maximum generator speed, the gearbox ratio, the tip speed ratio and

The 3 regions describe the control strategy. From the Table 4.4 it is seen that the
power capability in function of wind speed is divided into three regions of operation.
Region 1 is the start up phase. As the wind accelerates beyond the cut-in speed,

angle constant while controlling the generator torque. At the point where the wind
speed is higher than the rated wind speed of the turbine (rated speed), wind turbine
enters region 3 mode. In this region the power is kept constant and the controlling
parameter is the pitching angle.

This section is focusing on the fatigue eﬀects imposed on a operating wind turbine

the wake wind ﬁeld with aeroelastic codes provides correct and detailed estimation of
wind turbine fatigue as well as of wind turbine production [25]. Firstly, the IEC61400-

are calculated for the components of blade and tower. As it is described before, most
of the fatigue loads originate during normal operation, so the simulation were set up

chosen equals to 50 m/s(Vref). According to IEC 61400-1 ed.3 in order the results to
be reliable because turbulence is a stochastic process, 6 turbulence seeds should be
considered.

a structure is capable of sustaining the loads during its life. Equivalent loads are
also computed for a given number of load cycles. In the present study, the fatigue
evaluation was based on the Rainﬂow counting [30] of the load cycles. The number
of cycles that was chosen is 107.

The power production and loading patterns are important parameters to predict
the performance of wind turbines for layout optimization.
In general, these two
parameters deviate signiﬁcantly between a turbine that is placed in a wind farm and
a stand-alone wind turbine. Concerning the former, the most crucial factor to predict
the performance is the distance between turbines. In chapter 4 it is analyzed the
process to estimate the loads/fatigue of a stand alone wind turbine based on IEC-
61400 ed.3 standard [5], however this chapter introduces a Dynamic Wake Meandering
mechanism for the calculation of loads/fatigue of a turbine that is in the wake of the
upstream turbine or it is placed in an already conﬁgured layout of wind farm.
5.1
Dynamic wake meandering deﬁnition
The Dynamic Wake Meandering was developed with the objective to model the basic
wake ﬂow mechanisms with suﬃcient accuracy while keeping the model as simple
as possible [25]. The DWM model complex is based on the combination of three
elements:
• Modeling of quasi-steady wake deﬁcits
• A stochastic model of the downwind wake meandering
• Added wake turbulence
The assumption for the wake meandering part is that the transport of wakes in the
atmospheric boundary layer can be modeled by taking into account that the wakes
act as passive tracers driven by the large-scale turbulence structures in lateral and
40
==PAGE:51==
5
DYNAMIC WAKE MEANDERING MODEL (DWM)
vertical directions. Concerning the stochastic modeling of wake meandering it can
be concluded that a cascade of wake deﬁcits is emitted from the upwind turbine and
meandering according to changes of wind directions. The down-stream propagation
of these wakes is controlled by the mean wind speed of the ambient wind ﬁeld (Tay-
lor’s hypothesis), whereas for the dynamics in lateral and vertical directions a wake
cascade element is considered according to the large-scale lateral and vertical turbu-
lence velocities. The choice of the stochastic turbulence ﬁeld deﬁnes the stochastic
wake propagation. It should be mentioned that the turbulence box is generated using
a transverse resolution of one rotor diameter. The DWM model was implemented in
HAWC2 in order to calculate the aeroelastic response of the wind turbines. In addi-
tion, a coarse grid should be deﬁned with a grid size of the order of a rotor diameter
and a turbulence ﬁeld was generated from the Mann model.
5.2
Project workﬂow
The goal of the project is to develop an accurate and fast model for power prediction
and fatigue estimation of a wind farm for layout optimization. The main tasks of the
project is the estimation of Wind Climate, the prediction of Power Production and
the Fatigue estimation in wind farm. The main tasks implementation is carried out
using Matlab and HAWC2. In Figure 5.1 is illustrated the Project workﬂow.
First of all, the analysis of the wind climate of a wind farm is a fundamental process,
which is essential for the wind farm optimization. The goal is the characterization
and modeling of the wake aﬀected ﬂow ﬁeld inside a wind farm. This model should
be suﬃciently fast and detailed for accomplishing reliable prediction of power produc-
tion. More speciﬁcally, this model describes the wake deﬁcit and the wake turbulence
caused by wind turbines located upstream. The input parameters of this model are
the x, y and z coordinates, which refer to the downstream distance, as well as the
spanwise and vertical distance respectively. What is more, the hub height (zhub), the
thrust coeﬃcient (Ct), the free wind speed (u0) and the ambient turbulence intenisty
(I0) are some of the most important parameters to estimate the wake velocity (uw)
and the wake turbulence intensity (Iwake). Based on the estimation of the wake tur-
bulence intensity the modiﬁed wake decay constant (k∗) can be also calculated. Thus,
taking into account these parameters, as well as the power curve characteristics of
the corresponding wind turbine and the probability distribution of the site, the power
production of wind farm can be predicted.

6.2
Random search algorithm
In this section the Random Search (RS) [15] algorithm is described, which is used
for the optimization of Horns Rev oﬀshore wind farm. First of all, in order to ﬁnd
the optimal location of wind turbines, single or multiple objectives should be max-
imized or/and minimized. The objective function of this study is to maximize the
total power output of wind farm and minimize the loads. Based on that, the RS
algorithm starts from an initial feasible layout and then iteratively improves it by
taking into account the total power and fatigue. Concerning the latter, the loads of
blade root component were chosen to be minimized because in the process regarding
the prediction of fatigue/loads, the sea conditions (waves and currents) were not in-
cluded, which have direct inﬂuence in the tower of the wind turbine, so the blade root
considered as the most appropriate component to represent the equivalent loads of
Horns Rev wind farm. Moreover, the results for blade root ﬂapwise equivalent loads
(see Figure 5.10) were calculated based on a database that consists of diﬀerent kind
of distances between wind turbines and a range of turbulence intensities from 5% to
15%. However, the optimal solution might be in the intermediate of these values, so
the method of scattered interpolation [1] was used in Matlab to calculate these values
(see Figure A.15). It should be mentioned that in case of partial wake, the values
of loads that calculated based on horizontal oﬀset (0.5D) of the downwind turbine
were used, whereas for distances larger than 9D it was assumed that the wind turbine
produces similar power and experiences the same fatigue with the upstream turbine.
First step of the optimization based on RS [15] method is to consider a wind farm
that consists of Nwt wind turbines and deﬁne the location of each of them (X =
[x1, x2, ..., xNwt], [y1, y2, ..., yNwt]). The constraints of this algorithm were a) the bound-
ary layer coordinates in order wind turbines would not exceed the limits of wind farm,
as well as b) the distance between wind turbines could not be closer than 5 · D. Due
to the fact that RS algorithm improves the layout iteratively, it randomly selects one
wind turbine and moves it in a random direction with a random step [15]. Apart
from that, the algorithm saves the information of the improved move, whereas in the
case that the next move is worst than the previous one, algorithm keeps the previous
value and a ‘’history” is obtained based on power output and fatigue. The procedure

iterations should be adequate in order to maximize the chance of improvement.

CONCLUSIONS
7
Conclusions
This chapter of the report concerns the conclusions that can be drawn from the work
derived from the research. Therefore, the main research question which asks “whether
the optimization tool that was developed is really fast and reliable” is addressed. Prior
to, answering this the research subquestions should be treated.
The ﬁrst subquestion refers to the parameters which aﬀect the design of a wind farm.
The parameters that were identiﬁed in this study concern the wind parameters, such
as wind speed, wake deﬁcit (multiple wakes interaction), turbulence intensity, as well
as the parameters that are important to characterize a wind climate, such as wind
rose and weibull distribution. Concerning the aforementioned parameters, the wake
propagation and the power production of a wind farm can be extracted. Moreover, the
fatigue/loads that wind turbines experience throughout their lifetime, is an essential
factor for the design of layout of a wind farm.
The second subquestion concerns the reliability of the wake model that it has been
developed. Inevitably, wind climate is dealing with characterization and modeling of
the wake aﬀected ﬂow ﬁeld inside a wind farm. One of the goals of this study is to
develop a wake model that is suﬃciently fast and accurate to allow for an optimization
application and, at the same time, suﬃciently detailed for achieving reliable prediction
of power production. Concerning that, diﬀerent kind of wake models were examined
by using the latest developments in literature. Based on that, it was concluded that
the most accurate wake model was the Gaussian one. This model was derived by
applying conservation of mass and momentum, as well as by assuming a Gaussian
distribution for the velocity deﬁcit in the wake. However, this model was modiﬁed
further by taking into account the eﬀective turbulence intensity in the wake. Overall,
the results that obtained by the Gaussian model are in acceptable agreement with the
LES data, especially at the center of the wake. Nevertheless, it seems that the model
overestimates the wind speed close to the edges. What is more, it was considered that
the computational time of the model is acceptable for optimization application, due
to the fact that it was applied for Horns Rev wind farm and the total elpased time
for a speciﬁc wind speed and wind directions was 1 second, whereas in the case of a
single wind speed and the whole range of wind directions the code needs 60 seconds.
64
==PAGE:75==
7
CONCLUSIONS
Additionally, the loading patterns are important parameters to predict the perfor-
mance of wind turbines for layout optimization. In this study the aeroelastic code of
HAWC2 was used to estimate the loads/fatigue of a stand-alone wind turbine based
on IEC 61400 ed3 standard, whereas a dynamic wake meandering mechanism was
implemented in the code in order to predict the loads of a wind turbine that is in the
wake of the upstream turbine. Moreover, to save computational time, instead of deﬁn-
ing the whole number of wind turbines of Horns Rev in HAWC2 code, only two wind
turbines were considered and the loading calculations were carried out for diﬀerent
inﬂow parameters. It should be mentioned that due to lack of fatigue measurements
of wind turbines in Horns Rev wind farm, the results were not validated.
Last but not least, the Random Search algorithm was used for the optimization of
Horns Rev wind farm. The constraints of this algorithm were the boundary layer
coordinates in order wind turbines would not exceed the limits of wind farm, as
well as the minimum distance between wind turbines which was deﬁned to 5D. In
addition, an objective function was deﬁned that maximizes power production and
minimizes fatigue of Horns Rev wind farm. Each parameter (power/fatigue) consists
of a coeﬃcient. It was concluded that, these coeﬃcients are crucial for the deviation
of power and fatigue. Based on that, by changing these coeﬃcients, it is shown that
the power can be improved from 0.2% to 0.6%, whereas the fatigue can be reduced
from 1.7% to 2.2% of the initial one. However, by modifying these two coeﬃcients,
as well as by increasing the number of iterations the results can be further improved.

DISCUSSION AND FURTHER DEVELOPMENT
8
Discussion and further
development
In this study various assumptions were considered.
Therefore, future research on
this topic could try to withdraw these assumptions and uncertainties. First of all, the
wake model that was developed is based on Gaussian distribution, which is associated
with turbulent velocity ﬂuctuations. Thus, the method for calculating the wake ﬂow
can be divided into the following steps:
•
Estimate the wake turbulence intensity Iwake based on Frandsen formula.
• Calculate the modiﬁed wake decay constant kwake using the estimated value of
Iwake.
•
Calculate the wake deﬁcit based on Gaussian model but using the modiﬁed
coeﬃcient kwake.
As it is already mentioned, the results of this model are in acceptable agreement
with the LES data, however the wind speed close to the edges seems to be slightly
overestimated. Based on that, further investigations in wake decay constant kwake
could provide more conﬁdence in the model.
Moreover, in the upscaling process of the NREL WindPACT 1.5 MW to a 2.0 MW
wind tubine, the controller was tuned by using hs2pid.exe. Nevertheless, the HAWC-
Stab2 could be also used in order to apply Campbell diagram and check the resonance
of the system. This could provide more information about sudden increments of the
fatigue in the components of wind turbine.
In addition, the optimization of an oﬀshore wind farm layout needs to take into
account the meteorological conditions of the speciﬁc site. In this research some sim-
pliﬁed cases were applied for the analysis, such as a range of wind speeds and a speciﬁc
wind direction (prevailing), as well as a single wind speed and the whole range of wind
directions (∆θ0 = 400). A sensitivity study for diﬀerent weather conditions, would
provide better insight into the layout optimization.

DISCUSSION AND FURTHER DEVELOPMENT
Concerning Random Search algorithm, an objective function was deﬁned that maxi-
mizes power output and minimizes fatigue for a speciﬁc number of iterations. In this
study, that number was deﬁned to 1000, however the higher the number of iterations
the higher the chance of improvement. Last but not least, as it is already mentioned,
the objective function that was applied, consists of two parts, power and fatigue. A
link of these two factors with a cost function, would provide better insight into the
layout optimization.
