Abstract
It has been stated that there is a problem with the performance gap between
the theoretical and measured energy use. The building sector stood for between
20-40% of the total energy use in developed countries. In Denmark, the building
sector stood for 37 % in 2017 and in Europe, the building sector is generally
on a level with the industry and transportation. Therefore, it is important to
minimise this performance gap and to renovate the building so the energy use
can be lowered.
Furthermore, it is needed to be able to make the theoretic
improvement comply with what is seen in reality after erection/renovation.
This study is about renovated social houses from the 1970’ties. There have been
measured indoor temperature, humidity and CO2 in a renovated and a non
renovated apartment. For the non renovated apartment there have been made
measurement for two heating seasons one with an occupant and one without
an occupant. This makes the apartment extra interesting because it thereby
is easier to see how the occupant impacts the energy use. Weather data for
the same time periods have also been measured. This data is used to see if
and by how much the performance gap for heating power can be improved by
using measured data and data from reports instead of using standard values.
The measured data have been used as the set point for the temperature in the
apartments and for the weather and the reports have been used to deﬁne the
inﬁltration and to choose the materials and properties for the materials. The
simulation has been made in the program IDA ICE. It is mainly assessed on the
heating power but it is also checked that the temperature measured ﬁts with
the temperature simulated so the indoor climate simulated represent the actual
indoor climate.
The parameters and their impact have been investigated in a sensitivity analysis.
==PAGE:6==

This show which parameters have a big impact on the heating consumption and
which parameters there are less important to have precise data on. For example,
the occupant’s temperature set point and the assumptions made for internal gin
have a big inﬂuence on the heating power. Also, the thermal bridge can have
a big inﬂuence depending on if the setting is changed from good to typical
or changed from typical to poor. Furthermore, the sensitivity analysis can be
used in the future for obtaining more precise models and thereby minimising the
performance gap. Another important thing to investigate in the future is if there
can be made three models for diﬀerent kind of occupant behaviours to be able
to give a range for energy use. This might also aﬀect the user to become more
aware of their consumption if they know how much they can change their energy
use depending on their behaviour and thereby save money. The conclusion of the
study is that using measured indoor temperature and the actual weather for the
period simulated the simulated heating power will follow the measured heating
powers tendencies better. However, the simulated heating power ﬂuctuates more
compared to the measured heating power and will therefore never ﬁt 100 %.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The residential buildings energy consumption in Denmark takes 30.4% of the
total energy use for the year 2017 [25]. This means that the building sector is
an ideal place to start improving and thereby minimising energy consumption.
Today there is a problem with making a realistic assumption for the energy
consumption when building new houses and when renovating. This is leading to
a performance gap between the actual and the theoretical energy consumption
[10, 18, 19]. If the theoretical energy consumption would be done more accurate
it would be easier to calculate the potential saving for a renovation and thereby
better know if the savings are bigger than the energy used for the renovation,
and thereby if the renovation is viable.
C. Thomsen [22] show that the number of details and use of measurements in
dynamic modelling will make a more accurate energy consumption. It is wanted
to extend this study with a diﬀerent tool, which has more controls and more
settings for the controls so that the model can become more realistic and thereby
hopefully give a result closer to the actual result from the measurements. The
tool chosen to make these simulations is IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA
ICE) made by EQUA. The tool is an innovative and trusted program for making
whole-year dynamic multi-zone simulations with a lot of details. It reﬂects the
latest research and the computed results compare well with measured data and
are used for both study of thermal indoor climate and energy consumption. [26]

2.1
Building energy and renovation
A building in developed countries is assumed to use between 20 - 40 % of the
total energy use. In 2004 the percentage of energy used by buildings in the EU
was 37 % of the ﬁnal energy use. In comparison the industry consumed 28 % and
the transportation consumed 32 % [35]. This means that the percentage of the
consumption used for buildings in Denmark is within the assumed percentage
but a bit lower than the average in the EU. The reason for the building energy
to be on a level with transportation and industry is because of an increase in
population, comfort levels and an advancement of building service. Furthermore,
it is because of the increased time spent inside buildings[35].
75 % of the buildings for residential use in Europe in the year 2050 have already
been build today. This means that the renovation of buildings is an important
step for reaching the targets for the EU. However the more energy eﬃcient the
buildings are, the bigger impact does the occupants have [14, 19].
Over 50 % of the buildings in Europe were built before the requirements on
energy performance became more strict. These buildings are an important part
of improving energy eﬃciency in the building stock if renovated. Furthermore,
a renovation has the potential to improve the indoor climate, especially the
thermal comfort where draft and low temperatures can be a problem [21].

and 1970s [24]. The buildings investigated in the REBUS project, Blåkildevej

2.2 Impact of occupants regarding energy use

2.2
Impact of occupants regarding energy use
The occupants have a big impact on the energy use of the building [2, 9, 10, 12,
19, 22, 34, 40] and it is well documented that diﬀerent factors such as heating
set-point and assumptions when modelling occupants in building are some of
the reasons for the actual energy use to be diﬀerent from the theoretical energy
use. A wasteful use behaviour can add up to one-third to the buildings designed
energy performance, while conservative user behaviour can save up to a third
of the buildings designed energy performance[40].
An example of this is an
experiment done by the company 3M, where their workers were told to switch
oﬀall devices, lights, etc. when not in use. This lead to a drop in electrical
consumption from 15 MW to 13 MW in 15 minutes and further down to 11 MW
after 2 hours, which correspond to a saving of 26 % in electrical consumption
[9].
Figure 2.1: Graphical explanation on how the rebound and pre-bound eﬀect
can reduce the theoretical energy saving [8]
Another interesting way the occupants can inﬂuence the building performance,
and how it makes it diﬃcult to predict the energy use, are the diﬀerence in
behaviour between households, the User-building interaction, the rebound eﬀect
and the pre-bound eﬀect. The diﬀerence in energy use between households is
because when assumption for the calculations are made they are based on the
average/standard for all users, which leads to a diﬀerence between the expected
and the actual energy use. The user-building interaction is when the user lack
understanding of low carbon technologies, which can lead to increased energy use
==PAGE:22==
6
Literature review
and discomfort. An example of this is turning the thermostat on high to speed
up the heating of the room. The rebound eﬀect is where the user consumes more
energy than before implementing energy saving because they, for example, set
the thermostat higher or leave the heater on longer than before. The pre-bound
eﬀect is when the savings are lower than expected because of wrong assumptions
regarding the building operation and occupancy, for example, there have been
assumed one person but in reality, there live four people [10]. It can also be
a lower energy use than expected due to the occupants accept lower comfort
levels. This means that if the pre-bound eﬀect was there before renovation and
the rebound eﬀect is there after a renovation, then the energy saving is not as
high as calculated [8]. See ﬁgure 2.1.
A problem with the diﬀerence between the real and the predicted energy use is
that some building systems are diﬃcult for users to understand and/or operate
and are therefore not used optimally [15]. Furthermore, a big problem is that
when the building simulation is most useful (in the design phase) the parameters
are least certain. Thereby the conﬁdence in the simulation results are limited
[6].
J.Page et al [34] have made a stochastic model for simulating occupants presence
in an oﬃce building. The results show that they are able to reproduce important
characteristics for the occupants such as the time of arrival/departure, typical
long absences and eﬀective time in the zone. This model gives a more realistic
image of how the users interact with the building since it avoids the unnatural
peaks that arise from repeating the same pattern for each occupant. The model
does however not take into account the displacement of occupants from one zone
to another.
2.3
Types of measurements and models
Nguyen et al.
[9] shows that there are many diﬀerent methods for making
measurements, to make models and to implement energy saving in buildings.
Ispace furnishings is a gadget that can detect and learn occupants behaviour.
The gadgets communicate with each other so there, for example, can be made a
group for an apartment which allows the gadgets to coordinate their actions. It
remembers the user habits under particular environmental conditions to make
changes to the indoor environment according to those habits [9].

indoor environment such as relative humidity(RH), CO2 and temperature to
occupants compared to wired systems. Applying wireless sensors at building
renovation projects can help understand the building’s performance before ren-

IDA ICE results compared to the European standard prEN 13791. The results
showed that the IDA ICE model needed to be simpliﬁed to match the results
from prEN 13791 since the model is too rough. Other validations of the pro-
gram have been made and it proves that it can be used to carry out advanced
simulations of building energy use and indoor climate in individual zones of a
building [16].
There are many diﬀerent simulation tools developed to evaluate building per-
formance. However, they are designed by researchers for research purpose and
is therefore diﬃcult for building designers to use because the designers do not
recognise them as design support tools to the same extent as design tools. This
is likely because of the knowledge needed for using the tools as well of cost and
time of simulations [15].
H. N. Knudsen et al. [29] show that questionnaires can be used for evaluating
the tenants’ satisfaction and dissatisfaction with the indoor climate.
If it is
used before and after a renovation it can be used to evaluate the result of the
renovation and if the goals for the indoor climate have been reached. To get the
most accurate answers and minimise bias it is important to get a representative
sample, a high response rate, making the questionnaire short and uncomplicated
and let the questionnaire come from someone they know and respect. The results
of this report, which is made for the REBUS project, show that the general
satisfaction with the indoor climate rose from 17 % before to 91 % after the
renovation.
2.4
Results regarding the prediction of energy
use and black box method
A black box method is where the input and output are known but the mechanism
between is hidden or unknown. The black box model can be validated using
backtesting where known data is used as input to see how well it ﬁts with the
known results [39]. C. Thomsen [22] uses a black box method where measured
data is used for making a dynamic model. The results for energy use is compared
with the measured energy use for the same time period. The dynamic modelling
is based on the program TRNSYS. It concludes that the more precise modelling
used the closer you will get to the actual energy use.
As mentioned it has been documented in various reports that there is a per-
formance gap between the actual and theoretical energy use of buildings [10,
==PAGE:24==
8
Literature review
18, 19]. A Rotimi et al. [18] have investigated how to minimise the perfor-
mance gap using thermal simulation software and compare it to available site
consumption data and building regulation compliance models. The results are
done for a hotel. It shows that the thermal simulation software produces results
that are within 12 % of the actual energy use and that it is up to 23 % more
accurate than building regulation compliance models. However, the accuracy
of the simulation is only as good as the quality of the available input data for
the thermal model. For example the results are very dependent on the outdoor
temperature [3, 18].
D. S. Østergård et al. [16] show that using the measured data to make the dy-
namic model in IDA ICE you get useful inputs and make it possible to compare
the simulation results to the actual conditions in a case house. It is concluded
that the details and the number of assumptions included in a model have a large
impact on the simulation results. The indoor temperatures have a large eﬀect
on the calculated return temperature of the radiators. However, it is complex
to estimate and model but using standard indoor temperature on 20 degrees
was not good enough. Most importantly the report concludes that the results
indicate that a simulation model can provide reasonable estimates of the actual
heating system temperatures if the model is provided with actual radiator sizes
and realistic indoor temperatures.

4.3
Solar data
For the simulations in IDA ICE, it is important for the weather ﬁle to have data
on the diﬀuse horizontal and the beam (direct) normal solar radiation. This is
used in the program for calculating the solar gain. However, it is diﬃcult to
measure so there are no available measurements in the area of the apartments.
Therefore it has to be calculated. This is done using the total solar radiation and
sky cover since it is not possible to get more than one measurement of the global
solar radiation and thereby use more precise formulas for calculating the diﬀuse
and beam radiation. The Task Group on Energy Requirements for Heating and
Cooling [1] have made an algorithm for determining diﬀuse and direct radiation
falling onto a surface. This algorithm uses the cloud cover factor and the solar
position to determine the diﬀuse and the beam solar radiation incident, under
either a clear or a cloudy sky condition, on a given surface. Since the data for the
cloud cover of the sky has to be purchased it was chosen to purchase calculated
values of the diﬀuse and direct solar radiation from the company Meteoblue [31]
instead because their calculations are better and already tested compared to the
simpliﬁed calculation with cloud cover. The data from Metroblue have hourly
values and are as described based on simulations. The simulations are made for
one day based on weather observations and each day are then joined together
into a continuous time series for the period needed. The simulation forecast
used have been validated against 10000 weather stations for many years and the
results are very close to reality [32].

4.7
Sensitivity analysis on IDA ICE parameters
The impact of the parameters in IDA ICE needs to be investigated to be able
to see which have the biggest impact on the heating consumption, measured as
power in watts, and thereby which parameters there needs to be focused on, to
get the simulations as close to the reality as possible. From the literature study,
it is known that the outdoor temperature and the indoor temperature have a
big impact on the heating consumption. It is wanted to know how big and what
other parameters which have a signiﬁcant impact on the heating consumption.
This will be done by making a version handling in IDA ICE where all relevant
parameters will be investigated and compared on the heating power.
The version handling has been made using the layout of a non-renovated apart-
ment with the known materials and their values and radiators dimension after
the dimensional heat loss including 20 percent extra capacity to be able to de-
liver higher temperatures than the 20 degrees it was calculated with. Diﬀerent
parameter changes and diﬀerent combination of parameter changes were made
in each version to check the impact. The result can be seen in section 4.1. How-
ever, it is important to state that the parameters were only tested for a winter
month and thereby the parameters might have a big impact if checked for a
summer month. Furthermore, it was only compared on the heating power and
not the temperature in the apartment even though those are shown. This is
because the simulations of the apartments will also be measured on the heating
power. But it is still important to see if the temperature is satisfying and if the
radiators are able to give the heat that is wanted.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The results from this study can be used in the future for making better and
more qualiﬁed calculations of the energy use in buildings. However there is still
a weak joint i the calculation. This is because the occupants in relation to use
of appliances and temperature set point have a huge impact on the result. As it
have been showed in the sensitivity analysis many small changes in the setup,
not just the indoor temperature, have a big impact on the ﬁnal result. However,
the sensitivity analysis have showed that it can be used to show how much the
result can vary depending on the settings. The use of this study and how it can
be improved will be discussed in the section Future work.
In general the result comply with what was found in the literary review; that
the occupant and indoor temperature have a big impact on the heating power
consumption and that the heating power becomes more accurate when using
measured data as set point. However, the analysis have only been made on two
diﬀerent apartment and to be able to have valid results it is needed to make
more models and also use diﬀerent buildings and orientations. But this study
combined with what was found in the literary review still give an idea about
the tendencies.
Some of the things found in the results and analysis section did not meet the
expectation to what was expected.

with the expectations is the measured weather. In the weather ﬁle, the solar

6.1
Assumption for thermal bridges
The thermal bridges and their impact have been investigated a lot in this report.
This is because the impact was much bigger than expected but also because the
impact varies a lot depending on if the changes are made from good to typical
or from typical to poor. Furthermore, the documentation for what the thermal
bridges are is not a normal procedure to perform on buildings. It is not very
precise results you get and it will for most cases be a qualiﬁed guess.
An
assessment of the thermal bridges is normally done at the same time as a blower
door test because it then is possible to see where the leaks are and if they are
big. This is then used for assuming the thermal bridge. Because it has been

6.2
Sensor placement
Because it is not known where the exact location of where the measurement
instruments were placed in heating season 17/18 and it has been documented
that there is a big temperature diﬀerence between multiple sensors in the same
room there is an issue about how valid the measured data is. This is another
weakness in this study. The reason why it is important where the sensors are
placed is that the data might be aﬀected by the sun, radiator, window or height
and it is not possible to correct these possible impacts on the measurements. For
example in heating season 18/19, it is known that the IC-meters were placed
on the ﬂoor and the temperature, therefore, might be higher than what was
measured. The result of increasing the temperature with one degree changed
the RMSD from 9.97 W/m2 to 8.16 W/m2 which is a signiﬁcant reduction.
Therefore, the measured temperature adds uncertainty to the results because
even though it has been shown that the general tendency is not changed the
result are either a bit higher or lower and therefore still have a diﬀerent ﬁt
to the measured data. Since the temperature has also been proven to have a
high impact on the heating power it is even more problematic. An example
of how much the measured temperature can vary depending on the placement
can be seen in ﬁgure 6.1. This ﬁgure shows the temperature measured from
three diﬀerent places in the living room of the renovated apartment with three
diﬀerent instruments. The Wireless Sensor tag have most likely been placed
so the measurement gets infected by either the radiator or the sun because it
ﬂuctuates very extreme between 14 and 28 °C. Therefore this measurement have
not been used in any simulations. Furthermore it have been showed that if the
sensor is paced on the window pane and the window is open most of the time
or all the time then the indoor temperature follows the tendency of the outdoor
temperature.

6.3
Ventilation
The schedule for the ventilation is made from an assumption based on when
the occupants most likely will use the bathroom because it was realised that
the ventilation is only on when the light is on in the bathrooms/toilets. The
ventilation in the kitchen also has a big problem regarding the accuracy of the
schedules because they are simulated using the same schedule as the bathrooms
and therefore, is on both in the morning and in the evening. As it has been
shown in the sensitivity analysis the ventilation has a big eﬀect on the spikes in
the heating power and thereby, how well the simulated heating power ﬁts with
the measured. It has also been showed that it makes a huge diﬀerence if the
ventilation is on all the time or only two hours a day.
In reality, there is a chimney eﬀect from the ventilation pipes because the air
inside is warmer and therefore lighter than the cold air outside which leads to
cold air from outside to come in through other openings/leaks. This eﬀect is
not a part of the IDA ICE simulation and might have an impact on the heating
power depending on the outside temperature and wind speed.

Chapter 7
Future work
The use of the results made in this report is the knowledge of which parameters
there has a big impact on the heating power and how to make calculations based
on the sensitivity analysis of the models. The models of the future can calculate
how diﬀerent scenarios will aﬀect the renovation potential based on the inputs
and thereby give an interval for the energy needed based on diﬀerent sensitivity
inputs. Ideally, the input values will be based on some kind of measurements
or questionnaire so it is known what temperatures the occupant likes or if they
have a lot of equipment. The sensitivity analysis made will hopefully be able
to be used as a guidance for which parameters that have a big impact on the
heating power and thereby as stated to be more careful to get these values as
precise as possible. If this can be implemented it will lead to more realistic
results and thereby minimise the performance gap.
Ideally, it will be possible to make three standard occupant behaviour mod-
els based on three types of occupant behaviour. One with high consumption,
consisting of high indoor temperature and high use of electrical devised and
lighting. Another with average consumption, consisting of average indoor tem-
perature and average use of electrical devises and lighting. The last is for the low
consuming occupant which consist of low temperature and low use of electrical
devised and lighting. These three standard occupant settings can then be used
for calculating a result given as an interval. However, to be able to make these
three occupant behaviour model realistic many diﬀerent measurements needs

A hope for the three diﬀerent behaviour consumption’s is, that the results will

and thereby are able to save both money and be able to improve the emission
of harmful substances to the environment while still obtaining a good indoor
environment.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The study have been done on social houses where there is both renovated and
non renovated apartments. They have only been investigated on their perfor-
mance in the winder to be able to gain knowledge about how to make better
simulations and what parameters have big impact on the energy use. The mea-
surements and the quality diﬀers a lot both regarding time between measure-
ments and diﬀerences between the temperatures measured in the same room.
The diﬀerence in temperature have been shown for two rooms and it show that
the diﬀerence is signiﬁcant - up to several decrees.
It can be concluded that the use of measured temperatures for the indoor set
point and measured weather instead of standard values will lead to a heating
power closer to the measured heating power as well as ﬁtting the measured
heating powers tendencies better. It have been veriﬁed by a sensitivity analysis
that the occupants and the assumption made for the simulations have a big
impact on the simulated heating power. The more accurate the input is and
the closer it is to the reality the better the simulated result becomes.
The
simulated results are more dependent on the daily changes such as outdoor
temperature and sun. Therefore the simulated heating power ﬂuctuates more
than the measured heating power. This as well as not being able to simulate the
occupants exact behaviour for each day is some of the reasons why the simulated
heating power will never ﬁt 100 %. The occupants will aﬀect the heating power
by making it more ﬂuctuating and add spikes when they for example open
==PAGE:80==
64
Conclusion
windows or have guests.
For the diﬀerent parameter variations done in this report it have been showed
that the total heating power can vary between 66 % to 156 % of the total heat-
ing power for the initial detailed simulation. The saving achieved by renovating
the buildings when looking at the amount of district heating used is 73 % for
the two investigated apartments. It is an reasonable saving since most of the
building envelope has been improved. If constructions defaults happens during
erection/renovation it can have a big impact on the energy use and occupant
satisfaction with the indoor climate. The study can be used in the future for
making the theoretical energy use more accurate and thereby lower the per-
formance gap by making people aware of which parameters to be more precise
on. Furthermore, it can be used for guidance to make some general occupant
behaviour model based om measurements to be able to see what the energy
use are based on the diﬀerent occupant behaviour and thereby make it more
clear for the residence how big an impact they have and what they can save by
becoming more aware of their consumption.
