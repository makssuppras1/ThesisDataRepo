Abstract
Sorting particles of different plastics is important to increase their reusability. Sorting
shredded plastic particles by using a vat of water and a vertically moving sieve is a method
used by the company Trebo. The project aims to create a model which can replicate this
process. An experiment was performed on a smaller scale to use as reference for the
model.
By using a CFD-DEM coupling, the model was developed and found to replicate im-
portant aspects of the particle behaviour during it’s suspension. The model uses a Di
Felice drag model which accounts for the presence of other particles by using the volume
fraction of the particles within a fluid cell. No volume smoothing is used in the model,
since it introduced complexities and didn’t improve the accuracy of the particle behaviour.
Volume smoothing should however not be disregarded as a tool, only for this case there
was not a reason to increase the complexity.
The model simulated sorting plastics, both with a uniform and a non-uniform diameter.
The sorting was quantified with a sorting quality parameter which showed how much the
particles are sorted with each downstroke of the sieve. It also showed how using a non-
uniformly sized particles affects the sorting process.
The project highlights the importance of using a large timestep. When a small timestep
is used the rate of volume transfer between cells become too large, causing unphysical
particle behaviours.
The model could be used to further develop the plastic sorting method and gives a
notable insight into the particle behaviour.

1
Introduction
Plastics have become one of the most common materials used for a range of products
and their packaging. They are an integral part of this society and their correct disposal is
of high importance. Within the production of a single product, many types of plastics are
often used. By the end of a multi-type plastic product’s life cycle the different plastics will
need to be separated before they can be recycled. Separated, they can be disposed of
properly or even put back into a production line to be reused.
Since these plastic particles don’t have the exact same density, they can be sorted
by gravity. By shredding the plastics into particles small enough to mostly contain one
type of plastic and suspending them in water they can be accurately sorted. This method
is implemented by Trebo. plastic particulates are put into a vat of water with a sieve at
the bottom. The sieve has large enough holes for the water but not the particles to pass
through. The sieve is then moved up and down to repeatedly suspend the particles and
let gravity sort them by density.
The aim of the project is to replicate a smaller scale of this process by using a CFD-
DEM coupled simulation with the commercial CFD program STAR-CCM+. The model
should be able to recreate a sorting process with two different density plastics to a visual
and quantifiable degree. An experiment was performed which will act as a reference for
the development of the model. The objective of developing the smaller scale model is to
further the understanding of the sorting process and determine the appropriate methods
to use. Based on the findings of this project a full scale model could be developed, this
is however not within the scope of the project. Certain limitations exist with CFD-DEM
coupling which will be explored.

2.2
Fluid-Particle Coupling
2.2.1
Averaged Volume Method
With a Eulerian-Lagrangian approach, the particle motion is driven by the flow and
gravity. One-way coupling in STAR-CCM+ means the particles are affected by the fluid
but the fluid is not influenced by the presence of the particles. The packing of the particles
in the plastic sorting case is such that this one-way coupling method is insufficient. A two-
way coupling introduces a way for the particles to affect the fluid flow [8]. This is mainly
done using two methods:

• IFM (Immersed Family Method)
• AVM (Averaged Volume Method)
In the IFM method the mesh used is fine enough so that a single cell is smaller than
a particle. On the other hand, the AVM method uses a mesh where each fluid cell is
larger than a particle. The benefit of the IFM method is accuracy, as the fluid forces
are integrated over the surface of the particle. Compared to the AVM method in which
fluid forces within a cell are applied to the particle with empirical formulas [9]. Due to the
computational advantage of the AVM method, especially for the magnitude of particles
that are involved in the plastic sorting, it was chosen for the project.

Plastic Sorting Model
4.1
Model’s Motion and Geometry
4.1.1
Sieve Motion
Two main methods were considered for the motion of the sieve. For the first method
the cylindrical domain remains still and the sieve is moved up and down. This method
would require the sieve to be modelled exactly with overset mesh. With an exact geomet-
rical model of the sieve, each hole has to be resolved with a mesh fine enough to capture
the velocity gradient and pressure drop through it. With the other method the whole do-
main is moved, along with the sieve. The mesh would be stationary in this case and the
mesh around the sieve would not need to be much finer than it’s surrounding.
Since the method of coupling the Eulerian-Lagrangian phase is the AVM method it is
recommended to have a cell characteristic length to particle diameter ratio above 3 [4]. In
order to not be constrained by the option of smoothing, discussed in 5.4.2, and to avoid
having to fully resolve the sieve, the moving domain method was chosen.
Using a python program (see appendix F.1) a table was generated for each instance.
The generated table had two columns: a time column and a velocity column. These two
parameters are interpreted into STAR-CCM+ to determine the movement of the sieve.
To make sure the movement was physical it’s acceleration is limited to 200 mm
s2 which is a
value that represents the approximate average acceleration of the sieve in the experiment.
It is assumed that the upwards movement of the sieve in the experiment does not
have a significant impact on the particle packing or the comparability between the model.
While the sieve moves downwards, the particles are suspended and therefore the down-
wards movement is of sole interest to simulate. Therefore, to save computation time, the
movement in the plastic sorting model is strictly downwards. Making sure to pause the
sieve at the end of the downstroke long enough allows the particles to fully settle before
the sieve moves downwards again.

4.2
Initialization and Boundaries
4.2.1
Boundaries
The no-slip condition is applied to the walls of the cylinder and it’s reference frame
needs to be considered. To emulate the experimental conditions while the fluid region
moves, the no-slip reference frame of the cylinder walls can not be relative to the cylinder.
Instead, the no-slip condition will be relative to the initial point or Lab reference frame.
This ensures that relative to the walls’ no-slip condition, the sieve moves down.
The top and bottom boundaries of the cylinder should have minimal interactions with
the flow and no interactions with the particles. These boundaries are set to the Velocity
Inlet boundary type. At the velocity inlet, the flow velocity is known and set to 0 m
s with
respect to the Lab reference frame.
4.2.2
Particle Injection
Particles with a 6 mm diameter are injected into the volume of the upper cylinder by a
random injector. To replicate the experiment the number of particles injected is 9, 900 The
random particle injector is only available for DEM particles and is appropriate since the or-
dering of the particles is not controlled for in the experiment and it allows for injection into
a part that intersects with a defined region in STAR-CCM+ [8]. The particles are injected
into the populating cylinder, shown on figure 4.2. The populating cylinder has a height of
0.18 m After the particles have been injected they are allowed to settle completely, which
should happen within one second. The fluid displaced by the injected particles is ac-
counted for to some degree in STAR-CCM+ they do however introduce some disruptions
in the flow and therefore extra time is allowed for the particles and the flow to settle. Seven
seconds was found to be sufficient time before moving the sieve.
4.2.3
Pressure initialization
Due to the vertical pressure gradient introduced by the gravity the pressure in the fluid
region will have to be initialized in a gradient. With a standard initialization the pressure is
uniform in the region. This causes a pressure wave to appear at the start of the simulation,
which can be negated by initializing the pressure closer to it’s expected steady state.

The method of meshing has a big influence on the stability of the simulation. A cell
to particle size ratio smaller than 3.82 has been shown to increase the numerical insta-

length to particle diameter ratio of 1.63 has been found to prevent instability [5]. This was
considered for the generation of the mesh.
The mesh is tetrahedral general cylinder mesher with a base size of 22 mm and two
prism layers with a total thickness of 1.5 mm. The number of layers specified for the height
of the general cylinder mesher is 23.
Figure 4.3 shows the surface of the mesh at the sieve. The mesh is coloured according
to the cubic root of the cell’s volume, acting as it’s characteristic length. The lowest values
represent the cells closest to the cylinder wall, or prism layers. Two prism layers with a
total thickness of 1.5 mm are included for some increased resolution at the walls. the total
thickness of these prism layers is such that the particles’ centroid never crosses into them
as they intersect with the wall before entering the cell. Therefore the size of these cells is
not of concern with regards to numerical instability.

The effect of the timestep was examined further by injecting a single particle into the

limit of a single cell and allowed to fall freely with varying timesteps. The magnitude of
the particle’s acceleration was monitored and an example with the timestep of 0.005 s is

An acceleration of a physical particle would be a smooth decline as the particle reaches
it’s terminal velocity. Figure 5.12 shows the particle decelerating quickly at points exactly
coinciding when the particle’s centroid crosses from one cell to another. In order to quan-
tify these unphysical jumps, the particle’s acceleration rate of change, or jerk, is calcu-
lated. The jerk of the example with the timestep 0.005 s is shown on figure 5.13. The jerk
for other timesteps examined are shown in appendix C.
As the particle is first quickly decelerated and then accelerated, often with a slight
overshoot, the maximum jerk difference was chosen as the quantifying parameter. The
maximum jerk difference refers to the absolute difference in magnitude between the high-
est and lowest peaks in jerk for a simulation. Each simulation was run so the particle
would cross between cells five times and the maximum jerk difference was registered.
This parameter is plotted against the timestep of the simulation in figure 5.14.
Figure 5.14 shows an apparent correlation between the timestep of the simulation and
the magnitude of the jerk which a particle is subjected to. Although not directly comparable
to the plastic sorting model where the volume fraction of the particles within each cell is
much greater, this does give an indication to how a too low timestep can cause weird
particle behaviour. This is likely a product of the volume source of the particle moving
from one cell to another, meaning the volume available to the fluid is increased with a
rate dependant on the timestep. The smaller the timestep, the higher the rate of volume
change within a cell.

5.3
Young’s Modulus
An assumption was made that reducing the particle’s Young’s modulus by a factor
of ×100 would not impact the results of the simulated sorting. This is verified by compar-
ing the sorting performance of a simulation with a Young’s modulus of 10 and 100 MPa. A
realistic Young’s modulus, in the range of 2000–4000 MPa requires much more computa-
tional resources to simulate in good time. Therefore the effects of increasing it by only a
factor of ×10 will be examined.
The sorting quality is the parameter used to quantify the sorting performance of the
model. Perfectly sorted particles would have very clear two layers of particles. Their
interface would be the middle of the combined particle bed height, given the same number
of particles of both densities. All the denser particles would be below the average height
of the particles and the less dense particles would all be above the average height of
the particles, given the same number of particles of each count. Realistically, the particle
packing is not completely uniform and the surface of the particle bed is not perfectly flat
in it’s settled position.
Despite this, the average particle height is an appropriate variable from which to de-
fine the ideal vertical position of the interface between the two density layers. Using the
average particle height, the number of particles of both densities below this point are
counted. Equation 5.2 gives the sorting quality of the settled particle bed since the lower
layer should ideally only be populated by the denser particles. The sorting quality should
have a value of around 0.5 at the injection of the particles which would indicate an even
distribution of particles of both densities. The highest possible sorting quality is 1 making
the expected range [0.5 : 1].

The base mesh size is a parameter in STAR-CCM+ which specifies the value upon
which all relative size controls are determined. Increasing the base mesh size generally
increases the size of the mesh cells making it coarser. The Plastic sorting model uses the
finest usable mesh that does not introduce significant numerical errors.
Figure 5.16 shows the settling times for simulations with varying mesh base sizes. The
difference increasing this parameter has on the settling time does not seem great. The
statistics of the settling times are shown in table 5.2 and based on that data an ANOVA
test finds no significant difference with a p-value of 0.63.
5.4.2
Finer Mesh
Making the mesh finer without breaking the lower limits of the AVM can be done via
smoothing. Two options for smoothing exist in STAR-CCM+. The first method, called
volume source smoothing, uses a coarser sub-grid consisting of grouped fluid mesh cells
called Particle smooth source group. Like the fluid mesh cells, the sub-grid is static relative
to the region. The volume source of particles within a PSSG cell is spread equally to all
fluid cells within the PSSG, functioning the same as a coarser mesh.
The issue with the particle’s jerk as described in section 5.2.2 is not circumvented with
this smoothing method. Figure 5.18 shows the acceleration of a large particle (20 mm

7
Conclusion
This project aimed to replicate the experiment with a CFD-DEM coupled model and
demonstrate it’s sorting ability to a quantifiable degree. Using the visuals and the timings
obtained from the experiment, the model was developed. Showing similar layering and
characteristic particle behaviour, the model was partly successful in replicating the exper-
iment. The model successfully sorted particles of two different densities and showed the
effects of varying the particle diameter. Using the sorting quality parameter the sorting
was quantified.
The model could for example be used to examine the effects of varying the sieve
displacement, cylinder radius or number of particles. Comparisons between simulation
variations could provide an insight into the particle layering or sorting quality.
However, the model was not accurate when comparing the settling times with the
experimental values. Further examining other drag models or adjusting the empirical
constants in the Di Felice drag model could be of interest to achieve a more physically
representative model. Neither could the model be used to develop the specific geometry
of the sieve as that would require a mesh much smaller than the particles.
The importance of considering the timestep for a CFD-DEM simulation was highlighted
by the findings of the project. A lower timestep caused a large jerk for the particles, re-
sulting in a very unphysical particle behaviour. Additionally, a too fine mesh would cause
instabilities and therefore a coarse mesh had to be used. Should the fluid solution re-
quire a higher resolution the volume smoothing option could be considered. The model
assumes that the particles used are spherical and uniform in shape which might not accu-
rately represent a realistic case of plastic sorting at Trebo. Irregularly shaped particles will
have anisotropic properties such as drag which should be considered for further review.
Important particle behaviours were represented by using a CFD-DEM coupling to sim-
ulate the plastic sorting. The findings of this project could assist with the development of
plastic sorting methods and their applications.
