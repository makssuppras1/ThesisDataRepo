Abstract
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is a commonly used tool to model breaking waves, with
turbulence models playing a key role in capturing the flow dynamics. Two-equation models,
such as the Wilcox (2006) k −ω, where k is the turbulent kinetic energy and ω is the specific
dissipation rate, is widely used for these types of simulations. However, these models tend to
overestimate both turbulence production and the undertow strength in the inner surf zone.
To address this issue, the lag model, a turbulence model that introduces a third governing
equation for the eddy viscosity, νt, is tested as a potential solution. The lag model was first
tested in single-phase flows; flow over a backward facing step and flow around a cylinder, and
compared with the Wilcox (1988) and Wilcox (2006) k −ω models. The notable difference
between the two Wilcox models is the stress limiter, which was introduced in the 2006 version
of the model. The single-phase simulations showed that the lag model exhibited a similar
effect as the Wilcox (2006) stress limiter though of a higher magnitude. After confirming
that the lag model could predict simple flow scenarios, it was applied to the complex flow of
breaking waves. Firstly, the stability of the model for multiphase flow was tested, which was
based on Larsen and Fuhrman (2018)’s finding that nearly all two-equation closure models
are unconditionally unstable. The model was indeed found to be unstable and a stabilizing
term was added to the model. The stabilizing of the model prevented excessive turbulence
growth in nearly potential flow regions before waves breaking. When applied to spilling and
plunging breaker cases, the lag model demonstrated its ability to overall capture the dynam-
ics of the breaking wave well; however it overestimated the turbulent kinetic energy, k, in the
inner surf zone, for the spilling breaker, while underestimating k for the plunging breaker. In
both cases the lag model overestimated the undertow strength. A stabilized version of Wilcox
(2006) k −ω referred to as LF18 case 5 (Larsen and Fuhrman 2018), on the other hand, esti-
mated turbulence for the spilling breaker even higher than the lag model but more accurately
captured the undertow strength (though still higher than measured undertow values). The
lag model could therefore not improve the simulated results compared to existing turbulence
models, and the LF18 case 5 model remains the preferred model for predicting undertow
strength. Further development of turbulence models is required to improve the accuracy of
wave breaking simulations, particularly in the inner surf zone. The study highlights the need
for continued refinement of turbulence models to better capture complex phenomena, such
as the surf zone dynamics of breaking waves.

Introduction
In coastal engineering, accurately modeling wave dynamics is fundamental to understanding
their impact on coastal environments. Waves are a primary driver of sediment transport,
shaping coastal morphology and influencing shoreline stability over time (Sawaragi 1995).
Moreover, waves have the potential to directly or indirectly (through sediment transport)
damage infrastructure. With the ongoing effects of climate change, the frequency and inten-
sity of extreme wave climate events are increasing, posing significant risks to both natural
ecosystems and human-made structures along the coast.
In this context, Computational
Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has become an essential tool for investigating the complex processes
associated with breaking waves, offering detailed insights into wave motion, turbulence, and
interactions between waves and both the seabed and coastal structures. By enabling high-
resolution simulations, CFD provides a deeper understanding of the underlying physics of
these flows, which is critical for predicting the behavior of coastal systems under various
wave climate scenarios. In response to climate change, there will be an increasing need for
accurately predicting wave dynamics in the future. These predictions will support the design
of resilient and sustainable coastal infrastructure that protects vulnerable areas and promotes
the long-term viability of coastal development.
Modeling breaking waves involves complex flow dynamics, ranging from nearly potential flow
before breaking to the wave rising out off the water and eventually curling over the crest and
forming of a vertical jet stream, which splashes down into the water and subsequently form an
unsteady, turbulent wave boundary layer. The complex multiphase nature of surface waves,
caused by their interaction with air, poses an additional challenge to modeling. Incoming
waves generate a flow toward the shoreline, which is balanced by a counterflow in the opposite
direction, known as the undertow (Svendsen 1984). The undertow is strongest near the seabed
and plays a significant role in transporting sediment away from the coast, making it an
essential part of modeling breaking waves (Sawaragi 1995). Breaking waves are categorized
into different types of breakers.
This project focuses on spilling and plunging breakers.
Spilling breakers break gradually with a gentle descent, while plunging breakers are more
powerful, developing a steep, vertical face and a curling crest as they collapse. The region
between the wave breaking point and the shoreline is referred to as the surf zone, which
is commonly divided into two regions: the outer surf zone and the inner surf zone.
For
spilling breakers, the outer region refers to the area where the wave breaks and loses its
shape, while the inner region is characterized by the shoreward movement of a surface roller
with minimal change in form. In the case of plunging breakers, these two regions are more
distinctly defined: the outer surf zone extends from the point where the wave begins to break
to where the plunger hits the water, and the inner surf zone spans from this impact point to
the shoreline.
Many experimental studies of breaking waves have been made (e.g.
Ting & Kirby 1994;
Standsby & Feng 2005; Lara et al. 2006). The experiments can mainly show the effect of waves
on a small scale, whereas numerical modeling is an essential tool for scaling up and predicting
wave behavior in larger environments. The simulation of breaking waves involves turbulence
1
==PAGE:6==
modeling. The turbulence theory can be described using the Navier-Stokes equations, which
different types of models are designed to solve. The most commonly used approach to turbu-
lence modeling is Reynolds-averaging of the Navier-Stokes equations (RANS), which models
the effects of turbulence on the flow, rather than modeling the turbulence itself. The effect of
turbulence is estimated by the turbulent stresses using a turbulent viscosity, also called the
eddy viscosity, νt, which is governed by two equations. These models are therefore referred
to as two-equation RANS models. The most common two-equation RANS models are the
k −ϵ and the k −ω, where the k represents the turbulent kinetic energy, ϵ is the dissipation
rate of the turbulent kinetic energy, and ω is the specific dissipation rate. These models
are less detailed than alternative approaches (which will be described in the following) and
have shortcomings when simulating complex turbulent flows, but are computationally eﬀi-
cient and widely applied in engineering practice. Therefore the two-equation models have also
been widely used to simulate waves (e.g. Lin & Liu 1998; Bradford 2000; Mayer & Madsen
2000; Jacobsen et al. 2012; Brown et al. 2016; Larsen & Fuhrman 2018). Other approaches
to turbulence modeling include Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS), Large Eddy Simulation
(LES), and the Reynolds Stress Model (RSM). DNS solves the Navier–Stokes equations with-
out any turbulence modeling, capturing all turbulent scales with high accuracy; however, its
computational cost restricts its application to small or highly simplified flows. Some studies
of breaking waves have been made using DNS; e.g., Wang et al. (2016); Chan et al. (2021).
LES resolves the large turbulent eddies while modeling the smaller ones, offering improved
accuracy over simpler models yet still demanding considerable computational resources for
large-scale simulations. There are also examples of LES being used to simulate breaking
waves e.g.
Christensen & Deigaard (2001); Zhou et al. (2017).
The RSM, on the other
hand, directly solves transport equations for each component of the Reynolds stress tensor,
providing a detailed description of turbulence at a lower computational expense than DNS
and LES; it has been used effectively to simulate both spilling and plunging breakers (Li &
Fuhrman 2022). Despite these advances, two-equation models remain the most common due
to their relatively low computational demands, making them suitable for larger simulation
scenarios.
In previous studies, two-equation models have shown a tendency to overproduce turbulent
kinetic energy, even in pre-breaking regions where the flow is nearly potential (Lin & Liu
1998; Brown et al. 2016). Mayer & Madsen (2000) and Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) found
that nearly all two-equation models are unconditionally unstable. This instability leads to
an overproduction of turbulent kinetic energy and the eddy viscosity in both in the nearly
potential flow region (pre-breaking) and in the surf zone (post-breaking). Consequently, it
was impossible to accurately capture the wave physics using these models. Even in simple
wave train simulations, where the flow is nearly potential, there was significant production of
turbulent kinetic energy, causing the wave height to decrease over time. Larsen & Fuhrman
(2018) analyzed several two-equation models and discovered a simple way to stabilize them
by adding a single term to the model. Their stabilized Wilcox (2006) k −ω model signifi-
cantly improved wave modeling and was tested on both spilling breakers (Larsen & Fuhrman
2
==PAGE:7==
2018) and plunging breakers (Larsen et al. 2020; Li et al. 2022). Although the stabilized
model showed substantial better results compared to earlier models, it still overestimated
the turbulence in the inner surf zone and a stronger undertow in this region compared to
experimental observations (Ting & Kirby 1994). This shows that further modifications to
the two-equations models are still needed for the models to accurately capture the complex
dynamics of breaking waves. Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) were, however, able to more accu-
rately predict the strength of the undertow in the inner surf zone by reducing the value of
Wilcox (2006)’s stress limiter, λ1, to zero (explained in Section 2). However, this came at the
expense of the wave actually breaking, thus the model clearly did not capture the physical
wave dynamics and, therefore, should not be used to model breaking waves. Nevertheless,
this remains an interesting finding regarding the effect of the stress limiter. In a study by
Wilcox (2006), another model, the so-called lag model (first described by Olsen & Coakley
2001), was mentioned to exhibit a similar effect as the stress limiter. The lag model is not a
widely used model and have not been tested in simulations of breaking waves.
Building on these findings, the present study aims to improve breaking wave simulations
by addressing the limitations of existing two-equation models, particularly in the inner surf
zone. To achieve this, the performance of the lag model is tested. The lag model introduces a
third governing equation, to the existing two-equations models, describing the eddy viscosity,
νt. This model provides an alternative method for turbulence modeling, by incorporating a
delayed response of eddy viscosity to changes in the flow and thereby accounting for non-
equilibrium effects (Olsen & Coakley 2001). The aim is to test whether this approach can
capture the physics in wave dynamics, particularly in the surf zone, where traditional models
have shown limitations. The lag model is not a widely used model, thus the behavior of the
model is not well documented. The model was therefore first evaluated in simple single-phase
flows (flow over a backward-facing step and flow around a cylinder) before being applied to
complex breaking wave scenarios, including both spilling and plunging breakers. In this work,
the lag term is added to the Wilcox (2006) k −ω model. Given that traditional two-equation
models have been shown to be unconditionally unstable (Mayer & Madsen 2000; Larsen
& Fuhrman 2018), a stability analysis of the lag model is also performed. Key metrics in
the wave simulations, such as wave height evolution, turbulent kinetic energy and undertow
profiles, are analyzed and compared to experimental data from Ting & Kirby (1994), as well
as model results from the stabilized k −ω model described in Larsen & Fuhrman (2018).
This study provides insight into the lag model’s ability to capture flow and wave dynamics
and offers a general understanding of its performance and potential applications.

2.1
The closure problem

modeling turbulence will be presented in this section. In turbulent flows, the chaotic and fluc-
tuating nature of the fluid makes it diﬀicult to solve the governing equations directly. Unlike
steady flows, where the fluid moves smoothly and predictably, turbulence involves random
fluctuations in velocity, pressure, and other flow properties (Sumer & Fuhrman 2020). This

which simplifies the equations by separating the flow variables into two components: a mean
(time averaged) part representing the steady flow and a fluctuating part that captures the
turbulence (Sumer & Fuhrman 2020). The velocity is given by

To address the closure problem, two-equation turbulence models have been developed. These
models approximate the Reynolds stresses by relating them to known quantities such as the
turbulence kinetic energy density, k, and the specific turbulence dissipation rate, ω. Two-
equation models like the k −ϵ, and k −ω models use transport equations for these variables
to estimate the turbulent viscosity, also known as the eddy viscosity, νt, which can then be
used to estimate the Reynolds stresses without solving for the unknowns components (Sumer
& Fuhrman 2020). By approximating the Reynolds stresses using known quantities, two-
equation models offer a practical solution to the closure problem, allowing for the simulation
of turbulent flows like those found in breaking waves, without modeling turbulent fluctuations.
While these models are eﬀicient, they do have limitations when it comes to capturing the
full complexity of turbulence in highly dynamic regions like breaking waves. In the following
sections, the original Wilcox (2006) k −ω model and a stabilized version of the model, and
the lag model will be presented.

2.4
Lag model
The lag model, first introduced by Olsen & Coakley (2001), extends the conventional two-
equation turbulence modeling approach by incorporating a third governing equation to de-
scribe the evolution of eddy viscosity, νt. In this study, the lag term is applied alongside the
Wilcox (2006) k −ω model. The lag equation allows the model to solve for non-equilibrium
eddy viscosity, which are not well captured by traditional two-equation models which assume
an equilibrium eddy viscosity of νt = k/ω (Olsen & Coakley 2001). The governing equation
for the eddy viscosity in the lag model is expressed as:

transient behavior Olsen & Coakley (2001). The Wilcox (2006) k −ω model with the added
lag term will be referred to as the lag model.

3
Application of lag model in single phase flows
3.1
Model description
Before applying the lag model to more complex flows, it was first tested in two simple cases
involving steady flow over a backward-facing step and flow around a cylinder, both in 2D.
Testing a turbulence model on a simple flow case is an essential step before applying it to
more complicated flow scenarios. The single-phase flow scenarios were simulated using the
lag model, the Wilcox (2006) k−ω model, where the stress limiter λ1 = 0.875 and the Wilcox
(1988) k −ω model, where λ1 = 0. This was done in order to test Wilcox (2006)’s statement
that lag model demonstrated an effect similar to the stress limiter λ1. The CFD simulations
for the current study were performed using OpenFoam v2306. The source code for the Wilcox
(1988) k−ω model is available in OpenFoam and was used for the simulations. Li et al. (2022)
provided the source code for the single-phase Wilcox (2006) k −ω model, which used (2.8)
and (2.9), but without the buoyancy production term for the k equation, as the density is
assumed constant in single-phase flows. The lag model was implemented for in OpenFOAM
using the the framework for the Wilcox (2006) k −ω model. To adapt the model for the lag
formulation, the stress-limiter term ˜˜ω (2.17) was removed. This modification was necessary
since the lag term accounts for non-equilibrium eddy viscosity, effectively replacing the role
of the stress-limiter.
Consequently, the production term for ω is reduced to Pω = α p0,
since ω = ˜˜ω. The lag model reformulates the calculation of νt, which is a parameter deeply
embedded in the OpenFOAM source code. To accommodate this, the equation for νt was
implemented with a dummy variable named nutLag in the code and setting nut = nutLag.
This approach preserves the functionality of the built-in variable νt within OpenFOAM while
allowing the lag-specific νt calculations to proceed independently.

The lag model was implemented in OpenFoam, using the frame work for the multiphase
stabilized Wilcox (2006) k −ω by Li et al. (2022). The same modifications as described for
the single-phase model were adapted to the multiphase model. Additionally, the buoyancy
production term Pb (2.15) in the governing ω equation (2.9) which has previously been ex-
pressed as a function of νt, must now be expressed by k/ω, since the eddy viscosity is no
longer directly computed as νt = k/ω.
5.2
Periodic wave train
To demonstrate the effect of stabilizing the lag model, CFD simulations of a simple long-
term periodic wave train were conducted.
This case represents a free surface wave with
nearly potential flow conditions beneath it; therefore, there should be little to no turbulence
production in the simulation. This serves as a fundamental test that the turbulence model
must pass to ensure it meets the physical conditions of free surface waves before being applied
to more complex wave scenarios. The OpenFOAM setup for this wave train simulation was
developed by Larsen & Fuhrman (2018). Minor adjustments to the divergence scheme were
necessary to stabilize the progressive wave. These modifications followed the methodology
described in Larsen et al. (2019). The time derivatives were discretized using a Crank-Nicolson
scheme with a weighting factor of ψ = 0.3 and the convective terms were discretized using a
blend of an upwind scheme, minmod divergence schemes and a central difference schemes. The
computational domain spanned one wavelength, with periodic boundary conditions applied
at the inlet and outlet to replicate a continuous progressive wave. The bottom boundary
was set as a slip condition to maintain nearly potential flow behavior. The computational
grid consisted of regular cells with dimensions ∆x = ∆z = 0.01 m. The parameters of the
incoming wave were set equal to those used in the spilling breaker experiments conducted
by Ting & Kirby (1994). The initial wave was defined with a wave height of Hw = 0.125
m, a water depth h = 0.4 m, a wave period T = 2 s, and a wave number kw = 1.66
and no net volume change. The initial condition the turbulent kinetic energy was set as
k = k0 = ω∞ν and the dissipation rate as ω = ω∞= 2.71√p0.
The turbulent kinetic
energy production was calculated using (4.3) and found to be p0 = 0.55 s−2. The initial eddy
viscosity was set so that νt/ν = 1. The progressive wave train was simulated using both
the non-stabilized and stabilized versions of the lag model. The stabilization was controlled
through the implementation of the stress limiter parameter, λ2. By setting λ2 = 0, the model
is reduced to the non-stabilized version and by setting λ2 = 0.05 the model was stabilized.

5.3
Simulations of spilling breaking waves
After confirming that the stabilized lag model (which from here will be referred to as the lag
model) passed the test of simulating a progressive wavetain without any production of tur-
bulence, it’s performance was then evaluated when simulating spilling and plunging breaker
waves. These types of breaking waves have varying physical characteristics, but both types
significantly impact sediment transport along the coast. Including both types of breaking
waves allows for a comprehensive assessment of the model’s ability to simulate diverse wave
dynamics. Previous breaking wave simulations using RANS models tend to overestimate tur-
bulence in the surf zone, leading to a stronger undertow than observed in experiments. The
objective of this next section is to evaluate the performance of the lag model, particularly
in terms of turbulence production and undertow strength in the surf zone. As previously
mentioned Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) tested the stabilized Wilcox (2006) k −ω model with
different combinations of the stress limiters λ1 and λ2 in a spilling breaker simulation. The
results of these simulations, served as the basis for the current study. Particularly two com-
binations of the stress limiters; LF18 case 3: λ1 = 0.0 and LF18 case 5: λ1 = 0.2 (both cases
had λ2 = 0.05). LF18 Case 5 was found to be the preferred turbulence model for simulating
breaking waves, but still overestimated the undertow strength in the inner surf zone. LF18
case 3 overestimated turbulence in the outer surf zone but more closely resembled measured
data in the inner surf zone. However, Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) noted that although LF18
case 3 produced an undertow strength similar to measured results, it did so for incorrect
physical reasons and should not be used to predict undertow in the inner surf zone. They
recommended using the model in LF18 case 5. Recall that, since Wilcox (2006) noted that
the lag model demonstrated behavior similar to the stress limiter, it was deemed worthwhile
to investigate whether the lag model could correct the overestimation of undertow in the
inner surf zone while accurately predicting undertow in the outer surf zone.

beneath the wave following the breaking wave, as shown in figure 5.4(a). This phenomenon
was not observed in the study by Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) and should not occur, as that wave
is in the nearly potential flow region. This indicated that some turbulence had ”leaked” from

strong scientific justification for this specific value (Larsen & Fuhrman 2018). The value
could therefore be modified and it was tested whether increasing λ2 to 0.1 would reduce the

viscosity νt/ν simulated using LF18 case 5 with the two different values of λ2. It is evident
that increasing λ2 from 0.05 to 0.1 effectively removed the overproduction of turbulence
beneath the second incoming wave. Consequently, this adjusted value was applied to the rest
of the simulations (note that the lag model and the LF18 case 3 was also simulated using
both λ2 values and showed similar results). Additionally, the combined and individual effects
of using the bounded time scheme, as well as the two different λ2 values, were analyzed in
terms of their impact on turbulent kinetic energy and undertow in both pre-breaking and
post-breaking positions (results in Appendix). The overall effect was found to be minor.
Therefore, a bounded time scheme and λ2 = 0.1 were applied to all subsequent simulations.

6
Conclusion
In this study, the performance of the lag model in predicting wave breaking dynamics was
evaluated, specifically focusing on its ability to predict undertow strength and turbulent ki-
netic energy, k in comparison with two LF18 models. This was motivated by the known issue
in two-equation models, such as the LF18 models, where an over-production of turbulence,
and an overestimation of undertow strength, especially in the inner surf zone, is observed.
The aim was to assess whether the lag model, with an added stabilization term, could of-
fer an improvement to the existing two-equation models, by accurately predicting both the
turbulence production and the undertow strength in the inner surf zone.
Before applying the lag model to wave breaking scenarios, the model was tested in simple,
single-phase flow conditions to evaluate its performance. These simulations showed that the
lag model was able to capture essential flow features and exhibited a similar effect to the
Wilcox (2006) stress limiter, λ1. The findings here were consistent with earlier observations
by Wilcox (2006), where it was also noted that the lag model performed similarly to the stress
limiter. The results from the single-phase flow tests provided a foundation for the subsequent
application of the model to more complex wave breaking scenarios, where the dynamics are
inherently more complicated.
As demonstrated by Menter (1992c) and Larsen & Fuhrman (2018), nearly all two-equation
turbulence models are unconditionally unstable. A stability test conducted on the lag model
confirmed that it was also unstable. To address this issue, a stabilizing term was introduced,
effectively deactivating the turbulence model when the flow is nearly potential. The stabilized
model was then tested on spilling and plunging breaker cases, where it demonstrated stability
over long simulation periods. The lag model overall performed reasonably well in the breaking
wave cases, however, it still could not resolve the issue of overestimated undertow strength
in the inner surf zone. This region, where turbulence production is strongest, is particularly
diﬀicult to model due to the unstable turbulent fluctuations. The lag model did, however,
show a tendency to predict turbulence levels that were lower than the LF18 case 5 model in
the inner surf zone, but still not as accurately as desired. The LF18 case 5 model, despite
its tendency to overestimate turbulence, remained closer to the measured undertow strength,
especially in the inner surf zone. The performance of the lag model can be linked to that of a
stress limiter, as seen in both single phase flow tests and wave breaking simulations. In fact,
it appears to behave similarly to the LF18 case 4 model, where stress limiting limiter was set
to the original value defined by Wilcox (2006). The model is effective in reducing turbulent
fluctuations before and immediately after wave breaking and shows a lower turbulence than
the two LF18 models. However, this is linked with a lower flow resistance, which causes a
faster surface roller and in turn a stronger undertow.
One interesting avenue for future work is to explore the behavior of the lag model with values
of the constant a > 1. The current study used values of 0.175, 0.35 and 1, and found that
increasing the value only had a small positive effect on the undertow predictions. Testing
higher values could potentially improve the model’s ability to replicate turbulence profiles
44
==PAGE:49==
and undertow strength more accurately. It could, however, also result in the model behaving
similar to the LF18 case 3 model, which is not the wanted outcome. Such modifications would
need to be thoroughly tested to assess their impact on the model’s overall performance.
Overall, the lag model provides a promising approach to stabilizing turbulence in wave break-
ing simulations. However, it does not solve the problem of undertow overestimation, partic-
ularly in the inner surf zone, where turbulence levels are highest. The LF18 case 5 model
remains the preferred model for predicting undertow strength, although it too has limitations
in accurately modeling turbulence in certain regions. Further development of turbulence mod-
els is needed to better capture the complexities of wave breaking, particularly in the inner surf
zone. The need for improved models that can handle the nonlinear dynamics of real breaking
waves is critical for improving predictions of wave-induced flows. In real wave breaking condi-
tions, there are numerous complexities that go beyond what is captured in simple laboratory
experiments or simplified simulations. These complexities include the effects of wave angle,
interactions between multiple breaking waves, and the influence of environmental factors
such as wind and tide. These factors contribute to the dynamics of wave breaking, making
it challenging for turbulence models to capture all the flow dynamics. Therefore accurately
modeling these ”simple” wave scenarios is crucial for being able to apply the same models to
fully capture these real-world complexities. Further development is therefore still needed to
address these more complex phenomena.
