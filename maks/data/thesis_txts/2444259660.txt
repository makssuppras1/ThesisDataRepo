Abstract
The aim of this project has been to, through topology optimization, be capable of design-
ing a ’¼éuid ’¼éow domain that can passively ascribe particles with di’¼Ćerent key features to
di’¼Ćerent ’¼éow outlets.
The modelling of the ’¼éow is governed by the steady-state and incompressible Navier-
Stokes equations con’¼üned to a laminar ’¼éow with low to moderate Reynolds numbers.
To solve the di’¼Ćerential equations a ’¼ünite element method is employed using PSPG-
stabilized u1p1 elements in a Eulerian grid. A Brinkman term is added to the weak form
of the Navier-Stokes equations to allow for the permeability of the domain to be de’¼üned
in a continuous interval ranging from non-permeable solid to fully-’¼éedged Navier-Stokes
’¼éow and thus enabling the use of topology optimization.
The particles are modelled with Stokes drag acting as the only external force. The dif-
ferential equations of the particles are solved explicitly using a forward Euler method in
a Lagrangian frame of reference. The sorting of the particles is based on di’¼Ćerences in
density.
The physics of the ’¼éow and the particles are modelled with a one-way coupling, meaning
that the ’¼éow exerts forces on the particles but the particles do not exert any forces back
to the ’¼éow.
The outcome of the thesis has been a method that can successfully manipulate the domain
of a Navier-Stokes ’¼éow to control the pathlines of particles released within the ’¼éow. It is
seen to work very well forcing a particle to follow a prede’¼üned pathline and has also been
successful in sorting particles of di’¼Ćerent masses to exit the ’¼éow domain through separate
outlets. It must be mentioned that the results presented do not necessarily translate into
real physical and practical problems and the method should thus most be seen as a proof
of concept, but with further development it is believed that the method could be extended
to work in more physical and practical applications.

2.4
Particle-particle interaction
Particle-particle interaction describes the e’¼Ćects of collisions between particles in a ’¼éuid
’¼éow. These e’¼Ćects are obviously most prominent in a dense particle ’¼éow where particles
are prone to collision.
Throughout this thesis all the particle ’¼éows will be considered as dilute suspensions which
reduces the chances of particle-particle collisions and any forces that may arise from these
are thus neglected. Resulting in a particle model that is solely governed by lift and drag
forces from the ’¼éuid.
2.5
Gravity
Gravity is left out of the equation throughout this thesis. This is done as gravity is con-
sidered to be an out of plane force for all purposes investigated.
For some cases gravity could most likely be utilized and taken advantage of in the sepa-
ration of particles in a ’¼éuid ’¼éow. For these purposes implementation of gravity would be
quite simple as the gravity force is independent of place, time and problem state variables
and would thus not add anything to the sensitivity analysis. This can easily be seen from
the Lagrange multiplier equation of the particle in eq. (4.20) to (4.22) and the particle
sensitivities of eq. (4.23).
2.6
Rotational frame of reference
For some purposes of particle separation adopting a rotational frame of reference could
be bene’¼ücial to account for and utilize centrifugal forces.
Separation by centrifugal forces is used extensively in the industry. As an example it
is used in the preparation of blood samples to separate red and white blood cells from
plasma.
It is also used in some vacuum cleaners, where cyclonic particle separation
eliminates the need for vacuum cleaner bags.
For the purposes of this thesis a rotational frame of reference will not be bene’¼ücial and
centrifugal forces are neglected.

2.7

A rotating spherical particle in a ’¼éuid ’¼éow gives rise to an additional force called the
Magnus e’¼Ćect. The Magnus e’¼Ćect is a force perpendicular to the relative velocity of the
particle. It stems from the di’¼Ćerence in relative velocity between the bottom and top of
the particle due to the rotational velocity of the particle which by BernoulliŌĆÖs princible

The shear gradient of a ’¼éuid ’¼éow caused by the presence of no-slip conditions at the walls
of the ’¼éuid domain gives rise to additional e’¼Ćects aptly often referred to as wall e’¼Ćects.
These forces cause the particles to migrate laterally across streamlines. This phenomenon

the Sa’¼Ćman force is described which is in e’¼Ćect when particles are far from no-slip walls.
The Magnus e’¼Ćect and Sa’¼Ćman force are often very small and negligible [25] and are
ignored in this thesis. For simplicity other wall e’¼Ćects are neglected as well since the
main purpose of this thesis lies in utilizing the inertia of the particles to migrate across
streamlines of the ’¼éuid ’¼éow.

Chapter 4
Topology Optimization
In this chapter an introduction to topology optimization and how it is applied in this
report is given.
Design optimization has long been an active ’¼üeld of research for obvious reasons, be it
’¼ünding the ideal thickness of a load bearing beam or determining the most heat absorbing
shape of a heat sink.
Design optimization can be categorized into three di’¼Ćerent basic methods; sizing, shaping
and topology optimization. Of these three the most simple method is sizing.
Sizing deals with problems where the shape of the structure in question is already known
and the optimization problem entails ’¼ünding the optimal size for the given shape.
Shaping deals with problems where the bounds of the structure can morph and expand
allowing for more adaptive optimization, but shaping is limited by the connectivity of the
initial design. Meaning that no new holes or borders can appear not already de’¼üned in
the initial design.
Topology optimization allows for both sizing and shaping but also allows for new holes
and borders to appear as a result of this the only thing that is required to be known
beforehand in topology optimization is the extend of the design domain.
The start of topology optimization stems from the material distribution method for lin-
early elastic solid mechanic problems which was ’¼ürst introduced by [2]. Since then topol-
ogy optimization has spread to many di’¼Ćerent research ’¼üelds and has also become popular
within optimization on ’¼éuid ’¼éow properties.
This thesis is seen as a contribution to the ’¼üeld of topology optimization in the sense
that, to the knowledge of the author, topology optimization has never been applied to
Page 25 of 88
==PAGE:36==
’¼éuid optimization utilizing a coupling between particle tracing of individual particles in
a Langrangian model with an Eulerian model to describe the ’¼éow.
In appendix A the ’¼éuid switch introduced in [8] is implemented. This is an example of
an application of topology optimization on a Navier-Stokes ’¼éow. The ’¼éuid switch is a
’¼éuid mechanism that switches the ’¼éow between two outlets based on the inertia of the
’¼éow. This exercise was carried out in order to introduce the author of this thesis to topol-
ogy optimization in general since this was a brand new topic at the beginning of the thesis.
This chapter will cover how the interpolation function is used to transform a discrete
optimization problem into one that is continuous. Then it will move on to how the opti-
mization problem is formulated and go into depth with how the sensitivities are computed
for both the particles as well as the ’¼éuid ’¼éow and how these are coupled and ’¼ünally it
will be explained how the design parameters are updated to create a new design.

4.5
Method of Moving Asymptotes
The method of moving asymptotes is a gradient based method used to update the design
variables of constrained nonlinear optimization problems. The method was ’¼ürst proposed
in [20] where it was introduced as a method for structural optimization. It has since
expanded well beyond structural optimization and is now frequently used for all kinds of
optimization problems especially within topology optimization. The method has since its
introduction been updated in [22] to make it globally convergent.
The method of moving asymptotes is used to solve minimization problems formulated like
the one in eq. (4.11). It updates the design based on gradient information computed for
the objective function and constraints in the current design step while also utilizing the
designs from the previous two design steps.
The adaptation used in this thesis was implemented by Niels Aage, Associate Professor
at the Mechanical Engineering Department of DTU.

The implementation of the topology optimization model for designing inertial particle
separators has been shown to work as a conceptual method in the last chapter. Although

7.2
Particle and adjoint integration scheme
The integration scheme used to solve both the di’¼Ćerential equations of the motion of
the particles as well as the di’¼Ćerential equation of the Lagrange multipliers is a simple
Forward Euler scheme.
Forward Euler integration is a simple method for solving initial value problems, but the
simplicity also comes with drawbacks. The method requires the step sizes to be very
small to achieve a satisfactory level of accuracy for most cases. This is due to the fact
that the global truncation error of the forward Euler method is directly proportional to
the step size [7]. Another drawback is that the method can be numerically unstable for
sti’¼Ćdi’¼Ćerential equations. In appendix C a visual representation of the truncation errors
at di’¼Ćering time step sizes of the forward Euler method is given for the designs of ’¼ügures
6.37 and 6.29.
To achieve better accuracy for the particle tracking, higher order Runge-Kutta methods
could be employed although this was tried early on in the process of this project and did
not speed up the integration of the particle pathlines, but did reduce the accuracy of the
particle sensitivities for unknown reasons.
Instead even more re’¼üned methods like the ones explained in [15, 5] could be trialed. These
methods ’¼ürst solve for the particle paths locally in the elements with a given number of
steps de’¼üned a priori. These local points can be integrated for using any of the methods
mentioned above. Then the local points are used to ’¼üt a pathline using polynomials to
’¼ünd the exact point at which the particle exits the current element and thus enters into
the next.
Not only is this re’¼üned particle tracking model shown to achieve great pathline accuracy,
but it also gives the opportunity to calculate exactly how much time the particle spends
within each element and could thus also increase the accuracy when transforming the
particle sensitivities from the Lagrangian frame of reference to the Eulerian frame of
reference as described in section 5.1.

7.3
A di’¼Ćerent approach to the optimization problem
formulation
The general approach taken in this thesis in regards to formulating the optimization prob-
lem has been to use a bound formulation to force two di’¼Ćerent particle initializations to
advance towards di’¼Ćerent outlets in conjunction. The objective functions have thus been
to minimize the distance between the particles and their respective desired outlets at the
end of the domain.
Since these objective functions only minimizes to zero they could appropriately be im-
plemented as additional constraints instead. Presenting the opportunity of optimizing on
other objectives as an example to minimize the energy dissipation of the ’¼éuid through
the domain instead.
Minimizing the dissipated energy of the ’¼éow could possibly facilitate design parameters
approaching more discrete values in a ’¼ünal design, though this is purely speculative. But
the idea does stem from solid reasoning.
It can be seen from designs of ’¼ügures 6.13, 6.19, 6.21 and 6.29 and con’¼ürmed by the
respective design variable distributions shown in ’¼ügures 6.14, 6.20, 6.22 and 6.30 that a
lot of grey areas remain in the ’¼ünal designs, even when the objective functions and the
constraints are already ful’¼ülled. This leaves the optimization algorithm with no incentive
to ŌĆÖclean the design upŌĆÖ any further.
Optimizing on the dissipated energy instead of just using it as a constraint might pose
a more well de’¼üned optimization problem as minimizing energy dissipation can never be
ŌĆÖful’¼ülledŌĆÖ like forcing the particle to a certain position can.
This is also evident from ’¼ügures 6.4, 6.7 and 6.10 where the objective function was to
minimize the squared di’¼Ćerence between the particle paths and a sinusoidal curve. This
is an objective function that is much harder to ŌĆÖful’¼üllŌĆÖ as that would entail for the particle
to exactly emulate a sinusoidal curve throughout the entirety of the domain and as a
result the ’¼ünal designs are a lot more discretized as can be con’¼ürmed by ’¼ügures 6.5, 6.8
and 6.11.
Another idea for an objective function could be to minimize the duration of time the
particle uses to travel across the domain. This might also help the problem of reducing
the amount of ŌĆÖsolidsŌĆÖ the particle cuts through as discussed in section 7.1. Since cutting
through elements with little to no velocity would be very disadvantageous.

7.4
Including higher Reynolds numbers
Appendix D includes some results from running the same test as for ’¼ügure 6.37, but with
a ’¼éow with a Reynolds number of 50.
From ’¼ügure D.1 it can be seen that a so called checkerboard pattern is starting to appear
near the edges of the ’¼éow channel in the second half of the domain.
Checkerboard
patterns are patterns where the design will oscillate between ’¼éuid and solid from element
to element. This can cause elements that are de’¼üned as solid to act like ’¼éuids as all
the node values of the elements are de’¼üned by the surrounding ’¼éuid elements [1]. These
patterns typically become more common when the Reynolds number increases.
A common way to eliminate checkerboard patterns is to apply design ’¼ültering where the
design variables are updated with a weighted average of the surrounding elements. The
extent of ŌĆÖsurroundingŌĆÖ elements being de’¼üned by some ’¼ültering radius.
To truly be capable of producing accurate results from simulations with higher Reynolds
numbers it might become necessary to implement SUPG-stabilization as mentioned in
section 3.2.1 as well as to rework the drag model since Stokes drag is only accurate for
low Reynolds number as already shown in ’¼ügure 2.1.
7.5
Particle Physics
Several additions to the particle physics modelling could be made to have the proposed
optimization algorithm apply to a wide range of applications.
In some of the results presented in this thesis the size of the particle relative to the char-
acteristic size of the ’¼éow gets as big as 0.1. This would in a real application mean that the
forces exerted from the particles to the ’¼éuid could not be ignored. But for the purposes of
this thesis they are, as the method is seen as a proof of concept. In reality particles of this
relative size would call for two-way coupling between ’¼éuid and particles. Implementing
this would greatly increase computational time and complexity as the particles and ’¼éow
could not be solved for separately and neither could the respective sensitivities.
As already mentioned in section 2.2 a lot of forces are left out as the general idea of this
project was to utilize the inertia of particles to sort them. But it would be interesting to
include forces like wall e’¼Ćects as this could possibly enable the optimization algorithm to
improve on the designs of the micro’¼éuidic devices presented in [25].
