ABSTRACT 
In this thesis the concept of individual pitch control is tried in a DTU10MW reference wind 
turbine (WT) with overall objective the reduction of the fatigue loads of the wind turbine 
blades. This WT is a large scale benchmark system that has been developed in a 
collaboration between Vestas and DTU. It is an upwind, three bladed structure with a rated 
power of 10MW and it is designed for oﬀshore applications. The controller is designed 
using loop-shaping techniques and a transfer function that describes the dynamics of each 
blade in the frequency domain. To obtain the load reduction the controller attenuates the 
amplitude of the flap-wise bending moment perturbations at the blade roots. The controller 
is constructed by targeting the first two harmonics of the blade rotational frequencies of 
the WT. The input of the controller is the pitch angle of each blade and the output is the 
flap-wise bending moment at the blade roots. By using the so called ‘black-box’ system 
identification technique and the aforementioned input/output configuration the transfer 
function of the blade dynamics is derived. The derived controller is implemented in a WT 
model in aero-elastic code Hawc2. For this implementation the controller transfer function 
is being transformed from the continuous Laplace domain to the discrete Z-domain and 
from there to a time domain polynomial expression that relates the output with the time 
history of the output and the input. By using Hawc2 simulations open- and close-loop 
responses of the flap-wise bending moment are derived and compared in the time and 
frequency domain. The outcome of this comparison is that the close-loop system exhibits 
a high degree of perturbation attenuation. Last but not least the concept of equivalent 
fatigue loading is used to assess the performance of the controller in terms of fatigue. The 
outcome of this comparison is that the close-loop system achieves a high degree of fatigue 
load reduction with respect to the open-loop system.

CHAPTER 1 
 
1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 High level overview  
Over the last decade Wind Energy (WE) experienced a huge growth making it the fastest 
growing energy source on the planet.  Starting from few Gigawatt’s (GW’s) of production 
back in 2000 WE reached almost 600 GW’s of production in beginning 2019. Europe alone 
installed 11.7GWs of new WE in 2018 and secured investments of 16.7 GW’s capacity, 
respectively. This growth is expected to continue also in the upcoming decades in order to 
significantly reduce the CO2 emissions in the planet’s atmosphere. The dimensions and 
capacity of the wind turbines (WT’s) go hand-in-hand with the increase in WE Production. 
While a typical large scale WT in beginning of 2000 had a production capacity of 2MW 
and a rotor diameter (RD) of 80m, nowadays a typical large scale WT has a production 
capacity of 10MW’s and a RD of 145m. The reason for this fact is that the Power 
Production from a WT is directly proportional to the surface of a WT blades, hence, directly 
proportional to the WT blades length. With this in mind it is obvious that the WT 
dimensions will continue to grow over the coming years until the materials currently used 
for these structures reach their strength limits or until new materials replace the existing 
ones. The main driver of reaching material strength limits is the so called fatigue loading. 
Fatigue stresses increase with increasing WT dimensions. These stresses are the outcome 
of the loads acting on the blade roots and the rotor shaft [1] during rotation. Their main 
characteristic is that they change continuously from blade-to-blade and from blade 
location-to-blade location. The main source of these loads are: gravity effect, wind 
turbulences and wake, wind shear, tower shadow, yaw misalignment and nacelle tilt. The 
end effect of these loads is accelerated accumulated fatigue damage that reduces the 
operating lifetime of the WT [2]. 
  
An efficient and well established method to reduce these loads is by means of actively 
controlling the pitch angle (PA) of the blades in order to reduce the aerodynamic and inertia 
loads acting on the blades. At the moment, the industry focuses more on Collective Pitch 
Control (CPC) for achieving tower and blade load reduction. The principle of CPC is based 
on the assumption that there is a simultaneous adjustment of the PA’s for all the WT blades 
[3]. This method is very simple and easy to implement. As such it has been widely used 
over the last decades. The main assumption of CPC is that the WT blades experience very 
similar wind conditions and as such, symmetric loading. This assumption can be valid in 
many cases and for small to medium size wind turbines. With increasing WT blade lengths 
this assumption loses its validity due to the asymmetry in the loads that apply on the blades. 
More precisely, due to the big length of the blades the probability that the wind speed will 
vary across the rotor disc as a result of the vertical wind shear, wind direction change, 
turbulences and wake interaction increases significantly. 
  
To address this problem the individual pitch control (IPC) method has been introduced 
over the last few years. According to this method each blade is been controlled individually 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
14
and independently from the other ones. A point of attention for this method is the increased 
complexity and the need either of a high sampling rate or  the use of estimators into the 
controller structure to make sure that the model used is accurate enough for the controller 
needs. Typical IPC controllers have been proposed in [4,5,7,8,9,10]. This method has 
proven to be quite efficient in load reduction in low and high frequencies. In particular, 
standard controller design strategies such as H-inf, LQG and PI have been integrated in the 
IPC structure with great success. In [11] for example, typical PI and LQG controllers were 
developed and they have shown a load reduction complementary role. In particular, the 
former showed high load reduction at low frequencies and the latter high load reduction in 
high frequencies. Moreover, the work of [1,12,13] showed that IPC can not only show high 
performance in load suppression but can also result in robust system to unmodelled 
dynamics. 
  
The aim of this work is to develop an IPC controller to attenuate the vibrations occur on 
the flap-wise bending moment at the root of the blades and to reduce the amplitude of the 
frequency peaks that contribute the most in the fatigue damage of the blades. The novelty 
of this work is that the developed IPC controller operates in wind wake affected conditions. 
To the extend of the author knowledge this is the first time that an IPC controller has been 
designed and shown to be effective in this type of conditions. As a first step towards 
building this controller, the controller design methodology uses loop shaping techniques 
and a wind profile that only uses wind shear. Once the performance of the controller in the 
simplified wind conditions is established, a more realistic wind profile is applied. This 
wind profile includes turbulences from typical wind speeds fluctuations, wind shear, tower 
shadowing and turbulences from wind wake effects as a result of the influence from upwind 
neighboring WT’s. The wind wake effect is based on the models given in [14]. The overall 
objective of the controller is to be, on the one hand, high performing, stable and robust, 
and, on the other hand, simple enough in order to be easily implemented in a realistic 
application.

2.2 Simulation software packages 
The dynamics of the DTU 10 MW Reference WT is being modelled using the software 
package Hawc2. Hawc2 is a software that performs time domain operations and produces 
results of all the key WT variables, e.g. bending moments of blades and tower, 
accelerations, velocities and displacement of all the WT components for various wind 
conditions. For more information about this software the reader is referred to [16].  The 
obtained results are then being used either for system identification, controller design or 
controller implementation purposes. All these operations are performed using the software 
package Matlab. For controller implementation purposes, in particular, a code is developed 
which is able to perform IPC operations. In a given time step, the code receives as input 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
17
the pitch angle and flap-wise bending moment of the blades (in discrete-time) from Hawc2. 
In its structure it has an analytical expression that transforms this input into an output. This 
analytical expression is based on the implemented controller. The output of the controller 
is also a pitch angle. The output is send to Hawc2 and the process of receiving input and 
sending output starts again. All the parameters (feed-forward and feed-backward 
coefficients) of the controller are defined in this code too. The values of these coefficients 
are given in Appendix 1. Further information about this controller (e.g. considered 
sampling rate, discretization technique used, etc.) is given in Chapter 3. 
2.3 Operating Conditions 
The proposed controller of this thesis is assessed on normal power production conditions. 
The wind that is considered includes, as a first step, wind shear and, as a second step, tower 
shadowing, wind turbulences modelled using the Mann model [13], wind wake effects 
using the so called Larsen model [19] or/and Jensen [20] wind wake model.  For the 
remainder of this thesis the turbulence intensity is derived from the normal turbulence 
model (NTM) deﬁned in IEC-61400 [18]. For determining the wind shear the power law 
is used with shear coeﬃcient value equal to 0.2.

3. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
In this chapter the basic theoretical knowledge related to this thesis is shown. In particular, 
the fundamentals of system identification are given in Section 3.1. Furthermore, in Section 
3.2 the approach used to evaluate fatigue loading is described. Moreover, the key 
knowledge required to build the proposed controller is explained through the so called loop 
shaping techniques in Section 3.3. In Sections 3.4 and 3.5 a high level overview of 
transforming a frequency domain expression to a time domain response is outlined. A 
sampling rate assessment is given in Section 3.6. 
3.1 Fundamentals of system identification 
In order to build a model that is able to describe the dynamics of the wind turbine blade, 
which is a dynamic system, a system identification methodology is followed. The tool to 
materialize this methodology is Matlab Identification tool [17]. The structure of this 
methodology consists of the following steps:  
Step 1: Identify the signals that will be used as input and output to the model.  
Step 2: Select a structure of the model that is able to represent the true dynamics of the real 
system. In order to make a choice for the model structure the following key questions need 
to be addressed: 
 
Is there any information available about the system dynamics? 
 
If the answer is yes, the so called ‘Grey-Box models’ will be used. Such models have a 
Taylor-made structure. Such model provides the freedom to integrate into its 
structure existing knowledge about the system to be modelled. For example, if it is 
known the type of differential equations that are able to reproduce the system’s 
dynamics these equations can become part of the model. Note that these equations 
consists of a set of variables and a set of parameters. In order these equations to 
accurately reproduce the system’s dynamics they need two things: (i) Proper 
structure, e.g. proper order of differential equations, and (ii) proper values in the 
aforementioned parameters.  
 
If the answer is no, the so called ‘Black-Box models’  will be used. These models have 
a structure flexible enough to find the proper analytical equation or set of equations 
and the proper set of parameters in order to create a good relation between the 
chosen input and output signal. 
 
What type of model structure match best the relation between the input and output 
signals?  
Typical type of model structures to be considered are: 
 Linear parametric models such as: 
 Transfer functions (tf’s)  
 State Space Models (SSM’s) 
 Polynomials, e.g. ARX, ARMA, etc with or without delay and with or 
without disturbances. Both tf’s and SSM’s are different expressions of the 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
19
same thing. The tf’s consists of a number of poles and zeros in the Laplace 
domain while the SSM’s consists of a number of states that can describe an 
aspect of a system’s behaviour, e.g. velocity, acceleration or displacement 
of a moving object.  
Concerning delays: In principal, if a model is chosen with a high number of 
degrees-of-freedom a good match between the input and output signal will be 
obtained. The price though to be paid is (i) high model complexity, (ii) high 
computational power due to the high complexity, (iii) high difficulty to use 
such a model for controller design purposes. In some cases, by introducing a 
delay into the model that reflects the delayed response of the output signal 
with respect to the input signal it might be able to derive a model that is as 
accurate as a model with much higher complexity but without delay in its 
structure. 
Concerning disturbances: The added value of a model that integrates 
disturbance in its structure is the fact that correlates a number of outputs 
that include disturbance to each other and uses this correlation to predict a 
new output with higher accuracy than otherwise. 
 Process models that consists of a number of poles and zeros and a number of 
constants such as gain, delay and time constant(s). A transient response can be 
derived for example from an impulse or step in the input and simulating the 
resulting output. A frequency response can be derived either from a simple 
sinusoidal input for each given frequency applied to the model and then 
deriving the resulting output. 
 Non-parametric models that are curves that capture the system’s dynamics. 
Such curves are the transient and frequency response of a system 
 Nonlinear models that can reproduce complicated system dynamics by 
introducing analytical expressions that describe nonlinearities of the system 
 
How robust is the model with respect to uncertainty in the estimated parameters? 
 
For linear time invariant (LTI) systems a typical way to assess robustness is using 
the so called gain and phase margin. In particular: 
 
As per [21] the gain margin is defined as the reciprocal of the magnitude 
|G(jω)| at the frequency at which the phase angle is −180°. Deϐining 
the phase crossover frequency ω1 to be the frequency at which the phase 
angle of the open-loop transfer function equals −180° gives the gain 
margin Kg: 
Kg=1|G(jω1)|.  
In terms of decibels: 
KgdB=20logKg=−20log|G(jω1)|. 
 The phase margin is defined as the angle in degrees by which the phase 
angle is smaller than −180° at the gain crossover, the gain crossover 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
20
being the frequency at which the open-loop gain first reaches 1 or 0 
dB’s. 
 A system with gain margin around 5 – 10 dB’s and phase margin around 
45 – 60 degrees is considered robust enough towards unmodelled 
uncertainties and disturbances. 
 
Decide which estimation method should be used to calculate the values for the 
aforementioned adjustable parameters in the candidate model structure and calculate 
these values. 
 
Typical methods for parameter identification is e.g. the least square method, the 
bilinear method, etc. 
Least square method: 
This method minimizes the sum square of the difference between the measured 
response value and the estimated response value. The set of parameters that 
minimizes this sum are the best fit for the model. For further info for this method 
see, for example, in [17]. 
Bilinear method: 
This method is a 1st order approximation of the exact mapping between the 
Laplace- and the discrete- domain. This method has the ability to fit the system’s 
response at a particular frequency fat that makes it very popular in literature [31]. 
 
Step 3: Validate the derived model to assess whether it is able to reproduce, accurate enough, 
the dynamics of the system. This is done by:  
 
selecting an additional input and output set of signals. The input is fed into the model and 
the model calculates an estimate of the output signal. This estimate is then compared with 
the actual output signal. Based on the match between these two signals the model 
performance is being assessed. 
 
In case of a model that includes disturbances the residual error between the output signal 
and the estimated output can be correlated with the input signal.  If these two signals are 
uncorrelated then a good model is obtained. If they are correlated then there is hidden 
dynamics into the system that has not been modelled. Another approach to validate the 
model is by checking whether the aforementioned error is correlated with itself. If it 
uncorrelated this means that it is white noise, hence, the model is good. Otherwise, it is 
not.

The CSTF  (see yellow curve in Figure 3.3.2) corresponds to a low pass filter. In a low

is very easy to relate PC with T and S and assess the performance of the close-loop in terms 
of disturbance rejection and tracking by simple looking T and S. For example, if PC >> 0 
near 0 Hz, then T ~ 1 (0dB) and S ~ - PC. This means that the close-loop system will have 
good disturbance attenuation at low frequencies (since S ~ 0) and good tracking 
performance (since T ~ 1). Furthermore, if PC << 1 at high frequencies, then T ~ PC and 
S ~ 1 (0dB). This means that the close-loop will have good noise rejection at high 
frequencies but not so good tracking performance (since T far from 1) and disturbance 
attenuation (since S close to 1).

3.6 Sampling rate choice  
In order to decide upon the sampling frequency of the discretized system a balance between 
two main items should be achieved. On the one hand this frequency should not be too big 
in order to avoid excessive simulation time. On the other hand is should be high enough to 
avoid aliasing and frequency response warping. As per the, so called, ‘Sampling Theorem’ 
[29] the minimum sampling frequency should be at least twice the highest expected signal 
frequency. For this work the highest frequency of high importance is the fp4 that occurs 
close to 4 rad/s (0.65 Hz). In that sense, the lower bound of 1.4 Hz can be chosen. 
Nevertheless, in order to avoid the effect of a high order harmonic in the close-loop a 
sampling rate of 10Hz or higher should be a good choice.

4.1 System identification of the WT blades 
In this section the methodology described in Section 3.1 is followed in order to derive an 
analytical model in the frequency domain that predicts the system’s behavior for a given 
system’s input. It is important to mention is that the system identification is applied in the 
close-loop system, where the basic turbine controller is active. 
 
Step 1: Identify the signals that will be used as input and output to the model:  
The system’s input that will be used for this assessment is the pitch angle at the root of a 
WT blade. The system’s output is the flap-wise bending moment at the root of the same 
blade. The pitch angle and the flap-wise bending moment of a blade they are strictly 
dependent to each other. More precisely, in case of a pitch angle change there is a change 
also in the aerodynamic loads acting on the blade. As a result the flap-wise bending moment 
also changes. Moreover,  a change in the flap-wise bending moment influences the bending 
curvature and the angle of attack of the blade which influence the aerodynamic loads on 
the blade and the pitch angle of the blade. The simulations performed in Hawc2 are very 
realistic and as such include phenomena such as tower shadowing, wind shear, turbulences, 
gravity effects and many other. In order to derive the pure dynamics of the blade all the 
aforementioned phenomena are filtered-out from the derived input and output signals. 
Moreover, in order to excite the full dynamics of the blade an impulse response signal is 
used. Note that the Laplace transform of an impulse function is 1, thus, the system transfer 
function is equivalent to the Laplace transform of the impulse response. The goal here is 
to learn how a small pitch angle perturbation affects the blade root flap-wise bending 
moment. To obtain this it is important to avoid interference with the operating condition of 
the WT. Therefore, a sufficiently small pitch angle impulse is used for as sufficiently small 
time duration. The values of the pitch angle impulse and time duration were derived after 
an extensive simulation study. In this study a very small pitch angle (0.001 degrees) and 
time duration (0.01s) were used as a starting point. Using these values simulations were 
performed to confirm that there is no interference between the introduced impulse and the 
WT operating conditions. The values of the pitch angle impulse and the time duration were 
gradually increased in order to identify the point at which there is an interference with the 
WT operating conditions. After that an pitch angle impulse and a time duration were chosen

CHAPTER 5 
 
5. CONTROLLER DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION 
The aim of this chapter is to design and implement an IPC controller in order to increase 
the life time of the WT by reducing the influence of fatigue in the WT blades. The WT is 
subject to wind shear, tower shadowing, turbulent wind and wind wake that is induced by 
upwind neighboring WT.  The center of the wake occurs at the hub height of the WT  under 
the assumption that the upstream turbine is as tall as the downstream turbines. This implies 
that the mean wind speed upwind the WT is less than the wind speed in the WT 
surroundings. The considered model for the wind wake is based on the model introduced 
in [20]. This model is integrated into the Hawc2 software package.  The IPC controller 
achieves its objectives by reducing the perturbation of the flap-wise bending moment at the 
root of the WT blades. This reduction is obtained by suppressing the amplitudes of the fp1 
and fp2 peaks of the WT. The performance of the controller is assessed by comparing (i) 
the amplitude of the fp1 and fp2 and (ii) the equivalent fatigue load of the closed-loop 
system  with the open-loop system. The controller is implemented in Hawc2 and the time 
responses required to assess its performance are derived by means of simulations.  
The remainder of this chapter is structured as following: In Section 5.1 the architecture of 
the IPC controllers is explained. In Section 5.2 the controller strategy towards stability and 
robustness is outlined. In Section 5.3 the proposed controller is designed and implemented 
in Hawc2. In Section 5.4 the performance criterions used to assess the controller are 
discussed. Last but not least, the obtained results of the controller using Hawc2 are shown 
and discussed in Section 5.5.  
5.1 WT controller architecture 
The overall structure of the WT controller is described in [24] and shown in Figure 5.1.1. 
The IPC controller is part of this structure. The controller diagram is taken from [24]. The 
output of this system is the flap-wise bending moment at the root of each WT blade (𝑦ଵ(𝑡), 
𝑦ଶ(𝑡), 𝑦ଷ(𝑡)). The system has two types of inputs. The first one is the disturbance (d) that 
is added to the system as a result of the various phenomena that occur around the WT 
blades when interacting with the wind, e.g. turbulence, wind shear, wind wake, etc. The 
other type of inputs are the pitch angles  (𝜃ଵ(𝑡), 𝜃ଶ(𝑡), 𝜃ଷ(𝑡)).  Each pitch angle consist of 
two parts. The first part is derived from the collective pitch controller (CPC) and the second 
one from the IPC controller. The CPC input regulates the power output that the WT must 
produce, hence, it reacts in rotor speed changes. The IPC input aims at a reduction of the 
flap-wise bending moment perturbations related to the disturbance d (IPC input). The 
output of the IPC controller is a pitch angle value that is added in the pitch angle that is 
derived by the CPC unit.  The success of this controller structure is based on two reasons. 
On the one hand the CPC reacts at signals with much lower frequency content than the IPC  
and as such these two controllers can be considered as two separate systems. On the other 
hand the IPC ensures that the sum of the mitch angle command is zero. This fact guarantees 
that the CPC objective (power output control) remains unaffected by the IPC. 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
44
 
 
Figure 5. 1.  1:  WT controller structure 
 
A point of attention is that the IPC deals with high frequency signals. This is because the 
perturbation of the flap-wise bending moment at the root of the blades originate from the 
wind turbulences and wind wakes. In principle the wind turbulences and wind wakes are 
perturbations with high frequency content. As a result the operating bandwidth of the CPC 
controller is not affected by the aforementioned turbulences as the CPC reacts on the mean 
of the wind speed at the hub height. For an IPC to operate sufficiently a constant pitch 
angle correction is necessary. This correction is normally between 0.1 - 0.2 degrees per 
sec. This pitching rate puts a high demand on the blade pitching motors, see [2]. This is 
actually one of the reasons why the IPC focuses mainly on the first two frequency peaks 
of the WT. A positive fact is that with increasing sizes of WT blades the required pitching 
rate drops. This is linked to the reducing rotational speed at big size WT. An alternative to 
IPC for higher frequency peak is the so called active aerodynamic load control as it is 
proposed, for example, in [25]. These controllers are able to deal with the higher frequency 
peaks due to the high frequency bandwidth at which they can operate.

5.4 Performance criterions  
As already discussed in Section 5.2 the first key performance criterion  of the controller is 
the amplitude reduction of the frequency peaks at f1p and f2p of the close-loop system with 
respect to the ones of the open loop-system. On top of that the amplitudes of the rest fp 
frequencies and their harmonics should also remain within reasonable limits. This is 
important to note since it is common in LTI systems a reduction in one frequency peak to 
lead in an increase in another frequency peak. The theorem that describes this phenomenon 
is called the ‘Bode’s integral formula’ and is given, for example, in [27]. In the given study 
if there are frequency peaks far from the operating envelope of the WT that are being 
amplified as a result of the reduction of the frequency peaks occur at lower frequencies 
then an assessment should be carried out whether this increase can be an issue or it can be 
accepted. 
The second key performance criterion is the fatigue reduction of the WT blades. This 
reduction is achieved from the reduction of the perturbations of the flap-wise bending 
moment at the root of the blades as a result of the controller action. To assess the fatigue 
load reduction when using the controller the methodology described in Section 3.2 of 
Chapter 3 is followed. On the top of that, the following additional information is added.  
The fatigue loading is assessed for a period of 25 years life of the WT. For that period the 
total load cycles for every cycle bin and every wind speed is derived. As already discussed 
the Rainflow diagram is used to derive the load cycle bins and the number of load cycles 
for load cycle bins for every wind speed. Note that since the controller is focusing in wind 
speeds above wind speed rated (11.4 m/s) this assessment neglects the fatigue contribution 
of wind speeds with lower wind speed since it assumes that it is the same for both the 
system without the controller and the close-loop system. In this work 10 min simulation 
time is used for every wind speed to derive this info. What is still missing is the total hours 
that the wind is blowing with a given wind speed for the period of the 25 years. To derive

When observing the plots of Figure 5.5.2.5, where results for reference wind speed of 24 
m/s with the presence of wind shear, wind wake and wind turbulence are considered, it can 
be noticed that the controller is not performing well as it only attenuates slightly the 
perturbations. The reason for this is not related to the controller itself but it is related to the 
choice of both the time step of the controller and the time step of the model in Hawc2. 
More precisely, for reference wind speeds above 21 m/s Hawc2 requires a time step of 
0.02s in order to derive stable results. See for example in Figure 5.5.2.7. In this figure the 
power output is plotted for the exact same wind conditions. The only difference is the time 
step that is considered. If the time step is 0.1s then the power output converges to 0 MW 
while if the time step is 0.02s then the power output converges to 10 MW. The reason for 
this numerical phenomenon is the fact that the simulations with a time step of 0.1s  creates 
a power output overshoot which is very big. As a result the Hawc2 controller shuts down 
the WT in order to protect it. If a time step of 0.02s is used the power  overshoot is not big 
enough to trigger the same safety measure from the controller and as a result the controller 
drives the WT at its desired operating point which is 10 MW. On the other hand when 
transforming the controller from the Laplace domain to the Z-domain the discretized 
transfer function has much better fit when a time step of 0.1s is used than a time step of 
0.02s. See Figure 5.5.2.8. The combination of these two phenomena is the reason why there 
is a limited performance of the controller for wind speeds above 21 m/s. The improvement 
of the fitting of the discretized transfer function is the priority number one for future work.

CHAPTER 6 
 
6. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In this Chapter an overview of the obtained results is given and recommendations for future 
work are outlined. 
6.1 Conclusions 
In this work an IPC was designed and applied on WT model that was built and run in the 
Hawc2 software package. The model was built for the  DTU10MW Reference Wind 
Turbine. The objective of the controller was to reduce the fatigue loads acting on the WT 
blades when the WT is subject to wind shear, wind turbulence and wind wakes from 
neighboring upwind WT and tower shadowing. To achieve this goal the perturbations of 
the flap-wise bending moments acting at the root of the WT blades were reduced. In 
particular, the road map to achieve a high performing controller is outlined in the following 
few paragraphs. 
 
Firstly, the model of the WT blades was identified using the so called ‘black box’ technique 
as it was assumed that no information was available for the blade dynamics. The input used 
for the model identification was a step response of the blade pitch angle. The output of the 
model was the flap-wise bending moment at the blade root resulted from the pitch angle 
step response. Once the model was identified a second set of input/output signals was used 
to validate the model’s performance. This approach was used for all the wind speeds 
between the wind speed rated and cut-off limits. The developed model was able to 
accurately calculate the system’s response in all the considered wind speeds. This was very 
beneficial for the controller design since it reduced the complexity that is derived when 
using different blade models at different wind speeds. 
 
As a second step the dynamics of the blades was analyzed. The resonance frequency peaks 
of the blades were calculated and plotted in a frequency domain using the aforementioned 
pitch angle step response. Moreover, the shift of the resonance frequencies and the change 
of the amplitude of these peaks was studied when the wind speed was increasing from the 
wind speed rated to the cut-off limit. Based on this study it was shown that the first and 
third resonance frequencies remained almost unchanged with increasing wind speeds. On 
the other hand the second and fourth resonance peaks showed high sensitivity with 
increasing wind speed. In particular, the second resonance peak increased with increasing 
wind speed while the fourth resonance peak increased up to an extent, then showed a 
sudden drop and then started to increase again. Next to the analyses of the blade dynamics 
using a pitch angle step response, the frequency response of the blades was studied when 
the blades were subjected to realistic conditions such as wind shear, tower shadowing, wind 
turbulence and wind wakes from upwind neighboring WT’s. This frequency response was 
then used as a benchmark to compare the controller performance in the frequency domain. 
 
The following step was the introduction of the controller scope which was the fatigue load 
reduction of the WT blades by means of active control. The controller achieved its 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
61
objective by reducing the fp1 and fp2 peaks by adding band pass filters exactly at these 
frequencies. The controller was also designed to be stable and robust. This was obtained 
by introducing enough phase and gain margin in the open-loop transfer function. To assess 
the performance of the controller two criteria were used. The first one was the amplitude 
of the frequency peaks of the blades and the other one was the equivalent fatigue load of 
the flap-wise bending moment at the root of each blade for a period of 25 years. Once the 
performance criteria were established, the designed controller was implemented in a model 
of the DTU10MW Reference Wind Turbine that was developed in Hawc2. For this 
implementation the controller’s transfer function had to be transformed from the Laplace- 
to the Z-domain and from there using an analytical polynomial expression to the time-
domain.   
 
Using time-domain responses of the WT blades from the Hawc2 model the performance 
of the controller was studied. In particular, the open- and close-loop responses of the flap-
wise bending moment perturbations at the blade roots were derived and compared to each 
other. Based on this comparison it was obvious that a significant reduction was obtained in 
the close-loop system. Moreover, the frequency responses of these signals were also 
compared to each other with similar results. As a last step, the equivalent bending moments 
of the blades were derived for the open- and close-loop system. Once again, the equivalent 
bending moment of the close-loop system was significantly smaller than the one of the 
open-loop system. 
6.2 Recommendation for future work 
In Chapter 4 a frequency-domain model of the WT blades was developed. This model was 
able to accurately reproduce the blades response for all considered wind speeds. If there is 
the need to improve the model accuracy (for example, from ~85% of the current model to 
higher levels) then the considered wind speeds should be split in segments and for each 
segment a dedicated model should be derived. In this way, in one hand, the model accuracy 
will increase and, in the other hand, the complexity due to the high number of models will 
remain in relative low order. The segmentation of the wind speeds could be done based on 
the degree of frequency shift with increasing wind using the assessment done in Section 
4.2. 
 
In Chapter 5 a discretization method (‘Tustin’ method) was used to transform the 
controller’s continuous transfer function from the Laplace-domain to a discretized transfer 
function in the Z-domain. This method is very good when frequency matching is important. 
Using this method an accurate discretized transfer function was derived using a time step 
of 0.1s. However, due to convergence issues with the simulations in Hawc2 for high wind 
speeds (>20 m/s) a higher time step was required. A time step of 0.02s was proven to be 
sufficient for this purpose. When using this time step in the discretization process of the 
controller transfer function it was observed that the derived transfer function was not as 
accurate as expected. This is an item that requires attention as it is expected to improve the 
controller performance at higher frequencies and wind speeds. 
 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
62
The proposed controller has been designed using loop-shaping techniques and a matlab 
tool called the Controller Designer Toolbox. When using such an approach the 
performance and robustness of the derived controller greatly depends on the experience of 
the designer. A more systematic approach for IPC controller design should be used in the 
future. A good suggestion is the use of the H-infinity method [30] since this method has 
proven to be effective for this types of applications in many occasions [30]. 
 
If there is a need to reduce the high sampling rates required to derive an accurate model 
and also reduce the dependency from measurement error and noise, the presence of an 
observer can be advantageous. If an observer is to be used a set of requirements should also 
be developed that will guarantee that the observer/controller configuration will operate as 
one stable system during transition- and steady-state operations. 
 
A last item for future work is the controller performance in high frequencies. As it was 
already mentioned it is important to study how the proposed controller deals with high 
frequency signals. This is because the perturbation of the flap-wise bending moment at the 
root of the blades originates from the wind turbulences and wind wakes that have a relative 
high excitation frequency as shown in Chapter 4. For an IPC to operate sufficiently a 
constant pitch angle correction is necessary fact that puts a high demand on the blade 
pitching motors. In principle, this is one of the reasons for the IPC to focus mainly on the 
first two frequency peaks of the WT. The increasing WT blade lengths require decreasing 
pitching rate. This is due to the fact that big WT use reducing rotational speeds. Therefore, 
the future of the IPC in large scale WT’s is quite promising. If it is to focus at higher 
frequency peaks, an alternative for the IPC is the so called active aerodynamic load 
controller. Such controller is able to deal with the higher frequency peaks due to the high 
frequency bandwidth at which it can operate.
