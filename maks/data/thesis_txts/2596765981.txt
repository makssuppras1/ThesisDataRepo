Abstract | English 
This master thesis investigates how frame creation as a design thinking 
principle can be used to mitigate complex problem solving within health­
care. The casework and scope is based within the boundaries of Livskraft - 
an organisational development program initiated by the existing as well as 
the new North Zealand Hospital. The prime indicator of Livskraft is patient 
experiences, which among other things guides one particular quality devel­
opment project called Mål i Livskraft - a pilot activity with a broad, complex 
and dual objective of using patient experience data for quality improve­
ment purposes, whilst cultivating a hospital culture in which healthcare is 
considered a service. An ambition which by the design team is labelled as a 
patient-centrered-service approach (PCSA).
To facilitate the above objective, this thesis seeks to question how this can 
be achieved and by what means - an approach closely conjoined with the 
design thinking principle called frame creation, which represents a par­
ticularly well-suited strategy when dealing with complex and open-end­
ed challenges, due to its ability to create a novel standpoint from which a 
problematic situation can be tackled. The predominant theoretical focus of 
this master thesis is therefore to investigate how frame creation as a design 
thinking principle can contribute to solving the complex problem of culti­
vating and applying PCSA within healthcare.
The research concludes that the application of frame creation provides 
problem solvers with a systematic ability to move beyond conventional 
problem-solving measures and to discover new and valuable end-results, 
potentially more fruitful than the preceding solutions. The quality, applica­
bility and benefit of the proposed solutions, however, relies highly on the 
participatory manner of the project. Within the case framework, the ap­
proach assisted the formation of four new unified scenarios, which holds 
the potential to cultivate PCSA in the North Zealand Hospital.

1 Introduction
Within recent years, patient involvement 
has become an important area of ​action 
within the Danish health sector. Numerous 
initiatives, often led by political proposals, 
health researchers and health practitioners, 
seek to promote and identify approaches to 
practice patient involvement (Holen, 2015). 
The reasoning behind this movement is 
that patient engagement has been consid­
ered a critical factor in the quality improve­
ment and/or redesign of healthcare ser­
vices (Pomey, 2015). 
Recently, the Danish government and the 
Ministry of Health and Prevention have 
formed a renewed quality program which 
actors within the healthcare sector are ob­
ligated to respond to. The foundation of 
the program is based on the vision of posi­
tioning the Danish healthcare system in the 
forefront of the international scene (Minis­
teriet for sundhed og forebyggelse, 2015). 
The program contains numerous steps and 
initiatives which strive to address quality 
improvements based on patient needs and 
patient-perceived value, thus highlighting 
patient involvement as a key factor in cur­
rent and future clinical processes of treat­
ment (Ministeriet for sundhed og fore­
byggelse, 2015). The initiatives have been 
supported by budgeting with more than 40 
million DKK, designated to the develop­
ment of tools and methods for patient in­
volvement and patient-centred quality (Fi­
nansministeriet, 2019). 
The need of a revised strategy in regards to 
the delivery of healthcare services are not 
only spurred by political agendas of patient 
involvement: Future projections demand 
the attention and readiness of the health­
care sector, when faced with major demo­
graphic changes, with with an increased el­
derly segment (Danmarks Statistik, 2018), 
more chroniclers (Rosendahl et al., 2017), 
and more people suffering from multiple 
diseases and mental health issues men­
tal illnesses (Rosendahl et al., 2017). All of 
whom will, with an increased rate, require 
healthcare treatment while bringing differ­
ent expectations to the services offered. To 
mitigate future challenges of effective treat­
ment, hospitals must rethink how they pro­
vide their services, whilst still maintaining 
a high clinical standard and service level in 
alignment with patient needs. 
Over a number of years, the Danish gov­
ernment has invested more than 50 bil­
lion in new and modern hospitals, with 
the overall goal of building flexible hospi­
tals that are able to match the demands as­
sociated with future projections and chal­
lenges. In addition, the new hospitals must 
support improved coherent patient care, 
increased patient safety, greater efficiency 
and quality (Danske Regioner, 2018). One 
project is called New North Zealand Hospi­
tal (NHN), which differentiates itself from 
other construction sites, as it represents the 
only hospital that is built on an empty field, 
thus containing numerous architectural 
and physical possibilities. The associated 
potential has spurred the NHN manage­
ment to rethink the traditional and conven­
tional hospital boundaries. Based on this, 
they have initiated an organisational devel­
opment project they call Livskraft (English: 
Vitality) which contains the values, princi­
ples and concepts for the new hospital, that 
is expected to open in 2024 (Nyt Hospital 
Nordsjælland, 2019).
Livskraft is based on three value pillars:

1.1 Project framework: 
Patient experience and 
service
The scope of this thesis is based on one 
particular Livskraft project, called Mål i 
Livskraft, in the following denoted as the 
MIL-project. The project has been initiated 
by the Department of Organization Devel­
opment, Quality and Patient Care and is 
based at the existing hospital of North Zea­
land (NOH). 
The project rests on the main indicator for 
Livskraft: Patient Experience, and seeks to 
depict patients’ satisfaction with regards 
to their treatment, by means of a feedback 
terminal, placed at an ambulatory hospital 
department. The feedback is symbolized 
via “happy or not” buttons, which seeks 
to summarize the patient’s overall experi­
ence in a quantitative manner. Parts of the 
feedback undergoes a subsequential quali­
tative follow-up by means of phone calls to 
patients, selected through randomization. 
The qualitative insights are passed on to a 
project team within the ambulatory depart­
ment, which seeks to incorporate improve­
ments based on the emerging themes from 
the received patient feedback. 
The project differs significantly from other 
patient-centred feedback surveys, as it uti­
lizes one particular question which is rare­
ly seen within the boundaries of the health­
care sector: “Would you recommend this 
hospital?”. By using a phrasing that is usu­
ally observed within the private sector, the 
intent is to evoke a cultural change with­
in the hospital by pushing the mindset of 
clinicians towards perceiving healthcare 
as a business that delivers service to their 
patients. The goal is to move the organisa­
tion’s as well as the outside world’s under­
standing of the hospital’s role and function 
in an ever-changing society. 
The objective of MIL is therefore two-sid­
ed as it intends to 1) use patient experience 
data for quality improvement purposes, 
whilst 2) cultivating a hospital culture in 
which healthcare is considered a service. 
The overall intent of both objectives is to en­
able the best possible patient treatment and 
service experience, and to place the future 
hospital of NHN as prominent amongst pa­
tients and citizens (Nyt Hospital Nordsjæl­
land, 2019). In an effort to cover both sides, 
the main objective of the MIL-project is by 
the design team (and authors of this the­
sis) defined as an effort to develop a pa­
tient-centred service approach. This term 
will be abbreviated as PCSA and defined in 
Chapter 4.
The project has now in a pilot undergone 
the preliminary phase of small scale test­
ing in the ambulatory department, so far 
with positive numbers in regards to re­
sponse volume. The next step is to test the 
project’s applicability to the cross-sectorial 
team (TST) allocated in the emergency de­

1.2 Theoretical motivation
The recent governmental agendas of in­
creased patient involvement, have led to 
a consolidation of healthcare services and 
patient perspectives, and thus a movement 
within the healthcare industry to integrate 
and respond to patients’ needs when de­
signing healthcare services. NHN rep­
resents one of many hospitals who are cur­
rently incorporating these factors in their 
quality development programs, such as the 
MIL-project.
However, it is argued that legislation of 
this kind requires a fundamental cultural 
transformation, which goes beyond mere 
shifts in individual service offerings relat­
ed to the patient encounter (Carlström & 
Ekman, 2012). A change which could cause 
strain on the healthcare stakeholders’ abil­
ity to transition towards a well-integrated 
people-centred approach (Anderson et al., 
2013). These are important factors to consid­
er when embarking upon a project frame­
work which revolves around the MIL-proj­
ect group, as their capability to construct 
and perform patient-centred initiatives in 
cooperation with clinicians, might very 
well be affected by similar challenges.
The introductory themes associated with 
the MIL-project are multiple, contrast­
ing and complex, not only due to the proj­
ect’s dual objectives which constitutes our 
definition of PCSA, but also as a result of 
the transition towards the outbound and 
cross-sectorial care setting of TST. There­
fore numerous considerations must be 
taken into consideration, such as cultural 
change, service mentality, design of health­
care practices as well as cross-sectoral co­
operation amongst multiple actors. The 
problem scope of this thesis is thus seen as 
a complex and open-ended challenge, as 
these themes and challenges can not be re­
solved, nor met, by merely recommending 
an alternative to the current feedback ter­
minal. 
To create a substantial valuable outcome 
for the project group of MIL, this thesis 
seeks to question how this can be achieved 
and by what means. This approach is close­
ly conjoined with the design thinking prin­
ciple called Frame creation (Dorst, 2015), 
which represents a particularly well-suited 
strategy when dealing with complex and 
open-ended challenges, due to its ability 
to create a novel standpoint from which a 
problematic situation can be tackled (Dorst, 
2011).

delimitation 
The scope of this thesis is based on the 
MIL-project’s objective of creating a pa­

barks upon a transition that extends beyond

2 Methodology and approach
To obtain valuable knowledge on this mat­
ter, the thesis incorporates the cross-secto­
rial work of TST and the MIL-project as the 
case study, and implements a model from 
formal logic to describe four key reason­
ing patterns in design practice: deduction, 
induction, abduction and design abduc­
tion (Dorst 2011; Dorst, 2015). The model 
provides a foundation for understanding 
how and by which means a design prac­
tice can solve open and complex problems. 
It is in this context that frame creation is 
singled out as a suitable design thinking 
approach to meet the task of cultivating a 
patient-centred service approach (PCSA) 
within healthcare, due to its reasoning ca­
pabilities, also denoted as design abduction 
(Dorst, 2015). 
Before commencing an explanation of 
frame creation as an approach and method 
to answer the research question, the follow­
ing section will briefly introduce the first 
three reasoning patterns. This will serve as 
the foundation of understanding the fourth 
reasoning pattern; design abduction and 
how it is closely intertwined with framing.

When questioning the established patterns

2.2 Frame Creation - a 
methodological roadmap
The frame creation process is described by 
the means of nine steps: Archeology, Par­
adoxes, Context, Field, Themes, Frames, 
Futures, Transformation and Integration 
(Dorst, 2015) (see Figure 6). This division is 
designed to help the problem solver, i.e. the 
designer, by separating out the respective 
activities and sorting them out into coher­
ent units of thinking and doing, which can 
then be evaluated in regards to the thor­
oughness of their execution as the process 
moves on (Dorst, 2015).
Frame creation relies on a dual nature in 
which a problem situation is approached 
differently than conventional problem-solv­
ing techniques, and by proposing an alter­
native proposition to a solution. This dual 
nature informs the logic and structure of 
the frame creation process model, which 
rests on two nested circles - each of which 
respectively focus on the problem and the 
solution (See Figure 6). These steps of Fig­
ure 6 guides the overall course and activi­
ties of our design research. 
This section will briefly present the respec­
tive steps of frame creation. Each of them 
will serve as a methodological guiding tool 
for this thesis, with the objective to answer 
the research question.

2.2.2 Paradoxes
By investigating the apparent problem, 
it becomes easier to approach and exam­
ine the initial problem definition provid­
ed by the problem owner. This will en­

that, in the case study of MIL, challenges 
the problem owner from moving forward. 
The underlying reasons and issues are of­
ten intertwined and complex, due to clash­
ing rationalities, that cannot coexist (Dorst, 
2015). These rationalities will be expressed 
by using a series of “because” statements, 
visualised as a loop, that leads back to the 
initial objective. The explicit definition of 
paradoxes will reveal what makes the prob­
lem hard to solve, and thus support the in­

2.2.3 Context
The core paradox is for now set aside in or­
der to concentrate on ways to shift the prob­
lem situation. This step entails an explora­
tion of the inner circle stakeholders who 
have been involved in the problem situa­
tion before or those who will be needed in 
regards to possible solutions (Dorst, 2015). 
In the case of this thesis, the contextual step 
will explore the work and setting of TST as 
they, besides the MIL project group, repre­
sent the key stakeholders which are affect­
ed by the problem situation of how to in­
corporate PCSA in an outbound hospital 
setting. This step will entail various design 
methods, such as fieldwork, observation, 
contextual interviews and workshops, as 
well as an application of various service de­
sign tools.
2.2.4 Field
This step entails a radical widening of the 
context, creating an intellectual, cultur­
al and social space, referred to as a field 
(Dorst, 2015). By creating such a field, all 
potential players are considered, including 
those who might be connected to either the 
problem or solution (Dorst, 2015). By map­
ping all stakeholders beyond the inner cir­
cle, this step can facilitate the identification 
of their power, interests, practices and val­
ues, which can push the problem in a new 
direction (Dorst, 2015). 
2.2.5 Themes
The exploration of the field will inform the 
formulation of themes. This step entails an 
analysis of the underlying needs, motiva­
tions and values that are shared by the out­
er circle stakeholders (Dorst, 2015). These 
“universals” represent a selection of rel­
evant themes that can be different from 
those underlying the original paradox, and 
thus serves as the foundation for creating 
new frames (Dorst, 2015)
2.2.6 Frames
This step entails the creation of new frames 
based on the emerging themes from the 
field. These are the core of frame creation 
as they represent new patterns of relation­
ship that can lead to an entirely new sce­
nario with the ability to create value for 
the problem owner, which in our case rep­
resents MIL. This step is a largely creative 
leap and entails a creation of numerous po­
tential frames. A frame can be formulated 
as the implication of adopting a certain pat­
tern of relationship (a new “how”) which 
could lead to valuable outcomes (Dorst 
2015). This implication can be formulated 
as: 
If the problem situation is ap­
proached as if it is…, then … (Dorst, 
2015)

2.2.7 Futures
The proposed frame(s) represent possible 
answers or suggestions to how new pat­
terns of relationships should be designed 
to create a new solution. By doing so, the 
creation of a frame allows for an idea gen­
eration of elements. In this way, framing 
serves as the fourth reasoning pattern (i.e. 
design abduction) which facilitates the pro­
cess of normal abduction - the creation of a 
new element - a new what. With a fulfilled 
three-component equation of element, pat­
tern of relationship and outcome a new future 
is proposed. Whether these are found to be 
realistic and valuable, relies on verification 
in cooperation with the problem owners 
and key stakeholders.
2.2.8 Transformation
This step is a critical evaluation of what 
frames and directions for solutions are fea­
sible in the short term, or hold the poten­

a longer period of time (Dorst 2015). This 
particular step in the thesis process relies 
on stakeholder engagement, particularly 
the MIL-project group, as they represent 
the predominant problem owners. Like­

include, as the proposed futures also affect 
them. This will facilitate an identification of 
the necessary changes associated with the 
proposed ideas. This process is done with a 
selection amongst the frames, to eliminate 
those who will require too many changes 
in practices (Dorst 2015). This step results 
in a strategy and transformation agenda for 
achieving results.
2.2.9 Integration
The last step of the frame creation process 
seeks to ensure that the new frames and the 
development they initiate are well integrat­
ed into the organisation - this implies both 
problem owners as well as other players in 
the field (Dorst, 2015).

4 What is PCSA?
The two-sided MIL objective of using patient experience data for 
quality improvement purposes, whilst cultivating a service culture 
within their organisation, has sparked the creation of a novel con-
cept defined by the authors of this thesis. 
We refer to this concept as a Patient-Centred Service Approach 
(PCSA), which reflects the MIL-project’s objective as well as the 
desired outcome within the complex and open-ended challenge 
presented in the problem scope. For the same reasons, PCSA is 
not to be found within existing literature, as this definition is de-
rived from the specific case study of MIL. Before commencing 
to the initial steps of frame creation, with the intent to investigate 
how PSCA can be cultivated within healthcare, the first step is be 
to solidify what is meant by this term. 
The phrases Patient-Centered (PC) and Service (S) constitute the 
two dominant objectives of the MIL-project, while the last abbre-
viation (A) reflects the Approach required to attain a patient-cen-
tred service. The term approach is closely correlated with the 
verbs intention and action as any objective of cultural change 
and patient-centred care cannot be accomplished without a 
conscious and deliberate choice. Collectively, these phrases (i.e. 
PCSA) represent key elements to the overall objective of cultivat-
ing a patient-centred service approach within healthcare.

5.1 Method
This step is facilitated by semi-structured 
interviews with the MIL project group de­

The interviews have been facilitated by an 
interview guide as well as boundary ob­

Besides empirical data, a literature review

been that of a systematic review, with spe­

Patient AND (feedback OR survey)

As the scope of the project changed and

5.2 Patient-centred 
healthcare 
During the course of a lifetime, all consum­
ers will encounter the healthcare system, in 
the role of patients and recipients of a spe­
cific service. In this particular constellation, 
consumers are frequently at a disadvantage, 
especially in their lack of expertise com­
pared with service providers, such as med­
ical staff (Anderson et al., 2013). Healthcare 
==PAGE:29==
21
Archaeology  |  Part 2
organizations and its employees thus share 
a responsibility, as their actions may affect 
the well-being of patients (Anderson et al., 
2013). In other words, the employees who 
deliver the healthcare services have the op­
portunity and power to improve or nega­
tively affect consumer well-being (Ander­
son et al., 2018).
In this context, patient-centred care (PCC) 
has become a framework for a more inclu­
sive and adaptive healthcare system, which 
accentuates on seeing the patient as a per­
son (Mead & Bower, 2000), striving to in­
crease interaction quality between service 
entities (healthcare practitioners) and con­
sumer entities (patients), by placing them at 
the centre of well-being affecting decisions 
(Anderson et al., 2018). Patients, as consum­
ers of healthcare, have thus evolved from 
being passive recipients of service, to tak­
ing a more active role in their care (McCo­
ll-Kennedy et al. 2012; Ostrom et al. 2015). 
One might address patients as co-creators, 
as well as beneficiaries, of the health sys­
tem (Anderson et al., 2013).
The tendency of greater patient involve­
ment and PCC (Gut & Fuglsang, 2015) is in 
line the Danish governmental quality pro­
grams which recognize patient needs and 
perceived value as the foundation for qual­
ity improvements, thus stressing patient in­
volvement to be a key component in clin­
ical practices (Ministeriet for sundhed og 
forebyggelse, 2015).
5.3 Pathways to quality 
development 
Patient perceived quality is an important 
component in regards to healthcare quali­
ty in general (Gut & Fuglsang, 2015). The 
means to evaluate and understand what 
patients perceive as quality, is often based 
on tools which entail patient feedback sur­
veys and/or patient involvement (Gut & 
Fuglsang, 2015). Depending on the objec­
tives, the two methods differ in regards to 
their qualitative and/or quantitative na­
ture. 
Questionnaire surveys are often beneficial 
if the objective is a systematic follow-up of 
predetermined goals. The method is suit­
able if the purpose is to collect information 
from a larger group of patients in order to 
gain general and representative knowledge 
of known issues (Gut & Fuglsang, 2015). 
Qualitative methods for user surveys are 
predominantly characterized by being in­
terview-based, to collect descriptions of pa­
tients’ experiences in order to understand 
what is important to the patients (Gut & 
Fuglsang, 2015). It occurs that the two 
methods are combined to obtain a diverse 
and nuanced understanding of a given 
problem, as is the case with the MIL-proj­
ect. A detailed description of the project’s 
methods and approaches is presented in 
section 5.6.
A range of qualitative and quantitative tools 
and methods have been developed to facili­
tate quality development, based on patient 
experiences. The following will present the 
most prominent and practised initiatives 
within the public healthcare sector. Three 
of which are based on Denmark, which are 
denoted respectively as LUP, DLP and Pa­
tientinddragelsesguiden. The last quality 
development initiative, called FFT, is based 
in England. This particular method is in­
cluded in this review, as it contains similar 
methods to the MIL-project. 
The recent user-centric movement within 
healthcare encompasses similarities to the 
characteristics of service design (SD). This 
approach draws on user-centric, coopera­

as well as holistic views on the user’s expe­

(Fry, 2019). In this regard, SD is found to be 
beneficial for hospitals in general, due to its 
ability to contribute to PCC (Fry, 2019) and 
to support the transformation towards pa­

This approach is thus identified as an im­

5.3.1 Quantitative methods: LUP 
and DLP
The National Survey of Patient Experienc­
es (LUP) is a nationwide survey, which has 
been conducted every two years since the 
year 2000, and from 2009 annually, with 
the objective to depict patients’ perspec­
tives (Kompetencecenter for Patientoplev­
elser, n.d.-a). The survey is conducted on 
behalf of the five Danish regions and is fa­
cilitated by the Competence Center for Pa­
tient Experiences (KOPA). LUP is formed 
as a questionnaire which is passed out to 
250.000 patients who have either been ad­
mitted or have received outpatient treat­
ment, in connection to an ambulatory de­
partment. The survey contains questions 
that are anchored in both national and re­
gional goals, which have been developed 
according to their respective quality orga­
nization’s areas of action (Fuglsang, 2009).
Results from the LUP survey are, among 
other things, used as a benchmark indicator 
which healthcare organisations and hospi­
tals use for national and regional compar­
ison. Findings are likewise used to facil­
itate a quality review over time (Enheden 
for Brugerundersøgelser, 2001; Fuglsang, 
2009). Besides an intent to juxtapose pa­
tient experiences, results from the regional 
questions are reported back to the respec­
tive departments, to spark quality develop­
ment projects based on patient’s feedback 
(Gut & Fuglsang, 2015).
Within recent years, the capital region of 
Denmark has launched a large patient 
feedback project, facilitated by KOPA. The 
project is denoted as The Ongoing Patient 
Satisfaction Survey (DLP) and represents 
a concept for the continuous gathering of 
patient feedback, to support the regional 
ability to apply data-driven management 
(Kompetencecenter for Patientoplevelser, 
2016). This means that the involved depart­
ments and sections are expected to consid­
er the received patient feedback and assess 
whether it gives cause for adjusting oper­
ations. The project has been implemented 
on several hospitals within the capital re­
gion, including NOH. The patients partici­
pate by answering 3-5 questions on a stand 
or iPad after their hospitalization or outpa­
tient course (Kompetencecenter for Patien­
toplevelser, 2016).
5.3.2 Challenges: from result to 
quality development
One major challenge associated with the 
LUP survey, is that patient-perceived quali­
ty is far easier to measure than it is to apply 
to findings and turn them into concrete im­
provements. In other words, regardless of 
the numerous amounts of data that depicts 
patient experiences, any improvement ef­
fort lies within the respective hospital de­
==PAGE:31==
23

partment’s responsibility (Fuglsang, 2009). 
This particular challenge is associated with 
a variety of factors, which might represent 
barriers to change:
From an organisational perspective, if pa­
tient perspectives are not part of the estab­
lished culture within an organisation, re­
sources will be allocated to other areas of 
intervention, leaving patient-centred qual­
ity development un-prioritized (Fuglsang, 
2009). Another reason could be that the re­
spective quality development agency, un­
der which a department serves, may not 
function as efficiently as it could be. From 
a professional perspective, the reported re­
sults might cause a degree of scepticism and 
ownership hesitance amongst clinicians, 
perhaps due to the survey’s methodology: 
1) a great deal of time passes between data 
collection and feedback results. This might 
affect the relevance of the results, as clini­
cal operations in the mean time could have 
changed. 2) the data is not specific enough, 
causing difficulties to interpret and trans­
late results into concrete actions. This reluc­
tance might also be derived from a general 
objection to change, or a lack of resources 
(e.g. financial or competencies) necessary 
to facilitate any quality development initia­
tives (Fuglsang, 2009). 
Reviews of the DLP tool indicate that simi­
lar challenges might be present. The meth­
od was launched in 2015 in two different 
hospitals in the capital region. Since then, 
the method has been implemented in sev­
eral hospitals, including NOH. Due to the 
novelty, the approach is subject to ongoing 
revision, from which a series of suggestions 
have been marked (KOPA, 2015): DLP re­
quires a steering committee that can en­
sure continuous follow-up and progress, as 
well as multi-level project managers with­
in the hospital, the respective department 
and section. Those are the ones responsi­
ble for securing any local implementation 
of quality development initiatives (KOPA, 
2015). These organisational requirements 
are closely conjoined with the potential 
LUP challenges, as any patient-centred 
quality development evidently depends 
on leadership as well as an allocation of re­
sources and priorities. In addition to man­
agement, the revision of DLP appoints clin­
ical staff competencies as a key component 
to ensure valuable results (KOPA, 2015) in 
order to facilitate the necessary improve­
ments based on patient feedback. Yet again, 
the presence of the necessary competencies 
becomes an important factor, which might 
serve as a barrier, if these types of resources 
prove to be absent.

5.3.4 Challenges: resources and 
responsibility
The experiences of Patientinddragelses­
guiden are fairly sparse, given that the tool 
has only existed in 2 years. However, a re­
cent study regarding organizational user 
involvement has been conducted, to deter­
mine the experiences of the clinicians, who 
among other things represent the enforcers 
of day to day patient involvement (Defac­
tum, 2018). The following represents a se­
lection of the study’s most relevant find­
ings: 
Despite a range of advantages expressed by 
clinicians, the study indicates several chal­
lenges that are crucial to the progressing 
use of patient involvement. An extract of 
the advantages include an increased sense 
of security, a greater patient motivation to 
take co-responsibility in treatment, as well 
as fewer mistakes and improved well-being 
subsequent to the treatment course. Find­
ings, which indicate that patient involve­
ment may have a direct impact on safety 
as well as on the quality and efficacy of the 
care and treatment performed.
Other thematics drawn from the study, 
however, highlight a range of dilemmas as 
well as a need for change in practice to fa­
cilitate patient involvement. For instance, 
clinicians express that patient involvement 
requires additional resources in the short 
term, in order to gain the above-mentioned 
advantages in the long run. However, the 
need for resources might cause the initia­
tive to conflict with other priorities in clin­
ical practice. To this extent the interviewed 
staff expresses concern in regards to patient 
involvement responsibility, when to in­
clude it and to which degree, thus articulat­
ing a dilemma of organisational structure.
5.4 The FFT
From an in-depth description of the most 
prominent and applied tools and meth­
ods for understanding patient experiences 
in Danish healthcare, this section includes 
a case that applies both quantitative and 
qualitative measures, but differs in its ap­
proach as it includes elements known from 
the private sector. This particular case is 
thus considered relevant for the analysis 
==PAGE:33==
25
Archaeology  |  Part 2
of the MIL-project, as it contains similar 
methods from which key learning can be 
derived. 
The Friends and Family Test (FFT) is a tool 
for collecting patient experience, which has 
been managed and facilitated since 2013 by 
the publicly funded healthcare system in 
England, National Health Service (NHS) 
(NHS England, 2014a). The method has be­
come integrated into most NHS-funded ser­
vices (NHS England, n.d.-a). As opposed to 
other NHS surveys, which are defined by a 
considerable time-lag between the data col­
lection and the survey results, the FFT aims 
to provide feedback in near real-time with 
a continuous collection (NHS England, 
2014a). 
In April 2020, an update of the test is to be 
implemented, but the following descrip­
tion and analysis will focus on the version 
of the test used before April 2020.
5.4.1 The FFT approach
The tool revolves around one main ques­
tion “How likely are you to recommend 
our service to friends and family if they 
needed similar care or treatment?”, and ap­
plies optional follow-up questions, includ­
ing at least one free-text question (NHS En­
gland, 2014a). The mandatory question is 
answered on a 5-point Likert scale, ranging 
from “Extremely likely” to “Extremely un­
likely”, with an additional “Don’t know” 
option. In its first years of use, a score was 
calculated using a method similar to that of 
the Net Promoter Score (Reichheld, 2003), 
but since 2014 the presentation of the data 
has been based in the specific recommen­
dation percentage (NHS England, 2014a). 
The method of collecting the responses is 
not fixed, and can be achieved through pa­
per, postcards, tablets, kiosks, e-mail sur­
veys etc. The same is the case in regards 
to the time of collection relative to the pa­
tient’s service, which can be determined by 
each department. 
The Friends and Family Test guidance, pub­
lished by NHS England (2014a), provides 
general good practice recommendations 
to ensure a valid data collection as well as 
specific guidance to different areas of care.
For the forthcoming analysis of FFT, two 
case studies will be applied. FFT case stud­
ies and their usefulness can also be found 
on the NHS England website, although 
these may be overly skewed towards the 
positive.
In 2014, a review of the FFT was performed 
by the NHS to assess its degree of imple­
mentation, use and reception, with an intent 
to identify successful aspects and points of 
improvement (NHS England, 2014b). An­
other study published in 2017 , focused on 
general practices and the views of patients 
and staff regarding the FFT (Manacorda et 
al., 2017).
5.4.2 The question
The main question of FFT is most common­
ly found within the private sector, thus dif­
fering from the area of public healthcare. 
Within this context, this particular question 
has generated many differences of opinion 
in regards to the formulation and appropri­
ateness.
The 2017 study of general practices (Mana­
corda et al., 2017) reported that most par­
ticipants found the question to be inappro­
priate in the context of healthcare. Three 
reasons for this conclusion are empha­
sized: First, the framing of recommend­
ing was problematic since, in many cases, 
there were no alternatives for patients and 
therefore no choice to make on the basis of 
==PAGE:34==
26
Using frame creation to solve complex challenges within healthcare
a recommendation. Second, due to the na­
ture of healthcare needs, the likelihood that 
friends and family would need and receive 
the same care is low. Third, concerns were 
expressed that the question was too broad, 
leaving the comments too generic and not 
useful in regards to potential improve­
ments. The anonymisation of the data also 
seemed to limit the usefulness, as practice 
managers or clinicians could not connect 
the feedback to the specific episodes of pa­
tient care. 
In the 2014 review (NHS England, 2014b) 
similar concerns appeared in regards to 
the question. These predominantly re­
volved around the same themes as in the 
2017 study, such as lack of choice, inappro­
priateness with regards to treatment and 
health recommendations, as well as con­
cerns about the use of“brand loyalty” con­
cepts within healthcare. However, patient 
and public group participants did seem to 
quickly understand the principle and ex­
pressed that it would be easy for them to 
answer.
The phrasing of the question originates 
from the Net Promoter Score (NPS), a met­
ric widely used in the private sector as a tool 
for measuring loyalty (Reichheld, 2003). 
This tool has, however, been criticised on 
several points. One of which is that the hy­
pothetical and forward-looking nature of 
the question is less effective at predicting 
behaviour compared to a question about 
past behaviour (Spool, 2017; Weinstein, 
2018; Hall, 2015). 
5.4.3 Applicability and 
engagement
The NHS review (NHS England, 2014b) re­
ports that 85% utilize FFT with the intent 
to improve patient experience and that 78% 
find the FFT to increase the emphasis on 
patient experience. The real-time character­
istic of the FFT is seen as a strength, espe­
cially in regards to local service improve­
ments as frontline staff are able to associate 
feedback to the work practice at the given 
time. This, in turn, increases the sense of rel­
evance and motivation for staff to respond 
to the feedback (NHS England, 2014b).
Similar findings are evident in cases 
demonstrated on the NHS website, where 
service improvements have been executed 
on the basis of the comments from the FFT. 
For instance, one maternity ward has up­
graded their department by installing soft 
close bins to alleviate noise at night as well 
as an in-between meals snack scheme for 
hungry patients (NHS England, n.d.-b). An 
emergency department created more visi­
ble information about flow for the patients 
and reduced noise by undertaking repairs 
of squeaky doors (NHS England, n.d.-c). 
Besides FFT, this particular department ap­
plied numerous methods to facilitate their 
improvement process. For instance, they 
performed walk-throughs with patient rep­
resentatives and hired external consulta­
tion in regards to their ability to perform 
service improvement in healthcare (NHS 
England, n.d.-c).
The conclusions of NHS in regards to the 
usefulness and applicability of FFT is, how­
ever, in stark contrast to the findings of 
Manacorda et al. (2017). In this review, only 
4 out of 42 practices expressed positive 
views about the FFT, while only one could 
present examples of FFT related improve­
ments. Practice staff expressed that other 
surveys were found to be more specific and 
therefore more useful, compared to the ge­
neric comments of the FFT (Manacorda et 
al., 2017).
Engagement of staff is an influential factor

when discussing applicability and useful­
ness of the FFT in regards to service im­

related to the level of which clinical staff 
can understand and appreciate the purpose 
of the FFT (Manacorda et al., 2017; McIn­

to ensure specific improvement points on

an important factor to engagement and suc­

board level and exist throughout the entire 
organisation (McIntyre et al., 2014).
5.4.4 Challenges 
Although some NHS reviews report that 
FFT has been useful, a majority of users 
within the healthcare sector express con­
cerns regarding the overall method and 
approach as well as the applicability. The 
phrasing of the question has been found to 
generate numerous challenges: Firstly, dis­
putes in regards to the appropriateness in­
dicate that clinicians hesitate, when patient 
care is correlated to the notion that health­
care is considered a service or a brand. 
Secondly, the hypothetical nature of the 
question does not promote any tangible 
knowledge regarding behaviour or expe­
riences. This affects the ability to facilitate 
any substantial improvement of healthcare 
services, which is also demonstrated by the 
(Manacorda et al., 2017) review. 
These are important factors to consider, as 
they challenge clinicians’ ability to perceive 
FFT as a useful pathway to quality improve­
ment, which effectively causes a diminish­
ing level of ownership and engagement. 
5.5 Design methods
Quality development within healthcare 
is about improving the services offered to 
patients. The previous reviews of qualita­
tive and quantitative pathways to elevate 
patient care represent two approaches that 
use various means such as surveys and 
questionnaires to facilitate just that. With a 
similar objective of improving services, the 
principles and methods of service design 
(SD) can be seen as a supplementary ap­
proach when reviewing pathways to quali­
ty development within healthcare. 
Existing literature provides numerous ex­
amples that support this notion (Fry, 2019; 
Feldman & Cardoso, 2015). In the contin­
ued archaeological pursuit to unfold and 
understand the various efforts to facilitate 
quality development, the principles of ser­
vice design are thus considered an object 
of interest. The following section entails a 
literature review of service design in rela­
tion to healthcare as well as a presentation 
of how its methods and principles are cur­
rently applied at NOH. 
5.5.1 Service design in healthcare
SD is an interdisciplinary, human-centred, 
collaborative, creative and iterative ap­
proach (Blomkvist et al, 2010; Meroni & 
Sangiorgi, 2011) that applies methods from 
various fields such as psychology and an­
thropology, to obtain a holistic understand­
ing of service experiences (Feldmann & 
Cardoso, 2015). This design approach can 
be seen as an activity of planning and im­
==PAGE:36==
28
Using frame creation to solve complex challenges within healthcare
plementing change, with an objective of 
improving the quality of a given service 
(Fry, 2019). The activities of SD (although 
not linear) can be divided into four gener­
al phases: exploration, creation, reflection 
and implementation (Feldmann & Cardo­
so, 2015). 
In healthcare contexts, SD is seen as a way 
to involve the perspectives of patients and 
their relatives in the development process 
as the approach allows for their voices to be 
heard (Malmberg et al., 2019). By doing so, 
SD can facilitate the improvement of user 
experience (Zomerdijk & Voss, 2010). The 
participatory practices and design tools of 
SD enables valuable knowledge regard­
ing future services (Feldmann & Cardoso, 
2015), thus representing a promising ap­
proach to support transformation in health­
care due to its focus on elevating human 
needs and perspectives (Malmberg et al., 
2019). 
This ability is consistent with the cur­
rent transition towards a more inclusive 
and adaptive healthcare system (i.e. PCC) 
(Malmberg et al., 2019; Mead & Bower, 
2000), which recognizes patient needs and 
experiences as key components for clinical 
quality development (Ministeriet for sund­
hed og forebyggelse, 2015). For the same 
reasons, SD is considered to be an appro­
priate driver of healthcare’s paradigm shift 
towards PCC, due to its transformative ap­
proach, methods and processes (Malmberg 
et al., 2019). 
This is based on the notion that transfor­
mation and change does not come easily. 
Nor is it the case within the healthcare sec­
tor: Research attests that hospitals in par­
ticular are remarkably resistant to change, 
both structurally and culturally (McKee & 
Healy, 2002). Recent SD literature, how­
ever, indicates that SD processes can lead 
to changing habits by staging experiences 
that challenge existing assumptions and by 
the engagement of actors (Wetter-Edman et 
al., 2018), for instance, clinicians. 
5.5.2 Adoption of design methods 
within NOH
As the preceding section suggests, SD and 
its design principles are considered to be 
valuable approaches in the objective of ob­
taining a holistic understanding of patients’ 
service experiences as part of quality devel­
opment. This has likewise been acknowl­
edged by NOH, which has formed an Inno­
vation team to facilitate all innovation and 
development activities related to the new 
hospital of NHN. 
With an intent to grasp how this particular 
team is currently applying design methods 
and principles, a semi-structured interview 
with Marie Brøndum Bay, innovation con­
sultant, has been conducted (See appendix 
1a-d). The following will cover the most 
important findings in relation to current 
quality development pathways, before pro­
ceeding towards the MIL review. 
5.5.3 Process and approach of 
Innovation team
Designers and anthropologists constitute 
some of the profiles on the team and their 
activities are financed through a quality 
fund within the hospital. Their vision con­
sists of improving the overall patient jour­
ney and providing the best possible ser­
vice experience for the patient through the 
means of design thinking principles. This 
endeavour relies on processes in which 
both quantitative and qualitative methods 
are employed, which are supplemented by 
elements of service design. The approach of 
the Innovation team is in this regard con­
sistent to an interdisciplinary, human-cen­
==PAGE:37==
29

tred and collaborative procedure that relies 
heavily on partnerships with clinicians.
To this notion habits and attitudes of the 
clinicians are emphasized as important el­
ements to assist their intentions of improv­
ing the healthcare experience. This state­
ment is fortified by the Innovation team’s 
observation that clinicians are particularly 
prone to be engaged and motivated if the 
value generated from a given design proj­
ect is clear and assessed as advantageous. 
This case is in alignment with the preceding 
reviews, which suggests that the success 
rate of a given quality development meth­

According to the Innovation team, accep­
tance from clinicians is also a consequence 
of their evidence-based practice, which 
might very well collide with the mindset 
and approach of designers in general. Add­
ed to this defiance, is that care delivery is 
primarily driven by cost-benefit divisions 
based on effectiveness and service delivery 
requirements, not necessarily patient ex­
perience. The employees of the Innovation 
team must, therefore, be able to adapt and 
accept this context before proceeding with 
their endeavours. 
The Innovation team generally aims their 
attention towards clinicians that present 
themselves as particularly vigorous and 
ambitious, as these personalities represent 
prominent and potential ambassadors for 
change-related projects. These are often 
the clinicians who are temporarily released 
from their duties (financed by the Inno­
vation team fund) and trained to facilitate 
some of the development activities in the 
respective department. These activities en­
tail concept development and testing. 
The course of a given development project 
should in an optimal world be managed 
in a way that facilitates a short and agile 
process, to prevent any procrastinated and 
prolonged projects. The reasoning behind 
this vision is that compact development 
projects allow for numerous and parallel 
initiatives, and are more likely to result in 
valuable outcomes.
Compared to the MIL project group, the 
process and approach of the Innovation 
team, according to Marie, distinguish­
es itself in several ways. Even though the 
MIL-project does focus on future activities 
and objectives, the general function of the 
department is anchored in resolving day-
to-day tasks and problems with relation 
to the organizational operation. Their pri­
mary tasks are therefore based on prevail­
ing initiatives, while the Innovation team 
department pursues projects of more for­
ward-looking and innovative character. 
Consequently, the competencies of the two 
departments differ, as organizational de­
velopment and quality does not necessarily 
require the competencies of designers. For 
the same reasons, the MIL-project group 
and the Innovation team operate separate 
from each other.

with key learnings from the previous re­

this examination will serve as an analytical 
foundation for the development and iden­
tification of the clashing rationalities that 
influence the ability to apply and cultivate 
a patient-centred service approach (PCSA) 
within healthcare. 
The investigation is based on documents 
from the MIL-project (Mål i Livskraft pro­

5.6.1 The project in short
MIL is a project emanating from the organ­
isational development project of Livskraft, 
which carries on the values, the core nar­
rative as well as the strategic objectives for 
the New North Zealand Hospital (NHN). 
Within these organisational boundaries, 
patient experience has been identified as 
the main indicator for all project activities 
related to Livskraft. One of the objectives 
of MIL has thus been to investigate how 
patient experience could be measured in a 
meaningful way, as a foundation for deliv­
ering patient-centred healthcare as well as 
continuous quality development based on 
patient needs.
With the intent to reflect the visions of 
Livskraft, MIL differs significantly from 
other patient-centred feedback projects, as 
a main ambition of NHN is to serve as an 
object of reference amongst patients and cit­
izens, in regards to optimal treatment and 
service experience (Nyt Hospital Nordsjæl­

beyond traditional hospital settings, and 
exercise an approach that is usually associ­
ated with and observed within the private 
sector: They utilize one particular question 
which is rarely seen within the boundaries 
of healthcare: “Would you recommend this 
hospital to others?”. 
By doing so, the intent is to evoke cultur­
al change within the hospital by pushing 
the mindset of clinicians towards perceiv­
ing healthcare as a business that delivers 
service to their patients, and to move the 
organisation’s understanding of the hospi­
tal’s role and function. The broad nature of 
the question enables a quantitative “tem­
perature check” of the patient experience, 
whilst adding emphasis on other parame­
ters than clinical processes.

5.6.4 Key findings from the MIL 
review
Throughout the course of MIL, the proj­

in regards to the approach, the goals, the 
meetings, the interviews as well as the pro­

to facilitate and coordinate the collection 
of patient experience themselves, without 
the management of the MIL project group. 
This objective is partly based on the fact 
that the MIL group does not have the ca­

of clinic-based responsibility is built on the 
notion that clinicians ownership of patient 
experience projects can only be attained 
through engagement (i.e. by collecting the 
data themselves). 
The primary insights collected from the 
HappyorNot terminal are primarily quan­

the open feedback feature which only 4% 
of the patients used. The generated insights

engagement. This effectively means that

5.7 Archaeological topics
With the above review of current pathways 
to quality development (i.e. LUP, DLP and 
FFT), including the Innovation team and the 
MIL-project, we are now able to distill key 
learnings regarding the ambition of culti­
vating PCSA within healthcare. The follow­
ing section aspires to summarize these into 
archaeological topics, which represent the 
foundation for identifying the clashing ra­
tionalities and paradoxes that prevents the 
MIL-project from attaining their objectives.
5.7.1 Suitability for quality 
improvement
As the MIL-project seeks to attain several 
objectives, their approach has evolved into 
being somewhat broad, containing both 
quantitative as well as qualitative elements. 
The need for numerous methods originates 
in the ambition of framing hospital treat­
ment and care as a service, thus disrupting 
the current organisational culture. This is 
the reasoning logic to their main question 
phrasing, inspired from the private sector, 
which has also been utilized in the patient 
experience project of FFT. A common fea­
ture of this approach is that it generates a 
high response rate, but often at the expense 
of any qualitative content. This means that 
the MIL-project must include additional 
methods to facilitate measures of quality 
improvements. 
Similar findings assert themselves in the re­
view of LUP and DLP. Although these sur­
veys do not intend to evoke cultural change 
in regards to hospital services, they do rely 
on benchmark objectives, which, similarly 
to MIL and FFT, originate from the private 
sector. Although high response rates are 
easily attained, the primary challenges re­
main congruent in MIL and FFT: the gen­
erated data is not specific enough, causing 
difficulties to interpret and translate results 
into concrete actions of improvement. This 
leaves us with an essential question in re­
gards to the suitability of the respective ap­
proaches.
By setting multiple objectives, be it bench­
marking or cultural change, one might ar­
gue, that this contradicts the objective of 
quality development based on patient ex­
perience. As a result, one objective cannot 
be obtained without being subsidized with 
additional measures and resources.

which are responsible for allocating the 
necessary resources and priorities (KOPA, 
2015). This notion is likewise present when

in the framework of MIL, the primary re­

project management, but with an ambition 
of displacing any future project administra­

This ambition, however, relies on numer­

quality development project. For instance,

5.7.3 Ownership, motivation and 
engagement
Sense of responsibility is a crucial factor 
in regards to quality development, and in­
volves various levels within healthcare. 
While any intention or aspiration improv­
ing healthcare services depends on the 
support from department and manageri­
al leaders (Mills et al, 2019; McIntyre et al., 
2014), clinicians must be regarded as cen­
tral to this objective as they represent the 
linking element between patients, the care 
being offered and the ability to improve 
services. A notion, that is likewise stressed 
within the Innovation team. 
Quality development initiatives must, 
therefore, recognize and understand the 
underlying factors that affect clinicians 
ability to support and enforce the projects, 
i.e. their sense of ownership, motivation 
and engagement: 1) the necessary resourc­
es must be available, to alleviate clinicians 
from the dilemma of compromising pa­
tient care, and 2) it is essential to provide 
a useful pathway to quality improvement 
through the means of valuable and tangi­
ble feedback, which can facilitate substan­
tial improvements. 
5.7.4 Project prospects
The preceding review of pathways to qual­
ity improvement indicates that these ele­
ments are yet to be acknowledged, prompt­
ing the need for a revision of the current 
methods and approaches. Similar find­
ings assert itself in the case of MIL, as their 
two-sided objectives have generated a cas­
cade of resource-demanding elements, 
which counteracts crucial factors related 
to the project prospects, such as resourc­
es and ownership. Without this acknowl­
edgement, the project risks to never exceed 
beyond the organisational and managerial 
setting of Mål i Livskraft, thereby not to ob­
tain their ambition and objective of culti­
vating PCSA.

The archaeological topics derived from the 
preceding review represent fundamentals 
to why the MIL-project objective of PCSA is 
hard to obtain. These are according to 
(Dorst, 2015) denoted as paradoxes, and 
can be expressed by using a series of “be­
cause” statements, visualised as a loop, that 
leads back to the initial objective. Figure 12 
depicts the paradoxes and their respective 
connection. This process is preceded by 
several iterations within the design team 
(See appendix 3a-b), to reach a common 
understanding of the numerous clashing 
rationalities that are currently inherent in 
the problem situation. 
The MIL-projects’ objectives are based 
within the overall visions of Livskraft of 
creating a patient-centred hospital. Besides 
the continuous quality development based 
on patient experiences, the means to obtain 
their vision, are believed to be a changed 
culture within the entire hospital organi­
sation in regards to healthcare services in 
general. 
These objectives ultimately affect the 
MIL-project’s approach, which operates on 
the basis of the quantitative question of rec­
ommendation. This method is considered 
capable of pushing people’s by the MIL 
management mindsets and attitudes to­
wards considering healthcare as a service 
offering. The disadvantage, however, is the 
lack of detailed and valuable knowledge 
of patient experience which constitutes the 
foundation for quality development. The 
resulting factors are that clinicians are left 
with non-specific feedback, which in return 
diminish their motivation and engagement 
with the project or other quality develop­
ment initiatives in general. Likewise the 
consequence of using a question inspired 
from the private sector, is that the means to 
obtain valuable feedback must be expand­
ed to include qualitative methods which re­
quire excessive resources, both internal as 
well as external. Considering that a major­
ity of clinical departments do not prevail 
from unlimited resources, the MIL-project 
is in conflict with the primary responsibil­
ities of caregiving. This serves as a barrier 
for adopting and implementing the initia­
tive as a routine practice, which ultimately 
puts any resource-demanding quality de­
velopment project at risk of never exceed­
ing program activities. 
The visions and objectives of Livskraft and 
MIL, are therefore considered to be the very 
reasons that keep them from moving for­
ward. By visualising and articulating these 
paradoxes, the initial problem statement is 
expanded and understood on a much deep­
er level. This insight is for now shunned in 
the continued contextual analysis of the 
problem situation, with the intent to return 
to the clashing rationalities upon the con­
verging frame creation steps towards ac­
tion.

7 Context
In the following frame creation step, we ex­
plore the inner circle of stakeholders who 
might either be associated with or affect­
ed by the objective of cultivating PCSA. As 
such, this investigation moves beyond the 
organisational and managerial context of 
the MIL-project and into the tangible world 
of the clinicians, as they represent the pre­
dominant candidates to apply PCSA in 
their day to day patient care. In the frame­
work of this thesis, clinicians represent the 
outbound team of the cross-sectorial team 
(TST). 
The objective with this contextual endeav­
our is to depict the apparent complexity of 
TST, and to identify other important inner 
circle stakeholders related to TST, such as 
patient groups and municipal partners. By 
including all stakeholders, the ambition is 
to identify their respective values, needs 
and motivation which will inform a subse­
quent formulation of themes, a set of “uni­
versals”, which must be incorporated in the 
creation of new frames.
To facilitate this, a variety of design meth­
ods have been applied, such as fieldwork, 
observation and contextual interviews, 
supported by service design tools. 
7.1 Introduction to TST
The cross-sectoral team called TST is a rela­
tively new project within NOH, which has 
been operating since June 2019. The team 
has been formed as a result the recent gov­
ernmental reform within the Ministry of 
Health and Elderly, with objectives of annu­
ally reducing patient admissions by 40,000, 
as well as an 500,000 decrease of outpatient 
visits to the hospital (Statsministeriet & 
Sundheds- og Ældreministeriet, 2019). 
TST is located within the emergency de­
partment and consists of two main func­
tions: the discharging team and the out­
bound team. The work and the setting of 
the outbound team represents the elemen­
tary stakeholders related to the thesis ob­
jective and scope and will be reffered to as 
the TST for simplicity. The primary func­
tion of the TST is outbound patient care, 
which entails flexible and varying day- and 
evening shifts of commuting between pa­
tients referred to the team. 
Their function is seen as a supplement to 
the municipal efforts with relation to pa­
tient care and entails tasks such as catheter 
replacements, blood tests and medication. 
Currently, the TST cooperates with four 
different municipalities: Hillerød, Allerød, 
Frederikssund and Helsingør.
7.2 Fieldwork
With the intent to grasp the work and set­
ting of TST, the design team arranged to fol­
low two TST nurses through respectively a 
dayshift (8am-4pm) and an evening shift 
(2pm-22pm). Here, the primary role was 
to observe the nurses work routines, at the 
hospital as well as in the outbound setting, 
while they performed patient care. The ob­
servation was supplemented with clarify­
ing questions to the observed procedures 
and actions, whenever possible.
This approach is based on the interviewing 
method developed by (Beyer and Holts­
blatt, 1998) called Contextual inquiry. This 
particular technique is designed to gath­
er data from customers in the field in an 
open-ended qualitative manner, which en­
ables a fuller understanding of their work 
practices (See Textbox 4).

9 Themes 
The preceding analysis of context and field, 
allows us to move towards an important 
step of frame creation: The formulation of 
common themes. This step entails the sys­
tematic clustering of the identified values, 
needs and motivations of the inner and 
outer circle stakeholders. From these “uni­
versals” we can begin the formation of new 
frames to the problem situation. The cre­
ation of themes is according to (Dorst, 2015) 
considered to be the most abstract activity 
of the frame creation approach. Likewise, 
recognition of themes defines the depth 
and quality of the end result (Dorst, 2015). 
9.1 Means to consolidation
The formulation of themes is supported 
by systematic thinking principles derived 
from Contextual Design. These are applied 
to consolidate the respective data points 
regarding stakeholder perspectives (Beyer 
and Holtzblatt, 1998). This type of cluster­
ing, which is also represented in the general 
frame creation approach (Dorst et al., 2016), 
relies on similar features of the affinity dia­
gram. This particular model organizes, in a 
hiercally manner, the individual notes cap­
tured during interpretation sessions to re­
veal common issues (Beyer and Holtzblatt, 
1998). The following sections seek to clar­
ify and illustrate the process of clustering, 
which culminates in eight common themes 
amongst the inner and outer circle stake­
holders. 
9.2 Process of clustering
The process of clustering was performed 
through two rounds of iterations (See ap­
pendix 16a-e). Within the first pass, each 
value was placed on the wall and com­
bined, based on similarity, resulting in clus­
ters of values. These were reviewed and 
discussed in order to understand their rela­
tions. Through the second iteration, values 
and clusters containing several directions 
were reorganized until a stabilised set of 
unified value clusters emerged. These rep­
resent the eight themes derived from this 
frame creation step. The result is shown in 
Figure 19.

and concepts related to the respective label,

10 Frames 
From the abstract activity of delineating 
common themes to the core of frame cre­
ation: the progression towards new scenar­
ios, based on revised patterns of relation­
ships and elements. More specifically, the 
creation of a new what and how. In this 
step, we employ the themes and their con­
nectivity as a foundation for new frames, 
which can create value for the problem 
owner, i.e. the project group of MIL.
While the common themes might represent 
an inkling to new scenarios, the ideation of 
a new frame is largely considered to be a 
creative leap (Dorst, 2015). The intent with 
the following section is therefore to de­
scribe this particular process in detail. 
10.1 Process 
The preceding creation of a thematic but­
terfly diagram has been the revolving ele­
ment in which new frames can evolve, as 
this particular outline of “universals” have 
revealed important interconnections rele­
vant to the creation of frames. 
In what was construed as the front stage 
of thematics (i.e. sense of security, quality 
experience, acknowledgement and quali­
ty work), a linkage to possible frames was 
identified. Particularly, the universals of 
quality work, quality experience and ac­
knowledgement are strongly associated, as 
quality work prompts experiences, which 
in return facilitates acknowledgement from 
patients. Likewise this theme entails clini­
cians’ acknowledgement of patients’ feed­
back, which can spur the need and motiva­
tion to perform quality work. 
This thematic loop is by the design team rec­
ognized to entail essential elements to the 
objective of cultivating PCSA in healthcare, 
as it relates to the paradoxes discovered 
in the archeological phase. The universals 
of quality work, experience and acknowl­
edgement are in this regard considered as 
a process that cannot subsist without the 
right means of support. 
The creation of new scenarios should there­
fore be based in the identified paradoxes 
that stimulate the need of adopting new 
patterns of relationships - new frames. 
Dorst defines a frame as the implication 
that embracing a certain pattern of relation­
ship (e.g. a metaphor) will lead to valuable 
outcomes (Dorst, 2015). This implication 
can be formulated as: 
If the problem situation is ap­
proached as if it is [problem],
Then [solution]
The formulation of frames are guided by 
this implication. To ensure that the generat­
ed frames are sharp and precise, the design 
team utilizes yet another frame creation 
sentence from (Dorst et al., 2016): 
If we see the situation as one of [a 
frame]….. 
and we adopt its working principle 
[principle]…. 
then we will create…. [outcome]
The frame ideation process was accompa­
nied by several iterations (See appendix 
19a-c). Firstly, various “what if” statements 
were placed in the thematic butterfly dia­
gram. This approach, however, proved to 
be unsystematic and thus not fulfilling the 
objective to create a new pattern of relation­

were created based on values associated 
with a specific problem situation. This rep­

value definition, 2) frame, 3) definition of 
how, and 4) definition of what (See appen­

10.2.1 Dialogue
Collecting feedback within the cross-sectorial context of TST, poses a 
challenge in regards to the MIL-project’s current methods for collect­
ing patient experience, as the changing physical settings require an al­
ternate approach. This was part of the initial problem statement pro­
vided by the problem owners. 
However, based on the archaeological endeavor, other factors are con­
sidered to be of greater importance than an intent to design alterna­
tive means of measurement. Quality improvement projects often re­
quire clinicians to balance their profession, responsibilities as well as 
resources. And in regards to the TST context, this becomes even more 
apparent as an agenda of gathering patient feedback guided by the 
MIL phrasing (i.e. would you recommend) is considered to be an effort 
with ethical concerns - thus a situation that does not necessarily sup­
port intentions of neither acknowledgement nor quality development. 
Therefore, the framing should integrate a question of dialogue instead 
of measuring. This frame seeks to eliminate the ethical concerns asso­
ciated with the different (and vulnerable) context of outbound treat­
ment, amongst a segment that might not be susceptible to other ap­
proaches than of an interpersonal relation. To facilitate this frame, the 
pattern of relationship (i.e. the how) is seen as a wrap-up to a patient 
interaction, in which the patient is given the opportunity to provide in­
formal feedback on their experience with the treatment. Any responses 
are acknowledged and serves as a platform for discussion and debate 
amongst the TST nurses of the TST team. 
The elements (i.e. the what) required to do so, entail qualitative ques­
tions provided to the TST nurses, developed in a conjoint effort with­
in the team. A whiteboard is placed in the TST office which entails the 
feedback from patients. In this way, any tacit knowledge regarding pa­
tients’ experiences becomes an integral part of the clinicians mindset 
as well as day-to-day work. The feedback should likewise be treated 
as areas of interest within scheduled learning days or seminars facili­
tated by an appointed TST ambassador, in which clinicians can discuss 
approaches to integrate the feedback when evaluating and improving 
their services. See Figure 25 for various formulations and illustrations 
of the frame.

10.2.3 Rapid product development
A considerable challenge when commencing quality improvement ini­
tiatives within healthcare, is the resources required to facilitate it. This 
is the case of the archaeological review of current pathways to quality 
development, including the MIL-project, which relies on extensive 
quantitative and qualitative methods to facilitate and implement 
changes. As a result, quality development projects become extensive, 
protracted and resource demanding, which makes them difficult to im­
plement as a routine practice amongst clinicians. 
Instead, quality development projects should be reframed and ap­
proached as if they were rapid product development activities. This 
would allow for an accelerated process of testing and verifying spe­
cific concepts to determine whether or not an improvement initiative 
should be discarded or continued. By adopting a frame of this kind, the 
means would entail prototyping and user testing as well as relevant 
aids, an idea portfolio of improvement areas and external consultants , 
possibly from the Innovation team, KOPA or ViBIS, to foster the neces­
sary competencies amongst clinicians. 
Quality development would within this framing facilitate a new ap­
proach to expedite improvement initiatives concerning healthcare ser­
vices. See Figure 27 for various formulations and illustrations of the

12 Discussion
The predominant theoretical focus of this 
master thesis has been based on the prin­
ciples of frame creation, with an intent to 
investigate how this particular approach 
can contribute to complex problem solv­
ing within healthcare, with regards to cul­
tivating PCSA. Using frame creation in this 
particular context, incites the need to assess 
the method’s applicability as well as prac­
ticability, as this endeavour lies between 
the worlds of academic discussion and real 
world practice. Based on this notion, this 
thesis is considered to be a theoretically and 
empirically grounded design research that 
seeks to inspire design practitioners, and 
to contribute to existing literature of com­
plex problem solving, through the generat­
ed discoveries and insights. The following 
sections strive to summarize these, while 
reflecting on the employment of frame cre­
ation as a design thinking principle.
12.1 Uncovering the 
problem 
Frame creation seeks to enforce an alterna­
tive to the conventional problem-solving 
technique and logic by shedding the pre­
conceptions of existing frames before creat­
ing new ones (Dorst, 2015). This objective is 
led by the dual nature of frames, which in­
cludes a different approach to the problem 
situation and a proposition to a solution di­
rection (Dorst, 2015). This dual nature in­
forms the logic and structure of the frame 
creation process model, which likewise has 
guided the overall course and activities of 
our design research (See Figure 33). 
Within the first five phases (i.e. archaeolo­
gy, paradox, context, field and themes), the 
model is resolutely problem-focused and 
thus steers away from making any assump­
tions on the nature or type of solution. The 
reasoning behind this approach is based on 
the notion that designers are often present­
ed with an apparent problem situation de­
fined and framed by the problem owners, 
in which the outcome and the means to re­
solve the issue are fixed. This practice was 
likewise present in the complex case of MIL 
with regards to their efforts of measuring

ous preceding factors to PCSA, which may 
very well serve as barriers. In other words, 
we cannot formulate or approach a prob­
lem without framing it (Dorst, 2015). Only 
by deconstructing the problem situation 
into paradoxes, which point to why a prob­
lem exists, can an objective of creating new 
valuable solutions begin. 
12.2 Defining future 
scenarios
In the endeavour to discard existing frames 
while searching for new ones, the process 
moves into a broader problem arena and 
into the outer rim of context and field. With­
in this course, common themes are identi­
fied and analyzed, to allow for new frames 
to emerge. From that moment, the process 
leaps into the second nature of the frame 
creation and becomes a critical creative ex­
ploration of possibilities, thus increasingly 
converging towards action (Dorst, 2015). 
Within the continuous divergent approach 
of expanding the context and field, the de­
sign team were able to understand stake­
holders and their actions, in and around 
the problem situation, while reflecting on 
which parties might be involved in future 
scenarios. Likewise, this step forces an ex­
pansion of our horizon, which in return 
allows us to ponder the possibilities that 
are sparked by the emergence of common 
themes. This investigation represents a 
jump from the study of behavioral patterns 
of inner circle stakeholders, through the 
means of contextual inquiries, fieldwork, 
visual aids as well as co-creation, and into 
the realm of speculative thought. That is, 
we can only contemplate on how the exter­
nal parties of the field understand their re­
alities - any representation of values, needs 
and motivations are thus primarily based 
on assumptions and interpretations. This 
was likewise the case when contemplating 
on the deeper meaning and value amongst 
the players in the broader problem field 
surrounding TST and MIL. Here, a major­
ity of these perspectives were not subject 
to the same amount of contextual inquiry 
as the inner circle stakeholders, thus leav­
ing more room for interpretations and pre­
sumptions.

This is an essential factor to consider, when 
choosing the right themes, as these are 
based in both empirical and hypothetical 
approaches. Through the choice of themes, 
one is generating frames that are pertinent 
to the themes (Dorst, 2015). For the same 
reasons, this particular frame creation step 
is considered to be both difficult and high­
ly strategic, as decision making defines 
the depth and quality of the end result. 
The trick is to choose themes that are nei­
ther too literal or too close to the themes 
and frames that underlie current solutions 
(Dorst, 2015). Likewise, the chosen themes 
combined should be centered around the 
core and salient aspects of the design brief 
(Dorst, 2015). In this pursuit, each of the 
nine themes was subject to an exploration 
and deepening activity through the means 
of nomological networks (See section 9.3 & 
appendix 17a-d). This method was applied 
to broaden seemingly similar thematics as­

and to allow for new and relevant dimen­

frames. For instance, the resemblance be­

the sense that quality also represents supe­

that evoke images related to competition 
and innovation, which are rarely associat­
ed with healthcare. 
In practice, it takes quite a lot of iterations 
to select the right themes, in that they are 
balanced as a conjoint set and are able to 
spark a valuable new approach to the prob­
lem situation. This was likewise the realisa­
tion of the design team, as the exploration 
of themes proved to be both time consum­
ing and difficult, but nonetheless import­
ant and inevitable. Besides the nomological 
clouds, the emergence of the thematic dia­

result and proof of such iteration, which 
likewise facilitated valuable discussions 
and reflections of each themes’ suitability 
to the main objective of PCSA. 
Frames and futures have in this particu­
lar project constituted the final converg­
ing steps of the frame creation process. The 
end result of these phases should include 
a clear representation of a proposed future, 
and the ability to do so, is closely conjoined 
with the effectiveness of the respective 
frame (Dorst, 2015). Therefore, it is import­
ant to make each frame as sharp as possi­
ble. The honing of a frame is often achieved 
by combining themes or by combining sev­

this endeavour asserted itself in the refram­
ing of how PCSA can be cultivated with­
in healthcare. The preceding problem-ori­
ented steps have shown that this objective 
cannot be obtained through convention­

and the how, i.e. the entire MIL-projects ap­

sitions within the existing framework will 
only allow minor adjustments to the solu­
tions, which does not cover the objective of 
a patient centered approach. For the same 
reasons, only one element and one pattern 
of relationship, i.e. one frame, would fail to 
encompass the numerous sub-elements to 
PCSA. It must be a conjoint effort between 
several themes, which represent the four 
frames of Dialogue, Design tools, Rapid 
Product Development and Interactive cam­
paign (See Figure 21). 
Through the proposal of new futures to the 
problem situation, we reopen the clashing

of needs and values. What were identi­

12.3 Involving stakeholders
The suitability and fruitfulness of the PCSA 
frames as a proposed future, is however 
not for the design team to decide. This is a 
consideration of great importance, a notion 
that is likewise shared by Dorst: “A frame 
and the solution ideas it generates are only 
as good as the interest and commitment 
they spark in the parties who are needed 
to implement them” (Dorst, 2015 p. 79). 
For every three component equation of ele­
ment, pattern of relationship and outcome, 
the design team must seek assurance that 
the given frame can in fact lead to realistic 
and viable solutions. Within the converg­
ing steps towards action, i.e. frames, fu­
tures and transformation, which constitute 
the secondary nested circle of the frame 
creation model (See Figure 33), verification 
amongst problem owners and key stake­
holders is thus crucial. 
Transformation in particular, serves a criti­
cal evaluation phase. Here, frames and di­
rections for solutions should be assessed in 
regards to feasibility and potential (Dorst, 
2015). This step would have, in an optimal 
setting, relied on the engagement of the 
MIL-project group, as they represent the 
predominant problem owners, as well as 
the TST management, whose work practic­
es would be affected by the proposed fu­
tures. Likewise, the frame creation activity 
itself, could have been subject to a collab­
orative effort, amongst the design team, 
problem owners and stakeholders. How­
ever, due to the limited time and resources 
of the appointed stakeholders, this particu­
lar verification process is left pending in re­
gards to plausible changes and/or elimina­
tion of frames that might require too many 
changes in practice. For the same reasons, 
any strategy or agenda in regards to trans­
formation and integration, is not included 
as part of the solution. 
An involvement of participants at this 
stage could have contributed to the qual­
ity of each frame or perhaps even affect 
the very nature and direction of the pro­
posed futures. For a frame to come “alive” 
it should be inspiring and captivating, and 
thus relies on its ability to be embraced by 
all participants (Dorst, 2015). Likewise, the 
quality of a frame is reliant on its ability to 
provide a common ground amongst par­
ticipants, in regards to the problem and 
possible solutions (Dorst, 2015). Howev­
er, research shows that the communication 
of frames is not an easy matter, as people 
tend to adopt their own ideas much more 
eagerly, actively and fully than those of 
others (Dorst, 2015). These considerations 
are therefore important to include when 
passing on the proposed solutions to the 
MIL-project group and TST. 
The involvement of stakeholders does not 
only apply to the final stages of frame cre­
ation. In the endeavour to capture univer­
sal themes, which hold the potential for 
development (Dorst, 2015), we turn to the 
human dimensions to grasp the needs and 
values of the inner and outer circle of stake­
holders. This is a crucial part of any design 
practice (Dorst, 2015). These activities are 
inherent in the frame creation steps of con­
text and field, and rely on various means to 
obtain the necessary knowledge.

12.4 Resources required 
The level and degree to which stakehold­
ers are involved in design processes, is of­
ten associated with the resources available. 
And as the preceding section described, this 
was likewise the case in this research proj­
ect. Frame creation projects are in general 
considered to be a lengthy process, which 
requires getting familiarized with the prob­
lem, approaching actual and possible stake­
holders, motivating them to participate, etc. 
(Dorst, 2015). Studying a complex problem 
situation through archaeological research, 
is a process that cannot be rushed, which 
often requires two to three months (Dorst, 
2015). These activities are considered a key 
deliverable of the frame creation approach, 
as they can act as mirrors helping organiza­
tions to understand why and how they are 
stuck in their current problem-solving pro­
cesses (Dorst, 2015). 
The archaeological efforts of the design 
team was likewise found to be an activi­
ty that requires extensive research, before 
turning towards the clashing rationalities, 
i.e. the paradoxes, that prevent the problem 
owner from moving forward. This gives 
rise to the thought of how and by whom 
frame creation projects should be facilitat­
ed, especially within the healthcare sector 
where resources tend to be limited. 
Dorst states that the most significant bar­
rier to reframing, is the problem owner’s 
resistance to the journey, as they perceive 
the time and resources required to reopen 
a problem situation to be excessive (Dorst, 
2015). Therefore, clients often turn to quick 
solutions, which designers often refer to as 
“a fixation on their initial idea for the proj­
ect” (Dorst, 2015). In the initial stages of the 
research project, this was likewise the per­
ception of the design team: Although not 
resistant to change per se, the MIL-project 
group had become attached to the idea of 
measuring patient experience whilst pre­
serving the quantitative and service in­
spired question of recommendation. A fixa­
tion, which placed their initiative in a loop 
of barriers to the objective of PCSA. With 
this notion in mind, it can be argued, that 
the additional resources required to launch 
a frame creation project would account for 
the time consuming activities that a project 
such as MIL have already employed. This 
reasoning is in favour of the frame creation 
approach.

Resources, however, extend beyond the 
notion of time and effort. When employ­

ing complex problem situations, five ele­
ments of design practice assert themselves 
as particularly crucial (Dorst, 2015). These 
five practices - 1) coevolution, 2) develop­
ing problem situations, 3) handling frames, 
4) exploring themes and 5) fostering a dis­

frame creation model (Dorst, 2015). The 
ability to carry out these principles, is ac­
cording to Dorst considered congruent 
with a significant degree of design exper­
tise, respectively denoted as expert and 
master level (Dorst, 2015) Using frame cre­
ation as a design thinking principle within 
this particular research project, have under­
lined this notion. Among other things, the 
approach requires the ability to facilitate it­
eration processes, to ensure contextual en­
gagement and to shift problem situations 
based on the study of human behaviour. 
In other words, and based on the perspec­
tives of the design team, frame creation is 
an approach that should be practiced and 
facilitated by designers, to ensure a fruitful 
outcome. Any other conduct would simply 
require too much effort, for instance if the 
problem owners were to embark upon such 
a comprehensive approach without any ex­
ternal guidance. If this were the case, prob­
lem owners would quickly find themselves 
in a situation that leads back to the evident 
paradox of resources. With that being said, 
frame creation has proven to be a practice 
that relies on stakeholder engagement and 
co-creation. Without this, neither the prob­
lem situation nor the proposed solutions 
will be able to grasp and obtain a desired 
outcome. When applying frame creation, a 
balance between administrating, facilitat­
ing and co-creating must therefore be es­
tablished. 
12.5 Frame-based 
innovation levels 
The use of frame creation, as an approach to 
mitigate an open-ended and complex situ­
ation, relies on the conditions and patterns 
that underlie the problem: 1) the existence 
of opposing views or conflicting aims, 2) no 
obvious solution is apparent, 3) the prob­
lem can be placed in an expanded context, 
4) there is an open-minded champion with­
in the problem owner that is seeking a solu­
tion, and 5) earlier solution attempts have 
not resulted in a satisfactory resolution, 
to the point where there is a willingness 
to take a different approach (Dorst, 2015). 
These conditions are corresponding to the 
case of MIL, which have served as the foun­
dation of this research project:
The two-sided objective of MIL, i.e. the cul­
tivation of PCSA, has been the revolving 
factor that has generated a problem situa­
tion of clashing rationalities, which were 
likewise identified in Chapter 6. Here, the 
problem is not the objective itself, but rath­
er the patterns of relationship, the exist­
ing framing, in which the problem owner 
attempts to solve a problem: In spite of an 
obvious desire to create something new, the 
means to do so have to remain the same. 
This approach only leaves little space for 
new, innovative and valuable solutions, 
which does not seem to generate the de­
sired objective. This notion asserts itself in 
particular, throughout the archaeological 
activities which reveal the challenges asso­
ciated with the current pathways to quality 
development (See Chapter 5). Likewise, the 
initial meetings with the problem owners, 
i.e. the project group of MIL, revealed that 
they, to some extent, were aware that parts 
of their approach might be inadequate in 
regards to their objectives. The current 
equation of what, how and outcome, under

også anvendes i FFT. Et fælles træk ved denne tilgang er, at den genererer en 
høj svarprocent, men ofte på bekostning af det kvalitative indhold. Dette bety­
der, at MIL-projektet skal inddrage yderligere metoder, for at opnå et grundlag for 
kvalitetsforbedringer. Tilsvarende, genererer LUP og DLP høje, men uspecifikke 
responsrater, hvilket vanskeliggør fortolkningen og omsætningen af resultater til 
konkrete handlinger. 
Ressourcer versus patient pleje
Kvalitetsudvikling handler i høj grad om ressourcer og prioritering, som oftest 
påhviler den respektive afdeling. MIL projektet faciliterer på nuværende tidspunkt 
store dele af kvalitetsindsatsen, men med en målsætning om at overdrage denne 
til de respektive afdelinger på længere sigt. Denne ambition afhænger af flere 
faktorer, der er direkte relateret til ressourcer såsom økonomi, kompetencer og 
tid. Mange klinikere anskuer kvalitetsudviklingsprojekter som en interessekonflikt, 
da dette kan kompromittere deres evne til at praktisere grundlæggende patien­
tomsorg. Dette kan resultere i en generel skepsis og indsigelse mod ændringer, 
som kolliderer med MIL-projektets mål om at placere ejerskabet og ansvaret i de 
respektive afdelinger på længere sigt.

Interaktiv kampagne
Begrebet “service” har været et vigtigt element at undersøge, da det indgår som 
en essentiel del af MIL projektets PCSA målsætning. I deres projekt er begreb­

Dette lægger op til behovet for at redefinere målsætningen om at kultivere en

Sammenhæng mellem frames
Målsætningen om PCSA er en kompleks udfordring, da visionen bygger på 
adskillige faktorer, som ikke blot kan løses ved at ibrugtage én frame - ét 
løsningsforslag. Hver frame bør derfor betragtes som et element i en større hel­
hed, som en kombineret løsning. De præsenterede frames er udviklet ud fra de ni 
sammenhængende tematikker, og i særdeleshed det nedenstående loop mel­
lem kvalitetsarbejde, kvalitetsoplevelse og anerkendelse, som hver især menes 
at kunne understøtte PCSA. 
Som specificeret, vil en uformel og kvalitativ dialog mellem patient og kliniker 
fungere som en platform, der kan facilitere en debat mellem klinikere, der om­
handler tilgange til at integrere patientfeedback i kvalitetsudvikling (frame 1). For 
at muliggøre dette, skal klinikere have de nødvendige midler til at anvende pa­
tientens oplevelser til kvalitetsforbedring. Her vil designværktøjer fra brugerin­
volverende praksis være essentielle, da løsningen vil kunne give klinikere nye til­
gange til at idegenerere og konceptualisere forbedrede sundhedsydelser (frame 
2). Nye koncepter og ideer skal testes og verificeres på en agil og hurtig måde. 
Ved at inddrage en tilgang inspireret fra produktudvikling, vil klinikere være i 
stand til at bevæge sig ud over konceptuelle tanker og gå videre mod konkrete 
resultater (frame 3). 
Med målsætningen om at facilitere en fælles forståelse af service, ud fra et sund­
hedsfagligt perspektiv, udgør den sidste og fjerde frame en overordnet tilgang, 
hvorunder de tre andre løsningsforslag agerer. Ved at inkorporere alle løsnings­
forslag som en udviklingsproces, er det designteamets opfattelse, at både tanke­
gang og praksis vil kunne bæres i retning mod de overordnede målsætninger i 
Livskraft.

14 Conclusion
This master thesis has investigated how 
frame creation as a design thinking prin­
ciple can contribute to the complex and 
open-ended challenge of cultivating PCSA 
within healthcare. In this endeavour, the re­
search has aspired to employ the nine steps 
that define this approach, based on the 
MIL-project and the cross-sectoral work of 
TST. 
An eminent discovery to this research is 
that the application of frame creation pro­
vides problem solvers with the ability to 
move beyond conventional problem solv­
ing measures and to discover new and 
valuable solutions. The approach relies on 
principles, which require a significant de­
gree of design expertise as well as ressou­
rces, thus stressing the need for an external, 
expert or master level facilitator to handle 
the frame creation process and to ensure a 
valuable outcome.
Within the case framework, the approach 
has demonstrated the power of uncover­
ing the interconnected reasons to why the 
MIL-project has not been able to attain their 
objectives of PCSA. Likewise, frame cre­
ation in a systematic and creative way, fa­
cilitates the appearance of common themes 
and possibilities, which strive to push the 
existing frames and allow for new solutions 
to emerge. In return, this identification en­
dorses an end-result, potentially more 
fruitful than the preceding solutions. As a 
design thinking principle, frame creation 
has assisted the formation of four new uni­
fied scenarios, which hold the potential to 
cultivate PCSA within healthcare, without 
this being a momentary and fugitive effort 
within organizational program activities. 
The quality, applicability and benefit of the 
proposed solutions, however, rely highly 
on the participatory manner of the project, 
as stakeholder involvement increases the 
prospects of frame adoption and innova­
tion.

For every design project, it is common prac­
tice to reflect on the quality of the product 
or the concept. In our case, it is less straight­
forward, as the main deliverable of this the­
sis has not revolved around the new frames 
themselves, but rather the generated learn­
ings and knowledge from applying frame 
creation, as a design thinking principle, 
within healthcare. The decision of applying 
a research and learning objective, placed 
within the context of of MIL and TST, de­
rives from the need to balance academic 
learning and relevancy, while handing over 
relevant and useful knowledge to our prob­
lem owner. We believe that this balance has 
been achieved through this report as well 
as the client material. 
Throughout the project, the quality of 
learnings and knowledge have proven to 
be closely conjoined with the specific ap­
proach to frame creation, which ultimate­
ly also affects the secondary product of the 
thesis, i.e. the proposed futures. In other 
words, the quality of the product also af­
fects the quality of learning. Moreover, by 
applying an approach relatively new to the 
design team, the challenges have likewise 
contributed to the overall learnings, by 
highlighting the difficulties and relevance 
of each framing creation step. The same at­
terts itself in regards to the circumstances 
and limitations of the project, which might 
have affected the overall potential of the 
frame creation application.

15.4 Reflection on 
stakeholder interaction
Learning objectives
5	 Plan and execute fieldwork with 
multiple stakeholders to acquire

6	 Create and convey knowledge 
in a form suitable for the various

8	 Evaluate the chosen solution

Both fieldwork and the TST workshop 
proved to be tremendously valuable for 
the understanding of the context, as were 
the interviews with the MIL project group 
and other stakeholders. To evaluate the 
proposed frames and in order to gath­
er input from stakeholders, a workshop 
was planned for the 27th of March with 
representatives of the MIL-project group 
and TST. However, due to the outbreak of 
Covid-19 all resources in NOH were redi­
rected to handling the crisis, which effec­
tively meant that the workshop was can­
celled. 
The purpose of this workshop was twofold: 
1) to present our findings and frames and 
2) to co-create and discuss the proposed 
futures’ feasibility and practicality with 
stakeholders - this objective was deemed 
particularly important as it promotes frame 
ownership and adoption. For now, the justi­
fication of the proposed frames rely on our 
interpretation and knowledge. Optimally 
they would benefit from being verified by 
the relevant stakeholders. As a replacement 
to the workshop, client material in the form 
of a booklet has been developed and pro­
vided to the MIL-project group. This writ­
ten and visual format will most likely have 
another value for both stakeholders and 
the design team, than the workshop would 
have produced. The most profound risk of 
no evaluation and iteration in collaboration 
between client and designers, is that the 
proposed and potentially fruitful frames 
are discarded by the client due to a dimin­
ished usability.
In general, the degree of stakeholder in­
teraction has been lower than intend­
ed throughout the project. A stakeholder 
group that appears to be crucial, are the pa­
tients. In an optimal setting we would have 
included patients in workshops and inter­
views in order to get their perspective. Due 
to the patient segment of the TST, this was, 
however, not possible. Their values are 
therefore based on observation or second­
hand information from clinicians. Other 
interesting and valuable stakeholders rep­
resent municipalities and other existing or 
potential collaborators of the TST. But with 
a restricted project time frame and gener­
al bureaucracy, the interaction with outer 
circle stakeholders was limited. It is diffi­
cult to say just how the lack of interaction 
with patients and stakeholders within the 
outer field has influenced the value and va­
lidity of the frames. It is our belief that in­
teraction would have provided the design 
team with a deeper understanding, which 
could have led to different and possibly im­
proved frames.
