Abstract
A great challenge in breast cancer pathology is inter- and intra tumor heterogeneity. Traditionally,
pathologists have assessed immunohistochemical (IHC) stained cancer tissues in microscopes.
In addition to being time consuming and expensive this also holds great inter-reader variabil-
ity. Along with an increase in breast cancer incidence, this presses the need for new automatic
solutions.
The aim of this thesis is to investigate and develop new automatic methods for quantifying het-
erogeneity in breast cancer tissue samples (n = 161), and relate this to the metastatic spread of
cancer to sentinel lymph nodes. In this context, heterogeneity relates to the morphology and
distribution of cancer cells clusters. Based on this, a rich set of morphological and distributional
features (p = 213) is generated. This also includes a variety of tumor budding related features not
seen in previous works within this ﬁeld of research. Each tissue sample is classiﬁed with respect
to lymph node status using different machine learning models (logistic regression, LDA, and Ran-
dom forest). In addition to this, different feature selection, shrinkage and dimension reduction
methods were applied in order to optimize the classiﬁcation models.
No signiﬁcant relationship were found between the generated features and the response of lymph
node status with the features and models presented in this thesis.
Key words: Breast cancer, heterogeneity, growth patterns, morphology, lymph node status (metastatic
spread), cancer clusters, tumor budding, hotspots, PCK staining

Introduction
Breast cancer is a heterogenous disease both in its appearance and in the way it progresses. Develop-
ing more accurate diagnostics are therefore crucial to optimize future breast cancer treatment planning.
This thesis investigates the growth patterns of cancer cell clusters in tissue samples to examine if quan-
tiﬁcation makes it possible to predict the lymph node status of the patients, i.e. if the cancer has spread
to the lymph nodes. The breast cancer tissue sample is thus categorized as either being metastatic or
non-metastatic. The tissue characteristics (from now on referred to as features) are explained by mor-
phology and distributions of cancer cell clusters, as well as pathological scores of the samples, while the
categorization are performed using different supervised machine learning algorithms.
The following paragraphs will introduce the problem of this thesis, from the motivation of looking at
Breast cancer, to the emerging ﬁeld of Digital pathology making actual digital and automatized his-
tological assessment possible. This is followed by a short introduction to Visiopharm A/S, which has
provided the digital platform of the image processing and analysis conducted in this thesis. Finally, the
aim and background for the problem is introduced more thoroughly.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Breast cancer
Breast cancer is the most common cancer in the world among women. In 2012, it was
estimated that 1.7 million new breast cancer cases were diagnosed corresponding to 25% of all female
cancers [1][2] (Denmark: 4,897 new cases annually (average over the years 2009-2013) [3]). Over the
last decades a signiﬁcant increase in the incidence of breast cancer has been reported in the Western
world [1], illustrated in Figure 1.1(a). The main reason is prolonged longevity and lifestyle changes, but
also the larger number of women actually attending screening programs today [4]. Despite the increase
in incidence, the mortality rates are simultaneously decreasing, resulting from earlier diagnosis through
screening programs and signiﬁcant improvements in cancer treatments [5], illustrated in Figure 1.1(b).
The growing number of patients and diagnostic tests increases the workload and costs of cancer diag-
nostics and treatment, pressing the need for improved and standardized solutions.
Digital pathology
Microscopic examination of breast cancer tissue, also referred to as histopathol-
ogy1, is an essential part of diagnostics, prognostics and treatment planning in breast cancer. In clinical
medicine, histopathology more speciﬁcally refers to the microscopic examination of a biopsy or surgical
specimen by a pathologist after the specimen has been processed and histological sections have been
placed onto glass slides [6]. A common classiﬁcation method in histopathology is immunohistochem-
istry (IHC) staining, which refers to the process of detecting antigens (e.g. proteins) in biologic tissue
using antibodies [7]. Thin slices of tissue are then stained with reagents to visualize an antigen of inter-
est, also called a biomarker. An example of a IHC stained tissue section that visualizes a biomarker in
the cancerous cytoplasma with a brown color is illustrated in Figure 1.2(a).
Traditionally, pathologists have visually assessed IHC stained tissue samples using microscopy, which
in addition to being time consuming and expensive also holds a great inter reader variability [8]. In the
last decade signiﬁcant technological advances in the evolution of digital pathology has emerged in both
clinical and research areas. This is made possible by the development of modern scanners, introduc-
ing high microscopic resolution and rapid, automatic digitization of many numbers of slices. Digital
pathology has obvious advantages in its various application possibilities. Furthermore, it has revolu-
tionized the international cooperation in pathology, allowing tissue samples to easily be sent across the
world. Limitations as the need of large data storage space and investment in new technology and soft-

Digital pathology is still a new idea with the concept being widespread in medicine only over the past

to the increasing complexity and individualization of patient treatments. Getting automatic, standard-
ized, and objective analyses with a limited quality control is therefore considered the next critical step
in histopathology. The task of automating pathological tissue assessment is difﬁcult due to the huge
amount of data in form of cells and structures contained in histological images [12]. Nevertheless, var-
ious research groups and companies have already developed highly promising software, which is cur-
rently being implemented on a diagnostic research basis worldwide. Denmark has emerged as a pio-
neer in the clinical implementation of both digital pathology and automated assessment procedures,
running various pilot projects for supporting the manual scoring. Several research groups have thus
already shown that these automated, digital biomarker readings are more stable and less dependent on
the evaluating pathologist than manual reading with a microscope [9].
Visiopharm A/S
Visiopharm A/S (Hørsholm, Denmark) provides quantitative digital pathology solu-
tions to pathologists and scientists, with their clinical implemented image analysis program, the Visio-
pharm Integrator System (VIS). In the past years VIS has emerged in bio pharmaceutical companies,
research centers, and hospitals all over the world [13]. Visiopharm’s automated image analysis tech-
niques have the ability of scoring individual cells and biomarker expressions over large areas of tissue,
while providing the platform for designing customized image analysis protocols. This allows us to in-
vestigate relevant heterogeneity aspects in terms of quantiﬁcation, and makes VIS an optimal software
for this thesis. In Figure 1.2(b)+(c) an example of pre- and post image analysis execution in VIS, that
detects relevant cancerous tissue areas can be seen.
Aim: Quantiﬁcation of heterogeneity in breast cancer tissue
A great challenge in modern breast can-
cer pathology is how to deal with inter- and intra tumor heterogeneity of cancer tissue. Breast cancer is
highly heterogeneous both molecularly and histologically, because it has a variety of subtypes and can
progress very differently between patients and between different parts of the tumor tissue. In general
heterogeneity in breast cancer tissue has a broad deﬁnition covering almost all possible diversity as-
pects found. In relation to histopathology however, the interpretation of the term is of course visual and
related to issues such as morphology, distribution, intensity of stains, and biomarker quantity. An ex-
ample of intra-tumor heterogeneity, showing different densities and distributions of colored cells across
an IHC stained tissue, is illustrated in Figure 1.3.
The aim of this thesis is to digitally investigate and develop new methods for analyzing and quantifying
breast cancer tissue samples in relation to heterogeneity. The idea originated at Visiopharm A/S, who
learned from several external studies, that a speciﬁc phenomenon called tumor budding in colorectal

The breast cancer data acquired from Visiopharm A/S did not include any survival data on the patients,
and consequently the quantiﬁcation of growth patterns are deﬁned in relation to metastatic spread to
the lymph nodes. Metastatic spread holds a strong prognostic value, since it accounts for the deaths of
around one-third of all cancer patients [16]. Lymph node status is therefore considered a ﬁne surrogate
for survival, also because the majority of breast cancers metastasize via the lymphatic system and blood
vessels. Whereas blood can be analyzed by blood samples, the evaluation of metastases in the lymph
nodes calls for a histological interpretation and thereby surgery. Historically the surgical removal of
lymph nodes has the highest morbidity associated with breast cancer surgery. In addition to this, each
healthy lymph node removed, increases the patient’s side-effects in terms of pain and a deterioration
of the draining in the lymphatic system. Being able to actually assess the degree of metastatic spread
from looking at biopsies from the tumor could thus consequently prevent unnecessary surgery of lymph
nodes.
The quantiﬁcation of heterogeneity in this study is performed using different statistical classiﬁcation
models of machine learning. These models take morphological and distributional information on the
clusters as inputs to yield an output that either quantiﬁes the probability that the tissue have metasta-
sized and/or directly classiﬁes the tissues into classes of having metastasized or not. The overall goal of
the classiﬁcation is thus to produce a prognostic aid in the pathological evaluation of breast cancer to
secure a more exact outcome for patients.

Related work
Biologically, the aim of tumour buds seems clear, namely to ﬁght themselves through the per-
itumoural connective tissue, to evade the host’s defence and ﬁnally invade the lymphatic and
blood vessels with the consequence of local and distant metastasis - Lugli et al. [17]
In the past decades several studies have shown that the presence of a high degree of tumor budding
indicated a more aggressive activity of colorectal cancer and the incidence of budding was higher in
patients with lymphatic, venous, or perineural invasion compared with patients that did not exhibit
these histologic ﬁndings. It was shown by Hase et al. in 1993 [14], and later conﬁrmed by Koike et al. in
2008 [18], Nakamura et al. in 2008 [19], and Wang et al. in 2009 [20]. Some of these ﬁndings were also
found in esophageal cancer [21] and pancreatic cancer [22].
Tumor budding in relation to breast cancer is on the other hand a fairly new subject of investigations
(Liang et al. [23], Salhia et al. [24], and Gujam et al. [25]).
In 2013 Liang et al. [23] (n = 160) investigated the prognostic power of tumor budding in ductal breast
cancer. They concluded that the ﬁve-year survival rate was better in cases with low-grade budding com-
pared to high-grade budding. Furthermore, they found that specimens with high-grade budding were
marginally associated with the presence of lymph node metastases (P = 0.050, using Chi-square statis-
tics). They deﬁned tumor budding as an isolated tumor cell or clusters of <5 tumor cells found in H&E
stained slides at a microscopic magniﬁcation of 20x. The buds were counted in the ﬁve densest bud-
ding areas (manually chosen) - and the highest count per case was used as the number of buds. PCK
stained slides was used, when it was difﬁcult to distinguish budded cells from non-cancerous cells. The
specimens were ﬁnally divided into low-graded and high-graded budding groups.
Salhia et al. (2015) [24] (n = 148) also studied the grade of tumor budding in relation to lymph node
metastases. During the study period of Salhia et al. (2015) Liang et al. [23] was published. Salhia et al.
conﬁrmed that a high tumor budding is linked to lymph node metastases. The authors used digitalized
single slides whole tissue sections and matched preoperative core biopsies of invasive breast cancer of
no special type (NST) that were stained with PCK. In the whole tissue sections, a high degree of tumor
buds was associated with lymph node metastases (P = 0.03), showing a slightly higher signiﬁcance than
Liang et al. Salhia used a tumor bud deﬁnition of ≤5 tumor cells. The number of buds was counted
manually on the whole tissue samples at a magniﬁcation of 40x and were matched with the core biop-
sies. Areas with the highest concentration of buds were selected for scoring. The authors scored tumor
buds dependent on their location (invasive front or tumor center). The scoring was based on an average

A third study conducted by Gujam et al. (2015) [25] (n = 474) also investigated and concluded that there

In addition to studies concerned with tumor budding, we found a study by Beck et al. from 2011 [26],

on epithelial features is perhaps not sufﬁcient. Furthermore it was found that the interactions between
tumor- and stromal cells, seem to be of great prognostic value. None of the signiﬁcant features resemble
the ones used in this thesis and will not be elaborated further. The stromal importance can however still
be of interest since the features extracted from clusters as in our study will also have a relevance to the
stromal surroundings.
An important aspect of this thesis is to identify if there is any hidden information in the growth patterns
of breast cancer tissue, which have not yet been demonstrated. Our approach is different from the pre-
vious studies, since we wish to combine the very speciﬁc phenomenon of tumor budding with a variety
of other morphological and distributional features. This changes the agenda from ﬁnding a relation-
ship between a feature and overall patient survival, into a classiﬁcation problem of ﬁnding signiﬁcant
features that can contribute to the prognostic investigation of breast cancer patients, most similar to
the study by Beck et al. Furthermore we will introduce a standardized method for tumor bud detection,
by automatically producing a score. This also means that we will detect features from the entire tissue
instead of invasive margins as in the articles concerning tumor budding in breast cancer. The method-
ological details for both feature selection and tumor budding speciﬁcations will be further described in

3.1.1
Anatomy and function of the breast
Breasts are mainly composed of glandular tissue and stroma (supporting tissue). The glandular tissue
consists of 15-20 lobes built up by a cluster of lobules where the milk producing cells (alveoli) are located.
Milk produced in the lobules exits through small ducts (lactiferous ducts) that terminates at the nipple.
The stromal tissue includes adipose (fat) tissue, ﬁbrous connective tissue and a complex network of
ligaments, nerves, lymph nodes, and blood vessels that ﬁltrates the breasts. The anatomy of the breast
is illustrated in Figure 3.1 [5][7][27][28].

3.1.2
Development of cancer
In a fully developed organism, the size of most tissue and organs are approximately constant; if cells
die, they are replaced by new cells. If a cell type starts to divide faster than it dies due to alterations in
the growth regulation, an accumulation of cells in the given area is formed over time. This abnormal
cluster of cells is denoted a neoplasm meaning new growth or a tumor. Tumors are characterized by a
lack of normal regulatory control over cell growth and division, which make them grow uncoordinated
and autonomous.
A tumor can be benign or malign; if the tumor grows without invading the surrounding tissue, the tu-
mor is non-invasive and is said to be benign, whereas a tumor that grows into the surrounding tissue is
invasive and is said to be malign. A malign tumor is denoted cancer. The differences between benign
and malignant tumors are determined by (1) the morphology of the tumor cells, (2) the rate of growth,
(3) local invasion, and (4) the ability to metastasize [5][29].
3.1.3
Types of breast cancer
Breast cancer is not a single disease, but covers > 20 different subtypes. Most breast cancers are carci-
nomas, a type of cancer that starts in the epithelial cells (cells lining the glands and ducts of the breast).
These carcinomas are divided into in situ and invasive carcinomas. Other types of breast cancers can
occur such as sarcomas, which arise from the connective tissue (<1%) [2][30][32].
In situ carcinomas
In situ carcinomas are deﬁned as preinvasive lesions. The two most common in
situ carcinomas are ductal carcinomas in situ (DCIS) and lobular carcinomas in situ (LCIS); DCIS is lo-
cated within the ducts, while LCIS is located within the lobules. Both types are considered pre-malignant
and can over time develop into an invasive cancer if not surgically removed [2][29][31][32].
Invasive carcinomas
Invasive carcinomas are characterized by having spread to the surrounding tis-
sue. Invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC) is the most common type of invasive cancer and accounts for
∼80% of all invasive breast cancers. IDC distinguishes from DCIS by having broken through the wall
of the duct and has begun to invade the breast tissue. Invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC) is the second
most common type of breast cancer (∼10%) and distinguishes from LCIS by having broken through the
lobule wall and has begun to invade the surrounding breast tissue.
Invasive carcinomas are also characterized by their ability to spread to the lymph nodes and other sites
of the body, and thus in general they have a poorer patient prognosis compared to in situ carcinomas.
Breast cancer is a highly heterogeneous disease and many different subtypes of both IDC and ILC exist.
The no special type (NST) denotes the most common subset. Figure 3.2 illustrates the most characteris-
tic morphological differences in IDC and ILC [2][32][29].
3.1.4

predict the prognosis of the patient. Grading is performed according to the histologic or cellular charac-
teristics of the tumor, while staging is performed according to the clinical spread of the cancer. Different

3.1.5
Lymph node involvement
Besides grading and staging the tumor, an important prognostic factor is the lymph node involvement.
Around 75% of lymphatic ﬂuid from the breasts drains into the axillary lymph nodes (ALNs), and analyz-
ing their status of being positive or negative in relation to metastatic cancer has proven to be an impor-
tant indicator of patient survival. However, each healthy lymph node surgically removed increases the
patient’s side-effects in terms of pain and a deterioration of the draining in the lymphatic system. In the
late 1970’s it was found that an assessment of the metastatic spread to sentinel nodes (SN) in the breast,
were great indicators of the spread to the ALNs [37]. The sentinel node(s) are deﬁned as the ﬁrst node(s)
in the chain of lymph nodes to drain the breast [38]. A negative SN is predictive of the absence of metas-
tases in the ALNs. The removal of the SN instead of ALNs thus lowers the risks associated with surgery
and its side-effects signiﬁcantly. Today, thin slicing of the SN and subsequent examination is standard
practice. However, there is still risks associated to surgery. Furthermore, it is not always possible to
actually ﬁnd the SN [37].

Digital pathology
The aim of digital pathology is to examine and interpret images containing different cell- and tissue
structures at high resolutions. As described in Chapter 1, the ﬁeld of pathology has undergone great
improvement from conventional microscopy to digital images during the last decades. This does not
only ease the work of the pathologists but also make image sharing and storing much more accessible.
The slides used for digital pathology are essentially the same as the ones used for microscopic pathology.
The following sections elaborates on the preparation methods of histological tissue slides, biomarkers
used for staining and microscopic morphology of breast cancer tissue slides.
3.2.1
Preparation of specimens
The most common tissue preparation routines include three steps: Fixation →embedding and sec-
tioning →staining of the specimen. Fixation is performed to preserve the tissue structure for further
processing. A commonly used ﬁxative is formalin, which binds protein molecules together. Embedding
and sectioning involves an embedding of the sample in a medium (parafﬁn in this case), which replaces
the water in the tissue slice and solidiﬁes the sample. This allows the specimen to be thinly sliced in
sections of µm.
During the preparation steps, the natural appearance of the cells will unavoidably be affected by arti-
facts. These artifacts include shrinking of cells during ﬁxation, application of ripples and wrinkles during
cutting and when put onto glass slides. An example of artifacts in a tissue slide from the data set, along

3.2.2
Biomarker of breast cancer tissue
The preparation of specimens leaves the very thinly sliced tissue sections totally colorless. Therefore in
microscopic as well as digital pathological diagnostics, staining of tissue structures plays an essential
role in visualizing structures. The most commonly used stains in histology are hematoxylin and eosin
(H&E). Hematoxylin colors the cell nuclei blue and the eosin colors the cytoplasm, connective tissue
and other extracellular substances red [6][39].
As brieﬂy introduced in Chapter 1, there are different cancer related biomarkers which can be visualized
by IHC staining. In relation to cancer, a biomarker refers to a substance or process that is indicative of
the presence of cancer in the body [40]. In this study marking of cancerous clusters is done with pan-
cytokeratin (PCK). PCK is an antibody that binds to antigens in the cancerous cell cytoplasm and stains
it brown [13], see Figure 3.3. In relation to tumor budding, PCK staining seems to have some advantages
compared to H&E staining. As described by Liang et. al, it can be difﬁcult to distinguish between cancer
cells and e.g. inﬂammatory cells [23]. PCK staining seems to solve this problem.
In addition to the subtypes of breast cancer introduced in Section 3.1.3, breast cancers can be catego-
rized into subtypes based on histological biomarker ﬁndings, which can be used as an aid in the patient
treatment planning. The American Society of Clinical Oncology/College of American Pathologists (AS-

human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)1 [32][40][41].

3.3
Statistical binary classiﬁcation
A statistical classiﬁcation model (or classiﬁer) is used to categorize an input based on a set of features
to an output of different qualitative classes. This study deals with a binary classiﬁcation problem of cat-
egorizing tissue samples into classes of "metastases" or "no metastases". Since the tissue samples to
be analyzed are already binary scored in relation to metastatic spread in the sentinel nodes by pathol-
ogists, we are dealing with a supervised learning problem. This enables us to estimate parameters on
one part of the data set (training data) and subsequently evaluate the scoring accuracy of the model on
the remaining data (test data) that can predict the responses of new data. There are various supervised
classiﬁers to be used for this task, and it is not always easy to predict whether one classiﬁer performs
better than another. The result depends on the speciﬁc data in terms of characteristics of the inputs and
the amount of available data.
At the beginning of this thesis our knowledge of classiﬁcation methods was limited. Therefore we began
by taking a crash course in machine learning and slowly expanded our knowledge in the ﬁeld. The theory
below includes the following classiﬁers: Linear regression, logistic regression, linear discriminant analy-
sis, and Random forests. In addition to this different model optimization approaches are presented. The
formulas and theory used in this section are primarily taken from an online course in Machine learn-
ing [43] and an introductory book covering statistical learning [44].
3.3.1
Variables and terminology
This subsection introduces the variables and terminology used in this thesis. In supervised learning we
want to predict an output based on a set of inputs. In the following this input and output are referred
to as feature(s) and response(s), respectively. To build a supervised learning model, we need a training
set of data, that enables us to predict the response of new unseen data. The number of training data
samples is denoted n (equals the number of samples), while the number of features to make predictions
is denoted p (for predictors). A feature vector for a given patient is denoted x with length p, while the
response is presented in a vector y with length n. Collecting features from all tissue samples can thus
be presented in an n × p matrix X . Single elements from the vectors and matrix are written as x j , yi,
and Xi j , where i = 1,2,...,n and j = 1,2,...,p. The qualitative outputs of "metastases" or "no metastases"
are coded numerically by dummy variables 0 and 1. So consequently y will be a vector containing n
numbers of 0’s and 1’s.

Linear Discriminant Analysis
Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) is another linear classiﬁcation model, that seeks to ﬁnd the features
that discriminate the data into two or more classes. It originated as an extension of Fisher’s discriminant
analysis from 1936, taking some different assumptions than the method originally presented [49]. As
for logistic regression it involves the modeling of the probability that a response belongs to a certain
class. The difference however lies in the method of estimating the model coefﬁcients. Here LDA uses
Bayes theorem for probability estimation while taking some assumptions concerning the distribution of
features.

we need a class-speciﬁc density function fk(x) that express the probability P(x|y = k) and a prior prob-
ability πk that represents the probability P(y = k) of an observation belonging to the class k.

3.4
Feature selection and regularization
Different improvements can be made to the linear model, if it does not seem to behave properly. In
machine learning dealing with the so-called bias-variance trade-off is an inevitable issue. If the rela-
tionship between the features and their response is actually linear, the model is said to have a low bias.
If the relationship is however not linear, we are said to have a high bias and are underﬁtting the data.
The opposite problem of high variance or overﬁtting the data can also occur, and is the result of high
variance on the least squares estimates. This phenomenon often occurs when n is smaller than p, and
results in bad predictions on the response. The goal of linear classiﬁcation is therefore to ﬁnd the happy
medium in between under- and overﬁtting the data. As explained later in this thesis, we are dealing with
a rather small dataset which are to be explained by a larger number of features. This means that we will
most likely overﬁt our data. According to James et. al. [44] there are three main approaches for dealing
with overﬁtting: subset selection, shrinkage, and dimension reduction.
3.4.1
Subset selection
Subset selection is a way of reducing the number of features, by removing the ones that are irrelevant.
This will both ease the interpretability of the model and reduce the risk of overﬁtting. There are different
subset selection approaches available. The most exact of these, called best subset selection, investigates
the cost functions using all possible combinations of features. This results in investigating 2p models,
making it a very computational inefﬁcient approach. Furthermore, it seems to have statistical prob-
lems when p is large. More computational efﬁcient alternatives are the forward-, backward- and mixed
stepwise selection.
Forward- backward- and mixed stepwise selection
Forward stepwise selection investigates a restricted set of models, by iteratively adding the one feature
to the model that gives the greatest additional improvement to the ﬁt. The ﬁrst model assessed is the
null model, M0, onto which all p features are added one-at-the-time. The model showing the best re-
sult, given a speciﬁed criteria, e.g. largest F-statistics is thus denoted M1. In the next iteration, (p −1)
features are assessed, and so forth until there are no features left. In the end a resultant Mp models are
produced containing a different number of subset features. Choosing the single best model cannot be
done using the F-statistics, since the training error will always increase with the number of features due
to overﬁtting. The assessment must therefore be performed by making an adjustment to the training
error, taking account of the relative quality for the ﬁt regardless of model size. The Akaike information
criterion (AIC) has exactly this characteristic. The criterion was introduced in 1974 by Akaike [50], and
can be deﬁned for both least squares and maximum likelihood methods. The original version made for
maximum likelihood is deﬁned by:

again. In this way the best models for each number of features will be selected.

3.4.3
Dimension reduction
Principal component analysis
Principal component analysis (PCA) is a dimension reduction technique that compress the data into
new components that captures the essence of the original data, and is especially useful in data sets with
highly correlated features. The PCA generates a new set of variables denoted principal components,
which are linear combinations of the p features. The ﬁrst principal component captures the direction
of the data where the observations vary the most. This direction can also deﬁned as the component line
that minimizes the sum of the squared perpendicular distances between the observations and the line.
The second principal component spands the second most variation in the data, but must be uncorre-
lated and therefore perpendicular to the ﬁrst. In this way, we can end up with only a fewer number of
perpendicular principal components, that explains the variation in the data almost just as well as the
actual data [MATLAB][46][44]. An illustration of the PCA principle is seen in Figure 3.8.

3.5.1
Decision trees
Decision trees are characterized by segmenting the feature space into a number of regions using a set of
splitting rules and can be applied to regression and classiﬁcation problems. Decision trees use the split-
ting rules to partition the data into smaller and smaller subsets, that leaves a hierarchical pattern of the
data resembling a tree-like structure. Decision trees can structurally be said to consist of a set of nodes
and branches. In the start node the ﬁrst splitting rule is found, that splits the data into two internal nodes.
This procedure continues until a certain stop criterion is met, and the ﬁnal subsets of data are split into
the terminal nodes or leaves of the tree. Figure 3.9 shows an example of a regression tree and a classiﬁ-
cation tree, respectively, from James et al. [44]. Both trees consist of a number of splitting rules, which
starts at the top of the tree (years< 4.5 and Thal:a2). From this start node, the branches connects to the
internal nodes, from which branches again stems until a terminal node is reached. The outputs of these
can either be a quantitative measurement as seen in regression problems, or a qualitative measurement
as seen in classiﬁcation problems (e.g. yes/no).
The decision tree is build on the following to principles for the data [44]:
1. The feature space is divided into J distinct and non-overlapping regions, R1,...,RJ
2. For every observation that falls into RJ the same prediction is made, that is
(a) Regression; the mean of the response values for the training observations
(b) Classiﬁcation; each observation belongs to the most commonly occurring class of training
observations in the region to which it belongs.

3.5.2
Bagging
Decision trees have the disadvantage of often overﬁtting the training data, i.e. suffering from high vari-
ances. These high variances can be reduced using Bootstrap aggregation, or bagging. [44].
Bootstrapping involves taking repeated samples from the training set, and creating B different boot-
strapped data sets. Bagging is basically constructing B trees using the B training sets, and averaging the
resulting predictions. Since each tree has high variance, but low bias, averaging the B trees reduces the
overﬁtting of the ﬁt.
3.5.3
The approach of Random forests
Random forests basically uses the same approach as bagging. But each time a split in a tree is consid-
ered, a random subset of m features is selected as split candidates from the full set of p features. A fresh
sample of m features is taken at each split, and is typically chosen as m ≈pp. This means, that at each
split in the tree, the algorithm is not allowed to consider a majority of the available features. This is ben-
eﬁcial if there is one very strong feature and a number of other less-strong features. In the collection of
bagged trees, almost all trees will use the strong feature in the top split. Consequently, all of the bagged
trees will look similar to each other. Random forests however decorrelates the trees, which in general
yields better results than e.g. decision trees and bagging.

Summary on classiﬁers
After having introduced some different classiﬁers, let us summarize and elaborate on choosing one
method over the other. If we seek an interpretation of features and their relation to the response, it is
critical to choose a classiﬁer with great transparency. Here the linear classiﬁers (linear regression, logis-
tic regression and LDA) excels, because they are very interpretative on the direct relationship or inference
between the features and the responses. The problem of course arises, if they are not linearly related,
and only non-linear approaches can be used to explain the relationship. In general the interpretability

models are deﬁnitely preferred. Linear-, logistic regression and LDA are very similar in form, so the
quality of these classiﬁcations relies on whether or not the observations actually can be drawn from a
Gaussian distribution. Tree-based methods are in their simple form also very intuitive and easy to in-

value. Determining which classiﬁer to use thus very much depend on the data set in question, but anal-
ysis of the data can hopefully elucidate which methods are most obvious to use.

3.7.2
Cross-validation
Cross-validation works by holding out a subset of the samples when training a classiﬁer, and then ap-
plying the supervised model to the excluded samples. There are many approaches for performing cross-
validation. One way is the validation set approach, which split the available data into three groups of
training, validation and test sets. The validation set can thus be used to optimize the model and to give
a more realistic estimate of the test error than the one produced from the training error. When only a
smaller data set is available, the so called leave-one-out-cross-validation (LOOCV) is often used. LOOCV
holds out a single sample, trains the model on the remaining, and produces the estimate on the test
error of this single sample. This is done for all samples until n estimated test errors are produced. The
LOOCV estimate on the test error is found as the average of these n estimated test errors. The LOOCV is
more computational inefﬁcient than the validation set approach, but in generally yields a better result.
In the same way as LOOCV, a k-fold cross-validation can be performed. In stead of holding out a single
sample, it holds out a fold of k samples, and is therefore a bit more computational efﬁcient. Typical
number of folds used for testing the error rate are 5 and 10 [44].
3.7.3
Out-of-Bag Error estimation
Out-of-bag (OOB) error estimation is a method to estimate the test error of a bagged model, without
using cross-validation or the validation set approach.
The key to bagging is that trees are repeatedly ﬁt to bootstrapped subsets of observations. On average
each bagged tree makes use of around two-thirds of the observations. The remaining one-third of the
observations, which is not used to ﬁt a given bagged tree, is referred to as the OOB observations. The
response of each OOB observation can be made by passing them down the trees constructed with the
OOB observations and computing their error rate. This leads to a single OOB prediction by either av-
eraging these predictions (in case of regression) or taking the majority vote (in case of classiﬁcation). It
can be shown that if B is sufﬁciently large, the OOB error is approximately equal to the leave-one-out
cross-validation error. When bagging is performed on large data sets, cross-validation is computation-
ally cumbersome making OOB error estimations particular convenient [44][56].

Training and test set
The generalization performance of a learning method relates to its prediction capability on
independent test data. Assessment of this performance is extremely important in practice
since it guides the choice of learning method or model and gives us a measure of the quality
of the ultimately chosen model - Hastie et al. (2001) [46]
In the ideal setting the data set is split into three parts; a training, a validation, and a test set. The
training set is used for learning and training the model, the validation set is used for tuning the model
parameters, while the test set is ﬁnally used for assessing its general performance (accuracy). There is
no general rule on how to divide the data set since it depends on several factors including sample size.
Hastie et al (2001) [46] describes that a typically split can be 50% for training, 25% validation, and 25%
for testing. This setup however only works if the number of samples n is large. Therefore the data is
divided into a training and test set. Validation can however still be performed using cross-validation
approaches on the model.

5.2
Generating features
The ﬁrst step in generating features was to decide which output parameters to extract from the labeled
cancer clusters. A visual investigation of the tissue samples was therefore initiated with supervision
from Visiopharm A/S to see if any obvious features could be extracted which seemed to differ between
metastatic and non-metastatic tissue samples. In the examination, no evident features seemed to stand
out as predictors for categorizing the tissues, and tumor budding seemed to appear in both categories
of samples (but were not quantiﬁed). As a result of this examination, the features for classiﬁcation were
chosen on behalf of availability and the hypothesis concerning tumor buds. The next sections describe
the output parameters extracted using VIS and the generation of the features used in the classiﬁcation.
5.2.1
Output parameters from VIS
VIS has several morphological built-in output options as area, eccentricity, perimeter etc. Since no liter-
ature of our knowledge has suggested morphology of clusters to be informational on lymph node status
(other than the ones related to tumor budding), all possibly relevant morphological output parameters
are chosen. These are elaborated in Table 5.1. In addition to this, X and Y coordinates for each cancer
cluster is extracted, so they can be used for generating distributional features.
5.2.2
Exploratory analysis of output parameters
To asses the output parameters presented so far, an exploratory analysis were carried out. Histogram
of each output parameter is thus found in Appendix C (non-processed and zoomed histograms of the
output parameters) for metastastic and non-metastatic tissue samples. The histograms are informative
on the distribution of the parameters, and can be used to compare the two classes and see if any obvious
information is hidden.
First of all, the analysis shows that the two classes are clearly not separable. The analysis furthermore re-
veals that most of the histogram distributions are skewed both to the left and right. Quantifying skewed
histograms are not necessarily an intuitive task, since there is no clear center (as in symmetric distribu-
tions). According to [61] there are three typical metrics to use for skewed distributions; The mode, mean
and median. The mode describes the parameter value occurring most frequently in the histogram, and
is seen to take on identical values in almost all histograms. This quantiﬁcation therefore cannot be used.
Instead measures of mean, median, minimum, maximum and standard deviation of the parameter val-
ues are chosen, and used to generate the features expressing the cluster characteristics.

5.2.3
Tumor budding features
The tumor budding features of this thesis are generated from the output parameters presented. A tumor
bud is deﬁned as in most related works of Chapter 2: A single isolated cancer cell or a cluster of ≤5 cancer
cells (Salhia et al. [24], Gujam et al.[25]). Instead of manually counting the cells in the clusters to detect
tumor buds, a threshold value Tbud is set on the area of the clusters. This is done by visual examination
of several PCK stained tissue samples. A tumor bud is deﬁned as a cluster with an area ≤550 µm2, i.e.:
Tbud = 550µm2
Examples of tumor budding are shown in Figure 5.6.
The related works all use manual approaches quantifying tumor budding. Liang et al [23] and Gujam
et al. [25] counts the number of tumor buds in the ﬁve densest areas (manally chosen) and uses the
maximum bud count as a score. Salhia et al. locates 10 high-bud areas and uses the average as a score.
In this thesis, tumor budding features are generated by two automated methods designed in MATLAB
and VIS. In MATLAB, we developed an algorithm that ﬁnds the densest tumor budding area(s). This
algorithm is meant to resemble the results from manual counting in the densest areas. The output of
the algorithm is the number of buds in the densest area (Liang et al. and Gujam et al.), as well as an
average of the number of buds in the ﬁve densest areas (Salhia et al.). In addition to this, an APP that
ﬁnds the hotspots of a tumor bud produced heatmap is generated. The two approaches are referred to
as Tumor budding (MATLAB) and Tumor budding (VIS), respectively, and are elaborated below.

6.1
Stepwise selection for logistic regression
The built-in MATLAB function stepwiseglm.m is used for subset selection model optimization to gener-
ate a logistic regression model with reduced feature matrix dimensions. The function uses mixed step-
wise selection to determine the ﬁnal model based on the AIC, and iteratively evaluates the potential of
adding or removing of a feature. The AIC threshold value of adding a feature is set to zero, so a new
feature is only added if the change in calculated AIC going from one model to the next is less than zero.
In the same way, the AIC threshold value of removing a feature is set to 0.01, so a feature is removed if
the change in calculated AIC is higher than this value. Both of these values are defaults of the function.
The algorithm also checks whether any new feature is redundant, i.e. highly correlated with any other
features in the model. If this is the case, the feature will not be included, regardless of whether the AIC
is met. The initial model is speciﬁed by the null model, containing no features.
In addition to this, stepwiseglm.m enables an automatic inclusion of interaction terms in the model.
The feature matrix used in this thesis includes the squared values of the generated features, but due to
computational inefﬁciency interaction terms were omitted from the model. Since stepwiseglm.m can
generate the features of interaction terms automatically, a single tryout with this initial model is run.
6.2
Ridge regression and the lasso
The ridge regression and the lasso is performed using the MATLAB package glmnet_matlab [59], which
ﬁts the logistic regression model with a penalized likelihood approach. The package uses the elastic net
penalty as deﬁned in Eq. (3.21), except that it halves the Ridge regression penalty. Setting α = 0 thus ﬁts
the model using the Ridge regression penalty, while setting α = 1 ﬁts the model using the lasso penalty.
The optimization of regression coefﬁcients are found by maximizing the log-likelihood. Details of the
package are found in [59].
The function makes an initial ﬁt, to ﬁnd a sequence of 100 relevant values of λ. Subsequently a LOOCV
is run, for each value of lambda, ﬁnding a total of ’100 × number of samples’ classiﬁcation errors on the
training data, from which the optimal model coefﬁcients can be chosen. The function automatically
compute and visualize both the average cross-validation misclassiﬁcation error and standard deviation.
The best model coefﬁcients is based on the lambda that gives the smallest LOOCV average misclassiﬁ-
cation error or on the largest λ within one standard error of smallest misclassiﬁcation error. Which λ to
base the missclassiﬁcation error on is elaborated in Section 7.2.
In addition to testing the Ridge regression and the lasso, varying the effect of α adds the elastic net
penalty to the regression. To brieﬂy evaluate the effect of varying α, a hundred intermediate values are
tested, α = [0 : 0.01 : 1].
6.3
Sparse discriminant analysis
Sparse discriminant analysis (SDA) is a newer method made as an extension to the LDA and was pre-
sented by Clemmensen et al. in 2011 [55]. The sparse discriminant analysis is characterized by per-
forming feature selection and classiﬁcation simultaneously. The SDA algorithm is implemented in the
56
==PAGE:58==
CHAPTER 6. USAGE OF CLASSIFICATION METHODS
MATLAB package SpaSM by Karl Sjöstrand et al. and seeks to solve the problems that LDA has with high-
dimensional settings by shrinking and eliminating coefﬁcients using the property of the elastic net [53].
The elastic net is deﬁned as in Eq. (3.21), but split the tuning factors of the penalties into two separate
variables; the Ridge regression penalty tuned by γ and the lasso penalty tuned by λ. Details and refer-
ences of the SDA implementation can be found in the ofﬁcial website for the package [60].
The shrinking penalties for the elastic net are deﬁned by an optimization procedure that estimates the
lowest classiﬁcation error rate for different values doing 5-fold cross validation. Since the two penalties
cannot be optimized simultaneously, different constellations are tested. γ is deﬁned by the values of λ
chosen for Ridge regression, while the optimal λ is found by trying out different number of features in
the interval from 2-22.
6.4
Random forest
Random forests classiﬁcation is performed using the built-in MATLAB function TreeBagger.m that gen-
erates bagged ensembles of decision trees. The number of grown trees are optimized using the OOB
error estimation, which in general is considered a valid estimate of the test error. The minimum num-
ber of samples per tree leaf is set to 1 and the number of features randomly selected at each split is set
to pp.
6.5
PCA
The analysis is executed using a built-in MATLAB function pca. The exact details of this method is con-
sidered out of the scope of this thesis. Each feature is normalized so the direction with the maximal
variance is not automatically given by the feature with the largest values. The function outputs the PCA
model coefﬁcients, the coordinates of the projected data in the new dimensions, and the variance ex-
plained by each principal component.

Feature analysis
The four features selected by the three best performing classiﬁers (see Table 7.1) are elaborated below,
to investigate their ability to individually separate the two classes. Running a logistic regression using
these four features, their p-values can be found, i.e. how signiﬁcant their individual relationship is with
the response. The feature best describing the response (p = 0.027) is seen in Figure 7.12, and to some
extent really seems to split the data into metastatic and non-metastatic tissues for both training and test
data. Considering p-values < 0.05 as statistically signiﬁcant, the next three features are all considered
non-signiﬁcant (p = 0.060,p = 0.069, and p = 0.1165). These are plotted in Figure 7.13, 7.14, and 7.15,
and all fail to fail in transfer the trend from the training data to the test data. The question is, if the
feature with the statistical signiﬁcant p-value actually describes a tendency from growth patterns or if
it is merely the result of a coincidence? The feature is seen to describe the ’squared minimum value of
the major axis’ of a cluster. It is difﬁcult to argue why this particular feature should excel over the other
features. But the major axis of the "metastatic clusters" is predominantly smaller in the histograms. It
is however also noticeable that all the values are only given by three different values, and many samples
thus give the same value. Our hypothesis is, that the "minimum and maximum values" are in general
problematic features, because they have a tendency of taking on the same values. If these few values are
distributed in a fortunate way, a misleading result can be obtained by chance. Therefore, a veriﬁcation
of the results is carried out in the next section.

Discussion
This thesis investigates if quantiﬁcation of heterogeneity in relation to growth patterns of cancer cell
clusters can be used to predict the lymph node status of patients. A rich set of features are automati-
cally extracted and generated from IDC tissue samples, which are subsequently classiﬁed using relevant
machine learning methods. This chapter includes a small summary of the Results, which elucidate the
most important concepts of Chapter 7. Further to this, the high Misclassiﬁcation errors are discussed.
The developed Tumor budding features and their low performance compared to previous ﬁndings of a
signiﬁcant relationship between tumor budding and both lymph node status and overall patient survival
are discussed next. The ﬁnal paragraph of this chapter gives our recommendations for Future work.
Results
The results from the exploratory data analysis in Section 7.1 revealed that there was no obvious
clustering in the two dimensional feature space, which could predict the classiﬁcations of the tissue
samples. In addition to this, some of the features were found to be highly correlated. Ideally we do
not want correlated features, since they ultimately give the same information to the model and lowers
the model interpretability. But since their presence do not directly affect the classiﬁcation accuracy, all
features were kept in the model. Furthermore, the PCA showed that around 83% of variation in the data
could be expressed by the ﬁrst 10 principal components.
Classiﬁcation of the tissues into categories of metastatic and non-metastatic tissue were performed us-
ing Random forests and different model optimization approaches on logistic regression and LDA. The
classiﬁcation results of Data set 1 were presented in Section 7.2, and especially showed a tendency of
rightful classiﬁcation (MCE = 0.25) for logistic regression when using stepwise selection. The logistic
regression using the lasso and the SDA also showed a small tendency of correctly classifying the tissues
(MCE′s = 0.34). However, the cross-validation error of logistic regression using the lasso gave an average
MCE of 0.47, (Figure 7.5) and this ﬁnding suggested, that we should treat the results with caution.
A veriﬁcation study was launched in Section 7.5, and deﬁnitively made it clear that none of the results
were reproducible. This was done trying out many different constellations of data, and also by substitut-
ing the potentially problematic features deeming from minimum and maximum values with the 5th and
95th percentile. Overall, the impression from the veriﬁcation study was that classiﬁcation performance
of the models were just as good as a coin toss.
Misclassiﬁcation errors (MCE)
The high misclassiﬁcation errors of the classiﬁers can be explained
by a number of different factors. Among these, the most severe is that none of the features actually

explains the response using the models introduced so far. Linear and nonlinear models were included
in the investigation but without any success.
In addition to this, it is problematic to have such a relatively small sample size compared to the number
of features in the models. A large proportion of the samples were initially discarded when deciding only
to focus on fully scored IDC samples. This resulted in a data set of only 60 training and 32 test samples
(for Data set 1). Combining this small data set with the idea of investigating a proportional large feature
set is in hindsight an almost impossible task unless real explanatory features are present in the set.
In this thesis, a very large set of features were investigated. Logistic regression and LDA can only properly
estimate the model coefﬁcients if the criterion of n ≥p is met. But even though this criterion is met,
the prediction performance of the classiﬁer is still dependent on noise in the data (e.g. from outliers)
and on whether some of the features are linear dependent. Linear dependency reduces the rank of the
feature matrix and makes the outcome of the classiﬁer unstable and unreliable. To come around this
problem, feature selection, regularization and dimension reduction methods were applied. However,
these methods all seemed to have problems with overﬁtting. For feature selection and regularization,
the overﬁtting makes good sense, since the model still have a proportional large feature set to "select"
features from. According to James et. al. [44] around 5% of features will be shown to have a signiﬁcant
association with the response by chance. Choosing between so many features, at least some of the
features are thus bound to ﬁt the wanted outcome, when we have only 60 samples to ﬁt. Random forests
is characterized by running well on a large number of features, and is not bound by the criterion of n > p.
The fact that the random forests classiﬁcation errors are so high, therefore even further emphasizes that
the sample size is simply to small.
A larger dataset will always increase the accuracy of the estimated performance of a classiﬁer. So how
much data is needed? The general rule in classiﬁcation problems is "as much as we can get". But a
plausible ﬁrst step would be to collect a sample size bigger than the number of features to investigate.
Tumor budding
The idea that morphology and distribution of clusters could have a possible prog-
nostic value originates from the phenomenon of tumor budding. Literature show that a high amount
of tumor budding in the invasive front of tumors in colon cancer patients is clearly related to the prog-
nosis of patients [14][18][19][20]. In fact, tumor budding in colon cancer has been recognized as an
additional prognostic factor by the International Union against Cancer (UICC) [15]. Furthermore, newer
studies reveal the same indication of tumor budding in breast cancer patients [23][24][25].
Results of the study showed we were not able to reproduce the signiﬁcant association of any tumor
budding features with lymph node status. One feature related to tumor budding and selected by all
classiﬁers in Table 7.1, was "the number of buds in the hottest hotspot divided by the number of buds in
the whole tissue". This is an interesting feature, since it is not reminiscent of the tumor budding features
deﬁned in the related work articles. Instead it is a relational feature informative on the distribution of
buds in relation to the hotspot. When examining the feature in a histogram (Section 7.4) it however did
not seem to show any individual relationship with the response.
So why were we not able to reproduce any previous ﬁndings on tumor budding in breast cancer studies?
There are several problems to emphasize. First of all, more or less all studies from the articles presented
in Chapter 2 investigate the invasive front, i.e. the tumor-host interface of the tumors. The invasive
74
==PAGE:76==

front is in general meant to have a higher prognostic signiﬁcance compared to the rest of the tissue, but
no digital procedures are currently able to automatically detect this invasive front in tumorous tissue
slides. An important aspect of this study was to automatize the quantiﬁcation of heterogeneity, and we
were therefore bound to investigate whole-slide tissue samples. This naturally gave some limitations to
our approach compared to a manual interpretation. This also means that the labeling of tumor buds
were not based on a manual interpretation as in the articles but by a threshold value set on cluster area.
Furthermore the digitalization gave the aforementioned trade-off between resolution and the size of the
FoV which might have split some clusters in two. As a last thing, the articles split the tumor budding in to
two categories of low and high grading, and looked at the individual p-values relating them to different
prognostics. This approach is much more direct, than combining them with a lot of other features and
then look for a combined relationship to the response.
A second feature matrix approach
In an attempt to augment the data, an additional experimental fea-
ture matrix was created that used each cluster as a sample. The number of clusters within each sample
ranges from 1,254 to 80,470 (mean ≈17,000) so the feature matrix ended up with around 17,000·161 ≈
2,737,000 samples, i.e. n >> p. This feature matrix was divided into a training, validation and test
set. The idea behind the feature matrix was, that some of the clusters from metastatic tissues are in
some kind of "metastatic state", so they are separable from the non-metastatic clusters. The features
expressing the clusters included (1) morphological outputted parameters from VIS per cluster and (2)
the average euclidean distance to the cluster’s ﬁve nearest neighbors. This resulted in a total of p = 11
features. Since the feature matrix was made as an additional experiment, it was only investigated with
respect to Random forests.
For computational purposes the random forests classiﬁer was trained on a subset of 20,000 cluster-
samples (10,000 metastatic and 10,000 non-metastatic). To classify the original tissue samples as metastatic
or non-metastatic, the following approach was taken: If the number of clusters classiﬁed as metastatic
within a tissue sample was greater than a threshold T % the sample was classiﬁed metastatic, other-
wise non-metastatic. The threshold value was optimized from running the classiﬁer on a validation set,
where a threshold of 70% was shown to give the best result (i.e. the lowest MCE). A completely similar
approach, but using all tumor buds as samples, was also tested. However, both approaches however
gave really high MCE’s, and were therefore discarded during the project. Though looking at each cluster
as being metastatic or non-metastatic can greatly augment the sample size, it is therefore not recom-
mended for future studying. Unless of course given the current setup and features available.
Future work
Breast cancer prognoses are currently based on a variety of factors. According to Cian-
frocca et. al. [62], the most signiﬁcant prognostic factor for early-stage breast cancer patients is lymph
node status, and there is proven to be a direct relationship between lymph node involvement and re-
currences of cancer. Measures from grading and staging are also important indicators, together with the
ER, PR and HER2 status. These are only some of the prognostic factors taken into consideration today,
which emphasizes the complexity in estimating a patient prognosis. This means, that all new prognostic
factors with even just a small signiﬁcance are potentially interesting in combination with the remain-
ing factors. To our knowledge, the grading system is the only current prognostic factor that includes
morphology quantiﬁcation of the histological tissue samples. In addition to this, there is a consider-

able variability in grading among pathologists [26]. The idea of a novel automatized and image-based
quantiﬁcation on morphology to help predict patient outcome is therefore appealing.
Though none of the features investigated showed an evident relationship with the response of the test
set, we still recommend future studying within this ﬁeld of quantifying growth patterns. As digital sys-
tems for pathology assessment are slowly emerging, further development of automatic procedures for
histological interpretation will have great clinical value. And if hidden information is actually found in
these growth patterns, it can ideally give new insight to, and help us better understand, heterogeneity
of breast cancers and cancers in general. As an example for future studying, Salhia et. al [24] suspects
that ER-positive tumors with high degree budding may have an extraordinary high metastatic potential.
Using the Visiopharm software, which automatically detects ER cancer cells, this could be an interesting
additional study.
For future work in the same ﬁeld of investigation, a much larger data set, a new set of generated fea-
tures explored more thoroughly (perhaps by clinical inputs from a pathologist) and a response based
on survival data is recommended. Metastatic spread is not by itself an indicator of prognosis patient
survival, though it is used as a substitute in this study. Further development of an automatic approach
to score tumor budding is also recommended. Here emphasis should be put into ﬁnding a more exact
reproduction of the results found in related tumor budding articles, while still having an automatic fea-
ture extraction. In addition this, it could be advisable to ensure a perfect segmentation of the clusters.
As introduced, we had a trade-off problem choosing between resolution and splitting of cluster by the
FoV in VIS. Therefore, it is recommended, to optimize the segmentation process of the clusters. In this
study a perfect segmentation was not a prioritization, since the idea from the beginning was to use the
availability of a PCK APP to automatically detect cancerous clusters.
We will ﬁnally mention some new features, which could be interesting to look into. The ﬁrst are features
related to the intensity of staining. Tumor tissue will in general contain some amount of necrotic tissue,
which will have a lighter staining color (light brown) from PCK than more active cancer tissue. It is there-
fore not unlikely that features of intensity could have information on prognostics, if we could extract all
the "active" cancer cells based on their intensities. The necrotic tissue is often found in the center of
the tumor and such a differentiation could ideally segment the inner tumor from the tumor periphery
(i.e. so it express the invasive tumor margin). Quantiﬁcation of intensity were however not included
in this study, because the variety among staining intensities were considered to high. Our data sam-
ples were collected over many years (from 2002-2012), why the staining would deem from very different
staining contributions. Thorough knowledge of intensity variations between samples are thus needed,
to proceed these investigations.
Other interesting features to investigate are related to the stromal tissue. From the article by Beck et.
al [26] stromal cell patterns (spatial arrangement) were found to be predictors of overall patient survival
of breast cancer patients. Reproducing these stromal features could thus add new possible signiﬁcant
information to our feature matrix. Further to this features from tumor buds found in the stromal tissues
could also be generated.
Non-linear features could have been explored further in this study if we wanted to study more non-linear
relationships between features and response. In this study the squared numerical value of the original
features from the VIS outputs were generated, but in addition to this, we could also have included ad-
ditional power terms, interaction terms, exponential and logarithmic terms etc. These features were

however not considered realistic enough to be included, but potential information could of course still
be hidden here.
Finally, we have also worked to ﬁnd a feature that explained the distribution of the hotspots detected
in the tissues. The heatmap analysis conducted in this study only outputs scores of hotspots, whereas
we wanted to ﬁnd measure of the size and number of these hotspots above a certain threshold. We
managed to develop an algorithm for heatmap analysis that could extract and label the hottest hotspots
using a threshold intensity value on the heatmap. However preliminary studies showed that running all
samples through this heatmap analysis would take several days and it was therefore not included in the
study. This algorithm is elaborated in Appendix E, and is ready to run with an optimization of the run

Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to investigate and quantify heterogeneity in breast cancer tissue samples in
relation to sentinel node metastasis. We have suggested a rich set of quantitative features that charac-
terize different morphological and distributional features of cancer clusters in digital histological breast
cancer tissue samples. These features also include a variety of tumor budding related features not seen
in previous works within this ﬁeld of study. The entire feature generation was automatized so no in-
terpretation would rely on manual scoring subjectivity. To ﬁnd a relationship between the extracted
features and sentinel node metastases, three different classiﬁcation methods were investigated; Logistic
regression, LDA, and random forests. In addition to this, different feature selection, shrinkage and di-
mension reduction methods were applied in order to optimize the classiﬁcation models. All procedures
were tailored to whole-slide tissue samples.
No signiﬁcant relationship were found between the generated features and the response with the com-
bination of features and models presented in this thesis. There is deﬁnitely room for reﬁnements and
new approaches in the procedures presented, but since not even a slight tendency of rightful classiﬁca-
tion was seen in the veriﬁcation studies, we would not recommend further proceedings with the exact
set of features presented. The aim to digitally investigate and develop new methods for quantiﬁcation
in relation to heterogeneity was however still accomplished.

Heatmap analysis
This Chapter provides an elaboration of the additional feature that involves extracting the number of
high bud-dense areas in the samples, described in Chapter 8.
The heatmap analysis conducted in this study only outputs scores of the number of buds in hotspot/-
densest areas, whereas we wanted to ﬁnd a measure of the size and number of these larger hotspots.
We developed an algorithm for a heatmap analysis in MATLAB, which is able to identify and label high
intensity areas in the heatmap. However preliminary runs showed that running all samples through this
heatmap analysis would take over a week, why it was not included in the study. Instead the algorithm
was was run on a phantom data (n = 400), Figure E.1(a).
The algorithm locates each bud by (X,Y)-coordinates and "draws" a circle with radius r around it. All
pixels inside the circle are then given the value 1, Figure E.1(b). When two circles overlap, the values
are added, leaving bud dense areas with a higher pixel values. The heatmap is thus a pixel-vise color
representation, where high intensity areas are express the dense bud areas, Figure E.1(c).
To label the hotspots, all low-intensity pixels are removed by thresholding, Figure E.1(d), and the remain-
ing connected pixels are labeled. Hotspots with areas less than a threshold value are then removed, leav-
ing only the larger hotspots in the sample, Figure E.1(e). An pseudo-code of the algorithm is presented
in Algorithm 2.
Algorithm 2: Pseudo code for heatmap generated feature
for j=1:number of (tumor) buds do
1. Use (x,y)-coordinates to draw a circle with radius r around each bud
2. Add the value 1 to pixels within the circle
3. Remove low-intensity areas
4. Label connected components (hotspots) 5. Measure area for each connected component 6.
Remove high dense areas less than an area threshold value
end
