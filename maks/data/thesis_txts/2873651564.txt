Abstract
Graphene­based systems hold great promise for nanoelectronic applications and for ex­
ploring exotic electronic phenomena. Twisted bilayer graphene (tBLG)—formed by stack­
ing and rotating two graphene layers—has attracted particular interest due to its angle­
dependent electronic structure and reports of superconductivity at certain “magic” angles.
In this thesis, we investigate the electronic properties and ballistic transport of monolayer
graphene, AA­ and AB­stacked bilayer graphene (BLG), and large­ and intermediate­
angle tBLG using a DFT + NEGF approach as implemented in the TranSIESTA and Tbtrans
codes.
We benchmark basis sets proposed in [1] for transport calculations on monolayer graphene
and select the TZP basis set for all bilayer systems. AA BLG is found to be metallic, while
AB BLG is semiconducting at zero bias but exhibits enhanced conductance under in­plane
bias, suggesting potential for applications with its tunable electronic properties.
For the tBLG structures in our studied angle­regime, the low­energy dispersion near the
Dirac point K resembles that of decoupled monolayers. However, the density of states
near the M point reveals angle­dependent band flattening, giving rise to van Hove sin­
gularities. While these states lie outside the zero­bias transport window, they may sig­
nificantly affect transport under applied bias due to the link between conductance and
available electronic states.

Introduction
The past decades have seen a remarkable down­scaling of electronics; from chunky
room­sized computers to fast, portable, and sleek laptops along with the pocket­friendly
smartphone. These efficient computers — understood here as processing units — are
integrated into many modern technologies; from sensors to wind turbines [2].
Behind this transformation lies innovation in device components, many of which now op­
erate at the nanometer scale — in 2020 transistor gate lengths had already reached 5
nm [3]. However, physics imposes certain limitations on further downscaling. Quantum
effects can no longer be disregarded, and a macroscopic approach to nanodevices de­
velopment becomes insufficient, as the underlying atomic and electronic structure must
be accounted for [4].
The demand for ever smaller devices requires understanding of materials at a quantum
level. This has paved the way for novel quantum materials that have emerged as strong
candidates for next­generation devices due to exotic physical properties and atomic­level
tunabilities [5].
The family of carbon­based materials has attracted increasing attention in recent decades,
as the flexibility of carbon’s molecular bonds enables a wide variety of structures with
vastly different properties and possible applications [6].
One such material, the bulk
crystal graphite, has due to its layered structure proven interesting for energy technolo­
gies [7]. Graphite is composed of stacks of its two­dimensional allotrope, graphene [6],
and when devices are scaled down to the atomic level, it becomes natural to consider
two­dimensional materials.
A variety of 2D materials have demonstrated exceptional behaviour—graphene among
the prominent. Graphene exhibits excellent thermal and structural properties, along with
exotic electronic features [6], which have inspired extensive research into graphene­
based nanodevices [8]. Following one such research path, this thesis investigates graphene­
based systems with a focus on their electronic structure and transport behaviour.
1.1
Graphene: the beginning of two­dimensional
nanoelectronics?
The carbon atoms in graphene are arranged in a hexagonal, honeycomb lattice that forms
a two­dimensional atomic plane. Although its structure had been studied for decades,
graphene was thought to be a constituent of graphite (and other carbon allotropes) rather
than a stable, free­standing material. It was not until its experimental isolation by Novoselov,
Geim, and collaborators in 2004 that graphene was confirmed as an independent material—
the first member of the now extensive family of 2D materials [9]. Even at the time of its
discovery, graphene’s remarkable electronic properties were evident, inspiring its use
both as a platform for probing quantum phenomena and as a candidate for future nano­
electronic applications.
Graphene’s remarkable properties are, of course, rooted in its atomic structure. Each
carbon atom contributes four valence electrons occupying the s, px, py, and pz orbitals.
The s, px, and py orbitals hybridise to form in­plane sp2 bonds, resulting in occupied σ­
orbitals and unoccupied σ∗­orbitals. The remaining pz orbital lies perpendicular to the
plane and binds covalently to neighbouring pz orbitals, forming the delocalised π and π∗

bands, which constitute the valence and conduction bands, respectively [8]. In reciprocal
space, these bands touch at the high­symmetry Dirac points, giving rise to graphene’s
characteristic Dirac cones. Near these points, the energy dispersion is linear, and the
electrons behave as massless Dirac fermions—propagating similarly to photons, though
with a velocity approximately 300 times slower. This unique electronic structure leads to
several exotic quantum phenomena, including the integer quantum Hall effect and zitter­
bewegung [6]. Graphene exhibits many additional intriguing properties—too numerous to
detail here—and we refer interested readers to reviews such as [8, 6].
We instead focus on the properties most relevant to this project—namely, that electronic
states are inherently available near the Fermi energy and can be accessed through the
application of even small biases. The electronic structure of graphene can be readily
modified via doping or electrostatic gating (see figure 1.1, effectively shifting the Fermi
level [10, 11]. This shift results in a sharp increase in the density of available states at the
Fermi level, thereby enhancing conductivity. These tunable properties make graphene
highly attractive for nanoelectronic devices where specific transport behaviour can be
engineered.

1.2
Bilayers — engineering the electronic structure
Another approach to engineering graphene devices with desired electronic properties is
to base them on graphene stacks just a few layers thick. The presence of multiple lay­
ers alters the electronic structure due to interlayer interactions, which may lead to band
hybridisation and overlap of Dirac cones [12]. In particular, the two most studied configu­
rations of bilayer graphene (BLG)—AA­stacked and AB­stacked, see figure 1.2—exhibit
different electronic properties.

B2. AB BLG instead aligns top­layer B2 with bottom­layer A1, while top­layer A2 sits
above the centre of a hexagon in the bottom layer.

AA BLG is conductive, with linear dispersion near the Dirac points and a substantial den­
sity of states (DOS) at the Fermi level. AB BLG, by contrast, exhibits parabolic dispersion
near the Dirac points and a much smaller DOS at the Fermi level [12]. Interestingly, the
electronic structure of AB BLG is sensitive to an applied bias perpendicular to the layer
planes, which opens a band gap. Since the gap size scales with the applied bias, it can
be tuned externally [13, 14]. Another form of band engineering involves adjusting the
interlayer distance, which also significantly influences the electronic structure, as the de­
gree of band hybridisation is strongly dependent on this separation [13]. As in monolayer
graphene, doping has also been shown to affect AB BLG; under certain doping conditions,
the conductivity can nearly vanish [15].
Along similar lines are internally symmetry­breaking defects, such as shifting or rotating
one layer relative to the other. The latter case—twisted bilayer graphene—has especially
captured the attention of the research community due to its so­called ”magic” proper­
ties [16]. We are among the caught, as twisted bilayer graphene is the central focus of
this thesis.
1.3
Twisted bilayer graphene –– a twist of magic
Twisted bilayer graphene (tBLG) refers to a family of graphene bilayers in which the two
layers are rotated relative to one another, resulting in a structure that is neither globally
AA­ nor AB­stacked. This rotation introduces regions of local AA and AB stacking, which
give rise to long­range interference patterns known as Moiré patterns, whose size and
periodicity of depend on the twist angle.
The formation of these locally ordered and spatially modulated regions leads to striking
electronic properties. At certain twist angles—especially small ones, where the length
scale of the Moiré patterns becomes large—the electronic bands hybridise in such a man­
ner that they flatten near the Fermi level. This results in a substantial increase in the DOS
known as van Hove singularities (vHs)(see figure 1.3) leading to enhanced electronic
conductivity. Remarkably, at specific “magic” angles, tBLG even exhibits superconduc­
tivity[16]!

Band hybridisation is also observed at larger twist angles, both in the band structure and
DOS, although the most pronounced effects are typically confined to twist angles below
approximately θ = 10◦[18]. As a result, tBLG is commonly categorised into three regimes:
small, intermediate, and large twist angles. While the electronic properties of small­angle
tBLG have been extensively studied— through tight­binding (TB) based models or Density
Functional Theory (DFT) when possible — systematic investigations across a wider range
of twist angles using DFT remain limited. Even studies that include larger angles tend to
focus on the ”magic” regime [19].

Ballistic transport in tBLG has been explored across twist angles using TB models [20],
which successfully reproduce the three­regime behaviour. Within a DFT + non­equilibrium
Green’s function (NEGF) framework, however, transport properties can be examined with
greater accuracy, as interlayer interactions are treated from first principles. Moreover,
this approach enables the application of external bias. Although large­angle tBLG ex­
hibits transport behaviour similar to that of monolayer graphene [20], these properties
may change under bias, which perturbs the system from equilibrium.
A more comprehensive understanding of the electronic structure of tBLG and how varia­
tions influence transport properties — both at equilibrium and under bias — is valuable for
constructing graphene devices. Such insight is particularly valuable for band engineer­
ing, as it helps determine the availability and tunability of electronic states across different
rotations. Furthermore, since graphite naturally exhibits rotational misalignment between
layers, understanding tBLG also offers a route to deeper insight into the properties of
graphite itself.
1.4
Thesis outline
This thesis investigates how twisting the layers of bilayer graphene alters its electronic
structure and transport behaviour. We focus on large and intermediate twist angles, build­
ing a systematic understanding through a progression from monolayer to bilayer to tBLG
systems.
Chapter 2 introduces the theoretical foundations required for this work. We briefly outline
the central ideas of Density Functional Theory, and include an introduction to ballistic
transport and the non­equilibrium Green’s function formalism. This chapter also expands
our review of graphene­based systems, laying the groundwork for the analyses to follow.
Chapter 3 studies monolayer graphene as a calibration system. We evaluate several
basis sets, including a newly developed one tailored for graphene [1], and assess their
performance in structural, electronic, and transport calculations. This sets the stage for
consistent modelling of more complex systems. We ask:
How do the basis sets presented in [1] perform in transport calculations of
pristine graphene?
Chapter 4 extends the study to AA­ and AB­stacked bilayer graphene, where interlayer
interactions play a significant role. We benchmark functional choices comparing their
different levels of description of van der Waals interactions. We analyse how stacking
affects the band structure, density of states, and bias response. We evaluate conductance
via the Landauer formula and ask:
How are the differences in the electronic structure of AA and AB BLG reflected
in their transport properties?
Chapter 5 turns to tBLG. Using our calibrated set­up, we explore how electronic and
transport properties evolve with twist angle. We pay particular attention to the dispersion
near the Dirac point and van Hove singularities in the DOS. We then investigate how these
influence equilibrium transport properties within a DFT + NEGF approach. This leads to
the foundational question of the entire thesis:
What are the transport properties of large­ and intermediate­angle tBLG within
a DFT + NEGF framework?
The final chapter summarises our findings and reflects on the broader implications of this
work. We outline how our results contribute to the understanding of interlayer decoupling,

2.1.2
The Hohenberg–Kohn theorems and the Kohn–Sham equations
The foundation of DFT lies in the Hohenberg–Kohn theorems [23], which link the elec­
tronic ground state (GS) to the electron density. This connection recasts the Schrödinger
equation from a 3N­variable problem to one that depends only on three spatial coordi­
nates.

2.1.3
Periodicity and solids in DFT
While DFT reformulates the many­body problem in terms of the electronic density, it still
requires solving N single­particle equations, which scale with system size. This appears
limiting for large systems, but periodicity enables the treatment of solids.
Crystals are defined by a repeating unit cell (UC) with lattice vectors a1, a2, a3. Any lattice
translation T = n1a1 + n2a2 + n3a3 leaves the system invariant. By Bloch’s theorem, the
wavefunction can be written as
ϕik(r) = eik·ruik(r),
(2.9)
with uik(r + T) = uik(r) a periodic function. Inserting this into the KS equation yields the
crystal form:
[

where uik is now the eigenstate of the crystal Hamiltonian. This allows the KS equations
to be solved within a single UC using periodic boundary conditions, enabling efficient
simulations of solids [21].

However, DFT—particularly with GGA functionals such as PBE—is known to underesti­

2.1.4
Van der Waals interactions and the DFT­D3 correction
Standard local and semi­local functionals (LDA, GGA) fail to capture long­range van der
Waals interactions, which are crucial in layered materials. To correct for this, DFT can be
augmented with D3 dispersion corrections [27] adding a non­self­consistent term to the
total energy:
EDFT­D3 = EDFT + ED3.
(2.11)
Since the D3 term is not a functional of the electronic density, it is computed after the SCF
cycle. However, during structural relaxation, the correction contributes to the forces and
is recalculated at every geometry step.

2.2.2
Bilayer graphene and its stacking orders
Before discussing the electronic structure of bilayer graphene, we briefly clarify the stack­

(Bernal) bilayer graphene — illustrated in figure 1.2 — and tBLG. In AA BLG, atoms in

The DFT + NEGF software TranSIESTA, together with the utility code TbTrans, enables

vice. Using TranSIESTA, the Green’s functions and self­energies that describe coupling
between these subsystems are computed self­consistently. A typical calculation proceeds
in several steps:
First, a standard self­consistent field calculation is performed for the electrode to gener­
ate a .TSHS file, which contains the information needed to construct the electrode self­
energies. A dense k­point sampling in the semi­infinite (transport) direction ensures well­
resolved electrode states.
Next, the open system is solved using the Green’s function formalism: rather than solving
the Schrödinger equation as in usual DFT, the Green’s function of the scattering region
is computed by incorporating the self­energies from the electrodes. This captures the
effect of coupling to reservoirs. Since the transverse directions are treated as periodic,
additional k­point sampling is required to account for the various Bloch modes contributing
to transport.
Based on the Green’s function and electrode self­energies, a new density matrix is com­
puted. This step replaces the usual DFT procedure of building the density from occupied
KS eigenstates. Instead, the density matrix is obtained by integrating over energy and
summing over transverse modes 2.
The resulting density matrix is then used to update the DFT Hamiltonian, and the cy­
cle is repeated until self­consistency is achieved. Once convergence is reached, the final
Green’s function and broadening matrices can be used to compute the energy­dependent
transmission via Eq. (2.27). This step is performed by the utility code TbTrans, which eval­
uates the transmission function by summing over all transverse Bloch modes, requiring
dense k­point sampling in the directions perpendicular to transport.

In this chapter, we present ab initio studies of pristine monolayer graphene, carried out
using SIESTA, TranSIESTA, and TbTrans. Monolayer graphene serves as a computation­
ally inexpensive yet physically rich test system for calibrating our computational set­up
before applying it to more complex bilayer structures. We systematically investigate how
structural, electronic, and transport results depend on the choice of basis set, performing
convergence tests on selected parameters. Results are compared to converged plane­
wave calculations from VASP and to literature values, with attention paid to limitations of
localised basis sets. These tests provide the methodological foundation for the rest of this
thesis. By probing the cleanest possible system, we establish the reliability and robust­
ness of our computational set­up.
The chapter proceeds as follows: we first compare structural properties—total energy
and lattice constant—across native and optimised basis sets. We then analyse the band
structure, with particular focus on the linear Dirac dispersion and high­energy parabolic
bands. Finally, we construct a graphene device and assess how transmission functions
behave across basis sets and under applied bias, ultimately selecting the most suitable
basis for the bilayer studies that follow.
Basis sets considered
Specifically, we compare the basis sets SZ(P), DZ(P), and TZ(P). As TZP includes the
largest number of orbitals, it is expected to yield the most accurate results. In addition
to the number and type of orbitals, the cut­off radii and pseudopotential construction in­
fluence basis set performance. Native SIESTA basis sets apply the same parameters to
all orbitals, but tailoring these values individually can significantly improve accuracy [28].
Rather than performing a full optimisation ourselves, we evaluate recently reported opti­
mised basis sets for graphene [1] and compare them with the native options to identify
the optimal choice for our study.
To ensure that any observed differences stem solely from the basis set choice, all other
SIESTA parameters and settings are kept fixed throughout the tests.

3.1
Total energy and equilibrium lattice constant
We begin by conducting atomic relaxations with each basis set to determine which yields
the most stable structure (i.e. lowest total energy) and a lattice constant that best agrees
with reference data from both plane­wave calculations (VASP) and literature. This pro­
vides an initial check of how well each basis set captures the structural ground state of
monolayer graphene, and offers a first comparison point between the basis sets under
study.
3.1.1
Computational Methods
The total energy of a single graphene unit cell is calculated using SIESTA [28] for different
basis sets: native SZ, SZP, DZ, DZP, TZ, and TZP, as well as all graphene­specific basis
sets proposed in [1]. A vacuum region of 9 Å is included above and below the graphene
layer. For each basis set, we perform structural relaxation using the conjugate gradient
algorithm to obtain the most stable atomic configuration, from which the total energy is
extracted.
All calculations employ the GGA PBE functional [25]. During relaxation, the force toler­
ance is set to 0.01 eV/Å, the density matrix convergence threshold to 10−6, and the Mesh­
Cutoff to 400 Ry. We use pseudopotentials from the PseudoDojo project for all SIESTA
calculations in this work [42]. As we study a single UC of to atoms, the Brillouin zone is
large; we therefore employ a dense 42 × 42 × 1 Monkhorst–Pack k­point grid, consistent
with other ab initio studies of pristine graphene [43].
3.1.2
Total Energy
In all cases, the optimised basis sets from [1] yield lower total energies than their native
counterparts. This suggests that the optimised basis sets result in a more fully converged
ground state energy and therefore perform energetically better than the native sets [21].

to yield a highly accurate result).

3.1.3
Lattice Constant
The lattice constant a of graphene is a = 2.46 Å [12]. DFT typically predicts slightly larger
values for graphene, and the lattice constant reported in [1] is 2.466 Å.
To obtain an additional reference, we perform a structural relaxation using VASP [29],
employing a 42 × 42 × 1 k­point grid, 18 Å of vacuum, a plane­wave cutoff energy of
650 eV, and the PBE functional. The relaxed structure yields a lattice constant of 2.466 Å,
which we adopt as our reference value.

The calculated lattice constants deviate from the reference value by only a few percent
across all basis sets. The largest deviation, approximately 4%, is observed for the native
SZ basis, while the smallest deviation occurs for the optimised DZPF basis. In general,
increasing the number of orbitals leads to a reduction in the lattice constant, bringing it
closer to the reference value, as shown in figure 3.3. The optimised basis sets DZPF, TZP,
TZPF, and QZP all yield lattice constants within 5 mÅ of the reference, corresponding to
a difference of less than 0.2%.
Considering both total energy and lattice constant, the optimised DZPF and TZP basis
sets appear sufficiently converged for further calculations. DZPF includes 20 orbitals per
carbon atom, compared to 17 for TZP, making it more computationally demanding. Adding
an F­orbital to TZP would further increase the orbital count and computational cost, which
seems excessive for modelling graphene.
As our focus is electronic structure and transport properties, the remaining candidate ba­
sis sets are now evaluated based on their ability to reproduce the band structure and
transmission function of monolayer graphene, as previously reported in [44].

3.2
Electronic structure ­ capturing linear dispersion near K
As discussed in chapters 1 and 2, many of graphene’s exotic properties can be traced
to its electronic band structure. It is particularly important that our basis set accurately
reproduces the linear dispersion near the Dirac points as this behaviour dominates low­
energy transport. We also consider the unoccupied high­energy parabolic bands, which
are treated differently by plane­wave and localised bases. While these states lie well
above the Fermi level in monolayer graphene, they may play a role for band hybridisation
in the stacked systems considered later in this work.
We begin by comparing the electronic bands obtained using SIESTA with different basis
sets to a reference from the PW basis code VASP, alongside values reported in the litera­
ture. The band structure is calculated for both native and optimised versions of the DZP
and TZP basis sets, as well as the optimised DZPF. We then assess the effect of including
large­radius diffuse orbitals to better capture the high­energy parabolic bands.
3.2.1
Band structure reference calculation
We first establish a reference electronic structure calculated with VASP. Special attention
is given to the unoccupied states, which are known to be sensitive to vacuum thickness
in 2D materials. We therefore examine how the vacuum height above and below the
graphene layer affects the band structure. In VASP, the band structure is obtained by first
performing a single­point SCF calculation to generate the charge density, which is then
used to sample eigenenergies along high­symmetry paths in reciprocal space. In this
study, we follow the standard Γ–K–M–Γ path used in previous graphene studies [43, 8].
To ensure convergence of the VASP reference calculation, 1000 k­points are sampled
between each high­symmetry point. The PW basis of VASP extends far into the vacuum
region perpendicular to the graphene plane. Such vacuum regions are essential when
modelling two­dimensional materials with DFT to avoid interactions between periodic im­
ages. Previous graphene studies have employed vacuum thicknesses exceeding 15 Å
above and below the plane [45].

In contrast, the unoccupied bands—particularly the parabolic states near the Γ point—
show noticeable differences. These bands are notoriously challenging to describe using
localised atomic orbitals such as those employed in SIESTA [43, 1]. Although they are
more reliably captured with VASP, they remain highly sensitive to the vacuum thickness.
Thinner vacuum layers result in fewer unoccupied bands below 10 eV, and the lowest
such band appears at a reduced energy. Increasing the vacuum spacing from 8 Å to 18 Å
shifts this band upward from 2.87 eV to 3.17 eV, bringing it closer to reference values of
3.25 eV [43] and 3.24 eV [46]. These states will be revisited in our discussion of SIESTA
basis set performance. Based on this sensitivity, we adopt a vacuum thickness of 18 Å
as our reference.
3.2.2
Band structure comparison with SIESTA basis sets
Having established a PW reference using VASP, we now turn to the band structures calcu­
lated in SIESTA for the five basis sets: the native and optimised versions of DZP and TZP,
as well as the optimised DZPF. The band structures are computed using the parameters
detailed in section 3.1.1, with 300 k­points sampled along the Γ–K–M–Γ path. In addition,
we examine the effect of including a diffuse orbital with a large cut­off radius.
Figure 3.5 shows the calculated band structures for each basis set. All basis sets success­
fully reproduce the occupied states, including the Dirac cone near the K point. This region
is of utmost importance in our study, as we focus on small applied biases and therefore
operate close to the Fermi level. Minor differences are observed in the unoccupied part of
the spectrum, which may become relevant in bilayer systems where interlayer interactions
modify the electronic structure, potentially with unoccupied states at lower energies.
Panel A of Figure 3.5 compares the native (left) and optimised (right) DZP basis sets. In­
terestingly, the native DZP set yields one additional unoccupied parabolic band compared
to its optimised counterpart. For the TZP basis set (panel B), the trend is reversed: the
optimised set (right) produces one more parabolic band than the native version. Inclusion
of an f­orbital in the DZPF basis set (panel C, left) does not offer much improvement.
In all cases, the energy of the first parabolic band at Γ lies significantly above the ∼3 eV
value reported in [43, 46], reaching up to 8.32 eV in the case of the DZP optimised basis.
While these deviations are unlikely to impact our transport calculations—given that we
restrict our applied bias to a maximum of 1 V—they are nevertheless noteworthy.
These unoccupied, parabolic states are not entirely irrelevant to our study. Due to their
free­electron­like behaviour, they mainly occupy the vacuum region and form a continuum
of states. In stacked systems, such states have been linked to interlayer phenomena
and even superconducting behaviour in graphite­based compounds [43, 46, 47]. Due
to their spatial extension into vacuum, these states are difficult to capture using localised
atomic orbitals. However, their description can be improved by introducing diffuse orbitals
with extended cut­off radius. As shown in panel C (right) of figure 3.5, augmenting the
optimised TZP basis set with a single diffuse orbital lowers the energy of the first parabolic
band at Γ to 4.82 eV, moving closer to the expected value.

3.3
Transmission and the influence of the basis set
We have seen that all basis sets are capable of reproducing the linear dispersion near K,
which dominates the low­energy transport properties of graphene. As our main objective
is to study ballistic electronic transport—in the Landauer approach using the TranSIESTA
code—we finalise our basis set tests by investigating the transport properties they yield.
As discussed in section 2.3, the central quantity of interest is the transmission function.
We now examine how the choice of basis set influences the zero­bias transmission, calcu­
lated using TranSIESTA and TbTrans. The tested basis sets include: native and optimised
DZP, native and optimised TZP, and the optimised TZP with one diffuse orbital (cut­off
radius of 12 Bohr). These are compared both among each other and with references. We
then proceed to analyse how the basis sets behave under applied bias.
3.3.1
Computational methods
We initially consider a device composed entirely of graphene, with both the electrodes and
the scattering region consisting of four UCs along the transport direction and one UC along
the transverse direction, which is treated as infinite due to periodic boundary conditions.
The device is later extended to 30 UCs along the transport direction to accommodate
the long­range diffuse orbitals and to ensure proper matching between the electrode and
scattering region.

To obtain the transmission function, we first perform a TranSIESTA calculation for each

between calculations is the choice of basis set. A Monkhorst–Pack grid of 3 × 30 × 1

electrode and scattering region.
For TbTrans, we use a grid with 100 transverse k­points and an energy resolution of ∆E =
0.001 eV. This choice minimises the step­like features that can arise in underconverged
transmission spectra [44]1. Additional tests confirmed that further reducing the energy
spacing does not significantly affect the resulting transmission curves.
For the finite­bias calculations, the same simulation parameters are used as in the zero­
bias case. The only difference is the applied voltage, which shifts the chemical potentials
of the left and right electrodes such that µL = V /2 and µR = −V /2. These calculations
make use of the self­consistent density matrix and Green’s function from the previous bias
step, which are reused through the .TSDE and .TSHS files.

the band occupations, potentially involving higher­energy states in transport. We therefore
examine how the different basis sets perform under applied bias. In addition, the situation
becomes more complex in graphene bilayers, where interlayer coupling can hybridise
bands and cause diffuse­like unoccupied bands to appear at lower energies [12].

Beyond the Fermi energy, transmission drops to zero near ±0.5 eV, remaining near zero

While the general transmission trends remain consistent across basis sets, convergence
behaviour begins to diverge under higher biases. In particular, adding a diffuse orbital
complicates the SCF procedure and often leads to unstable or non­converging solutions
beyond ±0.5 V. This is likely due to the extended spatial reach of the diffuse orbital, which
increases the range of electronic interactions and introduces numerical instabilities—
especially in larger systems with inhomogeneous potential landscapes. Since the systems
investigated in subsequent chapters are larger and more complex, they require numeri­
cally robust basis sets that converge reliably under bias. For this reason, the optimised
TZP basis with a diffuse orbital is excluded from further bias­dependent transport calcu­
lations. It is also omitted from figure 3.10, as convergence could not be achieved within
1000 SCF steps using our standard settings.

3.4
Summary
In this chapter, we benchmarked several SIESTA basis sets—both native and optimised—
using monolayer graphene as a test system. Structural relaxations showed that the op­
timised basis sets from [1] yield lower total energies and lattice constants in excellent
agreement with plane­wave calculations. All basis sets accurately reproduced the Dirac
dispersion near the K point, which dominates low­bias transport. However, discrepancies
appeared in the unoccupied high­energy bands near Γ.
We tested diffuse orbitals with large cut­off radii to improve the description of these bands,
however, these orbitals caused SCF instabilities under bias. Since these states lie above
the energy window relevant to our study, we chose to exclude them.
We thus select the optimised TZP basis set for all subsequent calculations. It offers the
best balance between accuracy and numerical stability, and ensures a solid reference for
interpreting transport in bilayer and tBLG systems.

Bilayer Graphene ­ electronic properties
of two stacking orders
In this chapter, we investigate the electronic properties of bilayer graphene in two distinct
stacking orders: AA and AB stacking. These calculations extend the calibration presented
in the previous chapter for monolayer graphene and serve as a foundation for our later
study of tBLG. Since tBLG lacks a global stacking order and instead consists of local AA­
and AB­like regions, understanding the behaviour of these two reference configurations
within our computational set­up is essential.
We evaluate whether the TZP optimised basis set from [1]—previously shown to describe
monolayer graphene accurately—also provides a reliable description for BLG.
For bilayers, interlayer coupling is defining. This requires modifications to our set­up,
particularly the choice of exchange–correlation functional, which must account for van
der Waals interactions. To capture these effects, we compare total energies and interlayer
distances obtained using a range of functionals.
As in the monolayer case, we analyse the electronic band structure, density of states, and
transmission functions of BLG. We also explore how these properties evolve under ap­
plied in­plane bias, observing transitions between metallic and semiconducting behaviour.
These results support the idea that the electronic characteristics of BLG can be tuned by
external conditions.
Finally, since BLG has been widely studied [12], we compare our findings with earlier
predictions. These benchmarks help assess the accuracy of our approach and provide a
context for interpreting later results on tBLG.

asymmetry allows the system to relax to a more energetically favourable configuration with
a smaller average interlayer distance.

4.2
Electronic structure ­ a system with tunable bands
The electronic band structure governs transport properties, and like monolayer graphene,
BLG exhibits rich behaviour that is highly sensitive to interlayer separation, stacking order,
and applied bias [12]. In particular, the tunable band structure of AB BLG has attracted
significant interest, as mentioned in section 1.2.
Here, we examine how AA and AB BLG behave within our set­up and highlight key differ­
ences in their electronic structures. These differences may help explain the contrasting
transport properties discussed in later sections.
4.2.1
Computational Methods
The band structures of both AA and AB BLG are calculated using the same parameters as
specified in section 4.1.1, with a denser k­point grid of 21 × 21 × 1 for improved sampling
of the charge density. The Brillouin zone is sampled along the path Γ­K­M­K′­Γ, with
100 k­points between each pair of high­symmetry points. Since stacking can introduce
asymmetries between the K and K′ points, both were included to ensure a complete
representation of the bilayer band structure.

4.3
Semi­conductor or metal? Reconciling DOS and
transmission
In this section, we present the transmission functions of AA and AB BLG, both with and
without applied bias. We relate the observed transport behaviour to the band structures
and densities of states (DOS) discussed previously. AB BLG, in particular, shows non­
trivial behaviour: despite a finite DOS at the Fermi level, its transmission is nearly zero.
Moreover, its strong response to bias complicates its classification as metallic or semi­
conducting. The transmission results obtained here form the basis for the conductance
calculations presented in the next section.
4.3.1
Computational Methods
For both AA and AB BLG, we consider devices containing 36 atoms, with electrodes and
scattering regions comprising 12 atoms each. A Monkhorst–Pack grid of 3 × 15 × 1 k­
points is used for the device, and a denser 100 × 15 × 1 grid is employed for the electrode
calculations. The MeshCutoff is set to 300 Ry, and the density matrix tolerance to 10−4.
For the TbTrans calculations, we retain the default energy spacing of ∆E = 0.01 eV, but
increase the number of transverse k­points to 200, as tests indicated this yielded improved
results for our set­up. The bias is applied in the same manner as described in section
3.3.3.
To sample the DOS, we use a 71 × 71 × 1 k­point grid, as a dense mesh is required to
resolve fine features in the spectrum. A Gaussian smearing of 0.2 eV is applied to reduce
numerical noise, although this leads to broader, less sharply defined peaks. Various com­
binations of smearing width and k­point density were tested. We also compute the DOS
of monolayer graphene as reference. The DOS is sampled from relaxed UCs, as in the
band structure calculations.

One key reason for the lower absolute values observed in our study is that the nonzero
minimal conductivity of graphene near the Dirac point is believed to originate from mecha­
nisms not present in our simulations. These include evanescent modes—non­propagating
states that, despite their decaying nature, can transmit across short distances via quantum
tunnelling [55]. Capturing such contributions requires a very specific set­up: the scatter­

enough that these modes do not decay before reaching the opposite contact, and cou­
pled seamlessly to the leads. While the DFT + NEGF formalism is, in principle, capable
of resolving such modes, the geometric and numerical precision required is demanding.
As a result, many studies instead introduce disorder, which induces a finite DOS at the
Dirac point and thereby facilitates transport via conventional propagating channels.
In contrast, the present work focuses on pristine, ballistic graphene structures without
disorder and without enhanced coupling of evanescent modes. This idealised approach
necessarily yields lower conductance but enables a clean and controlled comparison be­
tween stacking configurations and applied biases. Our transmission function for mono­
layer graphene closely resembles that presented in [44], reinforcing the view that the
observed differences between systems in this study arise from genuine physical effects,
rather than numerical artefacts or poor coupling. Moreover, while ballistic quantum trans­
port is highly sensitive to parameters such as contact matching, doping, and geometric
constraints—all of which vary across studies—our use of internal normalisation ensures
consistency. Specifically, we normalise conductance with respect to the structure width
relative to pristine graphene, allowing for meaningful comparisons across stacking orders.
Given the methodological diversity in the field, we thus focus on internal trends and their
correlation with electronic structure, rather than on absolute conductance magnitudes.

5.1.1
Commensurate unit cell construction
The sizes of the commensurate unit cells depend strongly on the twist angle (see eq.2.21).
For small twist angles, these UCs may contain several thousand atoms, making DFT cal­
culations expensive. In this study, we therefore prioritise structures with fewer atoms per
UC, enabling a systematic exploration of increasingly large systems. While this excludes
the magic­angle regime, we instead focus on intermediate and large­angle tBLG, where
reliable simulations are feasible.
To construct the tBLG structures, we employ the Python package flatgraphene [33],
which implements the commensuration formalism by Shallcross [31]. In this approach,
each twist angle is uniquely defined by a pair of integers p and q, from which the com­

ing tBLG geometry as an ASE Atoms object. Aside from p and q, flatgraphene takes three
structural parameters as input. We use equal parameters for all tBLG structures: the
lattice constant of a = 2.47Å — from the relaxed monolayer graphene with the TZP opti­
mised basis set — a vacuum of 18 Å, and the reference AB BLG interlayer separation of
d = 3.35.

The variations in total energy are so small that we decide not to compare the total energies
of the tBLG structures. Section 4.1 already demonstrated that the small energy difference
between AA and AB BLG is on the verge of our set­ups’ accuracy (and DFT’s in general).

5.1.3
Device Construction
Transport devices are constructed from the relaxed UCs by extending them along the
transport direction using ASE’s repeat function. The resulting structures are then con­
verted into .fdf input files using cif2cell [57], enabling compatibility with SIESTA.
A minimal device typically consists of three UCs: one for each electrode and one for the
scattering region. However, this minimal set­up is only valid when specific conditions are
met. In particular, the internal auxiliary supercell (IAS)—used by SIESTA to model long­
range interactions [41]—must be consistent across the interfaces between the scattering
region and the electrodes. Additionally, the charge excess must remain small to ensure
physical reliability.
To satisfy these constraints, additional UC repetitions may be needed in the electrode or
scattering regions. For most twist angles, a minimal three­unit­cell set­up suffices. The
only exception is the device at θ = 21.79◦, which requires doubling the electrode length
to maintain a consistent IAS, resulting in a five­unit­cell device.

5.2
Electronic structure of tBLG: band crossings and van
Hove singularities
We now turn to the electronic structure of tBLG and its non­trivial electronic effects—
including band hybridisation, band flattening, and van Hove singularities. These features
are highly twist­angle–dependent and have been linked to emergent phases such as Mott
insulating states and superconductivity [12]. While the small­angle “magic” regime lies
beyond the scope of this thesis, even the large and intermediate twist angles studied
here show rich behaviour.
As in previous chapters, we first analyse the electronic band structures before turning to
transport. We compare the band structures across 8 tBLG systems and benchmark them
against monolayer graphene and AA and AB BLG. Special attention is given to the be­
haviour near the high­symmetry K and M points, where Dirac­like crossings and band
flattening are expected. This analysis is followed by a discussion of the corresponding
densities of states, where the evolution of vHs reflects the underlying band structure mod­
ifications.
5.2.1
Crossings at K, flattening at M
The band structures are calculated using the exact same set­up as for AA and AB BLG
in section 4.2.1. That is, the parameters are the same, as is the number of k­points
sampled along the path. Due to our use of commensurate UCs, we sample along the
path Γ −K −M −K′ −Γ. As the tBLG structures vary in size, so do their Brillouin zone
sizes and the distance between the high symmetry points, and therefore the path length.
To be able to compare the band structures, we therefore normalise the bands by path
length. For each tBLG structure, the normalised bands are presented in figure 5.4.
For the eight tBLG structures, the electronic bands are seen to cross at the Dirac point
K, and to be linear in its vicinity. This dispersion is akin to that observed in monolayer
graphene—but with a doubly degenerate character—rather than the parabolic dispersion
of AB BLG or the double crossing of AA BLG (see figure 5.4, last panel). This behaviour
has previously been reported for selected twist angles, and intriguingly points to the layers
behaving as if electronically decoupled [58, 59]. This could significantly affect transport
behaviour, since monolayer graphene in our set­up was found to be semiconducting due
to its low transmission at the Fermi level.
Beyond the Dirac point, we observe additional changes in the electronic structure. Com­
pared to AA and AB BLG, several new bands emerge below 5 eV. These bands may ex­
hibit shape distortions due to band folding, and their increased number primarily reflects
the larger number of atoms in the commensurate supercells. Caution is thus warranted
in interpreting these states. However, their appearance clearly signals band hybridisation
and a modification of the electronic structure arising from interlayer interactions.

5.2.2
Density of states and van Hove singularities
We now turn to the DOS of the tBLG structures and discuss the evolution of the van Hove
singularity peaks, which—as discussed earlier—originate from the flat bands near the M
point. As these flatter bands move closer to the Fermi energy, the corresponding vHs
peaks approach it as well, leading to a strong, localised increase in the DOS. For very
small twist angles, even lower than those studied here, the vHs peaks eventually merge,
a feature linked to the emergence of superconductivity [12, 16].
We have already seen in chapters 3 and 4 how the DOS offers essential insights into trans­
port behaviour, as the transmission is directly related to the availability of states near the
Fermi energy. The DOS for the tBLG structures is calculated using the same methodology
as for AA and AB BLG, but with a reduced Gaussian smearing of 0.05 to better resolve
the vHs. This reduction leads to slightly wiggly DOS curves in some cases, but the vHs
peaks remain easily identifiable. A 25 × 25 × 1 k­point grid is typically used1.

5.3
Transmission and conductance of tBLG
This section marks the culmination of our investigations in the present thesis, as we now
examine the transmission functions and zero­bias conductance of tBL. Building on insights
from monolayer graphene and AA and AB BLG, as well as the electronic band structures
and densities of states presented in the previous sections, we interpret the transport be­
haviour of tBLG across a range of twist angles. We find that the structures studied here—
spanning intermediate to large twist angles—exhibit significantly lower conductance than
either AA or AB BLG, suggesting that the graphene layers behave as effectively decou­
pled at these angles.
5.3.1
Normalised transmission
Figure 5.6 shows the normalised transmission functions for three representative twist an­
gles: a small angle (θ = 9.43◦), an intermediate angle corresponding to our commen­
suration limit (θ = 21.79◦), and a large angle (θ = 46.83◦). Because the commensurate
UCs vary significantly in size with twist angle—with larger UCs containing more atoms
and, nominally, more transport channels—normalising the transmission by device width
is essential. This allows us to isolate the influence of the Moiré periodicity, rather than
conflate it with geometric scaling effects.
The differences between the transmission curves are minimal, and similar behaviour is
observed for the remaining twist angles (see appendix C, where the effect of normalisation
is also illustrated). This trend aligns with the band structures and densities of states, which
were also found to be nearly identical near the Fermi energy across the tBLG structures.

Conductance decoupled
We use the transmission functions to compute the zero­bias conductance via eq. 2.23,
employing the same energy window of ±0.25 eV used for the AA and AB BLG systems.
The resulting conductances, listed in table 5.5, are remarkably consistent—varying by
less than 5% across all twist angles—and show no discernible correlation with geometry.

6
Conclusion & Outlook
This thesis has investigated the electronic properties and transport behaviour of graphene­
based systems using a DFT + NEGF framework, covering a progression from monolayer
graphene to AA and AB bilayers, and culminating in twisted bilayer graphene. In total,
14 distinct structures were systematically analysed. For each system, we computed and
discussed the electronic band structure, DOS, and transmission function, and evaluated
the zero­bias conductance using the Landauer formula.
We began by calibrating our set­up using monolayer graphene, benchmarking the newly
developed optimised basis sets from [1] for transport calculations. The TZP basis set
provided good agreement with reference results and converged reliably, establishing it as
a suitable choice for further studies.
We then examined AA and AB BLG to validate our approach for multi­layer systems.
After testing the influence of van der Waals corrections on structural relaxation, we pro­
ceeded to analyse the electronic and transport characteristics. In our set­up, AA BLG
exhibited metallic behaviour, while AB BLG showed a clear transport gap, rendering it
semi­conducting. We also probed the systems under bias and found AB BLG to become
increasingly conducting with bias, opening an intriguing path for engineering the electronic
behaviour of bilayer devices.
Building on this foundation, we extended our analysis to 11 tBLG systems with twist an­
gles ranging from 7.3◦to 46.8◦. All systems were structurally relaxed, and their interlayer
distances were found to be nearly identical—intermediate between those of AA and AB
BLG. The electronic band structures exhibited linear crossings at the Dirac point across
all twist angles, suggesting weak interlayer coupling. At the M point, the energy gap nar­
rowed for smaller angles, accompanied by the emergence of van Hove singularities in the
DOS that moved progressively closer to the Fermi energy.
Despite this increase in DOS near the Fermi energy, the transmission functions showed no
corresponding enhancement, as the peaks remained outside the active transport window.
As a result, all tBLG structures studied remained effectively semiconducting at zero bias.
Their conductance values were nearly identical and approximately twice that of mono­
layer graphene, consistent with the interpretation that they behave as two electronically
decoupled layers in this angle regime.
Key findings of our work include:
• The optimised TZP basis set from [1] work well for transport studies of monolayer,
bilayer, and twisted bilayer graphene.
• AB bilayer graphene acts as a semiconductor at zero bias, but becomes increasingly
conducting under applied bias.
• All twisted bilayer graphene structures show flat­band features at M that do not con­
tribute to conductance under zero bias due to the bands residing at higher energies
outside the transport window.
• Conductance across all studied large and intermediate angle tBLG systems is angle­
independent and vanishingly small.

bilayers. This foundation provides a starting point for future studies aimed at activating
interlayer effects through external perturbations such as bias — something we plan to
investigate further as will be discussed in this works’ last section, the outlook.
6.1
Outlook – limitations and paths to pursue
The findings presented in this thesis establish a framework for studying electronic trans­
port in graphene­based systems. Building on a solid series of zero­bias studies, a natural
next step is to explore how these systems respond to external bias. In the following, we
outline key limitations of our current approach and suggest future directions to extend its
scope.
For both monolayer graphene and AB­stacked bilayer graphene, we observed that apply­
ing a bias injects propagating states into the transport window, increasing conductance.
It would be highly interesting to investigate whether tBLG structures respond in a similar
way. In particular, it is compelling to study what happens as the bias window is extended
to overlap with the van Hove singularities (vHs), which lead to a significant enhancement
in the DOS. This increase in DOS could, in principle, be reflected in a corresponding surge
in transmission. Remarkably, the vHs in tBLG yield DOS values exceeding even those of
AA BLG, suggesting that under bias, tBLG might begin to exhibit the behaviour of a well­
conducting metal. While we do not expect superconductivity — as the vHs do not merge
at the Fermi level in this angle regime — it would nonetheless be intriguing to explore
whether bias can mimic some features of the low­angle regime, where superconducting
phases emerge [16, 18].
With our current set­up, small biases can feasibly be applied to intermediate twist angles
down to approximately 7.34◦. We are presently attempting to apply bias to the θ = 9.43◦
structure, although so far, this has only succeeded for large­angle structures, whose
smaller UCs make them computationally studiable. A key limitation is the size of the
Green’s function files generated by TranSIESTA, which can reach several hundred giga­
bytes. Although the code already employs advanced methods such as recursive Green’s
function algorithms and sparse matrix solvers [40], file size and memory requirements re­
main a practical constraint. We are nevertheless confident that intermediate angles can be
accessed at small biases with our current set­up, particularly by utilising high­performance
computers.
Another technical detail concerns the construction of the unit cells themselves. We ob­
served that the UCs generated using the flatgraphene package do not always corre­
spond to the minimal commensurate cells derived analytically [31]. While reconstructing
smaller UCs manually could reduce computational load for selected systems, this would
only partially address the broader challenge posed by large supercells at small twist an­
gles.
Understanding the bias response of tBLG across a range of twist angles is an important
step toward using these materials in engineered nanodevices, where the twist angle could
serve as a tuning parameter for specific transport properties.
Previous studies have also shown that the interlayer distance — and thus the coupling
between layers — affects the electronic structure of AB BLG under bias [13], enabling
tunable band gaps. It would be valuable to extend this idea to tBLG, where the electronic
structure is more complex due to interlayer hybridisation. In particular, it would be inter­
esting to study how variations in interlayer spacing influence the band flattening near M
and the associated vHs. Though not addressed in this thesis, out­of­plane corrugation

is another factor to consider, especially for small­angle structures where it has been ex­
perimentally observed [56]. If one could simultaneously tune the interlayer separation,
twist angle, and bias, tBLG could serve as a highly flexible platform for band structure
engineering.
Another direction for future work is to examine in greater detail the limitations of the cur­
rent set­up, particularly its inability to reproduce conductance values close to experimental
measurements. One potentially fruitful modification would be to change the electrode ma­
terial independently from the scattering region. For instance, using conductive AA BLG
as electrodes — which exhibit a non­zero DOS even at zero bias — may provide more
available states for transport and improve mode matching across the device. This could
result in enhanced conductance compared to fully symmetric set­ups. However, this ap­
proach introduces new challenges at the lead–scattering region interface, where material
mismatch can lead to reflection and losses. Sufficient matching may not be feasible in
such a heterogenous device but it is worth looking into.
Finally, the framework developed in this work can be readily extended to other van der
Waals heterostructures beyond graphene. Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), for
example, can exhibit semiconducting or metallic behaviour depending on their chemical
composition, number of layers, and stacking configuration. Materials like MoS2 offer band
gap tunability similar to that of graphene, through changes in stacking and layer count,
as well as external strain. Applying the same transport analysis presented here to such
systems could provide valuable insight into how their electronic properties evolve under
structural and external perturbations [60].
While this thesis has established a baseline for understanding zero­bias transport in bi­
layer graphene systems, the methods and insights developed form a robust foundation
for probing interlayer coupling and tunable transport behaviour — not only in graphene,
but across a wider class of two­dimensional materials. The road ahead is thus diverse,
with many twisting paths to follow. Our road goes ever on and on.
