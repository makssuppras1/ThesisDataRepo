Abstract
Wastewater treatment plant generates large amounts of sewage sludge every year. A
byproduct of this is sewage sludge. Most of the sewage sludge ash ends its lifecycle
at a nearby landfill. This provides an opportunity to implement a sustainable solution
by utilizing the ash as a supplement material in brick production by replacing 30%
of the clay material. The investigation of the 30% replacement of clay with sewage
sludge from the wastewater plant was carried out. A small brick production consisting
of sewage sludge ash and clay was on a laboratory scale examined where the properties
of the brick discs were tested and evaluated. The purpose of the investigation was
to determine the mineral content when firing at various temperatures. The samples
were then evaluated by testing parameters such as volume shrinkage, loss of mass, the
content of heavy metals, and decrease in porosity. There are no limits for allowable
concentrations of heavy metals in ashes in construction materials.
For this reason,
there is a great potential to utilize this in the construction industry.

Introduction
Denmark is among a number of countries in Europe that practice the incineration
of sewage sludge.
Sewage sludge occurs during the purification process of sewage.
When sewage sludge is mono-incinerated at a plant that only receives sewage sludge, a
phosphorus-rich sewage sludge ash (SSA) is obtained that can be reused and the SSA
is deposited in a special landfill for later recycling [1].
EasyMining focuses on technologies for the recovery and recycling of nutrients from
acid-washed SSA and has developed the Ash2Phos process, which is a circular solution
for the recovery of phosphorus from sewage sludge [2].
The Ash2Phos process converts SSA into a raw material for phosphorus extraction.
The technology extracts and recycles phosphorus from SSA using wet chemical pro-
cesses. Acid washing is a method where the pH of SSA is lowered by adding acid and
water [1]. SSA is not classified as sustainable residual waste when it is not recycled.
There is a significant and increasing focus on valuing and turning the phosphorus-rich
SSA into a sustainable waste product.
Phosphorus is one of the most important nutrients in mineral fertilizers and thus cru-
cial for agricultural production. Europe is dependent on imports of phosphorus. It is a
limited resource that leads to large climate emissions and poses a supply risk. That is
why the EU has included phosphorus on its critical raw material list, and the recycling
of phosphorus is therefore high on the EU’s agenda [3].
Biofos and EasyMining signed an MoU (Memorandum of Understanding) back in De-
cember 2018, which consists of a long-term collaboration between the parties to poten-
tially extract phosphorus for 20.000 tons of SSA annually for 20 years [4]. In addition to
the ongoing production of the SSA, it was also decided in the agreement to have 12.000
tonnes of SSA removed from BIOFOS’ landfill annually. At present, EasyMining is
building full-scale plants in Sweden and Germany, where these plants will treat sewage
sludge from Sweden, Germany, and from BIOFOS in Denmark [4].
A recent study investigated the properties of bricks by replacing clay with 0-50% of
untreated SSA and SSA treated with electrodialytic remediation, respectively. Here
it was shown that a replacement of 30% was satisfactory in relation to color prop-
erties, porosity, and strength [5]. This master’s thesis examines the influence of the
partial replacement of clay in bricks with SSA. The SSA used was pretreated using the
Ash2Phos-process in Sweden, and supplied by Advedøre WWTP, Denmark
In collaboration with the brick factory Egersund Wienerberger A/S, two types of clay

2.2
Sewage Sludge ash and Possibilities for Utilization
A wastewater treatment plant generates sewage sludge in the purification process. When
sewage sludge is incinerated, it ends up as a by-product, namely sewage sludge ash
(SSA). Denmark is burning approx. a third of the sewage sludge generated across the
country [9]. The sewage sludge ash (SSA) originated from BIOFOS in collaboration
with the Swedish environmental company Ragn-Sell’s innovation company, EasyMining.
2.3
Mono-combustion and phosphorus recycling
A sludge incinerator that receives mechanically dewatered sludge from a treatment
plant is placed in a receiving facility where the sludge is stored in silos. The first stage
is a pre-drying stage where dry matter is increased to 35-40% and the subsequent com-
bustion works without supporting fuel. The final combustion takes place in a furnace
(’fluidized bed’) and a boiler. There is an air heater built into the flue gas path which
ensures a high degree of air preheating as part of the energy optimization. The flu-
idized bed is the treatment technology that handles the sludge by incineration on top
of a "liquid sand layer" [1].
Heated combustion air is blown at high speed through a nozzle base under an ap-
prox. 50 cm layer of sand, which is thereby made to float as a liquid. The sludge’s
transit time is short, as the temperature in the oven is 800-900 . The hot floating sand
acts like sandpaper on the sludge and causes particles to break up. The ensuing particle
shape causes the sludge to burn out immediately[1].
The upward airflow sucks the ash and flue gas out of the stovetop. It is carried on
to the flue gas cleaning, consisting of an electro filter where the ash is separated and
the flue gas continues through bag filters and wet scrubbers, where the environmentally
harmful substances are captured. Flue gas condensation can occur, where the heat is
extracted by condensation of the water vapor content of the flue gas. Subsequently, SSA
consists of sand (silicate) and metal oxides (iron and aluminum). Flue gas condensation
can occur, where the heat is extracted by condensation of the water vapor content of
the flue gas. Subsequently, SSA consists of sand (silicate) and metal oxides (iron and
aluminum) [1].
The phosphorus (6-12%) contains iron or aluminum phosphate to which it is bound.
The heavy metals in the sewage sludge are bound in the SSA, but not mercury. This
is caught in the other cleaning. Potassium ends up in the ash. Sulfur is distributed
equally as SO4 2– in SSA and SO2 in the flue gas. Nitrogen is converted to N2 and a
little as NOxin the flue gas [1].

Phosphorus is chemically bound after the sludge incineration.
The majority is for
iron and aluminum and a smaller part is for calcium. Iron and aluminum phosphate
has low solubility in water, therefore it requires acid to release the phosphorus. Wet
chemical processes are therefore used to recycle phosphorus from sludge. Here the SSA
pH is lowered by adding acid and water and phosphorus, metals and heavy metals will
dissolve.
The dissolution of phosphorus, iron, aluminum, and calcium in SSA is shown at different
pH values in figure 7. The pH value must be below 4 before phosphorus (red) starts
to dissolve. Iron phosphate (orange) dissolves first and when the pH value is below 3
where aluminum phosphate (blue) also dissolves. A pH value below 1 causes all the
phosphorus to be dissolved. It is economically expensive to lower the pH value to 1
and a disadvantage to have all iron and aluminum dissolved. The pH value is therefore
lowered to around 1.5 and here the phosphorus yield is between 85-95% [1].

The acid wash includes either hydrochloric acid, phosphoric acid, or sulfuric acid. After
the acid treatment, heavy metals are removed and phosphorus precipitates as a high-
quality phosphorus product, which can be used as fertilizer or animal feed [1].
EasyMining stands behind the Ash2Phos process. The process shown in figure 8 is
divided into 3 steps: an acid step, an alkaline step where intermediates products are
produced, and a final preservation step where intermediates products are converted to
final products [10].
Step 1: SSA is dissolved in water and hydrochloric acid, which dissolves phospho-
rus along with other substances such as heavy metals, metals, and salts. The slurry is
filtered, and a residual product with insoluble substances called "silicate sand" and an
impure phosphoric acid, is sent on to the next step. The silicate sand, which primarily
contains inorganic silicate and metal oxides, can, after washing and neutralization, e.g.
used as a cement substitute in concrete or for the manufacture of bricks.

2.4
Sewage Sludge Ash Utilization in Brick Manufacturing
A study from 2021 investigated three SSA from mono-incineration plants [11]. SSA
came respectively from Germany and the rest from Denmark. The ash had been treated
with electrodialytic separation by separating phosphorus and heavy metals from SSA.
The recovery of phosphorus was successful with respectively 65% for one ash and 81-82%
for the remaining two. Successfully the electrodialytic separation managed to separate
SSA in the study with two outputs respectively phosphorus and another raw material
for use in constructions [11].
Another study from 2020 examined SSA from a Danish wastewater treatment plant in
brick discs of d = 20mm with respectively treated and untreated SSA as a supplement
to clay. The clay replacement varied between 0%, 10%, 30%, and 50%. The brick discs
were fired at temperatures of 950◦C– 1000◦C– 1050 ◦C. at 950 ◦C, porosity increased
with a high percentage of treated SSA and decreased again between 1000◦Cand 1050 ◦C.
At 1000 ◦C, all the brick discs showed acceptable open porosity, and densities.
At
1050 ◦C, the open porosity was very low, 12.5% and 13.6% with 30% and 50% clay
replacement with SSA-EDS, respectively. Here, brick discs with treated SSA had an
increased open porosity compared to brick discs with untreated SSA. The treated brick
slices also had a higher content of hematite formation, which gave the slices a reddish
color [5].

Mineralogy
XRD diffractogram of SSA-AWc illustrates a considerable amount of hematite (Fe2O3)
and quartz (SiO2) whereas for feldspar (Na(AlSi3O8)) constitute a small amount. The
amount of Fe found from the acid digestion test gave a very low result to almost
none. However, the XRD diffractogram presents a considerable amount of hematite
in the SSA-AWc. As for YCc (crushed yellow clay) and YCSc (crushed yellow clay
added sand) quartz, feldspar, and calcite (CaCO3) contain the same minerals. For RCc
(crushed red clay) and RCDc (crushed red clay added sand), the amount of quartz and
feldspar is higher, and a lower amount of calcite when compared to YCc and YCSc,
which is related to the type of clay given in this study.
Also, hematite occurs during the firing process for red clay brick material, which is an
indication of the reddish brown color. However, the SSA-AWc has an impact on the
yellow clay due to the considerable amount of hematite and thus creating the red-colored
brick discs. And from previous investigations, the presence of quartz and hematite, has
been detected inSS-AWc [14] [15] [19] [5].
From figure 2 clay minerals kaolinite, montmorillonite, mica, illite, and partly feldspar
show that these components will start to decompose when a firing temperature reaches
550 ◦C. From the decomposed form the clay minerals will convert to a new form
that binds quartz and remaining feldspar, a so-called glass-melt-condition. The study
[5] found that the presents of hematite contributed to the formation of a glass-melt-
condition.
The composition of clay minerals consists of varying amounts Al, Ca, Fe, K, Mg, and
Si, where the XRD analysis showed the presence of these minerals in SSA-WAc.

6
Conclusion
Acid-washed sewage sludge ash (SSA-AWc) as a substitute for clay is investigated in
this thesis. Two types of clay are provided from the Danish brick factory Egernsund
Wienerberger, one with 15% sand added and one with 6.7% sand added respectively.
The purpose is to examine the difference between the two. One is yellow clay and one
is red clay. Both types of clay are investigated for the production of brick discs with the
acid-washed sewage sludge from the wastewater treatment plant BIOSFOS in Advedøre.
The ash was pre-treated in Sweden with the new technology called Ash2Phos-process
where extraction of phosphorous is done as a part of a circular economy. Acid washing
is a method where the pH of SSA is lowered by adding acid and water. There is a
significant and increasing focus on valuing and turning the phosphorus-rich SSA into a
sustainable waste product.
The by-product of this method is used as a potential construction material and tested
in brick discs. Brick properties and firing temperatures are examined at a laboratory
scale level. With a replacement of 30% SSA-AWc in small brick discs, the investigation
shows a significant increase in minerals such as hematite and quartz. The properties of
the brick discs are tested for firing temperatures of 950 ◦C, 975 ◦C, 1000 ◦C, 1025 ◦C,
and 1050 ◦C.
Their characteristics are evaluated by testing parameters such as volume shrinkage,
loss of mass, and decrease in porosity. Another relevant observation during firing at
high temperatures such as 1025% and 1050% is the melt-down phase for the miner-
als where the spacing between particles becomes small and the minerals become more
compact and therefore resulting in a strong compound.
