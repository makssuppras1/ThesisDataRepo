Abstract
Immunotherapy has revolutionised cancer treatment, redirecting the research focus towards exploiting
the patient’s own immune system to combat cancer. Adoptive cell transfer poses a great advancement
within immunotherapy and includes T cell receptor (TCR) gene therapy where autologous T cells are
genetically engineered to express tumour specific TCRs. A major breakthrough within TCR gene ther-
apy is CRISPR/Cas9-mediated genome editing. Non-viral delivered CRISPR/Cas9 components have
several advantages compared to traditional viral transduction, including increased target-specificity,
reduced off-target effects, and reduced endogenous and transgenic TCR mispairing.
Here we present optimisation of non-viral CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene integration of transgenic
TCRs in primary human T cells.
We investigated the use of different homology directed repair
template (HDRT) concentrations with closed circular DNA as template, non-homologous end-joining
(NHEJ) inhibitors, and Cas9 targeting sequences for nuclear localisation of the template, in order to
improve relative and absolute knockin (KI) efficiency without compromising live cell count affected
by electroporation or introduction of HDRT.
We succeeded in KI of a NY-ESO-1 antigen specific TCR and a Merkel Cell Carcinoma neoantigen
specific TCR. Improved KI efficiency was achieved with a HDRT concentration of 20 nM and the use
of the small molecule inhibitor M3814. Additionally, the absolute KI efficiency was further improved
by antigen-specific scaffold-mediated expansion.
These results emphasise the promising potential of CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene integration for future
clinical application within cancer immunotherapy and pave the way for future experiments that harness
the power of the technology.

Introduction
Cancer is a leading cause of death worldwide and accounted for nearly 10 million deaths in 2020 [1].
Globally the cancer burden continues to grow and is expected to reach 28.4 million cases in 2040, an
increase of 47 %, in which the estimation of cancer-related deaths are 16.4 million [2, 3]. Survival
after cancer diagnosis is influenced by several factors including the type of cancer, available treatment,
cancer stage at diagnosis and treatment onset. Additionally, some cancer types are generally more
challenging to diagnose and/or treat compared to other types. For instance, lung cancer has a 5-year
survival rate of less than 20 % whereas for breast cancer 80 % of patients are alive after 5 years [4].
Conventional cancer treatment includes chemotherapy, radiation, and surgery used as single treatment
or in combination [5]. However, cancer recurrence, genomic damage, and detrimental side effects poses
a critical issue post treatment [6, 7, 8].
Cancers are highly heterogeneous and has the ability to evade the immune system. In fact, the im-
mune system plays a critical role in their development and growth [8, 9]. Therefore, most of the
existing therapies are not efficient enough to provide a complete clinical response against the disease
[10]. However, based on the interplay between cancer and the immune system, immunotherapeutic
strategies have been developed [9]. An essential immune cell for cancer therapeutics is the T cell which
is widely used for adoptive cell transfer (ACT) therapies [9]. Transfer of unedited tumour infiltrat-
ing lymphocytes (TILs) have shown high therapeutic efficacy in some clinical results [11]. However,
tumour biopsies are essential but are risky and anatomical unavailable for certain tumours [12]. This
emphasises, the great potential of ACT with engineered transgenic T cell receptor (TCR) T cells with
improved tumour targeting specificity prepared from the patient’s blood samples [11].
CRISPR/Cas9 have shown a promising efficacy and safety profile within Cas9-mediated knockouts
(KOs) in CRISPR/Cas9 genome edited T cells in clinics [13, 14]. Therefore, there is a rising interest in
trials using Cas9-mediated knockins (KIs) exploiting the inherent homologous directed repair pathway
for mutation correction and insertion of novel therapeutic constructs [13, 15]. CRISPR/Cas9-mediated
gene integration allows for precise, uniform, and safe genome editing, underlining the application of
CRISPR/Cas9-mediated transgenic TCR KIs [15].
The overall aim of the thesis project is to improve targeted T cell receptor KIs via CRISPR/Cas9-
mediated gene editing while enhancing the cell viability following electroporation.

1.1
Immune System
The immune system is responsible for protecting the body against foreign pathogens such as viruses,
bacteria, parasites, and fungi. However, this can also include host cells that do not act as healthy cells
e.g. cancer cells. An important function of the immune system, is its ability to distinguish self from
non-self thereby selectively killing foreign invaders. The immune system is divided into the innate and
the adaptive immunity [16]. The innate immune system is the first line of defence. It is fast acting
(minutes to hours), non-specific, and limits the spread of the infection while recruiting more immune
cells. Innate immune system covers defensive barriers like the physical barrier of the epithelial cell
lining and the mucus secretion in the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and genitourinary tracts. Cells of
the innate immune system includes neutrophils, innate lymphoid cells, and macrophages. These cells
do not have somatically recombined antigen-receptors and therefore do not provide immunological
memory [17, 18]. Adaptive immunity is the second line of defence and is more slow acting (days
to weeks) in a primary immune response than the innate immunity. However, contrary to innate
immunity, the immune response is much more specific as it is antigen-dependent and has the capability
of immunological memory. This ensures rapid and efficient immunity upon a secondary infection with
the same antigen.
The cells of the adaptive immune response are T cells and B cells which are
responsible for cellular and humoral immunity respectively. A key player of the interplay between
the innate and the adaptive immunity is the antigen-presenting cells such as dendritic cells which are
essential to activate T cells and thereby initiate effective immunisation [18].
1.2
T Cell Biology
T cells are effector immune cells from adaptive immunity and have an essential role in the cell-mediated
immunity against cancer, infections, and in autoimmunity [19]. T cells can express different isoforms
of the TCR genes. The majority of mature T cells in humans express the TCRα and TCRβ isoforms
which is known as αβ T cells whereas 0.5%-5% express the TCRγ and TCRδ isoforms, known as γδ
T cells [20]. This thesis will focus solely on the conventional αβ T cells which will be referred to as T
cells.
1.2.1
T Cell Development
T cells develop in thymus by TCR repertoire selection which generates effective self-tolerant T cells
[21]. The TCR is used in positive and negative selection. In the thymic cortex, hematopoietic lym-
phoid progenitors (double negative thymocytes) develop into TCR expressing CD4+CD8+ double

positive (DP) thymocytes. V(D)J recombination of TCRα and TCRβ chain, described below (section
1.2.1.1), ensure high diversity in the repertoire of TCR specificities [20, 21]. The DP thymocytes
will individually be presented to self-peptides on major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules
(class I and II) and those with low affinity binding will receive survival signals. Thymocytes failing
positive selection will be eliminated within the thymic cortex by apoptosis. The positively selected
cells will differentiate to either CD4+CD8−or CD4−CD8+ single positive (SP) thymocytes. The SP
thymocytes will migrate to the thymic medulla where the cells will be presented to self-peptide-MHC
complexes a second time [21]. If the TCR have high affinity for a self-peptide, the corresponding
cell will undergo apoptosis. This process is called negative selection and ensures T cell self-tolerance
[20]. Not all SP thymocytes with high affinity for self-peptides will die. Some will differentiate into
other subsets of T cells such as regulatory T cells (Tregs) which is important for immune homeostasis
[21]. During development in thymus, T cells differentiate into subpopulations. The major populations
are CD4+ and CD8+ T cells with approximately 60-70% being CD4+ and 30-40% being CD8+ [16].
Na¨ıve mature T cells leave the thymus and enters secondary lymphoid tissues such as spleen and
lymph nodes. Here they can interact with foreign peptides via DCs and thereby become activated,
differentiated and capable of eliciting an appropriate immune response [20].
1.2.1.1
T Cell Receptor
The TCR consist of an extracellular domain, a single transmembrane region, a membrane-proximal
connecting peptide, and a cytoplasmic tail [22]. The extracellular domain of the TCR contains a
variable immunoglobulin (Ig)-like domain (V domain) containing the antigen recognition site that
is responsible for binding to the peptide-MHC complex and a constant Ig-like domain (C domain)
responsible for the interaction with CD3 (figure 1) [20]. The V-domain contains six hypervariable
loops called complementarity-determining regions (CDRs). Three are located in Vα chain and three
are located in Vβ chain and are called CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3. CDR1 and CDR2 are encoded within
the germline TCR V gene segments whereas CDR3 are encoded in the V, D and J gene segments and
is formed by DNA recombination hence containing the most variability [22, 23]. The surface of the six
loops creates a single hypervariable site which is the antigen-binding site and determines the specificity
of the TCR [24]. The constant domain of the TCR is the same between T cells and the variable domain
varies between T cells, making them unique [23].

The differentiation of T cells depends on the type of infection. Intracellular infections and cancer

1.2.4
Immune Evasion
The cancer immunosurveillance hypothesis states that host immune cells survey the body for trans-
formed cells, the same way it protects the body from pathogens [6, 42]. This hypothesis would indicate
that an immunocompromised individual would have a higher tumour burden. However, mouse studies
have shown that immunodeficient mice had more immunogenic tumours compared to immunocompe-
tent mice [43]. This indicates a role of defective immunological monitoring where the immunological
killing is limited and instead participate in tumour cells evading eradication. However, deficiencies
in especially CD4 and CD8 T cells has also shown to increase the tumour incidences markedly and

were more susceptible to cancer development. Demonstrating, the immune system’s significant con-
tribution to immune surveillance. These contradictions have moved the scientific consensus of the
immunosurveillance hypothesis towards a broader hypothesis called cancer immunoediting [6].
Cancer immunoediting consist of three phases of tumour growth: the elimination phase, the equilib-
rium phase, and the escape phase [44, 45]. In the elimination phase, innate immune cells like NK
cells recognise transformed cells and start secreting IFN-γ which induces killing of tumour cells. Ad-
ditionally, they secrete chemokines such as CCL5 and XCL1 which recruits cDCs to the site of the
tumour cells [40, 44, 45]. The recruited cDCs will phagocytose the dead tumour cells and migrate to
the draining lymph node [44, 45]. The cDCs prime T cells, resulting in tumour antigen-specific T cells
that home to the site of the tumour and kill of tumour cells [40, 45]. CTLs, NK cells, and tumour-
associated macrophages (TAMs) will via secretion of IFN-γ and TNF-α, increase the anti-tumour
activity [40, 45]. This concept corresponds to the described immunosurveillance hypothesis [42, 44,
45]. The second phase is the equilibrium phase. Cancer cells that avoid killing in the elimination phase
are usually less immunogenic cancer cells. Cancer cells left in this phase consequently have enhanced
resistance towards immune recognition. Immune cells keep the tumour in a dormant state by prevent-
ing outgrowth, however, without eliminating it completely [40, 8]. The last phase of tumour growth is
the escape phase. Immune cells are no longer able to stop the cancer for outgrowing and the tumour
cells become malignant cancer cells [44]. The escaping cancer cells have an immune-resistant pheno-
type where they induce a immuno-suppressive TME and avoid immune recognition [40]. The tumour
cells can escape through many different mechanisms, for instance loss of tumour antigen or MHC-I
expression, increased resistance towards cytotoxins, and the formation of a immunosuppressive TME
[8, 40, 44, 45]. A major mechanism of tumour suppression is recruitment of tumour-derived Tregs
and myeloid derived suppressor cells which helps in suppressing CD8 T cell-mediated anti-tumour
immunity. M2 macrophages and their secretion of IL-10 and TGF-β also promote tumour growth.
Additionally, these immuno-suppressive cytokines can shift the balance from a TH1 immune response
towards a TH2 immune response [8].
Tumour escape is highly complex and the mechanisms are still not fully understood. A deeper knowl-
edge of the mechanism of cancer-evasion strategies as well as antigenic heterogeneity of tumours, can
help in designing therapies which are able to more efficiently target and eradicate cancer cells thereby
counteracting their evasion.

1.3
Immunotherapy
The complex interplay between the immune system and the tumour, during tumour development and
progression, has been a major research focus related to cancer therapies over the past few decades [9,
46]. The improved understanding has allowed development of specific therapies designed to exploit
protective immunological mechanisms, boosting the patient’s immune response.
This is known as
immunotherapy and involves activation of the immune system via cell therapies and attacking the
tumour directly [46].
Immune checkpoint blockade has already shown promising results in cancer
treatment and several of such therapies have been approved for clinical use in treatment of multiple
cancers [9, 46]. This therapy is based on administration of monoclonal antibodies directed against
CTLA-4 and PD-1, inhibitory receptors associated with immune exhaustion, particularly in T cells,
limiting the anti-tumour response [47]. However, despite significantly increasing the survival among
some cancer patients, including non-small cell lung cancer and melanoma, disease progression is still
observed in many patients following therapy [9, 48].
An alternative treatment option may therefore be adoptive cell therapy (ACT) which is based on
collecting and modifying patient tumour-infiltrating or peripheral blood immune cells and reinfuse
the cells back into the patient to mediate an anti-tumour response [48]. The strategy is to enhance
anti-tumour immunity by editing or enriching specific immune cells allowing the tumour infiltration
and resulting in direct cytotoxic effects. ACT can be categorised into three types with different mech-
anisms of action, ACT with tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), with chimeric antigen receptor
(CAR) modified T cells, and using T cell receptor (TCR) gene therapy[9].
TIL therapy involves ex vivo isolation and expansion of tumour-specific T cells extracted from a tu-
mour biopsy. TILs are administered via ACT concomitant with high doses of supportive IL-2 into
the patient treated with a preparatory lymphodepleting regimen. Despite promising clinical responses
with high efficacy for several cancer types, in particular melanoma, ACT with TILs has still not been
approved [49, 50]. Many patients have no response to treatment or relapse after initial response which
poses one of the major challenges for TIL-based ACT therapy [49]. ACT with gene engineered TCR
or CAR T cells, is in contrast to TIL therapy, based on the modification and expansion of periph-
eral blood lymphocytes. Peripheral lymphocytes are generally more accessible compared to collection
of lymphocytes from tumour biopsies for TIL therapy [51]. Anti-tumour reactivity is generated by
transducing tumour-specific receptors into T cells. For TCR gene therapy and CAR T cell therapy

1.4
Double-stranded Breaks
Living organisms are constantly exposed to DNA damage either caused by endogenous or environmen-
tal damaging agents. DNA damage can consequently lead to decreased cell survival and mutagenesis.
In response to this, organisms have developed different DNA damage response pathways to maintain
genome stability. One of the repair mechanisms is the double-stranded break (DSB) repair. DSBs are
primarily repaired by non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) or Homology Directed Repair (HDR) [59].
1.4.1
Non-homologous End-joining
In mammalian systems NHEJ is the predominant DSB repair pathway. The mechanism involves direct
rejoining of the two DSB ends, either with or without processing [59]. This can result in the addition
or removal of nucleotides at the ends, producing insertion or deletion mutations which collectively is
known as indels. The NHEJ pathway is therefore considered error-prone compared to HDR that is
typically error-free.
When a DSB is formed the broken ends are protected by immediate binding of the heterodimer
Ku70–Ku80 (Ku) (figure 4). The Ku heterodimer recruits and activates the DNA-dependent protein
kinase catalytic subunit (DNA-PKcs) which has important kinase activity for NHEJ. Ku additionally
recruits several NHEJ factors that form a long-range synaptic complex where the two DSB ends are
held, bringing them into proximity. Processing and ligation of the ends are enabled by the transition to
a short-range synaptic complex in which the ends are closely aligned. Transition into the short-range
synaptic complex occurs subsequent to autophosphorylation of DNA-PKcs, resulting in dissociation
from DNA, and ensures that the ends are ligated by DNA ligase IV (LIG4) immediately after becoming
compatible, hence limiting DNA end processing and minimising mutagenesis [59, 60].

1.4.2
Homology Directed Repair
HDR is another DSB repair pathway and compared to NHEJ described above, HDR is a more complex
and error-free pathway. Compared to the end-joining in NHEJ, HDR uses homologous sequences, usu-
ally located on sister chromatids, for the repair [59]. See Appendix A for a full overview of the HDR
mechanism. NHEJ can occur during the entire life cycle of the cell while HDR, due to the necessity
of an available sister chromatid, is restricted to the S and G2 phases of the cell life cycle [63]. Even
though HDR is less error-prone than NHEJ, dysregulation can cause genomic rearrangements which
can induce genomic instability [59].

in yeast and mammalian cells [66].

1.4.2.2
RAD51-independent HDR
The RAD51-independent repair pathway includes single-stranded template repair (SSTR) and single-
strand annealing (SSA). RAD52 binds the resected ssDNA and mediates SSA by annealing regions
of homology. This results in the deletion of intervening sequences. SSTR uses ssODN (or ssDNA) as
template and is dependent on RAD52 as well. The mechanism is still not fully understood. Especially,
processes within DSB resection, strand invasion, D-loop extension, and second end capture have to
be elucidated [59, 68]. However, in general the RAD51-dependent pathway is favoured in mammalian
cells whereas the RAD51-independent pathway takes over when RAD51 is suppressed [68].
1.5
CRISPR-Cas
Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) was first discovered in prokary-
otes as a key element of their adaptive immune system [69, 70]. The bacterial CRISPR locus contains
small ”spacers” of viral or plasmid DNA from previously infecting agents and short palindromic re-
peat sequences located between the spacers which together is transcribed into CRISPR RNA (crRNA)
and trans-activating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA). The CRISPR locus also contains CRISPR associ-
ated (Cas) genes which encodes Cas proteins with endonuclease activity [70, 71]. The crRNA and the
tracrRNA, together called guide RNA (gRNA), enables the Cas protein to target and cleave of foreign
DNA [69]. The prokaryotic CRISPR-Cas system function by three phases: Adaption, expression, and
interference. The adaption phase is the integration of new spacers of foreign DNA sequences into the
genome, mediated by Cas proteins. The expression phase is the transcription of the CRISPR array.
The Interference phase is the formation of the effector protein that can subsequently recognise, cleave
and inactivate the invading genome.
Via the adaption phase, the bacteria and archaea can store
foreign DNA in their own genome and create bacterial immunological memory [70]. This design has
later been adapted by scientist as genome editing tool in mammalian cells [69].
CRISPR-Cas systems are classified into two groups, Class 1 and Class 2 systems. Class 1 CRISPR
systems are most abundant in prokaryotes and is based on a multi-Cas protein effector complex for
antiviral defence, containing a Cas3 helicase/nuclease subunit. Class 2 CRISPR systems are based on
single-subunit nucleases with highly diverse properties depending on the specific enzyme [59, 71]. Cas9
is a endonuclease within Class 2 CRISPR system [59]. The most used CRISPR system for genome
editing is, for now, the CRISPR/Cas9 derived from Streptococcus pyogenes (spCas9) which will be the
Cas enzyme used throughout this thesis project and referred to as Cas9 [69, 71].

The DNA endonuclease Cas9 has two nuclease domains, HNH and RuvC, which are important to

strand (complementary to the spacer sequence). RuvC is the domain that cleaves the non targeted
DNA strand (non-complementary to the spacer sequence) [59]. The two endonuclease domains creates
a DSB in the targeted DNA. The gRNA and the Cas9 forms a complex called ribonucleoprotein (RNP)
which enables the guidance of the Cas9 protein to the specific target site [71]. The crRNA contains a
18-24 bp spacer sequence that is complementary to the target gene [69, 70, 71]. Besides the gRNA, the
Cas9 protein also contains a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) interacting (PI) domain which binds
to a PAM sequence on the target DNA [70]. The PAM is located adjacent to the spacer sequence and

depends on the Cas protein [69, 71]. Cas9 recognises the PAM sequence 5’-NGG-3’ where N is any
nucleotide. The enzyme cuts 3 bp downstream this sequence [72]. This is a promiscuous PAM sequence
and does not significantly limit the DNA targets whereas other Cas proteins have more specific PAM
sequences which narrows the target availability [69, 71]. In correlation to the prokaryotic CRISPR-Cas

the bacterial host genome which prevents the microbe from cleaving its own genome. Thereby, only
foreign DNA is cleaved [70].
1.5.2
NHEJ and HDR in CRISPR/Cas9
CRISPR/Cas9, in mammalian cells, relies on the two endogenous repair mechanism of DNA damage,
NHEJ and HDR (section 1.4). The outcome of the genome editing depends on the ”choice” of DSB
repair pathway [59, 69]. NHEJ is the more frequently utilised DSB repair as it is active in approxi-
mately 90% of the cell cycle and does not require a homologous donor template [71]. It usually results
in a frameshift mutation that can create a premature stop codon or a non-functional protein and
is therefore useful in CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene knockouts (KOs). Even though NHEJ is more
error-prone than HDR it does not create KOs every time. However, when the DNA repair is accurate,
the gene will be prone to another CRISPR/Cas9-mediated DSB. This can occur until NHEJ fails and
CRISPR/Cas9-mediated KOs will therefore usually have a really high efficiency. The KOs are useful
both within therapeutics but also in understanding of biological pathways and gene function where the
role of a specific protein is investigated [69, 71]. The more error-free pathway, HDR, can be utilised to
insert any desired nucleotide or nucleotide combination into the genome and is guided by a homologous
DNA template [59, 70, 71]. This, in theory, enables unlimited possibilities of gene knockins (KIs).
However, HDR is a DNA repair mechanism with relatively low event frequency compared to NHEJ,
thereby, limiting its application to create KIs. Additionally, HDR is restricted to proliferating cells,
as the repair mechanism is active during cell division [59, 71].
1.5.3
Challenges and Possibilities of CRISPR/Cas9
Traditional gene therapy used today (section 1.3), relies on viral vector delivery and integration of
transgenes. This delivery can result in both oncogenesis and immunogenic toxicity. CRISPR is an
alternative approach providing simple site-specific gene-editing. The controlled target specificity en-
ables lower frequency of mutagenesis and thereby fewer adverse events [69]. The first tools, described
for site-specific gene editing, were ZFN and TALENs. Compared to these techniques, CRISPR-Cas

==PAGE:32==
Alberte Hjortgaard Skriver
Louise Pii Frederiksen
system provides a low price, simple design, ease of use, repeatability, and high efficiency. However,
as traditional gene therapy, CRISPR also raises some issues, both technical and ethical. One of the
issues concerning CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene editing is off-target effects (OTEs) where gRNA mis-
matches with other DNA sequences in the genome and introduce mutations in non-target DNA [69,
71]. However, optimised gRNAs and engineered Cas9 variants have shown to reduce OTEs [73, 74,
75, 76]. For instance, the engineered Cas9 nickase (nCas9) combined with a gRNA pair has shown to
reduce OTEs by introducing single stranded breaks on both strands creating sticky end DSBs instead
of blunt end DSBs [73]. CRISPR/Cas9 formulation can also affect the OTE rate. It has been shown
that delivery of Cas9 and gRNA directly as RNPs instead of the form of a DNA expressing plasmid
or mRNA can decrease the OTEs [75, 76]. This might be due to the recombinant protein having a
limited working window whereas the plasmid and mRNA can be transcribed and translated continu-
ously [71]. Immunogenicity can also be a risk factor, especially using CRISPR/Cas9 in vivo. As the
CRISPR/Cas9 system derives from bacteria, Anti-Cas9 antibodies and anti-Cas9 T cells have been
found which elicit immune responses against the Cas9 enzyme [77]. This can be a challenge in clinical
settings. A method to reduce the immunogenicity can be to use homologs or ex vivo delivery of the
CRISPR/Cas9 components [69, 71].
A major technical issue, when inserting large genetic elements into the genome, is the low efficiency
and decrease in cell viability. The efficiency of the KI is dependent on the cell repair pathway and as
NHEJ is the default pathway, the KI efficiency will be affected since it is restricted to the less utilised
HDR pathway. The DSB that CRISPR/Cas9 induces will often trigger apoptosis pathways in the
engineered cells. p53 activation is usually seen in response to toxic DSB which subsequently leads to
apoptosis. The efficiency of the gene editing can therefore be higher in p53 suppressed cells. However,
this also increase the risk of survival of oncogenic cells [69]. This is an issue for clinical use of DSB-
inducing CRISPR therapy. However, the true relationship between CRISPR/Cas9 and carcinogenesis
still has to be investigated [71]. As for OTEs, engineered Cas9 variants can be used to limit the
cell toxicity like the previosly described nCas9 and also the catalytically inactive endonuclease dead
Cas9 (dCas9) [69]. dCas9 lacks nuclease activity and can be fused with transcriptional activators or
repressors and can thereby be used to inhibit or activate specific genes. This is less invasive and can
thereby reduce toxicity [71]. dCas9 can also be fused with cytosine deaminase or adenosine deaminase
which are able to make CG to AT or AT to GC point mutations without DSBs [69, 71]. A novel
CRISPR-Cas system is prime editing which have shown to improve the efficiency and accuracy of
genome editing [78]. Prime editing are able to insert, delete and do all 12 types of base substitutions

without a donor template or creating DSBs. It uses a impaired Cas9 fused to a reverse transcriptase
and a prime editing gRNA (pegRNA). The pegRNA contains the targeting RNA and the desired donor
template. The reverse transcriptase uses the pegRNA as template and can then insert any wanted
gene sequence at the cleavage site [71].
1.5.4
Improving Knockin Efficiency and Live Cell Count
DSB repair pathway regulation strategies can help stimulate or inhibit specific pathways thereby
resulting in more precise and efficient genome editing outcomes. Engineering of key reaction com-
ponents such as chemical modification of Cas9, gRNA, HDRT, or modifying the interaction between
CRISPR/Cas9 and the PAM sequence, can improve HDR efficiency [59, 79].
Mammalian cells have evolved mechanisms which are able to regulate the choice of repair pathway. 5’-
end resection is a important regulator of the DSB repair pathway choice while NHEJ needs blunt ends,
HDR needs 3’ overhangs (section 1.4.2) [59, 68]. Key factors of the mechanism can be directly boosted
in order to enhance the 5’-end resection, such as CtIP phosphorylation, thereby increasing HDR (table
1) [80]. Generally, factors of NHEJ can be inhibited or factors of HDR can be enhanced [68]. Main
regulators of the repair pathway are 53BP1 and BRCA1 [81]. 53BP1 negatively regulates the 5’-end
resection in G1 phase of the mitotic cell cycle, resulting in inhibition of HDR. On the contrary, BRCA1
promotes 53BP1 removal in the S phase of the cell cycle, thereby, enhancing HDR. Promoting 5’-end
resection via inhibition of 53BP1 is a strategy to regulate the repair pathway towards HDR [59, 68, 81].
LIG4 ligates the DSB ends in the final step of NHEJ and could also be a potential target for inhibition
[82, 83]. MMEJ is as previously mentioned (section 1.4.1) an alternative NHEJ pathway and it has
been shown that dual inactivation of NHEJ and MMEJ via inhibition of proteins such as Ku70/80 and
Polθ results in synergistically improved HDR and additionally prevention of off-target integration of
the donor template DNA [84]. However, inactivation of the end-joining repair pathways is associated
with increased carcinogenesis and low cell viability and should be tested carefully [85]. Other strategies
to promote HDR could be inhibition of DNA donor template degradation using ssODNs, enhanced
donor template transport to the nucleus via Cas9 targeting sequence (CTS), localisation of donor
template via covalent linking to the gRNA, or regulation of the cell cycle towards S and G2 phase [15,
59, 86, 87, 88]. The following table (table 1) shows different solutions which, potentially, can increase

Delivery Strategies for CRISPR/Cas9 Cargo in T Cells
Successful introduction of the CRISPR/Cas9 components into specific cells is crucial to allow efficient
and precise genomic editing [97, 98]. The current delivery strategies can be divided into two broad
categories, viral and non-viral delivery strategies. Viral delivery are based on cell transduction with

==PAGE:36==
Alberte Hjortgaard Skriver
Louise Pii Frederiksen
viral vectors encoding the DNA sequence of the Cas protein and gRNA. Adenovirus (AdV), lentivirus
(LV) and Adeno-associated virus (AAV) are among the most frequently used vectors for delivery of
the CRISPR/Cas9 system [97, 98].
1.6.1
Viral Delivery Strategies
1.6.1.1
Adenoviruses
AdVs are double-stranded DNA viruses capable of infecting both dividing and non-dividing cells
[97, 99, 100]. One of the advantages of AdVs is the high packaging capacity. The average genome
size is 36 kb and can contain approximately 8 kb foreign DNA. In addition, AdV vectors generally
do not integrate in the host DNA and the AdV genome remains extrachromosomal in the nucleus.
This minimises potential off-target effects that includes undesirable mutations at other sites than
the specific target site. However, one of the major challenges with AdV-mediated delivery is their
immunogenicity caused by its viral nature. Additionally, a large part of the human population have
pre-existing antibodies to AdV which may result in tissue inflammation and removal of the vectors
preventing the delivery [97, 99, 100].
1.6.1.2
Lentiviruses
LVs are single-stranded RNA viruses [100]. Similar to AdVs, LVs can infect both dividing and non-
dividing cells and also have a high packaging capacity [99, 101, 102]. They can contain two copies
of an RNA genome corresponding to around 10 kb. In contrast to AdVs, the lentiviral genome is
typically integrated into the host genome. This can prolong the transgenic expression time, however,
for delivery of the CRISPR/Cas system random integration increases the risk of unwanted off-target
mutations and is additionally associated with carcinogenesis. To reduce the random integration that
particularly poses a safety risk in therapeutic purposes/for clinical applications, non-integrating LV
vectors have been designed. The cellular lipid-bilayer covering LVs are less immunogenic than the
viral capsid on AdVs and AAVs and unwanted immune responses are therefore generally not observed
with LVs [99, 101, 102].
1.6.1.3
Adeno-associated Viruses
AAVs are small single-stranded RNA viruses, also capable of infecting dividing and non-dividing cells
[99, 102]. The AAVs are popular delivery vectors for CRISPR/Cas9 components because of their low
cytotoxicity and immunogenicity and, less integration into the host genome. The major limitation of
26 of 90
==PAGE:37==
Alberte Hjortgaard Skriver
Louise Pii Frederiksen
AAVs as delivery system is their small packaging capacity. The AAVs are smaller in size and genome
length and can therefore only carry genetic material of less than 5 kb. This poses a challenge for
efficient delivery because the CRISPR/Cas9 components cannot be delivered in the same AAV vector.
Despite a lower immunogenicity associated with AAVs, unwanted immune responses elicited by its
viral capsid might still be an issue as well as pre-existing immunity in humans [69, 99, 102].
1.6.2
Non-viral Delivery Strategies
The major drawbacks associated with viral delivery-strategies, pose a challenge for clinical application
[98]. Therefore, there is an increasing interest in non-viral delivery strategies. Different methods for
transfection of cells exists. This includes microinjection which involves injection of molecules into the
cell nucleus with microscopic needles and lipofection that is based on delivery with liposome or lipid-
nanoparticles encapsulating the components. Another example is gold nanoparticle-mediated delivery
techniques where the biological material is enwrapped around a gold core. Although these methods
have lead to successful delivery of CRISPR/Cas components to T cells, the efficacy is low [98].
1.6.2.1
Electroporation
In contrast, efficient delivery of DNA and RNA to T cells have been shown via electroporation [98].
Electroporation is based on the use of pulsed high-voltage electrical currents to temporarily generate
small pores in the cell membrane allowing small-sized cargo to diffuse into the cell [56]. Cargo molecules
can be delivered directly into the nucleus as result of a combination of cell-specific pulses and reagents
[98]. Compared to other delivery techniques electroporation is less dependent on cell type and are
capable of transferring cargo into cells that are traditionally difficult to manipulate [103]. Additionally,
electroporation provides larger cargo capacity enabling delivery of multiple components for example
Cas9 proteins and HDRTs. The CRISPR/Cas9 components, Cas9 and gRNAs, can be introduced
as plasmid DNA, mRNA or RNPs [104]. Delivery in DNA format requires both transcription and
translation before gene editing can take place. The DNA is transcribed into gRNA and Cas9 mRNA.
Translation of the Cas9 protein occurs in the cytoplasm where it binds to gRNA and returns to the
nucleus. For mRNA delivery, only translation is needed. Cas9 is directly translated in the cytoplasm
before localising to the nucleus together with gRNA. Neither transcription or translation is needed
with RNP format where the Cas9 and gRNA is delivered as a preformed complex that directly enters
the nucleus. Genomic editing can occur almost immediately in contrast to DNA and mRNA delivery
where Cas9 and gRNA have to be expressed first [104]. Prolonged expression of Cas9 is associated
27 of 90
==PAGE:38==
Alberte Hjortgaard Skriver
Louise Pii Frederiksen
with undesired off-target effects. The transient expression of RNPs and rapid clearance in the cell have
shown to reduce off-target effects while still maintaining editing efficiency when using this for delivery
[69, 105]. Additionally, higher targeting efficiencies have been shown for RNPs and electroporation
with RNPs are therefore often preferred over mRNA and plasmid DNA in T cells [103, 106, 107].
Delivering CRISPR/Cas9 by electroporation, instead of traditional viral transduction, also has the
advantage of enabling a more controlled homogenous expression of the introduced genetic material
compared to a more heterogeneous expression, typically, caused by the semi-random integration of viral
vectors [108]. Currently, electroporation is most commonly used for in vitro and ex vivo applications
[103].
The high voltages required for electroporation pose some risks including cytotoxicity, loss
of cytoplasmic content as well as irreversible alterations to the membrane physiology affecting the
cell viability [56, 109]. in vivo applications are therefore more limited, although not precluded. in
vivo delivery to skin and muscle cells has for example been shown and electroporation is currently
investigated as a tool to enhance DNA uptake following intradermal or intramuscular administration
of DNA vaccines [110]. However, the localised and limited penetration length makes it unsuitable
for delivery to T cells in vivo, for now [56].
Another challenge is the loss of cell viability upon
electroporation which can result in cell death [98, 111].
Electroporation is a promising technique
and despite some limitations, it has become one of the most frequently used strategies for delivering
CRISPR/Cas components to T cells due to its simplicity and effectiveness [98].
1.7
Clinical Application in T cells
A human phase I trial in three patients with advanced, refractory cancer tested the efficacy and
feasibility of of CRISPR engineered T cells. Simultaneous KO of endogenous TCR and the immune
checkpoint molecule PD-1 while introducing a NY-ESO-1 cancer antigen specific transgenic TCR were
monitored to investigate if the cells could persist in vivo. The NY-ESO-1 antigen is a CT antigen that
is not expressed in healthy tissue. The study showed that 40% of the T cells had a single mutation,
20% had a double mutation, and 10% had a triple mutation. Additionally, the engineered T cells
seemed to persist for at least 9 month in circulation and was found trafficking to the site of tumour in
all three patients. NY-ESO-1 antigens were reduced showing an on-target effect of the engineered T
cells. Only mild toxicities were observed, mostly due to lymphodepleting chemotherapy, while chro-
mosomal translocation were detected in vitro however decreasing over time after infusion into the
patients [13].

2.7.1
Genome Extraction
Extraction of genomic DNA was done according to the DNeasy Blood & Tissue Kit from QIAGEN
and the corresponding protocol for cultured cells ”Purification of total DNA from animal blood or
cells (spin-column)”. The T cells were counted on the cell counter (Nucleocounter NC-200, software:
Nucleoview NC-200) and a maximum of 5x106 cells were transferred to eppendorf tubes and centrifuged
at 300 x g for 5 minutes. The pellet were resuspended in 200µL PBS and 20µL Proteinase K was
added. 200µL lysis buffer, Buffer AL (without ethanol), were added to the cells and mixed thoroughly
by pulse-vortexing for 5-10 seconds to obtain a homogeneous solution and incubated for 10 minutes
at 56◦C. After incubation, 200µL ethanol was added to the samples. Again, the samples were pulse-
vortexed to a homogenous mixture. The solution was then transferred to DNeasy Mini spin columns
placed in 2mL collection tubes. The tubes were centrifuged at 6000 x g for 1 minute removing the
ethanol but maintaining the DNA in the filter of the spin columns. The collection tube and flow-trough
were discarded. The spin column were then washed sequentially with 500µL of the wash buffers AW1
and AW2 and centrifuged at 6000 x g for 1 minute and 17000 x g for 4 minutes, respectively. After
each spin the collection tube with flow-trough was discarded. Ethanol residues may interfere with
the subsequent PCR reaction and therefore it was ensured that the membrane of the DNeasy Mini
spin column was dry. Lastly, the DNA was eluted by adding Buffer AE directly onto the DNeasy
membrane. Before centrifugation, the tubes were incubated for 1 minute at room temperature. The
final DNA concentration can be increased by using 100µL Buffer AE, despite reducing the overall
DNA yield a little, and therefore this amount was used instead of 200µL. The extracted DNA can
now be used as template DNA for the PCR reaction.

Results
The results presented in this thesis project are divided into five main sections. The first section is
a proof of concept experiment that shows the utilisation of CRISPR/Cas9 to knockin a short DNA
sequence into T cells. The second section describes the application of the TCR-targeting RNPs and
their targeting specificity for KO of the endogenous TCR. Validation of the RNP efficacy is essential for
the third section, where the RNPs are used for KI of a NY-ESO-1 specific TCR. Different optimisation
steps were performed in order to improve KI efficiency. The fourth section demonstrates the integration
of an additional tumour-specific TCR specific to a Merkel Cell Carcinoma (MCC) neoantigen. Finally,
the last section shows the results of NY-ESO-1 specific scaffold expansion to increase NY-ESO-1 KI
efficiency. All sections are important in order to move towards using CRISPR/Cas9 as a tool for
improving cancer immunotherapy.
3.1
Knockin of EcoRI Restriction Site
The following section shows the KI of an EcoRI restriction enzyme recognition site of six base pairs
in primary human T cells. The sequence introduced is 5’-GAATTC-3’ and is digested by the restric-
tion enzyme EcoRI between G and A (5’-G↓AATTC-3’). The crRNA targets the safe harbour locus
AAVS1. Primers were designed to amplify the site of KI with an expected PCR product size of 1037
kb. After EcoRI digestion the DNA which contains KIs of 5’-GAATTC-3’ will be degraded in two
products with the sizes of 476 bp and 561 bp. The KI efficiency is validated via two methods, PCR
amplification and sanger sequencing.
Following restriction enzyme site KI, products before and after RE digestion were run on agarose
gels. This allowed for easy visualisation of efficient KI, as well as relative quantification of the KI by
measuring band intensity of the digested bands and normalising to the non-digested bands. HDRT
concentration of 50 nM, 100 nM, 500 nM, and 2000 nM were tested. The gels showed a successful
KI across the range of HDRT concentrations, evident by a visual digested band in both biological
replicates (figure 19). The bands at ∼1000 bp are the wild type (WT) DNA without KI whereas the
bands at ∼500 bp represent digested data from successful KIs. The KI bands are wider than the WT
band as the two digestion product sizes (476 bp and 561 bp) are close together. As expected, the
negative control (NC) with non-targeting gRNA does not contain any KI for any of the donors (figure
19).

4
Discussion
TCRs are crucial for an effective anti-tumour response as described in section 1. In this thesis we show
that it is indeed possible to perform CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene integration in primary human T
cells where successful KIs of both small and large genetic elements were accomplished. This emphasises
the potential of improving the efficacy and anti-cancer TCR specific integration for ACT with TCR
gene therapy, thereby, potentially increasing the overall survival of cancer patients.
This further
provides a useful tool for efficiently studying T cell receptor biology in vitro.
4.1
Knockin of EcoRI Restriction Site
Restriction enzyme digestion and Sanger sequencing were used to evaluate the KI efficiency of the
EcoRI restriction enzyme recognition site sequence in AAVS1 in primary human T cells. In both
methods, we saw increased KI efficiency, with increasing HDRT concentrations until a plateau was
reached at 500 nM. The variation in KI efficiencies differed between the two methods. For the optimal
HDRT concentration of 500 nM, restriction enzyme digestion gave a KI efficiency of 45.5% whereas
Sanger sequencing showed an efficiency of 78.3%.
The KI efficacy quantification results were based on the PCR amplification and enzymatic digestion
of the PCR product. The product was digested for 30 minutes (section 2.7.4). However, it can be
discussed whether 30 minutes was enough time to digest the total amount of PCR product with KIs.
Additionally, the amount of enzyme could be varied to optimise the digestion efficiency. Band quan-
tification with ImageJ is based on the light intensity of the pixels in the gel image. As the negative
control sample showed a positive KI efficiency, it indicates that the sensitivity of the band quantifi-
cation method we applied is questionable. The pixels from one lane can possibly affect the pixels
in the other lane and thereby give a false positive result. For comparison of different conditions, it
is therefore also important to run all samples on the same gel to ensure accuracy. Additionally, the
quantification method applied is highly manual when evaluating the light intensity peaks (figure 20)
which could result in personal bias. Figure 19 shows the gels from the PCR runs before and after
enzymatic digestion. The no digestion gels showed unspecific products at 350 bp to 1000 bp. This
could potentially affect the quantification analysis of the digestion gels as the unspecific products also
were located on 500 bp where the digested products were identified in the same size. The PCR was
conducted multiple times to identify the optimal template concentration, PCR cycles, and reagents
without being able to completely get rid of the unspecific products.

The Sanger sequencing output was visualised with the online software DECODR. The PCR product
were sent for Sanger sequencing along with the same primers used for PCR amplification. One of the
most critical limitations of Sanger sequencing is low quality sequences due to primer binding in the
first 15-40 bp and the ability of identifying single base pair differences in segments >900 bp [116].
The KI DNA product has a size 1037 bp which is above 900 bp and therefore might influence the
sequence quality. The overall aim of this experiment was not to identify the exact KI efficiency but
rather identifying an optimal concentration, making the sensitivity of the two methods subordinate.
The methods were used to evaluate the best HDRT concentration which both confirmed an optimal
concentration of 500 nM. The true KI efficiency probably lies between the quantification with ImageJ
and the Sanger estimated KI efficiencies. Additionally, we verified the use of CRISPR/Cas9-mediated
gene integration by electroporation. If the accurate KI efficiency was essential, other methods could
have been used. An expensive but highly accurate method is next generation sequencing which has
increased sensitivity compared to Sanger sequencing [117]. Additionally, the methods used could have
been optimised further to provide better quantifications. ImageJ output could have been improved
by increasing the enzyme concentration and the length of digestion to ensure that all KI DNA was
cleaved. The KI efficiency seen in the negative control could have been subtracted from the KI results
as background noise, thereby leading to a potentially more true KI efficiency. However, it would have
to be tested experimentally what would be the most correct method. We optimised the primer de-
sign for Sanger sequencing, however additional template concentration and primer optimisation could
further improve the precision of the assay. Another possibility was to knockin another DNA sequence
than the EcoRI recognition sequence. For instance a histidine-tag or a GFP could have been integrated
instead and quantified via flow cytometry.
The optimal template concentration cannot be directly translated as the optimal concentration for
transgenic TCR integration. The HDRT of 6 bp is much smaller than the TCR and is probably less
toxic for the cells. The HDRT concentration therefore had to be optimised for the TCR knockin
as well. Our results shows successful KI by using non-viral CRISPR/Cas9-mediated integration and
provides proof of concept of the gene KI tool.

4.2
Endogenous TCR Knockout
It is essential to design effective gRNAs in order to knock in or knock out gene elements by CRISPR/Cas9.
The tracrRNA must bind the Cas9 enzyme and the crRNA should be complementary to the target
DNA and not other targets in the DNA. Additionally, the target site of Cas9 induced DSBs should
include a 5′-NGG-3′ PAM sequence 3 bp upstream of the DSB. Our evaluation of the TRAC and
TRBC gRNAs, tested individually, showed great KO efficiencies of 99.5% and 85.9%. This indicates
that for these gRNA designs, the best gRNA for transgenic TCR KI must be in the endogenous TRAC
locus. Roth et al. [118] showed similar results. They tried to knockin a NY-ESO-1 specific TCR in
the TRAC locus and compared it to TRBC KI. They were able to reach a KI efficiency of 8.6% in the
endogenous TRAC locus whereas the TRBC locus only showed a KI efficiency of 1.0%, emphasising
the use of the TRAC gRNA.
Immunotherapy can be improved by combining anti-cancer strategies like cancer specific TCR gene
editing and checkpoint inhibition. Additionally, the α or β chain of the transgenic TCR can bind
to the α or β chain of the endogenous TCR resulting in mispairing and thereby unknown specificity
of the TCR. This indicates that gene editing of multiple loci by CRISPR/Cas9 in T cells should
be investigated either by KO of the endogenous TCR and/or by KO of checkpoint inhibitors such
as PD-1 simultaneously with transgenic TCR integration.
Chamberlain et al.
[119] showed that
CRISPR/Cas9 could be used to KO PD-1 expression in TIL products with an approximately 87%
reduction in expression on the cell surface. Additionally, no off-target effects were observed which
points out the possibility of using PD-1 KO in combination with anti-cancer specific TCR integration
[119]. We tested TRAC and TRBC gRNAs in combination to investigate the potential of simultaneous
transgenic KI and endogenous KO. TRAC+TRBC gRNA had a KO efficiency of 98.5 %. This was a
higher KO efficiency than TRBC alone and was similar to TRAC KO. However, from these results it
was not possible to determine whether both gRNAs have knocked out the appropriate/respective locus.
This requires further investigations but could be highly relevant for combinations of immunotherapy
strategies. Roth et al. [118] also investigated the KI of NY-ESO-1 specific TCR in TRAC while
simultaneously KO of TRBC. This decreased the KI efficiency from 8.6% to 5.2%, however, additionally
increased the KO as the remaining endogenous TCR where only 9.6% compared to 14.6% in TRAC KI
alone. They also tried to KI the transgenic α chain into TRAC and the transgenic β chain into TRBC
which decreased KI efficiency (3.3%) and increased the endogenous TCR (15.2%). This emphasises
the complexity of gRNA design.

4.3
NY-ESO-1 Specific TCR Knockin
Traditional viral gene integration is associated with high immunogenicity and random gene integration.
Non-viral CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene provides a simple, efficient and low cost delivery strategy for
genome editing. CRISPR/Cas9 designs can potentially reduce off-target effects and provides a solution
to produce safer and more efficient immunotherapy (section 1.6). However, replacing the endogenous
TCR with a new cancer antigen specific TCR is highly complex and requires several optimisation
strategies. We investigated HDRT concentration, Cas targeting sequences in the HDRT, and small
molecule inhibitors for improving KI efficiency.
Based on evaluation of the optimal HDRT concentration considering both KI efficiency and live cell
count, we determined an optimal concentration of 20 nM. Shy et al. [15] investigated the effect of
HDRT concentration on KI efficiency, live cell count, and absolute KI efficiency on a chimeric antigen
receptor (CAR) construct. They showed that the higher concentration, the higher KI efficiency with
the highest KI at 40 nM (10%). However, when reaching this concentration, the KI efficiency started
to decrease. The live cell count was approximately 75% relative to the non-electroporated control
and decreases drastically after 20 nM. The absolute KI efficiency was highest at 20 nM with 200,000
KI positive cells. Overall, they showed that KI efficiency and cellular toxicity was directly correlated
with HDRT concentration. Comparing these results to figure 24, we observed a similar trend for the
KI efficiency, although the highest KI efficiency was seen at 20 nM contrary to their results. The KI
efficiency decreased at 40 nM which could be due to toxicity of the template DNA. Additionally, Shy
et al. inserted a CAR construct, different from our NY-ESO-1 antigen specific TCR which could con-
tribute to the differences as well. The viability was unaffected relative to concentration with a live cell
count of approximately 15% throughout our experiment. A reason for the difference in live cell count
could be due to Shy et al. using PGA polymer which showed increased viability. We observed an abso-
lute KI efficiency of approximately 40,000, however this correlates with the lower live cell count. Our
experiment was conducted on biological replicates and the large SD shown on the figure (three donors)
indicates that there may be biological differences which could also be a reason why they vary from the
previous findings [15]. As a final remark, it should be noted that finding the optimal HDRT concen-
tration is a delicate balance between obtaining high KI efficiencies and maintaining a high cell viability.

To enhance the KI efficiency, we tested the effect of introducing CTS in the HDRTs. The Cas9 has an
added NLS which directs the enzyme to the nucleus. Adding CTS to the HDRT will potentially allow
Cas9 to shuttle the template DNA to the nucleus. However, we did not see any significant difference
in KI efficiency or live cell count between HDRT with or without CTS (Figure 25). Further, HDRT
without CTS showed a significantly higher absolute KI efficiency. This indicates that designing HDRT
with CTS does not provide the expected effect. Similarly Shy et. al [15] did not show any differences in
either KI efficiencies or live cell count with dsDNA HDRT designed with CTS. However, using ssDNA
HDRT designed with CTS showed an impressive KI increase of 35% but simultaneously reduced the
live cell count. The findings by Shy et al. indicates that ssDNA could be interesting to investigate as
HDRT for our KI experiments as well. The HDRT we used was designed as closed circular dsDNA
where CTS is located in closed ends (figure 25). This could potentially affect the binding to the Cas9
enzyme and thereby not provide the desired shuttle effect.
Small molecule inhibitors have been shown to improve the efficiency of HDR-mediated CRISPR KIs.
We tested two different inhibitors, M3814 and TSA, individually and in combination (MT) to evaluate
the effect on KI efficiency and live cell count. M3814 had the highest increase with a KI efficiency of
16.6% hence a 2 fold increase (figure 26). TSA did not seem to increase the KI efficiency compared to
the no inhibitor control sample. Additionally, the combination of TSA and M3814 was only slightly
higher than M3814 alone, indicating that TSA did not enhance the KI further. Shy et al. [15] shows
a similar pattern however with a higher increase in the MT and TSA sample. Chakrabarti et. al [10]
shows that TSA can be used for improving NHEJ in KO experiments which could be a reason why the
KI efficiency did not increase as much as the M3814. TSA promotes open chromatin structure which
can affect both DSB repair pathways and is thereby not restricted to HDR, however, improves mobility
and local concentration of RNPs and HDRT. Combining both M3814, which is a NHEJ inhibitor, and
TSA, which induces open chromatin structure, might provide a synergistic enhanced KI efficiency. It
appeared that the small molecule inhibitors did not affect the live cell count (figure 26) as the no
inhibitor control had a lower or equal cell count. The negative control without HDRT had a higher
live cell count indicating that the HDRT was the reason for the cellular toxicity. Shy et al. [15] shows
that the time span of inhibitor exposure affects the live cell count, decreasing the number of cells if
not removed after 48 hours. Additionally, after 48 hours the KI efficiency did not appear to increase
further. In all experiments presented in this thesis project inhibitors were removed after 24 hours and
further investigation of the optimal inhibitor time exposure and concentration could be interesting to
evaluate for improving the KI efficiency.

4.4
Merkel Cell Carcinoma Specific TCR Knockin
The possibility for CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene integration of tumour specific TCRs with different
cancer antigen specificity are important in immunotherapy as cancer can be highly heterogeneous
within and between cancer types. Two TCRs were tested, one MCC specific TCR with a human
constant region (HC), and one with a murine constant region (MC). The HC TCR had a KI efficiency
of 8.1% which was comparable to the NY-ESO-1 TCR KI with same conditions (6.5%) (figure 27).
However, we only observed 0.3% of the MC TCR KI. This is probably a result of the HDRT design as
the HC TCR and MC TCR are different. HC TCR is based on the endogenous constant region whereas
MC has incorporated the murine constant region and therefore the homology arms are designed differ-
ently and are located further upstream the gene. This might affect gene integration (figure 17 and 18).
We included the murine design, as TCR gene therapies often add a murine constant region in order
to minimise mispairing between the transgenic TCRα or TCRβ chains and the endogenous TCRα
or TCRβ chains and thereby unknown antigen specificities. CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene editing
provides the possibility to knockout the endogenous TCR, as stated earlier, thereby avoiding the use
of murine parts which increase immune-related adverse events. However, complete KO concomitant
with efficient transgenic TCR KI is still challenging and therefore the MC TCRs are still relevant.
Schober et al. [11] investigated mispairing in CRISPR/Cas9-mediated transgenic TCR integration
and showed that editing both the endogenous TCRα and TCRβ chain simultaneously led to accu-
rate TCRαβ pairing. Additionally, the CRISPR/Cas9-mediated KI compared to the traditional viral
transduction showed reduced mispairing, emphasising the promising potential of CRISPR/Cas9 gene
editing. However, concomitant TRAC and TRBC gene editing may decrease the KI efficiency [118].
KI of HC TCR and MC TCR both had low live cell counts with percentages of 5% and 2 % respectively
compared to the non-electroporation controls (figure 27). The HC and MC HDRT is designed with
CTS which might explain low live cell count as similar results was observed in our experiments where
the effect of CTS was investigated (figure 25). The same trend was seen for the absolute KI efficiency.
However, we showed that it was indeed possible to integrate a MCC neoantigen specific TCR.

4.5
NY-ESO-1 TCR Specific Scaffold Expansion
We achieved relatively high KI efficiencies, but observed relatively low total KI cell counts in all
our experiments.
For immunotherapy, large number of cells (> 1010) are needed to produce the
desired anti-cancer effect [120]. In order to use CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene editing for ACT, it is
therefore important to investigate the potential of expanding the transgenic TCR positive T cells.
We performed scaffold-mediated expansion of the NY-ESO-1 TCR specific T cells with and without
inhibitors (figure 29). The initial KI was approximately 8% (figure 26) and with the scaffold expansion
increased with an average of 9% (figure 29). To investigate the increase in absolute KIs the scaffold
stimulated absolute counts were compared to IL-2 stimulated absolute counts and showed that all the
samples had a relative increase with the highest being 140%, emphasising the potential of scaffold-
mediated expansion of cancer specific T cells for ACT. However, this experiment was conducted on a
single healthy human donor and should be repeated with biological replicates to account for individual
differences. Two scaffold-mediated expansions were conducted. The first time, cells were rested for
7 days and showed no expansion (appendix F). This might have been because the cells were not
rested entirely and therefore the resting time was increased to 20 days the second time. However,
approaching day 20 the live count started to rapidly decrease suggesting that the resting time might
have been slightly too long. A resting time between 7 and 20 days may therefore be interesting to
investigate, in order to achieve the highest possible increase in relative and absolute KI efficiency.
Further, we only expanded the cells with scaffolds for 6 days, and it is therefore likely that we would
see bigger gains with a longer scaffold-mediated expansion of the antigen-specific cells.
However,
the TCR specific expansion showed promising result for further optimisation. To reduce the T cell
exhaustion resulting from dynabeads stimulation, different activation methods could be investigated.
Shifrut et al.
[19] showed that activation of T cells with anti-CD3/CD28 beads exhausted the T
cells and thereby prevented the cells from responding to restimulation. However, using plate-bound
anti-CD3/CD28 enabled the ability to maintain cell response during second stimulation [19]. This
underlines the potential of using plate-bound antibodies or alternative primary stimulation techniques
to improve scaffold-mediated expansion and thereby the KI efficiency.

4.6
Future Perspectives
We optimised CRISPR/Cas9-mediated integration of tumour specific TCRs into primary human T
cells and showed promising results for CRISPR/Cas9 as a non-viral genome editing tool. The high-
est relative KI efficiency and live cell counts were observed with an HDRT concentration of 20 nM,
without CTS and using the NHEJ inhibitor M3814. We additionally showed initial indications that
we could further expand the KI T cells with antigen-specific scaffold-mediated stimulations. However,
further improvements are necessary before applying the method in clinical settings. The next steps
should include improvement of live cell count in order to reach a higher absolute KI efficiency and
thereby move towards applying the method for ACT. Shy et al. [15] have shown that using ssDNA
instead of dsDNA as HDRT can help improve the live cell count as dsDNA is more toxic for the cells.
Additionally, they showed that PGA polymers can help stabilise the HDRT, both increasing the KI
efficiency, and the live cell count. We designed our HDRT as ccDNA as Shy et al. showed increased KI
efficiency with this specific design [15]. dsDNA and ccDNA could be interesting to compare to evaluate
the toxic effect of open structure dsDNA versus closed circular dsDNA template and additionally their
KI efficiencies. Furthermore, as described, plate-bound anti-CD3/CD28 could be used to minimise T
cell exhaustion, enhance live cell count and T cell expansion via scaffold stimulation [19]. Finally, if
ssDNA shows increased efficiencies, then CTS could be investigated for KI improvement [15]. Besides
optimising the CRISPR/Cas9-mediated KIs, it is essential to increase the sample size (n) to reach
statistical power as well as repeating the experiments multiple times (> 2) to account for biological
and technical variabilities.
Another interesting aspect for using the engineered T cells in immunotherapy is the ability of the T
cells to elicit an appropriate anti-cancer immune response. This could be investigated via measuring
cytokine production after antigen exposure and killing assays where T cells are incubated with the
specific cancer cell.

5
Conclusion
In cancer immunotherapy there is much focus on creating adoptive cell therapies with high efficacy,
in improved and efficient ways.
In this thesis project we have presented optimised conditions for
CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene integration of transgenic TCRs in primary human T cells. We have de-
termined the optimal ccDNA HDRT concentration, and shown that inhibition of the NHEJ pathway
improves KI efficacy without compromising live cell counts. We have further shown that our protocol
can be used for KI of both already published TCRs (NY-ESO-1) as well as novel MCC-neoantigen
specific TCRs identified in the Sine Reker Hadrup laboratory. We were able to increase the absolute
numbers of NY-ESO-1 antigen specific T cells.
Clinical application of CRISPR/Cas9 systems still have some limitations to overcome and further
optimisation is necessary to ensure higher relative and absolute KI efficiencies, improved live cell
count, and decreased off target effects.
Our optimisation of CRISPR/Cas9 tumour specific TCR
KIs is part of the first steps toward improving immunotherapy with TCR gene therapy and shows
promising results for decreasing cancer-related deaths in the future.
