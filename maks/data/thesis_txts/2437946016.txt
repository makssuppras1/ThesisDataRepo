Abstract
The construction sector is a macro industry that involves many diﬀerent aspects of
construction. It is one of the driven forces of the economies around the world and
associates millions of entities and people. It is even the most diﬀused with one with
the most diﬀerent practices and regulations according to the geography, climate and
nations. On the environmental aspects, it is responsible for at least of the 31% of
the entire CO2 emissions worldwide.
In the recent years, the public awareness about the climate change in connection
with the world resource depletion is increasing the attention and demand for creating
sustainable societies for the preservation of the environment and the future society.
The circular economy of the products, the recyclability, and the re-use of the already
manufactured materials are becoming integrated with the construction industry. In
this optic, the Design for Disassembling principles aim to increase the focus on the
design aspect of the building, that has not only to design the construction of the
building but also designing the best solution for the dismantling of it at the end of
its life.
The goal of this thesis is to investigate the end of life alternatives of the most
impactful materials of a case study oﬃce building, and to evaluate, applying the
principles of design for disassembling, the environmental impact credits of module
”D” of the DGNB certiﬁcation system.
The preliminary analysis with the software LCAByg highlighted the three major
contributors in the bitumen sheets used for sealing in the roof, in the triple glazing
of the windows and in the concrete in the internal walls of the structure.
The
evaluation of the end of life scenarios was not able to provide a clear strategy for
disposing of the bitumen, while it highlighted as the best environmental solution
the re-using of the triple glazing and the recycling of concrete using the recycling
potential method.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Sustainability concepts in the modern society are taking a more predominant role in
all the sectors, from agriculture industry to constructions. Due to the many diﬀerent
perspectives and applications of these concepts, there is not a unique deﬁnition of
sustainability.
One of the most diﬀused deﬁnition of a sustainable product is a
product that meets the necessities of the present and the future society. In particular,
to be sustainable, a product has to meet the environmental aspects of the present
with a focus on the resources of the future.
In the case of the construction sector, to achieve the sustainability, the building
nowadays has to be designed as an ”integrated, planned and well-managed project
in all the processes of the life of the building” [1]. To achieve these goals, the con-
struction sector is moving forward many revolutions in the practices of construction,
management, design, quality of the work, material, and maintenance.
1.1
Environmental impacts of the construction
The production of the construction materials is one of the most energy demanding
and environmental impactful stages in the whole life cycle of the building. In par-
ticular, the production of concrete and steel is one of the most relevant, reaching
1
==PAGE:12==
considerable levels up to 10% of the entire production of CO2 of a country [2]. Some
esteems aﬃrm that the environmental impact from the construction sector world-
wide could reach up to the 31% of the total emissions of CO2 in a yearly base[3].
Even though the improvements in the eﬃciency of the production, the construction
sector remains one of the biggest contributor to the CO2 emissions, either in the
construction phase of the buildings, either in the demolishing phase, reaching the
level to represent 1/3 of the total solid waste stream in the world [4].
The enormous ﬂow of material involved in all the processes of construction and
demolition wastes (CDW) has a high embedded environmental impact. Every mate-
rial has a potential energy that remains intrinsically connected to the substance and
it derives from the manufacturing of it. In the majority of the cases, this embedded
energy is not used at all after the ﬁnal usage stage of the material which ends in a
landﬁll area. In some cases, this energy is converted into thermal due to incineration
and then used for other purposes for the creation of heat or electricity. This process
is all the cases releasing large quantities of CO2.
1.2
Shift of the impacts
In the recent years, the research on the construction materials had many steps
forward, producing better performing materials that can last for more. Insulation
and windows are more eﬃcient allowing to achieve high level of energy savings and
as a consequence cost. This important focus on the performance of the materials
had also changed the compositions of the materials used in general. It is, in fact,
more and more common to use composite materials, re-engineered materials, and
hybrid materials to achieve these low standards of consumptions. All these changing
in the way of construing modern buildings is shifting also the environmental impacts
of the building. Back in 10-20 years from now, the phase of the building where the
majority of the impacts were from was the usage stage of the building [3]. It is in fact
very energy demanding and polluting to produce heat, electricity and clean water.
2
==PAGE:13==
Moreover, these necessities are constant for the entire lifetime expectancy of the
building which could be variable from 50 to 80-100 years. If previously the impacts
from an average Danish house were approximately 60% deriving from the usage
stage of the house, now the majority of the impacts derives from the construction
and the disposal of it at the end of the use [3] [5].
1.3
End of Life options
Because of this shift of the most important environmental impacts, the study of
the End of Life (EoL) is becoming more relevant in the scientiﬁc research because
it takes a higher share of the impacts of the building. Especially in the last years,
issues like disposals of materials, landﬁlling, incineration, recycling, reusing and in
general circular economy are rising in attention from the media and the governments.
The perspective is changing with a more focus on the future generations and all the
eﬀects that our society is producing now with eﬀects that will be visible only in the
future. The Life Cycles Assessments (LCA) documents contain all the life phases
of the product to have a complete analysis of the env. impacts and resources used.
In particular, for the construction sector, the life phases of the building can be
summarized in 4 categories: Construction (A1-A3 + A4-A5), Use stage (B1-B7),
End of Life (C1-C4) and beyond End of Life (D) (DS/EN 15978)[6].

1.4
Circular economy
The waste management is not only a more eﬃcient way to treat the material in
construction but is a complex system that could provide many beneﬁts from the
environmental, social and economic point of view. More buildings nowadays are
facing a large degradation and urgent need of renovations/maintenance to remain
attractive in the market. Initiatives for a better management of the wastes could
5
==PAGE:16==
represent new research possibilities and new workplaces for the job market. These
could have great gains also from the welfare of the societies reducing the exposure
of chemicals and providing a healthier environment. Many countries in Europe are
following the guidelines of the European Commission in matter of Resource eﬃciency
and recycling implementing gradually a circular economy in the diﬀerent aspects of
construction.
The impacts of an eﬃcient resource/waste management from a circular economy
could be extremely relevant only with the close collaboration of all the supply chain
involved in the sector of construction.
Because of the many parties involved in
the various aspects of the building sector, it is fundamental for a good synergy to
increase the resourcefulness available.

1.5
EPD
One of the main issues that are still under development is the ability to compare the
environmental performances of diﬀerent products. It is in fact not easy to compare
diﬀerent manufacturing processes, energy sources, and relative emissions. It is also
not easy to study the future emissions because of the long-term time frame and
associated uncertainties. The diﬃculty to have a homogeneous method of product
declaration is slowly being solved by an accepted Environmental Product Decla-
ration (EPD) system which is adopted by the majority of the building materials
producers in Europe. This system includes all the processes involved in the manu-
facturing of the products based on the norms ISO 14025 and EN 15804 [9].
The Environmental Product Declaration is fundamental for the studies of Life
Cycle Assessments (LCA) and in general to compare diﬀerent products. Having a
unique system of identiﬁcation allows also to have a reference base to which test
the performance of the various components of the buildings. The EPD is also at
the base of various environmental certiﬁcates as LEED (Leadership in Energy and
Environmental Design) or DGNB [10][11].

1.6
Cities as mines for the future
Lifetime expectancy is often a key concept in deep relation with the principles of
recyclability, circular economy, and construction in general. Every substance, mate-
rial or component is designed and constructed to have a limited time period where
it maintains it’s speciﬁc characteristics. After this period that could end because
of time, exploitation or simple use, the component terminates it’s usage phase and
enters in the disposal phase. In the context of buildings, the materials are more
intended as components, and their time expectancy varies a lot from service to
service. The entire construction can be intended as a complex product made by
diﬀerent components, and like every object, it has a life expectancy.
In the years 70’-80’ in all the world it was registered a boom in the constructions
both residential and industrial. At the present, these buildings are becoming old,
too energy demanding, in need of deep renovation and in most of the cases too
expensive to renovate. The most common solution, also from the economic aspect,
is the demolition and the construction of more sustainable ones.In many of these
cases, the majority of the products are treated as wastes and represent just a cost
for the company to remove and dispose of.
The supplying of the raw materials is part of a large-scale issue that needs to
be analyzed also in a large scale to be solved for a sustainable development of our
societies [UN sustainable goals] [7][12]. In relation with the source of raw material,
since the 20th century, it was registered a high increase of material extraction which
in many cases borders on savage extraction and depletion of natural resources. From
Fig: 1.3 it is possible to note a extensive demand of construction minerals. This
trend is continuing to grow, driven especially by the emergent markets of Asia and
the Paciﬁc zones.
With the trend of the population growth connected with the
middle-class growth, the demand for new construction could increase of 70% in the
next 10 years [13]

The focus on the wastes as resources represents now and especially in the future
a fundamental supply of resources. The resource deﬁciency is one of the intercon-
nected issues in the 17 sustainable developments goals from the UN [7]. This topic
is at the base of goals like ”Responsible consumption and production”, ”Sustainable
cities and communities” and ”Life on land/water”. The embedded resources that
are already present in the cities and society are opportunities for new use of these
large quantities that reach volumes of thousands of m3 per person (Fig. 1.3). This
new concept of reusing the already made components is transforming the cities in
materials mines of the future of construction [14]. Some initiatives to use these
unexploited resources comes from the European Commission’s Buildings as Mate-
rial Banks (BAMB), which started a 9 million eproject in 2015 with the goal to
increment the awareness between the professionals in the sector, promote circularity
within the built environment and reuse of these materials avoiding virgin resources
[7][9][14].

To obtain a transition in the society and the construction practices, it is impor-
tant to change the approach towards the wastes and to start to take advantage of
the resources that are already present around us. Buildings, instead of being a con-
stant, ﬁxed standard in our society are becoming the temporal and dynamic storage
of building materials and components. This shift to the perception of constructions
is revolutionizing the necessities of the parties involved, opening new questions and
new opportunities. New requirements from the environment emissions, new design
and digital processes that in most of the cases are lacking competences are usually
the most challenging obstacles that are arising with the new perception of construc-
tion sector.

2.1
Design for Disassembling
In relation with the concepts of recycling, circular economy and reduce the wastes
and especially with the goals of the UN on the journey for a sustainable future, the
concept of Design for Disassembling comes relevant for the topic of this thesis.
The deﬁnition of Design for Disassembling (DfD) is not unique and varies according
to the speciﬁcs of every context where these are applied. One of the most common
deﬁnition refers to it as ”the inverse processes of construction” (Design for Disas-
sembly and Deconstruction - Challenges and Opportunities [15]). In the broad sense
of the deﬁnition, the DfD is ”a practice to ease the deconstruction processes and pro-
cedures through planning and design. Deconstruction is the process of demolishing
a building but restore the use of the demolished materials”.
In this context, the Design for Disassembling is a natural evolution of the pro-
cesses of design and construction that are applied since the beginning of the design
phase. The DfD is for necessity assessed in the early design for the building. As
a logical consequence of the composition of the buildings, the DfD is developed in
layers (see Fig. 2.1). The design for dissembling takes inspiration from the modern
processes of construction: The building is erected by the assembling of components
11
==PAGE:22==
that are prefabricated and then mounted in the construction site. Similarly to the
construction, the DfD is the reverse of the construction stage and it is dismantled
component by component. The entire process of dismantling the construction has
the purpose of reducing at the minimum the use of energy/wastes following the

2.2
Frequent issues of Design for Disassembling
There are many diﬀerent obstacles in implementing the Design for Disassembly
strategies according to speciﬁc factors that vary from building to building or from
component to component. In the following topics, there are summarized the most
relevant: Quality of the materials, level of recycling, the data management in con-
nection with reliability, lack of data, and the responsibility for dismantling.
• Quality of the materials One of the most frequent issues about design for
disassembling is the quality of various components after the dismantling or
disposal of the building. In many cases, the processes of dismantling are not
complete in all the aspects or lack of details. These generate bad management
of the disposal and are transposed in the loss of quality of the material. The
alteration of the quality of the components generates a loose of performance
and the impossibility to reuse the component for the same function.
This
is the case for example of structure component, usually made of concrete or
wood. On the other case, if the quality of the materials remains consistent, the
material is more likely reusable or easy to recycle in another product system.
13
==PAGE:24==
• Equivalent/Down cycling Another common issue that is linked with the
quality of the materials, arises after the full dismantling of all the components
of the building. There are usually two options available for recycling for every
component: recycling maintaining the same class of object, or down-cycling
the component. In the ﬁrst case, the new component will have the same or
equivalent function and the same properties as the starting material.
The
down-cycling option instead provides the use of the material as a low-level
category of the original one. In this last case, the physical, thermal or chemical
properties are one level below of the starting category. An example of the
down-cycling recycle id the concrete: at the starting point, the material is
designed to have speciﬁc strength properties. After the recycling process, due
to the impossibility to guarantee the same physical properties in the second
product, the concrete is down-cycled in another system with less demand of
physical qualities, for example, pavement or similar functions.
• Data reliability From Fig. 1.2 and Fig. 2.1, one of the details that can be
noticed is the numerous level of interaction in the supply chain of a circular
economy. The many passages and parties involved could cause a depletion or
a mismanagement of the information relative to every material or component.
The loss of traceability of data in a long-term frame could end up with the
inability of recycling the components of the wrong process with the consequent
reduction of recycling potential. The problem of reliability of the information
along the supply chain of construction is a cross-argument between many other
diﬀerent topics. Transparent communication and management of information
are one of the frontiers of the 21st century but there is not a unique approach
and there are still many systems used in the sector. These could cause in many
examples a loss in the value chain of the construction supply and a general
discourage towards the implementation of a circular economy.
• Lack of data As mentioned above and introduced in section 1.5, the procuring
of relevant and reliable data is one of the most common issues. The problem
arises in the moment of esteem the performance of the material with the
14
==PAGE:25==
consequent classiﬁcation of the material. To obtain a realistic and reliable
overview of the realization of a component, it is necessary to have precise
data about the material information. The Environmental Product Declaration
(EPD) is in this context the base for the correct assessment of the components
and relative End of Life processes. The diﬀerent minimum requirements are
in many cases representing a limitation and a source of uncertainties in the
technological representation of the Life Cycle Assessment.
• Who has to dismantle Because of the always more complexity of the build-
ings, also the dismantling is rising in complexity and competences.
If the
in-charged company has not received the adequate materials for the correct
dismantle, the eﬀectiveness of the entire dismantling will be aﬀected. The
same stands for the actual people if they have not access to special tools of
adequate training. The DfD should therefore also assess these in the moment
of design the construction.
2.3
Strategies and good practices of DfD
Alongside with the obstacles to implementing the Design for Disassembling strate-
gies, there are also some procedures for the DfD. In the guide ”Design for Disassem-
bly in the built environment: a guide to closed-loop design and building” there are
listed several guidelines about a correct DfD [16]. Below there are reported some of
the most valuable.
• Shift the design to a diﬀerent perception of design. Many business models
are in fact shifting their business from a linear system based on products to
a more interconnected branch of services. This change of services has to be
reﬂected also in the design of the construction to obtain an interactive and
dynamic building. Also, the time-frame is changing from short unique service
to a more adaptable long-term frame.
15
==PAGE:26==
• Simplify the structure and the components as like as the materials used. In
many cases, the construction is made of complex systems of components and
connections that result in an equally complex system to dismantle or maintain
functional. The corresponding can be applied to the material used. The trend
in construction is to use more complex components made of diverse layers of
engineered materials at high performance. In many cases these result diﬃcult
to recycle. Standard components are in this way the ones that have the highest
possibility of being reused or recycled.
• The designer has in this sense to support the focus on re-usability and recy-
clability of the components. The more value on these sustainability practices
could lead to a more integrated circular economy between producers of mate-
rials and designers.
• The value of the materials could also be aﬀected by a more extensive DfD.
More available resources from the already built environment in our cities,
could reduce the cost of the construction and in the meantime cut-down the
wastes (section 1.6). The actual trends for the construction sector and the
resource supply suggest a net increase in the cost of the natural resources.
The recycled/reused materials could represent soon an economically feasible
alternative to the virgin materials [13].
• The decision process should adapt in parallel with the previous arguments.
A more shared strategy on design processes and a consequential waste manage-
ment should involve also more decision makers. A strengthened collaboration
with the End of Life entities could beneﬁt the entire design process avoiding
energy wastes and especially time wastes. The stakeholders, from the client to
the surrounding inhabitants have beneﬁts from a more eﬃcient disposal and
an improved recycling of the building.
• Discourage bad habits. It is one of the most simple but valuable implemen-
tations for DfD. Penalize the use of hazardous materials or the not state-of-art
work.
The use of material from responsible sources could bring economic
16
==PAGE:27==
advantages. In general, the desertion of not sustainable practices is more ben-
eﬁcial for the broad society and perception of our limited resources.
This
psychological barrier is one of the biggest barriers to the creation of a more
resilient future.

Open/closed loops
In the context of recycling, the issue of predicting the future use of the material
arises. As stated in section 2.9, the outcomes are diﬀerent according to the chosen
system boundaries. The recycling available options depend also on the future prod-
uct system. The recycled material can be used for the same purpose of its original
scope (closed loop) or can enter in a diﬀerent product system (open loop). Example
of this is the recycled metal: the plastic bottle can be recycled again in the plastic
bottle or can represent a percentage in a diﬀerent plastic-made object.
Open loops are the most diﬃcult to assess in the LCAs because of the high
number of uncertainties. There is no constant information in forecasting the future
use of the material in diﬀerent scenarios. Therefore, the closed loops are the most
preferable because of the easiness and reliability of the studies of them. Fig. 2.7.

Energy supply
The energy supply market is one of the biggest and crucial in the future for its
implication in the development of a country. Because of its importance and diﬃculty
to predict, this argument divides the scientiﬁc sector in many aspects. The diﬃcult
to predict the long-term energy supply with a low level of uncertainty, especially
because of the inﬁnite variables that could inﬂuence the forecast.
To assess the emissions factor for the electricity and district heating, the analysis
takes into account two main documents about the energy supply until 2050 (New
emissions factors for electricity and district heating/COWI)[20] and (Energy sce-
narios/Danish Energy Agency)[21]. The scenario after the year 2050 assumes that
the projected emissions could remain constant because of the technology limits.
Electricity has diﬀerent sources of production with diﬀerent degrees of eﬃciency.
To eases the analysis, the eﬃciency of the power plants is assumed to be constant.
To obtain an average value of production, diﬀerent sources have been considered
equal with a diﬀerent percentage of eﬃciency. The diﬀerent emissions have been
considered only on the base of the ”fuel” used and not the diﬀerent power plant.
The main complication from the energy point of view is the prediction of the
emissions in the future. In the present time, the information about the emissions

==PAGE:36==
can be considered reliable. The analysis of the processes instead, is based on the
construction in the present time but involves the maintenance and the dismantling
in the future.
The software LCAByg is based on the forecast provided by the
agency COWI (New emissions factors for electricity and district heating/COWI)[20]
and it automatically base the emissions according to the year of execution in a
dynamic approach. The modeling through the software OpenLCA of the alternative
scenarios for the End of Life, instead, bases the emissions of the energy supply on
the present calculation. This hybrid assumptions between dynamic and constant
emissions bring intrinsically imprecision and uncertainties of the forecasts of the
environmental emissions of the analysis.
2.9
Allocation and comparison of buildings
One of the main issues of an LCA study is the diﬃculty to compare objects. To
have comprehensible results from an LCA, it is necessary to set some references
points to which compare the results. The evaluation is eﬀective only if it is in corre-
lation with an equivalent system to which compare the performance obtained. The
reference of a product to another is possible only if the functional unit is equiva-
lent. The same principle is applied to the evaluation of diﬀerent products. In the
majority of the cases, to make two products comparable, some assumptions or sub-
stitutions have to be taken. These allocation practices allow comparing two diﬀerent
processes/products assuming to substitute them with an equivalent known process.
In the construction sector, the allocation method is widespread to compare build-
ings. Because of the high complexity of the components, materials, and processes
used, it is very diﬃcult to obtain equivalent functional units. The ISO 14044 stan-
dard provides the guidelines to perform the allocation [22]. In the speciﬁc intention
of this thesis, the problem of allocation of the beneﬁts for recycling/reusing arises.
There are no clear guidelines of how to account the environmental impacts from the
module C3/C4 and especially module D with the recycling beneﬁts.
26
==PAGE:37==
The approach to the topic has opposite point of views toward the share of the
environmental beneﬁts. Some methodologies privilege the original system (100:0
systems) or on the contrary privileging the new product system (0:100 systems).
The hybrid is instead accounting the beneﬁts in equal shared proportions dividing
the beneﬁts into 50% to each product system.
The dispute on the correct account method of the End of Life is relevant for all
the policies connected with the recycling practices and also Design for Disassembling.
The potential beneﬁts can reduce of signiﬁcant percentages the whole environmental
impacts of the building and therefore giving access to high ration of classiﬁcation of
buildings. In additions, the behaviours toward recycling is inﬂuenced by the sharing
of the beneﬁts. All the future regulations and standards adopted can be inﬂuenced
by the diﬀerent sharing.

3.3.2
LCIA and allocation method
The Life Cycle Impact Assessment method for calculating the emissions of the prod-
uct is the keystone for the results. For the purpose of the analysis, it was chosen
to use the CML baseline 2011 updated to the version 4.4 (2015). The method will
produce the characterized results in the 11 impact categories. In the process of
creation of the Inventory for the analysis, it is also necessary to choose the desired
allocation method. Ecoinvent oﬀer two major systems: the APOS method and the
cut-oﬀmethod.
”The APOS method (Allocation at the point of Substitution) is an allocation
approach that uses expansions of product systems to avoid allocating within treat-
ment systems” [28]. This method is in an overall approach an aggregation of dif-
ferent data-set. It is meant to provide average data in the the case of shortage of
representativeness of the processes.
The cut-oﬀSystem model instead is based on the recycling content of the ma-
terial. The approach is designed to increase the responsibility of the producer of the
material up to it’s disposal. The eventual credits from recycling are not attributed
to the manufacturer (primary system) but to the recycled system (secondary sys-
tem) [29]. This allocation method eases the collection of process ﬂows and especially
the account of the impacts. All the recycling processes are border free and can be
34
==PAGE:45==
assigned to ﬁrst or second product systems without risk of double counting.

3.5.1
Bitumen
The material bitumen is mostly used in the construction sector as a sealant from
water and atmospheric agents. It is usually transported as rolls of sheets that are
unrolled in the building site. The most common application is in sealing the roof
41
==PAGE:52==
creating a waterproof layer to protect the insulation/structural material.
In the present case study, the bitumen sheets are applied in combination with a hot
glue that seals the junctions between rolls. Because of its origins from petroleum, the
bitumen sheets have a high environmental impact. Moreover, it is expected that the
technological lifetime requires frequent substitutions to ensure the water tightness.
The standard operative period is 20 years. The material research is thriving and
increasing the heat and atmospheric agents resistance, raising the time expectancy
to 25 years and even more [32][33]. The recycling content from one system to another
is remaining constant in the closed recycling loops (80%) as like the quality of the
product recycled by assumption. In the open loops, the recycling content is 10%
lower in the passage from one typology of product to another one (from bitumen
sheets to mastic asphalt) due to the changes of the physical properties. The reusing
scenario is not taken into account due to the limits of the connections of the product
to the structure. The bitumen sheets are glued to it, and this results impossible to
re-use the product into another system.
To model the End of Life scenarios of bitumen, 5 options will be taken into
account:
• Reference scenario The reference scenarios is the incineration of the bitumen
sheets at the end of the service lifetime. During the process, the heat generated
will be included as avoid product to produce electricity and heat for domestic
heating. The caloriﬁc value of bitumen (heating) is assumed to be 38 MJ/kg,
while the conversion of caloriﬁc value for electricity production is assumed to
be 17,11 kWh/kg [34].
• Recycling into mastic + incineration In this scenario the bitumen is re-
cycled into mastic asphalt for road production. The recycling percentage is
considered at 70% of the mass of bitumen sheet. The remaining 30% is dis-
mantled as the incineration reference scenario with relative heat and electricity
generation. The recycling allocation method is the cut-oﬀmethod where the
full recycled process is substituting the mastic production.
42
==PAGE:53==
• Recycling into bitumen + incineration The scenarios resemble the pre-
vious case but instead of being recycled into mastic asphalt, the bitumen is
recycled in a closed-loop into bitumen again.
• Recycling through recycling potential method in open loop The bi-
tumen sheets are recycled into mastic asphalt through the recycling potential
allocation method. The formula used to assess the Environmental Footprint
(EF) is the eq. 2.1 at page 28. In this scenario, the recycling percentage is
70% in the recycling steps. The material that is not recycled will be disposed
in incineration processes and as material landﬁll.
• Recycling through recycling potential method in closed loop This
scenario takes the same assumptions and allocation method of the previous
scenario, but the bitumen sheets are recycled again into bitumen sheet. The
percentage of recycling is assumed to be 80% because the eﬃciency is higher
when recycling a material in the same material in a closed loop.
The modeling of the alternative scenarios is done in OpenLCA software. The
following steps describe the recycling processes of bitumen:
• Dismantling of bitumen sheets The bitumen sheets are removed from the
building structure. The process presents diﬃculties because of the tightness
of the material to the structure. It is therefore a long process that has high
risks of damaging the material impeding a direct reuse of the material.
• Collection and sorting The material is treated into the sorting plant and
prepared for the recycling processes. The dirtiness accumulated in years of
service is eliminated through high air pressure jets.
• Melting The bitumen sheets are heated until complete melt. Catalysts and
reﬁning passages will remove hazardous chemicals.
• Recycling The liquid mix is transformed as mastic asphalt which is the co-
hesion component of the road.

4.3
Discussion
The interpretation of a Life Cycle Assessment contains always uncertainties related
to the methodology, the data accuracy and the reliability of the results. The goal
and scope deﬁnition oﬀer a support the interpretation of the results (see paragraph
3.3.1 at page 33). Being the goal of the analysis to investigate into alternative End
of Life options in the dismantling of a case study oﬃce building, the models take
into account particularly the module ”C” and especially module ”D” of the DGNB
certiﬁcation system [11]. Therefore, the results presented in the previous section
focus on one part of the impact assessment, without taking into account diﬀerent
scenarios for the manufacturing processes and the usage stage of the components.
The magnitude of the results changes in the various impact categories and in the
diﬀerent materials. The assessment is done using a characterized impact assessment
method, which generates the emissions to the environment in the diﬀerent categories.
65
==PAGE:76==
The typology of the outcomes doesn’t allow to sum results of diﬀerent categories
because of the diﬀerent unit of measure. With the present analysis, it is only possible
to compare the performance in each identical impact category (GWP to GWP or
Ozone depletion - Ozone depletion).
4.3.1
Bitumen
Due to the intrinsic characteristic of the material, the bitumen has high emissions
in terms of CO2 eq. The trend reveals that the bitumen sheets don’t have a high
impact in a single life cycle product, but in the full life cycle perspective of the case
study building, they are frequent substituted and the accumulated emissions end to
be the driving once in the component. From the impact assessment of the bitumen
(ﬁg. 4.7) it is possible to note a similar trend in the diﬀerent scenarios.
The cut-oﬀrecycling of bitumen to mastic asphalt (yellow) is generally the
worst performing scenario in quite all the impact categories except for the Eutroph-
ication Potential (EP) where it resembles the average performance of the other sce-
narios. In the category of fossil fuel depletion, the emissions of the open-loop(yellow)
are in fact 67% higher than the closing-loop (orange).
Similar to the yellow scenario, the reference scenario (light blue) has a constant
trend in the impact categories, ending to be one of the best solutions for the disposal
of bitumen. On the contrary, in the fossil fuel depletion, the scenario has an inversion
in the outcomes and it passes from a credit to an emission performance, even though
it remains the best solution between the cut-oﬀrecycling scenarios (see ﬁg. 4.7).
The trend is also inverted in comparison with the reference scenario of the other two
materials under examination. The bitumen in the disposal accounts for crediting
while the other reference scenarios represent the highest emissions in their categories.
Analyzing the Recycling Potential scenarios (green and blue), the outcomes
of both of them are similar in all the categories, ending to be diﬀerent only in the
fossil fuel depletion. From the graph 4.10, the progress of the distribution of the

emissions of the two alternatives tend to reduce the emissions until the ﬁnal product
systems that equal to result as credit. This last disposal probably ends to be so high
crediting because it is made mostly by recycled material and in the ﬁnal disposal,
all the energy produced by incineration is converted to avoid products. The results
of the 4 product systems are then summed together ending with a credit for the
closed-loop scenario in the ADPf category. The ﬁnal disposal of the bitumen/mastic
asphalt ends in a very high crediting, almost pairing with the emission in the ﬁrst
virgin material manufacturing.
In a overall synthesis, the reference scenario (light blue) is the best perform-

4.3.2
Triple glazing
Analyzing the behavior of the emissions of the triple glazing material, it is possible
to see how the emissions in all the impact categories are performing in similar ways
except for the reference scenario. The material is particularly suitable for recycling
into the glass again forming closed-loop recycling.
Even though the easiness or
recycling, the material is not maintaining the same level of purity in every recycling
step like the aluminum recycle for example [19]. This limitation is overcome by the
easiness of re-using the material into another production system without passing
through the recycling steps of sorting, crushing and melting again. The triple glazing
analysis is considering only the mineral material of the glazing and is excluding all
the frame material (wood/aluminum/PVC).
The reference scenario is the only one that out-stands from the others, simi-
larly to the reference scenario of concrete. In the process, there are no avoids of any
kind due to products or energy. In the scenario, the production is assumed to start
from virgin material and terminates to be landﬁll after the usage stage of 20 years.
In most of the categories, the diﬀerence between the other scenarios reach emissions
of 330% more.
The trend of the cut-oﬀrecycling (yellow), is instead mainly inﬂuenced by
the 80% of recycling of the glass that turns into avoiding process of triple glazing
manufacturing. The modeling of this alternative EoL resulted to be particularly
challenging because all the recycling processes were also involving the procurement
of the material. This issue was solved with the substitution of the recycling process
with the energy consumption of an electric furnace [35].
From the recycling scenario it was expected to perform in higher emissions in
comparison with the re-using scenario (grey). The two cut-oﬀalternative have
very similar outcomes in all the impact categories but the re-using performs on
average the 16% less. The modeling of the re-using scenario was done with the same
processes as the recycling one, except for the melting process that is not accounted.
68
==PAGE:79==
That 16% is, therefore, the sharing percentage of the process of recycling instead of
re-using the triple glazing glass.
Particularly relevant is to observe the emissions of the recycling potential
scenario (green). This alternative was the most suitable for accounting the emissions
in the bitumen material, while in the triple glazing this scenarios behaves very
similarly to the cut-oﬀmethods. From the ﬁg. 4.14 where the distribution of the
category is presented, it is possible to see how similar are the recycling steps and
the ﬁnal disposal. The ﬁrst production by itself accounts for the 75% of the entire
emissions. Also, the other categories have the same trend from the production until
the disposal. The numerical results can be found in tab. A.8 and A.9 in appendix.
From the last step of the ﬁnal disposal, it is also possible to see how the landﬁll
emissions do not inﬂuence so much the entire emissions. This can be explained by
the choice of landﬁll of the glass. In fact, the process has relatively low emissions
because it is an inert disposal and does not require particular processes to be treated.
In a overall analysis of the triple glazing material, the worst performing sce-
nario is the reference one (blue), while the re-using is the most suitable (grey). The
outcomes from the recycling potential scenario validate the considerations of the
high emissions from the production of triple glazed. It also explains the high im-
pacts in the reference scenario. At every substitution in fact, the triple glazing is
manufactured from the virgin material and this leads to very high emissions.
In the application of Design for Disassembling, the window components rep-
resent a perfect example of how the material can be design to be easy to assem-
ble/disassembled. Due to the frequent substitution, the beneﬁts of ease the process
of maintenance could bring very high beneﬁts to the overall impacts of the compo-
nent. Factors like speed of the process, cost, time consumption and others are not
considered in this analysis but could inﬂuence a lot the total emissions in the process
of maintenance of this component. All these aspects could decrease the emissions
of the re-using scenario, while in the meantime, increasing the recycling one. Even
the quality of the recycled product was not taken into account. It was assumed for

simplifying the calculations that the recycled glass would maintain the initial char-
acteristics, even if the actual practices register a decrease of the ﬁnal quality of the
material. It would therefore necessary to increase the complexity of the processes
with further extra emissions.
4.3.3
Concrete
The third component of emissions is the ”internal wall”, mostly composed of re-
inforced concrete for stabilizing the entire structure.
The material is treated as
simple concrete, while in the modeling of recycling it is treated as reinforced con-
crete. To ease the analysis, the steel bars were not considered. The current practice
for concrete recycling involves also the steel recycling. To limit the variables of the
material, these aspects were not taken into account.
The reference scenario (light blue) is the one with more emissions from the
general overview in ﬁg. 4.15. The scenario is based on the cradle-to-cradle analysis
and therefore the production from the virgin material is also included. Because of
that, the scenario does not include any credit from avoided products/processes and
therefore results in the most emitting.
The recycling (yellow) and re-using (gray) cut-oﬀscenarios, as like in the
triple glazing case, behave in similar ways. The recycling of concrete into concrete
is done by avoid production into other concrete.
The recycling process, as said
above, takes into account also the separation/sorting and crushing of the reinforced
concrete. The re-suing scenario instead is modeled on the assumption of the use of
heavy construction machines, in the speciﬁc with a with a diesel crane with power
over 74.57 kW for high load factor (see tab. A.4). The time used for the dismantling
and mounting operation is of the concrete elements is assumed to be 100h of work.
This assumption is based only on personal assumption. The standard practice of
the construction sector could conﬁrm or upset completely this value. On average,
the re-using scenario reduces the impacts of a 9%.
70
==PAGE:81==
Also, the recycling potential scenarios have similar trends between each other.
The open-loop which recycles concrete into gravel (green) is the highest in com-
parison with the closed-loop of concrete recycled into concrete (dark blue). With
congruent results, the open-loop represent the higher emissions. The recycling of
concrete to gravel represent in fact a down-cycling of the quality of the material.
The gravel production is also lower emitting because of the easiness of the processes
and the more rough grain of the material. Therefore, the potential avoided emissions
are lower than the potential avoided emissions of the concrete production.
In a general examination, the reference scenario is the one with more emission
due to the null avoids, while the recycling potential method of concrete to concrete
is the more suitable option. Nevertheless, the uncertainties factors intrinsic to the
concrete long life recycling span turn the results very diﬃcult to predict in such high
time frame. The present model does not take into account the external variables as
quality, atmospheric exposure, technological construction practices and etc.
The standard practice of concrete recycling suggests recycling the material into
gravel for various reasons, most of them related to the recycling processes and the
quality of the material at the end of the usage stage. In fact, in most of the cases,
the design of the structure included the fusion of the various elements, resulting
almost impossible to maintain the quality of the component in the dismantling
of the building. Moreover, the recycling process of reinforced concrete makes the
process energy demanding and also time-consuming because of the large volumes
to dispose of. The quality of the recycled concrete has also to be proved by stress
test to ensure the strength stability in the new usage. To reach these goals, the
quality of the material has to reach high standards and the recycled concrete not
always can guarantee these properties. These factors tend to lead the decision of
the construction companies to recycle the material into gravel.
In the optic of Design for Disassembling, the concrete elements have also
a high potential to be re-used after the usage stage.
The high strength quality
makes them suitable for the re-use into other structural elements. The challenge
71
==PAGE:82==
with these constitutional elements is to design reliable connections that ensure the
stability of the structure without compromising the integrity of the component in
a future dismantling.
Joints through bolts or other mechanical connections can
guarantee at the same time, stability and easiness of mounting/dismounting the
elements (see the dossier by 3XN studio at https://issuu.com/3xnarchitects).
4.3.4
Uncertainties
The LCA study is a multiple analysis based on an iteration process that aims to
increase the precision of the data and the reliability of the results.
The entire
calculations are founded over the LCI (Life Cycle Inventory) and LCIA (Life Cycle
Inventory Assessment). This section of the LCA study encloses the data source and
their precision. Some data-sets have a high precision representing a speciﬁc process
while others only resemble the actual process. The level of representativeness of an
LCA process with the actual process in the system can generate uncertainties in the
results. In annex in tab. A.2, A.3 and A.4, the various processes are listed with
the relative representativeness. Generally, the lowest source of uncertainty is from
the manufacturing of the product. The Ecoinvent database of OpenLCA includes
a very complete list of manufacturing processes with a high trust. The recycling
processes, instead, are the one with the highest uncertainty. There are not always
speciﬁc processes and assumptions over them were required.
In the Bitumen the highest uncertainties derive from the avoid processes in
the burning of the material in the incineration plant.
The results are based on
assumptions from other researches and these could also bring uncertainties (avoid
electricity and heat production). Another source of uncertainty In particular, the
process of re-melting the bitumen sheets is crucial in the recycling processes and
there are not clear references or deﬁned processes about it.
About the triple glazing, the processes in the modeling the EoL have a very high
representativeness with the consequent lower level of uncertainty. The recycling of
72
==PAGE:83==
the glass is also based over the assumption of an electric oven for melting the glass
and this could not represent in a satisfying level the real process of melting and
manufacturing the triple glazing.
The concrete modeling is also based over good representative processes, and
therefore the data should be more accurate and reliable. The only very uncertain
process is the dismantling of the concrete elements and the re-using of it. Every
building is a stand-alone project and there are no precise guidelines, neither data,
about the diﬃculty/easiness to face for a process like this. Therefore, the hours used
in the process are completely assumed with no actual references.
In the energy aspect there are also present uncertainties based on the sim-
pliﬁcation of the modelling the forecast of the environmental impacts (see energy
paragraph at page 25).The hybrid approach of this analysis to the energy supply
inﬂuences the results in all the aspects from the manufacturing to the recycling.

Chapter 5
Conclusions and recommendations
The preliminary study of this thesis involved the analysis and sorting of the en-
vironmental impacts of the structural components of an oﬃce building in Den-
mark. The evaluation was done using the software LCAByg utilizing the German
¨OKOBAUDAT database. The grouping and sorting of the components were done in
an overall material level and in a component level in the impact category of Global
Warming Potential (GWP 100).
The results pointed at the mineral materials as the most CO2 emitting materials
(51%), followed by metal and synthetic materials (16% each) and insulation materi-
als (11%). In a component level, the emissions in the whole life cycle of the building
outlined as the most contributing components:
• The roof (22%)
• The external wall supplements (18%)
• The internal walls (17%)
The analysis continued assessing these components and detecting as the most CO2
emitting materials inside every component:
• The bitumen sheets of the roof
• The triple glazing of the external wall supplements
• The concrete in the internal walls
74
==PAGE:85==
Once detected the materials where to focus the investigation of the end of life
options, they were evaluated with alternative scenarios from the reference disposal.
The reference scenario of incineration of bitumen was evaluated with the recycling
cut-oﬀapproach, recycling bitumen into bitumen or into mastic asphalt for road
production. Aside from the cut-oﬀmethod, the recycling potential approach was
used evaluating the same scenarios. The results pointed out diﬀerent outcomes.
Based only on the environmental emissions of the scenarios, the reference scenario
of recycling was the best performing while recycling into mastic asphalt was the
worst. This solution is in conﬂict with the impact assessment category of the fossil
fuel depletion, where the recycling potential methods perform better. Thus, it is
not possible to express a unique judgment on which solution is the best one.
From the emission point of view, the reference incineration scenario has a very high
crediting because of the incineration suggesting it as the most preferable solution.
Despite that, the complete incineration is deﬁnitely not the most preferable solution
in term of the non-renewable natural resources.
The triple glazing scenarios instead depict the re-using as the unique preferred
scenario. The other recycling alternatives are also sharing the same trend, validat-
ing the results obtained. Even though the performance of the scenarios, all of them
are preferable in comparison with the reference one, either from the environmen-
tal impacts and either for the natural resources used. From the DfD aspect, the
material is also the most suitable for being re-used thanks to its characteristics of
short time substitution and high resistance to atmospheric agents. The easiness
of its re-usability is dependent on standard parameters that have to be respected
in the new component as same dimensions for the installation, physical and visual
characteristics as like as the life expectancy.
The concrete material scenarios suggest the reference landﬁll as the least prefer-
able method and the recycling potential into concrete recycling as the most suitable.
All the recycling scenarios share similar trends validating themselves in this way.
The re-using scenario was expected to be the least emitting, but it performed very
similarly to the cut-oﬀrecycling.
In the optic of DfD, this scenario is also the
75
==PAGE:86==
most preferable. As like the triple glazing option, the re-use of concrete elements
requires the fulﬁlling of standard parameters as standard dimensions, quality, and
connections to the structure.
5.1
Recommendations
In the Life Cycle Assessment perspective, the drafting of the LCI documents,
further investigations are recommended in the selection of the least representative
processes as analyzed in paragraph 4.3.4. In the bitumen modeling, the allocated
process of re-melting the bitumen in the cut-oﬀrecycling scenarios could be better
represented by more precise processes. The same investigation could be done in
the representation of the triple glazing melting for recycling. Besides that, other
investigations could be done to esteem a more precise amount of hours or even a
more representative process for the mounting/dismounting of the concrete elements.
Additionally, it would be recommended to submit the research to an external entity
that could evaluate the present analysis, conﬁrming or not the results obtained.
In Denmark, the Green Building Council is the entity that monitors and evaluates
the environmental guidelines in the construction sector of the country, and other
independent entities encharged of assigning the environmental certiﬁcation are for
example the DGNB council also present in the country.
In the research at material level, further progress could be done by achieving
a less resource and emitting bitumen. The manufacturing of bitumen from more
environmentally friendly sources is already present in the market of construction
material. Unfortunately, this technology is still more expensive in comparison with
the traditional solutions in the market, resulting in use only in niche markets and
used mostly only in pilot projects.
Even an increase in the performance of the
technical life service could inﬂuence the emissions of the building in the whole life
cycle perspective of it. The reduced number of substitution of the bitumen layers
could, for example, cut 1/4 the total emissions.

Acronym
Deﬁnition
EF
Emissions and resources consumed (per unit of analysis) arising from the
production and the EoL stages of the product life cycle.
EV
Emissions and resources consumed (per unit of analysis) arising from the
acquisition and pre-processing of virgin material.
E∗V
Emissions and resources consumed (per unit of analysis) arising from the
acquisition and pre-processing of virgin material assumed to be substi-
tuted by recyclable materials.
Erecycled
Emissions and resources consumed (per unit of analysis) arising from the
production process of the recycled material, including collection, sorting
and transportation processes.
Erecycling,EoL
Emissions and resources consumed (per unit of analysis) arising from the
recycling process at the EoL, including collection, sorting, transportation
and recycled material production processes.
ED
Emissions and resources consumed ( per unit of analysis) arising from the
disposal of waste material (e.g. land-ﬁlling, incineration and pyrolysis)
R1 (Dimen-
sionless)
”Recycled content of material”. It is the proportion of material in the
input to the production that has been recycled in a previous system (0
=< R1 =< 1).
R2 (dimen-
sionless)
”Recycling fraction of material”. It is the proportion of the material in
the product that will be recycled in a subsequent system, i.e. the rate be-
tween recycled output and virgin material input. R2 shall therefore take
into account the ineﬃciencies in the collection and recycling processes (0
=<R2 =< 1).
QS
Quality of the secondary material, i.e. the quality of the recycled mate-
rial.
QP
Quality of the primary material, i.e. the quality of the virgin material.
