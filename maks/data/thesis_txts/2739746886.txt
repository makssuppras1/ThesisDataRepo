Abstract:
The main goal of the present thesis lies in the investigation of the performance boost potential
using stationary structures in the vicinity of Vertical Axis Wind Turbines (VAWTs). Diffusers
have been used successfully to boost the power performance of Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines
(HAWTs) by increasing the flow speed through the rotor disc. Apart from structures that in-
crease flow speed for the VAWT, there may also be a potential benefit of structures decreasing
flow speeds in the areas of the VAWT that use power: the part of the cycle where the blade
moves straight upwind. The potential for using flow speed increasing or decreasing techniques
may depend on whether the rotor is lift or drag-driven. Nevertheless, developing and polishing
from scratch the CFD simulation for one of the shrouded VAWTs is very time demanding. Thus,
only lift-driven VAWT, whose power coefficient potential capacity generally outperforms the drag
based VAWT, is presented in this thesis. In interest of computational speed and simplicity, inves-
tigations were carried out by the use of Two-Dimensional Unsteady Reynolds-Averaged
Navier-Stokes Computational Fluid Dynamics (2D URANS CFD) in the commercial soft-
ware STAR-CCM+. With the purpose of getting physically meaningful results, mesh sensitivity
and convergence methods for URANS simulations were studied by the use of the unsteady seg-
regated solver (unsteady SIMPLE algorithm) of STAR-CCM+. Moreover, Langtry’s Gamma
ReTheta transition model of Ref.[4], Ref.[5] and Ref.[6] was coupled to Menter’s K-ω SST turbu-
lence model of Ref.[7]. For simplicity, the lift-driven VAWT was chosen to be a two-dimensional
H-Darrieus (in 3D the twist angle of the blades is 0°), also known as the Gyromil VAWT. The
process to achieve the goal of the thesis was divided in 3 stages. First, test of the CFD sim-
ulation model in steady and unsteady state while getting the aerodynamic coefficients of the
corresponding airfoil in the simulation (one CFD simulation per airfoil model and only one airfoil
in the CFD simulation) that were part of the shrouded VAWT. Second, the setup of the first
stage was used as a base and some changes were added to be able to test the performance bare
VAWT. Third, the setup of the second stage was the new base and after adding the shroud and
some changes, the ultimate version of the simulation setup was fully developed.

3.1
Reynolds number:
The Reynolds number is defined as the ratio between the inertial forces (caused by mass and
motion) and the viscous forces. Consider the particular case of a structure submerged in the
outer-flow of an infinite fluid, which passes around the object and continues onwards. Accord-
ing to Ref.[8], thereby, the boundaries of the fluid are assumed to be sufficiently far from the
structure. For simplicity, the structure is assumed to be static at a fixed point and the shape
is assumed to be the two-dimensional section of a flat plate, in other words, an extremely thin
rectangle whose largest wall is parallel to the horizontal component of the motion of the flow.
So, if one chooses the length of the horizontal wall as the characteristic length, L, and one tracks
the path of a particle that travels around the upper side of the flat plate while calculates the

3.5
The boundary layer:
Recall the particular case of the static and two-dimensional section of an airfoil from subsection
3.1. When the wind approaches to the body, there will be a region around the surfaces that
are in contact with the wind flow and it will appear a velocity gradient; this is known as the
boundary layer region. Therefore, the tangential component of the wind velocity profile to these
surfaces will decrease when the perpendicular distance from a point at the mentioned region to
the corresponding surfaces becomes shorter.
According to Ref.[10] in the case of fluids of low viscosity, such as water or air, the thickness
of each layer is of a few microns. As a consequence, high velocity gradients can cause large
shear forces due to the fact that the tangential component of the wind speed increases a lot
from one layer to another of the flow. However, note that this is only valid in the particular
case of the subsonic flows, MaStatic
2D Airfoil < 1 [-], due to the fact that in that case the resistance
to flow deformation is a consequence of the viscous effects. In the case of supersonic flows,
MaStatic
2D Airfoil
> 1 [-], the resistance is a function of the compressibility or the elastic forces,
which are mentioned in Ref.[9]. Nevertheless, supersonic flows are out of the scope of the present
thesis, so, the VAWT simulations were done in subsonic flow.
3.6
Aerodynamic coefficients:
According to Ref.[11] integrating the surface pressure and shear stresses on the surface of a body
results in two force components, which are the lift and drag forces and both act on a point known
as the centre of pressure, about which the aerodynamic moment is zero. As it is mentioned in
[12], lift and drag loads are perpendicular and parallel to the direction of the relative wind flow,
respectively and it is much more efficient to use the lift force rather than simple drag as the
main source of propulsion or when extracting power from the wind. Therefore, all the blades of
the modern wind turbines look like propeller blades.
Note that the angle between the chord or the chordline of a 2D airfoil in standstill and the free
stream wind speed, U, is defined as the angle of attack. However, if the airfoil is rotating, the
angle of attack is redefined as the angle between the chord and the relative wind speed, Vrel.
In most of the airfoil data, the lift and drag forces are taken at the 25% of the chord length,
where in many cases the aerodynamic centre is located, since the aerodynamic moment is inde-
pendent of the angle of attack at that point. Note that the suction around the leading edge of

3.6.1
Aerodynamic stall in airfoils:
According to classical aerodynamics the lift force is generated from the pressure distribution
and the drag force is due to the friction. Nevertheless, this is only correct in the linear regime,
where the lift increases linearly as a function of the angle of attack. However, as it is mentioned
in Ref.[11], when the angle of attack increases beyond the limits of the small angle of attack
assumption (beyond the point of maximum lift), the streamlines do not follow the airfoil surface
and the flow is considered to be separated (collapse of the laminar separation bubble located at
the separation point), which results in loss of lift. This condition is called airfoil stall and due
to Eqn.(10) it is known that the point of maximum lift is mostly geometry dependent.
Therefore, there are many airfoil families with different shapes or geometries and each air-
foil family is usually designed for a specific application, for example: ARA (propellers), Drela
(sailplanes), NACA (general aviation), Risoe and NLR (wind turbines), FX and S series (motor-
sports and automotive industry). However, for the design of the VAWT of the present thesis
it was chosen to use NACA0018 airfoils due to the fact that this is a commonly known airfoil
and there is some experimental data available in the literature such as in Ref.[1], Ref.[14] and
Ref.[2]. In order to design the shroud it was chosen to use Selig’s s1223 airfoils due to the fact
that these airfoils are able to get high lift with small angles of attack. The experimental data
corresponding to Selig’s s1223 airfoils that was used for the study of the present thesis is in
Ref.[15], Ref.[3] and Ref.[16].

3.8
An approach to shrouded H-Darrieus VAWT aerodynamics:
It can be found in the literature, such as Ref.[12], that diffusers have been used successfully to
boost the power performance of Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines (HAWTs) by increasing the flow
speed through the rotor disc. Apart from structures that increase flow speed for the VAWT,
there may also be a potential benefit of structures decreasing flow speeds in the areas of the
VAWT that use power: the part of the cycle where the blade moves straight upwind.
The
potential for using flow speed increasing or decreasing techniques may depend on whether the
rotor is lift or drag-driven. Since the Giromill VAWT is lift-driven, the most simple structure
that suits to boost the potential of the corresponding VAWT is a diffuser of two blades that
increases the flow speed.
due to the fact that the flow that passes through the 2D H-Darrieus VAWT varies in space
and time, it is not possible to predict the behaviour of the performance of a two-dimensional
shrouded H-Darrieus VAWT in steady state. In addition, even if all the degrees of freedom re-
lated to the aerodynamic performance of the 2D H-Darrieus VAWT from Subsec.3.7, such as the
tip speed ratio, λ, or the diameter based rotor solidity, σ2D VAWT, are known, the power coefficient
can vary depending on many degrees of freedom that are related to the shroud. For example,
the shape of the airfoils of the shroud, their respective chord lengths, the angle of attack, their
relative position with respect to the shaft of the 2D H-Darrieus VAWT, etcetera.

4.1
Segregated solver and unsteady SIMPLE algorithm:
As it is mentioned in Subsec.3.6 the flow is assumed to be incompressible (MaStatic/Dynamic
2D Airfoil
< 0.3
[-] and constant density). Therefore, it was chosen to use the segregated flow model since ac-
cording to Ref.[25] this solver is appropriate to model incompressible or mildly compressible
flows for simulations with wind turbines due to the fact that in reality they always work at low
Mach numbers.
In Ref.[25] the segregated algorithm is compared with the coupled algorithm. The highlighted
relative strengths and weaknesses of the segregated algorithm are:
• The segregated algorithm uses less memory than the coupled.
• The number of iterations that the coupled algorithm requires to solve a given flow prob-
lem is independent of the mesh size. However, the number of iterations that the
segregated algorithm requires increases with the mesh size.
• The segregated flow model is recommended to be chosen for incompressible or mildly
compressible flows. However, the coupled flow model and the coupled energy model are
recommended for modeling compressible flows, natural convection problems and flows
with large body forces or energy sources.
The main reason is that the coupled solver
works significantly less efficiently than the segregated solver if the flow is incompressible,
for example, in the particular case of a lid driven cavity.
In addition Ref.[25] states a summary of some properties of the segregated solver:
• The segregated flow model solves the flow equations (one for each component of velocity,
and one for pressure) in a segregated, or uncoupled, manner.
• The linkage between the momentum and continuity equations is achieved with a predictor-
corrector approach.
• This model has its roots in constant-density flows.
• Although it can handle mildly compressible flows and low Rayleigh number natural con-
vection, it is not suitable for shock-capturing, high Mach number (for example, in the
particular case of strongly compressible flows), and high Rayleigh-number applications.
According to Ref.[25], in STAR-CCM+ the only unsteady models for time modeling that are
available to couple with the segregated flow model are the implicit unsteady model and the
PISO model. Here, PISO stands for ”Pressure-Implicit with Splitting Operators” and the seg-
regated solver coupled to the implicit solver forms the unsteady SIMPLE algorithm, which
stands for, ”Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure Linked Equations”.
4.2
Turbulence modeling:
Turbulence modeling is defined as the formulation and use of a mathematical model to predict
and recreate the effects of turbulence. Note that the RANS turbulence models use temporal
averaging of the Navier-Stokes equations and as it is mentioned in Ref.[26], due to the non-linear
convective term the averaging produce an additional ”turbulent” stress, which is known as the
Reynolds stress tensor, Tt. This tensor adds six independent unknowns that need to be
modeled in terms of mean flow quantities to solve the closure problem in turbulence.
30
==PAGE:31==
4.2.1
k-ω SST turbulence model:
In the last years, several turbulence models have been developed and each of them have their
respective strengths and weaknesses. As a consequence, it is necessary to know which model
needs to be used depending on the needs related to accuracy, computational cost and available
time to compute the CFD simulation that solves the formulated problem.
According to Ref.[27] the k-ω turbulence model suffers from undesirable free stream dependency,
whereas the k-ϵ performs with a fair behaviour at high Reynolds numbers, as long as the region
of interest stays away from walls. Menter, who developed the k-ω SST model in 1994, stated
in Ref.[7] that the idea behind the model is to retain the robust and accurate formulation of
Wilcox’s k-ω model from Ref.[28] in the near wall region, and to take advantage of the free
stream independence of Launder and Sharma’s k-ϵ model from Ref.[29] in the outer part of the
boundary layer.
Therefore, Menter’s k-ω Shear Stress Transport (SST) model uses k-ω formulation close to solid
walls and switched to a k-ϵ behaviour in the free-stream. Also, the model switches from the k-ϵ
formulation to the k-ω formulation by using a blending function.
In addition, the k-ω SST model can be used as a low Reynolds turbulence model without
adding any extra damping functions. This is suitable for adverse pressure gradient and
separating flows. However, it over-predicts the Turbulence Intensity (TI) in regions with
large normal strain, like stagnation regions and regions with strong accelerations. Nev-
ertheless, the k-ω SST model still over-performs the k-ϵ model in that sense.

4.3
Mesh dependency:
Ref.[30] states that partial differential equations that govern fluid flow and heat transfer are
usually not susceptible to analytical solutions, except for very simple cases. As a consequence,
in order to analyze fluid flows, the flow domains are split into smaller subdomains (2D or 3D)
and the governing equations are then discretized and solved inside each of these subdomains.
Note that it is important to ensure proper continuity of solution across the common interfaces
between two subdomains, so that the approximate solutions inside various portions can be put
together to give a complete picture of fluid flow in the entire domain. Thus, to fulfil this condi-
tion, it is necessary to add a reasonable number of subdomains, which are made up of geometric
primitives like quadrilaterals and triangles in 2D or hexaedra and tetrahedra in 3D.
According to Ref.[31], capturing the flow features of a wind turbine requires a comprehensive
understanding of physics in order to create a sufficient mesh topology. Achieving a mesh in-
dependent solution is an essential step in analyzed the results obtained using CFD techniques.
However, recall that in Subsec.4.1 it was mentioned that the number of iterations that the seg-
regated algorithm requires increases with the mesh size, which means that generating a mesh
that is too fine increases considerably the computational cost of the CFD simulations that use
the segregated flow solver. So, recall that it is important to find a balance between accuracy
and computational cost while taking into account the available resources and time to compute
the simulations. In the present study was considered that the mesh was good enough as long as
the results were accurate enough to provide physically meaningful results that did not change
crazily after expanding the distance between the boundary walls and the region of interest; and
increasing/decreasing (mesh coarsening and mesh refinement) the amount of cells and cell size
in the region of interest.
In addition, Ref.[26] states that for the γ-Reθ model to perform properly, the following mesh
requirements need to be satisfied:
• The γ-Reθ transition model requires a high-quality, refined low Reynolds number mesh.
• The wall-normal mesh spacing should ensure a y+ in the range: 0.1 ≤y+ ≤1.
• Either the all-y+ or low-y+ wall treatment formulations should be used.
• Special attention should be paid to the stream-wise mesh spacing near the transition
region.

• If transition is induced by laminar separation, then the mesh should be fine enough to
resolve the curvature of the body where the separation exists, and to capture the laminar
separation bubble.
4.4
Time dependency:
According to Ref.[32], to ensure a solution that is independent of the time-step, δt, that is chosen
for the unsteady or time marching simulation, it is considered best practice to follow the next
steps:
1. From preliminary simulations, estimate the temporal scales:
• Initial transients: Ttransients (e.g. development of boundary layers).
• Oscillations: Toscillations (e.g. vortex shedding).
• Courant (Co)/Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL)/Fourier numbers.
2. Choose an initial time-step (δtStart) that satisfy:
• Co (δtStart) and δtStart < MIN (Ttransients, Toscillations) /O (100)
3. Once a periodic solution is reached, perform a temporal convergence study by reducing
δtStart successively or/and change the temporal scheme to a higher order scheme.
In addition, in Ref.[25] it is recommended to account for the following information in order to
set a CFD simulation that uses the unsteady SIMPLE algorithm:
• Generally, the transient phenomenon governs the physical time-step: Ttransients < Toscillations.
• For periodic flow, an order of 50-100 time-steps per period is appropriate.
• The convective Courant number is a helpful indication for selecting the time-step size:
for time-accurate simulations, the convective Courant number should be 1.0 on
average in the zone of the interest (this value implies that the fluid moves by about
one cell per time-step). Larger values (10-100) is acceptable in other regions of the flow.
• For flows with free surfaces, if a second-order scheme for time integration is used, the
Courant number must be less than 0.5 in all cells.
• Time-step and inner iterations need to be adjusted to optimize convergence and CPU
usage. Smaller physical time-steps generally mean that the solution is changing less from
one time-step to the next, so that fewer inner iterations are required. There is clearly
an optimal balance of time-step size, under relaxation factors, and the number of inner
iterations for a given problem and desired transient accuracy.
• In the particular case of time-accurate simulations with adequate time-step and under-
relaxation parameters, 5 iterations per time-step usually are sufficient.

In fact, the stagnation point of an airfoil is known as the precise location where the flow

above the angle of attack of maximum lift. When the wind flow surrounds the airfoil, at the low
pressure side (flow acceleration side) a laminar separation bubble is generated at a point known
as the separation point, which changes the position as a function of the angle of attack and
other factors, like the stagnation point. In addition, as it shown in Figs.40-42, the thickness of
the laminar separation bubble increases as a function of the increase of the angle of attack
in the linear region. Subsequently, a dynamic stall vortex appears when the angle of attack
is increased and causes the laminar separation bubble to collapse and become into turbulent
separation. As it can be seen in Fig.43, in this region the streamlines do not follow the airfoil
surface shape and there is flow separation.

simulations. As a consequence, in Fig.38 it is very visible that due to the geometrical limita-

7
Conclusion:
Finally, it has been investigated the performance boost potential using stationary structures
around a vertical axis wind turbine.
Conclusions:
• Aerodynamic coefficients cannot be computed in a way that gives physically meaningful
results in a 2D simulation, duet to the fact that aerodynamic stall is a 3D effect. However,
even if the turbulence models overestimate these coefficients, assuming that the angle of
attack at of rotating airfoils is small enough to avoid stall while the VAWT is performing, it
is possible to get physically meaningful results to describe the behaviour of a bare VAWT.
• It is possible to use simple equations to calculate the relative increase of wind speed at
the center-line of two diffuser airfoils with high accuracy as long as it is a bare diffuser.
79
==PAGE:80==
However, the model still is not able to predict how the flow changes around the middle-
point of the two diffuser airfoils and since the equations to predict this behaviour are not
included in the actual model, it fails to calculate results along the x axis. However, even
if it is a crude model, still the results have a strong correlation and clearly increasing the
chord length causes ηVAWT Diam. to increase by following the trend of a curve, which means
that there is direct relation between ηVAWT Diam. and the chord length.
Assuming that the Reynolds number has a small impact in the behaviour of the VAWT
as long as it is high enough, a diameter based rotor solidity of 0.15 [-] a λ = 3 [-] and a
pitch angle of θ = 17° in the diffuser airfoils (the pitch angle of the rotating airfoils of the
VAWT is θ = 0° ) is the combination that allows to obtain a ηVAWT Diam. = 1.48 [-], or a
relative boost of the 48% with respect to a bare VAWT. However, note that the diffuser
does not extract the 100% of the flow, which means that it could be more feasable to use
a VAWT with a larger rotor radius and the relative increase would be even higher.
