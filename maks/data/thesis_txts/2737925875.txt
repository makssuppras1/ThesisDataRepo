Abstract
Severe psychiatric disorders pose great functional impairments and interfere substan-
tially with daily functioning. Among these, schizophrenia (SZ) and bipolar disorder (BP)
are prominent, affecting approximately 1% of the population. Considering their severity,
this high prevalence underscores the need for early diagnosis to minimize the impact
on patients. However, the specific neurological causes remain unknown despite the
knowledge of being at familial high risk (FHR), remarkably increasing the risk of de-
veloping the disorders. Hence, studying adolescents at FHR can ultimately help in
illuminating the neurodevelopmental changes and uncover possible alterations in the
brain. The main goal of this thesis is to identify potential group differences between
FHR groups of SZ and BP and a population-based control (PBC) group in a novel cross-
sectional and longitudinal assessment. This was conducted using electroencephalo-
gram (EEG) and behavioral data obtained from cohort studies with adolescents aged
11 and 15 engaging in a flanker task, on which we performed essential preprocessing.
The primary differences of interest lay in brain connections, which were found using
the sophisticated Dynamic Causal Modeling (DCM) method. Furthermore, this con-
nectivity analysis was guided by knowledge on inhibition control and the multi demand
network derived from the Flanker paradigm. A statistical analysis using Parametric
Empirical Bayes (PEB) showed strong evidence for differences in several parameters,
including group differences, suggesting longitudinal changes. Besides DCM, we also
conducted analyses of behavioral data, ERP components, and LRP amplitudes to al-
low for a greater interpretation of plausible correlations. On these levels, we performed
statistical analysis with mixed effects models to highlight differences. Overall, our re-
sults showcased statistically significant differences throughout all investigated levels.
Group differences suggested that FHR and PBC varied in performance, indicating neu-
rodevelopmental differences, with FHR-SZ being the most impacted group. However,
based on all result levels, a distinct difference from one group to another was hard to
distinguish, making it difficult to assess a longitudinal impairment in the FHR groups.
Finalizing our thesis, we have gained valuable insight into the dynamics of cognition
and neurodevelopment in FHR and PBC groups at different ages. Many of our results
corresponds to the literature and we have proposed reasonable suggestions to how
our findings can be explained and even correlated. Our thesis can prove to serve as a
foundation in the lacking field of cognition and neurological changes at FHR, but further
longitudinal research is required to validate our findings.

Resumé
Psykiatriske lidelser af alvorlig karakter medfører store begrænsninger for hverdags-
funktioner. Blandt disse er skizofreni (SZ) og bipolar lidelse (BP) fremtrædende, da
cirka 1% af befolkningen påvirkes. Da lidelserne er alvorlige, understreger denne høje
prævalens behovet for tidlig diagnose for at minimere patienternes påvirkning. Til trods
for kendskabet til, at individer med familiær højrisiko (FHR) har en femdobbelt risiko for
at udvikle lidelserne, kendes de underliggende neurologiske årsager stadig ikke. Der-
for kan studier af unge med FHR bidrage til at belyse mulige neuroudviklingsmæssige
ændringer i hjernen. Hovedformålet med denne afhandling er at identificere potentielle
forskelle mellem FHR-grupperne for SZ og BP og en populationsbaseret kontrolgruppe
(PBC) i tværsnits- og længdegående undersøgelser. Disse undersøgelser blev udført
på EEG- og adfærdsdata fra kohortestudier af unge i aldrene 11 og 15 år, optaget un-
der en Flanker-opgave, hvortil vi udførte essentiel preprocessering. De primære inter-
esseområder var forskelle i hjerneforbindelser, undersøgt ved brug af den sofistikerede
metode Dynamic Causal modeling (DCM). Denne konnektivitetsanalyse blev bakket op
af viden om inhibitorisk kontrol og multiple demand network i Flanker-paradigmet. Re-
sultater af statistiske Parametric Empirical Bayes (PEB) analyser viste stærke beviser
for forskelle i flere parametre også mellem grupperne. Udover DCM analyserede vi
også adfærdsdata, ERP-komponenter og LRP-amplituder for at fortolke mulige korre-
lationer på en større skala. Disse niveauer blev analyseret statistisk ved brug af mixed
effects models, som resulterede i flere signifikante effekter. Samlet set viste vores
resultater statistisk signifikante forskelle i hver enkelt testet parameter på alle under-
søgte niveauer. Gruppeforskellene antydede, at FHR og PBC varierede i præstation
tydende på neurologiske udviklingsmæssige forskelle, hvor FHR-SZ var den mest ud-
satte gruppe. Baseret på resultaterne af alle analyseniveauer, var det ikke muligt at
tyde en klar forskel fra én gruppe til en anden, hvilket gør det vanskeligt at koble FHR-
grupperne til tydelige kognitive svækkelser. Af denne afhandling har vi opnået værdi-
fuld indsigt i dynamikken i kognition og neuroudvikling hos FHR- og PBC-grupper i
forskellige aldre. Mange af vores fund stemmer overens med litteraturen, og vi har
foreslået hvordan resultaterne kan forklares og endda korreleres. Vores afhandling vil
kunne bidrage til grundlæggende viden i feltet omhandlende kognition og neurologiske
ændringer ved FHR, dog er yderligere udviklingsmæssig forskning nødvendig for at
bekræfte vores resultater.

1
Introduction
Schizophrenia (SZ) and bipolar disorder (BP) constitute two major severe psychiatric
conditions. SZ and BP are neurodevelopmental disorders where patients display cog-
nitive impairments in attention and executive function, among other manifestations [1],
which cause a significant decline in daily functioning. Critically for both disorders are
their highly heritable and polygenic characteristics. Children of parents with SZ or BP
are at five times increased risk (familial high risk (FHR)) of developing a mental disor-
der compared to the general population [2, 3]. Hence, investigation of FHR-SZ and
FHR-BP children and adolescents can potentially highlight key factors in the neurode-
velopment and mechanisms contributing to a mental disorder. Amongst neurodevel-
opment, the multiple demand network is especially important. Its connectivity struc-
ture allows for effective executive control, and dysfunction in the anatomically con-
nected brain regions is believed to be related to SZ and BP development [4]. Limited
neurobiological investigation studies exist, with the few only looking at young adults
or above.
Still, no specific change in neurobiological connections during inhibitory
control has been linked to the disorder development, illuminating the need for an ex-
ploratory assessment. Using the Eriksen flanker task to assess cognitive function and
electroencephalogram (EEG) to capture brain activity, it is possible to explore how an
FHR-SZ/BP group differs from a population-based control (PBC) group. Combining
this with powerful modeling and statistical tools, we can delve deeper into uncovering
cognitive functioning and neurobiological connectivity changes.

Theory
2.1
Neurodevelopmental Theories
The etiology of SZ and BP is often associated with a neurodevelopmental framework
that involves a complex interplay between genetic predispositions, early neurobiologi-
cal disturbances, and environmental factors that affect normal brain development [5].
Genetic factors play an important role in the neurodevelopmental theory of SZ and BP.
Both disorders have a significant genetic element, with numerous susceptibility genes
discovered that are heritable [6]. These genes participate in several brain processes,
such as synapse formation, the movement of neurons, and neurotransmitter systems
[5–7]. According to a meta-analysis of FHR, offspring of parents diagnosed with SZ
and BP are roughly five times more likely to develop the mental illness identified in
their parents’ disorders and roughly three times more likely to develop any disorder
than offspring to parents without any condition [3].
The interplay between genetic predispositions and environmental factors substantially
impacts the risk of developing SZ and BP. The etiology of the disorders is strongly
influenced by environmental factors, particularly early-life exposures, including win-
ter/spring birth, infections, and adverse childhood experiences. However, urban living,
migration, and cannabis are often encountered at a later stage, which has been the
focus of interest in relation to SZ but, to a lesser extent, also for BP [8].
The perinatal period is the first window of vulnerability for increasing the risk of BP and
SZ. Several prenatal and perinatal factors are suggested to play a role in the neurode-
velopmental etiology of these disorders. Toxins, prenatal infection exposure, maternal
starvation, and maternal stress are variables that can negatively impact the develop-
ment of the embryonic brain. Moreover, perinatal factors, such as perinatal hypoxia
and obstetrical complications, may interfere with the neurodevelopmental course and
increase the risk of developing SZ and BP in the future [9, 10].
Adolescence represents the second risk period of vulnerability to these disorders since
environmental stressors are closer to symptom onset. The peak onset of schizophrenia
occurs between ages 15 and 25. The incidence of schizophrenia shows an increase
beginning around the age of 14 years. The onset of bipolar disorder typically averages

between 12-24 years, where males are more prone to develop the disorder during
childhood. Schizophrenia manifests in about 39% of male patients and 23% of female
patients prior to reaching the age of 19 [11–13].
Throughout adolescence, while the total size of the brain stays relatively constant, there
are notable changes in the structure of both gray matter and white matter [11]. Struc-
tural Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) studies have shown that individuals diag-
nosed with SZ and BP have an increase in the size of the ventricles and a decrease
in cortical volume, particularly in the frontal, temporal, and parietal regions. It is hy-
pothesized that these alterations stem from either reduced synaptogenesis early in life,
exaggerated synaptic pruning during adolescence, or a combination of both [14, 15].
Moreover, diffusion tensor imaging studies have shown abnormalities in white matter
tracts associated with spatial cognition, attention, motor control, and executive function.
These may contribute to the clinical hallmarks linked to SZ and BP [16–18].
2.2
Inhibitory Control
Executive control is a high-order cognitive function that involves tasks like decision-
making, problem-solving, and planning, which makes effective executive control es-
sential for daily functioning [19]. Inhibitory control, a component of executive control,
refers to the capacity to inhibit an automatic response. Research suggests that in-
hibitory control depends on the multiple demand network, including frontal and parietal
cortices, rather than dedicated regions. This network facilitates the flexible allocation
of cognitive resources [20].
Inhibitory control also increases markedly during childhood and adolescence, simul-
taneously with the maturation of the multiple-demand network [21]. These changes
increase the efficiency and connections within the network, thereby supporting improve-
ments in inhibitory control, which may provide the mental flexibility needed to generate
alternative explanations. This trajectory is of fundamental importance since deficits in
inhibitory control are associated with multiple neurodevelopmental disorders, such as
SZ and BP [22].
In addition, poor inhibitory control has also been associated with lowered success in
education, social relationships, and regulating emotions in preschool children [23].

2.3
The Flanker Paradigm
The Eriksen flanker task was introduced in the 1970s by the publication of the paper
”Effects of noise letters upon the identification of a target letter in a non-search task” [24].
The task is a set of tests to assess selective attention in the inhibitory control domain
by the response to visual stimuli. In the task, the participant is presented with a target
stimulus surrounded by non-target stimuli, also known as ”flankers”. These stimuli
originally appeared as letters but have been modified to represent various symbols
and shapes. Instances where the flankers represent the same symbol as the target
stimulus are called congruent conditions. Instances where the flankers are different
from the target stimulus are called incongruent. During incongruent conditions, the
participant is introduced to a visuospatial conflict, where they inhibit irrelevant stimuli
and respond to the relevant target stimulus [25].
To evaluate the flanker task, reaction time (RT) and accuracy for each set of flanker
arrows are extracted to give an insight into global and selective inhibitory control.
2.4
Electroencephalography
EEG is a neuroimaging technique that involves the placement of electrodes on the
scalp to record the electrical activity caused by postsynaptic potentials of pyramidal neu-
rons in the underlying cerebral cortex. Most electrical signals on the scalp result from
the summation of the inhibitory or excitatory postsynaptic potentials generated sec-
ondary to neuronal depolarization. However, postsynaptic potentials are not the only
source of electrical signals detected in the brain. Action potentials, after-polarization,
and presynaptic currents all contribute to the voltage recorded by EEG electrodes cen-
timeters away [26]. EEG is particularly useful in cognitive neuroscience because of its
exquisite temporal resolution, enabling real-time brain activity monitoring [27].
The EEG electrodes are placed on the scalp with pastes, caps, or nets, and special
gels are applied to provide a conductive connection between the electrode and the skin
surface. Wires from the electrodes are connected to an EEG machine that contains
amplifiers to boost the raw electrical signal. The electrodes’ diameter is typically be-
tween 0.4 to 1 cm. Electrode montage can vary considerably between the clinic and
laboratories. The internationally recognized montage, and the clinical standard, is a
10-20 electrode montage with electrode locations based on standard position systems.

2.5
Event-Related Potentials
Event-related potentials (ERPs) are the brain’s electrical activity synchronized to spe-
cific events. ERPs are averaged EEG signals of the brain’s responses to stimuli, where
the brain is in a resting state before each stimulus and returns to a resting state before
the next stimulus. Therefore, an ERP signal cannot be observed in a single trial of
an EEG signal [30]. It is characterized by a wave complex that includes polarity, am-
plitude, and latency, all relative to the stimuli onset. ERPs allow the identification of
components that describe cognitive function, providing insights into some of the neu-
robehavioral symptoms that might be observed in adolescents with FHR SZ and BP
[31].
2.5.1
ERP Components
The N2 and P3 ERP components are strongly linked to the cognitive processes and
are considered markers for neural function in inhibitory control.
The N2 component is a negative waveform emerging approximately 200 ms after the
stimulus onset. N2 is primarily detected in the frontal and central lobes. It precedes
motor response, indicating its role in the initial stages of inhibition, where the cogni-
tive processes are mostly related to response inhibition, stimulus identification, and
distinction [32].

The P3 component is a positive going potential with maximum amplitude and peak la-
tency of approximately 300–600 ms post stimuli. Due to its late onset, it is associated
with the later stages of the inhibitory process, representing the higher cognitive func-
tions of information processing and attention engagement [33, 34]. It is mainly detected
in the central and parietal areas of the scalp. The amplitude of the P3 component de-
scribes the allocation of neural resources and the cognitive effort needed to evaluate
and solve a task. The latency of the P3 component describes the processing speed
for stimulus classification or cognitive capability [32, 34].
Various factors are known to affect both P3 and N2 amplitudes. ERP components are
known to show larger amplitudes in tasks where rapid stimulus identification and RT
are important, indicating the cognitive effort required for the task [35, 36]. Concerning
task difficulty, N2 and P3 are known to increase with task complexity [36], while the
components decrease through adolescence [37, 38].
2.6
Lateralized Readiness Potentials
Lateralized readiness potentials (LRPs) are obtained by extracting the brain’s electri-
cal activity related to motor preparation from ERPs of subjects performing tasks that
include motor responses. In the flanker paradigm, subjects respond to stimuli with
either the left or right hand. LRPs are derived by subtracting the activity measured
from electrodes placed on the hemisphere ipsilateral to the movement from the activity
recorded from electrodes over the contralateral hemisphere, isolating the lateralized
component of motor preparation [39].
LRPs offer a temporal and spatial perspective on the processes involved in preparing
for movement, indicating when motor preparation starts, even before actual movement
occurs. They highlight the lateralization of motor preparation, indicating the contralat-
eral motor cortex’s involvement in preparing for unilateral movements. The lateraliza-
tion process is critical for understanding the brain’s organization of motor functions. In
addition to studying motor preparation, LRPs examine cognitive processes such as
decision-making, attention, and response inhibition [39].

2.8
Dynamic Causal Modeling and Bayesian Methods
DCM was primarily introduced by Karl J. Friston in 2003 but has been further developed
through its popularization by many scientists. The Bayesian framework was introduced
to infer the brain network of causal relationships and hidden neuronal states, coupling
between brain regions, and the influence of coupling by change, using neuroimaging
time series. By using a dynamic input-state-output model as DCM, the responses are
induced by already known deterministic inputs representing the designed changes in
stimulation. This means that the responses are determined by both the inputs and the
internal state of the model system, along with multiple outputs, allowing the model to
represent the complexity of brain connections. Moreover, DCM utilizes bilinear approx-
imation to simplify the complexity of the brain network. The approximations reduce the
parameters of the system into three sets. First, the direct or extrinsic influence of in-
puts on brain states; second, the intrinsic or latent connections that couple responses
in one area to the state of others; and, finally, changes in this intrinsic coupling induced
by inputs [50]. The DCM framework considers experiments to intentionally disturb the
neutral dynamic state of the brain. These disturbances propagate through the network
of anatomically connected nodes and change the activity of different neuronal regions.
While other models assume that the responses are driven mainly by intrinsic noise and
randomness, DCM assumes that designed input changes drive the responses. An im-
portant aspect of DCM is how these experimental inputs influence neuronal responses,
which happens in two different ways. First, they directly influence specific anatomical
nodes for immediate response. Second, they indirectly influence the nodes by mod-
ulating the coupling between them - called effective connectivity, which also explains
the causal aspect of DCM regarding control theory [51].
2.8.1

brain system, making up one source model and ultimately describing the network of
neuronal sources. These models are based on the Jansen-Rit model. The neural mass

2.8.2
Spatial and Inverse Modeling
Practically, DCM is split into a first- and second level analysis. First level analysis
involves fitting the model to subject data, while second level analysis involves group-
level inferences and comparisons. First level starts with selecting the regions of inter-
ests (ROIs) based on the expected task effects and prior studies. The selected ROIs
are then defined with corresponding MNI coordinates. Next, a model matrix structure
is constructed based on the assumed connections and inputs. The external input is
defined based on the performed task, which sends a stimulus to an assumed brain
region, driving the connectivity. Furthermore, modulations are also specified based on
the connections assumed to be changed by the inputs, along with self-connections for
each brain region to allow for change over time [54]. To apply the data, an A-matrix
and B-matrix are defined. The A-matrix includes all assumed connections, while the
B-matrix includes the modulations, which are the connections expected to be modu-
lated by the task conditions. It is crucial to carefully model the connections relevant
to the given task paradigm. Constructing a full model with all possible connections is
far from feasible, as all the connections function as parameters for the model to pre-
dict brain activity and connection strengths, implying overfitting due to the complexity.
An estimation procedure is performed when the model is fitted to each subject’s data.
The goal of DCM is to approximate a posterior distribution of the parameters given the
model and the observed data. This is done according to Bayes’ rule in Equation 4.

Comparing the models can be based on a fixed effect analysis (FFX) or random effect
analysis (RFX).
FFX is defined by a homogeneity assumption where all subjects are treated as a single
group. Furthermore, it does not account for parameter variability between subjects but
is instead treated as noise, while the model structure is assumed to be identical across
all the subjects [56]. The results are also more uniform as they are simply based on
the summed log-evidences, which could easily affect the model choice.
RFX, however, utilizes the model performance frequency within each subject, allowing
for greater variability. Opposite to FFX, it is defined by a heterogeneity assumption. It
also allows for a possible underlying model diversity for each subject and, in contradic-
tion to FFX, explicitly models the parameter variability [57]. RFX is more advantageous
on larger sample sizes and can be used to generalize a larger population, as a single
large log-evidence does not affect the model choice. Another important advantage of
RFX is its feasibility in cognitive studies, where subjects might engage with different
strategies to the same stimuli.
Selecting the compared models is based on different measures, e.g., Model Exceedance
Probability (XP), Model Expected Probability (EP), and Bayesian Omnibus Risk (BOR).
The XP is the probability that a model performs better than any other model [58]. At
the same time, the EP explains the probability of a model outperforming competing
models, i.e., the likelihood of a model being the best model across all subjects. The
BOR is a measure that evaluates the risk that the difference between the models’ evi-
dence has occurred by chance. However, as BOR is an omnibus statistic, it does not
determine the best model alone; instead, it is used to define the protected exceedance
probability (PXP). The PXP is an extension of the XP and tells the probability of a
model being superior by considering BOR, hence providing a more conservative and
reliable measurement [57, 59].

2.9
Mixed Effects Model
Mixed effects models are statistical techniques developed explicitly for assessing data
that exhibits variability at several hierarchical levels. These models improve upon tra-
ditional linear regression by incorporating both fixed and random effects, making them
particularly appropriate for data with nested or hierarchical structures [61].
Random effects are components of mixed effects models, as they describe the inher-
ent variability at different hierarchy levels. Rather than assuming average relationships
across the entire dataset, random effects models account for variations within sub-
groups or clusters, which separates mixed effects models from fixed effects models
2.9
MIXED EFFECTS MODEL
13 of 70
==PAGE:25==
[62]. The primary objective of incorporating random effects in the model is to enhance
its flexibility to generate estimations. The model calculates parameters that describe
the overall dynamics of the population, rather than specific properties of individual sam-
ples, by randomly selecting effects from a population distribution with a known average
and variability. This approach improves the mixed effects model’s ability to generate
reliable findings about the studied cohort [63, 64].
Restricted Maximum Likelihood (REML) estimation is frequently used in mixed effects
models to estimate the random effects and residuals. The REML method provides esti-
mates of the maximum likelihood that do not depend on directly fitting the data. Instead,
it utilizes a likelihood function derived from a modified dataset, so irrelevant parameters
do not affect the estimations [65]. REML, in contrast to standard maximum likelihood
estimation, includes corrections for the degrees of freedom used by the fixed effects,
hence improving the accuracy of variance component estimations. This approach in-
cludes two sequential steps. In the initial stage, the random effects are modified to
estimate the variance components. Next, the fixed effects coefficients are estimated,
considering the variation between and within groups [66].
When utilizing mixed effect models, it is necessary to consider key assumptions to en-
sure the results’ reliability. Collinearity among predictors must be addressed to provide
unbiased estimates and reliable conclusions. It is assumed that the random effects and
residuals of the model follow a normal distribution. The random effects and residuals
are assumed to be independent at every hierarchy level. At every level of the hier-
archy, it is necessary to have suitable sample sizes in order to guarantee adequate
statistical power for detecting statistically significant effects. Another factor to consider
is the structure of the effects. Our thesis uses a hierarchical data structure and involves
categorized subjects in FHR and PBC. The FHR category is further divided into the sub-
groups FHR-SZ and FHR-BP. Correct identification of these structures is needed for
the model to assign variation to the appropriate effects, hence improving the model’s
accuracy and robustness [64].

3.2
Eriksen Flanker Task
Task-related functional brain activity and connectivity were assessed using a well es-
tablished paradigm, the Eriksen flanker task. Subjects performed a modified version of
the Eriksen flanker task, implemented in the E-Prime 2 stimulus presentation software
(Psychology Software Tools Inc, Pittsburg, USA). In this version, left and right-pointing
arrows serve as both flankers and targets, presented in congruent and incongruent
conditions to evaluate cognitive control and response interference.
The subjects were seated approximately one meter from a monitor in a quiet, comfort-
able environment. A total of 400 trials were recorded for each subject, divided equally
between congruent and incongruent conditions with a fixed order between subjects.
At the onset of each trial, a central fixation point was presented for 700 ms. Flanker
arrows were then shown for 100 ms, followed by the target arrow, which could be in
the same direction (congruent condition) or the opposite direction (incongruent condi-
tion) as the flanker arrows. The target arrow had a response window of 1000 ms. The
subjects were instructed to respond as fast and accurately as possible with their right
or left thumb by pressing the respective response button depending on the direction of
the target arrow.

3.3
Preprocessing of EEG Data
For the entire duration of the flanker task, EEGs were captured using an ActiveTwo
Biosemi (BioSemi, Amsterdam, Netherlands) system with a 128-electrode layout, a
sampling rate of 4096 Hz, and a temporal resolution of 0.3 ms. The EEG data prepro-
cessing was conducted in MATLAB (version 2022b) using the open-source FieldTrip
toolbox (version 20231025, Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition and Behavior at Rad-
boud University, Nijmegen, the Netherlands, https://www.fieldtriptoolbox.org/).
We segmented the EEG data in the BioSemi format (.bdf) into epochs to extract ERPs
of interest. The pre- and post-stimuli time points were defined, spanning from -0.5 s
pre-stimuli to 1.5 s post-stimuli. We implemented a baseline correction, setting the
baseline amplitude to the amplitude of the pre-stimuli period (-0.5 to -0.001) to account
for background noise. We used a third-order bandpass filter to enhance the signal-to-
noise ratio, high-passed at 0.5 Hz and low-passed at 40 Hz. In addition, a lowpass
finite impulse response filter was utilized to enhance signal quality further.
Artifact rejection addressed electrooculographic (EOG), jump, peak, and muscular arti-
facts. EOG artifacts from eye movements and blinks were detected by comparing refer-
ence channels 134 and 135, positioned above and below the right eye. Thresholds for
kurtosis larger than 5 µV and a maximum amplitude variation larger than 200 µV were
applied to detect EOG artifacts. Jump artifacts, characterized by abrupt differences
between consecutive data points, were assessed with a threshold of 30 µV Peak arti-
facts with unusually high or low amplitudes caused by electrode movement, sweat, or
other non-neural factors were flagged based on a threshold for absolute amplitudes
exceeding 100 µV. Muscle artifacts, typically from muscle contraction in the face or
neck, were detected through Fourier transformation with a threshold set for increased
power in the 31-60 Hz band compared to the 1-30 Hz band.
Following artifact rejection, we repaired bad channels by interpolation, using the tri-
angulation method based on neighboring channels from the BioSemi128 layout. This
interpolation gave us an estimate of missing channel values in each trial.
We synchronized the EEG data using time-locking, which determined the signal aver-
ages for correctly answered congruent and incongruent trials while excluding trails with
excessive artifacts. Ultimately, this generated two ERPs for each subject, one for each

RTs were extracted for each subject’s trials from the output .txt files generated by the

arrow presentation and the first response by pressing the button. We excluded trials
with incorrect responses, omission errors (no response within the 1000 ms response
window), and premature responses (RT < 200 ms) from the analysis.

The correctness was extracted for each subject’s trials from the output .txt files gener-

as 0 and correct responses as 1. The accuracy rates for each subject were calculated
as the mean accuracy over all trials, separately for congruent and incongruent condi-
tions.

3.6
DCM process
3.6.1
Data Preparation
The workflow of setting up and evaluating DCM models was carried out in MATLAB (ver-
sion 2022b), using the toolbox Statistical Parametric Mapping (SPM) (version 12.7771,
Functional Imaging Laboratory, UCL Queen Square Institute of Neurology, London,
UK).
SPM involves constructing statistical models to analyze imaging data and test hypothe-
ses about regionally specific brain effects. SPM incorporates the DCM framework,
making SPM exceptional for analyzing brain imaging data [71].
To utilize the SPM toolbox on our data, we had to convert the data from FieldTrip format.
We decided to average each subject’s trials, as the computational power needed to
perform DCM would otherwise be too large. This averaging was performed separately
for congruent and incongruent conditions.
Positions for EEG sensors and fiducials were assigned using default settings to link
the Biosemi128 electrode layout with the MNI coordinate space. We used a three-
shell sphere as the head model, with the spheres representing the inner skull, outer
skull, and outer scalp. This head model is needed for EEG data analysis in SPM, as it
requires the radius and conductivity of each physical boundary to predict the electrical
fields measured on the scalp [72].
3.6.2
Regions of Interest
Using functional MRI (fMRI), executive control was previously assessed in 11-year-
olds during a flanker task; 85 FHR-SZ, 63 FHR-BP, and 98 PBC children participated
in this study. This analysis was a part of the Danish High Risk and Resilience Study
of the VIA11 cohort, carried out by Line Korsgaard Johnsen [73]. The study examined

21 of 70

the differences in brain activity associated with task performance resulting from visu-
ospatial conflicts across the groups. Following the results of two meta-analyses [74,
75], task-related activation was identified in critical executive control regions. Based
on the clusters identified in the fMRI study and the meta-analyses, we selected ROIs
for our neuronal models. To concentrate on more superficial brain regions, we opted
to exclude subcortical clusters from our DCM on account of their elevated noise lev-
els. Additionally, clusters in the occipital cortex were excluded from our thesis despite
their notable activity in the fMRI study. Exclusion was done due to the similar function
performed by the frontal gyri, which is associated with the frontal eye field (FEF). We
selected the following brain regions as our ROIs due to their involvement in cognitive
and motor functions that are relevant to inhibitory control:
• Supplementary Motor Area (SMA): It is involved in planning and controlling move-
ment. Inhibitory control tasks require suppression of prepotent or habitual motor
responses [76]. Additionally, its role in motor preparation and the initiation of vol-
untary movements may provide a link to behavioral outcomes and LRPs of the
flanker task.
• Right Precentral Gyrus (R-PrCG): It is needed in the control of voluntary motor
movements and is involved in modulating motor responses under inhibitory con-
trol. It is well connected to the multiple demand network and is important for
integrating motor control and cognitive functions, relevant for inhibitory control
[77]. Furthermore, R-PrCG might provide information related to LRPs.
• Left Superior Frontal Gyrus (L-SFG) and Right Middle Frontal Gyrus (R-MFG):
They are included in the multiple demand network and are responsible for cog-
nitive processes such as decision-making, cognitive processing, and executive
functions. L-SFG plays a role in attention and executive function, which are es-
sential aspects of cognitive control. The R-MFG plays a crucial role in cognitive
flexibility and planning and is highly active during tasks that involve inhibitory con-
trol and conflict resolution [78].
• Left Posterior Parietal Cortex (L-PPC) and Right Posterior Parietal Cortex (R-PPC):
They are included in the multiple demand network and play a role in spatial per-
ception and visuomotor control. These regions integrate sensory information with

cognitive function and convert it to motor responses needed for spatial awareness
and movements [79].
3.6.3
Neuronal Models
We created two neuronal models to asses the brain’s dynamics during the flanker task
based on our selected regions of interest. The first model is simple and functions
as a fundamental framework. The second model is more complex and constructed
assuming that spatial, non-verbal, and attentional activities are primarily localized in
the brain’s right hemisphere [80]. Both models include a widely accepted constraint of
maintaining a certain level of symmetry regarding the mid-sagittal plane between the
hemispheres. This ensures that the representations of brain functions are balanced
and anatomically realistic [81].
Model 1, the less complex of the two, includes four main regions: L-SFG, R-MFG, L-
SMA, and R-PrCG, with their corresponding MNI coordinates: [−22, −6, 52], [28, −2,
52], [−6, −8, 50], and [46, 6, 30]. This model includes self-connections with forward,
backward, and lateral connections among all sources. The input is targeted at the
FEF, specifically at the L-SFG and R-MFG. Model 1 is highlighted in the blue square in
Figure 2, and the forward (AF), backward (AB), lateral (AL), modulation (B), and input
(Cu) matrices are depicted in Appendix Figures B.1, B.2, B.3, B.4, B.5, respectively.
Model 2 replicates the structure of Model 1 but includes two extra sources: the left and
right PPC, with MNI coordinates [−44,−44,40] and [38,−48,46], respectively. These
regions serve for new input, having self-connections as well as forward and backward
connections between L-PPC and L-SFG, as well as between the R-PPC and R-MFG.
Additionally, a forward and backward connection between the R-PPC and the R-PrCG
is established. Model 2 is illustrated in Figure 2, and the forward (AF), backward (AB),
lateral (AL), modulation (B), and input (Cu) matrices are illustrated in Appendix Figure
C.1, C.2, C.3, C.4, C.5, respectively.

In order to maintain uniformity and reliability, identical DCM settings were implemented
for both models. The analysis utilized the ERP data, separated into two conditions to
evaluate responses to congruent stimuli as a baseline and incongruent stimuli as mod-
ulation. The localization of neural sources was achieved using the Equivalent Current
Dipole technique in spatial modeling.
The peri-stimulus time window was defined as the interval from 0 to 600 ms to capture
the latency of ERP components, specifically the P3, which can manifest as late as 600
ms. Eight spatial modes were chosen for inversion to accurately capture the complexity
of the ERP signals. In addition, we implemented a first-order Discrete Cosine Trans-
form detrending method to mitigate low-frequency drifts in the data [82]. The stimulus
onset was predefined at 60 ms.
Ultimately, we fitted a DCM for each proposed model per subject.
3.6.4
Bayesian Model Selection
We used BMS in SPM to compare the two DCM neuronal models. Our objective was
to identify the best model to explain the observed data, which would be used for sub-
sequent analyses. The SPM batch system was used to configure and execute the
BMS analysis. We opted to set the BMS inference method to RFX, prioritizing model
performance frequency while accounting for inter-subject variability rather than using
summed Bayesian log evidence from fixed effect inference. Default settings were ap-
plied to the model space, and the family-level inference was configured to a single
family.
We evaluated the BMS results and selected the best performing model.

We initiated our PEB process by specifying design matrices for our cross-sectional and

assigned a separate row. This array is required for the spm_dcm_peb function in SPM.
We based our PEB analyses on the B matrix, reflecting the connections modulated by
the congruency effect. In addition, we independently calculated the variability between
subjects for each connection and utilized SPM’s default EEG settings.

5
Discussion
In our thesis, the main objective was to identify possible group differences between
being at FHR and PBC. We aimed to uncover these differences using behavioral data,
ERP components, LRP amplitudes, and DCM in an in-depth cross-sectional and longi-
tudinal analysis. The discussion will go over all significant results found for the tested
parameters as well as comprehensive interpretations.
From the flanker task, statistically significant differences in the effect of congruency
were found for all test parameters regarding behavioral data, ERP components, and
LRP amplitudes. These findings validate that the flanker task was executed correctly,
as we expected to observe a different performance under incongruent conditions. This
is crucial for the integrity of the experiment, which indicates that the reliability of our
results is robust and not due to chance.
5.1
Cognitive and Developmental Influence on Behavioral
Performances
The differences found between conditions for RT and accuracy reflect the greater cog-
nitive load and inhibitory control required to solve incongruent tasks, as the participants
performed slower and less accurate under this condition. The lower CVRT in incongru-
ent trials relates well to the higher RTs, as the responses were found to be slow but
very consistent.
We found differences in RT and accuracy based on gender, where females responded
slower with higher accuracy under both conditions. This could be caused by females
viewing the assignment as more complex or being more careful with their answers [84].
Researchers increasingly recognize that gender might impact cognitive function and
have demonstrated performance differences across males and females [85]. Our re-
sults correlate well with the majority of gender-based studies, suggesting that women
tend to have longer reaction times and higher accuracy in tasks that involve high con-
flict, like inhibitory tasks [85, 86].
The difference in accuracy for the age within VIA11 was unexpected, mostly because
we did not observe any age differences in terms of RT and CVRT and also because we
found no longitudinal difference in accuracy. An explanation could be the considerable
45 of 70
==PAGE:57==
variability in when individuals undergo neurodevelopment, particularly pronounced be-
tween ages 11 and 13 [87]. Furthermore, as the cross-sectional dataset is about half
the sample size of the longitudinal dataset, its accuracy is more sensitive to a larger
variance, possibly impacting the mixed effects model.
For the group difference, CVRT was found to be higher for FHR-SZ. This high variation
is linked to a deficit in sustained attention, considered a key feature of SZ, and is also
documented in high risk studies [88, 89]. We observed a decrease in reaction time
through adolescence. This correlates with the findings by Liuzzi et al., who investigated
the longitudinal changes of RT in a flanker task for children aged 12, 15, and 18 [87].
Together with the decrease in CVRT, these longitudinal differences could be explained
by the structural changes that happen across adolescence, such as synaptic pruning
[11]. Additionally, having prior experience with the task from VIA11 may have improved
performance, as subjects were more familiar with the experimental setting.
5.2
ERP Components’ Effect on FHR Cognition
We found elevated peak amplitudes for both N2 and P3 under incongruent conditions.
The amplitudes of these components are strongly linked to the execution of inhibitory
control and attention, confirming that incongruent trials in the flanker task require a
larger allocation of cognitive resources [36].
The ERP components are generally expected to decrease with age throughout adoles-
cence [37, 38]. We also found this for the P3 amplitude in the PBC group. To draw
any assumptions, we need to hypothesize that the PBC group exhibits optimal cogni-
tive resource allocation, making their amplitudes the baseline for what is considered
normal. Hence, we assume at the cognitive developmental stage in VIA11, a higher
resource allocation was required for the task. However, as they developed, less al-
location was required, as seen by the decrease in amplitude. However, this was not
seen for the FHR groups. They remained at the same amplitude level. Visually, we
can even inspect Figure 8(b) to see that PBC might have marginally lower amplitudes.
Together, we suggest that PBC may show cognitive development that is not seen in
the FHR groups. This is only a speculation, as to our knowledge, no former studies
have investigated the difference in longitudinal evolution of ERP components between
FHR groups concerning inhibitory control. Therefore, more research is needed to draw

connections between the ERP components and cognitive development in FHR groups.
We can hypothesize the same regarding N2. The inspected amplitudes decreased for
all groups, with the same tendency for PBC as in P3. However, the slightly larger N2
peak amplitude found longitudinal for FHR-BP compared to FHR-SZ and PBC contra-
dicts previous studies. These studies typically show that N2 amplitudes are lower in
SZ and BP patients compared to healthy controls [90].
But if our prior assumption is that cognitive development causes lower amplitudes, then
P3 and N2 must act independently. Based on this, we must suggest that FHR-BP and
FHR-SZ do not follow the N2 hallmarks of SZ and BP, indicating that the FHR groups’
initial stages of inhibition are not critically impaired, besides our suggestions highlighted
about P3.
We observed a cross-sectional gender difference in P3 peak amplitudes under both
conditions, consistent with previous research [91].
Several factors may have con-
tributed to the reduced ERP components in females, including those as young as 11
years. Attention, executive function, and spatial awareness are associated with hor-
monal variations, especially in progesterone and estradiol levels [91, 92]. These hor-
mones can influence neural development and processing [93]. Furthermore, there are
variances in the developmental trajectories across genders, where executive function
and attention mature at different rates in boys and girls [94].
5.3
Group Differences Are Not Driven By Impaired Motor
Preparation
Motor performance has previously been investigated in VIA7 and VIA11. These studies
evaluated motor skills using the Movement ABC-2, a standardized assessment com-
prising hand dexterity, aiming and catching, and balance. FHR-SZ displayed stable
reduced motor performance in manual dexterity, balance, and aiming and catching
compared to PBC. FHR-BP exhibited comparable motor performance to PBC, with
no notable disparities [95]. Other studies have found significant differences in motor
preparation with 50% reduced LRP amplitudes of SZ patients compared to controls,
while BP patients show no motor preparation deficits [43, 44].
We detected no differences in LRP amplitudes between FHR and PBC groups in our

cross-sectional or longitudinal analyses. This indicates that the decrease in motor per-
formance observed in the previous studies may not be linked to LRP amplitudes and
could be due to alternative mechanisms rather than motor preparation. However, we
are not able to quantify whether motor performance has had an impact on our results.
Nevertheless, our findings confirm that the observed behavioral and ERP components
are primarily linked to cognitive function, not motor preparation. The differences be-

opmental considerations. From 11 to 15 years of age, the FHR-SZ group may have
achieved parity with the PBC group regarding motor performance or motor preparation,
which a future follow-up study of motor performance may elicit.
We observed a notable LRP difference of age in our longitudinal assessment, which
may be attributed to the brain’s maturation from 11 to 15 years of age. However, to our
knowledge, this is the only study that has carried out a longitudinal assessment of LRPs
in adolescents, emphasizing the need for additional research to better understand the
development of LRP amplitudes and motor preparation in adolescents.

Impact of Task Congruency on Neural Connectivity
For the VIA11 assessment, our PEB analysis of the effect of congruency showed de-
creased connection strengths during incongruent trials in the high evident forward and
backward connections. These findings suggest that incongruent trials are generally
more demanding and allow for less efficient neural processing. This is also seen by
the incongruent trial’s lower accuracy and slower reaction time. With all the connection
differences being negative, there is a general indication that lower connection strengths
are seen throughout the multiple demand network during incongruent trials, which is
persistent with the literature [96, 97].
Since we observed a slight decrease in ERP components in the congruent condition,
we suggest that higher connectivity, hence more efficient neural processing, influences
the allocation of cognitive resources.
In the longitudinal PEB analysis combining VIA11 and VIA15 data, we observed that
three out of five evident differences in connection strengths associated with task con-
gruency (regressor 1) persisted over time. Specifically, the previously identified pattern

Interestingly, only one connectivity difference changed from VIA11 to VIA15, as seen in

and must, therefore, be present in VIA15. This was the only age-related difference
found between VIA11 and VIA15, possibly indicating neurodevelopmental changes re-

We found that the ERP component amplitudes decreased with age and that behav-

5.4.2
Complex Connectivity Patterns in FHR Groups
The found differences between the FHR groups and PBC (regressor 2) were both pos-
itive and negative. Hence, it is hard to assert that there is a one-sided cognitive distinc-
tion in our ROIs between being at FHR or PBC. However, we do observe weaker for-
ward and backward connections in the integration between spatial attention, executive
function, and spatial perception. This points to the FHR groups exhibiting alterations
in neural connections despite their young age. Our findings align with prior research
indicating altered connectivity in individuals with FHR for SZ [98].
It is again important to note that the results are not necessarily due to neurodevelop-
mental alterations caused by being at FHR. However, to try and suggest an explanation
for the larger connection strengths in FHR, the activation of compensatory neural mech-
anisms is plausible. Considering these areas’ involvement in high cognitive processing,
we suggest that the positive connections play a more active role in compensating for
the observed negative connections. Furthermore, the increased connection between
the R-PrCG and the R-MFG in the FHR group suggests a similar compensatory pattern,
describing an elevated level of coordination between motor and cognitive functions.
Nevertheless, the differences in connectivity between the PBC and FHR groups can not
be linked to RT and accuracy, as we found no behavioral group differences. However,
the disparities in CVRT between the groups could be due to the attentional deficits

Between the two FHR groups (regressor 3), the positive forward and backward con-

increased connectivity in the integration between cognitive functions and motor control
while no group differences in motor preparation were found. This is quite ambiguous,
but we speculate that it could be held responsible for some kind of overcompensation
associated with the known motor performance deficits that the FHR group exhibits. One
might hypothesize that this overcompensation can decrease LRP amplitudes, which
BP and SZ are known to develop [43, 44], given that the FHR groups develop the
disorders.

5.4.3
Gender Differences in Connectivity and Cognitive Performance
Several studies investigating functional connectivity in the multiple demand network
have found gender-related differences during inhibitory control tasks. Our results align
with the general trend showing stronger connectivity in females [86, 102].
Females indicated greater integration of neural processing between spatial perception
and motor control, attention, and executive function as they had higher forward con-
nection strengths. These higher cognitive functions might explain why females showed
higher accuracy in our thesis. Furthermore, it may help understand why studies sug-
gest that females generally perform better on tasks requiring sustained attention and
inhibitory control [91]. If the connection strength differences could be related to the
FHR groups, they might stem from males’ earlier onset of the disorders [11–13].
The differences between genders could also simply be explained by females typically
entering puberty before males and, therefore, experiencing earlier brain maturation
[91].
5.4.4
Variability in Cognitive Development Within VIA11
Similar to the behavioral analysis, we found evident differences within the VIA11 age
span. Again, this is most likely caused by the variability of when young adolescents
undergo brain development.
There should preferably be no differences within the same age group, as we wanted
to focus only on the age-related changes happening between ages 11 and 15. When
these forward connections clearly indicate that the VIA11 subjects are at different cog-
nitive levels, this makes it more difficult to compare VIA11 to VIA15 as it introduces
confounding to the results. Principally, this can be viewed as a source of error, as
age should be a controlling covariate with isolated ages and not make the observed
differences possibly be influenced by the variation within age.

5.5
Challenges and Considerations in DCM
The selection of ROIs for the first level DCM analysis within subjects is known to be one
of the most challenging parts of DCM, as these are absolutely crucial for the forward
model’s outcome. The regions must be chosen based on both anatomical and func-
tional validity, and in theory, there is no limit to the number of models one can set up
[103]. Therefore, defining such models involves extensive knowledge about the brain
regions involved in inhibitory control and the multiple demand network. Our selection of
the ROIs and connections in the A and B matrices were motivated biologically through
research and discussion with neurologists. We settled on choosing two models with
four and six ROIs, respectively. Comparing these by BMS turned out in favor of our six-
ROI model. This result was not unexpected, as increasing model complexity typically
leads to a better fit but a higher risk of overfitting [104]. An overfitted model captures
the noise instead of underlying patterns in the data, leading to poor performance as it
would fail to be generalizable. On the other hand, selecting a model of low complexity
may oversimplify the brain’s connectivity, potentially leading to high bias and, therefore,
not capturing critical interactions [105].
5.6
Future Work
Longitudinal studies are particularly valuable for elucidating the developmental trajecto-
ries of neural connectivity and inhibitory control. They provide a deeper understanding
of how these processes evolve over time and how they are influenced by genetic and
environmental factors. It would be valuable to include data from additional time points
besides VIA11 and VIA15 in our longitudinal analyses. Including additional time points,
such as at ages 17 and 19, could provide a more comprehensive understanding of neu-
ral maturation during late adolescence and early adulthood. Excitingly, the VIA19 study
is set to commence the data collection in August 2024, offering a perfect opportunity to
compare our results to newly gathered insights of the VIA19 subjects. The possibility
that some subjects actually develop BP or SZ during the later time points could further
enrich our understanding of the neural and behavioral changes in executive control.
Having the option to backtrack the differences in the subjects who develop BP or SZ
would allow us to examine the specific changes through adolescence compared to a
PBC group.

CHALLENGES AND CONSIDERATIONS IN DCM
52 of 70
==PAGE:64==
DCM predicts interregional brain connection strengths from ERP data by a model in-
version, as described in the theory section. It would be interesting to expand our under-
standing of how, or if, changes in these connection strengths would impact the P3 and
N2 ERP components. Measuring the magnitude of the impact caused by changing the
identified connection, which differed between time points, would be especially valuable,
as it would help verify some of our suggestions. This could be achieved by specifying or
manipulating connection strengths to predict the resulting ERP. Hence, it would provide
valuable insights into not only which but also how alterations in brain connectivity dif-
fer between risk groups. Another future implementation could be leave-one-out (LOO)
cross-validation, as we have identified several evident group differences. From these,
it is interesting to ask: Is the difference in a specific connection large enough to pre-
dict the FHR group of an unknown subject? LOO cross-validation might be able to
give the DCM and PEB models a sense of clinical relevance in the future, serving as
a tool to predict individuals’ high-risk group status. LOO cross-validation could also be
a valuable tool to estimate if our models are generalizable, i.e., if the model has been
overfitted and how the estimates fit on other datasets than the VIA11 and VIA15 [60].

Conclusion
Our thesis aimed to identify and characterize unique differences in cognition and neu-
rodevelopment in adolescents at FHR for SZ and BP. Using mixed effects models,
we assessed flanker task interference control in 11-year-olds by analyzing behavior
and ERP component amplitudes. The DCM method was successfully implemented
to analyze inter-region connectivity in the multiple demand network. Additionally, we
performed a longitudinal assessment between ages 11 and 15.
We found significant effects of task congruency regarding all levels of analysis. Un-
der incongruent conditions, reaction times and accuracy worsened, ERP component
amplitudes decreased, and lower connectivity was observed. Together, these findings
confirm that solving tasks related to inhibitory control requires larger cognitive efforts.
We also found significant gender differences in the trade-off between RT and accu-
racy. In addition, females exhibited lower P3 amplitudes and stronger connectivity,
consistent with literature suggesting that females mature earlier in terms of neurode-
velopment. Furthermore, we found significant age-related differences over time, with
performance improving and ERP component amplitudes decreasing. This aligns with
expected neurodevelopment during adolescence. However, the interaction between
age and group for P3 revealed that PBC experienced the most substantial neurodevel-
opmental change.
Regarding our main goal, we found group differences where FHR exhibited decreased
ERP components and altered connectivity. Especially FHR-SZ differed, displaying de-
creased connectivity compared to FHR-BP and increased CVRT, indicating decreased
sustained attention. These findings suggest that FHR exhibits neurodevelopmental dif-
ferences and that FHR-SZ is more impacted by its risk status than FHR-BP. We found
no group differences in terms of motor preparation, validating that our results reflect
inhibitory control and suggesting that motor performance does not correlate with motor
preparation. Despite the tendencies suggesting FHR has altered neurodevelopment,
and FHR-SZ is the most impacted group, the indications are ambiguous when con-
sidering all our results in conjunction. Hence, we can not confirm how the alterations
manifest in inhibitory control.
Finally, our thesis greatly contributes to understanding neural connectivity in the multi-

and possible replication of the results are needed for verification. However, further
research and study replications are needed to validate our results and ensure broad
applicability, with the upcoming VIA19 study holding promise in that regard.
