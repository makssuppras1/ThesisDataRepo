Abstract
Among the heavy industries, iron and steel is the largest contributor to industrial green­
house gas emissions, largely driven by its production, which is still heavily reliant on fossil
fuels. With ongoing industrial development across most countries, the demand for steel
is steadily increasing. Numerous academic studies have reported a wide range of tech­
nologies aimed at transitioning carbon­intensive production toward green steel manufac­
turing. It is therefore imperative for steel­producing companies to adopt these green steel
manufacturing practices. This study aims to develop a support to guide companies to
their transition towards green steel manufacturing. The guideline structures technologies
identified through a systematic literature review into a decision tree, highlighting key at­
tributes such as Technology Readiness Level, capital expenditure, and decarbonization
potential, to support prioritization, investment decision­making and avoid carbon lock­in.
Employing design research methodology in combination with hotspot analysis enhances
the guideline’s relevance and usability. This approach has been evaluated in collaboration
with four industry experts to assess the guideline in terms of its usefulness, usability and
trustworthiness. Input from evaluators, though limited in user representativeness, pro­
vided valuable insights that reinforced the practical relevance of the support and informed
several refinements to enhance its usability and alignment with industry needs.

Introduction
Background and Motivation
Steel is the foundation of modern life and it comes with a substantial environmental cost.
Its production process heavily relies on fossil fuel, thereby emitting greenhouse gas emis­
sions that is in fact roughly twice as much as the steel produced itself. The global demand
for steel stands at around 1.9 billion tonnes per year and accounts for 7 to 9% of anthro­
pogenic greenhouse gas emissions, which is greater than Russia or the entire European
Union (Rumsa, John, and Biswas 2025; Cornwall 2024).
Most of goods and services either directly consume steel as its primary material compo­
nent, or are manufactured by steel­based equipment (Rumsa, John, and Biswas 2025).
To reach net zero by 2050, the steel sector must be among the earliest to decarbonise,
creating pathways for other industrial products to follow. However, steel companies are
falling short in their climate target. In fact, not even half of the 50 largest steel producer
companies across the globe have set an emissions reduction goal for 2030, despite being
responsible for more than 60% of the total sector emissions. The remainder either have
no publicly available information, or have not stated specific emissions target by 2050
(Torres Morales and Birch 2024).
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA) (2025) the steel industry would be
one of the last sectors to still be using coal. There are however already numerous tech­
nologies, approaching readiness, with the potential of accelerating the steel sector’s net
zero targets, as would be discussed in this study. To avert long­term reliance on fossil­
based systems, companies must invest on these technologies, that would be collectively
referred to as green steel manufacturing. However, as will be highlighted in this study,
there are over 50 of these technologies that suggest to fulfil this gap of green steel manu­
facturing. This may be an overwhelming number of technologies for those companies that
have not set an emissions target by 2030. As also mentioned by IEA (2025), many coun­
tries are looking into introducing policies that focus on decarbonisation. Steel­producing
companies would eventually need to invest in green steel manufacturing not only for en­
vironmental reasons but also to meet regulatory compliance.
This study highlights the need for a guideline that would aid companies transitioning to
green steel manufacturing. By employing design research methodology, the process of
developing this guideline was able to iteratively integrate empirical insights with theoreti­
cal frameworks to ensure relevance and applicability to companies transitioning to green
steel manufacturing. This study therefore builds upon existing literature by (1) developing
a green steel manufacturing guideline structured around the design research methodology
framework; (2) combining a hotspot analysis to systematically determine the environmen­
tal impacts of steel manufacturing; and (3) limiting the focus of green steel manufacturing
to decarbonisation practices.

Methodology
Through a structured approach of conducting research, design research has a specific
goal of formulating and validating models and theories to eventually develop and as­
sess a support that helps improve design in practice and education (Bing and Chakrabarti
2009). The Design Research Methodology (DRM) by Bing and Chakrabarti (2009) has
been chosen in this study as the broader methodological framework to develop a green
steel manufacturing guideline. In a nutshell, DRM facilitates a more effective and efficient
approach of conducting design research through four specific stages, namely Research
Clarification, Descriptive Study­I, Prescriptive Study and Descriptive Study­II (Figure 1.1).

As green steel manufacturing is closely tied to sustainability, adopting a systematic ap­
proach helps in identifying issues and developing a targeted support. Given that several
of the learning objectives (LO2, LO3, LO4) of this study involves Hotspot Analysis (HSA),
this approach has been integrated with the flexible framework of DRM. A hotspot is “a
discrete activity within a single life cycle stage of a product category that creates rela­
tively high impacts compared to other life cycle stages” (Barthel et al. 2014, p.65). This
study utilizes HSA to gain insights into steel production and its associated environmental
impacts. These impacts underscore the critical challenges faced within steel manufactur­
ing.
There is no single way to perform an HSA and the process adopted in this study follows
the methodological framework outlined by Barthel et al. (2014) . The HSA is composed of
eight steps, however, as this study tailors these steps into the DRM stages, only the first
six steps were incorporated, accordingly (Figure 1.2).
Research Clarification. The initial stage involves delineating the research problem and
context. The aim is to establish a clear understanding of the underlying challenges, justify
the relevance of the study, and define research objectives. Step 1: Define Goal and Scope
and Step 2: Gather data, seek expert insight were instrumental in framing the study’s
focus and identifying key areas of concern.
Descriptive Study I. This stage is concerned with examining the current state of practice
in order to establish an empirical foundation for the research. This involves a system­
atic analysis of existing systems, processes, and stakeholder dynamics to systematically
identify knowledge gaps. The outcome is a structured and context­specific understand­
ing of the problem. Step 3: Identify and validate hotspots and Step 4: Respond to data
and stakeholder gaps, served as conceptual guidance for addressing the concerns of this
stage in a systematic manner.
Prescriptive Study. Building on the insights from descriptive study I, this stage is con­
cerned with the development of potential solutions, through what is called a support. The
support may take the form of a model, guideline, method, or tool aimed at addressing
the identified problems. The prescriptive phase is grounded in both theoretical insights
and empirical findings from the previous phase. This stage is guided by Step 5: Identify
and prioritize actions to ensure that the proposed support were relevant and actionable in
relation to the the specific hotspots determined.
Descriptive Study II. The second descriptive study involves the evaluation and refine­
ment of the proposed support through empirical application. The goal is to assess the
support against defined criteria within practical contexts. Step 6: Review and Validate
Findings with Key Stakeholders and Experts aligns well with the purpose of this stage, as
it emphasises the importance of evaluating the proposed support in real­world contexts.
This step guided the involvement of stakeholders and experts in reviewing the outcomes,
thereby contributing to the validation and refinement of the support.
Structure of this Thesis
As such, the rest of this report is conveniently structured with the stages of DRM: 2 Re­
search Clarification; 3 Descriptive Study I; 4 Prescriptive Study; and 5 Descriptive Study
II. Following these is 6 Discussion and Conclusion.

Research Clarification
The research clarification stage would formally leverage the first step of the HSA, Define
Goal and Scope by answering the what, why, how, who, where and when of this study.
This would assist in clarifying the focus of the study; developing the research questions;
and selecting the research type for this study. Thereafter, the HSA would materialize with
Step 2, that describes the process of gathering data.
Step 1: Define Goal and Scope
The goal and scope identifies the purpose of the HSA, the issues to be addressed, the
boundaries of the analysis, the resources required and the approach to stakeholder
engagement. Ultimately, the goal and scope can be summarised as answering the
questions of why, who, what, how, where and when, although the order in which these
are defined is not fixed and the process of finding answers will most likely be iterative.
Work during the goal and scope phase sets the tone for the rest of the project so will
ultimately decide whether the analysis delivers on its aims (Barthel et al. 2014, p.54).
What?
The industry for which this thesis aims to develop a guideline is steel manufacturing. Steel
is a versatile alloy of iron and carbon, containing less than 2% carbon, 1% manganese
and residual levels of silicon, phosphorus, sulphur and oxygen (World Steel Association
2025b). As majority of its composition is iron, several guidelines refer to the steel sector
as “the iron and steel industry” and thereby supporting initiatives that target both segments
collectively. Multiple guidelines available for green steel manufacturing identified in both
grey literature, such as company initiatives and government reports, and academic papers
were classified under two types and are described as follows.
Firstly, to promote accountability and transparency, there is a phletora of guidelines set by
certification bodies to document steel companies sustainability metrics, such as those by
International Financial Reporting Standards (2025), Low Emission Steel Standard (2024),
ArcelorMittal (2025), Global Steel Climate Council (2025). While these certification bod­
ies address varied needs and preferences of organisations, industries, and stakeholders,
they too often exhibit redundancy. Frequently, these guidelines request similar scope
in terms of emissions or Environmental, Social, Governance scores, that fulfil the same
objective. Despite this, they often propose alternative approaches, such as calculating
the scope of emissions using different system boundaries, which fundamentally leads to
calculation redundancies and inconsistencies in results. This has been stressed out by
a report by IEA (2023, p.7), highlighting the need for a “common emissions measure­
ment methodology” to “achieve robust, transparent, and comparable emissions intensity
data for steel production and products internationally”. The guideline being developed in
this study therefore does not aim to establish standards for calculating and declaring the
impacts of steel. Instead, it utilises the data derived from these pre­existing calculations.
Secondly, there have also been many “technology roadmaps” that suggest several strate­
gies to transition towards sustainable steel production routes that are most often based
on an analysis of several scenarios (Table 2.1). A similar purpose expressed by these
roadmaps can be reflected by the guideline being developed in this study. However, these
roadmaps are generally broad with a high level of detail (assessed according to number
of scenarios, range of technologies included, and scope of methodologies included), and

do not align with a design­research perspective. A design­research perspective allows
for a more intuitive, accessible and practical guideline. Accessibility and practicality were
central in developing the guideline, as it is intended to support practitioners, especially

would be explained in Who?. In contrast, most existing roadmaps target experienced

serve its intended purpose; or are perceived to be too abstract; or may not entirely align
with strategic and technological planning, pointing potential mismatch between the tool
and user needs. This further emphasises the need for a design approach that explores
how such tools are interpreted, adapted, or overlooked by users, taking special atten­

Why?
The most common method for producing steel is an energy­intensive process, greatly
reliant on the heavy use of fossil fuels. As such, it is inevitable that the steel industry
has significant environmental impacts as demonstrated by several Life Cycle Assessment
(LCA) studies: Conejo, Birat, and A. Dutta 2020; Olmez et al. 2016; Yellishetty et al. 2011;
Burchart­Korol 2013; Liang et al. 2020. These studies identified several impacts of the
steel industry to climate change, air and water pollution and, resource depletion. More
critically, its carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions represent a major share of its environmental
impact, contributing to 2.6 gigatonnes of CO2 (Gt CO2) in 2019 that accounts for nearly
one­quarter of global industrial CO2 emissions and 7% of the total emissions from the
energy sector (International Energy Agency 2020).
It is therefore imperative that the steel sector shifts to sustainable methods of production.
This transition began early, as highlighted by Björn Nykvist, a researcher at Stockholm
Environment Institute, in an article published by the Sweco Group. He raises concerns
associated with the transition, one of which is the challenge of identifying the optimal
choice among various technologies (Sweco Group 2024). This concern has also been
presented by the in­depth Systematic Literature Review (SLR) conducted as part of an on­
going project, “Green options for sustainable exploitation of iron ore reserves in Uganda”.
This SLR was carried out in September 2024, analyzing 52 articles based on Scopus and
Web of Science databases, that resulted to over 50 green steel manufacturing technolo­
gies (Figure 2.1).
Figure 2.1 classifies these technologies into 17 “design­for­sustainability” best practices1
that are further grouped into broader categories that reflect its overall strategy for sus­
tainable steel production (i.e., decarbonisation, resource recirculation, energy reduction,
resource optimisation and process optimisation). While over 50% of these design­for­
sustainability best practices relate to decarbonisation, there are also other strategies that
underpin the broad scope of technologies that encompass green steel manufacturing.
HSA has been identified to be an effective method for narrowing down the scope of tech­
nologies for green steel manufacturing, providing a comprehensive understanding to “sus­
tainability challenges [...] in an era of information overload” (Barthel et al. 2014, p.9).
These technologies would then serve to define the primary requirements to develop the
support. This HSA further assists the identification of life cycle stages and environmental
impacts that contribute significantly during the production of steel. These results allow
for a more comprehensive discussion on how these technologies could effectively target
these impacts.
1Design­for­sustainability best practices can be defined as any process, action, technology or system

Who?
The most readily accessible stakeholders given the geographical context and timeline of
this study are experts in the steel industry, including both academics and practitioners in
Europe. In general, these stakeholders are regarded to be well­informed in the context
of green steel manufacturing, not only owing to their professional expertise, but also due
to the commitment and involvement of Europe in advancing sustainability within the steel
industry. Since 2018, countries have been investing on so­called low­carbon projects as
consolidated by LeadIT Green Steel Tracker (Figure 2.2). It is significant that most of these
projects are largely driven by European countries, followed by some developing parts of
Asia. Exemplary projects in Europe are ULCOS (European Commission (CORDIS) 2009)
and HYBRIT (Hybrit Development AB (SSAB 2025).
Figure 2.2: Low­carbon projects announced according to LeadIT Green Steel Tracker
from 2018 to this year. Source: based on data from LeadIT (2025).
While the growth of low­carbon projects was steady and significant until 2021, invest­
ments have declined since, with few new projects announced this year. Regardless, just
recently the European Commission has provided €175 million to support collaborative re­
search in the steel and coal sectors. The development and diffusion of these green steel
project in Europe can suggest that these stakeholders operate within stronger frameworks
and support structures for sustainability transition. Practitioners that operate under such
contexts are more likely ahead in implementing structured sustainability strategies, and
can likely expect comprehensive and technically robust guidelines, that are often devel­
oped with R&D incentives and cross­sector collaboration. Their primary constraints can
be technological maturity and accessibility to affordable clean energy, rather than a lack
of understanding or commitment. In less mature contexts, however, there may be an
expectation that such guidelines should instead be pragmatic and easy to adopt within
existing industrial constraints. These insights reinforce the importance of designing a
guideline that addresses a contextual support gap, particularly for practitioners within or­
ganisations, that lack a mature sustainability framework and are therefore in the earlier
stages of transitioning to green steel manufacturing.

Where?
While the stakeholders considered in this study has been limited to those in Europe, the
support (i.e., guideline) should have a global focus. The first reason being that emissions
are not necessarily confined to a single region, as the steel production value chain and
its customers often span multiple countries.
The second and most important reason is that as reflected in Figure 2.2, companies in
its early stages of transition are found in other regions than in Europe, where low­carbon
projects are saturated. Furthermore, investments made for blast furnace projects far ex­
ceed low­carbon projects, especially in India, that is the second largest steel producing
country as of May 2025 (Figure 2.3).

To fully align with the needs of potential users of this guideline, this study adopts a global
scope, recognizing that the challenges and opportunities associated with green steel man­
ufacturing vary significantly across regions. While this study draws on perspectives from
the European context, that has emerged as an early adopter of green steel manufactur­
ing, it strives to maintain global relevance, by addressing broadly shared challenges rather
than anchoring its framework in region­specific policies or specific institutional context.
When?
The technologies relevant to green steel manufacturing, such as those selected as CO2
mitigation technologies by European Commission have been mapped to reach full­scale
deployment or commercialization (a Technology Readiness Level (TRL) of 7­9) in the
midterm, between 2030­2040 (Table 2.3). This indicates that, in the midterm, major tech­
nological breakthroughs are less likely than incremental efficiency improvements or scale­
up refinements. This suggests that the period from the short to midterm represents a
critical window for integration and scale­up, rather than radical innovation. This guideline
is therefore timely for organisations at their early phases of transitioning to green steel,
where informed investment choices and structured planning are critical to long­term sus­
tainability integration. By providing a structured and practical guideline for these organ­
isations, this guideline promotes the avoidance of long­term lock­in to carbon­intensive

activities, the two pathways are described as follows:
Blast Furnace­Basic Oxygen Furnace. The traditional means of commercially produc­
ing steel is via the the integration of the BF and BOF. The BF­BOF pathway is a combina­
tion of the BF, which produces pig iron, that is then charged into the BOF to produce crude
steel. The raw material inputs for the BF undergo a process called burden preparation,
in which necessary steps, such as crushing and agglomeration of iron ore, along with the
careful selection and preparation of coke and additives (e.g., limestone) are undertaken
to ensure that the properties of these materials are optimised for efficient steel produc­
tion. This burden is then “loaded” down through the conical shaft of the BF, and goes
through varying layers of temperature called “thermal reaction zones” (Schlegel 2023,
p.117), requiring significant amount of heat and energy, with a minimum energy use of
10.4 gigajoules per tonne of steel (GJ/tonne) (International Energy Agency 2007). With
coke acting as a reducing agent, this forms carbon monoxide with the air, which reduces
the iron oxide present in the ore, resulting to molten pig iron (Schlegel 2023, p.117). The
pig iron is then transferred to the basic oxygen furnace, where pure oxygen is blown in to
remove excess carbon and impurities, releasing further carbon emissions in the process
(Schlegel 2023, p.117). While the BF­BOF route is a carbon and energy intensive route,
with an emissions factor of 2.2 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of crude steel, it also remains to
be the dominant steel­making pathway, accounting for approximately 75% of global steel
production (Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA) 2022; World
Steel Association 2021a).
Electric Arc Furnace. The remaining 25% is produced via the secondary pathway, EAF.
The EAF offers a sustainable alternative to producing steel with its main feedstock being
scrap steel. Scrap steel is obtained from many different sources, as “steel can be recycled
again and again without the loss of key properties such as strength, ductility or formability”
(European Commission 2013, p.9). There are three main sources of scrap steel: those
that are internally generated during the production of steel (home scrap); those that are
produced from steel­products manufacturing plants (prompt or new scrap); and those that
are recovered after the end of a steel product’s life (obsolete scrap) (Yellishetty et al. 2011).
Most of the world’s (about two thirds) supply of steel are from obsolete sources, that often
contain “undesirable elements in alloys and coatings or commingled with other metals
and non­metallic materials” (Smil 2016,
p.115­138). Recycled steel, that is magnetic
can be easily separated from its non­magnetic components. However, obsolete steel
whose surfaces, due to rusting, may be heavily contaminated by toxic compounds, such
as tin, copper nickel, chromium, or molybdenum. As these admixture of other metals
and presence of hazardous compounds cannot be separated from steel itself, the most
practical solution would be to dilute their presence by mixing in greater amounts of high­
purity scrap or pig iron (Smil 2016). While EAF represents a promising way of green
steel manufacturing, one of its significant bottlenecks is its reliance on high­quality scrap,
that is a scarce resource. This is primarily attributed to the long average lifespan of steel
products, which is approximately 40 years (World Steel Association 2021b). This causes
a significant delay between its production and availability for recycling. Furthermore, the
melting of scrap steel is conducted using the electrical energy of the arc. Modern EAFs,
on average, consume between 380 and 600 killo­watt hours (kWh) of electrical energy to
produce one tonne of steel (Carlsson, P. B. Samuelsson, and Jönsson 2019). This may
be a source of indirect emissions, if electricity is not generated from renewable sources.
Despite this, its overall emission intensity is still negligible in comparison to the BF­BOF
(Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA) 2022).

Discussion I: Limitations of LCA in EPDs
The reliance of EPDs in LCA presents the need for granular material­specific LCA mod­
els to calculate the environmental impacts of steel (García Gutiérrez et al. 2024). LCA
databases such as ecoinvent, only provide information on the three generic types of steel:
carbon, alloyed, and stainless, that significantly differ from the “actual composition of the
over 3500 grades of steel on the market” (García Gutiérrez et al. 2024, p.2). As further
explained in the article: “...when calculating the environmental impact of a product con­
taining steel, it is common to approximate the actual steel grade with one of the three
generic steel grades available in the database (García Gutiérrez et al. 2024, p.2). This
lack of discretization of the environmental impact according to the composition not only
incurs an approximation but also hinders the decision­making process when selecting
one steel grade or another” (García Gutiérrez et al. 2024, p.2). Lastly, several papers
have criticised the difficulty in comparing data from EPDs, irrespective of having a PCR
(Olanrewaju et al. 2025; Konradsen et al. 2024; Moré, Galindro, and Soares 2022). For
instance, the paper by Moré, Galindro, and Soares (2022) reported that when assessing
the completeness of EPDs based on one PCR / sub­PCR, only 5.04% was reported to
comply with the requirements stipulated in International Organization for Standardization
(ISO) 14025. It further broadens the issue stating that the comparability between EPDs is
low, due to inconsistent methodology; loosely­defined elements such as functional units,

Hotspot Insights: Determining Gaps and Support Requirements
At this point of the study, it is important to reflect on the data gathered based on Step 3
of this analysis, and to bring these together into a consolidated perspective that would
determine the gaps to be addressed by the support, and thereby its requirements.
It was found that there are consistent hotspots found for the product types analysed which
are: AP, ADP­Fossil and minerals & metals, EP­freshwater and GWP. Among the find­
ings it was found that GWP or impacts to climate change appeared most frequently, and
was recurring in core processes for BF­BOF and upstream for EAF. Furthermore, when
participants were asked about the most important indicator in the evaluation of EPDs,
57% of them agreed that GWP remains to be the most important or often the only in­
dicator they focused on, particularly when presenting EPD based data to stakeholders.
Most companies also produce carbon steel, which is inherently linked to higher carbon
emissions.
Based on these findings, the support should focus on Climate Change impacts. It is
therefore reasonable to prioritize green steel manufacturing practices that are centred on
decarbonisation. Employing decarbonisation strategies could mitigate climate change im­
pacts by reducing or eliminating anthropogenic CO2 or greenhouse gas emissions. This is
consistent with the findings in the Research Clarification stage, wherein decarbonisation
was found to be the frequently cited category of design­for­sustainability best practices
(Figure 2.1). This has also been highlighted as the strategy related to most terms / concept
related to green steel manufacturing (Table 2.2). Therefore, in this study, decarbonisation
serves as the overarching focus of green steel manufacturing in the development of the
guideline. When asked about the factors that companies consider when adopting sustain­
ability and circularity practices, participants mentioned Cost and economic feasibility and
Policy and incentives as key factors. However, as stated in the Research Clarification, the
support should serve a global scope, therefore although it is of great importance, the role
of policies and incentives has been omitted to reduce regional bias. Irregardless, other
factors included that were determined to be of importance were: Supply chain and raw
material availability and Technology readiness and reliability. To strengthen the relevance
of the support, these factors are also included as requirements of the support.
The following are the gaps or requirements that the support should address:
• The support should include green steel manufacturing practices related to decar­
bonisation, specifically designed for companies producing carbon steel.
• The support should reflect accessible data of factors such as cost and economic
feasibility; supply chain and raw material availability; and technology readiness and
reliability of the green steel manufacturing practices.
• The support should serve a global scope.

due to a lack of data, or data of insufficient quality.(Barthel et al. 2014, p.60).

The SLR provided valuable data for key decarbonisation technologies that can be utilised
for developing the supporting framework (Table 3.10). Representing information on cost
and economic feasibility is the capital investment costs (most commonly referred to as
Capital Expenditures (CAPEX)) – that is the cost to acquire or upgrade a physical asset
such as equipment or property (Lea and Rowlan 2019). This cost is often reported in
techno­economic studies, expressed as a unit of cost per tonne of steel (in dollars or any
relevant currency). This enables a meaningful cost comparison across multiple technolo­
gies, each with varying capacity levels (a larger plant may have a higher CAPEX than that
of a smaller plant). By expressing CAPEX as a normalized unit of measure, the influence
of plant size is removed, offering a clearer picture of the relative cost­effectiveness of each
technology. Data collected related to capital investment costs is based on present­day
data and does not account for inflationary effects or future value adjustments. Decar­
bonisation potential (also called CO2 abatement potential) is expressed as a percentage.
Except for green HDRI­EAF, which compares to both EAF and BF–BOF, all decarbonisa­
tion references are relative to the BF–BOF route. It is important to note that the decar­
bonisation potential is assumed to address all emission scopes, including both direct and
indirect sources. The scale of technological development is usually expressed through
the numeric Technology Readiness Level (TRL) and is nicely summarized by Innovation
Fund Denmark (2019). All decarbonisation technologies included exhibit a TRL above
5, with the exception of those technologies combined with CCS (Keramidas, Mima, and
Bidaud 2023).
Hydrogen Direct Reduction of Iron Ore.
The Direct Reduction of Iron Ore (DRI) pro­
cess refers to the reduction iron oxide, in the form of iron ore into metallic iron to avoid its
conversion into liquid phase (Bhaskar, Assadi, and Somehsaraei 2020; Barati 2014). The
process involves iron ore in the form of pellet or fines that is fed into the shaft furnace and
is carried out at temperatures below the melting point of iron and typically in the range of
800–1200 °C (Logist et al. 2011). Traditional DRI process primarily involves reductants
generated from the partial oxidation of coal; reformation of methane; or partial combus­
tion of natural gas – all of which resulting to greater carbon emissions than the EAF route
(Barati 2014; Basirat and Nicholas 2022).

The idea behind HDRI is to eliminate carbon from the traditional process by sourcing the
reductant exclusively from 100% hydrogen. In the past century, this has been proven via
a two­stage fluidized bed process, known as Cincored (Weigel et al. 2016). To eliminate
a two stage process, fluidized bed and shaft furnace direct reduction processes have also
been demonstrated feasible. The latter was proven at a commercial level through a plant
called Trinidad, but has remained idle since 2010 for techno­economic reasons. Several
projects to employ 100% hydrogen via the shaft furnace direct reduction process, such
as Midrex and HYL/Energiron (Hydrogen content of 55­86%) processes demonstrate the
potential for significant technological breakthrough (Wang et al. 2021). While these have
exhibited the large­scale potential of HDRI technology, one of its major bottlenecks is its
dependence on low­cost green hydrogen. At present, 96% of global hydrogen is obtained
from fossil fuels, resulting to major CO2 emissions (Mac Dowell et al. 2021). Furthermore,
HDRI production may only be sustainably viable in areas with restricted availability of high
quality scrap (Schenk, Schultmann, and Offermann­van Heek 2011), as the absence of
carbon, results in an endothermic reaction, thereby raising the melting point of iron and
necessitating substantial amount of heat (Ling et al. 2024). If green hydrogen­based direct
reduced iron is combined with scrap steel are fed into the renewable based­EAF, then it
has a potential of reducing carbon emissions by up to 84% (Superchi et al. 2023).

CCS technologies involves capturing CO2, generally from
large industrial point sources that use either fossil fuels or biomass as fuel. If not be­
ing directly used on­site (referred to as Carbon Capture Utilisation and Storage), then
captured CO2 is either compressed and transported, via pipeline, ship, rail or truck, or
directly injected in deep underground geological formations, either offshore or onshore,
that is intended for permanent storage (International Energy Agency 2024). CCS is ad­
vantageous as it can be retrofitted with existing industrial plants, without interrupting cur­

2023, p.6) or through fuel gasification with oxygen (e.g., fuel integrated cost gasification

the post­combustion stage, as the majority of emissions arise from the blast furnaces,
which are integral to core processes1 (Amand, Tahmisoğlu, and Woods 2024). These
carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies encompass methods such as physical
separation, chemical adsorption, cryogenic processes, and membrane separation, all of
which capture CO2 from flue or exhaust gases (Madejski et al. 2022). Post­combustion
capture represents the only viable solution for existing coal­fired units (Madejski et al.

combustion capture methods. However, there are still several disadvantages associated
with integrating CCS technologies, especially post­combustion methods. One issue is the
indirect emissions generated to power these technologies, particularly when fossil fuels
are used (Amand, Tahmisoğlu, and Woods 2024, p.27). There is also a limited availabil­

emissions associated with transporting captured CO2 (Amand, Tahmisoğlu, and Woods

Smelting reduction­BOF (Smelt).
The significance of replacing BF with smelting re­
duction is the avoidance of using either coke or high­grade iron as the raw material in
the iron making process. The fundamental concept of smelting reduction is to melt the
pre­reduced iron ore / pellets with non­coking coal and oxygen in a reactor (S. K. Dutta
and Chokshi 2020). With the avoidance of processes required for BF such as coking and
ore preparations, smelting reduction consume less energy than the upstream processes
for the blast furnace and also reduces overall greenhouse gas emissions. The smelting
reduction process include one­, two­ and three step processes. Most frequently men­
tioned in several papers, including in the SLR is the two­step process, where the process
is divided to separate the solid­phase and molten­state of the iron. This type of process
include COREX, Finex, HISmelt, and HIsarna processes (Mansheng and Xu 2024).
Electrowinning.
Electrowinning involves dissolving iron ore in a suitable electrolyte
from which pure iron is recovered through electrolysis and deposited onto a cathode,
often called electrodeposition (Kavalsky and Viswanathan 2024). Although claimed to be
a promising sustainable iron­making technology, there are still several areas by which this
technology can be improved upon. Firstly, the dissolution process remain to be an ongo­
ing line of research, whereas this could be bypassed, the approach via bulk conversion
is limited by the iron ore particle size (Kavalsky and Viswanathan 2024). Furthermore,
the electrodeposition process is constrained by the inevitable occurrence of Hydrogen
Evolution Reaction, resulting to low concentration of deposited iron (Boehme et al. 2023).
Electrowinning is gaining attention due to ULCOwin, that is a sub project under the Ultra­
Low Carbon Dioxide (CO2) Steel­making program – a joint initiative by 48 EU companies
and organisations from 15 EU countries and as supported by the European Commission.
ULCOwin is a low­temperature (100~110 °C) alkaline electrolysis process to produce iron
ore that can be used as an input into EAF with scrap steel. The demonstration of this tech­
nology is currently limited to the laboratory; however, a proof of concept has already been
developed (West 2020; Yan 2018; Abdul Quader et al. 2016).
Discussion II: DfC Practices for Green Steel Manufacturing
Among the key decarbonisation technologies, scrap or low­carbon based EAF already
stands out as the most viable option, offering the highest decarbonisation potential, the
lowest CAPEX, and relatively low raw material requirements (Table 3.10). However as
already frequently mentioned, its reliance on limited availability of high­quality scrap be­
comes an issue for this production route.
While alternative decarbonisation technologies offer viable solutions, the challenge of lim­
ited scrap availability can also be addressed through various Design for Circularity (DfC)
practices. These practices, which are largely enabled by the improved recyclability of
steel, offer significant potential (Dworak and Fellner 2021). One of the greatest challenges
in recycling steel, however, remains the contamination of scrap steel. Nevertheless, there
is substantial interest in investing in DfC practices to mitigate these challenges, which
demonstrates how critical these practices are in advancing steel recycling and supporting
decarbonisation efforts (Table 3.7).
A focus on other environmental impacts of steel production such as its consequent effects
to resource use, measured by the indicator ADP, could potentially lead to green steel man­
ufacturing technologies that address the recyclability and circularity of steel. Furthermore,
additional metric provided in the EPDs could be analysed, such as data from “Waste Pro­
duction” and “Output flows” that could provide a better understanding of the potential for
such technologies that employ resource recirculation. Since the scope of the SLR only

The potential for circularity extends beyond scrap to include constituent by­products, such
as slag, dust, and process gases.
The use of alternative carbon materials, such as

It is also worth noting that there are also already several companies that employ such

By harnessing the potential for recyclability and circularity of steel, these practices also
allow for further reduction of CO2 emission by decreasing the dependency on the primary
production route and iron ore as a raw material, and therefore also indirectly affects the
decarbonisation technologies presented in this study. Regardless, expanding the concept
of green steel manufacturing to also include circularity practices could further enhance the
value of the support being developed.
Discussion III: Determinants of Operational Expenditure
The primary reason for representing costs as CAPEX in the development of the support
was its consistent availability across documents identified as relevant in the SLR con­
ducted. CAPEX was frequently recorded due to its comparability across technologies,
making it a reliable metric for cross­referencing. In contrast, total production costs, which
includes both CAPEX and Operational Expenditure (OPEX), was less consistently re­
ported. OPEX are periodic expenses associated with the maintenance and operations of
a business, product or system. This expense is a variable cost component, influenced by
several factors that vary significantly depending on the technology, operational context
and regional conditions. This section aims to discuss two important resources, namely,
renewable electricity and scrap steel, that both affect OPEX. The discussion extends to
the subsequent actions by the European commission and its members through regula­
tions, policies and initiatives in response to the variability of these costs.
The first factor that affects the OPEX of decarbonisation technologies is the availability of
low­cost, renewable electricity. Several of the key technologies, such as low­carbon EAF
and HDRI­EAF are highly reliant on exclusively being powered by renewable electricity to
prevent a rebound effect that could lead to increased emissions. It has been estimated
that 7.6 million tonnes of carbon dioxide (MtCO2) could have been conserved if electricity
were sourced solely from renewables (Bauer et al. 2025). As of 2022, Eurofer mapped
around 60 low­carbon projects scheduled to start before 2030 in Europe. The operations
of these projects would require 165 terra­watt hours (TWh) of decarbonised electricity
by 2030, that is equivalent to double the annual consumption of Belgium (EUROFER –
The European Steel Association 2022). Securing Power Purchase Agreements (PPA)
for long­term renewable electricity can alleviate the needed supply of steel­making firms
36
Developing a Guideline on Green Steel Manufacturing
==PAGE:49==
(Bauer et al. 2025). PPAs are contractual agreements of mutually agreed rates of renew­
able electricity between electricity producers and buyers. The European Commission pro­
vides measures to promote the investment in PPAs through the Electricity Market Design
Regulation (European Union Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER)
2025). Further the commission recognises that direct electrification may not always be
possible or cost­effective, thereby strengthening the provision of low­carbon hydrogen to
replace fossil fuel and natural gas as sources of high temperature heat. With the Euro­
pean Clean Hydrogen Alliance, the commission promotes investments in clean hydrogen
production and use (Commission 2025).
Secondly, as these technologies are also largely dependent in scrap steel, it is important
to secure sources for this secondary raw material. There has been a decline in the volume
of scrap used for recycling in Europe that is for two reasons: (1) insufficient domestic de­
mand and (2) competitive prices offered by non­European countries for purchasing scrap.
To address demand­side challenges, the commission plans to set cost­effective targets
to sectors conventionally reliant on primary steel. Under the Eco­design for Sustainable
Products Regulation, the EU can establish specific targets for the use of recycled ma­
terials in certain sectors, such as in the automotive industry. This is undertaken while
also ensuring that European industries remain globally competitive, and addressing the
potential cost increases associated with using recycled materials, which may be passed
on to consumers. In terms of supply, the commission seeks to create a single market
for metal scrap by harmonizing waste classification system through the Circular Economy
Act (Commission 2025).
The variable cost of these two resources is largely influenced by their availability across
Europe and globally, shaping the broader transition toward green steel manufacturing.
While the initiatives outlined above focus on the European context, they reflect a wider
global trend, as similar approaches are increasingly adopted elsewhere to support com­
pliance and drive decarbonisation efforts. In 2023, the IEA published that many countries
including China and India have also introduced several relevant decarbonization policies,
with special focus on circular economy and the use of scrap (IEA 2025).

Conceptualisation
It has been determined that the most appropriate framework that agrees with the de­
sign requirements and scope and assumption is a decision tree. Several other structured
frameworks, that could fit the level of information presented in this study (Table 3.10), are
pattern cards that describes each technology in detail through a set of cards; or simplified
flow diagrams or Sankey diagrams that represent flow quantities, such as decarbonisa­
tion potential or CAPEX, by varying the width of the arrows. However, to serve the aim
and function of the support as a guideline, the decision tree was deemed most appropri­
ate, whilst also integrating the information gathered based on the SLR. As emphasised in
an article from the Harvard Business Review, for complex investment decisions, it is cru­
cial to adopt an alternative representation of the relevant information. The decision tree
framework, as reflected in this perspective, proves particularly appropriate for illustrating
the pathways through which various potential outcomes are realised (Magee 1964). At its
core, a decision tree is a structured model that breaks down complex data into simpler,
homogeneous subsets through a sequence of binary decisions. It begins with a root node
representing the entire dataset and branches out into nodes based on yes / no or true /
false conditions, leading to terminal nodes that represent final outcomes (Mienye and

Elaboration and Realisation
This section outlines the development process and presents the final version of the sup­
port. The decision tree was developed in three iterations, each with additional features:
Iteration 1. The first iteration consists of its most basic functionality, that is to simply guide
users to the decarbonisation technologies (Appendix E). This involved the formulation
of nodes, defining the conditions at decision points, and establishing the corresponding
terminal nodes.
(1) Initial Classification: Primary vs. Secondary Production Route
The decision tree begins with carbon steel as the root node and differentiates between two
major steel­making routes: the primary route (BF­BOF) and the secondary route (EAF).
The first decision asks:
Is it currently being produced via BF-BOF?
-- Yes --> Is there access to renewable electricity and scrap?
-- No --> Is it currently being produced via EAF?
If the answer is Yes, the analysis continues by evaluating the feasibility of shifting to or
supporting an EAF­based production through available resources. If the answer is No, it
assumes the steel is already produced via the secondary route and proceeds accordingly.
(2) Verification of EAF Production and Technology Scope
The next decision node examines whether steel is already produced via EAF. If it is not,
the pathway concludes with a boundary condition:
Is it currently being produced via EAF?
-- Yes --> Is there availability of green hydrogen?
-- No --> Other decarbonisation technologies are beyond scope.
This indicates that the decision tree deliberately limits the consideration of decarbonisa­
tion options beyond those directly applicable to either BF­BOF or EAF­based production
pathways.
(3) Hydrogen Integration for EAF Pathway
If production is via EAF, the next step considers whether green hydrogen is available,
which enables integration with HDRI. Depending on this condition:
Is there an availbility of green hydrogen?
-- Yes --> Integrate EAF with (green) HDRI (HDRI-EAF).
-- No --> Shift / keep scrap or low-cabon EAF.
If green hydrogen is available, a transition toward HDRI­EAF is advised. Otherwise, main­
taining or transitioning to a low­carbon scrap­based EAF remains the recommended path.
(4) Further BF­BOF routes
If there was no access to renewable electricity and scrap in Step 1, then the decision tree
only considers those solutions related to BF­BOF. The decision tree then asks:

Is there access to non-coking coal?
-- Yes --> Are there further CO2 emissions that have not been
addressed?
-- No --> Integrate CCS technologies (BF-BOF-CCS)
If non­coking coal is not available, the pathway proposes the integration of CCS tech­
nologies with the existing BF­BOF process. Otherwise, the tree proceeds to a further
assessment of remaining CO2 emissions, that then leads to other technologies.
(4) Smelt technologies
If there was an availability of non­coking coal, it would then be possible to replace the BF
with Smelting reduction (Smelt). Following this substitution, the decision tree assesses
whether residual CO2 emissions remain unaddressed. If so, the pathway recommends
the integration of carbon capture and storage (CCS) with the smelting reduction process:
Are there further CO2 emissions that have not been addressed?
-- Yes --> Integrate Smelt with CCS technologies (Smelt-CCS)
-- No --> Replace BF with Smelting reduction (Smelt)
This terminates the decision tree. This iteration also involved the inclusion of key attributes
such as TRL, CAPEX, and decarbonisation potential (Appendix E)
Iteration 2. A colour scale was incorporated to distinguish technologies based on their
TRL: below five, between five and nine, and at nine (Appendix F).
Iteration 3. The last iteration included a list of reference, and the organisation of tech­
nologies according to their impact (decarbonisation potential and perceived benefits vs.
risks) and cost (Figure 4.2).
As a basis for comparison, a decision tree has also been employed by one of the tech­
nology roadmaps, Green Steel Manufacturing Guidelines (Figure 4.1). The purpose for
the decision tree, however, is not to function as a guideline, but rather as a process allo­
cation mechanism. Furthermore, this decision tree presumes that CCS is the preferable
choice due to its “perceived short­term cost” (Mienye and Jere 2024). In contrast, the
decision tree in this study de­prioritizes CCS options, positioning them as the last resort.
This is due to the associated risks with implementation outweighing its potential benefits
(Table 3.11), a consideration that fundamentally guided the structure of the decision tree.

Discussion IV: Relevant Frameworks on Developing Support
As mentioned in Conceptualisation, the level of information presented in DS­I could have
taken other frameworks such as pattern cards or flow or Sankey diagrams. This section
would discuss how information could have been fitted with using these frameworks, as
well as some advantages and drawbacks for choosing either.
Pattern Cards. Pattern cards were identified as a suitable format, particularly for sum­
marizing not only the key attributes (TRL, CAPEX and decarbonisation Potential) but also
the core characteristics of the technologies. With the assumption that most users already
possess a preliminary understanding of the key technologies discussed, further elabo­
ration on these key technologies was deemed unnecessary. However, it is important to
acknowledge a potential bias in this assumption, as most of the participants interviewed
during the descriptive study­I stage were perceived to have sufficient, if not extensive,
knowledge of green steel manufacturing. Pattern cards alone would also not have com­
pletely served the purpose that was defined for the support – that is to serve as a guideline
on the various technologies for green steel manufacturing.
However, given the potential users’ limited familiarity with the concept of green steel man­
ufacturing, it would have been more appropriate not to assume that they possess a pre­
liminary understanding of decarbonisation technologies. Therefore, the current support
mechanism provided through the decision tree could be enhanced by the integration of
pattern cards. Pattern cards function as structured, modular knowledge units that offer
further elaboration on key attributes of the technologies. Their inclusion becomes even
more beneficial when a broader range of technologies is represented, as this would al­
low for a greater level of detail and comparative depth across different decarbonisation
options.
Flow diagrams. A flow or Sankey diagram could have effectively illustrated the key at­
tributes of the system in a more visually intuitive manner. However, incorporating such
a diagram might have introduced unnecessary complexity, particularly in the absence of
detailed, plant­level data. For meaningful application, it would require a realistic break­
down of carbon emissions associated with each input resource and process stage. Fur­
thermore, while potentially more interactive than a decision tree, allowing users to input
specific plant characteristics would also demand a higher level of data availability and
user engagement to be functionally useful.
Given that several studies have already been conducted on the decarbonisation of steel
as presented in Research Clarification, it would be possible that stakeholders could find
greater value in exploring other impact categories related to DfC practices, such as re­
source consumption and waste generation. If this assumption holds, it is recommended
that the structure of the support framework be reformulated into a simplified flow or Sankey
diagram. This is based on the premise that a focus on DfC practices would likely generate
data grounded in material flow analyses, thereby providing the level of detail necessary
to substantiate such a representation. Furthermore, broadening the scope to other steel
types, particularly alloy steels, including stainless steel, are of significant interest in this
analysis, as the presence of alloying elements may affect their recyclability (Björkman and
C. Samuelsson 2014).
In short, pattern cards would have been well­suited for presenting additional background
information on the technologies, offering concise and structured insights. In contrast, flow
or Sankey diagrams would have been more appropriate for supporting a granular analysis,

level. With this discussion, a decision tree was found most suitable for the level of detail
provided based on the SLR conducted.

Discussion and Conclusion
With several discussion points already presented across the preceding chapters of this
study, this section is only meant to further elaborate how the LOs were met through out
this study.
The current steel manufacturing practices were identified through the processes included
in the EPDs for the chosen PCR. Based on an investigation of the BF­BOF and EAF
routes, climate change was identified as a hotspot impact in steel manufacturing, as in­
dicated by GWP. This was primarily associated with core process emissions from the
BF­BOF route and upstream processes in the case of the EAF route (LO2, LO3, LO4).
The ability to critically select and review relevant academic and grey literature was demon­
strated in the research clarification and descriptive study­I stages. In the Research Clar­
ification stage, several pieces of grey literature, such as technical and policy reports, in­
dustry reports, as well as some academic articles were selected to provide evidence and
illustrative examples to support the arguments developed during the scoping of the study.
The concepts of DfS and DfC were explored through the practices based on the empirical
data collected in Descriptive Study­I (LO5). As climate change was identified as the pri­
mary impact of concern, green steel manufacturing practices were limited to those related
to decarbonisation. Therefore the SLR employed in Descriptive Study­I was limited to de­
carbonisation technologies. Based on the frequency that technologies were mentioned
through out the SLR, these were limited to HDRI, CCS, low­carbon EAF and Smelt (LO1,
LO5). It was identified that these technologies only correspond to DfS practices, therefore
it was recommended that these are to be included in future studies.
Gaps were identified based on the SLR, that also defines the requirements that the sup­
port should meet. As the support should adhere to a global scope, the effect of policies
and regulations were excluded from the development of the support. However, a separate
discussion on how policies and regulations in a European context impact the operational
costs associated with the key decarbonisation technologies (LO8). Further design re­
quirements were defined in the Prescriptive Study (LO7).
A support in the form of a decision tree as a guideline, respecting the defined design
requirements was developed in three iterations during the Prescriptive Study stage (LO9).
This was evaluated during Descriptive Study II according to its usability, usefulness and
trustworthiness. Although the evaluators did not fully represent the intended users of the
support, the concept of the tool received some validation of its applicability and several
suggestions to its improvement (LO10). The feedback received highlights an unintended
educational effect of the tool; increased user insight and learning; and its ease of use.
Overall, the study fulfilled all learning objectives to varying extents. To conclude, through a
comprehensive evaluation of the impacts and hotspots of the current green steel manufac­
turing practices, this study contributes a guideline focusing on decarbonization technolo­
gies. These technologies were structured and developed into comprehensive guideline
in the form of a decision tree, intended for practitioners in companies new to the transition
of green steel manufacturing. Two design improvements emerged from the evaluation of
the support: (1) to include the references from the SLR, that was already incorporated in
the final iteration of the support; and (2) to broaden the scope of technologies.
