CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The need for a robot to navigate from A to B safely and securely is one of the most
fundamental problems in mobile robotics and it is a problem many has tried to tackle
over time. It is a task which is found within many areas of robotics, and in this project it
was chosen to focus our efforts on the challenge of navigation with a wheeled robot in an
indoor environment. This focus is motivated by the industrial use case of the company
Robotize1. Here, an industrial robot is used for transportation of pallets in internal
logistic scenarios. This is achieved autonomously and thus there are high demands for
the control part to ensure collision free and feasible trajectories.
Traditionally the planning efforts have often been focused on generating a reference
trajectory for the robot and then tracking this as close as possible. However, based
on the needs in the industry, which are more focused on getting smoothly from A to
B rather than following an exact reference to get there, it is proposed to change the
tracking objective from exact tracking of a reference to creating a form of road driving,
much more akin to how humans navigate in a car.
To accomplish this, a two-step Nonlinear Model Predictive Controller (NMPC) is
implemented based on different models for different scenarios. First of all it is proposed
to construct a temporal MPC planner which tracks a generated global time reference
trajectory. Here, collision avoidance is included in the form of a potential field approach.
Secondly, a spatial MPC controller is proposed where the objective is transformed from
a time-based reference to a more natural spatial representation of the progress. This
results in a planner which more naturally (similar to human driving) navigates the
environment. This redefinition of the model also results in a simpler obstacle avoidance,
as this can be implemented as simple state bounds in this case. These planners also
require a reformulation of the reference which needs to be converted into a curvature-
based reference. Based on this an adaption of an existing global planner is proposed,
which generates this reference while smoothing it as well.
Due to the dynamics of the industrial robot and safety considerations, several nonlin-
ear constraints on the configuration space needs to be accounted for in the optimization
procedure. This motivates an explicit modeling of these constraints, ultimately resulting
in more optimal trajectories as the model more accurately depicts the reality.
Finally, the planners have been tested in real world scenarios for the cases in which
this was possible. Due to the nature of the industrial AGV, the prototyping has mainly

CHAPTER 2
Related work
Throughout the years a lot of research has been done in the field of planning and control
for mobile wheeled robots.
Early high level planning algorithms are often based on graph-search algorithms
founded in the original work of Dijkstra [Dij59], which has since been extended in several
ways, most famously in the A* algorithm [HNR68].
These graph search techniques
are applied to the field of robotics by generating a discretized grid map of the global
surroundings, and then constructing a graph using the grid as vertices.
The specific global planner which has been used prior to the work here is based on the
work in Brock and Khatib [BK99] which implements a navigation function on the basis
of potential fields. Here, the edges in the grid map are weighted w.r.t. their distance to
an obstacle through a cost function.
A different approach is that of M. Lavalle [M L09] which proposed the Rapid Ex-
ploring Random Tree (RRT) which is suitable for generating dynamically feasible tra-
jectories in high dimensional configuration spaces. This works by growing a tree using
random samples through the environment while checking for collision free connectivity.
This method was improved for more optimal and smooth trajectories in Karaman and
Frazzoli [KF10] by rewiring the tree to the closest nodes on each iteration.
Based on the RRT from M. Lavalle [M L09], Richter, Bry, and Roy [RBR16] pro-
posed to incorporate a polynomial optimization step to generate trajectories which are
dynamically smooth in several derivatives instead of simple straight line trajectories.
These global planning techniques are mainly responsible for generating a reference
trajectory and do not engage in the control itself. This is traditionally achieved using
local planning techniques which are responsible of taking a global reference and following
it and many different approaches to solve this problem exists.
Potential field methods have had a high popularity through, among others, the work
of Khatib [Kha86] which was implemented based on a robot manipulator, and this has
later been extended in Khatib and Chatila [KC95]. However, these methods often suffer
under local minima.
In Simmons [Sim96], a maximization of an objective function is employed with re-
spect to the curvature and velocity dynamics of a non holonomic robot. Here, the op-
timal control is selected by forward simulation over a given horizon to achieve collision
avoidance.
==PAGE:16==
4
2 Related work
Beck et al. [Bec+10] introduces a plugin-based approach, where different plugins for
the individual parts of the control and navigation is used. This is also the approach that
is traditionally used on the robots at DTU.
Especially worth highlighting is the Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) Fox, Bur-
gard, and Thrun [FBT97] which employs a similar scheme as Simmons [Sim96] but
proposes to generate a dynamic window around the working point of the robot to limit
the configuration space that needs to be searched. While this method works well in
practice it does become computationally limited at larger velocities and does not handle
very constrained situations well, due to discretization effects of the velocity sampling.
A variant of this planner is also what is used on the existing robotic platform for local
planning.
Rösmann et al. [Rös+12] proposes a solution to the problem by employing a method
they call timed elastic bands.
Here, they use optimization techniques to generate a
locally optimal path. This is similar to what we propose in this study, but as many
other optimization methods they are also vulnerable to local minima which must be
handled explicitly.
Common for the early work is that they were founded on the limited computational
capacity of the computers at the time. The computational resources have since become
much greater and it is now feasible to consider the solution of the nonlinear problems
which governs the dynamics.
Houska, Ferreau, and Diehl [HFD11], Ferreau, Bock, and Diehl [FBD08], Ferreau
et al. [Fer+14], and Frasch et al. [Fra+14] implement solution methods for such non-
linear problems where especially the toolkit presented in Houska, Ferreau, and Diehl
[HFD11] has found widespread use in the literature. Here they construct a framework
for defining Optimal Control Problems (OCP) and handle the discretization using a
multiple shooting method [BP84] and condensing step. They also allow for easy use of
the optimization software in external software through a code generation feature.
In Mehrez, Mann, and Gosine [MMG13], reference tracking has been achieved using
the toolkit in Houska, Ferreau, and Diehl [HFD11] for generating the MPC controller.
Here the system is modeled w.r.t. the error dynamics of the system compared to a
reference robot. This also necessitates the generation of an (ideal) reference for both
state and controls.
Kamel et al. [Kam+] implements a nonlinear MPC controller for a Micro Aerial
Vehicle platform as well as a fixed wing system. The method has then been extended
in Kamel et al. [Kam+17] to include collision avoidance of other agents using a similar
potential field generation to what we propose here.
In Verschueren et al. [Ver+14] and Liniger, Domahidi, and Morari [LDM15], time
optimal driving techniques are explored based on the implementation of contouring
control on miniature race cars. This contouring scheme is similar to the work propose,
however, they do not implement it in a full scale scenario.

All global and local planning of mobile robots need an estimate of their positions.
This can either be done locally through odometry or globally through localization meth-
ods. The work here utilizes global localization as it is desired to navigate to a specific
point in the real world. Outdoor, this can often be achieved by the use of the Global
Positioning System (GPS), however this does not work indoors. Thus localization in an
indoor environment often relies on a map representation for the localization. The work
here accomplishes this map localization using a particle filter approach based on Monte
Carlo localization similar to Dellaert et al. [Del+99].
Of other methods worth mentioning is Kalman filter based approaches, such as Sieg-
wart, Nourbakhsh, and Scaramuzza [SNS11, pp. 331–342], where lines are extracted
from the laserscan and matched against a known map, or methods based on image tag
detection which relies on the extraction of 2D-3D correspondences from fiducial land-
marks [NBB15]. Recently visual inertial odometry [Blo+17] and visual localization based
approaches [Sch+17] have also gained popularity.

CHAPTER 3
Background and existing
robotic framework
This project deals with the case of an industrial mobile robot. The project is in collab-
oration with the company Robotize. Robotize specializes in internal industrial logistic
solutions, more specifically in the transport of goods in a factory environment. To solve
this task they have constructed a mobile robotic platform called GoPal400 as portrayed
on Figure 3.3(a). GoPal400 is a differential drive robot with a pallet lift integrated in
the construction. Using this pallet lift, the robot is capable of lifting pallets from a spe-
cific pallet stand and transporting them around. The challenging task is then to move
the pallet from the pickup station to the delivery station autonomously. The way the
system is configured now, is based on the Robotic Operating System (ROS)1 which is
an interprocess communication framework specifically targeted at robotics. The overall
control system is then structured as seen in Figure 3.1.
3.1
Pallet transportation use case
The main use-case for the robot at Robotize is the transportation of EUR-pallets from
A to B. Traditionally this is accomplished using forklifts operated by a human driver,
where Robotize aims to replace the human driver with an autonomous solution. Thus
the essential task for the control system is to first of all to generate a feasible route from
the current position to the target position. Secondly the controller should be able to
successfully move the robot along this route to the goal, while avoiding any (dynamic)
obstacles it will potentially meet along the route. This has been visualized on Figure 3.2.
3.2
The SMR and GoPal400 platforms
The project here has been undertaken on two different robotic platforms, the SMR
and the GoPal400. The SMR is a protoype platform (Figure 3.3(b)) developed at the
Automation and Control group at DTU Elektro, and is a small robotic platform suited
for indoor navigation. It is equipped with a range of different sensors, including wheel
encoders, a 2D laser scanner, and a Kinect camera.

3.3
The software framework
The SMR prototype platform is traditionally controlled using the Mobotware [Bec+10]
control software developed at DTU, whereas the framework on the Gopal400 is based
on ROS. Thus to correctly emulate the structure of the GoPal400 on the SMR robot,
the ROS framework has also been implemented here. The following section will cover
some of the existing and core elements of this framework.
3.4
Laser localization
One of the most important parts for the successful navigation of the system is the
localization system (AMCL) and the control framework (MoveBase and DWA planner).
The localization system is responsible for counteracting the inevitable drift of the robots
odometry state estimation w.r.t. a predefined map frame.
The localization is based on an Adapted Monte Carlo Localization algorithm, as
defined in Dellaert et al. [Del+99]. This localization scheme is based on the theory of
particle filters which is a probabilistic approach to localization. Here, a set of potential
candidate locations are simulated in the environment map. By projecting the current
laser scan it is possible to score each potential location by probability of being correct
when comparing to the map and then discard the low scoring ones. The current location
of the robot is then selected as the weighted center of mass of these particles. The full
details has been covered in Witting [Wit16] to which we refer.
3.5
MoveBase control of robot
To control the movement of the robot the move_base library is implemented. This is a
library wrapper which consists of different submodules, each responsible for a different
part of the control algorithm. Further, a low-level motor controller is equipped on the
robot which converts the forward and rotational velocities into individual wheel veloc-
ities and again into Pulse With Modulation (PWM) signals for the hardware actuator.
This motor controller effectively introduces a filter for the true model dynamics, as the
acceleration limits imposed are so restrictive that the resulting time constants are much
slower than the rest of the dynamics.
3.5.1
Costmap of obstacles
To enable the robot to avoid obstacles it needs a representation of the obstacles around
it. In the move_base library this is accomplished through the Costmap2D interface,
which is a grid-based representation of the environments as seen by the laser scanner.

3.5.2
Global planner
In the ROS framework the global planner is responsible for the generation of an overall
plan from the start position to the goal position. This is generated using a discretized
map of the environment as the basis for finding an collision-free route. The obstacles are
then inflated using a cost function which decays over distance to “push” the path away
from obstacles. This approach is a simple way of generating paths, however, in most
cases it returns a feasible route, which the local planner can then use as a reference.
It does however fail to take any of the dynamics of the robot into account, and as
such it might result in paths in which sections can result in the local planner getting
stuck. Currently this is achieved through the Dijsktra pathfinding algorithm as it is
implemented in the nav_fn node. Here, the costmap for each grid in the map is used
as edge weights in the Dijkstra algorithm. In this way it is possible to bias the Dijkstra
path finding away from any obstacles depending on the costmap so it is not purely the
shortest path [BK99; Wit16].
3.5.3
DWA local planner
The local planner which has been used on the robot previously is based on a variant
of the Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) [FBT97]. The DWA planner is a predictive
planner and the algorithm behind the general implementation will briefly be explained
here.
The planning algorithm works by considering a set of velocities which are then for-
ward simulated and compared to each other. The “best scoring” velocity sample is then
extracted and used to operate the system. Thus it can be separated into two stages, the
trajectory generation part and the trajectory scoring part.
3.5.3.1
Trajectory sampling
The DWA planner handles the dynamics of the given system by considering a “dynamic
window” around the current system state in configuration space when generating the
trajectories to be considered. This is done by observing the current forward and ro-
tational velocity of the robot and sampling the part of the configuration space which
will be reachable within the next control interval while obeying the acceleration lim-
its. This effectively generates a grid of velocities to be considered around the current
operating point. The resolution of this grid is available as a design choice to the user,
however, there is a trade-off between performance and resolution as the complexity scale
quadratically with the chosen resolution.
3.5.3.2
Trajectory scoring
The generated samples are then scored using several trajectory “critics”. This is the
crucial part as this process is responsible for selecting the next control signal.
The
==PAGE:24==
12
3 Background and existing robotic framework
trajectories are mainly scored with respect to three different criteria: nearness to the
reference path, forward distance achieved and whether it is collision-free.
The nearness to the path is calculated by sampling the trajectory and calculating the
sum of the deviations from the path at each point. The forward distance critics is based
on projecting an attractive point to a specific distance in the future on the reference.
The closer the endpoint for each trajectory is to this attractive point the higher the score.
The collision scoring is based on checking the minimum distance to an obstacle of each
point on the trajectory and scoring it w.r.t. that. If the specific sample will definitely
result in a collision it is discarded.
Each of the trajectories are then simulated into the future with respect to the current
position.
The critics are evaluated along each specific trajectory and the scores are
combined. The highest scoring trajectory is selected, and the respective controls are
output to the motor driver.
3.6
Drawbacks of DWA planner
While the general DWA planner is capable of bringing the robot from A to B, it does
have several drawbacks which limit its performance. First of all, it does not directly take
any velocity constraints into account (other than the acceleration limits of the robot).
Secondly, it only considers the possible control values at the current configuration and
predicts into the future as if the control input remained constant for a fixed period of
time. The constraints can potentially be handled by scaling the selected controls into
the valid set of velocities, however, based on the chosen controls this can result in the
robot almost not moving or getting stuck.
Further, while the planner does punish the controls depending on whether they are in
collision or not, the curvature nature of constant velocities results in suboptimal behavior
when an obstacle is in the way. This scenario can be seen on Figure 3.4 where the issue
of using curvature trajectories to navigate around obstacles become clear. The simulated
trajectories, while collision-free, will score very poorly in the other objective functions
(forward criteria and nearness to plan criteria), and thus the best scoring trajectory
becomes ambiguous and suboptimal behavior is ensured.
This drawback is an inherent limitation of the Dynamic Window Approach and often
the solution is to perform an expensive replanning of the global path at a fixed interval.
However, this effectively reduces the ability to navigate around dynamic obstacles and is
mainly suited for more static obstacles due to the slow update rate. In the work here we
remove the need for this replanning step by designing a local planner capable of handling
the obstacles directly in a suitable way.
This behavior also limits the maximum velocity of the DWA local planner due to the
amount of lookahead needed. Due to the minimum braking distance the planner needs
to simulate at least 3.2 seconds into the future, as per

have to be estimated based on experiments or through complex modelling steps. This is
not the case in this work, as fortunately the kinematics of the differential drive system is
well understood. The models used in this section have basis in Siegwart, Nourbakhsh,

the full model dynamics of the system should be considered in the model, however

4.2
Spatial transformation of dynamics
The time modeled dynamics just introduced describes the differential drive robot in the
conventional manner over a time horizon. This accurately models the robot in the time
domain, and enables the construction of an optimal control problem where the robot
should track a reference trajectory w.r.t. time. However, this is not always the desired
control scheme, and in an industrial use case, it does not directly align with what the
desired behavior of the robot is, which is simply to get from A to B, whereas the specific
trajectory is of less importance.
4.2.1
Reference
When defining the reference path as a series of time-dependent reference points on the
path as in Equation 4.2, the result is that the controller will try to track the reference as
much as possible, in order to reach each reference point at the corresponding time. Thus
the time variable is the variable which dictates the “progress” of the reference. However
in the case of a robot, the progress is more naturally defined by the driven distance along
the reference. Further, as the reference points in this sense are coupled with time, they
also implicitly dictate the velocity of the robot. This tight coupling with time results
in issues if the planner “gets ahead” or lacks behind the reference, as in this case the
controller would either have to brake or accelerate to catch up with the reference. This

towards the Voronoi regions. This is not the desired behavior in the method proposed
here and thus the logistic function has been chosen as a basis for the potential field.

5.1
Motivation for MPC
The motivation for Model Predictive Control (MPC) is most apparent when comparing
it to classical control methods. MPC is heavily based in optimization theory, where the
fundamental principle is the construction of a optimal control objective based on both
the current reference, but also future references along some horizon. Compared to LQR
control this ensures that the chosen control is not only optimal w.r.t. a constant reference,
but will also take future states along a potentially varying reference into account.
Compared to classical PID control this result in two quite different control methods,
as PID control is largely based on heuristics, where a tracking error for the reference
is used as the objective and no information about the dynamic system is utilized in
the control law. MPC control contrarily utilizes a model of the plant to predict future
behavior and act accordingly. In LQR control a model of the system is also utilized,
however, the control objective is still based on the regulation of a tracking error at the
current time w.r.t. a constant reference, and thus the advantages of the optimal control
dissappears if the reference changes in the future.
The basic principle of MPC has been visualized in Figure 5.1. Essentially an open
loop control problem is solved for some period Tc into the future, where an optimal
control sequence with respect to the control objective is found along the horizon.
If the model was a perfect model of the real world system, applying this open loop
sequence to the real plant should result in bringing the state to the set point. However in
the real world the model is never exact and even if it was, stochastic effects would affect
the result and thus this control scheme would not work in practice. Instead, only the first
control in the sequence is applied and on the next iteration new closed loop information
will be available through the state measurements and the process is repeated. In this
sense the control is converted into closed loop feedback control.
A simple analogy is that of a human driving a car towards a sharp turn in the road.
In the MPC sense the human will see the turn in the horizon and start decelerating the
car in anticipation before entering the turn to pass it more smooth. Whereas traditional
control is analogous to the driver only starting to brake after the car has entered the

Theoretically the optimization problem should then be solved over an infinite horizon
to ensure global optimality [FA02]. However, for practical and computational reasons
this is not feasible in practice. Instead the problems are solved over a finite horizon.
In this way MPC problems are inherently based on the principles of receding horizon
control, which is the iterative procedure of gathering state information, calculating the
control values for some time into the future, applying the first one and repeating. Here
the horizon is often chosen based on the complexity of the problem and computational
limits.
The specific steps of the control are as follows
1. Gather state initial state information (x0)
2. Optimize controls w.r.t. reference from T0 to Tf
3. Output first control in control sequence and repeat
In this sense the control will be optimal over the selected time horizon, and if this horizon
is long enough it will converge towards the overall optimal solution. Further by including

Another advantage of Model Predictive Control is the ability to incorporate general

Traditionally MPC control is often based on Linear MPC where the model which
is used for the optimization is a linear plant model. This can be the case even if the
underlying plant is non-linear, where an approximate linear model is instead constructed
around the working point of the plant.
This approach has especially been used in
chemical plants, which are slow systems and often remain around the linearization point.
Linear MPC further has its advantages in that the constructed optimization problem is
convex. This makes it possible to find a unique control sequence which minimizes the
control objective. Given that the problem is convex it can be guaranteed that the found
minimum is also the global minimum.

5.1.0.1
Economic MPC
Traditionally the information about which reference is the optimal has been up to the
“user”, and as mentioned in Section 4.2 this is also the case in the temporal model
defined here. Here the reference implicitly defines the velocity which the robot should
drive with, depending on the sampling distance which the trajectory has been generated
with.
Another variant of the MPC form is Economic MPC [RAB12] where the selection of
the optimal reference is included as a part of the optimization. Something that closely
represents the structure proposed in Section 4.2.3. In this sense the economic resource
becomes time, and the economic performance is then improved by reducing the time
taken to traverse the spatial horizon.
5.2
The ACADO framework
The controllers which have been designed in this project have been implemented using
the ACADO framework [HFD11]. The ACADO framework is designed to construct and
solve several classes of optimization problems with the focus on MPC control problems.
It is used mainly for the construction, generation and solution of the MPC control
problems and interfaces with several potential QP solvers for the optimization solution.
In this work the framework combined with the qpOASES solver, as mentioned earlier,
has been used. The framework implements the following software modules, all needed for
the efficient solution of (nonlinear) optimization problems, which will briefly be covered
here as presented in Houska, Ferreau, and Diehl [HFD11].
Symbolic expression
ACADO allows for a general way of defining the optimization
problems which leans closely up against symbolic mathematical equations. This allows
for an intuitive definition of the control problem, and when it is defined in symbolic form
also allows for automatic symbolic evaluation of the derivatives. This is an advantage
over other methods where the numerical derivatives have to be calculated instead.

Integration Algorithms
In all model predictive control problems the need to simu-
late the model of the system into the future arises. This is done through integration rou-
tines which simulate the model dynamics based on known inputs. ACADO implements
several implicit and explicit methods, with the Runge-Kutta methods worth mentioning.
In the problems constructed here an implicit Runge-Kutta integrator of order 4/5 has
been chosen based on its stability and popularity.
Discretization and Condensing of Dynamic Systems
Discretization of the sys-
tem is necessary in order to use the integrators or simulation of the dynamics, and thus
==PAGE:55==
5.2 The ACADO framework
43
an accurate and effective method for the discretization is needed. To accomplish this
the ACADO toolkit implements both single shooting and multiple shooting methods for
the discretization of the model dynamics. As the multiple shooting methods have shown
several advantages over single shooting methods, especially in the nonlinear case, this is
chosen here [HFD11; BP84].
Due to the structure of the multiple shooting method, it will result in a larger sparse
Nonlinear Program (NLP) as compared to the single shooting method. However, due
to the sparsity it can be reduced in complexity by reducing the number of states and
optimization variables in an condensing step [BP84].
Nonlinear Optimization Algorithm
This NLP can be solved by Sequential Quadratic
Programming (SQP) methods based on one of the optimization algorithms described ear-
lier.
In this project the qpOASES solver has been used, which is a dense active-set
solver [FBD08; FPK], capable of solving both linear and nonlinear QP problems. The
active-set algorithm consists of removing and adding (in)active constraints from the
problem and solving several QP’s using SQP in an iterative fashion (in the linear case
only a single QP needs to be solved) until convergence.
ACADO also incorporates what is called Real-Time Iterations, which is the scheme
it utilizes to ensure that real time demands can be satisfied and has been adapted
from Diehl et al. [Die+]. Here each control iteration is separated into two different steps.
A preparation phase, consisting of the above mentioned steps of linearization, discretiza-
tion and condensing of the problem, responsible for the majority of the calculations and
SQP iterations, and a feedback step which only needs to solve a single QP problem. The
advantage is that the preparation step can be done without collecting the information
about the initial state. In this way the initial state can be updated just before the
feedback step, resulting in up to date information being used.
The way this is accomplished is by defining the optimization problem with respect
to the perturbation from the previous state and solving it relative to this, when doing
the preparation step. In the feedback step the relative initial state is then measured and
the final QP based on this solved, yielding the complete solution [Die+]. This works by
exploiting the knowledge that the control is sequential, and thus the deviation from the
previous state will be small under short control periods. Further, the process is based
on a Gauss-Newton approach which will converge quickly when the perturbations are
small.
Code generation
The final feature covered in the ACADO framework which has been
utilized heavily here is the code generation feature. This feature makes it possible to
generate highly optimized C-code for the designed controller. This generated code is
then responsible for constructing the QP problems based on the model and acts as an
interface to the desired QP solver (here qpOASES).

5.3
Constraints
The implementation of the constraints defined in Section 4.3 can potentially be imple-
mented directly in the ACADO framework, however, several concepts can be applied to
improve the desired behavior of the system when encountering the constraints.
5.3.1
Soft/hard constraints
As the constraints are formulated in Section 4.3 they are defined as hard constraints. In
this formulation any violation of the constraints will result in the failure of the controller.
This can especially become an issue when the controller is operating at or near the
constraint boundary, as even small deviations in the state measurement can push the
initial state for the MPC over the boundary, and thus the controller can potentially fail.
As covered in Section 7.2, a large part of the time the controller operates it is at this
limit, due to the nature of the load constraints, further amplifying this issue.
Considering this it makes sense to loosen the requirements for the constraints slightly
and instead applying a large punishment on any violation [FA02].
In this way the
hard constraints can be converted into soft constraints. This can be accomplished by
introducing a slack variable into the inequality constraints [BV04]. By introducing and
keeping a large weight on this slack variable in the optimization, the properties of the
hard constraint can be maintained while increasing the robustness in the event that a
constraint is violated by a small amount.
In Section 3.4 it was covered how the localization method used on the robot is
based on a stochastic particle filter implementation. This can especially introduce small
jumps and deviations in the position of the robot as it moves around the environment.
Therefore it becomes necessary to implement the state bounds on the ey state in the
spatial controller as soft constraints. However, as mentioned in Section 4.4 the bounds
represent the obstacles, and as such even a small violation could mean collision. To
counter these two issues the soft width of the boundary is reduced with the equivalent
amount of slack allowed through the soft constraint. By doing this a high cost will be
associated with being very near the border, while still allowing free movement in the
general “lane”. As the state deviation in ey is already punished as part of the optimization

CHAPTER 6
Implementation of the
MPC structure
In this section the definition and implementation of the model predictive control prob-
lems based on the framework introduced in Chapter 5 will be presented.
As mentioned earlier in Chapter 3, a hierarchical control structure is currently imple-
mented through the move_base framework, which serves as a high-level state-machine
delegating the current navigation task between the controllers. Underlying in the Move-
Base architecture the control task is separated into two distinct parts for taking the
robot from point A to B. First, a high-level global path-finding algorithm generates a
feasible and collision-free path from the current pose of the robot to the desired goal.
This path is then simply returned as a sequence of equally spaced points leading towards
the goal. Second, a local planner takes this reference and is tasked with generating ve-
locity controls which move the robot along the reference while also avoiding collision
with any static or potentially dynamic obstacles in the environment.
In this work, a replacement for the existing planners are proposed on both levels,
and these will be introduced here.
6.1
Global Planner
First the implementation of the proposed global planner will be introduced and exam-
ined. In this project, two different global planners are designed and investigated, and
ultimately one is selected for the final use.
These are both capable of generating a
curvature-based reference as needed by the spatial MPC controller. The first is based
on polynomial optimization which utilizes a simple obstacle model to generate a collision
free trajectory, and the second is based on a smooth dynamic point model, which fits
a trajectory along a coarse reference in a least square sense. Ultimately the dynamic
point model is found to be superior for the use cases here.
6.1.1
Polynomial
The polynomial method is based on work originally proposed in Richter, Bry, and Roy
[RBR16] and implemented on a Micro Aerial Vehicle (MAV) in Witting et al. [Wit+18].
==PAGE:62==
50
6 Implementation of the MPC structure
This method can easily be exploited in relation to a 2D wheeled robot as well. The
working principle is based on first generating a very coarse but feasible straight line
path, and then fitting a polynomial trajectory through the intermediate waypoints.
6.1.2
RRT planner
As mentioned, the planner first generates a very coarse straight line collision-free path
from the start position to the goal. This is done by utilizing a Rapid Exploring Random
Tree (RRT) planner based on the principles in M. Lavalle [M L09] and Karaman and
Frazzoli [KF10] and the implementation in Witting et al. [Wit+18].
The iterative procedure of generating the RRT can be separated into several distinct
sub-tasks contributing to the final algorithm. In each iteration the generation of the tree
takes basis in the already existing tree, which is used as a seed for the next iteration.
A new sample is then randomly sampled within the map bounds. For this sample, the
closest node in the existing tree is then extracted. If the distance between these two
samples is greater than a specified max growth rate, the position of the new node is
clamped to be within this max distance but still in the same direction.
It is then checked whether a straight line collision-free path exist between the two
samples. If this is the case the connection is added as a new branch to the existing tree.
This process is repeated again and again, until it is possible to add the desired goal
node to the tree using this process. This tree ultimately consists of several branches
where the start position will be the root and the goal position will be a leaf node. It is
then possible to extract a straight line waypoint path by traversing the tree through the
parents of each node until the start(root) node is encountered.
This straight line path can be refined into a minimal representation by removing
any redundant nodes.
This can be achieved by starting at the goal node and then
iteratively test whether an collision-free connection exists between the first node and
the current goal node. If such a connection exists the waypoints in between the two are
simply discarded (as they are redundant). This process is then repeated with the newly
connected node as the “goal” node. This results in a minimum straight line waypoint
path from the start to the goal node.
Finally, this path can be optimized by fitting a polynomial trajectory through the
waypoints using the method proposed in Richter, Bry, and Roy [RBR16] and imple-
mented in Burri et al. [Bur+15]. Here a minimum jerk polynomial trajectory of a point
model is fitted to the waypoints by constraining the polynomial position for each way-
point. Collision-freeness is ensured by checking the trajectory for any collisions. If any
collision is found the violating segment is then split into two and an additional position
(waypoint) constraint is added in the middle as seen on Figure 6.2(c). The trajectory is
then again checked for any collisions and the process is repeated if needed. The entire
process has been illustrated in Figure 6.1 which shows the intermediate steps.

The fundamental task of the local planner is to take the global reference path and
“convert” it into closed loop velocity commands which bring the robot along it and
towards the goal position. In the planners designed here there are some fundamental
steps which are common for both and thus they will be introduced first.
Firstly, as the reference is simply a sequential list of points from the starting position
to the goal, the current position along the reference needs to be determined. This is
accomplished on each iteration by transforming the current position of the robot to the
map frame and searching for the first point in the sequence where the distance to the
current position is closer than the next point. All points prior to this are then pruned
from the plan, resulting in a reference path starting as close as possible to the current
position. This process is implemented in a MPCQueue structure, which is responsible for
the reference generation for both the controllers. By sharing the same structure for the
reference the transition between the planners become more robust as the reference will
be kept up to date and pruned, even when the specific planner is not in use.
Next, the newest information about the obstacles is collected from the costmap and
converted into the local obstacle representation which is used to generate the potential
field and the bounds on the road.
Finally, the state information about the robot is collected, that is the odometry and
the current position of the robot in the map which is necessary to achieve the feedback
in the control. The essential part in this process is that both of the sub-controllers share
the structures in order to reduce the computational overhead. This also guarantee that
they always operate based on the same information.

6.2.3
Combination
On their own these planners solve a subset of the situations the robot encounter well,
however, neither is suitable for every situation.
In particular the spatial planner is
not suited for the situations where the goal is closer than the spatial horizon. Thus a
combination scheme is proposed, where an overall planner governs the two MPC planners
and delegate the control to the most suited of the controllers depending on the situation.
Here the spatial controller takes priority, and the temporal planner is used in situations
where the spatial planner is unsuited. This is the cases at low velocities or when the
robot is close to the goal, and further if the deviation from the reference is outside the
constraints. If either of these conditions are met (or the spatial controller fails for another
reason) the control is instead seamlessly delegated to the temporal planner which is able
to handle these cases.
Further the combined structure is responsible for sharing the resources between the
planners, in particular the MPCQueue structure. A diagram over the information flow
and structure of the final planners can be seen on Figure 6.6.

Experimental results
The experimental results for this project is the product of both real world and simu-
lated experiments. Due to limited possibilities for large scale tests on the real GoPal400
platform most of the development and testing of the controller has instead been con-

experiments has been conducted in environments where the laser is able to see walls at
any time. This is due to the localization framework which relies on laser data being
available at any time, and which will fail if it “drives in the blind”. Thus the planners
have also mainly been tested in narrower scenarios, however, these are also the cases
which the general DWA planner mainly struggles in, and thus it is also these scenarios

7.6
Smoothness
It is hard to classify and quantitatively test what “smoothness” in robot navigation is
as it has more of a visual representation when observing the robot. Nonetheless, it is
a desire for robotic motion and control that it happens in a smooth way. One way
to classify this criteria is that there should not be unnecessary changes in the heading
of the robot, i.e. the robot should not deviate from the reference in a periodic sense.
However, this behavior can be hard to see based on an XY-plot in itself, and often it is
most obvious in the yaw heading of the robot which can oscillate back and forth. Thus
it makes sense to inspect the heading of the robot. This value alone is, however, not
directly telling of the behavior, as a change in heading might of course be necessitated
by a turn in the reference. What is directly correlated with the potential oscillatory
behavior is the deviation from the heading of the plan. If any periodic behavior or large
deviations can be observed in this error state it will also be visible when observing the
robot. Thus we investigate this error state when the robot is following a reference. This
has been visualized in Figure 7.19(a) which shows the deviation in heading superimposed
on the path of the robot as well as the deviation w.r.t. time in Figure 7.19(b).
As it can be observed, the only place where there is any major deviation from the
reference heading is when the robot is either turning or avoiding an obstacle. When the
robot is driving straight there is only very little deviation in the heading error which
would not have been the case if there was any oscillatory behavior. This points towards

CHAPTER 8
Outlook and Conclusion
8.1
Outlook and future work
While the work completed in this project is promising, it is of course not immediately
ready for the industry, and several challenges remain before the DWA planner can be
retired and replaced with newer forces. Thus this work should be seen as a primer for
methods to take the control of the robot from static and ad-hoc control into the realm
of more dynamic and adaptive MPC control which potentially allows to achieve much
smoother and confident control.
Further, there are several aspects of the dynamic problem which it has not been
possible to investigate in this project. The most obvious one is the situation that the
plant model effectively changes throughout operation, depending on whether the robot
is loaded with a pallet or not. In this work, the true dynamics of the system has also
been masked by the fact that a velocity motor controller is inbetween the MPC controller
and the actuator. This velocity controller is limited in such a way that the dynamics are
mainly governed by the acceleration limits. If MPC control is an avenue which is chosen
to be pursued further, then this black box should ideally be removed and the model
adapted to directly reflect the system. This would also make it possible to consider
more complex interactions, (e.g. wheel friction, motor dynamics) and allow for better
control.
One challenge in the system designed here is the process of solving the control using
nonlinear optimization procedures as this makes it hard to guarantee that the global
minimum will be found on every control iteration. It should be investigated whether it
is possible to provide an initialization for the system which ensures that the optimization
will converge to a feasible minimum. This could potentially be achieved by using the
constant velocity trajectory from the DWA planner as initialization for the optimization
step. Another approach would be to simply have a feasibility check after the velocity
is generated to ensure that the trajectory satisfy the requirements. It is not often that
the optimization procedure converges to the wrong minimum, however, in an industrial
setting it is important to guarantee success on every iteration.
Another proposal to adapt this work would be to extend the idea of hierarchical
controller levels proposed here with the existing planner as a fallback solution. Doing
this would result in a situation where the best case performance of the controller would
be significantly improved and, in the case of a failure or infeasibility in the optimization
step, the behaviour would fallback to the existing DWA planner, and as such it would
behave as it always has.
==PAGE:98==
86
8 Outlook and Conclusion
8.2
Conclusion
In this work, the problem of achieving autonomous navigation of a mobile platform has
been analyzed and addressed. First, an existing planner was examined and drawbacks
and limitations of this approach was identified. Based on the needs this introduced, MPC
control was found as a possible candidate for replacing the planner due to the predictive
capabilities as well as the ability to explicitly model (nonlinear) system constraints.
From this platform a suitable differential drive model formulation was identified and a
spatial reformulation of the problem was proposed to better reflect the desired behavior
of the robot. This spatial reformulation addressed several of the issues of traditional
temporal MPC’s, by decoupling the reference from any specific time. Instead it was
formulated as en economic MPC where the progress along the reference was sought
maximized.
Further, an optimization method for the accurate identification of the physical model
parameters was proposed based on numerical methods. This was implemented in Matlab
and on the SMR, resulting in significantly improved calibration of the odometry.
From these requirements and constraints, a two step MPC controller was designed
to achieve smooth, efficient and collision-free driving. This scheme was analyzed w.r.t.
criteria of smooth control and collision avoidance, and a suitable obstacle detection and
representation methods for each case was found and implemented. The controllers were
tested and validated on several real world and simulated scenarios, emulating the actual
situations which can be found in an industrial setting. A validation test on the industrial
robotic platform GoPal400 was also conducted, showing the possibilities and validity of
the proposed controller.
Finally, the MPC controller was compared to a general version of the DWA planner in
different scenarios and the ability to avoid obstacles were tested. Here the drawbacks of
the DWA planner were prominent as it was unable to traverse the test scenarios without
the replanning step unlike the proposed planners which succeeded.
Some challenges with the proposed method was also encountered, however these were
mainly associated with local minima situations and bad initialization which can affect
any optimization procedure. Through careful initialization of the problems it should be
possible to mitigate the effects of these.
Overall, the work resulted in a control setup which addresses several of the issues
with existing approaches. By constructing the constrained optimization problems, the
constraints of the platform can be handled more explicitly and the obstacle avoidance
becomes a more integrated part of the control objective. This in turn allows for a higher
bandwidth in the obstacle avoidance when compared to the replanning approach, paving
the way for a more dynamical and smooth obstacle avoidance in the future.
