Abstract 
Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL), Splenic Marginal Zone Lymphoma (SMZL), and Multiple 
Myeloma (MM) all originate from B cells, and are among the most common types of cancer diseases 
in the western world.  
To determine the similarities and differences between the antibodies presented and the secreted 
by these cells, in order to establish if they are coursed by an antigen driven response, the variable 
region of the antibodies was modeled by the use of the PIGS server. The 3D models generated were 
then structurally aligned based on the locations of the C, calculating a template model distance 
score in order to cluster the antibodies. The 3D structures were then visualized by the use of Pymol.  
3D models of 42 SMZL antibodies and 47 MM antibodies were made. It was possible to determine 
the reason for the clustering, based upon both the clinical, the genetic data and the pictures of the 
3D structure.  
The machine learning tool Random forest was used to improve the prediction modeling program, 
and to determine the importance of the sequences identity, between the model structure and the 
template structure, in order to predict the RMSD value.  
When clustering the three diseases together, the diseases split up in clustering clusters dominated 
by one disease could be found, indicating there is structural differences between the antibodies for 
the three diseases. A prediction model for the RMSD value based on the identity between the model 
structure and the template structures was determined.

Introduction 
Synopsis 
Lymphoproliferative disorders have become common diseases in western countries, accounting for 
almost 10% of all cancer deaths. Nonetheless, very often their pathogenesis is poorly understood, 
affecting our ability to properly diagnose and treat these diseases. In this study we have focused on 
chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), splenic marginal zone lymphoma (SMZL) and multiple myeloma 
(MM). Even though they all are cancer diseases originated from B cells, they develop in very 
different ways and have different symptoms and pathogenesis. For CLL and SMZL, there are 
evidences that external stimuli might be involved in the pathogenesis of the disease, where this is 
less established in the case of MM. The main goal of this thesis has been to study the structure of 
the lymphocyte receptors produced by patients affected by CLL, SMZL and MM, in order to highlight 
traces of such external stimuli. 
 
B cell receptors (also called immunoglobulins or antibodies) are molecules produced by the B-cells 
to recognize foreign antigens entering the body and present these to other immune cells. In order 
to detect as many antigens as possible the B-cells have developed cellular mechanisms that increase 
the genetic and structural variability of the antibodies they produce. Some of these mechanisms, 
such as clonal expansion and somatic mutations, are triggered by the binding of antibodies to their 
targets (antigens), and their effects can eventually be observed in the sequences and structures of 
mature antibodies (Murphy et al. 2011) 
 
In this study, that is part of a larger project in collaboration with Kostas Stamatopolous and Luca 
Arcaini, we produce three-dimensional models of the antibodies from CLL (1170 cases), SMZL (52 
cases) and MM (57 cases) patients, and determine similarities and differences of their structure in 
the region devoted to bind the antigen (antigen binding site, ABS). Antibodies from patients with 
the same disease are, in a significant portion of all the cases, more similar than antibodies from 
patients with different diseases. This is consistent with the hypothesis that a restricted number of 
antigens is involved in the development of the diseases, and that those antigens are disease-specific. 
 
In order to further improve the ability of our method to detect similarities between antibody 
structures, we have developed, as a final part of the thesis, a method to predict the accuracy of the 
structural models we generate.

Immunoglobulins 
Antibodies (Abs, or immunoglobulins, Igs) are multimeric proteins present in vertebrates’ blood and 
corporal fluids. They have a fundamental role in the immune system, aimed at identifying and 
neutralizing non-self molecules, such as bacterial or viral proteins. Their polymeric structure may 
vary depending on the type of antibody, but the basic structural unit they are composed of is highly 
conserved. Four chains form this unit: two large chains (heavy chains) and two small chains (light 
chains). Each of these chains is composed in its turn by repetition of the nearly identical structural 
domain (four domains in the heavy chains, two in the light chains). Although the general structure 
of all antibodies is very similar, a small region at the tip of the protein is extremely variable, giving 
rise to a huge number of antibodies with slightly different tip structures. This region is known as the 
hypervariable region. Each of these variants can bind to a different target, known as an antigen 
(figure 1)(Murphy et al. 2011).  
 
 
Figure 1: Illustration of the antibody structure of the PDB structure 1IGT. The colored part each indicates a different domain of the 
antibody. At the end of domain VL and VH is the antibody binding site located illustrated with the binding of an antigen (adapted 
frm http://www.bioc.uzh.ch/plueckthun/antibody/Introduction/IGGdomains/index.html) 
 
This large diversity of immunoglobulins allows the immune system to specifically recognize a wide 
variety of antigens. The part of the antigen recognized by an antibody, called epitope, binds with its 
cognate antibody in a highly specific interaction, allowing antibodies to identify and bind only their 
specific antigen among all the different molecules present in an organism. Recognition of an antigen 
by an antibody targets the antigen for attack by other parts of the immune system. Antibodies can 
also neutralize targets directly by, for example, binding to a part of a pathogen essential for the 
infection or by cross linking pathogen molecules and promote their precipitation (Murphy et al. 
2011).

Immunoglobulin sequence diversity 
The variability of the Igs is important as these molecules are devoted to recognize antigens and 
present these to other immune cells as a part of the adaptive immune system.   
  
The variable part of the Ig contains the so called hypervariable regions, which are regions of the 
sequence with highly variable amino acid composition when compared to less variable parts. These 
regions overlap with the so-called complementary determining regions (CDRs) that are defining the 
specificity of each antibody towards its antigen. There are six CDRs in total, three on the heavy chain 
and three on the light chain. Even though the regions are highly variable in sequence, the structure 
of their backbone atoms has been shown to be limited to a restricted number of conformations 
called canonical structures (CS) that are determined based on a few very conserved residues both 
found in the hypervariable regions and in the conserved beta sheet framework (Chothia et al. 1987) 
(figure 2).  
 
 
Figure 2: variable region of an antibody (4GGF).  The hypervariable regions of the variable region are colored, L1 (red), L2 (green), L3 
(blue), H1 (yellow), H2(magentas), and H3 (cyan), the framework of the antibody is colored in grey. The visualization was done in 
Pymol. 
 
The antibody binding site (ABS) sequence diversity is the effect of a number of different factors in 
the Ig development. The VH regions are determined by three genes, the variable (V), the diversity 
(D), and the joining (J) gene. Each gene is present in multiple copies in the genome and can be 
combined in a large number of combinations through a process named VDJ recombination that 
occurs during B-cell development. The same is the case for the VL region, but this region only 
consists of the V and J genes. Due to polymorphism the number of alleles can vary across people. 
For the Kappa light chain is there 34-38 different variable genes alleles, the lambda light chain has 
29-33 different alleles and for the heavy chain is it 38-46 different alleles. For the joining gene there 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
are 5 different alleles for the Kappa light chain, 4-5 for the lambda light chain and 6 for the heavy 
chain. The heavy chain has 23 different alleles of the diversity genes. When the genes are spliced 
together a few number of amino acids are used between the genes, which also contributes to the 
variability (Murphy et al. 2011).   
 
A second mechanism that further increases the variability of antibody sequences is the so-called 
somatic hyper mutation (SHM). It occurs to the mature B-cell upon activation. SHM are point 
mutations introduced in the whole rearranged V region to a rate much higher than in the rest of the 
genome.  The combined effect of gene recombination and SHM’s can theoretically give rise to more 
than 1015 different Ig sequences, and there are evidences that each individual can make 
approximately 1011 different antibodies (Murphy et al. 2011) 
 
Many different antibody structures are generated in response to the antigenic pressure present in 
the body. Antigens are molecules capable of inducing an immune response. Antigens can have a 
large number of different structures, which are the reason for the variability in the ABS on the 
antibody. When the ABS capable of recognize the antigen has been found, this antibody will be 
produced in a great amount in order to fight the infection (Murphy et al. 2011). 
 
Antibody modeling 
The three dimensional structure of antibodies can be determined experimentally by either X-ray 
crystallography or nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy. Both methods are time 
consuming and costly, which have led researchers to make prediction models to determine the 3D 
structure based only on the sequence of the antibody.  
 
The CDR loops of the antibodies are as described previously more variable then the rest of the 
variable region and in 1989 Chothia and Lesk discovered that this large sequence variability does 
not result in an equally large structure variability. They discovered that five of the six loops only use 
a limited number of conformations based on a few residues both in the hypervariable region and in 
the conserved beta sheet framework. Such conformations are named canonical structures (CSs) and 
led to the canonical structure method, which is also used by modeling methods to determine the 
loop conformations (Chothia et al. 1989). 
The few residues responsible for the conformation of the backbone were found do to their packing, 
hydrogen bonding or ability to assume unusual , , and  conformation (figure 3). 
 
==PAGE:12==

The H3 loop differs from the other loops since it can assume a very large number of different 
conformations. Only two CS conformations have been found, affecting a small portion of the whole 
H3 loop. This is due to the special characteristics of the H3 loops, the longest and most variable loop. 
Its region is formed by parts of all three of the heavy chain genes and by several random amino acids 
introduced in the VDJ rearrangement process (Shirai et al. 1996) 
 
Several studies have been done to determine the precise amount of CS structure for each loop and 
it is estimated that 80% of the L1, L2, L3, H1, and H2 loops belong to known CS’s (Al-lazikani et al. 
1997; Morea et al. 1998; Chailyan et al. 2011) (table 1).  
When modeling the loop, it can be assumed that if the target loop and the template loop have the 
same CS, the expected RMSD will be usually below 1.2 Å RMSD (Marcatili et al. 2013).   
 
Today a number of different webservers exist which can be used to predict the 3D structure of the 
variable region of the antibody. One of these is the Prediction of ImmunoGlobulin Structure (PIGS) 
web server, which is based on the comparison of the sequence of the antibody that is subject to the 
modeling procedure (target) to those of experimentally determined structures (templates) that are 
eventually used to build the antibody model. This method is called homology modeling and is based 
on the belief that the conformation of the backbone structure is limited to a number of conserved 
structures (Marcatili et al. 2008).  
 
The quality of the model, measured as the structural differences between the model and the real 
structure, and in general the similarity between different proteins can be assessed with different 
measure methods. Root mean square deviation (RMSD) and the template modeling (TM) score are 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
two of them. RMSD is the most common measure of protein structural similarity. The distance 
between corresponding residues is calculated after an optimal rotation of one structure to another 
(Kabsch 1978). The lower the RMSD value is the better are the structural similarities between the 
two protein structures.  
The TM score also measures the distance between corresponding residues, but weights the residues 
pairs at smaller distances relatively stronger than those of larger distances. This means the global 
topology are considered more important than the local structural variations (Zhang & Skolnick 
2005).

Clustering methods 
Clustering is used to group a set of objects based on the features describing them. For this study the 
antibody models were clustered based on their structural similarities in the ABS. 
There exist different methods for clustering, hierarchical clustering was used in this study. 
Hierarchical clustering is based on either the top-down or bottom-up principle. It means that all of 
the object either start to be at the top of the hierarchy in one group and then split into smaller 
groups going down (top-down) or that all of the objects start in each an individual group at the 
bottom of the hierarchy and then put together in larger groups going up (top-up) (Marcel et al 1998, 
6).  
 
To determine which objects should be grouped together there are a number of different methods 
to calculate the distances between and within each group at each step of the hierarchy. For the 
bottom up principle there are the ward, average, complete, and McQuitty linking functions(Murtagh 
2014). Each of these methods have a different criterion for how to calculate the distances between 
the objects, which gives a slightly different output. For the top down principle the methods are 
called divisive analysis (Diana) (Kumar et al. 2015). 
 
In order to determine which output of the clustering describe the clustering in the most accurate 
way each cluster is validated based on the consistency within a cluster and illustrated with how well 
each objects lies within the clusters, based on this each cluster are given a score called the silhouette 
score (Rousseeuw 1987). The highest silhouette score determines the best number of clusters and 
which clustering methods best to describe the objects.  
 
For this study the clustering of the modeled Igs are only as good as the quality of the models. A 
method for improving the clustering is by including an estimate of the model quality in the clustering 
procedure.  
 
Prediction model: Random forest 
In this work, we use the Random Forest technique to predict the reliability of each antibody model 
built and eventually to perform more reliable comparison between the different models. 
Random forest is a machine learning tool proposed by Leo Breiman in 1999. It is an ensemble 
classifier that uses many decision tree models and can be used for classification (categorical 
variables) or regression (continuous variables) application (Breiman 2001).  
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
Random forest is based on two techniques: bagging, in which we construct various trees on a 
different random part of the dataset, and random feature selection, in which a smaller random set 
of the overall number of features are chosen for the prediction of each tree. 
For regression the random forest prediction is done as the unweighted average over the collection 
of the total number of trees generated. This is based on the principle that each of the trees is slightly 
wrong, but when considering the average of all the trees the final prediction will be more precise 
(Breiman 2001). 
 
In order to achieve the best result, it is important to keep the individual error for each of the trees 
low. This is done by growing each of the trees to maximum depth and using bagging and random 
feature selection (Breiman 2001).   
 
One typical problem that can be encountered when training a machine learning method such as 
Random Forest is overfitting. It occurs when the model describes random errors or noise instead of 
the underlying relationship of the samples (Breiman 2001).  
 
To ensure that a predictor is not overfitted, the most common solution is to evaluate its results on 
a dataset not previously used for training. Cross validation is an iterative way to do this independent 
evaluation, performed by dividing the dataset in K subset. In turn, each one of the subset is taken 
apart, then the method is trained on all the remaining data, and finally the unused subset is used to 
evaluate the results. The accuracy of the method is defined as the average accuracy over all the K 
evaluation subsets. 
In Random Forest, cross validation is implemented in parallel with the training. For each trained 
tree, 1/3 of the samples, called Out-of-bag (OOB), are left out of the prediction and used to validate 
the model to prevent over prediction (Breiman 2001). 
 
A large number of features can be used in the Random forest prediction. To validate the importance 
of each of the feature, the Gini Impurity (GI) Index is used. The GI Index is a measure of how often 
a randomly chosen object from the dataset would be incorrectly labeled if the feature were not 
used for the prediction (Marcatili 2015, 16).

Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia 
Chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) is a disease that usually affects elder people and it’s the most 
common leukemia in the western world (Kostareli et al. 2012). CLL is characterized by a clonal 
expansion of neoplastic mature B lymphocytes. Its progression is heterogeneous: some patients 
never need treatments and other experience a more aggressive disease, which requires intense 
therapies. CLL cells are mostly found in spleen, lymph nodes and bone marrow (Chiorazzi et al. 
2005). 
The leukemic transformation appears to be connected, at least in a portion of all patients, by specific 
genomic alterations causing the deletion of specific micro-RNA genes and increasing the resistance 
of B cells toward apoptosis (Chiorazzi et al. 2005).  
 
Development  
Deletion of chromosome 13, specifically involving band 13q14 (del(13q14)), represent the most 
frequently observed cytogenetic aberration in CLL and occurs in approximately 55% of all cases and 
is part of the early genetic events leading to CLL. Animal models have shown miRNAs 15a and 16-1, 
which are located in the critically region del(13q14), play a role in the development of a monoclonal 
B-cell lymphocytosis-like disorder (Calin et al. 2002).  
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
In approximately 25% of the chemotherapy-naïve patients a deletion of chromosome 11 (el(11q) 
can be found in the advanced disease stages and 10% in the early stages. Band 11q23 is frequently 
encompasses by the deletion, 11q23 is harboring the gene ATM, which encodes for the proximal 
DNA damage rezone kinase ATM (Döhner et al. 1997) 
Trisomy of chromosome 12 are found in 10-20% of the patients, but the pathogenesis of this 
deletion are unknown and the prognostic implication are still up for debate (Seiffert et al. 2012).  
For 5-8% of the patients’ deletion of chromosome 17 has been found for these deletion the band 
17q13 is almost always included, in this band is located the important tumor suppressor gene TP53. 
A deletion in this gene is usually associated to a poor prognosis (Seiffert et al. 2012).  
 
Pathogenicity 
The pathogenicity of CLL is still not clear, but it is possible to divide CLL into subtypes depending on 
a large set of biological features as genomic abnormalities and immune cell signaling, which all 
contribute to the evolution and expansion of the neoplastic clones (Döhner et al. 2000; Burger & 
Chiorazzi 2013) 
The gene repertoire in CLL is restricted to a number of IGHV genes that is distinct from that of IgM+ 
CD5+ normal blood B cells. The three mostly used IGHV genes in CLL cells are the IGHV1-69, IGHV4-
34 and IGHV3-7 (Fais et al. 1998).  
The IGHV1-69 showed a preference for the IGHD3-3 and the IGHJ6 genes. The other variable genes 
do not show a preference for the diversity and joining genes (Fais et al. 1998).  
SHM have shown to have a great influence on CLL (Fais et al. 1998; Stamatopoulos et al. 2007; 
Murray et al. 2008). According to the amount of SM patients can be divided in two groups: 
unmuatated and the mutated. To define in which of the two groups a patient belongs to, the 
clonotypic IGHV gene is identified, compared to the closest germline gene and the difference in 
percent between the nucleotide sequence for the two genes is calculated. Cases displaying IGHV 
genes with an identity to the original germline gene equal to or greater than 98% are classified as 
unmutated and those with identity below 98% are defined as mutated (Damle 1999; Hamblin 1999).  
 
Mutational status for specific genes 
The number of SHM has been showed to depend on which IGHV gene the Ig express. The IGHV1-69 
has shown to carry very few or no mutations. IGHV3-7, IGHV3-23 and IGHV4-34 genes where 
significantly mutated, which has led to the belief CLL development is antigen dependent (Puente et 
al. 2011).  
The group of Hamblin, Stevenson and Chiorazzi showed in 1999 that a direct correlation between 
the survival rate and the mutational status can be established. Patients defined with mutated IGHV 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
genes generally follow a more indolent course and patients with unmutated IGHV genes tend to 
show evidence of advanced, progressive disease, adverse cytogenetic profiles, clonal evolution, and 
resistance to therapy (Jackson & Bartek 2009) 
The load of somatic mutations was the first accurate molecular marker used for disease 
prognostication and is still used as one of the strongest independent prognostic markers in CLL. This 
marker is important since it is independent of the actual tumor burden and do not change during 
the clinical course.  
 
Stereotyped BCR 
Ghia et al (2005) suggested that prognostic information could be determined not only from the 
usage of specific genes and their mutational load, but also from molecular features of the Igs among 
cases utilizing similar IGHV genes. This leads to the hypothesis of Igs from CLL patients can be divided 
into stereotypes depending on the Ig genes and the HCDR3 sequence (Ghia et al. 2005).  
  
30% of all CLL patients show the IGHV-IGHD-IGHJ region can be described as one of the stereotypes 
(Kostareli et al. 2012). The study by Agathangelidis et al (2012) determined these findings on an 
even larger dataset did not increase the percent of patients caring a stereotype Ig. This suggests 
that patients can be divided into two groups; stereotypes and heterogeneous (non-stereotyped).  
Two main types of patterns for the stereotyped Igs have been determined: “mainly combinatorial”, 
essentially encoded by the unmutated germline sequences of specific IGHD and IGHJ genes, and 
“combinatorial + junctional”, defined by junctional residues located between the IGHV-IGHD and 
IGHD-IGHJ genes. 
The stereotyped Igs can be divided into subsets, which defined by primarily on their sequence and 
the HCDR3 loop (table 3).

Splenic Marginal Zone Lymphoma 
Splenic marginal zone lymphoma (SMZL) is a distinct low grade B-cell lymphoma and is accounting 
for <2% of lymphoid neoplasm. It mostly affects elderly patients and involves the spleen, bone 
marrow and peripheral blood. The median survival for patients with SMZL is 10 years, where 70% 
need treatment, and 25% will have a progressive disease leading to an early death (Baliakas et al. 
2015).  
SMZL is one of three types of lymphomas originated from marginal zone (MZ) B-cell, the other two 
types being nodal marginal zone lymphoma and extranodal marginal zone lymphoma of mucosa 
associated lymphoid tissue (MALT lymphoma).  
 
The spleen is a secondary lymphoid organ in which B-cells are matured and activated. The spleen 
cleans the blood and the MZ B cells are being used as the defense against antigens transported by 
the blood stream. You could say the spleen works as a huge lymph node. The MZ B-cells are located 
in the marginal zone part of the white pulp in the spleen (figure 4).  
The marginal zone is defined as the outermost portion of the white pulp, the structure permits the 
transit of B cells from and to the bloodstream and facilitated encounter between blood borne 
pathogens with B cells and macrophages (Colombo et al. 2013).  
MZ B cells are thought to produce IgM antibodies without the signals from T cells that are usually 
needed for B cell activation. MZ B cells work as the first line of defense against bacterial infection 
before a proper response from the follicular (FO) B cells can be developed (Colombo et al. 2013).

The development of Marginal Zone B-Cell  
Four surface receptors are known to have an involvement in the development of MZ B-cells, toll like 
receptors (TLR), B-cell receptor (BCR), B-cell activating factor receptor (BAFFR), and NOTCH2 (table

and signals.  
Mutations in the NOTCH2 gene is found in approximately 20% of all SMZL patients and are the most 
frequent mutations found in SMZL patients. The NOTCH2 gene is part of the canonical NOTCH signal, 
which is mediated by NOTCH ligand-receptors interactions between neighboring cells. The DLL1 
mediated NOTCH2 signal has been shown to be critical for the MZ B-cells differentiation and 
retention in the splenic marginal zone (Du 2015).

Kruppel-like factor 2 (KLF2) is a transcription factor and has shown to be important as a regulator 
for developing of SMZL B cells. KLF2 is a member of the KFL family of transcription factors, which 
have a divers function in different cell linage. Studies have shown that KLF2 mutation is a frequently

in other kinds of lymphomas. The KLF2 inactivating mutation has a pathogenic role in SMZL (Ja et al. 
2014)  
 
SMZL and antibodies (BCR disease and antigens) 
Several studies have looked into the genetic variations in the Ig genes of patients affected by SMZL. 
Three IGHV genes are frequently observed among SMZL patients: IGHV1-2, IGHV4-34, and IGHV3-
33, accounting for approximately 45% of all cases. One third of all patients with SMZL uses the IGHV1 
-2 gene and almost all (90%) have the same variant of the gene - the allele 04. The IGHV1-2*04 allele 
is characterized by having a minimal amount of somatic hyper mutations and a longer CDR3 region, 
with common motifs (Baliakas et al. 2015).  
The SMZL Ig light chain also showed to prefer three different genes, the IGKV3-20, IGKV1-8, and 
IGLV2-14. This preference for specific genes has led to the theory that antigens might be involved 
in the clonal selection of the Igs (DU 2015).   
 
Several studies have shown that hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection is associated with SMZL. HCV is a 
hepto- and lymphotropic virus, which causes a chronic infection with both hepatic and extrahepatic 
manifestations. Among the extrahepatic manifestations HCV leads to development of autoimmunity 
and lymphoproliferative disorders. In a large study form Italy, they found evidence of HCV infection 
in 25% of the cases. The findings suggest HCV infection has a causative role in the development of 
SMZL (Yu & Lin 2013).  
==PAGE:24==

Patients with HCV associated cryoglobulinemia have clonal marginal zone-like B cells expressing 
IGHV1-69. The clonal expansion of IGHV1-69 expressing B-cells is thought to be antigen driven. For 
SMZL patients with HCV, IGHV1-69 has shown to be a frequently expressed gene and clinical data 
for the patients show an over representation of patients with autoimmunity (Yu & Lin 2013). 
 
Multiple Myeloma 
Multiple Myeloma (MM) is a hematological malignancy and characterized by abnormal 
accumulation of clonal plasma cells in the bone marrow. MM accounts for 10% of hematological 
malignancies and 1.6% of all U.S. cancer deaths.  
Significant prevalence differences are observed between age, gender, and ethnic group, which 
suggest a genetic predisposition to MM. For African Americans in Europe and America incidence of 
MM is two to three times higher, which makes it the most common hematological malignancy in 
this ethnic group. The median age at diagnosis is 69 years, with three out of four diagnosed at the 
age above 55 years, and two out of three of the patients being men. Improvement in the efficiency 
of the therapeutic strategies and supportive care have increased the median survival from 3 to 6 
years in the past two decades (Ghafouri-fard et al. 2015). 
 
The expansion of MM cells in the bone marrow can cause bone pain and fractures, symptoms of 
inadequate hematopoiesis, hypercalcemia, hyperviscosity, renal failure, infections, peripheral 
neuopathy and secretion of paraproteins, which are either Ig light chains or whole Igs. The 
paraproteins have several effects on the body, including impaired immune function, abnormally 
high viscosity of the blood and kidney damage (Ghafouri-fard et al. 2015)  
 
MM cells are similar to long lived, post-germinal center plasma cells, which are characterized by 
strong bone marrow dependence, extensive somatic hypermutation of immunoglobulin genes, and 
the absence of IgM expression. The difference between MM cells and healthy plasma cells are their 
ability to retain the potential to return to a lower proliferative state (Cagnetta et al. 2015).  
 
Recent studies show MM is characterized by a significant heterogeneity, which is mainly related to 
molecular characteristics of the tumor clone (Cagnetta et al. 2015).  
MM have a higher rate of genomic changes acquisition per cell division compared to normal cells, 
which is defined as genome instability. The specific genome instabilities leading to MM 
tumorigenesis are not fully understood, but the most frequently observed changes include 
hyperdiploidy, loss of chromosome 13, and specific translocation like t(11;14) (q13;q32); t(4;14) 
(p16;q32); or t(14;16) (q14;16) (q23;q32) (Cagnetta et al. 2015).  
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
 
Two categories, based on aberration in their genomic architecture, were identified for MM showing 
different prognostic outcome, hyperdiploid and nonhyperdiploid. The hyperdiploid accounts for 50-
60% of the MM patients and is defined as the trisomy of chromosomes 3, 5, 7, 11, 15, 19, or 21. The 
nonhyperdiploid karyotype is frequently characterized by translocations affecting immunoglobulin 
heavy chain (IGV) locus at 14q32, including t(11;14) and t(4;14) (Cagnetta et al. 2015).

Materials and Methods 
Dataset 
We assembled a sequence dataset consisting only of antibody sequences from patients of three B 
cell cancers diseases: Chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), Splenic Marginal Zone Lymphoma (SMZL) 
and Multiple Myeloma (MM). 
 
Stamatopolus provided the 1.909 CLL sequences and Arcaini provided the 52 SMZL sequences. The 
CLL and SMZL sequences were rearranged IGHV-D-J and IGKV-J or IGLV-J paired segments were 
sequenced from cDNA from patients diagnosed with one of the two diseases.  
For CLL, 342 had been used in a previous study be Marcatili et al 2013. Additional 397 had also been 
modeled by Marcatili (unpublished results), for a total of 739 of Ig models.  
The remaining 1.170 CLL Ig sequences were provided by Stamatopoulos and used for genetic 
analysis and to improve the prediction models. The 52 paired sequences from SMZL were provided 
by Arcaini and Zibellini along with clinical data on each of the patients, providing information on 
aggressiveness of the disease, HCV positive or negative and autoimmunity. The SMZL sequences 
were used for genetic analysis, mutation rate and for 3D model prediction.  
The provided CLL and SMZL sequences had been analyzed by using the ImMunoGeneTics 
Information System (IMGT) (http://www.imgt.org), which made it possible to analysis the genetics 
and mutation rates. 
  
Each of the Igs are given a mutational status based on the level of somatic hyper mutation in both 
of the V segments, the mutation level should not exceed 2%, for the Ig to be labeled “unmutated”, 
as defined by Marcatili et al (2013). For levels of somatic mutation equal to or transcending 2% for 
both the IGHV and IGK/LV, the Ig was labeled “mutated”.   
Based on other studies Agathangelidis et al. (2012) and (Kostareli et al. 2012) the CLL Igs are divided 
into stereotypes depending on the amino acids sequence of the HCDR3 loop as previously described 
(introduction CLL).  
For MM 57 Ig translated sequences (IGHV + IGK/LV) were retrieved from the GenBank by Tatiana 
Prokaeva and used to make 3D models and variable gene pair comparison (table 5).  
 
A second dataset is used to train our Random Forest for model accuracy prediction consisted of 
3370 models of Igs with an experimentally solved structure in the PDB database. Those models were 
done by systematically excluding the modeled antibody as template and by performing the 
modeling procedure using all the possible options present in the PIGS web server. The dataset

Pipeline 
This is the general pipeline used in this work. More details on each step are described in the next 
sections. Each antibody in the sequence dataset is modeled using the PIGS server and afterwards all 
the antibodies are structurally superposed against each other with the LGA package. For each 
alignment a TM score is calculated to determine how similar the antibodies are. A distance matrix 
is calculated from all the TM scores and used to produce a clustering, Hieratical clustering methods 
are used on the distance matrix and for each number of clusters a silhouette score is calculated to 
determine which of the clustering methods and number of cluster had the highest score and thereby 
the best way of describing the data.    
==PAGE:28==
 
27 
The APBS software is used to calculate the electrostatic potential on the solvent accessible surface 
of each model structures. Figures of the 3D models of the antibodies are generated with the Pymol 
software.  
 
The machine learning tool Random Forest is used on a dataset of antibody models from the PDB 
database in order to establish which of the variables are most important to obtain a good RMSD 
value and if it is possible to predict the RMSD value based on the variables available.       
 
Ig three dimensional Models 
The Prediction of ImmunoGlobulin Structure (PIGS) web server is used to make the 3D models of 
the variable heavy chain and variable light chain region of the immunoglobulin (webpage). The PIGS 
server models the sequences based on homology modeling and on the canonical structure method 
(Marcatili et al. 2008)  
Homology modeling is based on the assumption the coordinates of the backbone atoms of the 
framework are identical to one or more known antibody structures. PIGS uses 689 known 
immunoglobulins structures for the homology modeling (Marcatili et al. 2014) 
 
In this study multiple sequences are modeled in PIGS at the same time with the same settings for 
choice of methods, loop grafting methods, and side chain modeling methods. Best H and L chain is 
chosen as the choice of method, for the Loop grafting method the setting is set to graft all loops and 
the side chain modeling methods is used with the default settings. These settings give PIGS the 
biggest flexibility to model the antibodies, by allowing the usage of multiple templates.    
 
For the SMZL Igs a total of 42 models are built correctly using the procedure described above. The 
remaining models are not used further due to the output of incomplete models by the procedure. 
In four of the incomplete models no proper HCDR3 template is found, in one case no template for 
all the HCDRs is found, and in two cases no template for the IGK/LV are found. 
 
For MM a total of 47 models are built correctly and the remaining models are not used for further 
investigation. Four of them are due to no proper template for the H3 loop and the rest because no 
proper templates can be found for all of the LCDR loops.  
From the previous study by Marcatili et al (2013) and later unpublished data, 739 CLL models are 
provided for the 3D structural comparison of the diseases.  
 
==PAGE:29==

Structural analysis and clustering 
The completed 3D models for the three diseases are all aligned based on the structural 
superposition of the CDR regions with the LGA package (Zemla 2003). The superposition is 
performed on the CDR regions only, using L chain residues number 24-33, 49-53, and 90-97, and H 
chain residues 25-33, 51-55, and 92-104.  
 
Two residues are not considered to be aligned if the distance between their C’s is >4 Å. The C is 
provides a more detailed information on the direction of the amino acid side chain after 
superpositions.  
 
A TM score is calculated for each alignment. The TM score weights the residues pairs at smaller 
distances relatively stronger than those of larger distances. This means the global topology is 
considered more important than the local structural variations.

R is an open-source free statistical package. R uses a command-line syntax and for this study a 
graphical user interface called Rstudio is used.  
The R package gplots is used to generate a Heatmap visualizing the similarity between the Ig models 
TM scores and used to give an impression of the Igs ability to cluster. A heatmap is generated solely

Clustering methods are used to group together samples based on their similarities. All of the 
clustering methods are defined as hierarchical clustering. Hierarchical clustering builds the clusters 
as a hierarchy showing the relations between the models based on how similar they are.  To 
determine the best way of clustering the 3D models five different cluster methods are used, 
Average, Complete, McQuitty, Ward and Diana. 
 
To determine the best number of clusters for each of the different clustering methods a silhouette 
score is calculated for each method. A variable number of cluster is considered; in the SMZL 
clustering we calculate the silhouette for 1 to 15 clusters. In the clustering of all the Igs together, 
we calculate the silhouette for 1 to 100 clusters. This is done in R with the packages cluster, fbc and 
optpart. A dendrogram is drawn in R to visualize the clustering.  
 
Clustering analysis 
If different antigens are involved in the etiology of the three diseases, the Igs structures will cluster 
together depending on the antigen. The Igs within the same cluster will show structural similarities 
in the ABS, not necessarily depending on the similarity of the germline. If different antigens are 
involved with the three diseases the Igs will cluster depending on the disease. In order to check if 
this is the case a statistical test is performed to check the enrichment of any disease in the clusters 
generated form the structural data.    
 
The clusters are analyzed based on the number of diseases represented and the 3D structure of the 
immunoglobulins in them. The graphical representation of the clusters is generated with the A2R 
package in R. The A2R package makes it possible to illustrate where the Igs from the different 
diseases are located in the clustering and silhouette value for each split.   
 
For SMZL models alone the clusters are compared to the clinical data looking for correlation with 
any of the parameters analyzed in the data part and how the models are clustered. The parameters 
used for the analysis are the residue length of the H3 loop, mutational status, the SMZL score, HCV 
status, and IGHV gene. A H3 loop is considered long if the loop consists of 18 or more residues, this 
is based in the average of the residue length of all the SMZL Igs. The SMZL score tells how aggressive 
the SMZL disease is for the patients and it has three levels, high, intermediate and low, high being 
the most aggressive.

Images 
The antibody 3D structures are visualized and compared by calculating the solvent accessible 
surface using adaptive Poisson-Boltzmann Solver (APBS) (http://www.poissonboltzmann.org/). 
APBS illustrates the solvent accessible surface of the antibody and the electrostatic potential on the 
surface. The intensity of the molecular surface is set to -4 to +4 (Baker et al. 2001).  
PDB2PQR is used to prepare the structures for the APBS calculation. The Ig images are generated by 
using the 3D visualization tool Pymol with installation of the APBS plugin. 
 
Random Forest 
The machine learning tool Random forest regression method is used to predict the RMSD value 
based on the variables; identity of the sequence, identity of the framework, identity of the grafting, 
and for the CDR regions the canonical structure and the residue length of the loop are also used for 
the prediction. The prediction is done for the normal immunoglobulins in R with the package 
randomForest.  
 
To avoid over fitting, we don’t use models of the same antibody even though the models are 
produced using different options and different templates, this results in a training set of 168 Igs. 
The training set is randomized split into 5 equally large sets and used for the prediction.  
The model generated from the five training set is used on the whole dataset and validated based on 
the correlation between the predicted RMSD vs actually RMSD and the GI index of the variables.

Clustering for the comparison of the antibodies 
The Ig models for the three diseases were all aligned with the LGA package and based on the 
alignment given a TM score. All the scores were collected in a distance matrix. Five different 
clustering methods were used on the distance matrix and the silhouette score determined the ward 
methods gave the best clustering. The silhouette score increased up to a number of approximately 
500 clusters, which was considered excessive for 836 modeled Igs. For the biological comparison 
between the clusters, 41 clusters were chosen as this number had a local maximum in silhouette 
score (0.06) (figure 13).

As for the SMZL clustering, the Igs are clustered depending on if their light chain is Kappa or Lambda 
and all of the 41 clusters are either pure Lambda or pure Kappa. There are 15 Lambda clusters and 
26 Kappa clusters. SMZL Igs are present in 20 of the 41 clusters and MM Igs are present in 24 of the 
clusters. Eleven of the clusters are pure CLL clusters.

The correctness in the clustering of the model Igs are depending on the quality of the model used. 
For future clustering of modeled Igs the machine learning tool Random forest was used to make a

Random forest 
The machine learning tool Random forest is used to determine if there is a correlation between the 
RMSD value of a modeled Ig and the sequence identity between the model and the templates used 
for the modeling. This is done in order to optimize the model prediction for future antibody 
modeling.  
 
The prediction is done for the Global RMSD value, the H and L chains RMSD values, the RMSD values 
for the four CDR loops H1, H3, L1, L3 and the RMSD value for the ABS.  
The variables used for the prediction were the sequence identity between the model antibody and 
the template for each of the regions of the antibody. For each of the CDRs the canonical structure 
and the length of the loops were also used as variables, this gave a total of 27 variables used for the 
prediction.  
The variables used are evaluated based on how much they increased the node purity from the GI 
index. In general, the most important variables for the individual RMSD predictions is the 
corresponding sequence identity for region of the antibody (figure 22).

Discussion and conclusions 
Both CLL and SMZL have been suggested to be connected to antigen driven events. For CLL this has 
been suggested by Agathangelidis et al and Kostareli et al in several recent studies. For SMZL it has 
been suggested by Zibellini et al. (2010) and Yu & Lin (2013) .  
Both diseases use a restricted number of germlines and of gene combinations statistically unlikely 
to be observed by chance, thus reinforcing the antigen-driven hypothesis.  
The dataset used in this study showed the same trends with the most used genes for CLL being 
IGHV1-69, IGHV3-21, IGLV3-21, and IGKV3-20, which are the same for the before mentioned 
studies. For SMZL the most common genes were IGHV1-2, IGHV3-23, IGKV3-20, and IGLV1-47.  
Marcatili et al (2013) showed that similar restrictions could be observed in the antibody structural 
repertoire as well by clustering models of the CLL Ig variable regions. Several clusters were enriched 
in antibodies with a specific mutational status and belonging to the same stereotype class, often 
independently to the presence of a common gene usage.  
 
Only about 20% of the CLL Igs both in this study and in the previous study done on CLL Ig sequences 
belongs to one of the stereotype defined by the conformation of the HCDR3 loop, where the 
structural definition implemented by Marcatili et al. and used in this work has the ability to extend 
this by defining structural stereotypes that encompass a much larger (up to 70%) fraction of the 
dataset. 
At this point there has not been published any extensive structural analysis of SMZL Ig models. In 
this study I modeled and clustered 42 out of 52 SMZL variable regions. In previous studies on SMZL 
the focus had been on the variable genes and the relationship between SMZL and the hepatitis C 
virus (HCV). As in the original study by Marcatili et al (2013), we observe a structural segregation of 
models with Kappa and Lambda light chain.   
 
For the SMZL Igs the detailed clustering showed that both the clinical data and genetic data has a 
minor but clear correlation with the clustering.  
For HCV positive patients, the Ig structures showed to cluster very closely together and displaying a 
very similar structural surface even thought the genetics of the Igs showed differences in the 
variable heavy chain genes. This corroborates the idea that, in these cases, the development of 
SMZL could come from an antigen response driven by HCV.  
For the SMZL Igs the mutation status did not seem to have as great an influence on the clustering 
as it did for CLL. There have been no studies suggesting a relationship between the mutation status 
and the pathogenesis of the disease for SMZL patients.   
 
==PAGE:62==
 
61 
The HCDR3 loop conformation was mainly determined by the length and direction of the loop. For 
the H3 loops longer then 18 residues, two conformations could be seen in many clusters, either the 
loop was sticking out of the top of the ABS in a hairpin conformation, or the loop was lying in 
between the two chains to give the ABS a flat structure. For the Igs with shorter H3 loop the ABS 
conformation showed either to be cavity or having a flat structure. 
The H3 loops showed to have a great influence on the SMZL kappa clustering, which almost had a 
perfect split in clusters with long H3 loops and clusters whit short H3 loops.  
The SMZL Igs with a very short H3 loop were usually grouped together and, quite interestingly, had 
a long L1, which was almost uniquely present in these cases. The length of the L1 loop made it 
possible to locate on the surface structure.  
 
The MM Igs had very diverse gene pairings: out of 57 Igs only two had the same gene combination. 
For this study there was no clinical data available and for the gene analysis only the IGHV and IGK/LV 
genes were available. The analysis of the IGHV and IGK/LV gene pairs fits with the idea that the MM 
development come from mutation errors in the B cell development.  
IGKV1D-39, IGLV3-21, IGHV5-51 and IGHV1-69 were the most common of the variable genes used 
by the MM Igs. The two genes IGLV3-21 and IGHV1-69 were also very common for both SMZL and 
CLL.  
 
In order to understand if the clusters observed were disease-specific or possibly conserved across 
different diseases, we repeated the clustering merging all the Ig models of the three diseases 
together. The long term goal, that we could not pursue here for time constraints, is to add to this 
comparison a large dataset of antibodies, either modeled or experimentally resolved, not related to 
any lymphoproliferative disorder, so to have a complete view of the antibody structural landscape 
in the three diseases we studied.  
A problem that we encountered when comparing the structures from the three diseases was that 
the silhouette score used to determine the number of cluster increased up to the point of 500 
clusters, dispersing the approximately 800 Igs in mostly individual clusters. Therefore, we decided 
to use 41 clusters, corresponding to a local maximum in the silhouette score. This is a problem that 
is generally present when using the silhouette as a measure of clustering goodness and not specific 
to our data.  
Nevertheless, we could observe several clusters enriched in either SMZL or MM antibodies. This was 
especially true for the SMZL Igs where 27 of the Igs were placed in one of the six clusters with the 
lowest p value. This also correlated with the idea of SMZL to be antigen driven. The MM Igs showed 
to be more spread in the clustering, even though there was examples of MM Igs clustered very 
==PAGE:63==
 
62 
closely together. It would be interesting in the future to analyze these cluster in more detail to 
understand whether this effect might be connected to an antigenic stimulus or not. 
 
When comparing to the fact that there is 1015 possible combination of Ig sequences, the Igs from 
the three diseases clustered so closely together that it is not statistically possible, which could 
suggest the diseases are coursed be an antigen driven process. 
 
To my knowledge no previous study has been done to determine the structural similarities and 
differences for MM Igs and no study has been done to determine the genetics for MM Ig sequences. 
Based on the 57 MM Ig amino acids sequences gained from GenBank the variable gene pairs showed 
no consistency when compared to the two other disease CLL and SMZL, which both showed to have 
gene pairs more common then others.  
Based on the literature no studies suggest an antigen driven development for MM. The disease is 
developed from mutation errors during the B cell development. Nonetheless, we would like to 
further investigate a possible antigen involvement, given the small but significant structural 
similarity observed between some MM antibodies. Clinical data on the patients could give a more 
detailed understanding of the clustering as seen for the SMZL clustering. 
 
When comparing the sequence information with the 3D structure both within the same disease and 
between diseases similarities could be found in the 3D structure, which were not seen in the 
sequences. The clustering for both SMZL and the comparison showed H3 loops modeled in the same 
way for some of the Igs even thought the genes were different. An explanation could again be an 
antigen driven process resulting in the same ABS structure conformation.  
 
It was possible to model most of the Ig sequences from the three cancer diseases used in this study. 
Reasons for not having models of all the Igs could be found mostly in the missing loop templates for 
some of the CDR’s. At this point scientist have estimated only about 80% of the CS existing have 
been found. This means some of the Igs in this study could belong to some of the missing CS 
structures. There is also the possibility some of the structures determined in this study are not 
correctly modeled due to a wrong CS used as a template. Most of the errors could be found in the 
modeling of the HCDR3 loops, which is the hardest loop to model correctly, due to the length and 
number of possible loop conformations.  
We believe that in future, as the available number of antibody structures increases, the number of 
structures possible to predict by servers like PIGS will increase.  
==PAGE:64==
 
63 
In order to be able to take the quality of the predicted Ig models into account when measuring the 
structural similarity of models, I developed an algorithm, using the machine learning tool random 
forest, to predict the expected accuracy of an Ig model based on the sequence identity to the 
template sequences used for the modeling. I also determined which variables are most important 
for the prediction. This will be useful in improving the quality of the Ig models and thereby getting 
more accurate clusters when comparing the models.  
As expected, the sequence identity for the region to be modeled showed the highest importance of 
the outcome of the modeling. For some of the prediction the CS showed importance along with the 
loop length of the CDR.  
Especially for the H3 loop both the length and CS showed high importance for the prediction of the 
RMSD value.  
For the ABS the H3 loops showed to be the most important, as also suggested by other studies. The 
H3 loop is the most important CDR for the ABS, as it is the loop with most interaction with the 
antigen. To get a good RMSD value for the ABS the H3 loops is the most important loop to model 
correctly and with a good RMSD value.  
As the RMSD for the LCDR2 loop was not predicted the variable for this region showed to be less 
important, which also can be related to the fact that the L2 loops is the shortest of the CDR loops 
and with less CS’s.

Future Perspectives 
In this study we have seen trends on the clustering of the three diseases CLL, SMZL, and MM, but in 
order to determine if the models and alignment, thereby the clustering, gave the most accurate 
picture of reality further studies would be needed. 
 
The TM scores calculated were based on the whole alignment of the CDR loops, even though it is 
known that the heavy chain has a major role in the determination of the antibody specificity. This 
could explain the great impact the L chain showed in the clustering of the modeled Igs.  
In order to establish if the HCDR3 loops could have been clustered better an alignment based only 
on the H chain CDR’s could have been made and compared to the results from the combination of 
all CDR regions. This alignment would properly have shown a better clustering of the heavy chains 
to detriment of the overall ABS superposition accuracy.  
 
This study shows that despite differences in their sequences, the modeled Igs can still have a very 
similar backbone and surface. For a better understanding of the similarities and differences between 
the three cancer diseases a further investigation of the CLL, MM, and SMZL would be needed along 
with the use of “healthy” antibodies for the structural comparison.   
For CLL there is a lot of sequences available and with the use of the prediction model determined 
with random forest future CLL Ig models could be improved and thereby leading to better models 
and clustering.  
In this study only 42 SMZL Ig models were used for the comparison of the SMZL Igs alone. To get a 
full understanding of the Igs in this disease more sequences would be needed along with clinical 
data. This should be used to determine a more accurate number of clusters and as further proof for 
the antigen driven hypothesis.  
Some of the MM Igs despite the genetic differences showed to cluster together in the comparison 
cluster. This could indicate the disease is antigen driven even though this have not been suggesting 
by any other study at this point. To get a more accurate understanding of the MM Igs more 
sequences would be needed along with clinical data.

DTU Systems Biology covers the fields of cellular, molecular and structural biology, 
bioinformatics, computational biology, industrial biotechnology, biomedicine and health. 
Systems biology addresses both the overall properties of a biological system and uses 
scientific approaches to understand specific mechanisms. Therefore, much of the work 
carried out by our research groups is characterized by the incorporation of several 
academic disciplines. 
Fundamental biological research constitutes the point of departure for technological 
activities at DTU Systems Biology, and we put great emphasis on transforming scientific
