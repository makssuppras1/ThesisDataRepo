Abstract
Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are increasingly recognized as essential for grid
stability in renewable-dominated power systems, yet comprehensive business case eval-
uations for Nordic ancillary service markets remain limited. This thesis evaluates the
investment potential of BESS across different system durations (1, 2, and 4-hour config-
urations) participating in Nordic ancillary service markets, using Denmark’s DK2 bidding
zone as a case study.
An integrated methodology combining AnyLogic simulation modelling and discounted
cash flow analysis was developed to assess BESS performance under real market condi-
tions from 2020-2024. The simulation framework models multi-market participation across
Fast Frequency Reserve (FFR), Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR-N), and Fre-
quency Controlled Disturbance Reserve (FCR-D) markets, incorporating technical con-
straints, Limited Energy Reservoir requirements, and degradation effects.
Results reveal compelling investment opportunities across all configurations, with Inter-
nal Rates of Return ranging from 19.37% to 22.74%, substantially exceeding typical in-
vestment thresholds. Multi-market strategies consistently outperform single-market ap-
proaches, highlighting the value of operational flexibility. Critically, the 2-hour configura-
tion emerges as optimal, achieving the highest IRR of 22.74% .
Sensitivity analysis identifies revenue volatility as the primary risk factor, with a 10% rev-
enue decline reducing Net Present Value by 29%. Regulatory barriers, particularly the
”double tariff” structure that contradicts EU regulations, represent significant impediments
requiring policy intervention. The analysis reveals evolving market dynamics toward FCR-
N dominated participation, reflecting increasing renewable penetration and changing grid
stability requirements.
The research demonstrates that Nordic ancillary service markets can support profitable
BESS deployment while providing essential grid services for the renewable energy transi-
tion. However, success requires strategic market participation, optimal duration selection,
and continued regulatory evolution to address structural barriers. The integrated mod-
elling framework developed provides investors and policymakers with practical tools for
evaluating BESS opportunities in rapidly evolving energy markets.

Introduction
1.1
Background
A quarter into the twenty first century, the global energy landscape is undergoing a pro-
found transformation, marked by the accelerating shift toward renewable energy sources
and the accompanying need for greater grid stability. According to the International En-
ergy Agency’s Renewables 2024 report, renewables are expected to account for approx-
imately 46 percent of global electricity generation by 2030, up from around 30 percent in
2023 with solar and wind leading this growth [1]. While this transition supports global de-
carbonization goals, it also introduces significant operational challenges due to the inter-
mittent nature of renewable energy production. This variability was recently demonstrated
by the Iberian blackout in April 2025, where a sudden drop in solar generation from ap-
proximately 18 GW to under 5 GW led to a widespread grid collapse affecting millions of
consumers across Spain and Portugal [2]. The incident exposed the vulnerabilities of a
system increasingly reliant on inverter based renewable assets. In this context, Ancillary
Services (AS) have emerged as essential tools for maintaining grid stability, providing
rapid frequency and voltage corrections when unexpected imbalances occur.

of the growth, while hydropower and other renewables maintain a steady share. Source:
[1].

Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are particularly well suited to deliver these ser-
vices due to their ability to respond within seconds, accurately follow grid control signals
and scale across a wide range of system sizes. With declining costs, improved efficiency
and growing deployment across Europe, BESS technologies represent a critical enabler
for reliable operation of renewable dominated grids. As of 2023, total installed BESS
capacity surpassed 85 GW, up from less than 10 GW in 2016 with lithium-ion batter-
ies accounting for more than 90% of the technology deployed [3]. In 2024 alone, global
BESS deployments increased by 53% year on year, bringing cumulative capacity to ap-
proximately 150 GW, equivalent to around 363 GWh of energy storage and an estimated
market value of over 90 billion US dollars [4].

Europe continues to be a key driver in this trend, with an installed energy capacity of 61
GWh in 2024 led by countries such as Germany and Italy. As the market matures, pri-
vate equity and institutional investors have become increasingly active in the storage and
renewable energy space. In 2024, renewable energy mergers and acquisitions reached
approximately 117 billion US dollars with private equity contributing over 60 billion. While
fossil fuel investments also saw a temporary resurgence, renewables remained highly at-
tractive due to stable regulatory environments, predictable returns and long-term scalabil-
ity. Private equity firms are especially drawn to business models that combine generation
and storage assets, allowing for improved risk management and more robust cash flows.
For these investors, the core focus lies in the financial viability of a project. At the end of
the day, the returns speak louder than ambition.

1.2
Motivation
As the Danish power system transitions toward higher shares of variable renewable en-
ergy, maintaining system stability becomes increasingly dependent on AS. Denmark’s
DK2 region is projected to face increasing AS needs, driven by both the continued phase-
out of fossil-based synchronous generation and the expansion of offshore wind. Accord-
ing to Energinet’s Outlook for Ancillary Services 2024-2040, the demand for frequency
regulation services such as FCR and FFR is expected to double by 2030, with substantial
growth in both upward and downward reserves as system inertia decreases [6]. Addi-
tionally, new market designs, including 4-hour procurement blocks and more frequent
activations, are expected to shape how flexible assets like BESS participate in future AS
markets [6].
BESS is still relatively in the early stages of widespread deployment and research. Al-
though the concept of integrating battery storage into power grids has existed for decades,
utility-scale BESS projects only began to gain meaningful commercial momentum in the
early 2010s. Between 2018 and 2022, the installed capacity of utility-scale BESS more
than quadrupled, enabled by improving business cases and broader participation across
AS markets, arbitrage opportunities, and capacity mechanisms. Recent years have shown
that utility-scale BESS assets can yield highly attractive returns, creating what many re-
fer to as a “gold rush” effect. In the Nordic region, a study on a 1 MW / 1 MWh system
providing FCR-N services could achieve a payback period of just three years based on
historic capacity prices over the 2015-2020 period [7].
Several academic papers have explored the technical and economic value of BESS as-
sets participating in ancillary services in the Nordic region. Filippa et al. simulated a 1 MW /
1 MWh system participating in the FCR-N market in DK2 using frequency data, accounting
for degradation, tariffs, and taxation [8]. Engelhardt et al. investigated energy recovery
strategies in systems providing symmetrical services such as FCR-N, and emphasized
the operational difficulty of maintaining availability when frequency remains persistently
biased over multiple hours [9]. Zeenat Hameed et al. analyzed six years of historical AS
prices in the DK2 market, identifying patterns and profitability for BESS owners across
FCR-N, FCR-D, and fast frequency reserve (FFR) markets [10]. Building on this, Thing-
vad et al. compared single-market and multi-market bidding strategies, concluding that a

dynamic approach could improve annual profits by 22 percent and reduce battery cycling
by 51 percent compared to a static FCR-N strategy [7].
While existing research has provided insights into the revenue potential of BESS in AS
markets, these studies fail to address critical questions such as the optimal MWh capacity
for maximizing cash flows and return on investment in the Nordic market. They also lack
detail on the locational impact on capital expenditure (CAPEX). Furthermore, CAPEX is
often presented as a single figure, with no transparency regarding the underlying cost
components or assumptions. Many studies employ MATLAB or Python-based models
which, while common in academic settings, are less familiar to investors and offer limited
visibility into model runs and outputs. In addition, payback time is frequently used as
the primary indicator of investment attractiveness. Although it can indicate how quickly
an investment might be recovered, payback time does not account for the time value of
money, nor does it reflect long-term profitability or capital risk. Financial metrics such as
net present value (NPV) and internal rate of return (IRR) are more suitable for evaluating
investments, as they incorporate future cash flows, discount rates, and overall project
performance over time.
Investors do not simply assess technical performance but compare returns against other
opportunities. The minimum benchmark is often the national government bond rate, con-
sidered a risk-free rate. As of August 2025, the ten-year Danish government bond yields
approximately 2.3 percent annually [11]. Therefore, a project seeking private capital must
offer an internal rate of return significantly above this threshold to attract interest. This
thesis aims to extend the current body of studies by introducing a more detailed and
investor-oriented approach. We have developed a complete package consisting of a sim-
ulation model and a financial model, which together provide a comprehensive business
case evaluation of BESS participation in Nordic AS markets. Upon request, these models
can be provided. Being able to speak and understand the language of the investor is crit-
ical. Without a clear demonstration of value through robust financial modelling and risk
assessment, even technically sound projects may fail to secure funding. Bridging this gap
between project development and financial analysis is therefore essential for the needed
deployment of BESS projects.
Finally, it is important to emphasize that the AS market is continuously evolving. Market
rules, technical requirements, and participation structures are subject to frequent updates.
A study conducted only two or three years ago may no longer be representative of the
current regulatory and market environment. For this reason, asset owners and potential
investors must remain actively informed about such changes, as they have direct impli-
cations for revenue potential, risk exposure, and investment returns.

1.3
Problem Statement
This thesis examines the business case of a 1 MW BESS unit participating in the Nordic
AS market, using the Danish DK2 bidding zone as a case study. In this study, we extend
the analysis to compare the three different duration systems: 1 MW / 1 MWh, 1 MW /
2 MWh and 1 MW / 4 MWh. Using historical frequency real-time data and prices from
the DK2 market over a time period from 2020 to 2024, we simulate the participation of
these systems in selected ancillary services: FCR-N, FCR-D up, FCR-D down and FFR.
Both single market and multi-market participation are investigated. A central part of our
analysis will be to evaluate how AS revenues have evolved over time and how system
size affects earning potential.
The net result from these simulations will serve as the foundation for building a financial
model based on discounted cash flow (DCF) methodology, using evaluation metrics Net
Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of Return (IRR) to assess investment attractive-
ness. These indicators are widely used by private equity firms and infrastructure investors
to assess the financial feasibility of energy storage investments. As highlighted in recent
trends, capital allocation in renewable and storage projects is driven by strong underlying
business cases. By demonstrating the investment potential of different BESS configura-
tions under real market conditions, this thesis seeks to support more informed investment
decision-making and unlock private capital for utility-scale BESS deployment in Denmark.
To guide this analysis, the following research questions are posed:
1. What is the investment potential of BESS with varying durations when participating
in Nordic AS markets?
2. How does a single-market strategy compare to a multi-market strategy?
3. How can integrated simulation and financial modelling frameworks support invest-
ment decision-making for BESS projects in Nordic AS markets?
1.3.1
Structure
This thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 gives an introduction to ancillary services
in the Nordics. Chapter 3 outlines the methodology, covering the simulation model, anal-
ysis of battery degradation, and financial model. In this work, we first use a simulation
environment to model BESS participation in the Nordic AS markets, incorporating market
rules such as allowed bidding capacity, grid tariffs, and newly implemented limited energy
reservoir requirements. We then integrate BESS-specific technical parameters into the
evaluation, including degradation rates. Finally, we conduct a financial evaluation con-
sidering CAPEX, OPEX, grid connection fees, and other relevant cost components. Our
focus is on delivering a complete business case evaluation for the Nordic region while
building a user-friendly tool that can be directly used by investors and easily replicated
or adapted as market rules evolve. Chapter 4 presents the results of these three compo-
nents and discusses the implications of our findings. Chapter 5 concludes the thesis and
provides perspectives for future research.

2
Ancillary Services in the Nordic Power
System
At all times of the day, electricity consumption must be equal to production. In the Nordics,
this balance is secured by keeping the frequency of the system close to 50 Hz, with any
significant deviation posing a risk to the integrity of the system. To manage imbalances,
Transmission System Operators (TSOs) procure ancillary services, pre-contracted flex-
ibility reserves that can be activated to stabilize the grid. Energinet is responsible for
procuring these services and ensuring the security of supply in both Danish bidding zones,
DK1 and DK2 [6].
Ancillary services are procured through two complementary markets. In the capacity mar-
ket, participants are compensated for remaining available and for committing to place
bids. In the energy activation market, payments are made when capacity is activated,
either automatically in response to frequency deviations or manually via dispatch orders.
Energinet uses both contracted capacity and voluntary bids to maintain real-time balance.
The Danish system is unique in spanning two synchronous areas. DK1 (Jutland and Fu-
nen) is connected to the Continental European grid, where ancillary services include Fre-
quency Containment Reserve (FCR), automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR),
and manual Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR). DK2 (Zealand and Bornholm) is
part of the Nordic synchronous area, which employs a broader set of products, includ-
ing Fast Frequency Reserve (FFR), FCR for Normal Operation (FCR-N), and FCR for
Disturbances (FCR-D), alongside aFRR and mFRR (Figure 2.1).
Figure 2.1: AS products in DK1 and DK2. The synchronous area connection determines
which products are used in each zone [10].

Ancillary services vary in activation time, duration, and energy requirements. Fast-acting
products such as FFR and FCR-D deliver short bursts of power and are suited to technolo-
gies like BESS. Slower products such as mFRR can sustain delivery for longer periods
and are better suited to conventional generation or large industrial loads. Activation can
be automatically triggered within seconds by frequency deviations, or manually with con-
trol signals sent from Energinet’s control centre.
The increasing share of variable renewable energy has made balancing more complex
and less predictable, shifting the market towards assets that can react quickly to changing
conditions. Given that hourly market-based procurement, particularly suited to BESS,
is primarily available for FFR, FCR-N, and FCR-D, this thesis focuses on these three
services.

2.1
Fast Frequency Reserve (FFR)
FFR is an ultra-fast AS in the Nordic synchronous area that helps stabilise frequency dur-
ing severe disturbances in low-inertia conditions. It is intended to arrest the frequency
decline before slower reserves such as FCR-D are activated. In DK2, FFR is triggered
automatically when frequency falls below 49.7 Hz. This typically occurs after large gener-
ation losses or unexpected interconnector outages [12].
2.1.1
Technical Parameters and Participation
FFR providers must be able to deliver very rapid power output for a short duration. The
main technical requirements are:
• Frequency activation range: 49.7–49.5 Hz.
• Full activation: 100% of the contracted capacity within 0.7 s.
• Duration: Sustained delivery for at least 5 s, followed by a ramp-down completed
within 30 s.
• Recovery time: Full capacity available again within 15 min after activation.
Because the total energy requirement is low, FFR is well suited to lithium-ion batteries
and other fast-response technologies. Providers can deliver FFR either as a stand-alone
service or in combination with other products, provided all technical requirements are met.
2.1.2
Market and Operational Framework
Energinet procures FFR capacity in cooperation with other Nordic transmission system
operators through daily auctions. The process consists of a single D-1 auction where
participants may submit bids for one-hour blocks. Bids close at 19:00 on the day before
delivery.
Each bid specifies a fixed capacity in MW (minimum 0.1 MW) and a uniform price per MW
per hour. Energinet accepts bids in ascending order of price until the volume requirement
is met and applies a pay-as-cleared settlement. If two bids have the same price, the
selection is random.
Activation is fully autonomous, based on the provider’s own frequency measurement. No
dispatch signal is sent by Energinet. Providers must ensure that the committed capacity
is available for the entire contracted period. Any unavailability must be corrected within 30
minutes or reported immediately. If delivery fails, availability payments are forfeited and
the provider may be held liable for the cost of replacement procurement. Energy delivered
during activation is settled under the provider’s balance responsibility.
2.1.3
Relevance for Battery Energy Storage
The very short duration of delivery, combined with high availability payments, makes
FFR an attractive application for high-power, low-energy battery systems.
The near-
instantaneous activation requirement aligns closely with the capabilities of lithium-ion bat-
teries, which can provide precise and reliable responses in milliseconds. In DK2, where
low-inertia conditions increase the risk of frequency events, BESS is likely to play an in-
creasingly important role in meeting future FFR needs [12].

2.2
Frequency-Controlled Disturbance Reserve (FCR-D)
FCR-D is a fast-acting service in the Nordic synchronous area that counters large fre-
quency deviations after sudden disturbances. In DK2, it is procured as separate up-
ward and downward products: upward regulation is triggered when frequency falls below
49.9 Hz, typically after a generation outage, and downward regulation is activated above
50.1 Hz, often following large-scale load shedding or transmission faults. These reserves
form the first stabilising response before slower services such as aFRR or mFRR take
over. In 2023, Energinet’s procurement targets were 44 MW upward and 43 MW down-
ward [12].
2.2.1
Technical Parameters and Participation
Providers must respond rapidly and proportionally to measured frequency deviations. Ac-
tivation must start within 2.5 s and reach at least 86% of contracted capacity within 7.5 s.
Activation ranges are:
• Upward: 49.9–49.5 Hz
• Downward: 50.1–50.5 Hz
The total energy requirement equals 3.2 s at full power, and static reserves must deacti-
vate within 15 min. These requirements suit lithium-ion BESS, which can deliver precise,
high-power output almost instantly.
Limited Energy Reservoirs (LERs), such as batteries, must implement an energy man-
agement system (EMS) with Normal (NEM) and Alert (AEM) modes, maintain at least
20 min of continuous delivery, and reserve 20% of rated power for EMS control. This
reservation is waived for systems sustaining full output for two consecutive hours, such
as a 1 MW/2 MWh BESS.
2.2.2
Market and Operational Framework
Energinet and Svenska kraftnät procure FCR-D capacity via two daily auctions:
• D-1 Early Auction: Up to 6-hour blocks; bids close 00:30.
• D-1 Late Auction: Up to 3-hour blocks; bids close 18:00.
Bids must specify a fixed MW volume (minimum 0.1 MW) and a uniform price per MW per
hour in EUR or DKK, with DKK bids converted at the Nord Pool rate. Offers are accepted
in ascending price order until the requirement is met; all winning bids are paid the marginal
clearing price (pay-as-cleared). Price ties are resolved randomly.
Activation is autonomous, based on local frequency measurement, with no dispatch from
Energinet. Providers must ensure capacity availability, remedy outages within 30 min,
or notify Energinet immediately. Non-delivery forfeits availability payments and may in-
cur liability for replacement costs. No separate payment is made for delivered energy;
activations are settled under the provider’s balance responsibility.
2.2.3
Relevance for Battery Energy Storage
FCR-D is well suited to high-power, short-duration BESS due to its low energy require-
ment, high availability payments, and demand for precise, rapid control. Compliance with
EMS and LER rules is essential, particularly given activation uncertainty and growing
market competition.

2.3
Frequency Containment Reserve for Normal Operation
(FCR-N)
FCR-N is a continuously operating service in both the Continental European and Nordic
synchronous areas. Its purpose is to maintain system frequency close to the nominal
50 Hz by correcting small, continuous imbalances between generation and consumption.
In DK2, FCR-N is activated automatically and symmetrically for both upward and down-
ward regulation whenever frequency deviates from nominal within a predefined range [12].
2.3.1
Technical Parameters and Participation
FCR-N requires providers to adjust power output proportionally to measured frequency
deviations. The key technical requirements are:
• Frequency activation range: 49.99–50.01 Hz.
• Full activation: 100% of contracted capacity at ±0.1 Hz deviation from nominal.
• Response time: Activation must begin within 2 s of the frequency change.
• Sustained delivery: Continuous provision for the contracted period without energy
limitation.
Because FCR-N is a symmetric product with continuous activation, it is generally suited
to assets that can maintain stable output over extended periods. Battery systems can
participate, but their limited energy capacity requires careful state-of-charge management
to avoid depletion during prolonged activation.
Limited Energy Reservoirs (LERs), such as BESS, must implement an energy manage-
ment system (EMS) capable of maintaining the state of charge within operational limits
while delivering the contracted capacity. This is achieved through a Normal mode (NEM)
for standard operation and an Alert mode (AEM) for corrective charging or discharging
when SoC approaches defined thresholds.
2.3.2
Market and Operational Framework
In DK2, FCR-N is procured through the Nordic joint capacity market. Energinet partici-
pates alongside other Nordic TSOs in daily D-1 auctions. Bids must specify symmetric
capacity in MW (minimum 0.1 MW) and a uniform price per MW per hour. The market
clears on a pay-as-cleared basis, with offers accepted in ascending order of price until
the Nordic requirement is met.
Activation is entirely automatic, based on the provider’s local frequency measurement,
with no central dispatch. Providers must be available for all contracted periods and main-
tain compliance with measurement and monitoring standards. Failure to deliver results
in forfeiture of capacity payments and may incur liability for replacement costs. Energy
delivered or absorbed as part of FCR-N activation is accounted for through the provider’s
balance responsibility agreement.
2.3.3
Relevance for Battery Energy Storage
FCR-N is less energy-intensive than some other continuous services because it involves
symmetric regulation around the nominal frequency, often resulting in net-zero energy
over time. Nevertheless, prolonged deviations can lead to sustained charging or dis-
charging, which poses challenges for LERs. Effective EMS strategies are essential for
BESS operators to manage state of charge without compromising delivery. In the Nordic
context, FCR-N offers a stable revenue opportunity for batteries that can maintain com-
pliance over extended operation [12].

2.4
Overview of Assets Providing Ancillary Services in
Denmark
The Danish ancillary services market comprises different products in its two bidding zones.
DK1, synchronised with the Continental European grid, primarily uses FCR, aFRR, and

2.5.2
Market Access Roles
In addition to fulfilling the technical and procedural requirements, a facility must either
obtain the role of a Balance Responsible Party (BRP) or Balance Service Provider (BSP)
or enter into an agreement with an existing BRP or BSP. The BRP role provides access
to all AS markets in Denmark, whereas the BSP role is limited to markets without energy
delivery, specifically FCR, FFR, and FCR-D [15].
A BRP is a market participant that assumes financial responsibility for imbalances be-
tween the planned and actual production, consumption, and trade of electricity within its
portfolio. This role grants access to trade in the Danish day-ahead, intraday, and balanc-
ing markets, as well as delivering ancillary services if the technical and legal requirements
are met [16]. In practice, BRPs handle market bidding, real-time operational adjustments,
and imbalance settlement on behalf of the assets in their portfolio.

Methodology
The methodology is divided into three parts: the simulation model of BESS operation in AS
markets, the battery degradation analysis, and the financial model assessing investment
feasibility.
3.1
Simulation Methodology
To perform our simulations, we used the AnyLogic simulation environment, a professional-
grade platform supporting agent-based, discrete-event, and system dynamics modelling.
AnyLogic is built on Java, allowing for flexible and powerful model logic while enabling real-
time interaction with parameters, time-evolving graphical outputs, and multi-level tracking
of variables. Unlike static tools such as MATLAB or Python, AnyLogic’s interactive in-
terface makes it well-suited to communicate complex system behaviour to stakeholders
without programming expertise.
Due to platform limitations on agent creation, our models were developed in AnyLogic
University Edition via a virtual desktop connection provided by DTU, enabling full-year
simulations of hourly AS participation.
To structure the simulation study, we adopt the General Simulation Methodology as pro-
posed by Rossetti (2016), which provides a structured six-phase framework for devel-
oping and executing discrete-event simulation projects [17]. This methodology is widely
accepted for managing simulation complexity in engineering and decision-support appli-
cations. Since our simulations are deterministic, dynamic and discrete by nature, our
focuses lie within phase 1, 2, 5 and 6 of the methodology. Phases 3 and 4 are applica-
ble to stochastic simulations requiring multiple runs with random seeding and statistical
analysis. Since our model is deterministic with no internal randomness, it produces fully
reproducible outcomes, making these phases unnecessary.

3.1.1
Phase 1: Problem Definition and Conceptual Modelling
The initial phase of the methodology involved defining the purpose of the simulation: to
evaluate the earning potential of a 1 MW BESS across three different durations under real
market conditions. Each simulation spans a full calendar year, using historical data from
2020 to 2024, and focuses exclusively on participation in the AS markets. The primary
outputs of interest are the accumulated net revenues and the resulting State of Charge
(SoC) profiles, the latter of which are used to assess degradation effects associated with
service provision.
The simulation process began with a single market participation strategy, allowing for a
more focused understanding of individual market dynamics and system behaviour. This
was later extended to a multi-market strategy to assess the added value of market stack-
ing. A comparison between the two approaches is then performed to determine which
configuration delivers superior financial outcomes. As with any simulation study, the as-
sumptions underpinning the model were documented to ensure transparency and consis-
tency throughout the analysis.

3.1.2
Assumptions
• We assume perfect foresight in the Day Ahead market for AS, specifically using D-1
early auction prices. In addition, we assume that all hourly bids are awarded at the
marginal price. A bid is only marked as non-participated if the SoC falls outside the
required limits defined by Energinet. In such cases, revenues are set to zero for the
affected hours. As a result, our simulations fully comply with Energinet’s technical
requirements, eliminating the risk of incurring penalties for service non-delivery.
• A full year simulation in this study spans from 1 January at 00:00:00 CET to 1 Jan-
uary at 00:00:00 CET the following year. For example, the 2024 simulation begins
on 1 January 2024 at 00:00:00 and ends on 1 January 2025 at 00:00:00.
• We assume 100 percent round trip efficiency (RTE) in our simulations for the sake
of simplicity. In real-world applications, RTE typically ranges between 85 and 95
percent for lithium-ion batteries, depending on the specific technology and system
configuration [18].
• We assume that the battery retains its initial energy capacity throughout the entire
simulation period. The BESS would degrade over time due to both calendar aging
and cycling. As discussed later in this thesis, degradation cannot be approximated
simply by dividing total energy throughput by nominal capacity. Instead, a more
accurate estimate is obtained by analysing the SoC curve extracted at the end of
the simulations.
• All monetary values used in this study, including historic prices and grid tariffs, are
expressed in Euros (€). While data from Energinet is available in both Danish kro-
ner (DKK) and Euros, we use Euros as the primary currency for consistency and
broader relevance. The Euro is widely accepted as the de facto currency for busi-
ness transactions across Europe and is more familiar to most investors and stake-
holders. In addition, the exchange rate between the Danish krone and the Euro
is kept highly stable by Denmark’s National Bank. Denmark maintains a fixed ex-
change rate policy under which the krone is closely pegged to the Euro at a central
rate of DKK 7.46038. This policy has been in place since the early 1980s, ensur-
ing reliable and predictable currency conversion. As such, converting financial data
from DKK to Euros introduces no meaningful uncertainty into the results [11].

3.1.4
Phase 2: Model Building and Data Collection
Following the identification of market mechanisms, data sources, and regulatory require-
ments in Phase 1, the second phase of the methodology focused on translating these
real-world dynamics into a simulation environment.
To achieve this, we adopted an agent-based modelling (ABM) approach. ABM allows the
system’s behaviour to emerge from the interactions of individual entities (agents), each
operating according to defined parameters. This is particularly valuable when modelling
energy storage, where decisions related to activation, SoC management, and market
participation occur at multiple timescales and depend on evolving external signals.
In our model, the simulation logic was divided across three agents, each corresponding
to a key operational layer of the BESS within the AS framework:
• The Availability Payment Agent, responsible for evaluating SoC feasibility and
calculating fixed capacity payments.
• The Frequency Agent, which handles real-time response to grid frequency devia-
tions and tracks energy throughput.
• The Activation Settlement Agent, tasked with computing revenues and costs from
actual energy exchanges, including spot market interactions and grid tariffs.
This structure also allowed us to align with the granularity of available datasets: frequency
data is recorded at 3-minute intervals, whereas market prices and availability signals fol-
low an hourly resolution. The agents are designed to interoperate across these time
domains, enabling synchronization of real-time system behaviour with hourly market par-
ticipation logic.
By integrating this architecture with the cleaned and structured data collected in Phase 1,
we constructed a model capable of simulating the operational and financial performance
of a BESS under real historical conditions.

3.2
Battery Degradation Methodology
Degradation refers to the gradual loss of capacity and efficiency over time, primarily driven
by two mechanisms: calendar aging and cycle aging. While calendar aging results from
time-dependent chemical processes even during storage, cycle aging is directly linked to
the frequency and depth of charge-discharge cycles [31].
3.2.1
Calender Aging
Findings from Gräf et. al. [32] indicate that temperature variation within battery racks
strongly affects degradation rates. Under optimal thermal conditions (lowest observed
average temperatures), calendar aging proceeds at approximately 1.03% per year of ca-
pacity loss. This value represents the minimal degradation rate observed in utility-scale
BESS and will be adopted in this study as the baseline calendar aging rate. This rate
represents actual annual capacity loss and does not require further adjustment.
3.2.2
Rainflow Counting Method
To estimate degradation resulting from cycling activity, the Rainflow Counting algorithm
is applied to the battery’s SoC time series. Originally developed for fatigue analysis in
mechanical structures, the Rainflow algorithm is well-suited to detect and quantify cycles
of varying depths and frequencies from fluctuating SoC data. The Rainflow method is one
of the standard approaches for estimating the number and intensity of cycles in battery
testing scenarios [31].

3.3
Financial Modelling Methodology
Financial modelling serves as a decision-making tool to evaluate the attractiveness of an
investment. Its primary objective is to assess a project’s profitability over its operational
lifetime by translating projected revenues and costs into annual cash flows and discount-
ing these to present value. This approach follows the principles of private economic eval-
uation, where the guiding question is: Should we invest in this particular project? By
focusing exclusively on the project’s cash flows, the model ensures that investment de-
cisions are based on measurable financial performance rather than accounting profits or
broader socio-economic considerations [36].
The model follows a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) framework, which is the standard
method for assessing the economic feasibility of investment projects. DCF analysis in-
volves:
1. Identifying all relevant revenues and costs associated with the project,
2. Converting these into annual net cash flows over the project lifetime,
3. Discounting future cash flows to present value using a project-specific discount rate,
4. Deriving key financial metrics such as Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate
of Return (IRR), which serve as decision criteria for investment evaluation [37].
3.3.1
Discount rate
The discount rate applied in a private economic analysis represents the investor’s re-
quired rate of return. It accounts for the time value of money, inflation, and the project’s
specific risk profile. Unlike a socio-economic evaluation, where discount rates are lower
as they reflect societal opportunity costs, private economic discount rates are higher, as
they incorporate market, sectoral, and project-specific risks.
For this purpose, our study applies the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) as the
discount rate in the DCF model. The WACC reflects the blended cost of equity and cost
of debt, weighted by their proportion in the project’s capital structure, and adjusted for
corporate tax effects [38]:
WACC = E
V re + D
V rd(1 −T)

In principle, the cost of debt is derived from the interest rate charged by lenders, adjusted

required return shareholders demand for investing, reflecting opportunity costs. It can be
estimated using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), which incorporates the risk-free
rate, market risk premium, and project beta to capture systematic risk.
In this study, we assume a WACC of 10%. The assumed rate is informed by industry
practice rather than theoretical derivation. This aligns with typical private investment ex-
pectations in the renewable energy sector.
3.3.2

NPV quantifies the difference between the present value of future cash inflows and the
present value of cash outflows, thereby indicating the net economic benefit of the invest-
ment when expressed in today’s monetary terms. The NPV can be expressed as:

A positive NPV (NPV > 0) indicates that the investment is expected to generate returns
above the required rate of return, making it financially attractive from a private investor’s
perspective. Conversely, a negative NPV (NPV < 0) suggests that the project fails to
meet the return threshold and should therefore not be undertaken [37].

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
IRR determines the annual effective compounded return rate at which the NPV of all
project cash flows equals zero. It represents the break-even discount rate: if the actual
discount rate is below the IRR, the project generates a positive NPV and is considered
financially attractive; if it is above the IRR, the project is unprofitable.

In Excel, the IRR function iteratively searches for the discount rate that makes the NPV
equal zero, starting from an initial guess until a result within a defined accuracy is reached

3.3.4
Decision Criterion
In a private investment context, the IRR serves as an intuitive measure of return, easily
comparable to the investor’s required rate of return (i.e., the discount rate). The decision
rule is:
• Accept the project if IRR > r
• Reject the project if IRR < r.
A higher IRR indicates a greater margin above the required rate, implying a more finan-
cially robust project [37].

3.3.10
Operating Expenses (OPEX)
The following details the OPEX assumptions made.
Balance Responsible Party (BRP)
The BRP service fee is set at 10% of annual revenues. This percentage reflects typical
industry practice for assets of 1 MW. Larger projects (e.g., 10–50 MW) often benefit from
reduced fees, closer to 5%.
Operation and Maintenance (O&M) Costs
Operation and maintenance (O&M) costs represent the recurring expenses necessary
to ensure reliable operation of BESS throughout their service life. Although literature
and vendor sources provide varying estimates for these costs we adopt the O&M cost
assumption from the U.S. National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) 2024 Annual
Technology Baseline (ATB) [18], which applies an annual fixed O&M (FOM) rate of 2.5% of
installed CAPEX. This value includes all operational expenses and battery augmentation
required to maintain the system at its rated capacity over a 10-year lifetime.
This cost estimation approach is considered appropriate in this study, as it naturally scales
with system size and investment level. Larger BESS installations typically require more
extensive maintenance, monitoring, and component replacement, leading to higher ab-
solute O&M expenditures. By linking O&M costs directly to CAPEX, the estimates remain
proportional to the scale and complexity of the project, avoiding the inaccuracies that
would arise from applying a uniform flat cost across all configurations.
Furthermore, the O&M scope according to NREL [18] covers a broad range of cost com-
ponents, including:
• Administrative fees and labor
• Insurance and legal fees
• Land lease payments and property taxes
• Operating labor and site security
• General, scheduled, and unscheduled maintenance
• Battery augmentation and component replacement
• Project management and associated overhead
Reliable estimates for CAPEX and OPEX in utility-scale BESS projects remain somewhat
limited. Reported CAPEX values vary widely across sources due to differences in sys-
tem design, location, and data collection methods. While some datasets provide detailed
breakdowns, these are often market-specific and not directly comparable.
OPEX figures are subject to similar uncertainty. They are frequently derived as a fixed
proportion of CAPEX rather than from detailed operational cost assessments. Exact costs
of individual OPEX components are generally not reported, making a detailed breakdown
difficult. While some sources provide fixed OPEX estimates for a given battery size, these
do not account for the higher costs associated with larger systems. For this reason, we
adopt an approach where OPEX is expressed as a percentage of CAPEX, allowing oper-
ational costs to scale appropriately with project size.
These uncertainties underline the need for project-specific cost assessments and more
standardized reporting. Improved transparency in CAPEX and OPEX data would enhance
comparability and support more robust investment evaluations.

Figure 4.10: FCR-N charge/discharge count by year for a 1-hour system (2020–2024).
Across the period, discharge events consistently outnumber charge events, reflecting the
asymmetry in frequency deviations and the need for more frequent upward regulation than
downward regulation. However, the gap narrows significantly from 2022 onwards, with
charge counts increasing year-on-year. By 2024, the number of charge and discharge
events becomes almost balanced, indicating a shift towards more symmetrical operation.
The convergence between charge and discharge events over the past five years reflects
the growing share of variable renewable generation in the Nordic system, leading to more
frequent periods of overproduction. This has increased the demand for downward regula-
tion in FCR-N. From a commercial perspective, this operational balance is advantageous.
Charging during downward regulation triggers the sale of excess energy on the spot mar-
ket when SoC limits are reached. In contrast, prolonged periods of upward regulation
require purchasing energy from the spot market to restore SoC, which can lower mar-
gins. The observed trend therefore signals a shift towards more profitable operational
conditions for FCR-N.
With FCR-N’s growing market presence, industry discussions around current regulatory
tariff structures have gained greater relevance. Grid tariffs have a disproportionate impact
on FCR-N revenues due to the market’s high activation rates and energy throughput. A
particularly significant barrier is the “double tariff” structure, due to BESS being labelled
as both producers and consumers. This results in network charges being applied twice,
once when charging and again when discharging, effectively penalising high-throughput
markets like FCR-N. A substantial share of FCR-N revenue comes from energy activation
settlements. In 2024, pure FCR-N simulations showed that energy activation settlements
accounted for 28% of revenue in the 2-hour system and 40% in the 4-hour system, while
grid tariff costs represented 9% of total revenues in both systems. It can be argued that
this double tariff structure directly contradicts EU regulations, which stipulate that energy
storage should not be subject to double charges for stored electricity when providing flex-
ibility services to the grid [49].

The following EU provisions establish the legal basis for cost-reflective, non-discriminatory
network tariffs and explicitly prohibit double charging for energy storage [49]:
Electricity Market Regulation, Article 18, Section 1: ”Charges (tariffs) ap-
plied by network operators for access to networks, including charges for con-
nection to the networks, charges for use of networks, and, where applicable,
charges for related network reinforcements, shall be cost-reflective, trans-
parent, take into account the need for network security and flexibility and
reflect actual costs incurred insofar as they correspond to those of an effi-
cient and structurally comparable network operator and are applied in a non-
discriminatory manner. Those charges shall not include unrelated costs sup-
porting unrelated policy objectives.”
It further appears from Article 18, Section 1, that: ”...The network charges
shall not discriminate either positively or negatively against energy storage or
aggregation and shall not create disincentives for self-generation, self-consumption
or for participation in demand response.”
It appears from the Electricity Market Directive, Article 15, Section 5b,
that: ”Member States shall ensure that active customers that own an energy
storage facility: ...are not subject to any double charges, including network
charges, for stored electricity remaining within their premises or when provid-
ing flexibility services to system operators;...”
This issue extends beyond individual markets, as recent analysis shows that only five EU
Member States have effectively addressed double charging, while twelve states (Den-
mark included) are not addressing the issue at all. Despite the Clean Energy Package of
2019 specifically aiming to prevent such charges for flexibility services, implementation
across Member States has been insufficient, creating an unfair competitive disadvantage
for energy storage against conventional flexibility providers [50].
In the report outlined by GreenPower, they propose that phasing out availability tariffs
and introducing effect-based payments rather than pure energy tariffs could better align
costs with actual grid impact, potentially reducing the burden on high-frequency activation
services like FCR-N.

Industry Transparency and Market Maturation
A notable development in the BESS sector is the emergence of transparent performance
reporting, exemplified by Enspired’s initiative to publicly disclose actual operational rev-
enues. Enspired has become the first and only optimizer (BRP) to publish real BESS
revenues, with performance data independently certified by KPMG Austria [54].
This transparency addresses a critical market barrier: the lack of verifiable performance
data. The industry has been relying on backtests and bankable forecasts, with optimizers
having ”an intrinsic motivation to show high numbers” while bankable forecasts are ”by
nature, pessimistic to ensure credibility with banks” [55].
The broader implications are significant for market development. This transparency ini-
tiative could accelerate institutional investment and establish industry benchmarks [55].
As our analysis demonstrates strong fundamentals for Nordic BESS projects, transparent
reporting mechanisms could prove crucial for translating theoretical potential into actual
capital deployment.
This transparency trend represents a market maturation process that, combined with the
favorable economics identified in this thesis, positions the BESS sector for accelerated
growth and institutional acceptance.

Conclusion
This thesis has conducted a comprehensive business case evaluation of BESS participa-
tion in Nordic AS markets, addressing critical questions about investment potential, opti-
mal market strategies, and financial viability across different system durations. Through
integrated simulation modelling, degradation analysis, and financial assessment, the re-
search provides valuable insights for investors and policymakers considering large-scale
BESS deployment in the Nordic region.
The analysis demonstrates strong investment attractiveness across all examined BESS
configurations, with Internal Rates of Return ranging from 19.37% to 22.74% substan-
tially exceeding the 10% discount rate threshold. Critically, the relationship between stor-
age duration and investment returns proves non-linear, challenging the assumption that
longer durations always deliver superior returns. The 2-hour configuration emerges as
the optimal investment profile, achieving the highest IRR of 22.74% by striking an ideal
balance between capital efficiency and revenue generation capability. While 4-hour sys-
tems generate higher absolute revenues, their significantly higher CAPEX requirements
erode relative returns, demonstrating diminishing marginal benefits beyond the 2-hour
threshold.
Multi-market strategies consistently outperform single-market approaches across all du-
rations and years, with advantages ranging from 20% to 71% depending on market con-
ditions. This superiority stems from operational flexibility and the ability to dynamically
allocate capacity to the most profitable services. The analysis reveals evolving market
dynamics, with a clear shift toward FCR-N participation from 2020-2024, reaching over
80% of bids for 4-hour systems by 2024. This trend reflects changing grid conditions
as variable renewable generation increases demand for continuous frequency regulation
services.
Sensitivity analysis identifies revenue volatility as the primary risk factor, with a 10% rev-
enue decline reducing NPV by 29%. This merchant risk exposure reflects the inherent
volatility of AS prices, which lack the long-term contract protection available to wind and
solar projects. CAPEX uncertainty represents the second major risk, with technology and
supply chain volatility affecting project economics. Regulatory barriers, particularly the
”double tariff” structure that contradicts EU regulations by charging BESS assets twice
for the same energy flows, emerge as significant impediments requiring policy interven-
tion. Industry Transparency and Market Maturation Enspired’s pioneering transparency
initiative, represents a crucial market development that addresses information asymmetry
between operators and investors. This transparency trend, combined with the favorable
economics demonstrated in this thesis, positions the BESS sector for accelerated institu-
tional investment and market maturation.
The findings support several key recommendations for stakeholders. Investors should
prioritize 2-hour BESS configurations and multi-market strategies while carefully manag-
ing merchant revenue risk through diversified market participation. Policymakers should

strategic market participation, optimal duration selection, and continued advocacy for
regulatory reforms that recognize energy storage’s essential role in enabling the renew-
able energy transition. The convergence of technological maturity, regulatory evolution,
and demonstrated financial viability positions Nordic BESS markets at an inflection point
where theoretical potential can translate into large-scale commercial deployment, sup-

6.0.5
Cost Trajectory Discussion
The alignment between the NREL and BNEF trajectories, despite their differing market
scopes, indicates that recent BESS capital cost reductions are a global trend. Following a
temporary increase in 2022 driven by supply chain constraints and elevated raw material
prices, costs have not only returned to pre-2022 levels but declined to historic lows, with
2024 recording the most significant year on yea decrease. This sustained downward
movement strengthens the economic case for storage investments by lowering capital
expenditure requirements, improving project returns, shortening payback periods, and
broadening the range of financially viable applications. In this context, the observed cost
trajectory provides a compelling basis for assessing the current profitability potential of
utility-scale BESS deployments.
