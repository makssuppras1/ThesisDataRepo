Abstract
Traditional wired power transfer solutions for neural implants present significant limitations,
including movement restrictions and potential environmental influence. This thesis aims to
develop a Wireless Power Transfer (WPT) system optimized for behavioral research conducted
on mice, thereby addressing these limitations. The presented WPT solution is capable of stable
and position-free power transfer over a wide operating area. Critical components, including
the power amplifier, transmitter, and receiver were designed and integrated into a functional
prototype. Two feasible solutions for the transmitter were analyzed and evaluated to find
the most viable approach. Extensive testing demonstrated that the prototype delivers stable
operation and position-free power transfer within the designated operating area with high lateral
and angular misalignment tolerance. These results indicated that the WPT system offers a
reliable solution for powering the neural implant. This work provides a viable concept for future
application, facilitating neural research conducted on mice.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wireless Power Transfer (WPT) technologies paved the way for numerous technological and
research advancements over the past decade. The possibility of wirelessly powering different
instruments, sensors, and implants made it possible to carry out experiments that would be
impossible or limited without this technology. In specific cases using wired technology would
interfere with the experiment or directly influence the results. Neural implants are also affected
by limitation, as wired power transfer can restrict the range of experiments or limit the subject’s
movements. Throughout this work, the design of such a system is carried out.
1.1
Defining the Problem
As the topic suggests, this thesis’s subject is the development of a WPT system concept aimed
at powering a neural implant. This research was initiated by a research team at the University
of Copenhagen. They aim to carry out behavioral experiments with mice to study how external
stimuli to the brain affect the different processes in the subject’s nervous system. The theory
and experiments suggest that the effect of such stimulation can be shown by assessing the
result of behavioral observation.
The discussion on neural science in this work is intentionally limited, as the main focus is the
research of the WPT system. This thesis does not go into the details of neural science and
only covers necessary information that directly influences the requirements and design goals for
the WPT system.
The main aspects of the system that are directly influenced by the specific research are the
area of operation, wireless receiver geometry, its capabilities, and misalignment tolerance of the
transmitter and receiver. Each requirement was clearly defined at the beginning of the project
to avoid future confusion.
The area of operation is the main factor that limits the movement of mice. This kind of
behavioral research requires a comfortable environment for the subject to avoid environmental
influence. The requested operating area was 50 cm by 50 cm, where the mice can freely move
around. The height of the operating area must comply with the average height the mice’s head
can reach while standing, which is 6 cm to 8 cm. This means that wherever the receiver is
placed within this area, it should reliably work. An illustration of the envisioned arrangement
can be found in Figure 1.1.

Research Objectives
Recognizing these objectives and challenges, this research focuses on the development of a 3D
WPT system, which is specifically designed for this experiment. The goal is to research, design,
and build the complete system that enables position-free power transfer to the mentioned
neural implant. Available solutions and the existing literature should be carefully analyzed to

validated and tested to ensure all requirements and goals are fulfilled. The following chapters

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter elaborates on the essential background theory that lays the foundation for the
design process, which is explained in the following chapters. The investigation of WPT theory
was followed by a thorough literature review. The possible solutions were evaluated based on
multiple factors and the most suitable solution was chosen. This was followed by an architectural
design to finalize the system components.
2.1
Wireless Power Transfer
WPT technologies can be categorized in different ways [1]. Far-field solutions, such as Microwave
Power Transfer, Acoustic Power Transfer, etc. These offer significant transfer distances with
usually low efficiency and transmit power. On the other hand, near-field solutions can offer
outstanding efficiency and transmit power. Two notable examples are Capacitive Power Transfer
(CPT) and Inductive Power Transfer (IPT). Modern IPT systems are preferred in many cases
because they offer excellent adaptability. A range of well-studied solutions exist for smartphones,
wearable gadgets, and even electric vehicles.

2.1.3
Magnetic Resonant Coupling
Magnetic Resonance Coupling (MRC) is a phenomenon that occurs when inductive coupling is
present between two or more magnetically coupled resonators. MRC WPT systems can achieve
higher efficiencies and longer transmit distances than traditional inductive links, as shown in [4]
and [5]. Underdamped resonators generally have a high Q-factor and require very little energy
input to stay in resonance. Due to the inductive coupling between two magnetically coupled
resonators, it is possible to supply both resonators with energy, even though only powering
one of them directly. This behavior can be utilized in WPT, by forcing the transmitter and
receiver coils to have matching resonant frequencies. This can be done by either designing
both coils to have the same self-resonant frequency, which matches the operating frequency, or
by connecting external capacitors to them to form RLC circuits. Another solution is to form a
4-coil system, by adding 1-1 external resonators near the TX and RX coils. The illustration
of the 4-coil WPT system can be found in Figure 2.3. The enhanced efficiency and transmit
distance can be advantageous in many situations and enable the system to be used in mid-range
applications, unlike traditional IPT which is limited to a close range. If one or both of the coils
are detuned from the common resonant frequency, the transmitted power decreases significantly
[6]. Due to the connection between the quality factor of a resonator and its bandwidth, the
higher the quality factor the narrower the bandwidth is. In systems with very high TX and RX
quality factors, the effects of detuning can cause even more significant power drops.

2.2.1
Challenges Associated with Coil Size Disparity

As previously determined the achievable efficiency with high coil size disparity is low. To
overcome the inevitably low efficiency multiple properties of the system require particular
attention during the design phase. As (2.6) suggests the efficiency is highly dependent on
the quality factor of both coils, so generally it is a good way to maximize the quality factors,
particularly the receiver [1].
Another way to compensate for the low efficiency is to increase the power output of the
transmitter coil, to attain a higher magnetic flux density. Increasing the input power can
introduce thermal issues, voltage and current stresses on surrounding components, and unstable
operation. These factors must be analyzed while designing the specific module of the system.
Having a low coupling coefficient does not only cause negative consequences. Compared to
classically sized WPT systems it also carries advantages and possible simplifications. The most
notable difference is the naturally small mutual inductance. As previously mentioned in Section
2.1 almost all input parameters also depend on the impedance of the receiver side, not only on
the transmitter. As Equation (2.2) and (2.1) suggest both the input and output impedances
and voltages change when the coupling changes (i.e.: the position of the coils is changed).
This behavior can cause changes to the resonant frequency of the TX and RX sides, detuning
the system and decreasing or increasing the input power. In extreme cases, when the coils
become overcoupled, frequency splitting can occur [8]. Frequency splitting causes a second
resonant peak in the transfer characteristics and complicates the tuning and implementation
of compensation. Impedance matching might become unavoidable, further complicating the
design procedure and control scheme. In systems with radical size disparity, this problem can
become minor or even negligible. If the highest achievable coupling coefficient still falls into the
loosely coupled region, it allows the free placement and movement of the receiver regardless of
the transmitter coil. The effect is advantageous when a small receiver is powered over a large
area, as it allows for a constantly changing coupling coefficient.

effect, the number of turns should be kept low or the spacing of the turns should be kept high.
As the number of turns directly influences the strength of the field, it might be insufficient
to use a coil with a low number of turns. If the spacing between turns is large, the magnetic
field will be concentrated in the direct proximity of the center [11]. An illustration of the
general model and the expected field distribution of such a coil can be found in Figure 2.5.
The aforementioned issues make this solution risky because it is still unknown if it will result in
sufficient field strength and distribution.

2.3
Metamaterials in Wireless Power Transfer
Metamaterials are artificially engineered materials, that exhibit properties not found in nature.
Among the various types, such as Acoustic, Mechanic, Thermal, etc., electromagnetic (EM)
metamaterials are especially well studied [16]. Studies have shown that WPT applications can
also benefit from the usage of electromagnetic-type metamaterials [1] [17] [18].

3.1
System Architecture
The first step of the process was to outline the high-level design of the system alongside
the initial specifications. The operating frequency was one of the requirements that had
to be chosen early on because it impacted all other system components. High frequencies
are preferred, to achieve a higher power transfer for the small receiver. Considering existing
systems with similar TX-RX size ratios, f ≈6.78 MHz was chosen as the goal [9]. A full
bridge inverter was selected as the power amplifier to drive the transmitter. GaN technology

operation, on-board signal generation was considered, using an FPGA. Based on the chosen
implementation in Section 2.2.2, the transmitter consists of the TX coil, compensation network,
and the metamaterial slab. To comply with the initial requirements and guarantee the operation,
the total transmitting area was selected slightly bigger, 60 cm by 60 cm. The receiver side

3.4.2
PCB Design
To optimize the PCB layout the following list guidelines were followed:
• Place all components in close proximity around the gate driver and GaN transistors.
• The gate and power loops of the transistors should be prioritized, to reduce the stray
inductance.
• Place ceramic capacitors close to the devices to reduce high-frequency noise.
• The bypass capacitor should be placed right next to the power switches to minimize
ground bounce and reduce power supply noise.
• Separate the components into logical groups to minimize the length of interconnected
traces.
• Use a ground plane to minimize noise by providing a low-impedance path for the currents.
• Choose a sufficient width for all high current traces, separate them from the low power
traces, and minimize the overlap between layers to reduce parasitic capacitance. The
overlap between the switching node and power input traces would add to Coss of the
GaN transistor, increasing losses.
The optimal layout for the power loop was optimized by implementing an internal vertical
layout, which can provide as low as 0.5 nH loop inductance [30]. An illustration of the layout

Improvements
In regards to the results acquired in Chapter 4 it was decided that the transmitter needs to
be improved. Instead of the plan to power the receiver from the bottom, other options were
evaluated. It was found that it is possible to generate a close-to-uniform field within a solenoid
coil. With this solution, the receiver should be placed inside the coil to interact with the field
properly. In exchange for only allowing observation of the mice from above instead of clear
visibility from every side, it can significantly simplify the structure of the system.
5.1
Solenoid Coil Design
To comply with the requirements, the size of the solenoid was kept the same 60 cm by 60 cm,
which allows for some extra room for the 50 cm by 50 cm operating area. The total height
was chosen as 10 cm to allow the transmission to work up to at least this height.
As an absolute minimum, the coil had to have at least N = 2 turns to form the 3D geometry.
Similarly to the planar coil, the Biot-Savart law (2.8) tells, that more turns can generate a
stronger magnetic field. For the initial estimation, N = 3 turns were chosen and the coil was
simulated in Ansys HFSS and Maxwell 3D. The simulated inductance was LTX = 12 48µH
and resistance was 0.9811 Ω, which ended up so low due to insufficient meshing, which was
limited by simulation time and hardware resources as previously described. The self resonant
frequency was fself,sim = 14.26 MHz. The low value of self-resonant frequency meant, that
N = 3 was the maximum number of turns considered during the design.
The magnetic field distribution of the coil was also simulated, illustrated in Figure 5.1. The
induced field showed that the average field density was slightly higher near winding than in the
center, but did not contain any significant disturbances or spikes. Based on the experimental
result acquired in Section 4.2, the threshold value of Mag(H) = 2.4 A
m, with Iin,p2p = 2 A
was reached in all positions within the transmit area.

Concluding Thoughts
The redesigned transmitter provided a better and more robust solution than its predecessor with
minimal limitations regarding the visibility from the sides due to the winding. All 3 designed
transmitters were tested and they provided a similar performance. With this in mind, it is
advantageous to choose the simplest receiver design, with a single-layer winding. It provides the
same performance as the dual and triple-layer versions but has a significantly simpler structure.
Due to the high size mismatch between the transmitter and receiver, the achieved coupling is
low. This provides the benefit of using multiple receivers simultaneously, due to the minimal
effect they have on the transmitter. The receiver works well within the operating area with
a significant angular misalignment tolerance, which provides stable operation while the mice
move around.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to develop a wireless power transfer system for neural science applications,
specially designed for neural implants that cannot be powered by traditional solutions. The
primary objective was to develop a solution that overcomes the limitations of wired systems
and enables position-free wireless power transfer over a wide area.
The first results demonstrated that the metamaterial slab concept wasn’t suited for the
application. The initial simulations showed promising results, but the measured field distribution
was far from uniform. Revising the simulation revealed that the prototype was extremely
sensitive to the operating frequency. The input characteristics of the system made the tuning
challenging and the efficient operation impossible.
The concept was redesigned with a solenoid transmitter, which showed the potential for
improved features. The new prototype provided a close-to-uniform field distribution within the
operating area. This alternative enabled the position-free operation of the optimized receiver
with high angular misalignment tolerance and the size disparity of the coils permitted the usage
of multiple devices simultaneously.
Despite the effectiveness of the solution, scaling is limited. If a larger operating area is required,
the self-resonant frequency of the coil would be a limiting factor. This makes the solution only
optimal for studies conducted on small animals.
Future research should focus on further improving the transmitter system to facilitate better
scalability. The implementation of control would enhance stability and increase safety. The
receiver coil could be improved by utilizing the benefits of planar magnetics.
In conclusion, this work provided a solid concept for position-free wireless power transfer systems
to improve the quality of behavioral research with neural implants.

Project Description
The project is aimed at developing a position free wireless power transfer system for a
neural implant. As the first step thorough research is conducted within the field of position
free wireless power transfer. Possible solutions and methods are investigated and compared
in order to find the most suitable direction. The chosen solution will be separately researched
and evaluated for the future design. All requirements and parameters will be verified to
ensure the feasibility of the solution. As the next step an analytical/preliminary design will be
conducted. The design is going to be verified with the help of simulation tools. With the
verified
design
in
hand
the
implementation
will be carried out. This includes the
development, construction and validation of the design. The finished system will be
thoroughly tested and verified over a suitable time period.
Description of Related Tasks
1.
Preliminary research:
The preliminary research consists of literature review of numerous relevant research
papers relevant to the topic, such as wireless transmitter and receiver design,
compensation topologies and other supporting topics. All possible solutions are collected
to ensure that no feasible ideas are ruled out in this phase.
2.
Research and evaluation of possible solutions:
The second research step concentrates on the comparison and investigation of the
possible solutions to choose the most appropriate one for the specific use case. The
strengths and weaknesses of each are collected and compared in order to find the most
promising one.
3.
Preliminary design:
The preliminary design phase concentrates on developing and verifying the most crucial
parts of the system using analytic and simulation tools. These components include the
transmitter and receiver coils, power inverter and supporting circuitry.
4.
Planning and implementation of the final design:
The implementation step includes all necessary steps from finalizing the preliminary
design and linking the system components to building and debugging the implemented
hardware. Some of the steps are PCB design, design of the structure and finalization of
the driving and power signals.
5.
Verification, measurement and testing of the final design:
To ensure long term stability, thorough measurements and testing is conducted on the
constructed design. This step includes all necessary preparation for the measurements
and inspection of the measured data.
6.
Documentation:
The documentation step consists of writing the thesis and the creation of necessary
diagrams and graphs.

C.1
H Field Measurement I - Theory
To measure the magnetic field distribution a search coil was used, which is an arbitrary coil that
is placed in a varying magnetic field. Any changing magnetic field lines that cross the surface
of the search coil induce a voltage on the coil that can be measured with an oscilloscope.
The principle behind the operation of the search coil is electromagnetic induction. The flux
through the search coil can be defined using Faraday’s law of induction. The effective flux
through the search coil equals the product of the coil area (A) and the vector component of the
field (H cos α) lines perpendicular to the surface of it. Faraday’s law of induction tells us that
any changing flux (Φ) also induces a voltage (V ) change on each of the coil turns (N). Using
these two principles the voltage of the search coil can be defined using the following equation:
