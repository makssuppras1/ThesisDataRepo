Abstract 
Laccases are polyphenol oxidases, which contains copper atoms and are capable to oxidize phenolic 
and non-phenolic compounds. This gives them advantages in a range of industrial processes such as 
dye decolourisation in the paper and textile industries. White-rot fungi produce ligninolytic enzymes, 
including laccases, and have been shown to be effective in degrading colourants from wastewater in 
the textile and paper industries. Three different fungal laccases capacity for decoloursing of tattoo 
pigment was investigated, by using Lambert Beer’s law of absorbance for verification.  
The pre-decolourisation study showed decolourisation over a period of 24 hours by Trametes 
Versicolor (TV) and Pleurotus Ostreatus (PO) laccases on True Magenta, Dark Red, Bahama Blue 
and Golden Yellow. The decolourisation by TV and PO laccases resulted in: - 62% (TV) and 29.16 
% (PO) (True Magenta) 53.41% (TV) and 47.15% (Dark Red), 7.3% (TV) (Bahama Blue) and 0.11% 
(TV) (Golden yellow). 
The True Magenta dye was selected for the decolourisation study 1 performed with the following dye 
concentrations: - 0.3mg/ml, 0.71mg/ml and 1.5 mg/ml. The decolourisation rates after 24 hours with 
TV resulted in a decrease with the lowest concentration of 48.46% (0.3mg/ml) and highest of 54.61% 
(1.5 mg/ml). The reference samples also showed a decrease by 56.35% (0.3 mg/ml) and 53.25% (1.5 
mg/ml). The decolourisation rate after 24 hours with PO showed a decrease with the lowest 
concentration 39% (0.3 mg/ml) and highest 57% (1.5mg/ml). The reference samples showed to 
decrease by 66% (0.3 mg/ml) and 68% (1.5mg/ml) 
 
The Dark Red dye was used for the decolourisation study 2 with a dye concentration of 1.021 mg/ml 
with TV. By centrifuging the samples one and two times, a difference of 7% in decolourisation rate 
occurred, as well as difference of 8.7% for the reference samples.  
 
The kinetic experiment was performed with ABTS as the benchmark substrate at different 
concentration (0.2mM, 1mM, 5mM and 10mM). The kinetic parameters Vmax and Km were 
determined for TV and PO with the following results: - Km=501 µM and Vmax=0.00050µM/min (TV) 
and Km=884.7µM and Vmax=0.00017µM/min. 
Laccases were not successful in decolourisation of the tattoo pigments, because the reference samples 
showed a decolourisation rate similar to the samples treated with enzymes. This indicated that the 
enzymes did not any effect on the tattoo pigments.

Resume 
Laccaser er polyphenol oxidaser, som består af kopper atomer og har kapaciteten til at oxiderer 
fenoliske og ikke-fenoliske komponenter. Dette har givet dem fordele i en række forskellige 
industrielle processer så som affarvning i papir and tekstil industrien. White-rot svampe producerer 
ligninolytiske enzymer, så som laccaser, som har vidst sig at være effektiv til at nedbryde farve 
komponenter i spildevand fra papir og tøj industrien. Kapaciteten af tre forskellige svampe laccasers 
evner til at affarve tatoverings pigment var undersøgt ved at anvende Lambert Beer’s absorbance lov.   
 
Pre-affarvning studiet vidste affarvning over en periode på 24 timer ved brug af Trametes Versicolor 
and Pleurotus Ostreatus laccaser på tattoveringsfarverne True Magenta, Dark Red, Bahama Blue and 
Golden Yellow. Affarvning ved TV og PO laccaser var følgende:- 62% (TV) and 29.16 % (PO) (True 
Magenta) 53.41% (TV) and 47.15% (Dark Red), 7.3% (TV) (Bahama Blue) and 0.11% (TV) of 
(Golden Yellow). 
Farven True Magenta var udvalgt til affarvning studie 1 udført ved følgende farve koncentrationer: - 
0.3mg/ml, 0.71mg/ml and 1.5 mg/ml. Affarvnings raten efter 24 timer ved brug af TV resulterede I 
et fald ved den lavest koncentration på 48.46%(0.3 mg/ml) og den højeste på 54.61% (1.5 mg/ml). 
Reference prøverne vidst også et fald på 39 % (0.3 mg/ml) og 57% (1.5 mg/ml).  
 
Farven Dark Red blev anvendt i affarvning studiet 2 med en farve koncentration på 1.021 mg/ml med 
TV. Ved centrifugering prøverne en og to gange for hvilke en forskel på 7% på affarvnings raten 
optrådte, så vel som en forskel på 8.7% for reference prøverne.  
 
Det kinetiske eksperiment var udført med ABTS som benchmark substrat ved forskellige 
koncentrationer (0.2 mM, 1mM, 5mM and 10mM). De kinetiske parameter Vmax og Km var bestemt 
ved brug af TV og PO og gav følgende resultater:- Km=501 µM and Vmax=0.00050µM/min (TV) og 
Km=884.7µM og Vmax=0.00017µM/min. 
Laccaser var ikke succesfulde ved affarvning af tatoverings farver, fordi reference prøverne vidste 
tilsvarende affarvnings rate som for prøverne indeholdende enzymer. Dette indikerede at enzymer 
ikke havde nogen effekt på tatoverings pigmenterne.

1. 
Introduction 
Today new methods to remove tattoos are desirable, since the currently widely used laser technology 
treatment is often associated with side effects and complications for the patient. Laser treatments are 
often associated with pain because of the burning and scaring of the skin[1]. Around 600.000 people 
in Denmark have one or more tattoos[1]: amongst them, 10-20% have considered having their 
tattoo(s) removed. 
Tattoos are applied to the skin by permanently injecting tattoo inks into the dermis (middle layer of 
the skin)[2]. Literature studies have proved that laccases found in white rot fungi (WRF) have been 
shown to be effective in the dye degradation of such pigments as Azo- and Phtahlocyanine pigments 
from the waste water of the textile and paper industries[3][4]. The same types of synthetic pigments 
have been found amongst the assortment of tattoo pigments within this project and in general. Various 
types of pigments are available in tattoo inks and this gives a broad range of different pigment classes. 
The hypothesis in this thesis is that laccases have the ability to degrade or bleach tattoo pigments due 
to their oxidizing capacity. In this research project the focus will be on obtaining experimental data 
to research whether the hypothesis can be verified. To investigate the data derived as a consequence 
of the research, Lambert Beer’s law of absorbance will be used to verify or reject the hypothesis. 
 
The aim of this project is mainly divided into two parts: - firstly and primarily to investigate if laccase 
enzymes have the capacity to degrade or bleach tattoo pigment, secondly to develop a method that 
can provide quantitative assessment of such capacity, by means of absorbance measurements. The 
following report consists of several sections and subsections. In the first section there will be a brief 
description as to why laccase enzyme is a possible and suitable candidate for removing tattoo inks.  
Furthermore, an introduction of several important factors will be given describing the essentials of 
the nature of laccase and its catalytic mechanisms. The skin physiology will also be briefly described 
in relation to the understanding of the dermal environment as well as the influence that the laccase 
enzyme has beyond this project. Moreover, an overview of dye decolourisation studies by laccases 
will also be given, leading to a comparison of selected tattoo pigments for this project and other 
commonly found pigments found in the literature. A discussion will be based on the theoretical 
considerations stated in the introduction which follows and is supported by the experimental work.

2. 
The characterization and structure of laccases 
Laccases is a group of copper-consisting polyphenol oxidases that fall into the group of blue multi-
copper oxidases. These enzymes have a broad substrate specificity including non-specific oxidation 
of non-phenolic and phenolic compounds, which they achieve by using molecular oxygen which 
functions as an electron acceptor. Several organisms, such as bacteria, fungus and plants, have been 
shown to produce these enzymes. Laccase activity has been found in Basidiomycetes, including white 
rot fungi (WRF): however, the three WRF laccase candidates from Trametes Versicolor, Agaricus 
Biporus and Pleurotus Ostreatus, are selected candidates for the research of this project. Additionally, 
such an extracellular enzyme system of WRF plays an important role in the degradation of the plant 
polymer lignin. Fungal laccases have a molecular weight (MW) between 50-100 kDa[5]. High redox 
potentials are often associated with these enzymes, referring to their ability to remove electrons from 
substrates[6]. In general, laccase is characterized by having a globular molecular architecture, divided 
into three different copper sites (T1, T2 and T3), containing four copper atoms referred to as Cu1, 
Cu2, Cu3 and Cu4 [5]. Figure 1 shows the location of each copper atom respectively at the different 
copper sites. The T1 site is acting as the active site of the laccase and has the function of removing 
electrons from the substrate and shuffling them into the tri-Nuclear cluster (TCN). In combination, 
the T2/T3 copper sites make up the tri-Nuclear cluster (TCN) and are linked to molecular dioxygen 
(figure 1)[5].

3. 
Reaction mechanism 
Oxidases require oxygen if they are to react with substrates. The oxidation is carried out by four single 
electron donations from the substrate to the laccase. Hence, the donation of four electrons is required 
for a complete oxidation of molecular oxygen to water in the TCN site [8]. In addition, the electron 
donation creates a free radical (cation) and the instability of this allows further oxidation of the 
substrate to continue with four single electron donations in total (Figure 2A). Furthermore, the 
electron donation is postulated to be controlled by the redox potential difference between T1 and the 
substrate. Increasing redox potential of the laccase improves substrate oxidation[6]. Non-enzymatic 
10 
==PAGE:12==
reactions may also occur, such as polymerization. This phenome will be described and taken into 
consideration in later section “Mechanism of action”[7]. 
 ‘Laccases non-specific substrates specificity overlapping non-phenolic compounds’ is extended by 
using a redox mediator system. Mediators are small organic molecules, benefitting from having a 
high oxidative capacity of non-phenolic components. As an example, ABTS (2, 2’-azinobis-(3-
ethylbenzthiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) is widely used as a mediator system. The mechanism is driven 
by enzyme oxidation of the mediator. Once the mediator molecule has been oxidized it acts like an 
‘electron shuttle’, which produces highly reactive radicals. The free radicals diffuse away from the 
enzyme, having the capacity to oxidize non-phenolic substrates or any other substrates (Figure 2B) 
[5].

4. 
The decolourisation of dyes by oxidative enzymes 
A wide range of synthetic dyes are found in the waste water from industries such as the textile and 
paper. Among colour-removing agents, WRF have been proved to play an important role because of 
their non-specific ligninolytic enzyme system, including laccase, manganese- (Mn) and lignin (LI) 
versatile peroxidase. Literature studies concerning decolourisation include a range of different 
colourants; however, due to the non-specific ligninolytic enzyme system of WRF, they have a broad 
11 
==PAGE:13==
substrate specificity and are therefore found to have the capacity to degrade a range of dye 
compounds, especially Azo, Phtahlocyanine-, Anthraquinonic and Triphenylmethane pigments[10].  
 
The studies included in this review show the decolourisation of different dye classes by different 
ligninolytic enzyme systems. In this overview, I will mainly focus on laccase-catalyzed 
decolourisation of different dye classes, with and without an assisting redox mediator system. 
Implementing a redox mediator system has been proved to improve and enhance decolourisation and 
further successful decolourisation of dyes that have shown  themselves to be resistant to 
decolourisation alone by laccases[11][12][13].  
 Dye decolourisation is also believed to be carried out by other ligninolytic enzymes such as Li- and 
Mn peroxidases in the presence of H2O2: this aspect will also be considered. Furthermore, the 
following overview will also give a description of the characteristics and structures of pigments that 
have shown themselves to be decolourise by enzymes from WRF. The summarizing of existing 
literature regarding decolourisation by a ligninolytic enzyme system is given in table 1. A high 
number of publications are available regarding this topic and therefore an attempt is made to present 
a broad overview of types of pigments- and WRF to aid understanding of it. Table 2 will contain 
representative structures for all the different dye classes found in the literature. 
 
4.1 
Decolourisation of Azo dye 
 
4.1.1 
Azo pigment chemistry. 
The chromophore group Azo linkage is the part of Azo pigments responsible for generating colour. 
The Azo linkage is created by a double binding between two nitrogen atoms. The nitrogen atoms are 
linked to carbon atoms, where at least one is included in an aromatic ring. Furthermore, the second 
carbon atom bound to the Azo linkage is mostly part of an aliphatic derivative or acetoacetic acid [3]. 
  
4.1.2 
Ligninolytic enzyme degradation of Azo dyes 
Due to the decolourisation of Azo pigments, the results from a range of studies regarding the action 
of laccases on Azo dyes are derived from vitro experiments with many different Azo compounds. 
Phenolic groups appear often in Azo dyes. Phenolic compounds are, as mentioned earlier, typical

laccase substrates. The catalytic action of laccases on phenolic groups in Azo pigments generates a 
phenoxy radical at the carbon bearing in the Azo linkage[3].

cultures of Ganoderma sp. En3 [14], Trametes Polyzona with and with out a redox mediator 
system[11], Paraconiothrium variabile with and without a redox mediator system[4], whereas 
activity of other lignin enzymes (Mn-, Li peroxidases) was absent. Furthermore, the presence of Mn-

4.1.3 
Ligninolytic enzyme degradation of Phtahlocyanine dyes 
Phtahlocyanine dyes consist of a Phtahlocyanine complex, a macrocyclic compound having an 
intense green-blue colour. Elements from the periodic table tend to make coordination complexes 
with Phtahlocyanine, where copper is coordinated with the complex. Substituted Phtahlocyanine 
compounds reveal themselves as being less thermally stable and having a significantly higher 
solubility in organic solvents, in contrast to non-substituted Phtahlocyanine complexes[18]. Low 
water solubility holds true in both cases[18].   
Elsewhere, the presence of a ligninolytic enzyme system has also been found able to decolourise 
Phtahlocyanine dyes. Laccases together with Li peroxidases, have been reported to be the main 
enzymes for decolourisation of Phtahlocyanine pigments by Phanerochaete chrysosporium [19]. Li 
peroxidase from Phanerochaete chrysosporium has been found to be able carry out the 
decolourisation of Phtahlocyanine pigments without the presence of laccases[17]. 
 
4.1.4 
Other commercial synthetic dyes 
Other commercial dyes including Anthraquinonic, Triphenylmethane and Indigoid dyes have also 
been found in the waste water from the textile and paper industries. Ligninolytic enzyme systems 
have also been proved to be involved in decolourisation of such matter.

In the case of Triphenylmethane dyes, laccases have seemed to be the main enzyme for 
decolourisation from Peniophora sp.[20] with a mediator system, Trametes Trogii S0301 [21], 
Trametes polyzona [11] and Paraconiothyrium variabile [12], all with and without a mediator system. 
Furthermore, the presence of Mn peroxidase, laccases and Li peroxidase from Phanerochaete 
chrysosporium[16] seemed to be useful for decolourisation of Triphenylmethane dyes with a mediator 
system. 
Regarding Anthraquinonic dyes, a successful decolourisation was obtained by laccase from Pleurotus 
Ostreatus, both with and without a mediator system. Furthermore, Mn peroxidases is present along 
with laccases from Pleurotus Pulmonarius and it has also been shown to be involved in 
decolourisation of Anthraquinonic dyes[22]. Ligninolytic enzymes, including Li peroxidases and/or 
Mn peroxidases, when combined with laccases from Schizophyllum commune, have been shown to 
be involved in decolourisation of Triphenylmethane-, Anthraquinonic and Indigoid dyes[23].

5. 
Laccase for tattoo degradation in transdermal delivery 
The purpose was to use laccase enzyme for degradation of tattoo inks in the dermal layer by 
transdermal delivery. The following sections will describe the principles and challenges of using 
transdermal delivery for enzymes.  
 
5.1 
The skin physiology 
In order to design a transdermal system, the understanding of the skin’s physiology, the types of 
substances and delivery choices are necessary. The following sections will focus on the barrier of the 
human skin, tattoo behaviour in the dermal environment and transdermal delivery as the 
administration route of choice. 
The skin is the largest organ of the human body and is the body’s first line of protection against the 
outside environment[28]. The skin consists of three major barriers; the subcutaneous tissue 
(hypodermis), dermis and epidermis, listed from the deepest to the outermost skin layer, as pictured 
in Figure 3[28]. 
The tattoo inks are injected into the dermis and appear as small granules, leaving behind pigment in 
the zones which are visible from the outside of the epidermis. The action of tattoo procedure causes 
the body to start an inflammatory process. This process causes the immune cell type macrophages to 
consume the pigment. However, these cells are not capable of dissolving or disposing of the pigments 
and this is the reason that the tattoo ink remains permanently in the dermis[29]. A recent study by[30] 
has determined that the concentration of tattoo pigment in the human skin ranges between 0.60 to 
9.42 mg/cm2 in the tattooed area. 
The epidermal layer is further divided into four sub-layers: the basal layer, the squamous layer, the 
granular layer and the cornified layer. These layers are listed from the innermost layers to the 
18 
==PAGE:20==
outermost layers. The structure of the epidermis does not include blood vessels, and is therefore 
dependent on nutrient supply and waste removal via the dermis. However, the layer is exposed 
directly to atmospheric oxygen, together with the oxygen supply from the dermis, which in total 
supplies the epidermis with oxygen. Oxygen is consumed in all layers of epidermis and dermis, apart 
from the outer layer of epidermis, the cornified layer[2]. 
The cornified layer consists of dead keratinocytes (horny cells) which have the purpose of protecting 
the underlying skin, preventing water loss and protecting against penetration from foreign 
substances[2]. The cornified layer is roughly 10-100µms thick[2]. Large polyhedral-shaped 
keratinocytes comprise the basic morphology of the layer. Keratinocytes are surrounded by a lipid 
matrix. 
Before entering the dermal layer, a porous membrane zone acts as an interface between the epidermal 
and dermal layer. This zone is responsible for the trafficking of cells, fluids and for maintaining the 
integral bond of the two layers. The dermal layer is located beneath the epidermis and is the thickest 
layer (1, 5 to 4mm) of the three main layers[28]. The layer functions as a bulk that will ensure tensile 
strength of the skin by the production of collagen and elastin[2]. The barrier of dermis is mostly 
acellular, but also consists of layers of blood vessels, lymphatic vessels, nerve endings and hair 
follicles. The network of dermal capillaries is connected with the circulatory system by horizontal 
branching from the arterioles and venules. The structure of the layer helps to control the body 
temperature, the supply of nutrients and oxygen from the blood vessels to the surrounding layers[2].

Fick’s first law of diffusion 
 Some investigations have shown that skin diffusion can be quantified by Fick’s first law of diffusion 
(eqn. 1)[32]. To use the model it is assumed that the thickness of the skin remains constant. Regarding 
this project, the mathematical model of Fick’s first law of diffusion is capable of making a relatively 
simple and straight forward explanation for the enzyme diffusion; the flux of enzyme diffused through 
the cornified layer was shown to be inversely proportional in relation to the thickness of the cornified 
layer. The model shows that the diffusive flux is related to the concentration of enzymes with the 
system in a steady state. Furthermore, it has been claimed that the flux of enzymes was moving from 
a region of high concentration to a region with lower concentration, making it proportionally related 
to the concentration gradient[33]. In other words, the flux will move from high concentration to low 
concentration through a concentration gradient. 
Manipulating any of the parameters of the Ks, D and of the equation (1) makes it possible to regulate 
the diffusion. The changing of these parameters can be attempted when performing structural 
modifications of the enzymes; such actions are often seen to improve skin diffusion[32].

5.3 
Transdermal delivery 
The transdermal route is mostly used in medical treatment for the delivery of drugs that need to reach 
the blood system through the skin. In this case, the purpose was to use laccase for degrading tattoo 
inks located in the dermal layer.  Transdermal delivery is often seen to be capable of only handling 
small amounts of substances because of the organisation of the skin. From that point of view, this 
makes the potency of the enzyme an important consideration.  
The skin has a very good barrier function that makes the transdermal route a challenge. Addressing 
the area of transdermal delivery, focus is on the cornified layer because of its hydrophobic nature in 
contrast to the underlying layers, which are more hydrophilic. Since enzyme diffusion through the 
20 
==PAGE:22==
skin can be a challenge, it is therefore important to alter the hydrophilic/hydrophobic balance of the 
enzyme surface so that it gives the enzymes the right properties for crossing the skin barrier. An 
unbalanced relationship will limit the enzyme diffusion through the skin. Consequently, the 
hydrophilic/hydrophobic balance of enzymes has to be examined in different directions to identify 
which enzyme properties are compatible with the nature of the skin. 
Owing to the selective nature of the skin, the size limitation of molecules that are capable of crossing 
the skin is very challenging from a transdermal delivery point of view. Enzyme diffusion through the 
skin will be limited due to that, because enzymes are often too large to cross the skin barrier[34]. It 
is therefore likely that the diffusion of laccases through the skin will be limited on that basis. As an 
alternative, it may be necessary to consider ‘enhancing strategies’. 
The stability of laccases when entering the dermal environment might also be problematic in relation 
to challenges posed by the process, the reason being that transdermal delivery of proteins is often 
related to stability issues[35]. According to [36], laccases have been reported to be highly stable: this 
might indicate that they could be resistant to protein degradation of proteolytic enzymes (proteases) 
found in the skin [36]. Further stability issues are associated with the skin’s temperature and pH-
value. The skin temperature relies upon several factors, such as the surrounding temperature and 
physical activity level. Skin pH-values are shown to be between 4 and 7[37]. 
 
5.4 
The dermal barriers and enzyme absorption 
Fick’s first law of diffusion, equation (1), has shown itself to be useful for describing the enzyme 
diffusion through the cornified layer. As previously mentioned, changing the variables of the equation 
(1) make it possible to regulate the dermal diffusion of enzymes. Enzymes that should diffuse in order 
to cross the cornified layer are, as mentioned earlier, often associated with challenges because 
enzymes often do not fulfill the right requirements for such a purpose. However, in order to enhance 
the enzyme diffusion, the previously mentioned challenges need to be evaluated to find a proper 
solution.   
Concerning the size of the enzymes, the subject of enzymes crossing the skin barrier is controversial, 
since only molecules with a molecular weight < 500 Da are capable of passing through the skin or 
hair follicles. Normally, the size of proteins are found above this range and therefore do not cross the 
skin layers[34]. Regarding this project, fungal laccases are also found outside this range, with a MW 
between 50-100 kDa, as mentioned earlier.  
21 
==PAGE:23==
As touched on earlier, the cornified layer acts as a hydrophobic compartment, because its morphology 
consists of lipid matrixes. The lipid matrixes form a tight junction which prevents the enzymes 
diffusing through the cornified layer[38][2].Therefore, it is essential that the hydrophobicity of the 
enzymes has the right balance between hydrophilic/hydrophobic characteristics, in order to be able 
to cross the skin barrier. The number of the partition coefficient (logP) expresses the degree of 
hydrophobicity of a compound and needs to be between 1-3 in order for enzymes to cross the skin 
barrier[35]. Unfortunately, enzymes are often outside that range in this context. 
Laccases having the capacity of decolourise the tattoo pigment in the skin are, together with the 
degraded product, thought to leave through the blood vessels in the dermal layer. The degraded 
products can be large to leave the skin through the blood vessels, and will therefore remain in the 
skin. In the case where the enzyme and degradation products can be absorbed into the blood vessels, 
the products will enter the circulatory system, where the intention is that they will pass through the 
hepar and thereby be removed from the body. There are also possibilities that the pigments will be 
accumulated in the body. 
 
5.5 
The mechanism of action 
The mechanism of action by laccases in the skin depends on a several factors that might have an 
effect on the degradation of the tattoo pigments. An overview of parameters that are considered to 
impact the degradation of tattoo pigments in the skin is given in following section. The process of 
how laccases and degradation products leave the dermal layer will also be taken into consideration. 
As mentioned earlier, the laccases reaction mechanism is driven by oxygen, which in the dermal 
environment will be supplied by the bloodstream in the dermis and by the surrounding atmosphere. 
It has been found that the concentration of oxygen obtained by cutaneous respiration (uptake of 
oxygen from the air) depends on the blood flux in the outer-lying capillaries, as well as the flow 
velocity. Increasing blood flow in the skin raises the cutaneous respiration level and improves oxygen 
uptake. It has been found that the oxygen uptake from the surrounding air for humans with a skin area 
of 1, 7 m2 is 0.90 ml O2/(m2*min) counts for around 0.4 % usage of the oxygen supply while resting. 
The pulmonary oxygen supplies the rest of the oxygen needed by 230 ml O2 min-1[39]. 
As previously mentioned, laccase enzymes have been demonstrated to have the capacity for 
decoloursing a range of synthetic pigments among Azo- and Phtahlocyanine pigments. These pigment 
classes are commonly found used in tattoo dyes. Degradation of Azo pigments is often connected 
22 
==PAGE:24==
with a high level of concern, because of the toxic amines so generated. The common use of Azo 
pigments in tattoo dyes appears therefore very concerning. Such toxicity problems have been shown 
to be avoided by using enzymatic treatment of laccases. The enzymes use a free radical mechanism, 
an N-demethylation for degradation of Azo dyes, hereby avoiding impacts of toxic amines[3].Such 
properties make laccases a favorable choice for degradation of tattoo pigment in the skin. 
 
On the other hand, laccase degradation of Phtahlocyanine pigments has been proven to increase the 
toxicity in contrast to the untreated pigment compounds, because of the Cu2+ ion formed during the 
degradation[19]. Therefore, laccase treatment of these compounds in the skin causes some concern 
due to their health impacts. 
 
Regarding the catalytic mechanism of laccases, polymerization has shown itself to have taken place 
in many cases. As mentioned previously, laccases withdraw electrons from the reducing substrate 
and further convert them into free radicals: sometimes the radicals have been linked together, thereby 
creating polymerization products[7]. Polymerization products are observed as dark-coloured 
complexes, these substances having a high MW and low solubility. This can prevent them from 
entering the blood vessels and therefore they stay in the dermal layer. Dark spots on the skin will be 
seen as a consequence[40].

The Spectrophotometry 
Carrying out the project, spectrophotometry will be applied for testing the enzymatic decolourisation 
of tattoo pigment. The following section will provide a description of the basic principles of 
spectrophotometry.  
Spectrophotometry is a method that measures the amount of light that a solution absorbs. In addition, 
the method is often applied as a quantitative analysis used to determine the amount of molecules in a 
solution. Any chemical compound absorbs, transmits, or reflects light over a given specific 
wavelength interval. Instrumentally, a spectrometer is used. It functions by passing a light beam 
through a solution (containing molecules of interest). The apparatus detects the intensity of light 
reflecting from the solution as measured by its detector. The beam of light contains protons. The light 
stream of protons that encounter the solution has the possibility of being absorbed by the solution. 
Such absorption will reduce the number of protons that will be presented in the light beam, resulting 
23 
==PAGE:25==
in the decreasing intensity of the light beam. As far as the wavelength of light is concerned, it can be 
divided into two sub-classes, UV(ultraviolet)-visible spectrophotometry and IR(infrared) 
spectrophotometry[41]. 
 Regarding this project, only UV-visible spectrophotometry is taken into consideration, because 
measurements only are performed in the interval between 280 and 650 nm. The UV light range is 
between 185 and 400 nm and visible light in the range between 400 and 700 nm in the electromagnetic 
radiation spectrum. In relation to the visible spectrophotometry, the absorption or transmission of a 
given compound can be determined by detecting its colour[41].  
 A sample that absorbs across all wavelengths of the visible range is defined as black. Equally, if light 
is transmitted at all wavelengths in the visible spectrum, the result will be white. In between these 
two extremes, the colour of light absorbed by a certain solution is detected as its complementary 
colour. Figure 4 gives an overview of the colour cycle, illustrating the pairs of complementary colours 
[41].

Pre-decolourisation study of tattoo pigments 
A pre-decolourisation study of the dye: Dark Red, True Magenta, Golden Yellow and Bahama Blue 
were carried out. The decolourisation was determined by continuous spectrophotometric 
measurements.    
As previously mentioned, the phenolic content of the Dark Red dye was expected to have a high 
potential for decolourisation concerning laccase activity. Furthermore, the Phtahlocyanine pigments, 
pigment green 7 and pigment blue 15 were also expected to have potential for decolourisation by 
laccases.     
 
These hypotheses and the interest of the decolourisation of the True Magenta and Golden Yellow 
were tested by determining the decolourisation in the presences of the laccase activity of Trametes 
Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus incubating at 300C for 24 hours. An absorbance value at 1.5 was 
preferred as a starting point for the experiment for each dye respectively. The dye concentrations that 
should fit should absorbance are listed in table 21. It can be seen that the solution of the Bahama 
Blue- and the Golden Yellow dye reached a much lower absorbance value than 1.5. Therefore it 
seems likely that the solutions must have a lower dye concentration than expected (Table 21).     
 
As seen in Figure 17 and 22, a remarkable decrease of the absorption of the True Magenta- and Dark 
Red dye was seen after 24 hours when treated with both Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus. 
Trametes Versicolor showed a great decolourisation ability regarding True Magenta with 62 % dye 
decolourised, where the Pleurotus Ostreatus showed a decolouristion 29.16 % True Magenta dye. 
The Dark Red dye was decolourised 53.41% by Trametes Versicolor and 47.15% by Pleurotus 
Ostreatus.     
 
An important observation was that Trametes Versicolor had very poor decolourisation ability 
regarding the Bahama Blue dye, with 7.3% dye decolourised after 24 hours. Regarding Trametes 
Versicolor’s ability to decolourise the Golden yellow dye, close to zero percentages of dye were 
decolourised after 24 hours. Sediment was not observed in any of the samples of each dye 
respectively. It was also observed that the absorbance of the reference samples (untreated samples) 
was also decreasing slightly as represented in Table 22.

Decolourisation study 1 
In response to the pre-decolourisation study, the True Magenta dye was chosen for the purpose 
showing whether changing the dye concentration was affecting the laccase activity. The 
50 
==PAGE:52==
decolourisation of the dye was performed in the presence of the Trametes Versicolor- and Pleurotus 
Ostreatus laccase, testing for 24 hours. Furthermore, the experiment included also reference samples, 
controls of the Dark Red dye. The hypothesis was that increasing dye concentration was affecting the 
laccase activity by increasing the reaction rate and thereby performing a faster decolourisation. This 
postulation was made on the basis of Michaelis-Menten model of enzyme kinetics. The demonstration 
of the hypothesis was performed on dye suspensions in the presence of laccase enzyme incubating at 
300C in a time study of 24 hours.  
 
Comparatively, the samples of the True Magenta dye treated with Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus 
Ostreatus showed a significant decrease after 24 hours (Figure 17-18). The laccase activity from 
Trametes Versicolor demonstrated the decolourisation ability of the three dye concentrations (Table 
24 and 25) by the following percentages; 0.3 mg/ml: 48.46% (abs. change 0.26), 0.71 mg/ml: 43.57% 
(abs. change 0.46) and 1.5 54.61 (abs. change 0.855). In the case of laccase from Pleurotus Ostreatus, 
it shows following decolourisation ability of the three dye concentrations by following percentages: 
0.3 mg/ml: 39% (abs change 0.25), 0.71 mg/ml: 53% (abs change 0.65) and 1.5 mg/ml: 57% (abs 
change 1.23) (Table 25).   
 
Unfortunately, the reference samples (untreated samples) were also showing a decrease to a similar 
level, which was not found to have occurred in the pre-decolourisation study. The reference samples 
for the study with TV were decreasing by following numbers: 0.3 mg/ml: 56.3% (abs. changes 0.31), 
0.71 mg/ml: 58.05% (abs. changes 0.59) and 1.5 mg/ml: 63.25% (abs. changes 0.89). The reference 
samples for the study with PO showed following results: 0.3 mg/ml: 66% (abs. changes 0.4),0.7 
mg/ml: 71% (abs. change 0.9) 1.5 mg/ml: 68% (abs. change 1.44). 
 
 The same phenome was observed with the untreated sample of the Dark Red dye (Table 25). Based 
on this experimental data, it appears that the dyes were not decolorized, and the reduction of the 
absorption was probably due to the fact that the dyes were precipitated over time.

The enzyme dynamic study of ABTS 
 
11.12.1 
ABTS kinetic study 
The objective of this experiment was to determine the effect of substrate concentration (ATBS) on 
the enzyme activity. The laccases Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus were tested on four 
substrate concentrations as listed in Table 13. As seen in Figure 23 and 24, the reaction rate by 
Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus were positively correlated with the substrate (ABTS) 
dosage used. Based on the plots (Figure 23-25), it can be seen that increasing the concentration of 
ABTS increases the initial rate and the amount of oxidized ABTS over time. 
The initial rate was calculated based on the plots ΔAbs vs. time, as illustrated in Figure 25 and 27. 
The enzyme kinetic parameters Km and Vmax were estimated based on the Hanes plot by plotting 
against [S]. As expected, the Hanes plot was showing a linear relationship for both enzyme candidates 
(Figure 25 and 26). The value Vmax of the Trametes Versicolor was five times larger compared to 
Pleurotus Ostreatus. The Km value of Pleurotus Ostreatus laccase is significant higher compared to 
Trametes Versicolor laccase. The results demonstrate that the Pleurotus Ostreatus laccase is in need 
of a higher substrate concentration in order to reached its maximum reaction rate (Table 28). 
Regarding the literature, a significantly lower Km value has been reported by the author[47], at 
20.2µM and higher Vmax at 8µM/min, compared to the values obtained in this study.

12 
Discussion 
The following chapters of this project will carry out a discussion, based on the previous results 
presented, regarding laccases potential as a colour-degrading agent of tattoo pigments. The results 
will be discussed by making a comparison with known literature, in an attempt to reflect on the 
relevance of the results found in this particular study. Furthermore, this section of the study will also 
include a discussion regarding the challenges encountered during the experimental work and how 
they have affected the results. 
 
12.1 
Enzyme assay 
By the use of ABTS and Syringaldazine as benchmarks of laccases actions, it was possible to 
determine the enzyme activity of Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus and Agaricus Biporus 
laccases. Reasonable enzyme activity was seen for all three candidates regarding ABTS oxidation. 
Syringaldazine oxidation confirmed activity of Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus 
laccases. However, this was not the case for laccase from Agaricus Biporus, which was not able to 
oxidize Syringaldazine. The experiment was performed several times, yet continued to show negative 
results. This could be due to a low redox potential of Agaricus Biporus laccase, which means that it 
has a poor ability to remove the electrons from the substrate, which could be the reason for its lack 
of oxidation of Syringaldazine.     
 
All experiments were carried out with commercial tattoo dyes from the supplier of Intenz ink and 
pure synthetic pigments from the company of Kreamer pigments. The pigments belong to different 
chemical classes with individual characteristics. All tested tattoo pigments are represented in Table 
4, together with an illustration of their respective chemical structures. 
12.2 
Absorbance scan 
Absorbance scans were performed in the range between 280-650 nm. The spectra created on the basis 
of the scans gave the possibility of identifying the wavelength with the highest corresponding 
absorbance value of each pigment respectively. Before beginning the scans, a solubility test was 
performed on the tattoo pigments. Several solvents were tested, in the attempt to identify a proper 
solvent where the pigments remained soluble. Water and ethanol were chosen as the final solvents in 
61 
==PAGE:63==
considering the nature of laccases. Using water and ethanol as solvents caused the pigments to remain 
as suspensions in water and ethanol.  
The absorbance scans were successfully obtained for the Golden Yellow, the Bahama Blue, the Dark 
Red and the True Magenta dye in water.  Based on Figure 8-12, it was possible to find the wavelength 
having the highest absorbance value of the above mentioned dyes. It was not possible to measure an 
absorbance scan of the True Black dye, the Dark Green pigment, Iron oxide (red pigment) and 
Titanium oxide (white pigment) in water respectively. The Bahama Blue dye and the Dark Green 
pigment both comprise of the same pigment Green 7 and therefore it was surprising that an 
absorbance scan not was not possible to be created for the Dark Green pigment. This could indicate 
that the formulation of the Bahama Blue dye may have an influence at that point. On the other hand, 
it was not surprising that an absorbance scan was not obtainable for the True Black dye, since the 
black dye absorbs all light, thus leaving no light to be detected. However, low solubility may also 
have an impact in this case.  
With that taken into account, the focus was on the Golden Yellow, the Bahama Blue, the Dark Red 
and the True Magenta dyes. Since absorbance measurements are thought to be used for demonstrating 
decolourisation, a linear relationship between the absorbance and dye concentrations needs to be 
fulfilled if Lambert Beer’s law of absorbance should be applicable. The plots in Figures 13-16 
demonstrate the relation and make it possible to apply the method in order to measure the 
decolourisation of tattoo pigments.    
 
12.3 
Pre-decolourisation study 
The decolourisation plots were used as graphical tools, demonstrating the decolourisation monitored 
by the Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus laccases in the pre-testing study. As mentioned 
earlier, high enzyme affinity was expected for pigment Red 170 of the Dark Red dye, because of its 
content of a phenolic group which are known to be a preferred laccase substrate[5]. As expected, the 
Dark Red dye which contains the pigment Red 170 proved to be sensitive to the enzymatic treatment 
of Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus laccases: this could be explained by its content of a 
phenolic group. The Dark Red dye also contains the pigment Carbazole Violet and this is relevant 
because[3] reports that laccases from WRF have been proven to be able to oxidize such compounds 
by assisting a redox mediator system. The lack of a mediator system in this case means that the 
decreasing absorbance of the Dark Red dye could be related to the degradation of the pigment Red 
62 
==PAGE:64==
170. The Dark Red was decolourised at a rate of 53.41% by Trametes Versicolor and 47.15% by 
Pleurotus Ostreatus after 24 hours. 
The pigments Blue 15 and pigment Green 7 of the Blue Bahama dye were also expected to have 
potential for decolourisation by laccases due to the literature by[19]. This author has demonstrated 
decolourisation of Phtahlocyanine dyes with very similar structures by laccase activity. However, 
some structural differences were identified with these dyes compared to the tattoo pigment, including 
the number of bonds from the copper sub-unit and the nitrogen’s atoms comprising the ring structure 
of such pigments. Furthermore, sulphate groups attached to the ring structure were also observed in 
the pigment structures from the literature[19](Table 1), which were not found in the tattoo pigment, 
pigment Green 7 and Blue 15 of the Bahama Blue dye. The pigment Green 7 consists of chloride and 
bromide linked to the Phtahlocyanine ring (table 4): this was not seen in the pigment from the 
literature.  On the other hand, only a very small change in the absorbance was seen in the Bahama 
Blue dye, with a decolourisation rate at around 7.3% by Trametes Versicolor after 24 hours. 
Implementation of a redox mediator system could possibly enhance the decolourisation. [19] 
proposed that Mn-peroxidases also play an important role in the decolourisation process by removing 
the Cu2+ ion from the complex[19]. The absence of Mn-peroxidases could explain why the 
decolourisation was unable to occur. The co-substrate H2O2 is required for Mn-peroxidases to enable 
the catalytic process; thus such requirements are found to limit their use for this particular purpose, 
due to the features of the enzymes which are required to operate in a dermal environment where H2O2 
is not naturally produced. 
In the case of decolourisation of the True Magenta dye, significant reduction of the absorption was 
obtained after 24 hours. Decolourisation by enzymatic treatment of quinacridone compounds has not 
been documented earlier in the literature.  Therefore, the result of the experiment is considered both 
surprising and a relative success, in that the laccase activity is shown to have affinity for such 
compounds. The True Magenta dye exhibited a decolourisation rate over 24 hours of 62% in the 
presence of the Trametes Versicolor laccase and 29.16% in the presence of Pleurotus Ostreatus.    
Laccase activity did not reflect any decolourisation of the Golden Yellow dye after 24 hours. 
Decolourisation of Azo dyes with and without a mediator system has been widely demonstrated by 
[48]. The dyes from these studies have been shown to have the same basic structure as the two Azo 
components, pigment Orange 13 and Yellow 97, in the Golden Yellow dye. Sulphate- and phenolic 
groups are seen in the dyes of these studies (see Table 1). However, whilst pigment orange 97 contains

presented in the Azo dyes from the literature [48]. Therefore, the degradation of the two Azo 
components, orange 13 and yellow 97, could possibly be improved by the use of a mediator system. 
If a decolourisation of the tattoo dyes has taken place, as seen in the pre-testing study, the absorbance 
change per minute is significantly lower for all the dyes respectively (see table 22) compared to those 
of ABTS and Syringaldazine, which showed an absorbance change per minute of: ABTS: 0.1026

and Pleurotus Ostreatus will therefore achieve a lower initial rate by degrading the tattoo pigments.  
 
12.4 
Decolourisation study 1 
The decolourisation study 1 needed to follow the kinetics of the enzyme reaction in relation to 
different dye concentrations of the True magenta dye. The first readings showed that the absorbance 
of the dye samples was decreasing after 24 hours (Figure 17-18). Furthermore, the decolourisation 
rate of the dye concentration 0.71 mg/ml from the pre-testing study was found to be 62% due to 
treatment with Trametes Versicolor. It was therefore surprising that it was decreasing to 48.46% in 
the decolourisation study one. It was the opposite for the Pleurotus Ostreatus laccase in the 
decolourisation study 1, where the decolourisation rate increased from 29% to 55% with a dye 
concentration at 0.71mg/ml. As previously mentioned, the concentration of pigment in the dye 
formulation is not known. This could explain that the concentration of the pigment in the samples, to 
some degree, is unpredictable. Furthermore, it is likely that centrifugation removes different amounts 
of pigment from the samples and thereby can make the dye concentration differ. It appeared that the 
decolourisation rate was positively correlated with the dye concentration regarding the treatment of 
the Pleurotus Ostreatus laccase. This could indicate two things: from an enzyme kinetic perspective, 
increasing dye concentration increases the initial rate of the reaction, resulting in a higher amount of 
product. On the other hand, it could also be that the dye solution was saturated and that made the 
pigment precipitate over time.    
Unfortunately the reference samples (without samples) of this experiment seem to decrease as well, 
to the same level of absorption measured in the samples treated with enzymes (Figure 17-18), a trend 
which was not apparent in the preliminary decolourisation experiments for any of the dyes.

Decolourisation study 2 
The differences between the experimental set-ups of previously studies needed to be evaluated. It was 
therefore not appropriate to make a final conclusion based on previous experiments. The pre-testing 
study was performed on the basis of enzymatic treatment working on dye samples which had been 
centrifuged, whereas in the decolourisation study 1 the enzymes were working on dye suspensions. 
It was therefore decided to make a new experiment where both conditions of the former studies were 
included. This set-up allowed an evaluation to clarify if any of the conditions may affect the dye 
decolourisation process. The testing of such matters was performed on the Dark Red dye, following 
the procedure found in the section of experimental work. Both experimental conditions were 
demonstrating the same trend of results, showing that the reference samples without enzymes were 
decreasing significantly at a rate of 50 %(dye suspension) and 41.3% (dye solution). The absorbance 
of the enzyme treated samples seemed also to decrease at a rate of 61% (dye suspension) and 54 %( 
dye dilution). The experiment showed that the enzyme-treated samples were decreasing by around 
10 % more compared to the reference samples.    
 
 The experiment was repeated and the same results were obtained. Therefore, it can be concluded that 
the specified experimental conditions do not have an effect on the trend of the results. However, it is 
surprising that the reference samples that should reflect those seen in the pre-testing study now 
demonstrate a decrease as well. The enzyme treated samples from the decolourisation study 2 showed 
a decrease of about 10% more compared to the reference samples, which could indicate that the 
enzyme treatment has some effect. Furthermore, the above mentioned difference between the 
reference samples and the enzyme treated samples might also be a result of the unpredictable dye 
concentration that has been observed in previous studies. With all of the observations taken into 
account, it is an indication that the enzyme treatment may might not have had the predicted effect, 
which is otherwise concluded from the pre-testing experiments.  
 
This gives grounds for speculation in regard to finding a plausible explanation for the difference in 
the outcome between the pre-decolourisation study and the decolourisation study 1. Furthermore, the 
results also calls into question the use of Lambert Beer’s law of absorbance, should it be correct that 
the pigments have precipitated over time.

Azo dyes have been proved to be very stable due to temperature, pH and resistance towards oxygen 
and light exposure (W. University & Award, 2014). Despite this, elevated heating is shown to be 
prone to causing instability of tattoo pigments by[49] as mentioned earlier. The pigments were stored 
in the refrigerator. The experimental work on the pigments was carried out at a temperature of around 
30 0C. This increase in temperature may lead to instability in regards to the tattoo pigments. On the 
other hand, 300C was used for carrying out the experiments in order to create similar conditions 
surrounding the skin’s physiology. The choice of temperature has maybe been too high and possibly 
needed to be decreased to a lower level. Furthermore, the pH value is thought to stable because of the 
presence of the 50 mM NaOAc buffer (pH 5); however, as earlier mentioned, some synthetic pigments 
have shown themselves to be less stable at different pH values. As previously mentioned, light and 
oxygen have also been found to influence the stability of some colourants. Relevant testing needs to 
be taken into consideration for investigation to see if such parameters impact the stability of the tattoo 
pigments.   
Another stability issue could be related to Glycerol, which acts as a stability agent in the dye 
formulation. During the experimental work the dyes have been diluted and the risk of reduction of 
Glycerol might therefore also impact the stability of the pigments.  As a result of these considerations, 
a combination of several factors could have influenced the stability of the dissolved colour and 
thereby be responsible for the difference in results between the experiments.   
The tattoo pigments are found to be encapsulated within granule vesicles as described earlier. Such 
constellations might prevent enzyme-pigment interactions, thereby avoiding dye decolourisation. 
From that point of view, dye pre-treatment seems to be necessary. This pre-treatment needs to be 
capable of breaking down the vesicle and thereby releasing the pigment. Pre-treatment, including 
sonication or treatment with protease enzymes, is suggested for such purposes: further specifications 
can be found in a later section called “future perspective”. 
 
12.6 
Enzyme kinetic experiment 
A kinetic experiment was carried out to determine the effect of substrate concentration (ABTS) on 
the enzyme activity. The experiment was carried out using varying ABTS concentrations, 0.2mM, 
1mM, 5mM and 10mM with Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus laccases. The initial rate 
was calculated based on the slopes obtained from the Figures 23 and 24, plotting the absorbance 
against time. Regarding the Figures 23 and 24, it was shown that increased substrate concentration 
66 
==PAGE:68==
of ABTS increased the amount of oxidized ABTS, which resulted in increased absorbance values. 
Hanes’ plot was performed, by plotting against [S], and thereby the kinetic parameters Vmax and Km 
were determined. Trametes Versicolor showed a Vmax five times larger than Pleurotus Ostreatus. 
Pleurotus Ostreatus showed a significantly higher Km at 884.7µM value than Trametes Versicolor 
laccase at 501 µM. Based on that, Trametes Versicolor laccase therefore had a higher affinity for the 
substrate compared to Pleurotus Ostreatus laccase. The kinetic parameters Km and Vmax found for 
Trametes Versicolor laccase oxidizing ABTS at pH 5 at 250C by [47] were found to be significantly 
different for those that have been obtained in this study. The Km and Vmax values for Trametes 
Versicolor laccase and Pleurotus Ostreatus laccase degrading the tattoo pigment are thought to be as 
follow: Km value is thought to be higher and the Vmax to be lower.  
Looking to the future, laccases should be included in a transdermal formulation used for degrading 
tattoo inks in the skin. The enzymes have to cross the skin barrier to target the tattoo pigment in the 
dermal layer. Fick’s first law of diffusion is held to be useful for describing the enzyme diffusion 
through the cornified layer. It is possible to regulate the diffusion through the skin by changing the 
parameters in equation (1). As previously mentioned, enzymes crossing the dermal barrier are 
associated with a wide range of challenges that will limit their diffusion potential. The problems 
regarding the hydrophobicity, size and stability of enzymes are all parameters that need to be 
evaluated to obtain a successful diffusion through the skin. Structural modifications of the enzymes 
could be a strategy for changing the surface of the enzyme and obtaining the right 
hydrophilic/hydrophobic properties.  
 
As previously mentioned, laccases should be able to reduce the toxicity of Azo dyes, this making 
them suitable for using with the skin. Laccase degradation of Phtahlocyanine dyes, on the other hand, 
was shown to increase their toxicity. Thus, it is challenging to use enzymatic treatment of such 
purposes due to the risk of health impacts.

Future perspectives 
The research performed in this thesis has provided key information about using laccase for 
decolourisation of tattoo pigments. Improvements are needed to overcome the problems that are 
found to prevent success in that direction.  
 Previously, research work showed that laccases activity did not have any effect on the 
decolourisation of tattoo pigments. At the same time, some parameters have been found that might 
impact the process. The surrounding granule vesicles of the tattoo pigments are thought to be the 
main reason that could prevent the enzyme-pigment interaction. Therefore, pre-treatment of the tattoo 
dyes is thought to be a possible way to overcome this limitation and should be extended to further 
studies. Sonication or treatment with protease enzymes could be promising strategies for opening the 
granule vesicles, thereby releasing the pigments. 
From the point of view that laccases have the capacity to decolourise tattoo pigments, the degraded 
products might not have the ability to leave the skin through the blood vessels as mentioned earlier. 
As a consequence, the products will be accumulated in the skin and can create skin damage. 
Therefore, treatment after the decolourisation process might be necessary to prevent accumulation of 
product in the skin. Ultrasound could be used to degrade the pigments into smaller products that could 
be absorbed through the blood vessels.

Conclusion 
Laccases have become important due to treating waste water with dye colourants from the textile 
industries. The tattoo dyes True Magenta, Dark Red, Bahama Blue and Golden Yellow were used in 
this project for investigating if laccases had the capacity to decolourise these tattoo pigments, which 
have shown structural similarity with other pigments from the textile industry that have been 
successfully decolourised by laccases.   
The enzyme activity of the three laccase candidates Pleurotus Ostreatus, Trametes Versicolor and 
Agaricus Biporus was determined by using the benchmark substrates ABTS and Syringaldazine. All 
three laccases were shown capable of oxidizing ABTS, having an activity at 9.71 U/mg (TV), 8.91 
U/mg (PO) and 0.140 U/mg (AB). Pleurotus Ostreatus and Trametes Versicolor were also showing 
oxidation of Syringaldazine, having an activity at 3.71 U/mg (TV) and 2.08 U/mg (PO). The Agaricus 
68 
==PAGE:70==
Biporus, on the other hand, was not showing any oxidation of Syringaldazine. It was suggested that 
this might be caused by a low redox potential of the laccase.  
A solvent test was performed to find a suitable solvent for performing absorbance scans of the tattoo 
dye and pigments. Water and Ethanol were chosen as solvents. The absorbance scans were performed 
in the testing interval between 280-650 nm for determining the wavelength with the highest 
absorbance values of each respective tattoo dye and pigment. The True Magenta, the Dark Red, the 
Golden Yellow and the Bahama Blue each showed a visible spectrum in water. Iron Oxide, Titanium 
Oxide and the Pigment Green 7 were not capable of performing proper spectra. Based on the 
concentration study, it was concluded that Lambert Beer’s law could be used for describing the 
concentration of the samples. This conclusion was based on the linear relationship between 
absorbance and concentration.  
A Pre-decolourisation study was performed with the tattoo dyes True Magenta, Bahama Blue, Golden 
Yellow and Dark red, which were treated with Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus laccases 
for 24 hours. A significant decrease was observed in the True Magenta and Dark Red dye with both 
Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus, with a decolourisation rate of True Magenta with 
Trametes Versicolor laccase at 62 % and 29% with Pleurotus Ostreatus over 24 hours. The Dark Red 
dye was also decreasing in the presence of both laccases, with a decolourisation rate of 53.1% with 
Trametes Versicolor and 47.15% with Pleurotus Ostreatus over 24 hours. Decolourisation of the 
Dark Red dye was expected: the dye contained the pigment Red 170, which had a phenolic group. 
The Bahama Blue dye showed a very limited decolourisation rate in the presence of Trametes 
Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus, at around 7.3% over 24 hours. The Bahama Blue dye contained 
the two Phtahlocyanine pigments, Pigment Green 7 and Pigment Blue 15. It was there unexpectedly, 
so that limited decolourisation had taken place, because the literature had described that laccases had 
the ability to decolourise such compounds[19]. It can be concluded that a mediator system can 
possibly improve the reaction. The Golden Yellow was hardly changed in the absorbance, resulting 
in less than 1% of decolourisation. Compared to the oxidizing of the benchmark substrates ABTS and 
Syringaldazine with Trametes Versicolor and Pleurotus Ostreatus, the absorbance change per minute 
is significantly lower for the tattoo dyes in the pre-decolourisation study. On that basis, it can possibly 
be concluded that the enzymes have a lower affinity for the dyes.

Laccase assay: 
Laccase activity is determined spectrophotometrically by measuring the oxidation of ABTS, which 
develops a green-blue color. The absorbance is measured at 420 nm and the molar absorption 
coefficient ɛ is 3.9 × 104 M−1 cm−1. Activity is based on determination of the rate initial rate, where 
the substrate, S, is in excess, so the initial rate vi is close to the maximum reaction rate Vmax. The 
oxidation rate of ABTS is observed by measuring the change in absorbance (∆A) per min. 
 
Procedure For plate assay (microplate assay) using following volume for assay: 
 
1. Make a 100 mM NaOAc buffer pH 5 
2. Make a stock solution of 30 mM ABTS in 10 ml milli-Q-water. Must be stored in the fridge 
and protected from light (wrap in aluminum foil) 
3. Premix Buffer and substrate (ABTS) in a volume corresponding to X no. of needed measure
ments (X • 300µl) 
4. Add 10 µl enzyme (diluted or non-diluted) to a microtitter well and initiate reaction by addin
g the buffer/substrate mix. Start measuring at 420 nm immediately after. 
5. Try different dilutions of enzyme until a reaction curve similar to the picture is obtained.
