Abstract 
Brief Summary of the Thesis Project 
Morocco launched its new energy strategy in 2009 to lead an energy transition towards 
green energies and reduce the dependence of its economy on imports of fossil fuels. the choice 
of renewable energies is also motivated by the very large resources available to Morocco, 
namely, the interesting renewable energy resources (mainly wind and solar) in several regions 
of its territory; the development of this type of energy has experienced growth with the launch 
in 2010 of the two main programs; the integrated wind program and the integrated solar 
program of 1GW installed capacity each (PPA contracts guaranteed exclusively with; 
respectively the Moroccan TSO ONEE for wind and the Moroccan Agency for Sustainable 
Energy (MASEN) for solar, as well as the release in 2010 of law 13-09 which allowed for the 
first time private actors to be able to develop renewable energy projects and sell the electricity 
produced to private customers via PPA contracts, a prerogative which was exclusive to the 
national TSO (ONEE) before this law. Private investors subsequently developed several 
projects and other tenders were launched as part of the support mechanisms to achieve the 
country's transitions and energy independence. To accelerate its energy transition, Morocco has 
increased its objective by increasing the share of renewable energies from 42% of installed 
power planned in 2020, to 52% by 2030. Both wind and solar sources coexist in several regions 
of the country, therefore developing Hybrid Power Plants (HPP) will have a chance of becoming 
an interesting technological choice. 
In this context, it becomes appropriate to study the new opportunities that HPPs create, the 
benchmark for the development of HPPs in other countries around the world and how HPPs 
can potentially facilitate the further integration of renewable energies into the Moroccan 
electricity system and attract more investments. 
Objective of the Thesis Project 
This paper focuses on the following objectives: 
- 
Study the current context of the electricity market in Morocco with its challenges 
and opportunities. 
- 
Present a benchmark of HPP projects that have emerged in other countries. 
- 
Study the new technical advantages and economic benefits brought by HPPs. 
- 
Think about which scenarios and which incentives are necessary to make the 
development of HPPs in Morocco possible. 
- 
Make a projection on the possible evolution of the electricity market in Morocco 
including the ancillary services market with better integration of HPPs. 
Study Methodology 
To meet the objectives mentioned above; I have first proceeded with a literature review to 
describe the energy market in Morocco and present the subject of HPP and its advantages. then 
I have conducted a benchmark analysis to illustrate the main HPP projects around the world as 
well as their configuration and objectives before studying examples of markets which have 
carried out reforms and regulatory innovations to motivate the technological choice of HPPs. 
A case study was conducted to explore different technological configurations through a 
Python optimization algorithm at two sites in Morocco (Site 1 at Boujdour Region and Site 2 
==PAGE:7==
 
 
at Midelt Region). This algorithm objective’s function was maximizing the Net Present 
Value (NPV) for each sizing configuration. The inputs for the algorithm are including solar 
data, wind data, energy sales tariffs, and Capex and Opex for the three technologies: PV, Wind, 
and BESS1. The objective was to study the optimized sizing proposed by the algorithm and to 
determine if economically justified hybrid configurations are possible. 
Additionally, I have evaluated different scenarios (sensitivity studies) for the two sites. The 
selection of these sites was based on several criteria: existing solar and wind projects, distinct 
wind profiles, and the high solar resource common to both sites. Specifically, the Boujdour 
region—like other southern regions—is known for high wind speeds with very high capacity 
factors. In contrast, the Midelt region exhibits medium wind speeds and capacity factors. This 
justifies the interest in studying the technological choices at the two sites with differing 
characteristics in terms of solar and wind resources. 
Key Findings and Conclusions 
The review of the regulatory framework in countries that have introduced new laws and 
policies shows good examples of recent reforms and market adjustments aimed at making HPPs 
emerge. The benchmark provides a clear idea of the extent of HPP deployment worldwide, with 
examples of the main and largest HPP projects. 
The case study conducted demonstrates that HPPs are a viable and economically interesting 
solution in the context of Morocco. The simulations presented optimized design solutions with 
overplanting2 and integration of storage. Overplanting is profitable and justified in the 
simulations studied with “maximizing NPV” as the objective function. The use of batteries 
improves the profitability of HPPs, but under certain conditions related to the price of batteries 
and the capacity factor of natural solar and wind resources. 
 
1 BESS: Battery Energy Storage System 
2 Overplanting: This involves installing more capacity than the contracted amount to ensure consistent supply.

1. Background 
1.1. Energy Sector in Morocco 
1.1.1. Energy Dependency in Morocco 
Morocco relies entirely on imports for fossil fuels, impacting its energy security and 
economy (1). Fossil fuels, as indicated in Figure 1 dominate Morocco's energy mix, with nearly 
90% of its total primary energy supply (TPES) and 80% of its electricity supply. In 2017, oil 
accounted for 62% of TPES, followed by coal (22%) and natural gas (5%). That year, the energy 
import bill was 69.5 billion dirhams (about $7.3 billion, or €6.3 billion). Domestic production 
of oil and gas remains minimal, but exploratory efforts by ONHYM and companies like Sound 
Energy and Eni show promise. Morocco expects a 5% annual increase in electricity demand, 
partly due to rising use of cooling devices. The supply of Algerian natural gas depended on 
extending the Maghreb-Europe gas pipeline agreement beyond 2021. However, due to political 
reasons, Algeria discontinued the contract in October 2021. As mentioned by the News agency 
Reuters (2), Morocco has arranged with Spain to receive liquefied natural gas (LNG) and use 
the Maghreb pipeline for delivery. Long-term plans include increasing natural gas share through 
new supply routes, including a pipeline with Nigeria and constructing an LNG terminal for 
greater supply flexibility.

According to the Moroccan Ministry of Energy Transition and Sustainable Development, 
the energy dependency ratio has decreased from 97.5% in 2009 to 90.51% currently (4).

By 2030, renewable energy sources are planned to provide 52% of the total installed

As part of the national energy efficiency strategy for 2030 (5), Morocco plans to decrease 
energy consumption by 20% by 2030 through energy efficiency enhancements and is actively 
involved in the development of green hydrogen. 
Morocco is advancing in its quest for energy security and commitment to clean energy and 
climate goals. This aligns with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 7 (SDG 7), 
focusing on affordable, reliable, sustainable, and modern energy. The Moroccan government 
has nearly achieved universal electricity access in rural areas -as mentioned in Figure 4 and is 
harnessing the nation's substantial renewable energy potential. Despite rapid growth in 
renewable energy's contribution to electricity, its share in total final consumption (TFC) has 
declined over the past decade due to rising energy demand. 
Currently, Morocco's renewable energy objectives are limited to electricity. To align with 
SDG 7's goal of significantly increasing renewable energy in the global energy mix by 2030 
(measured in TFC), it is recommended that the government establish targets for modern 
renewable energy usage in residential and transportation sectors. This would greatly aid in 
reducing fossil fuel dependence across the economy. As Morocco primarily depends on 
imported coal, oil, and gas for its energy, there is a significant opportunity to lessen these 
imports by exploiting domestic energy resources, thereby reducing reliance on oil and coal.

Electric Power Transmission System  
Since the launch of the national energy strategy in 2009, and according to Moroccan Energy 
Transition & Sustainable Development Ministry (4), significant investments in the development 
and strengthening of the Electric power transmission system have been made: 
 13.26 billion MAD (2009-2022), or nearly 1 billion MAD per year (vs. 0.6 
billion/year during the previous decade). 
 + 9,088 km of VHV and HV line, an increase of approximately 46%. 
 The total length of the lines reached, at the end of 2022, 28,663 km compared to 
19,575 km in 2008. 
 The total number of electrical transformation stations reached 292 stations 
(including 72 in the VHV/HV category) 
The acceleration of the energy transition presents several challenges for the Electric power 
transmission system, including: 
 Capacity limitations due to the rapid expansion of renewable energy sources. 
 Reduced flexibility and stability of the network, necessitating enhancements in 
storage, hydraulics, gas, interconnections, digitalization, and more. 
 The requirement for significant investments. 
 The need for competitive and transparent pricing, with tariffs for using the 
Electric power transmission system to enhance investor visibility. 
 The integration of new production and consumption sectors such as desalination, 
hydrogen, electric mobility, and the decarbonization of the economy. 
 The imperative to enhance network performance, including improvements in 
loss rates and power continuity, exemplified by reducing the System Average 
Interruption Duration Index. 
The measures being taken to address transport challenges; according to the Moroccan 
Energy Transition & Sustainable Development Ministry could be summarized as following:

Integration of renewables  
The integration of variable wind and solar PV generation into the Moroccan power system 
is supported by various flexibility sources: International interconnections, pumped storage 
hydropower (PSP), CSP with storage, and gas-fired generation. Morocco benefits from CSP 
technologies as well as hybrid CSP+PV plants as they are dispatchable and contribute to system 
flexibility. The first CSP project Ouarzazate (NOOR Ouarzazate I, II, II) see chapter 1.1.5, 
designed to have three hours of storage capacity, actively contributes to meeting peak demand. 
NOOR Ouarzazate II and III do have storage capacity of 7 hours. The Midelt project (Noor 
Midelt I, II, III) see chapter 1.1.6  will also be designed with a major portion of solar production 
with storage capabilities. Today, around 37% of the power mix -installed capacity- comes from 
variable renewable energy, and there are no major system integration concerns. 
However, with rising shares of variable renewables, notably wind power, system integration 
will grow in importance. Investment in grid expansion and demand side flexibility will have a 
significant part to play. Morocco also has plans to strengthen interconnections, notably with 
Spain and Portugal, and to construct an LNG terminal to expand flexibility from natural gas use 
in power generation (as well as to use it for industrial processes and in the urban residential 
sector in substitution of LPG). 
ONEE believes system integration will become a principal issue when the share of wind 
power in total generation has grown further. The main concern is the economics of more flexible

capacity from thermal plants (coal, natural gas) and the management of peak demand. Natural 
gas-fired power plants are planned under the Gas to Power project. 
The MTEDD (Moroccan Ministry of Energy Transition and Sustainable Development), 
ONEE and Masen are developing a detailed plan of the electricity mix by 2030, which will take 
into account the balance between all renewable and non-renewable energy sources (particularly 
natural gas) so as to ensure that the system has sufficient flexibility.

Organization of the electricity sector 
In Morocco, although the sectors of electricity generation and distribution have been 
opened to private investment, the country still lacks a structured wholesale electricity market. 
The national electricity utility, ONEE, essentially operates as the exclusive buyer and seller of 
electricity, without any financial exchange of electricity trades happening outside its purview. 
The regulations governing the trading of surplus electricity, which are anticipated under the 
self-consumption legislation and Law 13-09, are expected to be defined by the ANRE during 
2024. 
Figure 7 shows how the electricity sector is currently organized in Morocco. 
 
 
Figure 7: Current organization of the electricity sector in Morocco. 
Today, one of the challenges of this organization of the electricity market in Morocco is 
that ONEE continues to supply electricity to MT (Medium Voltage) and HT (High Voltage) 
customers at regulated prices (subsidized by the state), resulting in a loss of potential revenue 
for the open market, which was created to foster competition and allow the Moroccan industry 
to benefit from the downward trend in prices of renewable energies.

Policy 
 National Energy Strategy 
In 2009, Morocco adopted an energy strategy, based essentially on renewable energies, the 
development of energy efficiency and the strengthening of regional integration. 
This strategy, which was broken down into programs with precise objectives and 
accompanied by targeted legislative and institutional reforms, allowed Morocco to become a 
country producing energy from renewable sources, and to improve the energy dependence ratio 
which has passed. from 97.5% in 2009 to 90.51% in 2019 (4) while it was totally dependent on 
abroad for the satisfaction of its fossil energy needs as indicated in chapter 1.1.1. 
The 2009 National Energy Strategy planned to develop renewable electricity so that it 
represented 42% of installed capacity in 2020, this figure was revised upwards in 2015, with a 
target of 52% by 2030 (4). Today, Morocco even wants to exceed the 52% mark. 
It is important to note that this energy strategy is part of the country's efforts to honor its 
nationally determined contributions (NDCs) (9), namely the unconditional target is now set to 
18.3% emissions reduction by 2030 compared to business as usual, while the conditional target 
has been increased to 45.5% reduction by 2030 compared to business as usual. 
The technological choice to bet on renewable energies becomes almost obvious for a 
country which depends on imports from abroad and wishes to reduce its dependence on the 
availability and volatility of fossil fuel prices on international markets; The graphs below in 
Figure 8 & Figure 9 give us a clear idea of the downward trend in the Levelized Cost of 
Electricity from renewable sources compared to fossil fuel sources:

 Energy Efficiency:  
Regarding Energy Efficiency, the National Strategy for 2030 (5), which aims to achieve 
energy savings of around 20% by 2030 (11), is encapsulated in five (5) strategic orientations. 
These are executed through actions aimed at the energy-intensive sectors of the Kingdom, 
namely industry, transport, construction, agriculture, maritime fishing, and public lighting. 
The 5 strategic directions are: 
 Compliance with the fundamentals of energy efficiency through any new 
investment 
 Integration of the obligation to respect energy efficiency in all public 
expenditure and all projects benefiting from State support. 
 Structuring and professionalization of the energy efficiency sector 
 Positioning energy efficiency at the heart of the issues and concerns of 
professionals and citizens 
 Mobilization of resources and improvement of coordination for the development 
of energy efficiency 
- 
Green Hydrogen Initiative:  
In 2021, Morocco's Ministry of Energy, Mines, and Environment laid out a green hydrogen 
initiative under the 2019-established National Hydrogen Commission, aiming for a demand of 
30 TWh by 2030 and 307 TWh by 2050. This ambition requires 2GW from renewable sources. 
Centered on three main areas, the strategy involves market and demand improvements, 
technological advancements for cost efficiency and integration into the local industry, and 
investment in financing and cluster formation. Morocco's renewable energy framework and its 
strategic location near Europe enhance its prospects in the green hydrogen sector. 
 
 
==PAGE:17==
15 
 
The implementation is divided into three stages: 
 From 2020 to 2030: Initial phase focusing on domestic utilization and exports 
via pilot projects, despite initial high costs. 
 From 2030 to 2040: Aiming to decrease production costs and incorporate green 
hydrogen in electricity generation. 
 From 2040 to 2050: Broadening applications to include residential heating and 
urban transport, thereby strengthening the global viability of green hydrogen. 
Additionally, the strategy prioritizes domestic ammonia production to reduce dependence 
on imports. 
 Decarbonization of national industry:  
On October 1st, 2023, the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), also 
known as the carbon tax, was implemented to support the transition to a green economy. While 
initially in a transitional phase without taxation, it will become fully operational by January 1, 
2026. From then on, it will impose a carbon cost on goods exported to Europe. Morocco, with 
Europe as its main trading partner taking nearly 65% of its exports, is significantly affected. 
Key industries like automotive, aerospace, textiles, fertilizers, and food processing face a major 
competitiveness challenge for exports. 
Among other initiatives, the Ministry of Energy Transition has set up a program to provide 
all industrial zones in the Kingdom with renewable energy projects with a total capacity of more 
than 800 MW. 
 Integrated desalination plant program 
The development of seawater desalination plants in Morocco is part of an integrated 
national strategy. The goal is to synergize with Morocco's Green Hydrogen Program and the 
development of renewable energies for existing, ongoing, and planned desalination stations.

Moroccan Grid Code (CRENT) 
The latest version - the first common national version- of the Moroccan grid code (CRENT: 
National Electricity Transmission Network Code) (12) was published on December 20, 2021 
and entered into force on January 3, 2022, it is established by the National TSO (GRT) and 
subject to approval by the National Electricity Regulation Authority (ANRE). This CRENT sets 
out, in a non-discriminatory manner, (i) the technical requirements concerning the connection 
and access to the national electric power transmission system, including interconnections, (ii) 
the rules concerning the planning and operation of said network as well as (iii) the modalities 
for the exchange of data and collaboration between the TSO and all network users. 
The current grid code does not define Hybrid Power Plants and does not authorize 
overplanting. 
Regarding frequency balancing, the current law stipulates that the Transmission System 
Operator (TSO) assesses and determines annually the power of the balancing reserves necessary 
to ensure the security of the network, and each production unit ensures that it provides the power 
of the balancing reserves that it has undertaken to make available. If the TSO finds that the 
balancing capacities made available to it may not be sufficient, the TSO may (temporarily) 
request the production units to make one or more additional balancing capacities available. 
these services at a price determined according to a mechanism established by the TSO and 
approved by the ANRE. 
Then for the BlackStart service, the law stipulates that the TSO determines the means to be 
made available for the reconstitution of the network which are subject to mandatory provision. 
The current law therefore does not provide for compensation linked to ancillary services 
(frequency balancing, voltage and reactive power adjustments, the BlackStart service).

Power to X initiative: 
"Power to X" (PtX or P2X) refers to a technological process that converts electricity into a 
different form of energy, known as the "X-vector." This conversion can result in various energy 
types. For instance, when electricity is transformed into heat (referred to as Power to Heat), it 
can be used to fulfill industrial requirements or to supply heating networks. Alternatively, 
electricity can be converted into a synthesis gas (labeled as Power to Gas), such as hydrogen, 
which is often utilized for mobility. Another possibility is the production of methane, which can 
be injected into the gas network for diverse applications including industrial processes, heating 
solutions, or transportation needs. (https://infomineo.com/natural-resources-energy/power-to-
x/) 
Morocco, with its significant potential in renewable energy sources like solar and wind, and 
a strategic position bridging Europe and the rest of Africa, aims to become a key player in the 
green fuel sector, especially in producing green hydrogen. This ambition is part of the Power to 
X initiative. The Ministry of Energy forecasts that by 2030, Morocco could capture 2 to 4 
percent of the global green hydrogen market. Moreover, as one of the world's largest ammonia 
==PAGE:27==
25 
 
importers, Morocco is also focusing on becoming a leader in producing green ammonia, 
utilizing renewable energy sources. This move is directed towards meeting the long-term needs 
of both its domestic market and the export-oriented fertilizer industry. (trade.gov) 
Morocco, recognizing the potential in green ammonia, sees it as a key player in satisfying 
local fertilizer industry demands and tapping into international markets over time. Currently, 
Morocco relies heavily on ammonia imports, mainly from Ukraine, Trinidad & Tobago, and the 
USA, for producing phosphorus-based fertilizers. By transitioning to domestically produced 
green ammonia, Morocco could bolster its fertilizer manufacturing sector. 
From a production standpoint, generating 1 Mt (million tons) of green ammonia 
necessitates about 3 GW of power, aligning with Morocco's existing import volume. This shift 
to in-house ammonia production not only promises self-reliance for Morocco but also opens 
avenues to diversify its market outreach. Additionally, exporting clean ammonia aligns with 
environmental goals, as it could slash greenhouse gas emissions by approximately 95%, 
benefiting both Morocco and its trade partners. (21)

aims to power all its industrial facilities with green energy by 2027, utilizing wind, solar, 
hydroelectric, and cogeneration sources for electricity production. OCP intends to generate

2. Introduction to Hybrid Power Plants 
2.1.1. Definitions and Technical Configurations  
 
- 
Definition 
There is no universal definition of HPP, however we can cite some of the most used 
definitions:  
We can find a definition that dates to 2005 in (23), which suggests the HPP to be defined 
as a power system, using one renewable and one conventional energy source or more than one 
renewable with or without conventional energy sources, that works in “stand alone” or “grid 
connected” mode the following definition. 
WindEurope (24) defines the HPP as a power-generating facility that converts primary 
energy into electrical energy, and which consists of more than one power-generating modules 
connected to a network at one connection point. These might also include different forms of 
energy storage. Moreover, it is usual to find systems that combine renewable sources (wind, 
solar, hydro) with fossil fuels (e.g. diesel generators). 
The National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) (25) specifies HPP as power plants 
that contain two or more technologies that may potentially include wind turbines, solar PV, 
solar CSP, storage, geothermal power, hydropower, biomass, natural gas, oil, coal, or nuclear 
power. 
==PAGE:28==
26 
 
Berkeley Lab (26) reports cover “co-located hybrids” that pair two or more resources (e.g., 
multiple types of generation and/or generation with storage) that are operated largely 
independently behind a single point of interconnection, and “full hybrids” that also feature 
coordinated operations of the co-located resources. 
- 
Technical Configurations  
Regarding types and linkage, there are 3 configurations (27): 
The configuration named "Co-located" concerns the case of 2 (or more) technologies that 
are on the same site and share the same connection point to the network but without necessarily 
being linked for operations (see Figure 13). 
The configuration named "Full-Hybrid" covers the case where two (or more) technologies 
are located in the same site and share the same connection point and are linked in operations. 
The different technologies are completely integrated (see Figure 14).

2.1.2. Technical Advantages and Economic Benefits of HPPs 
 
The technological choice of HPPs can be the origin of several advantages, these advantages 
depend on the context of the project and its specific constraints (local network constraints, 
availability of the resource, land value, development costs, CAPEX of infrastructures, costs of 
maintenance, ...). 
The advantages cited above will not necessarily be applicable to all HPP projects. 
 
- 
The possibility to optimize the use of the network (24) by installing more capacity 
than the authorized one in the connection agreement, what it is translated into grid 
development savings, maximizing the use of the existing network, and solving the 
bottleneck problem in many areas or countries where new grid deployments are not 
allowed. Although the installed capacity at the connection point increases, the 
maximum evacuation capacity remains the same, so the impact on the network is 
limited. Based on my experience in the field, this objective is very relevant for 
certain areas in Morocco where the network capacity is limited, the southern 
provinces are a good example; some projects were curtailed for some time before 
they could evacuate the maximum of their installed capacity, and the authorization 
of most projects in this area was often conditioned by fairly heavy investment in 
network reinforcement. 
- 
The southern network requires an upgrade to be able to evacuate the power of 
emerging renewable projects to the north where the main consumption centers are 
based. developing hybrid projects in these areas - with large wind and solar 
resources - can optimize the use of the network and strengthen the development of 
renewable assets. 
- 
When wind and solar resources are complementing (e.g. negatively correlated) at 
the site (illustration in Figure 16)  higher yearly capacity factor, more stable 
power output over time with less ramping issues and instantaneous peaks than pure 
wind or solar plants   the possibility for partially scheduled power dispatch  
eased fulfilment of system- , site- and owner-specific power production 
requirements (e.g. load following production, flatter power generation profile over 
day / season / year);

Ancillary services: storage technologies (29), can be utilized to address the grid 
balancing problems and enable to developers and plant owners to have more 
participation in the electricity markets, this participation can ensure new revenue 
streams depending on the markets, which is an incentive for HPPs and renewable 
projects in general. 
- 
Even when wind and solar resources are not correlated, HPPs can be advantageous 
in terms of dispatching power in the market. For example, if electricity market prices 
are negatively correlated to wind power (due to high share of wind), the HPP can 
potentially earn revenue from the market, when prices are high, thanks to the solar 
resource. 
- 
One single grid connection point needs to be set up in most cases  reduced

- 
CAPEX reduction: In (30) it is determined that “at least 20% overcapacity of wind

connection can be saved by aggregating wind and PV power plants.

OPEX reduction: For PV systems and WTGs similar power electronic maintenance 
expertise is required and can be performed by the same technicians and hence reduce 
the OPEX cost of two separate maintenance teams. 
- 
Land usage efficiency: The energy output per surface of land used is increased with 
HPP, mine clearance is necessary in certain areas of Morocco before the start of

capacity and evacuate more energy by opting for HPP.

Reduced subsequent network use tariffs paid by grid end-users.

3.2. Examining New Emerging Policies in ‘’New Adopter’’ Countries 
 
New national policies and regulations designed to encourage and integrate Hybrid Power 
Plant (HPP) configurations are indeed emerging, but they vary from country to country and 
from market to market. This variation depends on each country's progress in energy transition, 
their energy mixes, and their specific energy needs. Below, examples of regulations and policies 
from various countries are provided to benchmark different approaches for integrating HPPs 
into the energy landscape across different global contexts. 
The Australian National Electricity Market (NEM) was originally designed to be powered 
by a few large coal power plants, with the generated energy then distributed to major load 
centers, including main cities and mining areas. However, this setup faced challenges with the 
rapid deployment of renewable energy sources. The existing transmission system struggled to 
handle the new capacity requirements, leading to significant curtailment of generation. To 
address this issue, many developers, began to explore the integration of Battery Energy Storage 
Systems (BESS) near solar farms. This approach aims to mitigate generation curtailment by 
allowing for energy storage during periods when it would otherwise be curtailed. 
Recently, the Australian Energy Market Commission (AEMC) which acts as the rule maker 
for Australian electricity and gas markets, has introduced a new rule for Primary Frequency 
Response (PFR) incentive arrangements. The AEMC Information note says: ‘’ The AEMC has 
made a final determination and a final rule to introduce new incentive arrangements that will 
reward generation and load that help to control power system frequency. The new 
incentive arrangements will complement the existing obligation for large generators to support 
the secure operation of the power system by responding automatically to changes in power 
system frequency.’’ 
 
 
==PAGE:37==
35 
 
The core elements of the final rule are (34): 
- 
Continuation of Mandatory PFR: The rule confirms that all scheduled and semi-
scheduled generators will continue to automatically respond to power system 
frequency changes beyond June 4, 2023. 
- 
Double-Sided Frequency Performance Payments: This new process, starting 
June 8, 2025, rewards generation and load behaviors that help control power 
system frequency. It values frequency response from any eligible unit with 
appropriate metering. 
- 
Reporting Obligations: AEMO and AER have new reporting duties regarding 
aggregate frequency responsiveness and frequency performance payment costs. 
AEMC believes this final rule will encourage more efficient operation of power plants 
along with innovation and investment in new frequency control capability. 
New policy updates in the USA have emerged during 2023 to encourage HPP 
configurations; I am referring here to FERC Order No. 2023 (35) issued by the Federal Energy 
Regulatory Commission (FERC): 
- 
Requires transmission providers to allow more than one generating facility—or 
storage resource—to co-locate on a shared site behind a single point of 
interconnection and share a single interconnection request. 
- 
Allows interconnection customers to add a resource to an existing interconnection 
request under certain circumstances, without that addition being deemed a “material 
modification” that would push the modified application to the “back of the queue.”

3.3. Comparative Analysis of HPP Main Functions/Battery Use Cases 
 
There are different functions/use cases of the battery; these functions vary depending on 
the initial motivation of the HPP, the technological configuration of the HPP (Solar, Wind), the 
network requirements (Grid Code) as well as the structure of the energy sales price (Spot 
market, PPA, ...); these different functions are mentioned below: 
Arbitrage: In power plants, arbitrage involves strategically storing electricity generated 
during periods of low demand or high renewable production, utilizing battery systems. This 
stored electricity is later injected to the grid at peak times or when energy price is higher, taking 
benefit on the fluctuation in energy prices to maximize revenue. 
Peak Shaving: Peak shaving in power plants utilizes battery storage to reduce the load 
drawn from the grid during peak demand hours. This strategy helps power plants lower 
operational costs by avoiding high demand charges and minimizes the need for activating less 
efficient peak-time generators, thus enhancing overall energy management.  
==PAGE:39==
37 
 
Grid services: Power plants use battery storage to provide essential grid services, such as 
frequency regulation, voltage control, and emergency backup. These services support the 
stability and efficiency of the electrical grid, ensuring reliable power supply and quality while 
accommodating fluctuations in demand and supply dynamically.  
Renewable firming/Curtailment mitigation: Renewable firming at power plants involves 
using batteries to smooth out the variability in power output from renewable sources like wind 
and solar, ensuring a consistent and reliable power flow to the grid. Curtailment mitigation 
allows power plants to store excess generation from renewables, reducing the need to throttle 
down production when production exceeds grid capacity or demand. 
Backup: For power plants, backup power involves deploying battery storage systems to 
maintain electricity supply during grid disruptions or failures. This capability is crucial for 
ensuring operational continuity, particularly in critical facilities and areas where consistent 
power availability is essential for safety and economic activities. 
The example of USA (given the HPP track record + data availability) is an interesting case 
study to get an idea of the major trends in storage usage as well as the trend of new projects 
coming into the pipeline. I will refer mainly to USA market in what follows to illustrate these 
trends. 
In the United States, self-reported data from operators to the EIA (33) is a good illustration 
of how different uses are distributed and which use cases are most common, while knowing 
that individual power plants often serve multiple purposes (See Figure 19) 
- 
The most frequently reported battery use case is grid services, with renewable 
firming and curtailment mitigation being especially significant in PV+Storage 
hybrids. 
- 
Wind+Storage installations often aim at grid service markets. 
- 
PV+Storage is commonly utilized to stabilize PV capacity for resource adequacy. 
- 
Backup power and energy arbitrage are the less commonly reported use cases by 
operators. 
It is anticipated that both Arbitrage & Renewable firming/Curtailment mitigation will be 
important use cases, as discussed in chapter 4.

4. Case Study & Analysis 
4.1. Methodology 
 
The case study’s objective is to assess different Hybrid configurations/sizing in two sites in 
Morocco (Midelt Region and Boujdour Region). Both regions are known for their interesting 
solar and wind resources. in terms of wind resources, Boujdour comes first with wind speeds 
around 10m/s, while the average speed in Midelt is around (7-8m/s). In general, the southern 
regions of Morocco are known for their very interesting renewable resources. 
The Python code used in this optimization sizing was provided by DTU for the HPP 
optimized sizing and analysis. The code’s optimization algorithm, based on the input, looks for 
the optimal sizing (Wind, Solar, Storage) to maximize Net Present Value (NPV). Then I have 
extended the initial code provided by DTU to generate the plots of chapters 4.2 and 4.3. 
The data needed to perform the simulations on the python code are mainly: solar data, wind 
data, energy sales tariffs, and Capex and Opex for the three technologies: PV, Wind, and Storage 
(BESS). 
The Solar and Wind data are generated by the CorRes tool; a tool to simulate wind and solar 
generation time series, developed at DTU Wind Energy (37). I generated the data on both sites 
1 and 2 for the same period from 01/09/2018 to 31/10/2019. For the energy sales tariff, I made 
an estimate based on local market practices (local developers' tariffs in Morocco) based on my 
experience in the field. 
The Capex and Opex data are based on data provided by DTU and corrected/modified based 
on my experience of the field.

4.3.1. Discount Factor 
 
The 1st Hybrid configurations simulation is done thought an analysis of 3 Discount Factors 
(7%, 10% and 12%). The results are reported for both sites in the following tables (see Table 
11 and Table 12). 
The first observation to make is that the algorithm proposes a sizing that overplants the 
Hybrid plant and those for both sites and for the different discount factors 
(Wind+Solar+Storage>Grid capacity). This can often seem counterintuitive that an 
overplanting sizing can also be an economically viable solution. The resulted sizing would have 
been different with a different algorithm’s objective function; I would expect less overplanting 
with NPV/CAPEX as the objective function. 
The algorithm significantly prioritizes wind at Site 1 due to the high wind production, while 
rather solar which is prioritized at Site 2. 
Storage is only proposed in one case (Site 2 at Discount Rate of 7%), this can be explained 
by two factors: the first is that the investment on batteries does not favor the maximization of 
the NPV within the given grid constraints and discount factors. hence the interest in studying 
simulations with discounts on the cost (Capex) of the batteries, the second is the difference 
between the wind energy of the two sites (Wind Capacity Factor calculated equal to 60% and 
26% respectively to Site 1 and Site 2); the profile of the energy produced by site 1 will be 
relatively flatter during the hours of the day. 
The Discount Rate significantly impacts the ''motivation'' to invest as the NPV of the HPP 
decreases with higher discount factors showing the diminishing returns as the cost of capital 
increases.

5. Conclusions 
5.1. Summarizing the Thesis Questions and Objective 
 
HPP (Hybrid Power Plants) is an emerging technological configuration that presents a 
solution for some of the main challenges of renewable energies, such as intermittency, difficult 
predictability, production lag with consumption (non-dispatchable), and the need for ancillary 
services on the electric power transmission system. Some countries have taken initiatives to 
promote the integration of HPPs and are now ahead in terms of policies and regulatory 
frameworks. Morocco, firstly, has very interesting renewable energy resources, and secondly, 
is a country with a clear roadmap to integrate more renewables into its energy mix and succeed 
in its energy transition, with the well-known objective of reaching 52% of its energy from 
renewable sources by 2030. 
Thus, the objective set for this thesis report was studying the potential of HPPs in Morocco. 
To achieve this, the report begins with a description of the Moroccan energy landscape with a 
focus on renewable energies, followed by an introduction to HPPs and their benefits. It then 
proceeds to a global benchmark of HPPs around the world (focusing on some main countries) 
to present the main achievements to date in terms of installed capacity and regulatory reforms 
and innovations. The case study of two sites in Morocco serves as a demonstration and 
verification of the economic feasibility and profitability of HPPs in Morocco. The questions 
that need to be answered were: Can HPPs become an economically profitable solution in the 
context of Morocco? What reforms and policies can promote HPPs in Morocco? Will HPPs be 
interesting in all regions or only in specific regions in Morocco? 
 
 
==PAGE:54==
52 
 
5.2. Results and Findings 
 
From the benchmark review above, some key ﬁndings emerge: HPP is a serious option for 
a better integration of renewable energies in the energy mix; and many countries have made 
reforms to encourage developers to move towards this technological choice, as demonstrated 
in the benchmark by the regulatory reforms in the United States and Germany, the new incentive 
arrangements in Australia, and the RTC tenders in India. More and more large HPPs are 
developed and built around the world. 
The first data analysis of the case study has showed varying profiles between wind and 
solar in both sites (1 and 2), an observation that can be generalized to many regions in Morocco, 
given that the two sites are representative of the renewable energy potential in Morocco, 
especially in sites with high wind potential as observed in the data at site 1.  
Related to storage, the case study also has confirmed that the impact of integrating storage 
in these high wind potential sites is not always obvious, particularly due to the absence of 
compensation with ancillary services that can improve revenue streams. In such cases, storage 
only becomes attractive with a significant battery discount. 
Furthermore, the case study demonstrated that overplanting makes the hybrid power plant 
(HPP) production flatter and increases revenues. Overplanting is a profitable sizing solution, 
especially in sites with a "non-high" capacity factor as seen in the simulations at Site 2. In these 
sites, despite overplanting, the percentage of time where the HPP can produce more than the 
grid capacity remains limited. Hence, the interest in overplanting to increase production 
(revenues) and improve the operation of the electrical infrastructure. 
Finally, the case study illustrated how the use of batteries for "arbitrage" improves the 
economic profitability of the project. The case study demonstrated that the integration of storage 
in the sizing improved the NPV (Net Present Value) of the HPP, even though the price of the 
battery is an important criterion in the technological choice, especially when the developer is 
not rewarded for ancillary services. 
5.3. Suggestions for Future Work 
 
Considering the results drawn from this study; I would propose the following research 
topics: 
- 
The study case was based on the objective function ‘’maximizing NPV; it would be 
interesting to simulate optimized sizing based on other objective functions as 
“’maximizing NPV/CAPEX” and “’minimizing LCOE”, as developers, investors 
and planning authority might be interested by other objectives than maximizing the 
NPV. 
- 
A detailed study of the Moroccan legal framework to assess the specific "reforms" 
adapted to the context and needs of the country to motivate/encourage HPPs would 
be of great use to the stakeholders concerned in Morocco.  
- 
Another sensitivity analysis that would be interesting is to simulate HPPs with 
different energy prices; projects can be developed with relatively low energy prices 
in regions with great wind potential (example of the southern regions of Morocco). 
==PAGE:55==
53 
 
- 
Southern Morocco is known for its very interesting Solar+Wind resources, today the 
development of renewable projects in these regions is ''limited'' by the constraints of 
the transmission network. It would therefore be interesting to study how HPPs can 
ensure that more energy is evacuated with the same network structure, and under 
what conditions for developers and planning authorities. 
6. Recommendations 
 
In Morocco and to date, among the Hybrid configurations, only the PV+BESS 
configuration is considered in the recent MASEN tenders; HPPs - as detailed enough in this 
report - can provide answers and solutions to the challenges that slow down/limit or at least 
delay the integration of renewable energies in the energy mix; there is therefore opportunity in 
considering more in the reflection of the planning authorities the hybrid technological choices 
(Wind+Solar; Wind+Solar+BESS; Wind+BESS).  
Compensation for ancillary services will undoubtedly increase developers' appetite to 
invest and innovate, ultimately helping Morocco succeed in its commitment to renewable 
energies, advance its energy transition, and reduce its dependence to fossil fuels. It remains to 
be seen how these compensations will be financed in the future. 
Overplanting might be a solution (among others) to address the constraints of the 
transmission system in southern Morocco. Given the need to evacuate energy produced in the 
south (where there are abundant renewable resources) to the north (where the major 
consumption centers are located) it would be beneficial for the planning authorities to consider 
these options in their reflections for future planning.
