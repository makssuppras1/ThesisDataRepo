Abstract
The need for ever faster and more powerful integrated electronic devices has let to a
new cross disciplinary field of research were 2D materials, electronics and photonics
come together to create novel approaches to fabrication and functionality of nano
scale integrated devices. The work of this thesis seek to combine the properties
and methods of 2D material heterostructures and photonics to create a novel float-
ing gate field effect transistor. Earlier work have reported the use of photonics in
combination 2D material devices for novel applications. However, in this thesis the
attempt to operate a floating gate transistor with an ultra fast THz pulsed laser,
for creating devices with can perform at unprecedented speeds is made. By assem-
bling specific 2D materials, namely graphene and hexagonal boron nitride, into a
heterostructure and patterning it using E-beam lithography a micrometer scaled
floating gate device has been realised successfully.
The subsequent testing revealed a high performing graphene channel but also un-
expected behaviour in the transfer characteristics of the device. Satellite peaks on
each of the charge neutrality point appeared. Their source was investigated and was
found to be intrinsic to the device itself. These peaks have, to the knowledge of the
author, not previously been repported in literature and are extremely intriguing as
the results obtained here indicate that they posses a sort of memory effect which is
back gate voltage dependent.
This thesis also report measurements, showing a direct effect of the THz exposure
on the device. By exposing the device while simultaneously conducting a DC mea-
surement on it, drops or increases of the current in the channel of the device was
observed. In addition, the THz exposure proved to cause long term changes in the
device, suggesting charge transfer and changes in the doping level of the floating
gate. These changes were persistent over the course of about 14 hours which indi-
cates a long retention time for the device.
As fabrication of more functional devices was unsuccessful in the time frame of the
project, the results in this project stem from one sample only and should thus be
considered carefully. The reproduction of these results is naturally desired for this
project and should be pursued as the next step. For this purpose, extra reference
test structures was created on the same 2D heterostructure in each device, in order
to confirm that the effects observed, was due to the floating gate layer. Other exper-
iments like varying the field strength of the THz pulses should also be carried out as
they would give more information on the mechanisms behind the transport of the
carriers in the device. Reproducing the results and expanding on the experimental
side of the project should provide more insight to the mechanism behind these novel
floating gate transistor devices and push the state of the art forward.

Introduction
Background
Since the invention of the solid state transistor by John Bardeen, Walter Brattain
and William Schockley in the late 1940’s[1] our world has drastically changed as
technology, and at the core computing power, has scaled with a pace that is hard to
comprehend. The goal of ever increasing power on smaller and smaller footprints has
led to the discovery of a host of new technologies but also new challenges which have
to be overcome. Following the invention of the solid state transistor, the metal-oxide-
semiconductor-field-effect-transistor (MOSFET) was introduced[2]. This device was
truly the start of a new regime as it was made of silicon and silicon oxide which
could be scaled to an unprecedented level compared to contemporary devices. It gave
birth to complementary metal oxide semiconductor (CMOS) technology[3] which is
the name for the fabrication processes used to make the billions of transistors we can
fit into a single chip today, of which the MOSFET is the most used type of transistor.
Back in those times (late 1940’s to early 1960’s) the idea for a memory device, based
on the MOSFET platform was also envisioned. It would take a small decade before
the first mention of a floating-gate-metal-oxide-semiconductor[4] would surface, thus
marking the beginning of CMOS-based memory technology.
Fast forward to the world of today, we are more connected than ever thanks to these
inventions. The amount data which is created, transferred and stored are increasing
by an astounding amount daily and proves to be a huge challenge for engineers. As
data transfer speed, storage capacity and energy consumption of devices has have
to be improved significantly to keep up with the demand, CMOS technology has,
so far, been the key. In this aspect, miniaturization has been the name of the game
since the invention of the solid state transistor. The more transistors you can fit
in a smaller amount of space, the more powerful your device will be. The progress
made in scaling down these technologies is in itself a true feat of human genius and
ingenuity as billions of transistors can be realised within the foot print of a postage
stamp today. However, in the last couple of decades it has been become clear that
this down-scaling can not continue. As the devices become smaller and smaller,
physical features in the transistor are on such a short length scale, that quantum
effects start to dominate over the classic behaviour needed for normal transistor
operation. Thus in recent years, there has been a large motivation to look elsewhere
for performance. That same motivation is what has driven the research for this very
masters thesis, more specifically the development of a novel floating gate field effect
transistor.

State of the art
The isolation and characterization of graphene, a carbon 2D material, by Andre
Geim and Konstantin Novoselov in 2000[5] (for which they got the Nobel prize)
gave rise to a new field of experimetnal research in 2D materials. Specifically the
combination of different types materials into van der Waals heterostructures[6] al-
lowed for a new way to tailor specific material properties for specific applications. 2D
van der Waals structures is therefore a hot topic when it comes to research in novel
field effect devices. The second ingredient, is the use of light to enhance performance
of these 2D heterostructure devices. The combination of electronics and photonics
has given rise to a relatively new research field, namely, Lightwave electronics[7].
Lightwave electronics seeks to symbiotically interface electronics and photonics in
the pursuit of enhancing performance of devices. State of the art research emphasise
on implementing photonic components at the chip level i.e. a CMOS compatible
level, to realise faster operation speed of logic units as well as lowering the energy
consumption significantly[8]. Combining 2D material heterostructures and lightwave
electronics in the framework of a floating gate field effect transistor and thus realise
an novel opto-electric device is the purpose of this thesis.

The place where literature seems to leave a door ajar in terms of creating novelty,
does not lie in the individual components but the sum of their parts. To the best of
the knowledge of the author of this thesis, at the time writing, utilising the ultra fast
nature of light to operate a 2D material floating gate field effect transistor by field
induced tunneling has yet to be attempted. Some work[9, 10, 11, 12] in literature
does use light to manipulate floating gate devices comprised of 2d material het-
erostructures, but does not use the potential of ultra fast operation. In the frame of
a memory device, which is one of the most common uses of floating gate transistors,
what is usually reported, is the use of standard electronic pulses for operation[13, 14]
who’s time scale is in the order of nano seconds. In the examples where light is used,
it is usually to induce doping or tunneling to the floating gate but on a very slow
time scale[11, 10], using high intensity, visible spectrum wavelengths. The work that
does utilize ultra fast pulsed light in combination with 2D heterostructures, do so
with the purpose of manipulating, sense or generate new photonic phenomena, like
terahertz detection or generation[15, 16]. The use of ultra fast pulsed light to oper-
ate floating gate transistors at the time scale of femto seconds, with the purpose of
utilizing the floating gate for logic operations, switching, memory applications etc.
is thus still unexplored territory.
What is proposed in this thesis is to make a floating gate field effect transistor device
which can be operated with THz pulses. Using a 2D heterostructure comprised of
graphene and hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) and irradiating it with a THz laser
pulse, the hypothesis is that the strong electric field associated with THz pulse will
cause charge carriers to tunnel through a thin tunnel barrier of hBN to the floating
gate. Due to the asymmetric nature of a THz single cycle pulse, the electric field
associated with the pulse should only be big enough for the carrier to tunnel in one
direction. Thus the transport to and from the floating gate can be controlled by the
polarization of the THz pulse (See fig. 2 for an illustration).

In practical terms, multiple floating gate 2D heterostructures will be assembled
and characterized electrically, using standard electrical characterization methods.
Following characterization of the devices, they will be put into an optical setup where

Theory
The purpose of this chapter is to introduce theoretical concepts, which are to be
utilized in design and fabrication of devices as well as for experimental methodologies

1.1
Properties of 2D materials
For a floating gate device, the electric properties of materials that make up the
device is of the utmost importance. However the mechanical material properties are
also necessary to consider, as they have multiple implications on the performance of
a device as well as when assembling 2D heterostructures[17]. These are everything
from enhanced electronic properties, such as higher carrier mobilities in graphene[18]
to considerations on different adhesion forces between different 2D materials during
fabrication of heterostructures. The following sections will cover the electric and
mechanical properties of the materials in the heterostructure.
1.1.1
Graphene
As mentioned in the introduction, graphene has been used extensively in research
due to exceptional electric and mechanical properties. Graphene consist of carbon
atoms arranged in a two-dimensional hexagonal lattice which leads to a unique elec-
tronic structure. Each carbon atom has four valence electrons for which three of
the electrons form so-called sp2-hybridized orbitals which are located in the plane of
the lattice. The orbitals form strong in-plane atomic covalent bonds, also called σ-
bonds that lead to very high mechanical strength[19]. The last valence electron, the
π-electron, has two pz-orbitals facing out of the plane of the lattice on each side. Be-
cause this orbital is separated from the other orbitals, electrons associated with the
pz-orbitals can move from atom to atom in the lattice. This kind of electron trans-
port can f.ex. be modeled as nearest and next-nearest neighbour interaction using
a Tight Binding model[20]. Thus the π-electrons dominates the electron transport

1.1.2
Hexagonal Boron Nitride
Hexagonal Boron Nitride is a wide band gap material, with similar crystal structure
to graphene as its atom are also aranged in a hexagonal lattice. It has grown very
popular for use in 2D heterostrutures as dielectric, especially in combination with
graphene. With a band gap of ∼5.97 eV[18] it is suitable as an electric insulator in
2D heterostructures for electronic applications. Because multi-layer hBN is close to
atomically flat[18], it allows for graphene to conform to the surface of hBN crystal,

making it close to atomically flat as well. This reduces out of plane wrinkles and
defects, effectively enhancing the electric properties of graphene[22]. Additionally
hBN works great as a protective layer for heterostructure devices as it is relatively
inert and thereby keeps contaminants away from the more sensitive layers within
the stack of 2D materials and prevents oxidation of the layers[22].
1.2
Electric properties and characterization of 2D materials
Throughout literature on the electric properties of 2D materials, some basic con-
cepts and parameters are usually always referenced as they provide a benchmark
for the performance of the devices. These include, but are not exclusive to: The
carrier concentration n, the mobility µ, resistivity/conductivity ( ρ, σ), resistance/-
conductance (R, G). The following sections will go through the description of these
parameters and how they can be obtained and applied to provide information about
the electric performance of 2D heterostructure devices.

Another very useful way to get information about the sheet resistance in a 2D

with additional calculations, samples of irregular shapes can also be measured with
the van der Pauw method. There are three conditions that needs to be met for a
van der Pauw measurement to be valid:
1. The contacts to the sample must be at the perimeter and they must be small
relative to the size of the sample
2. The thickness of the sample and the conductivity must be uniform
3. The sample must be continuous i.e. it must not contain isolated holes
Besides the uniformity of the conductivity, the three conditions can be met rela-
tively easy for mechanically exfoliated 2D materials and devices, which are the type
of materials, used throughout this thesis.

2.1
Design
Even though multiple variants of devices have been fabricated, they have all been
assembled using the same approach. Due the attractive van der Waals (vdW) forces
between the layers of certain 2D materials, they can be stacked on top of each
other to form a heterostructure.
This allows for complete customization of the
properties of the heterostructure device[28].
The approach is therefore to stack
different 2D materials, one at a time, until the heterostructure is comprised of the
desired functional layers.
2.2
Process overview
The process used to assemble the devices is a called the “Dry transfer method”.
It is based on picking up one crystal, using a crystal picked up in a previous step.
That way, the interfaces between the crystals will never touch anything else than
the surface of the other crystal, producing very clean interfaces in the stack. When
the desired layers have been assembled in the stack, it can be dropped down on a
substrate. Theentire process of making the deivce can roughly be broken into the
following parts:
1. Flake exfoliation
2. Locating suitable crystal flakes
3. Stacking the crystals into heterostructure
4. Defining device area and designing electrode contacts
5. Lithography processing

piece of graphene using the stamp, now containing a hBN crystal, a temperature of
40 ◦C was used (fig. 2.6, 4-7). Due to the vdW forces between the hBN crystal and
graphene, the graphene crystal will tear off along the edges of the hBN crystal when
picked up, leaving behind the rest of the graphene on the substrate and covering the
hBN completely in the overlap between the graphene and the hBN crystal. As the
heterostructure only contains two different 2D materials, namely graphene and hBN,
layered alternately, the process described above is just repeated for every layer until
the desired heterostructure has been achieved (fig. 2.6, 8-10). When all the desired
layers have been assembled, the heterostructure is placed on a special substrate
where gold electrodes with pads have been defined in another lithography process.
The heterostructure is placed in the middle of the pattern, marked by four cross
hairs. The temperature is raised to 120 ◦C in order to melt the PPC and PDMS.
While in contact with the heterotructure, the polymer melts and the stamp is slowly
lifted away from the substrate leaving some of the melted polymer on the surface
of the substrate. The melted polymer will act as a protective layer until further
processing (fig. 2.6, 11).

E-beam exposure
Once coated in resist, the sample is ready to be exposed, using E-beam lithography.
The reason for using E-beam lithography come to rise mainly due to two reasons:
Firstly, that the actual area of the heterostructure is determined by the sizes of
flakes it is made of. It is hard to find very big flakes during the exfoliation and
keep them in one piece during the stacking process. Therefore, the final size for
devices made in this project, is typically in the area of approximately 20 to 40µm2.
This means that the contacts have to be very small to fit as many as possible along
the edge of the device. The distance between the contacts can be in the order of
<200 nm to make sure no shorts occur. The wires themselves are ∼1 µm. E-beam
lithography can define patterns with very high precision and very small feature
size and that is why it is used for this process[31]. The other reason is that every
assembled heterostructure is going to be in a different shape and size due to the
irregular nature of the exfoliated crystals. Using standard UV-lithography would
not work as a predefined mask would have to be used, restricting the pattern shape
and size. By using E-beam lithography one can define any pattern on the surface
of the heterostructure for lithography and expose multiple heterostructures with
different patterns in one batch. The specific tool used to make E-beam exposure
is an Elionix ELS-125 E-beam Lithography System. Some key parameters of this
system are:
- 5 nm line width at 125 kV
- 1.7 nm diameter & minimum proximity effect at 125 kV
- 2 nm beam diameter at 1 nA beam current

As the aim of the thesis is develop an optically operated floating gate FET, the

methodologies within physics: Optics (Photonics) and classic electronic character-

The following chapter will go into the differ-

them with the purpose of uncovering the opto-electronic properties of novel 2D
heterostructure devices.
3.1
Raman spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy uses in elastically scattered photons for characterization of dif-
ferent materials. A laser excites states in the material to a virtual energy state and
when the states returns from the virtual state is has been excited to, a photon is
released. If the photon returns to a different level than what it came from originally
it will cause a shift in the peak of the spectrum. This is called Raman scattering.
The characteristic peaks obtained through Raman scattering are unique fingerprints
based on the vibrational modes of the molecule being hit by the laser. By knowing
the energy of the molecular modes in the material being characterized, they can be
matched with the Raman spectrum and identified.
Practically a Raman measurement is very simple. The sample to be measured is
placed inside the Raman spectrometer. Using a microscope attached to the spec-
trometer, the area of interest on the sample is located. The measurements is started
and the laser turns on triggering the Raman scattering process. After that a Raman
is spectrum is read out form the machines and the sample ahs been characterized.
In this work, a Thermo Fisher DXR3 Raman Microcope was used for characterizing
the samples.

The reason for making the custom holder was, partly, to make sure maximum in-
teraction between the beam and sample while maximum the electric field across the
tunnel barrier. As mentioned in section 1.4 Fowler-Nordheim tunneling allows for
tunneling through a barrier when an electric field is present across the barrier. As
the electric field is perpendicular to the wave vector, the electric field component
across the tunnel barrier is largest when the beam is incident on the cross section of
the device. However, the interaction area between the beam and the sample also has
to be considered[32]. The spot size of the beam is in the order of ∼60 µm2 and the
cross sectional area of the device is ∼0.6 µm2 so the interaction area is very small if
the beam is incident to the cross section of the sample. That is why it was chosen
to make the holder at a fixed angle of 45◦as a compromise between maximizing the
interaction area while keeping the electric field component across the tunnel barrier
as large as possible. fig. 3.5 depicts the two situations described.

The optical setup, sketched depicted in fig. 3.6 is a high power laser setup for THz
generation and detection. This type of setup is sometimes called THz Air-Breakdown
Coherent Detection (THz-ABCD). It uses a four wave mixing (FWM) scheme to
generate THz pulses in an air plasma and Terahertz Field Induced Second Harmonic
generation (TFISH) for detection of the THz-radiation[33].
As the experiments
performed in this project seeks to utilize the electric field from the THz pulses, the
detection of the THz was only used to make sure the light was hitting the sample.
The following description will therefore only cover a leymans explanation of the THz
generation in the setup.
A femto second laser produces a 800 nm beam which goes into a beam splitter. The
beam splitter splits the beam into a “pump beam” and a “probe beam”, where
the pump beam will contain roughly 99% of the original power output from the
laser.
The pump beam is then focused through a lens into a β-barium borate
non-linear crystal (BBO). The crystal converts the wavelength of the light from
800 nm to its second harmonic at 400 nm. This process is called second harmonic
generation (SHG). After passing through a dual wavelength wave plate, which shifts
the polarization of the light, the two pulses are focused in to a single spot in the air,
effectively mixing the two wavelengths and forming a plasma. From this plasma the
THz pulses are generated. The beam the goes into a plexi glass box which is purged
with nitrogen in order to reduce water vapour as the THz beam is attenuated by
water vapour. The light is then collimated using off-axis parabolic mirrors and any
non-THz residuals from the plasma are filtered out using a silicon wafer. Finally
the THz beam hits the sample, sitting in the sample holder. The expected field
strength of the THz pulses incident on the sample is in the order of 1 to 3MV cm−1.

5.1
Satellite peaks
First seen in fig. 4.3a, the satellite peaks were not a result expected, as they are not
usually associated normal floating gate transistor characteristics. In order to verify
that this was not due to external factors in the measurement setup, the device was
tested again in another setup (fig. 4.6). The peaks were still there which points
towards it being a phenomena intrinsic to the device. Additionally the contact test
in fig. 4.9 also suggest that these peaks are dependent on the floating gate itself as
the position of the contacts (next to or across the floating gate) seemed to have an
effect on the magnitude of the peaks.
Using the data obtained, multiple attempts were made to find dependencies between
the satellite peaks and parameters which could be controlled. A first indication of a
parameter on which the peaks were dependent, were the gate voltage. More precisely,
the magnitude of the gate voltage sweep and its direction. The test conducted in
fig. 4.8 show that the magnitude of the gate voltage and the direction of the gate
sweep (forward/backward) dictates the position of the peaks. This could suggest
some kind of “memory effect” for which the position of the peaks are predetermined
by the magnitude and direction of the sweep. Another interesting observation is that
the position of the peaks (in relation to the gate voltage) seem to be independent
of the CNP of the device. They seem to be somewhat symmetrically placed around
a Vg of 0 V.
In an effort to uncover the mechanism behind this effect, the peak positions and
magnitudes were plotted against the magnitude of the gate voltage sweep in fig. 5.1.
The position of the peaks (relative to gate voltage) seem to be linear from gate
voltage magnitudes of ±10 V and up and almost constant from ±5 V to ±1 V. In
terms of the magnitude, or their current values, the overall I-V characteristics seem
to have an effect. The peaks are smallest close to the CNP and the data normalized
to the current value of the CNP suggest that the peaks could be dependent on the
electron/hole mobility and carrier doping as the magnitude of the current values
looks to be higher for positive gate voltage values compared to negative values.

5.2
Effects of THz radiation on FGFET
This section will mainly discuss the effects observed in section 4.1.4 and section 4.1.5.
The distinctive split in the CNP of the device suggest two regions of different doping
in the current path. However, the source of this split is unclear.The results indicate
that the fundamental mechanism behind the effect might not be a field effect. The
conclusion is based on the fact that the field from the back gate and the field from
the THz radiation is comparable in magnitude. Considering the thickness of the
oxide, encapsulating hBN and tunnel barrier, the field strength is approximately in
the order of ∼1.835 × 106 V cm−1. The field strength of the THz is in the order of
∼1×106 V cm−1. The fact that fields are of the same magnitude, suggests that what
is observed in fig. 4.11 is not directly due to tunneling. If that was the case the split
in the CNP should disappear as soon as a back gate sweep is performed. On the
contrary it is observed that the CNP split persists for multiple back gate sweeps as
seen in fig. 4.12. Additionally the split was only observed after the THz exposure.
These observations makes it likely that the split in CNP is due to a photo effect, of
which tunneling could play a part.
5.2.1
FN-tunneling
The original hypothesis for the working principle of the device designed in this
project was that FN-tunneling would facilitate charge transport from the channel to
the floating gate in the presence of a strong electric field (here the THz pulses). The
results in fig. 4.10 and fig. 4.11 shows that THz radiation has both have immediate
and long term effects on the device. The drop in current in fig. 4.10 and the split of
the CNP after THz exposure could suggest that the THz field successfully facilitated
transport of charge in some manner. However it is not entirely clear where the charge
comes from and where it ends up.

5.2.2
photo-induced doping in hBN
As mentioned in the introduction to this section, the results indicate that some kind
of photo-induced effect could be involved in the process. Some literature[35, 36]
report that photo-induced excitation in defect states of hBN lead to a doping of
specific device regions. By shining a light on an area of the device and simultaneously
applying a back gate they can effectively shift and split the CNP of their device.
The latter which is also observed in the result reported here. They argue that the
doping effect occurs because occupied defect states in the hBN are excited by the
light and then transported to the graphene layer via the applied field from the back
gate. This leaves behind oppositely charged carriers in the hBN, screening the back
gate. Depending on whether the light from the laser covers the whole device or
not, this will lead to either a shift in the CNP or an induced hetero junction which
appears as a split in the CNP. This could also explain why the split in the CNP
persists as photo induced charge carriers are trapped in the defects in hBN and thus
not sensitive to back gate when the sample is not irradiated. The only problem with
this explanation is the energies involved in this process. In their report, they use a
laser energy of 2.33 eV, which they argue is high enough to excite defect states in the
hBN and graphene (2.8 eV and 2.6 eV for nitrogen vacancy and carbon impurities
respectively), in combination with a high intensity of 2 −4 mW. In this work the
laser energy is in the order of ∼4 meV@1 THz while the intensity is on the order of
0.3 mW. These energies are not high enough to excite the states mentioned in the
papers and so is in conflict with the hypothesis of photo induced doping.
However, qualitatively, the result might still be of use, if one assumes that the
split in the CNP in this work is due to a doping effect in the floating gate (or
the channel), caused by the THz irradiation. The mechanism by which the charge
carriers are excited/transported might not be the same but the idea that a hetero-
junction could arise from doping in the floating/channel by THz irradiation might
still be valid based on the observations made so far. Therefore, such a situation
was simulated for the device, the results of which will be discussed in the following
section.

5.4
Future experiments
To sum up the discussion an outlook on the future experiments of this project will
be given.
The immediate experiment which needs to be conducted is to do the same measure-
ments as presented, but with more/new devices. The reason for results only being
presented for one device in this work is because all the other devices made failed.
More elaborate tests was also planned for the one device that did work, which will
be discussed shortly. However, eventually the one working device also failed. It
is obvious that none of the results obtained so far have true value if they cannot
be reproduced. Thus new devices have to be fabricated and some with the same
structure as the device that worked, to attempt to reproduce the results obtained.
In addition, to verify that the results observed indeed is due to the floating gate,
a reference structure in the form of a hall bar (hBN encapsulated graphene) were
defined for the new devices. By having a hall bar adjacent to the floating gate, made
of the same heterostructure stack, one could probe both the floating gate device and
the hall bar simultaneously during THz measurements. By using two SMUs (one
for the floating gate and one for the hall bar) the current could be monitored on
both devices at the same time. What is hoped to be achieved is that the floating
gate will behave as reported in fig. 4.10 while the hall bar should exhibit a different
behaviour, which underline that the effects observed indeed is due to the floating
gate structure. In fig. 5.5 a picture of one of the new devices with an adjacent hall
bar (which unfortunately failed) can be seen.
Figure 5.5: Picture showing one the new device with an added Hall bar for reference

Another feature which was added to the new devices was contacts to the floating
gate. This may seem counter intuitive as the idea of the floating gate is that it is
not connected to anything. However, for the purpose of characterizing the device, it
can prove very useful. By having a contact to the floating gate one more accurately

From literature[34, 37, 38, 39, 40, 41]it is also known that the tunnel barrier thick-

Making devices with different tunnel barrier thicknesses and floating gate materials

Finally for the electrical characterization, pulsed measurements[42] in a probe sta-

Taking the outlook even further, one of the ultimate goals of the project is probe
the effect of the THz irradiation on its extremely short timescales. What happens
with the device at the instant the THz pulse hits the device, what are the effects
of field strength, polarization, incidence angle etc.? The prospect of being able to

the right methods. Examples of one of these method could be a THz time domain

other possible advantage of this measurement scheme is that the need for probing
the sample electrically with physical contacts is not strictly needed, reducing the risk
of having a faulty device. Additionally, THz pump-probe schemes[44] would allow
one to examine ultra fast phenomena which would otherwise be difficult/impossible
to capture with normal electrical measurement methods.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In conclusion the aim of demonstrating non-volatile charge transfer to a floating gate
via THz irradiation, was not fully achieved. Some type of non-volatile charge trans-
fer due to THz irradiation was partially demonstrated as the split in CNP indicates.
However the exact process by which this charge transfer occurred is still unknown.
Whether the charges actually got trapped in the floating gate or somewhere else is
not entirely clear, but the results obtained from measurements on the device, along
with the simulation results, indicate that the floating gate have an influence on the
system and that THz radiation possibly could change the doping in the floating
gate. Additionally, the time tests demonstrated long time scale charge retention.
This was demonstrated by the fact that the CNP split slowly merged back to a sin-
gle CNP over course of approximately 14 hours. Nevertheless, additional, tangible
evidence and experiments are required before reaching a definitive conclusion on the
mechanisms behind these effects.
New and unexpected phenomena also occurred in the from of satellite peaks. At-
tempts to find similar results in literature failed, however, a type of memory effect in
the satellite peaks was demonstrated in this project. The appearance and behaviour
of these satellite peaks are thus only partly understood. A lot of work during this
thesis went into the investigation of these phenomena to try and find answer. How-
ever, due to the fact that only one functioning device could be measured, some of
the tests needed to shed more light on the matter, could not be conducted. This
also means that the investigations on the field amplitude dependence, polarization
dependence and geometry of the structure, could not be done as well.
With all these partial, and mostly qualitative, conclusions to the aims of the the-
sis, the conclusion might seem non-satisfactory, but a lot of new knowledge was
acquired during this project. Lightwave electronics is still a relatively new field and
tasks that might seem trivial as individual processes can be very time consuming
when combining measurements and methods from two different branches of physics.
Assembling a team of two different backgrounds in physics to make a novel experi-
ment type work has been truly fruitful and a lot great experience has been gained
during this thesis. In terms of the development of THz operated floating gate FETs,
the results of this thesis has provided some fundamental results which could prove
useful for future studies. The most noteworthy is observation of a direct response
of the device to the THz radiation.
