2. Abstract 
Snakebite is recognized as one of the world’s most neglected tropical diseases by the World Health 
Organization. More than 5 million people are bitten each year leading to 400,000 amputations and 
more than 100,000 deaths. Animal-derived antivenoms are the only available treatment option. 
However, these medicines are largely unavailable, expensive, and associated with adverse effects due 
to their heterologous nature. The detrimental consequences of snakebite underline the need for a new 
generation of antivenoms. 
This thesis is part of a larger research project aiming at developing the world’s first recombinant 
antivenom based on an oligoclonal mixture of human antibodies. The approach is to discover human 
recombinant antibodies against the most important venom fractions from clinically relevant snakes 
and express these antibodies in mammalian cell cultures.  
In this project, phage display selection has been applied to discover human scFvs against toxins from 
Naja melanoleuca (Forest cobra) venom. First, snake venom was resolved in 33 fractions by RP-
HPLC. Fraction 1 (short neurotoxin), 8 (long neurotoxin), 9 (possibly three-finger toxin), and the 
combined fraction 18 and 19 (cytotoxin and phospholipase A2) were identified as being among those 
of highest medical relevance. The venom fractions were biotinylated and utilized in phage display 
selections to discover toxin-targeting antibodies. Evaluation of the selection outputs was performed 
through a plate test, which revealed that the selections were unfortunately unsuccessful. Although 
selections against selected antigens were repeated several times with different conditions and 
attempted error correction, successful selections were never obtained in this master thesis project. 
Instead, previous selection outputs against N. melanoleuca venom fractions were used for subcloning 
and assessment of scFvs. Subcloning of antibody DNA and transformation into competent BL21 E. 
coli was successfully performed for selection outputs against fractions 1, 8, and 18. The binding of 
these scFvs to their cognate toxins was evaluated by monoclonal scFv ELISA. Using this assay, 122 
scFv hits against fraction 8 and 13 hits against fraction 18 were obtained. However, no hits were 
found against fraction 1. Further tests are needed to fully elucidate (cross-)target specificity of the 
scFvs hits. Nonetheless, these results indicate the first successful discovery of human scFvs against 
two of the four most medically relevant venom fractions from N. melanoleuca. Moreover, they 
constitute the first successful subcloning and assessment experiments in the Tropical Pharmacology 
Lab, where functional scFv hits were identified. 
In the future, scFvs hits should be further investigated by ELISA including cross-reactivity studies, 
capture assays, and possibly Surface Plasmon Resonance experiments to estimate their binding 
characteristics in depth. In parallel, the most promising scFvs should be sequenced. Based on binding 
and sequence data, selected scFvs should be converted to human IgGs, expressed in mammalian cells, 
and tested for their neutralizing ability in vivo in mice.  
Overall, this project brings the development of a recombinant human oligoclonal antivenom against 
N. melanoleuca, and possibly cobras in general, one small step closer, as human scFv hits against 
fractions 8 and 18 have, for the first time, been obtained.

3. Resume 
Slangebid anses af World’s Health Organization som en af verdens mest negligerede tropesygdomme 
og rammer årligt mere end 5 millioner mennesker. Af disse ender 400.000 mennesker med 
amputerede lemmer og 100.000 mennesker dør. Modgift er den eneste behandlingsmetode og på trods 
af at den eksisterende modgift har reddet tusindvis af liv gennem tiden er behandlingen både dyr og 
associeret med mange bivirkninger, fordi den er baseret på antistoffer isoleret fra dyr. De forfærdelige 
konsekvenser slangebid har i mange samfund og den begrænsede mulighed for behandling gør 
slangebid til et stort problem, som skal løses ved en ny generation af modgifte. 
Dette speciale er en del af et større forskningsprojekt, som har til formål at udvikle verdens første 
modgift baseret på menneskelige antistoffer. Disse antistoffer skal have specificitet imod de mest 
medicinsk relevante toksiner fra klinisk relevante slanger og skal producers i CHO cellekulturer.  
I dette projekt er fag display selektion blevet brugt til at finde scFv antistoffragmenter med specificitet 
imod relevante toksiner isoleret fra Naja melanoleuca gift. Det første trin var, ved brug af RP-HPLC, 
at fraktionere slangegiften og isolere de relevante giftfraktioner, fraktion 1 (short neurotoxin), 8 (long 
neurotoxin), 9 (formodet three-finger toxin) samt 18 og 19 (cytotoxin og phospholipase A2) 
kombineret. Toksinerne blev først biotinyleret og så brugt som antigener i fag display selektioner. 
Selektionerne blev, ved brug af plate test, vurderet til at være mislykkedes og trods mange følgende 
selektionsforsøg lykkedes det aldrig.  
I stedet blev tidligere udførte selektioner mod fraktion 1, 8 og 18 fra N. melanoleuca gift benyttet til 
efterfølgende forsøg. Fra selektionerne blev scFv-generne isoleret og klonet ind i en vektor som så 
blev transformeret ind i kompetente E. coli celler. Denne kloning var succesfuld for selektioner mod 
fraktion 1, 8 og 18. Bindingen mellem de enkelte scFver og deres toksin targets blev vurderet gennem 
monoklonal ELISA. Dette forsøg pegede mod positive resultater for 122 scFver mod fraktion 8 og 
13 scFver mod fraktion 18. Ydereligere ELISA eksperimenter er nødvendige for at bekræfte 
specificiteten af scFverne til slangetoksinerne. Ikke desto mindre peger indikationer mod at der i dette 
projekt er fundet de første humane scFver med specificitet til to af de fire mest medicinsk relevante 
giftfraktioner i N. melanoleucas gift. Yderemere, er dette det første succesfulde forsøg på kloning og 
monoklonal ELISA fortaget i Tropical Pharmacology Lab.  
I fremtiden bør scFverne blive testet for deres evner til at krydsreagere med andre slangetoksiner. Der 
bør også udføres capture assays og Surface Plasmon Resonanse bør muligvis bruges til at analysere 
bindingen mellem antistofferne og deres targets grundigere. De mest lovende scFver bør sekventeres 
og baseret på sekventeringsresultater skal de bedste udvælges og konverteres til fuldt humane IgG 
antistoffer. IgGerne skal så produceres i CHO cellekulturer og testes for deres evne til at neutralisere 
N. melanoleuca toksiner in vivo i mus.  
Dette projekt har bragt målet om at udvikle verdens første humane modgift mod N. melanoleuca, og 
måske endda mod kobraer generelt, et lille skridt tættere, da humane scFver mod fraktion 8 og 18 er 
blevet opdaget for første gang nogensinde.

5. Project aim 
Snakebite envenoming is on the World Health Organization’s (WHO’s) list of the world’s 20 most 
neglected tropical diseases causing deaths and disfigurements in tropical parts of the world [1,2]. 
Currently, animal-derived antivenom is the only known effective treatment. This traditional 
antivenom has been produced in the same way for more than 120 years, and although it has saved 
many lives, the treatment is known to be associated with adverse effects that can be severe and in rare 
cases even fatal.  
The work conducted in this thesis is part of a bigger research project aiming at developing the world’s 
first human oligoclonal recombinant antivenom. The objective of this thesis is to discover human 
single chain variable fragments (scFvs) against the most medically relevant toxins in the venom of 
Naja melanoleuca. N. melanoleuca is an elapid snake that has been categorized as a category 1 snake 
of highest medical importance by WHO, due to its deadly bite.  
The initial step of this project is to isolate the medically relevant toxins from the complex venom of 
N. melanoleuca. Phage display selection is then used to discover human scFvs with high affinity to 
these toxins. The success of the selections is evaluated and the scFv-encoding genes are then 
subcloned into E. coli. The scFvs are expressed and their toxin binding abilities are then tested using 
ELISA. In future projects, these scFvs can be converted into fully human IgGs and tested for their 
neutralizing capabilities in vivo.  
Overall, this project aims to progress the discovery of human antibodies against the medically 
relevant toxins from N. melanoleuca venom to bring the first human oligoclonal recombinant 
antivenom one small step closer to saving people from the terrible consequences of snakebite 
envenoming.

6. Introduction 
6.1. Snakebite - one of the world’s most neglected tropical diseases 
Snakebite envenoming is a major public health problem affecting hundreds of thousands of people 
each year. The threat of snakebite is highest in the tropical and subtropical areas of the world with 
the most affected areas being rural as well as low socio-economic areas [1,3]. Snakebite was in mid 
2017 placed on WHOs list of the 20 most neglected tropical diseases due not only to the major impact 
that snakebite has in many societies, but also due to the very limited resources put into antivenom 
research [4]. 
While some estimations, suggest that venomous snakes cause 2.5 million bites, 250,000 sequelae, 
and 85,000 deaths each year [5] (Figure 1), others suggest closer to 5.4-5.5 million bites, 400,000 
amputations, and 20,000-138,000 deaths on an annual basis [6,7]. However, evidence from 
community-based surveys in some countries indicates that the actual toll is even higher than what is 
estimated based on statistics from hospital reports [8]. It is clear that snakebite envenoming is a major 
problem due to the deaths toll, but also because surviving victims are often left with disfigured or 
amputated limbs and as a consequence lose their ability to provide for their families.

6.2. Venomous snakes 
Venomous snakes are represented by 725 species, divided into four families: Viperidae, Elapidae, 
Atractaspididae and Colubridae [9]. The snake families responsible for the most severe cases of 
envenoming are Elapidae and Viperidae (Figure 2) [10]. The elapid snakes have small fixed fangs 
between 1 and 5 mm in length [11] whereas vipers have much longer retractable fangs allowing for 
penetration of the venom into the deeper tissues [12]. The most common clinical consequence of 
envenoming by elapid snakes is neurotoxicity [13], causing progressive descending paralysis, which 
becomes life-threatening when paralysis of the respiratory muscles occurs. Envenoming from a viper 
is most commonly associated with cytotoxicity, hemotoxicity, and sometimes myotoxicity [10]. The 
symptoms of a viper bite are swelling, blistering, hemorrhage, and necrosis of the skeletal muscles.  
6.2.1. Snake toxins  
Snake venom is a highly complex and diverse mixture of toxins [14]. Snake toxins are different both 
in their amino acid sequence, size, and conformation, and they also exert a wide range of effects. 
These effects can be neurotoxic, cytotoxic, hemotoxic, or myotoxic. In this section, the three groups 
of toxins found in highest abundance in N. melanoleuca venom will be described. 
Three-finger toxins (3FTxs) is a group of non-enzymatic toxins with a size of 60-74 amino acids 
[15]. The toxins contain up to five disulfide bridges, of which four are conserved within all 3FTxs 
[16]. The 3FTxs have a characteristic three-dimensional structure consisting of three b-stranded loops 
extending from a small, globular, hydrophobic core. Despite the common scaffold of the 3FTxs, they 
display a wide range of biological effects, both neurotoxic and cytotoxic.

a-neurotoxin is a well-known 3FTx that exhibits its neurotoxic effect through binding of muscle 
nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChR), thereby inhibiting acetylcholine binding. This impairs 
neuromuscular transmission causing respiratory arrest in the snakebite victim [17,18]. Two well-
known groups of a-neurotoxins are the type I and type II a-neurotoxins, also referred to as short and 
long neurotoxin, respectively. The short neurotoxins have a length of 60-62 amino acids whereas the 
long neurotoxins are 66-74 amino acids long [17]. Both neurotoxins target the same site of the 
nAChR, however, the toxins display quite different amino acid sequence [17]. This difference might 
explain why it seems that antibodies with cross-reactivity between long and short neurotoxins are 
difficult to obtain through immunization [19,20]. 
Cytotoxins are the second largest group of 3FTxs with a length of approximately 60 amino acids [21]. 
In contrast to the neurotoxins exerting neurotoxic effects the cytotoxins exert cytotoxic effects. 
Cytotoxins form pores in the lipid membrane of different cells, which causes disruption of the 
membrane, and as a result induce tissue necrosis [22].  
Phospholipases A2s (PLA2s) are enzymatic toxins that hydrolyze glycerophospholipids. The PLA2s 
are present in the venom of both elapids and vipers. The average size of PLA2s are 115-125 amino 
acids and they usually contain seven disulfide bonds, although some contain six or eight [11]. The 
PLA2s exert a wide range of biological effects including both myotoxicity, neurotoxicity, and 
cytotoxicity [23].  
Snake venom metalloproteinases (SVMPs) are a group of proteinases which are highly abundant in 
the venom of vipers, varying from 11% to more than 65% of the total protein content in the venom 
[24,25]. They vary in size from 20 to 100 kDa [26–28]. Many SVMPs act by degrading the 
components of the basement membranes underlying the capillary endothelial cells, which causes the 
disruption of blood vessel walls and allows for escape of the blood. SVMPs are the primary venom 
component responsible for local and systemic hemorrhage [29]. Other SVMPs act through disruption 
of the hemostasis mediated by pro- and anti-coagulant effects, resulting in either activation or 
inhibition of blood coagulation [30]. 
6.2.2. Naja melanoleuca 
N. melanoleuca, also referred to as the forest cobra, the black and white-lipped cobra, or the black 
cobra, is responsible for a considerable share of deadly bites and is therefore classified as a category 
1 snake of highest medical importance by the WHO (Figure 3) [31]. It is a highly venomous member 
of the elapids and is found in sub-Saharan Africa where it is the largest of the cobra species reaching 
up to 3.1 m in length. N. melanoleuca can deliver more than 1 g of venom per bite and the clinical 
manifestations of such a bite are progressive paralysis, which in severe cases can lead to paralysis of 
the respiratory muscles resulting in death unless mechanical ventilation is provided. Lauridsen et al., 
2016 [32] have published the only toxicovenomic study of the venom of N. melanoleuca. This study 
revealed the presence of 52 proteins in the venom, eluted in 33 fractions using reverse phase high 
performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) (Figure 4). Of the 33 fractions, four were identified 
as toxic at a dose of 0.53 mg/kg in a mouse lethality test. All toxins from the four fractions were 
identified as belonging to the group of 3FTxs.

Through immunoprofiling, three antivenoms have been shown to recognize N. melanoleuca venom 
toxins [32]. However, N. melanoleuca venom was only included in two of the three immunization 
mixtures of polyspecific antivenoms. These are VINS African and South African Vaccine Producers 
(SAVP). The third antivenom, VINS Central Africa, is produced based on immunization with venom 
from three vipers and Dendroaspis polylepis, suggesting cross-reactivity between D. polylepis and N. 
melanoleuca venoms. 
6.3. Traditional antivenom  
The world’s first antivenom against snakebite envenoming was in clinical use in 1895 and was 
invented by A. Calmette [33]. The antivenom consisted of the serum from hyper-immunized horses. 
Later, it was discovered that the toxin neutralizing ability of serum was due to the presence of 
antibodies. This lead to improved production, where further separation of the antibodies from the 
remaining serum was performed, and the antibodies were then used for treatment instead of crude 
serum. Very little has changed in the method of production of antivenom during the past 120 years 
since antivenom was invented [34] and antivenom is still the only effective treatment against 
snakebite envenoming [35].  
Antivenom is produced by immunization of larger animals with a mixture of snake venoms, following 
collection of the antibodies from the animal’s blood (Figure 6). The first step of antivenom production 
is capture and venom collection from a snake. Then, an animal, typically a large mammal such as a 
horse or a sheep, is injected with non-lethal amounts of snake venoms through 1-1.5 years, in order 
to immunize the animal. The blood is collected from the animal, and by means of plasmapheresis the 
plasma is separated from the blood cells after which the cells are returned to the animal, while the

Animal-derived antivenom has saved many people from the fatal outcomes related to envenoming 
from many types of snakes. However, the treatment can be associated with adverse effects [36,37], 
sometimes severe and even deadly [38]. This is due to the non-human origin of the antibodies, which 
upon administration can result in generation of an immune response towards the antivenom. The 
severity of the potential adverse effects is partly due to the large amount of protein administered 
through treatment. The high dosage is necessary because of the relatively small percentage of 
therapeutic antibodies in the antivenom. As an example, one study showed that 97.5-99% of the 
antibodies in an equine scorpion antivenom were non-therapeutic [39], whereas other studies report 
that 64-95% of the antibodies in serum-based antivenom are non-therapeutic [40–42]. By the addition 
of an affinity purification step in the production, a product with higher potency can be obtained, but 
with a significant increase in production price as a result [43].  
Most people suffering from snakebite envenoming are from the tropical/subtropical and rural areas 
of the world [44,45]. Only around 2% of all snakebite victims receive antivenom treatment [46,47]. 
The low treatment statistics are both due to the high price of antivenom, its low availability, and the 
fact that many victims seek traditional healers instead of hospital treatment [48,49]. Antivenom 
production is very complex and not cost-effective. This makes antivenom an expensive product that

6.4. Future perspectives within snake antivenom therapies 
Though current serum-based antivenom has saved many lives in the last 100 years, the technology is 
highly outdated and there is a great need for a modern treatment. There are many different approaches 
that can be applied to develop novel snakebite therapies and these therapies could be based on several 
kinds of molecules. In the following, a brief description of five different approaches will be presented.  
Advanced immunization. The current method of antivenom production utilizes immunization of 
animals and could be optimized by changing the immunization procedure. One such change could be 
to immunize with a mixture of the medically relevant toxins from snakes and not whole venom, which 
contains many proteins which are not toxic to humans. In this way, a more potent antivenom could 
be obtained, allowing for decreased dosage and therefore possibly fewer adverse reactions. However, 
purification of medically important toxins is a time-consuming task and it can be difficult to obtain 
the desired amounts of the toxins needed for immunization. Instead, a strategy could be to design and 
express recombinant toxins that can be used for immunization or to immunize animals with DNA 
encoding toxin epitopes instead of the entire toxins. Both strategies have the advantage that collection 
of snake venom would not be required and that the mixture of antibodies in the serum might be more 
relevant in relation to toxin neutralization. These methods have already been used to produce serum 
based antivenom with neutralizing effects against snake venoms [52–57]. Nevertheless, the methods 
are still based on animal-derived antibodies associated with adverse effects upon administration to 
humans and the production method is still very expensive and not scalable. Moreover, the product 
has high batch-to-batch variation, which is why each batch has to go through extensive testing before 
being administrated to humans.  
Small molecules. Development of small molecule drugs that can inhibit the different medically 
important toxin groups in snake venoms is also a strategy pursued by some researchers [58] and 
several small molecules with inhibitory effects against snake toxins have been discovered [59]. The 
advantage of small molecules is that they can be produced at a low cost. However, the price could be 
high if many different small molecules are needed in a potential treatment (which is what would be 
expected) and they all require different production methods. Overall, small molecules might be 
advantageous in combination with other treatments in the cases where drugs that have already gone 
through clinical trials can be applied to snakebite treatment. 
Peptides. There are many potential advantages of using peptides as therapy for snake bite 
envenoming. The discovery costs for peptides are low in comparison to other alternatives. Peptides 
are small and they have a large volume of distribution which is beneficial for neutralization of locally 
acting toxins. They have the potential to be administered through non-intravenous routes which would 
allow for treatment by non-medically trained people [60]. The disadvantages of using peptides are 
that they have a short half-life [61] and that the toxin affinities for a linear peptide is most probably 
lower than that of antibodies due to their molecular differences allowing antibodies to form more 
stable interactions with their target. 
==PAGE:16==
	  
16 
Antibody fragments. An antibody fragment is a piece of an antibody containing the domain 
responsible for antigen binding without the Fc region. There are many different types of antibody 
fragments. The binding domain can be discovered using in vitro phage display selection and can then 
be converted to the desired antibody format. The antibody fragments have, until now, mainly been of 
murine or camelid origin but can be of human origin. One of the advantages of antibody fragments 
in comparison with whole antibodies is their small size, allowing them to penetrate into the deeper 
tissue, however, their half-life is significantly shorter than that of whole antibodies [62]. This trade-
off will be discussed further in section 6.5. The selection process is very expensive, although the 
fragments can be produced at low cost in bacterial or fungal fermentations after discovery.  
IgG-based recombinant oligoclonal human antivenom. The final approach for neutralization of 
snake venom that will be presented in this overview is the approach being pursued in this thesis, 
namely the development of an IgG-based recombinant oligoclonal human antivenom. The concept is 
to develop monoclonal human antibodies targeting each of the most medically relevant toxins in the 
snake venom in question. The monoclonal antibodies should then be produced by cell culture in 
oligoclonal mixtures [51,59]. The advantage of this approach is that one experimental setup can be 
used to discover antibodies against all snake toxins, however, the phase of antibody discovery is 
highly expensive. Assuming that the antibody format is IgG and that the production is in mammalian 
polyclonal cell cultures, the production could be carried out in a cost-effective manner and the 
antivenom is estimated to be cheaper than the serum-based antivenoms currently on the market [51].  
6.5. Development of a recombinant oligoclonal human antivenom 
There are many things to consider when developing the world’s first recombinant oligoclonal human 
antivenom. Choices of which snakes to target, which toxins to neutralize, and which antibody format 
to use in the antivenom are important to the developmental process. Andreas H. Laustsen took these 
decisions when initiating the research project which this thesis is part of.  
First, the decision concerning which snake venoms to generate antivenom against had to be taken. 
The snakes should be clinically relevant, meaning that their venom should be lethal and pose a threat 
to humans. Another thing to consider is the type of symptoms the envenoming causes. Whether the 
snakebite results in local tissue damage or paralysis has an influence on how the envenoming should 
be treated. It was decided to target snakes from the elapid family, due to their venom composition, 
mainly consisting of neurotoxins and limited amounts of locally acting toxins. This is beneficial since 
it is generally considered more difficult to neutralize the effects of the locally acting toxins than 
systemically acting neurotoxins (personal communication with Andreas H. Laustsen). 
The next step was to decide which toxins to target in the snake venom. Snake venoms consist of many 
different toxins, yet not all of them are crucial to neutralize as some only target reptiles, insects, or 
other non-mammalian species [63]. To guide the process of choosing which toxins to neutralize, the 
Toxicity Score was introduced [64]. The Toxicity Score is a measure of the clinical relevance of a 
toxin. The two simplest and most relevant factors in defining the relevance of a toxin is the intrinsic 
toxicity, measured by the median lethal dose (LD50) of the toxin in mice, and the abundance of the 
toxin in the venom. If a toxin has a low LD50 and is only present in very low abundance it might be 
less relevant to neutralize than a toxin with an intermediate LD50 that is present in very high 
==PAGE:17==
	  
17 
abundance in the venom. The Toxicity Score is defined as the relative toxin abundance in the venom 
in percentage divided by the LD50 of the toxin. The Toxicity Score is, however, limited to 
determination of clinical relevance of toxins with lethal effects, such as neurotoxins, whereas the 
relevance of cytotoxins, causing local tissue damage, cannot be assessed using this tool.  
The last important decision regards the antibody format used in the antivenom. Toxin binders have 
been developed in many different antibody formats such as IgG, F(ab)2, Fab, VHH, and scFv [65–68] 
(Figure 7). The largest of the formats listed in Figure 7 is the IgG, which has the benefit that it has a 
long half-life in the body. This is partly due to its size, but mainly due to its Fc region, which is 
recognized by the neonatal Fc receptor and therefore gets rescued from lysosomal degradation 
through receptor-mediated recycling [69]. The large size of the molecule does, however, limit its 
volume of distribution which is why IgG does not penetrate into the deeper tissues. The antibody 
fragments F(ab)2, Fab, scFv, and VHH, all have a reduced serum half-life compared to IgG [70] 
mainly due to their lack of Fc region but also correlated to their size. The antibody fragments are 
smaller and have a larger volume of distribution, which allows them to penetrate deeper into the tissue 
than IgG. When choosing an antibody format, it is important to choose one that has a pharmacokinetic 
profile matching the one of the targeted toxins. When targeting elapid venoms with a high content of 
neurotoxins, tissue penetration is not the most important feature needed for the antivenom since 
neurotoxins act systemically. In this case, it is more beneficial to choose a format, such as IgG, that 
circulates in the blood and can neutralize the toxins before they reach their target. For envenoming 
by vipers, there is a greater need for neutralization of toxins in the deeper tissues, as many of the 
toxins in these venoms are locally acting toxins. In this case, it might be more beneficial to choose an 
antibody fragment with a high volume of distribution. It is difficult to estimate the importance of the 
half-life of the antibodies. However, a phenomenon called recurrent envenoming, where 
envenomation that appears treated returns a few days following envenoming due to the slow release 
of toxins from the bite site, is known [71]. In this case, a single injection of antibodies with long half-
lives might be sufficient treatment, whereas a treatment based on antibody fragments might need to 
be administered several times.  
Based on the considerations discussed in this section, the clinically relevant snakes from the elapid 
family was chosen as a proof of concept case. The toxins targeted by the antivenom has been chosen 
by use of the Toxicity Score. The format of the antivenom will in this case be IgG, though future 
recombinant antivenoms might contain antibody fragments if they are raised against locally acting 
toxins.

6.6. Phage display 
Phage display selection is a tool for antibody discovery. The technique utilizes filamentous phages 
displaying antibody fragments on their coat proteins, which allow for panning of the phages against 
different antigens. Before giving an in-depth description of the steps in phage display 
selection; panning, binding, washing, elution, and amplification, a thorough introduction to the wild-
type M13 phage and the engineered M13 phage used for phage display will be given. 
6.6.1. The M13 phage 
The M13 phage belongs to a group of filamentous phages collectively referred to as Ff phages. This 
group only infects the subspecies of E. coli that carry the F factor and express the F pilus, because 
the adsorption of the phage to the bacterium requires binding of a phage coat protein to the tip of the 
F pilus on the bacterium [72]. The M13 phage is neither temperate nor lytic, instead the phage 
establishes a chronic infection of its host, where it continuously releases new phages. The phage 
contains a single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) genome which is 6,407 bp [73]. The genome consists of 9 
genes encoding 11 different proteins. 5 of these proteins are coat proteins and the remaining 6 proteins 
are involved in replication and assembly of the phage. The M13 phage has a length of 900 nm and a 
width of 6.5 nm [74]. The most abundant of the coat proteins is the capsid protein G8P which is 
present in approximately 2,700 copies in each phage and envelopes the phage genome (Figure 8). 
The remaining four proteins are each present in approximately 5 copies. A schematic representation 
of the phage and its proteins can be seen in Figure 8 and information about the genes and proteins of 
the phage are listed in Table 1.

The first stage of the M13 infection is the adsorption (Figure 9). Adsorption takes place through 
binding of the N2 domain of the G3P to the tip of a F pilus on the surface of E. coli. The F pilus will, 
under normal conditions, assemble and disassemble continuously. Upon phage binding to the pilus, 
it will disassemble and bring the phage closer to the surface of the bacterium. Binding between the F 
pilus and the N2 domain of the G3P mediates a release of the N1 domain on the G3P, which then 
binds a co-receptor, TolA, on the surface of the bacterium. There are three Tol proteins; TolA, TolR 
and TolQ, and they are all essential for infection as they mediate depolymerization of the phage coat 
and translocate the ssDNA into the bacterium [75,76].

Once the genome of the phage has been injected into the bacterium, the host RNA and DNA 
polymerases will synthesize a (-) strand complementary to the ssDNA strand of the M13 phage. The 
two strands will form a supercoiled double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) phage genome, termed the 
replicative form (RF) (Figure 10) [77]. The RF is replicated through rolling circle replication once 
the replication-associated protein G2P has reached appropriate accumulation. The G2P nicks the RF 
at a specific non-coding region on the (+) strand. The 3’ end generated from the nick will be elongated 
using the (-) strand as a template. Throughout the elongation, the original (+) strand is displaced by 
the Rep helicase as the new (+) strand is synthesized. When one round of replication is completed the 
original (+) strand is recircularized by the G2P and converted into a new RF. In the early phase of 
infection, all newly formed ssDNA (+) strands will be converted into RF. In later infection stages, 
the concentration of G5P will start accumulating and sequester the newly generated (+) strands, thus 
preventing the conversion of the ssDNA into the RF. When the G5P binds the ssDNA, it dimerizes 
in a back-to-back conformation leading to the conversion from the circular appearance of the ssDNA 
to a more rod-shaped appearance. G10P plays an essential but unknown role for the stable 
accumulation of ssDNA (+) strands. In addition, a packing signal formed by a hairpin structure in one 
end of the ssDNA is required for correct packing of the phage genome. The ssDNA bound by the 
G5P protein is the substrate for phage assembly (Figure 10) [75,76].

The assembly and budding of the phage is a five-step process that includes: preinitiation, initiation, 
elongation, pretermination, and termination. During preinitiation, an assembly site, consisting of 
G1P, G11P and, G4P, all interacting through their periplasmic domains, is formed. A cylindrical 
structure consisting of 12-14 G4Ps mediates close contact between the cytoplasmic and the outer 
membrane. The central cavity of the structure is about 8 nm in diameter and has a total mass around 
600 kDa. The dimensions of this pore-like structure might be a limiting factor for the size, which a 
displayed polypeptide can have during phage display. In addition to the large structure generated by 
G4P, a multimeric complex containing 5-6 copies of both G1P and G10P is generated, potentially 
forming a second channel [75,76].  
Initiation only begins once preinitiation has resulted in formation of the assembly site, the G7P and 
G9P are located at the tip of the particle, and the G5P is bound to the ssDNA. The G7P and G9P then 
interact with the packing signal of the phage genome which facilitates contact with the G1P, resulting 
in binding of host-encoded thioredoxin. During the elongation step, the G5P bound to the phage 
genome is replaced with G8P throughout the translocation of the DNA through the channel across 
the membrane (Figure 10). The translocation continues until the phage genome has been completely 
coated with G8P at which point both G3P and G6P are necessary to release the phage from the 
bacterium. If either G3P or G6P is missing, a second phage genome can be loaded through the pore, 
an incident that during normal conditions takes place around 5% of the time. During pretermination, 
membrane-embedded G3P complexed with G6P is incorporated at the terminal end of the phage 
particle. Termination is the release of the phage through a conformational change in the G3P-G6P 
complex [75,76].  
6.6.2. Using the M13 phage as a tool in antibody discovery 
Phage display selection was first described in 1990 by McCafferty et al. [78]. The method takes 
advantage of the unique opportunity of directly linking a protein to its DNA sequence through a 
phage. In phage display, an antibody fragment such as a VHH or a scFv is linked to the G3P of the 
M13 phage. A library containing phages that present different antibody fragments on their G3P can 
then be panned against an antigen to which specific binders will be discovered. Many different phage 
libraries have been developed, where different antibody formats have been fused to the G3P or other 
coat proteins of the M13 phage [79]. 
The libraries do not only vary in the type of antibodies displayed, but also in their level of diversity. 
The library used for discovery of specific scFvs against toxins from N. melanoleuca in this study is 
the IONTAS library. The M13 phage utilized in the IONTAS library has been genetically modified 
to fit the purpose of antibody discovery. Several changes have been made to the M13 phage, the most 
obvious one being attaching a human scFv to the G3P (Figure 11). Moreover, the phage has been 
engineered into a phagemid, which needs superinfection with a helper phage, such as the M13K07 
phage, in order to multiply. The helper phage contains all genes needed for replication and these are 
expressed upon superinfection of a bacterium already infected with the phagemid. To ensure that the 
helper phage is utilized to assemble as many phagemids as possible and limit the reproduction of the 
actual helper phage, the origin of replication of the helper phage is defective, resulting in 90-98% of 
the packaged phages being phagemids (personal communication with Dr. Aneesh Karatt Vellat). All 
==PAGE:23==

genes necessary for replication are carried by the helper phage, including the gene encoding G3P. 
The most important gene carried by the phagemid is the III-scFv fusion gene.  
When constructing a library of phages for antibody discovery, it is of high importance to ensure 
monovalent scFv display. If inconsistencies occur and several phages carry more than one scFv, it 
will result in phage selection based not only on affinity but also avidity (Figure 12). This leads to a 
selection of phages carrying two low affinity scFvs over phages carrying one high affinity scFv since

tag that is cleaved by trypsin, resulting in separation of the scFv from the G3P (Figure 13). The G3P 
can then bind F pili and infect E. coli.

Phage display selection is a high throughput method used to discover antibodies specific to different 
antigens. The protocol includes rounds of 5 steps (Figure 14). The first step is panning, where the 
library is added to a vial coated with streptavidin or neutravidin to which the toxin is linked. The

An element that largely influences the success of the phage display selection, is the antigen 
presentation. There are different ways to present an antigen with different advantages and 
disadvantages (Figure 15). The simplest form of antigen presentation is to coat the antigen directly to 
the plastic surface of a vial or a well. The advantage of direct immobilization is that the selection is 
only against the antigen, making it unnecessary to go through the deselection steps that are essential 
when presenting antigen through immobilization for instance via a biotin linker. The major 
disadvantage of direct immobilization is that the antigen might go through conformational changes 
upon binding of the well, potentially leading to denaturation of the antigen. Selection of antibodies 
against a denatured protein might result in development of antibodies that have low affinity to the 
native toxin, which within the field of antivenom will not be discovered until in vivo experiments are 
initiated. Additionally, the antigens might have preferences in their binding to the surface, which 
might result in areas of the antigen that are not exposed and therefore no scFvs with high affinity to 
this area will be accumulated. Another method of antigen presentation is immobilization through a 
biotin linker. For this method, it is necessary to chemically conjugate biotin to the toxin. Biotinylation 
of toxins can be a challenging task, especially since the biotin to toxin ratio is crucial. If a toxin is 
over-biotinylated it might heavily decrease the available surface area on the antigen, thereby 
preventing phage binding. The advantage of immobilization through a biotin linker is that the toxin 
will be lifted from the vial by first coating the vial with streptavidin or neutravidin and then adding 
the biotinylated toxin that will then bind the streptavidin or neutravidin. This allows for an improved 
display of the antigen and thus the discovery of antibodies against more toxin epitopes [81]. A 
disadvantage of immobilization through a biotin linker is that addition of streptavidin or neutravidin 
introduces another molecule that the phages can bind to, which can lead to accumulation of 
streptavidin or neutravidin binding antibodies. To avoid unspecific binding, several deselection steps 
are implemented in the phage display protocol when working with antigens that are immobilized by 
a biotin linker.

7. Experimental strategy 
The objective of this thesis is to discover human scFvs against the medically relevant toxins from N. 
melanoleuca. To do this, venom from N. melanoleuca is fractionated using RP-HPLC. Then, the 
medically relevant fractions from the venom are biotinylated to allow for better antigen presentation 
during phage display selections. The biotinylation is analyzed by matrix assisted laser 
desorption/ionization - time of flight (MALDI-TOF) (Figure 16, A).  
Phage display selections are carried out against the biotinylated medically relevant toxins, to discover 
antibody fragments with high specificity to these toxins. The binding is then assessed through plate 
test, where it is possible to get indications about whether binders to streptavidin have wrongfully been 
accumulated or whether binders to snake toxins have been rightfully selected (Figure 16, B).  
By subcloning the genes encoding toxin-specific scFvs into E. coli (Figure 16, C), these can be 
expressed in solution. Then, monoclonal ELISA can be performed to assess the binding of the scFvs 
to their toxin target (Figure 16, D).

8.2. Methods 
8.2.1. Venom separation using reverse-phase HPLC 
For 18 rounds of HPLC 40 mg of N. melanoleuca venom was dissolved in 4.4 mL of water containing 
0.1 % trifluoroacetic acid (TFA: solution A), aliquoted into ten portions of 220 µL and frozen at 
- 20 oC. Before fractionation, aliquots were centrifuged for 1 minute at 10,000 g to remove debris. 
Fractions were separated using RP-HPLC (Agilent 1200) on a C18 column (250 x 4.6 mm, 5 µm 
particle; Supelco). Before fractionation was started the column was run through with solution A for 
20 minutes. Elution was carried out using an elution rate of 1 mL/minute with a gradient of 
acetonitrile supplemented with 0.1 % TFA (solution B). The program used was: 0% solution B for 
5 minutes, 0-15 % solution B for 10 minutes, 15-45 % solution B for 60 minutes, 45-70 % solution 
B for 10 minutes ending with 70 % solution B for 9 minutes [84]. Fractions were collected manually. 
After completing this program, the column was run through with solution A for 20 minutes to allow 
for calibration before the following round of fractionation.  
==PAGE:33==
	  
33 
8.2.2. Evaporation and pooling of fractionated venom 
Collected venom fractions were either evaporated using a vacuum centrifuge at room temperature for 
8-24 hours or evaporated using nitrogen evaporation for 48 hours followed by freeze drying for 
24 hours. After evaporation, fractions corresponding to the identical fractions from each round were 
dissolved in 150 µL phosphate buffered saline (PBS) and pooled. Protein concentrations were 
determined using a NanoDropTM Lite Spectrophotometer using PBS as blank measurement. The 
concentrations were corrected using the average value of the extinction coefficients of the proteins in 
each fraction.  
8.2.3. Biotinylation and purification of toxins  
In the first round of biotinylation 400 ng of fractions 1, 8, 9, 18+19 and 22 were biotinylated using a 
toxin to biotin molar ratio of 1:1.5. In the first round of biotinylation toxin concentrations were not 
corrected by the extinction coefficient. In the second round of biotinylation fractions 1, 8, 9 and 18+19 
were biotinylated using ratio 1:1 whereas fraction 22 was biotinylated using 1:0.75 toxin to biotin 
molar ratio. All biotinylations in the second round were carried out using toxin concentrations 
corrected using extinction coefficients. Biotin was dissolved in 170 µL of milliQ water directly after 
removing it from the freezer and dissolved biotin was added to the fractions within a few minutes. 
All samples were vortexed for 5 seconds and incubated at room temperature for 30 minutes. To purify 
biotinylated toxins from the first round of biotinylation the Vivaconâ 500 purification kit was used. 
Biotinylated toxins were transferred to individual columns and the columns were inserted in a 
collection tube and centrifuged at 14,000 g for 30 minutes. The flow through was discharged and 
samples were washed a total of three times by addition of 300 µL PBS and centrifugation at 14,000 g 
for 30 minutes. After washing, the collection tube was cleaned with PBS and the column was inverted 
and inserted in the collection tube. 100 µL of PBS was added to the filter and samples were eluted by 
centrifugation at 14,000 g for 30 minutes. The second round of biotinylated fractions were purified 
using the Amiconâ Ultra - 0.5 mL Centrifugal Filters Ultracelâ - 3K. The biotinylated toxins were 
loaded onto the filter (50-150 µL) and centrifuged for 45 minutes at 14,000 g. Filters were washed 
three times with 200 µL of PBS followed by 30 minutes of centrifugation at 14,000 g. The toxins 
were eluted by inverting the filters and transferring them to clean collection tubes and centrifuging 
for 2 minutes at 1,000 g. After elution protein concentrations were measured using NanoDropTM Lite 
Spectrophotometer with PBS for blank measurement.  
8.2.4. C18 stage tipping 
The pH of the biotinylated and unbiotinylated fractions was measured and adjusted with 2 µL of 
10% TFA (or 10 µL of 0.1% TFA) to bring pH below 2. C18 filters were punched out using a syringe 
and placed in a DL-10 Diamond tower 10 µL tip. The filters were activated by addition of 20 µL 
60% acetonitrile and 0.1% TFA (elution solution) and spun down. Following activation, the filters 
were never left to dry for more than 2 minutes. The filters were then washed by addition of 20 µL of 
0.1% TFA (equilibration solution) and spun down. 5-15 µL of the pH adjusted biotinylated fractions 
were added to the filters, spun down, and the flow through was collected. The filters were then washed 
twice with 20 µL equilibrium solution. After washing, 50 µL of equilibrium solution was added to 
the filters to enable storage at 5 oC in a Falcon tube until MALDI-TOF was run. On the day of the 
==PAGE:34==
	  
34 
MALDI the filters were spun and 10 µL of elution buffer was added. The tips were transferred to 
clean Eppendorf tubes and samples were eluted by spinning. 
8.2.5. MALDI-TOF 
The Ground Steel MALDI Target plate was cleaned by washing once with deionized water and then 
with 70% ethanol. 1 µL of each sample was added onto a well in the plate. 1 µl of Sinapinic acid 
(trans-3,5-dimethoxy-4-hydroxycinnamic acid) matrix was added and mixed thoroughly with each 
sample. The standard for calibration was prepared by mixing 0.25 µL of 10 nM bovine insulin 
dissolved in 1% TFA, 0.5 µL 10 nM equine cytochrome c dissolved in 0.1% TFA, 0.5 µL 10 nM 
equine apomyglobin dissolved in 0.1% TFA with 1.25 µL of matrix. Samples were analyzed by 
UltraFlex MALDI-TOF mass spectrometer (Bruker Daltonics, Germany, Bremen) set with 1.00 GS/s 
digitizer (Acquiris DP240) and a Pulsed Ion Extraction (250 ns of delay time). TOF-TOF Acquisition 
was taken at 60% of laser intensity (1500 shots). The device was operated in positive linear mode, 
ION source mode 1 and 25 kV of acceleration voltage. The machine was calibrated by shooting the 
standard and identifying the peaks manually as the three proteins on the computer. After calibrating, 
each sample was shot 1500 times.

9. Results and discussion 
9.1. Isolation of medically important venom fractions by RP-HPLC 
The first experimental work conducted in this thesis was fractionation of the venom from N. 
melanoleuca. Fractionation was carried out using RP-HPLC to resolve the complex venom into 
33 venom fractions (Figure 17, A). Collectively, 18 rounds of fractionation were run, and much 
attention was put into collection of the fractions in an equal way in each round, to get a good antigen 
preparation for further work.  
The most prominent peaks were fractions 1, 2, 7, 8, 9, 18+19, 20, 21, 22, 27, and 28. To distinguish 
between medically relevant and less relevant venom fractions, the chromatogram was compared to 
one from a toxicovenomic study of the venom of N. melanoleuca conducted by Lauridsen et al., 2016 
(Figure 17, B). The venom was fractionated according to the same protocol and the only difference 
in methods between the two chromatograms was the origin of the snake specimens. The specimen 
investigated in this study originated from Cameroon and the one from Lauridsen et al., 2016 
originated from Uganda. There are many prominent differences between the two chromatograms, 
however, this is not surprising as it is known that the venom composition of N. melanoleuca differs 
greatly between specimens from different geographical locations. It has even been suggested that 
there should be a reclassification of the N. melanoleuca species (personal communication with Mia 
Øhlenschlæger, Dr. Nicholas Casewell, and Dr. Wolfgang Wüster), a claim that is further supported 
by the chromatogram obtained from another fractionation of N. melanoleuca venom performed in 
this work where the venom from two specimens from Ghana and Tanzania was polled (Figure 17, 
C). This chromatogram differs even more from the one presented in Lauridsen et al., 2016 than the 
one used in this study.  
As the venom from N. melanoleuca differs greatly between specimens from different geographical 
locations, it is not optimal to identify toxicity of venom fractions from one specimen based on a 
toxicovenomic study of a specimen from another geographical location. Despite this, since only one 
toxicovenomic study has been conducted on the venom of N. melanoleuca it is better to predict 
toxicity based on this study instead of no study.  
The major fractions 1, 8, 18+19, 20, and 27 from this study is suggested to correspond to fractions 3, 
8, 14, 15, and 25 from the toxicovenomics study (Figure 17, B). However, fraction 9 from the 
Cameroonian specimen is not present in the venom of the Ugandan specimen. From the 
toxicovenomic study it is reported that the venom fractions with the highest Toxicity Score are 
fractions 3, 8, 14, and 15. Fractions 3 and 8 with Toxicity Scores of 59.2 and 88, respectively, are 
both comprised of 3FTxs. Fractions 14 and 15 with Toxicity Scores of <20.4 and <24.0, respectively, 
are comprised of 3FTxs and PLA2s. As fractions 3, 8, 14, and 15, with the highest Toxicity Scores, 
could be identified in the chromatogram from this study, the most relevant fractions to conduct further 
work on are suggested to be fractions 1, 8, 18+19, and 20. Additionally, it was decided to include 
fraction 9 in the study as the fraction was prominent and not characterized in the study of the Ugandan 
specimen.

9.2. Analysis of biotinylation by MALDI-TOF indicates over-biotinylation 
The chemical linking of a biotin molecule to a snake toxin is advantageous because it allows for better 
presentation of the toxin in the following phage display experiments. In this case, a biotinylated toxin 
can be presented in a semi-soluble state where the toxin is lifted from the surface of the vial/well in 
contrast to directly binding the toxin to the surface of the well, which might result in less optimal 
presentation of the antigen.  
In the first round of biotinylation the fractions were biotinylated in a 1:1.5 toxin to biotin molar ratio. 
This resulted in clear over-biotinylation (for MALDI-TOF results see appendix 13.1). The second 
biotinylation round was carried out in a 1:1 toxin to biotin molar ratio. The non-biotinylated and 
biotinylated fractions were then analyzed using MALDI-TOF. The MALDI-TOF of unbiotinylated 
fractions can be used to characterize the toxins in each fraction, which will be presented in section 
9.3. Comparison between MALDI-TOF of biotinylated and unbiotinylated fractions can additionally 
be used to determine the degree of biotinylation of each fraction.  
MALDI-TOF results from biotinylated and unbiotinylated fractions 1, 8, 9, and 18+19 can be seen in 
Figure 18. The addition of a biotin molecule to a toxin, results in the addition of 473 Da to the toxin’s 
molar mass, which is how biotinylation patterns can be determined through MALDI-TOF analysis. 
Biotinylation of fraction 1 has primarily resulted in addition of one or two biotins per toxin, though 
toxins without biotin and with three biotins are also present in smaller quantities (Figure 18, A+B). 
Fraction 8 has primarily two biotins per toxin, secondarily one and three biotins per toxin, and thirdly 
a very small amount with four and zero biotins per toxin (Figure 18, C+D). In fraction 9 the majority 
of the toxins are coupled to two biotin molecules, some to one and three biotins, while almost none 
are unbiotinylated (Figure 18, E+F). Fraction 18+19 is the fraction with the most optimal 
biotinylation where the majority of the toxins are unbiotinylated, almost as many are coupled to one 
biotin, whereas only a small amount of toxin has two biotins molecules bound (Figure 18, G+H). 
The most optimal biotinylation pattern is to have no toxin biotinylated more than once, which is 
almost the case for fraction 18+19. In such a case a large amount of the toxin will be unbiotinylated, 
however, this will not be able to bind the wells during phage display selection, since streptavidin is 
coated on the well and a toxin needs a biotin molecule to bind the well. In this case, all unbiotinylated 
toxin will be washed away whereas only the single biotinylated toxin will stick and all presented 
antigens will have the same level of biotinylation. In the case of over-biotinylated toxins as seen for 
==PAGE:43==
	  
43 
fraction 9, the toxins will be presented in different biotinylation states which is less optimal. 
Furthermore, over-biotinylation will result in less available epitopes on the toxins which, in the case 
with 3FTxs and PLA2s, might be a problem due to the small size of the proteins.  
  
Subconclusion 
The MALDI-TOF results indicate the best biotinylation pattern for fraction 18+19 suggesting that the 
best phage display selections will be against this fraction.

9.4. Phage display selection and plate test 
Phage display selections were conducted using the IONTAS phage library consisting of phages that 
display human scFvs on their G3P. First, the phages were panned against a biotinylated venom 
fraction from N. melanoleuca and after allowing the phages to bind, the vials were washed to remove 
phages displaying scFvs with unspecific binding to the toxins. Then, the bound phages were eluted 
using trypsin, and the eluted phages were amplified in E. coli. Three rounds of phage display 
selections were conducted with the purpose of concentrating the high affinity binders in the selection 
outputs from round to round.  
The initial phage display selections conducted in this thesis were against fraction 1 (biotinylation 
attempt 1, for MALDI-TOF see Appendix 13.1) using the IONTAS k library. The selection 
conditions were designed to test the effects of different streptavidin deselection methods. 
Traditionally, deselection of streptavidin binders in a library is conducted using streptavidin-coated 
beads (Dynabeads) that are incubated with the library prior to panning against the streptavidin bound 
antigen. Following incubation of the library with the beads, the two are separated to remove 
streptavidin-binding phages from the library.  
In the initial selection experiment this deselection method was compared to two other deselection 
methods. One was through incubation of the library in a vial coated with streptavidin before panning 
and the other was through deselection of streptavidin binders by incubation of the library with soluble 
streptavidin (10 µg/mL). As a control, a selection without deselection of streptavidin binders was 
conducted. Additionally, one selection with direct coating of the unbiotinylated venom fraction to a 
vial was conducted to see the effect of direct immobilization in comparison to immobilization through 
a biotin linker. An overview of the 5 selections is available in Table 8.

streptavidin, blocking vials and library with milk, addition of the library to the vials, elution of the 
phages, infection of E. coli, and plating. One round was completed, though still no colonies were 
obtained. As streptavidin deselection and all toxin-containing steps were removed from the protocol 
the number of steps to trouble shoot in the protocol were significantly reduced making the trouble 
shooting process simpler.  
9.5. Trouble shooting of phage display selection protocol 
As no colonies were obtained from previous selections, the problem was narrowed down to lack of 
bacterial growth on plates. As TG1 cultures grew as expected in liquid media (2xYT) the problem 
was suspected to be caused by lack of resistance to ampicillin, as several batches of plates (2xYT-
AG) had been used for previous unsuccessful experiments and the plates themselves were therefore 
==PAGE:49==
	  
49 
not considered to be the problem. TG1 cells acquire resistance to ampicillin upon infection by 
phagemids. Phagemid infection could be unsuccessful if: (1) TG1 cells were incubated at incorrect 
conditions and were not expressing the F pilus or if the TG1 stock was compromised, (2) if the phage 
library was non-infectious, (3) if the trypsin used for phage elution was ineffective, or (4) if neither 
the toxin nor streptavidin was able to bind the MaxiSorpTM vials used for selections. The four 
identified steps that could be responsible for the unsuccessful selections were chosen for 
troubleshooting using simple selection conditions against only streptavidin (no toxin) with no 
streptavidin deselection steps. 
TG1 cells and incubation conditions. Selections were carried out using a different stock of TG1 
cells while alternating the incubation conditions. The incubation conditions are important since TG1 
cells have decreased expression of their F-pilus, which is necessary for infection, during growth at 
temperatures below 37 oC [85]. 
Infection of TG1 cells with phages was carried out both in a ‘presumably’ 37 oC incubation room and 
in a 37 oC water bath. Additionally, it was tested if any effect was obtained by incubation without 
shaking for 30 minutes followed by 30 minutes of shaking, as this, in theory, would allow for 
undisturbed attachment of the phage to the tip of the E. coli F pilus.  
No colonies were obtained after the first round of selection under any of the described conditions. As 
both the TG1 stock used in all experiments and the incubation protocol had been used with success 
previously, this step was not assessed to be the likely cause of the failing experiments. 
 
Subconclusion 
Neither the TG1 cells nor the incubation conditions were concluded to be the cause of the repeated 
experimental failure. However, as both the cells as well as the incubation conditions had been used 
previously and generated successful selections, it was anticipated to be unlikely that the cause of 
failure should be identified in this step. 
 
Phage library. The phage preparation used for selections could be the problem, as the batch of phage 
library had never been used in the laboratory where all experiments from this thesis were conducted. 
The batch had also not been used with success at IONTAS, though it had only been used for few 
selections there.  
A phage preparation can become non-infectious if the preparation contains proteases, as these can 
cleave the linker between the G3P of the phage and the scFv. In this case, phages will not bind an 
antigen in the vials and will instead be removed through washing.  
To test if the phage preparation was responsible for the unsuccessful selections, phages from the 
Predator library, a human heavy domain library, were rescued. Then selections using both the newly 
rescued Predator library, as well as the new batch of IONTAS library, and an old batch of IONTAS 
library were conducted. After one round of selections, no colonies were obtained from either library.  
==PAGE:50==
	  
50 
To ensure that the phages in the libraries were indeed able to infect the TG1 cells, an experiment was 
conducted where the three libraries were used for direct infection of TG1 cultures without panning 
them against an antigen. As an expansion of this experiment, the libraries were also incubated with 
an equivalent concentration of trypsin to the one used in the elution step prior to infection, to test 
whether the library was ‘bald’ (i.e. not carrying a scFv) as ‘bald’ phages are rendered non-infectious 
after trypsin treatment. Phage libraries were diluted equally and 103 or 105 phages were added to 5 
mL TG1 culture at OD600 = 0.5. This was done to ensure that phages rather than bacterial counts were 
the limiting factor, to allow for detection of a potential drop in number of infectious phages expected 
to be associated with trypsin treatment. This experiment produced an uncountable number of colonies 
for all tests without trypsin addition, confirming the infectious ability of the libraries. Approximately 
100-fold decrease in colony counts was observed when trypsin treatment was implemented. This drop 
in colonies was expected, as 90% of the phages in the IONTAS library are ‘bald’.  
The results of this experiment suggested that the library was not the cause of the unsuccessful 
experiments as newly rescued Predator phages and an old batch of IONTAS library revealed similar 
results. Additionally, phages from all libraries were infectious when no selection was performed.  
 
Subconclusion 
The IONTAS library used for all selections was cleared from suspicion as it could infect TG1 cells 
to the same extent as an old preparation of IONTAS library and freshly rescued Predator. 
Additionally, suspicion about trypsin elution rendering phages non-infectious, was also cleared.  
 
Trypsin elution. Ineffective elution of phages could result in low colony count, as phages are needed 
for acquisition of antibiotic resistance. To test this, a new aliquot of trypsin that in opposition to the 
aliquot used for all other experiments, had not been thawed and frozen repeatedly, was used. The 
selection did not reveal any colonies.  
An experiment with different trypsin incubation periods was conducted, where the trypsin elution 
step was 15, 25, 35, and 65 minutes. However, no colonies were obtained from this experiment.  
As the elution step was still suspected to be responsible for the lack of colonies and fresh trypsin 
could not be delivered within the project time frame, an alternative elution method was used. In this 
experiment, elution was carried out by addition of 10 mM glycine with pH 2.5 following 10 minutes 
of incubation. The pH was neutralized before the infection step. This method of elution has previously 
been a commonly used elution method. The experiment did not generate any colonies. As two 
completely different elution methods revealed identical results, it indicated that the problem leading 
to unsuccessful selections was not in the elution step, though it would have been beneficial to conduct 
selections with freshly prepared trypsin.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:51==
	  
51 
 
Subconclusion 
Elution with a new trypsin aliquot, variation in trypsin incubation times, and substitution of enzymatic 
elution with acid elution all revealed unsuccessful selections. This suggests that the elution step is not 
the cause of unsuccessful phage display selection. 
 
MaxiSorpTM vials. The final attempt to identify the cause for the low colony counts was to test the 
vials used for panning. The surface of the MaxiSorpTM vials is supposed to bind the antigen they are 
coated with whether this is directly with toxin or with streptavidin. However, if the surface has lost 
the ability to bind these antigens, then both the antigens and the phages will be removed from the vial 
during washing which could explain why bacterial infection is unsuccessful.  
Instead of the vials (which had proven functional at previous selections), selections were conducted 
in MaxiSorpTM microtiter plate wells, but to no avail. 
 
Subconclusion 
MaxiSorpTM coated vials were eliminated as the problem causing failed phage display selections as 
MaxiSorpTM coated microtiter plates revealed similar results as previous unsuccessful experiments.  
 
After testing the TG1 cells, incubation conditions, phage library, elution, and the vials without 
identifying the issue causing the lack of colonies, no further phage display selections were attempted.

9.7. Monoclonal ELISA 
After successful subcloning of six selection outputs, the binding of the individual scFvs to their toxin 
target was determined through monoclonal ELISA. Due to limited time and materials, only one 
monoclonal ELISA experiment could be conducted in this project. As approximately 2,000 
monoclones were obtained through subcloning, not all could be tested.  
Routinely, 300-400 monoclones per selection output is initially tested in a ‘rough’ ELISA experiment 
without duplicates or neutravidin and PBS controls. This type of experiment only requires one well 
in a microtiter plate per tested clone. Then, only the clones showing promising results are retested in 
an ELISA, with duplicates of samples and both neutravidin and PBS controls. This type of ELISA 
experiment requires 4-6 wells per tested clone depending on whether controls are tested in duplicates 
or not.  
It was decided to carry out the ‘rough’ type of ELISA, as this allowed for testing of 864 clones from 
three different complete selection outputs raised against both fractions 1, 8, and 18 instead of only 
testing one selection output. Even though this ELISA generates less accurate results without controls, 
it allows for testing of more colonies, thereby increasing the chance of finding good binders in a short 
period of time.  
Monoclonal ELISA was conducted by replication of glycerol stocks into 2xYT media supplemented 
with 2% glucose and 50 µg/mL kanamycin. The following day, the cultures were inoculated into 
autoinduction media, a media specially designed for optimized expression of scFvs. On day three, the 
supernatant from the cultures in autoinduction media was added to MaxiSorpTM plates coated with 
neutravidin and toxin. Following incubation and washing the monoclonal ANTI-FLAG® M2-
peroxidase (HRP) antibody was added to the wells. After addition of OPD substrate, plates were 
incubated for 26 minutes, the reaction was stopped, and the absorbance was measured.  
288 monoclones (three 96-well plates) were tested from each of the three chosen selection outputs. 
The selection outputs against fractions 1, 8, and 19 were all from the third selection round using the 
k library (Table 11).

9.7.1. Fraction 1 
Following monoclonal ELISA of clones raised against fraction 1, the results (Figure 21, A) were 
sorted according to absorbance. As can be seen from the graph, even though 288 monoclones were 
tested, no good binders with a minimum absorbance of 1 were detected from this output. The low 
absorbance is not expected to be caused by experimental failure, as higher absorbance were detected 
for monoclones in the two other selection outputs tested in the same experiment. 
By comparing the results of the monoclonal ELISA to plate test results obtained for the non-
subcloned selection outputs, it is clear that poor results should have been expected (Figure 21, B). 
The plate test revealed that the selection output to mainly contain scFvs with unspecific binding to 
the vial and not specific to the toxin in fraction 1. Thus, monoclonal ELISA results confirm the plate 
test results. 
However, it is important to consider that the antigen preparation used for phage display selections 
and plate test was different from the antigen preparation used for the monoclonal ELISA. This is 
important as differences in the antigen between the two batches could account for the poor ELISA 
results, as selection against antigen batch 1 might not reveal good binding in an ELISA test against 
batch 2. The antigen preparations can be compared by their MALDI-TOF results (Figure 21, C+D). 
These results reveal that the two antigen preparations to each contain one toxin and one toxin dimer. 
The toxins differ in MW with more than 2,000 Da, however, the MW obtained from the MALDI-
TOF conducted by Mia Øhlenschlæger had many deviations from what would be expected and 
suspicions about proper calibration of the machine were raised. Considering this information, the two 
antigens appear to be extremely similar both in toxin composition and biotinylation pattern. This 
suggests that poor ELISA results are not due to differences in antigen preparations but rather 
accurately reflect the quality of the selection output.  
Revisiting the MALDI-TOF results, the toxin in fraction 1 is over-biotinylated, which combined with 
the small size of the toxin might explain why the selection was unsuccessful as the excess of biotin 
molecules might have prevented phages from binding the toxin.  
 
Subconclusion 
The selection output from round three using the k library against fraction 1 generated poor 
monoclonal ELISA results indicating that no toxin-specific binders were obtained from this selection. 
Results corresponded to those acquired though plate test and the failed selection might be explained 
by over-biotinylation of the antigen preparation used.

9.7.2. Fraction 8 
Results from the monoclonal ELISA performed with 288 monoclones expressing scFvs raised against 
fraction 8 generated much more promising results than for fraction 1 (Figure 22, A). The monoclones 
were sorted according to absorbance and 42% (122) of the tested monoclones had an absorbance

quality of the selection and no direct conclusions can be drawn from them. However, apart from the 
initial experiment, the remaining three seem to indicate that toxin-specific binders were accumulated

might have been even more promising had a better antigen preparation had been utilized.

From selections against fraction 8, round 3 using the k library 122 monoclones showed promising 
results in the monoclonal ELISA experiment. This supports previously generated results from 
polyclonal ELISA, though these are neither conclusive nor completely reliable. Results indicate that 
this selection was successful and that scFvs against fraction 8, the most medically relevant fraction 
in N. melanoleuca venom according to Toxicity Score, has been obtained.

9.7.3. Fraction 18 
ELISA of monoclones expressing scFvs from pannings against fraction 18 showed results indicating 
that binders were obtained (Figure 23, A). As seen from Figure 23, A, 13 monoclones corresponding

9.7.4. Limitations of the obtained monoclonal ELISA results 
The monoclonal ELISA results generated in this project can be used to discriminate binders from 
non-binders. However, there are many limitations to the ELISA test. First, the ELISA results are not 
only determined by the binding affinity of the scFvs to their antigen, but also by the expression level 
of the scFvs. This means that a high affinity scFv that is poorly expressed might give lower 
absorbance levels than a scFv with intermediate affinity to its target that is well expressed. 
Additionally, the quality of the antigen preparation has a big influence on the ELISA results. As an 
example, results obtained in this thesis suggest that better binders are obtained against fraction 8 than 
fraction 18+19. However, this might partially reflect antigen quality and correspondence to the 
original antigen preparation used for selection.  
An extremely important point to stress is that all positive results obtained from monoclonal ELISA 
which in the previous sections have been presented as indications of binding between the scFvs and 
their toxins, might be incorrect. These positive hits can either reflect binding between scFv and toxin, 
scFv and neutravidin, or reflect unspecific binding of the scFv to the well. It is not possible to 
distinguish between these three scenarios based on the results obtained in the performed ELISA 
experiment, as no neutravidin or PBS controls were included. However, as neutravidin was only used 
for the second selection round during phage display in the used selection outputs, it is unlikely that 
neutravidin binders have been accumulated over toxin binders. Additionally, previous results 
obtained from monoclonal ELISA using both neutravidin and PBS controls have revealed extremely 
low absorbance for all controls. Though this cannot be translated directly to the experimental results 
of this project, it encourages optimism. 
 
Subconclusion 
Though monoclonal ELISA results indicate that specific scFvs have been obtained against two of the 
four most medically relevant venom fractions from N. melanoleuca, all monoclones should be 
retested using both neutravidin and PBS controls to confirm that hits reflect binding between scFv 
and toxin, rather than scFv and neutravidin.  
 
9.8. Future cross-reactivity tests 
In addition to directly retesting all monoclones with positive results, cross-reactivity tests should be 
conducted to determine the level of cross-reactivity between obtained scFvs and different toxins. In 
cross-reactivity test, an scFv is tested against one or more antigens different from the antigen used 
for the phage display selection. High levels of cross-reactivity have been observed previously 
(unpublished results), where scFvs cross-react with toxins that closely resemble those that they were 
raised against. Discovering cross-reactive antibodies is beneficial, as less antibodies are needed in an 
antivenom if each antibody can be used against two or more toxins. Additionally, obtainment of 
highly cross-reactive antibodies might be very beneficial as snake venoms, even within one species, 
can deviate greatly dependent on its geographical origin.  
==PAGE:64==
	  
64 
As a suggestion, selections against fraction 18+19 should be tested for binding against fraction 20 as 
both contain PLA2 and cytotoxin 1. The selections could also be tested against PLA2-containing 
fractions from other cobras whereas selections from fraction 8 should be tested against fractions from 
other cobras containing long neurotoxins.  
The level of cross-reactivity in a selection output can be increased by conducting cross-pannings 
where the antigen is switched between selection rounds when compared to traditional one-antigen 
pannings. Though the selection outputs used in this project were generated through traditional 
pannings, future phage display selections against N. melanoleuca venom could be conducted through 
cross-pannings against fraction 18+19 and fraction 20.  
10. Perspectives and future research 
In this thesis, the initial steps of venom fractionation, toxin biotinylation, phage display selection, 
plate test, subcloning, and monoclonal ELISA have been conducted for some of the most medically 
relevant venom fractions of N. melanoleuca venom. The most immediate future steps are those 
presented in section 9.8., regarding further ELISA testing. Additionally, selection outputs against 
fraction 1 and 20 should be subcloned. For an overview of the overall progress of development of a 
human recombinant antivenom against N. melanoleuca venom, see Figure 24 where previous work 
as well as work performed in this thesis (both successful and unsuccessful) for the medically relevant 
venom fractions is presented.  
In future work the scFvs should be tested in capture assays, where the toxin-specificity of the scFvs 
is evaluated without expression bias. For the most promising scFvs their binding characteristics 
should be assessed using Surface Plasmon Resonance. The scFvs should be sequenced and the most 
promising should be converted to fully human IgG and expressed in mammalian CHO cells, which 
is one of the most expensive stages in the developmental process. After expression, IgGs should go 
through ELISA tests before neutralization studies should be conducted in vivo. Through in vivo 
studies in CD-1 mice, the antibodies should initially be tested for their protective abilities against one 
venom fraction and the minimal protective toxin to antibody ratio should be determined. Once 
antibodies with neutralizing ability have been discovered against all the most medically relevant 
venom fractions, these should be tested for their neutralizing ability against whole N. melanoleuca 
venom. Most in vivo studies within antivenom are conducted according to WHO guidelines, where 
venom and antibodies are pre-incubated before injection, which is why this type of test should be 
performed. Additionally, it would be beneficial to test neutralization in a study where venom and 
antivenom are administered without pre-incubation at different injection sites, in order to closer 
resemble natural envenoming. The steps lying after the discovery phase are currently very uncertain 
as this type of antivenom will most likely have to be tested as a drug and not a blood product as is the 
case for current antivenoms.

11. Conclusion 
In this thesis, venom from N. melanoleuca was resolved in 33 fractions by RP-HPLC, and the 
chromatogram was compared to one published in a toxicovenomic study of this snake venom. 
Definite differences between chromatograms from specimens from three different geographical 
locations (Cameroon, Ugansda, and polled Ghana and Tanzania) further support previous claims 
about the need for a division of N. melanoleuca into several sub-species. Though there were many 
differences between the two chromatograms, definite similarities were also observed. From this 
comparison as well as a MALDI-TOF analysis of the fractions, the four most medically relevant 
fractions were identified as fractions 1, 8, 18+19, and 20. All of them contained toxins from the 3FTx 
family while fractions 18+19 and 20 additionally contained toxins from the PLA2 family.  
The venom fractions were biotinylated to prepare them for optimal display during phage display 
selection. The initial phage display selections carried out in this project were deemed unsuccessful 
after plate test results were obtained. The following selections could not be completed as no colonies 
were obtained after the first selection round. Although several troubleshooting experiments were 
conducted, no selections were successful in this master thesis project.  
For subcloning, previous selection outputs against N. melanoleuca were used. Antibody DNA from 
six different selection outputs against fractions 1, 8, and 18 were successfully subcloned and 
transformed into BL21 E. coli cells which generated between 129 and 593 colonies from each 
selection output.  
Monoclonal ELISA experiments were performed to identify clones expressing scFvs with high 
affinity to the selected snake toxins. Using this assay 122 hits against fraction 8 and 13 hits against 
fraction 18 were obtained. However, no hits were found against fraction 1. Further tests are needed 
to confirm target specificity of these scFv hits. Additionally, cross-reactivity tests should be 
performed along with capture assays and possibly Surface Plasmon Resonance.  
The results of this study suggest that human scFvs against two of the four most medically relevant 
venom fractions of N. melanoleuca, fraction 8 and 18 have, for the first time ever, been obtained. 
This brings the goal of developing the world’s first recombinant human oligoclonal antivenom against 
N. melanoleuca, and possibly cobras in general, one small step closer to reality.
