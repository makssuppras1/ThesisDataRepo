Abstract
Up to 1 billion people suffer from neurological disorders, leading to research in optoge-
netics and infrared neural stimulation as possible treatment methods. The scope of this
thesis is to construct an assembly for multifunctional neural interfaces. The assembly in-
corporates a ferrule to couple light, electrode pins for electrophysiological measurements
and a barbed fitting for connecting tubing. The design and development was carried out
on a Phrozen Mighty 8K 3D printer. Two different resins were used, namely a prototyp-
ing resin, Phrozen Aqua 3D, and a biocompatible resin, Formlabs Biomed Clear. The
3D printer was optimized and settings were found at which designs could be printed at
high resolutions, with both resins. The design was done in several stages until a satisfac-
tory assembly was created. The final weight of the fully assembled designs were 0.2225
g for the Phrozen resin and 0.2485 g for the Formlabs resin. Another special device
was developed that only incorporated fluid delivery. The final design was characterised
optically, electrically and by its fluid delivering capabilities. For the optical characterisa-
tion, the fiber attenuation was shown for two types of fiber. It was shown that light was
contained in the core when a laser was connected and that the light spread nicely into
a brain phantom. Electrical characterisation was done by measuring the impedance of
25 µm tungsten electrodes inserted in the fibers. This was done through electrochemical
impedance spectroscopy. It was found that the impedance stabilises after soaking the
electrodes in a Phosphate-Buffered Saline (PBS) solution overnight. It was also shown
that for reliable measurements the PBS solution needs to stabilise before use. Finally
the impedance was measured when applying 1 V and 5 mV. The 5 mV measurements
were very noisy but more realistic. The impedance was measured to lie between 1146
kΩand 206.44 kΩfrom 1 to 150 Hz. The average impedance between 900 and 1100
Hz was 77.23 kΩ. To conclude characterisation, fluid delivery was investigated through
pressure measurements and testing of delivery rate versus output rate. It was shown
that the output rate equaled the delivery rate (p=0.005). The pressure was shown to lie
well below the blowoff pressure of 7.21 bars when delivering at the tested delivery rates
for microfluidic channel diameters of 50 µm and 15 µm. It was shown that the fibers
could deliver fluid to a brain phantom, using Trypan blue dye. This experiment was
repeated in vivo at H. Lundbeck, showing promising results. The dye was delivered to
the dorsal striatum of three mice. The delivery was successful for two tests while there
was problems with flow in a third test where dye was injected in the collosum. The
assembly shows great potential for further development.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Approximately one billion people suffer from neurological disorders, including migraines,
Alzheimer’s disease and brain trauma. There is an important need for reading and stim-
ulation of the brain in order to possibly treat these neurological disorders [1].
The
discovery of optogenetics has helped pave the way for a potential solution. It has been
found that if certain proteins are injected in the brain, the brain cells will begin respond-
ing to light stimulation, making it possible to induce neural activation [2]. This has led
to many milestones being reached in the field of neuroscience. Combining optogenetics
with electrophysiology makes it possible to both stimulate and read neural signals from
the brain simultaneously.
Optogenetics relies on the delivery of light to the brain. Different techniques are utilized
to deliver light, such as connecting an external lasing source to an implanted fiber or
inserting micro LEDs directly into an implant [3]. In recent years neural interfaces, also
known as multifunctional optical fibers, have been developed. They deliver high precision
optical signals, covering the whole optical spectrum, to target areas of the brain, both
in vitro and in vivo [2],[4]. An issue concerning neural implants is chronic implantation
in the brain. Neural implants have a large rate of rejection, especially if the material
of the implant does not have the same material properties as the brain. This can lead
to scarring in the brain stemming from the foreign body response. Development of a
minimally invasive implant is of great importance. In recent years design of implants
with a Young’s modulus similar to that of the brain has been of special interest, as this
has been shown to reduce scarring [3]-[5].
An extensive topic of research is developing neural interfaces combining optogenetics
and electrophysiological readings. Reading and stimulation of neural signals are both of
paramount importance when developing a multifunctional neural interface. Many types
of implants have been developed such as fiber based optical neural interfaces. DTU
Photonics has developed several multifunctional neural interfaces, containing an optical
waveguide, tungsten electrodes and Microfluidic Channel (MFC). The neural interface
combines optogenetics, electrophysiological data and medical delivery [4].
Many different methods of packing neural interfaces have been proposed. Most packages
are not practical to mass produce. They are used mainly for testing in the lab and not
for creating a long lasting implant. An examination of packaging neural interfaces in a
sophisticated and easily constructed packaging is required. A method of mass developing
an easy to construct assembly could be through the use of high precision 3D printers.
3D printers can create small plastic casings in which connectorization of light, electrodes
and fluids is possible.
When designing an assembly that needs to be implantable, several requirements need to

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter presents the theoretical background for understanding the ideas behind the
design choices of creating a neural interface assembly. The main topics discussed are:
neurological treatments using light, multifunctional fibers, optical fibers for implants, as
well as the fundamentals of 3D-printing.
2.1
Light Sensitive Treatment Modalities
Since it was discovered that light could be used as a modality to stimulate the brain,
the topic has been of major interest [6]. The treatment method have been of special
interest in medical treatment of neural disorders. The three major techniques that are
investigated in this section are optogenetics, Infrared Neural Stimulation (INS) and
photopharmacology.
2.1.1
Optogenetics
Optogenetics work by stimulating a targeted area of the brain with light. Before stimu-
lation, the targeted area of the brain is injected with a light sensitive protein also called
microbial opsins. Opsins make neurons reactive to light stimulation [2]. Optogenetics
was discovered to work with millisecond precision on the mammalian brain in 2005 [6].
Some opsins used in optogenetics can be found in figure. 2.1. Taking the ChR2 opsin as
an example from figure 2.1, the optogenetic method works by engineering the ChR2 gene
together with a promoter that drives a desired expression. This engineered construct
is delivered to the brain using a viral vector. After delivery, the ChR2 opsin reaches
the cell type that is desired for activation. A fiber is implanted in the same area of the
brain and light is delivered. The ChR2 opsin injected depolarizes the cell membranes
of the neurons thus lowering the threshold for neural activation and increases neural
firing. Alternatively, if a construct with NpHR had been injected, neural firing would
have decreased instead [15].

From the previous example three main components of optogenetic examination can be

or specific cell types [16].
2.1.2
Infrared Neural Stimulation
INS is a way of inducing neural firing without having to inject opsins into the brain [17].
INS has been tested in various parts of the brain and body, such as the peripheral nerves,
cranial nerves, the brain and the heart [18]. INS has some key benefits such as being
usable alongside electrophysiological measurements without causing interference. This
is due to the low energy levels of the Infrared (IR) light. IR wavelengths are between 780
nm and 1 mm. Deep brain targeting is very effective with INS as the penetration depth
is easily tuned by varying the wavelength of the IR light. INS is able to achieve high
spatial resolution on the micrometer scale [17]. The most effective wavelengths used in
INS have been shown to range from 1844 to 2120 nm, but wavelengths between 1400
to 1600 nm have also been used effectively [18]. In this context, effect can be boiled
down to the highest safety margin, so effectiveness of stimulation of the target versus
damage to the tissue. This opsin free way of targeting specific regions in the brain works
as the biological tissue in mammals effectively absorb IR wavelengths. The absorption
mainly comes from the water in the tissue.
The IR light alters the temperature of
the targeted area of the brain. The neurons in the targeted area heats up, generating
thermal variation in the neurons leading to neural activation. The underlying biophysical
mechanisms of INS are still not clear. One theory is that the heat from the IR light
lowers the electrical capacitance of the cell membranes thus depolarizing the cell. The
potential in the cell rises above the threshold for neural activation, thus starting neural
firing. [19]. The effect of INS has been shown to be fully reversible as it only affect the
cell membrane while active [18].
2.1.2.1
Photopharmacology
Another popular way of treatment using light is photopharmacology. Photopharmacol-
ogy works by utilizing photochromic compounds. Photochromic compounds have two
conditions: they are transformed from one form to another when illuminated by light
and they induce different effects depending on if they are illuminated or not [20] [21].
Light influences the photochromic compounds by changing their pharmacokinetic or
pharmacodynamic properties.
Photochromic compounds get injected directly inside the cell or at the surface. An ex-
ample could be photochromic ion channel blockers. They are compounds that block ion
channels and opens others. This leads to changes in ion transports from extracellular to
intracellular regions [20].
==PAGE:20==
2.2 Multifunctional Implantable Fibers
8
Synthetic photoswitches are another commonly used compound. They enable fast and
reversible control of biological systems. Photoswitches works in two ways: they act as
the link between two parts of a drug. If the photoswitch is illuminated the photochromic
compound isomerizes thus changing the distance between the two parts of the drug caus-
ing activation [20]. Such a switch could be the photoswitchable ATA, it acts on GluA2
receptors, but is deactivated by blue light (480 nm) illumination. [22].
The second use of photoswitches involve binding the photoswitch to a one-piece drug.
Light stimulation changes the sensitivity of the receptors towards the drug. This was
used to control drugs acting on glutamate and GABAA receptors [20].
One of the biggest obstacles in the above treatment methods is how to reliably deliver
medicine and light to the brain. Recently implantable multifunctional optical fibers have
been developed. These fibers have the ability to deliver light and medicine simultane-
ously.
2.2
Multifunctional Implantable Fibers
Several types of implantable multifunctional fibers are being developed or have been
developed in the past years. Multifunctional fibers have many different use cases, for
example optogenetics or INS. Multifunctional fibers are structured fibers, that along
with an optical waveguide, incorporate microfluidic channels and/or electrodes in the
cladding. Optical waveguides are presented in section 2.3.1.
2.2.1
Drug Delivery using Multifunctional Implantable
Fibers
An important purpose of multifunctional fibers, is their ability to deliver chemical agents
reliably and precisely to specific areas in the brain. Neural activation can be altered by
delivering different pharmacological compounds.
This can lead to increased control
of specific receptors in the nervous system, or can give control of specific genetically
identifiable neural populations [23].
Traditional pharmacology rely on delivering compounds via the blood stream and only
a small part of the compound gets through the blood-brain barrier. There has been a
great demand for easy delivery of pharmacological compounds directly to the brain, to
achieve more effective delivery. In recent years, this has been achieved by drawing fibers
with MFCs in the cladding of optical fibers [24]. Recently, multifunctional fibers have
been developed, capable of delivering soluble pharmacological compound using MFCs,
while reading neural signals using electrophysiological measurements [25]. These neural
interfaces have become highly popular in the field of optogenetics and photopharmacol-
ogy.

2.2.2
Optogenetics and Electrophysiology
During optical stimulation of neurons, it is important to track if the correct neurons are
are targeted. There are many ways to track activity in the brain, such as Magnetic Reso-
nance Imaging (MRI) or optical functional imaging [26]. Another popular and practical
way to track neural activity is through electrophysiological measurements. Electrophys-
iology works by implanting electrodes in the targeted area of the brain and measuring
changes in potential. Electrophysiology has the advantage that it can be implemented
directly in optical fibers, with the electrodes being placed in the cladding of the fiber.
Such fibers are commonly called optrodes. Electrophysiology is a technique used to keep
track of neural activity, by measuring the electrical currents and potentials occurring
in living creatures. Electrophysiology can achieve spatial resolution as low as 10 µm,
with a temporal resolution in the subsecond scale along with sensitivity of 10 µV [27].
A benefit of electrophysiology is its high spatial and temporal resolution. Electrophysi-
ology measures changes in membrane potentials such as action potentials or Local Field
Potential (LFP) [28].
Neurons have a membrane potential.
This membrane potentials has a threshold at
which neural firing is activated. Action potentials are brief peaks of energy exceeding
the threshold for neural firing. Action potentials are caused by the switching of ion gates
in cell membranes. The change is very quick and is measured at approximately 1 kHz.
Another commonly measured potential is the LFP. It is a slower change in potential,
that happens as a consequence of several action potentials occurring in the same area.
This is a slower change happening at 1 to 150 Hz.
Compared to other implantable tracking modalities, electrophysiology is noninvasive on
the cell level and fades with time, while being non toxic [29]. The implanted electrodes
often sample at a frequency of 20 kHz. The issue with electrophysiological measure-
ments is that they are prone to signal degradation, crosstalk between the electrodes
and artifacting. The artifacts appear due to photo-electric artifacting, when electromag-
netic waves at specific wavelengths impede on the electrodes. Free electrons are effected,
generating measurable currents. This creates sharp artifacting peaks that needs to be
removed computationally [29]. It has been discovered that by using glass fibers this
artifacting disappears [30]. Compared to MRI, electrophysiology is an invasive method
of measurement as it has to be implanted, thereby causing damage to the brain.
2.2.3
Implantable Electrodes and Optrodes
Electrodes are the main component of electrophysiological measurements. They can
roughly be divided into two categories: noninvasive electrodes and implantable elec-
trodes [31]. Noninvasive electrodes are primarily used for electroencephalography (EEG).
Implantable electrodes is a term used for the electrodes used in invasive surgery. They
are implanted on either the dura matter, on the cortex or fully inside the brain. Im-
plantable electrodes can once again be split into two different categories: penetrating and
non-penetrating electrodes. As the names implies, the electrodes are either implanted
on the surface or penetrates inside the brain. When combining optical fibers and elec-

10
trodes for electrophysiological measurements, microwires are used. Microwires are small
and flexible reducing the chance of causing inflammation in the brain due to mechanical
mismatches, while having a high signal to noise ratio. Microwires made from certain
metals generate a toxic response in the brain leading to inflammation [32]. Tungsten
electrodes have been found to have high biocompatability and good mechanical prop-
erties [33]. The problem with tungsten is its large Young’s modulus of 120 GPa. The
Young’s modulus determines the stiffness of solid materials defined as the ratio between
tensile stress and the tensile strain. Since tungsten is very stiff compared to the Young’s
modulus of the brain (3kPA), it can cause scaring in brain tissue if directly implanted
[31]. To circumvent this issue the electrodes are usually implanted surrounded by a
flexible polymer with a sharp tip.
2.2.3.1
Optrodes
When measuring neural activity, it is important to be close to the area of stimulation.
The maximum distance, at which action potentials can be measured, is approximately
100 µm [34]. To get close to the center of activity, optrodes are used [35]. Optrodes
show great potential in simultaneous stimulation and reading of neural activity [36].
Integration of the microelectrodes directly in the cladding of the optical fiber reduces
the issue of the high Young’s modulus of the electrode, as the cladding of the fiber can
be made very flexible [35]. An issue with optrodes is the fact that drawing the fiber
directly with the electrodes inside is very challenging. The electrodes therefore needs to
be manually inserted into MFCs made in the fiber cladding. The issue has been partially
solved by filling MFCs with indium and connecting the tungsten electrodes in a small
cutout in the fiber [17].
2.3
Optical Fibers for Implants
When constructing optical fibers for implantation, many considerations needs to be taken.
Naturally, the fiber needs to work as an optical waveguide, while some multifunctional
fibers also incorporate other features, such as MFCs and electrodes. It is important that
these optical fibers are not too stiff and that they are made of materials that can be
inserted into the brain.
2.3.1

visual spectrum of light from 380 nm to 750 nm. Usually Near Ultraviolet (NUV) and

in the visible range. Optical waveguides work by guiding light. The light is usually
contained inside of a fiber core surrounded by a cladding. The light is contained in the
core due to the core having a larger refractive index than the cladding surrounding it,

2.3.2
Polymer Fibers
The most common types of optical fibers for terrestrial communication are usually made
from silica. When designing an implantable fiber this is not an ideal material. The high
stiffness of silica can lead to scaring in the brain. Polymer fibers, on the other hand, are
much softer. The two most common abbreviations for polymer fibers are Polymer Op-
tical Waveguide (POW)s and Polymer Optical Fiber (POF). Some advantages of POFs
for implants are their high strain limit of 3%, flexibility, non toxicity and high biocom-
patability in the soft tissue of mammals [26]. Common materials used when drawing
POFs are Plexiglass (PMMA) and Polycarbonate (PC). POFs made from PMMA are
optically transparent in the range from 350nm to 850 nm and have an absorption coeﬀi-
cient of 15 dB/m at 650 nm. PC has the same optical transparency range as PMMA but
is slightly more absorbing in the visible range from 350 nm to 750 nm. PC has higher
glass transition temperatures, when being drawn. Fibers with a PC core and PMMA
cladding have been shown to work effectively in vivo for recording and stimulating in
the brain and spine of anesthetized rats [35]. When creating fibers for use in the IR
range, the absorption is mostly driven by losses due to molecular vibrations. It has
been proposed to use fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP) copolymers and Polysulfone
(PSU) in this optical range instead. These materials are very soft and are desirable when
creating implantable neural interfaces, while still keeping a high NA [17].

2.4
Resin 3D-Printing
3D printing was first reported by Hideo Kodama in the early 1980s [38]. It is a technique
that involves curing resins to harden them. This is done by photocuring, also known
as UV curing, heat curing or other curing methods.
This section will give a short
introduction on resins and resin 3D-printing. Finally, the section will cover different
types of resin 3D printers as well as resolution of the printers.
2.4.1
Resins
Resins generally speaking, are substances with a high viscosity that can either be found
in nature or manufactured synthetically.
Resins are specific types of polymers that
are a primary component in plastics and many coatings. The term is mostly used for
prepolymers that sets thermally. The term is also used for prepolymers that are cured,
such as epoxy resin or photocurable resins [39]. These photocurable resins are also often
refereed to as photopolymers [40]. From this point forward, photocurable resins will be
simply called resins unless specified.
When 3D printing, it is important that the materials used are lightweight and durable.
The properties of each resin depends on the manufacturer and the resins chemical for-
mulation. Different manufacturers design resins for different printer types. Some resins
are designed for high powered lasers and other resins for weaker lasers. There are sev-
eral kinds of 3D printing resins on the market, from extra durable mechanical resins, to
==PAGE:26==
2.4 Resin 3D-Printing
14
medical grade resins, all the way to flame retardant resins. Since these resins are all
made of different compounds, they have different requirements for how the 3D printer
is configured and set up. The most important factor when choosing a resin, is that the
curing wavelength matches the wavelength of the chosen laser. If this is not done, the
resin will not harden and cannot be used. The resin will cure even with a weaker laser,
at the curing wavelength, but needs to be exposed to light for a longer time, compared
to a strong laser.
2.4.2
The 3D-Printer
Most resin 3D printers work in roughly the same way. A light source illuminates pho-
tosensitive resin, thereby hardening it. This process is then iterated in layers until the
full design has been printed. A general sketch of a Masked Stereolitography (MSLA) 3D
printer can be seen in seen in figure 2.3A.
Figure 2.3: MSLA 3D printer reproduced from [41].
Figure. 2.3B shows a general model of the internals of a 3D printer. The printer works
by filling the resin into the vat. The bottom of this vat is covered by a Non-Fluorinated
Ethylene Propylene (nFEP) sheet. The light from a laser shines through the nFEP
thereby hardening the resin.
Above the vat a build plate is controlled by a precise motor moving the build plate in the
z-direction. This plate is moved down to the bottom of the vat with only a small space
between the build plate and nFEP sheet. The z-axis resolution is defined by how small
of an increment the motor can move and thus how small the space becomes between the
build plate and the nFEP sheet.
When resin is exposed to light it hardens and sticks to the nFEP sheet and the build

plate. The build plate is designed to have higher adhesion than the nFEP sheet, making
the printed design stick to the build plate. The build plate retracts and the layer sticks
to the build plate. The build plate is then lowered into the resin again after a short
pause at the top, but at one layer height higher than before. This step is then repeated
with the build plate being lifted a little higher each time, printing the design in layers.
It is important to note, that if the exposure time of light is too short, the resin will
not stick to the build plate and will instead break off and float in the resin or stick to
the nFEP sheet. If the exposure time on the other hand, is too long, light bleeding will
occur, leading to resin outside of the desired area hardening. This leads to a fine balance
when setting the exposure for printing [42].
2.4.3
Resin Printer Types
The biggest difference in resin 3D printers is how the resin gets exposed to light. The
three most common type of resin 3D printers are, Stereolitography (SLA), Digital Light
Processing (DLP) and MSLA. The different printing types can be seen in figure. 2.4

SLA is the traditional type of 3D printing. The resin is exposed directly by a UV-laser
source emitting light at the same wavelength as the resins solidifies at. The laser is very
precise and causes the resin to solidify in the given pattern of the design. This type of
printing is slower, as the laser traces the design in each layer and does not illuminate
the whole layer at once. The xy-resolution is measured in beam width as the size of the
laser determines resolution in the layers. This printer type is very high detail [43]. SLA
has minimal light bleeding as the laser is very precise and only a small amount of light
bleeds out from the area under illumination.

MSLA also know as LCD printing, cures one full layer at a time by utilizing an LCD
display and a LED UV light array. The LED array functions as the light source, while
the LCD display projects the light pattern of the specific layer of the design onto the
vat. The resolution of MSLA printers is given in pixel size instead of beam width, as
the resolution depends on the amount of LEDs in the design. This method keeps a high
detail rate and is faster than the SLA printer [43].
DLP uses an arc lamp projector to solidify the resin. The arc lamp projects the design
of the specific layer directly onto the vat. To generate the optical pattern, DLP printers
uses a Digital Micromirror Device (DMD) inside of the projector. The DMD consists
of several mirrors. These mirrors reflect the light in a certain pattern onto the surface
of the vat. This is the fastest way of 3D printing since it exposes the resin to a larger
amount of light while still maintaining a high resolution [43]. An issue with DLP printers
compared to some MSLA printers is that they have more bleeding, meaning the light
shines on parts of the resin that are not meant to harden.

This chapter contains the fundamentals of setting up and validating 3D prints. The
chapter also includes the prototyping steps and final design. This chapter will cover
setting up the 3D printer for printing with the resins, how to create support structures
for printing and slicing a print. The prototyping will also be covered. A short design

3.1
3D printing
After preparing a design in a CAD program, 3D printing can generally be split into
two steps: preparation of the 3D model on a slicing software, and printing and post
processing of the design [42]. These two steps vary depending on the 3D printer type
and brand. Some printers have proprietary slicers while others use open source software
to prepare the prints.
The slicer used in this thesis is Chitubox Basic, which is a free program that is recom-
mended for use with Phrozen printers [44].
The printing process in this thesis was done on the Phrozen Mighty 8K which is a
MSLA type printer. The printer has a 10” LCD resulting in a build volume of 218 x 123
x 235mm. The LCD has an 8K resolution leading to a print resolution of 28µm in the
xy-plane. The motor moves in the z-direction with a resolution of 10µm [45].
3.1.1
Preparing the 3D Print on a Slicing Software
The first step when preparing a 3D print after designing the print in a CAD program,
is slicing it. The slicing software is where the 3D design is converted into a printable
format. In the slicer all the settings are calibrated for the printing process. The slicer
usually read .stl (stereolithography) files, which are mesh files exported from the CAD
program [42].

3.1 3D printing

3.1.1.1
Print Setting and Exposure
When in the slicing software the most important steps of the print are defined such
as choosing the layer height of the design and adjusting the printing settings.
The
settings chosen in the slicer are paramount to achieving a high resolution print. The
resin manufacturer usually attaches general print setting to get a successful print [46].
A common rule of thumb is that the resin specifications, are a surefire way to achieve
successful prints. These resin settings are generally given for layer heights of 50 µm.
However these settings will have a small loss in detail. If a high amount of detail is
needed the settings needs to be optimized, which is done by printing resolution targets
which will be discussed in section 3.2.
The settings listed below are usually tweaked when optimizing prints [42]:
• Bottom Layer Count: The amount of layers which are defined as bottom layers.
Bottom layers secures adhesion to the build plate. They are usually not part of
the intended design but are designed to be removable after the print is finished.
• Layer Height: Sets the height of the printed layers. This setting greatly effects
other settings. A smaller layer height means that most of the other settings needs
to be lowered to secure acceptable fidelity in the design. Smaller layer heights
increases the time of printing, but also increases detail in the print. 50 µm is the
standard layer height. A layer thickness below 50 µm is considered fine printing,
while a layer height of 10 µm is defined as ultra-fine printing.
• Exposure Time: The time that that the printer will expose the layers to the
light source. Changing the exposure times affects the quality of the print. Each
individual resin has its own chemical formulation and require different exposures
to harden. Overexposure can lead to light bleeding, so more resin than desired,
ends up hardening. Underexposure on the other hand effects the design as well, the
resin might not harden at all, or stick to the nFEP sheet. For non clear resins, six
seconds, of exposure are usually enough for hardening. If the resins are clear this
exposure time can be significantly longer, as not all the light is absorbed. Bottom
Layer exposure time is 8 to 12 times longer than for normal layers, except for clear
resins where the bottom exposure time is 3 times as long.
• Lifting Distance: Defines the distance the build plate rises after each layer has
been exposed. This is done to ensure that the print releases from the FEP and
sticks on the build plate. Lifting distance is tunable but changing it is generally
not recommended.
• Lifting Speed: the speed of the motor when lifting the design off the nFEP sheet.
Changing this setting can increase the build speed but also put a strain on the
model if it is pulled to quickly from the nFEP shhet. Generally, the speed should
be set at around 1-2 mm/s.
• Rest Time After Retracts: This setting defines how long the build plate rests
at its highest level before being lowered back into the resin. Changing this can be
==PAGE:31==
3.1 3D printing
19
beneficial if there are places in the design where resin needs to leak out before the
next exposure cycle.
These different settings are repeated both for the bottom layers and the top layers when
printing. These are all adjusted in the slicing menu of the slicer. The slicer software has
many of the popular resin settings presaved. Since the proposed design is printed at 30
µm, the resin settings need to be optimized for this layer height.
After having set up the printer, it is important to place and orient the design correctly.
It is possible to manually rotate the design or move it from the center of the build plate,
in case there is a compromised area on the nFEP sheet. Rotating the design, using
the rotation tool, can be very practical if there are hollow areas. Angling the design
can help unwanted liquid resin leak out of the design. Slicer software have many more
features for adjusting designs, but they were not used in this thesis. The features were
for creating holes in the design or for scaling the design. These tools are very practical
as redesigning in CAD programs can be time consuming, so small changes are usually
easier to do directly in the slicer.
3.1.1.2
Creating Support
When 3D printing, it is not advised to print directly on the build plate. This is due to
the bottom layer exposure washing out details in the print, since the higher exposure
time cures more resin than required. To circumvent this, the design is lifted from the
build plate by supports. Another benefit of supporting a design, is that it enables the
design to be rotated, letting leftover resin leak out, if it pools in the design. When
adding supports, it is essential to add them at appropriate locations and in appropriate
numbers. The placement and amount of support is especially important for big prints,
or prints with large overhangs, so things not supported by resin underneath. Having
inadequate support in overhanging areas, can lead to bending or breakage. Supports
helps with adhesion to the build plate, as they create contact areas between the build
plate and design. Depending on the size and structure of the design, different sizes of
support can be used. If the design is big, or there is a large overhang, bigger supports
might be required. If the design is small, smaller supports might be adequate. Bigger
supports have the disadvantage of requiring more resin while being harder to remove.
Bigger supports can create small indents where they connect to the design. Usually it
is beneficial to face the least important part of the design down towards the supports.
Slicer programs can add supports automatically or they can be added manually. The
automatic supporting adds supports where it detects there are needs for support struc-
tures. The simplest method of adding supports is usually by letting the slicer add them
automatically.
Afterwars supports can then be removed or added depending on the
design features [42]. When adding supports the most important part is to add support
at the bottom most point of the design, as it otherwise will not connect to the rest of
the structure.
After the design has been prepared by adding supports and adjusting the settings, it is
ready for printing. The design is sliced by the software and converted into a compatible

file, where the printer can recognize and print each individual layer.
3.1.2
Printing and Post Processing of 3D Print
After the design has finished printing, it needs to be post processed. Post processing
consists of removing the design from the build plate, as well as washing and post curing
it. After the print finishes successfully, it will stick to the build plate. The design is
ideally removed using a plastic spatula, but if the design is very hard to remove, a metal
spatula might be needed. However, using a metal spatula can scratch the build plate
and should be avoided. After removing the design, it will contain residual resin, from
being continuously dipped in the resin during the printing process. The residual resin is
washed off using either ethanol or isopropanol. This thesis uses ethanol, as it is a safer
way to wash the design, as isopropanol is carcinogenic. Washing can be done by hand
or by an automated ultrasound washer. In this thesis the designs were hand washed as
there were no available washer. This might lead to some resin sticking as hand washing
is less thorough than machine washing, leading to not all resin being washed off. After
the wash, the designs are soft and can easily break. This is due to the fact that if the
design was to fully harden in the 3D printer, the amount of exposure would lead to
bleeding of light, thus making the print unusable.
To harden the designs after washing, a post curing chamber is used. They work much
like a microwave oven with a rotating plate in the middle and contains several LEDs
emitting at the curing wavelength of the resins. The LEDs are placed in the chamber
so the whole design is illuminated. The Sunlu Post curing chamber was used in this
project. It is important that the design has completely dried, before it is post cured. If
the design is wet it can lead to the design not curing properly and it will be fragile or
feel wet to the touch [47]. Depending on the size and fragility of the design the support
can be removed at any point during these post processing step. The most common is to
remove them after washing the design, but it can also be done after post curing if the
design is very fragile.

Before printing the prototypes on the 3D printer, the resins have to be validated. The
validation is done to optimize the settings, to get the highest resolution of the print.
The most important setting changed are the two exposure times.

is a small design that is intended to be printed directly on the build plate. Resolution
targets contains several features that get blurred or disappear if the exposure and print
settings are not optimized correctly for the resin. How precise the optimization needs to
be and what needs to appear depends on how complicated and detailed each individual
design is [49]. The Phrozen XP Finder V2 resolution target is shown in figure 3.1.

the design does not harden enough, leading to extrusions or thin parts easily breaking.

3.3
Design of Assembly
The design of the packaging was done in several prototyping stages. The designs was
inspired by what has been published in the literature with the hope of creating a more
sophisticated design, viable for mass production [8] [50] [51]. The goal was to develop a
packaging for neural implants, which can stimulate parts of the brain with light, deliver
fluids and read electrophysiological data. This meant that the design of the assembly
could be split into three design points, reiterating the problem statements:
• How to deliver fluid easily to a multifunctional fiber?
• How can electrodes be implemented in an assembly, giving access to uncomplicated
electrophysiological measurements?
• How can fibers easily be connected to an external light source?
The final design requirements were that the assembly was easy to assemble, while weight-
ing below 2 grams. The design of the assembly ended up being split into two parts to
accommodate the fact that the electrodes had to be lead out and away from the fiber.
3.3.1
Design Overview
The design of the packaging had to consider the three main purposes of light delivery,
fluid delivery, and electrical measurements. A short design overview will be given below:
Light Delivery was at the center of the design. The idea was to use a ferrule to connect
the POF to a light source. The ferrule was placed in the center of the design and the
rest of the assembly was designed around the ferrule holder. The fiber could then be led
through a channel and connect to the ferrule inside of the design, figure 3.4.
==PAGE:39==
3.3 Design of Assembly
27
Fluid Delivery was one of the cornerstones in the design. It was decided that the
design should contain a hollow chamber, where fluid enters and passes into the MFCs in
the fiber. The chamber also allowed for more openings in the fiber, since the chamber
encompasses the fiber, figure 3.4. The fluids are delivered using a plastic tube connected
to a fitting. This fitting was designed as a barbed fitting to allow for easy connection
and disconnection of the tubing. Barbed fittings were chosen over other fitting types
such as push-to-connect fittings and compression fittings due to their simple design and
an aim to avoid use of other materials, such as o-rings or metal. Barbed fittings also
works better with soft tubing [52].
In other designs the connection is usually made of metal or harder plastic making it easier
to create durable connections [8], but this leads to them not being fully 3D printable.
To create a fully 3D printable design that did not leak, and knowing that the pressure
would not go too high, it was decided to use barbed fittings. Barbed fittings are easily
3D printable and deliver good connectorization below 8 Bar. Most barbed fittings are
designed for tubing larger than 1/16 inches (1.58 mm) [53].
Electrophysiology was the last major part the design to be considered. It was decided
to use a pin connector to connect to external connectors. The design for the electrode
part was initially chosen so that the electrode and the pin would connect using conduct-
ing glue but was later changed so that the pin connectors and the electrodes interlocked
inside the electrode channel, figure 3.5. To avoid short circuits of the electrodes two
channels separating the electrodes were made.
Device assembly had to be considered as well. The design was chosen to be rounded
to cut down on weight compared to a square design. The design could not be made
as a single assembly as that would make it impossible to connect the electrodes to the
pins. The assembly was therefore split into two parts. These two parts had to be as-
sembled. Initially the parts were intended to be clicked together and then sealed. This
evolved, first to a rail connector and at the end the assembly was assembled by making
the electrode part fit inside of the MFC part of the assembly.

3.3.3
Sealing
The design of the packaging had to be done in two parts to enable the electrodes to be
led out of the design. To make sure that there were no leaks from the design the two
parts had to be fused together and sealed. Three different materials were investigated
as materials for sealing: the resin used for printing, UV sensitive glue and epoxy. The
designs were sealed and it was tested if the sealing had led to a blockage of the MFCs
in the fibers or the liquid chamber. If the ratio between successful sealing and blocked
channels was too high, another sealing method was attempted. The sealing tests were
done alongside the prototyping to see if different prototypes reacted differently to dif-
ferent sealing methods. The sealant was carefully applied around the fiber at both the
input and output of the fiber channel using a small syringe to create a complete seal.
3.3.3.1
Resin Sealing
Resin sealing was tested on both the Phrozen and Formlabs resin, since the resins had
different densities, leading to the idea that the effectiveness of the sealing depended on
the viscosity of the resin.
Designs made of Phrozen and Formlabs resins were sealed with both types of resin,
since the design did not change depending on the resin. Good results were obtained,
with designs, that did not block the MFCs of the fiber.
Since the resin had a low
viscosity, it quickly got into cracks and crevices. To ensure that the resin hardened
quickly, a UV gun was used to cure the resin. This proved to be a good, but labour
intensive method. When the resin was hardened using the curing chamber, the resin had

problem was where the resin sealing was applied with the Phrozen Aqua Clear 3D resin.
The Phrozen resin had to be applied on the outside of the design and not by the fiber

UV Glue Sealing
The glue was tested because of its higher viscosity compared to resin. The glue was
applied using the tip of a syringe around the holes where the fiber and ferrule were
placed. The glue was also put around the entire edge between the two parts to make
sure no leakage would occur. UV glues was not a viable method of sealing, as the curing
time of the UV-glue was simply too long, and even though it had a higher viscosity
compared to that of resin, the slow curing time of the glue meant that it would still
penetrate and enter the MFC.

3.3.3.3
Epoxy Sealing
The final material tested for sealing was epoxy. Epoxy is even less viscous than the UV

design until it can be tested and used. A solution to this could be fast drying epoxy.
The epoxy sealing immediately gave good results as the first designs showed a complete
seal. The epoxy could be placed at both the base of the design as well as by the ferrule
holder simultaneously. Due to epoxy’s high viscosity a complete seal could be achieved,
and the liquids were contained in the chamber, with minimal loss in other parts of the
device.

4.2
Electrode Characterisation
Multifunctional fibers often include electrodes to enable electrophysiological measure-
ments. This requires good knowledge of the impedance of the electrodes. The impedance
characterisation was carried out by Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) ex-
periments. It is a technique which measures the electrical properties of materials. EIS
is especially practical for impedance measurements of electrodes which will come into
==PAGE:59==
4.2 Electrode Characterisation
47
contact with biological matter. Compared to normal impedance measurements, EIS con-
siders the electroactive parts of the solution, in which the electrodes are inserted. EIS
measures the current response of the electrode over a wide range of frequencies. The
EIS measuring device used in this thesis is a Hioki IM3590. The Hioki IM3590 works by
determining the transfer functions of the property of interest [59]. In this thesis, the ab-
solute impedance and phase of tungsten electrodes with a cross sectional diameter of 25
µm were measured. The electrodes were inserted in a 0.01M Phosphate-Buffered Saline
(PBS) solution to mimic the electrical properties of the brain. PBS is a water-based
saline solution containing disodium phosphate and sodium chloride. It is important to
note that the impedance is measured directly at the interface between the solution and
the electrode.
The Hioko IM3590 can do two- or three wire EIS, where the difference is if the device
measures current, or current and voltage, for better results. The Hioko IM3590 was set
up in a three electrode measurement configuration. The setup consisted of a reference
electrode, a counter electrode and the electrode under test. The reference electrode was
provided by Mettler-Toledo InLab Expert Pro pH and is made of silver/silver chloride.
The counter electrode and electrode under test were both made of tungsten. The Hiokio
IM3590 contains four input ports: high potential , high current, low potential and low
current. The high current and high potential ports were connected to the clamp con-
nected to the electrode under test. The low current port was connected to the counter
electrode and the low potential port was connected to the reference electrode. EIS ap-
plies a potential to the electrode under test and measures the resulting current generated.
The current change arises due to three chemical processes in the solution: The charging
and discharging of an electric double layer created at the interface between the electrode
and the PBS solution. A second process occurs due to a redox reaction at the surface
of the electrodes. The last process is a diffusion of the redox ions moving to the surface
of the electrodes. Each of these processes are characterised by different time constants,
where the redox diffusion has the longest time constant [60], [61]. These time constants
are then found and the transfer function can be calculated, giving the impedance and
phase as an output.
When characterising the electrodes, the Hiokio IM35I90at had an instant readout mode.
This was used to verify if the electrode was in contact with the solution. The test setup
can be seen in figure 4.9. Every measurement was taken with an averaging of 10 mea-
surements.

4.3.1
Characterisation of Microfluidic Channels and
Barbed Fitting
The pressure was measured with a Wika A-10 pressure gauge. It measures up to 100
bars [65]. The Wika A-10 was set up in a two wire design, where the output of the
Wika A-10 delivers a current representing pressure. Wika A-10 gives an output current
ranging from 4mA to 20mA. 4mA is used as an active zero. The current range works as
a proportional representation thus at 4mA there is 0% pressure and at 20mA there is
100% pressure, equaling 100 bar. The Wika A-10 is a differential pressure gauge. It does
not measure absolute pressure but instead measures the difference in pressure between
the environment and what presses on it. This difference is measured by a piezoresistive
material inside of the Wika A-10. When this material is pressed by either a liquid or
gas, the resistance changes thus changing the current, as a constant voltage is delivered.
The Wika A-10 comes pre-tested showing a linearity error of: 0.21% at 0 bars and an
error of 0.17 % at 50 bar.
To read the signal, a 250 Ωresistance was used to read a voltage, ranging from 1V to
5V. This voltage was read by either an Arduino or an Analog Discovery 2. The Arduino
had 10 bit resolution leading to a resolution of 0.102 bar. The data was read using
the Arduino code in appendix. 5.1.1. The code converted the voltage to bars and the
data was processed in Python. The Analog Discovery 2 is a digital oscilloscope with
proprietary software. This software has a logger function in which the voltage over the

and processed directly. The Analog Discovery 2 had a 14 bit resolution, leading to a

The next test involved finding the blowoff rate. The mean (n=5) blowoff pressure was
found to be 7.21 bars for the thin silicone tube and 6.09 bars for the thick tube (n=2).
This was also the reason for the switch to a single barb fitting, as the multibarb had a
blowoff pressure of 5.52 bars when attached to the thick tubing. This could be because
the smaller tubing can get a better grip as it is more flexible. If the fitting was wet
before attaching the tube, the blowoff pressure fell to 6.34 bars. This is a bit lower than
the industry [67]. The lower blowoff rate can be due to the softness of the tubing, but
is probably also due to design features. The rounded edges of the barbs makes it easier
for the tubing to release as the grip is not as tight. The length of the barbed fitting also
plays a role, as the tubing does not have a lot to grip. A higher blowoff rate could be
achieved by a bigger percentual increase in barb diameter from top to bottom, but to
the detriment of connection and disconnection of the tubing. Since the pressure is never
intended to go very high, the barb size was concluded to be a good middle ground.

4.5
In Vivo Fluid Delivery
The delivery of fluid through 15 µm MFC channels was tested in vivo at H. Lundbeck, to
test delivery of trypan blue dye to a mouse brain. The surgery was performed at Lund-
beck’s animal testing facility, by Nicole Fadahunsi. H.Lundbeck provided everything
needed for testing of fluid delivery. The design under test was the special design, only
for fluid delivery described in chapter 3.4.6, as well as an older design, where a special
tube connector had been developed, matching the barbed fitting on the full design.
4.5.1
Method
The in-vivo test was carried out by delivering trypan blue dye to the dorsal striatum
on three eight week old C57BL/6J mice. Delivery was done using a CMA microdyalisis
pump, and a syringe connected to FEP tubing. This tubing was connected to the designs,
which was inserted in the target area of the brain. The mice were anaesthetised in a
gas chamber, before being prepared for surgery. The mice were secured on a stereotaxic
frame. The brains were exposed and 1% trypan blue dye was infused. The infusion rate
was set to 0.3 µL/min, after flow was achieved. After the dye had been infused, the
mice were immediately euthanized and the brains were extracted. The brains were put
in a 10% neutral formalin buffer for 24 hours, at room temperature. Then the brains
were transferred to a 30% sucrose solution for dehydration. The brains were snap frozen
==PAGE:73==
4.5 In Vivo Fluid Delivery
61
with dry ice, before being sectioned in 30 µm intervals on a cryostat. All three surgeries
were performed on the same day. The last mouse was under anaesthesia for over two
hours, due to challenges with achieving flow in the last device. The results and pictures
are provided by Nicole Fadahunsi.
4.5.2
Results
Coronal slices of the brain can be seen in figure 4.26, figure 4.27 and figure 4.28. It can
be seen that the first two injections were largely successful and the last injection was
partially successfully. All three brain slices have dye in the brain. The biggest outlier can
be observed in figure 4.28, where the dye is not located in the right region. Figure 4.28
suggest that the probe was inserted in the corpus collosum which is formed from denser
matter, than the dorsal striatum. This could be due to a manual error when placing
the probe. For the third mouse, there was trouble achieving flow before insertion and
the mouse was under anaesthesia for over two hours. This might be due to the tubing
expanding. It was shown in figure 4.22, that liquid only starts flowing after a certain
pressure is achieved. For a delivery rate of 1 µL/min it took over 5 hours to achieve
flow. With 5 µL/min it was found to take around 40 minutes. If one of the microfluidic
channels had been blocked, this would have required an even higher pressure, and thus
an even longer time frame. The design shows great potential for further testing as the
results were largely successfull for fluid delivery.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
5.1
Discussion
The aim of this thesis was to create an assembly for neural interfaces capable of stimu-
lating the brain with light, reading electrical signals and delivering fluids to MFCs in a
multifunctional fiber. The assembly had to be 3D printable and easy to assemble. First
suitable resins were chosen for 3D printing and prototyping. The resins chosen were
the Phrozen Aqua 3D for prototyping and Formlabs Biomed Clear as a biocompatible
alternative. The resins were chosen based on their biocompatability and due to the fact,
that a clear resin was desired, in order to observe the internals of the print. The resins
were tested on the Phrozen Mighty 8K 3D printer and the settings were set, so that
the designs were printed with a high resolution. First, exposure targets were printed
to determine the settings for printing with the resins. The first prototypes were then
designed and printed. It was decided that the assembly should contain two parts, a part
for electrodes and a part for fluid delivery and optical connection. The prototype was
designed in an iterative process. The final design had a small elctrode part, which only
contained electrode channels and a fitting for an electrode pin. The fluid delivery part
had a hollow chamber for delivery liquid to the MFCs of the fiber and a barbed fitting,
as well as a halo for stabilising a ferrule for light coupling. The fully connected design
weighted 0.2225 g and 0.2485 g for the Phrozen and Formlabs resins respectively. While
the assembly was in the prototyping stage, different tests were carried out. Different
materials were tested for sealing and connecting the assembly. The resin used for print-
ing was tested first and it was seen that due to the low viscosity of the resin, it easily
entered the fluid chamber and blocked the MFCs of the fibers. Some designs worked
with resin sealing, but the failure rate was very high. Both the Phrozen and Formlabs
resins were tested as sealants. Similar tests were carried out on UV sensitive glue. This
sealing also lead to blockages. Even though the glue was more viscous than the resin,
the increased curing time of the UV glue meant that it was not a viable solution for
sealing and connecting the assembly. Finally epoxy was tested as a sealant. It had a
very high viscosity and showed great potential as a sealing agent. The epoxy had the
highest full curing time, but the high viscosity led to very few blockages. The blockages
that occurred happened if the fiber was accidentally moved into curing epoxy. When
sealing with epoxy, it is important not to put too much epoxy in between the assembly
parts. The epoxy can get pushed into the fluid chamber if the epoxy has nowhere else
to go, leading to blockages. For all three sealant types, it is paramount that the exposed
MFCs of the fiber stay in the fluid chamber and do get in contact with the sealing. If
==PAGE:76==
5.1 Discussion
64
the openings for the MFCs touch the sealant, they will surely be blocked.
Different types of barbed fittings were investigated to see which type was most reliable
and stable. It was found that for less flexible tubing used in the start of the project,
double barbs led to less breaks of the barbed fitting. This was probably due to more
resin being used in the fitting, making the design more stable. After switching to a
softer tubing, a single barb design was used instead, leading to a good seal and higher
blow off pressure. A special assembly was also created for in vivo measurements of fluid
delivery. This design was made during the prototyping phase and therefore had double
barbs. The number of barbs showed to be of little importance, as the pressure when
delivering fluids never gets close to the blowoff pressure of the fitting. After the final
prototype had been designed, the assembly had to be characterised. Three principal
forms of characterisations were performed. Characterisation of light delivery through
the fiber, characterisation of the electrodes and their electrical impedance, and finally
the MFCs were characterised in their fluid delivery, pressure curves and an in vivo ex-
periment conducted at H. Lundbeck.
The fiber attenuation was plotted and shown, using the cutback method, and a super
continuum laser. The results revealed that both fibers had good transmission in the IR
spectrum excluding two peaks between 850 nm to 900 nm. The PC PMMA performed
better at 450 nm. Near field images of the fibers showed that the light in the different
fibers was contained in the core after exposing MFC and inserting electrodes. The light
was finally tested in a brain phantom and showed good scattering of the light.
Characterisation of the electrodes showed that low potential impedance measurements
at low frequencies, such as those observed from LFP potentials, contained a lot of noise.
At higher frequencies, such as those coming from action potentials, the noise decreases
significantly. The measurements were taken from 1 Hz to 20 kHz, as this is the usual
sampling rate when measuring electrophysiology. It was shown that the error of the
measurements decreased by 23% if the electrode was soaked in a PBS solution overnight.
It was also tested how the PBS solution stabilised over time by testing an electrode
on day 1 and day 3 of mixing the solution. This showed that the general impedance
was much lower on day 3, due to the ions in the solution having stabilized. The er-
ror was higher, since the error in the measurements did not change from day 1 to day
3. It was also tested how the impedance and error changed when characterising the
impedance at 1 V or 5 mV. This showed significantly higher impedance and higher er-
rors at 5 mV across the whole frequency spectrum. This needs to be considered when
measuring electrophysiology, as the action and membrane potentials are usually in the
µV range. The impedance at 5 mV, between 0 to 150 Hz was between 1146.46±206.09
kΩto 206.44±24.07 kΩ. The impedance between 900 Hz to 1100 Hz was on average
77.23±2.1 kΩ.
Finally, the fluid delivery capabilities of the assembly was tested. The assembly had a
blow off pressure of 7.21 bars. It was shown that at lower delivery rates, the pressure
increase was negligible. The pressure did not get close to the blowoff pressure, even over
a sustained time of delivery. It was shown that the relationship between the delivery
rate and the output rate was linear as expected. Testing of pressure was done for all
three fiber types, and the pressure increase was shown for different fibers and delivery
==PAGE:77==
5.1 Discussion
65
rates. These pressures were all shown to be sustainable for at least 45 minutes.
The in vivo fluid delivery test was done and shows great potential for future tests and
implementation in vivo. The test was done on the fiber with 15 µm MFCs and were
carried out on C57BL/6J mice. 1% Trypan blue dye was infused through the fiber into
the brains. Dissection of the first two brains showed good delivery at the intended target
area, the dorsal striatum. The last dissection showed that the fiber had been placed in
corpus collusum which was not the target area. This last test had problems with gener-
ating flow and getting the liquid through. This is probably due to the tube expanding
instead of sending the liquid through the MFCs. This happens due to the small diameter
of the channels, meaning that the it is easier for the liquid to expand the tubing, than
sending liquid through.
The assembly shows great potential for future use. The assembly is lightweight and
easy to assemble. If epoxy is applied in the correct amount and the fiber is kept fixed,
the success rate is very high. The success rate is especially high for the fibers where
electrodes had been implemented, as the glue worked as a natural plug for the fiber,
limiting movement.
To conclude, the research questions posed in the introduction have been answered. State
of the art packaging and assembly solutions have been presented. Suitable resins that
are clear, lightweight and biocompatible have been chosen for the assembly. Different
sealing and connection methods were tested on the packaging leading to the use of epoxy
as a sealant. The non 3D printable components were found to be a ferrule, electrode pins
and epoxy. The design was tested in vitro for light delivery and electrical impedance.
Fluid delivery, was tested both in vitro and in vivo, conducted at H.Lundbeck. It was
shown that the proposed assembly is suitable for its intended use case. The last question
yet to be answered is how the package can be improved for later use.
5.1.1
Future Works
The developed neural assembly has several potential development paths. Many things
can still be optimised and there are several tests that are yet to be done. The barb
could be made longer to increase blowoff pressure. Simultaneous tests of the different
functionalities was not done in vitro. Theoretically, the electrodes, fluid and fiber core
are independent of each other, but it still needs to be confirmed. The electrode and fiber
functionality was not tested in vivo. It was also not tested if the slope at the bottom
of the assembly helped when grafting the assembly onto skin. Another interesting test
could be longer in vivo experiments, focusing on how the design works when chronically
implanted. Finally optimising the assembling of the assembly could be vital for making
it mass producible. The design is easy to assemble but takes a lot of precision work and
concentration. If a holder of some kind was developed, assembling would become much
easier.
