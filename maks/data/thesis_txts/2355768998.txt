Abstract 
The global trend of sustainable urban growth imposes the focus of urban planning authorities on applying cost 
effective solutions to improve building performance on a neighbourhood or a larger scale. New generation 3D 
city modelling tools can effectively model multiple buildings, microclimatic effects and energy systems, giving 
an early view of the potential of sustainable scenarios to prioritize the energy interventions. Therefore, an 
effective estimation of the aggregated building energy use can indicate areas with high energy use intensity 
helping evaluating energy retrofit strategies to improve the stabilization of national energy networks. 
In the current study, the accuracy of a relatively new urban modelling tool, umi, is tested for a real case study 
for a small district in Sønderborg, Denmark. Advanced metering infrastructure provides heating and DHW 
measurements from buildings connected to the local district heating network. The building sample includes a 
variety of buildings constructed from 1931 to 2004. A total of seven building archetypes are created based on 
Danish typologies to represent 44 single-family houses and terraced houses. The urban model is created within 
the Rhino 3D CAD environment and the thermal model calculations are conducted through the plug-in of umi 
that integrates the EnergyPlus simulation engine. 
At a second level, in order to further validate umi, the archetypes created for the case study are simulated with 
a more detailed building simulation tool, IDA ICE. The results of the energy demand of the archetypes in IDA 
ICE are extrapolated to represent the energy demand of the district. The aggregated building energy use from 
both modelling tools is compared to test deviations from the real measurements.  
Additionally, scenarios of energy retrofits in line with Danish building typologies are implemented to calibrate 
the energy use of older buildings (1931-1950) according to real measurements. In particular, the case of 
renovation improves significantly the model’s accuracy decreasing the deviation from the measurements. 
Finally, some valuable design guidelines are concluded. 
Keywords 
Urban energy simulation tools, umi, building archetypes, Danish building typologies, energy retrofit, urban 
modelling, aggregate heat demand

1 Introduction   
In order to tackle the environmental challenges and meet the policy-driven carbon reduction targets, reducing 
the energy consumption and associated GHG emissions in urban cities is of outmost importance. Official 
records show that cities house more than half of the world’s population and is expected to host 75% until 2050. 
At the same time, cities account for 75% of energy consumption and 80% of CO2 emissions worldwide 
(Provoost, 2013).  
 
In particular, space heating and cooling together with water heating are estimated to account for nearly 60% 
of global energy consumption in buildings. Considering the fact that space and water heating demand is 
dominated by the use of fossil fuels, potential improvements in the building energy infrastructure represent a 
large opportunity to reduce energy consumption. Noted also that cooling demand is increasing especially in 
countries with carbon electricity systems as in China and United States (Interational Energy Agency, 2013).  
 
Urbanization and population growth indicates the implementation of smart and sustainable solutions by the 
city governments. The urbanization with smart control can have a positive effect on the climate and the 
economy as it can lead to less transport and a higher level of mobility. Furthermore, the urban density 
represents an excellent opportunity for integration of energy systems and networks of a city (i.e. transportation, 
communication, infrastructure, etc.) and creation of a more sustainable living (Copenhagen Cleantech Cluster, 
2012).  
 
The need for solutions that are in line with more sustainable urban growth and concepts feasible across a range 
of climates and cultures highlights the importance of the neighbourhood to be an economically, energy efficient 
and socially viable place, so that people want to live there (Reinhart, et al., 2013). More specifically, the 
solutions linked with sustainable development could vary from building retrofits (i.e. building envelope) to 
use of endogenous resources (i.e. waste heat) distributed with small grids to a neighbourhood scale (Fonseca 
et al., 2016).  
 
Ongoing efforts to transform the energy systems into more sustainable systems include the building integration 
of renewables organized on the urban scale. Yet the problem of supply and demand mismatch has not been 
overcome, since it requires extensive network or storage solutions. One solution could be to install networked 
renewable energy and storage systems so that energy may be shared and exchanged between buildings 
(Allegrini et al., 2015).  
 
In practice, a common solution to address the energy supply and demand mismatch is to treat them 
independently through regulation and voluntary certification.  One of the most popular green building 
certification programs used worldwide is LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) and its 
objective is to improve the efficiency of the new or retrofitted buildings though quantitative and qualitative 
methods. Additionally, energy supply has been moderated through a combination of renewable energy and 
financial incentives. Yet few methods are recorded to help the planners and policy makers to balance energy 
supply and demand interventions together as one issue (Best et al., 2015).  
 
To gain a better understanding of the energy supply and demand concepts, the authorities of the city should 
require planning tools and methods that can model the energy demand of buildings and meet the needs of the 
city’s stakeholders with regard to energy monitoring and planning. These tools need to be easily managed and 
interconnected with actively maintained databases so that they can be used and updated for a wide range of 
urban contexts (Cerezo, et al., 2014).  In contrast to the past, 3D city modelling tools are becoming essential 
tools for urban planners and municipalities to coordinate the decrease of building energy demand, to analyse 
the energy supply concepts as well as to plan strategies for sustainable transportation (Nouvel et al. 2013).

Objective of the study 
The present project deals with the urban energy simulation of a real case study in a Danish district. The case 
study is simulated with Urban Modelling Interface (umi) (Reinhart et al., 2013) and the accuracy of the tool is 
tested. Two different ways are proposed to validate the tool. Specifically, the simulation results of umi are 
compared with real energy measurements and results from another detailed building performance tool IDA 
ICE. 
 
In Section 2, an investigation of modelling in urban scale is performed. A literature review based on simulation 
methods and tools for urban building energy models is documented in order to select the appropriate software 
for the analysis of the case study. 
  
Section 3 presents a theoretical background, considered prerequisite to follow the study, that describes briefly 
the two simulation engines selected, umi and IDA ICE. Some information about the district of the case study 
is given, too. 
 
In Section 4, the methodology includes the description of the case study and the approach that was developed 
to handle the building sample and create building archetypes, which they are scaled up to represent the district. 
In particular, sources of information used to collect inputs for the archetypes are documented. Details and 
limitations about the modelling of the case study in umi and IDA ICE are highlighted. Guidelines for the 
analysis of the results give a better overview to the reader before moving to the next section. 
 
The results of simulating the case are presented and analysed in Section 5. The analysis includes the 
comparison of umi and IDA ICE with the real measurements, simulating first on individual level the archetypes 
and then in full scale modelling. Following, some scenarios of renovation are formed in order to improve and 
calibrate further the urban energy model. Deviations from real measurements are reported.  
 
Finally, based on the results some inaccuracies and limitations of the simulation tool are indicated. 
Uncertainties about the assumptions made to create the archetypes are discussed. Future work to investigate 
and overcome the limitations of the tool is suggested. At the end of the report, the bibliography and an 
Appendix with extra information of the study are presented.

2 Literature review 
 
Modelling methods for simulating energy use of the building stock 
Nowadays, a large share of published literature on reducing energy consumption in buildings deals with the 
treatment of energy demand of neighbourhoods or districts as a whole. Therefore, aggregation techniques have 
been developed to quantify the energy demand in an urban context. These techniques facilitate the local 
authorities to define policies for energy efficiency measures and changes in building operation (Choudhary, 
2011). Additionally, having a holistic view of the aggregate building demand can help identifying future energy 
needs as well as confront issues about resource use and power system stability. In particular, as the residential 
building stock is a dominant energy consumer, measures for shift of the building demand or energy retrofits 
of buildings can achieve flexibility and stabilization of the energy grid (Gianniou, 2014). 
 
According to (Swan & Ugursal, 2009) there are two main approaches to model the energy use of the building 
stock of a region or a country: top down and bottom up. Each approach is based on different levels of input of 
information and gives outputs with different applicability. The top-down and bottom up modelling techniques 
for estimating the residential energy consumption of a district are illustrated in Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1 Top down and bottom up modelling methods (Swan & Ugursal, 2009). 
 
2.1.1 Top down method 
The top down models treat the building stock as an energy sink and do not analyse energy consumption at the 
individual building level (Swan & Ugursal, 2009). In particular, these models work on an aggregate level, 
drawing information from national databases of building performance (i.e. building features, energy use, etc.) 
and representing a historical time series of energy consumption or CO2 data.  
 
The effect on the energy consumption in buildings can only be interpreted in a macroscopic base due to long 
term changes or transitions in the residential sector. Top down models can be categorized in econometric and 
technological models. The first category include macroeconomic indicators (i.e. gross domestic product 
(GDP), income, fuel prices, and employment rates) as well as climatic conditions (i.e. the population-weighted 
temperature for a country)  (Kavgic et al., 2010). On the other hand, the technological top down models refer 
to broad characteristics of the energy use (i.e. appliance ownership trends).  
 
The strength of the top down approach is its simplicity since the user makes only use of aggregate data, easily  
trackable and widely available. However, the reliance on historical data indicates also the major limitations of 
this approach: To rely on data of past energy economy, not capable to model discontinuous changes in 
technology (i.e. the effect on the energy consumption after a large increase in housing construction). In

addition, since the information about the energy consumption related to individual end-uses is limited, there is 
not much to identify the potential of improvements in specific areas (Swan & Ugursal, 2009). 
2.1.2 Bottom up method 
The bottom up models treat the buildings on a disaggregate level and are based on a set of archetypical 
buildings that are representative for the building stock. Therefore, they need extensive databases with empirical 
data to define each archetype in detail (Kavgic et al., 2010). The bottom up models calculate the energy 
consumption for individual houses or a group of buildings and their results are extrapolated in order to estimate 
the energy consumption of the building stock of a region or a country (Swan & Ugursal, 2009). It should be 
mentioned that due to the detail modelling, these models could be used to identify cost effective solutions or 
carbon reduction emissions with respect to the available systems and technologies. 
 
According to (Kavgic et al., 2010), this approach can be divided in two methods regarding the input 
information: the statistical and the engineering method. The statistical method is related to the historical 
information about energy consumption - end uses (data based on energy bills and simple survey information). 
However, this method can be proved poor for prediction of the energy consumption of certain end uses. On 
the other hand, engineering methods rely as well on the energy consumption of end-uses but they are based 
explicitly on the use of equipment (power rating) and the thermodynamics and heat transfer of the systems. Of 
course, the level of the input is much higher while the method lacks of estimating unspecified loads. 
 
The geometry of the buildings, the climate properties as well as the occupancy and equipment schedules are 
some of the input data into bottom up models. The strength of this approach is its possibility to define in detail 
the energy consumption of the end uses and identify the potential improvements. Yet limitation of this 
approach is the high level of expertise that is required to gather a large amount of technical data. Moreover, 
the human behaviour, represented in the occupants and equipment schedules, can only be modelled by 
stochastic assumptions (Swan & Ugursal, 2009).  
 
Bottom up modelling approaches 
In (Gianniou et al., 2015), two methods for aggregating the building energy demand for urban models were 
reported. In the 1st method the aggregation is based on a total energy consumption of the building stock. The 
2nd method is based on the aggregation of the demand of reference buildings.

The most common method to estimate the energy demand of a building stock is by adding up the demand of 
the individual buildings. Individual models for every building have to be created while the results from all the 
simulations are added to represent the building demand of the building stock. Equation (2.1) describes the

2nd method – Aggregation based on archetypes 
While the former method can be applied for small group of buildings, detailed data collection becomes almost 
impractical for larger urban areas. Hence, it is crucial to develop a method to abstract the building stock into 
‘building archetypes’. The term of archetypes implies a group of buildings with similar properties. The building 
stock can be categorized according to construction age, use of building, energy systems, building shape and 
climate. The building stock can be also classified based on the energy consumption. This case of categorization 
is indicated especially for non-domestic buildings where the demand depends more on the building use (similar 
activities) rather than on its construction and physical characteristics. For example, a set of office buildings 
will consume approximately the same energy expressed in kWh/m2, because ‘the energy consumption is 
dominated by the demand of activity related services’ as stated in (Choudhary, 2011). 
 
A template of building characteristics is formed that contains the thermal properties, operational schedules and 
building systems of the archetypes. Noted that the description of an archetype building can be based either on 
an example building (actual building that is considered representative of a building sample) or a virtual 
building (based on statistical and empirical data). With this method, the number of the different building types 
that require data is significantly reduced and the simulation process is simplified. It has to be mentioned that 
this approach is considered more appropriate to compensate for the poor data quality, if data for a part of 
building stock are not available (Reinhart & Cerezo Davila, 2016). According to the same study, this 
aggregation method has been extensively used in bottom up building stock models to test energy efficiency 
policies and new technologies at a regional or national level. In Europe a validate building typology that 
includes building archetypes for 20 countries is documented in TABULA WebTool (see Section 4.2.1). 
 
 A common expression of the aggregation of the energy demand of a set of uniform buildings (archetypes) 
within a district is normalized and expressed as kWh/m2/year. The former value refers to energy use intensity 
(EUI) that is widely used because the stakeholders can have a better overview of the energy use of a district 
(Choudhary, 2011).  The equation described (Gianniou et al., 2015) is:

Modelling in urban scale 
According to (Reinhart & Davila, 2016), the energy modelling of buildings in urban scale can be divided in 
three main parts:  
• 
The data collection, 
• 
The thermal model which is simulated and generates results  
• 
The validation of the results 
 
Data collection 
The data, required by the urban energy simulation tools or methods, include a variety of details for the building 
stock. Input to the model can be data for building envelope shape, properties of materials, window-opening 
ratios as well as climate data for the city. This information is collected usually from existing databases such as 
the cadastral office of the city, municipalities, energy utilities, meteorological services or collected on site. 
Noted that in the case of a new neighbourhood most of the building related data have to be generated from 
scratch. 
 
Other ways of obtaining the required data are related to city wide Geographic Information Systems (GIS). GIS 
data supplemented by geometric building data or with open source semantic formats such as CityGML can be 
considered as a reliable and sufficient database to build simulation models of whole cities. Several countries 
among Europe have already started using CityGML formats to store their files, as they can store both geometry 
and building data.  
Thermal model 
After collecting all the data required to form a building template, the data are inserted into a thermal model. 
Thermal calculations take place and then the results of the simulation are reported to the user. How detailed 
the calculations are, depends on the structure of the thermal model. Due to the complexity of modelling on 
urban scale, urban models are comprised of steady state heat balance equations and can simulate up to a single 
zone. The simulation results are aggregated to estimate the demand of the building stock according to methods 
described in Section 0. It has to be mentioned that the former methods neglect the influence of the urban 
context on the building performance (shading, orientation etc). In order to include these aspects, simulation 
tools have been developed (i.e. Urban Modelling Interface-see Section 3.1.1) that apply steady state equations 
in single zone models but also consider the surroundings of the buildings. Steady state methods usually are 
considered appropriate to estimate the heating needs while dynamic models of simulation engines (i.e. IDA 
ICE) are suggested to approach the cooling needs of buildings.  
Validation  
The results of the simulations are compared with real measurements in order to conclude if an urban simulation 
tool or method is reliable and can be used extensively. Usually, the simulation results can diverge quite 
significantly when energy demand of individual buildings is compared in monthly or yearly basis with real 
measurements. Yet the deviations increase further when comparing the peak loads of the hourly profiles. On 
the other hand, the deviations become smaller when aggregate building energy demand is compared in yearly 
basis with simulation results. In (Reinhart & Cerezo, 2016) the authors conclude that the inaccuracies of the 
urban modelling simulation tools are acceptable on the level that the results of urban modelling are used for 
guiding decisions indicating the interventions required. Due to uncertainties that are related to the definition 
of the archetypes, it is difficult to estimate the error and calibrate the model. In particular, since there is 
restricted access to building data,  there is not enough information to define building characteristics such as 
thermal properties of materials, infiltration rates or occupants behaviour.

Investigation of simulation methods and tools for urban building energy models 
Within the framework of the present study, a literature research was conducted to investigate the existing 
methods and simulation tools for urban contexts and energy systems. The purpose of the research was to 
conclude to the simulation tool that it is going to be tested later (Manousos Syngelakis, 2016) 
 
The categorization in tools and methods is considered on the base that tools use other environments and 
simulation engines to develop the thermodynamic model and handle visualization. In addition, there are 
methods that use geographic information system (GIS) databases to collect and store the building data and then 
develop their own calculation processes (models) to estimate the thermal loads, lighting loads, transportation 
etc. The outputs of the methods can be more targeted, since the developers of the methods can combine 
individual models and tools in order to be capable of isolating performance aspects and addressing areas of 
interest. Emphasis is given on tools that do not address only specific aspects or are restrained to simulate a 
limited number of buildings but on multi-disciplinary and holistic tools that: 
• 
Give a detailed description of the thermodynamic model and performance of the building and the 
energy systems used 
• 
Provide the user with option to combine multiple systems of the energy infrastructure (i.e. district 
heating, gas boiler, heat pumps) 
• 
Contain geographic information systems (GIS) for the tracking of spatiotemporal data.  
• 
Integrate energy mapping and give maps of heating, cooling or lighting density that indicate potential 
of energy improvements in targeted areas  
• 
Contain energy and economic indicators that analyse the impact on the energy demand on an urban 
area 
• 
Through optimization algorithms and processes identify the optimal ways of exploitation of the 
resources in an urban context 
• 
They are easily adapted to new supply and energy technologies. 
It should be mentioned that an extensive survey has been conducted to gather a wide range of methods and 
tools for urban modelling developed until recently, but no review can be exhaustive enough considering the 
fact of ongoing research worldwide. The scope of this review is to give a brief description of each tool or 
method indicating the depth that specific fields have been modelled as well as the limitations of the models. 
2.3.1 Urban simulation tools 
SUNtool 
According to (Robinson et al., 2007), ‘the first integrated dynamic model which facilitated physically rigorous 
analyses of relationships between urban form and building energy consumption’ was SUNtool. It was 
considered a new generation simulation tool that was developed to support the urban planning and help 
designers to optimize the sustainability of urban neighbourhoods (on the order of 50-500 buildings). The 
research project was funded by European Comission, however the software itself was not been publicly 
available. 
 
The software of SUNtool was structured in two parts, namely the interface and the solver. The interface was 
developed in Java providing the user with the potential to select the global location with representative climate 
data for the location. In addition, an iDefault dataset contained the details about the age and the type of use of 
the building. A 3D geometry modeler was also included to help the user to define the geometry of each building 
through visualization. Noted that there were additional simulation settings in order to define HVAC systems 
as well as renewable energy and water processing technologies embedded or included in a central network. 
After the settings of the model were defined, the model details were converted to XML files to be used as an 
input to the C++ solver. The solver model was consisted by 4 sub-models: i) The microclimatic model 
==PAGE:24==
8 
 
(radiation and temperature prediction approach) ii) The thermal model, iii) The stochastic model (occupants’ 
behaviour) and iv) the Plant model (fuel type and selection of required equipment i.e. boiler).  
An application of Suntool was demonstrated for a project located in Athens in Greece. The objective was to 
optimize the building energy consumption with respect to the façade glazing ratio and urban street proportions 
expressed by height to width ratio. The use of the simulation tool indicated that with less transmitted solar 
energy and by having the same fabric heat losses, heating energy consumption rises with the height to width 
façade glazing ratio. Complementary study regarding the Olympic Village in Athens investigated the 
connection between the building and the urban area and energy consumption. The introduction of renewable 
energy technologies was endorsed to minimize net energy consumption by fossil fuels. 
 
SUNtool was considered a unique tool for urban modelling, but it was susceptible to a series of drawbacks. 
Although the stochastic use of water appliances in buildings was taken into account in the model, other aspects 
such as the recycling water or rain water could not be modelled. Other uncertainties of the model dealt with 
the complex nature of the microclimate conditions and the energy interaction of the buildings with the street 
lighting. Noted also that in this tool no attempt was made to model transport. Future work indicated i) the 
introduction of some quantitative measure of environmental sustainability that can guide the optimization 
process and ii) the formation of a computational procedure in order to search environmental sustainable 
solutions in urban projects. 
 
CITYSIM 
A few years later an attempt has been made to overcome some of the former limitations by the development 
of the new software called CITYSIM (Darren Robinson et al., 2009). CITYSIM was considered as the 
successor of SUNtool and provided the user with a comprehensive simulation of resource flows at a larger 
urban scale than before. Noted that the access to the software was not available (Rodríguez-Álvarez, 2016). 
 
Regarding the optimization of urban design variables with resource minimization, ‘a new evolutionary 
algorithm (EA)’ was developed in order to control the geometric forms of buildings and optimize the solar 
potential by passive and active means. The authors developed their own optimizer based on a hybrid of the 
CMA-ES and HDE algorithms. The objective of (EA) algorithm was to minimize the energy needs of a district 
(reduction of the energy demand) by limiting the vast parameter space of possible changes in the urban context. 
It should be mentioned that the new optimizer was successfully linked to the EnergyPlus software (Kämpf & 
Robinson, 2009). 
 
The structure of CITYSIM was similar to SUNtool as it had a Java-based graphical user interface (GUI), a 
defaults database that included the building types and specifications and finally a solver.  The core models of 
CITYSIM consisted of 1) the thermal model that included radiation models, 2) the behavioural model that 
describe the occupants’ behaviour which is in principle stochastic and 3) the plant and equipment model. The 
latter model included the HVAC systems as well as the energy conversion systems. The Graphical User 
Interface (GUI) produced an XML file that contained information about the geometry of the urban plan, the 
energy resource flows, the climate file and the database related to the core models. The file was parsed to the 
CITYSIM solver that simulated on an hourly basis the resource flows and created the details of the urban scene 
(Darren Robinson et al., 2009). 
 
Case study 
CitySim’s core models were applied to a group of buildings in the district of Matthaus in Basel in Switzerland. 
For the conduction of the project useful data (provided by means of the national CENSUS 2000 and a recent 
visual field survey of the district), completed the physical description of the buildings. The 3D information of 
the whole district was available by the Cadastral Office and the meteorological data were given by the weather 
==PAGE:25==
9 
 
station In Basel. The application consisted of 26 buildings (from 1970’s to 1990’s) that were divided in three 
zones in order to access their thermal performance.   
The zones included a historical part of the city not susceptible to changes (Schutzzone), a part of the city that 
was also protected and cannot change the external appearance of the buildings (Schonzone) and a part that 
could be modified with a permission of the authorities (Zone 5a). The three administrative zones can be 
observed in Figure 1 (Kämpf & Robinson, 2009). Noted that a procedure was developed to load the missing 
physical properties of the buildings from the available data into the model. A series of modifications and 
renovations (improvements on the windows and wall insulation) were studied with the aid of the simulation 
tool. For the optimization, the objective function that was used was the sum of the ideally yearly heating and 
cooling demands for the simulated urban area. The algorithm EA found an optimum in the aggregate ideal 
heating and cooling demand of the 26 building and managed to reduce the energy demand from 4.95 GJ to 
3.96 GJ (about 20%) (Darren Robinson et al., 2009).

According to (Robinson et al. 2009) further development of the simulation tool was under way to tackle the 
uncertainties in relation to the design of new urban development that could have significant impact on the 
energy performance predictions. Techniques for more detailed simulation could be incorporated in order to be 
able to model urban resource flows with regards to buildings and transport, together by applying optimization 
algorithms. Synergetic exchanges of energy and activities between buildings had to be taken also into 
consideration to improve the circularity of urban flows. Yet concepts of embodied energy and cost analysis of 
materials, water consumption of electrical appliances and transportation were missing of CITYSim as well as 
they were missing of the predecessor tool, SUNtool. 
 
 A few years later CITYSim came again to the front line with a new research of (Emmanuel & Kämpf, 2015). 
That paper presented a verification of CITYSim results using two different approaches. The first approach, 
namely the Building Energy Simulation Test (BESTET), was to compare the results of the CITYSim, related 
to heating and cooling loads, with a set of reference validated simulation tools. The comparison of CITYSim 
showed compliance with the BESTET validation approach resulting in differences for the peak load demand 
of heating and cooling that did not exceed 1%. The second approach was to verify the results of CITYSim 
experimentally, by comparing them with the monitoring of the yearly consumption of a campus building 
(EPFL) located in Lausanne. Higher differences were demonstrated in the order of 5% questioning the 
hypothesis on which the model was based (climatic data, occupation schedule, temperature set points). Yet it 
was proved that the CITYSim results were consistent with reality and that it was a reliable tool for estimating 
in an early phase the heating and cooling needs of buildings in an urban context (Emmanuel & Kämpf, 2015).

SYNCITY 
The same year as CITYSim, an integrated tool kit for urban energy modelling was demonstrated (Keirstad et 
al., 2009). The tool kit, namely the SynCity, was the main action of the BP Urban Energy Systems project at 
Imperial College London launched in 2005.  Its purpose was to facilitate (Keirstead, Samsatli, & Shah, 2009): 
• 
the planning and layout of urban planning,  
• 
the socio-economic structure of the area and its activities and  
• 
the optimal combination of energy carriers and technologies in order to meet the energy demand.  
 
Figure 3 Structure of SynCity  (Aruna Sivakumar, 2010). 
 
SynCity represented an integrated modelling platform in Java for urban energy systems, using a mathematical 
programming approach developed in GAMS. It included 4 sub-models that were linked in one toolkit  
(Allegrini et al., 2015). The models were related to the optimal placement of buildings as well as to the 
minimization of the cost, carbon or energy consumption. Daily activities of each citizen within the city were 
also simulated through stochastic decisions. Additionally, an optimization model was used to design the overall 
energy-supply strategy for the city, as a map that contains all the resource distribution networks, the location 
of each conversion process and information at each time step of resource, export and production rates (Aruna 
Sivakumar, 2010). The structure of the model is illustrated in Figure 2.  
 
The former platform was used to assess the design of an eco-town plan in United Kingdom. An optimized 
layout that reduced approximately by 40% the transportation and energy services costs was proposed. The 
approach however presented limitations (Best et al., 2015): 
• 
It did not allow analysis of individual buildings while the model could only be simulated for 
representative periods of the year. Therefore, the temporal and geographic resolution was limited and 
applicable to smaller geographic scales. 
• 
In SynCity each subsystem was developed on different platforms (GAMS, UrbanSim, C++) increasing 
the complexity of the tool. 
• 
A better interplay between normative supply and dynamic demand models that respond to realistic 
demands for every case should have been investigated.  
 
Two more conceptual tools that are used lately in the early stages of urban planning are ‘Urban Modelling 
Interface’ (umi) and ‘Urban Energy Management’ (MEU). Having a friendly user interface, they provide quick 
and easy access to define configurations of advanced energy systems. Noted that Umi is an open source 
program whereas for the MEU platform a license is needed. For the sake of structure of the report, umi is 
explained in detail in Section 3.1.1, since it is the tool selected for the analysis of the case study.

Urban Energy Management (MEU) 
In 2013, a study about a web based tool developed in EPFL University was released (Rager et al., 2013). The 
web platform (http://meu.epfl.ch) was developed in order to facilitate urban energy monitoring and planning 
as a decision support tool that could be used by decision makers (local authorities and other stakeholders). 
MEU used a bottom up approach based on the available supply and demand data. The project was extensively 
tested and validated based on real case studies in four Swiss cities, namely La Chaux-de-Fonds, Neuchâtel, 
Lausanne and Martigny (Rager et al., 2013). The revised of the platform provided access to the user to perform 
simulations for entire cities comprising of thousands of buildings with the same level of detail (Capezzali et 
al. 2014). 
Regarding the structure of the MEU platform, the Cadastral office of each city provided the data related to 
building infrastructure (i.e. construction year, geometry building footprint etc.). Meteorological data as well 
as data about energy resources and conversion systems were also gathered. MEU used a GIS carto-graphical 
interface to record the data with the option to select the year of the construction. Later on, an additional 
functionality introduced the possibility to provide the platform with available data of ArcGIS Server (ESRI) 
services (Capezzali et al. 2014). The database that contained all the geospatial and temporal data was created 
in PostgreSQL (Rager et al., 2013). 
 
After the data are stored and structured the energy simulation takes place. In particular, the building software 
that was used for the dynamic building energy demand simulation was CITYSim. The former software, linked 
to the platform as a customized web-service, offered a variety of energy conversion technologies, from 
conventional boilers and heat pumps to integration of renewable equipment. MEU calculated the energy 
consumption in terms of a set of energy related and environmental annual indicators for an urban context (i.e. 
annual final or primary energy demand in disaggregate level). The results of MEU could be used as a way to 
monitor the energy consumption per year. Moreover, the results could be either exported in tables or in maps 
for better visualization.  
 
The web platform provided also the possibility to define energy networks (district heating, natural gas, etc.) to 
be added as additional maps and visualized. Yet the networks could only be used for visualization purposes, 
preventing the user to connect the buildings to existing or future networks and test pre-designed scenarios or 
make a technology compatibility check. Noted that an effort was conducted to plug in to the platform 
commercial software that was capable to simulate dynamically energy networks, but still there were problems 
of compatibility. Such a software integrated in the web based platform could determine whether a new district 
could be connected to the existent network or not.  
 
The method, on which the web-platform was based, indicated its disability to be used in cases that the measured 
data were limited since it could lead to large deviations from reality. Additionally, lack of information 
regarding the building retrofits recorded in cities’ datasets could result as well in deviations. Last but not least, 
differences could arise from the assumption that the energy systems were based in Swiss norms and fixed 
efficiencies corresponding to unrealistic conditions for other cities or countries. For all the former reasons, it 
was considered essential for the cities to consistently take care and update the necessary data sets to facilitate 
the development of the platform (Capezzali et al. 2014). 
 
CityBES 
A newly developed platform that provided a set of analytic and modelling features to improve the energy 
efficiency of buildings and reduce carbon emissions was introduced in (Hong, et al., 2016). CityBES was a 
web-based platform developed in Lawrence Berkley National Laboratory that could simulate energy 
performance of buildings from a district up to a city scale (10000 or more buildings). In order to handle 
simultaneous simulation of many buildings, a parallel computing architecture was implemented. CityBES 
==PAGE:28==
12 
 
offered a wide range of analysis of the building stock with regard to energy efficiency measures, energy 
benchmarking, greenhouse gas reductions, operation improvements and energy cost analysis. 
 
CityBES consisted of three layers of software architecture; Data layer, Algorithm layer and Software layer 
(see Figure 4). The first layer used CityGML database to represent and exchange 3D models. The geometry of 
buildings as well as other related to urban environment features (bridges, vegetation, transportation network 
etc.) were imported to the platform from CityGML database. Additional data that are required for CityBES 
model included weather data and building characteristics extracted from different city data sources. The 
platform had also a database with energy end uses data and energy benchmarking in order to enable the 
comparison with simulation results and identify energy savings potential (such as LED lighting upgrade,  
ventilation systems upgrade, etc.). For the economic analysis of energy retrofit of buildings, energy and 
investment costs, discount rate and payback years were included in the database of the platform. 
 
 
Figure 4 Structure of CityBES (Hong et al., 2016). 
 
Regarding the energy modelling, CityBES uses EnergyPlus simulation engine in combination with OpenStudio 
software development kit (SDK). The latter provided an object-oriented interface to manage the building 
energy simulations on large scale.  
 
CityBES offered a variety of features to filter the building stock after the simulation with regard to building 
type, energy use intensity, construction age and more. In addition, two common and widespread datasets that 
categorise the performance of the buildings, namely the Energy Star Portofolio Manager and Building 
Performance Database (BPD), were integrated in CityBES. In particular the energy performance of selected 
buildings of the model could be evaluated through the comparison with standard buildings of the former 
datasets to benchmark their performance on district level. 
 
A data analysis and cluster algorithm provided recommendations for the operational improvement (i.e. reduce 
the use of HVAC system during nigh-time or weekends). The biggest potential and radical solution that 
provides CityBES was the energy retrofit analysis. After the user filtered the building stock, a series of ECMs 
(energy conservation measures) could be selected from the platform’s database including energy costs and 
discount rates. The ECMs were implemented on the building stock and simulated to evaluate the energy 
savings and the costs. The retrofit measures dealt with the building envelope, lighting, equipment, HVAC 
systems or DHW systems.  
==PAGE:29==
13 
 
The visualization of the CityBES enabled a 3D view of the building stock together with a floating window that 
showed a list of characteristics for each building as well as the energy use and potential energy retrofit.  
 
In (Hong et al., 2016) five technical challenges of CityBES were highlighted. The first challenge had to do 
with collecting adequate data extracted from CityGML, a model that is under development. Moreover, tracking 
the energy use data of the building stock from energy use services remained a challenge. Third, more retrofit 
measures had to be modelled in EnergyPlus and ECMs database. Fourth, modelling the building stock of a 
whole city required significant computing resources, as thousands of building energy simulations had to run 
multiple times to test the retrofit measures. Finally, inter-building effects had to be integrated in EnergyPlus 
in order to consider effects such as the urban heat island effect and radiant heat exchange between the external 
surfaces of buildings. 
2.3.2 Urban simulation methods 
3D city model with CITYGML 
A 3D city model with CityGML was an outcome of the cooperation of the Energy and Geo-informatics 
departments of Hochschule für Technik Stuttgart (Nouvel et al. 2013). In particular, the model represented an 
integrated heating demand calculation process that was based on CityGML model. 
 
CityGML is an open access model that is used for data base modelling and supports 3D geospatial 
visualization, spatial analysis, heating demand calculations and refurbishment scenarios simulations. The 
software industry as well as big companies have accepted and widely used CityGML interfaces, establishing 
CityGML as ‘the international standard of the Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC) for the representation and 
exchange of 3D city models since 2008’ (Gröger & Plümer, 2012).  
 
The 3D CityGML model was developed in a Java based software CAT3D that enabled the extraction of the 
geometric information from CityGML database. In addition, the integration of the management concept of 
CityDoctor was applied to deal with the control and completion of the 3D model. The building parameters 
were stored in the database that was used for the heating demand analysis of the buildings. The parameters 
could be defined in the Levels of Detail (LoD) of CityGML (LoD1-Building block model, LoD2-Roof form, 
LoD3-Positioning of the façade windows and LoD4-Modelling of the indoor space).  Regarding heating 
demand calculation, a quasi-static algorithm was used based on ISO 13790 and compatible with the 3D city 
model. 
 
The accuracy of the model was tested for two districts in Germany, Grünbühl in Ludwigsburg and Rintheim 
in Karlsruhe. The first case included buildings constructed near 1970 with only a small part of them being 
refurbished (around 1%). Necessary data for the buildings were either obtained from the municipality of 
Ludwigsburg or collected on site. After the 3D city model was built (detailed only at Level of Detail 1-building 
block model), the heating demands for the whole district were calculated with CityGML and compared with 
real energy consumption data. The results of the model overestimated the real demand per building block 
between 5 and 30%. Additionally, a scenario of refurbishment of all the buildings according to the German 
standard KfW-Effizienzhaus-85 was tested, in order to indicate energy savings. The potential of energy savings 
was investigated in a time span of 40 years, including a cost analysis.   
 
The second case, study situated on the district of ‘Rintheimer Feld’, included around 30 buildings (22 multi-
family houses and 8 tower buildings) built at different construction periods. For this purpose, the buildings 
were classified in six archetypes according to their energy efficiency. Heating demands per building category 
were simulated and then aggregated to estimate the heating demand of the district. Noted that some 
assumptions had to be stated regarding the way of heating (gas boilers efficiencies). The results of the 
==PAGE:30==
14 
 
simulations were more close to the real demand recording the largest deviations in cases of high energy 
consumers. 
The limitations of the model as in many other cases depended on the building data availability. Geometrical 
and thermal uncertainties as well as uncertainties related to occupant behaviour could have a significant 
influence on the accuracy of the model. Collecting accurate data for the building stock of the city proved rather 
difficult. The study indicated that private owners could enhance the quality of the data by providing 
information about the construction of the buildings or any later refurbishments (Nouvel et al. 2013).  
 
Method for simulation energy supply and demand 
A more recent novel method that simulated endogenously energy supply and demand within a district on an 
hourly basis was presented in 2015 by (Best et al., 2015).  The model was developed in Python and was able 
to simulate energy supply and demand of electricity up to 102 MW (peak), heating and cooling for a 
neighbourhood or a cluster of buildings up to 103 MW. Making use of a modular, dynamic framework the 
model allowed to define and modify multiple building types and energy supply technologies. In particular, it 
was constructed using three modules: an energy demand simulation, an energy supply characterization and 
dispatch and finally analysis and optimization (see Figure 4). 
 
 
Figure 5 Analysis and optimization module (Best et al., 2015). 
 
• 
Demand module 
The demand decision variables were representative of all the building types considered by the user. By 
defining these variables, the user created a load profile for the building area. The input of the demand 
module was related to hourly load profiles of heating, cooling and electricity for the area. There was a 
possibility to insert simulated profiles from other tools i.e. EnergyPlus, Trace etc. These profiles were 
aggregated into a look-up table for further analysis and optimization. Supplementary to the energy demand 
simulation, the distribution losses of the energy systems as well as the electrical demand for street lighting 
of the ’community’ were calculated. 
 
• 
Supply module 
The supply module included as input operating and design parameters that were based on the performance 
of each supply technology. The modification or addition of pieces of equipment could be easily specified 
by making use of a set of classes to define objects that correspond to different types of equipment (heat 
==PAGE:31==
15 
 
pump, chiller etc.) specified by the user. The ability of multiple class objects was provided by the dynamic 
nature of Python (classes are created at runtime and be further modified after creation). 
Output of the model included heating, cooling and electricity costs and emissions as well as losses of the 
equipment. A common input-output framework was considered in order to accommodate every required 
technology. Noted here that every type of energy technology that could be used in the model was validated 
with respect to industry-leading software or standards to make the model even more accurate. 
• 
Analysis and optimization module 
In this simulation module the look up tables created in the demand module as well as the class objects 
created in the supply module were used to calculate the annual cost, carbon dioxide emissions and Total 
Fuel Cycle efficiency (TFCE). The optimization was achieved through an algorithm that optimized the 
performance according to one or more objectives defined in the analysis. The algorithm named Non-
dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) was implemented to search in an efficient way a large 
solution space subject, balance potential conflicting objectives and give as much as possible an accurate 
representation of the building area investigated.  
 
The optimization was tested for a community near San Francisco in California. The community included 21 
building types, 32 combined heat and power engines and 16 chillers. Different building uses and energy 
technologies were tested. The results were compared in terms of annual cots, emissions and efficiency of the 
total network. From these combinations some valuable design guidelines were concluded (Best et al., 2015). 
Indicated future work of this novel model presented in (Best et al., 2015) revealed the constraints of the model. 
Inclusion of energy resources such as solar energy and energy efficiencies of technologies had to be integrated 
in order to capture the full effects of the energy infrastructure. Additionally improvements on the calculation 
of the thermal and electrical losses could be made by adding a network component that could allow having 
more complicated zoning constraints resulting in a greater applicability to real cities. Last but not least, the life 
cycle cost calculations could be enhanced to capture a more detailed economic value of the community.  
 
Urban Energy Index for Buildings (UEIB) 
An up-to-date and very recent method developed by (Rodríguez-Álvarez, 2016) evaluated the energy 
performance of buildings in large urban areas from few blocks to an entire city. The Urban Energy Index for 
Buildings (UEIB) was based on the idea of simplifying the urban geometry into a simpler notional grid that 
could still retain information on all the key parameters with respect to the energy performance. Average values 
of the geometric parameters of the actual urban fabric were used so that “the energy assessment could be 
performed for a single element, which represented the average for a specific area under analysis”. Regarding 
the load calculations, the model was categorized in thermal model and lighting model. The thermal model 
consisted of i) the climate and comfort band (weather profile), ii) the heat loss coefficient (conductive and 
convection losses), iii) the internal heat gains (occupancy patterns and iv) the unobstructed façade ratio (solar 
access determined by orientation, solar geometry and obstruction angle). Moreover, the lighting load was 
complemented by: i) the average daylight factor (DF) ‘calculated for a virtual middle floor of the characteristic 
block’, ii) the indoor illuminance (multiplying the outdoor illuminance by the daylight factor), and iii) the 
lighting load that was calculated yearly. Noted that spaces on the perimeter of 6 m around the area of a building 
were assumed to be passive zones. For the rest zones a fixed lighting power was assumed for different 
categories (i.e. offices, industrial or other facilities).   
 
For the validation of the model, 28 urban samples from 5 different cities were used to calibrate and test the 
model (each city had 5-6 samples). The cities selected were Berlin, London, Paris, Barcelona and Madrid and 
they represented a broad range of urban typologies. The modelling of the urban samples was conducted by 
using the energy software Climatelite and in a second stage by applying the UEIB thermal and lighting model. 
The samples were modelled in Climatelite with great detail to represent the actual urban geometry. The results 
of the detailed modelling were compared with the simplified models (notional grid) of UEIB. In the study of 
==PAGE:32==
16 
 
(Rodríguez-Álvarez, 2016), the results  of UEIB were presented with respect to the potential of the method in 
three different ways: spreadsheet, diagram and GIS model. The spreadsheet included inputs that could be 
modified and intermediate cells as well as results about heating and cooling demand. The second format of 
UEIB was the formation of regional diagrams. These diagrams provided information about the average energy 
loads of the urban areas examined in relation to their FSI (floor space index) and GSI (gross space index). In 
the end, the UEIB-GIS model consisted of a series of geoprocessing tools programmed for different climate 
and loads. The input of the model included the buildings footprint and height. The output represented maps 
that indicated heating, cooling and lighting density. In case of London, the results of the UEIB-GIS were 
compared with the London Heat Map (a heating density map). Similar patterns were found. 
 
According to the same study, the UEIB constituted a powerful method that although its simplicity could be 
applied to different urban contexts and climates. It could be also used by planning teams and agencies to 
investigate different strategies and retrofitting policies in a particular urban context. Nevertheless, the method 
lacked of defying specific microclimatic features as vegetation, natural soil and water bodies. UEIB was not 
recommended for small domains lower than 20 hectares. In that case other tools might be more representative. 
(Rodríguez-Álvarez, 2016).  
 
City Energy Analyst (CEA)  
A computational framework for analysis and optimization of energy systems in a neighbourhood level was 
introduced in (Fonseca & Schlueter, 2015). The approach was revised and additional functionalities were 
presented in 2016 (Fonseca et al., 2016). The City Energy Analyst (CEA) was based on a novel method that 
facilitated the characterization of spatiotemporal energy consumption patterns of demand and potential 
infrastructure solutions. The model of the CEA was developed in the open-source programming language 
Python v2.7. Simply, it represented a platform for the allocation of spatiotemporal data fed by way of 
Geographic information System ArcGIS V10.3 (Fonseca et al., 2016). The novel method integrated many 
aspects of urban and energy planning such as spatial analysis, dynamic building energy simulations, conversion 
technologies and energy systems simulations as well as energy systems optimization and energy mapping. The 
CEA used two bottom up approaches (statistical and analytical) to calculate the energy services in buildings. 
 
The framework of the CEA integrated seven databases: i) The weather database that used the software 
Meteonorm to store time series of weather data in the model. ii) The urban database in which all buildings and 
infrastructure were geo-referenced, including information, such as building footprint, occupancy types, 
construction year, etc. The information was gathered from national databases and open street maps. iii) The 
archetype database that described in detail the properties of the building stock such as the building envelope 
or HVAC systems. iv) The distribution database that contained information about occupancy schedules, 
ventilation rates, hourly consumption for appliances, lighting, etc. v) The sensor database that contained data 
about non standardized energy services and data related to infrastructural components (i.e. mass flow rates 
etc.). vi) The metrics database including technical and economic metrics of conversion, distribution and storage 
technologies on a neighbourhood scale. vii) The targets database including performance values for optimal 
selection of infrastructure. 
 
According to (Fonseca et al., 2016) the CEA used a series of calculation modules to simulate the model defined 
by the input of the databases. A graphical representation of the databases combined with the calculation 
modules is depicted in Figure 5. The numbers on the figure represented the calculation modules defined below. 
  
The modules are described very briefly in order to understand the potential of the framework:  
1) The demand module for calculating the demand of the energy in buildings. 
2)  The resource potential module that contained several sub models such as roof top solar potential, 
ambient heat of geothermal, lake water or waste heat sources and industrial processes.  
==PAGE:33==
17 
 
3) The systems technology module that aggregated on the levels of costs and energy several 
technologies (i.e. heat pumps, solar technologies, boilers, etc.). 
4) The supply and optimization module that optimized the thermal networks by minimizing the 
investment and operation costs. Additionally it was used to minimize the annual costs of the energy 
systems as well as the associated CO2 emissions.  
5) The decision module that dealt with sensitivity analysis and multi-criteria analysis (social, economic 
and environmental) to select the optimal configuration of energy systems. It also included a life 
cycle analysis and a comparison of the performance of buildings with local averages of end-use 
energy consumption (benchmarking). 
6) Last but not least, a 4D interface was added provided the user with a visualization of the time 
dependent energy flows between buildings and infrastructure (Fonseca & Schlueter, 2015).  
For validation purposes, the CEA framework was tested in a real case for the city of Zug in Switzerland. The 
city covered approximately 25 ha and consisted of a total of 1392 buildings (residential, commercial and 
industrial buildings) (Fonseca & Schlueter, 2015). Additional research tested the application of the framework 
in part of the city that was then occupied only by the industrial sector (Fonseca et al., 2016). Four different 
scenarios oriented in either business, commercial or residential development of the area were set up. The results 
of the scenarios indicated the potential of the area regarding solar sources, waste heat and lake water that could 
result in relative savings of CO2 emissions and a decrease of primary energy consumption. 
  
It should be mentioned that since the framework integrated many calculation modules in order to perform the 
energy and optimization analysis, it demanded also high computational times (up to several days). Mainly 
because the simulations of the modules could not be connected or run in parallel. Future work indicated by 
(Fonseca et al., 2016) suggested the development of data clustering algorithms to reduce the number of data 
points of analysis on condition that they keep the input data in the system unchanged to preserve the dynamic 
behaviour in the system.

2.3.3 Challenges of urban modelling 
The review conducted in the previous section covers a broad prospective on the building and energy systems 
modelling on urban scale. Urban modelling aims at supporting the urban planners and local authorities to 
perform precise diagnostics about the building stock as well as to plan strategies for reducing the energy 
consumption and the carbon emissions. These strategies can be targeted either on individual building level 
(refurbishment of the existing building and energy systems) or on a neighbourhood/district level dealing with 
distribution and storage technologies. These technologies can indicate a ‘smarter’ exploitation of the resources 
of an urban district with integration of district heating networks or renewable energy resources. Yet modelling 
approaches developed until lately face a series of main challenges: 
 
Data availability and storage 
The biggest challenge for urban building energy modelling is associated with the collection of detailed data 
that represent the building stock. The restricted access to measurements of energy use in buildings as well as 
the incomplete knowledge of the thermal properties and loads of buildings may result in significant deviations 
from real consumption data. The former deviations make even more difficult to calibrate the building models 
accurately.  To deal with this problem, city municipalities and governments have to allow public access to 
building energy use data. Aligned to the former effort to facilitate the collecting of building related data, 
surveys related to private owners could provide information about the building construction or later 
changes/refurbishments. It is also of outmost important that the cities will update the required, for the urban 
building energy modelling, databases.  
 
Methods for pre-processing the building related data is another field that further investigation is needed. Gaps 
in information data must be filled in by estimating the missing data and transforming the available ones. In 
particular, spatial clustering algorithms can facilitate the analysis of energy consumption patterns on a 
neighbourhood/district scale. Clustering algorithms could also deal with the selection of representative 
archetypes to build the city model. Moreover, the need for demand and supply resource data highlights the 
importance of spatial information to capture the local geometry of a neighbourhood but also to get information 
at a city or national scale. The mapping of decentralized systems requires new geo-spatial databases and 
methods for urban analysis. The development of energy related tools based on GIS is essential in order to 
model the spatiotemporal energy consumption patterns from neighbourhood to larger scale and identify the 
potential of renewable energy resources and infrastructure solutions. According to (Allegrini et al., 2015), 
modelling tools that are developed in CAD and GIS environments (i.e. Umi in Rhinoceros-based CAD 
environment) have bigger prospects than exporting complex data structures to external analysis tools.  
 
Integration of energy networks and optimization 
The integration of optimization processes in holistic tools that are capable for parametric analysis at an urban 
context is necessary. Surpassing the possibility of visualization, several efforts have been made to integrate 
the network flows and processes in the building energy simulations. Combining energy systems on a city scale 
brings new challenges regarding the modelling. Optimization algorithms need to be developed to identify the 
optimal ways of exploitation of the conventional and unconventional energy sources and systems in the urban 
context.    
                                                                                                                                         
Behaviour of the occupants 
Another challenge that needs to be overcome in individual building level but also for larger group of buildings 
is the occupant behaviour. The occupants should be treated as individual agents and not like machines that 
repeat the same activities at the same time. Stochastic modelling regarding the presence of the occupants and 
the human interactions is significantly important because it can influence the heat calculations resulting in 
deviations between the simulated and the actual energy consumption. 
 
==PAGE:37==
21 
 
Detail simulation in individual building and city level 
The decision makers in the early stages of urban planning require simple tools that can simulate in detail both 
building and urban contexts. The expansion of individual to a "group of buildings” simulation imposes the 
introduction of more performance aspects of building infrastructure. It also stresses the deficiencies of the 
simulation engines to model microclimatic effects (i.e. urban heat island, or local wind conditions), 
transportation, water use, etc.  In the direction of more accurate and complete simulations, a variety of case 
studies has been investigated and knowledge on simulating specific conditions has been recorded to help the 
developers improve the models. Still the challenge is difficult to be overcome since the use of holistic tools as 
CitySim that attempt to integrate many aspects of the urban system can be very complex and computationally 
demanding. A solution to address the former challenge is the development of methods that integrate into one 
united environment, specific models that each one can simulate a different aspect of the urban system. The 
integration can be performed in a way that they can exchange information and run individually or in parallel. 
Modelica is a co-simulation environment but the complexity of the building models does not allow the 
simulation on a neighbourhood scale (Allegrini et al., 2015).   
 
A description of the tools and methods has been attempted to highlight the findings but also to stress the 
limitations of each approach. Although great advances have enhanced the urban building energy modelling, 
there are still a lot of limitations that need to be overcome. After research, it can be conducted that there is not 
such a tool or method that can integrate all the aspects of an urban context and gives the best combination of 
all the processes considered. Finally, a strongest cooperation between the urban planners and other 
stakeholders and the building modelling community is essential to develop a framework of complete modelling 
and optimization of urban systems.

3 Theoretical and modelling background  
After the importance of the urban simulation has been outlined and the most important tools and methods have 
been highlighted in the literature review, an urban simulation tool has been selected to test its accuracy. The 
selected tool was umi and the choice was based mainly on the open-source access of the software. Umi is a 
newly developed urban simulation tool, thus it was considered interesting to demonstrate an application of the 
tool for a real case study. Hence, the tool was tested and validated based on a case study in a small district in 
Sønderborg, Denmark. In practice, there are only a few urban simulation tools that integrate multiple energy 
performance aspects and can indicate the energy intensity of problematic areas. The former indication can 
improve the building performance and make urban neighbourhoods more resource efficient. 
 
At a second level, in order to validate further umi, the archetypes created for the case study were simulated 
with a more detailed building simulation tool. The tool that was used was IDA ICE, a software that can run 
detailed energy and thermal simulations on individual level. The results of the energy demand of the archetypes 
were extrapolated to represent the energy demand of the district and compared with the results of the urban 
simulation tool. Deviations between real measurements and results from the simulation tools were investigated. 
A description of the simulation tools IDA ICE and umi, which were later used for the analysis of the case study 
is attempted in this section. 
 
Description of the simulation tools used for the analysis 
3.1.1 Urban Modelling Interface (umi) 
Umi is a recently developed urban modelling tool (Reinhart et al., 2013), that estimates the operational energy, 
daylight and walkability within an urban context. It is a plug-in that uses: 
• 
a Rhinoceros-based CAD environment to allow to model complex geometry in buildings,  
• 
EnergyPlus for dynamic energy simulations,  
• 
DaySim/Radiance simulation engines to calculate the radiation 
• 
a series of Grasshoper and Python scripts to model other building related aspects (i.e. transportation) 
or to evaluate the walkability of a city layout and the associated carbon emissions.    
 
 
Figure 7 Umi model for a district in Boston (Reinhart et al., 2013). 
 
Umi-Energy  
Umi is based on up to date existing simulation engines supported by development teams that made the tool 
even more attractive since it is friendly for users that are already familiar with EnergyPlus or Daysim. The tool 
also benefits from past and new developments of the individual simulation tools. Umi relies on a bottom up 
approach using archetype buildings. The approach is based on integrated microclimatic conditions (i.e. self-
shading or urban heat island effect) that result in differences in energy use of buildings. The structure of Umi 
is similar to the one  developed by (Robinson et al., 2007), Suntool. The user can create consistent models of 
==PAGE:40==
24 
 
a city or a smaller district (i.e. defining building envelopes, trees, streets, etc.) using an intuitive layering 
structure in Rhinoceros. The model of an urban district located in Boston can be seen in Figure 7.  
 
Regarding the energy simulation, umi generates EnergyPlus files and simulates either individually or in parallel 
the buildings on a yearly base. The EnergyPlus models are defined in two steps. First the building volume is 
broken down in different levels. Then the core and envelope zones are generated automatically by umi. All the 
zones have the same characteristics (i.e. construction types, schedules, etc.), while the interior zones are 
assigned as air surfaces. Adjacent surfaces between buildings are considered adiabatic surfaces. The HVAC 
systems are based on EnergyPlus ideal air loads, while the user can define coefficient of performances for the 
systems (Reinhart et al., 2013).  
 
Thermal ‘Shoebox’ models 
The basic method for the simulation of the thermal model is that umi enables the “abstracting of an arbitrary 
building massing into a meaningful group of thermal shoebox models” (Dogan & Reinhart, 2013). The method 
is based on a hypothesis that the thermal zones within a building are maintained at similar temperatures. For 
every thermal zone adiabatic interior surfaces are assumed. All the zones generated in the building are 
simulated individually and the results are extrapolated to estimate the energy use intensity of the whole 
building. The model is structured in the following way: 
 
Input model 
First a 3D CAD model is inserted through the Rhinoceros design software. The model represents the massing 
model and is saved on the building layer. There is potential to define the building as one envelope (box) or as 
a more complicated design (stacked boxes). The urban context of the district can be inserted as well but has to 
be stored on separate layers.  
 
Insolation analysis 
After a robust geometry of the buildings is inserted, cutting planes are generated that break out the buildings 
in segments (smaller parts-boxes). A series of virtual sensors are placed in the segments in order to estimate 
the incident solar radiation. Each sensor represents a part of a segment within a building. For more complex 
geometries more sensors are generated. A clustering algorithm is used to divide the sensors into clusters that 
have similar seasonal solar loads. The mean incident solar radiation of a cluster of boxes is identified. The 
shading situation of the cluster defined by sky view obstructions results in the formation of a shading mask for 
every cluster.  
 
The information available enables the creation of ‘shoebox’ models that integrate realistic characteristics of 
the orientation and shading situation of the buildings. The surfaces (‘patches’) for every shoebox correspond 
to building area ratios and represent boundary conditions such as ground floor, roof or walls of the building. 
 
Simulation of the shoeboxes 
Since the thermal model behind umi has been briefly explained, it is time to elaborate on umi interface and the 
building details that are defined by the user. Physical and usage characteristics of the buildings regarding the 
construction elements, the glazing, the energy systems, the operational schedules and the internal loads can be 
defined in umi template. Umi offers the potential to define multiple building templates to represent buildings 
with different characteristics in an urban context.  
 
According to (Dogan & Reinhart, 2013), the developers of umi find it practical to define basic templates that 
include lightweight and heavy constructions with good or poor insulation properties and usage pre-sets. There 
are 4 default building templates provided. Two of them deal with residential and lodging buildings, one for 
office spaces and another one for retail buildings.  
==PAGE:41==
25 
 
An example of umi’s template can be seen in Figure 8. There are different tabs that you can define information 
about materials, construction elements, operational schedules and internal loads, conditioning, ventilation 
technologies and domestic hot water (DHW). The ‘Zone information’ tab includes all the information required 
to define the ‘Building Template’ tab. The settings of each category in the ‘Zone information’ tab have been 
defined in previous tabs. Once the user creates the full template and assigns the ‘Building Templates’ to the 
buildings in the urban context designed in Rhino, the energy simulation can be conducted. ‘Shoebox’ models 
are generated and thermal simulation is taking place for each cluster. 
 
Figure 8 Umi template. 
 
 
Simulation results 
Umi generates exemplar zones – ‘shoebox’ models, simulates them and then scales the results up to the total 
building area. The number of exemplar zones is partially user-controllable in ‘Advanced settings’, depending 
on how accurate the user wants the results (fast simulation usually generates less accurate results). In particular, 
the results of every zone are extrapolated considering the area that each ‘shoebox’ occupies within the building. 
The results are mapped on the building envelope demonstrating the energy consumption of the whole building. 
Other performance aspects of umi  
Grasshopper (another plug-in in Rhino 3D) provides toolsets to simulate daylight conditions and 
transportation, using a Python based viewer to visualize the building performance (Reinhart et al., 2013). Umi 
calculates the yearly daylight availability for each individual building storey in the building. The underlying 
program used in Umi was ‘Urban Daylight’ and was based on Daysim. Outputs of the program can be climate-
based metrics: the daylight autonomy (DA) and the continuous daylight autonomy (CDA). Hot spots can be 
identified by the tool indicating which parts of courtyards or streets required local shading. The mobility was 
another aspect of urban planning that Umi integrated. Umi includes a sustainable transportation module that 
measures walkability (how friendly is an area for walking) as well as associated carbon emissions (Reinhart et 
al., 2013). 
Limitations and future work 
In absence of a “connectivity network between the zones”, natural ventilation systems  based on an airflow 
network cannot be simulated. In that case accurate pressure coefficients in all the openings of the envelope are 
required. This demands analytical CFD simulations,  difficult to model as it could increase significantly the 
simulation time. Nevertheless, it is not considered necessary for the early design of the model. Moreover, a 
more sophisticated daylight approach that can improve the accuracy of the lighting model of buildings could 
be further developed (Dogan & Reinhart, 2013). Noted that umi is not ready yet to support very big urban 
models due to certain 3D geometry limitations that come from Rhino and workflow limitations of Umi and 
Grasshopper components. Smaller models of districts should be created instead, which would then be 
==PAGE:42==
26 
 
combined and comprise the whole city model. Further limitations of umi with regard to design possibilities  
are reported in Section 4.4.3. 
 
The Department of Architecture at MIT introduced Umi as an urban modelling tool that could study the 
operational energy of buildings, the daylight and the walkability of new neighbourhoods. The biggest 
possibility of the program lies on the rapid urban energy analysis in an urban context with a minimal model 
setup effort. Yet it should focus more on the retrofitting and urban densification confronting the limitations of 
transferring up to date data from GIS to CAD databases (Reinhart et al., 2013). 
3.1.2 IDA ICE 
IDA ICE is a simulation tool that enables the analysis of the consumption of an entire thermal envelope. There 
is also potential to analyse the indoor environment, by designing individual zones that represent different 
rooms or areas in the building. The interface of the program offers a range of default databases including 
construction elements and materials, air handling units, HVAC systems. Hence, the design of the building can 
be simple (comprising of one zone with a standard heating system) or much more complex with a combination 
of systems applied in multiple zones inside the building envelope. The settings of IDA ICE interface are 
presented in Figure 9. 
 
 
 
Figure 9 IDA ICE interface. 
 
It has to be mentioned that in IDA ICE load calculations can be performed using ideal room units. When there 
is no information about the available room units (HVAC systems), ideal room units can be used instead. On 
condition that the maximum capacity of the units is large enough, they can always cover the heating/cooling 
needs of the buildings. They are not connected to any system in the plant of the building as they do not have 
physical location in the room. Usually they are used to calculate the loads in order to dimension the systems 
of a building and they do not cause any overheating effects.

The significance of the area of Sønderborg and its ambitious saving targets by renovating the existing building

smart meters installed in each house provide heating measurements for houses connected to the network. 
Acquiring data from the local district heating service made possible the study of the energy intensity of the

4.2.1  Building databases 
The sample of the investigated buildings was rather extensive. In order to generate a complete building dataset 
for simulation as well as develop a general method to handle increased datasets, the analysis of the building 
energy consumption was based on archetypes. Archetypical buildings that represent the building stock have 
been extensively used in bottom-up modelling. The energy consumption of the archetypes was then 
extrapolated to estimate the energy consumption of the houses located in the street of Sønderborg. The method 
that was introduced in the current study, to enable the modelling and energy simulation of the neighbourhood 
in Sønderborg, indicated a collection of data from different sources to define each archetype in detail. For this 
reason, a series of databases was used: 
 
BBR data  
The  Danish National and Building Stock Register (BBR) is a nationwide registry that contains data for the 
Danish building stock. The registry was created in 1976 by gathering questionnaires which were distributed to 
the owners. BBR is continually updated by each municipality in accordance with the building regulations 
established by the Ministry of Housing. It contains information about the location (geographical coordinates) 
and the use of the building, installations of water and drainage, kitchen and heating systems. Some guidelines 
for the construction materials (about the roof and external walls), construction year and year of renovation are 
reported. Further details about the number of floors, gross floor area and the total living space of the building 
as well as additional spaces of every property (warehouses and parking lots) are also included (The Danish 
Ministry of Housing, 2007). BBR data are stored and are available to public through a Public Information 
Server (The Danish Ministry of Housing, 2001).

total building area which is the sum of the living space of the building including the utilized area of attic/ 
basement and extra floor. Noted that the area is measured at the outside of the external walls (gross surface 
area). The building use was then recorded and only the residential buildings were used for the analysis. An 
assumption was made to overcome limited documentation of BBR data. The total living space was assumed

4.2.2 Analysis of the building sample 
The archetype characterization process required to manually investigate which buildings of the available 
sample are appropriate for the study. As part of this process, a share of the available building sample had to be 
discarded due to inconsistencies or missing data. The 1st dataset (measurements of 2015) included 50 buildings. 
From that set, 4 buildings were found to be non-residential (1 church, 1 health centre, an automobile repair 
shop and a car dealership). Additionally, 3 SFH were discarded due to the huge energy consumption that 
resulted after process of the measurements and could not be explained. It was not always straight-forward to 
identify the floor areas as reported in BBR or validate these information with Google Maps. Thus, 2 more SFH 
had to be discarded. Another 3 dwellings that were apartments in block of flats were discarded due to lack of 
available information about the consumption and the position of the apartments. The final modelled set 
consisted of 38 buildings representing a 76% of the total building sample. Within the remaining set 24 
buildings were identified as apartments in Terraced houses, 11 buildings as SFH (detached houses) and 3 
buildings as detached houses with 2 floors. The application of each building is further explained below. The 
2nd building sample included 6 SFH, all of which were considered proper to use for the analysis. The two 
building datasets can be seen in detail in Table 30 and Table 31 of Appendix. The tables include the building 
use, construction year, floor area and EUI of each building. For data privacy concerns the addresses of the 
examined houses have been omitted.

The orientation of a building refers to the way a building is situated on a site as well as to the positioning of

A Reference weather file for Copenhagen, Denmark was used to represent the weather in Sønderborg. The

Denmark is one climate zone (no significant altitude differences), the weather file was considered sufficient to

Geometry of the buildings  
Some pieces of information about the geometry of the buildings and the assumptions made are reported here.  
• 
The geometry included the floor area of the buildings extracted from BBR register and a ratio between 
the dimensions of them (length to width) extracted from Google maps. 
• 
All the examined houses had a pitched roof. In case of houses with an attic, it was assumed that the 
pitched roof was extended through the whole height of the attic.  
• 
According to TABULA WebTool, a standard value of 2.5 m represents the clear floor height of all the  
building types and construction periods. The same floor height was considered for the main floor of 
archetypes that include an attic (as an extra floor). For the sake of simplification, the height of the attic 
was assumed to be the same. The former assumptions are depicted in Figure 18. 
• 
In case of houses that did not have an attic, a ratio between the clear height of the ground floor and the 
total height of the building was used with the help of Google maps - Street View. The former ratio 
enabled the estimation of the building height indicating that the total height of the building was over 
2.5 m. 
• 
The building sample and the information from the BBR register indicated that the heated space 
includes the ground floor and the attic. Therefore, for the current analysis the basement if any was 
neglected as it was considered an adiabatic space.  
• 
All the windows were designed in a height of 0.6 m from the floor in order to avoid problems of draft 
and have space for radiators under the windows. The ratio of the height to width ratio in all the 
windows was assumed to be 1/3.

4.4.1 Model setup in umi 
The case study regarding the buildings of Sønderborg was modelled in umi in two steps. First the archetypes 
were modelled individually and then an urban context was created in umi including all the buildings of the 
building dataset. Noted that since they were two building datasets with measurements of different years 
(consisted of 38 and 6 buildings respectively), the datasets were modelled separately in two different files.  
 
Different ‘Building Templates’ (see Figure 8) were created to represent the characteristics of the building 
archetypes. The conditioning in the building was defined only by giving the heating set-point and the 
operational schedule, the maximum heating capacity and the COP of the technology.  
 
For modelling the building samples in urban scale, a specific method was followed. Images extracted from 
google maps and adjusted with the right scale were inserted in umi in order to track the map and design the 
buildings in the exact position as in reality. The urban context designed in Rhino 3D with the aim of Google 
maps for the 1st dataset can be seen in Figure 21. After the subtraction of the images, the urban configuration

4.4.2 Model setup in IDA ICE 
As umi is a more simplified tool (the user does not have the possibility to define the interior zones in detail), a 
simple design of buildings comprised only of one zone was selected for the analysis in IDA ICE. Additionally, 
since there was not enough information about the dimension of the heating units installed in the buildings of 
Sønderborg, ideal heaters were applied to calculate the energy demand.  
 
The reason for the former simplified modelling of the buildings was to enable the comparison of the two 
simulation tools. The modelling of the archetypes was considered proper to be built under the same terms and 
possibilities in both tools, even though in IDA ICE there is potential for more detailed modelling. The geometry 
of the buildings as well as all the other inputs described in Table 9 were implemented into the building 
simulation software to generate the building models. Using the simulation tool IDA ICE (version 4.7.1), 
detailed calculations were performed simulating the energy consumption of the archetypes. The results of the 
simulations were extrapolated by aggregating the demand of the archetypes to represent the energy demand of 
the area studied in Sønderborg. A graphical representation of the models defined in IDA ICE can be  seen in

4.4.3 Assumptions of modelling in the two programs 
Some assumptions are stated in this section in order to elaborate on the limitations of the programs and the 
method developed to overcome these limitations and proceed with the simulations: 
• 
The building datasets contained heat consumption data including DHW, thus only the simulation 
results for DHW and heating were included in the analysis. 
• 
Umi does not support multi-zone models and therefore does not allow to model different floors with 
different settings. As the basement was considered a non-heated area, it had to be excluded from the 
modelling of the building. In particular, a building is modelled as a box in umi. A floor to floor height 
ratio divides the ‘box’ in layers representing the number of floors. The building settings including the 
properties of the building envelope are applied on the ‘box’, thus they are the same for all the floors. 
An example of a building with height of 5 m and a floor to floor height  of 2.5 m that divides the 
building in two floor is depicted in Figure 24. 
 
 
Figure 24 Example of a 5 m tall building. 
 
• 
For the sake of modelling, it was assumed that both floor area and attic occupy the same space, since 
the areas were not very different. This assumption was applied in case of houses that had an attic either 
they were SFH, TH or two family houses. 
• 
For simplification reasons, the attic was modelled as an extra floor sharing the same loads and 
operational schedules with the ground floor. In case of a two family house which included two floors 
plus an attic, the attic and the 1st floor were assumed to share the same loads as in the case of a SFH 
with an attic. 
• 
In case of terraced houses, the apartments were modelled as adjacent boxes. An adiabatic surface was 
applied in the connecting area of the walls (specified as ‘Boundary objects’ in umi layers list).  
• 
Windows could not be modelled in the roof parts because of the modelling limitations in both 
programs, thus they were designed only on wall surfaces.  
• 
Another limitation of umi simulation tool is that it does not support complex designs of buildings. It 
can only simulate buildings in the shape of rectangular boxes. Designs of buildings with a pitched roof 
are not supported by umi, therefore the calculations to determine the floor area and consequently the 
energy consumption in that case they would be incorrect. 
 
4.4.4 Modelling approach used for the analysis 
The former strong limitation of the program defined the whole approach of the modelling of the archetypes in 
the two simulation engines. Since all the examined buildings had a pitched roof, a specific methodology was 
developed to cover a general and a more detailed approach. For this reason in the current study, two approaches 
were investigated: 
 
==PAGE:62==
46 
 
The first approach included a building model in a rectangular shape with its actual height but without a pitched 
roof. This approach was based in a more general philosophy, that the user has information of the building 
height and the dimensions of a building but other design details are neglected.  
 
The second approach was more sophisticated as an assumption was made to determine an equivalent of a 
building with a pitched roof (see Figure 25). An ‘equivalent building’ was considered not regarding the 
respective building volume but with respect to the heat losses. Since the buildings examined were studied only 
from the energy aspect, it was considered proper to adjust only the thickness of the walls so that the energy 
losses would be the same as in the case that the building had a pitched roof.  
 
The aforementioned energy losses included the transmission losses (losses from building envelope and thermal 
bridges) and the ventilation losses (only losses from natural ventilation considered). For this reason, the 
simulations from IDA ICE were used. The ‘Delivered energy’, as a result of IDA ICE, reported in detail the 
losses of the simulated buildings. All the building archetypes with a pitched roof were simulated under extreme 
weather conditions with a constant temperature of -12oC (dimensioning temperature) and the losses were 
identified. Then tests were conducted for every archetype individually to adjust the wall thickness of the 
buildings with a flat roof in order to have the same energy losses as the buildings with a pitched roof (see 
Figure 25). 
 
The 2nd approach was developed by taking into consideration the design of the building and not treating the 
building as a box. Yet concerns about the improvement of the method are related in finding some patterns to 
automate the adjustment of the wall thickness with respect to the energy losses and the shape of a building. 
 
 
Figure 25 Schema of the ‘equivalent’ building.

ICE and real measurements: 
• 
The 1st scenario aims to approach more accurately the real consumption measurements.  
• 
The 2nd scenario aims to enable the comparison of the two simulation tools, umi and IDA ICE, in a 
basis of a simple design of a rectangular shape building with common settings defined. In addition, an 
indication, based on the results, of how much in detail umi simulates buildings in comparison with a 
validate detailed simulation software like IDA ICE, can be concluded.

6 Discussion 
A series of challenges faced to build the urban building model in umi are discussed below. Since the sample 
of the investigated buildings varied, the analysis of the building heat consumption was based on archetypes. 
The method classifies the building stock to building categories that represent groups of buildings with common 
characteristics. Although the method enables to handle increased datasets, it presents some limitations.  
 
Extracting knowledge from building databases 
The biggest challenge was associated with data availability, including climate data, building geometry, 
construction elements and operational schedules. Since it was not possible to gather all the building 
characteristics from one database, the data were extracted from various sources such as the Danish National 
Building Register (BBR), the validate building typology for Danish residential buildings documented in 
TABULA WebTool and Google-maps.  
 
In particular, information about the recorded building stock, such as construction year, building use and floor 
area have been determined from BBR register. Following, based on these data, the U-values for the 
construction elements of the building envelope were found using TABULA WebTool. It should be highlighted 
that these U-values are based on example or virtual buildings selected as typical examples to represent a 
specific building type and age in Denmark with respect to the heated area, construction, energy certificates etc. 
The importance of accurate estimation of U-values in energy modelling of a building is significant as it can 
lead to over/under-estimation of the heat demand of the building in question. When modelling multiple 
buildings in an urban context, the errors are increased and can lead to serious miscalculations of the EUI of a 
district.  
 
Denmark started to use mandatory Energy Performance Certificates (EPC scheme) for owners of residential 
houses in  1997. This means that before 1997, there is no relevant documentation that can certify if the building 
guidelines/regulations of each construction period have been followed for the construction of buildings widely 
in Denmark. In some periods the regulations were stricter and some others less tightened for the contractors to 
follow depending also on the region and municipality. Hindering further the estimation of accurate U-values 
for the building stock, it is assured that most of the old constructed houses have undergone usual energy 
refurbishments most commonly reinforcing the U-values of the building envelope or replacing glazing. 
 
Renovation of the buildings 
Regarding older Danish buildings, there is information from the BBR register if the buildings have been 
renovated. However, it only applies to renovation works that require a building permit. This share of renovation 
of building stock is extremely limited. In addition, registration of the renovation state that is included in the 
updated version of BBR does not specify what kind of energy retrofit has been undertaken. Hence, there is no 
information whether or not the retrofit is related to energy upgrade. Buildings are not always being renovated 
to save energy but for other reasons, i.e. other planned renovation works regarding security or design reasons. 
Nevertheless, renovation gives an added value to real estates. 
 
The U-values for usual renovation of Danish residential houses, followed to calibrate the model in the present 
study, were in line with recommendations documented in TABULA WebTool. These values are in agreement 
with the requirements for energy upgrading of building components according to Danish Building  Regulation 
2010. They apply in case the building components are totally replaced without taking into account economic 
considerations. In any case, these requirements can be satisfied if the work is economical, technical and 
architectural feasible. The TABULA tool is built on the assumption that the full thermal envelope of the 
selected house  is being upgraded (not all buildings within the same category). Hence, concerns about the 
renovation measures implemented in the present study to the building sample are expressed whether or not 
they represent reality. Mainly because upgrading the whole building envelope is expensive and naturally results 
==PAGE:78==
62 
 
in high energy savings and higher for the building stock as a whole. The implementation of the former 
renovation measures can have significant reductions in the energy demand, especially of older buildings. 
 
In order to enable the energy modelling on urban level and take advantage of its benefits, it is essential to 
document the current energy state of buildings refurbished or not in databases like BBR register or other 
national databases. Otherwise, it is very unlikely to estimate simulation uncertainties to reduce errors and 
calibrate urban models for potential use in other cases. In that direction, energy performance certificates can 
help keeping updated national databases for Danish residential building stock. Newly constructed buildings 
that follow specific requirements can be modelled with accuracy simply because the actual thermal 
characteristics of the building envelope comply with the ones described in national databases. To facilitate the 
collection of building data, surveys regarding private owners could contribute decisively to update the 
databases for later changes/refurbishments of the older building stock. Cities should keep the building related 
databases updated. 
 
Restricted access to energy data 
Aligned to the former problem of poor quality information, urban modellers require access to data of energy 
utilities and building energy audit data. Due to privacy concerns energy utilities constraint the sharing of such 
information. Nevertheless, the use of energy use data to create archetypes that correspond to real conditions 
and calibrate urban models do not infringe on the privacy of the owners and should be shared with the public.  
 
Experiencing the former restriction to energy data, it has to be reported that even for the educational purpose 
of the present investigation a lot of time and procedures have been acquired to access the energy data of few 
houses of Sønderborg that delayed the research and restrained the author from  further investigation.  The data 
regarded only houses that are connected to district heating network of one company. Extrapolating the urban 
modelling to a city or national scale, the access to energy data from different energy utilities increases the 
challenges of data availability and restraints the research on urban planning as a lot of time is wasted on 
acquiring data. Therefore, city municipalities and governments should collaborate to allow public access to 
building energy use data. 
 
Integration of geospatial data to simulation tools 
Modelling in a large scale gives also emphasis on the use of geospatial information to capture the local 
geometry of a neighbourhood up to a whole city. Simulation tools that integrate geographical information 
systems (GIS) to obtain input data for dozens to thousands buildings can facilitate the modelling of multiple 
buildings by visualizing building geometry. When modelling is enabled spatiotemporal energy consumption 
patterns of a district or a city can be acquired and the potential of infrastructure solutions and a more efficient 
interconnection of the building stock with the power system can be identified. Yet the integration of GIS format 
to umi plug-in is under development. 
 
Building archetypes and categorization 
In fact if the current study could be extrapolated to a national scale, GIS format in combination with other open 
semantic formats such as CityGML would be the only solution to model whole cities by automatically 
generating the general shape of the buildings. In addition, accounting for the complexity of building energy 
systems, constructions, operating schedules as well as uncertainty and variability of users’ behaviour and 
weather conditions, the method of archetype and categorization is recommended to simplify the case.  
 
In general, urban models in small scale show homogeneity in the construction, HVAC systems and other 
building technologies while bigger scale models differentiate. To tackle this problem, other types of 
categorization might be more appropriate for the analysis. For example abstracting the building stock in 
categories regarding the building systems or energy consumption might be a more suitable solution when the 
==PAGE:79==
63 
 
building sample is huge or there is not enough information about the constructions and renovation state. 
Another type of classification could be also based on building use. This type is preferred for non-domestic 
buildings (i.e. offices), where the energy consumption is heavily influenced by the demand for similar activities 
and in a second level by the construction and physical characteristics of buildings. 
 
Occupants behaviour 
Last but not least, the occupant behaviour is another challenge that can result in deviations between the 
simulated results and the measurements. In practice, there is always a difference between the simulated and 
the measured consumption, even when the demand is climate adjusted. There is a tendency that buildings with 
low energy demand consume more energy and vice versa. This can be attributed to the users’ behaviour. 
Building occupants should not be treated as robots that repeat the same actions every day but as individual 
autonomous subjects that can act unpredictably. Furthermore, the heating setpoints set by the occupants can 
be very critical when determining their energy use. Therefore, a much more stochastic and detailed way to 
model the occupant behaviour is suggested, that can compensate for the over/under-consumption of 
newer/older buildings.

7 Conclusions of the present analysis – Future work 
Urban modelling on a district or larger scale enables the analysis of the energy performance of small 
neighbourhoods to entire cities consisting of thousands of buildings. Urban simulation tools facilitate planning 
and evaluating retrofit strategies for buildings, as they can identify the potential of energy interventions and 
infrastructure solutions. In the current study, two building datasets consisting of 38 and 6 buildings all located 
in Sønderborg were used to test the accuracy of a new urban modelling tool. 
 
Two scenarios of building information level were developed to model realistically the buildings. Since umi 
did not provide the option to model houses with pitched roof, an attempt was made to model realistically the 
heat losses by altering the geometry of the houses (Scenario 1). Scenario 2 treated the buildings as boxes 
without considering any further details about their geometry. According to the simulation results, Scenario 1 
was considered a better approach for SFH that have an attic or an extra floor, while Scenario 2 presented 
smaller deviations for houses that consist only of a single floor without an attic.  
 
Initially, the analysis of the 1st building sample with umi on district scale showed significant deviations from 
the real measurements in the order of 30% and 60% for Scenario 1 and 2 respectively. The former constraint 
indicated further analysis in order to calibrate the urban model. Therefore, renovation cases for the older 
building stock have been formed according to energy regulation of 2010. The analysis indicated that the 
optimal assumption for the renovation was a case that included a combination of deep renovation measures 
implemented to the total set of buildings constructed in 1931-1950.  
 
In particular, the case including the energy refurbishment state of the buildings improved significantly the 
model’s accuracy resulting in less than 2% deviation for the building design determined by Scenario 1. Yet 
concerns were pointed out about the set-up of the renovation cases since there was no information available 
that could indicate the extend of the energy upgrade. Regarding the 2nd building sample that included more 
recently constructed buildings, the deviation of the simulation results from the measurements with regard to 
the heating season (October to April) was smaller compared to the deviation in the annual base. Particularly, 
the design of Scenario 2 seemed to approach better the measurements with a deviation approximately 6%. 
 
The former analysis was conducted using two different simulation tools. A validate detailed simulation tool 
IDA ICE and the under development urban simulation tool, umi. In general, umi simulations on district scale 
seemed to approach better the heat demand of the buildings, while concerns were expressed when examining 
the individual building level as the simulation uncertainties increased. An urban simulation tool like Umi takes 
into account effects that regard the interaction of buildings (such as shading or heat exchange between the 
external surfaces of buildings). In fact in an even larger scale than the one investigated, urban simulation tools 
are supposed to approach better the heat demand of a district, as the assumptions made to represent the building 
archetypes accumulate in a more generalized basis.  
 
What can be concluded about umi is that it is a promising and powerful tool which needs further development  
to overcome some limitations of the program that arise when simulating on large scale. Umi can be introduced 
to the building design community as an early design tool to assess the EUI of neighbourhoods and promote 
and test retrofit policies in urban scale. 
 
Future work 
Future work indicated by the author of the report suggests the review of the assumptions made to represent the 
archetypes as well as the expansion of the possibilities of the simulation tool. Regarding the geometry and the 
accurate orientation of buildings, the integration of GIS data format in Rhino that can work in parallel with the 
plug-in of umi could accelerate significantly the building modelling in district or larger scale. In particular, 
GIS data, if available for the city examined, describe accurately the building geometry and building height. 
==PAGE:82==
66 
 
Furthermore, in order to allow the use of tool worldwide, the developers should enable the design of buildings 
with pitched roof.  
 
Focusing on the assumptions made for the formation of archetypes, further investigation with regard to 
occupant behaviour and window opening ratios is recommended. A more stochastic profile for the users’ 
behaviour should be developed under further investigation. In addition, the total glazing area of the buildings 
was based on generic means and rules of thumbs that discard the design limitations for every building. Thus, 
more information should be gathered in order to avoid errors in the calculated heat losses. Noted that the former 
observations regarding the glazing area and the occupant behaviour might not show considerable differences 
on the individual building level, but it is believed that they have bigger influence in larger scale modelling of 
buildings.  
 
Finally, in the current study, a limited number of buildings was  investigated covering mainly terraced and 
detached houses. The majority of them were buildings constructed in 1931-1950. As it came out, the former 
houses have undertaken refurbishment measures that was difficult to determine as they were not registered to 
a database. Hence, the building sample can be called in question and considered as not appropriate to reach to 
general conclusions about the accuracy of the tool. Additional research is suggested investigating a larger 
building sample that could enable the modelling with greater accuracy in order to observe patterns and tackle 
uncertainties of the tool.
