Abstract
In order to convert the energy systems of today to what is commonly known as the 4th
generation district heating grid, the district heating system must become smarter in terms of
creating inter-connectivity between energy producers but also producers and consumers. A core
element in smart grids of the future is system ability to be ﬂexible. Flexible energy systems
features many elements. What this study proclaim is that a substantial amount of ﬂexibility exist
between district heating producers and district heating consumers. The heating ﬂuid connecting
these two, have the potential of creating a ﬂexibility potent enough to signiﬁcantly reduce the
spike of heat demand that occurs every day between 7 and 9 AM. Using the heating ﬂuid as
such is in the known literature called network storage. Within the concept of network storage
there is unused potential. This potential exists in the cold half of the heating grid which until
now have not been considered.
The future role of the heat pumps in the heat sector can no longer be denied. Furthermore,
countries like Denmark, with well established district heating networks, are at this moment
exploring the options for integrating heat pumps in the aforementioned smart and ﬂexible energy
systems. In the state of the art research regarding heat pumps, it becomes clear that heat pump
has a lot to contribute with in regards to ﬂexible and smart solutions.
This project tests diﬀerent options for temporarily increasing the energy content of a district
heating network. Especially increasing the cold half of networks will be in focus as this has
been neglected in past studies. The tests are carried out in working case network and practical
challenges with network storage will be identiﬁed.
The result of the study show that network storage is capable of reducing the heat production
9.5 / 36.6 / 16.7 kWh by shifting the load. This was achieved for the FlexHeat network though
test. Simulations showed reductions of 13.6/16.2/22.9kWh. In a peak load occurrences network
storage is also capable of reducing the peak production with up to 11 % for the FlexHeat. It is
important is emphasise that the degree of the heat load shift depends strongly on the size of the
network and to what degree the temperatures can be alternated. The peak load reduction will
vary according to the demand density and the network in question.
An proposal for key values was made to quantify the potential in speciﬁc terms. The heat
storage in the network of 0.88 kWh/m3-K was achieved in the FlexHeat network using traditional
charge with supply pipelines. Using the return pipelines achieved 1.03 kWh/m3-K of heat storage.
In order to strengthen the validity of these key values, further studies most be made. The
application of such key value can, to the authors opinion, move future research towards a more
accessible quantiﬁcation. Future research will possibly discover the need for parameter and
variable amendments to the key value concept.
The results of this studies shows that the increased expenses for both heat loss and COP will
exceed the ﬁnancial gain of even the most favourable power scenarios of the 2018 power market.
In the future scenarios the incentive for moving the load rose as the price became more sensitive
towards the spot price.

Introduction
The aim of this master thesis is to evaluate the option of combining network storage with the
growing district heating technology, heat pumps. This study will create a simple quasi static
model for calculating the potential in the network, and a model calculating the eﬀect of changed
temperatures on a heat pump producing district heating. Three diﬀerent options for storing
heat in network pipelines will be tested on an operating network. This network is placed on the
outer part of Nordhavn in Copenhagen and is disconnected to the remaining Copenhagen(CPH)

1.1
Motivation
The ambition of a green and renewable future has made its mark on big parts of the world’s
population, politicians and companies. This ambition is among others manifested in Ørsted,
HOFOR and DK. The Danish energy producer Ørsted’s goal of being coal free by 2023 [7]. The
Copenhagen utility company of gas and heat HOFOR’s goal of being CO2 neutral by 2025 [8].
The state of Denmark’s goal of producing completely renewable heat and power by the year 2035
[9].
The goals are many and ambitious, however indiﬀerent without actions and technology change.
Already, it seems some goal are hard to reach. As of 2017 the emission of CO2 was just under 72
gCO2,eq/kWhth, from the average District Heating(DH) production in the metropolitan area of
Copenhagen. 1 This is seen in ﬁg. 1.1. This sets the projected development of emission to hit
zero between the years 2025 and 2030, 5 years later than the goal. The danish power production
were, according to Energinet [10], on average emitting 437 gCO2,eq/kWhe. This was only an
8 % improvement from the year before. A speed which sets the national emission from power
production 25% above taget in 2035.
Lund and Clark [11] determines that a self-suﬃcient power sector is the best ﬁnancial way to
reach the above mentioned goals. A self suﬃcient power sector means to use Danish generated
power in Denmark as opposed to rely on importing power from surrounding countries. Jakob Z.T
[12] reaches the conclusion that both more cross-border and cross-sector inter-connectivity will
improve power economics and reduce emissions. However cross-sector inter-connectivity shows
a better performing network. This study seeks to increase the power to heat interconnection
between the power and heat sector, which already exists with combined heat and power (CHP)
and power to heat (PtH). The latter has very low capacity and production in DH of Denmark. A
higher inter-connectivity can be achieved by increasing the heat sectors ability to use power and
preferably, when it is in surplus. To use surplus power would often mean to produce heat outside
regular demand periods.
Heat sector emissions are closely connected to the power sector emission. The production of
power and heat is coupled in a way that often means production of one requires the production
of the other. Such co-generation is of a long tradition in using CHP production in the Danish
energy sector as described in Werner and Frederiksen [6]. In the past and present this have
created big eﬃciency advantages. However due to ﬁnite storage capacity of either heat or power,
incoherence occurs where either to much power or heat is produced. This is especially true for
periods of high supply of RES. Here over production of power from CHP plants increase the
emission because of the demand for heat. In ﬁg. 1.2 an example of power composition develops
throughout a day. As seen the large power plants raise in production during 6 and 12 AM. This

1.2
Context
The ability to make low emission energy is not enough if its managed unintelligent. Creating
more intelligent control strategies to form so-called smart energy systems is essential for combating
climate change, says Basciotti et al. [15]. The concept of smart energy system does not have an
exact deﬁnition but Connolly et al. [16] describes smart energy system as enabling “new forms of
ﬂexibility within the energy system”.
This ﬂexibility is a recurrent element in the concept of smart energy systems. Here the timing
between heat demand and production is a key element. This in particular is an important tool
for dealing with the challenge of intermittent production of RES. Flexibility is also useful when
handling consumption patterns with high peaks according to Vandermeulen et al. [17]. This
study will study to the networks ability to shift heat demand in time, in order to better suit the
production unit and thus creating ﬂexibility.
When using storage it is possible to reduce DH peak load production and doing so with cost
and energy eﬃciency. Verda and Colella [18] claim that energy use can be reduced up to 12 %
while costs can be reduced with up to about 5 % by using regular storage tanks in district heating.

1.3
Research
In 1989, Von Gerd Oeljeklaus [21] wrote that decoupling of heat generation and demand could
be achieved by storing heat in the heating ﬂuid of networks. Since then even more reason for
doing so have increased. However the method is not being used to the full potential writes Brian
Mathiesen et al. [22]. The state of the art research on the topic of network storage(NWS) will be
reviewed in this section including the knowledge gaps and the research questions of this study
will be described.
1.3.1
State of the art research
Numerous studies have modelled the eﬀect of supply storage in pipelines and found that
network storage have the potential to reduce peak load and possibly emissions. Daniele Basciotti
et al. [23] modelled the DH network of Altenmarkt, Austria. Here they showed that using
DH-pipelines they could store up to 0,55 MWh over a period of 4,5 hours. They found that their
case network could be used as storage for up to 15 % of the peak load by raising the supply
temperature. The authors found that the DH network of Altenmarkt could in fact shift peak
load in time. They did so by raising the supply temperature from 100 to 110◦C. Thus resulting

over production for that particular network. The discharge period could thus be reduced with

return pipeline. In this study the use of return storage was not investigated.
A load shift similarly to network storage is found when using a network building mass to
store energy. Buildings heat capacity can also contribute with storage in the network. This was
quantiﬁed in Coninck and Helsen [26]. They fund that buildings connected to the DH network can
contribute with was is called heat inertia, thus increasing the networks storage capacity. HOFOR
has been actively involved in research on the topic of short term heat storage in buildings and
the ﬂexibility it brings. One paper of this research is presented in Sandersen and Honor´e [27].
Here it was found that peak consumption was relocated by reducing heat supply thus shifting
the load to a later time. Thereby having the termal mass of the buildings to carry latent heat for
between 3-5 hours, which was found to be the best through testing. These test where conducted
in a Copenhagen combined housing and commercial district.
1.3.2
Research gaps
There is still questions yet to be answered regarding storing heat energy within DHN. As
heating technology develops so do the gaps in the understanding of network storage. It remains
to be investigated how much heat pumps in DH are aﬀected by network storage options in
particularly the increase in return temperature. It generally know that higher DH temperatures
decrease the viability of large scale heat pumps but not whether the lower COP is compensated
suﬃciently by the gains of a ﬂexible production window.
General key values for appraising a networks storage potential is lacking. This is expressed
in the two reviews of Vandermeulen [17] and Hennessy [28]. Both of which, have conducted
literature review of network storage. They seek a generalised, simple and concise method to
evaluate the potential of networks storage potential. Studies like [23] expresses their storage
potential in absolute values as 3,0 MWh. This is making them diﬃcult to compare or use in
other contexts. If the results were in the form of key values like kWh/m3-K they could much
easier be compared with and projected upon other networks. Hennessy [28] reviewed ”short-term
storage in district heating distribution networks”and his summarised shortcomings were:
• Calculations of storage capacity are approximated and not standardised
• Modelling does not take additional stresses on pipes into account
• There is no estimation of the threshold size of a network needed to enable storage

1.3.3
Research questions
This study intent to answer the following research questions. The research questions will serve
as boundary for the hypothesis stated in the following subsection.
This study will investigate diﬀerent possibilities of using the network for storing heat and what
the storage potential would be. This will be done by testing network storage on the FlexHeat
Network(FHN) located in the outer district of Nordhavn, Copenhagen. FHN in Nordhavn is
detached from the remaining part of the Copenhagen DHN, making it possible to isolate the
eﬀects. The tests will serve two purposes. Firstly, it will identify and evaluate the practical eﬀects,
challenges and limitations of network storage. This will be done by applying three diﬀerent
versions of network storage strategies. This include storage in the supply and return pipelines
and one combining the two. Secondly, the purpose of tests will be to collect data samples which,
will serve as validation for a numerical model.
The eﬀect of network storage will be examined for a two stage Ammonia heat pump. As
mentioned in section 1.1, heat pumps are gaining foothold within the heat sector of both
households and DH, thus research that combines network storage with heat pumps is lacking.
Researching this question targets answering how temperature changes from NWS will impact
the HP COP(Coeﬃcient Of Performance).
The degree to which NWS aﬀect the distribution heat loss and thus the eﬀectiveness of the
storage will be examined. This will be investigated by gathering data from both test and data
from simulations based on modelling. These data will be used to evaluate this question for
diﬀerent storage strategies.
The temperature changes for the heating ﬂuid inside the DH are known to aﬀect the CHP
plants. Therefore it will be analysed to what degree network storage temperature changes will
impact these CHP plants as they are likely to continue as they are likely to be a signiﬁcant DH
heat technology in Denmark/CPH for the coming years. An analysis of the general eﬀect on back
pressure plants and extraction plants will be discussed for the temperature interval of network
storage.
The extra compressor work (due to extra loss and worse COP) will be evaluated against the
ﬁnancial gain of using cheap surplus power from the power grid. This study will examine the
feasibility of shifting a heat load, on a short term basis, exploit lower power prices at the cost
of the expected decreased eﬃciency in both production and distribution. Using HPs creates an
opportunity for exploiting ﬂuctuating power prices. Thus it will be investigated whether cost
savings are possible if production load can be moved to low price periods. Heat storage in general
can do this, if short term network storage can be installed easily and cheaply. The long term
ﬁnancial beneﬁts of using network storage will not be studied.
It will be investigated whether it is possible to deﬁne a number of key values to apply to general
networks to estimate the potential for network storage. Can these key values can be expressed in
form of e.g. MWh/m3-K, MWh/km-K or will a explipliﬁedisation need a equation format. An
exempliﬁed usage of the key value will be made and applied to the DHN of Copenhagen, using
the learnings from tests and simulations.

1.4
Hypothesis
The heat capacity in the district heating pipelines are capable of shifting load as perceived by
the heat production to periods more preferable. This can be done by temporarily changing the
temperature setup of either supply, return or both in the distribution network.
Sensible storage in pipelines can be utilised with ﬁnancial- and environmental-feasibility. This
can be achieved by creating a widened ﬂexibility for the heat production and thereby better
ability to exploit the intermittent power production.
Sensible storage within district heating pipelines is possible to implement upon the FlexHeat
network of Nordhavn without making network invasive methods. It can be done so completely
without aﬀecting the consumers consumption patterns.
1.5
Methodology
The approach to the problem in this study is ﬁrstly to create an overview of the existing
literature. This overview should act as an oﬀset for choosing which storage option are going to
be evaluated. From the initial options considered, a test series will be formulated and carried out
on the case subject. The data from these tests shall serve as initial data for the evaluation of the
options. Also the data shall be used for validation of the simulation model. The validated model
will supply the main data of the evaluation.
The simulation model will consist of two parts. The ﬁrst part is a network model calculating
the distribution of DH water from the plant to the consumer nodes, taking into account; heat
loss, the distributional delay of the energy consumption and the collective heat accumulated in
the networks pipelines. The second part is the heat plant itself modelled to quasi static follow
the demand in eﬀect and temperature set point.
To further evaluate the storage options in a more general sense, the strategies will be
implemented in ﬁctive and more common constitution inspired by the Copenhagen network with
regards to network and heat technology mix. Here the eﬀects will be investigated for especially
condensing CHP plants.
A feasibility analysis will be made answering whether the options have ﬁnancial and environ-
mental value. The ﬁnancial analysis will include a number of scenarios for power prices. e.g. a
ﬁxed power price and a variable price following the typical spot price. The environmental analysis
will also use a number of scenarios for the emission throughout production hours of a day.

consumption. The relationship between demand and production as explained here is depicted in

1.7
Terminology
The terminology can vary depending of the perspective of the writer. In this section the
terminology of this study is established.
The deﬁnition of ﬂexibility and its usage in the heat and power sector. The word ﬂexibility
is used in a large variety of studies and articles and do contain multiple perspectives and is
therefore diﬃcult to deﬁne generally. In the context of smart heat network or 4th generation DH,
Vandermeuelen et al. [17] provides the deﬁnition of ﬂexibility as ”the ability to speed up or delay
the injection or extraction of energy into or from a system”. Hennessy et al. [28] acknowledges this
views but point out that it is missing an aspect of ”cross-sector inter-connectivity. where the avai-
lability and capacities of coupling points between energy systems is also important”Throughout
this thesis, the term ‘ﬂexibility’ is used to refer to a systems ability to disconnect production and
consumption in order to better integrate all energy productions. Speciﬁc to the district heating
sector, ﬂexibility is thereby among others the ability to create a short term heat storage eﬀect in
order to widen the production window of the heat while complying to the usual demand and
utilising ﬂuctuations in power production, price and emission.

The network is located in the furthest corner of the Nordhavn district in Copenhagen and

2.1.1
Heat production
The heat production of the FHN is produced by a heat pump supplied with groundwater
as heat source. The heat pump is a two stage ammonia reversed rankine cycle that lifts the
temperature GW temperature to the appropriate DHN temperature. This is usually a temperature
lift of 60 K from the 10 ◦C groundwater to about 70 ◦C supply temperature. With such as
temperature lifts, two compressor stages are appropriate in order handle the otherwise very high
discharge temperatures. A simple PID of the heat pump is shown in ﬁg. 2.2
The evaporator seen furthest to the left is a shell and plate type. This is a rather new type of
HEX but roughly resembles a plate HEX. Furthers to the right, is the condenser divided into
three units. A desuperheater, condenser and subcooler which all three is shell and tube type. The
compressors are electrically driven reciprocating compressors with 6 pistons each. In between
the two compressors an open ﬂash intercooler insures only saturated vapour enters the second
compressor. The same does the naturally circulated separator connected to the evaporator.
Operation of the heat plant is divided into two intervals. Heat pumps charging and TES
discharging. When the heat pump is charging it usually runs at 80 % load. This is approx. 640
kW th condenser eﬀect and 180 kW e compressor eﬀect; the COP is then 3.5. As explained in
section 2.1.4 the 640 kWth is way above the usual heat demand of 200-300 kWth. Thus the heat
storing is a substantial part of the production. The diﬀerence between production and demand
is stored in the Thermal energy storage (TES) next to the plant. Production is started when
the tank is empty and continues until the storage tank is full. Hereafter the HP shut down and

Method
Within the method section three main parts are described. The approach regarding test
description and execution. The method used in modelling the numeric model here under the HP
and network models. The method used in calculation of the feasibility study.
3.1
Test description and execution
The purpose of the tests is to apply the suggested theory in practice on a working district
heating network. As mentioned in section 1.3.3 one of the research questions is to investigate the
reactions of large scale heat pumps to network storage. Another question is to research how to
use network storage in the return line. The part of the questions that can be answered by testing
will be attempted answered in this tests.
Theory and practice can diﬀer because of the various real-life conditions and the limitations
of simulations. Testing such strategies is therefore necessary in order to discover unexpected
challenges in the theoretical concepts.
The method applied in the tests is temperature and valve manipulation in order to create a
short term heat storage eﬀect. This can possibly widen the ﬂexibility of district heating through a
bigger production window for the heat producer. Creating this ﬂexibility between the production
and consumption of heat can have ﬁnancial, environmental and logistical beneﬁts.
The FlexHeat network is part of a large project consortium of test. This particular test is
numbered test 2. Supply, combined and return charges is applied respectively in 2a, b and c. All
tests were planned and formulated by the author as a part of the thesis. The tests were managed
by HOFORs operating engineer.
3.1.1
Primary testing
Three primary tests were conducted for the data collection. The description of these is
disclosed in this section.
Test 2.A was performed to examine the storage capacity achievable by charging the supply
pipeline. The level of charge was 10 K. The change was applied at the heat plant from the supply
reference temperature of 70 ◦C thus increasing it to 80 ◦C. The plant was meant to be operating
with condenser eﬀect of 700kWth equivalent to 87.5 % load but ran at 500kWth equivalent to
62.5 % load. The 10 K charging phase was initiated 45 minutes after the heat pump start up.
The time it took too fully charge was observed as a result. The loaded phase was planned to
last 30 minutes but was reduced to 10 minutes during the testing. The discharge phase was
initiated by returning the supply temperature to 70 ◦C. The time it took to fully discharge
was observed as a result. After approx. 10 minutes of discharging down to 70◦C, complications
arose in operation and the supply temperature arose to 75 ◦C and the condenser eﬀect arose to
700kWth. This unintended regulation was inserted in test 2a, at time 22:47. This has introduced
a number observations for which analysis will be made of. In the remainder of the report this
will be referred to as ”The incident in 2a”
Test 2.B was performed to examine the storage capacity achievable by charging in the supply
and return pipelines combined. The charge applied was 10 K on the supply and aiming to increase
return temperatures 10 K as well. The charge was applied to the heat plant from the supply
reference temperature of 70 ◦C thus increasing it to 80 ◦C. Simultaneously, manipulation of the
substation valves was applied to Terminal 2 & 3. Both charges was initiated 25 minutes after the
heat pump start up and the period of charging was observed as a result. Force opening M1 valve
(see ﬁg. 2.6a) created an increase from the initial return temperature 35 ◦C. The test aimed at a
10 K increase for the two terminals 2 and 3 and then having an average return increase to 45 ◦C.

3.1.2
Data limitations
Real-life circumstances can bring some practical challenges to a scientiﬁc experiment. The
challenges apparent before, during and after the tests was performed, will be disclosed in this
section.
The production window and thus the testing window is limited to about 3 to 4 hours which
limits the data collection from the HP to this time period. In order to keep everything in the
network running under usual circumstances, the operation of the heat pump remains on its usual
production pattern. This means about 80 % heat load and production time of 3 to 4 hours. This
is further explained in section 2.1.1. The implications of this short window is that longer storage
period can not be tested with suﬃcient data collection. Furthermore, the complete collection of
data of the HPs reaction to the discharge can be impaired.
The HPs reaction to return temperature from the terminals was diluted because the HP is
producing above demand. The excess heat produced is stored in the heat storage tank, thus
drawing water from the cold end of the storage tank while taking in the return water from the
network. This causes the temperature contribution from the terminals return to be diluted. The
ratio depends on the imminent ﬂow demand at the substations which varies during the tests.

3.2.3
The process of building the model
The construction of the model have in this study gone through a number of simulation phases.
These will brieﬂy be presented below with emphasis on the validation of the numeric model using
experimental data shown in section 4.1, and obtained through the tests described in section 3.1.
This section is written in relation to the learning process of construction numerical models.
Initially an conceptual model was made to create an idea of where the key elements of the
model and what to focus on for the upcoming work. At this point the model is made up of a
draft of the process. Fundamental concepts such as heat exchange, energy conservation and heat
loss is formulated. Here there is complete freedom regarding parameter values, interconnections
between the fundamental concepts. In parallel a literature study is being performed in order
to retrieve technical knowledge of the functionality of study speciﬁc concepts. Furthermore the
literature servers as temporary scope for what the model is expected to produced when ﬁnalised.
Thus the process of building the model have moved through the the conceptual, into a physical
and mathematical where the concepts have been enlighten and beginnings to be expressed in
equations of assumptions for the working of the model. Hence creating a model level where the
process is operating in design conditions still with freedom in lesser parameter. From this model
concepts of what is interesting to test experimentally is facilitated. Calculation at this stage
being made to narrow down the test expectations and description.
A numeric model of which can be operate within the design conditions is continuously
being veriﬁed with the conceptual understanding of the sub-processes. Here the mathematical
formulation of HEX process was especially a case in point. Here the veriﬁcation eventually lead to
an mathematical treatment for choosing appropriate condenser temperatures in the LMTD setup.
This is seen in the EES code present in the appendix appendix A.8 under the procedure LMTD,
line code 257 to 295. This phase and process was repeated until functionality of the model can
embrace moving (partially) into the level of process dynamic (quasi static) concepts. At this level
complexity of the model have replace the freedom of component dimensions characterisation.
Leaping into validation of the model the model is deprived of all parameter freedom and
process-/case speciﬁc adjustments is made to reach as model capable of solving the established
model and study purpose. During validation certain correction in the model construction was
made in order to reach compliance between model and test data. Output from the heat pump
compressor polynomial was prior to the validation a known object of concern. The polynomial
was corrected with a factor worsening the resulting mass ﬂow and power consumption. These
corrections is seen in the appendix appendix A.8 line code 200 to 255. Furthermore, correcting
the assumed subcooling from 10◦C to 27 ◦C in the condenser. Lastly it is worth mentioning the
correction applied to the heat conductivity of the pipe insulation. Here the resulting value was
0.016[W/m-K]. Seen in appendix appendix A.8 line code 5.
The product of the validation was not a fully validated model, as crucial elements of the
model was to simpliﬁed in order to reach a fully consistent data. These elements include ﬂow
process’ such as pressure driven ﬂow and also the process of quasi-static calculation was never
fully functioning. Never the less did the validation process establish a high enough conﬁdence in
the elements in the model which did to a degree, followed the experimental data. These include
charging eﬀects, timings and the entirety of the heat pump element.
3.2.4
Assumption in the modelling
Making the model a number of assumption have be made. These have been necessary in order
to either keeping the problem as simple as possible or because the entities are unknown.
• No mixing in the pipe cross section as result of fully or partly developed ﬂow. This means
Page 24 of 98
==PAGE:39==

Master thesis
June 2019
to essentially use uniform ﬂow through out the network creating a ﬁxed heat change wave
through out the pipelines
• Pipe laying entities have been assumed. Distance (2xD see ﬁg. 3.3) have been assumed to
be 0.5 meters. Depth (H see ﬁg. 3.3) have been assumed to be 1.5 m. steel pipe thickness=
2mm. Top layer soil / ambient temperature = 8 ◦C.
• The test performed show challenges regarding control and change of the return temperature.
In the modelling it is assumed that the return temperature and heat eﬀect from the
substations is instant and under complete control. A rather rough assumption and source
margin of error. This point will need further research.
• Quasi static behaviour is assumed. Usually DHN can behave very sporadic with ﬂows and
temperatures ﬂuctuating quite a bit. But the simplicity of FHN and the few number of
consumer nodes makes quasi static an okay assumption.
• The distribution pressure loss and secondary distribution pump is not considered in the
calculations.
• Pump work for groundwater pumps are neglected. As is all auxiliary pumps. While the
groundwater pumps actually use a considerable amount of power (8/10 kWe), they are
controlled to operate at constant load at all time the heat pump is active.
• Neglecting the soils heat capacity will course an underestimation of the heat loss during
charging and overestimate the heat loss during discharging. As seen on ﬁg. 3.5 the soil

shows an increase about 20 % for the worst case. This will roughly translate to 0.8 K and
0.4 K increase on the ground area of 0.63 and 3.8 m2 cross-section.
• For the heat pump a number vapour compression cycle parameter have been assumed as
no measurement and few manufacturing data have been available. Subcooling is set to 25
K. Intermedia pressure is most likely ﬁxed at around 1300 kPa but the pressure in the
model varieties with following constrain: Pi = √Pl ∗Ph. U and A values for the 4 HEX
have be estimated and ﬁxed from known operating point.

4.3
Key values
Standardising the results shown in section 4.2.3 have the aim to remove the network speciﬁc
impact on the ﬁgures and aim at identifying a common applicable value for other networks. The
values to be shown below, is the amount of thermal energy which a network can remove from the
discharge period.
In creation of these key values the amount of storage that is applied most be quantiﬁed. For
supply storing this is simply the same as the supply temperature meaning an increase from 70◦C
to 80◦C is equal to a storage level of 10K. For return it is diﬀerent. Here the average temperature
of the network return pipeline temperature is the storage level. For this an example is used for
the FlexHeat network. Terminal 1, 2 and 3 have a return temperature of respectively 30, 30
and 40 with and average temperature 29,65. The temperature perceived by the heat pump and
plant is instinctively lower than 40 because of dilution as described earlier. However the average
temperature i 35.7 ◦C. Thus an storage level of 6,12K have been realised by the 10K increase at
Terminal 3. The resulting storage level will thus variate according to where in the network the
charge is applied from and what the intensity of it is.
For storage in the supply line the test performed show that 0.824 kWhth/K-m3 was stored. In
simulations supply storage resulted in 0.980 kWhth/K-m3 for a 9 K storage level. Simulation
show no variation in this number with varying storage level. The storage level do not change the
key value in the supply line.
For storage in the return line the test performed, show that 0.946 kWhth/K-m3 was stored.
In the simulations for return storage some varyation between storage levels was found. For a
return storage level of 6,2K the key value is 1.020 kWhth/K-m3. The key value is retrieved for a
storage level of 9.6K.
For comparison the maximum sensible storage in water would is equivalent to the Speciﬁc
heat capacity of water. Based on Cp and density, the results can be rewritten to 1.138 kWh/K-m3
for 70 ◦C and 1.156kWh/K-m3 for 30 ◦C.

Discussion
In this section the results obtained in the study will be upheld against each-other in a
comparison between model and test data. This will then be put into perspective using the known
literature. The product will be used to answer the research questions proclaimed in section 1.3.3.
Each research question will be handled one by one and all questions will be tied together at the

5.1
Testing, identiﬁcation of practical eﬀects, challenges and limitations,validation
Shifting the heat load through testing was successful using sensible storage in the supply lines,
return lines and a combined. A signiﬁcant part of the heat supply was reduced over a controlled
period of time. Three diﬀerent levels of storage was applied with three diﬀerent strategies. Using
supply storage, 10 kWh heat production was successfully moved, using return storage it was 17
kWh, and for supply and return combined 37kWh heat production was moved from the discharge
period forth to the charging period.
The level of storage mentioned above is referring to the level of temperature change. The
supply charge was applied with ∆T = 10K. Return storage level was applied with ∆T = 11K.
The combined storage had an applied storage level of ∆T = 25K. Supply storage level is in
this study determined as the increase in temperature from the heat pump. However, dealing
with partial or exclusive return storage, the task of determining the storage level was more
complicated compared to the supply storage. In this study the return storage level is determined
as the weighted average temperature increase.
The method for obtaining the load shifts using return storage was developed through control
and manipulation of the substations. The method had to be fabricated as an existing method did
not exist in the literature. Ideally a mixing loop would have been used. This would have allowed
a control mixing at the location of the loop, and would have made the storage level more certain.
However, this was not available and a sub-optimal method had to be developed. Manipulation of
the regulating valve M1 was assessed to create the best alternative to the mixing loop. Thus
valve manipulation was applied to two out of three of the consumers and heating the return side
of the network was made possible. This increased ﬂow realised, resulted in a signiﬁcant charging
eﬀect. On average the network stored 12,3 kW during the return charging. At the initiation of
the charge the stored eﬀect peaked at 56 kW. Having control over the consumers substations,
is necessary in order to create return storage in the way it was done in these tests, and it did
inﬂuence the heat consumption. An increase in heat consumption occurred during the charging
period of both return and combined storage. The reason for the increased consumption was an
unintended increase in DHW at the two terminals manipulated.
The validation process led to important corrections, balancing some parts of the model with
the test data, other parts of the model remained inaccurate due to the pre-installed limitation
of the model under pressure diﬀerence. Validating the compressor power consumption exposed
inaccuracy in the modelling serial compressor solution. However, the model was corrected with
an appropriate factor on both mass ﬂow and power consumption of the low stage compressor.
Doing so balanced the values with the data produced through testing. Validation of volume ﬂow
out into the network and through each of substations show critical issues and diﬀerence between
the model and test data.

and return temperatures. Supply temperature in connection with the role of heat pumps role

5.3
Network loss from test, model - Neglected pipe and soil Cp
The distribution loss in the FlexHeat network is increased signiﬁcantly when subjected to
supply and combined storage, while return storage aﬀects the loss to a lesser degree. From the
test data it was not possible to determine the distribution loss as the testing periods were so
compact that substantial steady state periods could not be achieved either with or without
charging obtained. Because of this, the energy stored was estimated based on an assumption
that the distribution loss was only 3 %. This would be a very low distribution loss compared to
what would be expected. However, when interrupting the result from the tests, only 3 % would
produce reliable outcomes. Using the model to calculate the distribution loss it would be in the
range of 6 to 8 % depending on the heat demand and the applied strategy. To this, heat capacity
of the pipe and soil material will contribute with higher loss during charging. The heat loss will
be equally lower in the discharging. Basic heat transfer state that higher temperatures result
in higher heat gradients and loss. In addition to this, the heat shield released from the supply
pipeline surrounds and limits the loss from the return pipeline.
The steel pipes and soil surrounding them, both have a certain thermal heat capacity which
have been neglected in this study. However, later estimates of the thermal capacity disprove
that their contribution is negligible. Especially, the steel mass of the water carrying pipes will
be exposed to a signiﬁcant temperature increase and will because of the medium sized heat
capacity and high density contribute with 0.0064 kWh/m for a 10 K increase. Estimating the
soils potential is however more intricate and will take a further and more dedicated study to
examine.

NWS eﬀect on the Copenhagen network and its production
Until now the study have treated storage of heat in an isolated small network with a heat
pump supply. However applying NWS to a large network such as CPH DHN is far more complex.

this study and will have to be treated in future work. However, if in this paragraph the changes8
is directly aﬀecting the primary heating plants of Copenhagen9 some concept of ripple eﬀect of
network storage can be approximated. Simple back-pressure CHP plants’ electrical eﬃciency
directly aﬀected by higher temperatures. Higher temperatures will raise the back pressure for
which the the steam can be expanded to. Therefor will a combined increase of return and supply
temperatures lower the power output.In the work of Torben et al. [1] the back-pressure electrical
eﬃciency is estimated to decrease between 1 and 3 % for a 10 K temperature increase in supply
with proportional10 increase in return. Also the heat production could be aﬀect by changes
in eﬀectiveness of the caloriﬁers11. This is however depending on the speciﬁc changes in HEX
temperature diﬀerence and ﬂow. The extraction CHP plants are better equipped to handle
the temperature change. A combined and proportional increase of temperature will in a low
temperature scenario aﬀect the extraction plant very limited in regards to the electrical eﬃciency.
However, the power loss factor by heat extraction is increased. This means that the ”price”in
terms of loss power production is higher when NWS changes is applied to an extrication plants.
The above mention impacts is true for supply temperature scenarios above 66 ◦C according to
ﬁgure 9a in [37]. Flue gas condensation is in modern CHP is also aﬀected by increased return
temperature but here it is small changes in only the latent heat utilisation of the ﬂue gas which is
reduced. Concluding this paragraph the impression that primary CHP plants is better equipped
to handle the temperature changes of network storage than that of heat pumps. This is impression
is consistent with that of [23] and [18]. These is further below section 5.5.
Applying return storage in practice will be challenging. Partly due to the learning from this
study and partly additional issues. The learnings from test 2b and 2c was among others how
to determine/express the resulting storage in Kelvin as the mixing of diﬀerent return streams
compile and the temperatures dilute. Also a challenge was to simply activate the increase or
storage, where substation manipulation gave the desired eﬀect, but had impact on the consumer in
terms of higher consumption. This increased consumption was deposited in the DHW tank, from
which it is unclear whether the consumers was able to take advantage of. It is possible that the
increase heat energy deposited in the tank was blurred out in excessive heat loss or whether the
tank was capable of containing the heat energy and simply store it in the subsequent hours. The
additional issues appears when it is realised that control over consumer substations is in practice
rarely an option. In theory substations are the property of the individual DH consumers whose
information within is conﬁdential. Strategies where consumers experience increased consumption
would be unacceptable. District heating morning peak consumption is to a large extend caused by
DHW. A large amount of DH costumers in CPH live in multi-story building. Here a considerable
amount of water subside in the tertiary systems. However, permanent bypass and mixing loop
will be present around distribution networks like in Copenhagen. Such mechanism is described in
[6] page 291. Diﬃculties regarding classifying the level of storage emerged as return storage was
introduced. Opposite to supply storage will storing in the return pipeline mix and dilute through
out the network. Earlier in this section it is explained how it was solved for this study. However,
the complexity of huge network as with Copenhagen might not allow such approaches.

5.6
Key values, usability and application on CPH
Key values can be used to establish coherence between research studies and will thus raise
the quality of it. Furthermore, the use of key values can be used to valuate the shift possible
networks were numeric model cant be used to ﬁnd such values. Brian Vad Mathiesen et al. [22]
raises the point that key values can be used to expand the use of NWS.
The key values of this study was estimated to 0,88 kWhth/m3-K for supply and 1.03kWth/m3-K
for return. Whether these values is suitable for generalisation is not investigated. Firstly, future
work should extract key values based on existing literature’s published values, which then can
be compared this studies’. Secondly further experimental data could give very good basis for
recognising the accuracy. Eventually the format of these key values will maybe have to change
into a equation format in order capture network diversity.
Using the key values upon the collective Copenhagen distribution network with storage level

Conclusion
It is increasingly interesting to use the diﬀerent energy systems to create smarter grid that can
assist and reinforce each other. Thus the favourable qualities for each system can be ampliﬁed.
Furthermore, challenges regarding intermittent production will be reduced when systems have
the ability to control injection and extraction of energy into the grids, in order to store energy
when energy is produced. This kind of ﬂexibility can to some degree be obtained by using the
district heating systems magnitude. Using district heating systems in collaboration with heat
pump technology, the ﬂexibility can assist and reinforce the power grid system. Network storage
have the same kind of beneﬁcial properties as other thermal energy storage’s in regards to load
shifting and thus ﬂexibility contribution.
The method of using network storage to shift heat demand is conﬁrmed using the smaller
network of FlexHeat in Nordhavn, Copenhagen. Furthermore, it was shown that using both
supply pipelines and return pipelines is possible for storage of heat energy. One method of using
consumer substations to apply storage to return pipelines was investigated through testing. This
method was successful in creating return storage. The method had the downside that it gave
consumer disadvantages. Using the substation method was causing consumer level changes. The
potential beneﬁts and disadvantages of including consumer level changes should be investigated
in future work.
The traditional strategy in network storage is to raise the supply temperature, however with
raising also the return temperature the accumulated storage eﬀect will approximately double the
accumulated eﬀect.
In summery, storage of heat in the pipelines of existing district heating grid with heat pump
supply, was investigated with the FlexHeat heat pump as case. The FlexHeat heat pump case is
found to be a satisfying representative of general district heating heat pumps. For which it was
found to have signiﬁcant impact to increase either supply, return or combined pipelines.
The coeﬃcient of performance for the heat pump showed changes between -0,8%/K and
0,3%/K in the interval of network storage appropriate storage levels. It was evident that return
strategies was aﬀecting the heat pump the least. The distribution network heat loss showed
changes between 1.12%/K and 1.04%/K, also here the return strategies was aﬀecting the network
loss the least.
The ﬁnancial analysis of the deteriorated COP in relation to the power price optimisation,
was found to be insuﬃcient to conclude feasibility of the exploitation of cheap morning prices.
However, the results indicated that the COP decrease and distribution heat loss increase was
exceeding the ﬁnancial gain. Future power price scenarios where the spot price is predominant
the feasibility appeared more likely, especially where the spot price morning tendencies were
ampliﬁed. It was shown that heat pumps in district heating context can be used in combination
with network storage but on the condition that the network is operated at at medium district
heating temperature.
The application of return network storage was successfully realised in the FlexHeat network.
Both experimental and theoretical data shows that a thermal energy charge can be stored within
the return pipelines. This conﬁrms that discharging network storage can be utilised to lower the
heat production for a period and possibly during peak load.
The eﬃciency of heat pumps is aﬀected signiﬁcantly when used in collaboration with network
storage strategies. However the results from the FlexHeat heat pump that return network storage
aﬀects the heat pump to a less degree than supply storage.
Both test and model data conﬁrms that peak shaving is possible using network storage.
Results show that using supply storage the heat production over one hour can be reduced with
13.6 kWh, and a maximum peak capacity can be reduced with 13.8 kW. Using return storage the
