Preface
This M.Sc. thesis is an original work of Magdalini Psarra (M.Sc. Environmental Engineering)
and Maria Saridaki (M.Sc. Industrial Engineering Management). It was carried out during the
period of January 23rd to June 23rd 2017 at the Department of Civil Engineering, Technical
University of Denmark (DTU). Both authors contributed equally to the ﬁnal report.
Despite the diﬀerent M.Sc.
studies of the authors, their common background in Civil
Engineering along with their interest about sustainability and construction led to the selection
of the topic related to environmental management, the introduction of Life Cycle Costing
in AEC industry. The strong willingness to conclude to a useful result for the industry, led
to the development of frameworks for straight-forward application of LCC in construction
projects developed. The cooperation with an engineering consultancy company contributes to
understanding the lacks and needs of the Danish industry regarding LCC.

Abstract
The full integration of LCC in Architecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry opens
the way towards more sustainable construction projects as it enables the decision makers to see
the long term eﬀect of alternative solutions into the ﬁnal cost. The aim of this study is the
development of a framework that can be directly linked to design models and costs libraries and
accurately calculate the Life Cycle Cost (LCC) of a building or individual building components. In
order to manage the diverse user backgrounds, two frameworks were developed. Firstly, a simple
building model was designed in Revit to facilitate the development. Framework A is based on an
excel tool that oﬀers a friendly graphical interface, created for users with limited-programming
background. Framework B follows a more automated procedure through a visual programming
environment based on an Integrated Dynamic model. In both frameworks, the V&S price-books
are used through the Sigma Estimation tool for identiﬁcation of LCC of the examined elements.
After optimizing the procedures, the frameworks were validated in a large scale building.
Afterwards, the framework was applied in a case study, provided by the collaborating company,
and the LCC of window components is calculated. The ﬁnal results were compared with the
results calculated by the company and by LCCByg. Diﬀerences are reported in nearly all life-cycle
stages. In order to examine diﬀerent options and provide suggestions to the company, a sensitivity
analysis was performed in which the construction, use and renovation stages were identiﬁed
as the most contributing to the ﬁnal life cycle costs. Based on this analysis various scenarios
were developed and the LCC in each of them was calculated. The scenarios involved diﬀerent
materials, alterations in the frequency of operation works and diﬀerent renovation options. The
results highlight the importance of taking into account the whole LCC in decision making and
encourage the integration of sustainable materials with higher initial but lower maintenance and
operation costs into new construction projects.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Scope of the project
In construction industry, the investment cost is considered the decisive factor for the construction
of a building. However, the last few decades, there is an increased interest towards sustainability
and its role into the ﬁnal decision making in terms of design choices and selection of building
materials (Hofer et al., 2011). The interest has been shifted towards buildings with low operating
and maintenance costs, which will reduce the ﬁnal cost in the end of the building’s lifespan.
Recently, the Danish Building Institute has recognized that one of the main reasons, leading
to building failures and deﬁciencies is the lack of appropriate LCC in construction projects
(Bygningsstyrelsen, 2017).
Through the concept of sustainable constructions, the decision-making in the early design
phase of a building is inextricably related to the consideration of life-cycle costs (LCC) of
materials and components. The wide use of the Building Information Modeling (BIM) concept
in the construction industry and its beneﬁts regarding the information enrichment and visual
representation of a building (Eastman et al., 2011), can create the base for the introduction of
an LCC tool that can be utilized by BIM users and the AEC industry.
The purpose of the present study is to provide a tool which will enable the practitioner to
see the long term eﬀect of the decisions and the changes from the model to the ﬁnal costs. In
order to achieve accurate LCC calculations of an entire building or individual components, two
frameworks have been developed. In order to manage the diverse user- background, the ﬁrst oﬀers
a friendly graphical user interface (GUI) as it is based on an excel environment, created for users
with limited-programming background, while the second follows a more automated procedure
through visual programming environment based on an Integrated Dynamic (ID) model. In both
frameworks, ﬁrstly, the user manually selects the diﬀerent “works” through the life cycle of the
project from a price-database by using a Cost Estimation tool. Then through the models, the
LCC is calculated after retrieving the quantities of the elements from the Revit Design Model.
The implementation of the above-mentioned frameworks will have a crucial impact in the
ﬁnal decision among diﬀerent design options and diﬀerent materials. Eventually, this leads to a
new era of more sustainable and economic viable construction business.

CHAPTER 2
LCC in AEC industry
This section provides an overview of the application of LCC in construction. After deﬁning the
term LCC, and a short historical background of its development, the current status in Denmark,
under the context of the DGNB certiﬁcation, is discussed. This chapter also entails the basic

2.1
Sustainability in the AEC industry
According to Brundtland (1987) “Sustainable development is the development that meets the
needs of the present without compromising the future generations to meet their own needs”. The
term of sustainability entails three basic aspects, which are called "the Triple Bottom Line (TBL)"
and were ﬁrst deﬁned by Elkington (1998). These are the economy, the environment and the
society, which coexist and interact, as it is presented in the Figure 2.1 below.
Figure 2.1: The three aspects of the Triple Bottom Line
The focus towards sustainability was adopted by the AEC industry and close attention was
given to the impacts that buildings have on the triple bottom line (Angel, 2008). The built
environment is considered today as one of the main contributors in climate change and resource
depletion, therefore higher attention is placed towards resource eﬃciency and environmental
impacts of the construction industry. An emphasis towards a more sustainable approach in
building design and life cycle performance that will ensure all the above has started to gain
momentum. Sustainable building practice attempts to identify how the built environment has an
impact on the natural world and subsequently oﬀers a balance between suﬃcient building and
environmental performance (Nornes, 2005). The consideration of alternative methods that take
into account sustainability aspects will improve performance and will lead to a shift towards
renewable resources (Angel, 2008). This approach will in turn reduce needs like maintenance

and repair which have a direct impact on the consideration of the total costs as durability will
be preferred.
2.2
LCC deﬁnition and concept
The term of life cycle thinking is in line with what sustainability encompasses and represents.
The purpose is to avoid burden shifting by assessing the use of natural resources and the impact
a product or a process has on the environment, the economy and the society through its entire
life cycle. The life cycle of a product involves the extraction of raw materials, the processing,
manufacturing, distribution, use, recycling, reuse and ﬁnal disposal of the material (Sonnemann,
2015). The Figure 2.2 below indicates the concept.
Figure 2.2: Example of the life cycle of a product system (Ecoinvent, 2017)
2.2.1
LCC deﬁnition
LCC is closely connected to the previously mentioned holistic approach when considering the
impacts of a product or a service. It is a methodology that enables the consideration of all costs
related to a product or activity throughout its life cycle. According to the deﬁnition given by the
International Standardization Organization (ISO) for LCC, Life-cycle Costing is (ISO 15686-5)
an “economic assessment considering all agreed projected signiﬁcant and relevant cost ﬂows over
a period of analysis expressed in monetary value. The projected costs are those needed to achieve
deﬁned levels of performance, including reliability, safety and availability” ((ISO), 2008).
According to another popular term, LCC concerns mainly the investment- and operational
costs. Its greatest advantage is that it can be used to compare diﬀerent options with each other
(Norman, 2007; A. J. Dell’Isola and S. J. Kirk, 2003) based on several key factors such as costs,
quality, and comfort over the entire life cycle of the product (Collier, 2009; Flanagan et al., 1989).
According to Sonnemann (2015) there are two types of LCC, the ﬁnancial and the environmental.
The ﬁrst one refers to the economic costs throughout the product’s life and assists in investments’
decision making. It is the one that is currently in use by many companies in the early design
phase. The latter refers to the weighting of environmental impacts into monetary terms (e.g.

costs of emissions etc). There is an eﬀort to link those two approaches into one LCC but still the
uncertainty of the attempt is considered high.
2.2.2
Life Cycle phases in AEC
The concept of LCC can be fully implemented in the AEC industry, since it involves the whole life
cycle of a given asset. Under this context, according to the deﬁnition given in ISO 15686-5:2008,
the following stages are included:
• construction
• use/operation
• maintenance
• End of Life
The consideration of the renovation, when needed, during the whole life cycle of the asset is
also considered a signiﬁcant part of LCC, that needs to be added either as part of maintenance
or as a new stage, as also highlighted in the proceedings from EU Commission on Life Cycle
Costs in construction (European Comission, 2003). Below, Figure 2.3 illustrates the diﬀerent
stages of LCC that will be analyzed in this study.

2.3
Calculation of LCC
2.3.1
Data requirement for LCC
In order to perform a complete LCC analysis, the requirement is not only limited to the costs
related to the life cycle stages as mentioned above. Since many costs occur in diﬀerent time
periods and since they are directly related to the type of building, its lifespan, its technical
requirements etc., the following initial set of data needs to be collected beforehand (Lansink,
2013):
• Data on actual costs in each of the ﬁve distinct life cycle stages of the construction project.
This costs do not include only construction elements, but also costs for energy and water
demand, drainage etc.;
• Data regarding speciﬁc quantities of elements( e.g. areas, pieces etc.);
5
==PAGE:26==
CHAPTER 2. LCC IN AEC INDUSTRY
• Data for conversion of costs occurring in diﬀerent time periods (discount rate, price
development of diﬀerent cost groups.);
• Data regarding the quality and purpose of the construction, its technical parameters and
its expected lifespan as well as the lifespan of the materials used. Additionally, frequencies
of maintenance and other works should be deﬁned.
Before the deﬁnition of the actual costs and the other parameters, it is very crucial for the
practitioner to deﬁne the system boundaries, and what to include or what is considered to be
out of scope.
Regarding the consideration of the diﬀerent cost categories, based on each life cycle stage,
some of the costs that can be considered are the following, according to RICS (1999) and ISO
(2008):
• Construction Costs: Land acquisition, construction assets, taxes, loans etc.;
• Operation/ Use Costs: Energy, cleaning, water, drainage, insurance, utilities etc.;
• Maintenance Costs: Maintenance of external and internal ﬁttings, electrical and mechanical
equipment, gas, lifts etc.;
• Renovation Costs: Costs for replacement/ renewal of components that either exceed their
lifetime or are worn out;
• End of Life/ Demolition Costs: Disposal, demolition, recycling, reuse, taxes, etc.

calculations are summarized in Figure 2.4:

2.3.2
LCC equation
When applied in a building, LCC takes into account the above required data, for each of the life
cycle stages, from the design until the materials’ disposal and end of life.
Since costs can occur on diﬀerent time periods, they need to be transformed to present values.
To compare future costs on the basis of the current value, a discount and an escalation rate
should be used. These factors have to be used because today’s money will not necessarily have
the same value tomorrow. Future costs, such as operational costs, have to be discounted to their
present values before they can be compared with such items as investment costs (Kemps, 2012).
There are two main factors that aﬀect the price of a future cash ﬂow in the present.
1. The ﬁrst is the general discount rate which is used to discount future cash ﬂows in present
money. The technique is called discounting and from that the Net Present Value (NPV) of a
cash ﬂow occurs. The discount rate can be either deﬁned by the investor or by governmental
organizations (Fuller and Petersen,1996). In Denmark the price of 5.5% is recommended
(Green Building Council, 2016)
2. The second is the inﬂation rate which reduces the power of money over time, therefore
the same amount of money in the present will have a reduced value in the future, due to
inﬂation. The recommended inﬂation rate in Denmark is 2% (Green Building Council,
2016).
As it can be derived, the two rates have the opposite eﬀect in a future cash ﬂow transferred
to present.
In order to calculate the current values of a future ﬂow , both of the factors need to be
considered. For this purpose, two diﬀerent approaches have been developed. Both of them will
yield to the same present value and are the following( Fuller and Petersen, 1996):
1. current prices and nominal discount rate. The nominal discount rate does not take into
account the eﬀect of inﬂation as this is considered in the current prices.
2. constant prices and real discount rate. The real discount rate depicts the real earning
power of cash ﬂows, including the eﬀect of inﬂation. The real discount rate is given by the
formula presented below:

2.3.3
Implementation of Environmental and Social Parameters into LCC
Flanagan et al. (1989) highlighted the importance in the use of other types of data such as
occupancy and quality data in the LCC calculations. One challenge with this type of data,
however, is that they can be considered rather subjective and less readily accountable than direct
cost data. According to Bakis et al. (2003) LCC estimation in the design stage is based more
on performance and cost data of the building components, which will also be the focus of the
present study. The contribution of quality data during the use phase is considered signiﬁcant
but not easily quantiﬁable, therefore will not be examined as part of this study.
One the other hand, the approach regarding environmental parameters needs to be discussed.
The “traditional” LCC does not become an environmental accounting tool just because it contains
the words life cycle (Gluch and Baumann, 2005). During the last few years, discussions have
initiated regarding the implementation of environmental parameters into the accounting of the
total costs during the life cycle. According to the study by Haddad et al. (2007) the environmental
damage can be evaluated by the beneﬁt or damage it does to the community in general. This is
expressed in environmental economics theory as the “total willingness to pay” to remove material
and minimize the impact caused by the emissions. Therefore, by translating the costs related to
removing or preventing the emissions of a material or activity to the environment, into monetary
terms, the environmental costs can somehow be quantiﬁed and added to the total life cycle costs.
The tool suggested by Haddad et al. (2007) introduces this methodology, however for the present
study, this is considered outside the main scope. The related environmental impacts will not be
quantiﬁed as they are considered of entailing a higher degree of uncertainty and therefore are not
easily comparable to the rest of the costs. However, an LCC analysis should be supplemented
with an life Cycle Assessment (LCA) analysis in order to provide a complete overview of the
impact of the construction project.

2.4.3
Main disadvantages of LCC based on literature
The LCC Analysis gives, as discussed previously, an estimation of the costs through the whole life
cycle of the project and this is something that can rise high uncertainties and lead to problems
regarding its implementation today. The main problems and its limited use from the industry
today are discussed below.
Buildings are considered as long time investments, which are associated with large environ-
mental impact through the whole life cycle. However, the long lifetime mentioned entails high
uncertainties, which depend on external factors such as business and economical ﬂuctuations or
political decisions and market changes (Gluch and Baumann 2005). According to Emblemsvåg
(2003) unreliable data, or lack of reliable data, may lead to unreliable results which make LCC
unfavorable. This is also supported by the survey performed by Chiurugwi et al. (2015 ) in 50
consultancy professionals in UK, which shows that only 20 % are favorably disposed to the use
of LCC or decision making.
Additionally, performing an LCC Analysis or an LCC Calculation is not convenient or simple
and therefore it is not always feasible to be executed by all stakeholders (Lansink 2013). From
the industry’s perspective, it is often more expensive to conduct an LCC analysis than rely
on the empirical judgment and rules of thump of the practitioners, since LCC entails also a
considerable level of uncertainty (Sparks and Mchugh 1984). In this context, the lack of time
in already tight schedules is also one additional reason that companies avoid to perform LCC
(Nornes 2005). According to article by Cole and Sterner (2000), the limited adoption of LCC is
mainly due to the insuﬃcient knowledge of practitioners.
Additionally, LCC must be executed in an early phase of the design stage in order to achieve
the desired eﬀect (Dhillon 2011). The disadvantage of this is that neither all actors are already
involved in the beginning of the development nor all the information is available. The amount of
assumptions that need to be made in this stage may lead to misleading results (Lansink, 2013).
From the clients’ perspective, however, the situation is similar, as the lack of appreciation of
the beneﬁts of LCC is also notable. The clients are often unwilling to spend money and time to
receive an LCC analysis, which leads to its limited use by the construction companies, since it is
still something that is not requested. In order to deal with this situation a lot of countries have
recently established standards that require an LCC and LCA analysis of the project in order to
ensure conformity. Denmark is one of these countries, however this practice is not universal and
does not represent the majority of the countries (Bygningsstyrelsen 2017).
Additionally, it is notable that there is little evidence of the degree in which the LCC is
performed by the companies and the way it inﬂuences stakeholders. There is a variety of LCC
software available but there is limited documentation of its use and its deﬁciencies. The majority
of the published studies approach the subject theoretically with insuﬃcient documentation
regarding its contribution in actual percentages compared to the traditional practice. The tools
available either require a high degree of information from the user and as a result a lot of eﬀort
and time, or they are based on a lot of assumptions which make their results unreliable. However,
there are not yet available any literature reviews on the advantages and disadvantages of the
available software, as well as its level of application, while the majority of what is found online,
is not open source, therefore a well established comparison is not plausible. The analysis on the
next chapters will be focused only in the case of Denmark and the software currently used by
Danish companies.

2.4.4
Limitations of the application of LCC based on literature
One of the highest concerns and major limitations regarding the implementation of LCC is the
uncertainty of results. According to Gluch and Baumann (2005) there are three main types of
uncertainties that may aﬀect investment decisions for a building:
• Physical uncertainties
• Business uncertainties
• Institutional uncertainties
The ﬁrst category refers to all the uncertainties regarding lifetime and physical characteristics
of the materials in the construction and the diﬀerences that may occur between the expected
and the real performance of the materials. The second category refers to unpredictable changes
in the market, the supply of goods or their prices and ﬁnally, the institutional category refers to
all the uncertainties that originate from change in regulations (e.g. prohibition of materials etc.).
Additionally the monetarism of LCC suggests to also assign a price on environmentally related
costs. However it is considered impossible to include all environmental aspects into monetary
terms, which results in loss of important details and insuﬃcient representation of the actual
situation.
Furthermore, the discount rates which are attributed to diﬀerent categories of future costs
derive from today’s estimations and predictions about their ﬂuctuation in the years to come and
mainly depend on the assumptions of the practitioner and the method used. As a result very
diﬀerent results can be obtained.
Finally, there are conceptual confusions in terms of what is considered to be a lifetime
regarding LCC. To be more speciﬁc, there are four diﬀerent lifetimes that can be used in
buildings: the economic, technical, physical and utility lifetime (S. Kirk and A. Dell’Isola 1995).
Economic lifetime refers to the building’s time when it is proﬁtable, technical lifetime is the
time when the building can use the same technology, the physical is the time period when the
building can be occupied and used and ﬁnally the utility life is when the building can satisfy
building standards. Depending on the choice of the lifetime, signiﬁcantly diﬀerent results can
occur. Generally, when performing an LCC calculation we usually consider the economic lifetime
of the building, however this depends on the speciﬁc case study and the interests and scope.

2.5.1
DGNB Certiﬁcation Scheme
The German Sustainable Building Council (DGNB) was established in 2007 by 16 initiators
from the construction and real-estate sector. The aim of the organization is the promotion of
more sustainable and economically eﬃcient buildings. For this reason, in 2008 a certiﬁcation
system was developed by the DGNB in close collaboration with the German Federal Ministry of
Transport, Building and Urban Aﬀairs (BMVBS). Today, DGNB consists of more than 1,200
members (architects, planners, the construction industry, investors and scientists) throughout
the entire world. It also collaborates with organizations around the world (in Austria, Bulgaria,
Denmark, Switzerland and Thailand) in order to develop and operate nationally adapted DGNB
Systems. In Denmark the DGNB system was established and adopted in 2012 by the Green
Building Council Denmark for sustainable buildings and urban area development (Haugbølle,
2015).
The certiﬁcation system is composed by around 50 sustainability criteria (e.g. thermal
comfort, design for all and sound insulation), covering the six key aspects of sustainable buildings:
environmental, economic, socio-cultural and functional aspects, technology, processes and site.
During the assessment of a building, the ﬁrst four aspects are equally weighted (each one accounts
for 22.5 % of the total, where the process accounts for 10 % and the site quality is given a separate
grade), underlying the importance of the economic aspect in building sustainability (DGNB is
the only system that recognize the economic aspect as much important as the environmental).
Moreover, the assessments are based on the entire life-cycle of a building, focusing on an
improvement quality of life of the user. The Figure 2.8 below describes the rating system among

2.6
Building Information Modelling (BIM) and LCC
Deﬁnition and Beneﬁts of BIM
Building Information Modeling (BIM) is deﬁned as “a digital representation of physical and
functional characteristics of a facility. As such it serves as a shared knowledge resource for
information about a facility forming a reliable basis for decisions during its life-cycle from
inception onward” according to The National Building Information Model Standard Project
Committee (2015). The concept of BIM was developed at Georgia Institute of Technology in the
late 1970s and has increased rapidly the last decades due to the signiﬁcant beneﬁts that provided
in construction projects.
The BIM concept exceeds the three spatial dimensions and the provision of 3D construction
models. Currently we are used to refer to a ﬁve-dimension BIM, which, apart from the 3D
components also entails schedule constraints (4D BIM- time) and cost related information
(5D BIM- cost). This type of models enable the visual understanding of the progress of the
construction and its relation to costs. This technique has the potential to improve management
and delivery of construction projects in the future. The ﬁgure 2.12 below graphically presents
the 5D BIM concept.
Figure 2.12: 5D concept in BIM
According to National Institute of Building Sciences (NIBS) (2015) the basic principles of
BIM are the following:
• Interoperability: which refers to the ability to exchange data between applications;
• Parametric Objects: which refers to the design components that entail geometric and
non-geometric information (e.g. cost estimating data and scheduling etc.);
18
==PAGE:39==
CHAPTER 2. LCC IN AEC INDUSTRY
• 3D Modelling: which enables the design of the model in a 3D format with all the physical
and functional features visible.
Some of its features constitute also the main beneﬁts in terms of control and communication
in the construction and are the following:
• BIM provides the ability to communicate and share information with the project team
including professional, operatives and the client (Eastman et al., 2011; Miettinen and
Paavola, 2014).
• A centralized BIM model brings together the various disciplines such as designers, engineers,
builders and cost etc. (Thurairajah and Goucher, 2013).
• All development information can be stored and accessed any time (Meadati, 2009).
2.6.1
BIM and Cost Estimation
BIM has the key ability to provide cost estimation from quantity take-oﬀof materials based
on the given geometry data within the BIM model and maps components of three-dimensional
building models to material, labor and equipment cost data (McAuley, Hore, and West, 2012;
Sylvester and Dietrich, 2010). BIM is also capable to generate take-oﬀs, counts and measurements
directly from a model, so information remains consistent throughout the project and changes
can be readily implemented in the whole model (McAuley, Hore, and West, 2012).
Although there is a lot of potential from the collaboration of cost estimation and BIM
modelling, by the process described above, the cost estimation directly via BIM models has
presented limited adoption from the construction sector. This can be mainly attributed to the
fact that the cost of BIM implementation, training and legal system, creates doubt for the
sub-contractors to be involved with BIM (Bingham, 2011).
Similarly, cost management practices have also limited BIM adoption (National Institute
of Building Sciences (NIBS),2015). In addition, Wu et al. (2014) found that this limited BIM
adoption is also due to the substandard models, inconsistencies in design information and data
exchange tools and formats that are being used in the practice. Therefore, BIM is mainly used
for the design and early construction phases, without making use of its full potential, which,
in terms of cost, will assist the managers to safely predict costs and ﬁnd ways to reduce them
(Sunil et al., 2017).

2.6.3
BIM and Life Cycle Approach
The past few years, a growing interest from the research community towards the collaboration of
BIM with life cycle management of the construction is evident. Towards this purpose there have
been various studies, which highlight the importance of the collaboration of the two principles,
followed by tools that allow the extraction of information from the BIM model into another life
cycle application. The tools have been mainly targeted towards the life cycle assessment of the
construction projects and the environmental impacts through their whole lifespan. According to
Bueno and Fabricio (2016) this can be attributed to the following characteristics of the BIM
models:
• The nature of the BIM models which allows the exchange of information since they cover
all physical and functional characteristics of the building.
• The BIM model is able to store, share and exchange data with other applications;
• Results from various analyses can be produced in documentation format
• The models can cover all the life cycle stages
• They encourage the collaboration of the whole design team and the exchange of information
The interaction of BIM with a life cycle management approach is so far limited to the context of
LCA. In this ﬁeld today the Tally application seems to be the most prominent one, still with
diﬃculties regarding in the interpretation of the results by the users (Bueno and Fabricio, 2016).
However,the BIM model, is not extensively used to provide estimation software with the data
requirements for LCC, such as escalation of future expenditure and/or PV costs, discount rates
and study periods. According to Goh and Sun (2015), BIM might be a vehicle for achieving
value in LCC, after incorporating the speciﬁc values for the calculation of escalating costs into
the model. The need of a connection of BIM and LCC was also identiﬁed by McAuley, Hore,
and West (2012) who have suggested the development of a tool in a spreadsheet format, where
the LCC are calculated after correct extraction of the quantities from Revit Models. However,
this tool still faces signiﬁcant limitations mainly due to the fact that it requires a considerable
amount of data from the user and therefore is considerably time consuming.
By this review, therefore, the need for the introduction of a tool that can successfully link
BIM with LCC and external cost libraries together is highlighted. This tool will easily help the
user to take decisions between alternatives and give a clear and precise overview of the costs
throughout the life cycle.

3.3
Methodology
After the deﬁnition of the limitations and challenges in performing LCC in danish construction
projects, the methodology of the approach followed in terms of this thesis, is analyzed below.
For the general approach to problem solving the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) Cycle, also
called the Deming Cycle, was used. This problem solving approach is chosen in order for the most
eﬀective solution to be found, while also the continuous improvement through several iterations
can be achieved.
The ﬁrst step is "Plan" and it involves the establishment of the main objectives for the delivery
of the results. In this step a test in a small scale can be performed ((ISO), 2008). In this context,
a simple model building was created and, with the use of the danish V&S cost libraries, speciﬁc
costs for the main components were set during the life cycle of the building and their present
value was calculated manually in an excel spreadsheet. Therefore the requirements for the tools
that would be developed, in terms of the results, were established.
Afterwards, the "Do" phase involves the implementation of the plan and the execution of the
process ((ISO), 2008). In this phase, therefore two separate frameworks have been developed:
• Framework A which is in a spreadsheet format. After the creation of the cost library
in Sigma software the data is extracted to the spreadsheet were the time eﬀect of money,
along with the frequency of the diﬀerent works, are inserted. The new data is returned to
Sigma which is connected with the exported information of Revit design model and the
total costs for the respective quantities are calculated. The Dynamo interface is only used
in this case, when there is a discrepancy between the unit deﬁnition in Sigma and Revit.
Additional description will be presented in the next chapter.
• Framework B which is mainly developed in a VPL environment. Again a cost library
in Sigma software will be created. Then, Dynamo interface will be used for retrieving all
the elements along with their quantities and key parameters from the design model. The
implementation of LCC calculations as well as additional modiﬁcations will be done in
Dynamo as well. Afterwards, the Dynamo interface is automatically linked with Sigma
software and a Sigma project is created. The Sigma Project will be connected with the
Sigma Library and the Total LCC of the project will be calculated.
The third element of the circular process is "Check" and it involves the comparison of the
27
==PAGE:48==
CHAPTER 3. PROBLEM STATEMENT & DESCRIPTION OF METHODOLOGY
results obtained with the ones expected, to ascertain any diﬀerences ((ISO), 2008). In terms
of the present study, this step involved the validation of the results obtained from the two
frameworks with the initial ones, from the manual calculations.
The fourth and ﬁnal element is "Act" and it involves all the actions for the continuous
improvement of the processes ((ISO), 2008). Therefore, the two frameworks were optimized,
based on the results of the "Check" step in order to provide functions and options and create a
more user-friendly environment. Afterwards they were applied to a large-scale project in order
to verify their functionality. In Figure 3.1 below, a visual representation of the PDCA system
along with the steps that it involved in the present study is presented.

The process is circular, therefore, the steps can be repeated, if new requirements are deﬁned.
In terms of dealing with the case study building provided by the collaborating company, the
need to review and repeat the process was essential. The project oﬀered by the company did
not meet the initial design requirements deﬁned, therefore a reformulation was needed and after
alterations the project was validated in both approaches and results were obtained.
A sensitivity analysis was accomplished in order to identify the life cycle stages that aﬀect
mostly the ﬁnal result. The sensitive stages were tested by applying diﬀerent scenarios and

4.2.2
The main challenge of Sigma Library - Unit Transformation
The Sigma Project can be now connected with the Sigma Library through the Type Codes. As
it is mentioned above, the Sigma Project contains all the elements with their quantities and their
Type Codes as it is exported from the Revit Model. The Sigma Project is connected to the
Sigma Library from which it gets the sum of the unit costs of all works for all Type Codes and
multiple them with the quantity of the corresponding Type Code. Then the Total Cost of the
project is calculated.
When the connection was performed, a crucial challenge was identiﬁed. It was recognized
that the cost of some elements’ works in Sigma Library (from V & S pricebook) was given in a
diﬀerent unit than the one extracted from Revit in the Sigma Project. For instance, in the case
of the Simple Building Model, the windows are exported from Revit to Sigma as pieces (pcs or
stk) and even if in most of the works the cost is given also per window piece, there are some

incorrect calculations.

can occur.
In the case of the Simple Building Model, this challenge was resolved easily by manual
transformation of units in the Sigma Library as there were only two works that their price
was given in a diﬀerent unit than the expected; the price of cleaning of the windows is per
m2 and the painting of the door is also per m2, where both elements are exported in pieces.
Moreover, in the Simple Building Model there is only one type of window and one type of
door with speciﬁc dimensions, so the conversion of the price from per square meter to piece of
window/door was simply calculated. For instance, the price of cleaning one m2 of window is

be 1, 16(m2) ∗231, 31(dkk) = 268, 77dkk. The Sigma Library can now be modiﬁed and the unit
will change into pieces (stk) and the unit price will now be 268,77 dkk/piece. By the manual
correction of the defective units, the Sigma Library is ready to be connected to the Sigma Project.

4.4
Manual Calculation of LCC
In order to calculate the LCC of the Simple Building Model, the costs of the works in the Sigma
Library that occurred in the future should be modiﬁed according to the NPV equation (see
Chapter 2). Firstly, the calculations of LCC will be performed manually in an Excel ﬁle.
Firstly, the Sigma Library, with all the selected works and their unit costs, is exported to an
excel ﬁle. There, the works are categorized based on their life cycle stage, as diﬀerent equations
are used for the LCC calculation of each stage. In the construction stage, the costs of the works
remain the same as they occur in the present. In maintenance and use stages, the works occur
more than one time through the life time. As these works are in the future, the price of them
diﬀers depending on the time of occurrence. For instance, in the maintenance stage of the walls,
the work of painting has a frequency of 8 (Table 4.1). That means that it will happened 8 times
during the 50 years of the lifetime of the project: in the 6th, 12th, 18th, 24th, 30th, 36th, 42th
and 48th year. According to the NPV equation, the price of painting a wall will be diﬀerent in
the present, diﬀerent in 6 years, diﬀerent in 12 years, etc, due to the inﬂuence of inﬂation and
discount rates. In the demolition stage, the works happen in a speciﬁc time, in the end of the
lifetime of the project. Again, based on the NPV equation the price will change.
The basic equation of LCCPV is presented in Chapter 2 (eq. 2.3). Equation 2.3 calculates
the LCC for a speciﬁc time in the future. Therefore, it should be modiﬁed in order to meet
the requirements of the calculations in diﬀerent stages. The input data of the equation are
the present cost of the work, the inﬂation rate, the discount rate and the time that the work
will occur. In the case of Simple Building Model, the cost of the works per element unit are

CHAPTER 5
Development of Framework A: An Excel
based Tool
In this chapter, the development of the ﬁrst Framework for the generation of LCC, is described.
The initial set-up as well as the deﬁnition of the coding system are analysed. Afterwards, the
development of the excel tool that used for the life-cycle calculation is presented along with the
LCC results. Finally, the method is optimized and tested in a large scale project.
5.1
Description of the Framework
The creation of the ﬁrst Framework for calculating the LCC is mainly based on the idea that the
costs from Sigma Estimation tool can be modiﬁed in order to include the time eﬀect of costs and
the "new" cost ﬁgures can be subsequently connected to Revit in order to be multiplied with the
quantities of the components.
Since this modiﬁcation of costs could not be done inside Sigma, as any coding was impossible,
any alteration should be proceeded outside the program. The main idea was the extraction of
initial costs in an Excel ﬁle, where they would be modiﬁed and afterwards be imported back
to Sigma, with altered new values. The reason for the selection of Excel as a middle-ware was
mainly based on its ability of direct connection with Sigma (possibility for import and export
directly), but also to the familiarity of the GUI and therefore its usability by a large portion of
the future practitioners. The modiﬁcations are, therefore, performed in an initial stage after the
creation of the Sigma Library and be imported back to Sigma, where they can be connected to
Sigma Project, which has the extracted quantities from Revit. The Figure 5.1 below presents a
general overview of the main steps of the process.

As discussed in section 4.2.2, the main challenge of Sigma Software in terms of its connection with
Revit Model was the non-conforming unit values between the two software. This occurs when
speciﬁc cost values have a diﬀerent unit measurement than the regular, which is also how the
quantities are extracted from Revit to the Sigma Project. For example, in the case of windows,
the default unit of measurement is in pieces, however the cost of cleaning of windows is given in

modiﬁcation, by calculating the amount of m2 in each of the window piece and therefore giving
a new cost price per piece of speciﬁc window types.

ﬁnd a generic solution, which will be subsequently applied to each project, with the minimum

and then, only for those, correct the quantities and perform the unit transformation through

One very important aspect of the Excel Tool, that needed optimization and further additions,

chance of making an error when misplacing the frequency to the work, which can occur in the

wrong calculations. Finally, the possibility of some default values was nit doable in the previous
version. The generation of results could not be accomplished in cases where all the data is not
available.
In order to resolve the above mentioned challenges, a code was programmed in VBA that
mainly enabled the following:
• The creation of dialog boxes which request the user’s input and automatically place it in
the respective cells.
• The pre-selection of default values for the rates, lifetime and frequency with which the
required cells will be ﬁlled, if the user pressed "Enter" in the dialog boxes.
• The identiﬁcation of the same works throughout the library and the automated ﬁlling of
the respective frequencies with the same number whenever the speciﬁc work appears. The
user is therefore asked for the respective frequency only one time.

In this chapter, the development of the second Framework used for LCC calculations, is described.
Firstly, an overview of Framework B, which is an Integrated Dynamic (ID) Model, is given. Then,

6.1
Description of the Framework
The second approach of LCC calculations includes the development of an ID Model that oﬀers
a more automated procedure of LCC calculations.In this model the use the VPL-Dynamo is
developed. Dynamo is used in order to control and pilot the exportation of the Revit Model in
Sigma Estimation tool.
Framework B is characterized as a distributed Model as there are diﬀerent tools which
integrate with each other and are used for data development. As the main calculations are
developed in VPL, the Framework is characterized as an ID Model, that according to Negendahl
(2015)is "a combined Model composed of a geometric model controlled in a design tool dynamically
coupled to a visual programming language (VPL), which is again dynamically coupled to a building
performance simulation (BPS) environment".
In this Framework, the LCC calculations are not proceeding by modifying Sigma Library
and transorming the unit costs in unit LCC, as in Framework A. In contrast, in the ID Model,
VPL-Dynamo is used as the middle-ware for facilitation the exportation of the Revit Model to
Sigma Estimates and calculating all the LCC information required.
When the Revit Model is developed and the Sigma Library is created, the procedure for
the total LCC calculation of the project can be initiated. Firstly, the Revit Model is connected
with Dynamo and a permanent link is created. In Dynamo, all the calculations of LCC and all
the needed modiﬁcations in order to overcome the challenges of the connection with Sigma are
proceeded through VPL. As the modiﬁcations are unique for each project, the Dynamo nodes
have to be adapted in any new case. After the adjustment of Dynamo in any new project, the
Model is ready to run and a Sigma Project is created in Sigma Estimation tool. The Sigma
Project includes all the information of LCC calculations and when it is connected to the Sigma
Library, the total LCC of the project is automatically generated. A general overview of the
procedure of the ID Model is illustrated in Figure 6.1.

these codes, a permanent link between Revit Model and Sigma Library is created (Figure 6.2).

described in the previous chapter, for the Excel Tool. A standard method for coding is suggested

• the element (e.g. window, wall etc.)
• the life cycle stage

For these reasons, the creation of the library in the present case was also a challenge. The

CHAPTER 8
Sensitivity Analysis and Scenarios
This chapter involves both the identiﬁcation of the parameters that aﬀect signiﬁcantly the ﬁnal
LCC and the development of realistic scenarios to test the change in the LCC and help the
company decide between alternatives and develop a well formulated plan. This methodology
introduces an approach that can be adopted by the company for optimizing the decision making
towards the most economically sustainable option.
8.1
Sensitivity analysis
The sensitivity analysis in this step was performed in order to test the contribution of a change
in one life cycle stage to the ﬁnal LCC results. This will assist in identifying the main focus
areas where the scenarios will be developed.

8.1 (Laurent, 2016).

20% while the other costs are kept the same. In the construction stage the increase on the costs

not necessarily the case vice versa for renovation costs. It has to be outlined that the change
could also occur to frequencies of works instead of cost prices, however since the sensitivity

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
In this chapter a discussion regarding the developed frameworks, their advantages and disad-
vantages as well as their validity is included. The requirements of the implementation of the
Frameworks are deﬁned in terms of the design of the model, the required information and the
knowledge of the user. Then the challenges of the Company’s case study as well as the LCC
results by the implementation of the Frameworks and the results of the diﬀerent scenarios are
also discussed. Finally, recommendations for further research are provided.
9.1
Advantages and limitations of the tools
After performing the case studies and evaluating the two developed tools, it can be concluded
that both of them could be potentially used in the AEC industry and lead towards a more
sustainable construction by enabling the assessment of the whole life cycle in decision making.
However before taking a formal decision regarding the use of one tool, its main advantages and
limitations, based on the observations derived by the case studies, should be considered. It is
believed that each tool can be applied to diﬀerent occasions, when diﬀerent level of information
is available, depending on the time that the practitioner is willing to spend as well as the level of
his experience and background knowledge.
The development and implementation of the two frameworks, led to conclusions about the
usability, the advantages and the limitations of each one. Framework A, which is an Excel based
Tool, simply exports the cost library created in Sigma, modiﬁes it in Excel and then imports
it back as a new library this time. The last one is afterwards connected with a Sigma Project,
where all the quantities of the elements are exported. Framework B, which is an ID Model,
creates a new Sigma Project through Dynamo where the life-cycle calculations are performed
and then is connected with the pre-existing Sigma Library and generates the ﬁnal LCC. On the
one hand the ﬁrst Framework updates the Sigma Library with LCC values and on the other
hand,the second creates a new Sigma Project that includes life-cycle parameters by using VPL.
These two diﬀerent approaches into solving the problem with generating LCC by the use of a
cost estimating software, provide them with unique characteristics, advantages and limitations
and target their use towards diﬀerent user categories. The advantages and limitations of each
one are analyzed below, in order for the user to make a formal decision regarding the selection of
the most appropriate one, based on his abilities and the project requirements.
Framework A is seen as a more simpliﬁed tool, which can be used by users from the AEC
industry who are little or no familiar with visual programming. It provides a solution to LCC
generation with the minimum interference of the user with the programming code. On the other
hand, Framework B provides an ID Model, which can be used by practitioners with experience
in handling VPL. Although it is more powerful, in terms of providing a permanent link between
the design program, Sigma Software and LCC, it cannot be used by users who are inexperienced
with programming. However, this permanent link is what gives the user the possibility to see the
eﬀect of changes directly to the costs by simply pressing a button. In the Excel based tool, the
101
==PAGE:122==

user has to modify the library and run again the tool, so the result will take some minutes to be
generated again. It has to be noted that in the Company’s case study, where a lot of changes had
to be made due to the eﬀect of the diﬀerent scenarios, the ID Model was preferred. By using the
ID Model the implementation of changes was easier, the ﬁnal results were presented per life-cycle
stage, while also not much eﬀort or time was required since the model was correctly built once.
What was also concluded by the implementation of the Frameworks was that the Excel based
tool is stiﬀin terms of design requirements. In DTU building for example, there were windows
with diﬀerent dimensions, assigned to the same type in Revit. The Excel Tool, however, is not
programmed to adjust to the size of each window separately, but by assuming that every window
of the same type has the same dimensions, takes the dimensions of one representative of the
category. Generally, if the design team has included in the same type elements with diﬀerent
unit values (for example areas) the Excel Tool cannot accurately calculate the cost. On the other
hand, the ID Model connects the cost values with each element directly, by the creation of lists
in Dynamo, so it is more ﬂexible to human errors in the design process. Additionally, Framework
B (ID Model) could be more easily adjustable to cases where the construction elements were not
deﬁned by their type, compared to Framework A (Excel Tool), where a prior categorization of
them into diﬀerent types is needed for the model to work.
Under the time perspective, the Excel Tool is quicker to set-up the ﬁrst time and can easily
generate results, especially if all the requirements of the design model are met. Framework B
does not generate results quickly the ﬁrst time as it requires eﬀort from the user in order to
understand the process and modify it to the speciﬁc case. However when the modiﬁcations are
set, the tool can operate and generate LCC instantly, regardless of the changes. This advantage
is especially valuable in terms of running quickly diﬀerent scenarios or performing sensitivity
analyses, as it was also pointed out above. Framework B can be adopted by the company and
after creating a general model that includes the most common elements and a Sigma Library
with all the cost information from suppliers or cost libraries, it could be used in almost any
project and generate results instantly.
Finally, the creation of library in the two approaches may diﬀer. In the Excel based Tool,
the library depends on the total amount of diﬀerent elements that will be used for the LCC
analysis and on the diﬀerent types of each one. On the ID Model, the library depends solely
on the diﬀerent cost values and categories that there are in the project (since every work is
connected to an element in Dynamo). When applying the second Framework to a large scale
project with signiﬁcant amount of diﬀerent elements this can be useful as it minimizes the time
spent in the library built up. This, for instance, occurred in the case of DTU building, where
the door category included many diﬀerent types of doors, with the same works throughout the
life cycle. However, for Framework A, the doors could not be included under the same category,
because they did not have the same dimensions, which led to the creation of many diﬀerent door
elements in the Sigma Library and as a result considerable amount of time spent.

9.2
Validity
The validity of the two Frameworks is investigated in terms of the data used and the results
generated. Since both Frameworks are based on Sigma and Revit and they use V&S price books
as their basic source of input data, their validity can be conﬁrmed in this aspect. Additionally,
the results of both Frameworks have been veriﬁed with LCCByg, which is also a valid tool, and
under the same assumptions generate the same outcome.
The main factor that can aﬀect the validity of the LCC results is the human error. The
optimization has been eﬀectively tried to minimize the errors in both tools. For Framework
A, the main reason that can produce errors in the optimized version is the misspelling of the
names in the life cycle stages or their characterization with diﬀerent names. Additionally, the
non- conformity with the suggested guidelines regarding the nomenclature of the elements may
produce errors in the ﬁnal outcome. It is highly likely that the user will identify easily the
source of the problem, since VBA code is an interpreted language, therefore each line is executed
directly. Additionally, the many controllers, pre-checks as well as user guidelines deﬁned, ensure
that the errors will be minimized as possible. The user guide, available in Appendix E navigates
the practitioner through the process and makes sure that everything is deﬁned correctly.
For the ID Model the errors may originate from either wrong or missing alignment of the
information in the VPL environment. The user may assign the wrong frequency or unit value in
an element or may forget to assign a code in a speciﬁc work. If the user assigns wrong frequency,
then the result will not be valid and there is unfortunately no possibility to avert this. On the
other hand, when assigning the wrong unit value or missing a code, the Sigma Library is not
properly connected to the project and the user will be able to recognize the problem in the cells
103

with zero results. Generally, the user has to pay attention and speciﬁcally follow the guidelines
suggested in Appendix F in order to assign the correct values through the process and then the
tool can run for any future modiﬁcation and adapt to various scenarios.
9.3
Deﬁnition of requirements
In order to ensure the validity of the tools and the generation of as reliable results as possible,
a set of requirements, for both Frameworks has been identiﬁed. This will ensure that a future
practitioner will ﬁrstly check whether he meets them and afterwards will apply the Framework
selected for the project. The ﬁnal outcome will therefore have the optimum quality possible.
9.3.1
Design Requirements
The case of DTU building where the windows were designed in curtain walls, led to the existence
of diﬀerent window dimensions under the same type. This made it almost impossible to ﬁnd the
windows with diﬀerent dimensions in the same type and afterwards separate them from the rest,
especially in the case of Framework A. The whole structure of the library and the assignment
of Type Codes was made based on the assumption that under each type of element the same
properties exist. As a result, the general requirement that the preliminary building elements
should have distinctive types and therefore distinctive dimensions is set as design requirement
(COBIM, 2012).
Additionally, as it was concluded from the Company case study, it is also important for the
diﬀerent elements designed in the model, that have a distinctive type, which describes their
function instead of being generic models (e.g. the wall should be under the wall family type etc.).
9.3.2
Information Requirements
One big challenge during the implementation of the Frameworks was the unavailability of
information with regards to facility management and maintenance works but also with regards
to speciﬁc cost data.
Firstly, one setback was the identiﬁcation of correct costs throughout the lifetime of materials.
Since there are no available open-access standards regarding maintenance and use guidelines of
components, the information had to be retrieved from company EPDs, which showed signiﬁcant
discrepancies regarding the recommended works throughout the life cycle. The most reliable
information was found after contacting supplier companies, most of which did not have any
information available online (Appendix D). Therefore, for a practitioner from the AEC industry
it can be challenging to deﬁne speciﬁc use or maintenance costs if he has not enough knowledge
about the life cycle of the elements. A lot of time is required to be spent in advance in order to
get access to reliable information. For this purpose the use of Construction Operations Building
Information Exchange (COBie) standards is recommended. COBie is a U.S standard, adopted
from U.K government in 2011 and it is related to managing asset information including space
and equipment, closely associated with BIM (East, 2007). COBie can include information from
various resources and communicate them among the diﬀerent parties involved in the project. For
example, this can include equipment lists, product data sheets, warranties, spare parts lists, and
preventive maintenance schedules. The importance of this information in eﬃcient facility and
asset management is already widely recognized (East, 2007).

9.4.1
Challenges and Approach
The project given by the company did not meet the design requirements, as they were deﬁned in
the previous section. This unfortunately led to problems regarding the extraction of the correct
quantities for each of the elements for LCC evaluation. This along with the absence of information
regarding use and maintenance and the uncertainty related to the present construction cost, led
to the decision to focus only on the window components.
The design problem was resolved by making several alterations to the model, and especially
the Dynamo Model, in order to be adjusted to the case where elements, such as windows, are not
deﬁned but are presented as Generic Models. This approach highlights the adaptability of both
frameworks to each project and their operability in various cases. Therefore, the requirements set
should not be seen as prohibitive for using the frameworks in other occasions, but as an indicator
that underlines the need to make alterations to their structure in advance so that they could be
adapted to almost every project. In any case however, the conformity with the requirements
will save time and eﬀort. Regarding the information requirements, direct communication with
experts in the ﬁeld and extensive literature review resolved the problem, however the company
should consider holding maintenance and operation guidelines for their main components.
Additionally, since the building had ten types of windows, from two diﬀerent companies, with
diﬀerent functions and costs, the creation of the library had to be organized and structured in
detail and the codes should be assigned correctly for each case. This could more easily lead to a

9.4.2
Results and comparisons
After the initial modiﬁcation and organization of the structure, the generation of results from
both frameworks was accomplished. The comparison among the two frameworks did not generate
any diﬀerences. The results were subsequently compared with the LCC results provided by the
company and the ones generated by LCCByg.
Regarding LCCByg, although the total diﬀerence from the basic case was 7,2%, there were
considerable diﬀerences in the various life cycle stages mainly due to the diﬀerence between
precise calculations and estimation by percentage. The most notable diﬀerence is with regards
to maintenance costs, which have an increase of 81,1% in LCCByg compared to the basic case.
This can be mainly attributed to the fact that LCCByg provides a general suggestion regarding
maintenance of 1% per year for all types of windows, which results in a signiﬁcantly higher value
for the total LCC. The maintenance of windows is not considered high, especially when these
are ﬁxed and only contributes to 7% of the total costs, if an accurate consideration of all the
costs is applied. Perhaps a diﬀerentiation between ﬁxed or open windows in LCCByg should be
made and a lower maintenance cost, at approximately 0.5% per year should be applied in the
ﬁrst category. Additionally, by this percentage estimation, the belief that a material with higher
investment costs may result in lower LCC due to lower maintenance, is not supported, since the
higher the initial cost, the higher will be the maintenance. On the other hand, the tools showed
that, when selecting a material with higher construction costs (e.g. aluminium windows) this
will lead to lower maintenance costs (Appendix D).
Operation costs in LCCByg are decreased by 4% compared to the results of implementing
the developed Frameworks, which is not considered signiﬁcant and can be mainly attributed to
diﬀerences in the initial assumptions about price regulation. The renovation cost, when calculated
in LCCByg showed an increase of 17.5%, compared to the results from the Frameworks. This
considerable diﬀerence is mainly attributed to the consideration of a percentage of 125% of the
initial construction costs as renovation costs, which results in an overestimation, compared to
the costs of actual re-installation and purchase of the new material. Therefore maybe a lower
percentage should be considered from LCCByg. Finally, the demolition costs are not considered
by LCCByg, however the results from the case study showed that they only increase by 0,4%
in the ﬁnal LCC, therefore their contribution is not considered signiﬁcant and could easily be
omitted, when it comes to a big project. Perhaps, when comparing speciﬁc alternatives, one to
one, the recyclability or the take back costs may have a small inﬂuence to the ﬁnal outcome and
can be considered. All in all, the ﬁnal demolition stage is considered more important in terms of
LCA and energy recovery.
The comparison between the outcome of the frameworks and the results from the LCC
calculations performed by the company showed a decrease of 16.4% on the total LCC. This is
mainly attributed to the diﬀerence in the renovation costs. When the company implemented LCC
considered a lifetime of windows of 50 years, although the manufacturers suggested maximum
30 years. This diﬀerence, along with divergence in the total area of windows, as derived from
Revit and the consideration of demolition costs, resulted in delivering a higher ﬁnal LCC in this
study. The costs in the diﬀerent life cycle stages apart from renovation costs and demolition
costs, which now are included, showed the same diﬀerences as in the previous comparison. The
maintenance costs are 81.1% higher in the company calculations and the operation costs are 8%

lower due to diﬀerences in the assumptions and in the areas included.
From the comparison above, it can be concluded that the Frameworks developed can eﬀectively

9.4.3
Scenario Analysis
In order to assist the company to the selection between alternatives, while also providing an
approach to decision making, a sensitivity analysis was performed in order to identify the main
focus areas of scenario development. Since the construction, use and renovation stage were
identiﬁed as the ones which mainly inﬂuenced the ﬁnal LCC, four diﬀerent scenarios were
developed.
The ﬁrst two scenarios included comparisons with the two other alternative window options
(aluminium and PVC) for the oﬃce areas.
The results indicated that PVC was the most
economically eﬃcient option throughout the LCC, due to the low construction and maintenance
costs. However, it has limitations in its application to oﬃce buildings due to the width of
the frames and their eﬀect to the aesthetic result, as well as its limited - availability to every
dimension. Therefore, they can be considered an option, as long as they meet the architectural
and design requirements. On the other hand, the aluminium windows had the higher costs,
compared to the other two options, while their lower maintenance and demolition costs could not
counterbalance the high initial costs. The selection of them can be considered in case of speciﬁc
architectural and aesthetic requirements of the building.
The third scenario investigated the eﬀect of operation costs and tested the scenario of cleaning
once instead of twice per year. The decrease on the total LCC was 10%, which highlights the
contribution of this category to the ﬁnal outcome. Therefore, the consideration of alternative
options such as self-cleaning glass, which does not have operation costs, should be considered.
Additionally, perhaps the option of windows with a part which is not ﬁxed and will provide an
easier cleaning in the higher ﬂoors, is recommended.
The ﬁnal scenario involves the diﬀerent renovation options.
It was concluded that the
renovation of the whole window frame in the 30 years, results in signiﬁcantly higher LCC,
compared with the option of no renovation, even when the energy savings of the newer window
were considered. However, the renovation of only the window glass and not the total frame, under
the assumption that after 30 years the frame will be in a good condition, is the most preferable
option, while the energy savings of the new window counter balance the initial investment costs
for the renovation. Therefore, this option is recommended to be taken under consideration from
the company.
9.5
Recommendations for further research
The two Frameworks developed clearly demonstrate the large potential of LCC considerations in
AEC industry in the future. However, since it is still in its infantry, there are several aspects that
can be considered in future research and a signiﬁcant amount of additions to be introduced. Since

==PAGE:129==
CHAPTER 9. DISCUSSION
this project is seen as an initiation of a path towards economic sustainability of construction, the
suggestions analyzed below will assist in its evolution and establishment.
9.5.1
Further development of the Frameworks
Although the Frameworks can so far provide an accurate estimation of LCC of a construction
project, there are areas that are not taken into account but have to be considered for a holistic
assessment. Energy and water consumption are two of the most signiﬁcant parameters that
are not considered in terms of this study, apart from an approximation used in the scenarios
evaluation. However these two parameters are highly inﬂuential to the ﬁnal cost values and
therefore a current expanded version of the models should take them into consideration. In this
context, the extraction of the energy consumption through a simpliﬁed energy simulation tool
would be recommended, in order to get acceptable values for expected consumption and costs.
Additionally, the consideration of sensitivity analyses inside the Frameworks, either in the
Dynamo Model or inside the Excel Tool should be included in future versions. The contribution
of each stage in the ﬁnal results can already be provided by an overview of the costs in Sigma
Library. However the contribution of each work and and each stage by percentage can be
modelled and presented. In this context, the graphs presenting the costs in each life cycle stage
can be programmed to be automatically generated in the user interface, which will enable the
practitioners to identify the hot spots between alternatives.
Finally, especially for Framework B, its transition to a fully integrated dynamic model could
be achieved by the transfer of the LCC results from Sigma to Revit, through Dynamo. This
action will enable the practitioner to see the ﬁnal results directly inside the design model, which
will result in a better interaction and understanding of the eﬀect of changes in cost values. Due
to time limitations and the complexity of the task this could not be achieved in the present study,
but it is one of the highest priorities regarding future improvements.
9.5.2
Use of LCC in AEC industry
The use of case studies identiﬁed one of the biggest challenges in the ﬁeld of LCC which is the
unavailability of standardized information regarding not only cost data but also maintenance and
operation works for construction elements. An investigation of ways to standardize the amount
of data oﬀered by manufacturers is suggested. The use of speciﬁc COBie templates and the
provision of standard information regarding lifetime, use and maintenance of each material will
be valuable for practitioners in LCC and LCA ﬁelds. The inclusion of an holistic approach to
construction could not be realized if speciﬁc information is not available and the ﬁnal outcome
will entail high degree of uncertainty. A standardized method of delivering information needed
for LCC (according to requirements analyzed above) is recommended for further investigation.
In this context, the availability of cost Libraries which are open source and stand-alone will
enable Frameworks to be independent from Cost Estimation software in the future while still
providing a direct connection to design models.
9.5.3
Suggestions for Sigma Estimates and LCCByg
The identiﬁcation of the main limitations of each of the Frameworks can lead towards some
suggestions for further improvements which will enable their future use for accurate LCC
calculations.

Regarding Sigma Estimates software, the implementation of the LCC formula inside the code,
along with the possibility to the user to insert frequencies and rates, will add more value to the
tool and assist in establishing its use for LCC calculations. The connection with Revit and V&S
price books is already established so the modiﬁcation of the code is the only step needed towards
the LCC market.
Regarding LCCByg, one possible suggestion is the insertion of further categories and sub-
categories of the construction elements, that will enable the comparisons between alternatives.
Diﬀerence in maintenance values between the diﬀerent options is considered signiﬁcant in this
context, while the possibility to insert speciﬁc maintenance rates and dates in the future, apart
from the yearly maintenance percentages will lead to more accurate calculations and give more
ﬂexibility to the tool.
9.5.4
Expansion to other aspects of sustainability
The need to investigate the sustainability of a construction project directly from the design
model is expressed by the AEC industry. The development of the Frameworks in the present
study looks into the economic sustainability of a construction project, however the other two
aspects should not be overlooked. Under this context the projection of sustainability indicators
inside the design model is recommended. According to DGNB, each of the three pillars of
sustainability(economic, social and environmental quality) contributes to a 22.5% to the ﬁnal
score. This highlights the importance of the social parameters to the ﬁnal outcome and their
contribution towards sustainability. Although until now there have been attempts to measure the
construction project in economic and environmental terms and tools that enable an integration of
the relevant parameters in the model have been developed, the same is not applied to the social
indicators. Therefore it is recommended that values as thermal comfort, indoor air quality etc.,
which belong to the social categories of DGNB, are measured and included inside the BIM model.
This will enable the overall assessment of the construction into various criteria and decision
making towards sustainable solutions that take into account all the pillars of sustainability.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The main outcome of this project was a solution to the lack of existing LCC software that could
be directly linked to design models and calculate as accurately as possible future costs for the
whole lifespan of the construction.
The solution emerged by the development of two diﬀerent Frameworks, each of them targeted
towards practitioners with diﬀerent needs and technical knowledge. Framework A is an Excel
based Tool that can be easily applied through a friendly and familiar GUI. Since it does not
have speciﬁc requirements it can be used by a broader range of practitioners. On the other
hand, Framework B is an Integrated Dynamic Model, mainly targeted towards users with some
experience with VPL. This model, although requires more eﬀort in the initial set up, has the
advantage of a permanent link with the design model which will enable the practitioner to see
directly the eﬀect of changes in the model to the LCC by simply running it. Additionally it is
more ﬂexible even in cases when the initial design does not meet the requirements set.
Both of the Frameworks analyzed have been tested in a model building speciﬁcally designed
for this purpose, while their validity was conﬁrmed also in a large scale project. On a second
iteration the Frameworks were optimized in order to oﬀer a better user experience and interaction.
The correct set up is also ensured by various pre-checks established in order to minimize the
human error. The Frameworks can be implemented by the AEC industry, by weighting the
advantages and limitations of each one and choosing the option which will ﬁt better the project
and the requirements set.
The second part of the present study involves the use of the Frameworks into a case study
building oﬀered by a engineering consulting company with which there was close collaboration.
The design model did not meet the requirements deﬁned for the regular application of the
Frameworks, while there was signiﬁcant lack of information available. These limitations led to
the restriction of the focus area towards only the windows of the building. For this purpose, the
tools were modiﬁed to ﬁt a case where the requirements were not met and after the conduction
of interviews with specialists in the area, the ﬁnal LCC were generated.
The results obtained were compared to both the results from the company and from LCCByg.
The main diﬀerences underlined the need for the consideration of precise calculations for the
operation and maintenance stage since they highlight the comparative advantage of materials with
higher initial cost but lower overall LCC and their selection against other with lower economic
sustainability. Therefore, the developed Frameworks can be eﬃciently used for comparison
between alternative solutions which leads in more sustainable decisions.
The main stages that have an impact on the ﬁnal score in Company’s case study, were
recognized by performing a sensitivity analysis, in order to enable the development of realistic
scenarios. The most sensitive stages were the construction, operation and renovation. For these
stages the application of diﬀerent types of windows with diﬀerent maintenance requirements
and initial costs was investigated. UPVC windows were considered the most proﬁtable option,
however they have signiﬁcant aesthetic limitations and diﬃcult application to large openings. The
111
==PAGE:132==
CHAPTER 10. CONCLUSION
exploration of the future use of self-cleaning windows was also suggested, as it can signiﬁcantly
reduce operation costs. Finally, the diﬀerent scenarios on renovation emphasize its importance
for energy eﬃciency in the long run. The option of the renovation of only the window glass
is recommended. The scenarios can be utilized by the company and assist in future decision
making and management of elements through operation stage.
To conclude with, this study showed that the integration of LCC tools that can be directly
connected to design models and produce accurate results can be used by the AEC industry and
lead towards more sustainable construction projects in the future. Without a doubt, there are
further developments that can be implemented in both of the frameworks in future editions.
However, both ID Model and Excel based Tool can serve as a basis for more accurate LCC with
reduced human errors and a new era towards the active consideration of life cycle into design
and construction decisions can be established. BIM and LCC are two terms that can closely
collaborate and the ﬁrst steps towards this direction are accomplished.

APPENDIX D
Interview with window manufacturer
Q: What is the presumed lifetime for your windows?Is it safe to say that they have a lifetime of
30 years?
A: Yes, it is safe to assume that. Usually the lifetime of the windows exceeds this number,
but the number of 30 years is safe enough, because this is when the user usually needs to change
them and beneﬁt from the new more eﬃcient windows. So basically, the change is not because
the windows cannot be used but because of technology advances in this ﬁeld.
Q: What is the lifetime of the glass? Is the number of 10 years realistic? Is it diﬀerent than
the one of the window in total? Can we change that solely?
A: Again here, the lifetime of the glass of course is higher than this, however, yes perhaps
every 10 years the user needs to change them, for example add another layer of glass, or add
shading inside the glass etc. This ﬁeld evolves rapidly and we cannot know or imagine how it
will be in 10 or 20 years from now. Generally, when the building owner is looking into ways for
its building to be more energy eﬃcient in the long run, he chooses to change the windows more
often. Yes of course you can change that solely but in the long run its is more cost eﬃcient to
change the whole window than only the glass. Q: What other types of windows do you have?
A: We basically have two types. The aluminium and the steel ones. The steel is more
expensive and it generally requires the same type of maintenenace. For regular construction we
recommend the aluminium, except of cae the the client speciﬁcally asks for it, due to architectural
purposes and when there is a requirement for big openings, since steel is stronger and can support
this type.
Q: In the maintenance should we consider the case of changing windows because of the cracking
of the glass? How often will that happen based on your experience?
A: This does not happen often and it is very rare so I do not think you should consider it.
Q: In our case study building we have the following types of windows (show table), what is the
presumed maintenance for those types? Do you agree with what we have? What about the use?
A: Regarding the maintenance we suggest the lubrication of the moving parts approximately
once a year and other than that no speciﬁc maintenance is required. Our windows are aluminium
so there is no maintenance required for the speciﬁc material. Maybe you need to change the
sealing strips once every ten years and inspect them every year. Regarding the use we suggest
cleaning to occur at least once per year to the whole window, so that you can avoid any additional
maintenance throughout the lifetime of windows.
Q: Do you have any information about the price of the windows?
A: Yes, generally a type like that has a price of 4000 kr per m2. The glass itself costs about
600 kr per m2. If you add shading inside the glass then the price increases to 2000 of glass per

Dynamo: is a "visual programming tool that aims to be accessible to both non-programmers and

Project parameter: is a parameter in Revit Model that is assigned in multiply categories, sheets,

2017)

works along with their costs are included.
Sigma Project: is a "project" in Sigma Estimation tool, where either is created automatically
by exporting the elements of a design model for Revit, or it can be created manually in Sigma
and the user should insert all the quantities of the examined elements.
Type Code: is a project parameter in Revit Model.

Microsoft, VBA itself got upgraded in 2010 with the introduction of Visual Basic for Applications
