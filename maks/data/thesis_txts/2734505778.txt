Abstract
This thesis investigates the development and impact of social interventions aimed at
improving the mental well-being of youth, aged 16-24, within the context of the Danish
Design Center’s ”Thriving Youth” mission, recognising the growing mental health crisis among
young people in Denmark. Adopting the Social Implication Design method (SID) from Nynke
Tromp and Paul Hekkert’s book ”Designing for Society,” the research explores innovative
strategies that could foster a more resilient and mentally robust youth population (Tromp &
Hekkert, 2019).
Through a comprehensive review of existing literature, including national reports on mental
well-being, and the deployment of effect-driven design principles, the study identifies key
societal and systemic factors contributing to the mental health challenges faced by young
Danes. It emphasises the importance of integrating behavioural science, social psychology,
and design theory to devise interventions that not only address the symptoms but also tackle
the root causes of poor mental health among youth. The aim is to develop proactive
intervention strategies to foster robust youths, utilising the SID-method as a framework for
social design, providing a unique approach to the social challenge.
The thesis narrows its focus on the impact of superstimuli and exercise, presenting
actionable interventions such as Education System Priorities and Ethical Phone Design aimed
at promoting self-development. These interventions are evaluated with stakeholders on their
potential to promote a thriving youth, with a focus on preventative measures and the
empowerment of young individuals to navigate life’s complexities successfully. In addition to
the interventions, the thesis introduces a preliminary tool as an outcome of the process. This
tool, tailored for DDC, aims to make the SID framework practical and user-friendly in
workshop formats. Its purpose is to offer a structured and insightful approach, assisting
clients in transitioning from mission statements to actionable goal statements for more
effective interventions, all through the lens of the SID framework.
Findings from this research highlight the critical role of holistic and context-specific
approaches in designing social interventions, contributing to the discourse on mental health
by offering a blueprint for future initiatives aimed at creating a supportive environment
where young people can thrive. Despite encountering challenges in both methodology and
subject matter, the thesis offers valuable perspectives to further the conversation on youth
well-being.
This work has practical significance for a broad audience, including policymakers, educators,
and mental health professionals seeking to implement effective strategies for youth
well-being.

Learning Objectives
Learning objectives of the thesis: 
• Manage a project in which you address a societal challenge in a complex sociotechnical
system (STS) 
• Scope and map a STS using the Social Implication Design method 
• Analyse the interaction between elements of a STS 
• Explore a STS and anticipate its future state to enable effect-driven innovation. 
• Frame and reframe a relevant opportunity for innovation in a STS 
• Apply design thinking to design interventions in a STS 
• Apply behavioral theory to the design of interventions in a STS 
• Reflect upon the design process and the role of STS in addressing societal challenges 
• Demonstrate their ability to support their findings and approach by identifying and
incorporating relevant literature related to their research objective

Thriving
A condensed version of DDC: A state of balance and
well-being characterised by the ability to navigate life’s
complexities. It manifests through positive interactions
within structures and social relations, contributing to a
sense of energy, initiative, and life satisfaction. Not de-
fined by constant happiness or absence of diagnoses,
but by meaningful engagement with life’s diverse path-
ways.

Introduction
This chapter outlines the project’s background, its rationale, and scope. It offers insights into the
motivations for initiating the project and the objectives it aims to achieve.
1.1
The Growing Mental Health Crisis
In recent years, mental health has emerged as one of the most pressing challenges of our
time, affecting individuals, families, and communities worldwide. According to the World
Health Organisation, approximately one in four people globally will at some point experience
a mental health disorder in their lives, highlighting the crisis’ extensive reach (OECD/EU, 2018;
WHO, 2022).
Denmark is renowned for having a comprehensive welfare system, serving as a robust safety
net for its citizens. It has achieved a remarkable ranking of second in the 2023 World
Happiness Report (Helliwell et al., 2023). Yet, despite these achievements, an unsettling
paradox emerges: An unprecedented number of young Danes are grappling with mental
health issues, a tendency that is emerging in most Western countries. Concerningly,
Denmark currently ranks as the European country with the most significant increase in
mental health problems amongst youth (Deloitte, 2023).
Data indicates a widespread decline in mental health across all age groups, particularly youth
aged 16-24. Within this group, one in three young women and one in five young men
self-report experiencing poor mental health. This trend is bolstered by an increase in
symptomatic indicators such as stress, headaches, and stomachaches, indicating a
deepening mental health crisis (H. A. R. Jensen et al., 2022; Signe et al., 2020).
This development is not only problematic because living with poor mental health puts them at
higher risk of developing somatic illnesses, such as depression and anxiety, but also because
it trails out into all aspects of their lives, causing difficulties. It strains relationships, hampers
performance in work and school, complicates parenting and deteriorates physical health
(Signe et al., 2020). Moreover, this crisis inflicts a hefty financial toll on society, costing over
110 billion DKK annually in lost productivity, sick days, and early retirement (OECD/EU, 2018).
The worsening state of mental health, especially among the youth, has captured national
attention and sparked debates on its causes. It has prompted the Danish government to
prioritise improving young people’s mental health, inspiring the Danish Design Center (DDC)
to launch a pivotal mission, ”Thriving Youth”.

1.3
The Project Domain, Scope and Goal
To solve this societal issue, we venture beyond the traditional design frameworks by using
the SID method framework. This framework is notable for addressing broader societal
impacts rather than focusing solely on individual or user-driven outcomes. The framework is
grounded in the newly established direction within the loosely defined social design
approach, named Designing for Society. (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019)
In this thesis, we aim to navigate the complexities of mental health issues and scrutinise the
societal discrepancies between our current lifestyles and the ideal states that would benefit
the common good. By leveraging insights from behavioural science, social psychology, and
design theory, we aim to illuminate these critical concerns.
To establish a foundation for our investigation, we will first clarify what it means for youth to
thrive. We will identify who falls under the category of ’youth’ and articulate the specific
outcomes we aspire to achieve through our project.

1.3.2
Who Are the Youth?
In this thesis, we define ’youth’ as individuals aged 16 to 24, selecting this age range based on
three key reasons: firstly, it includes those currently facing the most significant mental health
challenges; secondly, it corresponds with the DDC’s targeted demographic; and thirdly, it is
consistent with the age categorisation utilised in Sundhedsstyrelsen’s National Health Report
(“DDC – Danish Design Center | Vorby”, n.d.; H. A. R. Jensen et al., 2022).
Although our primary focus is on this specific age group, our investigation broadens to
encompass both younger and older demographics to ensure a thorough understanding of
the systemic factors affecting mental health and to pinpoint opportunities for interventions.
Moreover, our study predominantly examines the dynamics affecting youth in Copenhagen,
Denmark. This geographic focus allows us to explore the unique challenges and
opportunities presented within an urban context.

1.3.3
Our Chosen Domain - A Robust Youth
We have scoped our project intending to foster a ”robust youth” — a term we use to describe
resilient and mentally strong young individuals. This decision stems from our desire to
reduce the dependency on professional healthcare services by cultivating a society that
empowers youth to thrive amidst challenges.
Robustness, in this context, refers to the capacity to effectively handle difficulties and
regulate emotions. We characterise robust youth as those who demonstrate adaptability,
emotional regulation, and resilience. These individuals are equipped with an inner strength
and resourcefulness that allows them to resiliently navigate through life’s fluctuations.
Acknowledging the impossible challenge of eliminating all adversities faced by young people,
and recognising the importance of these challenges in fostering growth and development,
our objective becomes clear: To arm them with the skills and mindset needed to flourish in
challenging situations, ensuring they are capable of surmounting obstacles and becoming
stronger through their experiences.
Rather than concentrating on diagnosis, the focus is on preventing the onset of mental
health conditions. This involves supporting individuals who are experiencing distress,
regardless of whether they have received a diagnosis. The main conditions we aim to prevent
are depression, anxiety, stress, and general dissatisfaction with life, especially when these
issues are not rooted in biological factors. We aim to identify the initial causes that lead to
poor mental health among youth, rather than focusing solely on strategies for recovery from
such states.
1.3.3.1 The Overall Goal
The goal of our thesis is to develop a proactive intervention strategy to better the mental
well-being of the Danish youth in the future. Through a holistic understanding of the domain,
we aim to identify appropriate actions that can be taken to facilitate the desired
transformation, using the SID framework.

The first ”skolebørnsundersøgelse” (Health Behaviour in School-aged Children Study) was
conducted in 1998, with a focus on psycho-social aspects. It assessed general well-being,
relationships with classmates and teachers, participation and influence in the school
environment, and instances of bullying (Signe et al., 2020). In the 2000s reports, mental
health was mainly addressed in relation to alcohol consumption, with only a brief mention of
stress, depression, and anxiety symptoms in connection with physical activity (Signe et al.,
2020).
By the 2010s, reports began to highlight mental health issues more significantly,
incorporating them into the sections dedicated to health and well-being. The emphasis
expanded to cover key areas of concern such as anxiety, depression, pressure, eating
disorders, body image, and loneliness (Signe et al., 2020).
The delay in recognition of mental health as a field of its own may be attributed to the lack of
established terminology, as well as to the prevailing assumption that children are unable to
effectively articulate their mental state (see [D4.1]). The 2000s worked as a pivotal period for
shaping this understanding, with efforts focused on distinguishing between mental health
and mental illnesses, as well as defining and measuring them accurately.
Given the significant differences in data measurement and societal context between now and
30 years ago, drawing comparisons may not be viable. Nonetheless, the trends observed in
the last two decades indicate a rise in mental health issues across all age groups
(H. A. R. Jensen et al., 2022).
While the heightened emphasis on the mental health of children and youths represents a
shift in the right direction, the field remains filled with uncertainties. Currently, there is no
definitive evidence to pinpoint the causes of this decline, with research still focusing mainly
on symptoms.

The primary study highlighted in the review is Sundhedsstyrelsen’s National Health Report
from 2021, which examines the health of the Danish population and is updated every four
years. Additionally, the review includes VIVE’s welfare and well-being report from 2022, which

years. The Rockwool Foundation’s report delves into the mental health of the Danish

own data. Deloitte and Kraka mainly focus on mental well-being in educational settings.

VIVE - Børn og Unge - Velfærd og Trivsel
VIVE conducts an intricate study examining various demographics and parameters, including
background, household finances, and regional differences across Denmark. The research
tracks the development of children at the ages of 3, 7, 11, 15, and 19 over an extended

study launched 15 years ago. The methodology includes self-reported surveys, with parents
answering on behalf of the youngest participants (Heide Ottosen et al., 2022).
The study delves into a wide range of factors, such as tooth brushing habits, school
satisfaction, social engagement, loneliness, self-efficacy, life satisfaction, and physical
symptoms like headaches and stomachaches, among others. It adopts a comprehensive
approach, focusing on symptoms and their development. Similar to Sundhedsstyrelsen, VIVE
aims to offer data on the health and well-being development of the Danish population,
specifically focusing on children and young adults (Heide Ottosen et al., 2022).

Rockwool Foundation has conducted evaluations on the two aforementioned reports the

2.1.2.4 Deloitte and Kraka - De Smukke Unge Mennesker
Deloitte and Kraka’s report places significant emphasis on well-being within educational

2.1.3
Evaluating the Reports
Although it is a positive development that official reports acknowledge the complexity of
mental health by employing a wide range of indicators for its measurement, this diversity can
also make extracting meaningful insights more challenging. The surge in reports and articles
addressing mental health issues has also resulted in a jumble of scattered data, making it
easy for important knowledge to get lost or overlooked.
Most reports focus on symptoms rather than delving into the root causes. When the reports
do aim to derive meaning from these indicators, there is often an emphasis on the
importance of avoiding obesity, excessive drinking, loneliness, and financial instability that
potentially oversimplifies the issue. While numerous theories exist regarding the increase,
clear answers remain elusive.
Deloitte’s report is among the few that attempt to identify connections and root causes,
marking a positive beginning. It is crucial that more reports follow suit to ensure that these
pioneering efforts are not isolated (Deloitte, 2023).
A point of concern may also be that the majority of these studies work with self-reported data,
where individuals assess their own health, which biases the results. However, the increase in
self-reported poor well-being could in itself be a significant indicator that action is needed.
Deloitte raises the question of whether the declining well-being might be exacerbated by the
heightened attention in society on mental health issues, potentially creating a self-fulfilling
prophecy as the dialogue continues to focus on the struggles of young people. However,
these remain speculations. Deloitte also points to social media and increasing societal
pressures as possible contributing factors. (Deloitte, 2023)
The global rise in reported mental health issues cannot be considered in isolation from the
increased focus and resources allocated to mental health. As doctors become more
attentive to the mental health of children and youth, and as society grows more accepting of
mental health challenges, individuals are more likely to report experiencing poor mental
health. This could contribute to what seems like an exaggerated increase in mental health
statistics. Nevertheless, even when accounting for this potential bias in reporting, the figures
pointing to poor mental health are still worryingly high (Signe et al., 2020).

Design Approach
This chapter provides a thorough description of the chosen design approach, comparing it to
other existing approaches and explaining why this was appropriate for our project specifically.
3.1
Designing for Society
To address global societal challenges it appears that the most straightforward solution would
be for us, the global population, to significantly change our lifestyles to achieve a sustainable
and just world. Yet, this seemingly simple solution is also one of the most daunting. If the
answer is so straightforward, why can’t we all adjust our lives accordingly?
Addressing these challenges demands a reconsideration of our approaches to
problem-solving. The issue has evolved from being purely about design to also
encompassing human behaviour. Instead of merely focusing on creating the best product for
a seamless lifestyle, the emphasis is now on integrating desired behaviours into technologies.
This approach entails assessing the societal value of products and services to prevent
designs that are redundant, meaningless, or potentially harmful to society. To tackle social
challenges effectively, a novel approach is required—one that prioritises human behaviour
and our relationships with each other, with society, and with the technologies that are
integral to our daily lives (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
The approach ”Designing for Society” does exactly this. It falls into the loosely defined
approach of ”social design” and involves the act of creating solutions FOR society with the
sole intention of improving society, as Tromp & Hekkert states:
”Social design exists only to improve society above all else” (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019)
In this report Designing for Society and social design are used interchangeably,
although it is recognised that there are a variety of definitions for the approach, they
all share the same objective (see historical note on the following page).
In social design, the emphasis is placed on social impact over individual needs, distinguishing
it from approaches such as user-centred design. Its main goal is the ’greater good’, but it
carefully considers individual users, striving for a balance that promotes a society in which
everyone’s needs are met and people can thrive. Social design aims to ensure that no social
group is harmed or disadvantaged, and ideally, it benefits society as a whole.
Social designers focus on understanding the existing social constructs and the interactions
between humans and technology—known as socio-technical systems. This understanding
helps them identify where and how to make interventions for positive change.

Historical note
Over time social design has adopted many meanings and definitions across
professions and country borders but lacks criteria that can properly define it and
differentiate it from other design processes. In the work of Tromp and Hekkert,
2019’s, book ”Designing for Society”, they juxtapose and compare their extensive
research on social design with Dorst’s definition of design activities.
Dorst distinguishes four steps in any given design process (1) Object/objective of the
activity, problem statement and or solutions; (2) Actor - the people performing the
activity, the designer or design team/organisation; (3) Process - the structure and
dynamics of activity; and (4) context in which the activity takes place, if it impacts the
activity Dorst, 2008.
Tromp and Hekkert conclude that it is the objective that serves as the key
determinant in identifying a project as social design, highlighting the objective as the
common thread across past social design projects.
A potential limitation of social design is the implicit nature of its social outcomes
(interventions and effects), which are seldom scrutinised. Social Implication Design (SID) is an
approach to social design that seeks to fill this gap by promoting and precisely defining
societal improvements, making clear their importance in projects, and backing them with
objectively valid reasons. The focus is on clearly articulating the intended behaviour change
and its effects on both individuals and the environment, supported by solid scientific
evidence (explained in detail in section 3.2).
3.1.1
Effect-Driven Design
In social design, a key part of the process involves exploring, understanding, and determining
the social impact that the design aims to achieve. This focus on creating a positive societal
impact is what justifies its existence, making effect-driven design a fundamental aspect of the
approach.
Effect-driven design views products and services as tools for societal change, highlighting that
the design should be guided by the desired social improvement rather than the other way
around. It advocates for starting with the end goal and working backwards to the means,
emphasising the value the design will bring and redefining the problem by exploring the
”why” behind its occurrence. This approach emphasises the need to grasp and tackle the
underlying causes of social problems.
This method requires abductive reasoning, which is used when the only clear aspect is the
value the intervention should produce (Dorst, 2011). It involves making educated guesses or
generating hypotheses that explain the observed phenomena, even when direct evidence is
not yet available.

Intuition plays a crucial role in navigating the problem space. While it may not immediately

3.1.2
Human Behaviour
”Behaviour is what connects people and shapes society” (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
Behaviour is anything people do that impacts others or the planet and is observable,
meaning a change in behaviour should also be noticeable. For example, ”going for a run” is a
behaviour because it’s an observable action, whereas ”considering going to the gym” is not,
as it’s an internal thought process without external manifestation. Human behaviour is
influenced by a multitude of factors, including habits, beliefs, culture, environment, cognition,
the behaviour of others, mood and emotions. Understanding these factors is crucial for
effectively influencing behaviour in the context of design and societal change (Tromp &
Hekkert, 2019).
One of the advantages of focusing on behaviour in design is that it can be measured — either
by its presence, absence or frequency. Design can directly facilitate or encourage specific
behaviours, and it allows us to envision different social realities. This implies that through
carefully designed artefacts, there’s a potential to address some of the significant challenges
facing the world (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019). As Tromp & Hekkert stated with the quote:
”Only when behaviours are facilitated, fostered, changed or diminished does a design
contribute to social change.[...]it is in our acts that we affect the life of other human
beings, and as such, behaviours are an important lever for societal transformation.”
(Tromp & Hekkert, 2019)
Behaviour can address both individual concerns - things that matter personally to people -
and collective concerns, which are matters of importance to society as a whole. When
individual and collective concerns conflict, a social dilemma arises. This is the root of many
societal challenges, an example being running a red light on a bicycle despite risking the
safety of others. Understanding this conflict is crucial for understanding social complexity
and identifying where design can intervene (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
Personal benefits often take precedence over collective well-being, with short-term gains
overshadowing potential long-term consequences. This explains why people might act
against their better judgement for immediate gratification, even when aware of the negative
impacts. Social design aims to address these individual barriers, encouraging actions that
benefit society as a whole. It involves creating contexts where making the socially responsible
choice becomes instinctive, established through thoughtful design (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
The crux in addressing social dilemmas lies in balancing societal benefits with individual
convenience. Designers must navigate between these two perspectives, aiming to design
solutions that not only solve the issue at hand but also make the socially beneficial action the

3.1.3
Systems Thinking and Holistic Reasoning
In addressing complex issues, cause-effect thinking often falls short, especially as problems
increase in complexity. This is where systems thinking, characterised by synthetic and holistic
reasoning, comes into play. Instead of isolating the problem, systems thinking views it as a
component within a larger interconnected system (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
When dealing with the intricacies of human behaviour, effective interventions require a
nuanced understanding of the context that co-shapes behaviour. Human behaviour is
context-specific, necessitating an exploration of the non-linear relationships between
interventions and desired behaviour change. Rather than adhering to a linear
cause-lead-to-effect approach, systems thinking acknowledges that these relationships are
dynamic, co-evolving, and co-shaping each other also referred to as system-as-cause thinking
(Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
This approach facilitates a substantial re-framing of problems by recognising a wide range of
factors influencing the issue and identifying numerous opportunities for interventions. By
creating connections between multiple domains, systems thinking reveals that the optimal
place for intervention might not be where the problem manifests. This encourages a
comprehensive exploration of the intricate web of relationships within a system to identify
effective points for intervention and bring about meaningful change (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
3.1.4
Mediating Artefacts - Mediation Theory
Mediation of artefacts refers to the influence an artefact can have on human behaviour.
Design profoundly influences our lives, as seen with tools like Google Maps enabling greater
independence in exploration, albeit at the cost of reduced personal interactions. This
intersection of technology and behaviour raises critical questions about control, ethics, and
responsibility. Should the emphasis be on the user’s choices, the product’s inherent agency,
or the designer’s ethical obligations? The debate extends to the moral implications of design
decisions and who bears the responsibility for the outcomes of these interactions. A classic
example of the conflicting agency is the argument: ”guns kill people” vs. ”pencils misspell
words” (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
Philosopher Bruno Latour argues that it is in the joining of the two, human and object that
the action occurs, sharing the responsibility between, for example, a gun and its user. This
perspective shifts moral agency to include both designers and their creations, underscoring a
shared duty in shaping societal behaviours and outcomes. Thus, understanding the influence
of artefacts on human actions is essential, highlighting the significant role designers play in
crafting the world we navigate (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).

3.2
Social Implication Design method (SID)
The Social Implication Design method (SID) is a manifestation of above above-described
approaches to social issues. It is a concrete tool that facilitates the effect-driven approach
and guides the designer through the design process, using different reasoning. The method
works as a thinking tool that creates room for creative freedom and not as a recipe that can’t
be strayed from.
SID method springs from the well-established Vision in Product method (ViP), but differs in
that the ViP method designs take its starting point in a given product and that it is directed at
designing for individual use and not for society (Hekkert & van Dijk, 2011). SID asks the
designer to take very specific steps to consider the relationships between people and
systems that define society.
There are four phases in the SID method that guides you from a design brief to a solid design
proposal. The four stages are (0) Debriefing, (1) Anticipating the future, (2) Goal setting and
(4) Developing the intervention, see Figure 3.1
The first two phases, debriefing and anticipating the future, aim to understand the context of
a project, also known as the world level. Debriefing focuses on the client’s objectives while
anticipating the future involves looking at how the world around us works, to understand
how it develops at different rates and in different ways. At this stage, maintaining an open
and non-judgemental mindset is crucial, as it’s too early for solutions to emerge (Tromp &
Hekkert, 2019).

==PAGE:34==

Once the context is clear, the next step is to define a desired behaviour, the core of the Goal
Statement phase. This involves deciding on the effect the future intervention should facilitate,
requiring designers to navigate between individual and collective needs to increase the
chances of addressing the problem effectively.(Tromp & Hekkert, 2019)
The final phase involves developing the intervention at the artefact level, where the aim is to
turn the ”abstract” goal into a tangible solution. This highly creative stage allows for
exploration through sketching, discussion, and other creative methods, acknowledging that
the intervention is one of many forces that could steer society towards the desired outcome,
not the sole solution. Validating the intervention through early testing with experts or
potential users is critical to assess its viability (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
3.3
Why This Approach?
This section explains why we chose the SID framework for this project by comparing it to the
Double-Diamond, drawing out the advantages, and putting it into the perspective of this
context.
3.3.1
Comparing to the Double Diamond
The Double Diamond framework is a framework in design thinking that consists of four
phases: Discover and Define, followed by Develop and Deliver. It emphasises iterative
problem-solving, starting with exploration, defining the problem, generating ideas, and finally
delivering solutions. It provides a structured yet flexible approach to the design process,
emphasising the importance of both exploration and refinement in achieving innovative and
user-centred solutions (British Design Council, 2005),
The SID framework promotes an open and organic research process, emphasising early-stage
observation over immediate judgement. This approach allows for a deeper, more nuanced
understanding of societal needs and challenges. While both the SID and double-diamond
framework navigates through problem and solution spaces, SID’s unique emphasis on
societal effects, especially through behavioural change to create effect-driven design, marks a
departure from conventional design thinking, setting it apart in ambition and scope.
3.3.2
Advantages of using the SID Framework
The SID framework effectively ensures data analysis, goal setting, and interventions align with
creating an effect-driven social change. It’s particularly suited for broader societal challenges
like enhancing youth well-being, moving beyond user-centred approaches to prioritise the
collective good.
The integration of behaviour design within social design exemplifies a powerful method for
achieving direct social impact. This approach ensures that the actions taken align closely with
the desired societal effects. By setting clear goals that encompass behaviour change, the SID
framework guarantees that interventions directly influence the target group’s behaviour,
Designing social interventions for a future of thriving youths
17
==PAGE:35==
3      Design Approach
leading to tangible social benefits. This methodology underscores the effectiveness of
behaviour design in creating meaningful societal change.
Moreover, SID uniquely bridges the gap between engineering and social sciences, tackling
social issues traditionally outside the purview of engineering with systematic and
methodological rigour. This interdisciplinary fusion not only broadens the scope of
engineering but also enhances the potential for socio-technical systems design to address
complex societal challenges. The SID framework’s strength lies in its innovative merging of
fields, offering fresh perspectives and solutions to problems often reserved for humanitarian
disciplines, positioning it as a powerful tool for interdisciplinary collaboration.
3.3.3
In the Context of This Thesis
There are three main approaches when taking on social design: (1) To improve the
conditions of underrepresented people, (2) To improve the performance of public sector
bodies and (3) To build social capital. (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019)
Our thesis places itself under the third approach - to build social capital. This approach
prioritises projects that benefit entire communities over individual groups, enhancing
well-being and resilience. Although our primary focus is on youth, the broader community
benefits due to the interconnectedness with aspects like work and parenting.
We believe that adopting the Social Implication Design (SID) approach can support DDC’s
transition goals, offering fresh perspectives on its mission to foster thriving youth. While
DDC’s methodology is predominantly design-driven, it inherently aligns with the effect-driven
strategies found in SID.
By incorporating the socio-technical aspect, we aim to bridge the gap between social needs
and technological capabilities, ensuring that our solutions are holistic and deeply integrated
into the fabric of society. This nuanced incorporation of socio-technical considerations will
enhance the impact and relevance of our interventions within DDC’s framework for social
transition.

4.2.2
Context Factors - The One Where We Collect Information
What are context factors?
Context factors are elements of the current world that provide a multifaceted view of
the future. They are classified into four types:
Trends: What human experiences are in flux?
Shared experiences, beliefs, and behaviours that are in a state of change. E.g.
People are increasingly concerned about the climate crisis
Developments: What aspects of the world are in flux?
Changes in the world and environment around us that are biological, political,
technological, or demographic. E.g. providing home delivery is increasingly a part of
business strategies
State: What aspects of the world are static now?
A static description of the current context, where significant change is not expected
in the foreseeable future. E.g. Denmark is divided into regions
Principles: What things are universal and constant in life?
Fundamental laws that govern human life and help us understand various
phenomena. E.g. People often ascribe negative events to external factors, and
positive events to themselves
(Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
Purpose: To collect information and divide it into relevant elements that give a holistic
understanding of the domain. By dividing it into aspects of time, it is easier to identify what is
static and what is in flux, which is necessary when anticipating the future.

What is a DAG?
A diagram that shows the relationships between variables, particularly for

Context Clustering
What is context clustering?
Clustering context factors together in meaningful ways to illustrate the underlying
forces driving peoples behaviour in the domain (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
Purpose: To make sense of the hundreds of individual context factors we collected. It’s more
than just categorising; it’s about finding meaningful connections between seemingly disparate
elements. Through this process, we look for emerging patterns that indicate how these factors
collectively influence behaviour in a given context.
Take, for instance, the intersection of these three factors in our project:
1. Financial situations play a big role in pressuring the youth.
2. Social comparison theory suggests that people value their personal and social worth by
assessing how they compare to others.
3. It is becoming more popular for youth to buy more, to keep up with social media trends.
When these factors are clustered together, they narrate a compelling story. We termed this
cluster ’Financial Pressure from SoMe’. The text accompanying this cluster is as follows: The
culture of comparison, intensified by social media, not only creates unrealistic standards but
also increases youth consumption to meet these standards, leading to mounting financial
pressures that distort the real value of life experiences.
In defining each cluster, we consistently followed the structure:
• Force: Identifying the driving factor, such as social media amplifying a culture of
comparison.
• Behaviour: Observing the resulting actions, like the rise in youth spending to keep up
with these standards.
• Implication: Understanding the consequences, such as increased financial pressure
and its impact on life experiences.
This approach not only delineates the problem but also hints at the changes needed and
their potential future implications.
The clustering process was iterative and thoughtful. We started by randomly grouping
factors, seeking spontaneous connections. As we progressed, we continually re-evaluated
and restructured these groups, looking for missed links and deeper meanings. The DAG was
instrumental in this process, serving as a reference to ensure no aspect of the system was
overlooked. It was like piecing together a complex puzzle, slowly but surely.

Behaviour Theory
The next chapter covers behavioural theories related to social dilemmas and decision-making,
serving as the core theoretical foundation for our project. While these theories are crucial, our
exploration is not confined to them alone. Our approach to social design integrates both
individual and societal behavioural insights, broadening our perspective and enriching our
understanding of complex social interactions.
5.1
The Evolutionary Human
When confronted with social dilemmas, what motivates people to make certain choices and
behave in particular ways?
Throughout human history, humans have been hard-wired for one primary purpose; survival.
In psychological cognitive terms, this instinct has ingrained in us the need to conserve energy,
ensuring that we don’t have to expend unnecessary effort and energy in gathering food
(Phillips, 2024)
From an evolutionary perspective, behaviours that benefit others without direct personal
gain can often be understood in terms of benefiting the individual indirectly or their kin. Such
behaviours were likely favoured by natural selection because they contributed to the survival
and reproductive success of the individual’s genetic relatives (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
Humans have historically lived in social groups characterised by competition and dominance
but also by the capacity for compassion and collaboration. Research indicates that humans
have developed complex mechanisms for navigating social dilemmas. These mechanisms
include a balance between self-interest and the well-being of the community, indicating an
evolutionary advantage to cooperative behaviour that strengthens group cohesion and
survival chances (Tomasello & Vaish, 2013).
The Altruistic Behaviour
When we explore behaviour from an evolutionary perspective, the focus shifts to the
long-term effects of behaviour, not just the immediate individual motivation behind it. This
distinction is crucial, as highlighted by De Waal, 2008, who separates ”the function of
behaviour” from ”the motivation for behaviour.” Essentially, this approach examines the role
behaviour plays in survival aspects like reproduction and social bonding, as well as the
deeper motivations driving specific behaviours, such as hunger or thirst. These motivations
can be psychological, physiological, or emotional, underlying the actions individuals take to
meet their basic needs and ensure their well-being and continuation.
According to De Waal, our capacity for altruistic behaviour—helping others or sharing
resources, even at a personal cost—is rooted in our ability to empathise. He suggests that
Designing social interventions for a future of thriving youths
36
==PAGE:56==
5      Behaviour Theory
altruism is fuelled by empathy, which itself is often sparked by feelings of kinship or the
expectations of reciprocal actions, operating through the Perception-Action Mechanism
(PAM) that bypasses cognitive deliberation. Empathy can be understood in three tiers:
1. Emotional contagion, where we mirror or take on the emotions of others, driving us to
alleviate their distress as a way to reduce our own discomfort.
2. Sympathy, which involves recognising and differentiating between our own emotions
and those of others, yet still feeling compelled to respond to their emotional state.
3. Empathetic perspective-taking, which is the most mentally demanding level, requiring
us to imaginatively place ourselves in another’s situation, fully understanding their
viewpoint through a cognitive, imaginative process.
These levels illustrate the complexity and depth of empathy as a driver for altruistic
behaviour, emphasising the blend of automatic emotional responses and more deliberate
cognitive efforts in fostering social bonds and cooperative behaviours (De Waal, 2008).
The Co-operative Behaviour
Tomasello and Vaish (2013) explain human cooperation’s evolution with the
”interdependence hypothesis,” which identifies two developmental phases. Initially, the drive
to cooperate stemmed from a survival need, with individuals recognizing the importance of
their collaborators’ well-being for the success of the group. This led to caring about each
other’s outcomes as their own success was interconnected.
The evolution continued into a phase of ’group-mindedness’, emphasizing adherence to
group norms as essential for survival. This shift meant survival was increasingly tied to being
part of a group rather than just individual relationships with immediate kin. The group
adopted impartial, objective norms that everyone followed, enabling self-regulation of
behavior through emotions like guilt and shame, marking a move towards behavioral
self-governance (Tomasello & Vaish, 2013; Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
Our ancestors benefited from group membership, which offered access to resources,
protection, and mating opportunities. Positive social interactions increased survival and
reproductive success, embedding a natural desire for acceptance and fear of ostracism in
our evolutionary heritage. This explains our innate drive for social cohesion and prioritisation
of collective well-being, highlighting how deeply our social behaviours are rooted in our
evolutionary past. These evolutionary steps have shaped us into socially aware and sensitive
creatures today, highlighting the intricate interplay between individual survival instincts and
collective cooperation. (K. D. Williams, 2007).
5.2
The Social Human Being
As mentioned in The Evolutionary Human above, the fear of rejection or not belonging
strongly drives us to act in ways that benefit the collective. Paradoxically, while we rely on the
group for survival and are willing to do anything for its good, we also desire to stand out, and
Designing social interventions for a future of thriving youths
37
==PAGE:57==
5      Behaviour Theory
make our own decisions. Reflecting on our primal instincts, there’s a constant dilemma
between being part of a group and asserting dominance within it (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
In the contemporary world, where survival isn’t as directly linked to group dependence, the
temptation to prioritise personal goals over collective needs can seem more attractive. These
social dilemmas - the choice between acting for the collective or for oneself - have always
existed. Individuals constantly weigh what will benefit them the most: acting for the greater
good or pursuing selfish gains. (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019)
The drive to distinguish oneself from others is a powerful motivator, rooted in human
competitiveness and the desire to dominate, regardless of the cost. This competitive nature
extends to wanting to be perceived as more beautiful, attractive, or wealthy than others.
However, in contemporary society, such motivations can lead to negative outcomes, both
socially and individually. While these drives may have once been crucial for survival, they can
now be counterproductive or even unethical (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
To counterbalance these instincts, societies have developed various norms, laws, and
regulations to promote collective well-being. Descriptive norms encourage us to follow
others’ behaviours to avoid standing out too much. Reciprocity norms ensure that acts of
kindness are returned, fostering fairness. Injunctive norms define what behaviours are
socially approved, guiding us to conform to group standards. Additionally, societal values like
respect and formal rules and legislation define the limits of acceptable behaviour (Tromp &
Hekkert, 2019).
All these drivers work together to guide our decisions in favour of the group, serving as the
social glue that keeps communities functioning harmoniously (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
5.3
The Irrational Human
We often behave in ways that seem irrational, yet there’s a predictability to our irrationality.
Recent studies emphasise the profound impact of unconscious processes on our actions,
revealing that many of our decisions occur without active thought. While theories on social
dilemmas provide a clear framework for understanding our behavioural options, they don’t
fully capture the complexity of how we experience and navigate these situations in everyday
life.
According to the dual process theory, human decision-making is driven by two systems:
System 1, which is fast, instinctive, and effortless, driven by emotions and intuition; and
System 2, which is slower, logical, and reflective. Despite human inclination to identify with
System 2, we actually spend the majority of our time operating in System 1 (Kahneman,
2011). Emotions often guide our decision-making more than logic, especially when faced with
complexity.
Given the vast amount of information we encounter daily and the multitude of decisions we
must make, our brains employ shortcuts, or heuristics, to streamline this process and

conserve energy (Kahneman, 2011). These mental shortcuts are crucial for efficiency, as
constantly engaging in detailed analytical thinking (System 2) for every decision would
overwhelm us.
While efficient, heuristics reveal the limitations of human rationality, particularly in complex
systems like contemporary society. In today’s complex society, it’s challenging for any
individual to grasp all the nuances and causal relationships fully. This complexity and the
uncertainty of outcomes can lead to choices that appear illogical. While acting for the
collective benefit is rational in the long run, short-term gains often take precedence.
Moreover, being in the midst of a situation can skew our decisions due to emotional
influences, complicating decisions further when they involve multiple factors.
Imagine a situation where people have to choose between receiving 100 DKK now or 110
DKK in a week. Most would choose the immediate reward, despite a higher payoff for waiting.
Contrastingly, when the choice is between getting 110 DKK in a year and a week versus 100
DKK in a year, people tend to wait for the extra reward. This inconsistency illustrates how
uncertainty and various factors can lead to decisions that seem irrational (Kahneman, 2011;
Tromp & Hekkert, 2019). When faced with even less clear-cut outcomes, such as the
environmental impact of carbon emissions or the relationship between calorie intake and
weight gain, individuals may exhibit inefficient discounting. This means they may not
accurately weigh the long-term consequences against immediate gratification. The perceived
value of an outcome versus the effort or effect required influences decision-making
processes.
5.4
Creatures of Habits
Humans adopt shortcuts and habits (heuristics) as a way to make sense of the complexity of
the world. Changing these automated or emotional behaviours requires willpower and
self-discipline. The conscious cognition needs to be activated to overrule the unconscious,
and that requires energy. Choosing between socialising with a neighbour versus watching TV
showcases our struggle against impulsive desires for comfort. After a day filled with mentally
taxing tasks, resisting the lure of easier, more comfortable options becomes significantly
harder due to depleted self-control resources (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019).
To facilitate behaviour change, according to the COM-B model proposed by Michie et al.,
2014, at least one of three components must be altered: Capability, Opportunity, and
Motivation (see section 4.4.1.2). This involves changing one or more components related
either to the target behaviour or a competing behaviour, such as choosing a healthy diet
(target behaviour) over snacking on processed foods (competing behaviour). The change in
one of the components needs to be in a way that puts the system into an entirely new
configuration and it is necessary to minimise any risk of the system reverting.
Creating effective change requires a system shift to prevent reverting to old habits, that can
occur in the components. Michie further distinguishes the components into physical and

In our fast-paced society, the ripple effects of stress are reshaping family dynamics and
children’s development. Overwhelmed by the demands of modern work culture, parents
increasingly lean on quick, digital fixes like screens to manage their children, sidelining
meaningful engagement (Vilkår, 2021). This shift is mirrored in educational settings where
the focus on performance overshadows the intrinsic value of play for an early age (Dubgaard,
2023). The consequences for youth mental health are significant: entrenched screen habits
from a young age, diminished family connections, worsened sleep, and a dearth of essential
social skills(O’Connor et al., 2003; Rasmussen, 2020). These challenges are compounded by
lifestyles that limit outdoor activity and interaction with nature, essential elements for holistic
development. To foster resilient youth, it’s crucial to address these systemic issues,
promoting a balanced approach that values well-being and quality family time alongside
societal progress. (Milberg et al., 2020; Molly Wright, 2021)
3. Reactive Society
Societal and business responses to the mental health crisis are often trend-driven,

In response to the growing mental health crisis, society reacts with solutions spanning

addressing root causes, normalising mental health issues without comprehensive support,
contributing to the ongoing debate regarding the significance of the issue (Dubgaard, 2023).
The focus on individual responsibility overshadows societal support, and productivity norms
can lead to segregation and alienation, particularly for those not fitting the ’standard’

6.5.1
The Power of Human Weaknesses
In today’s fast-paced society, we encounter a unique paradox. Our innate human needs
clash with the demands of a rapidly evolving world. This dissonance creates an imbalance
between what fosters a thriving population and the pressures imposed by modern
corporations and society.
6.5.1.1 The Role of Superstimulus
Imagine this: a goose, diligently guarding its nest, suddenly faces an unexpected dilemma.
Along comes a large, stark white and intriguingly round object - a volleyball - landing with a
soft thud nearby. To us, it’s just a ball, but to the goose, it’s as if the ultimate egg has just
appeared. Here’s the twist – despite the goose’s sharp instincts for nurturing and protection,
this volleyball creates a dilemma, as it prefers the artificial allure of the ball over its own eggs
(Tinbergen, 1951).
This phenomenon is called superstimulus. Essentially, most animals have innate instincts
that draw them towards certain essentials like food, sex, and eggs, which are deeply rooted in
their evolutionary drives. When these natural stimuli are exaggerated to an extreme level,
beyond the usual attractions, animals can be led to prefer an artificial version over the real
thing. This intensified stimulus is more potent than anything they might encounter in the wild,
compelling them to opt for the artificial choice over the natural one (Barrett, 2016).

49
==PAGE:70==

It’s not just other animals that can be deemed foolish; humans are just as gullible. In fact,
humans are the species most frequently tricked by superstimuli because that’s where the
profit lies. Big corporations have become experts in identifying what naturally draws us in,
extracting it from its original context, amplifying its appeal to extreme levels, and then
delivering it back to us in a form that’s hard to resist (Brendborg, 2023).
6.5.1.2
Dopamine Driving Our Behaviour
Dopamine is a key, but often misunderstood, neurotransmitter that influences not the
pleasure we feel from activities, but our desire to engage in them. It’s crucial to distinguish
between the urge for something (wanting) and actually enjoying it (liking). This distinction is
important because dopamine is more about fuelling our cravings, such as for unhealthy food,
rather than the satisfaction we get from consuming it. It’s heavily linked to addiction due to
its role in creating desire, not pleasure. (Schultz et al., 1997).
Dopamine also acts as a ”learning molecule,” signalling the brain about potential rewards,
which is essential for learning that certain actions lead to rewards. Essentially, dopamine
drives our behaviour towards these rewards without being the source of pleasure itself,
correcting a widespread misconception (Schultz et al., 1997).
Over time, one can develop a reduced sensitivity to dopamine, requiring increasingly higher
levels to achieve the same effect. As a result, dopamine stacking - like using a phone while
eating junk food and watching a movie - can overshadow the gratification found in
slower-paced pleasures. With evolution prioritising energy conservation, quick and effortless
dopamine sources seem more appealing, making it challenging to find satisfaction in
activities that release dopamine more slowly, such as exercise or reading (Phillips, 2024).
The coupling between dopamine and superstimuli provides corporations with the perfect
recipe to manipulate our instincts.
6.5.1.3
Let the Manipulation Begin: The Addictive Design of Social Media
Social media platforms have mastered superstimuli, by using specific features that tap into
our weaknesses, making us spend more time on them and consume more content than we
plan to. This is part of what’s called the ’attention economy,’ where more screen time
translates directly into higher profits from advertising (Davenport & Beck, 2001;
Van Sparrentak, 2023).
By identifying our basic human desires, like social recognition, entertainment, and status,
delivered in concentrated doses, these platforms keep us scrolling. They’ve developed
algorithms that learn our preferences and tailor content so compelling that it feels like it was
made just for us, ensuring we stay engaged longer than we ever intended (Brendborg, 2023).
TikTok exemplifies this manipulation at its finest. It feeds on our love for video content,
presenting one tantalisingly optimised video after another. This creates a loop of
micro-anticipation dopamine shots, where we’re always hoping the next video will be even
more captivating, leading us down a rabbit hole for hours. TikTok has set the standard so

high that other platforms are scrambling to replicate its success, making this highly targeted,
addictive content ubiquitous (Brendborg, 2023).
6.5.1.4 The Effect on Individuals, and Especially Youth
In Denmark, the trend towards digital media consumption among adolescents is concerning,
with 16 and 17-year-olds spending more than five hours daily on social media, compared to
adults who spend around two hours (Heide Ottosen et al., 2022). This highlights the strong
appeal of these platforms to younger audiences. Furthermore, in the EU youth aged 16-24
are online for over seven hours each day, and a quarter of children and young people exhibit
’problematic’ or ’dysfunctional’ smartphone use, indicative of compulsive behaviour patterns,
a problem that research shows is on the increase (Van Sparrentak, 2023).
Social media’s allure significantly affects users, particularly the youth, whose brains are still
developing. This period of life is characterised by heightened neural plasticity, making
adolescents especially vulnerable to environmental influences, including the habit-forming
nature of digital platforms (Sydnor et al., 2023).
The prefrontal cortex, responsible for decision-making and impulse control, is one of the last
areas to mature during adolescence. This developmental timeline means that while
teenagers are highly capable of learning and adapting, they are less able to resist addictive
temptations, such as those presented by social media (Domenech & Koechlin, 2015).
Adolescents also have an increased craving for social rewards combined with an
underdeveloped capacity for impulse control (Walker et al., 2017). Social media platforms,
with their endless streams of likes, shares, and comments, directly tap into this craving,
offering immediate social rewards at the tap of a screen. This creates a potent lure for young
users, drawing them into a cycle of constant engagement and, potentially, into patterns of
use that mirror addiction (Van Sparrentak, 2023).
A study from Harvard University has highlighted the enormous profits — estimated in the
billions — garnered from advertising aimed at American youth. This focus on the younger
audience is not coincidental; it’s a strategic move to capitalise on their heightened
susceptibility to social media’s allure (Raffoul et al., 2023).
The consequences of this relentless pursuit of dopamine-driven activities are far-reaching.
Research has established links between problematic smartphone or internet use and a range
of mental health issues, including depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, and body image
disorders (Van Sparrentak, 2023). Adolescents, in their formative years, are particularly at
risk, with studies suggesting that excessive screen time can impact brain development,
affecting areas related to attention, memory, and neurodevelopment (D. G. Smith, 2023).
Furthermore, there’s growing evidence to suggest that intensive social media use can alter
the structure of the brain, mirroring the effects observed with addictive substances like
alcohol and heroin (Van Sparrentak, 2023). The sedentary lifestyle associated with excessive
screen time poses additional risks, potentially increasing the likelihood of early
neurodegeneration (Van Sparrentak, 2023).

6.5.1.5 Autonomy and Our Basic Needs
As Kim Van Sparrentak, a member of the European parliament, aptly points out, ”No
self-discipline can beat Big Tech’s tricks, fuelled by armies of designers and psychologists to
keep you glued to your screen” (Yakimova, 2023). This statement sparks a crucial debate on
whether true autonomy and freedom of choice exist in our digital age (Rashid, 2021).
As mentioned, wanting is not the same as liking; our dopamine-driven desires can linger even
after our interest wanes. In essence, just because you choose something does not mean you
wish you chose it. This phenomenon is evident in the desire for reduced screen time among
a significant portion of the youth (Brendborg, 2023).
Time spent behind screens detracts from activities vital for physical and mental well-being,
such as physical activity, outdoor time, and relaxation. Research indicates that adolescents
who limit their electronic communication tend to be happier, and individuals who take a
break from social media for a week report notable improvements in well-being
(Van Sparrentak, 2023).
For the sake of autonomy and freedom of choice, we can choose to continue to not do
something about this development. However, then we must accept that this path may lead
to adverse health outcomes, including weight gain, poor health, shortened lifespans, and
deteriorating mental well-being.
Examining Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs reveals how our pervasive use of digital devices and
exposure to superstimuli disrupt our innate rhythms and fundamental needs. The hierarchy
of needs is often depicted as a pyramid with five levels. From the bottom of the hierarchy
upwards, the needs are; Basic needs (physiological & safety), psychological needs (love and
belonging & esteem), and finally self-fulfilment needs as self-actualisation (Maslow, 1943).
While the model faces criticism in its totality, there’s a consensus amongst academics on the
significance of fulfilling the basic needs for human survival and well-being (“Praise & Criticism:
Hierarchy of Needs (Maslow)”, 2016). Yet, these needs are often overlooked. Nearly half of
the Danish population struggles with sleep issues, partly due to excessive artificial light
disrupting circadian rhythms, and over half the population fails to meet national or global
recommendations for physical activity (H. A. R. Jensen et al., 2022). These trends are also
apparent in the discussions held during our focus group sessions, where several participants
expressed their desire to dedicate more time to physical activities such as climbing or yoga,
or simply to getting more sleep (See [D3]).
6.5.1.6 Physical Activity: A Path to Better Health
One of the activities that really should not be overlooked and is essential for well-being is
physical activity (Singh et al., 2023). Physical activity releases dopamine slower than the

superstimulated alternatives and therefore seems less rewarding (Phillips, 2024). Luckily the
high tolerance for dopamine is reversible, allowing for healthier options (Brendborg, 2023).
For mental health, physical exercise is essential and has been identified as the number one
most effective remedy to ensuring long-term positive mental health (Singh et al., 2023). This
is because exercise is a powerful antidote to the stressors of daily life. It triggers the release
of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), which promotes the growth of new brain cells,
particularly in areas crucial for memory and learning. Boosting BDNF through exercise may
help manage conditions such as depression and anxiety (P. J. Smith & Merwin, 2020).
Moreover, exercise enhances responses to anxiety disorders by facilitating learning
processes critical to certain therapies. It also moderates the body’s stress response and
reduces inflammation, both of which are implicated in mental health disorders (Holmes,

with benefits extending beyond mood improvement to encompass cardiovascular health and
overall well-being. The growth of new brain cells and increased neuroplasticity, facilitated by
exercise, are associated with its antidepressant effects (Trachman, 2022).
In conclusion, exercise is a multifaceted tool for improving mental well-being, with benefits
extending beyond mood improvement to encompass cognitive health and resilience against
mental health challenges. Prioritising physical activity is essential for achieving a healthier
and more fulfilling way of life in our fast-paced world.

8.4.1.1 Motivation for the Intervention
A significant decline in physical activity is observed among young adults transitioning from
ages 16-19 to 20-29 (Bak, 2022). This period marks a peak in the diversity of sports they
participate in, yet coincides with a reduction in the overall time dedicated to physical
activities. This decline often aligns with life transitions post-high school, where changes in
environment or new beginnings pose challenges in maintaining sports involvement(Bak,
2022). See worksheet [C6.3] for overview of events in youths lives.
Youth participation in sports unions frequently diminishes during this phase (Bak, 2022). This
trend may stem from increased demands and commitments required by sports unions, such
as extensive training sessions and weekend competitions, or the pursuit of elite coaching
outside union structures (see evaluation interview with Tentschert [I9.1]). Alternatively, it
could reflect a desire to prioritise social connections, with limited opportunities for casual,
enjoyment-focused participation, highlighting a distinction between sports for younger
individuals and adults (S. Y. M. Jensen & Petersen, 2018; Nielsen, 2017).
Currently, the culture of physical exercise present in primary and secondary education and
workplaces doesn’t extend into higher education settings, as pointed out by Tentschert [I9.1].
Young people show a preference for experimenting with various sports without committing

Existing Measures
Educational measures
Educational institutions currently incorporate physical activity to varying degrees. Primary
and secondary schools are required to offer sports classes weekly, with additional
”mini-sport” programs in some municipalities for younger children. Various online resources
are available to integrate sports into educational settings. (EMU, n.d.; “Velkommen til
Gladsaxe minisport”, n.d.)
At the university level, sports and physical activities are often organised by student-led groups,
with the range and type of activities varying between institutions. For example, the University
of Copenhagen (KU) has a wide selection of sports and boasts around 4,000 members, which
include both KU and non-KU students (KU, n.d.). CBS offers sports through various individual
organisations, with CBS Paddle being notably popular among students (CBS, n.d.-a, n.d.-b).
Everyday measures
Copenhagen’s infrastructure supports an active lifestyle, promoting cycling through extensive
bike lanes and offering parks and outdoor exercise facilities (WHO, n.d.). Personal devices like
Apple Watches and exercise apps also play a role in encouraging movement by reminding
users to stay active.
These initiatives and recommendations are part of a broader effort to encourage physical
activity among young adults, aiming to improve physical health and well-being.

8.4.2.1 Motivation for the Intervention, through lack of existing measures
Our intervention aims to enhance physical activity among youth by addressing the critical
need for more available time in their schedules, which is currently consumed by excessive
social media usage. With the average young Dane spending approximately 5 hours and 28
minutes daily on social media (Heide Ottosen et al., 2022), this significant portion of the day
dedicated to screen time presents a substantial barrier to physical engagement.
Despite existing EU measures and national laws in Denmark focusing on consumer
protection and data usage, there’s a notable gap in legislation specifically aimed at combating
digital addiction and its detrimental effects on physical health and mental well-being, a
summary is shown below but more details can be found in the deliverables booklet.
Summary of legislation’s
• The European Parliament’s draft report on addictive design calls for action to fill these
legal gaps, indicating an awareness of the issue but lacking immediate legal
enforcement (Van Sparrentak, 2023).
• Current legal frameworks like the Digital Services Act (DSA) and the AI Act tackle
manipulative practices but fall short of comprehensively addressing the addictive
nature of digital designs (“EU AI Act: first regulation on artificial intelligence | Topics |
European Parliament”, 2023; “The EU’s Digital Services Act”, 2023).
• In Denmark, although there are general consumer protection laws and guidelines for
screen use, they do not directly combat the addictive elements inherent in digital
platforms (“Consumer Regulation: Danish Consumer Contracts Act”, 2013; “Marketing
Practices Act”, 2017).
This regulatory vacuum underscores the urgent need for our intervention strategy, which
seeks to address the direct link between excessive screen time and the decline in physical
activity among the youth. By advocating for increased regulations and implementing

8.4.2.2 Our Intervention: Ethical Phone Design
To combat digital addiction, we identified the need for the following regulations as seen
below. Mock-up examples of a selection of these regulations can be seen in Figure 8.10, used
as a boundary object to combat misunderstanding, but the regulations can be brought about
in practice in a number of ways.
User Interface Design
• Limiting Infinite Scroll: Mandate breaks or stopping cues in content feeds to prevent
endless scrolling.
• Autoplay Restrictions: Require explicit consent from users before autoplaying video or
audio content.
• Reducing Sensory Overload: Set guidelines to limit excessive animations, sounds, and
visual stimuli that can be overwhelming or addictive.
Notification management
• Limit Frequency: Set a cap on the number of notifications an app can send to users
within a timeframe.
• User Control Over Notifications: Give users more comprehensive options to control
what types of notifications they receive and how often.
Content and interaction
• Age-appropriate Design: Implement different interface designs for children and
adolescents, with stricter limits. e.g. validation of age.
• Safeguards Against Compulsive Behaviours: Restrictions on features like ’likes’ and
’streaks’ that promote compulsive checking or usage.
Data privacy and Personalisation
• Transparency in Personalisation: Require platforms to disclose how user data is used in
personalising content and what impact this has on content visibility.
• Opt-out Options: Provide clear options for users to opt out of personalised content
feeds.
Health and Well-being Considerations
• Mandatory Health Warnings: Include warnings about the potential addictive nature of
the service.
• Collaboration with Health Experts: Involve psychological and health experts in the
design process to assess the potential impact on mental health.

9.0.1
Introduction
The tool, named ”The Behaviour-Change Tool for Social Impact” (see Figure 9.1), is designed
to foster social impact by targeting specific societal behaviours. It is intended to be adaptable

using future design. The full tool can be found in the Deliverables Catalogue.
This tool is a preliminary exploration awaiting testing with real clients. It serves as an
introductory boundary object, visualising the process for clients and reflecting essential
aspects of the SID framework.

within the Thriving Youth mission, it became evident that while their existing tools like ’Vorby’

the most sense to conceptualise into a tool. First, discussing the context factors and context
structuring, Tentschert noted that they were mainly interested in seeing the mechanisms we
had identified. Additionally, they already have effective tools for exploring and setting a
domain with their clients. Instead, it was identified that DDC could benefit from incorporating
behavioural design theory to enhance the clarity and impact of goal statements, serving as a
foundation for interventions. This insight led to the development of the tool (see [D4.7]).
As the SID framework was first released in 2019, its process has, to the best of our
knowledge, not been converted into a shorter workshop/tool context before. Therefore, the
effectiveness of completing the process in a short time span is uncertain. We proceed with
the assumption that DDC and its clients bring substantial intrinsic knowledge to the table,
potentially reducing the need for the comprehensive data collection phase, and hence
allowing for a faster process. If nothing else, to start the process of reflecting on their
mission’s desired effect.

10.1
Discussion of The SID Framework
This section discusses our reflections of using the SID framework for social design, touching
upon the challenges of using a new framework, how we adapted it to our needs, as well as
other ethical and inherent biases you must consider with a process such as this one.
10.1.1
The Challenge of Using the New SID Framework
Adopting the SID framework introduces a set of challenges due to its novelty and the limited
resources available beyond the book Designing for Society (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019). This
results in a process that can feel experimental and subject to individual interpretation. Such
ambiguity can be a double-edged sword, fostering creativity while also requiring significant
discernment from designers to navigate effectively.
A notable critique lies in the framework’s approach to the artefact level, which seems at odds
with its broader societal ambitions. While SID encourages thinking beyond individual impacts
towards societal change, the focus on designing tangible products or services as
interventions risks reverting to an individualistic-centred mindset, potentially diluting the
intended societal impact. However, this is perhaps a general concern when working with
mediating between artefact and societal level, and thus may be a challenge when working
with social design as a whole.
The knowledge paradox further complicates the process, presenting a dilemma where
decisions must be made early on with limited information, potentially affecting the project’s
direction and outcomes. Although the notion of knowledge paradoxes is true for most design
projects, this challenge is exacerbated by the comprehensive nature of the method, which,
while thorough, may require more time than available to fully explore and articulate findings.
Moreover, SID’s interdisciplinary demands highlight the vast amount of knowledge needed
across different fields. This requirement enriches the process but also introduces complexity,
necessitating extensive research and dialogue to achieve a holistic understanding. While this
interdisciplinary approach opens up new perspectives, it also underscores the lengthy and
sometimes unending journey towards fully comprehending and addressing the problem at
its core.

10.1.2
Adapting the Framework
We recognised the need for additional methods beyond those in the SID framework to
address the complexities of our project on thriving youth. Finding the original framework’s
approach to analysing data overly simplistic, we added methods like the DAG and timelines
to give a holistic understanding of the research, preventing valuable information from being
lost in the sea of post-it notes (see section 4.2.3.1).
Another method that is not officially a part of the Hekkert and Tromps framework is the
anticipation of the future story (see section 4.2.3.2, and the Deliverables Catalogue). This was,
for us, crucial for projecting the long-term impact of current trends of the mechanism,
moving our focus from the immediate to future implications.
The ideation phase within the SID framework presents a level of openness that leaves room
for interpretation and adaptation. Our project encountered a limitation with the framework’s
recommended ’Interaction’ method, which did not fully meet the extensive needs of our
initiative (Tromp & Hekkert, 2019). This led us to develop the COM-B-persona matrix
brainstorm, tailored to keep our project’s focus on behavioural outcomes (see section
4.4.1.2).
Integrating behavioural theory into the ideation phase introduced additional complexities,
particularly in maintaining a societal perspective. The COM-B theory, which primarily focuses
on understanding individual behaviour, needed to be adapted for our broader societal
ambitions. We addressed this challenge by employing multiple personas to represent a
wide-ranging target group. However, these personas were considered within an individual
context, enabling us to thread individual behavioural theories through to a societal level.
This strategy, while innovative, brought about its own set of challenges during the phase
where interventions needed to be categorised and elevated back to a societal level. The
process demanded several iterations to ensure that the interventions, though rooted in
individual behavioural changes, were effectively scaled up to achieve the intended societal
impact. This iterative process was crucial in aligning our ideation efforts with the project’s
overarching goal of fostering societal change through behavioural outcomes.
However, we acknowledge that mastering frameworks and optimise them to their fullest
potential requires time and experience. Acknowledging this knowledge paradoxes, our
approach to the project would be different if we were to start today, leveraging the insights
we have gained on the framework.

individual. Arguably, all design mediates society and influences the human experience, where
the SID framework aims to shape society’s behaviour for the ‘greater good’ as a positive
societal impact. It intentionally navigates the fine line between guiding societal behaviour and

Comparison of Existing Literature and Interventions
This section compares our work with existing literature and interventions, illustrating the
unique contributions and value our thesis brings to youth well-being research.
10.4.1
Existing Literature
Our thesis distinguishes itself by merging theoretical analysis with practical interventions,
addressing the well-being of the Danish youth in ways that existing literature often does not.
Previous studies have indeed identified various factors influencing well-being, but our project
advances this by focusing on the underlying behavioural patterns and proposing systemic,
actionable interventions. We aim to create an environment that emphasises proactive
well-being at both societal and individual levels, thus filling a gap in the literature which tends
to offer reactive rather than proactive solutions.
Our work does not introduce new factors affecting well-being; instead, it seeks to understand
how these factors interconnect. For instance, while health reports might point out an
increase in stress among the youth, we delve into how this could be related to other
environmental factors, such as the rise in social media usage. We bring together diverse
sources for a holistic comparison, providing insights into how these elements interact and
influence each other, something often missing in existing research.
Moreover, our thesis dares to suggest immediate underlying causes and their interrelations,
as well as potential future outcomes. While other studies may avoid making such direct
connections in the pursuit of scientific neutrality, our approach is based on plausible causal
links, aiming for actionable outcomes based on these insights.
By creating systemic interventions based on underlying behavioural patterns, our thesis
bridges the theoretical and practical, setting a precedent for future work in the field of
well-being. Our interventions are designed not just to react to existing issues but to
proactively build a culture where well-being is prioritised at all levels.
10.4.2
Existing Interventions
Assessing existing interventions in the field of youth well-being presents a notable challenge
due to its broad scope. However, a closer look at the overarching interventions reveals a
clear distinction: Our approaches are markedly more specific and actionable. Many existing
reports tend to highlight problems, relying on societal response without offering targeted,
intervention-based solutions (see section 2.1). Although raising awareness on the issue is
crucial to creating change, the actual impact of such strategies can be hard to quantify.
Nonetheless, the increase in societal language on the topic and exponential focus on mental
health issues indicate that this approach may be having a positive effect.
When comparing our Education System Priorities intervention to existing interventions it is
clear that Denmark already boasts a solid sports infrastructure, offering ample opportunities
for activity (WHO, n.d.). Our interventions aimed to enhance this already strong system,

interventions represents a significant limitation. This gap underscores the need for future
studies to empirically assess the interventions’ effectiveness, moving beyond theoretical
assumptions to provide evidence-based validation. This is both true for the interventions and
the tool, both of which DDC will receive as deliverables for future work (see Deliverables

the approach could have benefited from additional analytical methods to provide an even
better understanding of the system holistically. Future work should explore systematic ways
of gathering insights, such as through continuous knowledge collection and employing more
analytical tools for a more comprehensive understanding of the issues at hand.

Conclusion
Partnering with the Danish Design Center (DDC) and their ’Thriving Youth’ initiative, this thesis
aims to improve the worsening mental health of young individuals aged 16-24 in
Copenhagen, Denmark. This was tackled through a relatively new social design approach,
Social Implication Design (SID), that resulted in the development of innovative interventions.
By leveraging the SID framework, we have not only scrutinised the current landscape of
youth well-being but also proposed actionable interventions aimed at fostering a more
supportive and engaging environment for young individuals.
Our exploration began with an in-depth analysis of the prevailing challenges affecting the
youth, identifying key areas where targeted interventions could make a significant difference
to their robustness. The mechanisms we selected - The Power of Human Weaknesses - and
the interventions we’ve proposed, including the Ethical Phone Design and Education System
Priorities, reflect our commitment to addressing these challenges holistically.
The validation process, involving feedback from experts and stakeholders, has underscored
the potential impact of our interventions. By focusing on enhancing physical activity and
mitigating the effects of passive screen time, we aim to contribute to a more balanced and
healthy lifestyle for young people.
Moreover, the development of the Behaviour-Change Tool for Social Impact, which is going to
be used in DDC’s Thriving Youth projects, exemplifies our approach to creating tangible
resources that can assist in the implementation of effect-driven interventions. This tool
represents a bridge between theoretical research and practical application, offering a
structured method to translate broad mission statements into specific, actionable goals.
Reflecting on our journey, it is evident that while challenges remain, our thesis contributes
valuable insights and tools to the ongoing discourse on the mental health of youth. By
addressing both the symptoms and underlying causes of prevalent issues, our work lays the
groundwork for future research and interventions that prioritise the holistic development of
young individuals.
In conclusion, this thesis represents a significant step forward in designing social
interventions for a thriving youth. Through our research, interventions, and the development
of practical tools, we have highlighted the importance of a multifaceted approach to
enhancing youth well-being, paving the way for continued innovation and impact in the field.
