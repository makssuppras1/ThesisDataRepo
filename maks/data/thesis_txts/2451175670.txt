Summary (English)
The goal of the thesis is to lay the foundation for a data driven approach to
analysing team strategies in Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive by answering
the following questions. Is it possible to quickly get an overview of the veto
process by interacting with a visualization, which is based on the opponents
previous matches? Is it possible to quickly study the opponent teams strategy by
automatically extracting prevalent patterns in their play style and only present
the rounds which seem to have repeated patterns in them? This is done using
data that has been collected from HLTV.org
Time, roster changes, match type and opponents ranks are found to have an
eﬀect on the veto process. An interactive visualisations is created that allows for
ﬁltering veto processes on these features. This is a novel approach to analysing
the veto process that strongly decreases the time that is needed compared to
existing methods.
A set of 17 matches from a chosen time period all featuring the same team
and the same map is chosen as the basis for an analysis of that team’s in-game
strategies. A set of criteria is formed based on domain knowledge of the game
that is used to ﬁlter the interesting rounds from the uninteresting rounds. An
extensive and exhaustive feature engineering process is done on the interesting
rounds.
The most promising features are found to be the movement of the
players and the utility that they use.
Diﬀerent clustering methods are used on these features and evaluated on 4
diﬀerent metrics. The round ﬁltering shows big promise in terms of optimising
the process of analysing teams. The subsequent clustering does not manage to
categorize the remaining rounds into well-deﬁned strategies.

Summary (Danish)
Målet for denne afhandling er at at lægge fundamentet til en datadreven frem-
gangsmåde til at analysere hold strategier i Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive.
Dette er gjort ved at besvare følgende spørgsmål. Er det muligt hurtigt at få et
overblik over veto processen ved brug af en interaktiv visualisering over mod-
standeres tidligere kampe? Og er det muligt hurtigt at studere strategier i mod-
stander holds strategier, ved automatisk at udvinde fremtrædende mønstre i
deres spillestil og kun fremvise runder med gentagelser af disse mønstre? Det-
te er undersøgt ved brug af indsamlet data fra HLTV.org. Tid, holdmedlems
udskiftninger, kampens type og modstandernes rank har alle vist at have en be-
tydning for veto processen. En interaktiv visualisering er bygget, som kan ﬁltrere
veto processerne på disse værdier. Dette er en ny tilgang til at analysere veto
processen som mindsker tidsforbruget krævet i sammenligning med eksisterende
metoder.
Et sæt af 17 kampe er valgt for en given tidsperiode med samme hold og samme
bane, for at danne grundlaget til at analysere holdets spil strategier. Et sæt af
kriterier are dannet baseret på domæne viden indenfor spillet, som bliver brugt
til at ﬁltrere uinteressante runder fra. En vidtgående og udførlig feature engi-
neering process er udført på de fundne interessante runder. De mest lovende
værdier er funden til at være spillernes bevægelse og deres brug af granater.
Forskellige metoder til at gruppere dataen er brugt på disse værdier og evalu-
eret gennem 4 valgte metrikker. Den indledende runde-ﬁltrering viser lovende
egenskaber for at optimere processen til at analysere hold. Det lykkedes ikke
de efterfølgende grupperinger at kategorisere de interessante runder baseret på
veldeﬁnerede strategier.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU Compute in fulﬁlment of the requirements for
acquiring a M.Sc. in Engineering.
The thesis deals with a data driven approach to optimizing team strategy anal-
ysis for competitive Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive.
The thesis presents the results of two ways that a data driven approach can
improve on the way coaches currently approach team analysis in competitive
Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive. The ﬁrst way is to collect data from diﬀerent
match pages online and visualise them together. The other way is by segregating
rounds of interest from those that hold little strategic value.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the motivation behind the project. This is the followed
by an introduction to the core gameplay of Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive
that is needed to understand the project. Finally a problem statement for the
project is formulated and an initial project plan is given.
1.1
Motivation
Esports is a word that has come along way since it was ﬁrst coined as "eSport"
in 1999 by the Online Gamers Association[GW08]. Today it can be found in
the online edition of the Oxford English dictionary[Oxf]. Esports is very much
comparable to traditional sports with professional athletes, coaches, managers,
betting, product endorsement, leagues and large tournaments [Li17] [HRK17]
[JMKO17]. It is not only the word for "electronic sports" that has evolved since
1999, esports has become an entire industry. It has oﬃcially broken into the
mainstream, which is evident by discussions of including esports in the 2024
Paris Olympics[New17], as well as the 2020 Asian Games. Almost all aspects of
the industry are increasing, and there has been an almost exponential increase
in the prize money that can be won by attending and winning tournaments all
over the world[New17]. The large interest has turned dedicated players into
athletes and teams with entire organizations around them.
==PAGE:16==
2
Introduction
Not only this, but the ease of access to esports has resulted in many new teams
and players becoming involved in the scene. This has made it increasingly hard
for the coaches to always be aware of the competing teams and what strategies
they use. This is not alleviated by all the time that is required to watch other
teams in order to analyse their playstyle. With esports being so integrated with
computers and comparable to sports it is no wonder that numerous websites en-
able players and spectators to view and analyse aggregated summary statistics
of all kinds, but there is a shortage of using the abundance of data available from
esports as well as advanced data analytic which can be facilitated through the
use of machine learning[JSSS18]. Maybe all of this data can be used to diﬀeren-
tiate between the important and unimportant replays of matches and highlights
that the coaches need to review in order to analyse opposing teams. This could
lead to a data driven approach to replace the more conventional approach of
watching replays that will allow the coaches to focus more on developing their
own teams instead of analysing other teams.
To create a data driven approach to watching replays and highlights more ap-
proachable a speciﬁc Esports game is chosen to act as a prototype. This game
is Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive. Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive is, com-
pared to the other Esports games, more intuitive. It is also a game where it is
possible to easily acquire a lot data that can be used for the analysis.
1.2
Introduction to Counter Strike: Global Of-
fensive
This section will go through the basics of Counter Strike Global Oﬀensive, ab-
breviated as CSGO. Starting with an explanation of what type of game it is.
This is followed by descriptions of the diﬀerent game mechanics and elements
that play a large role in deciding the outcome of a CSGO match. Then one of
the playable maps from the game will be explained and ﬁnally the competitive
CSGO scene will be described.
1.2.1
Gameplay aspects and often used terms
Counter Strike is a team based, ﬁrst person shooter game. Each team consists of
ﬁve human players. Each player can move around in an enclosed 3-dimensional
space, with the speciﬁc layout of objects, such as buildings, being determined
by a chosen playing ﬁeld called a map. Each player can carry diﬀerent types of
equipment. The most important type is the weapon, where a main weapon
==PAGE:17==
1.2 Introduction to Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive
3
such as a riﬂe or sniper, a pistol and a knife can be carried at once. Players can
also buy utility in the form of protective armor and grenades such as ones
causing explosions or blooming smoke in a small areas to obscure vision. To get
an idea of the visuals, see ﬁgure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: An ingame view of Counter Strike.
The game is split into rounds. Multiple goals or win conditions exist for a
team in each round. The simplest goal for both teams are to kill all members of
the opposing team, mainly by shooting them. Each player has a ﬁxed number
of health points or hit points, which indicates death when reaching zero. The
player is then eliminated from the round, but re-spawns in the next round.
Weapons can be used to deal damage on hit, the amount depending on the
aﬀected body region. Some weapons are strong enough that only a single bullet
is needed if the head is hit. If a win condition is reached by a team, they win
that round. A game or match consists of up to 30 rounds, with the ﬁrst team
reaching 16 round wins, declared winner of the match. A match may end in a
draw, or extra rounds can be played as overtime, just as extra playtime can be
added in other sports.
The teams are playing as one of two asymmetric sides at the start of the game,
the Terrorist side and the Counter Terrorist side, abbreviated T and CT.
The goal of the T side is to plant the Bomb at one of two designated areas
called A- and B Site. When the bomb has been planted, the T side has to
defend it for 40 seconds. The goal of the CT side is ﬁrst of all to disallow the T
side to plant them bomb. Each round is around 1 minute and 50 seconds, and
the CT side will win if the time runs out. In case the bomb is planted by the T

side, the CT side will win the round if they are able to defuse the bomb before
it explodes. Both teams can also win the round by eliminating all opponents as
seen, however if the bomb is planted, the CT side needs enough time to defuse
it, even if all terrorist members are dead.
1.2.2
Economy
Each team is assigned one side for 15 rounds, called the ﬁrst half, and is then
swapped around afterwards in the second half. At the start of each half, all
players start with 800 money and a basic pistol as weapon. Money can be used
to buy weapons and equipment at the start of the round. 800 is very little,
so the ﬁrst round is limited by this. The players on the team that wins the
round will be awarded 3250 money each, while the losing team is only awarded
1400. If a team loses multiple rounds in a row, which is quite common, they will
accumulate a lose streak, granting increased money per loss. If some players
survive the previous round, they will keep their equipment for the next.
The available money and carry over equipment for the whole team, called their
economy, is a large factor in terms of success and strategic options. For this
reason, speciﬁc types of rounds are deﬁned based on the equipment. An Eco
round is when a team has bought very little to save money, often using the basic
pistol and zero or a few grenades. A force-buy is when a team spends what
little money they have, often if they feel they will be short on money in the next
round even by ecoing. This is often upgraded pistols, body armor and some
grenades. A full buy is when teams have bought close to all they want. An eco
round will often be followed by a full buy, to maximise the chance of winning
one of the two rounds. As such, while the game is based on rounds, some eﬀect
carry over and aﬀects the whole match.
1.2.3
Grenade types
To better understand the possibilities teams have for tactical exploration, it is
important to know the types and eﬀects of grenades in Counter Strike. These
are often the backbone to any strategy. Five types exists. Each player can carry
four grenades at once, and is limited to a single of each kind, other than the
ﬂashbang, of which two can be carried at once.
The ﬂashbang grenade can be thrown, and after a short time will detonate.
All players looking in the direction, be it opponents, teammates or the thrower
himself, will be blinded for a short while, the visuals on the monitor simply
==PAGE:19==
1.2 Introduction to Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive
5
changes to bright white. The duration is based on the distance between the
player and the detonation position, as well as the angle at which the player
was looking at the detonation position.
This grenade is often used to push
opponents away from locations. Given an example where one opponent waits
around the corner ready to shoot. Instead of running into his vision, the player
ﬁrst throws a ﬂashbang around the corner, and then runs in. The opponent will
then be blinded and is either an easy target, or he may have ran back to a safer
location. This is a very powerful tool for taking control, but also for use against
bombsites, where the opponents can not retreat.
The smoke grenade can be thrown, and after a short while will detonate into a
cloud of smoke. This smoke is present for 15 to 20 seconds, and blocks all vision
going through the smoke. It can therefore often be used to block oﬀareas. The
player may move through smoke, but when exiting, the enemy will be able to
see the player a short moment before the player, himself is outside the smoke
and can see again.
The High Explosive or HE grenade can be thrown, and after a short time
will detonate and deal damage to all players, be it teammates or opponents, in
the vicinity. Damage is generally blocked by obstacles, such as walls.
The molotov and incendiary grenade do the same thing, but when buying
is limited to the T and CT side respectively. When thrown, the grenade will
detonate upon hitting the ground and spawn a small area of ﬁre. All players
moving in ﬁre will take damage over time. The ﬁre may spread out slightly
during this duration, which is around 7 seconds. So while walls will block the
grenade, it is possible for ﬁre to spread around a corner.
The last type is the decoy grenade.
Very inexpensive, this grenade can be
thrown and after landing will start to make gunshot sounds. This grenades use
is limited since it is often simple to guess the decoy.

1.2.5
Tournament formats, organisations and the profes-
sional scene
When it comes to professional Counter Strike, tournaments are hosted as for
many other esports and sports. This will pair professional teams against each
other in diﬀerent formats. For larger tournaments, there is usually some initial
round, such as group play for a couple of days, and then playoﬀs such as quarter,
semi and grand ﬁnals. In the initial stage, both Best-of-one and Best-of-three
matches are often played, with later stages being best-of-three and sometimes
best-of-ﬁve in ﬁnals. Competitive Counter Strike works in the way, that there
are seven possible maps to play. Depending on the format, teams take turn
banning and picking maps. Banning means that a team choose a map that will
not be played. For a best of three format, it is usually one ban per team, then
one pick, then one ban, and the leftover map is the third map if needed. The
seven choosable maps are mainly static, but sometimes a map will be swapped
out for another map.
A main focus point for this project is on the coaches of teams. The 5 players
on a team have to spend a lot of time practicing to perform the best they
can when it comes to the mechanics of the game.
For this reason, there is
less time for strategic planning. This is one of the main jobs of many coaches
[Bor19],[Teb19] . Coaches will need insight into what maps are favored by other
teams, and which are preferred by his own. This allows for creating a plan over
the veto process, and ensure that the team ends up playing the best possible

maps. A coach can also prepare diﬀerent in-game tactics for a team to practise
and he can study strong opponents to try and ﬁnd key play styles and possible
weaknesses in their play. And while the daily activities of coaches surely diﬀers
from day to day and team to team, a lot of time is spent on both looking at
their teams play, but also replays of other teams matches.
Played matches of tournaments are recorded and saved as demo ﬁles. These ﬁles
allow one to re-watch the game, from any players perspective, in the Counter
Strike client application. Coaches can use these ﬁles to investigate both the
performance of their own players, and the play style of other teams. This process
can be very time consuming, especially if one has to manually look at every game
to ﬁgure out important details of that match. For this reason some tools exist to
help with this process. These are however very focused on portraying the match

In this project data pertaining to competitive Counter Strike Global Oﬀensive is

opponents previous matches? Is it possible to quickly study the opponent teams

Unsupervised Learning
Unsupervised Learning is the task of identifying patterns in data where each
observation Xi = (x1, x2, ...xn) does not have a corresponding response variable
Yi. This means that there is no ground Truth information about the observa-
tions. Instead diﬀerent methods are used to try and perform a segmentation
of observations. The segmentation is done by clustering the observations such
that the observations within each cluster are as closely related to each other as
possible. After the clustering has been done a cluster analysis can be used to
form descriptive statistics about the clusters that can be used to infer whether
or not the observations within the clusters are similar or not. The idea of sim-
ilarity or dissimilarity between the observations can be made measurable by
using diﬀerent distance metrics to compare the observations.
In the following section three diﬀerent unsupervised methods that can be used
to cluster observations will be presented. The ﬁrst method is K-means, which
is an algorithm that uses Euclidean distance to compare the similarity of the

3.1.3
Agglomerative Clustering
Both K-Means and K-Medioids clustering depends the speciﬁcation of the de-
sired number of clusters. When the desired number of clusters is not known it
can make it hard to use these algorithms. In contrast to these hierarchical clus-
tering does not depend on this speciﬁcation. Instead of specifying the desired
number of clusters a measure of dissimilarity between groups of observations,
that is based on the pairwise dissimilarity of the observations in the group. The
strategy is then to create a hierarchy where all observations are in their own
cluster at the bottom and all observations in one cluster at the top. There are
two diﬀerent paradigms that can be used with hierarchical clustering, bottom
up and top down. The top down approach are called divisive and and bottom
up approaches are called agglomerative[WW07]. Divisive start at the top with
one big cluster, and then recursively split one of the clusters as the current level
into two new clusters until all the observations are in their own clusters. The
splitting is chosen such as to create the largest between-group dissimilarity. Ag-
glomerative start with all observations in their own clusters, and the recursively
combine two clusters, until all observations are in one large cluster. The merg-
ing of two clusters is based on the smallest intergroup dissimilarity.
This type of clustering can easily easily be displayed as a tree, where the top
node contains all the smaller clusters nested inside of it, such that the top node
is split in two, which are then again split in two recursively until there is only
one observation left. All these nodes represent the value of dissimilairyt at which
two clusters are combined. The observations are inthe leaf nodes of this tree
structure. This kind of plot is referred to as a dendrogram. For all agglomera-
tive and some divisive algorithms it is always possible to plot a dendrogram of
the clustering. Because the methods have monotonely increasing dissimilarity
between the clusters as more and more clusters are merged. Agglomerative clus-

A warp path W = w1, w2, ..., wK is constructed, where K, max(m, n) ≤K ≤
m + n is the length of the path and each element wk, 0 < k ≤K is a pair of
points, (i, j), where i, j is indices from S and T respectively. In other words,
the warp path are a sequence of pairing of elements from S and T. [SC07] This
warp path can be found by using dynamic programming [CLRS09] to construct
an n times m matrix and then evaluate the following recurrence
γ(i, j) = d(si, tj) + min{γ(i −1, j −1), γ(i, j −1), γ(i −1, j)}
(3.16)
where d(i, j) is the distance in cell (i, j) and γ(i, j) is the cumulative distance
of d(i, j) plus the three, previous, adjacent cells [RK04]. The cell (m, n) will
then be the minimum distance warp path possible, where all points in each
sequence are used at least once. The distance function used are arbitrary, but
usually the euclidean distance are applied.
As can be seen when ﬁlling the
matrix, the end points of each time series have to be matched. Intermediate
points of one sequence may be paired multiple times with diﬀerent points in the
other sequence, but never with a point corresponding to an earlier index of the
sequence. More formally, two elements from the warp path wk = (i, j), wk+1 =
(i′, j′) need to have monotonically increasing indices, such that i ≤i′ ≤i+1, j ≤
j′ ≤j + 1 [SC07].
Constraints may be applied to the warping distance.
This is often done by
limiting the diﬀerence between indices i, j. A simple way to do this is by a
constant c, such that |i −j| ≤c, which creates what is called the Sakoe-Chiba

band [SCWL90], [RK04]. As the straight forward algorithm to construct the
matrix requires O(m·n), this constraint can be used to limit the number of cells
that require ﬁlling. Another possible beneﬁt of using a constraint, is to limit
the warping of the time series. Setting a the constant to 1, will force i = j,
which is simply calculating the distance along the diagonal, equivalent to make
no alignments between the two time series.
Setting the constant to inﬁnity
will calculate the whole matrix. In some cases, this may allow for too freely of
alignment, and a constant in between may give better accuracy for classiﬁcation
and clustering tasks of sequences [RK04]. Many other types of optimisations
have been researched through the years [RCM+12]. This can in theory reduce
the running time all the way down to O(n) complexity for many tasks [RK04],
especially if an approximation are allowed [SC07]. This is not something we will
cover in this report, as the performance where not a problem for the smaller
length time series used.
The time series may be multivariate. In this case, DTW is applied as normal,
but the distance function needs to handle multiple variables. A simple choice
would be to just take the euclidean distance in the higher dimension.
It is
also possible to split the multivariate time series into multiple univariate time
series, one for each feature. DTW can then be calculated for each feature, and
the results summed [MK16]. Experiments suggest the best choice are very data
dependent, but in general, if the features are highly correlated or tightly coupled,
calculating the multidimensional distance may be preferable, but splitting the
time series seems more robust against random lag [MK16].

by simply taking the mean value of all scores. In the same vein, a high score
means a general consensus among the points, that they are well separated and
tightly clustered, with a few low scoring points not skewing the results much.
One drawback of this method, is of course that a good clustering are deﬁned by
well separated clusters, which may not always be the best measure for diﬀerent
cluster shapes.

Computer Vision
Another approach to converting images into descriptors is to use diﬀerent feature
extraction algorithms to compute features based on the image. This approach
has some advantages over the use of hashing functions, because it can be used
to describe how much of the image that is covered in movement, or grenades. It
==PAGE:46==
32

could potentially also generalize better because it could help extinguish the dif-
ferent maps from each other. Even though there are diﬀerent feature extraction
algorithms the general approach is the same for each one. First all the images
are run through the feature extraction algorithm, which is going to result in as
much as 500 to 1500 features for each image. These features are then clustered
with k-means or k-medoids using many clusters, such as to be able to cluster
all these features together and reduce the number of features to a manageable
number of clusters center that have hopefully captured the diﬀerent features
correctly. Once we have these cluster centers, we build a bag of words for all the
images. A bag of words which will essentially contain a row for each image, and
a column for each cluster center. Then each feature form the image is used to
increment the column of the cluster center that it belongs to. This essentially
creates a histogram for each image. This histogram is normalized such that we
can infer how much of a given image is made up of features from a given cluster.
These histograms can the once again be clustered with k-means clustering and
the clusters can be analyzed.
This approach is inspired by a very successful approach to comparing texts with
each other, where each column would represent a word and the count would be
how many times that word appeared in a given text. In order for this approach
to be applicable the visual words needs to be found automatically, such as to
not depend on manual labeling of the images. This brings us to the diﬀerent
approaches for extracting these features that describe the visual words that are
present in the images. An assumption about these features that needs to hold
for this approach to works is that similar features belong to the same visual
world

Transform(SIFT) algorithm.

Oriented FAST and rotated BRIEF, abbreviated as ORB[RRKB13] is a feature
detection algorithm that combines a modiﬁed version of the FAST keypoint
detector[RD09] with a binary patch descriptor called BRIEF[CLF11]. FAST
is used to ﬁnd keypoints in an image which can will then later be turned into
a patch for which a BRIEF descriptor is then calculated. The keypoints are

found by doing a series of tests for each pixel, where the pixels who pass the
tests are kept. First an appropriate threshold t is selected. Then for each pixel
that is checked a 16 pixel circle is created around that pixel. Then a test is
performed to see if the two pixels in the same column as the pixel of interest
are tested if they are brighter or darker than the pixel, and if they are the same

the pixel of interest. Then at least three of the 4 pixels that are on the same

the threshold combined. The number n is chosen to be 12. the FAST keypoint

from a mean of 0.5 and added to a vector T. Then with a greedy search the
ﬁrst test from T is added to the feature descriptor nd. Then the next test from
T is compared against all tests in nd and if the correlation is greater than a
threshold it is discarded, if not it is added to nd this is done until nd has 256
tests in it.
3.6.3
FAST and BRIEF based features
ORB and SIFT are both general purpose feature detector and descriptors. This
means that there is great emphasis on ﬁnding keypoints that are scale invari-
ant and descriptors that are rotational invariant. In most situations this is a
very desirable attribute when the goal is to solve the correspondence problem of
recognizing features across images that might have been zoomed in or zoomed
out, or the camera might have been tilted to one side. This is however not the
case when it comes to our construction of image-based observations. The size
of the players does not change from observation to observation, nor does their
rotation. Given our desire to cluster the images based on the features that they
exhibit it might not be desirable to have rotation invariant features, because we
do want to distinguish between a players looking in diﬀerent directions. Being
able to recognize keypoints at diﬀerent scales does not really have an impact on
the images, but does introduces some extra computation time that is unneces-
sary, and because of this SIFT and ORB are compared to a keypoint detector
and a feature descriptor that do not exhibit these properties. Because ORB
has proven to reasonably well, we have chosen to use FAST and BRIEF as a
comparison.
The implementation of FAST is the same as that for ORB except that it does
not add an orientation to the patch by calculating the moments of the image
patch as is done in section 3.6.2, furthermore it also just runs on the image, and
does not create an image pyramid to detect features at diﬀerent scales.
BRIEF also works the same as it does in section 3.6.2 except that the image
patches are not rotated, and instead of running an algorithm to maximize the

4.2.1
Demo Parsing
A lot of work has gone into parsing Counter Strike demos. A demo is a ﬁle
containing all information from a previously played game, normally used by the
Counter Strike application to replay everything that has happened. The format
of the .dem ﬁles are based on googles protocol buﬀers [Goo]. In short, it is
a way of serializing data in a more ﬂexible and eﬃcient way than many older
approaches. For a better description, we refer to Appendix C, which contains
a previous report, written for a special course investigating presentation and
analysis of Counter Strike data [JMJ19]. The interesting parts about proto-
col buﬀers are section 4.1.2 Demo parser for a description and section 5.3.1,
Research existing solutions better for a short discussion on the actual usage of
protocol buﬀers by Valve. Bednárek et al. also discusses some of the technical
aspects and problems of constructing a parser [BKYZ17].
The important part about the demo ﬁles is, that these can be read with third
party tools, allowing for abstraction beyond protocol buﬀers and bit-wise ma-
nipulation. We are utilising an open source library written in golang, which can
be found on https://github.com/markus-wa/demoinfocs-golang under the name
demoinfocs-golang [mw19]. This library handles iteration of a demo ﬁle, in the
same order that events happened in the actual game. The main features used
are the well-presented interface for parsing the packet structure, including the
parts that require manual binary translation. The library also handles much
of the data as entities, such as players or equipment. These entities are auto-
matically updated with the correct information such as positional data for each
frame, where manual reading of the demo only stores velocity and direction of
entities. The library makes it possible to collect and use this information at
any given time point in the demo. Throughout the iteration of a demo ﬁle, the
library also allows for event subscription, matching the game events presented
by the demo format.

4.2.1.1
Demo Problems
Construction of the demo parser has been an iterative approach, both based on
features, but also on correctness. Many of the demo ﬁles behave diﬀerently in
many diﬀerent parts of the ﬁle. Some speciﬁc problems and the implemented
ﬁxes are described below, as validity of data is of great importance, but testing
has been diﬃcult.
• Some demos starts with a high round number.
To make it worse, the
match state are also set to true, as if the match was oﬃcially ongoing.
Generally, the match state will change to false, and then back to true, and
the round will reset to 0. Two problems are introduced from this. The
ﬁrst is that rounds are used for indexing arrays. If the game is marked as
started, and the ﬁrst round has number 12, the index are out of bounds.
The second, is the uncertainty of which rounds are correct and which are
not. In this case, the rounds are clearly not part of the match, and the
solution has therefore been to check the length of the rounds array before,
add missing rounds up to the round number, and when the round resets
to 0, we simply overwrite the entries at the start, which contains no data.
This introduces new problems, as we will now see.
==PAGE:64==
50
Data
• Some demos starts after round 0 but with the actual game. Seen rarely,
some demos are started a bit too late, such that it may only record data
starting around round 1. This means that in our solution, round 0 will
be empty, but as long as the round number not resetting, all the played
rounds should be stored correctly.
• Some demos have slightly diﬀerent frame and tick values than 128 and 64.
In practise, this means that it may not be an ﬁxed tick interval between
each frame, as an example, 2 ticks interval per frame in the case of 64
frames per second and 128 ticks per seconds. This means that we may end
up saving ticks with an interval of 32, for it to suddenly be and interval of
34 for one or more frames. With regards to the data, we are just extracting
per frame data and as such, it is still correct. The problem comes in when
the visualisation tool assumes 32 ticks between each position update. This
makes it harder to look up positions as of now, and can desync the visual
timer of the round.
It has been ﬁxed such that we always check the
interval, and if it is more or less ticks than expected, we add a desync
value, to force the saved tick to be correct intervals. If the desync is very
bad, this could make our visualiser seem sped up for some rounds. It is
noted however, that the desync is reset for each round, since the interval
is based on the movement start tick in each round, and that rounds rarely
desync much.
• Some demos are split into two parts, which seems clearly indicated by
the name of the demo ﬁle. A solution requires these two ﬁles to be read
in sequence, with the ﬁrst being parsed normally, and the second simply
starting in a later round. But this would directly contradict our solution
to the former problem, where a ﬁle starts falsely with a late round. It
could be solved with boolean values indicating that it is part of a split
demo. Nevertheless, for both the teams we are using, only on split demo
is found. We have therefore chosen to simply ignore it.

match goes into overtime and resets the round number to 0 again, we will
overwrite valid rounds. Likewise, if the overtime are continued, we may
overwrite rounds in the middle. This has not been ﬁxed, but the chance

This goal of this project is to see if it it is possible to optimize the process of
preparing for upcoming matches or tournaments by automatically highlighting
the diﬀerent tactics that the opposing teams are using. This is a hypothesis
that does not have an actual ground truth that can be validated without asking
the team that is being analysed if the highlighted tactics are in fact the tactics
that they use. To make matters even harder to verify, we would need to load up
the game and replay the diﬀerent match demos in order to inspect the diﬀerent
rounds or ﬁnd recordings online to scroll through. Because this would be very
time consuming task for us and because it would be a poor presentation of the
results to just report back list of important rounds, we developed a 2D viewer.
This 2D viewer is based on a prototype that was created in a previous course.
See appendix C for details. This prototype however was very bare-bone and
did not meet the requirements that we needed for this course and because of
that we further developed it to visualise more things from the rounds as well as
fundamentally changing how the view is updated. As stated in this technical
report which can be found in appendix C the viewers update scheme is to count
how many frames has been rendered and then once a set number of frames has
been counted the positions of the players are updated. This approach worked
well when it came to only visualising the movement of the players. It did not
however work well for visualising the use of utility, that is the use of grenades,
together with the player movements. This is because we would either need to
create arrays with the same length as the player movement arrays and then
essentially have no information when the utility is not used, and then correlate
the positions of the players with the start position of the grenades, or in some
other way translate the throw times in-game to the a frame count that can
be used to visualise the utility in the viewer. Because of this we moved away
from the frame based updates and instead implemented a tick based updating
scheme. The game data is already tick based, and as such we can simply use
the tick information directly from the game to update the viewer. We save the
game state every 32 ticks which corresponds to 4 times every second. So the
viewer now needs to update the tick value 4 times every second, and then use
the corresponding tick value to look up the state of the game. The viewer is
based on a native update scheme where the browser calls a speciﬁed function up
to 60 times a second, but this might vary depending on the computer and how
many other things the user is doing. Because of this, a timestamp is passed to
the render function which can be used to increment the tick value. In listing 4.1
the update scheme can be seen. First we deﬁne the desired frames per second
update, and calculate the the number of milliseconds we want to allow to pass
for every tick update. Then we also setup an interpolation interval which is used
to interpolate the position between the current position and the next position
of the players and grenades. The interpolation is updated 20 times a second,

Chapter 5
Feature Engineering
A lot of data can and has been collected from demo ﬁles of previous matches.
Not only are there a large amount of diﬀerent features such as equipment and
movement, each feature contains many values. In this chapter, the following will
be done. We will start oﬀwith a discussion on limiting the size and complexity
of round data. This culminates with the introduction of a snapshot, which is
data for a speciﬁc 10 second time frame of a round. These snapshots are used
by a selection of methods, to investigate executes in rounds. These methods
will be investigated thoroughly and the usage and processing of data analysed
and discussed.
5.1
Limiting the Time Window of a Round
Much of the information in a demo is based on speciﬁc time-points in the round.
A players position changes many times a second, grenades are thrown at speciﬁc
time points and so on. The goal of this section is to investigate and discuss how
a round can be represented eﬃciently for analysis with minimal loss. With no
preprocessing, a round can be saved as an N x M matrix, with N time points,
each with M features. For multiple observations, this will either become a 3d
tensor, or a matrix where observations are very large vectors of features for all
==PAGE:80==
66
Feature Engineering
time points. With 128 updates a second, this is clearly too large. The parser
already limits this to 4 points per second. While the optimal rate cannot be
easily determined, it is limited what can be done in a single second. As long
as crucial information are not skipped, but rather mapped to a diﬀerent chosen
time-point, this resolution should not aﬀect the quality much.
Still, the observations are very large, and more importantly, much of a round is
not very interesting from the perspective of execute investigation. At the start
of the round, the two teams always start far away from each other in predeﬁned
areas of the map. This makes the ﬁrst portion of a round redundant as long
as we know the time players start interacting. The ﬁrst parts of the round can
therefore be removed. The same is in fact true for the latter part of a round,
when a team has either failed an execute, or successfully conquered a site and
planted the bomb. This is harder to ﬁlter.
To generalise, if we can ﬁnd a point in the round where the interesting executions
start, which holds for arbitrary rounds, this would enable us to reduce the size
of the data drastically. We can even set an upper bound on time. If we assume
we have the time point at which an execute strategy starts, we can use the
timespan in which grenades are active to know that the execute now has either
been attempted or dropped. In both cases, the information we seek is covered.
The most used grenade for blocking vision is the smoke grenade, whose eﬀect
is also the longest duration. This can take multiple seconds before detonating
into a cloud of smoke, which are present for 15 seconds. It should therefore be
possible to catch the interesting parts of a round needed for our analysis. using
less than 30 seconds.
There are some risks with this approach. One risk is in rounds with multiple
executes. Often, there is enough utility on a team to both use it for map control
and executes. It quickly runs out however, if multiple executes are attempted. A
team may also want to save some for after an execute, to defend the conquered
site. Time also plays a factor, in that teams do not have unlimited time to
execute. Nevertheless, this can happen and only one will be captured. It does
happen, that an execute is simply a fake, meaning a team acts as attacking one
site, but attacks the other instead. This means their utility will often be spread
between both sites, and there will often be some time between the fake and the
real execute. In this scenario, information may be lost, either the fake or parts
of the real attack, due to either selecting the latter or 30 seconds not being
enough to contain both executes. This can also happen when an execute fails
and the team rotates to the other site.
To remove even more complexity, we can also try to view a round as a single
observation ignoring time. An example could be collecting economic information
from a team, as well as their positions at the start of an execute. This could also
==PAGE:81==

67
contain kills, bomb plants, grenade detonation positions and more, depending
on what information is determined to be important. The biggest change, for
good or bad, is the removal of temporal information. In theory, one could look
at grenade positions and player positions, and see if that is enough to determine
the type of execute or lack thereof. Of course, most information regarding the
movement and actions of players are lost in this simpliﬁcation.
We start by investigating the problem of choosing the right starting point in
a round. To be more precise, we deﬁne this timing as being the point in the
round, which we deem most likely to contain an execute in the near future. We
will now walk through the functionality and explain how we determine this, by
creating what we call a snapshot of a round.
5.2
Snapshot Deﬁnition and Generation
A snapshot of a round is deﬁned as accumulated data taken from a speciﬁc,
short, time interval of a round. The term snapshot comes from the idea, that
this data should be interpretable as a single image, although it does contain
temporal information. The beneﬁts of using snapshots are both that the data
should be simpler to work with, but also to allow for ﬁltering out non wanted
rounds. Two aspects will be discussed here. The ﬁrst is how and when to select
a time for a snapshot. The second is what data are stored in said snapshot.
To generate snapshots, we iterate through the data in a round, from the start
to ﬁnish. As seen, a round is stored as a collection of objects and events, which
changes over time. Since we are interested in attacks against a site, we need to
evaluate what determines an attack. The most basic version can be found by
positions. If the terrorist team starts moving close to and onto a site, an attack
has happened. Often, resistance is met, and the team may fail to reach the
objective, yet it is still an attack. In this case, an attack could be determined
as the point when a terrorist enters a site, and the go back in time. To also get
attacks that do not immediately succeed, it could be taken when most of the
ﬁghting is happening, but this is not easy to deﬁne, as engagements can vary a
lot in intensity and length. Another thing we can use are grenades. Successful
attacks almost always includes a series of well timed, well placed grenades, such
as smoke grenades obscuring vision in a speciﬁc areas. Grenades are mainly used
to push forward, to take control of new areas of the map, inclusive the sites.
It is therefore possible to use the number and positions of thrown grenades, to
ﬁnd points of interest. Grenades have the beneﬁt that players can only carry
four at a time, and the amount thrown in a round is therefore limited.
==PAGE:82==
68
Feature Engineering
Looking at grenades also has the added beneﬁts of ensuring that events of strate-
gic importance may happen in the round. In other words, it is possible to ﬁlter
out rounds where the terrorists are not using grenades to attack a site, for rea-
sons such as low economy, or the opponents trying to attack rather than wait,
or the Terrorists simply failing before getting close to a site for an attack. All
these rounds are of little interest for this project scope, since they are not ap-
plying any strategies of much value. In that sense, we can collect these into one
group, and depending on what the coach are interested in, the rounds can be
investigated or ignored. We also ﬁlter oﬀrounds, with less than three players
on the terrorist side alive.
The way the grenades are used can be separated into steps. First, a sequence
of grenades are generated by ﬁnding the ﬁrst grenade thrown by the terrorist
team. Then all grenades thrown inside a time interval, currently set to ten sec-
onds, are added to the sequence. This is done for each grenade, and we collect
as many sequences as there are grenades thrown. Some of these are removed
based on criteria. The ﬁrst is we are only interested in Smoke grenades, Incen-
diary grenades and Flashbang grenades. Then, sequences with less than three
grenades are removed. Afterwards, grenades thrown far away from the site are
removed as well when determining an attack. The distance has been chosen
manually as a radius from the middle of sites, containing the important areas
around each site. Lastly, some of the sequences will be subsets of others, for
example if 4 grenades are thrown in a short time followed by a pause, the last
three and two grenades will also be saved as a sequences. These are removed.
While ﬁltering, a site is assigned to each grenade, such that we can automati-
cally sort rounds based on A and B site executions. This is based on which site
the grenades are closest to.
All these sequences are seen as valid snapshots. Sometimes, no snapshots are
found and the rounds are ﬁltered oﬀ. Sometimes one is found, and we simply
use the one found. In some cases, multiple are found. This often happens when
an attack are slowed down into multiple phases of grenades, or when grenades
are used after a site has been taken, to defend the planted bomb. In this case,
one has to be chosen as representative for the round. The most promising way
found to automatically choose the best snapshot, was by greedily choosing based
on the ones with most smoke grenades, since these are generally the stable parts
of an execute strategy, and then based on the total amount of grenades in the
sequence. In the rare cases where sequences are still left, the ﬁrst sequence are
taken. The argument are that we would rather have an early part of the attack,
than grenades used after a site has been taken. This approach is quite naive,
though have shown good performance when manually evaluating snapshots of
rounds.
When a sequence has been chosen for the snapshot, data can be collected. This is
based on the time point at which the snapshot starts and some time afterwards,

set to 10 seconds as standard. From here, we extract all player positions, all
grenade trajectories, as well as equipment of players and if the bomb was planted.
Note that we include all grenades in this time interval, not only those in the
chosen sequence, since some will often be used to defend against attacks from
behind. Other data are also created, such as what grenades each player threw
and which zone they were thrown from. The mentioned zones are names of
areas on the map, extracted from the demo ﬁle together with position. All the
data are only collected for the chosen team. The opponents are not included.
The argument is, that there is too much variance in the opponents play, and a
team often have little knowledge of their opponents positions. For this reason,
many executes actually start before larger engagements, and it is these exact
strategies we wish to ﬁnd.
Later on in the attack, the problem is that the
opponents choices directly inﬂuences how well the execute worked, and how
players are moving. This means the further forward we look in the snapshot,
the more variation we should see. This is also a main reason for keeping the
low time interval of ten seconds. The argument for having opponents positions
and grenades are that they directly inﬂuence the actions of the terrorist team.
But our current goal is to simplify our data into a manageable scope, and for
this reason, we choose to not double the size of the data with opponents as well.
As a side note, another interesting aspect could be to look at diﬀerent teams
reactions to certain types of executes, see if patterns exist there. But that is a
whole diﬀerent topic.
One problem that arise, both for generating sequences and extracting data, are
that no direct way of mapping players to a real life team exists in the data. To
be more speciﬁc, we have a set of players that are identiﬁed by self chosen aliases
in the game. Five of these names play together on one in game team, such as
the terrorist side. But the actual team name, such as the Astralis organisation,
are not part of the match. We therefore have to map player names to a team,
to determine if the right players are playing on the terrorist side. If the player
names were ﬁxed, this would be straight forward, however players can choose
themselves from game to game. In general in the competitive scene, names are
rarely changed, and are mostly small deviations or sponsor preﬁxes to names.
Most of the time, we have been able to match names simply by checking each
player against a set of used names, and if two or more match, we accept them
as the team. Extracting the names of the players in all rounds has then allowed
us generate a small mapping of aliases to a single name manually. Examples of
this are the player with most aliases, Magisk, who have gone under the names
Magisk’, ’Mag1sk-’, ’MagiskbOY’, ’Magiskb0Y’ and ’ast. Magisk’. For future
analysis of diﬀerent teams, this could pose a bigger problem.

Binary and aggregated observations
Taking inspiration from some metrics of [KLC18], we have introduced our own

features are what types of equipment a player is carrying, what zone he is in
at the start of the snapshot, if he threw grenades, whether he died and if the

binary values. The total number of features are 40 per player, and an extra two

total features. Euclidean distance could be used on these. We will now look

5.5.1
Equipment
One aspect of a round, is the economy available to a team. Both weapons, armor
and grenades costs money, and teams often have to choose between spending
or saving for later rounds. With regards to our snapshot algorithm, one would
expect many rounds of low economy would already be ﬁltered oﬀdue to a low

amount of grenades use able. Nevertheless, it can be interesting to investigate
the state of available equipment for each round.
Some choices have to be made with regards to representation of weapons. The
simplest would be to aggregate the monetary value of a teams equipment to
a single value.
This is enough to ﬁlter rounds into categories such as low,
medium and high, but does not say much else. The other immediate choice
would be to simply list all equipment. Many diﬀerent weapons exist, and this
can quickly become overwhelming.
As a middle ground, we have chosen to
divide weapons into categories, mostly matching the categories in game. This
results in 8 categories, although only 5 are actually used as we shall see. The
pistol category are split into the ones a player starts with Basic Pistols, and
the ones that cost money, Buy Pistols. These are generally used when money
is low. As medium cost alternatives, we have the SMG’s and Shotguns, as well
as the Scout, a low cost sniper riﬂe.
The most used weapons are part the
Rifles, where both T and CT players have two expensive weapons that are
almost always used if money permits. There are also expensive Heavy Snipers
and Machine Guns. Grouping weapons like this both allows us to get a quick
idea about the overall value, while still allowing for distinctions between equal
valued rounds such as one with 4 SMGs and one with two riﬂes. Since players
can carry both a main weapon and a pistol at the same time, we only count the
main weapon, if one is carried, else we count the pistol type. These features are
extracted as binary values per player and also as a count by team. Grenades
are saved in the same way, with features for each of the ﬁve types.
Another choice is when to extract the equipment data. One choice is to take the
equipment at the start of the round, the other is to take it at the start of the
recorded snapshot. The snapshot will contain the events that has happen early
in the round. This includes used utility and dead players with no equipment.
Weaponry can also be dropped and picked up throughout the round. A team
will often have a game plan at the start of a round, which is dependant on their
own economy. At the same time, changes to these plans can easily happen in
the round, based on the opponents decisions. A case can be made for taking
the equipment at the start of the snapshot. This gives the current amount of
weaponry and utility that can be used, however the latter is already indirectly
recorded in the snapshot. We are mainly interested in the economic type of
round, which is best determined at the start. In this case, we are also ensuring
that all 5 players are alive and their value can be taken into account. We have
therefore chosen to record the values at the start of the round, a few seconds after
the players begin moving in case a last second buy comes in. One possible change
could be to save weapons at the start, but utility at the snapshot. Summary
statistics for the total equipment count per round can be seen in table 5.1.

5.6
Grenade Detonations
In this section, we will investigate attempts at using the speciﬁc positions of
grenades. While it is interesting where a grenade is thrown from, one could
argue that the detonation location are even more important. This stems from
the fact that each grenade has an area of eﬀect. A smoke obscures vision in a
small circular area. A molotov spawns ﬁre that spreads in a similar sized area.
A ﬂashbang temporarily blinds player looking towards the detonation point,
the length depending on their closeness. Grenades are therefore crucial pieces
to limit the eﬀectiveness of opponents and minimize the number of locations
where enemies can ﬁght back.
Figure 5.9: Plot over the number of rounds having a speciﬁc number of
grenades thrown. Split on grenade type.
==PAGE:97==

The main problem in representing grenades, be it detonation location or tra-
jectory, are the variable amount of grenades used per round. In ﬁgure 5.9, the
number of rounds having zero, one, two etc. grenades thrown of the diﬀerent
types are shown. For most grenade types, between 0 and 5 are thrown in each
round. Most ﬂashbangs are thrown overall, and HE grenades are rarely used
compared to the three other types.
This means that it is not possible to directly construct similar length observa-
tions, unless absence of grenades can be encoded in a viable way. Sadly, a sound
value has not been found. Using the value zero for missing grenades could as
well be a grenade in this location. Zero coordinates would also inﬂuence the re-
sults of normal metrics such as euclidean distance diﬀerently based on the other
grenade positions. Negative values pose the same problem for distance met-
rics, although our coordinate system are limited from 0 to 1024 on both axes.
Instead, two diﬀerent approaches has been investigated, mainly computing a
custom distance matrix for all rounds, and changing the encoding of grenades
to be based on zones instead.
5.6.1
Custom Distance Metric for Grenades
While calculating a custom distance metric means we will not have access to
well deﬁned features for a round, plenty of analysis can be done based on dis-
tances alone. This does not mean we are without problems. The general idea
is to calculate the distance between rounds, based on the position of grenade
detonations. A straight forward choice would be to take the coordinates and
calculate the euclidean distance between each pair of grenades. Since grenades
are often used for diﬀerent purposes, the pairs can also be split on the grenade
type, such that distances are calculated between smokes, ﬂashbangs and molo-
tovs separately. The HE grenades are discarded for this since they are few, and
very varying when investigating generated snapshots, see ﬁgure 5.9, and we have
a hard time deﬁning a distance for missing grenades.
For two sets of grenades, there are choices on how to calculate the distance.
One example could be matching grenades based on the order in which they are
thrown. As an example, the ﬁrst grenade in each round will be paired, and
their distance taken, the same for the second ones an so on. Either the sum or
possibly the average distance of pairings could then be used as ﬁnal value for
that grenade type. Taking the average instead of summation would have the
beneﬁt of not adding distance between rounds, just from the fact that a round
has many grenades. Instead of using time, grenades could also be matched based
on their position, so grenades in the same area in the two rounds are compared,
regardless of time. This would be the easiest way of comparing with similar
==PAGE:98==
84

visual snapshots, where grenades are not clearly timed, only their position and
trajectory. An example of this can be seen in ﬁgure 5.10 where smokes and
molotovs are labelled. If grenades are separated by type, we could either just
match 1 with A, 2 with B and so on if the labelling are based on time. Otherwise
we could pair 1 and C and 2 with D, since these are closest to each other. The
single molotov in each round would have to be paired in both cases.

A problem arises, when an uneven number of grenades are thrown. The question
is then, what is the distance between two rounds, one with 2 smokes and the
other with 4 as in ﬁgure 5.10. The rounds can still be almost equal in many
aspects. We also have the case, that the snapshots may include slightly diﬀerent
amount of grenades based on the timing, for executes that are alike. For this
reason, we do not want the rounds to be very far. One solution is to add the
distance of the extra grenades in one round, to the closest one in the second
round. As such, one grenade in the second round would be used in multiple
pairings. Following the example, the smoke grenade pairs would then be 1 with
C, 2 with D, 2 with B and 1 with A. It may be beneﬁcial to ﬁrst take the average
distance of the ﬁrst two pairs, and then aggregate the distance of the last two
together since grenades are reused in these. This would help distinguish similar
rounds with few and many grenades in common.
The second major problem is when no grenades of a speciﬁc type are thrown in
one of the rounds. This is seen to mainly happen for molotovs, in fact 19 out
of 65 rounds for A site and 26 out of 81 rounds for B site does not contain any
molotovs, while only 3 rounds are devoid of smoke grenades for A site, and 5 for
B site. An argument could be made, that rounds in this case are very diﬀerent

5.6.2
Grenade Positions encoded as Zones
Another attempt was made to capture grenade detonations by dividing each
area around each Site into a 5 times 5 square grid. A grenade would then be
encoded as 25 features with a single 1 in the feature corresponding to the grid
cell in which the detonation position lie inside, the rest of the features 0. The
resulting grid can be seen in ﬁgure 5.11. This gives very long observations when
many grenades are used, and to ensure equal length, many 0 features as well as
empty grids. There is also the new problem when padding zero grids, that if the
grenade type should be encoded, we have to sort the grenades based on type.
We then have to get the maximum number of smokes used, pad all observations
with that amount where needed, then do the same for the two other grenade
types as well. This ends up giving even longer observations, around 5 times 25
features per grenade type. One solution to this problem could be to remove the
time aspect as for earlier binary observations, and simply have three grids, one
for each grenade type, with each feature in the grid being the count of grenades
in that cell, removing the player speciﬁc information. Another approach could
be to encode the grid into X and Y values, of length 5. Aggregating data for
this abstraction however, does not clearly describe the grenades anymore, as
one would not know which pairs of X and Y values are matching, and we are
limited to using 10 features per grenade.
This solution does not remove the problem with deﬁning similarities between
rounds with diﬀering amount of grenades. In fact, if the distance is based purely
on diﬀerences in the cells, two grenades in diﬀerent cells would account for a
distance of 2, while a missing grenade would only add the distance of 1. While it
seems an undesirable eﬀect, the corresponding observations were still compared
to the results of using the precomputed distances.

5.6.3
Evaluation of methods
Analysis have been done for the diﬀerent choices of distance metrics as discussed.
These are matching grenades in the order they were thrown in each round,
matching grenade positions by grenade type and matching grenade positions
ignoring type. These have been applied to calculate a distance matrix for all
rounds. The matrices can be used with distance based clustering algorithms, the
simplest of which is hierarchical clustering. We will not give a detailed evaluation
of each approach as this is non-interesting for the results of the project. Instead,
we will focus on the most notable problems for the diﬀerent approaches and
select a single approach, covered in the results section.

The grid-based approach performed poorly in general. The silhouette coeﬃcient
were close to 0 for more than a few clusters, indicating no clear separation. No
clear tendency in the snapshots were found by investigating both for 2 and for
10 clusters, since 10-15 clusters gave the best Adjusted Rand Index.
Then comes custom distance calculation. By ignoring grenade types, we would
expect the best ability to separate areas. But investigating the clusters, most

is that no sound guideline has been found, so even if a good value was determined

overﬁtted for our small data set.
5.7
Player Movement Analysis
Another important aspect of a round is how players move through the map. We
wish to investigate how well rounds can be compared based on this information
alone. A consequence of this is moving into the domain of time series analysis,
where players have a set of coordinates at each time step. We limit this to x
and y coordinates in the two dimensional plane. For similarity measurement of
players movement trails, Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) has been chosen for
multiple reasons. The ﬁrst is the diﬀering length of time series, stemming from
player deaths. DTW can work with these without problem, albeit the warping
window has to be as large as the length diﬀerence. In [RK04] it is mentioned
==PAGE:102==
88
Feature Engineering
how DTW does not performs similar to re-interpolating the shorter series to be
equal length. The second is the idea of pauses in player movement. Stutters
may appear, which could be delays in an execute, or shots taken before moving
forward. The hope is that DTW should, in many cases, be able to match around
this. Other beneﬁts are that DTW is often appropriate for shape based similarity
of shorter time-series [ASW15]. And while point location matter the most, it
is the shape of the trail we are interested in. As both a bonus and a possible
problem are, that end-points have a large inﬂuence on the ﬁnal score. This is
believed to be beneﬁcial for starting location, where we are very interested in
similar placement of players. However, the variation in movement increases over
the duration of the snapshot, and as such, the endpoints will possibly be very
diﬀerent even for similar executes.
To directly compare results with the visualised extracted snapshots of rounds,
the same time interval is used for the length of the movement. The movement
can therefore be extracted directly from the visual snapshot JSON data, while
removing the cluttering of multiple keys and tick values. The end result is a
dictionary, ﬁrst splitting based on A or B site executions. Then for both sites,
each player has two long lists, one with all X coordinates for all rounds, the
other Y coordinates. The structure is shown in ﬁgure 5.12. This makes it so
both X and Y data can be indexed based on round number, but are otherwise
stored as single variable time series. Since all demos have stored 4 positions a
second, the length of each timestep are the same for all rounds, and are implicit
based on index in the observation data.

5.7.2
Extending to multiple players
For looking at the movement of multiple players in a round, a problem exists
when some players are missing. The snapshots have been ﬁltered for 3 or more
players alive. This means between 3 and 5 player trails are available for each
round. The same problem as for grenades exist, in that no good value for missing
players are found. One possible option we have for this case, is to use the death
position of missing players and possibly some of their earlier movement. This
data is not part of the snapshot, but could be collected. The reason this is
not done, is because players death position is no indicator for an execute, and
players may often die early due to initial ﬁre ﬁghts. Instead, we look into using
the positions available for each pair of rounds.
The ﬁrst idea is to match what trails we can per round. The question is then
how to pair players of rounds. Matching players to themselves only, can both
be beneﬁcial and not.
If players have similar roles in executes or rounds in
general, this would make sense to do. However, for deﬁning types of executes,
the speciﬁc player moving through a speciﬁc area are of no concern.
Three diﬀerent distance calculations have been done.
The ﬁrst is based on
matching players to themselves in every round. In rare cases, two rounds will
have three players alive, and only one may then be present in both rounds. For
this reason, we simply match all players present in both rounds, and take the
mean distance. The distance for each match are calculated in the same way as
was done for a single player.
The second distance measure, is to ignore the speciﬁc player and look at anony-
mous trails. Choosing which trails to match for two rounds are not straight-
forward. The chosen approach is to match the trails in the best possible way,
that is, the match that gives the lowest distance, but uses three unique trails
in both rounds. While it can be very computationally expensive to ﬁnd the
optimal pairing of elements, we are limited 5 trails per round. This gives us a
total of 25 possible pairings of trails, example match trail of player 1 with player
1 or player 2 and so on. For the two rounds being compared, this is encoded as
tuples, (x, y) where x is a trail from the ﬁrst round and y from the second. All
possible parings of three trails can then be found by taking all tuple combina-

. A paring is only valid however, if a trail is only used once in each
round, and they have be ﬁltered based on this. For all valid pairings, we then
simply calculate the distance for each pair of trails and add them together, and
in the end take the minimum found distance. Note how rounds with less than
ﬁve players would not allow 25 pairings, but fewer. We can ignore this case,
since the distance for using a missing player is inﬁnity, and will never be chosen
as minimum distance.
One problem we see with this approach, is that only a subset of trail are taken
into account, and since most players funnel through mid when they move, three
trails may be taken amongst these, and the players moving further away are
simply ignored. For this reason, the third approach is to use up to ﬁve trails per
round, but allow reuse of trails for rounds with missing players. The approach
is therefore close to the same as for three trails. First all pairing of players alive
are constructed, and all possible combinations of these are constructed. The
combinations are again ﬁltered, such that players are unique for each round.
For rounds with less than ﬁve players, we keep track of the diﬀerence and which
players are used. If the rounds have equal amount of players alive, we are done.
If one has more, we take the players of this round, not used in the optimal
pairing, and match the distance to the closest trail in the other round. The end
result are the optimal distance plus the extra matches.
This approach forces all players to be taken into consideration, but are not
without ﬂaws. One, probably rare, example is when two rounds of 5 players
are compared. One round has 2 players in the Banana zone, the other only 1
player. In this case, one of the trails in the ﬁrst round has to be matched to one
far away. Say there was only 4 players alive in the second round. It would now
be possible for both Banana trails to be matched with the single one, and the
distance would be lower without any good reason.

One of the inherent problems with creating a good representation of a round is
that it is going to vary in length. This is because the amount of grenades uses,
or even the amount of players that are a live, at the time were the snapshot is
taken varies. This is further complicated by the lack of meaning full padding.
This problem can however be alleviated through the use of K-means clustering
of the features. The most simple way of doing this is to creating observations
that are based on the vector space model approach but instead of using the
desired features these are clustered and the cluster centers are used instead.
The transformed movement and utility information that is already being used
in the viewer can be reused for this purpose. These 2D coordinates can ﬁrst
be clustered, and by reusing the same number of clusters for each observation
the length of the observations will no longer vary. It is decided to separate the
grenades from the movement of the players, because it is not distinguishable
based on the coordinate whether it is the former or the latter. This is done by
splitting them into two separate histograms that can can be concatenated after
they are both normalized. The normalization is necessary to make sure that the
grenades do not end up outnumbering the movement.
In order to estimate the length of the histograms that should be used to rep-
resent both the movement and the utility we inspect the silhouette score and
gap statistic that can be calculated on the data. Firstly the movement data
is addressed. The silhouette score is calculated for the clustering and it turns
out that the data requires 47 clusters to achieve a silhouette score around 0.55
furthermore the minimized gap statistic suggests that 1 cluster should be used.
Because of the poor scores from clustering the movement data attempts are
made to use a sequence of coordinates instead of only using one coordinate for
each player. This does nothing for the gap statistic but the silhouette score
now suggests 2 cluster. This can be seen in ﬁgure B.7 in appendix B. From
our domain knowledge we know that 2 clusters is not be enough to capture the
desired resolution, and with a maximum of 5 coordinates for one observation
using more than 5 or 6 clusters will lead to very sparse histograms.
A similar story unfolds for the use of utility, where 2 clusters is suggested by
both the gap statistic and the silhouette score, this is because K-means wants to
cluster everything around banana into one cluster and all the grenades around
the A site into the second cluster. Attempts have been made to only use molo-
tovs and smoke grenades but this has done little to change the suggested number
of clusters. Figure B.6 in appendix B shows the coordinates of the utility plotted
as white dots on the map. This plot also lends itself to the suggesting that 2
clusters is best. Figure 5.18 shows the silhouette score for both the utility and
the movement, with the number of clusters on x-axis and the silhouette score
on the y-axis.

Discussion
Creating a data driven approach to watching replays and highlights is a project
that is best solved iteratively. Throughout this project we have attempted to
iteratively ﬁlter more and more data to reduce the time that a coach needs
to spend on analysing other teams through looking at their veto process and
watching match replays.
The veto process has been a good place to start,
because the optimization has been simple. Just by collecting all the data into
one page time has already been saved, by being able to inspect all the data
at once instead of having to navigate through a website to gather information
bit by bit. The process has been further streamlined by allowing the coach to
interact with the visualisation and ﬁlter on certain aspects of the data.
The rounds have also been interactively ﬁltered reducing the amount of rounds
that should be watched in order to analyse a given team. The most eﬀective
approach here has been to use the snapshot ﬁltering.
When certain simple
criteria have been meet the round is considered important and saved for further
inspection.
If we look at the matches that we have used for the analysis of
Astralis’ T side strategy, then this alone has been used to reduce the number
of rounds that should be inspected from 211 to 146.
These rounds are also
divided into A site and B site executes, which automatically allows the coach to
focus his attention on one site if he wants too. For the continued analysis the
A site executes have been used. This is because of the structure around A site,
that allows for a larger variety in the strategies of the teams, instead of using B
site which is only accessible through Banana for the terrorists. All subsequent

Problems
The data that is available to us comes from the communication between the
server and players that are playing CSGO through each of their clients. This
means that there is a lot of information that aﬀects the teams, that is not
available to us in the data. Things that can aﬀect how the match is played
out is whether or not it is an online or oﬄine match.
Whether or not it is
the ﬁnal of a tournament, a deciding match or one that has little consequence
for the team. On top this the mental state of the players can change between
matches, and rounds, depending on whether or not the team is winning or

Conclusion
This project has aimed to lay the foundation for a data driven approach to
analysing team strategies in Counter Strike: Global Oﬀensive. It has done so
by ﬁrstly collecting necessary data for presentation and analysis from a website
called HLTV.org on the 15th of March 2019.
The data consists of the veto
process, score, date, ingame data and tournament from matches from the top
30 competitive Counter Strike Global Oﬀensive teams at the time. The dataset
contains 4324 matches.
This dataset is explored and important factors that aﬀect the veto process of a
team are investigated. Time, roster changes, match type and opponents ranks
are found to have an eﬀect on the veto process. An interactive visualisations
is created that allows users to ﬁlter on these important features and experience
how the veto pattern changes.
To build the second data set for execute investigation, this visualisation is used
to ﬁnd a team and a time period that can be used for the second part of the
project. The interactive visualisation is a novel approach to analysing the veto-
process that strongly decreases the time that is needed to analyse a team com-
pared to the existing method which involves browsing HLTV.org and moving
back and forth between diﬀerent match pages.
Astralis is chosen as the team to analyse strategies on. This is based on the
interactive visualisation, which shows that their veto pattern has not changed
dramatically during 2018. On top of this their roster has not undergone changes
==PAGE:158==
144
Conclusion
during the last year. To make the problem of identifying team strategies more
tangible it is chosen to investigate one map. The map is chosen to be Inferno
because it is the Astralis most played map during 2018. Furthermore a small
time period is picked that feature three large back-to-back tournaments, limit-
ing how much their strategies can evolve.
As such, the second data set consists of all the matches that Astralis has played
on Inferno from this time period. Because all of the match data is encrypted and
compressed into binary ﬁles, a visualisation tool that was previously prototyped
is extended and improved such that it is possible to validate the data that is
being parsed from the matches. It is reworked such that the underlying engine
works in the same manner as Counter Strike so that it can be used to replay
matches as well.
Then a set of criteria is formed based on domain knowledge of the game that
is used to ﬁlter the interesting rounds from the uninteresting rounds. The ex-
tracted match data for the interesting rounds then forms the basis for an exten-
sive and exhaustive feature engineering process.
Firstly the features from an existing paper are recreated. Then these are used
together with K-means as a starting point. Because this approach did not yield
any useful results, other features are explored. The most promising features
have been found to be the movement of the players and the utility that they
use. This has been found out by inspecting the rounds manually. Several dif-
ferent methods are used to compare the rounds based on these features.
The movement is compared with Dynamic Time Warping focusing on the tem-
poral aspect. The grenades have been used together with the in-game zones
focusing on the spatial aspect. The grenades and the movement are combined
into images that are converted to keypoint and image patch descriptors.
The methods have been evaluated on 4 diﬀerent metrics. Adjusted Rand Index
where the clustering is compared to a manual labeling of the rounds, silhouette
score, separation of area and well-deﬁned trends. The two latter scores being
custom scores that have been made speciﬁcally for this project. Calculating
the keypoints through SURF and clustering, the resulting image histograms has
yielded the best results. Through inspection of the gap statistic and silhouette
score it has been found that using the keypoints instead of the raw coordinates
creates better data for clustering.
The most eﬀective approach here has been to use the snapshot ﬁltering. When
certain simple criteria have been met the round is considered important and
saved for further inspection. The subsequent clustering has not managed to
separate the remaining rounds into clusters that can be categorized as diﬀerent
strategies.

Introduction
The Counter-Strike team North has expressed that they would like data-analysis features for Counter-
Strike matches. There exists tools for automated analysis of matches, but they are somewhat lacking.
This has given us the opportunity to create a tool that can make automated analysis of Counter-Strike
matches with a set of features critical to the strategic mission of North.
This opened up the requirements for the minimum viable product. Our application should be able to.
• Preprocess data from GOTV demos and store them in persistent storage.
• Create an application containing a set of features
– Display preprocessed data from Counter-Strike demos.
– Provide a heat-map of movement, kills and grenades, which can be ﬁltered based on type
of round, selected round or speciﬁc players.
– Provide summarizing statistics of games, including kills, deaths, assists etc.
– Display data regarding how teams VETOed in tournaments.
– Filter matches based on participating teams and time of the match.
Throughout this project we will document our development on a functioning prototype which can
showcase a limited set of features of this project. We will also reﬂect on lessons learned regarding
future possibilities of features and projects.
The structure of the report is as follows. First we will introduce in simple terms the game of Counter-
Strike. We will then discuss multiple design considerations. This includes choice of application type
and architectures, business analysis and data storage. A walk-through of the main designs and im-
plementations are given afterwards. We conclude with a reﬂection on the ﬁnal solution with missing
functionality and future work, as well as lessons learned from the problems that was faced, mainly in
the data parser and database.

The Counter-Strike Global Oﬀensive domain
Before diving into details with our project, we will describe Counter-Strike in brief terms.
In competitive Counter-Strike (CS), the only type of map is ”bomb defusal”, and we will only consider
this mode for the entire report.
2.1
Game overview
There are two teams in CS, terrorists (T) and counter-terrorists (CT), and the size of a team is 5
players. After half a map, the teams are switched around.
The playﬁeld of a single Counter-Strike match is a ”map”, which has four speciﬁc zones: The T spawn,
the CT spawn, A site and B site as well as a large layout connecting diﬀerent areas. The spawn zones
are where the T and CT teams spawns in the beginning of each round. As such, a single match can
be seen as multiple rounds, where the map resets between each round.
Each player on a team controls a character. This character is seen in ﬁrst person perspective and can
run around. A character can equip diﬀerent items including weapons to ﬁght the opposing players.
3
==PAGE:195==
In each round, the T team starts with a bomb and their goal is to either kill all CT’s or plant the
bomb and defend it until it explodes. The T team wins if the bomb explodes, even if they are already
dead. The goal of the CT’s is to prevent the T’s from planting the bomb by either killing them, or
preventing them from planting it until the time limit of the round is over. If the T’s should successfully
plant the bomb, CT’s can defuse it (if they are not killed by a T in the process), and if they succeed
in doing so, they win the round - even if there are still T’s alive.
There are a total of up to 30 rounds played on a map. After 15 rounds, the T’s become CT’s and CT’s
becomes T’s. If a team wins 16 rounds, they win the map. If it is a draw (15-15), it usually goes into
overtime. Overtime consists of 6 rounds with increased starting money, and if a team wins 4 of these
rounds, they win the game. If it is a draw again, overtime is repeated until a winner is found.
2.2
Economy
An important aspect of Counter-Strike is economy. In the beginning of the game, each team starts
with a small sum of money. Whenever a player kills an opponent or their team wins a round, they
gain money. The loosing team also gains money, but not nearly as much as the winning team. Money
is kept between rounds.
Money can be used for grenades, weapons and armor, which all gives an advantage. Keeping your
economy good enough allowing you to buy good gear is essential to winning the game.
2.3
Matches
A match in Counter-Strike may contain a collection of ’maps’, in which two teams plays against each
other. These can range from 1 to 7 (usually best of 1 or 3), where the team who wins the majority of
these maps wins the match. Each map has its own layout of areas.
In order to determine which maps should be played, a veto-vote is initialized. Here teams pick or
remove maps from a ﬁxed pool, to determine which maps to play in a fair manner. The choice of
map can make a diﬀerence, as some teams may be better at some maps, or may have prepared special
tactics for speciﬁc maps.
2.4
Tournaments
A tournament in Counter-Strike works much like other sports. Teams are competing against each
other, which ends out in a ﬁnal, where the winning team will receive both fame and money. Winning
these are critical.
2.5
Teams
A Counter-Strike team consists of 5 players and a trainer. These teams usually prepare tactics, analyze
their opponents and train their teamwork and motor skills with each other.
2.6
.dem ﬁle format
In order to replay Counter-Strike matches .dem ﬁles might be used. These ﬁles contains the data of
an entire match, and can be replayed in a build-in player inside the Counter-Strike application.

3.5
Persistent storage
As we want to present data from matches, we need a way to retrieve the data that should be displayed.
One way could be to extract the data from the .dem ﬁles directly when requested by a user, but this
could be a slow process. In order to make the user-interaction as smooth as possible, we want to
pre-compute much of the data that has to be shown. This will also help with data analysis tasks in the
future. Obviously memory is too expensive and non-persistent for collected data, so we want to store
it in some way on a disk. We can either use a ﬁle system or database, and as a database can provide
nice features in terms of indexing, integrity of data, authentication and connectivity we choose that.
In general there are relational and document databases, also known as ”SQL” or ”NoSQL”. In a rela-
tional database, data are indexed in tables, where each element should be easily accessible. However,
before storing data, the exact structure of your data has to be determined in order for you to be able to
design you database. A document database, also sometimes called a ”key-value” store is more eﬃcient
in development, as it can be seen as a nested json. However, this makes indexing of elements nested
in the json non-trivial.
As we have team members who are already familiar with SQL databases, and we want our data to be
indexable by multiple means, we have decided to go with SQL. We choose MySQL as it is free and
widely used in the industry.
3.6
What should we store
As .dem ﬁles range from 200mb to 500mb in general, we have to consider which data we want and
need to store.
Our goal is to minimize the amount of processing needed to display the data in our application.
As we have identiﬁed the primary things we want to display are player positions, kills (alongside
positions), veto choices and map-statistics.
The matches gathered from hltv.org are 128-tick, meaning that all there are stored events up to 128
times per second. Regarding positions, this data is more than suﬃcient.
In order to minimize the size of our data, we have decided that we do not have need for positions of
players on every tick, but rather have positions of players four times per second. This gives a high
enough resolution for movement to be smooth with interpolation, and low enough not to cause too
much disk, traﬃc and memory usage.
Regarding veto choices and map statistics, most of this are only needed once per match or played
map, or possibly a couple of times per round. As such, the space will only require a fraction of that of
positions.

For the tools to parse .dem ﬁles and extract the needed information, there are few limitations, as it
should just be able to run on a Linux machine. We have to be aware of the license of the tools, making
it legal for us to use them for commercial use. If at all possible, we want to implement our own working
solution. The main criteria should be adaptability, as new data is surely needed in future extensions.

4.1.2
Demo parser
In our ﬁnal design, we ended up using two parsers for the demos. The demoﬁles are a ”recording” of all
the information that has been sent between the players game client and the host. This information is
sent as messages. These have been serialized with Googles protocol buﬀers. Protocol buﬀers is a way
to serialize structured data that works independent of any programming language. Protocol buﬀers
can be used to create protocol buﬀer messages which can be used to structure the data that one wishes
to serialize. These messages are written in a .proto ﬁle that can be used both for serialization and
deserialization. In this serialization diﬀerent messages encoded as key-value pairs. Where the key is a
number that is assigned in the corresponding .proto ﬁle. In the serialization the key encodes both its
key and the type of the value which can be used to ﬁnd the length of its value. The key is serialized
using a binary format that uses varint to serialize integers. This means that you only use as many
bytes as needed to represent an integer, using a minimum of one byte. When the integer is being
parsed the parser will look for the most signiﬁcant bit one byte at the time until it ﬁnds one that is
0. the remaining 7 bits are used to store the twos compliment representation of an integer. Once the
ﬁnal number of bytes has been found, all the most signiﬁcant bits are removed and the order of the
bytes is reversed in order to get the actual integer value. This allows for the binary ﬁles to be smaller
than normal binary ﬁles, which makes them well suited for message that are send across a network.
The diﬀerent types then need to be handled diﬀerently by the parser.
Python parser
Throughout the project a python parser for the demo ﬁles has been made. It was deemed ﬁt to create
the parser in python given the strong support form the statistics and machine learning communities as
the idea is to utilize the parsed data for those types of analysis. Python is a language that is weakly
typed which is not ideal for the handling of binary data, because of this large parts of the parser was
ported to cython, which is a python library that compiles to C. This made it possible to make python
strongly typed and gave the added bonus of improved computational time when parsing ﬁles. Given
the large task of implementing a csgo demo parser it was constructed in an agile fashion. Such that

Database
For the most optimal solution, we should have a completely normalized database where everything we
need to query is indexed properly. We designed our solution for this in beginning, allowing us to do
quick queries for anything. This would prove especially useful for future work in a large application.
However, already with data just for our prototype, the database scheme got very complex and hard to
maintain and update. Due to time constraints and limited knowledge in advanced database design, we
have chosen to go with a rather simple design for out solution combining commonly searched values
with stored jsons. This way, we will get the simplicity and ease-of-use of a document-db alongside
eﬀective queries without getting redundant data. The jsons are also small enough (sub 20 mb) at a
suﬃcient resolution, making it possible for the website to still operate in timely manner.
We have decided to focus on fast and simple queries, and by doing this we do not have a fully normalized
database. We can expand on this in the future, as the needed queries grows.

5
Reﬂection
5.1
Final results
Our ﬁnal result did not have all the functionalities of the MVP, but most of them.
We have created a web application that are able to display pre-processed round data from Counter-
Strike matches, display summarizing statistics of games and display veto data. In the veto data we
were able to ﬁlter them based on participating teams, type of match and date of the match.
However, our web application does not provide a heat map of movement, kills and grenades, as was
initially a goal.
Our web application is almost ready to be hosted, but it will require some work regarding dependencies
16
==PAGE:208==
and certiﬁcates, alongside handling credentials for the database in a more secure way. We also need
to populate our database with more data, in order to make it more useful.
5.2
Time limit exceeded
Initially we hoped for a more expanded application than the one we have as our ﬁnal result.
This is due to a multitude of challenges we faced.
We had to extend our deadline due to the fact that everything took longer than expected. In particular
we spend additional time on React, the python parser and database, as we either underestimated the
diﬃculty of learning new material or creating an eﬃcient feature-rich database.
5.3
Lessons learned
5.3.1
Research existing solutions better
In the beginning of the project when we looked into existing parsers that could be used to process
the demos, we discovered that these solutions had a hard time ﬁnding all the players in the match.
Because most of them are not maintained, they have started to not work because the demo format
has changed in such a way that the player connected event at the beginning of the match has been
removed, and because of this, the existing parsers cannot ﬁnd the player names, simply because most
of them never got beyond the point of parsing the predeﬁned messages.
Creating a parser could have been the entire scope of the project in itself.
The sheer amount of
complexity combined with the diﬀerent kinds of functionality that needs to be supported before a
parser can actually be used has come as a surprise. Even though the data is serialized with Google’s
Protocol Buﬀers, it takes more than just unpacking the data to create a working parser. Data also
needs to be formatted, ordered, and emitted from the parser. This task has been made more even
more diﬃcult because of the way that Valve has used the Protocol Buﬀers.
Protocol Buﬀers has been used to serialize some of the more general messages, but not the entities nor
the diﬀerent string tables which actually hold all the essential information that is needed to string all
the data from the small messages together. This kind of ”lazy” use has made the task seem simpler
than it has been. We ended up needing to implement all the code, including bitwise reading and
manipulation, that handles these string tables and entities to create a proper parser. In the end we
made it to the point where we can parse the string table with the user information in, but we still
need the entities before we can start collecting positional data from the small messages.
This part ended up taking a lot more attention than expected to implement properly. As a result of
this we learned that, given the project scope, we should either have changed it to focus exclusively on
the parser or spent more time on looking into existing solutions that would process the data for us.
5.3.2
Complexity of database design
While a relational database should be fully normalized, it requires full design and requirements of said
database, which is overwhelming for a small project. As such, the large design was scrapped and a
simpler scheme adopted, which may have to be extended with new features.
5.3.3
General web development for inexperienced developers
Using a popular web framework might not have been the best solution, when our primary focus was to
make a functional webpage. It turned out that while frameworks do make things more structured, they
17
==PAGE:209==
often focus on functionality and structure for larger applications. As such, multiple requirements were
needed to get React running well, which took time. There were also some problems with integration
of for example webgl. Alternatively, we could have used default Html and Javascript to display our
processed data.
5.4
Future work
Throughout the report we have presented some places where the features are lacking. Fortunately for
us, we can continue our work.
We expect to continue our work on the project, partly through spare time and partly through part
of the groups master thesis. This allows us to ﬁx the pitfalls we currently have in our application,
alongside implementing new and cool features.
5.4.1
Optimize speed and reliability
It would be beneﬁcial to us and our users if our service are reliable and fast. This makes the user
experience nice.
A lot of the reliability is regarding the integrity of the data in the database. If this data is somehow
wrong, we are presenting our customers with false data or not presenting anything at all.
Automate parsing: If we fully automate the parsing, we can populate the database faster.
Create proper failsafe parsing: Currently our db-pusher.py will break under most errors. We
want to make sure that it only stores correct information, and only once.
We want to introduce
mechanisms to make sure that the data cannot fail and cause artifacts or missing data in the database.
Index as needed: Future features might result in currently unknown queries, which means that we
will have to either accept slow query times or create optimized queries. Most of this problem can be
resolved by normalizing the database.
Identify parse edge cases: Sometimes the parsing might fail. We have to identify most scenarios
where this can happen in order ensure minimum amount of failures, as a failure results in data that
should exist, not existing.
Implement automated testing: Currently our solution has no tests. This makes it hard to identify
if we break something on changes. Automatic tests should be included in order to be able to quicker
identify system breakage.
Update the database continuously: Create running jobs for updating the data in the database,
to make sure we have the newest data from hltv.org.
Optimize demo-viewer: Currently the demo-viewer is loading in a big json in the beginning, before
it can play anything. We could optimize this, by loading smaller chunks, minimizing the waiting time
before the demo is playable.
5.4.2
Making the app publish-able
In order to make our application publishable, we still have some work to do.
Implement authentication: This allows teams to upload demos only available to them, and us to
limit access to paying customers.
18
==PAGE:210==
Make it possible for users to upload .dem ﬁles: This is needed in order to make it possible to
parse personal demos.
Add payment options: If we want income from user subscriptions, we need to ﬁgure out a way to
receive money. We can do this externally from our application, but we still need to be able to ﬂag
users as paying.
Enhance security: Before publishing we should enhance our security by scanning our service for
possible vulnerability, and make sure that all authenticated calls are actually authenticated. We also
need to make sure that in case of a breach, we are able to detect and contain it.
5.4.3
Adding data presentation features
Right now our data presentation is a bit limited, and we want to improve on this.
Create heatmap: A heatmap of events and movement, as we described in our MVP.
Plot grenades: Plot the trajectories of grenades.
Plot z-axis: Currently we only plot two dimensions in our player, and we want it to be possible to
identify which ﬂoor players are on, on multi-level maps.
Show events on playback: In our map playback, we also want to plot special events such as kills,
deaths, grenades etc.
Demo-viewer: While the demo-viewer does visualize the movement of the players it is obvious that
the presentation needs some work. A graphical user interface that can be layed out on top of the
movement needs to be added. Small things such as team names, round number, elapsed time and
current standing, as well as player names, and current equipment needs to be added to make the
presentation more whole.
Furthermore a clever rendering technique should be developed for visualizing the diﬀerent in height of
the diﬀerent players. This is because some of the maps do have sections that are on top of each other.
This is not distinguishable in the current version of the player. Functionality for visualizing the bomb
once it has been planted as well as displaying grenades is also lacking. This is also important to add
because it often aﬀects the behaviour of the players, and without that, one might misinterpret why
the players are acting in a speciﬁc way.
6
Conclusion
We implemented basic functionalities, allowing us to process data from Counter-Strike matches. These
functionalities did not live up to our MVP, but we were able to showcase the possibilities. We were able
to visualize movement and position, with rounds discretized by the parser. We created a veto-view
where we aggregated data from multiple vetoes, allowing us to ﬁlter the displayed data.
While we strived for a greater application with a much sooner deadline, we were not able to do this
due to misjudgement of the challenges.
However, this lays a great foundation of what can be an excellent application, and a magniﬁcent master
thesis.
