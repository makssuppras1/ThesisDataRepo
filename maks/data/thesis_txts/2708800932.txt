Abstract
In this Master Thesis we propose an FPGA algorithm implementation that runs a compu-
tation in order to determine the quantum bit state of a single triplet electron spin qubit.
The computation is the transformation of the principal component analysis, and it is
implemented on both Intel Stratix 10 MX 2100 FPGA and Xilinx Virtex-7 VC707 FPGA.
The proposed algorithm is parallelized in order to be able to determine the quantum state
for multiple quantum bits at the same time. We achieve a maximum FPGA clock speed
Fmax of 208 MHz for the Intel Stratix 10 MX 2100, and we demonstrate it at a clock speed
of 156.25 MHz using Xilinx Virtex-7 VC707. The proposed FPGA algorithm has a latency
of 12 clock cycles.
For a system with three quantum bits the algorithm requires 5851.74 mW of power and
0.34 % of ALMs, 0.82% of M20K RAM blocks and 0.91% of DSP blocks resources when
measured on the Intel Stratix FPGA.

Chapter 1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background and motivation
The development of quantum computers is expected to oﬀer an abundance of computational
power in the future.
With more computational power available, the development of
new medicines and better materials is expected to be accelerated. Additionally, other
computation-heavy activities such as weather forecasting and modelling of climate change
can be optimized in terms of time, power consumption and accuracy. At the time of
writing, the number of quantum bits implemented in a single quantum computer is counted
by the tens to hundreds[7, 8, 9]. The required number of quantum bits, in order to solve
the practical computational problems of the modern world, is in the millions[10], thus
keeping scientists and engineers in work for the next years to come. This thesis focuses on
a small part of this endeavour: improving the readout of such a quantum computer, for a
speciﬁc kind of quantum bit.
1.1.1
Quantum bit
A quantum bit (qubit) is the building block that holds the smallest unit of information in
a quantum processor, similar to a bit in a traditional computer.
A fundamental property of a qubit is that it loses its information over time. The time a
qubit is able to retain its information, is called the coherence time, after which we say that
the qubit decoheres. After this coherence time, it cannot be known what state the qubit
was in, and so the information is no longer valid.
For a quantum algorithm to make any sense, it has to work on valid information that
has not decohered, which leads to a strict limitation on the time it takes to execute the
quantum algorithm.
The information in a qubit is also at risk of becoming invalid for other reasons. In order
to address the issue of false information, a lot of research is put into designing an eﬃcient
quantum error correction (QEC) scheme[11, 12]. To implement QEC, each qubit has to be
accompanied by a number of additional qubits that we call QEC qubits, whose purpose is
solely to tell the QEC about the validity of the original qubit.
The QEC scheme can now perform qubit readout of the QEC qubits while leaving the
original qubit untouched, which is an underlying criterion. The QEC still has to be
performed within the decoherence time, from which it follows that the QEC qubit readout

has to be carried out fast, leaving time to also correct the original qubit in case it is faulty.
Early implementations of QEC schemes on small scale quantum processors have recently
been demonstrated[13, 14]. QEC is currently a necessity for the development of quantum
computation in practice, and this motivates the quest of this Master Thesis.
In this thesis, I will consider measurement data from singlet-triplet electron spin qubits[15].
This type of qubit is implemented using the spins of electrons, localized in a semiconductor
such as gallium-arsenide (GaAs). Figure 1.1 shows the design of a singlet-triplet electron
spin qubit made by Federico Fedele as part of his PhD project at the Center for Quantum
Devices, University of Copenhagen, which was the basis of measurements taken for this
thesis[1, 16].
The picture in a) is a top view of the device, which is embedding four double quantum dots,
Q1 through Q4. For each double quantum dot, there is a sensor quantum dot, S1 through
S4. The signal from these sensors, read out by radiofrequency reﬂectometry methods, is
used in this thesis to classify the qubits (Q1-Q4) formed in the double quantum dots.
The 3D model in b) shows the cross section of the same device. The stack has been
designed in such way, that a two-dimensional electron gas, 2DEG, is formed between the
GaAs substrate and the aluminum gallium-arsenide, 67 nm below the surface. A 2DEG is
a sheet of very many free electrons roaming around.

By applying a voltage potential on the gates, that is seen in a), an electrostatic ﬁeld is
penetrating down through the stack. Electrons in the 2DEG are being pushed by the
electrostatic ﬁeld, and so it is possible to form islands that are depleted from electrons
by controlling the gates at the surface. It is further possible to open or close channels
where electrons can ﬂow. This allows to trap single electrons in the quantum dots by ﬁrst
depleting the quantum dot using the plunger gate, and afterwards reducing the potential
slightly.
The diagram in ﬁgure 1.1 c) is a zoom of one double quantum dot and one sensor dot.
On top of the zoom is a circuit diagram indicating how the sensor dot is modelled by a
variable resistance RS1 in parallel with the parasitic capacitance Cp1. With the lumped

inductor L1, the three components form a resonator circuit, which is being energized from
the signal applied to VRF .
Pane d) shows the the transmission coeﬃcient, S21, as a function of frequency. Port 1 is
connected to the TX line of the dilution refrigerator, which is sketched in ﬁgure 1.2, while
port 2 is connected to the RX line of the dilution refrigerator. Looking at the solid line, it
shows 4 resonance frequencies, one for each of the 4 sensor dots. They are intentionally
placed at 4 diﬀerent frequencies by using diﬀerent inductance values. We can then probe
the reﬂection of each of the 4 resonator circuits, and the measurement technique is therefore
referred to as reﬂectometry. The parasitic capacitance Cp1 and inductance L1 govern the
center frequency of the resonator circuit, while the varying resistance RS1 of the sensor
dot inﬂuences the Q factor of the resonator circuit and therefor also its bandwidth.
The connected line to VRF has characteristic impedance Z0, whereas the RLC circuit
of the sensor dot has impedance ZRLC1. The sensor dot impedance is varying together
with the resistance of S1, RS1. The impedance mismatch between Z0 and ZRLC1 re-
sults in parts of an incoming RF burst being reﬂected back. The reﬂected signal can
be evaluated as a DC signal, Vref, at baseband by employing homodyne downconversion[17].
A qubit of this architecture is mainly operated in two quantum states, namely singlet and
triplet, which are deﬁned by the spin of 2 electrons conﬁned in a tunnel coupled double
quantum dot [1]. In the singlet state, the two electrons are having the opposite spin of
one another, which allows them to be localized in one quantum dot. For the triplet state,
the two electrons are having identical spin. The triplet state cannot exist inside a single
quantum dot due to the Pauli exclusion principle, thus the triplet state occupies two
quantum dots. The singlet and triplet state therefore constitute a 0 and 1 state for the
quantum bit. Since the adjacent sensor dot (connected to our RF reﬂectometry circuit) can
sense whether (a) one dot is doubly occupied (singlet) or (b) both dots are singly occupied
(triplet), we are able to perform qubit readout and distinguish between the singlet and
triplet qubit states.

1.2
State of the art
With continuously improving material quality, fabrication technique and control capabilities,
longer coherence times (T2) are being demonstrated for semiconductor spin qubits each
year. Speciﬁcally, in the last 10 years, coherence times beyond the microsecond timescale
have been shown repeatedly [18]. Spin qubit readout within this timescale has also been
established in proof of principle experiments[17], making use of either RF quantum point
contacts (RF-QPC) or RF single quantum dots (RF-SQD) for readout. The RF sensor
signals are typically down converted and read out using homodyne sensing technique.
Reference [1] shows the state of the art implementation of homodyne circuits for four qubit
simultaneous readout, while also revealing the challenges for scaling up to many qubits.
In recent years, there has been an increasing interest in utilizing the speed from FPGAs
to implement control systems for quantum devices [19, 20]. One reason for this, which is
also a motivation for this thesis, is that the latency in communication between the card
that digitizes the homodyne signal, the statistical analysis performed on the computer,
and the waveform generator performing a new operation on the qubit based on this result,
is a bottleneck for qubit operation. This is also seen in industry, where state of the art
quantum hardware from companies like Quantum Machines are implementing their control
hardware using FPGA[21].
A new method has been proposed, which discards the entire downconversion part, and
employs a statistical method directly on the raw reﬂected signal. The statistical method is
called Principal Component Analysis, and it has been demonstrated to work in the Master
Thesis by Bjørnholt, C., 2022[4].
It is the purpose of this Master Thesis project to implement quantum bit classiﬁcation
using Principal Component Analysis transformation. In this thesis we propose to use an
FPGA for the live transformation, which will lead to a fast readout, which again can
become a step towards more reliable quantum computation.
1.3
Limitations
The purpose of this thesis is to demonstrate how a quantum bit readout can be carried
out using the Principal Component Analysis. An FPGA implementation of the quantum
bit state classiﬁcation algorithm is presented along with an introduction to the origin of
the data.
The introduction chapter of this thesis includes a very brief introduction to what a quantum
bit is, and what is meant by a quantum bit readout. Though it should be stated that
this Master Thesis is not meant to be on quantum physics, and so this ﬁeld will not be
addressed any further.

2.1
Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a method that can be used to analyse the cluster-
ing of data. PCA performs dimensionality reduction of the original data. It means that
by applying PCA, the original data can be represented or estimated using less amount of
data. PCA results in a set of vectors called the principal components −→
wf, where f is an
index of the principal component and this notation is used throughout this work.
Each of the principal components are by deﬁnition perpendicular to each other. The prin-
cipal components are generated so that the ﬁrst principal component represents the largest
percentage of variance of the original data, the second principal component represents the
second largest variance, and so forth.
Given the principal components, the data can be transformed by projecting it onto them
(see section 2.1.1). Each principal component accounts for a certain percentage of variance
of the original data. If just the ﬁrst principal component is applied, it might not represent
enough variance from the original data. On the other hand, the more applied principal
components, the more variance will be accounted for.
When PCA is applied to measurement data from a qubit system, the categories that the
data is clustered into are the diﬀerent quantum states of the qubit system. The number of
principal components for the PCA to ﬁnd, or the number of dimensions after applying the
dimensionality reduction should equal the number of qubits in the system.

Timing budget
Quantum error correction requires fast readout of the qubit to be able to react and thereby
perform the correction. The correction of the qubits has to be conducted within the
coherence time of the qubit, otherwise the information is lost. Nowadays, electron spin
qubits with coherence times of the order of 10-100 µs are regularly being reported, and
therefore it is desired to reach a readout time of ∼1µs[22, 23, 24, 25].
The full readout includes sending an RF burst towards the quantum device, digitizing the
reﬂected RF signal, transferring the measurement data to the FPGA, performing the PCA
transformation and ﬁnally outputting the determined state.

2.3
Field Programmable Gate Array
Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA), is an integrated circuit (IC) made up of multiple
layers[28, 29]. All the logic cells are distributed in one layer, but they are all disconnected
in the default conﬁguration. Another layer is ﬁlled with wires, that runs in the left-right
or top-bottom directions. The wires are all disconnected, but they form a grid, that has
the potential to interconnect any logic cell to anywhere else in the IC. Additionally, the IC
has layers of transistors, which act like conﬁgurable switches. The transistors are placed in
a grid, and give the ability to interconnect the input- and output ports of the logic cells
to the wires in the other layer. The transistors have ﬂoating gates and after it has been
conﬁgured into one state, it remains in the same state as long as the IC is powered up.
An FPGA IC is often integrated with, or placed in connection with, a non-volatile memory
that holds the conﬁguration ﬁle of the FPGA. At power up, the binary ﬁle can be loaded
onto the FPGA and sets all transistors accordingly. The non-volatile memory can then be
reprogrammed to hold a new binary ﬁle, when the FPGA is desired to have a new behaviour.
When the IC is powered up, all the connected logic cells in the IC are also powered up and
can work simultaneously. This way, data can be proccessed in parallel in an FPGA.
It is the length of the interconnecting wires that determines propagation times. The wires
are being routed and placed by the vendors software suite as part of the implementation
process. After this step, the software can conduct an analysis to ﬁnd the longest signal
path in the implemented design. This will inﬂuence the maximum clock speed with which
we can run the FPGA.
There are other similar, customizable IC technologies that provide diﬀerent advantages
and disadvantages in comparison to the FPGA. FPGA is especially advantageous for proof
of concept, fast to market application and applications with low number of devices[28]. In
terms of performance, FPGAs allow to process data in parallel, which is a crucial beneﬁt
for fast data processing and communication. In a proof of concept application, FPGA
beneﬁts from its nature of being re-programmable, which makes it possible to change,
upgrade and add to the ﬁrmware. Since the physical IC is already ﬁnished in an FPGA, it

3.2
Design
The ﬁrmware is written in the hardware description language VHDL version 2008[31]. In
order to compile the ﬁrmware for the Intel Stratix 10 I use the software suite Intel Quartus
21.3[32] and the Xilinx Vivado 2018.3[33]. The proposed ﬁrmware is attached in appendix
A.1.
In the design of the FPGA algorithm, we assume that our incoming data is arriving
sequentially. This means that the data are not available all at once, but at the rate it can
be delivered from the digitizer which is acquiring the data. On the other hand, the principal
components −→
wf are available already before the beginning of the computation. The same
applies to the mean value uf, since they are generated from the principal component
analysis. Both are stored in memories available in the FPGA.
In order to have a portable design, I tried to use as few Intellectual Property (IP) cores as
possible, as they are vendor speciﬁc. From the resource optimization perspective, this is in
general not an issue, as we are aware that the compiler in the vendor’s software suite will
optimize the design by making the best use of the available resources, including e.g Digital
Signal Processors (DSPs) used for multiplications, of the speciﬁc FPGA.

3.2.4
Computational block
One block is designed to process the data as described with the mathematical operation in
formula 3.1 and output the classiﬁed qubit state based on the result.
The working principle of the computational block is to calculate preliminary dot products
at the same rate, as soon as the input data is fed into it. In the next stage of the computa-
tional block, the preliminary dot products will be summed up cumulatively, to form the
ﬁnal dot product after the last data has been processed in the previous stage.
In the following stage, the mean value is subtracted from the cumulative sum.
The computational block can in principle be reinstantiated as many times as there are
qubits in the system, in order to determine the qubit state of all of the qubits in parallel.
As described in section 2.1, the PCA results in a unique principal component for each of the
dimensions. For this reason, each of the computational blocks will have its own principal
component to transform the input data. When a computational block is instantiated, it
is given an ID, that corresponds to which qubit it is classifying. The ID is important for
the computational block in order for it to point to certain RAM blocks, that holds the
correct principal components. The number of computational blocks to be initialized and
implemented in the FPGA is governed by a constant, that can be set accordingly to the
number of qubits in the system.

The addition is implemented by ﬁrst casting the std_logic_vectors into sﬁxed data type,
then the two operands are added together using the addition operator from the ﬁxed_pkg

The adder is implemented in a concurrent fashion and so adds 0 clock cycles of latency.
The register component is storing the input value on the rising edge of the clock and is
cleared on an active reset signal.
Accumulation is happening by connecting the output of the adder component to the input
of the register component, and then the output of the register component to the second
input of the adder component. The input to the accumulator component is connected to
the ﬁrst input of the adder component, then the input value on the accumulator component
is being summed with the result from the last summation. The result of the accumulator
is connected to the output of the register component, thus the result is available 1 clock
cycle after the input signal is available in the input of the accumulator.
The cumulative sum leads to a 1 clock cycle latency of the FPGA algorithm.

3.2.4.4
Quantum bit state classiﬁcation
The input of this block is the PCA score resulting from the subtraction of the mean value.
This value can either be greater or smaller than zero, as described in equation 2.4. This
block is implemented by only evaluating the sign bit of the input signal. If the sign bit is
zero, the input signal is a positive number, whereas if the sign bit is one, the input signal is
a negative number. Based on this evaluation, the classiﬁcation is one for positive number
(triplet state) and zero for negative number (singlet state).

3.4
IP Cores
The algorithm was ﬁrst developed for the Intel Stratix FPGA, and afterwards ported to
the Xilinx Virtex-7 FPGA. In the development process, the algorithm was designed for
portability. Because IP cores are vendor speciﬁc, we limited the usage of IP cores.
In the proposed design of the algorithm, IP cores are used for two main purposes:
• Random Access Memory, which is used for internal storage of the PC vectors wf.
RAM is also used to store mean values uf even though it is only used to store a
single value for each dimension. RAM is used for storage of the mean values because
the mean values originate from the same place as the PC vectors, thus the simplest
way to handle them is to load them in the exact same way. Finally, RAM is also
used in the injector to store real measurement data coming from older measurements.
In the Intel Stratix implementation, we use the 1-PORT Intel FPGA IP[38] and for
the Xilinx Virtex-7 application we use the Block Memory[39, 40].
All the IP cores used for memory in the algorithm, are setup through VHDL libraries
that are speciﬁc for the vendors. Then this wrapper ﬁle is included into the algorithm
where it is needed. This way, a constant, boolean variable which is set in common
package can be controlling whether the algorithm should be compiled to the Intel
Stratix FPGA or to the Xilinx Virtex-7 FPGA.
The wrapping VHDL ﬁles that includes the IP cores have a ﬁlename ending with
either "_intel.vhd" or "_xilinx.vhd".
• Probing results signals and sourcing input signals. These IP cores allow the user, via
the vendor’s software suite, to monitor and send signals into the FPGA fabric live as
the FPGA is on an running the algorithm.

In this chapter we present the key ﬁndings from this project. We begin with presenting a
simulation of the FPGA algorithm, followed by a demonstration of the FPGA algorithm
implemented in hardware. We verify that the computation is conducted at the expected

high speed ADC and DAC build into the FPGA IC. The implementation of the Xilinx

4.3
Timing
4.3.1
Algorithm latency Nlatency
Algorithm latency Nlatency is the number of clock cycles from the last measurement data
arrives at the computation block until this measurement data is taken into account in the
qubit state classiﬁcation.
All the stages in the computational block together result in a 12 clock cycle latency. 3
out of the 12 clock cycles origin from the delay block which delays the input data to the
computational block by 3 clock cycles. This is necessary as it takes 2 clock cycles to read
out the values from the RAM and the remaining 1 clock cycle is there because a clock
signal is used to activate the readout from the RAM. 7 clock cycle latency origin from
the calculation of the dot product. One clock cycle latency origins from the accumulation
block and lastly, the comparator is allowed one clock cycle latency to classify the PCA
score into either singlet or triplet state.

OUTLOOK
5.1
Potential impact
This work was speciﬁc to singlet-triplet electron spin qubits, but the PCA-based developed
FPGA classiﬁcation algorithm could be applied to any qubit architectures that are currently
using RF-based quantum state readout. Qubits of some of these architectures have longer
coherence time, which in turn loosen the very strict timing requirements that we have seen
when working on the singlet-triplet electron spin qubit [13, 23, 47, 48].
The ability to read out the qubit state within the coherence time, or at 1 µs in our case, is
a crucial necessity in order to perform conditional quantum gate operations. Conditional
quantum gate operation is a certain reaction, based on the result from the ﬁrst readout,
and this is an essential functionality in several quantum algorithms such as quantum error
correction, QEC, and quantum teleportation[49, 50].
Quantum teleportation is promising for communication due to its ability of transferring
information using a channel that, by the laws of physics, are secured against eavesdropping.
Quantum teleportation depends on conditional gate operations which again rely on the
outcome of qubit readout[49, 50].
In both applications, QEC and quantum teleportation, the qubit state classiﬁcation can
be implemented using principal component analysis as proposed in this work.
In the current hardware setup it is not possible to retrieve the reﬂectometry measurements
in real time to the FPGA. This issue can be addressed by using another digitizer, or even
better by using an FPGA with an analog-to-digital converter core embedded, which is
an ongoing development that began a few years ago[51, 52]. This project is planned to
be continued on one such device called Xilinx RFSoC, which leads to the next section
describing future work.
5.2
Future work
Since we are limiting ourselves to not use DSPs so that we have a portable design, there
is not an option to pipeline the multiplication operation. It is currently the dot product
block that is limiting the clock speed F_max. This creates a bottleneck related to the
design of the ﬁrmware and solving it could result with a faster processing.
42
==PAGE:53==
Chapter 5. OUTLOOK
43
The Xilinx RFSoC FPGA could also result with faster processing as it allows to have 8
incoming samples at each clock cycle, instead of just 4 as in the case of this study. With
this technology, double the amount of samples could be processed within the computation
time. Xilinx RFSoC was used to demonstrate qubit readout and control in an earlier study
[19], although it should be noted that the mentioned study does not include any PCA
implementation on board. In light of the given developments, our study can be moved to
an RFSoC FPGA in the future and real live data acquired from the reﬂectometry can be
processed.
Furthermore, there is a micro controller unit on Xilinx RFSoC. This gives the opportunity
to implement PCA in a higher level programming language which is faster to implement.
Having the PCA on board removes the need for a computer in the system. This way, a
standalone system is achieved and it becomes simpler to run the PCA and update the
principal components for the qubit state classiﬁcation.
With the reported latency of the FPGA classiﬁcation algorithm of 76.8 ns, the latency
is not the bottleneck in achieving fast qubit readout. Instead, the main part of the work
is now to increase signal to noise ratio (SNR) and achieve a better contrast between the
quantum states within the coherence time.
The scientiﬁc research within quantum devices is moving towards more and more qubits
within a quantum processor. Our measurement data used in the presented work was taken
with just one qubit and then simulated to represent a three-qubit system. An obvious next
step, is to tune multiple qubits in a real system to prove the concept when operated using
real data. As demonstrated, the FPGA classiﬁcation algorithm is prepared to classify the
quantum state of many qubits in parallel by instantiating equally many computational
blocks. From the nature of the PCA analysis, the spread of the clusters, and thus the
quality of the quantum state classiﬁcation, decreases for each dimension added. This issue
could be addressed by adding multiple input channels, where each channel solely performs
the PCA analysis and qubit state classiﬁcation for only a subgroup of the total number of
qubits in the system, thereby maintaining a suﬃcient quality of the classiﬁcation.

Chapter 6
CONCLUSION
This thesis aimed at implementing quantum bit classiﬁcation by employing PCA
transformation. FPGA was used for the transformation in order to achieve a fast readout,
which could be helpful for conditional gate operations in quantum algorithms such as
Quantum Error Correction. We aimed to reach a latency of less than 200 ns for the FPGA
algorithm.
The data we used for the qubit state classiﬁcation is simulated data that represents
measurement data from a three qubit system. Using PCA, we extracted three principal
components where each component is related to which quantum state each corresponding
qubit is in.
We developed an FPGA ﬁrmware using VHDL that can do the classiﬁcation of a single
qubit in the Intel Stratix 10 MX 2100. We also designed a data injector as part of the
design to feed measurement data to the algorithm. Later, we made it possible to run the
ﬁrmware in parallel so that it can classify multiple qubits simultaneously. Finally, we
ported the ﬁrmware so that it is able to run on the Xilinx Virtex-7 VC707.
In the design of the ﬁrmware, we got a 12 clock cycle latency. In the hardware, it took 2.95
µs to process the qubit state classiﬁcation using 1801 samples as estimated. Running at a
FPGA clock speed of 156.25 MHz in the Xilinx Virtex-7 VC707, the 12 clock cycle latency
translates into 76.8 ns, well-within the 200 ns latency aimed at the start of the thesis.
The developed ﬁrmware is able to operate at a maximum FPGA clock speed of 208 MHz
when data from three qubits are processed in parallel in the Intel Stratix 10 MX 2100.
This used 0.34% of the ALMS, 0.82% of the M20K RAM blocks, 0.91% of the DSP blocks.
