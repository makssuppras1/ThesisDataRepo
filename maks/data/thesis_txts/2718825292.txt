Abstract
The Bayesian optimization, in short BO, method can be applied in various contexts. In
this thesis we describe the method step by step to give an understanding before applying
the method in different experiments. We describe the use of an autoencoder in order to
lower the dimensionality of the original data, which is important due to the inefficiency of
the BO method, when working with high dimensional data. We describe how a Gaussian
process can be applied to model the underlying function mapping the input data to their
corresponding results. This is followed by the definition of acquisition functions, which
are used for choosing the best new candidate solution. We apply theory in three separate
experiments.
The first experiment is working with artificial data and is given a simple true underlying
function. From this experiment we show how the algorithm progresses and succeeds in
finding the global optimum. All code for this experiment is found at https://github.com/
kofoed12/lambo_kofoed.
The second experiment applies the BO method on protein sequence data to find op-
timal sequence design maximizing for logarithm of partition coefficient, P, (logP) and
quantitative estimate of drug-likeness (QED) values.
From this experiment we show
the application of BO in a multiple objective optimization task. We wish to see the ef-
fects of lowering the latent dimensionality. When making this change we find that the
resulting hypervolume decreases significantly. From further modification we show that
by adding multiple sampling of batches while optimizing, we can get results following
the progression of the original solution. All code for this experiment is found at https:
//github.com/kofoed12/lambo_kofoed.
The third experiment uses the BO method to optimize the design of Mario levels. The goal
is to maximize the number of jumps performed by a pretrained agent for it to complete the
level. Secondly we wish to have a high probability of the agent completing the level. From
this we show how to apply constraints to the BO method, and the effects from this. Adding
the probability of completion as a model constraint, raised the completion rate of the best
candidate significantly but also lowered the number of jumps performed. Seeing that the
agent came with a lot of uncertainty due to its stochastic nature, we upped the number
of simulations for each iteration. This gave us results with more jumps performed while
getting a completion rate approximately equal to the experiment with modelled constraint.
All code for this experiment is found at https://github.com/kofoed12/minimal_VAE_on_
Mario_constrained.

Introduction
Bayesian optimization is a method used to find global optima of unknown black-box func-
tions. It uses probabilistic modelling and computations to arrive at solutions. It is espe-
cially useful for problems where the underlying black-box function is expensive or difficult
to evaluate for a given point. This can both be in the case of very computationally heavy
functions or for which an evaluation of the function corresponds to an expensive and time
consuming experiment.
In this project we look at optimization problems with both drug design and vision design
in video games. Drug design is a typical case where the Bayesian optimization method
is very useful since the processes of creating and testing different protein sequences are
very expensive, difficult and time consuming. This involves the creation of a protein with
a specific sequence in the laboratory and the following necessary experiments to gather
the efficiency metrics used for the model.
The method can be equally useful in other fields, where the key in solving the problem is
finding the global optima in as few iterations as possible to lower the number of neces-
sary black-box function evaluations. This could be in problems as hyperparameter tuning,
scheduling or other types of material design optimization[1].
We will use two prior solution methods as baseline results for our two separate problems
in drug design and vision design of video games respectively. More specifically the first
prior solution is a project in optimization of protein sequence design and the other is a
project wishing to optimize level generation for Mario the video game.
We will look into how we can modify the original baseline methods to get better results.
Some modifications might also just be made to simply get a more generalized solution
and by this make the model more applicable for new problems. We wish to show that a
model architecture can be useful in other contexts than originally used, with only small
changes necessary. For the protein sequence design, we wish to look into lowering of
latent dimensionality and try to make modifications elsewhere in the architecture in such
a way that the final results are comparable to the original solution results prior to dimen-
sionality reduction. The reduction is made to show potential of a big refactoring of the
model architecture and from this get a more generalized solution.
For the Mario level design optimization task, we look into how we can apply modelled
constraints to the problem.
Adding the constraint to the optimization problem shows
how Bayesian optimization can handle changes in problem formulation without needing
to change the general method construction significantly.
Through the chosen experiments we will be able to see the diversity and efficiency of the
Bayesian optimization method, as well as the limitations. We will be working with exper-
iments that are optimizing for multiple objectives simultaneously as well as experiments
with added of constraints.

Bayesian Optimization
As this project is focusing on the use of Bayesian optimization, it is important to have some
background understanding of the general flow of Bayesian optimization and the various
parts within. The basic version of a Bayesian optimization is simply built around surrogate
models and acquisition functions. In this project we will use Gaussian processes as our
surrogate models. This does not work efficiently for data with high dimensionality, hence
we will need autoencoders to transform the data into a lower dimensional latent space.
The flow of the Bayesian optimization method used in this project can be seen in figure
2.1
Figure 2.1: Flowchart for Bayesian optimization method. GP stands for Gaussian process.
We start with transforming the data to a lower dimensional latent space. A model is then
fitted for the underlying function. From this the best candidate is chosen for experimenting.
After getting the results of the candidate it is added to the original data which finishes an
iteration.
This flow shows what happens after one iteration of the Bayesian optimization method.
We see that the final steps are experimenting on the found candidate solution and adding
this solution with its respective results to your original data, creating a new data set for
the next iteration. However as the experimenting part may be very expensive and time-
consuming, it can be necessary to simply add the chosen candidate with its expected
results. In this way, multiple candidates can be computed before doing the necessary
experiments to gain exact results.

2.1
Autoencoders
As we saw from figure 2.1, the first step in our method is the transformation of data, by
use of an autoencoder.
Autoencoders are neural networks used to create representation of input in a low dimen-
sional space[2]. This is exactly what is needed for Bayesian optimization, as this only
works efficiently with lower dimensional data. In general it is built by two neural networks,
namely an encoder network and a decoder network. The encoder network is mapping the
original data into a latent representation with low dimensionality. This creates the wanted
dimensionality reduction. The decoder network is used to reconstruct the latent represen-
tation back to its original form. The architecture of a simple autoencoder can be seen in
figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Architecture of autoencoders. This is used with the MNIST data as an exam-
ple. Image from [3].
The goal of the autoencoder is to get the reconstructed input as close to the original input
as possible, while still having the requested dimensionality in the latent representation,
shown as z in figure 2.2. However when the number of nodes in the network exceeds the
number of data inputs, the autoencoder is learning an identity function, meaning we get
a perfect match from input to target. With this we are at risk of overfitting the model to the
given training data[3]. Because of this risk we need to add some modification to the basic
autoencoder.
There exists many different variations of autoencoders. In this project we will use a de-
noising autoencoder and a variational autoencoder for the protein sequence design and
Mario level design problems respectively.
2.1.1
Denoising Autoencoder
The denoising autoencoder, in short DAE, modifies the basic autoencoder by corrupting
a certain amount of the input vector and using this corrupted version as its input to the
autoencoder. It will then use the original input in the loss function, comparing this with the
reconstructed input[3]. The architecture of a DAE is shown in figure 2.3.

There it can be seen how the input gets partially corrupted before being applied in the
network. How to corrupt the data, and the proportion of data being corrupted, varies
dependent on the problem. The corruption can be addition of Gaussian noise or random
masking of certain input nodes, meaning setting these to zero. The proportion set to
zero can be seen as a hyperparameter for the user to optimize. Generally it is between
30 −50%[4].
2.1.2
Variational Autoencoder
The Variational Autoencoder, in short VAE, is very unlike the other autoencoders as it is
a probabilistic graphical model[5]. Unlike the regular and denoising autoencoders, which
encodes a latent fixed vector, the VAE encodes a latent distribution. This means that the
goal of the encoder is to compute a distribution with a mean and standard deviation, which
can describe the given input x. Using these distribution values, we can sample vectors,
z, which can be seen as the latent fixed vector representation arrived from the encoder.
The decoder then generates the reconstruction, x′, by use of a trained approximation for
a posterior on x given the sampled input z. This can be seen in figure 2.4. From the figure
it is seen that the input goes through a neural network, where at the end two vectors are
computed from the same layer. One vector corresponding to the mean of the computed
distribution and the other corresponding to its standard deviation. Then a latent vector is
sampled using these two vectors. The probabilistic decoder then uses the sampled latent
vector, z, to compute posterior distribution from which it reconstructs the input[6][7].
Reparameterization trick
We see that the network needs to generate samples. Sampling is a stochastic process,
which is problematic when training a network. When training a network we use backprop-
agation. This is only possible to do with deterministic nodes, and not possible with random
nodes. To get around this we introduce the reparameterization trick. The trick is to add
a stochastic node ε to the network. This stochastic node follows a standard normal dis-
tribution and is multiplied on the standard deviation vector. By adding this we still have a
stochastic node, but we do not wish to optimize the stochastic node and it can be seen as
a fixed node. We can also see that this has been implemented for figure 2.4. Now that we
have used the reparameterization trick, we can then backpropagate the gradients[8][9].

2.2
Gaussian Processes
Given that the data is either originally with low dimensionality or translated into a latent
space with low dimensionality, we can fit a Gaussian process model, in short GP model,
on the data. The aim of a GP model is to find an underlying function, f, which can map
the input data, x, into its corresponding targets, y. This is done with no prior knowledge
of the function beforehand[10].
The first part of fitting a GP model is initializing the Gaussian prior distribution values. The
prior consists of a mean value and a covariance. The mean value is set to be equal to
0 for the prior, before we add any data. The covariance is set to be a specific chosen
covariance kernel function. The hyperparameters for the chosen kernel is optimized by
giving the Gaussian process initial input data, x, with corresponding targets, y. There
exist different types of kernels, which can be used, when creating a Gaussian process.
You can even combine multiple kernels.
2.2.1
Matérn kernel
In the experiments used in this project, we use the Matérn kernel. The Matérn has two
hyperparameters in l and ν. The length-scale parameter, l, is used to describe how far two
data points can be apart and still have significant influence on each other[11]. The second
parameter, ν, is used to control the smoothness of the resulting function. Higher values
of ν constrict the solution to smoother functions. This makes the user able to control the
balance between fitting perfectly to the given data and keeping the solution smooth. The
Matérn kernel is given as[12]:

2.2.2
Fitting the Gaussian Process
When fitting our Gaussian Process we first fit the mean to be constant and equal to the
average of the given input data, x. We then optimize the hyperparameters of the Matérn
kernel. This is done by using an optimizer to provide maximum a posteriori, MAP, of the
parameters over a chosen prior. Since we choose to have the mean as constant, our prior
is then uniformly distributed. Because of this, providing an MAP is equal to maximizing
the log-marginal-likelihood, LML, of the Gaussian process, where we are using the given
targets, y, to marginalize over[14][15]. When maximizing the LML, we can find multiple
local optima. To improve our chances of finding the global optimum, we can initialize the
optimization step using various random starting points. Then we choose the parameters
with the largest LML as our optimal parameters.

2.2.4
Gaussian Process Classification
The theory described above is based on Gaussian Process Regression. This is the regular
modeling of Gaussian processes, however in our experiments, we are also looking at the
addition of a Gaussian process classification model. The big difference here is that the
targets are seen as classes and our predictions are seen as probabilities of belonging to
certain classes. This means that our predicting function, f, is not a Gaussian distribution,
as it works very badly with discrete labels as targets. Instead we use a logistic link-
function.
2.3
Acquisition Functions
After fitting a GP model with the training data, it is then possible to compute acquisition
values using acquisition functions. The acquisition function uses the posterior for the GP
model to propose new data points to be experimented with and then added to the data
set.
The acquisition function is important to handle the exploration/exploitation trade-off. If
we were only interested in choosing the new test data point with the highest expected
CHAPTER 2. BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION
7
==PAGE:12==
target value, we could get this directly from our GP model. We would simply choose the
x∗with the highest expected function value, maxx∗¯f(x∗). However this way we are very
prone to potentially getting stuck in a local optimum. To avoid this, the acquisition uses
the prediction uncertainty as an argument to force the Bayesian optimization method to
explore new areas, with potential to score values superior to already known data[17].
We can choose ourselves where to use the acquisition function. In some problems we
might have a pool of relevant candidate points. Then we would simply apply the acqui-
sition function on all candidates and choose the best candidate. If we do not have a
candidate pool, we would often discretize over a search area, and then compute acquisi-
tion values for each discretized point in the search area. This can quickly become a lot of
points if we want a lot of precision in our possible points, especially if the number of latent
dimensions is very high. This is why it is so important to use an autoencoder or in other
ways reduce dimensions.
There exist different acquisition functions. In this project we are using the expected im-
provement function and the multi-objective version of it, known as the expected hyper-
volume improvement.

2.3.2
Expected Hyper Volume Improvement
The expected improvement function only works for single-objective optimization, mean-
ing the targets, y, must be one-dimensional. Specifically for our protein sequence de-
sign problem, we are working with multi-dimensional targets, as we wish to optimize the
designs over multiple metrics. Since EI can not handle this, we must instead use the
expected hypervolume improvement function, EHVI.

Computing hypervolume
After deriving our Pareto frontier, we can set a reference point to calculate the hyper-
volume of the Pareto frontier from. The reference point must not be better in any of the
objectives compared to all points in the Pareto frontier[19]. In our experiment it is set to be
just a little worse than the nadir point, meaning the worst possible point. This way we can
compute the hypervolume for any test data, without risking computational errors. For the
small apples and oranges example, the nadir point is xnadir ∈(apples, oranges) = (0, 0),
as these are the lowest values in each objective. Therefore setting the reference point to
r ∈(apples, oranges) = (−1, −1) is reasonable.
When we have defined our reference point, we can compute the hypervolume. This is
computed firstly by computing spaces from the reference point to each of the points in
the Pareto frontier. The space can be formulated as, space(p, r) = {v ∈RN|s ⪰v ≻
r}, where N is the number of dimensions, p ∈P is a point in the Pareto frontier and
r = (r1, . . . , rN) ∈RN is the reference point. Here v are all the possible points, which
are weakly dominated by the Pareto frontier point, p, and at the same time dominates
the reference point, r, themselves. The space becomes a rectangle in two-dimensional
space and a hyper-rectangle for higher dimensional space.
When we have computed the spaces for each of the Pareto frontier points, we can then

2.3.3
Adding constraints
Specifically for this project we are looking at the addition of constraints to the problem.
This is useful when not all inputs have a feasible solution. The constraint can be simply
computed or be a difficult problem to solve in itself. Many library acquisition functions have
input fields for constraints. However these are linear constraints to where the solution y is
allowed in the objective space. We wish to add constraints, which are much more complex
and rooted in the composition of the input data X.
For the protein experiment, we wish to add a constraint, which should make sure, that we
do not, move too far away from prior data. This does not need to be modelled, but instead
we must check the distances, of our candidate solution inputs to prior data input.
For the Mario experiment, we wish to add a constraint, which should make sure, that
Mario completes the candidate level. To apply our constraint to the problem, we choose
to add an extra GP model. This model is made to model the probabilities of complying with
our constraint. The best way to model this is by use of a Gaussian process classifier as
described earlier. The classifier has two classes, where the probability of being in class 1
is corresponding to the probability of complying with the constraint. We can then multiply
our computed acquisition values with their corresponding probabilities. We can choose
here to add or subtract the computed acquisition value by a chosen amount. This way we
change the weighting between high expected improvement and high belief in fulfilling the
constraints.
If the constraint is hard, meaning a violation of the constraints is not tolerated, we would
add a value to the expected improvement. This way the EI values gets proportionally
closer to each other, meaning the difference in probability becomes more significant. To
make sure, we do not propose a solution with close to no expected improvement, we
should only add a value to solutions over a chosen threshold, preferably close to zero.
If the constraint is soft, meaning a violation of the constraint can be tolerated if the ex-
pected gain is great enough, then we would subtract a value from the expected improve-
ment. This way the EI values get proportionally further away from each other. This means
that the difference in probability becomes less significant.

2.4
Finishing the Bayesian optimization iteration
After computing the acquisition values, with or without an added constraint, we choose the
best computed candidate. When the candidate is chosen, we should run the experiment
on the candidate input, decoded to its original form, and get the true candidate solution
values. When this is done, we can add the candidate to our data. This is the final step of
a Bayesian optimization iteration.
If the experiments are too time-consuming or expensive, one could choose to add the best
candidate to the data and use its distribution means as its artificially true solution values.
Then multiple iterations can be ran without having to create the experiments, giving us
multiple candidate solutions. From here an expert in the field can choose which candidate
they believe to be the most interesting, based on a deeper understanding of the data.

3.1
Protein sequence design optimization
We first look into experimenting with the protein sequence design optimization problem.
The specific task we wish to optimize uses the ZINC data. The ZINC dataset includes
commercially available and annotated small molecules[26]. The data is represented as
discrete SMILES strings, which describes the structure of the molecules. The SMILES
strings are given in a way for most language models and editors to be able to convert the
strings back to two or three dimensional modelling of the molecules[27].
Protein sequence design optimization is a problem which many field are very interested in
solving. The goal of the optimization task can be very different dependent on the problem
it wishes to solve. For any task it is however often very difficult and expensive. This is
due to the difficulties of creating new protein sequences in the laboratory and testing them,
which is also very time consuming. This is why using the Bayesian optimization method
for these tasks are especially relevant as it excels in finding relevant candidate solutions
in few iterations, as well as giving multiple possible solutions for an expert in the field to
choose from.
In the specific task, which we are finding solutions for, the model is optimizing, by maxi-
mization, with regards to logP and QED simultaneously, making it a multi-objective opti-
mization task. The logP values describes the hydrophobicity of a protein sequence. A high
logP value means the molecule is hydrophobic, while a low value means it is hydrophilic.
Because of some of the flaws with using logP as an objective value, the second objective
for the task is QED (Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness). QED is a metric which uses
8 molecule properties to measure the similarity of a proposed protein sequence to that of
marketed oral drugs[28]. Both of these metrics are flawed, but there has yet to be found a
superior alternative. For this project the focus is more on the efficiency of using Bayesian
optimization to optimize with regards to certain metrics more so than the usability of the
chosen metrics. I.e. we are more interested to see if we can find a protein sequence
which is non-dominated in terms of logP and QED, than to discuss the relevancy of logP
and QED as metrics for optimal protein sequence design.
All experiment runs are saved by use of Weight and Biases. They can all be seen at https:
//wandb.ai/kofoed12/lambo. All relevant charts can be seen in the chem/opt_metrics tab.
The specific runs used in the results section are mentioned in their relevant sub section.
The models are using the BoTorch and GPyTorch framework and packages throughout
for the Gaussian process models and acquisition functions[29][30].

3.1.2
Modifying the baseline model
Our initial wish was to modify the baseline to do a grid search when optimizing, instead of
the masking method used initially. However looking deeper into the code and gathering
greater understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of a denoising autoencoder,
we find that this would need a complete refactoring of the code in a way that would make a
comparison with the baseline inapplicable. The reasoning behind this is described further
in the discussion section. We instead choose to sample multiple batches for each opti-
mization step and choose the best performing batch sample for each optimization step.
Each sample has added feature noise to make sure we expand the exploring area and
lower the chances of the samples being roughly the same. This modification should make
it possible to store better performing batches without the need of fitting the GP model.
3.2
Mario level design optimization
Our second experiment is the Mario level design optimization. For this experiment Mario
levels will be generated and a pretrained Mario agent is used to play through the generated
level. In this problem we wish to optimize the enjoyment of playing through a generated
Mario level.
All experiments are based around the same pipeline, to make them as comparable as pos-
sible. All experiments use the same variational autoencoder, for encoding and decoding
the generated maps. The network architecture is build as linear feed forward network, us-
ing hyperbolic tangent as its activation function. Most importantly here, is that the latent
dimension is given as zdim = 2. This is the latent representation used, when fitting and
optimizing our model.
Before running our Bayesian optimization iterations, we first run 10 random samples. This
means that we give the agent 10 randomly chosen latent representations, which are then
decoded to its original representation before the agent plays through the level. From each
sample we save the latent representation, the number of jumps performed by the agent
and whether the agent completes the level or not. The agent finishes a level by getting to
the finish line. The agent does not finish a level, if it gets killed by a mushroom, falls down
a hole in the ground or if it has not gotten to the finish line before exceeding the time limit
of 30 seconds.
After computing all the initial samples, the method goes through a chosen number of
iterations of the Bayesian optimization algorithm. Each iteration starts with fitting a GP
16
CHAPTER 3. EXPERIMENTS
==PAGE:21==
model using relevant information from initial samples and samples chosen due to prior
iterations. The method computes a 2-dimensional grid with values from -5 to 5. These
values corresponds to possible latent representations of Mario levels. For each point in
the grid, the acquisition value is computed, using the fitted GP model.
When the acquisition values are computed for all grid points, we choose the best perform-
ing point as our next sampling point. We decode the latent representation grid point to
get the corresponding Mario level. The agent plays through the chosen candidate level
and we save the results from playing through the level, like for the initial samples. This
finishes a Bayesian optimization iteration.
After running through the chosen number of Bayesian optimization iterations, we find the
latent representation with the best expected performance and run 100 simulations of the
corresponding level for this point. The reason for the 100 simulations is the stochastic
nature of the pretrained Mario agent.
3.2.1
Baseline model
As described before, the optimization goal for this problem is maximization of enjoyability.
Enjoyment is however not easily quantifiable, so the method used as our baseline chooses
to see the number of jumps necessary, for the Mario agent to complete the level, as the
degree of enjoyment from that specific level, meaning more jumps makes the level more
enjoyable. This is not a perfect metric for enjoyment, but it is easy to quantify and evaluate
whether or not the solution identifies a new map in which the agent needs to jump more
than in any of the randomly generated initial maps.
Besides the number of jumps it is also important that the agent actually completes the map.
After the agent plays through the map it returns different result fields. This includes the
field ”jumpActionsPerformed”, but also the field ”marioStatus”. This is set to 1, if the agent
completes the level and 0 if not. There could be maps with unreachable jumps, too many
enemy mushrooms or complete blockages. Given a map which can not be completed, we
find that the agent simply jumps around at the beginning until a timer goes off. This means
that if we were to simply optimize for jumps, the model would propose unplayable levels.
To stop this from happening the baseline method simply sets the number of jumps from a
proposed level to 0, if the level was not completed. This method works as intended, but
it is also fitting the model prediction by using the actual results of the experiment.
3.2.2
Playability model
We first experiment with looking at changing the goal of the optimization to optimize for
the probability of completing the level, we call this playability. This is done simply to see,
that the Bayesian optimization method also can work when the problem is a classification
problem instead of a regression problem, where class 0 is not completing and class 1 is
completing. This is simple to find a maximum for, as all completed levels are seen as
global optima.
3.2.3
Playability constrained model
After implementing the Bayesian optimization task with only playability, we then combine
the two tasks of optimizing playability and number of jumps. This is done by computing
two separate GP models and multiplying the posteriors for each model when calculating
our acquisition function. This means that the model value used in our acquisition function
corresponds to the expected number of jumps for a given map, multiplied by the expected
probability of completing the map. Here it is possible to experiment with different weighting
between jumps and playability, depending on what we wish to primarily focus on optimizing
for.

5
Discussion
The results gathered from our experiments shows the advantages and limitations of using
the Bayesian optimization method. We have shown how we can change the formulation of
our problem and modify the method to manage the change. The experiments also deals
with handling the balance between different optimization tasks, both in terms of working
with hypervolumes, as seen in the protein sequence optimization experiments, but also
with the recurring theme of constraint additions.
5.1
Protein sequence design optimization
When experimenting with the protein sequence design optimization task, we notice that
the ways of experimenting is very bounded by the nature of a denoising autoencoder. A
DAE is not optimized for decoding random samples of the latent representation, meaning
that we can not simply compute the acquisition function over a grid of latent representa-
tion points. This would be very problematic in many other applications of the Bayesian
optimization method. However for this specific task, it is not a problem. It is not a problem,
as the specific constraint for this task is making sure that the proposed new sequences
are sufficiently close to an original sequence. This means that actually searching through
the latent space is not that relevant, given that we in the end must remove all candidates
that are varying too far away from original sequences.
The original pipeline for the baseline experiment takes this into account, as we only search
through candidate solutions which are derived from original sequences with masking. This
way all candidates are sufficiently close to an original sequence, while also matching
nicely with the design of the DAE. From this we can conclude that the baseline method
is optimized to fit this specific problem close to perfectly. From the paper related to the
baseline method, we also find that the results derived from here are very impressive,
showing that the use of Bayesian optimization in this way is very effective.
Even though this baseline method shows great performance for this specific problem, it
is not very generic for all other problems. If we wished to make a more generic pipeline,
we would change from a DAE to VAE, so we can search throughout the latent space. We
would need a lot of data, to make sure the VAE can appropriately represent the sequences
in a sufficiently low dimensional space, for a grid search to be suitable. We would then
have to add a constraint to assure the proposed solutions are attainable. This is a lot
of extra work in conflicting directions, as we change the proposals to work freely just to
constraint them afterwards. This would not give better results than the baseline method,
however it could give us some insights to which undiscovered sequences could be of
relevance to dive deeper into.
5.2
Mario level design optimization
When experimenting with finding an optimal level for Mario, we found that the Bayesian
optimization method could indeed find a level better than any randomly chosen. This was
the case for all models with those being known as; the baseline model, the playability only
model and the playability constrained model. The very noteworthy part of this optimization
problem is the huge variance derived from the stochastic Mario agent. The stochastic
nature of the agent meant, that the same level could sometimes have the agent using
very few jumps while other times needing much more jumps and even fail to finish. When
simulating the level generated for the playability constrained model, shown in figure 4.17,
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
37
==PAGE:42==
we get a run where the agent uses 74 jumps and finishes, while it more regularly only
needs around 18 or 20 jumps to finish. This variance is not that significant, when averaging
over 100 simulations. It can however change the model predictions greatly, when only
simulating each sampling point once, while running the Bayesian optimization method.
This may give some sample results, which are not easily reproducible, and because of
this the model will have difficulties giving appropriate proposed new sampling locations.
These problems are based on the problem formulation of the true value. This means that
the underlying function might be more affected by the stochastic noise, than the behaviour
of the level. This lowers the reproducability significantly, as we might get good results
for a level in one experiment, but very bad for the next. We know that some levels are
impossible for the agent to finish, due to the limits for how high and far Mario can jump. We
also see from the playability experiment, that there are levels, which Mario will complete
every time. The big problem is the inconsistency of the agent, when facing a 4 block jump.
We see from our results that this can vary greatly.
This is why we chose to add extra simulations for each iteration and use the mean and
median results of the simulations. We saw that this gave much better results, both for
the new baseline results as well as for the playability constrained model. For the base-
line model, we saw a significant increase in playability rate, with the jump counts being
approximately the same. For the playability constrained model the modification had the
inverse effect, as it has a significant increase in the jump count, while staying close to the
same in playability rate. For both cases this lead to a considerable gain in average jump
count conditioned on completing the levels.
We also shortly commented on the fact that the specific results, derived from our ini-
tial baseline and playability constrained experiments, did not completely dominate each
other. We also commented about the baseline model weakly dominating the playabil-
ity constrained model, when both had extra simulations. This shows, that we could also
change the problem formulation significantly and look at it as a multi-task optimization
problem, as for the protein sequence design optimization problem. This way we could
get a Pareto frontier, giving us the optimal number of jumps, for each playability rate.
This could be seen as having levels, which increase in difficulty, seen by the reduction of
playability rate.
Even though we find the results of our modified playability constrained model to be better
over all compared to the baseline, we can still not conclude that we have gained a better
model. Even though the results of the final level are based on a lot of simulations, this is
not the case for the samples used when fitting our model. This makes it very difficult for
the model to find a usable underlying function.

Conclusion
Our experiments with the toy example optimization problem shows clearly the effects of
Bayesian optimization. It gives a great illustration of how the algorithm progresses from
one iteration to the next. It specifically shows how the method switches between con-
verging towards local optima and exploring undiscovered areas to see if there are better
solutions elsewhere. This is the main idea behind Bayesian optimization. We also see
how a constraint can be applied to the problem formulation and handled in the algorithm.
In the example, we show how the constraint itself can be modelled by a Gaussian pro-
cess as for the objective function, however it does not have to be if the constraint is more
simple.
We found that the baseline model for the protein sequence design optimization task, was
optimized very well in terms of pipeline structure. It was setup nicely to handle the fact,
that we can not generate protein sequences far from already existing sequences. This
was handled by use of a DAE and looking through candidate sequences by masking of
predetermined sequences from the computed Pareto frontier. This way all the candidate
sequences were sufficiently close to prior data, and no constraints was necessary. The
nature of the DAE does not work well for uniformly searching through a latent space, like
we could for the toy example and Mario level design tasks. This shows that the solution is
very specified for the sequence design task and not very general. To make a general so-
lution for this problem, the dimensionality reduction should be done through a variational
autoencoder instead and we would then have to additionally add a constraint. This would
not grant better results for the specific optimization task, but could show which undiscov-
ered areas seems to have the highest potential, if we were able to create sequences from
these areas.
From our experiments with Mario level design optimization, we see that the Bayesian
optimization algorithm succeeds in terms of arriving at a candidate level, which is higher
in both playability rate and jump actions performed, compared with randomly sampling
levels. The big problem for this optimization task was the big uncertainty derived from the
randomness of the Mario agent. This was handled in later experiments, where multiple
simulations was run for each iteration. This improved the end results significantly for both
the baseline model as well as the playability constrained model. After this modification
the two models ended with nearly identical results.
From all experiments we conclude that Bayesian optimization can work well for many
different tasks. The importance lies in the problem formulation and giving the necessary
information for training of the Gaussian Process models. The experiments show how the
method successfully uses the trade-off between finding the absolute local optimum and
exploring the search area to make sure it does not miss a potential global optimum. This
is done, while at the same time minimizing the number of necessary iterations needed to
nicely fit the model and give well-founded proposed optimal points.

7
Future Work
As described in the discussion section, we find that the baseline model pipeline has opti-
mized the protein sequence design optimization task. If we were to work further with this
problem, a complete refactoring of the setup is necessary. This would include changing
the autoencoder to be a variational one, as this way it can handle being given latent rep-
resentations of uniformly distributed samples and decode these into sensible protein se-
quences. Changing the setup, would most likely still not give better performing candidate
samples, but could give larger insights to some underlying functionality of the sequence
designs. It could show that the model sees great potential in certain protein sequential
ordering. This would change the objective of the optimization task, from looking into op-
timal sequence designs to finding correlations between specific sequence structures and
their expected functionality.
The Mario level design optimization task can be examined further in various different di-
rections. We made the change of having multiple simulations for each iteration, but it is
possible to investigate this further. We could save the uncertainty of the jump count for
each iteration and this could be applied when computing the covariance kernel. We could
also change the Gaussian process model for the playability constraint from a classifier to
a regression model. Then we would use the probability of the value being over a chosen
limit, in this case we have used 5, but it could be changed.
If we choose to change the playability model to GP regression, then we could also look
into changing the problem to a multi-task optimization problem. This moves the playabil-
ity measure from a constraint to an objective function. This way we would get a Pareto
frontier solution giving us highest expected jump counts for various levels of expected
playability rates. Each point in the frontier could be seen as a level, where the decrease
in playability rate can be seen as an increase in difficulty.
It could also be interesting to look into other objectives, as jumping is a very noisy metric
and also might not be the best for evaluating enjoyment of levels. Perhaps a combined
function based on metrics such as time spent, number of mushrooms spawned, jump ac-
tions performed and maybe more, could create a better approximation of level enjoyment.
These are not changes to the Bayesian optimization algorithm but rather the objective
function it is attempting to model.
