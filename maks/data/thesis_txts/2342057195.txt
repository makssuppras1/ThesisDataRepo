Abstract 
The Free Energy Perturbation (FEP) approach with Metropolis Monte Carlo has been 
implemented for the direct calculation of the configurational partition function of the guest 
molecules in the gas hydrate cavities. Crystallographic data of the water molecules is used to 
construct the geometry of the cavities, then the simulation is carried out in order to calculate 
the configurational partition function. From the configuration partition function, the Langmuir 
adsorption constants defined in the solid solution theory of van der Waals and Platteeuw are 
calculated. 
The new implementation is used to calculate the Langmuir constants of methane and 
ethane in the small and large cavity of hydrate structure sI, and nitrogen and propane in 
hydrate structure sII. The results obtained with the new method are compared to those 
obtained with the approximation of the potential over a hypothetical spherical cavity with the 
model of Lennard-Jones and Devonshire. The inclusion of the real geometry of the cavities 
compared with the spherical approximation shows a great decrease in the value of the 
Langmuir constants, especially for molecules with sizes closer to the cavity radius. Additionally, 
the free volume is depicted as a result of the Monte Carlo simulation to give a more qualitative 
understanding of the intermolecular interactions predicted by the Kihara potential between 
the guest and the host molecules. 
MULTIFLASH v6.0 – KBC Advanced Technologies (A Yokogawa Company) is linked with a 
Python interface that calls a FORTRAN subroutine with the implemented methods. With the 
new implementation and MULTIFLASH the gas hydrate equilibria of the system methane-water 
it was evaluated at temperatures from 240 to 330 K. MULTIFLASH and FEP are used to fit the 
molecular parameters of the Kihara potential to experimental dissociation pressure and 
fractional occupancy ratio, measured with Raman spectroscopy. Optimum sets were found 
with absolute average deviation (AAD) of approximate 2.8 %.

1 Introduction 
Gas hydrates is the common name employed to denominate clathrate hydrates of natural 
gas. A clathrate is an inclusion compound in which a specific type of molecule, called the host, 
forms a lattice structure that entraps another type of molecule called the guest [1]. Particularly, 
gas hydrates are ice-like solid complexes with a well-defined crystalline structure in which the 
water molecules (host) form a lattice structure with interstitial cavities or cages that enclose a 
gas molecule (guest), typically sufficiently small molecules present in the natural gas, such as 
methane, ethane, propane, carbon dioxide or hydrogen sulfide. In these lattice structures, 
water molecules are linked by hydrogen bonding and whereas there are no chemical bonds 
between the gas molecules and the water molecules, the gas molecules stabilize the hydrate 
structure with weak van der Waals forces, which could not exist without them [2]. 
Water clathrates were discovered by Davy in 1810, who carried out experiments with 
saturated solutions of chlorine and water at temperatures near the freezing point of pure water 
[2], [3]. After that, clathrates remained as a scientific curiosity, and by the end of the decade it 
was shown that several gas molecules can form hydrates, including Cl2, Br2, H2S, CO2, CH4, 
C2H6, C2H4, C2H2, C3H8, and the noble gases argon, krypton and xenon [4]. It was not until the 
1930’s that the industry and more researches became interested in studying gas hydrates 
behavior when Hammerschmidt determined that clathrate hydrates were responsible of 
clogging pipelines of natural gas which operated at conditions near 0 °C. This discovery 
stimulated the research and development in this field by the investigation of the physics and 
phase behavior of gas hydrates. Predictive models and methods to inhibit hydrate formation 
were developed and regulations of the water content in gas natural were implemented. 
The first method for predicting the formation of gas hydrates was developed by Wilcox 
and co-workers in 1941, and then modified by Carson and Katz in 1942. It is called the K-value 
method because it is based on distribution coefficients in which the hydrates are treated as a 
solid solution, similar to an ideal liquid solution [5]. The latter allowed the determination of 
the pressure and temperature of hydrate formation from natural gas [4]. However, this method 
is not capable of predicting equilibrium-phase composition [6] and leads to errors when non-
hydrocarbon gases are present [5]. Another method, often called gravity method, was 
developed also by Katz in 1945. This is a simpler method that permits the estimation of the 
==PAGE:14==
 
2 
 
hydrate formation conditions from charts given a gas gravity [4]. Even though these methods 
offer an easy way to determine the formations conditions of gas hydrates, they are limited and 
it is not possible to extrapolate.  
After some years of studying gas hydrates, and thanks to the development of laboratory 
equipment, it was possible to determine the hydrates structure via X-ray diffraction [4]. It was 
discovered that gas hydrate forms different kind of structures, and that each structure is 
composed of cavities in which the gas molecules are enclosed. With a well-defined structure 
for gas hydrates, it was when van der Waals and Platteeuw in 1959 proposed a model for gas 
hydrate equilibria based on statistical thermodynamics [2]. This method enabled the 
prediction of properties of mixtures from parameters obtained from pure components [4]. The 
model treats the hydrate phase a solid solution, where the solute are the gas molecules that 
are entrapped in the lattice structure. This entrapping process is expressed as physical 
adsorption similar to the Langmuir Adsorption model. The theory assumes that all the cavities 
are spherical with a fixed size (cannot be distorted) and at most they can contain one guest 
molecule. Moreover, that the state of the gas molecules inside the cavities can be considered 
equal to its ideal gas state, and no guest-guest interactions can occur. With this new model, it 
was possible to determine the phase behavior of gas hydrate in equilibrium with the vapor, 
liquid or ice phase, by the calculation of the chemical potential of water in the hydrate. 
However, when compared to the K-value and gas gravity method, the van der Waals and 
Platteeuw model is more difficult to implement. 
With the improvement of computers, in 1972, Parrish and Prausnitz developed an 
adequate algorithm, using the van der Waals and Platteeuw model, to be used in a computer, 
and versatile for industrial applications. Nowadays, the solid solution theory of van der Waals 
and Platteeuw, followed from Parrish and Prausnitz, it is the most currently used model for 
determination of gas hydrate equilibria [7]. A more detailed explanation of this model and gas 
hydrate equilibria is given in chapter 3. 
As mentioned before, the oil and gas industry is concerned about gas hydrates because 
they may be formed in pipelines of natural gas when water is present at high pressures and 
even at temperatures above 0 °C. However, they may have other applications, that include 
storage and transportation of natural gas [7], and even the storage of hydrogen [8]. More 
recently, there has been an interest related to post-combustion CO2 capture with gas hydrates 
==PAGE:15==
 
3 
 
[9]. Natural gas hydrates are also found in nature, mainly in artic regions with permafrost and 
in deep seabed, which leads to a potential gas resource from hydrates [10]. 
Given the increased number of application of gas hydrates through the years, several 
attempts to improve the accuracy of the van der Waals and Platteeuw have been carried out 
by tackling the assumptions made during the development of the model, or simply to make it 
more efficient and versatile. For example, Holder et al. [11] introduced a simpler way of 
calculating the chemical potential of water in the liquid phase with respect to a hypothetical 
empty hydrate defined in the model, eliminating the need of using correction for the pressure 
and for reference hydrate curves. Additionally, a different fugacity approach for the calculation 
of the empty hydrate lattice has been implemented [12], [13]. Additionally, McKoy and 
Sinanoglu [14] compared different potentials for the evaluation of the guest-host interactions 
in the cavities, and concluded that the most suitable for the calculation was the Kihara 
potential [15]. 
Attention also has been paid to the spherically assumption for the cavities. As proposed 
by van der Waals, the spherically averaged model of Lennard-Jones and Devonshire is used to 
evaluate the partition function of the guest in the cavity and then obtained the Langmuir 
constant that is used to express the physical adsorption of the guest molecules in the hydrate 
lattice. John and Holder [16], Tester et al [17], and Sparks et al [18], used integration technics 
and Monte Carlo simulations to evaluate the full potential of the guest-host interaction 
including crystallographic data for the host positions in the cavity. They concluded that the 
spherical assumption was a poor approximation, because the Langmuir constants are greatly 
affected when the “real” geometry is used for the calculations, especially for large molecules. 
Furthermore, the inclusion of host molecules further than the first shell (cavity), also has a 
significant effect in the calculated value of the Langmuir constant. 
Ballard [19] accounted for the distortion of the gas hydrate lattice by the introduction of 
the activity coefficient of water in the hydrate as a function of a volume difference due to the 
adsorption of the gas molecules. Klauda and Sandler [12] in their fugacity approach, accounted 
for this matter for various guests by introducing a guest-dependent vapor pressure of the 
hydrate, as well as guest-host interactions with the first, second and third shells. Later, they 
introduced guest-guest interactions in the calculation of the molecular potential [20]. In the 
publications mentioned in this paragraph, the authors reported improvements in their 
implementations when compared with the van der Waals and Platteeuw in the determination 
==PAGE:16==
 
4 
 
of dissociation curves of pure gases and mixtures. However, in general the models contain a 
greater number of adjustable parameters. 
In a different approach, molecular simulation, and especially Monte Carlo simulation, has 
been used for the direct calculation of the guest occupancy in gas hydrates [8], [21], [22]. In 
this technique, the full lattice with all the cavities is modeled and the simulation is carried out 
by randomly inserting or removing guest molecules in the cavities and also allowing them to 
move inside the cavities. The result is the occupancy of the gas hydrate as a function of 
temperature and pressure, similar to the Langmuir adsorption isotherms. The advantage of 
these methods is that the investigation of multiple occupancy is rather easy, and also the 
evaluation of guest-guest interactions, the addition of further host shells, and examination of 
flexible hydrate lattice. 
The development of equations of state has improved the prediction of hydrate phase 
equilibria by taking into account the non-idealities of the fluid phases. One of the mayor 
examples is the use of the advanced Cubic Plus Association (CPA) equation of state [23], which 
combines the physical term of the Soave-Redlich-Kwong (SRK) cubic equation of state with an 
association term similar to the one develop in the SAFT theory [24]. With this model, it is 
possible to provide good predictions of systems containing associating fluids that form 
hydrogen bonds, such as water and alcohols. The implementation of such equations increase 
accuracy, especially when associating fluids are used to inhibit the formation of gas hydrates 
by displacing the dissociation curve to higher pressures, or lower temperatures [25]. 
Being able to determine the occupancy is important for applications that require a good 
prediction of the composition of the gas hydrates, and how they occupy the different cavities 
in a given structure. These applications include the determination of the storage capacity for 
gases such as methane, hydrogen and carbon dioxide, through the promotion of gas hydrates. 
Methane and hydrogen are important because with the occupancy it is possible to define the 
energy density of hydrates of these gases, for example, for a “safer” transportation and storage 
of hydrogen [8], or for the determination of the amount of methane that is contained in the 
natural gas hydrates in the permafrost region and seafloor [22], [21]. The latter has been very 
important lately because an increase of the ocean temperature could break the equilibrium of 
these natural gas hydrates, releasing great amounts of greenhouse gases to the atmosphere, 
and promoting climate warming [20]. The determination of the storage capacity of the CO2 
==PAGE:17==
 
5 
 
and conditions, may allow the application of gas hydrates for sequestrating this greenhouse 
gas from flue gases [9]. 
The aim of this work is to tackle the spherical approximation for the hydrate cavities with 
the implementation of Monte Carlo methods to evaluate the Langmuir adsorption constants 
and being able of predicting both the occupancy of the guest, and the dissociation curves for 
pure gas hydrate and mixtures.

2 Gas hydrates 
2.1 General Properties 
As stated before, gas hydrates are solids with a crystalline structure formed by water 
molecules that are held together in a lattice by hydrogen bonds. The host lattice formed of 
water molecules is thermodynamically unstable, so it requires guest molecules inside the 
cavities to stabilize it, otherwise the structure cannot be formed. The size of each cavity or 
cage formed in the lattice, that encloses a gas molecule, must be such that permits the water 
molecules to form strong hydrogen bonds, and it is the relatively weaker van der Waals forces 
between the guest and the host molecule that provide stabilization to the cavities and the 
whole structure [3]. Molecules that have strong functional groups, or that may form hydrogen 
bonds with the water molecules in the lattice prevent the formation of clathrates, like HCl and 
NH3. Although the guest molecules are required to stabilize the gas hydrate structure, in real 
situations the cavities in the host lattice are not fully occupied, and they are regarded as non-
stoichiometric compounds. 
Another property of the guest molecules that influence the characteristics of gas hydrates 
is the size. Depending on the size of the gas molecule, gas hydrates can form one of the three 
most common crystal structures of defined size: structure I (sI) and structure II (sII) in a 
centered cubic cell, and structure H (sH) in a centered hexagonal cubic cell. However, just 
structures sI and sII are more relevant for the oil and gas industry [7], and this work will just 
focus on those two. Furthermore, the different structures are composed of cavities or cages, 
the ones that entrap or enclose the guest molecules, and their size and distribution depends 
on the entrapped molecule. Both structures sI and sII are composed of a large cavity and a 
small cavity, different for each structure. A more detailed description of the structures and 
cavities is given in the section 2.2. 
Besides the chemical nature of the guest molecule, whether it has strong functional 
groups or may form hydrogen bonds, molecules that form gas hydrates lie within a range of 
size. In a heuristic manner, they should be large enough not to get out of the cages and not 
so large that they cannot fit in the largest cavity of a given structure.  
==PAGE:19==
 
7 
 
As gas hydrates have a crystalline structure, their characteristics are similar to the 
characteristics of ice. Although ice is formed by pure water, most gas hydrates are formed of 
about 85 % water in molar composition [4], which makes them, for example, having less 
density than liquid water and thus they float. Physically, gas hydrates look and have similar 
behavior as wet snow and can stick to metal surfaces [7]. 
 
2.2 Structures and Cavities 
This work is focused just in gas hydrate structures sI and sII, because those are the 
structures of more interest for the oil and gas industry [7]. However, there is known that some 
compounds form structure sH and with a few exceptions some compounds form different 
structures [4]. As stated in previous sections, the different structures of gas hydrates are 
conformed by different cavities. Structures sI and sII have two cavities each, one “large” and 
one “small”. The cavities are polyhedrons made of water molecules bonded to each other by 
hydrogen bonds. Both structures are contained in a unit cubic cell, meaning that the arrange 
of host and guest molecules distributed in a cube represents the repetitive unit of the hydrate. 
The nomenclature that is commonly used to name the cavities follows this form: nm, where m 
is the number of faces of the polyhedron with n edges that correspond to a specific cavity [4], 
[13].  
The crystallographic data presented in this work and shown in the following paragraphs 
is taken from references [3] and [4], and corroborated with the works of McMullan and Jeffrey 
[26], and Mak and McMullan [27]. 
Structure sI is conformed of two cavities that from here on will be called the small cavity 
of structure sI and the large cavity of structure sI. The small cavity of sI is a polyhedron named 
pentagonal dodecahedron with formula 512, meaning that is a polyhedron with 12 faces and 
each face is a pentagon with 5 edges of equal lengths and equal angles. The large cavity of sI 
is a polyhedron of 14 faces called tetrakaidecahedron, with formula 51262. This formula 
indicates that this cavity is formed of 12 pentagonal faces plus 2 hexagonal faces. The cubic 
unit cell of structure sI has a lattice constant of 12.03 Å at 248 K, and it is formed by 42 water 
molecules arranged in 2 small cavities (pentagonal dodecahedron) and 6 large cavities 
(tetrakaidecahedron), where the oxygens lie on the vertices of the polyhedrons. According to

3 Gas Hydrates Equilibria 
3.1 Phase Diagrams 
Given any of the possible applications of gas hydrates, the most important feature is to 
be able to predict the gas hydrate phase equilibria for different kinds of hydrate formers and 
the mixtures relevant for the industry. Ultimately, the prediction of the phase equilibria, or the 
dissociation conditions, gives the knowledge of the temperature, pressure and other 
properties, such as occupancy of gas hydrates, that are important depending on the different 
applications either to inhibit its formation or promote it.  
The phase diagrams at gas hydrate formation conditions for the single hydrates of 
methane and propane are shown in Figure 3.1. The notation for the equilibrium lines is the 
following: H for hydrate phase, V for vapor phase, Ice for ice phase, Lw for aqueous liquid phase 
and LH for hydrocarbon liquid phase. Each line of the phase diagram represents an equilibrium 
line where three phases coexist. Each point in the equilibrium line represents a monovariant 
or uni-variant point, and by fixing the temperature the pressure is directly specified [3]. The 
hydrate equilibrium lines Ice-H-V, Lw-H-V and Lw-V-LH, are most commonly called hydrate 
dissociation curves, and separate the region where hydrates form. To the right of the 
dissociation curves hydrates will never form, and to the left, hydrates may be formed according 
on the overall composition [7]. 
Another referenced features in Figure 3.1 are the intersections of the equilibrium lines. 
These intersections are defined as quadruple points, and are unique for each component [4]. 
For both components, methane and propane, there is an intersection labeled with Q1, the 
lower quadruple point where the phases Ice-Lw-H-V coexist. The lower quadruple point 
indicates the point where the gas hydrates start to form from ice instead of from water. The 
upper quadruple point, labeled as Q2, is the intersection between the hydrate dissociation 
curve (Lw-H-V) and the vapor-liquid equilibrium (VLE) line of the hydrate former and water. In 
this point the four phases Lw-H-V-LH, coexist with each other. As it can be seen in this figure, 
methane does not have Q2, and this is because the vapor pressure line and critical point are 
==PAGE:27==
 
15 
 
not high enough to form that intersection. This means that some gases, such as methane and 
nitrogen do not have upper quadruple points [4].

3.3 Gas Hydrate Phase 
The solid solution theory of van der Waals and Platteeuw fills a gap between the molecular 
properties of gas hydrates i.e. crystallography data of the structures and guest molecules, and 
the macroscopic properties e.g. pressure, temperature and density [4].This theory, as derived 
by its founders, was developed from statistical thermodynamics and a detailed derivation is 
presented by Sloan and Koh [4]. However, Hendriks and Meijer [7] showed that the model can 
be simply derived from chemical reaction theory. 
The basic assumptions of the solid solution theory are stated as follows: 
1. The contribution to the free energy of the cages is independent of the mode in which 
the guest molecules occupy the cavities.  
==PAGE:30==
 
18 
 
2. One cavity can contain at most one guest molecule 
3. The interaction between guest-guest molecules is neglected. The partition function of 
the guest in the cavity is identical to its ideal state. 
4. Classical statistics are valid (no quantum effects needed). 
The first three assumptions are similar to those stated by the Langmuir adsorption model 
[32]. The first assumption implies that guest molecules does not distort the cavities, or the 
analogous to the occupation sites in the Langmuir theory, and in the same way the second 
and third for the occupation sites and the adsorbed molecules.

the same pressure and temperature, the occupancy of the first guest will be greater than that 
of the second guest. This behavior depends on the size of the molecule and the cavity that is 
more likely to occupy. In other words, the value of the Langmuir constant for a relatively large 
molecule in a small cavity will be lower than a smaller molecule in the same cavity because it 
is not likely for the large molecule to occupy the small cavity.

4 Monte Carlo Methods 
Monte Carlo methods are numerical methods used for numerical integrations of complex 
integrals. In which the analytical, or the numerical integration with conventional methods i.e. 
trapezoidal rule, Gaussian quadrature, are not suitable for the task, for example for integrals 
over complicated configurational space. These methods are based in random sampling and 
probability. The evaluation of the integrals is carried out by calculating the average of all the 
random positions generated with a random number generator in the configurational space of 
interest. In broad manner, it is possible to say that the Monte Carlo methods are differentiated 
depending on the technique used to do the random sampling. 
In the past, when the computation power was very limited, the implementation of these 
methods was rather difficult. However, around 1948, Nicholas Metropolis designed and built 
the computer MANIAC with Los Alamos team, one of the first electronic digital computers [39]. 
Later, Metropolis and his colleagues developed techniques and algorithms for using the Monte 
Carlo methods on the MANIAC [40]. The method developed by Metropolis gave the foundation 
for the Monte Carlo method used in molecular simulation, often called Monte Carlo simulation, 
Metropolis Monte Carlo or Markov Chain Monte Carlo [41]. 
This chapter is intended to give the basis of the Monte Carlo methods in order to apply 
them in the calculation of the configurational partition function presented in chapter 3, 
equation (3.17). The application of these methods to the evaluation of the Langmuir constants 
will be covered in chapter 5.

4.2 Monte Carlo Simulation 
From Monte Carlo integration, we define that the variance for the estimate of an integral 
may be decreased depending on the density distribution function selected, which for Monte 
Carlo integration is the uniform density distribution. The fact that this distribution is constant 
will increase the variance in such parts of the domain where the value of the integrand is not 
proportional to the distribution function. The selection of an adequate distribution, that is 
proportional to the function on the integrand, to reduce the variance is called importance 
sampling. However, the reason why Monte Carlo integration uses the uniform distribution and 
not a tailored distribution when dealing with multidimensional integrals in some complex 
domains, is because in order to find the adequate distribution we must be able to integrate 
analytically the function. But if we were able to integrate the function, the numerical 
integration would not be needed on the first place [41].

5.2 Implementation 
In the next sections the implementation of the models to calculate the Langmuir 
adsorption constant for gas hydrate equilibria is described. First, the spherical approximation

Now that we are able to generate uniform random points in the concentric sphere, it is 
necessary to check if the random points lie inside or outside the cavity. The Möller-Trumbore 
algorithm of ray casting is implemented [48]. This algorithm checks if a ray intersects with a 
triangle in ℝ3. Hence, when a point is generated a straight line is constructed between the 
random point and the center of the cavity. Then, the calculation is carried out with all the 
triangles in the mesh and if an intersection is found, it means that the point lies outside the 
cavity.  
The implementation of the ray casting algorithm was tested and it greatly increases the 
computation time required for the evaluation of the partition function with Monte Carlo 
integration. It was decided to “turn off” the ray casting algorithm, and instead create a 
concentric sphere with radius equal to the average radius of the cavity. Thus, the evaluation 
of the partition function is carried out in this “interaction sphere”. Both methods were tested 
and the results were nearly identical. This can be explained by the fact that in the sections near 
the water molecules on the lattice there is a zone of high energy, where the Boltzmann factor 
is close to zero and do not affect the final result for the averages. Therefore, it was decided to 
implement it with the “interaction sphere” instead of using the ray casting algorithm. In Figure 
5.3 are shown the three cavity geometries with their corresponding interaction spheres with 
radii equal to the average radius of each cavity.

6 Results 
The FORTRAN module containing the methods and algorithms for the determination of 
the Langmuir Adsorption constants is compiled in a library file and called from an interface in 
Python. Python is selected as being open source, moreover, in order to take advantage of its 
packages for optimization and data presentation. For the calculation of the Langmuir 
constants, the three molecular parameters of the Kihara potential and the characteristics of 
each cavity must be determined (Summary of the Methods). The calculation is straightforward 
and no additional numerical methods are needed, besides the ones described in section 5.2. 
A single run for one temperature with 106 steps for Monte Carlo simulation and Monte Carlo 
integration takes around 6 s in a standard computer, which results highly adequate. However, 
when calculating for several temperatures, and especially during the fitting procedure, it could 
take from 0.5 to 3 h depending on the initial estimates and the algorithm used for 
optimization. 
This chapter is divided in two parts. In the first part, section 6.1, it is presented the 
calculation of the Langmuir constant of different components in order to test the performance 
of the Monte Carlo methods. Finally, in the second part, section 6.2, it is presented the result 
for the calculation of gas hydrates equilibria for different components with the application of 
the implemented methods.

6.1 Calculation of the Langmuir Adsorption Constants 
The calculation of the Langmuir adsorption constants with the inclusion of 
crystallographic data of the host molecules in the cavities has been done before by John and 
Holder [16], Tester et al [17], Sparks et al [18], among others. In these previous works, the 
estimation of the configurational partition function was carried out with numerical integration 
using the multidimensional Gauss-Legendre quadrature and Monte Carlo integration, 
however, the results obtained with Monte Carlo integration were used just as reference for the 
multidimensional Gauss-Legendre quadrature and were not reported. Moreover, they 
presented an extensive investigation of Langmuir constants values with different sets of Kihara 
parameters, where the inadequacies of the spherical approximation were evidenced. As a first 
check, the Langmuir constants calculated in this work were compared to the results reported 
on those investigations.  
Table 6.2 shows a comparison of the calculated Langmuir constants with the methods 
implemented in this work and the ones reported by Sparks et al (1999) for the small cavity in 
structure sI. It is worth mention that the cavity parameters used in this work for the spherical 
approximation and the free energy perturbation are not the same as the values implemented 
by Sparks et al, and the comparison is done in a qualitative manner. From this table, it can be 
seen that the results obtained with the Lennard-Jones and Devonshire spherical 
approximation are in good agreement, but the results obtained with the Monte Carlo methods 
differ in orders of magnitude from the ones evaluated with multidimensional Gauss 
quadrature by Sparks et al. In particular, the Langmuir constants calculated with the Monte 
Carlo methods in this work are closer to the values obtained with the spherical approximation. 
When first examined, it was thought that the differences were consequence of a coding or 
implementation error. However, it was observed that it was possible to obtain results in the 
same order of magnitude with those of Sparks et al if the hard core parameter (𝑎) of the Kihara 
potential as implemented in the Monte Carlo methods was doubled. The conclusion for this 
issue may be attributed to the way the Kihara potential is used in their implementation in the 
multidimensional integration, which is not explicitly reported. When McKoy and Sinanoglu [14] 
presented the spherical averaged potential equation with the Kihara potential derived from 
the Lennard-Jones and Devonshire model, they assigned a zero hard core for water, and this 
was also taken into account by Parrish and Prausnitz [30]. Therefore, when using the Kihara 
potential in the Monte Carlo methods as shown in equation (5.3), the molecular parameters

6.1.1 Structure sI 
The Langmuir constants of methane and ethane, in the small and large cavity of sI, as a 
function of temperature are plotted in Figure 6.1 to Figure 6.3. From the shape of the 
functionality of the Langmuir constants with temperature, it can be concluded that at lower 
temperatures, the occupation of a given cavity with a guest molecule is more favorable, with 
higher values of the Langmuir constant. At high temperatures, it is more difficult for the guest 
molecule to occupy the cavity, so the increased in pressure for the formation of gas hydrates 
at higher temperatures [4]. In addition, it can be seen that the Lennard-Jones and Devonshire 
(LJD) spherical approximation gives greater values for the Langmuir constants when compared 
to the Monte Carlo integration (MCI) and Monte Carlo simulation with the free energy 
perturbation approach (FEP). The later may be attributed to the fact that the potential energy 
of the guest-host interactions is smeared over the surface of the sphere, whereas when the 
real host positions are used, each water particle in the cavity contribute individually to the 
potential depending on the distance between the host and guest molecules. Therefore, the 
effectiveness of the particles that are further from the guest is less in the real cavity, whereas 
in the spherical approximation this effectiveness is not mitigated because the potential is 
averaged over the surface.  
For methane and ethane, the large cavities show greater values of the Langmuir constants 
than the small cavities, indicating that it is more likely to occupy the large cavities than the 
small cavities as there is more free volume where the guest molecules can move without 
reaching configurational space with high potential energy. For methane the differences range 
in the same order of magnitude, but for ethane the differences of the Langmuir constants 
between the small and the large cavity are of about 6 orders of magnitude. The low values for 
ethane in the small cavity indicate that the ethane molecule may be too big for the cavity and 
there is a high potential energy between the molecule and the host particles in the lattice. In 
==PAGE:71==

the other hand, high values of the Langmuir constant in the large cavity show that the ethane 
molecule has an appropriate size where the interactions with the host molecules give low 
values for the potential energy i.e. the depth of the intermolecular potential (𝜖). In addition, 
Figure 6.4 evidences a great difference between the spherical approximation LJD and the 
simulation with the real host positions due to the asymmetry of the large cavity of sI 
(tetrakaidecahedron) and the size of the ethane molecule.

6.1.3 The Free Volume 
Tester et al [17] defines the free volume as the volume inside the cavity where the 
probability of finding the guest molecule is relatively higher. This volume can be obtained 
from a Monte Carlo Simulation as discussed in section 4.2. After the simulation is complete, 
the volume can be approximated with the positions of high probability obtained with the 
Metropolis sampling. However, for the direct calculation of the Langmuir constant this method 
introduces uncertainties. Figure 6.19 shows the probability density of finding a methane 
==PAGE:81==
 
69 
 
molecule inside the cavities of structure sI. The density probability is depicted with the surface, 
where the red color indicates a high probability and the blue color a low probability. It is clear 
from this figure that it is more likely to find the methane molecule around the center of each 
cavity, because near the lattice, with water molecules in the vertices, the high energy gives low 
values of the Boltzmann factor.  
 
 
Figure 6.19 Depiction of the free volume (light red) for methane (left) and ethane (right) in the small cavity 
of structure sI from the available positions (solid red) in a FEP simulation.  
 
A coarse representation of the free volume obtained from the Monte Carlo simulations 
with the free energy perturbation approach in the previous sections are shown in Figure 6.20 
to Figure 6.23. The free volume is depicted by the red polygon around the center of each 
cavity, and it is formed from the coordinates of the available positions obtained during the 
simulation. The positions shown in the plots do not have the same probability, because as 
discussed earlier, the positions closer to the lattice have high values of potential energy and a 
low probability. The red shaded “sphere” is a coarse representation of the “actual” volume that 
a molecule could occupy in each cavity, which is determined from the collision diameter 𝜎+
2𝑎 of the corresponding molecule. In other words, 104 distributed sample points were taken 
from each FEP simulation carried out in the previous section and plotted with the coordinates 
of the different cavities. Each point corresponds to the center of the molecule in question, 
then a sphere of diameter 𝜎+ 2𝑎 is plotted centered on each of the points, and with all the 
points the red shaded “sphere” is formed. In the case of the FEP simulation, the free volume 
==PAGE:82==
 
70 
 
(polygon) has a nearly spherical form because the simulation is carried out in the “interaction” 
sphere with the spherically averaged potential LJD.  
Figure 6.20 and Figure 6.21 show the free volume for methane and ethane in the cavities 
of structure sI. As expected from the results discussed in the previous section, the free volume 
of ethane is smaller than the free volume of methane in both cavities, because it is a bigger 
molecule. In addition, it can be noted that the free volume of both methane and ethane is 
greater in the large cavity than in the small cavity, showing that the molecules have more 
available space and so the value of the Langmuir constants is larger in the bigger cavities. 
Although the “actual” volume depicted by the red shaded “sphere” seems identical for both 
molecules in each type of cavity, the “actual” volume of methane in both cavities is slightly 
greater.

6.2.1 Methane Hydrate 
The gas hydrate equilibria of the system methane-water were studied with the new 
implementation linked to MULTIFLASH. The experimental data for dissociation curves available 
ranges from temperatures of approximate from 150-330 K, with pressures from 0.53 to 105 bar 
[4]. The data sets used in this work are those of Gayet et al [51], Adisasmito et al. [52], de Roo

hydrate-vapor-liquid region for the methane-water system. Gas hydrates properties, such as 
fractional occupancy, fractional occupancy ratio and hydration number are also examined. 
However, it is worth mention that the data found in the literature for compositional properties 
of the methane hydrate present a considerably amount of scatter, and it is yet unclear if the 
current data is correct or not [19], [49].

From Raman, or NMR spectroscopy it is possible to determine the guest composition of 
gas hydrates as a fractional occupancy ratio, and from them other properties may be derived, 
e.g. hydration number, structural transitions. The fractional occupancy ratio (large cavity over 
small cavity) is the ratio of the area of the peaks corresponding for the guest in the small and 
large cavity, in the spectra obtained from the Raman or NMR studies. It is also usual to 
calculate values of the fractional occupancy of the guest in each cavity from de fractional 
occupancy ratio, however, it requires the use of the van der Waals and Platteeuw model to 
determine the fractional occupancies from the fractional occupancy ratio [59]. In this work it 
was decided to fit directly to the fractional occupancy and test the performance of the 
implemented models.  
The results for the guest composition in the methane hydrate at equilibrium conditions 
are plotted in Figure 6.27 to Figure 6.28. In these cases, all the fits are presented for both 
models because there are observable differences in opposite to the dissociation pressure 
where the results were nearly identical. In Figure 6.27 the experimental fractional occupancy 
ratio is compared to the prediction of MULTIFLASH and the models, showing relatively good 
agreement. Different modifications to the van der Waals and Platteeuw model have been 
tested to these data and all of them follow the same trend [19], [22], [60], the fractional 
occupancy ratio increases with temperature in the hydrate-vapor-ice equilibrium line, and 
decreases with temperature in the hydrate-vapor-liquid equilibrium line. Klauda and Sandler 
[49] discuss that data obtained from Raman spectroscopy may be more inaccurate than data 
from NMR because more assumptions must be made, but the data is scarce. This matter leads 
to question if the data of the fractional occupancy ratio of Jager1 [61] and Subramanian1 [62] 
is accurate, because both were determined with Raman spectroscopy. In the other hand, the 
data from Kini1 [63] was measured with NMR and it differs from the other sets for about 10 % 
in the values of the fractional occupancy ratio. Even though the data from Kini contains few 
data points, it may suggest the same trend followed by the data of Jager and Subramanian 
mentioned before.

Subjecting the Monte Carlo methods to optimization is not a trivial task. Optimization 
methods that require the estimation of derivatives, Jacobian matrices and/or Hessian matrices 
are not suitable to be applied with this kind of algorithms. As the nature of the Monte Carlo 
methods is stochastic, derivatives cannot be calculated or the approximation with finite 
differences as it is usually done with other complex models is rather complicated. The latter is 
because the small variations of the independent parameters, e.g. Δ𝑥≈10−4, often required to 
estimate the numerical derivatives of the functions do not lead to a correct approximation 
because in these limits the data is noisy with variations proportional to the standard deviation. 
To avoid this, a large number of steps will be required, even for Monte Carlo simulation. The 
option followed here was to use a “conjugate gradient algorithm” developed by Powell [64], 
in which the minimization is carried out by changing one parameter at a time. This method is 
already implemented in the Python package scipy2

7 Conclusions 
A new method based on Monte Carlo simulation was implemented to calculate the 
Langmuir absorption constants in the solid solution theory of van der Waals and Platteeuw for 
gas hydrate modelling. The method allowed the calculation of the configurational partition 
function of the “real” cavity with the host position from crystallographic data as the change in 
free energy when a perturbation is applied to the spherically averaged potential of Lennard-
Jones and Devonshire (LJD).  
The calculation of the Langmuir constants from the configuration partition function of the 
guest molecules in the cavities was evaluated with the free energy approach (FEP) with a Monte 
Carlo integration (MCI) as an initial check, and LJD model. The results show that the inclusion 
of crystallographic data in the simulation considerably affects the value of the Langmuir 
constants obtained with LJD. In almost all the cases it was found that the Langmuir constant 
obtained with FEP was lower than the ones calculated with LJD, suggesting that the 
perturbation process is spontaneous (positive values of the free energy change). The latter 
implies that work is required to smear the potential energy of the individual guest-host 
interaction over the surface of the sphere. Moreover, the Monte Carlo simulation permits the 
qualitative analysis of the free volume of the guest molecules inside the cavities, providing 
some understanding on why some cavities are occupied more by a certain type of molecule 
over another. 
Additionally, the FEP approach was implemented in the calculation of dissociation curves 
for methane in the hydrate of structure one. It was found that several sets of parameters give 
the same average absolute deviation when fitted to experimental dissociation data. Therefore, 
it was necessary to constrained the optimization by the inclusion of compositional hydrate 
information, such as the fractional occupancy ratio in order to find the optimum values of the 
Kihara parameters. The results obtained with FEP and LJD for the molecular parameters are 
very close to each other, with exception of the energy parameter which is slightly different. 
This was observed when the compositional hydrate data was included in the fitting procedure. 
Several explanations may be derived to explain this behavior: (1) Assuming that the 
experimental data of the hydrate composition used is correct, the calculated values are highly 
correlated to the size parameter of the Kihara potential and for a molecule like methane, the 
==PAGE:95==
 
83 
 
spherical approximation is adequate to predict the hydrate properties and the results with FEP 
are compensated with the slightly higher values of the energy parameter. (2) The Kihara 
potential (3 parameters) gives good results because it is possible to fit experimental data with 
a large combination of values, which at the same time indicates that during the fitting 
procedure some physical meaning is lost when the parameters compensate for other 
assumptions implemented elsewhere in the model and/or MULTIFLASH.  
To tackle the difficulties mention above, it is necessary to test the model with the study 
of more systems and to obtain more reliable compositional data for gas hydrates. Explore the 
possibility for the implementation of a more adequate intermolecular potential function for 
the guest-host interactions in the hydrate cavities. The addition of subsequent water shells 
and the investigation of the geometry for the guest molecules (in this work are assumed 
spherical). For all these tasks, Monte Carlo simulation with the free energy perturbation serves 
as a good tool because any modification required to the current implementation would not 
require of great effort and will allowed to push the limits of the solid solution theory of van 
der Waals and Platteeuw.
