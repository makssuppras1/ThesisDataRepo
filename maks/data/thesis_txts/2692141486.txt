Abstract
The rotor eﬀective wind speed is a metric that indicates the average value of all the dif-
ferent wind velocities across the rotor plane, and is a useful and important parameter that is
used in wind turbine control algorithms. In the present study, the analysis is performed with
the help of DTU developed HAWC2 aeroelastic code and is based on the Vestas V52 wind
turbine. The goal of the project is to examine and compare the performance of some of the
most common estimation methods, namely the Kalman Filter, the Extended Kalman Filter
and the Power Balance Estimator. Aeroelastic turbulence simulations performed with the
help of the DTU developed HAWC2 software, based on IEC 61400-1:2019 recommendations,
indicate that the Power Balance and Kalman Filter estimation methods perform better in
estimating the rotor eﬀective wind speed, than the more specialized Extended Kalman Filter
method, and all the estimation methods perform better at lower wind velocities. Addition-
ally, a sensitivity analysis of the Mann model turbulence parameters is performed, in order
to examine the eﬀect of diﬀerent turbulence characteristics on the performance of the ﬁlters.
Bigger length scales and anisotropy factors seem to result in smaller statistical errors and
therefore rotor eﬀective wind speed values that are closer to the true average free-wind speed
on the rotor plane.

1
Introduction
The wind energy sector is growing rapidly with many countries turning towards wind
power production as the main driving force that will help them fulﬁl their carbon neutrality
pledges and goals that they have set for the immediate future [Climate Home News, 2021,
Carbon Neutrality Coalition, 2021]. In order to increase wind turbine power output, man-
ufactures have built larger and larger blades, but practical problems such as extreme loads
and material strength limitations, are getting more and more prominent as the size of the
blades increases, bringing the need to have a realistic indication of the wind speed across
the rotor plane front and center. For this reason, engineers are trying to ﬁnd diﬀerent ways
to estimate this elusive value. The Rotor Eﬀective Wind Speed (REWS) is an important
parameter used by the control systems and algorithms. Control systems highly depend on
their inputs, like wind speed, which makes precise measurements essential.
1.1
Background
A wind turbine generates power by converting the kinetic energy of the wind into elec-
trical energy. It follows that a larger rotor area captures a larger amount of wind, therefore
increasing its power production. The wind, however, is almost never steady and laminar, but
it is characterized by turbulent disturbances. In addition, surface friction results in shear
forces that decelerate the wind closer to the turbine’s tower base [Honrubia et al., 2010].
Other eﬀects, such as the tower shadow eﬀect [McSwiggan et al., 2008, Cheng et al., 2018],
contribute into creating a highly chaotic wind ﬁeld, which is characterized by variations in
wind speed and direction.
Additionally, in order for a variable speed, variable pitch (VSVP) wind turbine (WT)
to extract the maximum wind power at below and above rated wind speeds, the pitch and
torque controllers use information about optimal pitch, optimal tip speed ratio (TSR) and
rotational speed. All these are highly dependent on wind speed and, therefore, as already
mentioned, precise wind speed information is necessary.
It exists, therefore, the need to identify a wind speed value that is characteristic of
the whole rotor area, as the wind speed measured at the nacelle or at hub height from
a meteorological mast away from the wind turbine, is not a good indicator of the wind
that the entire rotor area experiences. Eﬀorts to estimate this elusive value have already
been made based on LIDAR measurements [Held and Mann, 2019] and based on simula-
tions [Soltani et al., 2013] and various estimation models have been tried such as a Power
Balance Estimator [van der Hooft and Engelen, 2004], Immersion and Invariance method
[Ortega et al., 2013, Liu et al., 2009], Disturbance-Accommodating Control [Johnson, 1986],
Kalman Filter and Extended Kalman Filter [Song et al., 2017, ho Hur, 2021] and
[Knudsen et al., 2011], but no standard model has yet been largely adopted.

Objectives
1.2
Objectives
The objective of this project is to apply diﬀerent methods of estimating the rotor ef-
fective wind speed based on existing models and examine the performance of each method.
Speciﬁcally, the models studied in this project are the Kalman Filter (KF), Extended Kalman
Filter (EKF) and the Power Balance Estimator (PBE). Then, the turbulence parameters will
be changed in order to identify the sensitivity of the estimation methods and whether dif-
ferent estimation methods perform better at diﬀerent turbulence ﬁelds.
1.3
Structure
In order to make this project easier to navigate, a list of the chapters with a small
description is given below.
Chapter 2
In this chapter the basic concept of a WT’s operation is presented. The fundamental
equations that will be used throughout this project, such as the torque and TSR equations
are laid out. A description of the basic model that will be used in the simulations is given.
Then a presentation of the general characteristics of the Vestas V52 wind turbine, which will
be the WT studied for this project, is given.

In chapter 3 the basic parameters of the HAWC2 and HAWC2S models are established.
The operation of HAWC2S and its use in the construction of the power coeﬃcient curves are
described. Then the parameters and the process of creating the turbulence ﬁeld is explained.
Chapter 4
Chapter 4 presents the theoretical background of each estimation method starting from
the power balance estimator to the Kalman Filter and lastly to the Extended Kalman Filter.
The fundamental equations and working process of each method is laid out.
Chapter 5
The methodology and results are analyzed in this chapter.
The adaptation of each
method for wind speed estimation is explained, followed by their results. A small discussion
on them and on the limitations of each method is also presented here.
Chapter 6
Chapter 6 includes a statistical analysis of the results. This is necessary in order to
compare and evaluate the performance of each estimation method.
Values used for this
analysis include the RMSE and the error variance. The rotor eﬀective wind speed is also

2.1.1
Introduction to main concepts
A WT’s main purpose is to convert the energy of the wind into electrical energy, therefore
WTs can be thought of as wind energy converters. Many diﬀerent WT designs have appeared
through the years, but the most dominant one is the three-bladed, horizontal axis WT.
The maximum amount of energy extracted from the wind is given by the equation[Hansen, 2015]:
Pmax = 1
2 ˙mV 2 = 1
2ρAV 3
(2.1)
However there is an eﬃciency limit on the energy conversion. As laid down by the
German Physicist Albert Betz [Hansen, 2015], due to the principles of mass and momentum
conservation, no turbine can capture more than 16/27’s of the available kinetic energy of
the wind. For this reason a power coeﬃcient term is introduced, which is the ratio of the
actual power extracted from the wind over the maximum available power. So equation 2.1
becomes:
P = 1
2ρAV 3Cp
(2.2)
where ρ is the air density [kg/m3], A is the rotor sweep area [m2] and V is the wind
speed [ms−1]. From equation 2.2 one can see that the extracted power from the wind is
related to the wind speed raised to the third power. From early on, therefore, there is a need
to have a good estimation of the wind speed.
The aerodynamic power P is not the actual power fed to the electrical grid. A typical
rotational speed of a wind turbine rotor is 10-50 [rpm], whereas for the generator 1000-3000
[rpm] [Hansen, 2015], therefore a gear box is inserted between the rotor and the generator,

2.1.3
Wind Turbine Control
It is clear that the wind resource on a given region is characterized by many diﬀerent
wind velocities.
The objective of the measurement engineer is to collect data from the
location of interest, identify the various wind velocities, their direction and their probability
of appearing during a given time period, as well as perform data normalization as described
in [International Electrotechnical Commission, 2017]. This means that the WT has to be
able to produce power within a deﬁned range of wind velocities, with one of them being
the rated velocity, meaning the velocity at which the wind turbine starts generating rated
power for the ﬁrst time. Below that wind speed, the turbine has to operate at optimal power
coeﬃcient and above it the turbine has to keep the power generated at rated in order to
protect the generator, but also keep the rotational speed at a ﬁxed value, in order to prevent
over-speeding and noise pollution. The concept of WT control is introduced in order to
achieve this goal.
Two control regions are therefore recognised and there is a clearly deﬁned control goal
for each region. For simplicity, in this chapter the below rated region will be deﬁned as

The HAWC2 and HAWCStab2 Models
The Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation (HAWC2) is a pro-
gram developed by the Aeroelastic Design Research Program at DTU Wind Energy, Risø
Campus in Denmark. The program is intended to run WT aeroelastic simulations in time
domain. It is a powerful tool that allows the user to perform, among others, wake modeling,
eigenvalue analysis, aerodynamic and hydrodynamic modeling, and simulate vertical axis
wind turbines and horizontal axis wind turbines with 1,2,3 or more blades.
HAWCStab2 is a separate program designed to perform aeroservoelastic simulations in
the frequency domain for steady states computation and stability analysis. HAWCStab2 is
a graphical user interface and the accompanied HAWC2S version is command line based.
The input to both HAWC2 and HAWCStab2/HAWC2S is an htc ﬁle; both programs share
the core structure, but each program has their own speciﬁc commands. The htc ﬁles for the
Vestas V52 WT are provided by the gitlab repository [Vestas, 2021].
For the purposes of this project both programs were used. HAWC2S was used in order
to create the Cp curves (see chapter 4.1.1) and HAWC2 was used to perform the turbulence
simulations.
3.1

3.1.1
Turbulent Wind Parameters
Wind velocity under realistic conditions is almost never steady and laminar.
Air
friction, wind gusts, thermal eﬀects and temperature ﬂuctuations and wakes from obsta-
cles cause ﬂuctuations in wind velocity referred to as turbulence. The IEC 61400-1:2019
[International Electrotechnical Commission, 2019] recommends the Mann turbulence model
as model for simulating turbulence inside a 3D velocity ﬁeld, with the use of a Mann tur-
bulence grid box. The Mann box is a method developed by [Mann, 1994] that creates a
synthetic turbulence ﬁeld used in simulations. In essence, the Mann box is a virtual box of
dimensions speciﬁed by the user, that resembles a wind tunnel. Each dimension of the Mann
box is divided by grid points, the number of which is also speciﬁed by the user. Each grid
point stores a wind speed value; if the blade element lies between grid points, at a certain
simulation time step, the wind speed value at that point is interpolated based on the grid
points around it. A visual example of the method is given in ﬁgure 4.

and is especially useful when dealing with noisy sensor measurements. The method uses
a recursive solution to the discrete-data linear ﬁltering problem. The process essentially
consists of two steps; a prediction step and a correction step and is assumed to be one of the

the prediction and the measurement. The Kalman ﬁlter is applied on discrete-time processes
that are described in a form of a state-space equation. The new state χk is calculated based
on the previous state χk−1.

An important parameter to consider is that the Kalman ﬁlter relies on linear dynamic
systems and noise inputs that are independent and gaussian with zero mean.
As R.E.
Kalman states: "Physical random phenomena may be thought of as due to primary random
sources exciting dynamic systems. The primary sources are assumed to be independent gaus-
sian random processes with zero mean; the dynamic systems will be linear." [Kalman, 1960]
Variations of the ﬁlter have been developed in order to deal with non-linear dynamic systems.
As mentioned above, the ﬁlter relies on discrete-time processes that are described in a
state-space equation. However, equation 4.7 is continuous in time, transitioning smoothly
from one state to the next. The system during the simulation is sampled in dt = 0.02 second
intervals, therefore making the need for a discrete-time system apparent. To achieve this,
the MATLAB® function c2d() that converts a model from continuous to discrete time using
the zero-order hold method, was used.

value.
As the TSR reaches the limit of the look-up tables, the REWS estimation becomes
unstable and returns high negative and positive values, until it exceeds the limit and returns
NaN values. When the TSR returns back inside the pre-determined range of the Cp look-
up table, the power coeﬃcient again takes realistic values and the REWS estimation is
continued as given by equation 5.4. This is a common problem when estimating wind speeds
close to cut-in or cut-out, and can be overcome either by enlarging the range of the look-up
table or by establishing workarounds completely skipping the pre-computed power coeﬃcient
mapping as presented in [Lio et al., 2021].

6.4.2
R-squared
The coeﬃcient of determination, R2, is a metric that shows how much of the variance
of a dependent value is explained by the variance of the independent value. For the case of
REWS it shows how much of its variance is explained by the variance of the measured wind
value. An R2 = 0 means that there is no correlation between the two values and the model
is not a good ﬁt to the measured data. An R2 = 1 on the other hand means that the two
values are 100% correlated and the model ﬁts very well to the measured data.
A simple linear regression was performed (see ﬁgures 7(b), 11, 13(c)) using the MAT-
LAB® function polyﬁt() in order to ﬁnd the slope β and y-intercept α. Since this is a
simple linear regression with only one dependent and one independent value, the coeﬃcient
of determination is simply the square of the correlation coeﬃcient. The function corrcoef()
was used to ﬁnd the correlation coeﬃcient R and then the value was simply squared to get
the R2 (eq. 6.8).

7
Sensitivity Analysis
The goal of this chapter is to analyze the eﬀectiveness of the estimation methods with
regards to the variability of the turbulence parameters. By ﬁtting the turbulence parameters
to a speciﬁc site, the engineer can expect a certain performance from the estimation methods.
Since this project is based on the turbulence model presented by [Mann, 1994], the studied
factors are thus going to be the length scale (L) and anisotropy (shear distortion) factor (Γ),
a description of which is given below.
7.1
The Mann Turbulence Model Parameters
J. Mann uses the Von Kármán [von Kármán, 1951] energy spectrum described in equa-
tion 3.2 for his model on turbulence in neutral atmospheric conditions.
• Length scale (L) is a length scale from von Kármán’s energy spectrum deﬁnition and
deﬁned based on the turbulence scale parameter (Λ) (see chapter 3).
• Anisotropy factor (Γ) is the scale distortion parameter. In ideal isotropic turbulence,
a Γ = 0 indicates that all three wind component’s have the same value.
• Dissipation factor (αϵ2/3) is again taken from von Kármán’s energy spectrum deﬁ-
nition and describes the rate of viscous dissipation of speciﬁc kinetic energy.
To get an accurate representation of the turbulence ﬁeld of a speciﬁc site, these param-
eters must be derived based on the measured spectra of the three wind components. The
IEC 61400-1:2019 [International Electrotechnical Commission, 2019] recommends a Γ = 3.9
as this value corresponds better to normal atmospheric conditions giving a scaling ratio over
the wind component’s variance:

8
Conclusion and Suggestions for Further Work
A brief summary of key takeaways of the project is listed below. The project aimed
to compare diﬀerent REWS estimation methods and therefore the summary will be divided
into two parts. The ﬁrst part discusses issues, problems and limitation of the project. The
second part is concerned with suggestions for improvements and subjects for further work.
8.1
Estimations: Key Takeaways and Limitations
The purpose of this project is to compare common REWS estimation methods, examine
their performance and study their behavior in diﬀerent Mann model turbulence parameters.
Among the three chosen estimation methods (EKF, KF and PBE) the KF and PBE methods
perform best, with the PBE considered to be superior due to its simplicity compared to the
other two methods. The estimation performance changes according to the mean wind speed
and the baseline value used for the statistical analysis (either Vhub or RAWS). Furthermore,
the Man model turbulence parameters inﬂuence the estimation performance signiﬁcantly.
Higher anisotropy factors and length scales almost always correlate with lower MEE, MSE,
variance and RMSE and the coeﬃcient of determination in some cases reaches values above
0.95.
• The WT model is based on a number of assumptions. Speciﬁcally, it assumes an
inﬁnitely stiﬀdrive train and its oscillations are neglected. In practice, this method
relies on many noisy signals (such as the low speed side shaft torque) and low pass
ﬁlters must be applied ﬁrst. This is not the case with HAWC2 simulations as the output
values are noise-free. Indeed others in literature have found that dynamic models give
better results [Soltani et al., 2013, Ma et al., 1995].
• The HAWC2 simulations are in a way limited. The simulation time is 700s, the ﬁrst
100 of those are omitted from any calculation as the turbine startup happens during
that time. This means that the project is limited to six 10 minute periods for each
simulation as six seeds are used for each wind speed and turbulence parameter.
• The Power Balance Estimator is the simplest method, required the least calculation
time and presents the best performance of the three estimation methods even though
the WT upon which is based is very simpliﬁed. Its advantage lies on the fact that it
doesn’t require complicated algorithms and it is easy to implement.
• The Kalman Filters show promising results even if they lack in performance com-
pared to the PBE. Their performance might improve with better tuning of the noise
covariance matrices Q, R, but their main drawbacks is their sensitivity to TSR and
blade pitch changes outside the range of the Cp look-up tables, their increased com-
plexity and the higher calculation time compared to the PBE that might make them
unsuited for real time estimation. The EKF in particular has the worst performance.
One possible reason might lie with the wind speed model and a more detailed model
that includes turbine dynamics can improve the performance.

8.2
Suggestions for Further Work
• Filter complexity is another issue. Even though one would imagine algorithms such
as the EKF would result in better performance due to their specialization, their com-
plexity seems to be a hindrance. The non-linearity of a wind turbine model demands
methods that take this into account in order to produce better predictions, but this in
turn requires more ﬁne tuning of the individual parameters. In the case of the EKF
such parameters are the process and noise covariance matrices (Q & R), which have
the disadvantage that are diﬃcult to estimate and specialized algorithms for this job
are complex [Odelson et al., 2006]. All these lead to ﬁrst, higher chances of errors and,
second much higher estimation times, which is a big drawback if the estimation method
must comply with real-time constraints.
• The pre-computed power coeﬃcient look-up tables are created under steady
inﬂow conditions and stiﬀblades and tower, and thus omit the inﬂuence of turbine
dynamics and turbulence variations that occur under normal operation. A method
that tries to go around this issue is presented in [Lio et al., 2021].
• Regarding the sensitivity the REWS estimation is more accurate across all estima-
tion methods for higher anisotropy factors Γ and legnth scales L. Any industry-ready
REWS solution should therefore take these factors into consideration.
8.2
Suggestions for Further Work
• It has been shown that algorithms for tuning the noise covariance matrices for Kalman
based estimators improve the performance of the ﬁlters.
Better Q, R estimation
via an adaptive ﬁltering approach based on innovation and residual is studied in
[Akhlaghi et al., 2017] and a more accurate Q tuning through a direct and a sensitivity
method is presented in [Salau et al., 2009], both achieving better EKF performance. It
is recommended one of these or a similar method to be adopted in any future research.
• Even though the PBE achieves overall very good performance and is the best among the
methods studied here, it can be improved further by updating the drive train equation
2.7 to include WT dynamics. In this project most of the dynamics and losses in the
gear box or generator are circumvented by measuring the mechanical torque before
the gearbox, but in cases where only the generator torque measurement is available,
a more detailed drive train equation is necessary, as well as an accurate estimation of
the torque losses on the generator and gearbox.

C
Sensitivity Analysis
As seen in ﬁgure 34, the wind speed at 235s increases rapidly from 8 ms−1 to 15 ms−1
and then back to 9 ms−1. At this point the WT controller probably started increasing the
pitch from 0◦and didn’t have time to respond to the sudden gust, due to the high blade
inertia. This caused the controller to over-correct and a sudden drop in rotational speed
is seen at 247s. The Kalman Filter uses the rotational speed as an input for the REWS
prediction. The ﬁlter worked correctly with its given inputs, therefore the sudden increase
and then drop in the output (ﬁgure 34). Another such gust, but this time much smaller can
be seen at around 535s, causing a second, smaller, spike in pitch at 541s and its eﬀect can
be seen on the REWS output at the same time.

As seen in ﬁgure 21 the ratio of variance σ2
v seem to decrease for Γ = 4 for higher length
scales. This seems to be a consistent trend on many diﬀerent seed simulation but it’s not
always the case as seen in ﬁgure 35(d), where the ratio increases at lower rates and is even
smaller at Γ = 4. This is the exception to the rule, however, and it is also the simulation
where the σ2
w ratio is bigger compared to other simulations. Generally, larger eddies produce
higher σ2
v compared to σ2
iso and this probably has to do with the lower αϵ(2/3) values for
higher anisotropy factors and length scales.
Another observation concerns the ratio of variance σ2
u, which stops increasing or has a
decreasing trend for higher length scales. This also seems consistent across the diﬀerent seed
simulations with the exception of ﬁgures 35(c) and 35(e) where the ratio has a continuously
increasing value.
It must be noted that these are 10 minute simulations and longer simulation times might
show clearer trends.
