Summary
Environmental concerns are increasingly driving the evolution of the energy sector. In
efforts to reduce air pollution and the emission of greenhouse gasses, many countries
developed zero-emission vehicles such as full battery electric vehicles, hybrid electric
vehicles, and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEV). As long as the world needs
more fuel-eﬀicient vehicles to smooth climate change, then the hybrid and plug-in
hybrid’s role becomes more critical.
This master thesis is part of a research project, aims to investigate the modeling of
series-parallel plug-in hybrid electric vehicle for transforming minibusses by focusing
on electrical, mechanical, and battery storage systems to obtain a reduction in fuel
eﬀiciency of plug-in electrical hybrid mini-busses subjected.
In this study, the series-parallel of plug-in hybrid electric vehicle in MATLAB/Simulink
platform modeled, and the details of electrical and mechanical powertrains discussed
and simulation equipped with PHEV will be presented, and results discussed and
analyzed.
The model is combining of advantages of a hybrid electric vehicle (HEV) with an
electric motor (with no pollution and high available power at low speed) and an inter-
nal combustion engine (high dynamic performance and low pollution at high speed)
and integration to an extensive battery storage system. By compare HEV, PHEV
has a more comprehensive battery system and can be rechargeable.
An introduction of vehicle dynamic, permanent magnet synchronous machine
(PMSM), internal combustion engine (ICE) with planetary gear/ power split device
(PSD), and energy storage are given. In this model, the PMSM motor is modeled by
ICE with deliverable high torque at the given RPM and maximum deliverable power
limit. Also, energy management control is set up to control energy in two scenarios.
Results show the improved fuel consumption performance in two scenarios. Con-
sequently, the studied PHEV presents a potential for implementation on PHEVs.
The final simulation in the first scenario shows, 7.12 L/100km engine with a 49
(kW) electric motor and 32 (kW) battery pack in blended mode strategy and in second
scenario, 2.71 L/100km engine with 35 (kW) electric motor and 25 (kW) battery pack
in all electric-range mode strategy simulated.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Nowadays, energy demand becomes a big challenge for the present and future trans-
port system. Moreover, increasing global warming and producing greenhouse gases
(GHG) such as of Carbon Dioxide (CO2) due to heavy fossil fuel consumption are
affecting climate change and rising sea levels. So the global efforts to reduce CO2 by
improving in fuel eﬀiciency of automobiles in the transport section. Environmental
concerns and energy supply security have pushed governments to explore environ-
mentally friendly, eﬀicient, and sustainable transportation solutions. Interest in the
electrification of vehicles is growing due to the environmental benefits that can be
achieved regarding emissions and fuel consumption.
In Europe, to reduce emissions and fuel consumption, it established more restric-
tions to transport vehicles and fuel used and reduce the emission by 60% within
2050. EU 2020 target enactment for reducing CO2 emissions from vehicles and de-
fine penalty for manufactures for excess emission and monitoring emissions of new
vans [23]. Denmark has set the goal to be entirely operated by renewable energy by
2050 [24]. According to the Paris agreement from 2018, Denmark has set a ban for
the sale of cars, both diesel and petrol, from 2030 and also Plug-in Hybrid Electric
Vehicle (PHEV) from 2035 [24].
Apart from environmental aspects, economic and political aspects must also be
taken into account. In this context, society has begun to understand that Internal
Combustion Engine (ICE) should not be the future of mobility. Hence, alternative
vehicles are being developed during the last decades. Among them, electric vehicles
(EVs) are one of the most outstanding solutions.
Hybrid Electric Vehicle (HEV) are the first generation of electrified vehicles that,
in addition to an ICE, they have an electric machine and electric energy storage. The
electric vehicle power demands can vary from a wide range of 15 (kW) (low speed
urban) to 100 (kW) (aggressive highway) base on vehicle characteristics, energy, and
range. However, most hybrid electric vehicles can achieve a limited drive range, but
PHEV is most differentiated from hybrid by increasing electric range (energy) and
power onboard [P.1-9 [10]].

1.1 Background and Motivation
2
PHEV are much more energy-eﬀicient than combustion, and they are cost-effective
over battery electric vehicles and fuel cell electric vehicles. PHEV combines the advan-
tages of EV and HEV; the difference between PHEV and HEV is that it can charge
electrochemical energy storage with electricity from an off-board source.
Usually,
there is large battery storage compare with HEV to displacement fuel potential [25].
PHEVs are the particular type of HEVs, a combination of electric power and me-
chanical power using conventional combustion engines and electric machines. The
significant difference in PHEVs is the possibility of store energy by plugging it into
the external power outlet. The stored energy can propel the vehicle on short trips,
reducing vehicle dependency on petroleum and potentially CO2 emissions.
PHEV are generally either a series-parallel hybrid electric vehicle, series or parallel
hybrid electric vehicle. The main difference between them is that HEV focuses on
a small electric motor and a bigger combustion engine. However, PHEV focuses on
the small engine and bigger electric propulsion with the external charging adaptor,
which is highly desirable for the city’s driving cycle [Ch.14 [6]]. The series-parallel is
more complex than simple series or parallel HEV/PHEV. This configuration uses two
electric machines and is mainly applied in front-wheel drive. One of the advantages
of this system is high dynamic performance, and low pollution at low speeds by the
internal combustion engine and the advantage of zero-pollution and high available
power at low speeds by electric motor drive [26].

This thesis studies a series-parallel hybrid power-train configuration for plug-in hy-
brid minibusses. It is the combination of series-hybrid and parallel-hybrid. A control
strategy was developed for a series-parallel hybrid vehicle, which means the engine
and electric motor can work together or individually. The mechanical and electrical
power engage together in a compound way, and also, there is a planetary gear as

1.2
Problem Statement
Using PHEV reduces dependency on petroleum and CO2 emission, and achieving this
goal depends on battery size and the rest of the vehicle components. Hence, there
is intercommunication between fuel eﬀiciency and component sizing. Here, modeling
PHEV raises some questions:
• How can sizing components increase all-electric range?
• How much fuel consumption decreased?
• What is the improvement in fuel eﬀiciency when uses only a battery/ motor?
• Which powertrain model used to gain high eﬀiciency?
1.3
Research Objective
The main objective of this master thesis is to model a fully hybrid electric vehicle in
the Matlab platform. It has divided into more detailed sub-objectives systems. The
aim of the project is:
• Gain knowledge about the fuel eﬀiciency of the PHEV in two different scenarios
• Understanding of contribution of the main component in the hybrid system and
critical parameters to performance of the model
• Modeling of the electrical, mechanical, and battery systems in MATLAB/Simulink
• Compare the scenarios by affecting on fuel consumption
• Analyze and validate result from the Simulink model in different scenarios

• Implement of electrical, mechanic and battery system
• Implement the control strategy for two different scenarios

2.2
HEV Powertrains Topologies
The three topologies for HEV powertrains proposed:
• a) Series HEV and series PHEV: The series configuration uses only the
electric motor to propel the PHEV, the series powertrain needs two electric
machines.
In practice, for the series design, the battery pack will have the
ability to propel the PHEV at almost all speeds and acceleration rates. The
critical difference between a series and a parallel powertrain is that the series
powertrain has no mechanical linkage from the combustion engine to the wheels.
The combustion engine runs a generator that generates electricity to charge the
battery or turn the motor to propel the vehicle [28].
In series powertrain mechanical engine power transferred to electric power with
generator and then back to mechanical power with the motor, such the trans-
mission eﬀiciency is low. However, the engine can operate point (torque/speed)
to maximize the eﬀiciency of the engine [Ch.1 [4]].
While the electric motor has been connected to the inverter/converter, it looks
like a series EV. In this type, distribution power between two sources is ad-
vantageous during fluctuating speed scenarios. However, the difference in series
plug-in hybrid electric vehicle and series hybrids is powerful battery and inverter
but less powerful engine [P.1-11 [10]]. Diesel-electric locomotive use series HEV
type and e Chevrolet-Volt use series PHEV power-trains type.
• b) Parallel HEV and parallel PHEV: The parallel configuration uses an
ICE, with a traditional mechanical drive or an electric motor to propel the
PHEV. In parallel, the transmission needs to set up two torque sources, the
engine is mechanically coupled to the transmission, so engine speed depends

2.3
Comparison of Hybrid Levels
There are different hybridization levels between hybrids on the market: micro, mild,
full, and plug-in. Most of the mild hybrid and all full hybrid and plug-in hybrids have
a regenerative braking system. A regenerative braking system is a technology that
converts kinetic energy to chemical energy and stored in the battery when the driver
pushes on the brake pedal. In regenerative braking, the electric motor reversed; hence
instead of using electricity to turn the wheels, the rotating wheels turn the motor and
create electricity. Applying energy from the wheels to turn the motor slows down the
vehicle. This is done by the car’s force turning a generator connected to the wheels
that charge the battery [29].
• Micro Hybrid: A micro-hybrid combined with an alternator/starter that uses
==PAGE:22==

down the engine when the vehicle is resting, like in a traﬀic light, and auto-

During cruising, the vehicle is propelled only by ICE. As a result, a typical fuel

• Full Hybrid: Full hybrids use an engine as the primary source of power, and

use the electric motor as the only source of propulsion for low-speed and low-
acceleration driving, such as stop-and-go traﬀic or backing up. The electric-only
driving mode can greatly increase fuel eﬀiciency under some driving conditions.
When a full hybrid vehicle is started initially, the battery typically powers all
accessories, and the gasoline engine only starts when the battery needs to be
charged, or the accessories need more power than available from the battery.
For low-speed driving mode and reverse, the electric motor uses electricity from
the battery to power the vehicle.
In cruising mode, both the gasoline engine and electric motor are used to propel
the vehicle. The gasoline engine contributes power to the drive train directly
and to the electric motor with the generator. For heavy accelerating/ passing,
the gasoline engine and electric motor are both used to propel the vehicle, and
additional electricity from the battery may be used to power the electric motor
[29]. Typical fuel eﬀiciency increase around 40-45% compared to a non-hybrid
[31].

2.4.1
Operating Principles of PHEV
Plug-in hybrid electric vehicle compares with a hybrid electric vehicle demand greater
depth of discharge (DOD) due to the higher dependence on the electrical energy
source; because of the different operation patterns that PHEVs have from the HEVs.
Specific operation modes classify PHEV operations as charge-depleting (CD) mode,
charge-sustaining (CS) mode, AER mode, and engine-maintenance mode [6]:
• Charge Depleting (CD): In CD mode the vehicle only powered by on-board
battery until State of Charge (SOC) of battery is reach to lowest SOC limita-
tion. This mode is used at low speed in traﬀic jams and urban areas. In series
and parallel powertrain configurations, the PHEV considers operating power in
CD mode. In this mode, externally any ICE assistance until the battery pack
depleted, the PHEV operates 100% CD mode. When operating in CD mode,
including assistance from the ICE, the PHEV is considered operating in com-
bination CD mode. Therefore, a combination CD operation is possible in both
the series and parallel configurations [28].
CD mode is primarily used to take advantage of electric driving so that less
fuel is used and fewer emissions are produced. However, recharging the PHEVs
also helps to extend the CD range. By installing charging stations and imple-
mentation charging infrastructures in public areas such as workplaces, parking
lots, or in front of supermarket stores, PHEVs can be easily recharged, and CD
ranges can be significantly increased in daily driving.
• Charge Sustaining (CS): In charge sustaining mode, the engine is engaged,
and the vehicle works until the battery can recharge by the grid. The objective
of this mode is to operate two power sources as eﬀiciently as possible. This
mode is used when batteries are depleted and driven in charge depleting mode
[Ch.3 [4]].
CS mode uses both the engine and the electric machine to supply the vehicle
power while keeping the SOC of the battery pack at a constant level. In PHEV
operations, CS mode is more often used after the CD range when the battery
power is discharged to a certain low threshold; Then, the engine and electric
machine together start to supply the vehicle [6].
This advantage takes the HEV powertrain benefits; hence, high fuel eﬀiciency
is gained when the battery SOC is kept at a certain level.

• All-Electric Vehicles (AER): AER mode uses an energy source to power

Electric Motor
An electric motor is an electromechanical machine that is one of the critical com-
ponents of electric vehicles to converting electric energy to mechanical energy. An
electric machine is typically used to generate electricity to charge the battery or oper-
ate a motor called a generator, electric machines are reversible to use as a generator
or motor. This section is organized to provide brief information on electric machines.
The electric current flowing throws the wire core in the magnetic field, and it
has created the force which rotates the coil and creating torque. The electromag-
netic force is fundamental of electric machines and motion created by that. This
theory comes from Maxwell’s equation and describes that electricity and magnetic
should be considered interdependent phenomena [Ch.7.3 [36]]. In order to have a
high-performance drive using in Permanent Magnet Synchronise Machine (PMSM),
Field Oriented Control (FOC) chooses that is the best solution to high performance.
In most hybrid electric vehicle powertrain, using PMSM. The main advantages of
an electric machine, compared with an ICE, are [35]:
• Constant high torque at low speeds
• Very high eﬀiciency
• Instant torque delivery
• Energy recuperation capability

categorized by copper losses, core losses, mechanic losses, and stray losses. There
are two types of magnetic core losses, hysterics losses and eddy current losses [6]. In
permanent magnet losses allocated on stator side, especially copper losses and iron
losses, and rotor participate by eddy current losses and stray iron losses [37].

Hysteresis Losses
Hysteresis loss causes the magnetization and demagnetization of the stator in
different directions. The idea is that the magnetizing force (current) is not fol-
lowing the magnetic flux. Nevertheless, when the magnetizing force (current)
decreases, the magnetic flux follows gradually. So when the magnetizing force
reaches zero, the flux density still has a positive value. Once the flux density is
reached zero, the magnetizing force must be applied in a negative direction.
Hysteresis is dependent on the strength of magnetic flux density and excitation
frequency. An illustration of the residual magnetizing force by increasing and
decreasing the flux density is shown in figure 2.11.

Suppose the engine is connected in series with the electric machines since the electric

load or charging the battery when the vehicles operate in the hybrid electric mode to
extend the PHEV range.
Hence, the size and power rating of the engine can be minimized, and high engine
eﬀiciency is required at constant operating regions. On the other hand, if the engine
outputs power in parallel with the electric machines, the engine is responsible for a
large part of the powertrain’s power. Therefore, the engine should maintain its power
and size based on the power ratios in PHEVs between mechanical and electric power.
For some PHEVs with large battery packs, the engine may only work as a backup
when the battery depleted to extend the driving range and reduce customers’ range
anxiety. Engines in PHEVs may also apply different technologies compared with en-
gines in conventional vehicles. For example, the Atkinson cycle is used in some of the
PHEVs to improve vehicle eﬀiciency. Atkinson cycle allows the engine intake, com-
pression, power, and exhaust strokes that all happen in one revolution of a specially
designed crankshaft.
Toyota Prius Plug-in Hybrid, Ford C-Max Energi, and Honda Accord Plug-in Hy-
brid all use Atkinson cycle for their engine propulsion. Toyota Prius Plug-in Hybrid,
for instance, achieved 38.5% thermal eﬀiciency by using Atkinson cycle in its 1.8-L
gasoline engines [6].

2.5.6
Power Split Device (PSD)
The PHEV has the potential to reduce fuel consumption and to improve emissions. A
series-parallel PHEV and full hybrid electric vehicle typically use a planetary gearing
to split the engine power that the vehicle can optimally control to direct the engine
output. It is a gearbox that couples the output of the internal combustion engine
(ICE) and with the final stage of the electric motor/generator. It transmits the me-
chanical propulsion from motor, engine, and generator by combining them.
Power Split Device (PSD) allows to mixing the power from both sources, also
power independently, and creates an infinite range of gear ratios between the com-
bustion engine crankshaft and final drive [13]. PSD also allows the combustion engine
to charge the battery or provide power to wheels as needed [29].
It is using cyclic gear, which arranged in ring gear, sun gear, a carrier, and pinion
gear, where the engine connected to the carrier, generator to the sun gear, and motor
to the ring gear and final driveline.

Vector Control and Driving System
In a synchronous reference frame, vector control is the best way to control PMSM.
Moreover, vector control is a precise control method for steady-state and transients.
FOC (also named vector control or decoupled-current control) controls spatial vectors
of magnetic flux, stator current, and voltage.
This type of control is a closed loop that uses the speed of the shaft and provides
by the encoder as feedback in the control strategy. Also, FOC has a current loop to
control electromagnetic torque, which is produced by the machine. FOC can operate
smoothly over full speed, generate full torque at zero speeds, and accelerate fast accel-
eration and deceleration. The vector control with a pulse-width modulated (PWM)
inverter uses field-oriented control based on the voltage model of the PMSM motor.
The vector control system with a Current-Regulated PWM inverter uses FOC based
on the current model of the PMSM motor. So the type of inverter determines the
structure of the control scheme [46].
Research studies show that in AC and DC, the torque production mechanism is
similar, by using park transformation, current corresponding field-magnetizing flux
and torque generation in AC machines can be decoupled, so field magnetize flux can be
controlled without affecting the dynamic response of the torque and vice versa. One
of the strategies in FOC is to keep the d axis current Id = 0, and the produced torque
is proportional to the q current component. The main advantage of this control is
that it has accurate speed control and good torque response, and achieved full torque
at zero speed. High cost, and to drive the machine, a modulation technique is needed

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This work aimed to investigate different simulation scenarios account for the inves-
tigated two powertrain configurations based on fuel eﬀiciency in designing a plug-in
hybrid electric vehicle powertrain. In this study, a series-parallel plug-in hybrid elec-
tric vehicle by modeling electrical, mechanical, and battery storage systems to obtain
a reduction in fuel eﬀiciency of plug-in electrical hybrid mini-busses was subjected. A
permanent magnet synchronous machine (PMSM), internal combustion engine (ICE)
with planetary gear (PSD), and Li-ion battery energy storage are modeled.
Two different scenarios were modeled and simulated using MATLAB/Simulink
powertrain blockset to obtain the performance metrics, acceleration, driving range,
fuel eﬀiciency, and emissions. The equivalent consumption minimization strategy was
used as the energy management system in all examined simulations. PHEVs model
has the full ability to work on the AER for a specific demanded range and can be
directly charged from the off-board power grid. As a result, they provide high degrees
of fuel displacement and emission reductions.
The final simulation of a plug-in hybrid powertrain in the first scenario as a
blended mode strategy shows a 7.12 (L/100 km) engine with a 49 (kW) electric motor
and 32 (kW) battery pack. The second scenario, as all electric-range mode strategy
shows 2.71 (L/100 km) engine with 35 (kW) electric motor and 25 (kW) battery pack
simulated. The results showed that using an energy management strategy can attain
high fuel eﬀiciency even in an aggressive drive cycle.
