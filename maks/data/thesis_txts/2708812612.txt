Abstract
This master thesis presents an experimental investigation of non-linear systems. Prediction of struc-
tures behaviour in nonlinear systems is of importance in monitoring structures integrity and safety.
Numerical software are already used for nonlinear analysis, but not much research have been conducted
on experimental models to validate and verify the results.
Two steel specimens are exposed to visual plastic deformation, due to an base excitation generated by a
shaking table, by introducing an aluminium piece. The plastic deformation will arise, due to the strong
base excitation, and the plastic deformation are forced to take place in the weaker aluminium part of
the specimen.
Simultaneously two fiber-based numerical models with distributed plasticity are created in the software
Open System for Earthquake Engineering Simulation (OpenSees), with the main goal of accurately
imitating the response of the laboratory specimen in real time.
The experimental and numerical models are compared in terms of modal properties and responses. The
frequency domain decomposition (FDD) technique has been used to determine the modal properties of
the experimental models, and the modal properties of the numerical model is calculated internally.
Throughout the experimental testing, it was observed, that it was practically possible to excite the
specimen to permanent distortion. Further the experimental investigation concludes, that the numerical
models of OpenSees imitates the dynamic behavior of laboratory specimens to an adequately accurate
level. This conclusion is based on the factors of comparison of modal properties, signal error estimation,
linear dynamic behavior, nonlinear dynamic behavior and evolution of modal properties.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Effective damage detection is an important aspect in monitoring the integrity and safety of structures,
as undetected damages can lead to catastrophic failure involving loss of human lives and economical in-
vestments. Damage detection is associated with structural health monitoring (SHM), which has proven
to be of vital importance to maintain the original condition of structures.
The sustainability of a
structure depends on correct foresight of damage detection, because predicting harmful dynamic phe-
nomenons, in which structures are susceptible, is important. These are especially forceful phenomenons
of nature eg. wind, waves or earthquakes. Damages can also occur as various kinds of deterioration,

In damage detection a general standardized method is to calculate the modal properties of an initial

damage has been inserted. This method is based on a before and after calculation procedure of the

not capture the effect of the damage in the moment, it occurs. The prediction of the response for a

Theoretical background
This chapter will cover the necessary theoretical background to understand this master’s project. The
main focus will be on the theory of general structural dynamics for multi degree of freedom systems
and its application to strong ground motion responses.
3.1
Structural dynamics
The topic of structural dynamics processes how to understand the vibration pattern of structures, typi-
cally when subjected to horizontal forces, such as ground motion or wind. Structures can be categorized
as single degrees of freedom systems (SDOFs), or multiple degree of freedom systems (MDOFs).
3.1.1
Mass component
3.1.1.1
SDOFs
The SDOFs systems are idealized as a concentrated mass supported by a massless structure, with an
overall stiffness in the lateral direction. The SDOFs are a correct simplification of an overall vibration
pattern of a certain structure. The differential equation governing the lateral displacement u(t) without
any external excitation and damping can be described by Equation 3.1 [14] shown beneath for SDOFs:
m¨u(t) + ku(t) = 0
(3.1)
Here m equals the lumped mass of the structure, k is the stiffness and ¨u corresponds to the acceleration,
because an overdot denotes differentiation with respect to time.
3.1.1.2
MDOFs
The MDOFs systems are more advanced systems, where the discretization is defined with a finite
number of degrees of freedom (DOF). DOFs are defined, as the number of independent displacements
required to define the displaced positions of all the masses relative to their original position. The mass
is divided into sections of the structure, often by lumping storey-masses at intervals along the length
of the vertical axes. The MDOFs are preferred, when detailed investigation of forces and deformations

3.1.2
Stiffness component
3.1.2.1
Linear elastic
Linear elastic systems describes a linear relationship between the lateral force fs and resulting defor-
mation u.
This form of elasticity is often seen in materials, that are subjected to relatively small
deformations, where the loading and unloading curves are identical. The relationship for linear elastic
systems can be described as Equation 3.3 [14]:
fs = k · u
(3.3)
The linear relationship implies that it is a single valued function of the displacement, therefore the
stiffness remains unchanged during elastic deformation.
3.1.2.2
Inelastic
During a strong excitation the force-deformation relation, for a structural metal component undergoing
cyclic deformations, is expected to become inelastic at the larger amplitudes of deformation. This means
that the unloading and reloading curves differ from the initial loading branch. The force-deformation
relation becomes path dependent, which relates to the direction of deformation, which is increasing or
decreasing. This form of plastic deformation due to strong excitation is, what this master’s project
seeks to imitate correctly. How the material behaves, depends on the assumptions of the chosen stress
strain law. The beneath Equation 3.4 [14] describes the resisting force as an implicit function of the
deformation:
fs = fs(u)
(3.4)

3.4
Ductile material behavior
The behavior of ductile materials is important for this thesis, since the experiments will be conducted
on steel and aluminium specimens. In Figure 3.2 the engineering stress strain curve [17] is displayed.
Figure 3.2: The figure shows the engineering stress strain relationship for ductile materials. The figure

The proportional limit indicates, where the linear elastic region stops. Within this region, according to
hooks law, stress is directly proportional to strain and Young’s Modulus of Elasticity is constant. The
proportional limit is indicated as point A in Figure 3.2.
The elastic limit is the threshold for a ductile material to behave elastically. By exceeding the upper
yield stress point the ductile material will not be able to return to original shape, thereby permanently
distort. Therefore is the elastic region defined from the starting point until the point of B in Figure 3.2.
The plastic region occurs as the upper yield stress point is surpassed, which indicates the maximum stress
required to initiate plastic deformations. This means that ductile materials exhibit plastic properties.
In the plastic region, between the upper yield stress point and the ultimate stress point, strain hardening
will simultaneously occur. Strain hardening affects ductile materials, by making them stronger and more
diﬀicult to deform. Because increasing stress is required to produce additional plastic deformation.

4.6 Modal identification test of specimens

4.6
Modal identification test of specimens
Two modal identification tests of the concerned specimen has been performed before every dynamic
vibration test, to be sure to have a benchmark set of modal properties for the numerical model to
compare with.
This is done, to ensure that all screws have been tightened, and it is the correct
specimen which is being tested. Because accurate estimation of modal properties for the test specimens
are of high importance for this master’s thesis, in order to make a precise comparison between numerical
model and physical model.
4.6.1
Modal identification
The experimental modal identification is conducted using the Frequency Domain Decomposition (FDD)
technique introduced by Brinker et al. [21]. The method is based on output-only for which the modal
properties are identified. The method is based on measuring the response of a structure to a random
excitation, and calculate the modal properties by extract the singular values of the power spectral
density (PSD) matrix. The singular value component are plotted at the respective frequency line, and
the peaks on the plot will correspond to the natural frequency [21].
The OMA-toolbox, which contains several functions built in MATLAB, was provided as part of this
thesis in order to estimate the modal properties for the test specimens. A detailed description of the
toolbox and the functions can be found in [22].
4.6.2
Test methodology
A detailed description of the experimental modal identification technique is covered hereafter as iden-
tification of modal properties are a necessary part of this thesis.
Recent studies like [23], [24] have shown how environmental changes like temperature and wind speed
can affect the modal properties. On this basis all experimental tests on the specimens are performed in
a laboratory to avoid any external factors, which could affect the modal properties.
The basis of the modal identification method is to excite the specimen using a vibration based technique.
The excitation is conducted using a tool, like a pencil or an allan key, to excite the specimen with small
vibrations. The excitations must be random both in time and space to excite all important mode shapes
and provide reliable data. Further the excitation must not introduce any external force, as this will
lead to inaccurate data. The test duration of the excitation has to fulfill the requirements, stated in
Equation 4.1 [22]. Because the damping ratio has not been determined beforehand, a standardized
damping ratio of 2% was deemed satisfactory to use in Equation 4.1.

4.7
Generating ground motion signals
In this section the three signals, which have been used as excitation input for the shaking table, will be
introduced.
First and foremost the idea of three signals for the shaking table is founded on the ability to capture
the different behaviors of the aluminium material. The goal is to produce three different white noise
signals, that in their strength categorizes as weak, medium and severe excitation. Each signal have
different sought after material behavior, which can be seen beneath:
1. Signal weak, designed to trigger linear elastic behavior within the elastic region.
2. Signal medium, designed to trigger slight plastic deformation. The purpose is to reach at least
the transition region of the aluminium by crossing the proportional limit, but hopefully transgress
slightly beyond the upper yield point.
3. Signal severe, designed to trigger plastic deformation of the aluminium far beyond the yield point
into the plastic region.
The reason for these three categories is to test three different stages of the behavior of the material and
to be able to visualize the deformations during the laboratory testing.

Shaking table procedure

The procedure for exposing the four specimens to dynamic base excitation, is done by using a shaking
table of the model APS-400. The specifications about the shaking table can be obtained from this pdf
[27]. The shaking table will take one of the aforementioned signals as input. The program used for
collection of output and control of the input is the software NI Signal Express.
Sensors are placed strategically to capture the response of the specimen during the excitation. One
sensor is always placed on the shaking table in the direction of the excitation to measure the acceleration.
Regarding the columns, two sensors are placed, one midway on the specimen, and one all the way in
the top, both in direction of excitation. Regarding the frame four sensors will be placed, two for each
storey, measuring in the direction of excitation.
The signals will be executed sequentially, starting with the weak signal, progressing through the afore-
mentioned signals, ending with the final severe signal. There will be no adjustment of the specimen
in between the different excitation. This can possibly affect the frequencies a bit, which is not ideal,
but because of limited specimens, other options are not possible. The measured base excitation of the
shaking table, will be fitted to start at zero, since the starting point of the measurements is not exactly
zero. The obtained excitation will now be ready to be processed in the numerical model.

CHAPTER 5
Numerical setup
This chapter will deal with the numerical modelling of the laboratory specimens in this thesis. The
numerical part is the essential core of the thesis, since the main goal is to be able to numerically
imitate, by comparing and validating the modal properties and response of experimental investigation
by a replica numerical model in OpenSees.
5.1
Introduction to numerical setup
The numerical models are created for the purpose of validating the methodology of the experimental
investigation. This validation between numerical and experimental models is based on a principal of
gradually increasing the detailing of the experimental, but especially the numerical models. Firstly a
2D structure in the presence of a simple cantilever beam will be modelled and fitted to the experiential
model. Afterwards the diﬀiculty progresses to a 3D two-store one-bay frame. The operation and success
criteria of the model comparison remains the same for all models. First success criteria is to imitate
the modal properties, ie. natural frequency, in OpenSees, to be within close proximity to those of the
experimental model. Secondly is aimed for a reasonable match of the response between the numerical
modal and the experimental model, afflicted by the same excitation. The purpose of matching the
natural frequencies is to gain confidence and substantiate the fact, that the OpenSees models are
identical to that of the experimental models. Whereas the match in response will provide confidence in
the non-linear calculations and results, to ensure the observed response of plastic deformation can be
imitated and thereby verified by the numerical model.
5.2
Opensees numerical software
The open source software Open System for Earthquake Engineering Simulation (OpenSees) is used
for numerical modelling in this thesis. OpenSees is developed by the Pacific Earthquake Engineering
Research center (PEER) with the main scope target research and educational fields. OpenSees is a
software without a graphical user interface (GUI), thus demands the user to be cautious in modelling.
OpenSees works as an interpreter, where the models are built in the programming languages TCL or
Python upon the wide library OpenSees provides. The benefit of OpenSees is expressed in the variety
of materials, elements and analysis parameters, thus making it suitable to conduct nonlinear analysis.

obtained from the numerical analysis.

6.2.6
Frequency evolution
When materials undergo plastic deformation, energy will dissipate from the system. This will cause
a change in the stiffness matrix, since the deformation weakens the material in the moment it occurs.
The change in the stiffness matrix can be measured and converted into a change in natural frequencies.
This change in natural frequencies relates to how big the damage is, since large drops in the frequency
also portrays large deformations. The numerical model is able to capture the frequency evolution for all
of the signals, which are shown in Figure 6.14, Figure 6.15 & Figure 6.16. For the weak signal, which
portrays linear elastic material behavior, there is no expected frequency drop, which appears clearly in
Figure 6.14. For the medium signal it is possible to see a very small frequency drop at times, where the
yielding point of the stress and strain curve is reached. These drops in frequencies are very low and is in
compliance with the moment curvature, of the aluminium piece for the medium signal in Figure 6.11b.
The severe signal creates a significant drop in frequency, and by plotting the exact time of where the
nonlinearity occurs together with the evolution of the frequency, it is obvious, that it is the nonlinearity,
which provokes the frequency drop and what time it happens. Unfortunately it has not been possible
to validate the frequency evolution calculated by OpenSees. Therefore will the frequency evolution only
appear for the beam and not the frame.

6.4.6
Sources of errors
It has been discovered, that the experimental specimens used in this thesis are not the most optimal
structures. This is discovered in hindsight. The reason for this is, because of the connection piece.
In OpenSees the modelling of the connection is very simplified, which actually makes the structure
too stiff. Another source of error, could be because all our experiments are run in succession, which
mean that, when the last severe excitation is exposed to the structure, it can possibly have changed in
frequencies, due to the other signals. This change is primarily due to loosening of connections, together
with possible minimal deformations inflicted by the medium excitation. In hindsight it would have been
advantageous to have a laboratory specimen, which is completely rigid in the elements, that are of no
interest and flexible in the elements of interest. It is also a good idea to have higher frequencies, so small
changes does not inflict big deviations of the natural frequencies, as identified in the column specimen.
==PAGE:85==
CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Four laboratory specimens have been examined during this master project, consisting of two cantilever
columns and two frames, in order to answer the main goals of this master’s thesis, stated as:
• Can a numerical model in OpenSees imitate the response and plastic deformation of a laboratory
specimen?
The experimental investigation concludes, that the numerical models of OpenSees imitates the dynamic
behavior and detects plastic deformations of the laboratory specimens to an adequately accurate level.
This conclusion is based on comparison of modal properties, signal error estimation, linear dynamic
behavior, nonlinear dynamic behavior and evolution of modal properties.
There have been successfully established numerical models in OpenSees with similar modal properties
as the laboratory specimens. The largest allowable deviation in the modal properties for the first mode
is 3%, which still yields quite good comparison in response between the experimental and the numerical
model.
The quantity of the deviation is around 0.05Hz, which is evaluated as being insignificant.
The rise in deviation occurs, when introducing a secondary material and a connection piece, leading
to expanding the complexity of the model. Thereby it has been proven, that the modal properties,
calculated by OpenSees, is in agreement with those identified, using the FDD technique.
The OpenSees model has succeeded in predicting behavior in the elastic region for all signals, in which
the laboratory specimens have no visual plastic deformations. The numerical prediction of linear behav-
ior is supported, by two methods of investigation, including displacement figure and moment curvature.
When comparing the responses, due to the excitation, an agreement of at least 89% was seen. The good
agreement in the responses has confirmed the elastic observation of the numerical and experimental
models. The accurate imitation of the response between the models has been of primary concern, since
it defines the most credible premise for the detection of permanent distortion in OpenSees. Overall
OpenSees mimics linear response of the laboratory specimen close to perfection.
It has been found practically possible to excite some of the laboratory specimens to be exposed to
inelastic deformation, due to the severe excitation. From the experimental investigation of the column,
permanent plastic deformation has been observed only in the aluminium pieces. It has been possible to
observe plastic deformation in both steel and aluminium for the frame. Further the OpenSees model

has succeeded in predicting all the permanent distortions of these laboratory specimens. The inelastic
behaviour has been confirmed by the moment curvature and with an offset in the displacement figure.
By comparing the responses of the numerical and experimental models in the nonlinear regime an

with increasing number of inelastic deformations the similarity decrease.

It has not been possible to confirm the frequency evolution, calculated by the numerical model in

By this thesis it has been established by experimental investigation, that OpenSees is a very reliable

CHAPTER 8
Future work
The experimental work performed during this master’s thesis proves to show plastic deformation in the
aluminum pieces, when the laboratory specimens are excited by the severe signal on the shaking table.
However, for the frame specimen it has been observed, that the steel column just beneath the first
steel slab is exposed to plastic deformation as well. For further work, it will be interesting to move the
aluminum pieces to other vulnerable locations of the specimen, and investigate the plastic deformation
there.
The connection piece fulfilled the requirement of attaching an aluminum piece to a steel column, however
it was discovered that the errors related to the numerical modelling were related the simplified modelling
in OpenSees of the connection piece. Therefore it is suggested to find alternative ways to model the
connection piece or rethink the connection in the laboratory specimens.
In general an optimization of the models is deemed advantageous, since it has been discovered, that the
beam is prone to errors due to standardization of models that span over natural frequencies of 1.42Hz
to 2.42Hz, regarding the sensitivity of the low frequencies.
The frame is prone to errors regarding
unexpected deformations. It is advised to build stiffer steel columns, that ensure deformations only
take place in expected locations.
8.0.1
Interesting observations
The experiments is conducted in sequence of the weak, medium and severe excitation without changing
nor touching the specimen. It has been observed, that the target frequency of the specimen, in order
to obtain the best correlation, is not identical. Lets for instance assume that the model change slightly
during each excitation. It could be interesting to further explore this phenomenon, and make a numerical
model that would take this into consideration.

A.2
ShellMITC4
”element ShellMITC4 $eleTag $iNode $jNode $kNode $lNode $secTag”.
The ShellMITC4 element uses bilinear isoparametric formulation with modified interpolation[28]. The
arguments which have to be defined is a element tag, the four corner nodes (defined in counter-clockwise
direction) and the a section tag, which refer to a previously defined section. For this thesis only the

A.3
ElasticMembranePlateSection
”section ElasticMembranePlateSection $secTag $E $nu $h $rho”.
The ElasticMembranePlateSection is an isotropic section and suited for plate and shell analysis. The
arguments which have to be defined is a unique section tag, the modulus of elasticity (Young’s), Poisson’s
ratio, depth of section and a mass density. When a mass density is defined a consistent mass matrix
will be established however rotational mass will be ignored.

A.4.3
Geomteric transformation
The geometric transformation is the procedure to move from a local coordinate system to a global
coordinate system in OpenSees. The types of geometric transformation which are used through this
thesis is Linear- and PDelta transformation, for the frame and beam respectively.
Frame 3D:
geomTransf Linear $transfTag $vecxzX $vecxzY $vecxzZ
Beam 2D:
geomTransf PDelta $transfTag
For a 2D problem the argument is only the transformation tag which have to be defined. The orientation
of the local x-axis follows the element length and if the element is aligned with the positive y-axis the
local y-axis is orientated with the negative x-axis. The local z-axis is found by the right-hand rule [28].
For a 3D problem there are no predefined orientation of the local axis, and thus, apart from a transfor-
mation tag, also the orientation have to be defined in a vector [x, y, z] eg. [0, 0, 0].
