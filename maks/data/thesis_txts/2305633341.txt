Abstract
One of the most important quality parameters concerning soft drink concentrates is
the viscosity. This project is made in collaboration with CO-RO A/S, who produces
fruit-based soft drink concentrates. The soft drink concentrates are added sodium
carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) to increase the viscosity. However, the presence of
cellulase originating from the fruit will aﬀect the desired functional properties of the
ﬁnal product. Cellulase is able to degrade cellulose in the added CMC, leading to a
viscosity loss.
The current method for estimating the cellulase content is by rheological measure-
ments. The apparent viscosity is measured at two time intervals; within in 1 hour
(before degradation) and after 3 days (after degradation) hereby relating the viscos-
ity loss with the cellulase activity. The main drawback of this method is the long
waiting period (3 days). Therefore, a faster detection method for cellulase using
thermal measurements was investigated using a thermal activity monitor (TAM).
The viscosity loss was conﬁrmed by measuring both the apparent and complex vis-
cosity at the two time intervals, and based on ﬂow measurements the solutions were
found to be shear-thinning. The viscosity loss was investigated during 3 days to
investigate the behaviour of the viscosity loss. Results showed that the viscosity loss
was a gradually occurring process.
A model capable of predicting the cellulase content in fruit concentrates was devel-
oped based on thermal measurements. The model was based on a simpliﬁed system,
with a constant CMC content while varying amount of cellulase (from Aspergillus
niger). The model was able to predict cellulase content within the simpliﬁed sys-
tem, however, when applied to fruit concentrates it was not able to distinguish fruit
concentrates according to their cellulase contents.
It was concluded that even though the degradation of cellulose by cellulase could be
measured with the TAM in a simpliﬁed system, further work is needed before the
method can be applied in practice.

Introduction
Fruit-based soft drinks are consumed all over the world and are available in almost
all shapes and sizes, tastes and concentrations. Soft drinks are a widely used term,
however, it has no strict deﬁnition but is normally used to describe water-based
sweetened beverages containing fruit pulp.
Acid, most commonly citric acid, is often added to soft drinks. Other ingredients
such as colour, antioxidants and polysaccharides can also be added, changing the
functional properties of the ﬁnal product. The ﬁnal product can be divided into
two main groups: soft drink concentrates and ready-to-drink products (often car-
bonated) [1, Ch.1].
The quality of soft drinks can be deﬁned from diﬀerent perspectives, the consumers’,
the wholesalers’ and the industries’ among others. Quality parameters include tex-
ture, colour, ﬂavour and the stability of these parameters. A common requirement
made by the consumers is identical products from batch to batch. This requires
information about the raw material which will change with the seasons and manu-
facturing process. The process should be adjusted according to the raw materials,
assuring low batch-to-batch variation [1, Ch. 2].
A general description of a fruit beverage production is provided to gain an overview
of the processes and process conditions. A ﬂowchart showing the most common unit
operations can be seen in Appendix A.
The production begins with the reception of the raw fruit. It is important to ensure
the quality of the fruit as it will directly inﬂuence the properties of the ﬁnal product
[2, Ch.1].
The next step in the process is crushing the fruit. This can be followed by heating
and addition of enzymes. Enzymes are often added to increase the yield and clarity
1
==PAGE:7==
1. INTRODUCTION
of the ﬁnal product. Pectinase is used to extract and clarify, it degrades soluble
pectin (a polysaccharide) present in the cell wall of plants. This increases the rate
of the extraction process. Cellulase can also be added to increase the yield by de-
grading the cellulose matrix in the fruit [2, Ch.12][3]. Additionally, cellulase can be
used to increase the yield of pigment from the skin of the fruit [4, Ch.1].
The fruit is now ready to be mechanically pressed. If a clear juice is intended as
the ﬁnal product the next step is to clarify the juice. This can be done by the addi-
tion of ﬂocculating agents which promote the aggregation of suspended particles in
the liquid, increasing sedimentation. After these steps the juice is centrifuged and
ﬁltered, both steps help remove larger particles from the juice. The juice is then
pasteurised (ensuring food safety), cooled, packed, stored and distributed [2, Ch.
12, 15].
The fruit concentrate is produced by lowering the water content hence producing a so
called concentrate. This can be done by evaporation where water is removed from
a liquid food, however, heat labile compounds such as aroma will be lost during
evaporation if the correct process measures are not taken. This could for exam-
ple be the addition of an aroma distillation column where the heat labile aroma
compounds are collected during the evaporation and re-introduced at a later stage
ensuring the aromatic qualities of the concentrate [5, Ch. 14]. As described, each
production step contains parameters which can be varied or modiﬁed depending on
the intended properties of the ﬁnal fruit beverage product.
This master’s thesis is made in collaboration with CO-RO A/S, which is a Danish
owned company producing a wide range of fruit based beverages such as Sunquick,
Sun Lolly and Suntop.
The aim of the collaboration was to investigate the eﬀects on the functional prop-
erties when adding a polysaccharide to soft drink concentrates. At CO-RO sodium
carboxy methyl cellulose (CMC) is added to increase the viscosity of soft drink con-
centrates, ensuring particles in the soft drink concentrate will be evenly distributed
throughout the concentrate. However, the possible presence of cellulase from fruit
concentrate is a problem in the ﬁnal product.
Cellulase is able to degrade cellulose in the added CMC, this lowers the viscosity of
the soft drink concentrate hereby eﬀecting the desired functional properties of the
concentrate. To address this problem CO-RO has developed a rheological method
2

where the cellulase activity is quantiﬁed by measuring the loss in viscosity of the fruit
concentrate after the addition of CMC. The problem is solved by dilution; the fruit
concentrates which have a high concentration of cellulase are diluted with samples
which have a lower concentration. This method takes three days and more eﬃcient

The aim of this master’s thesis is to optimise the method for detecting cellulase

concentrate added CMC both before and after the viscosity loss caused by cellulase
degrading cellulose from CMC are investigated and characterised.

Enzymes
Enzymes are proteins which selectively catalyse numerous biological reactions. They
are naturally present in fruits and vegetables and are an important factor during
growth and ripening. The ripeness of fruits and vegetables are essential for quality,
however, unwanted enzymatic reactions can also take place. These can impact a
wide range of parameters from colour and texture to ﬂavour and odour. It is there-
fore essential to understand the enzyme kinetics in order to optimise product quality
and stability [4, Ch.1].
A reaction takes place when two reactants collide and interact hereby forming a
product, however, the reaction can only occur if the energy from the collision is high
enough to overcome the activation energy. A catalyst increases the reaction rate by
lowering the activation energy. The Gibbs free energy (i.e. the thermodynamics)
remains unchanged, an enzyme is only able to increase the reaction rate but cannot
change the equilibrium of a reaction [10, Ch.23].

active site of the enzyme, E, forming a complex, ES, which is then broken down to
the product, P, and the enzyme:

Cellulase has a more complex mechanism which is described below.
3.1
Cellulase
Cellulase is a group of enzymes that are able to degrade cellulose. The composition,
number of components and mechanism depend on the origin which cellulase derive
from [12, 13]. During the last decades there has been an increasing interest in cellu-
lase, and its mechanism, due to its role in the conversion of plant biomass (cellulose)
to fuels and chemicals [9, 14, 15, 16].
The degradation of cellulose is impacted by a number of parameters such as the
degree of polymerisation, accessible area and the presence of lignin. Cellulose degra-
dation involves a multienzyme system. One suggested mechanism includes endoglu-
canase, exoglucanase and β-glucosidase, a simpliﬁed overview is shown in Figure

The degradation of cellulose by cellulase can be described by burst-phase kinetics;
an initial burst followed by a fast decline in the reaction rate. The remaining degra-
dation of cellulose takes days. The mechanism of the reaction course is aﬀected by
product inhibition, enzyme ratio, and the substrate concentration [17]. The high
solid content in the fruit concentrates or CMC solutions might hinder enzymatic
activity due to crowding [18].
Cellulases are commercially produced mainly from

and to a
lesser degree Aspergillus niger [9, 19]. Trichoderma spp. is an insuﬃcient producer
of β-glucosidase but the most eﬃcient producer of endoglucanase and exoglucanases.
Aspergillus niger has a more eﬃcient production of β-glucosidase [19]. It is a fungus
which is found ubiquitously in soil, and is known as black mould infecting certain
fruits and vegetables. The composition of the enzyme depends on the requirements
of the habitat [14]. In this thesis cellulase from Aspergillus niger was used during
the experimental work.
Measuring the Enzymatic activity of Cellulase
The catalytic activity of cellulase is generally diﬃcult to assay. Several methods have
been described to estimate the activity in real-time, however, getting precise results
have proven diﬃcult since no optical change occur. The methods are generally based
on measuring the change in product concentration over time. They often include an
inactivation step, puriﬁcation, and extraction of sugars, however, the results become
uncertain when trying to convert the concentration measurements to a reaction rate
[17].

Food Additives
Food Additives are used throughout modern food production. Food additives are
substances intentionally added during processing to change functional properties of
a given food, and are also classiﬁed as substances which are not characteristic ingre-
dients of food or consumed as food on its own [20, 21].
In the European Union (EU) food additives are identiﬁed with an E-number and
must be included on the ingredient list of the food product. Furthermore, function
of the additive should be speciﬁed [20, 21].
Food additives can improve the food quality by controlling the structure and mois-
ture of the food in combination with prolonging shelf life. Food additives are classi-
ﬁed according to their chemical nature and their functionality. A food additive can
have more than one function hence being a so called multifunctional additive [2, 22].
The addition of food additives is also used to meet consumers’ demands for food
and beverages; it must have consistent properties such as taste, colour, and texture.
The consumers’ view on additives also aﬀect the food industry. Some additives are
viewed as unwanted whereas others are more ”label friendly”. The EU has for ex-
ample been lobbied to accept the name cellulose gum on labels instead of sodium
carboxy methyl cellulose (CMC) [22, Ch.6].
In this thesis the main focus of food additives was polysaccharides functioning as
stabilisers, thickeners, and gelling agents in fruit concentrates.
4.1
Functional Properties of Polysaccharides
Polysaccharides are high molecular weight polymers. They are often classiﬁed ac-
cording to their chemical structure e.g. if they are linear or branched and the types

of monosaccharides. Polysaccharides are also known as hydrocolloids characterised
as water loving, long-chain polymers, often referred to as gums and derived mainly
from plants [7, Ch.3], [22, Ch.1]. This section provides an overview of their func-
tional properties:
Viscosity
Hydrocolloids are to a wide extent used to increase the viscosity of food products,
which will change the texture and mouthfeel of the product. One application is in
fat-reduced products where fat is removed and hydrocolloids are added to ensure
that the same texture as the full-fat product [22].
Suspension
A suspension is made by changing the settling point.
This can be done by the
addition of hydrocolloids which create a yield point. The yield point ensures that
the insoluble particles are immobilised in the suspension. A common example is
salad dressing where xanthan gum is added to e.g. suspend spices [22].
Emulsion
An emulsion is created by mixing two (or more) immiscible liquids. This is done by
reducing the size of droplets of one liquid so it will be dispersed within the other
liquid. However, the stability of the emulsion can vary and hydrocolloids are added
to stabilise the emulsions. Typical applications are ice creams where carrageenan,
locust bean gum and guar gum are added [22, 5].
Gelatinisation
Common types of gelling agents are carrageenan, gelatin, pectin and agar.
Gel
formation is often used for desserts, some gelling agents will form the gel when it
comes in contact with calcium ions (dairy based products) [22].
4.2
Sodium Carboxymethyl Cellulose
Cellulose derivatives are commonly used to modify functional properties of foods.
They are produced from cellulose and are used as stabilisers and viscosiﬁers in the
food industry. The production of cellulose derivatives was ﬁrst patented in Germany
11

in 1918 where it was introduced as a viscosiﬁer of cake dough. The applications of
cellulose derivatives are now enormous. Cellulose derives from the cell walls of plants.
For the production of cellulose derivatives wood and cotton are the most common
sources [22, Ch.6].
CMC, also known as cellulose gum, is a white, odourless, tasteless powder, and the
simpliﬁed chemical structure can be seen in Figure 4.1. The functional properties
of CMC depends on the structure; the degree of polymerisation (DP), the degree
of carboxymethyl substitutions (DS), and its uniformity of the substitution. Each
repeating unit has three sites where the substitution can take place and therefore a
theoretically maximum of three carboxymethyl substitutions. However, the substi-
tution levels are not this high and commercial types typically have a DS of up to
1.5 [22, Ch.6].

CMC is commercially available in diﬀerent grades having varying functional prop-

5.1
Rheology
Rheological data is used for a large number of applications: process engineering,
analysis of food texture and shelf life analysis to name a few. In rheology there is
a distinction between Newtonian and non-Newtonian materials. A Newtonian ﬂuid
is deﬁned by having a proportional relationship between the shear stress and the
shear rate with an intercept through zero. Fluids not obeying this are referred to
as non-Newtonian. The rheological behaviour of food products is most commonly
characterised as non-Newtonian [26, Ch.1].
Another distinction is the time dependency of a material. When a material, at con-
stant temperature and pressure, is subject to a constant shear rate and the viscosity
changes over time it is classiﬁed as time dependent. If the viscosity only changes
with changing shear rate it is time independent. In Figure 5.1 a schematic overview
of the classiﬁcation is given. It shows the distinction between non-Newtonian ma-
terials. However, a material can be classiﬁed as time independent and thixotropic;
one classiﬁcation does not eliminate another [26, Ch.1]. The ﬁgure also presents
a number of the most common mathematical models used to describe rheological
behaviour, these will be further presented in Section 5.1.2.

5.1.1
Selection of Geometry
Selection of the right type of geometry used to measure the rheological parameters
is essential. The choice of geometry is based on a large number of considerations
such as the viscosity of the sample, the amount of sample available and needed to
get a representative sample, does the sample include larger particles, and is it prone
to slip during measurements [26, Ch.1].
The most common choices of geometries include the parallel plate, the cone plate,
and concentric cylinder. For the experimental work in this project a 60 mm serrated
parallel plate (60 PP Ti serrated) set-up was mainly used. The larger plates were
chosen to increase the representativeness of the sample and the serrated surface was
chosen to minimise the risk of slip during measurements. In addition, a solvent trap
was used to avoid evaporation during measurements.
5.1.2
Selection of Mathematical Model
Flow measurements or rotational rheology can be used to investigate properties such
as the consistency index and the ﬂow behaviour. The choice of model used to de-
scribe the ﬂow behaviour is inﬂuenced by numerous parameters. Foods containing
hydrocolloids will often show a shear-thinning behaviour [22]. In the following sec-
tions the most common models and terminology used to describe concentrated fruit
drinks containing hydrocolloids are introduced. Additional mathematical models
can be found in the literature.
The Power Law
The power law is often used to describe the behaviour of hydrocolloids at low con-
centrations where the solution is time-independent [22, Ch.6] [24]. The power law
relates shear stress, τ, to the shear rate, ˙γ:
τ = m · ˙γn
(5.1)
where m is the consistency index and n is the ﬂow behaviour. The ﬂow behaviour
can be classiﬁed according to the value of n as following:
n < 1 : Shear-thinning
n = 1 : Newtonian behaviour
n > 1 : Shear-thickening

5.1.3
Thixotropy
Thixotropy is used to describe the time-dependency of a solution. This is often
quantiﬁed by a hysteresis loop where the area can be used as an estimation of the
degree of thixotropic properties. The larger the area, the more thixotropic. How-
ever, the area of the hysteresis loop will depend on the experimental conditions and
is therefore not generally comparable. Measuring the hysteresis loop is often done
by increasing the shear stress which will decrease the viscosity. This is followed by
decreasing the shear stress where the viscosity will recover hence creating a loop.
The thixotropic behaviour is caused by changes in the food structure. After the
food added polysaccharides is produced and packed, it will settle and form a three-
dimensional structure. When shear stress is applied the structure will break and
lose viscosity. After resting, the food will once again settle and the structure will
recover (or partly recover), thus regaining its viscosity [26].
5.1.4
Yield Stress
Yield stress is a widely discussed phenomena. Yield stress is deﬁned as the minimum
shear stress required to initiate ﬂow. In 1985 Barnes and Walters published the
article ”The Yield Stress Myth” where they challenged the yield stress phenomena
by arguing that everything ﬂows given suﬃcient time or sensitive enough measuring

equipment. Even though yield stress remains a much discussed topic it is commonly
used as a parameter in food engineering where production time is a limiting factor
[26, Ch. 1].
5.1.5
Viscoelasticity
The viscoelastic behaviour of a ﬂuid can be characterised by oscillatory rheology,
where stress or strain is applied harmoniously to the sample. This method can be
used to understand the elastic and viscous behaviour of viscoelastic food. Oscillation
can also be described by the moduli, and the complex viscosity, η∗. The elastic
(storage) modulus, G′ is a measure of elasticity (the ability of the material to store
energy), and the viscous (loss) modulus, G′′ measures the ability of a material to
dissipate energy (energy lost as heat) [26, Ch. 5].
A common experimental method consists of two parallel plates where a sample is
placed in between. The lower plate is ﬁxed whereas the upper is moved horizontally.
A frequency sweep can for example indicate whether the structure is predominantly
elastic or viscous. Knowing the dominating modulus can provide information about
the stability of the fruit concentrate solutions, if sedimentation is likely to occur,
and if the complex viscosity is dependent or independent of the increased frequency
[27].
5.1.6
The Cox-Merz Rule
The Cox-Merz rule can be used to describe the empirical relationship between the
apparent viscosity from rotational measurements, where the shear rate is varied,
with the complex viscosity from oscillatory measurements, where the frequency is
varied. A ﬂuid follows the Cox-Merz rule when:
η∗(ω) = η(˙γ)|ω= ˙γ
(5.5)
Where, ω, is the frequency in rad/s from the oscillatory measurements when cor-
rected with 2π. If the rule applies, it can be assumed that viscosities are independent
of the measuring method. This can be helpful when deciding how to measure. The
rule applies to many synthetic and biopolymer dispersions. The rule has been ap-
plied to a large number of food materials, however, no conclusive correlation has
been made. Tomato paste follows the Cox-Merz rule when introducing a shift factor
[26, Ch. 5].

Selection of Operating Parameters
Rheological properties are dependent on the choice of operating conditions. The
temperature aﬀects a wide range of the properties and should be chosen carefully.
This choice should be based on diﬀerent considerations i.e. is the experiment de-

to the sample during measurements. The number of points recorded during the
measurement will also aﬀect the results.

5.2
Microcalorimetry
Calorimetry can be deﬁned as the quantitative measurement of heat changes that
takes place during any reaction [28, 29]. The ﬁrst calorimeter was made by Lavoisier
and Laplace in 1780, and was used to measure heat changes during respiration. The
calorimeter consisted of a closed container surrounded by ice. In the container a
guinea pig was placed, and during respiration the ice melted. The melted ice was
weighed and used as a measure of generated heat [30, 31].
The ﬁrst calorimeter used to study food was made by Dubrunfaut in 1856, and
was used to study heat changes during fermentation of wine [32]. Many types of
calorimeters are nowadays used, and in this thesis the main focus is isothermal
calorimetry where the measurement of heat (thermal power) is performed at constant
temperature. In general, calorimetry can be described by
∆Q = C(T) · ∆T
(5.6)
where ∆Q is the change in heat (J), C(T) is the heat capacity which is temperature
dependent (J/K), and ∆T is the observed temperature change [28, 29].
For small heat changes (a few degrees), the temperature is proportional to the ex-
changed heat and the constant of proportionally is the heat capacity.
In isothermal calorimetry the temperature change is corrected by applying (or sub-
tracting) electrical energy in the same magnitude. This compensation is based on
the Peltier eﬀect [29]. The temperature diﬀerence between the glass ampoule and
the heat sink will be converted into electric voltage by the thermopile which is then
recorded, and the thermal power, P, measured in W/g can be calculated by:
P = ϵVs −Vbl
M
(5.7)
where ϵ is the calibration coeﬃcient of the calorimeter in W/V, Vs is the recorded
voltage from the sample, Vbl is the recorded voltage from the baseline and M is the
mass in g of the sample [33].
For the microcalorimetric experiments a Thermal Activity Monitor (TAM III, TA In-
struments) was used. The TAM III can be operated in isothermal, step-isothermal
or temperature-scanning mode. The isothermal mode was chosen; the two main
functions of this mode are to ensure the isothermal conditions and detecting ther-
mal changes from the sample. The temperature of the liquid bath (containing oil)

method [28, 30].

The heat signal is the result of two samples, the sample and a reference, a so called

However, it is important to keep in mind that isothermal calorimetry is a general

5.2.1
Thermal Processes in Fruit
The challenge when measuring enzymatic activity in fruit concentrate by isothermal
calorimetry lies in the additional thermal processes which also take place. Numer-
ous thermal processes can occur in fruit concentrate; enzymatic activity, microbial
activity, and chemical processes such as sugar inversion.
Microﬂora is naturally present on the surface of fruits. It consists of a variety of
23
==PAGE:29==
5. EXPERIMENTAL METHODS
yeast, moulds, and bacteria. During ripening, environmental aspects play an impor-
tant part. Factors such as air, soil, compost, insect, physical damage, infestation,
and water will aﬀect the rate of the spoilage. The fungal infection of a fruit is often
dependent of the entering of the primary cell wall. Most fungi cannot enter the cell
wall without cell wall degrading enzymes or physical damage of the fruit. Once it
has entered the skin, the fungus has good growth conditions [34, 12].
Fruit based products are often pasteurised and/or otherwise heat treated, which will
lower the concentration of microﬂora. However, some pathogens are able to survive,
mainly the heat labile and acid tolerant ones. Survival of the pathogens can be
caused by insuﬃcient conditions during pasteurisation (time and temperature) or
an uncommonly high concentration [34, 12].
In addition, production conditions such as the addition of enzymes, pasteurisa-
tion temperature, bulk storage, and bottling will also aﬀect the concentration of
pathogens. Ingredients, such as sugar, antioxidants, and preservatives, added to the
products will also aﬀect which reactions that can, and are, taking place during the
shelf life [34, 22].
5.2.2
Applications in Food Science
Calorimetry has been used to study a number of phenomena related to food. F.
G. Galindo et al.[33] describes the use of isothermal calorimetry as a tool for mon-
itoring and predicting the quality of minimally processed fruit and vegetables, as
an example of how a factory producing minimally processed fruit and vegetables
(mainly peeled and cut) can use it throughout the production is used. The paper
investigated the optimal ratio between surface area and volume of the cut fruit, the
mixing of fruits, and the eﬀect of antibrowning treatments. This is done by measur-
ing the heat development which is related to the metabolic behaviour. These studies
can help optimising shelf life [33, 35].
C. Alkint et al. [36] used isothermal microcalorimetry to predict the shelf life of car-
rot juice. In this paper it was concluded that the colony forming unit (the number
of bacteria or fungal cells in a sample) was not directly related to the isothermal
measurements but could be correlated with the sensorial shelf-life. Nevertheless, the
method can be used to accelerate shelf life studies.
In a review by L. Wads¨o et al. [32] an overview of the applications in food science
was provided. Among other application the paper presents; wound respiration of
fruit and vegetables, milk fermentation, microbiological spoilage where the addition

Study of enzyme kinetics using isothermal calorimetry have also been done. The
use of isothermal titration microcalorimetry has been used to investigate the degra-

activity of cellulase by isothermal titration calorimetry. Here the hydrolysis heat of

The frequency sweep was performed to investigate the viscoelasticity of the solutions.
The stress was kept constant in the linear viscoelastic region estimated from the

Method
The rheometer was equipped with a 60 mm serrated plate geometry and
9 mL solution was transferred. The frequency was increased from 0.01 Hz to 100 Hz.
Based on the stress sweep the stress was kept at 1 Pa throughout the measurements.
Results
The elastic modulus, the viscous modulus, and the complex viscosity are
plotted against the frequency for the 1.5 % and the 3 % CMC solution in Figure

solutions, as described in Section 4.2. In addition, the complex viscosity of the 3 %
CMC solution decreases with increasing frequency, whereas the 1.5 % CMC solution
remains stable with increasing frequency.

6.1.3
Temperature Sweep
A temperature sweep was performed to investigate the rheological properties of
the samples after heating. During the production of fruit-based soft drinks heat
is applied multiple times. This can eﬀect the functional properties of the added
hydrocolloids, and the temperature sweep is made to investigate these eﬀects.
38
==PAGE:44==
6. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
Method
The sample was left to settle for 3 minutes before starting the temper-
ature sweep. The temperature was increased from 20  to 70  during a 4 min
period, at 1 Hz and 1 Pa. This was followed by cooling to 20 , where the temper-
ature remained constant for 5 minutes before a frequency sweep was conducted.
Results
The results of the frequency sweeps (after heating) showed no noticeable
change when compared with the original frequency sweep performed within 1 hour.
The results are shown in Appendix D. As expected, the complex viscosity and the
moduli all decreased with increasing temperature, as a result of the heat enabling
the particles to move further apart.

7
Discussion and Future Work
The degradation of cellulose by cellulase was successfully characterised by rheological
measurements as well as thermal activity measured by TAM.
Throughout the experimental work a number of simpliﬁcations and assumptions
have been made to facilitate the complexities of the problem. These include, among
others, the composition of cellulase in the fruit pulp, the CMC solution, and keeping
the pH and temperature constant.
Investigating a wider pH range could be useful for the full understanding of the CMC
solution, as the stability of CMC changes around pH 3 [22, Ch. 6]. This also applies
for the temperature which will aﬀect both the enzymatic activity and rheological
properties. The basis of the assumptions and simpliﬁcations, and their eﬀect on the
results are further discussed in the sections below.
7.1
Loss of Viscosity
The change of functional properties impacted by the degradation of cellulose in CMC
aﬀects the stability. The degradation of cellulose causes a loss in viscosity which is
measured by CO-RO using Analyseforskrift nr. 65, Appendix B. The method allows
cellulase to degrade cellulose by leaving the samples in a heating cabinet for 3 days
at 40 °C. This requirement was investigated by measuring both the apparent and
complex viscosity throughout 3 days, see Section 6.4. Here it was conﬁrmed that
the degradation continuously takes place during 3 days. Furthermore, the measured
viscosities were plotted against time to investigate the appearance of the degrada-
tion, see Figure 6.12.
A future development could include additional measurements of the complex viscos-
59
==PAGE:65==
7. DISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
ity during the degradation of cellulose. Gathering information about degradation
curves for fruit concentrates with diﬀerent cellulase activities could initiate the de-
velopment of a predictive method.
If a suﬃcient amount of measurements (range of cellulase activities) were gathered,
the slope of the linear part of the degradation could possibly be used for prediction.
Further work would be needed to deﬁne the relation of changes in the complex vis-
cosity during time, and at diﬀerent frequencies. The method could consist of mixing
fruit concentrate with the CMC solution placing it at 40 °C and measuring the vis-
cosity a suﬃcient number of times (3-5) during a work day. The slope of the new
fruit concentrate sample could then be compared with slopes from the ”database”
and the ﬁnal viscosity loss could hereby be estimated.
The method, in Analyseforskrift nr. 65, only includes measuring the apparent vis-
cosity at 48.88 s−1 (Appendix B). To see if this method is suﬃcient for assessing the
viscosity the apparent and complex viscosity was compared using the Cox-Merz rule.
It was found that the viscosity loss could be more accurately deﬁned using the com-
plex viscosity measurements (a frequency sweep). The current method (Appendix
B) used to assess the viscosity loss was therefore found to be somewhat insuﬃcient.
Comparing the loss in viscosity should be done carefully keeping in mind the nu-
merical diﬀerence between the apparent and complex viscosity.
The rheological properties were further investigated by looking at frequency sweeps
both before and after degradation of CMC. Results showed a large decrease in vis-
cosity after degradation in combination with a predominantly viscous solution. This
explains why the solutions are no longer able to suspense particles in the solution
resulting in sedimentation (Section 6.1.2).
7.2
Enzyme Kinetics
The degradation of cellulose by cellulase is a complex multienzymetic process. The
degradation of cellulose was measured as a viscosity loss using rheology and as
thermal activity with calorimetry. The degradation was in both cases investigated
at constant pH and temperature. The temperature was kept at 40 °C, as it is the
theoretical optimum temperature for cellulase and hereby increases the rate of the
degradation [39].
However, calorimetry measurements could be used to ﬁnd the
optimum temperature of cellulase in the fruit concentrate based products.
The

TAM could be operated at diﬀerent temperatures around the theoretical optimum
temperature of cellulase. The rate of the thermal activity could then be used to ﬁnd
the optimum temperature, which could be used for optimising experimental work.
Another parameter which can aﬀect the enzyme kinetics is the solid content of the
substrate [15, 18]. A high solid content might hinder the mobility of the enzyme
resulting in a lower reaction rate. This parameter has only partly been accounted
for during the experimental work. The concentration of the CMC solutions where
varied between 0.3 %, 1.5 %, and 3.0 %, while the fruit concentrate content was kept
constant, as seen in Table 6.7. However, the results did not show any signiﬁcant
trends, therefore further work is needed to investigate this parameter.
A correlation between the solid content and enzymatic activity could be interesting
to investigate by calorimetry. The experimental design could consist of solutions with
a constant concentration of cellulose and cellulase while varying the concentration
of an additional thickener. The additional thickener should not be degradable by
cellulase e.g. xanthan gum. If the enzymatic activity is dependent on the solid
content the calorimetric measurements would show decreasing thermal activity for
increasing xanthan gum content.
These results could help optimise the ratio of
solutions for getting faster results.
7.3
The Predictive Model
A model able to predict the content of cellulase was successfully developed using mi-
crocalorimetric measurements (Section 6.5.3). The basis of the model was isothermal
microcalorimetric measurements on a 3 % CMC solution with varying amounts of
cellulase from Aspergillus niger. Results showed that the model was able to predict
the cellulase content within this simpliﬁed system, however, diﬃculties arose when
applying the model on fruit concentrate data. The predicted cellulase content did
not correspond to the assigned cellulase activities by CO-RO, and could not be used
to distinguish the fruit concentrates. The results of the predicted cellulase content
for the fruit concentrates were aﬀected by the assumptions which the predictive
model was based on.
The model uses the slope of the normalised heat as an input to predict the cellulase
content. Based on the burst-phase kinetics of cellulase it was estimated to work
with the slope from 10-20 hours. This assumes that the slope in this range would
describe both the burst-phase but also the rapid decline in reaction rate (thermal
61
==PAGE:67==
7. DISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
activity). However, the length of the burst-phase and decline might change both
for diﬀerent concentrations and compositions of cellulase. Looking at the thermal
activity as an expression for the reaction rate is also an assumption as explained in
Ref. [17], where the relation has been experimentally conﬁrmed. Here the thermal
activity is described as a true measure of hydrolysis rate.
The slope of the fruit concentrate was corrected before using it as an input in the
predictive model. The slope was corrected by subtracting it with the slope of the
corresponding pure fruit sample. Here it was assumed that after the subtraction, all
thermal activity would be caused by the degradation of cellulose. However, the cor-
rection might cause an underestimation of the cellulase activity. Part of the thermal
activity in the pure samples might be caused by cellulase degrading the naturally
occurring cellulose. This could lead to a general underestimation of the cellulase.
Moreover, the model is only based on Aspergillus niger which is a simpliﬁed so-
lution.
The source of cellulase in fruit derives from diﬀerent sources which will
aﬀect the ratio between endoglucanase, exoglucanase, and β-glucosidase. As a con-
sequence, this will change the reaction rate (thermal activity), see Section 3.1. This
assumption will increase the uncertainty of the prediction when using the model on
fruit concentrate with an unknown ratio between the diﬀerent enzymes.
7.4
Additional Thermal Processes
To quantify the cellulase content the thermal activity in fruit concentrate was mea-
sured, however, accounting for all thermodynamic processes and separating them is
a challenging task; as stated in Section 5.2 the TAM provides an unspeciﬁc output.
The measured thermal activity can derive form any reaction. Therefore designing
an experiment to investigate the desired reaction becomes the main challenge.
Thermal processes in pure fruit were investigated as discussed in Section 7.4. The
results were used as a rough estimate for all additional thermal processes. However,
this method does not account for the source of thermal activity hence there is no
distinction between microbial, chemical or enzymatic reactions.
Sodium benzoate is added to the CMC solution in ratio 2 g/kg as a preservative to
inhibit or reduce the activity of microorganisms. It is assumed that the cellulase
activity in the fruit concentrates is present prior to the addition and is not aﬀected
by the addition of sodium benzoate [40]. However, the thermal activity of pure fruit
62
==PAGE:68==
7. DISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
concentrate would most likely be aﬀected by the addition of sodium benzoate. This
is not accounted for when measuring the thermal activity and using it as a corrective
measure for predicting the cellulase content of fruit concentrate solutions. The eﬀect
of the sodium bezoate should be investigated to gain information about the changes
it has on the thermal activity. Investigating the eﬀect of sodium benzoate would
optimise the baseline and hereby the baseline.
To further investigate the source of thermal activity a number of solutions were au-
toclaved, see Section 6.5.4. The aim of the autoclaving was to eliminate biological
reactions and hereby only measure the thermal activity from chemical reactions.
The thermal activity deriving from chemical reactions were measured and ranked by
the slope of the normalised heat. The ranking corresponded to the amount of fruit
concentrate thus the solutions are now independent of the enzymatic activity.
However, the high temperature of the autoclaving can have initiated reactions which
would normally occur with a lower reaction rate or not at all. Based on visual ob-
servations it was clear that the autoclaving impacted the properties of the solution.
The solutions had changed colour from orange to brown.
This is most likely a result of non-enzymatic browning, both caramelisation and the
Millard reaction [41]. Caramelisation is the pyrolysis of sugar whereas the Millard
reaction is a chemical reaction between an amine-containing compound and a reduc-
ing sugar [7]. Based on these considerations it was decided that the measurements
could not be used as a ways of distinguishing chemical and biological reactions as
they do not represent normal chemical activity.
7.5
Relating Rheological and Microcalorimetric
Measurements
Both rheological and microcalorimetric measurements were able to detect the degra-
dation of cellulose. Even though the microcalorimetric method provided a faster way
of estimating the cellulase activity (in the simpliﬁed system) only the rheological
measurements could be used to understand the eﬀects on the functional properties.
Assuming the ranking of cellulase activities and the predictive model could be op-
timised for thermal measurements a combination of the two methods could provide
a possible way for faster measuring the enzyme activity. However, a correlation be-
tween the thermal activity and the change in rheological properties would be needed.

Conclusion
The viscosity is an important quality parameter for fruit-based concentrates. In
this project, the viscosity loss of fruit concentrate mixed with CMC was success-
fully by rheological measurements, and the thermal activity of cellulase degrading
was successfully measured with TAM. Measurements were performed after two time
intervals, within 1 hour (prior to degradation of CMC), and after 3 days (after degra-
dation of CMC).
Rheological measurements conﬁrmed the viscosity loss in fruit concentrate mixed
with CMC. Both the complex viscosity and the apparent viscosity showed the vis-
cosity loss. These were compared with the Cox-Merz rule for Pineapple 860 mixed
with a 3 % CMC solution in a 1:10 ratio, which showed that only measurements
made within 1 hour followed the rule. The numerical value of the viscosities after 3
days were therefore found to be dependent on the measurement method (oscillatory
or ﬂow). In addition, the degradation of cellulose by cellulase was continuously in-
vestigated throughout 3 days. The results showed that the viscosity of the solutions
gradually decreased over 3 days.
Thermal experiments, made prior to mixing solutions, showed thermal activity indi-
cating additional thermal processes taking place. A predictive model was developed
based on a simpliﬁed system consisting of a 3 % CMC solution and varying amounts
of cellulase from Aspergillus niger. The model was able to predict an amount of
cellulase within this simpliﬁed system. The predictive model was used on solutions
consisting of fruit concentrate and CMC solution with the aim of predicting the
cellulase content. The results were, however, not comparable with the assigned cel-
lulase values from CO-RO. Ways to improve the predictive model were discussed in
Section 7.3.
The use of rheological measurements as a possible predictive tool for the viscosity

The idea of using a combination of thermal measurements and rheology was dis-

Even though the results obtained during this project indicate that the TAM can
