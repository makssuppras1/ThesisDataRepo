Abstract 
 
Triclosan is one of the most commonly found chemicals in sludge at waste water treatment plants and as sludge is 
used as fertilizer on fields, triclosan ends in the environment. This study aims to explore different climate 
conditions’ effect on triclosan toxicity towards plants. A test with pea plants, Pisum sativum L., in vials with clay 
granules and nutrient solution with different concentrations of triclosan, control, 1 mg/L, 3 mg/L and 8 mg/L 
was carried out. A set of controls and plants exposed to the three triclosan concentrations was placed in each of 
four different climate chambers, one chamber with ambient conditions (19°C, 400 ppm CO2), one chamber with 
elevated temperature (24°C, 400 ppm CO2), one chamber with elevated CO2 (19°C, 700 ppm CO2), and one 
chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 (24°C, 700 ppm CO2). The transpiration rate for each plant was 
measured during the experiment. The results showed, that the toxic response to triclosan was strongest in the 
chamber with elevated temperature, where an EC50 of 0.24 mg triclosan/L was found, the second highest toxic 
response was in the chamber with elevated CO2, where the EC50 was 1.80 mg triclosan/L. The combined effect 
of elevated temperature and CO2, showed greater resistance towards toxicity from triclosan than the chambers 
with only one elevated climate condition especially for 1 mg/L exposure, and the EC50 value for this chamber was 
found to be 2.69 mg triclosan/L. In the ambient chamber, the toxic effect was smallest, causing an EC50 of 5.24 
mg triclosan/L which is 22 times lower than in the chamber with elevated temperature. The uptake study showed 
8.2-92.3% of the added triclosan found in the low exposure plants with an average of 48%, but no trend was seen 
in these data or plant concentrations of triclosan in relation to climate conditions. Metabolism of triclosan could 
have had an important effect on these data. Therefore, this study concluded, that changing climate has an important 
impact on the toxicity of triclosan towards pea plants, but not on accumulated concentration, and the toxic effects 
are expected to be higher in the future climate.

Introduction 
Triclosan is a biocide widely used because of its antimicrobial abilities in a range of different products as toothpaste, 
mouthwash, deodorants, cosmetics, antibacterial soaps, shampoos, plastic, toys, hospital equipment, household 
cleaning products, antibacterial sportswear and many other (Samsøe-Petersen et al. 2003 ; U.S. Food & Drug 
Administration 2008). 99% of the use of triclosan is in personal hygiene products as toothpaste and deodorants 
(Miljøstyrelsen 2013), which will end in the household drain and be led to the waste water treatment plants. Here 
most of the triclosan is removed as it binds to the sludge (Mogensen et al. 2008 ; Samsøe-Petersen et al. 2003 ; 
Danish (Q)SAR Database, year unknown), which is later used as fertilizer on the fields and enters the environment. 
In Mogensen et al., triclosan and 31 pharmaceuticals suspected to cause the largest environmental problems were 
investigated. Sewage from Danish treatment plants was tested and triclosan was found in 10 of 10 samples and 
was the compound found in the highest concentrations. It is possibly problematic, as triclosan has been assessed 
to be toxic to the aquatic environment, able to accumulate in the environment and is potentially endocrine 
disrupting and can potentially develop triclosan resistant bacteria (Miljøstyrelsen 2013 ; Bedoux et al. 2012). In 2003 
(Samsøe-Petersen et al. 2003) it was assessed, that triclosan did not impose a risk to the aquatic environment from 
the effluent concentrations of triclosan in water treatment plants although the PNEC value was lower for water 
than for soil. For soil, there was potentially an environmental risk, when fertilising fields with sludge, as the risk 
quotient (PEC/PNEC) was estimated to be between 0.4 and 219 (no effect has a PEC/PNEC<1). Only worms 
and plants were tested and the most sensitive organism in the terrestrial environment was a plant. Therefore, 
knowledge about triclosan’s toxic effect to animals as well as plants are of importance. In the EU, it was decided 
in 2015 to ban the use of triclosan in disinfectant soaps and biocidal products as mosquito repellent and algae 
removal products (Miljøstyrelsen 2015). In Denmark, triclosan is now only used in few cosmetics and as an 
industrial chemical, which should lower the amount that ends up in the environment. 
A toxicological response from an organism depends on many different parameters. One that can affect toxicity is 
climate. The climate of the world is now changing and in the future, some of the important changes include the 
increase of the average temperature on Earth and the atmospheric concentration of CO2. Increases in temperature 
and CO2 are already seen and are currently happening at a rate of averagely 1.3°C globally each century (Dessler 
2012) and 2 ppm CO2 each year (Siewertsen 2016). Therefore, the changing climate’s effect on toxicology should 
be investigated. This leads to the problem statement of this study: 
How are elevated temperature and atmospheric CO2 concentrations affecting the toxicity of triclosan towards pea 
plants? 
The hypothesis is, that elevated temperatures will make the plants transpire more, leading to an increased uptake 
of triclosan. Elevated CO2 concentrations are expected to reduce the stomatal opening of the pea plants, which 
will lower the transpiration, thus leading to a decrease in toxicological response. To test this hypothesis pea plants 
will be exposed to triclosan in 4 different climate chambers: one with ambient conditions, one with elevated 
temperature, one with elevated CO2 and one with both elevated temperature and CO2.

Previous research 
Toxicity of triclosan 
There are several studies on the toxicity of triclosan, here the primary focus will be on plants. Prosser et el. (2014a) 
studied the effects of triclosan on seed emergence and plant growth on radish, carrot, soybean, lettuce, spring 
wheat and corn. The tests ran for 34-85 days, and the test was conducted in soil with triclosan. For radish, carrot 
and soybean, the soil was mixed with biosolids containing 7.6-46 mg triclosan/g dw and in another experiment 
soybean, lettuce, spring wheat and corn was tested in soil mixed with biosolids containing 7.6-307 mg triclosan/g 
dw to resemble agricultural conditions. Only radish showed a decrease in seed emergence, with the biosolid added, 
which in the article is suspected to be of other reasons than triclosan poisoning, and all plants except corn showed 
improvement in growth when mixed with biosolids compared to not mixed with biosolids even at the highest 
triclosan exposure. 
Other studies provide EC50 values for different plants. In a study of rice and cucumber (Liu et al. 2009), it was 
found, that roots were more sensitive than shoots. For rice (Oryza sativa L.) a root and shoot length EC50 was found 
to be 57 mg/kg and 243 mg/kg respectively, and for cucumber (Cucumis sativus L.) a root and shoot length EC50 
of 108 mg/kg and 277 mg/kg respectively, was found. An aquatic toxicity test found algae to be the most sensitive 
aquatic organism of the tested (also included lemna, daphnia and fish), where a 96 hour test with reduced biomass 
as the target parameter of Scenedesmus resulted in an EC50 value of 1.4 µg/L (Orvoz et al. 2002). Another study 
collected toxicological data from other studies and presented them. Here, toxicity values for 2 additional 
angiosperms were stated: bigpod sesbania also known as coffeweed (Sesbania herbacea (Mill.) McVaugh) with a seed 
germination LOEC of 100 µg/L and a root length LOEC of 10 µg/L and wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) with a root 
and shoot length EC50 of 147.8 mg/kg and 195.6 mg/kg respectively (Bedoux et al. 2011). Sesbania herbacea was 
the most sensitive of the four and is in the Fabaceae family like the pea plant tested in current study. Therefore 
the pea plant could also be sensitive to triclosan. 
Several studies found, that triclosan is degrading into metabolites in soil. In one study (Prosser et el. 2014a), it was 
observed, that during their experiment, which was between 34 and 85 days, the triclosan amount in the soil had 
reduced 84-97% and had a half-life in soil mixed with biosolid of 12.7-193 days. In another study, it was found 
that only 4.4-23% of triclosan was left in soil after 64 days (Samsøe-Petersen et al. 2003). Triclosan is also broken 
into metabolites in plants (Macherius et al. 2014 ; Prosser et al. 2014b) 
 
 
The effect of climate change on toxicity 
No studies were found, that had tested the effect of either elevated temperature or CO2 on triclosan toxicity. Yu 
et al. (2007) tested the weeping willows’ uptake and metabolism of cyanide, which showed no accumulation in the 
plants with temperature though the metabolism rate increased with increasing temperature. The transpiration of 
the trees was increased with increasing temperature and no plants showed sign of toxic effects. A study of elevated 
temperatures’ effect on the uptake of Cd and Cu into rice showed, that a higher amount of the metals was taken 
up at increasing temperature (Ge et al. 2015). 
 
The effect of increased CO2 concentrations on plants is known to be reduced stomatal opening, resulting in 
decreased transpiration (Taiz et al. 2015). Most C3 plants’ photosynthesis is at ambient levels limited by the CO2, 
level, which means that an increase will lead to higher photosynthesis and also an increase in growth if the plants 
are not limited in nutrients or light (Taiz et al. 2015 ; Lambers et al. 2008). If the transpiration is lowered as an effect 
of an elevation in the CO2 level, the water use efficiency (WUE) is expected to increase as well (Ågren and 
Andersson 2012).

Method 
The pea toxicity test 
To make the pea toxicity test with triclosan, a nutrient solution was mixed in accordance with the modified version 
of ISO 8692 nutrient solution recipe as presented in Appendix A, Table I. Some of this was used for mixing a 
triclosan solution, where 1 L of nutrient solution was poured into each of two 2 L conical flasks, where 8 mg of 
triclosan was added to each flask along with one stirring magnet. The flasks were wrapped in tinfoil to prevent 
photochemical reactions, the top was sealed of, and the flasks were placed in a fume hood, each on a magnetic 
stirring device until the triclosan had dissolved completely (days). The triclosan stock solution was then transferred 
to blue cap bottles covered with tinfoil and stored in a cold room (approximately 5°C). The other exposure 
concentrations were made by mixing some of the stock solution with nutrient solution and were also covered in 
tinfoil and stored cold. The remaining nutrient solution was likewise stored cold to minimise the number of 
uncertainties.  
The peas were dried Pisum sativum L. which were put in a beaker and covered with water for 46 hours, before they 
were put in a germination apparatus (see Figure 1.Left), distributed in 3 different layers with a few millimetres of 
demineralised water in each layer, ensuring at least 1 cm of space in all directions between the peas, to prevent 
decay. The peas were left germinating for 5 days, and the placement of the layers in the germination device was 
switched regularly in the meantime (i.e. top layer switched to the bottom position and next time to the middle 
position). When the peas had roots between 2 and 6 cm in length, the peas were put in 40 ml test vials. Prior to 
this, the vials had been numbered, weighed, filled with around 13 g of seramis (clay granules) and weighed again. 
A system was used to assign the peas to the vials ensuring random selection, decreasing biased choosing. The pea 
was carefully inserted in the seramis by shaking the root down into the seramis with the pea on top, laying on or 
free of the seramis. The apparatus was weighed again and the assigned concentration solution filled in. The vials 
were placed in cardboard holders with space between each vial as seen in Figure 1.Right. The peas were put in 
climate chambers and the exact placement of the box was marked. 
 
 
Figure 1: Left: Germination apparatus with germinating peas. Right: The pea test setup 
The pea test was carried out in 4 climate chambers located at RERAF (Risø Environmental Risk Assessment 
Facility), DTU Risø. The chamber settings were an ambient chamber, a chamber with elevated temperature, a 
chamber with elevated CO2 and a chamber with both elevated temperature and CO2; the specifications for the 
settings for each chamber can be seen in Table 1. During the experiment, the temperature, CO2 and humidity in 
each chamber was measured, and the data can be seen in Appendix B, Figure I-IV. In each chamber, peas were 
tested in 4 concentration groups with 4 replicas in each group and 4 blind samples (without peas) were tested. The 
concentrations were control (0 mg/L), 1 mg/L (low), 3 mg/L (medium) and 8 mg/L (high). The light level at

actual content of triclosan; this was also done in the middle and end of the experiment to see if the concentration

The pea plants were taken out with 1-2 day intervals, where each vial was weighed to determine the water loss 
since last measuring point. The target result is each plants transpiration, but the top of the vials was not covered, 
so the measured value is transpiration from the plants and evaporation of fluid from the vial. After each plant was 
weighed, the plants that needed refilling of solution were refilled. A balance between filling up the vials and letting 
the water level decrease as much as possible without drought was sought, as the roots of the peas had to be aerated 
to prevent oxygen deficit. The plants were then placed back in the chambers in their marked positions, ensuring 
that the orientation of the plant was in the same direction as previously. This procedure was repeated every time 
the plants were attended to during the experimental period of 17 days. Since the solutions were stored cold, they 
were taken out as early as possible the days where solution was filled in, to avoid exposing the plants to a big 
temperature decrease, which could affect their systems and influence the results. In the end of the experiment, 
each tested apparatus was weighed, then the pea plants were carefully taken out and cut into root and shoot and 
each part was weighed. The pH was checked in fluid left in the vials, to see if this was different than expected. The 
plants were dried in a freeze dryer and weighed again. 
 
 
Other laboratory work 
A test to determine the partitioning coefficient, Kd, of triclosan’s partitioning between seramis and solution was 
made by filling 3 conical flasks with seramis and triclosan stock solution, which was wrapped in tinfoil and sealed. 
The top was also covered with parafilm. The flasks were placed in a 20°C room and each flask was retrieved at 
different times (after 1 hour, 2 hours and 67 hours), and the fluid in the flask was poured into a vial for analysis. 
A test at time 0 hours was also taken directly from the stock solution. 
 
The plants exposed to the low concentration dose were analysed with a GC/MS (Gas Chromatography Mass 
Spectrometry). The fluid samples from the Kd experiment, and the fluid samples of the solutions with different 
concentrations used in the pea test were analysed as well. 
 
Uncertainties 
The largest uncertainty of the pea toxicity test was maintaining the balance of the water level in the vials, ensuring 
that the plants did not experience neither drought nor oxygen depletion which both affect the health of the plants 
and therefore disturb the picture of the climatic impact on triclosan toxicity. The plants that transpired the most 
ran out of water in less than 24 hours at the end of the experiment making it difficult to keep up with the water 
refilling. The plants transpiring the least could have their roots completely covered by solution for many days in a 
row which could lead to an extra plant inhibiting factor intensified by, but not directly caused by the poisoning. 
Besides this, differences in water level in the vials could have an impact on the evaporation from the vials, causing 
a background evaporation that is not constant. Furthermore, the way this test is done, where the toxicant is added 
==PAGE:10==
7 
 
at the very beginning of the experiment, makes it impossible to determine which plants are naturally unhealthy 
especially for the high concentrations. Values from plants that seem as if they did not develop as a healthy plant 
would, are discarded. An unhealthy plant or a plant that did not thrive after planting it in the seramis, is easy to 
spot for the controls and the low concentrations, but as the toxicity level increases, it is impossible to see, which 
plants never started developing naturally and which were so inhibited by the high levels of triclosan, that they never 
grew. This means that all plants are included in the high concentration group, but plants are taken out of the other 
concentration groups, potentially resulting in data showing a higher toxic effect for the high concentration group 
plants, than the real effect. 
Besides this, there were uncertainties concerning the stock solutions of triclosan as these were mixed with magnets, 
that had a coating of plastic. Triclosan has an affinity for plastic which means, that triclosan might sorb to the 
magnet, decreasing the concentration in the solution (Koc et al. 2014). Also in one of the triclosan stock solutions, 
impurities were found in the flask after mixing. The impurities looked like parafilm, which it likely was as the 
parafilm covering the top of the flask had broken. Here a sorption to the parafilm pieces could occur, resulting in 
a lower concentration in the high concentration groups filled with this solution, and if the pieces end up in one of 
the vials, an increased exposure could occur.  
In the Kd test, some of the seramis had pulverised and some of the seramis dust was transferred with the fluid 
into the vials for GC/MS analysis. As time passed from the extraction of the fluid into the vial until the analysis 
was made, some of the triclosan might have continued to partition between the soluble phase and the seramis, so 
the time the sample should represent is not the time where the partitioning stopped, and could affect the result of 
how fast an equilibrium is reached between triclosan in solution and in seramis. 
 
Data analysis 
All plant data was assessed focusing on the reliability of the data and excluding erroneous data, which was data 
from the plants that had died from another cause than toxic response to triclosan. This was done by investigating 
the amount of transpiration for each plant throughout the experiment, comparing it to the transpiration of the 
other plants in this group and to the level of the blinds. If a plant gave transpiration values in the same range as 
the blind evaporation, and not in the same range as the other plants from this concentration group and visual 
characteristics did not imply that the plant had lived, the data from this plant was removed from the dataset.

(NRT), which makes the toxic response among the chambers more comparable. The following equation was used

Discussion 
Investigation of the environmental factors’ fluctuations  
 
To make sure, that the data collected was actually representing the climate situations intended, data from sensors 
at RERAF of the climate conditions throughout the entire experimental time period, was looked into. The 
temperature fluctuations can be seen in Appendix B, Figure I and II. In Figure I it is seen that most fluctuations 
were within ±0.04°C and no values were far above or below the set point for chambers set to 19°C (ambient 
chamber) and 24°C. For the chamber with elevated CO2, something went wrong in the temperature data extraction

There might have been a non-stable humidity level in the chamber with elevated temperature, which could impact 
the data. If this is the case, fluctuations in the transpiration in this chamber should be detected. 
Light 
The light level was measured for each plant position to see if the differences in light influenced the data. The light 
within each chamber differed from 5.8 % in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 to 13.9 % in the 
chamber with elevated CO2. The light level was however lower in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 
compared to the others, as the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) was only around 140 µmol in this 
chamber, around 210 µmol in the chamber with elevated CO2, and around 260 µmol in the ambient chamber and 
the chamber with elevated temperature. Therefore, the low light level in the chamber with elevated temperature 
and CO2 could affect the difference among the chambers.

The pea toxicity test 
 
Transpiration 
The transpiration data measured throughout the experiment is presented in Figure 2. Here it is clearly seen, that 
triclosan have an effect on the transpiration rate of pea plants within the range of tested concentrations as, for all 
chambers, the transpiration rate of the plants exposed to the low concentration (1 mg triclosan/L) is higher than 
the transpiration rate of the plants exposed to the medium concentration (3 mg triclosan/L), which again has a 
higher transpiration rate than the plants exposed to the high concentration (8 mg triclosan/L). The controls are 
transpiring more pr. hour than the low concentration exposure plants for most cases, but in the ambient chamber 
the controls are transpiring less. Different things can have caused this, and to see if the actual effect of the triclosan, 
NRT should be investigated as the difference in transpiration rates could be caused by healthier plants in the low 
concentration group in the beginning of the experiment. For all chambers, not much variation is seen in the 
transpiration rate for the plants exposed to the high concentration of triclosan, which means that this concentration 
was very toxic no matter climate conditions, and a strong inhibition on the growth was seen here (Appendix E, 
Figure 6). 
The average transpiration rate figure is useful to get an overview of the differences in transpiration rates caused by 
the climate effects. In the ambient chamber, the control transpiration rate is low compared to the other 3 chambers 
at t=280 or 266 although the transpiration rate for the plants exposed to triclosan is quite similar to the values 
found in the chamber with elevated CO2. As there is least difference between the transpiration rate of the controls 
and the plants exposed to triclosan in the ambient chamber, it seems as the effect of the toxicant is smallest here. 
In the elevated temperature chamber, the transpiration rate for the control is higher than in the ambient chamber 
as was expected, and here it is seen, that the poisoned plants are all much affected, and only the plants exposed to 
low concentrations can actually be said to transpire still as the transpiration rate in the medium and high exposure 
group is below the blind, which means that it is uncertain how much is actually transpiration and how much is 
simply evaporation from the vials. The toxic effect could be caused by the increased transpiration rate caused by 
the temperature which leads to more refillings of triclosan solution and therefore higher uptake of triclosan in the 
early stages of the plant development. It was expected, that the transpiration pr. hour in this chamber would be

higher than in the chamber with elevated CO2, especially for the controls, but the difference was not as pronounced 
as expected. 
 
If there was in fact a large variation in the humidity in the chamber with elevated temperature, it might be seen in 
the transpiration; the transpiration rate could increase with decreasing humidity, caused by a more negative water 
potential at low humidity. In that case, an elevation of the transpiration rate should be seen at the same time as the 
minimums in the humidity figure, at t=100-180 and t=310-330, which is not the case. At 100-180, the plants are 
still transpiring very small amounts and growing slowly, so an increase here would not be visible. It should be 
visible at 310-330 hours if the humidity had decreased, but no increase is seen at this point, so in case the humidity 
did change, it does not look like it affected the plant transpiration much. 
 
In the chamber with elevated CO2, the transpiration rate of the control is much larger than expected compared to 
in the ambient chamber, and is at the same level as the control in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2, 
until 266 hours, where the transpired mass pr. hour starts to decline in the chamber with elevated CO2. The control 
plants in this chamber were large and the decrease can be caused by the plants shading each other and lack of 
space. In the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 the transpiration pr. hour was surprisingly high and it 
does not seem as if the low concentration of triclosan had a significant effect on the plants in this chamber. It was 
excepted, that the transpiration rate would be at a level between the ones found in the chamber with just one of 
the parameters elevated, as it was expected, that the parameters had opposite effects on transpiration rates. If a 
plant experience multiple stressors at once, the effect of one stressor might worsen the effect of another; for 
instance, drought and high temperatures combined are worsening the effect of one (Taiz et al. 2015) or the effects 
of two stressors could alleviate each other. In this case, they both separately result in increased transpiration pr. 
hour, and when combined an increased transpiration rate, compared to ambient conditions, is achieved.   
 
NRT 
In the figure showing the NRT (Figure 3), it is easier to compare the toxic effects among the chambers. Here it is 
seen that an elevation of the temperature is, as suspected when looking at the transpiration rate graph, the climate 
condition in which triclosan is most toxic. In this chamber the NRT for all exposed plants is below 45% after 280 
hours. This gives a very low EC50 of 0.24 mg/L, which is the lowest found in this experiment. The pattern for the 
chamber with elevated CO2 is similar to the one for the elevated temperature, except the NRT for the plants at 
medium and low concentration stop their former rate of decrease at t=200. The NRT for both actually improves 
slightly in the end. It was expected, that the plants in this chamber would transpire less than in the other chambers, 
and therefore take up less poison, and be the plants that experienced the least NRT decrease. However, the 
accumulated transpiration in this chamber was much above the ambient and at the same level as in the chamber 
with elevated temperature and CO2.  
 
The ambient chamber was where the decrease in NRT was happening at the slowest pace. Especially until 180 
hours, the decrease is similar for all the exposed plants within the chamber, and after this point, the low 
concentration NRT increases ending around 85% and the medium in this chamber at t=280 is above 60%, resulting 
in a high EC50 of 5.24 mg triclosan/L. In the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2, there is seen a very 
large difference in toxicity in the lox exposure group, that does not seem to experience actual inhibition, and the 
medium exposure plants that are inhibited at a similar level as the high exposure plants. This result can be compared 
to the one found in the chamber with elevated temperature; when the concentration reaches a toxic level, the toxic 
effect is very strong. It was seen in the Yu et al. (2007) study, that uptake and metabolism of cyanide was increased 
at increasing temperatures. The present findings could seem as if there is a high amount taken up of the toxic 
compound, and until a certain concentration, the metabolism is keeping up, ensuring the detoxification of the 
compound (elevated temperature and CO2), but at a certain concentration, the metabolism is not keeping up with 
the large uptake, resulting in very strong poisoning. Another explanation could be, that some of the metabolites 
of triclosan could be more poisonous than triclosan; this would lead to a very fast NRT drop if combined with a 
stimulation of the plant’s metabolism. Macherius et al. 2014 found plants’ metabolism of triclosan to create 33 
different metabolites of triclosan. The toxicity of these should be assessed when investigating the toxicity of 
triclosan on a larger scale.

For all chambers the high exposure groups are following the blinds as the total mass lost from the blinds, in all 
chambers, at all times, is actually higher than the mass lost by the high exposure plants. For the elevated 
temperature chamber, the medium concentration group also has a lower transpiration than the evaporation from 
the blinds.  
 
The result of the NRT value is somewhat uncertain, as the mass of water lost at the first measuring point is used 
for the controls and the group that is comparing to (transpiration rate in the NRT equation at time 0), and for the 
first measurement, this value has very little to do with the health of the plant as the evaporation is so strong at this 
point, compared to the transpiration (the mass loss in blinds is the largest). Still a relationship between the 
transpiration for the control and an exposed group at another time is useful, but the idea of normalization is lost 
in this experiment. If the NRT should be improved to include normalization, the plants should first all be grown 
without poison, until they had developed enough to distinguish the transpiration of the plants from the evaporation 
of the blinds. This point should be t=0 and this transpiration used in the NRT formula, and at this point poison 
could be added. Then, the peas should be grown in bigger vials as space limitation could otherwise cause the 
experimental period to be too short to obtain the relevant data. 
 
Total transpiration, WUE and growth 
The accumulated transpiration is seen in Figure 4.A. Looking at the controls, the difference caused by the varying 
climate can be seen. It was expected that the plants in the chamber with elevated temperature would transpire 
more than the plants in the ambient chamber, which is the case. This means that the plants exposed to triclosan 
would, because of the climate conditions, be stimulated to transpire more, and therefore take up more of the 
solution with triclosan in and therefore experience the highest level of toxicity. It was also expected, that the plants 
in the chamber with elevated CO2 would transpire less than the plants in the ambient, which was not the case. At 
the low concentration level, the plants in the high temperature chamber and the plants in the elevated CO2 chamber 
showed the largest toxic effect, as these are the chambers where transpiration was deviating the most from the 
controls. In the ambient chamber, the transpiration is slightly increased at low exposure, which could simply be 
caused by the differences in the plants or by slight hormesis, which is a stimulation of a plant caused by a low 
dosage of poisoning (Stebbing 1982). At the medium exposure concentration, the plants in the ambient chamber

Looking at Figure 4.B for the WUE, it is again noticed, that the CO2 does not follow the pattern expected. 
Stimulation of growth and reduction of transpiration, which was expected, would both increase the WUE, and 
therefore it was expected to be highest in the controls. The figure of average growth, Appendix E, Figure VI, does

lowest, as expected because of its high transpiration and low growth. The WUE in the chamber with elevated 
temperature and CO2 showed a quite stable WUE as the growth and transpiration mostly followed each other. 
EC50 
Many different parameters influenced the plants resulting in the EC50 values found. It was seen that the toxic effect 
was largest in the chamber with elevated temperature, where the EC50 was found to be 0.24 mg/L. This was caused 
by the fact that in this chamber, two stress factors, the elevated temperature and the toxic exposure, were present 
and that the transpiration should be highest here, so uptake and toxic effect would have been strong in the 
beginning, but with time, the high toxicity caused a severe reduction in the transpiration. Elevated CO2 is not 
expected to be a stress factor, but an improvement, so the two chambers with elevated CO2 was expected to have 
an advantage compared to the chamber with elevated temperature. The next most toxic treatment was in the 
==PAGE:18==
15 
 
chamber with elevated CO2, resulting in an EC50 of 1.80 mg/L, which was expected to be the chamber where the 
smallest toxic effect would be seen. As the CO2 was much higher than expected in this chamber, the alleviating 
effect anticipated from lower toxic uptake was not seen. The combined effect of elevated temperature and CO2, 
causing an EC50 of 2.69 mg/L was not as expected either. The two parameters on their own resulted in EC50 values 
lower than the EC50 found for the combined effect, which could mean that the two parameters could, after all 
have a toxicity decreasing effect on each other. If the large CO2 level brought a little stomatal closure compared to 
ambient CO2, the transpiration would be kept at a lower level, than the elevated temperature would otherwise 
result in. This could mean a lower uptake of chemicals, provided that the triclosan is taken up at the same rate as 
the water. Combined with an improved metabolism at elevated temperature, this could cause an outcome, where 
plants were inhibited less by triclosan than when only the temperature or the CO2 was elevated. This would only 
be the case if the metabolites were not more toxic than triclosan, otherwise the chamber with elevated CO2 would 
be the one of the three, where the smallest toxicity should be found. The least toxic effect was found in the ambient 
chamber, where the EC50 was 5.24 mg/L. This means that a temperature increase of 5°C compared with today’s 
climate conditions results in an almost 22 times stronger toxic response! To compare these finding to those of

Uptake of triclosan into peas 
 
In Table 3, the results of an uptake analysis carried out on the plants exposed to the low concentration of triclosan, 
are presented. Note, that the plants were harvested in the end of the experiment, so the test duration is between 
403 and 418 hours. It was chosen to only investigate the uptake in the low exposure concentration plants as they 
had the least transpiration inhibition, so the uptake was the least limited as possible by the toxicity. The total mass 
of transpired water for each plant was used to calculate the mass of triclosan the plants got exposed to. The actual 
value of fluid exposed to the plants is close to this, as the plants in the low concentration group transpired almost 
all the available water mostly, so this is a good estimate. It is seen, that the plants in the chamber with elevated 
temperature and CO2 transpired the most, and was therefore exposed to the largest amount of triclosan. Looking 
at the concentration in wet shoot for all chambers, it is seen, that the values cover a range of factor 2 (0.4-46.4 
mg/kg), but half of the values are around 10 mg/kg. There is likewise a large range in the root concentration (2.7-
70.6 mg/kg), and no connection is seen between the concentrations in root or shoot and the water content or total 
transpired mass. It is noticed, however, that for the chamber with elevated CO2 and the chamber with elevated 
temperature and CO2, the concentration is higher in the roots than in the shoots. For all chambers we know, that 
when triclosan was found in the shoot, it was a result of uptake, as the shoot is not directly exposed to the solution 
with triclosan, but for the roots, some of the measured triclosan could have sticked to the surface of the roots and 
not actually been taken up. 
 
The total uptake of available triclosan ranged from 8.2-92.3% (one value was 0.4 mg/kg, but as this was only for 
the shoot, it cannot be described as the total uptake) and the average was at 48%. This means that triclosan is quite 
efficiently taken up by the plants. As the half-life of triclosan in water is 60 days (Danish (Q)SAR Database, year 
unknown), a significant breakdown of the triclosan is not expected in water in this experiment, which ran for 
around 17 days. Photolysis could be a relevant breakdown process, as the test vials were in light during the entire 
experiment (no diurnal light/darkness cycle) and the vials were not wrapped to prevent the light of the chambers 
reaching the water in the vials. The half-life of triclosan exposed to photolysis in water depends on light source, 
pH and type of water, but ranges from a few minutes to days, and could therefore very well be relevant in this 
study.

Prosser et al. (2014b) found a decrease in triclosan concentrations with time in plants. For radish, the concentration 
fell from 24.8 to 2.1 ng/g dry weight in the root from day 19 to 34 of the experiment and in the shoot it fell from 
33.7 to 13.7 ng/g dry weight in the same time period. Day 19 was the day with the highest triclosan concentration 
in the experiment for radish. In carrots, the picture was the same: at day 45 of the experiment, the highest 
concentration was found in the plant at 49.8 ng/g dry weight in the carrot root and 18.3 ng/g dry weight in the 
shoot and the concentrations had decreased to 5.5 ng/g dry weight in both root and shoot at day 69. For soybeans, 
the largest concentration in the roots was found at day 54, where the concentration was 48.1 ng/g dry weight and 
it had fallen to 8.4 ng/g dry weight at day 81 of the experiment. This looks like different plants are taking up and 
breaking down triclosan at different rates. In the experiment, triclosan was only added once, at t=0. In current 
study, the plants were exposed to triclosan in water, as opposed to in soil in the other experiment. In water, the 
uptake of triclosan could be faster, as the triclosan here is not binding to the environmental compartment. This 
fact combined with the continuous application of triclosan to the plants, would make the triclosan concentration 
rise continuously in current study, but breakdown of triclosan in the plant would also be expected to occur within 
the timeframe of this experiment, which is 16.8-17.4 days. From the other study, it is not possible to say anything 
about the uptake and metabolism rate of triclosan in peas compared to the other plants, but in that study, there 
was 15-27 days between the maximum and the end concentration, where the concentration had decreased greatly. 
If the rate of metabolism is similar for the peas, some of the added triclosan would expectedly be broken down 
too, and the total uptake of available triclosan would therefore be higher than the stated and not be able to reach 
100%. As the chambers with 24°C were expected to have higher metabolism rates than in the other chambers, one 
could expect, that the triclosan amount measured in these chambers were lower than in the chambers with 19°C, 
but as the transpiration pr. hour was also highest in these chambers for healthy, new triclosan would be filled in 
more often, increasing the exposure until the toxic effect would inhibit the transpiration. For the slowly transpiring 
plants, the rate between uptake and metabolism of triclosan might have been more balanced, and therefore a lower 
percentage of the available triclosan could be found here. However, there is no apparent pattern in the percentage 
of total triclosan among the chambers. To investigate the actual uptake of triclosan, a test for the metabolites of 
triclosan should have been made as well, and this could give the rate of biodegradation of triclosan as well.

Conclusions 
From the pea toxicity test it was seen that triclosan was toxic to the pea plants, as increasing exposure 
concentrations led to decreasing transpiration rates in all chambers. The exposure of 8 mg triclosan/L was very 
inhibiting for all climate conditions. The largest toxicity was seen in the climate chamber with elevated temperature, 
where all plants exposed to a concentration of triclosan was inhibited to a great extend as the NRT was below 50% 
for all exposure groups. The increased temperature enhanced the transpiration rate causing a large inhibition from 
early on in the experiment. The EC50 value for triclosan found in this chamber was 0.24 mg/L. The second highest 
EC50 was found in the chamber with elevated CO2 and the plants in this chamber were not affected as expected – 
the transpiration rate was higher in this chamber than in the ambient chamber, although the WUE was improved 
in the chamber with elevated CO2 at low and medium toxic exposure. In the chamber with elevated temperature 
and CO2, the combination of the elevation of the two climate conditions showed an improving effect compared 
to when only one condition was elevated, especially for the plants exposed to 1 mg triclosan/L and in this chamber 
the WUE was contained more or less constant in the controls and at low and medium exposure. The least toxic 
effect was found in the ambient chamber, where the decrease in NRT had a flatter slope. The EC50 was 5.24 mg 
triclosan/L, which is 22 times lower than the EC50 found in the chamber with elevated temperature, so climate 
conditions have a very important effect on toxicity of triclosan towards pea plants and is expected to increase in 
the future. 
 
In the uptake study, 8.2-92.3% of the applied triclosan was found in the plants, with an average of 48%. There was 
no correlation between climate conditions and the concentration of triclosan in the plants or the amount of 
triclosan found in the plant in relation to the applied amount. As plant metabolism of triclosan and photolysis in 
the vial have likely occurred, the rate of these two triclosan degradation processes are affecting the results, especially 
in the chambers with elevated temperature, as the metabolism is expected to be faster here. Therefore, it cannot 
be said, that the amount of triclosan found in the plant at the time of analysis was the amount taken up, so to 
improve the uptake analysis, the concentration of triclosan metabolites should also have been investigated and an 
analysis of the fluid left in the blinds at the end of the experiment could have been analysed to investigate 
photolysis. 
 
Overall, the climate conditions have a big impact on the toxicity of triclosan to pea plants, but not on the 
accumulation of triclosan in the plants. As peas are grown in soil and this study was tested for an aqueous solution 
it is difficult to say if the current environmental concentrations would pose a risk to pea plants.

Abstract 
Salt in soils are making large areas unfit for agriculture, and the problem is expected to increase in some parts of 
the world in the future. The aim of this study is to investigate the global climate change’s impact on the toxicity of 
salt towards plants. Experiments were carried out on willow cuts (Salix viminalis L. crossed with other willow 
species) pregrown for 4 weeks, and germinated peas (Pisum sativum L.) with controls and 3 test concentrations, 0.3 
g NaCl/L, 1.0 g NaCl/L and 3.0 g NaCl/L in four climate chambers with different settings; ambient (19°C, 400 
ppm CO2), elevated temperature (24°C, 400 ppm CO2), elevated CO2 (19°C, 700 ppm CO2) and elevated 
temperature and CO2 (24°C, 700 ppm CO2). The willows were grown hydroponically and the peas were grown in 
clay granules and test solution. The transpiration of the plants was continuously measured.  In all chambers in both 
tests, the transpiration decreased with increasing salt concentrations. The climate condition resulting in the highest 
and lowest transpiration was elevated temperature and elevated CO2 respectively in both experiments, but the 
climate conditions leading to the highest and lowest toxicity of salt differed in the tests. In the willow test, the 
normalised relative transpiration (NRT) showed highest toxic effects in the chamber with ambient conditions and 
the chamber with elevated temperature, while the calculated EC50 showed highest toxicity in the ambient chamber. 
In the pea test, both the NRT and the EC50 values showed highest toxic response in the chamber with elevated 
CO2 (0.84 g NaCl/L) and the chamber with elevated temperature. The lowest EC50 (2.40 g NaCl/L) was for the 
chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 and was 3 times lower than the highest value. The willow trees were 
able to take up salt at a slower rate than water though this ability was impaired when the toxic response was 
inhibiting transpiration though the salt extrusion was continued throughout the experiment for all treatments. It is 
concluded, that climate conditions do have an impact on the toxicity of salt towards plants, though the conditions 
causing highest toxicity can vary between plants.

Introduction 
The Earth’s climate is changing. It is not possible to tell exactly how it will change and the change will not be 
uniform. We see changes in the CO2 level in the atmosphere as the rising CO2 concentration is directly affected 
by human activities and emissions. The average global concentration of CO2 has increased from a preindustrial 
level of 280 ppm to the current level of around 400 ppm (Siewertsen 2016a ; Lambers et al. 2008) and is presently 
increasing with 2 ppm a year (Siewertsen 2016b). The average global temperature is also rising. From 1906 to 2005 
the temperature rose 0.74°C on average at the Earth’s surface and the rate is increasing - measurements show, that 
since 1978 the average global rise in temperature is 1.3°C pr. century (Dessler 2012). Furthermore, changes in 
precipitation patterns are expected, that lead to more rain falling during heavy rainfalls leading to increased runoff 
and less water available for plants. The dry areas are expected to experience water shortage and drought more 
often and intensely and the wetter areas will experience flooding and landslides more often. The change in 
precipitation patterns can therefore become a big problem for securing food production in the future.   
A big problem for agriculture now is high concentrations of salt in the soil. Because of toxic levels of salt, around 
380-400 million hectares, which makes up approximately 3% of the total land area in the world, are not usable for 
agricultural purposes (Lambers et al. 2008 ; Aringhieri 2010). The problem is large in arid areas, where the 
evaporation from soil is higher than the precipitation, causing salty groundwater to rise into the unsaturated soil, 
leading to an upconcentration of salt as the scarce precipitation is not enough to wash out the salt (Mongillo and 
Zierdt-Warshaw 2000). Furthermore, irrigation water of low quality and improper drainage can lead to salinity 
problems as well. In low lying areas salt water infiltration or flooding can be the reason for salinity. These reasons 
for saline soils are affected directly or indirectly by climate change and is therefore continuously a relevant issue.  
Saline soils are problematic to most plants, as NaCl is toxic to plants, either directly because of the ions and the 
ion imbalance they can impose (lack of Ca2+ and other) or by causing the same symptoms as water stress and the 
growth of many plants is inhibited. Some plants however have coping mechanisms to reduce the toxic effects. An 
example of this is willow trees, that can excrete salt from their roots. The combined stress effect of the changing 
climate and the saline soils is important to investigate as one stress could potentially impair or improve the effect 
of the other (Taiz et al. 2015). This problem leads to the overall problem statement of this article: 
How is the toxicity of salt towards willow trees and pea plants affected by increasing temperature and CO2 
concentrations? 
To investigate this, willow trees and pea plants will be exposed to different concentrations of NaCl in climate 
chambers with different settings: one ambient, one with elevated temperature, one with elevated CO2 and one with 
elevated temperature and CO2. The hypothesis is, that an increase in temperature will lead to a higher water uptake 
and thereby a higher salt exposure, while a higher CO2 level could lead to more closed stomata, resulting in less 
transpiration and therefore a better water use efficiency (Taiz et al. 2015). In this study, the result on the toxicity of 
salt in a situation with elevated temperature and CO2 at the same time will also be investigated.

Previous research 
In this section, some of the previous research by other authors related to this topic is presented. 
Salt toxicity to willow trees 
In the article “Plant uptake of NaCl in relation to enzyme kinetics and toxic effects” by Trapp et al. (2008) the 
toxicity of NaCl towards willow trees was investigated. The experimental setup was more or less the same as in 
the present paper, where willow trees are grown hydroponically in water with different concentrations of NaCl 
added. Transpiration was investigated as a measure of plant health and the test concentrations ranged from 0-20 g 
NaCl/L, constant light, 27±0.5qC and 30±5% relative humidity. Trapp et al. found that for salt concentrations 
£0.2 g/L, there was no effect on the normalised relative transpiration (NRT) but for ³0.4 g/L there was a limitation 
of the transpiration. At 2.0 g/L the inhibition of NRT after 48 hours was more than 80%. The breakthrough point 
was found at 1.4 g/L for 240 hours and an EC50 was around 0.8 g/L at 96 hours after the salt was added. 
In the article “Toxicity of 56 substances to trees” by Clausen and Trapp (2017) results for many experiments has 
been collected and presented. There are several results for willows (S. viminalis L.) exposed to NaCl tested 
hydroponically. One study found that at 312 hours after salt addition the EC50 was 1.25 g NaCl/L and for 600 
hours the EC50 was 0.25 g/L. Another study found an EC50 after 72 hours of testing at 0.8 g/L. 
The effect of climate change on plant toxicity and uptake 
One study (Yu et al. 2007) examines the effect of elevated temperature on the uptake and metabolism of cyanide 
by weeping willows grown hydroponically. The conclusion was, that cyanide did not have a toxic effect on the 
trees at the tested concentration (0.98 mg CN/L) for none of the tested temperatures (11-32°C). There was no 
accumulation of cyanide in the trees as a result of elevated temperatures, although the uptake of cyanide increased 
with temperature, as the metabolism of cyanide increased with temperature as well. Another study investigated the 
effect of elevated temperature on cadmium and copper uptake in rice grown in soil (Ge et al. 2015). This study had 
4 day and night temperature sets tested: 25/18°C, 25/23 °C, 30/18 °C and 30/23 °C and the conclusion of this 
study was that warming increased the uptake of Cd and Cu, where the Cd especially increased in the shoots (5-12 
times compared to lower temperature conditions) of the plants and Cu increased in the roots as much as in the 
shoots. Furthermore, the increased temperatures made the metals more bioavailable to the plants and caused in 
increase in growth of the rice plants. 
Several studies found, that CO2 alleviates the negative effects of NaCl toxicity. A study of barley plants (Pérez-
López et al. 2009) showed, that the negative effect of salt on the water status in plants was improved by higher 
CO2 concentrations (700 ppm, where the ambient was 350 ppm), the water use efficiency (WUE) and the 
photosynthesis was improved, the stomata were more closed and thereby dehydration was lowered. Pérez-López 
et al. (2013) found in another study of barley plants, that NaCl decreases the N metabolism, but at low salt 
concentrations (80 mM NaCl), the N metabolism can be improved by higher CO2 levels. The growth of barley 
was impaired by NaCl, but elevated CO2 moderated this growth reduction.  
A study of bermudagrass (Yu et al. 2015) showed that NaCl (150 mM NaCl) caused reduction in transpiration, 
photosynthesis, stomatal conductance and more, but these negative effects were improved by a higher CO2 level 
(800 ppm compared to 400 ppm). In a study of olive trees, Melgar et al. (2008) tested the effect of 100 mM NaCl 
and 700 ppm CO2 on a salt sensitive and a more salt tolerant cultivar of olive (Olea europea L.). For the salt-tolerant 
cultivar, there was no change in the growth at elevated CO2 compared to ambient for the plants tested with or 
without salt. For the salt sensitive cultivar however, there was an elevated growth in the situation with elevated 
CO2 compared to ambient conditions for the plants without salt, but this increase in growth was not seen under 
saline conditions. For both tree types, the transpiration fell at elevated CO2 levels and the WUE increased for both 
non-saline and saline conditions compared to ambient. When exposed to salt, the uptake of salt was increased, the 
growth was lower and the uptake of CO2 and the WUE was declining compared to without salt. For the salt 
==PAGE:35==
5 
 
tolerant olive tree, the level of CO2 had no effect on the concentration of salt in the plant, but for the salt sensitive 
plant, the concentration in leaves and roots was lowered by elevated CO2.

The experiment 
An experiment was conducted to test the changes in toxicity of salt towards plants with changing climate. Two 
different plants (willow trees and peas) were used in two different setups. The experiment was carried out in 4 
climate chambers at RERAF (Risø Environmental Risk Assessment Facility) at DTU Risø. In all chambers, there 
were light during the entire day cycle to simplify potential modelling, as we here have only one situation instead of 
four (day, transition from day to night, night and transition from night to day). Here one chamber was set to 
simulate ambient conditions, another chamber had elevated temperature, one had an elevated CO2 level, and the 
last had both elevated temperature and CO2. The details of the settings for each chamber can be seen in Table 1.

The willow test 
Willow trees were chosen as test organism as willow cuttings from one plant will sprout and the new trees are 
tested to secure minimal genetic differences. Another advantage of willows is that they can survive for a long time 
under flooded conditions (Jackson and Attwood 1996 ; Grosse et al. 1996) and can thereby be grown 
hydroponically. The hydroponic test setup as opposed to willows tested in soil, simplifies the system as it only two 
environmental compartments (tree and solution) instead of three (tree, solution and soil).  
A hydroponic willow test was carried out as described in A Phytotoxicity Test Using Transpiration of Willows (Trapp et 
al. 2000) with a few alterations testing the effects of climate change on the NaCl toxicity towards willows. Willow 
trees (Salix viminalis L. crossed with other Salix species), grown at DTU in Kongens Lyngby between 
Lundtoftegårdsvej and Helsingørmotorvejen, were cut 4 weeks before they were moved to the chambers in which 
the tests would take place. They were cut, so all of them originated from the same plant. Stems with a diameter of 
1.5-2 cm were chosen and cut into pieces of 30-35 cm of length. The willow cuts were placed in buckets with tap 
water, with the bottom end of the willow in the water. The buds are examined to locate the bottom and top of the 
willow stem. The willows were after 1 week moved to a climate chamber at DTU Risø, where the temperature was 
25qC. Five days before the salt solution was added, the trees were given a number each, weighed and each was put 
into an Erlenmeyer flask with 350 ml of nutrient solution. The flasks were wrapped in tin foil to prevent algae 
bloom in the flask and parafilm was used to seal the top of the flask around the tree, so evaporation from the flask 
was avoided as shown in Figure 1. The nutrient solution used was prepared according to a modified version of the 
ISO 8692 nutrient solution recipe (see Appendix B, Table I). The trees were now placed in the chambers set to 
the conditions they were to be exposed to during the rest of the experiment and each tree’s precise location and

adding the salt. The trees had to transpire at least 5 g/day to make sure they were healthy enough to test. Only one

The trees were then ranked by their transpired mass of water and from this given a test concentration of salt to 
avoid having all the healthiest or largest trees with high transpiration in the same concentration group. The tree 
that transpired the most in each chamber was set to be a control, the one that transpired the next highest amount 
was set to be the highest concentration, the tree that transpired the third highest became the medium 
concentration, the next became the lowest concentration, and this order was repeated until all trees were assigned 
a concentration. There were 4 replicas of each of the salt concentrations and 5 controls in each chamber. The 
concentrations tested were 0 g NaCl/L (control), 0.33 g NaCl/L (low concentration), 1.0 g NaCl/L (medium 
concentration) and 3.0 g NaCl/L (high concentration). The salt solutions were made by dissolving pure NaCl in 
nutrient solution. The conductivity in each solution was measured with an EC-meter (Electrical Conductivity) and 
the Erlenmeyer flasks were emptied and the solution with the correct concentration filled in each bottle and they 
were wrapped in tin foil and parafilm again. Each apparatus was weighed. The number of leaves longer than 5 cm 
was counted for each tree. During the next 3 weeks, the apparatus was weighed regularly to measure the 
transpiration from each tree and the conductivity in each flask was measured every 4-6 days. The bottles were filled 
with the tested concentrations when the solution in them was scarce. After 3 weeks, the experiment was stopped, 
the apparatus weighed, the conductivity measured, the tree weighed and the number of leaves left over 5 cm 
counted for each tree. 
Uncertainties 
The biggest uncertainty was caused by declining health for the willow trees as the environment in the flask was 
constantly humid or in fluid. This could cause illness and fungi attacks of the trees. Uncertainties caused by this 
was therefore minimised by only using the data where the decline of transpiration in the controls was not generally 
seen.  
When the conductivity measurements of the fluid in the Erlenmeyer flasks were made, the tree was taken out of 
the apparatus, to be able to fit the EC-meter probe into the flask. When doing this, the tree was placed on a clean 
paper towel and droplets of water would stick to the roots of the tree when taking it out of the flask and the 
droplets would be lost before the reinsertion of the tree into the flask. This loss of mass from the system would 
==PAGE:38==
8 
 
imply a higher transpiration result in the next measurement than the actual. The loss of mass was measured (around 
2 g pr. plant) and compared to the amount transpired between each measurement, this was seen as an acceptable 
uncertainty. The problem with mass loss also occurred when leaves were falling of the trees. 
Another uncertainty was a pest attack to plants in the buckets during the initial period of root and leaf formation. 
In one bucket, a few aphids were found and an illness probably transferred by the animals, affected the trees. In 
the other two buckets, larvae were found, a Lepidoptera larvae in one bucket many leaves were eaten, and two 
larvae (Lepidoptera and unknown) in the other bucket, where no leaves were damaged. Other uncertainties could 
be a lower transpiration result or harm to the plant caused by water shortage or unintendedly disturbing the plant 
by a large and sudden change in water temperature, when filling up the flasks with solution, that had been stored 
cold. The latter uncertainty is only relevant for the beginning at the experiment as the solutions were taken out and 
stored at room temperature from early in the experiment. 
The pea test 
The change in salt’s toxicity towards pea plants in different climates was also tested. This test method is still under 
development and the purpose is to make a fast and cheap test for toxicity (and uptake) on annual plants, grown 
from seeds. 
Dried peas (Pisum sativum) were covered with water and soaked for 3 hours. The water was discarded and the peas 
were laid in a germinating apparatus with at least one centimetre space in all directions between the peas and 
demineralised water was added to each level in the apparatus (Figure 2.Left). After 4 days, the roots of the 
germinated peas were 1-5 cm and the most straight ones were chosen for the experiment. The experiment was 
carried out in 40 ml vials with 20 vials in each chamber. There were 4 replicates in each chambers of the blinds 
(vial without plant, everything else is the same as in the other vials) and the 4 test concentrations: control (0 g 
NaCl/L), low (0.33 g NaCl/L), medium (1 g NaCl/L) and high (3 g NaCl/L). All vials were numbered and weighed, 
then filled with approximately 13 g of seramis in each. The seramis was prior to this burned for 24 hours to 
extinguish bacteria. The pea was inserted and the apparatus weighed again before it was filled with solution and 
the total weight was measured. The water level should at all times be below the pea to prevent decay. The solutions 
were nutrient solution (as described earlier) with dissolved salt. The conductivity of the solutions was measured 
with an EC-meter.

The vials were put in a box with subdivisions as seen in Figure 2.Right and put in the chambers. The box’ precise 
location was marked and the orientation of each vial was controlled. Each apparatus was weighed every 1-2 days 
to give an indication of the transpiration and put back in the exact same location afterwards. Water was added to 
the vials in a way that prevented oxygen depletion of the roots, by occasionally letting the water level drop to a 
minimum in the vials. The conductivity of the solution filled in was measured, and the vials were weighed again. 
This procedure was repeated for 3 weeks. The last day the vials were weighed and the pea plant was taken out, 
divided into root and sprout and the parts were weighed and dried and then weighed again. The pH in the fluid in 
the vials (where any was left) was measured to make sure it has not changed dramatically.  
Uncertainties 
To secure constant water availability and at the same time avoiding oxygen depletion (too much water) and drought 
(too small amount of water) is difficult and therefore some plants can have experienced periods of drought and 
other plants have experienced oxygen depletion in the root zone, both affecting the health of the plant. Besides 
this, the transpiration could change with the water level, so maintaining a good water availability balance can cause 
a small error in the transpiration. Another parameter is the size of the seramis. In some vials the grains are small, 
reducing the plants’ ability to grow long roots as the roots are stopped by the tightly packed seramis. Also, when 
growing the plants from seed in this experiment and adding the concentrations of salt immediately, it is not possible 
to investigate the health of the plants prior to the poisoning risking that initially unhealthy plants exposed to the 
higher levels of NaCl will not be noticed but seen as a result of the toxicity of salt towards the plant. Another risk 
linked to this is, that the distribution of peas with high transpiration on the concentration groups is not even, even 
though the peas are chosen to each group with a randomisation method.

transpirations were investigated to see, if they should be considered alive. All willow trees were still considered

the experiment without it being a result of a high exposure concentration, was taken out. The transpiration was 
evaluated together with a visual investigation of the plant to see if it had lived and developed leaves or not.

The climate conditions in the chambers were tracked, and the results are seen in Figure I-III in Appendix A. It is

measurements might be affected dramatically. 
Overall it is assessed that the differences in environmental factors are acceptable for this test.  
Analysis of data from the experiments 
It was expected that elevated temperature, compared to ambient conditions, would in general lead to a higher 
transpiration from the plants, as the stomata would be more open which would facilitate heat and water leaving 
the plant. This was expected to result in a higher uptake of water by the plants, leading to a higher toxic effect 
from the salt. For the plants exposed to elevated CO2 conditions, it was expected that the stomatal opening would 
be smaller, resulting in less transpiration and thereby a smaller uptake of salt and a lower effect of salt toxicity. As 
the transpiration would lower, the WUE would increase. Most C3 plants (as willows and peas are) are CO2-limited 
and will therefore increase growth when the CO2 level rises. Most C3 plants grow 30% faster at CO2 levels of 600-
750 ppm compared to ambient conditions (Taiz et al. 2015). This would increase the WUE further. As salt causes 
a slower plant growth (Lambers et al. 2008), this increased growth from CO2 is expected to alleviate the effect of 
the salt. The plants grown in the chamber under ambient conditions are expected to show a response to toxicity 
between the toxicity experienced by the plants in the elevated temperature chamber and the elevated CO2 chamber. 
As elevated temperature and CO2 are expected to have opposing results on transpiration and toxicity, there are no 
expectations to whether the combination will be dominated by the effect of the elevated CO2 or temperature or 
the effects will even out by each other or create a mutually worsening or improving effect. 
The willow test  
Transpiration 
The transpiration of each plant is seen as a measure of health – the higher the transpiration, the better the plant is 
doing. The climate conditions, however, also affect the transpiration. In the test with the willow trees, it is clear, 
that the salt has an inhibiting effect on the plants, as the trend is the same for all chambers at 460 hours: the 
transpiration decreases, when the salt concentration increases (Figure 4). Until around 200 hours, the low 
concentration of salt does not cause a reduction of the transpiration in the ambient and the elevated CO2 chamber. 
From the controls, it is possible to see the effects of the climate on the transpiration, see Figure 6. The lowest 
accumulated transpiration of the controls found was, as expected, in the chamber with elevated CO2 and the 
highest transpiration in the chamber with elevated temperature. In the chamber with elevated temperature and 
CO2 the transpiration was between the amount of the two before mentioned chambers, which was also expected, 
and had a high transpiration, which could imply that the effect of the high temperature was stronger than the effect 
of the high CO2 on the stomatal opening.

NRT 
When comparing across the chambers, the transpiration is partly an effect of the climate influence and the 
difference of climate influence is expected to be seen in the controls, but for the other plants the transpiration 
level is a result of both climate impact and toxic effect and therefore gets more complicated - therefore NRT is 
used for comparing across the chambers (Figure 5). Here it is seen that all plants exposed to the high concentration 
of salt was inhibited almost equally when the results are normalised, though the NRT in the chamber with elevated 
CO2 show less inhibition (NRT of 34%, where the other chambers’ plants have a NRT of 25-27%). The overall 
toxic effect is smallest for the elevated CO2 chamber, but there is not a clear picture of the ranking of the other 
chambers in relation to inhibition, though the chamber with the elevated temperature in general result in a NRT 
the same level or lower than the NRT for the other two. The NRT drops dramatically in the chamber with elevated 
temperature already at the first measurement after addition of salt (at approximately 20 hours), which is partly 
because the transpiration of the control is very high at this point and lowers afterwards, but the influence of the 
salt is already seen for the plants exposed to the high salt concentration. The elevation of the NRT for the low and 
the medium concentration of salt is seen again at the next measuring point (70 hours) as the control is transpiring 
at a lower level again. In all chambers, the effect of salt is seen fast, already at the first measuring point (20 or 50 
hours). It could seem as if there was a link between the transpiration caused by the climatic factor and the NRT in 
the tests with salt in the water, where more transpiration caused by the climate effect (as seen in the controls) leads 
to more uptake of salt in the exposed plants and therefore more toxicity – which means that a high transpiration 
in the controls would result in a low NRT for the exposed plants.  
The effect of the salt in the chamber with elevated temperature was from the beginning expected to be stronger 
than in the ambient chamber. Only for the plants in the low concentration group (0.33 g NaCL/L) were the 
elevated temperature conditions more poisonous than in the ambient chamber. When looking at the mass 
transpired in total for the first 120 hours in the two chambers, it is noticed that for the low concentration, the 
plants in the ambient chamber and the chamber with elevated temperature have reversed their transpiration pattern 
from the control – now the plants in the ambient chamber transpire more than the plants in the chamber with 
elevated temperature, which means that there is a stronger inhibitory effect on the plants in the elevated 
temperature chamber. The total transpiration pattern is expected to be as in the controls unless the plants 
experience a poisonous effect, that would shift the normal pattern. From this approach, it is seen, that in the 
medium level of salt exposure (1.0 g NaCl/L), the inhibition of the plants in the ambient and the elevated 
temperature chambers are most affected, which is the case, and in the high concentration (3.0 g/L) all but the 
plants in the chamber with elevated CO2 are very affected. 
Concentration of salt in water and plants 
However, transpiration cannot be seen as a measure of uptake of salt alone, as the willow trees are able to extrude 
some of the salt again through their roots. Therefore, the rate of excretion is examined (Figure 7). If the Ct/Ct-1 
is above 1, the concentration of salt in the water has increased, which means, that the willows take up salt at a 
slower rate than they take up water, thus are still capable of extruding salt. The Ct/Ct-1 is not only a measure of 
the plants’ health, but also the amount of transpiration. The plants in the elevated CO2 chamber transpire the least, 
therefore the increase of salt concentration in the water is slow, which results in a slow increase in Ct/Ct-1 
compared to the other chambers. The chambers with the high temperatures have a high transpiration which leads 
to a high Ct/Ct-1 in the beginning (around 120 hours), but as the concentration in the water increases rapidly 
(Appendix E, Figure VI), especially for the medium and the low concentration group, the extrusion gets slower as 
the toxicity is starting to affect. The ability to avoid the toxic effects of the salt, especially in the medium group, is 
inhibited in the elevated temperature chamber, and the concentration stays almost stagnant throughout the rest of 
the experiment. This shows in the plant uptake of salt (Figure 8), where the salt uptake for 120 hours is surprisingly 
low for the medium concentration, but after this point, the mass of salt increases significantly. The uptake of salt 
in the low concentration group in the elevated temperature chamber is also very low for around 120 hours, which 
is partly because the Ct/Ct-1 for the low concentration group is highest in this chamber, and partly because the 
==PAGE:51==
21 
 
transpiration is relatively low for this group compared to the transpired amount in the ambient chamber. The 
uptake of salt in the elevated temperature chamber when exposed to the high concentration is the highest uptake 
for all concentrations and all chambers at this point in time. The minimum salt uptake was seen in the chamber 
with elevated CO2, which can be explained by the low transpiration in this chamber. It was expected that in the 
ambient chamber and the elevated temperature chamber, the uptake would be slightly increased compared to in 
the other chambers because of the difference in the time the experiment had been running between these and the 
other two chambers, but this was not obvious. It should be noted, that the uptake is actually the removed mass 
from the water, and salt concentration in the plant has not been measured.  
For all chambers, the Ct/Ct-1 is just above 1 for the most of the time in the high concentration group, which 
shows, that at this concentration level, the amount of salt needed to be pumped out to make the same percentage 
change (Ct/Ct-1) is higher, but also the inhibition is largest here, so the transpiration is small leading to a slow 
change in the concentration even if the salt pump is still functioning. In Appendix E, Figure VI, the development 
of the concentration in water is presented. Here it is seen, that still at the high exposure dosage, the concentration 
is rising, although it is not much for the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2. At 460 hours, for all 
chambers, the Ct/Ct-1 for the high concentration is very close to 1; in the ambient chamber it is 0.9994 and in the 
elevated temperature and CO2 chamber, the value is 1.0011. In Trapp et al. (2008), it was seen, that there was a 
breakthrough concentration, where the willow trees were so inhibited, that they were no longer able to pump out 
the salt. Here they had a salt uptake at the same rate as the water uptake (Ct/Ct-1=1), and at concentrations higher 
than the breakthrough concentration, the toxic effects were very strong. In Trapp et al. (2008), the breakthrough 
point for 240 hours was 1.4 g NaCl/L. In the experiment conducted in current paper, the breakthrough point 
might have been reached at 460 hours to be 3 g NaCl/L for ambient conditions and for elevated temperature and 
CO2, but to be sure, that this was actually the breakthrough, the experiment should have run longer, to check if 
the salt pump improved again. At 220 hours into the experiment, the lowest Ct/Ct-1 values was 1.04 and found 
in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 at 3.0 g NaCl/L exposure. The reason for the different findings 
could be, that the Trapp et al. experiment was carried out at 27°C and 30% relative humidity, which could both 
lead to higher transpiration in the controls than in this experiment, and therefore a larger toxic effect for the plants 
treated with salt caused by a higher uptake. 
It is furthermore noted, that for most cases (all chambers and salt exposure levels), the Ct/Ct-1 peak is at the first 
measuring point, which can be caused by an inhibition of the salt pump and the transpiration after initial exposure, 
but for the low dosage in all but the elevated temperature chamber, and the medium dosage in the chamber with 
elevated CO2 and the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2, the peak is at the second measuring point (220 
or 240 hours), which means, that the plants in the categories with the later peak can withstand the salt stress for a 
longer time. It would be interesting to investigate, at which total mass taken up by the plant, the peaks in Ct/Ct-1 
appears, to see if there was a connection here, but for this, measurements with smaller intervals should be made. 
It was investigated, if there was a trend in the Ctree/Cwater relation and the time of the peak, but no trend was 
found. 
The highest transpirations for the plants treated with salt was, for all concentration groups, found in the chamber 
with elevated temperature and CO2 at 100 or 120 hours. The lowest NRT was not seen in this chamber, which led 
to the expectation, that the Ct/Ct-1 for this time must be highest in this chamber, but this was not the case. The 
highest Ct/Ct-1 was for all plants in salt solution seen in the elevated temperature chamber. The reason for this 
can be the difference in the measuring time and, that the start concentrations differed a little among the two 
chambers. Otherwise this could mean, that the plants in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 are better 
at coping with or avoiding the toxic effect in some way, for instance beneficial metabolism, enzymatic reactions or 
that the plants were generally healthier when exposed to both elevated temperature and CO2. 
Water Use Efficiency 
The Water Use Efficiency (WUE, note that it is for 460 hours) was expected to be highest in the elevated CO2 
chamber, which was also the case for all concentrations, although the difference was not tremendous. The finding 
==PAGE:52==
22 
 
is in coherence with the Pérez-López et al. (2009) study, which showed, that WUE was improved when plants were 
exposed to higher CO2 levels. The WUE for the elevated temperature chamber was expected to be below the 
WUE of the ambient chamber, as the trees in the elevated temperature chamber were expected to transpire more, 
but this was only the case for the controls. The WUE in the elevated temperature chamber was actually higher at 
low salt concentrations than at the controls, caused by a large decrease in the transpiration. When looking at the 
EC50 values for the ambient and the elevated temperature chamber (Table 2), it can be seen, that the salt poisoning 
is more toxic to the plants in the ambient chamber, than to the plants in the elevated temperature chamber. This 
could be the result of the poor WUE seen in the ambient chamber, as the plants would spend more water pr. 
growth here and combined with the Ct/Ct-1, which was lower in the ambient than in the elevated temperature 
chamber, the plants here are not good at extruding the salt taken up.  
For the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2, the WUE in the controls was the second highest, which is 
surprising considering the high transpiration in this chamber (see Appendix G, Figure X). This was found to be 
caused by the high growth in this chamber (Appendix G, Figure XI). The growth is also the cause of the negative 
WUE seen, as the growth was negative for high salt concentrations. The high growth in this chamber can be caused 
by the stimulation of the higher CO2. The growth was also expected to increase in the chamber with higher CO2 
level, which was not the case, not even for the controls, only at high concentrations, where the plants in the elevated 
CO2 contained growth. The plants in the elevated CO2 chamber had the least reduction in growth caused by salinity 
compared to the controls, and this alleviation in the salinity induced growth reduction was also found in the study 
by Pérez-López et al. (2013). The WUE of the plants in the elevated temperature and CO2 chamber have a quite 
stable WUE except at the high concentration level, which could be explained by the maintained high salt extrusion 
capacity (Ct/Ct-1) seen in the low and medium exposure groups, which is still high at t=460. In this chamber the 
reduction in transpiration and growth caused by elevated levels of salt show similar trends.  
EC50 
The EC50 values presented are for 100 or 120 hours, and these show, that the least toxic effect is found in the 
elevated CO2 chamber, which is as expected from theory and comparing with the findings of other studies (Yu et 
al. 2015). For all the chambers, the EC50 value calculated was between 1 g NaCl/L and 3 g NaCl/L, which 
corresponds well with the results found in the NRT, where all NRT values found for the medium concentration 
(1 g/L) were above 50% and all were below 50% for the high exposure (3 g/L). In the ambient chamber and the 
chamber with elevated temperature, the experiment had run for longer than in the other two chambers, which is 
expected to have a decreasing effect on the EC50, so for comparison with the other chambers, they should be 
adjusted up a bit. This gives the result that the toxic effect is smallest in the chamber with the increased CO2. The 
second lowest EC50 is found in the elevated temperature chamber, which is not the same as seen from the average 
NRT, but the reason could be that the NRT values in this chamber have the largest spread (see Appendix F, Figure 
VIII) and therefore the uncertainty in this chamber for a linear regression is higher. Also, the NRT calculated for 
a group of plants is not the same as the average of NRTs calculated individually for each plant. The highest toxic 
effect found from the EC50 was for the ambient chamber and the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2, 
which corresponds well with the finding of the lowest observed Ct/Ct-1 value, and therefore the closest to a 
breakthrough concentration was seen in these chambers. 
Comparing with the results found in other studies, the Trapp et al. (2008) study found an EC50 after 96 hours of 
0.8 g NaCl/L, which shows larger toxicity than seen in any chamber in this study. This value is most comparable 
with the value found for the elevated temperature chamber because of the high temperature in the mentioned 
study, and remembering that the value should be higher in the value found in present study because of the time 
difference, the numbers can be compared. In present study, the EC50 was more than double compared to the other 
study, but this EC50 was also the one with the highest uncertainty caused by the high deviation, so this might be 
part of the reason for the difference. Another reason could be variations in the conditions where the plants were 
grown (e.g. light level and CO2 concentration in the air) and the health of the plants at the beginning of the 
experiment caused by differences in the time the willows were developing leaves and roots before exposure, and 
==PAGE:53==
23 
 
the aphid attack in current research. It is difficult to compare directly with the results from the other studies 
presented in Clausen and Trapp (2017) as the exposure times are not equal, but current paper found results that 
resemble the results of “Rasmussen 2007” best, where the EC50 value after 312 hours of exposure was 1.25 g 
NaCl/L. As the exposure time should be considered, this study agrees fairly with the values found in the other 
studies. 
The Melgar et al. (2008) study showed for both salt tolerant and salt sensitive cultivars a declining transpiration and 
elevated WUE at increased CO2 concentrations, both for controls and for plants treated with salt; current study 
agrees with this finding. The study furthermore found that, when the plants were exposed to salt, the growth and 
WUE would fall compared to the plants grown without salt. The willow trees tested are fairly salt tolerant, as they 
can extrude salt, but the findings that were characteristic for the salt tolerant olive specie did not comply with the 
results seen in the willow test nor were the characteristics for the salt sensitive olive species, except that the salt 
sensitive cultivar had a lower salt uptake when exposed to CO2 than when at ambient conditions. 
The pea test 
Transpiration 
In the pea test it is seen in Figure 9 that the plants’ transpiration rate was impaired by salt, as for all chambers. The 
transpiration level was decreasing with increasing concentrations except for the plants exposed to low salt dosage 
in the chamber with elevated temperature at around 400 hours and the rest of the experiment, as the transpiration 
here was higher than for the controls. The transpiration of the plants exposed to the high concentration was low 
throughout the experiment in all chambers and at a comparable level, even lower than the evaporation from the 
blinds in many cases. The peas generally had a more stable transpiration in the elevated CO2 chamber, which had 
the lowest maximum transpiration for all but the high exposure group. In the chamber with elevated CO2, the 
transpiration for the low exposure trees was especially noted, as it does not have such a large variation from the 
beginning to the middle of the experiment as the low exposure plants in the other chambers do. In the chamber 
with elevated temperature and CO2, the low and medium salt dose plants have a high transpiration in relation to 
the controls compared to the other chambers. In this chamber, the decrease towards the end of the experiment is 
however rapid, compared to the other chambers that keep a quite constant level between 400 and 500 hours. The 
reason for the fall in the control, low and medium transpiration in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 
at t=332 hours, could be caused by space limitation, as the vials they grew in were quite small. A decrease in the 
transpiration is also seen in the ambient chamber around this time. Therefore, in the further investigation of the 
NRT will be for the first 332 hours. 
Compared to the willows, it is striking, that the transpiration development does not follow the same pattern. In 
the willow test, the willows primarily kept a constant or decreasing transpiration after t=0, but for the pea test 
there is a peak between 200 and 400 hours, which is because the peas were only just germinated at time 0, and the 
transpiration increases as the plant grows and leaves and roots develop. Therefore, the time from the start of the 
experiment to where the effect of salt is seen, is longer for the pea test than the willow test, where the effect could 
be seen almost immediately. Had the pea test stopped at the same time as the willow test, the conclusion might be, 
that salt did not have an impairing effect on the plants, as the transpiration values of the salt exposed peas were 
both larger than, at the same level as, and smaller than the controls. The blinds would still be evaporating more 
than the plants transpired, which would cause a huge uncertainty and make the data unusable. 
NRT 
In Figure 10, the NRT for the pea experiment was investigated. It is clearly seen from this figure as well that the 
salt has a toxic effect on the pea plants as the NRT is decreasing with increasing salt concentration, except for the 
low concentration in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2. For the first 100 hours, the plants exposed 
to salt are showing no or only a small inhibition caused by the salt; some cases even show stimulation, but after 
100 hours, the effect of the salt poisoning starts to show. The NRT furthermore shows that for the low and 
medium salt concentration, the impairment is strongest in the chamber with elevated temperature and the chamber 
==PAGE:54==
24 
 
with elevated CO2. Compared to the willow experiment and the other two chambers, the inhibition of especially 
the low concentration is noteworthy, as the other values show an NRT around or just above 100%, but in the 
chamber with elevated CO2 the NRT has decreased to 60%, which means that the toxic response is quite strong 
in this chamber. This is also shown by the EC50 value for this chamber, 0.84 g NaCl/L, which is the lowest EC50 
found for the pea and willow test. 
For the high concentration, the inhibition is at the same level in all chambers, around a NRT of 30%. A stimulation 
of the transpiration is seen in the beginning of the experiment for the plants exposed to the high concentrations 
in the elevated temperature chamber, and slightly in the ambient chamber, but decreases fast after t=100. In the 
other two chambers, an inhibition is seen from the start of the experiment. As the plants are so young at this time 
and the transpiration is so small, the evaporation is dominating here, and what is seen might not actually be a 
stimulation but rather the random differences in evaporation from all vials. In the chamber with elevated 
temperature and CO2, the low concentration causes a stimulation of the transpiration throughout the 332 hours 
of the experiment. What is seen here can be hormesis – that small amounts of a compound that is toxic at higher 
levels, stimulating the plants (Stebbing 1982), or this could simply be because the NRT is calculated using the start 
transpiration of controls and plants exposed to low concentrations, and as mentioned before, this measured value 
might not say anything about the plant’s health at the start, but more of the evaporation from the vial and the big 
uncertainty related to this. What is noticed though, is that the transpiration of the plants exposed to the low 
concentration transpire as much as the controls in this chamber (Figure 9) which means, that there might not be a 
significant effect of the salt in this case. Still, this would lead to the same conclusion as before: that the plants 
exposed to elevated temperature and CO2 conditions have the least inhibition. This result is also the one found, 
when looking at the EC50 value for the pea test which is 2.40 g NaCl/L for 332 hours of testing. This EC50 value 
is almost 3 times as high as the one found in the elevated CO2 chamber, which shows that the climatic conditions 
have a great impact on toxicity. 
In this experiment, the NRT is not as good a measurement for the health of the plants as in the willow test, as the 
initial transpiration, which is used in the NRT calculations, does not make much sense in the pea experiment, and 
as salt was added initially, the value used as the initial transpiration value for a healthy plant, was after the salt was 
added. If NRT should be a better expression for the plant inhibition, the test method could be altered. Alterations 
improving the NRT could be growing the peas in nutrient solution without any toxicants at first, until the plants 
had developed leaves and roots and it was clear which plants should be discarded before adding the salt. At the 
start of the toxicity test, the vials should be drained of solution and the solution with the salts should be added. 
This is possible as the seramis stays tightly in its structure even when turned upside down. This alteration would 
give a useful start transpiration value, but if this was implemented the peas should be grown in bigger vials, so the 
experiment could run longer without the space limitation. 
It is interesting, that the pea plants did not show a stronger toxic effect compared to the willow test as all or almost 
all the water in the pea test setup is taken up by the plants for the cases where the plants are transpiring the most, 
as these vials were often emptied by the plants between the refilling of water. This means, that even if the peas 
were capable of some level of salt extrusion, they would take up the salt anyway as all water was taken up in most 
of the low and some of the medium concentration plants. The conductivity was only measured for two 
concentration groups in one chamber, and is therefore uncertain. Furthermore, the fluid had to be diluted to obtain 
a larger volume, that was measurable (the instrument was incapable of measuring the small amount of fluid left in 
the test vials), which makes the measurement even more uncertain. However, the conductivity in the fluid showed 
an increase in the medium concentration fluid in the elevated temperature and CO2 chamber from 1.2 g NaCl/L, 
(which was the concentration in the medium concentration fluid, filled in at the last refill) to 3.4 mg/L and the 
concentration in the high dose fluid had increased from 3.1 g NaCl/L to 7.0 g NaCl/L, so it looks like there is 
extrusion.

Water Use Efficiency 
In Figure 11.B, the calculated WUE for the pea experiment is shown. Here it is seen, that the WUE is highest for 
the plants in the elevated CO2 chamber in the controls and low exposure groups, as expected (Pérez-López et al. 
2009 ; Melgar et al. 2008), but when the toxic effect increases in the medium and high exposure groups, the WUE 
is very affected. This strong inhibition of the plants agrees with the very low EC50 value found in this chamber and 
the strong inhibition seen in the NRT for this chamber. The WUE for the elevated temperature chamber was 
expected to be lower than the WUE of the plants in the ambient chamber and the elevated CO2 chamber, which 
was also the case for the controls and the low and medium concentration groups. In the elevated temperature 
chamber as well, a strong inhibition was seen for the high concentration group, which resulted in a WUE around 
0. The WUE for the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 had a quite stable value across the exposure 
concentrations, which corresponds well with the high EC50 found in this chamber. The figure of growth of the 
plants in the pea test (Appendix G, Figure XI) show that there was no growth in the plants with high concentrations 
in the chamber with elevated temperature and the chamber with elevated CO2, which causes the low WUE. When 
looking at these plants, it was clear, that these plants wilted before they developed; the roots were small, and in the 
elevated temperature chamber, the shoot development was only 0.5-1 cm and had no leaves. In the elevated CO2 
chamber the shoots were 0.8-2.2 cm and 2 out of 4 had developed a few leaves before they died. In both chambers 
they died long before the experiment ended, and dried out which resulted in the growth of around 0 g in both 
chambers. The plants in the ambient chamber have a slight increase in WUE until the high exposure – at the 
highest concentration, the WUE suffers a dramatic decrease. Compared to the willow test, the ambient peas are 
not affected as much in the WUE, as the growth keeps high in the control, low concentration and medium 
concentration plants. 
Compared to the willow test, the pea test gives reversed results. In the willow test, the largest toxic effect was seen 
in the ambient chamber and the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2, but in the pea test the larges toxic 
effect was for the plants in the elevated temperature chamber and the elevated CO2 chamber. There are many 
differences between the two types of plants that can affect their response to climate alterations. One is optimal 
temperature and CO2 level, which may be different for the two plants. The plants are also very different – one is 
a tree and the other is a herb, and the experiment setups were very different, especially as the peas were poisoned 
already during their early development. Furthermore, their enzymatic reactions, metabolism or excretion rates 
might be influenced differently. Still some measured differences are noted, that could cause different reactions. 
One is, that the expected differences in transpiration caused by the climate (controls) were much clearer in the 
willow test, than in the pea test. Comparing Figure 6.B and 11.B, it is seen, that the percentage difference in total 
transpiration was much larger in the willow test. The transpiration in the elevated CO2 chamber in the pea test is 
surprisingly high, which means that the stomata was not closed as efficiently as in the willow test, so the benefits 
of the reduced uptake of salt was not experienced. As mentioned earlier, elevated transpiration in the pea test leads 
to elevated uptake of salt as the solution volume was reduced enormously between refills. The growth however, 
for the peas in the elevated CO2 chamber was very strongly improved for the controls. In both tests the plants 
grown in the chamber with elevated temperature maintained high transpiration and a quite stable WUE, but the 
toxic response was very different for the two experiments.

Conclusions 
For both plant species, salt showed toxic effects as an increase in salt concentration led to inhibition of the 
transpiration rate. Also in both experiments, the high exposure of 3 g NaCl/L resulted in a more or less lethal 
result for all chambers and a NRT of around 30%. 
In the willow test, the climate conditions causing the highest transpiration was elevated temperature conditions, 
and the lowest transpiration was caused by elevated CO2 as expected. This led to the result in the NRT graphs, 
that the toxic effect of salt was lowest in the chamber with elevated CO2 and the second lowest in the chamber 
with elevated temperature and CO2. The toxic effect was seen already within the first 20-50 hours in the willow 
experiment in all chambers, but 0.33 g NaCl/L was not a toxic dose for the trees in any of the chambers. 
As the transpiration was stimulated most in the chamber with elevated temperature, the highest uptake would be 
expected here, but the plants in this chamber were, especially in the beginning, very efficient at excluding salt, but 
this efficiency decreased as the salt inhibition took effect and the concentration in the water increased. No 
breakthrough (Ct/t-1=1) pattern was seen in the experiment, though it might have occurred for 3 g NaCl/L at 
460 hours in the ambient chamber and the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2 though in the chamber 
with elevated temperature and CO2, the excretion rate kept high for 0.3 and 1 g NaCl/L. The WUE was high in 
the chamber with elevated CO2 as was also expected but the growth was not improved by elevated CO2. There 
were no fixed expectations for the plants in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2, but they had high 
growth and transpiration, compared to the plants in the other chambers and a stable WUE. 
The EC50 values gave different results on toxicity than the NRT figures did. The EC50 value showed the highest 
toxicity in the ambient chamber (1.49 g NaCl/L) and in the chamber with the elevated temperature and CO2 (1.80 
g NaCl/L). Both however agreed, that elevated CO2 resulted in a lower toxicity than in the ambient and that 
ambient conditions were among the most toxic. 
In the pea test, the expectations were also in accordance with the results; the transpiration in the chamber with 
elevated CO2 was lower than in the ambient chamber and the WUE was higher for the control and low exposure 
plant. The climate condition causing the highest transpiration was the chamber with the elevated temperature as 
well. However, the toxic response was reversed in the pea test compared to the willow test, as the EC50 was lowest 
in the chamber with elevated temperature and the chamber with elevated CO2. The difference in EC50 values was 
largest in the pea test, where the largest EC50 vas 3 times bigger than the lowest (2.40 and 0.84 g NaCl/L 
respectively). In the pea test the NRT gave the same result as the EC50 value; the toxicity of salt is highest in the 
chamber with elevated CO2 and lowest in the chamber with elevated temperature and CO2. The difference can be 
caused by the difference in the test setups, the age of the plant when the test started or plant specific differences. 
Overall it was found that the climate conditions do have an important effect on the toxicity of salt towards both 
willow trees and pea plants, but whether the toxic effect of salt will increase in the future climate depends on the 
plant.

Temperature correction for conductivity values 
A temperature correction coefficient was made. For this, two values were needed of the same solution at 
different temperatures. Measurements of the calibration fluid was chosen, as there was a measurement of this 
unchangeable fluid for each time conductivity was measured throughout the experiment. The measured 
conductivities and the corresponding temperatures of the calibration fluid (12 measurements) were plotted (see 
Figure V), but the results were not as expected, but looked random.
