Abstract 
Digital Design Tool for Timber Constructions 
Modern timber construction is still very little known in Denmark, compared to other countries like Germany, 
Sweden and Norway. One of the barriers of introducing wood in the building industry in Denmark is the lack of 
knowledge. In architecture firms, as Henning Larsen Architects, there is no easily accessible knowledge to use while 
designing timber structures in the early project phases. 
This thesis investigates the possibilities of helping the architects designing timber structures with the desired 
properties. Easily accessible information to support the design and decision process avoids mistakes and saves 
resources. The thesis will focus on a for Henning Larsen typical multi-storey office construction. 
The investigation researches the difficulties wood has on the Danish market and how it can be advanced in the 
industry and in architectural practices. For this, the base knowledge was elaborated, which is needed to compare 
different construction systems and their properties with each other. These parameters include structural spans and 
thickness, as well as grid size. Acoustical properties concern the sound insulation, fire safety focuses on the 
resistance in case of fire. An important point in timber construction are the ecological benefits, which are here 
compared for different construction systems. Finally, cost and time are crucial in most projects, why data for the 
systems was gathered and compared. 
This base knowledge is then implemented into a digital design tool, which supports the architects in choosing an 
appropriate system that matches their needs, generating an overview output over the timber structure and its 
properties.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Baseline 
1.1.1. Problem Statement 
Modern timber construction is still very little known in Denmark, compared to other countries like Germany, 
Sweden and Norway. One of the barriers of introducing wood in the building industry in Denmark is the lack of 
knowledge. In architecture firms, as Henning Larsen Architects, there is not enough easily accessible knowledge to 
use while designing timber structures. Another obstacle is the implementation of knowledge into the design 
process, especially in the early design phase. 
1.1.2. Problem Analysis 
As Denmark has not the same tradition of building in wood, as for example Norway and Sweden, the concrete 
lobby has been dominating the Danish building sector for decades (Quinn, 2019). There is though an interest in 
constructing more in wood, which comes from both the architects but also the clients. The motivation behind the 
recently increased interest are mainly sustainability and the aesthetic possibilities, which wood offers. This growing 
interest is enhanced by the media attention, which international but also Danish timber projects receive. At the 
same time, there are not enough specialists available, supporting the architects and engineers in the specific 
questions of designing a timber construction. Knowledge ready to apply is not easily available, which has a 
deterrent effect onto inexperienced designers and hinders the development of new ideas and the improvement of 
the status quo. 
There is a time lag in educating specialists in this field, but the designers themselves could solve many issues, if they 
would have access to the needed resources. To overcome this shortcoming, a systemized access to the needed 
information could help the architects designing timber constructions, which are correctly planned and reach the 
full potential of this material. The implementation of this knowledge needs to be quick and easy, otherwise it will 
not be used in the fast-paced early design phase of a building.

1.2. Objective 
This project is aimed at improving the architects’ knowledge about timber construction and to include that 
knowledge into the design process, to find better solutions with the desired properties. The base knowledge has to 
be researched and put into an overview, which helps comparing the systems to each other. This helps preventing 
mistakes and thus extra costs in the design process, which occur, when planning a structure without enough 
information. The whole project is aimed at Henning Larsen Architects and their typical structures. The scope of 
this thesis are multi-storey office buildings in wood. 
Henning Larsen Architects is a Copenhagen based international architectural practice with offices in several 
countries. The projects range from summer houses to new headquarters for big corporations and masterplans of 
whole districts. Office buildings are typical projects and profit from a certain repeatability, why it is aligned with 
this thesis’ focus. 
The investigation includes questions like 
 
What are the biggest obstacles for building in wood in Denmark? 
 
What kind of knowledge are the architects lacking in designing timber structures? 
 
How can timber construction in Denmark be advanced? 
 
What is the future of timber construction and how can architecture be oriented towards it? 
 
Which influence has the architecture onto the timber construction? 
Then, base knowledge is investigated and processed, for example 
 
Which timber structures are viable for the mentioned office typology? 
 
What are viable spans and grid dimensions? 
 
What are typical thicknesses and build-ups of structures? 
 
Which acoustical properties can be achieved? 
 
What is the fire resistance of the building parts? 
 
What are the ecological benefits of the chosen structure? 
 
What is the economic aspect? 
 
How can the buildings be more systemised and rationalised? 
 
This base knowledge should then be implemented into a digital design tool, which supports the architects in 
choosing an appropriate system that matches their needs, generating an overview output over the timber structure 
and its properties.

1.3. Material and Methods 
1.3.1. Methods 
The theoretical part of this thesis will consist of timber related knowledge and market studies. The insights into the 
industry mechanisms will be gained through expert talks, which are both conducted at Henning Larsen, but also 
outside of the company, with professionals from related fields. The research of the profound timber knowledge 
will both happen based on working experience at Henning Larsen and literature research. The base knowledge will 
be gathered and evaluated with a simple point system, to highlight the strengths and weaknesses of the 
constructions and to visualise their properties. 
Based on this information, a digital tool will be developed, which implements this broad timber knowledge 
through easy visualisations into the design process.

It is important to have access to the most recent studies and information about timber construction and the trends

timber projects from the UK is used for comparisons and numbers of typical buildings (Think Wood, 2018). 
Internet sources and tools will only be used where no other sources are available. The author’s experience is 
brought in to validate the accuracy and other sources are used for quality checks, where some information is

1.3.3. Programs 
The last two decades brought a big development in software applications, which offer new possibilities in 
architecture, rationalisation and free-form design. The most common programs in architecture and engineering are 
computer-aided design (CAD) software, which help drawing and modelling structures in 2D and 3D. 
Rhinoceros is a CAD software by McNeel, which is often called Rhino or Rhino3D (see Figure 1). The program is 
widely used in architecture and design practices, where free form models are generated. Grasshopper 3D is a visual 
programming language for Rhino, which helps automating and parametrising the design process. There are 
numerous plugins and tools for Grasshopper and Rhino, which help improving the design through analysis of the 
structure or variable parameters to quickly change the Rhino model. 
 
 
Figure 1 - Example of a free-form 3D model from Rhino 5.0 (Source: food4rhino). 
 
Rhino is common in the earlier design stages of architecture practices and is also used at Henning Larsen 
Architects. In the later phases of a project, the designing and detailing process is though continued in Revit. 
For this thesis, Rhino 6 and Grasshopper were used to program a plugin, which helps in designing the timber 
structures. It incorporates the knowledge gathered for this thesis and translates it into a visual output, which can be 
used for further design of a building. This supports designing based on the right parameters even for architects 
without knowledge in timber construction.

2. Theoretical Part 
2.1. Current State of Research 
2.1.1.1. Climate Change 
Climate change has been an increasingly important topic over the last few decades, as it has become visible that the 
humans’ behaviour affects our planet. The building industry is responsible for up to 40% of all energy 
consumption in the EU and 15% of the global CO2 emissions are from the operation energy for buildings alone 
(REBUS, 2020). It is generally agreed on, that the CO2 emissions have to be reduced to minimise the heating of the 
atmosphere. Building in wood is a way to reduce CO2 emissions and energy consumption in the building industry 
(reThink Wood, 2015). This is due to reduced fossil fuel emissions in the production stage and the ability of 
carbon sequestration (Quinn, 2019). 
Wood is a sustainable material, which has a minimal impact onto the climate, sequesters carbon and is completely 
renewable. Sustainability is an often used and misused term, describing the co-living of all species without harming 
each other in a way that has serious consequences on the future of the biosphere. 
2.1.1.2. Modern Timber Construction 
Modern timber construction evolved from traditional structures within the last 50 years, starting in central Europe 
with countries as Germany, Austria and Switzerland. With some time-delay, the Scandinavian region also shifted 
towards the modern timber construction. This excludes Denmark though, as here, the tradition of building in 
wood is less prominent, as more bricks were used and recently mostly concrete with a very strong lobby. 
Northern America is another region, where timber construction is predominant and which managed the shift to 
modern timber construction. Together with Australia and the UK, they are home to some of the highest timber 
structures so far.  
Starting as a construction typology rather for niche projects, timber construction became by now respected and 
more widespread. In the last decade, the race towards wooden skyscrapers has begun and buildings like the 
Mjøstårnet, the currently highest timber building in the world, received good media coverage. 
In the meanwhile, research has been conducted for more sustainable, rationalised and economic timber structures, 
but also to solve some of the constraints and improve the possibilities for what is viable to build in wood. As the 
more extraordinary structures like high-rises and big domes were pushed forward, some more conventional 
structures like commercial and residential buildings did not get the same attention. There is already a high degree of 
systemisation and parametrisation in free-form shells in wood. The same trends are though not present in 
rectangular multi-storey buildings, although there is a much higher number of them. There is a big potential in 
focusing on the rationalisation of these typologies, but digitalisation alone is not the solution to advance timber 
construction.

2.1.1.3. Digitalisation 
When looking at the process of designing, planning and building a timber building, it is important to include the 
stakeholders into the base processes. This means for example integrating the contractor’s experience early into the 
design process, to achieve the best results. In this way, mistakes can be avoided, as for example designing a timber 
building with an identical layout as a conventional concrete structure. (Antemann, 2020) 
There is available software and some tools and plugins specialised in timber constructions. A well-known CAD 
software for timber structures is CadWork, which has the advantage of including features specifically designed for 
wood and the possibility to transfer the model directly to producing machinery as CNC mills and robots. The 
following figures show an example of a free-form beam with the required connections modelled in CadWork and 
during production. 
 
Parametric design tools include Beaver for Rhino, which is a Grasshopper plugin for the structural analysis of 
timber structures according to Eurocode 5. GluLamb is a plugin for Rhino, allowing the modelling of free-form 
timber with outputs as fibre-orientation, analysis of the amount of bending or location of intersections.

2.1.1.4. Advantages and Disadvantages of Building in Wood 
There are many good reasons of why to build in wood, as sustainability arguments as a very important factor, but 
also aesthetics and degree of quality, prefabrication and time savings. The reasons why to build in wood (as 
displayed in figure 4) will not be discussed in in detail in this document, where not necessary for the understanding 
of choices, as there are many existing publications about advantages and disadvantages of timber structures, as for

Among common challenges in building in wood are strict regulations with inflexible authorities and engineers, 
which make it difficult to introduce new ideas, as building in wood. Others include challenges in fire safety, mostly

2.2. Introduction to Timber Construction 
2.2.1. Mass Timber and Light Timber Frames 
2.2.1.1. Construction Materials 
Building in timber is traditionally based on solid planks and beams cut out of softwood and occasionally 
hardwood, for higher durability or strength and where no other species were available. The most commonly used 
species in European and Northern American timber construction are still coniferous woods, as for example spruce, 
fir and pine. This is due to them being widely available, comparably fast-growing and them being very suitable for 
processing and construction. In modern timber construction, the wood is often used as processed products instead 
of the traditional way of building with solid wood. Some of the most common products include glued laminated 
timber (glulam), laminated veneer lumber (LVL) and cross-laminated timber (CLT). Glued products have several 
advantages as improved structural properties, controlled surface quality and more economic use of the raw 
material. Boards like LVL and CLT have the advantage of transporting loads in more than one direction, which 
reduces the anisotropy, which helps as well with deformation due to changes in the wood’s humidity (Henning 
Larsen, 2020). The mentioned products are widely described in literature, why a more detailed specification is not 
given here. 
The two main categories of timber construction systems are light timber frame structures and mass timber 
structures. Light structures are made of small elements as beams, joists, frames etc. with their smallest dimension 
under 80 mm, as for example timber frame structures. Mass timber describes systems made of boards (for example 
CLT or LVL) or bigger elements as glued laminated beams (Brandon, 2018). 
2.2.1.2. Mass Timber Construction 
Mass timber construction has become increasingly popular over the last two decades around the world. It is often 
considered an easy way of building in wood, why especially CLT has become widely used. This can be explained 
due to the relatively simple calculations, prefabrication and connections, which are manageable by engineers and 
contractors without big experience. The boards’ behaviour is comparable to concrete, which makes it a good choice 
for beginners of timber construction. It shows, that especially CLT has become increasingly popular in the Nordic 
countries in the last few years, as it is the ideal construction system to begin building in timber. LVL is less used, 
due to the higher price and characteristics as more processing and a higher percentage of adhesives, although the 
strength of LVL is at least 10 % higher than CLT (Kolb, 2008). Composite structures with timber and concrete are 
becoming more popular in mid to high-rises in wood, as they allow for longer spans and higher stiffness, combined 
with a better footfall-sound insulation (ApS, 2001).

2.2.1.3. Light Timber Frame Structures 
Opposed to mass timber, light frame structures demand more knowledge and preparation, as there are some 
specific rules and connections which are not known to people usually building with concrete. This makes more 
engineers and contractors refrain from these structures, although they have been used for example in the German-
speaking countries (as Germany, Austria and Switzerland) for many decades. Timber frame and panel 
constructions have advantages as a lower weight and thickness of the build-up, possibility to easily include 
insulation and installation in the construction and savings in material-use (Kolb, 2008). 
 
2.2.2. Common Construction Systems 
2.2.2.1. Frame Structure 
A frame structure consists of a skeleton from beams and columns and any type of floors, as it can be seen in the 
following figure. Traditionally, frames were used for example in balloon frames for family homes in Northern 
America. The modern frames discussed in this thesis are of bigger dimensions and in a bigger grid size, giving a 
flexible skeleton structure for multi-storey construction (Kaufmann, 2018). 
Depending on the chosen floor type, transversal beams are not necessary, which is for example for the panel floors 
with CLT, LVL and box structures. A frame construction gives bigger freedom and flexibility in the layout, as 
there is no need for bearing walls. This is ideal for office typologies and other buildings with changing tenants or 
usage. The bracing can be solved with bracing walls or diagonal stiffening between the frames, for example with 
steel or wood crosses.

2.2.2.2. Panel Construction 
Panel construction was developed from Northern American frame construction, which was built on-site. As a 
further development, the panel construction are elements with various degrees of prefabrication, usually at least 
including the frame plus one side closed with a board (see following figure). With the highest degree of 
prefabrication, the elements are delivered to the building site finished with interior and exterior cladding, windows, 
electric sockets etc. Together with CLT structures, panel construction is the most popular timber construction 
system in Europe, especially for walls. The panels have the advantage of being comparably thin with a good 
insulation value, as the cavity between the studs is insulated. This makes it a popular choice for exterior walls and 
façade elements (Kaufmann, 2018).

2.2.2.3. Box Slab Construction 
A special type of floors is the box construction, consisting of rectangular boxes forming hollow beams. This

thermal insulation if used as a roof layer. Several boxes mounted together give a similar structure as panel

2.2.2.4. Dowel Laminated Timber 
Dowel laminated timber consists of planks that are joined to solid load-bearing elements. There are various 
connection systems, from nails to screws, adhesives or wooden dowels (see figure 11). Horizontal elements as 
bracing planks on top and bottom or added boards increase the rigidity of the panels. Originally, dowel laminated 
timber was developed mainly for floor structures, achieving good strength and stiffness while using inferior quality 
boards. The upright standing boards are structurally ideal and the floor has therefore the lowest structural height of 
all timber floors. The system is now also used for walls, although bigger openings for floors and windows need 
horizontal transversal beams (Kaufmann, 2018).

2.2.2.5. Cross Laminated Timber (CLT) 
CLT is an engineered timber panel used since the 1990s. It consists of layers arranged crosswise to each other, 
reducing the anisotropy and uneven shrinking and deformation. The panels are built up by an uneven number of 
boards, usually three, five or seven, with the same grain direction for the two covering layers (see Figure 11). 
CLT has become increasingly popular over the last two decades and is now one of the main construction methods 
for low and midrise structures in Europe, Northern America and Australia. The system is so popular because it is 
comparably easy to apply and introduce into new markets, it has very good structural properties at a high degree of 
freedom and is economical. As a versatile construction material, CLT is also used in combination with many other 
systems and materials, e.g. in composite timber-concrete floors. 
 
 
2.2.2.6. Laminated-Veneer Lumber (LVL) 
LVL is an engineered wood panel fabricated of thin veneers glued together, usually around 3 mm thin, which are 
unlike CLT mostly oriented into the same direction. This makes the panel directional and the properties more 
comparable to the ones of glulam. The removal of wood defects in the veneers and the increased uniformity give 
better structural strength compared to solid wood. LVL can be used in similar applications as CLT, as mass timber 
walls and floors (see following figure). It is especially strong as wall panels due to the directional layers of the wood. 
LVL is also used locally to reinforce weak points and joints in other constructions (Kaufmann, 2018).

2.3.2.2. Challenges Apparent on All Markets 
Investors often are afraid of having to pay a higher price when building in wood compared to traditional structures.

2.3.2.3. Potential 
The forest area in the EU increased by 5% between 1990 and 2010, in contrary to the rainforest areas in other parts 
of the world, where forests still are exploited. It has to be kept in mind that the forests in the EU only account for 
approximately 5% of the world’s forests. Currently, there is less timber harvested than the annual increment of 
wood mass in Europe and Northern America (Eurostat, 2016). This means, that there is the potential to build 
more in wood while still sourcing the material sustainably. In Germany, around 85% of the annual wood increment 
is harvested, of which 51% are used currently as construction timber, but theoretically, 65% could be used as 
construction timber. Just 35% of the annual wood harvest would be necessary though to build the entire volume of 
new buildings each year (Kaufmann, 2018). 
The following diagram shows the wood harvest in Germany and what the round wood is used for. The total annual 
increment of wood mass is 80 million m3, the harvested wood is mostly softwood, which then is used in the 
construction and packaging industry (Thünen, 2017). Germany has a relatively low percentage of energy wood, as 
the main energy source for heating are fossil fuels. 
There is potential to build much more in wood, to reuse and recycle all wood and to harvest more hardwoods. The 
percentage of leaf trees in Danish forests increased from ca. 30% to over 50% in the last two decades, a source that 
has more potential in construction. The most widespread hardwood species are beech and oak (Nord-Larsen, 
2017). In recent years, research has focused on developing more construction timber of hardwoods, to use this 
source for more than solely heating. Challenges in using hardwoods include processing, applying adhesives and 
lower dimensional stability, while the biggest advantage is the superior structural strength of hardwood. New 
products using hardwoods are among others beech LVL and glulam made of ash and oak.

2.4. The Main Parameters in Timber Construction 
2.4.1. Structural Capacities 
2.4.1.1. Anisotropy 
The main chemical composition of wood are cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin, as it can be seen in the following 
figure on the left. The cellulose forms the structural skeleton of the wood, hemicellulose consists mostly of sugars 
stored in the cell walls and lignin is the cement holding the fibres and cell walls together. As a tree grows, it builds 
cells and fibres with longitudinal orientation. This makes the material anisotropic, describing a material, which 
does not have identical properties into all directions, as for example the strength, deformation and shrinkage values. 
In the case of spruce and pine, this means that the tensile strength in fibre direction (longitudinal) is around 30 
times higher, than the strength orthogonal to the fibres (tangential), see figure on the right. The differences in the 
cell build-up and structure of the fibres explain the varying properties of the different wood species. 
Another characteristic of wood is that the tensile strength in fibre direction is approximately twice as big as the 
compressive strength (Træinformation, 2014). This can be explained due to cellulose in the fibres showing big 
tensile strength, whereas the lignin holding the fibres and cells together fails already at lower impacts.

2.4.2. Fire Safety 
2.4.2.1. Bias in Timber Construction 
The devastating city-fires of the Middle Ages raised concerns against the use of wood in cities. Many towns even 
prescribed the use of stone and bricks instead, as this helped to reduce the risk of serious fires. With the 
development of a well-functioning fire brigade system, the risk of big fires could be greatly reduced (Schiøtt 
Sørensen, 2014). The bias against the use of wood is continuing to this day though, although wooden construction 
has to follow the same fire requirements as any other material and is not more susceptible to fire than a concrete or 
steel structure (Dragsted, 2017). This bias is difficult to get out of the heads of clients and authorities, which are the 
ones deciding about the implementation and approval of using wood. This prejudice is for example illustrated in 
the article “Kronik: Træbygninger kan føre til altødelæggende bybrande”, where renowned Danish fire experts 
discuss why it is irresponsible, to build in wood (Hertz, 2019). The article is based on outdated knowledge and 
strong roots in the Danish concrete industry. A way to change this and to help the success of wood in Denmark can 
be achieved by proving its safety in case of fire via simulations, laboratory testing and pioneer projects. 
A Swedish study from 2016 showed, that there in fact statistically are around three times fewer cases of fire in 
timber buildings than in conventional structures. Among the 10’000 studied timber buildings, there were only 73 
reported fire incidents between 1998 and 2014, of which only 1 caused a fire so big, that the building had to be 
demolished, a number which is lower than in conventional structures. Reasons for that are amongst others that 
multi-storey buildings mostly are newer and constructed after current fire regulations (Eriksson, 2016). 
2.4.2.2. Fire Safety in Denmark 
The regulations and codes for fire safety are mostly national and vary from country to country. Nevertheless are 
certain rules similar in most countries of the EU. The buildings are usually classified according to their height; the 
higher the building, the more restrictive the regulations. The rules are defined for the partition elements as floors 
and walls between fire units, which for example are between apartments or separate big public buildings into 
smaller compartments. The fire regulations set as the goal to protect people, rescue teams and the buildings and 
goods themselves, as it can be seen illustrated in the following figure.

To achieve safe buildings in case of fire, the Danish fire regulations give the options to either prove the performance 
of the fire protection or to follow the preaccepted solutions from the example collection. The simulation and 
evidence of a sufficient fire performance is connected with expenses and is therefore avoided as far as possible by 
the client. As the example collection focuses on traditional structures made of incombustible materials, concrete 
has a clear advantage compared to timber in terms of the building codes (Qvist, 2020). In January 2020, new rules 
in the Danish building regulation BR18 about the building permits took effect, which prescribe the approval of fire 
safety and statics for buildings in categories 2 – 4. Although the approval via a certified consultant or third party 
might be more costly and time consuming, it gives the timber construction more possibilities to incorporate 
structures, which are not mentioned in the preaccepted solutions and makes the permits less dependent of the 
approval by authorities (DI Dansk Byggeri, 2020). 
2.4.2.3. Combustion Behaviour of Wood 
Wood is a combustible material, but burns comparably slow, because of the forming of a charred layer on the 
surface of the wood, which slows down the burning process. The combustion of wood is steady, with a speed of 
ca. 0.4 – 0.8 mm/min, depending on the species. The harder the wood, the slower the combustion process. 
Engineered products as CLT have a base charring rate of approximately 0.65 mm/min (CEN, 2004). 
When a timber construction is on fire, the strength of the wood is kept gradually until it is burnt completely 
through, which is in contrast to the deformation of steel in case of fire or the spontaneous failure of reinforced 
concrete. This makes it a highly predictable and calculable material for the performance in case of fire 
(Træinformation, 2014). The following figure shows the charring of wood and a comparison of structural steel and 
wood and their loss of strength in fire. While steel quickly loses all its strength, the charring of timber slows the 
combustion and loss of strength visibly down.

2.4.2.5. Classification of Buildings 
After the Danish code BR18, buildings are categorised into different use categories, risk classes, consequence 
classes, construction classes and fire classes, after which the requirements for the fire safety are defined. The 
classification is rather complex and it is advised to be done by a fire engineer, but the following description will give 
typical classes for office buildings, as they are relevant for this thesis. 
Buildings are put into six different use categories (Danish: anvendelseskategorier), according to if the users know 
the emergency exits, if they can rescue themselves, if there are beds in the building and if there is a limitation of the 
maximum number of people in a room. The higher the class, the stricter the requirements to the fire measures are. 
As an office is not a sleeping location and the people are physically able to rescue themselves, offices can be 
categorised in the lower use categories. The employees usually have knowledge of the building and therefore of the 
emergency ways, which leads to a use category 1 for offices (BR18, §85, Table 1). 
The following figure illustrates the concept of the different use categories, from 1 for residential buildings, offices 
and similar to 6 for hospitals and elderly homes. 
 
 
Figure 29 - Illustration of the different use categories (DI Dansk Byggeri, 2020). 
 
The four risk classes describe the use of a building, the complexity of evacuation and the fire load in the relevant 
sections (see following figure). The higher the risk, the higher the risk class and the bigger the fire safety 
requirements will be. Depending on the height of the example office building, it will be categorised in another risk 
class. If the top floor is not higher than 9.6 m above terrain and one floor underground, it is categorised in risk 
class 2. If the top floor is between 9.6 and 45 m above terrain with maximum one floor underground, it is in risk 
class 3 and if the building is higher or has more floors below ground, it is in risk class 4. The most likely case for the 
example office building discussed in this paper will be risk class 3, although classes 2 and 4 might apply in some 
cases as well (BR18, §86, Table 2).

2.4.2.7. Fire Safety in BR18 
The installation of a sprinkler system helps to lower the temperature in a room in case of fire, which reduces the 
flammability of the surfaces and slows the combustion process down. This could be an advantage in the 
documentation of the fire load and safety of a timber construction, as the sprinklers would protect the load-bearing 
timber structure. Although the positive effect of sprinklers is part of the European fire regulations and it is allowed 
to use them in the documentation process in many countries, e.g. the UK, it is not allowed to include them in 
Denmark to achieve better fire ratings (Dragsted, 2017). In Denmark, an automatic sprinkler system has to be 
installed in multi-storey timber buildings with fire sections of more than 2000 m2 for buildings in use class 1. The 
maximum area of a fire compartment is 10.000 m2 (§ 123, Table 4.5 in Bilag 3 to chapter 5 in BR18). 
The example collection prescribes incombustible materials of the class A2-s1,d0 for structures with a height of the 
top floor more than 9.6 m above ground and a resistance of R60. It is often believed, that it is not allowed to build

performance of the building can be calculated and proved, there are no clear set rules about the maximum height of

2.4.2.9. Fire Safety of the Chosen Construction Systems 
The fire safety of the different construction systems is compared over the standard build-ups of floors in offices 
with a span of 6 meters from table 41 on page 122. The build-up of the floor is not regarded in this overview, as 
mostly the resistance of the ceiling is of importance. The fire resistance is just to give a general direction and has to 
be evaluated in every single case. 
The surface of all systems can be fireproofed by encapsulating with gypsum boards. CLT and other mass wood 
systems have comparably slow charring rates due to the heavy and compact material. The thick material guarantees 
good fire resistance with sufficient remaining stability after a fire of over an hour. A problem for systems like CLT 
and LVL is the delamination due to failure of the glued bond. When a layer of material falls of, the newly exposed 
wood does not have a char layer and therefore, the combustion process will be faster again, just like in the 
beginning of a fire. New developments in the adhesives brought improvements and the delamination can mostly be 
eliminated (Kopczynski, 2018).

2.4.3. Acoustic Properties 
2.4.3.1. Introduction to Sound Insulation 
An important parameter in timber construction are the acoustic properties of a system. As the sound insulation in 
older buildings traditionally is rather weak, people expect a very low noise level in newer buildings and get bothered 
by the neighbour’s noise easier, than some decades ago. A bad sound insulation in buildings is not only irritating 
the users, but can also lead to stress and health problems (Aschehough, 1987). 
Sound insulation should not be confused with room acoustics, which is about the ambient sound in rooms and for 
example the dampening of unwanted reverberation. This paper only focuses on sound insulation, as the room 
acoustics in wooden buildings do not differ a lot to those in conventional structures. 
The first challenge with the sound insulation in timber constructions lies in the nature of the material. As more 
people are bothered by the low frequencies of impact sound, this can be reduced by constructing with heavy 
materials, where concrete has a natural advantage over wood, which is much more lightweight (Høsøien, 2016). 
Another challenge is the bias and lack of knowledge in timber construction. Many people know wood beam 
ceilings from old buildings, which have a very poor sound insulation and they expect new timber buildings to 
perform equally bad (Glasø, 2011). There have been built cheap and insufficient wooden slabs over the last 
decades, which unfortunately reinforces this prejudice. The planners need to have the knowledge of how to 
properly add the needed layers, add weight to a wooden slab and to make sure the connections and execution are 
done professionally. If those rules are kept, a wooden construction performs equally well as solid structures and 
have even advantages for very high performing box-in-box structures as music rooms or concert houses (Forum 
HolzBau, 2013). The extra layers needed in wooden buildings come though usually at higher costs, but CLT has 
the advantage of being relatively heavy and thus is ideal as an affordable alternative to concrete slabs (Qvist, 2020). 
2.4.3.2. Sound Theory 
The regulations and codes vary from country to country, but they usually divide the sound insulation into footfall 
sound and airborne sound, with the distinction between measured values and values obtained in lab tests. The 
loudness of a sound is expressed in Decibel (dB), which is a logarithmic scale, doubling the volume for every 
increased 10 dB. The perceived volume level changes are illustrated in the following figure.

Airborne sound is transmitted over the air from room to room through walls and floors. The values are given as the 
weighted-apparent sound reduction index R’w from field measurement. The value describes how much the 
airborne sound is reduced from one room to another, thus the higher R’w, the better the sound insulation. If only 
the laboratory measurements Rw are available, the sound reduction index has to be corrected with an allowance of 
3 dB to include the flanking sound transmission (Geyer, 2016). 
Footfall or impact sound is transmitted through direct impacts onto the floor or a flanking building part. The 
values are in L’n,w from field measurements or Ln,w from laboratory measurements, where a correction factor of 3 dB 
should be included for the flanking transmission values as well. L’n,w describes, how much sound is transmitted 
through a building part from the sound of a standardised forge, thus the lower the value, the better the sound 
insulation. 
2.4.3.3. Sound Insulation Regulations 
The regulations include six different sound classes, from A to F, where A describes the best sound insulation. 
Class C is required to fulfil the standards in BR18 and is the most common sound class, although it is only designed 
to satisfy 80 % of the building users (Glasø, 2011). If a better sound insulation is wished for, classes A or B should 
be chosen.

2.4.4. Environmental Properties 
2.4.4.1. Climate Change 
Our climate is changing rapidly, and the point of no return might already be passed. To slow down or even invert 
the effects of the climate change and global warming, we need to use more sustainable resources and the goal of 
limiting the global warming to 1.5 degrees still will have a big impact on the earth. Globally, the building industry 
stands for 36% of the global energy use and 39% of the energy-related CO2 emissions. 
Apart from renewable energy production and energy efficient buildings, an underestimated factor is the embodied 
carbon, which is emitted while producing the constructing materials in the first place. Up to 11% of the total global 
greenhouse gas emissions alone come from the manufacturing of building materials, see figure 38 (UN 
Environment, 2017). As traditional building materials as bricks, concrete and steel require energy intense mining 
and high temperatures in production, their emissions are much higher than for example for wood, especially 
concerning the use of fossil fuels. Around 7% of the global CO2 emissions come from the cement production alone 
(Qvist, 2020). 
A tree does not only sequester carbon from the atmosphere, structural timber is also less energy consuming in 
production and transportation. This makes wood and other bio-based materials to the only construction materials, 
which can have a negative CO2 balance (Henning Larsen, 2020). The use of wood in construction is sustainable, if 
the material comes from responsible forestry and if it replaces other materials in the building, which have a higher 
global warming potential. 
The EU forests and wood products absorb up to 11% of the European greenhouse gas emissions. This highlights 
the importance of sustainable forestry and storing CO2 in built wood (Eurostat, 2016).

2.4.4.2. Energy Consumption and CO2 Emissions 
The energy consumption of a building is divided into the embodied energy (used for construction) and the 
operational energy (throughout the use-stage of the building). The operational energy presents with up to 95% the 
biggest part of the energy consumed during the whole lifetime. The more energy efficient the operational systems 
of a building are, the more important the part of the embodied energy becomes. Wood is an excellent material 
concerning energy efficiency and has the unique capacity of sequestering carbon during its growth. Even excluding 
the sequestering capability, the production of 1 ton of concrete is releasing about 5 times more carbon emissions 
than construction timber (Kopczynski, 2018). 
2.4.4.3. Transportation 
Timber is transported from the forest to the material producer, then in some cases passed on to the prefabrication 
manufacture and finally transported to the building site. The main mode of transportation are mostly lorries. The 
biggest roundwood producers in Europe are Sweden, Finland, Germany and France. Many of the timber 
manufacturers as Stora Enso and Binderholz are placed in these countries, reducing the transportation distance 
from logging site to manufacturing. Denmark does not have big material suppliers or timber prefabrication yet, 
why many of the materials and prefabricated elements are delivered from Sweden and Germany, but also Austria 
and the Baltic countries (Eurostat, 2016). Compared to in-situ concrete frames, a CLT structure requires about 
85% less transportation. This is due to the light weight and the economical use of the material (Quinn, 2019). 
2.4.4.4. Life Cycle Assessment 
There are many discussions about what to call sustainable and what not. This thesis focuses on the environmental 
aspects of it, but not the social or economic sustainability. A Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is used to evaluate the 
environmental impacts of products and services over life stages, from the raw material, production and 
construction, assembly, use stage, demolition until recycling, waste disposal or incineration. There are five main 
stages defined, with more or less well-known data and scenarios, see figure 39 (LCAByg, 2020). 
==PAGE:59==

It is generally agreed on, that the most common indicator to compare environmental benefits is via the global 
warming potential (GWP) in an LCA. Wood and other bio-based materials have the ability to store carbon from 
the atmosphere over their complete lifespan. This carbon sequestration is a big advantage when aiming for low 
emissions. The carbon dioxide is though emitted once the material is decaying or incinerated in the end of life stage. 
Therefore, the wood should be kept in use as long as possible. (Krieva, 2020). The goal is to increase the lifespan of 
the wood through a cascade use, from bigger parts, using the scraps for e.g. particleboards, re-using and recycling 
materials down to the incineration with generating heat and energy as the last stage. 
In the LCA of bio-based building materials, there is the discussion about how far the sequestration effect of the 
material is accounted for and the stored CO2 can be subtracted from the total GWP. Some countries and 
regulations allow the negative values, benefitting the timber industry, as it gives measurable advantages building in 
wood. The future regulation EN15804 from July 2022 gives though new guidelines about the storage handling and 
biogenic carbon emissions, not allowing the calculation with the sequestering effect of wood. This will potentially 
harm the timber industry and is a step back, as it lets concrete and other materials seem to have a lower GWP than 
wood, giving them advantages in sustainability certifications (Lewis, 2020). Figure 40 shows a comparison of a 
whole building’s LCA in timber and concrete. For both materials, there are two scenarios for a best and worst case, 
depending on the exact type of material, amount of steel reinforcement, impregnation etc.

2.4.4.5. End of Life Scenarios 
For a sustainability evaluation, it is important how the building and materials are treated at the end of life stage. 
The building should be designed with a long lifespan in mind, which can be achieved through the choice of robust 
constructive solutions, durable materials and adaptable design, which allows adjusting the layout of the building to 
new and changed living situations. The lifespan of a CLT structure is often defined as 80 years, after which the 
material either is reused, recycled or disposed. 
Reusing CLT and other timber structures is the ideal way of keeping all material in the circulation and maintaining 
100 % carbon sequestration. It can be difficult finding a specific situation to reuse the material and building parts in 
their exact same form. 
The amount of recycled building materials has increased in the last years, with a recycling rate of 80% of all 
construction and demolition waste in Denmark, which is a high percentage, compared to other countries. Timber 
structures are chipped and reshaped into new wood products as oriented strand boards (OSB) and particle boards. 
This end of life stage also maintains the sequestration of 100% CO2 as all material stays in circulation and the new 
products can be used in any building project. The reworking of the wood emits minimal CO2 emissions. 
There are different ways of disposal of timber structures. The best case is an incineration with energy recovery, 
which generates energy and heat, omitting CO2 release of fossil fuel, while though emitting the sequestered carbon 
stored in the wood. If the wood is incinerated without energy recovery, the benefit of the carbon sequestration is

2.4.4.6. Sustainability Certification 
There are several certificates to qualify a building to be sustainable. The best known ones in Denmark are from 
Deutsche Gesellschaft für Nachhaltiges Bauen (DGNB), Building Research Establishment Environmental 
Assessment Method (BREEAM) and Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED). The certificates 
are part of the sustainable development, giving clients the possibility to certify their building according to different 
rules in environmental, economic and social sustainability. Many certificates reward the environmental benefits of 
building in wood, which can be a reason for the clients to choose timber. 
Green Building Council Denmark chose the German DGNB certification as the standard evaluation method for 
sustainable buildings in Denmark, based on the European Standards. It is a holistic approach of a building’s 
quality, regarding sociocultural aspects, functional and environmental aspects, economy as well as technical and 
process quality. There are different levels to achieve, from bronze to platinum certification. 
The wood itself can be certified too, guaranteeing sustainable forestry and responsible resource use. The two most 
common certificates in Denmark are Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) and Programme for the Endorsement of 
Forest Certification (PEFC). Of the Danish forest area, approximately 50% is PEFC and 22% is FSC certified 
(Quinn, 2019). It is important to not blindly trust the certificates, but to use the wood responsibly and choose 
manufacturers which source their material from trustworthy sources, to make sure not to support the exploitation 
of forests or illegal lumbering and trade.

2.4.4.8. Additives in the Different Timber Systems 
The amount of glue used in a system is another factor in the sustainability evaluation, as the chemical ingredients 
used in the additives can be harmful for the environment. The regulations guarantee that the users of buildings stay 
unaffected by the additives and the codes became much stricter over the last years. Where it was common practice 
to use formaldehyde in additives for particle boards and other derived timber products in the past decades, 
regulations today limit those toxic ingredients. The threshold value for volatile formaldehyde in the room air is 
recommended to stay below 0.1 ppm (parts per million) by the World Health Organization (WHO). Untreated 
solid wood is a natural source of formaldehyde, but which is very low with only 0.01 ppm.

are still more costly though. The use of PF is decreasing as well, as the dark adhesive is not very popular for visible

building site. Of the CLT manufacturing phase, around 30% of the emissions come from the use of adhesives, the 
remaining GWP is from processing the timber (Chen, 2019).

2.4.5. Economical Evaluation 
2.4.5.1. Cost Drivers in Timber Buildings 
The direct material costs of CLT are around 30 – 40% higher than the ones for traditional materials, often giving a 
false impression of timber buildings being much more expensive. When comparing the whole construction of a 
timber building, different factors help saving costs, which leads to an overall price for a timber building comparable 
to a concrete building. 
Timber structures are lightweight with around 500 kg/m3, which is 80% less than concrete. This reduces the 
groundworks and needed foundation, which leads to cost savings between 10 – 20%. In some cases, this also means 
that more units can be built, which helps to generate additional income. This was for example the case for the 
project Dalston Works in London, which is built over a transit tunnel. As this restricted the maximum load for the 
building above, CLT was an ideal choice, as it allowed to build 121 apartments instead of 86 which would have 
been possible in a concrete building. 
The transportation costs of a CLT frame is typically only around 10% of the overall cost, which is due to the big 
reduction of transportation by 80 – 85% compared to traditional structures (Think Wood, 2018). 
As soon as the planners acquire more experience with timber constructions, the costs are expected to fall. The 
expertise will help in working with a standardised work flow, choosing the best solutions, having reliable suppliers 
and contractors and avoiding mistakes. Those factors combined will make timber construction more competitive 
(Mahlum, 2014).

2.4.5.3. Time Savings 
A very important factor in saving money is through the shorter construction time of timber buildings. Although 
the planning phase of a timber building often requires more time because of the level of detail in prefabrication, the 
ordering time, prefabrication and installation are much shorter than with a concrete structure. This results in 
general in a 20 – 30% shorter overall construction time for wooden buildings and 25 – 50% if only regarding the 
structure over the foundation (Adserballe & Knudsen, 2020) (Think Wood, 2018). Saving time lowers the costs of 
the building, as 50 – 70% less personnel is needed on the building site and the tenants of the new building can move 
in many months earlier. Additionally, the prefabrication process in the factory is very efficient and precise, with 
tolerances of around ± 3 mm and the surfaces do not necessarily need additional treatment or cladding, which saves 
on the finishing (Think Wood, 2018).

2.5.2.4. Environmental Properties 
The environmental performance of the different timber systems are rated in the following table, based on 
chapter 2.4. and tables 22 – 26. The material use describes how much wood there is needed in the structure, 
according to table 22. The less material needed to achieve the required functionality, the better and thus the higher 
the score. This is inverse to the CO2 storage potential, as the more material is used, the bigger the CO2 
sequestration. The bigger the carbon storage, the higher the score. 
The additives include both the adhesives but also the steel fasteners, as listed in table 24 and appendix b. Dowel 
laminated timber with wooden dowels is the only system without any additives and reaches therefore the maximum 
score in the evaluation. The more adhesives, the lower the score, as it can be seen for example for LVL, which 
requires much more adhesive than CLT. Additionally, the type of adhesive generally used for LVL is not 
formaldehyde free, although the limit values are not exceeded. For the panel construction, the bracing OSB boards 
include comparably high amounts of additives. OSB boards are statically stronger than e.g. particle boards and 
include less and formaldehyde free adhesives. Composite timber-concrete based on CLT has the highest amount of 
included fasteners. The additives in the concrete are not regarded in this evaluation, but only in the overall global 
warming potential (GWP). 
The GWP focuses on the energy used and the emissions generated under the manufacturing of the materials. The 
sequestration effect of the wood is included, so the more material a system uses, the bigger the CO2 sink, which 
reduces the impact of transportation and production. Therefore, the smaller the GWP according to table 25, the 
higher the evaluation score in the following table. The focus is on the GWP per square meter of construction, this 
is why material intense systems score higher. 
The end of life evaluation describes the possibilities for reuse and recycling of the systems, as described in-depth in 
chapter 2.4.4 and table 26. Currently, most of the timber gets incinerated with energy recovery, but there is 
potential for more reuse and recycling of the material. The end of life evaluation shows the potential of the 
different systems for more circular approaches, the highest score describing a system, which is simple to reuse and 
recycle and which does not include problematic chemicals.

3.1.2. Objectives of the Digital Design Tool 
To integrate the base timber knowledge, as researched in chapter 2, a digital design tool will be developed. A 
software or plug-in can be integrated easier in the existing design process, than letting the architects read many 
pages of a timber design manual. The program has to be intuitive in use, to make it accessible to every architect or 
other involved planner. The design tool is aimed to solve the abovementioned challenges and introduce the timber 
knowledge into the design process.

The freedom in the design is naturally biggest in the early design stages, while the design knowledge increases 
towards the end of the project planning. The typical timing of an engineer entering the project is midway through 
the design process. The earlier a consultant is called into a project, the more efficient the structural design will be. 
Especially for building with wood, it is important to involve sources of knowledge early, as the possibilities are 
limited to introduce timber in a later stage or to change the system. The earlier the ideal timber structure is 
determined and suitable dimensions are found, the better, as this gives design solutions with the best performance 
and reduces the risk of abandoning timber and switching to concrete instead, when meeting obstacles caused by the 
chosen timber structure. This can either happen through involving engineers or timber specialists very early in the 
conceptual design phase, or by using resources as a digital design tool, which can help to test different viable 
solutions of building in timber. 
Therefore, it is decided to implement the base timber knowledge into a digital design tool for the early design stage 
during the conceptual and schematic design, before the engineers enter the project and verify the structure. The 
target group are architects and designers of buildings in early design phases. This should not limit the tool to this 
target though, but it should be useful for later stages as well as for other involved people, as engineers and 
consultants. 
For the case of Henning Larsen, Rhinoceros is mostly used in the early project stages by design architects. 
Therefore, the digital design tool is aimed to be a plug-in to Rhino, which can easily be used by architects, which 
were briefly introduced to the tool.

3.1.3. Limitations of the Digital Design Tool 
The digital design tool will not be able to solve all timber related challenges and answer all questions. The person 
using the tool still needs some fundamental knowledge about construction and timber. The possibilities and results 
given are just suggestions and by no means thought as a final design solution and the results are only as good as the 
input data. It should only guide the design process in finding the best suitable solution for the given case. 
The data included in the tool needs to be maintained and updated according to the inventions and findings in the 
industry. It should be extended with new and updated knowledge, as the frame of this thesis limited the possible 
data input, although there would be many more resources that are valuable and topics to include. Therefore, the 
tool will be built up in such a way, that changes and additions can be made easily. A responsible person or small 
team will conduct the maintenance, while the users of the tool are welcome to report mistakes and suggest changes 
to be implemented. The tool has therefore to be designed in such a way, that it can be passed on to another 
responsible and not limit the use or moderation of it to a single person. 
Furthermore, the tool will only focus on multi-storey office structures and leave out other typologies, as 
commercial and residential buildings, hotels, schools, etc. Some of the findings can though be translated into results 
for other typologies as well, if the use and dimensions of the building and rooms are similar to the ones in a typical 
office. 
3.1.4. Survey among Henning Larsen Employees 
In autumn, a survey was held at Henning Larsen, to find out more about the opinions and experiences the

3.3. Functions of the Tool 
3.3.1. Method of the Mode of Function 
The early design phase of most competitions and volume studies at Henning Larsen and other architectural 
companies are done in Rhino. As the timber tool is aimed at this stage, it is made for the use in Rhino. Many 
projects already use Grasshopper for parametric design and analysis, why also the timber tool is using Grasshopper. 
This will increase the chances of the architects using and implementing it in their projects, as they are used to a 
workflow with plugins and analysis tools via Grasshopper. The timber tool has to be self-explaining and includes 
therefore a description of the functions and visualisations of the solutions. 
A web-based version is planned for the future, as this will increase the number of potential users within Henning 
Larsen but also outside of the company. As the web-based solution will lose some of its functionality, which only 
works as a Rhino integrated tool, it was decided to wait with integrating a version for the internet and instead to 
focus solely on the tool built in Rhino 6. 
The tool was built up in Rhino 6, using a Henning Larsen script and the plugin Human UI, which helped 
generating the user interface of the digital design tool. The data input happens through a database of the timber 
knowledge in Excel, which is integrated into Grasshopper with live-updates. This makes changes in the tool quick, 
as the data can be adjusted in the Excel file, if the timber knowledge is extended or new data is available. It is 
important to appoint a responsible for the tool, who keeps the database up-to-date. The final result of the analysis 
can always only be as good as the input from the database and by the user, therefore it is crucial to maintain the 
Excel file. The following subchapters will list the main functions of the tool. 
The tool and its functions can either be installed and tested with the grasshopper file directly, or an overview of the 
tool can be watched by following the QR-code below or this link:

3.4.4. Adjustments 
In the first version of the digital tool, the displayed results only showed box elements and dowelled elements. This 
has several reasons and had to be corrected. Firstly, the Excel database results in the same total points for some of 
the systems, as for example for CLT and LVL, which made the tool only choose the first mentioned one in the list 
and ignored the second one with the same ranking. The same is the case for systems with almost identical ratings, as 
it was not possible to see the other possible systems with lower rankings. This was solved now, as in the bottom 
section a complete list of all possible solutions with their percentage in the different categories is displayed. Out of 
this, the architect can not only choose the first option chosen by the program, but also decide to go for another 
option, because it might have a higher rating in a property (as e.g. fire safety), which is important for the project.  
 
Another issue was, that the total points were calculated from 11 different parameters in four categories. Only the 
structural span and thickness were fixed values, which are clear and decide if a system is displayed or not at all. As 
there are for example only 2 parameters for the economical evaluation (time and costs), but 5 for the environmental 
score (material use, CO2 storage, additives, GWP and end of life scenarios), the environmental properties got more 
weight by default than the economical ones. Fire safety and sound insulation also both got 2 parameters each. To 
balance this out and to reflect the real preferences of an architect’s decision, a default weighting had to be added to 
the parameters, which then again can be adjusted by the individual weighting, which can be made by the user in the 
tool. The weighting was adjusted, until realistic solutions were displayed for all the different choice options. The 
following table shows the added default weighting, which is saved in the tool for each system.

Discussion and Summary 
3.5. Discussion of the Results 
The tool is a very helpful starting point when considering a timber structure and lacking knowledge in how to 
implement it. It can help avoiding mistakes, e.g. by choosing too big grid sizes, and can show clearly, what the 
impact of a bigger span is on costs and thickness of the build-up. It especially helps professionals with little to none 
experience in timber construction in finding the best system, instead of just picking a random one, they maybe have 
heard of. 
There are though also sides to pay attention to in this digital tool. Firstly, not all parameters are included and not all 
possible structures are regarded. Some typologies might be similar and the same parameters as for offices can be 
used, some typologies are very different though. Also, not all possible properties are included in the tool, as the 
scope of the thesis would not allow for that. Foundation and cores of buildings were for example completely 
excluded in this thesis. With the build-up of the tool, it is though possible to add more parameters into the 
calculation. 
Another point which is subjective is the evaluation of the score and the added weighting. These factors can 
influence the results and determine, which solution is showed as the preferable. The advantage about the Excel 
database is though, that the evaluation can easily be changed or adjusted, if new knowledge from further research is 
gained. 
The result of the tool also always has to be checked by the user, as the output only is as good as the input. If the user 
inserts unrealistic data, there is no guarantee, that the suggested solution will make sense, although the tool tries to 
avoid this as much as possible. 
Lastly, it has to be mentioned, that the tool is far from being completed and will stay in a current development, 
which is why it has to be used with a critical eye and be updated and maintained regularly. Using grasshopper 
allows for later changes and the implementation of new techniques, data and plugins. 
3.6. Implementation of the Tool 
It is the wish of Henning Larsen Architects and the author of this thesis to use the results and the tool as a help in 
the design of timber structures. Implementing a new tool into the daily design process is not something, which just 
happens by itself, but there is a clear strategy needed to communicated, inform, educate and support the new tool. 
Another important component is the time factor, as new practices need some time to become routines and get 
adapted by most architects. Therefore, an implementation strategy was planned, to make best use of the tool and 
the newly gained information. This strategy has to be flexible, that it can be adjusted along the way and follow the 
actual needs rather than a strict plan. Responsible for the implementation of the tool is the author of this thesis.

3.8. Reflections about this Project 
In this section, I want to add a brief reflection about this thesis from the author’s point of view. 
All in all, I am very content with the final result of the research and the development of the timber tool, as I see 
great benefits and a practical use in the implementation at Henning Larsen. As always in a project like this, there are 
though also things figured out along the way, which could have been done better. Some of these points are 
therefore mentioned here in the following, to reflect about my own work process and to avoid the same mistakes in 
my future work or other people’s projects. 
As I am very enthusiastic about wood and fight daily for the implementation of this material into more buildings, it 
was also an ideological project for me with the aim to gain as much knowledge and resourceful information as 
possible, to enhance the timber industry. This made me take a broad approach to the topic, including many 
different parameters and aspects. Although I narrowed them down and defined a scope for the thesis, which was 
possible to achieve in the given time, there could have been further cuts in the topic. 
One aspect which was not too important in the research at last were the walls with their build-up, as it is more often 
the slabs that define the structure and system of a building. Therefore, the walls could have been excluded for this 
thesis. 
As in many projects, the time pressure made it impossible to reach all of the imagined outcomes. The course of this 
thesis was partly more difficult because of the spread of Covid-19 and the effects it had on DTU, but also the work 
at Henning Larsen. As much of the work at Henning Larsen had to be done from home office and the employees 
where gathered in smaller groups, it was not possible to reach out to all people, which would have been interesting 
or relevant to this project. The scripting was also more complex, when help only is possible through calls and 
remote control of the computer, instead of quick and fast help while working next to each other in the office. Even 
though the work was more challenging, the result of the thesis was not compromised, as I was prepared to the 
special circumstances and was already used to the digital communication tools through home office in spring 2020. 
A delay in the programming of the digital design tool was caused by the survey, which was started later than 
expected. This meant, that the results came relatively late into the tool design, when it generated more work to 
implement the findings. 
A positive outcome was, that I learned much more about scripting in Grasshopper, than I had expected. I started 
without any knowledge and can now change and add functions to the tool by myself. As the tool will need more 
work, this is helpful, as it will be easiest for me to continue with developing the tool than handing it over to another 
responsible, who might not have much experience in timber construction. 
An example of what I learnt through the scripting in Grasshopper was, that proper naming of the linked files is 
crucial. I changed the place and name of the database and linked images several times in the beginning of the work 
at the tool, which caused extra work, when I had to reconnect them in Grasshopper to the new file’s location. 
Finally, I can say that I am happy to contribute to more sustainable construction through my work at Henning 
Larsen and this new knowledge and the digital design tool are another step to more timber construction in 
Denmark and around the world.

Global Warming Potential 
The GWP of the different systems is calculated over the volume per square meter of floor structure, as given in 
table 22 on page 63. For the example of CLT, this means that the GWP for LCA stages A1 – A3 is -707 kg CO2-
eq/m3, including the sequestration effect of wood. A CLT slab of 200 mm thickness means 0.2 m3 CLT/m2, so the 
GWP per square meter results in -141 kg CO2-eq/m2. 
For the panel construction, the amount of OSB was in the previous section calculated to 1/3 of the volume and the 
glulam frame to 2/3. OSB has a GWP of -772 kg CO2-eq/m3, glulam as -615 kg CO2-eq/m3. This means, that the 
panel construction has an overall GWP of -667 kg CO2-eq/m3. 
The composite timber-concrete consists of 1/3 reinforced precast concrete and 2/3 CLT.

˃ 
The client appointed an engineer, which did neither have 
the will nor the knowledge of building with wood. 
Therefore the original design went from wood to a 
conventional concrete construction. 
˃ 
My experience is not so direct! It is second hand - in the 
background team. And the feedback is from several 
different projects. Cost is a big issue, esp. in DK. Also the 
higher risk of working with an unknown (in DK) is 
ultimately a cost factor (potential cost in terms of risk). I 
hear that insurance is also a barrier in DK - heard from 
Rambøll that a client wanted to do a low-story building in 
timber, but their insurance company said no, it had to be 
concrete or no insurance. This has to change. 
˃ 
In my experience, architects and most engineers are easily 
convinced about the benefits of timber but other 
stakeholders such as clients, financers, authorities and 
contractors are not so easily convinced. This is because of 
the perceived increased cost and risk of making a timber 
construction. It is difficult to understand exactly why there 
is an increased resistance to timber in Denmark because the 
climate, weather conditions, etc. are very similar to other 
countries where wood is used a lot (UK, Germany, Austria, 
Switzerland, Norway, Finland etc). 
˃ 
Usually wood constructions in DK is caught up in fire 
regulations - which is a shame as it is fully possible to build 
fire safe constructions in wood. However regulations is 
already about to change so it should be easier in the future. 
˃ 
In the UK insurance on timber buildings is a lot higher and 
can be a barrier to getting timber in the design. 
˃ 
Perception of cost from other members of design team. 
˃ 
1. Availability within the region. 
2. Lack of skills within the first line of 
workers also impacts the mind-set of the 
client to build with wood - no one wants 
to be the "pioneer" in a region where 
buildings in the wood are rare. 
˃ 
Misconceptions about the safety of 
wood, or the refrain, "we've always used 
steel/concrete/etc." have derailed early 
conversations. The burden of proof is on 
the design team to really sell wood. Some 
clients (this is specific to an American 
market) don't see wood as a "low-
maintenance" material.
