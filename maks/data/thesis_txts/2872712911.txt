Abstract
With the growing integration of offshore wind energy through High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) sys­
tems, there is an increasing need for robust and fault­resilient control strategies, particularly in scenarios
involving HVDC converter blocking. In this context, grid­forming control has emerged as a promising
approach to enhance system stability and reliability. This thesis employs a real­time electromagnetic
transient (EMT) simulation model in which both grid­following (GFL) and grid­forming (GFM) wind
turbines are integrated into a symmetrical monopole HVDC test system. Converter blocking logic in the
HVDC link is implemented to enable coordinated fault response analysis under DC and AC faults. The
dynamic performance of the offshore wind farm is examined through detailed simulations. Key system
responses including power flow recovery, chopper energy dissipation, voltage stability, and converter
blocking are thoroughly investigated. A comparative evaluation shows that grid­forming wind turbines
maintain voltage reference capabilities more effectively than their grid­following counterparts during DC
faults. The findings emphasize the importance of coordinated converter blocking and careful controller
tuning to ensure stable fault ride­through and support the reliable integration of offshore wind into future
power systems.

Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
In the transition towards sustainable energy systems, wind power is expected to play a central role in
helping countries meet their renewable energy targets. Among the different technologies, offshore wind
power has emerged as one of the most promising sources. It offers access to stronger and more stable
wind conditions, enables the installation of large­scale farms, and reduces land use constraints.
As offshore wind farms are located further from shore, high­voltage direct current (HVDC) transmission
has become one of the most efficient and technically viable solutions for delivering power to the onshore
grid. Compared to high­voltage alternating current (HVAC) systems, HVDC offers lower losses over
long distances and reduced cable requirements, making it well suited for large offshore installations.
However, integrating HVDC links into the power system brings new technical challenges, particularly
in the control and behavior of power electronic converters. Traditionally, wind turbines operate using
grid­following (GFL) control, which relies on the presence of a stable voltage reference provided by the
offshore HVDC converter. This dependency limits the operation of the wind farms, as the turbines are
unable to operate or support the grid during disturbances if the reference is lost. In the event of offshore
converter blocking or failure, the wind farm loses its primary synchronizing signal, jeopardizing stability
and continuity of operation.
As the penetration of renewable energy sources increases, so does the demand for wind farms to con­
tribute actively to grid stability, particularly in terms of voltage and frequency support. These growing
requirements call for more advanced control strategies. Grid­forming (GFM) control has gained increas­
ing attention as a means to address this challenge. Unlike GFL control, GFM converters can operate
independently of an external voltage reference by regulating their own output voltage and frequency.
This enables them to maintain stable operation even under weak grid conditions or temporary isolation
if the HVDC converters can’t operate. As such, GFM control is considered a key enabler for the reliable
integration of large­scale offshore wind into future power systems.
1.2
Methodology and Structure of the Thesis
This thesis investigates the fault response of a grid­following and grid­forming wind turbine in an HVDC­
connected offshore wind farm. A simulation model is adapted and expanded to include both grid­following
and grid­forming control strategies. The system consists of an aggregated offshore wind farm connected
to an onshore grid via an HVDC link. The behavior of the system is evaluated under a range of fault
scenarios, including DC faults in the HVDC transmission system and AC faults on both the offshore and
onshore sides.
The primary goal is to assess how a grid­following and grid­forming wind turbine behave under fault
conditions, particularly in cases where the HVDC converters block. The performance of both is com­
pared.

Offshore Wind and HVDC Technology
This chapter provides an introduction on HVDC technology and it’s relevance, especially in the case of
transmitting power from offshore wind farms. The technology, along with it’s advantages, application
cases and system configurations are introduced. This section will also provide an introduction on grid­
forming and grid­following control and give a brief overview of the application cases.
2.1
HVDC Technology
As the overall consensus of shifting power generation away from fossil based generation, renewable
energy sources (RES) have gained worldwide attention. Due to the wide distribution of potential large­
scale wind sources all over the world and with technology maturing, wind energy is the most exploited
RES. Although most of the wind power nowadays is produced by onshore wind, offshore wind has been
of high interest, due to it being a highly unexploited wind resource and because of better wind conditions
offshore. As technology matures, the capacity of wind farms and their distance to the shore will increase.
Therefore, there is a clear need to investigate the power transmission technologies, to connect offshore
wind farms to the shore. In recent years, various transmission options have been analyzed and investigated
based on their technical and economical feasibility [1].
High Voltage Alternating Current (HVAC) nowadays dominate terrestrial transmission networks. It is
a simple and cost­effective solution for power transmission and reliable integration of large­scale RES.
Therefore, most of the present large offshore wind farms are connected through HVAC transmission
systems [1]. However, a HVAC systems has bottlenecks that make it unsuitable for certain applications.
This is mostly because of the reactive power produced by the cables, which increases proportionally
with the square of the AC voltage and the cable length. Installing reactive power compensation units
along submarine cables is not only expensive, but also challenging. This therefore restricts the amount of
reactive power that can be transmitted over the cables and consequently, the length of the cable in offshore
HVAC applications. Additionally, in offshore application, compensation devices can mostly only be
deployed at the ends of the line and have very little impact [2]. Due to these collective disadvantages,
HVAC transmission systems for the grid connection of large and remote offshore wind farms, in most
cases, are not preferred.
Consequently, high­voltage direct current (HVDC) transmission systems have become an interesting al­
ternative because they offer more attractive solutions for power transfer over long distances. Contrary to
HVAC, the power losses for this technology are significantly lower over long distances. Reactive power
is also not present in HVDC links, therefore no compensation for it is required. They can also inter­
connect asynchronous networks and systems that operate at different frequencies [3]. Estimated HVDC
economic break even distance can vary, but for overhead lines they typically lie between 300 and 800
km. For offshore underground cable links, the typical range is between 50 and 100 km. [2].

2.2
Converter Types
The fundamental principle of HVDC transmission involves two converter stations, one acting as a rec­
tifier and the other as an inverter. They are connected by a transmission medium, typically submarine
cables, which can be seen in Figure 2.2. The offshore converter sends power into the link and acts as
the rectifier, while the onshore converter at the receiving end terminal acts as the inverter. The different
system layouts between HVAC and HVDC are depicted in Figure 2.1 and Figure 2.2. Apart from the
dc­link, the topologies are the same.
Figure 2.1: HVAC transmission system design [4]
Figure 2.2: HVDC tranmsission system layout [4]
HVDC systems depend on the converters to properly transform the power from AC to DC. The two
main technologies are Current Source Converters (CSCs), also known as Line Commutated Converters
(LCCs), and Voltage Source Converters (VSCs). The main differences are summarized in Table 2.1.
LCC­based HVDC systems have typically been issued for long­distance connections, which link distant
generation sources to load centers through bulk power transmission. This has become more prominent as
the power ratings for LCC HVDC systems have reached up to 10 GW [4]. However, the LCC technology
is based on Thyristors, which can only be turned on. The switching off is caused by reversal of the voltage
across the thyristor, which causes frequency distortions in the output waveform. This therefore requires
large passive AC filters, which add to the overall footprint of the system. Furthermore, while transferring
active power, the LCC absorbs a significant quantity of reactive power [4].
VSC HVDC systems were proposed as an alternative. They are built with insulated­gate bipolar transis­
tors (IGBTs), which can be turned on and off. The commutation occurs irrespective of the grid voltage,
allowing reactive power to be controlled independently of managing active power. Compared to LCC­
based systems, VSCs generally have higher installation costs, lower power ratings, and greater losses in
most cases [4]. Nevertheless, the advanced controllability of the VSC HVDC technology has allowed it
to provide grid support to the connected AC network. Therefore, VSC based transmission system have an
increasingly wide range of application, especially in combination with remote RES like offshore wind.

2.2.1
VSC for HVDC
Since voltage source converters (VSCs) are the preferred converter technology for HVDC applications,
particularly in offshore wind, they are introduced in more detail in this section. VSCs are named for
their ability to behave as voltage sources, enabling bidirectional power transfer between the AC and DC
sides. They are composed of three legs, one for each AC phase, which can be seen in Figure 2.3. One leg
consists of two arms, so 6 arms in total. Each arm consists of multiple Insulated­Gate Bipolar Transis­
tors (IGBTs), which are self­commutated and can be precisely controlled using Pulse Width Modulation
(PWM) techniques.
There are several types of VSC topologies, classified based on the number of voltage levels they can
output. The most common are:
• Two­Level VSC: This is the most basic form of VSC, which can be seen on the left in Figure 2.3.
Here the AC output is switched between two discrete voltage levels, −1
2Udc and +1
2Udc. Each arm
is made up of a series connection of IGBTs that must switch simultaneously [5]. While simpler and
widely adopted, this topology produces higher harmonic content and thus typically requires more
filtering.
• Modular Multilevel Converter (MMC): First introduced in 2003, MMCs represent a significant
advancement in converter technology. Each arm in an MMC consists of multiple submodules con­
nected in series. These submodules act as independent two­level half­bridge converters with their
own capacitive energy storage. This configuration enables the synthesis of a staircase­like AC out­
put waveform with a much lower harmonic content, closely resembling a sine wave. While the
MMC offers improved performance and scalability, it comes at the cost of increased control com­
plexity and computational requirements [5]. The MMC layout is shown on the right in Figure 2.3.

2.2.1.1
Fault Handling in Half­Bridge MMCs
While Modular Multilevel Converters (MMCs) offer numerous advantages in HVDC applications, they
present important protection challenges, particularly in the event of DC­side faults. This limitation is
especially pronounced in systems using half­bridge (HB) submodules (depicted in Figure 2.3) and used
further on in this project.
A major shortcoming of the HB­MMC topology is its inability to block DC fault currents. Although
the IGBT switches can be turned off (i.e., ”blocked”), the freewheeling diodes within each submodule
continue to conduct. This allows fault current to flow uncontrollably from the DC link to the AC side or
through the converter. In a blocked state, when neither IGBT in a submodule is fired, the diodes switch
ON and OFF based on the current direction and capacitor voltage[6].
Converter blocking, therefore plays an important role. Although it does not physically stop fault current
flow through the converter, it prevents active switching and avoids injecting additional energy into the
system [7]. In this way, blocking mitigates further escalation of the fault.
2.2.2
System Configurations and Technologies
HVDC systems can have various layouts and the configuration is primarily dictated by the application
scenario, technical constraints, and cost considerations. The most commonly implemented configurations
include monopolar, bipolar, back­to­back, and multi­terminal topologies, each offering distinct advan­
tages and operational characteristics [6].

2.2.2.1
Monopolar Configuration.
There are two main types of configurations in monopolar HVDC systems. They can be symmetrical,
meaning they have two high voltage lines (shown in Figure 2.4 (a)) or asymmetrical, which have one
high voltage conductor and an earth / metallic return (Figure 2.4 (b) and (c)).
The symmetric monopole configuration consists of two high­voltage conductors with opposite polarities
and an earth reference that can be implemented using direct grounding, resistive grounding, or high­
impedance methods. During normal operation, no current flows through the ground, it is only utilized
during DC fault conditions. This configuration allows the use of standard transformers since there is no
DC component stress, which would otherwise be present in asymmetric configurations [6].
The asymmetric monopole is a simpler layout consisting of one high­voltage conductor and a return path
through either a metallic conductor or the earth. While it is economical and straightforward, its use has
declined due to environmental concerns associated with earth return currents and the need for special
transformers that can withstand DC bias stress [6].
Figure 2.4: Symmetric and asymmetric HVDC scheme configurations [6]
2.2.2.2
Bipolar Configuration
A bipolar HVDC system consists of two independent monopolar systems sharing a common neutral and
typically comprising four converters, two at each terminal, which can be seen in Figure 2.5. This config­
uration enhances system reliability and enables partial power transfer even in the event of a fault on one
pole. If a converter fails, the system can continue operation using the other converter and the remaining
HVDC cable as a return path, provided appropriate switching infrastructure is in place. However, bipolar
systems are more expensive and are generally used when the power transmission requirement exceeds
the capacity of a single monopole [6].
2.2.2.3
Back to Back Configuration
Back­to­back HVDC links are deployed when two asynchronous AC systems, operating at different fre­
quencies or without phase alignment, need to be interconnected. They configuration is shown in Fig­
ure 2.6. In such systems, both converters are located at the same site, eliminating the need for DC cables.
The configuration operates at relatively low DC voltages (typically below 150kV) and high current lev­
els. This configuration is compact and cost­efficient and the system experiences only converter­related
losses [6].

2.2.2.4
Multi­terminal Configuration
Multi­terminal HVDC (MTDC) systems extend the point­to­point structure of conventional HVDC into
a meshed or branched networks, that compromise more than two converter stations. These systems can
be realized in several topologies, including series, parallel, and meshed layouts.
In a series configuration, all converter stations share the same current path, and the voltage varies depend­
ing on the local power flow. However, this configuration is rarely implemented due to its complexity and
coordination challenges. The parallel configuration, in contrast, maintains a uniform DC voltage across
all terminals, with each station injecting or extracting current independently.
Meshed configurations are the most flexible, offering multiple paths for power flow, which enhances re­
dundancy, system availability, and fault resilience. As seen in Figure 2.8, the meshed topology connects
one converter to multiple, so the power flow can go towards multiple stations. The meshed topology,
however, requires sophisticated control schemes such as DC voltage droop control and coordinated pro­
tection systems to ensure dynamic stability and fault isolation. As offshore wind penetration increases
and inter­connectivity between countries becomes a priority, the development of robust MTDC systems
is becoming a key research and deployment area [6].

2.4
Control Strategies of an HVDC link for Wind Farms
Effective control strategies are essential to ensure stable and reliable operation of any HVDC transmission
system. This section introduces the fundamental control philosophy applied in point­to­point HVDC sys­
tems. For clarity and consistency with the implemented model, the example of a symmetrical monopolar
HVDC configuration is used.
In a typical HVDC link, both terminals are equipped with converters capable of controlling active and
reactive power. However, a critical task in the overall control strategy is maintaining the power balance
in the DC link. Therefore, one converter station must always take on the role of regulating the DC
voltage. The second converter station, is typically set to regulate either AC voltage or reactive power

exchange with the connected AC system. This separation of control tasks avoids conflicts between the
two terminals and ensures a balanced and coordinated power flow over the HVDC link [1]. Figure 2.10

enforces active power control. Reactive power or AC voltage control can be implemented independently
at both terminals based on local grid requirements.

2.5
Grid­Forming and Grid­Following Control
In most HVDC systems designed for large­scale offshore renewable integration, the offshore wind farms
rely on the offshore HVDC converters to provide a stable AC voltage reference for synchronization.
However, in the event of a disturbance, such as a fault in the DC grid or a malfunction in the HVDC
control system, this voltage reference may be temporarily lost. In such scenarios, the inability of the
HVDC converter to maintain controllability introduces the risk of a temporary islanding condition for the
DC­connected wind farm. Grid­forming (GFM) control plays a pivotal role in addressing this challenge.
Unlike conventional grid­following control, GFM­enabled converters can self­synchronize and maintain
voltage and frequency references locally, even in the absence of a grid­formed signal. This capability
allows the wind farm to remain energized and stable during converter blocking events, significantly en­
hancing the resilience and operational security of HVDC­connected offshore wind farm. As stated by
InterOPERA in [9], GFM control strategies are essential to ensuring that wind farms can ride through
short­term disturbances and restore normal operation autonomously once the HVDC converter is back
online. This section will therefore outline the differences between GFL and GFM control.
2.5.1
Conceptual Differences between Grid­Forming and Grid­Following control
Traditionally, most wind turbine converters have operated using grid­following (GFL) control strategies.
A GFL converter behaves like a current source with high output impedance, as shown in Figure 2.11 (a)
and injects current into the grid based on a predefined active and reactive power reference. This control
strategy requires an external reference for the voltage angle, which is usually derived using a Phase­
Locked Loop (PLL). While effective in strong grid conditions, GFL converters lack autonomous grid
support capabilities and are dependent on an external voltage source for synchronization.
In contrast, grid­forming control has gained attention in recent years as a viable method for enhancing
the stability and resilience of power systems with high shares of converter­based generation. GFM con­
verters are modeled as voltage sources with low output impedance (Figure 2.11 (b)) and are capable of
establishing and regulating voltage at their terminals. This allows them to maintain grid operation even
in the absence of a strong external voltage reference. GFM units can self­synchronize with the grid by

2.5.2
Synchronization Control
The synchronization mechanism is a fundamental aspect that differentiates grid­following and grid­
forming converter control strategies. It determines how converters align with the grid voltage in both
steady­state and dynamic conditions.
2.5.2.1
GFL converters
Figure 2.13 shows the control diagram of a grid­following converter, which is a typical vector current
control. In GFL converters, synchronization is achieved using a Phase­Locked Loop (PLL), which es­
timates the phase angle and frequency of the grid voltage at the point of common coupling (PCC). This
estimation is crucial for transforming measured AC signals into a rotating reference frame (typically dq).
The control structure typically consists of an outer loop, which regulates the DC­link voltage (for active
power) and the AC voltage (for reactive power), generating current references i∗

takes place in the DVC and AVC control loops. The generated current references, are then tracked by
an inner current control loop operating in the dq frame. Voltage feedforward through a low­pass filter is
often added to enhance the dynamic response. The PLL plays a pivotal role in this scheme, acting as the
synchronizing element that continuously adjusts the converter’s internal angle to match the grid [11].

2.5.2.2
GFM converters
In contrast, GFM converters do not rely on a PLL. Instead, they internally generate a voltage reference,
effectively acting as voltage sources. Synchronization is achieved through power­based control mecha­
nisms, where the converter adjusts its output frequency and angle based on the balance between measured
and referenced active power.
The general structure of a GFM controller can be seen in Figure 2.14. It includes an outer loop comprising
Active Power Control (APC) and Reactive Power Control (RPC). These loops regulate the frequency and
voltage magnitude, respectively. The outputs are then used to synthesize the voltage reference vector,
which is passed to the inner voltage and current control loops [11].
The power synchronization mechanism in GFM converters, emulate the dynamic behavior of synchronous
machines, often implemented through virtual synchronous generator (VSG) concepts or synchronverter­
based methods. This enables GFM converters to naturally contribute to grid stability by providing inertia­
like responses and supporting voltage regulation in weak or islanded networks.

Zero­Sequence Control
In the zero­sequence control loop, the zero sequence current is controlled to track it’s reference. In

using a standard PI regulator.

modular structure enables the system to maintain stable operation under a wide range of grid conditions,
including asymmetrical and unbalanced faults. The priorities and limits of each control path are adjusted
dynamically based on fault flags and the system state to ensure compliance with fault ride­through re­

4.3
HVDC Converter Protection and Ride­Through Logic
To assess system performance under various fault conditions, several additions were made to the model.
During a fault, the converter must block to prevent current flow and unsafe operation. Additionally, a
wind turbine with fault­ride­through (FRT) capabilities was implemented. Once the FRT connection re­
quirement set by Energinet [18] are exceeded, a tripping mechanism was implemented. The exact steps
and implementation are described below.
4.3.1
HVDC Converter Blocking Strategy
Since the MMCs used in this model are of the half­bridge (HB) type, converter blocking is a necessary
protective measure. During DC­side faults, HB submodules cannot interrupt the fault current path. This
is due to their internal structure, where the upper IGBT’s freewheeling diode allows current to bypass the
switch and flow through the DC link into the AC network, even when the IGBT itself is turned off. This
inherent vulnerability makes the HB­MMC particularly susceptible to uncontrolled DC fault propagation.
Consequently, converter blocking is widely adopted in MMC­HVDC protection schemes [19].
One key advantage it enables, is the rapid de­energization of the converter, which minimizes the risk of
damage to sensitive power electronic components[7]. Due to the rapid propagation of fault currents on the
DC side, which lack a natural zero crossing, fault detection and interruption can be particularly difficult.
Blocking the converter isolates it from the faulted DC system and prevents uncontrolled current inflows
that could severely damage Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs) and submodule capacitors [6].
In weak grid conditions, especially where grid­forming converters operate near stability margins, a volt­
age collapse can cause control loops to become unstable. Blocking provides a controlled shutdown path,
allowing the system to avoid control divergence or oscillatory behavior [8]. Economically, it provides a
cost­effective alternative to expensive DC breakers, especially in offshore systems where physical space
and cost are constrained [20].
Based on the location of the fault, certain converters are blocked. As summarized in Figure 4.5, both
converters only block when the fault is in the DC­link. This prevents the fault propagating onto either

Nature of the Fault
Pole to ground faults are the most common type of faults in MMC HVDC grids [21]. The main root

dation occurs when insulation materials deteriorate over time. Electrical treeing is caused when partial
discharges within the insulation material create tree­like conductive paths that lead to faults [22]. Due to
this fault type being the most common, it is being investigated within this project.

Grid­Forming Wind Turbine and Fault
Response Analysis
This chapter presents the methodology used to implement grid­forming (GFM) control in the wind tur­
bine, including the necessary adaptations made to the existing grid­following (GFL) MMC control scheme
within the HVDC link. It also details the results of previously applied fault scenarios and compares the
performance of GFM and GFL­controlled wind turbines under these conditions.
The control architecture for the system is summarized as follows:
• Wind Turbine: operates in grid­ forming (GFM) mode. The WF is modeled in an aggregated
manner and each WT is modeled only through a GSC, which controls f and VAC.
• Offshore MMC: operates in grid­ forming (GFM) mode. This converter controls VAC and the
offshore system frequency f.
• Onshore MMC: operates in grid­ following (GFL) mode. This converter controls VDC and VAC.
Figure 5.1: Overview of the base system with a GFM wind turbine and an HVDC link
In contrast to the grid­following WT­based case discussed in the previous chapter, the grid­forming wind
turbine developed in this chapter, focus exclusively on the grid­side dynamics of the wind turbine. As
illustrated in Figure 3.2, the GSC previously controlled the DC link voltage in GFL mode. With the shift
to GFM control, the GSC now regulates voltage magnitude and frequency directly. To streamline this
change in control responsibility, the DC link has been substituted by a fixed voltage source which delivers
a constant voltage of 6.5 kV.
5.1
Wind Turbine Control Implementation
This section outlines the implementation of the Virtual Synchronous Generator (VSG) control strategy
applied to the wind turbine. The approach enables grid­forming operation and replaces the conventional
grid­following control used in earlier configurations.
5.1.1
Virtual Synchronous Generator
In recent years, several control strategies have been developed to enable inverter­based resources (IBRs)
to mimic the dynamic behavior of synchronous generators. Among these, the Virtual Synchronous Gen­
Technical University of Denamrk
47
==PAGE:59==
erator approach has emerged as a widely adopted method for enabling GFM operation. The main moti­
vation behind using VSG control lies in its ability to mimic the rotor behavior of a synchronous machine
in terms of damping and inertia. This allows the converter to self­synchronize with the grid without the
need for a phase­locked loop (PLL), as discussed in more detail in Chapter 2. The VSG control scheme
effectively models the swing equation of a synchronous machine, therefore introducing virtual inertia and
damping into the converter dynamics. While it captures the characteristic of the rotor, it typically ignores
the excitation system dynamics of a synchronous generator (SG) [24].
The control diagram used to implement VSG functionality in the wind turbine is shown in Figure 5.2.
Compared to standard grid­following control, the two key additions are the Power Synchronization Loop
(PSL) and the Reactive Power Control (RPC) loop. The PSL governs the phase angle by controlling the
active power output, while the RPC loop sets the converter voltage magnitude based on reactive power
feedback. Together, these elements enable the wind turbine to operate in a GFM mode.

turbine

5.1.5
Current Control and Limiting
After the d­ and q­axis components are generated from the virtual impedance block, a current limiter
ensures that the current doesn’t overshoot. Unlike synchronous generators (SGs), which can typically
withstand currents up to 5–6 times their nominal value for short durations, power electronic converters
are constrained by the thermal and electrical limitations of semiconductor switches. Allowing excessive
current in the semiconductor switches during fault conditions may result in device damage. It is therefore
Technical University of Denmark
50
==PAGE:62==
crucial to incorporate current limiting techniques when designing VSG control schemes. In this case, the
current limiting scheme is an instantaneous saturation limiter that prevents overcurrent problems during
transients or abnormal operations[24].
Following the current limiter, the current control loop is implemented as shown in Figure 5.5. This is a
standard inner­loop current controller with decoupled control of the d­ and q­axis current components.
It is known that in the synchronous reference frame, the two components are inherently coupled through
the cross terms ωLf, where Lf is the filter inductance of the grid side converter. To enable independent
control, the control loop must decouple the d­ and q­axis dynamics. This is achieved through feedforward
compensation of the cross terms [5].
The output of this control block is the voltage reference in the dq frame. Using the instantaneous phase
angle θ, obtained from the power synchronization loop, the voltage reference is transformed back to the
abc frame and used to generate the modulation waveforms for the converter[24].

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the control strategies and fault behavior of an HVDC­connected offshore wind
farm, with a particular focus on the role of grid­forming (GFM) and grid­following (GFL) control methods
in ensuring system stability. As renewable energy continues to penetrate the electrical grid, maintaining
reliability in converter­dominated systems becomes increasingly challenging. The research presented in
this thesis addresses these challenges by simulating an offshore wind farm scenarios and analyzing the
performance of various control schemes during and post fault conditions.
In the DC fault analysis, the GFM­controlled wind turbine demonstrated better performance than its GFL
counterpart. The GFM turbine exhibited a faster power recovery, quickly ramping back up to 1pu, and
maintained connectivity and support for the voltage throughout the fault duration. This is a key advantage,
as GFM control enables the wind turbine to independently establish a voltage reference, eliminating
its dependency on the offshore HVDC MMC. In contrast, the GFL turbine could not ride through the
longer 200ms fault case, highlighting its reliance on the converter provided voltage signal. Without this
reference, the GFL wind farm required a full re­synchronization post­fault, delaying system recovery.
The AC fault cases revealed that fault location significantly influences system response. In the onshore
AC fault scenario, only the onshore converter blocked. As a result, the wind farm regardless of whether
GFM or GFL control was used, showed little to no dynamic response, since it remained electrically
unaffected. However, the onshore and offshore MMCs exhibited unstable and oscillatory behavior. With
the onshore MMC unable to control the DC link voltage due to its blocked state, the offshore MMC
continued injecting power, creating a large power imbalance. This excess power was dissipated through
the DC chopper circuit. Notably, the onshore MMC could not fully block current due to residual paths
through the antiparallel diodes in the IGBT modules. This highlights the need for enhanced converter
blocking strategies in future HVDC system designs.
In the offshore AC fault scenario, the GFL controlled turbine showed more stable behavior. The GFM
turbine temporarily lost its ability to create a voltage reference, due to the fault’s direct impact on the
offshore grid. Consequently, it was forced to disable its voltage reference during the disturbance and
required more time to re­establish it post­fault. This delay negatively affected its fault ride­through per­
formance in this specific case.
In summary, GFM control showed clear advantages in the DC fault scenario, particularly due to its ability
to maintain a stable voltage reference independently of the HVDC system. In the offshore AC fault case
however, the GFL turbine performed better than the GFM turbine. These results suggest that GFM control
is a highly promising approach, especially for improving system resilience during HVDC disturbances.
With further tuning and optimization, GFM strategies could likely be enhanced to also outperform GFL
control under a wider range of fault scenarios, making it a key technology for the next generation of
offshore wind integration.

6.1
Future Work
While this thesis has provided valuable insights into the control behavior of HVDC­connected offshore
wind farms, several areas remain open for further investigation and development.
One notable finding was the lack of system response from the wind farm during onshore AC faults,
particularly when only the onshore MMC blocks. In the current setup, the offshore wind farm continues to
operate as if no fault has occurred. This points to the need for implementing a higher­level communication
layer between the wind farm and the HVDC system. Such a layer could be used to signal fault events
and coordinate a controlled ramp­down of the wind farm’s power output, reducing power mismatch and
alleviating stress on the system components.
Moreover, the current wind turbine control approach resets the PI controllers upon the start or end of a
fault. This reset behavior can cause abrupt control transitions and reduce system stability. A more robust
strategy would involve freezing the PI controller states during fault events and resuming them post­fault
without reinitialization. This method could reduce control overshoot and oscillations, thereby improving
post­fault recovery and grid support.
Another critical area of improvement lies in converter blocking during DC faults. The present converter
model allows residual current paths due to the intrinsic behavior of IGBT diodes, limiting the effectiveness
of the blocking scheme. Future work should explore more advanced converter blocking methods.
