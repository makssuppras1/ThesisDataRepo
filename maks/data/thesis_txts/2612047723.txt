Abstract
i
Abstract
The aim of this study is the comparison of three ventilation systems that can be
installed in existing buildings during renovation process. The evaluation is based on
the fan energy consumption and indoor climate according to Category II of DS/EN
15251:2007[1] and ISO/TR 17772-2:2018[2]. The examined ventilation systems are
a typical centralized, decentralized-apartment based and decentralized-room based.
The centralized system consists of a central Air Handling Unit (AHU) and a sin-
gle duct network connecting all the apartments of the building. The decentralized-
apartment based contains a separate AHU and single duct network for each apart-
ment. The room based consists of room ventilation units (RVU) with heat recovery
that are placed in the dry rooms of each apartment and a single exhaust fan con-
nected with all wet rooms through a single duct network. The performance of each
ventilation system is tested with dynamic simulations, for three different renovation
levels, regarding improvements on glazing, insulation, building airtightness and ther-
mal bridges. The fan energy consumption per year of centralized system is on average
the highest among the ventilation systems by 27% and 48% compared with the apart-
ment based and the room based respectively. The unbalanced airflows of the room
based system creates under-pressure in the apartments that results in higher energy
consumption for district heating. This affects the energy frame of the building, as
it is higher in room based systems despite the lower energy fan consumption. The
ventilation airflow rates of room based systems are lower than the other two systems
leading to lower total heat recovery per year by 20.5%. The absence of heating coil
in the AHU of every system results in low supply air temperature from 10 to 16oC
for 25% of Danish heating season and from 16 to 18oC for 50% for the centralized
and the apartment based systems, while the room based systems present air supply
temperature of 18oC and higher for 50% of heating season. In terms of indoor climate,
the level of relative humidity ratio and operative temperature is similar for all the ex-
amined ventilation systems with the room based giving a bit higher average operative
temperature during summer. In the apartment based systems CO2 concentration in
bedrooms is higher than 1200 ppm for 50% of sleeping time in apartment based and
6% for the room based systems, while it remains below the limit of 1200 ppm for
the rest rooms and in most cases of the centralized systems. Room based systems
are more dependent on window opening for room cooling, as the airflow rate through
the windows during summer period is higher than the other two systems. The ven-
tilation systems are assisted from window opening to reduce operative temperature
during summer, while CO2 concentration is lower in this period than winter. The
performance of apartment based systems regarding energy consumption and indoor
climate is the best among the systems and a good solution for mechanical ventilation
in renovated apartments, taking into account the required space for its installation,
otherwise room based system is the most optimal choice.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Due to the absence of mechanical ventilation, buildings were constructed with gaps
in the façade as Natural Ventilation (NV) was the strategy to ventilate along with
air vents, especially in the wet rooms to remove the steam, humidity or pollutants
that are created by human activities[3]. The drawback of NV is the dependence of
indoor environment of the building on outdoor conditions, as the particles of the air
penetrate the envelope of the building and they carry inside any pollution of outdoor
air. Therefore, the location of the building affects significantly its indoor environment
and NV should be avoided in highly populated urban areas [4].
Nowadays, there is an effort to reduce the energy consumption of the buildings by im-
proving their insulation and airtightness during the renovation process. In Denmark
many houses were constructed a long time ago, so there is a need to introduce some
improvements according to the provisions of Buildings Regulations (2018)[5]. In order
to reduce the energy consumption of old buildings, the windows are replaced with
more energy eﬀicient glazing along with better frames to reduce the heat losses from
these areas of the construction [6]. As a second option would be the improvement
of the building airtightness that reduces the amount of infiltration through the exter-
nal walls, as the connection between airtightness and heat demand is almost linear [4].
Improving the airtightness of the buildings will result in reducing the air change rate
in the zones through the gaps on the façade. Therefore, more air must be supplied
from NV, but its dependence on outdoor conditions can restrict its usage to short
periods of the year. According to Kim and Baldini research, in five European coun-
tries, fan-assisted NV is possible to be used around 22-32% of the year [7](fig.2 in
Appendices). More specifically, in Copenhagen it can be used from April till October
and the hours correspond to 22% of the year. Installation of a mechanical ventila-
tion (MV) system with heat recovery can ensure a proper indoor climate and avoid
mold growth by transferring the necessary amount of outside air in the building zones
and improve the quality of indoor air with the lowest possible energy consumption [7].
Moreover, a combination of MV and NV is called Hybrid Ventilation system (HV)
which can significantly reduce the energy demand of the Air Handling Unit (AHU).
By taking advantage of the outdoor conditions, occupants can chose to cool or refresh
the air of the apartment according to their wish [7]. Foldbjerg tested the period of
time during a year that HV can be used in three different cities of Europe, including

Copenhagen where HV can be used for 36% of the year, while the reduction in fan
energy was around 33% [8].
On the other hand, more challenges would come up
during the design of such systems due to the integration between occupants’ desires

1.1
Systems description
Nowadays, MV systems are widely used to ventilate the buildings. Their usage keeps
the indoor air refreshed to eliminate the risk of mold growth, when moisture stays
on the walls of the construction for a long time[10]. Also, bad air quality with high
humidity levels can affect allergies and cause health issues on respiratory system by
biological agent that thrive in such environments [11], while occupants’ performance
on daily tasks can be affected, as well [12]. Nowadays, MV systems are designed
based on the demands of the building to ensure proper indoor climate and with heat
recovery which is an eﬀicient, cost-effective solution despite the higher manufacturing
cost in order to save energy [13]. Finally, the control of MV systems can be managed
by the occupants or operate independently based on their setup[12].
MV systems can be classified into different categories according to their size and
the air distribution process that they follow. Moreover, there are different variations
on the way the air is disturbed through the fans to the building, the most common
are supply only, exhaust only, balanced airflows and balanced airflows with heat
recovery[14][15]. The most optimal system for countries like Denmark would be a
system with balance airflows and heat recovery in order to meet the current energy
requirements of the Standards[12], [15]. Systems with heat recovery reuse the heat
from the extracted air to increase the temperature of supplied air. Heat recovery
eﬀiciency depends on maximum value provided by the MV system manufacturer, the
proper design of the MV system and outdoor conditions, while maximum heat recov-
ery eﬀiciency could be up to 90%. Also, balanced airflows don’t over-pressurize or
under-pressurize the building to prevent heat losses in form of infiltration or exfiltra-
tion through the building envelope[15].
1.1.1
Centralized Ventilation System
A conventional Centralized Ventilation (CV) system is considered as the most used
system in buildings. This system consists of a long network of supply and exhaust
ducts that are installed between the ceiling and the next floor level. This double duct
system must be in balance between the supply and exhaust side, meaning that the
total airflow rate must be the same. In this way, heat losses are minimized, because
less air comes through the façade as infiltration or exfiltration. Typically centralized
ventilation system is designed to supply air to the dry rooms, like bedrooms or liv-
ing room, whereas the extracted air will be pulled out from the wet rooms, such as

or the basement of the building to distribute the incoming air from outdoors and
extract the old one. Finally, AHUs are usually equipped with a heat exchanger to

system can be seen of figure 1.1, that includes the position of the central AHU in

1.1.2
Decentralized Ventilation Systems
Decentralized Ventilation (DV) system is referred to mechanical ventilation instal-
lations with short air distribution passage due to the smaller duct networks or the

complete absence of them.
One type of DV system can be used for single family houses or a single apartment of
a multi-storey building. It consists of a local AHU, short duct distribution network
of two different passages for the supplied and exhausted air and heat recovery sys-
tem[17]. The supply is distributed into the apartment on dry rooms and extracted
from bathroom and kitchen, while airflows should remain in balance, like the CV
system[9]. Figure 1.2 illustrates an apartment based DV system with local AHUs in

in blue color, while green was used to represent the exhaust side.

Another type of DV systems are the Room Ventilation Units (RVUs) that can be
placed in different rooms of the apartment. RVUs are mounded on the external wall
of the building, as they don’t use ducts to distribute the air inside the zone. RVU is
a complete AHU that consists of a heat exchanger with heat recovery, air filters and
two fans to supply and exhaust the air [9],[18]. Moisture emissions in the wet rooms
can be removed through a central duct network with exhaust only fan. A scheme
of this system is presented in figure 1.3 with the RVUs placed on the external walls
of dry rooms with supply and exhaust fans, while a duct network with exhaust only
side is used for wet rooms.

1.2.1
Fan energy
The fans of the AHU consume electricity to operate, thus it’s important to keep the
energy demand in low levels and improve the energy frame of the building due to the
low energy consumption proposed by Building Regulations.
Calculations and field tests were done from Kim and Baldini during their research re-
garding the fan energy consumption among centralized, semi-decentralized and decen-
tralized ventilation systems (comparison of the systems in figure 1 in appendices)[7].
The outcome showed that regardless of the cooling or heating loads, decentralized
systems resulted in higher energy savings compared to the others. More specifically,
figure 1.4 illustrates the difference in percentage of fan energy consumption, in which
the decentralized systems consumed 1/5 of the energy that was necessary in central-
ized systems and 1/3 of the semi-decentralized.

1.2.4
Noise generation and transmission
The level of noise is a quite important factor for a good indoor climate, as high level
of noise can cause health issues or affect the performance of the occupants on their
activities[21]. Therefore, the generated noise from the system should be reduced by
==PAGE:19==
1.2 Related work
11
investing on low-noise equipment and schedule frequent and proper maintenance of
the devices[22].
Noise generation from the centralized systems and decentralized apartment based sys-
tems can come from its compartments, such as the duct network, vents, grilles and
fans. Moreover, it is possible to travel from one room to its attached one through the
ducts. Therefore, it’s important to install silencers in the system close to the sources
of noise generation, which can be be behind fans, flow generators and dambers [23].
In RVUs, production of noise is due to usage of fans which are located inside the exter-
nal wall or due to the air circulation inside the AHU. According to Manz[24], airflow
rate and sound pressure level are highly connected to two types of heat exchanger
that are used in the RVUs. The results showed that the noise level on the graphs
exceeds the maximum limit of 30dB(A) during day according to Danish Standards.
A relevant graph with the increase of noise along with the increase of the airflows is
presented in figure 1.13.
Figure 1.13: Expected sound pressure level in a typical sleeping room[24]
Therefore, in order to decrease the noise level from the RVU operation, if there is no
space for silencers the airflow has to be at lower levels. Despite the noise creation
inside the room, the absence of duct network makes the noise transmission between
the rooms impossible [25].
1.2.5
Air distribution
There are different challenges and advantages in air distribution between centralized
and room based units.
Big and open spaces are more eﬀiciently ventilated with
==PAGE:20==
12
1 Introduction
centralized ventilation systems, as the distance from the external wall to the deep-
est point of the zone, such as building core, could be impossible to be covered by
RVUs[26]. On the other hand, RVUs that are installed on the external wall of rooms
that can be found in an apartment can be ventilated directly with air from outdoors
without the need of ducts [27][24].
1.2.6
Other issues addressed in literature
Regardless of the performance of the RVUs compared with the other MV systems,
one advantage is their easy installation in the external wall of the façade without
extensive work. Therefore, in cases like renovation of an old building, when there is
not enough space to create a suspended ceiling to install the ducts or a central AHU,
RVU could be the most optimal solution [18].
In case of fire, RVUs are a safe solution, as smoke and flames will not be able to spread
through different rooms due to the absence of ducts [25].
Regarding centralized
systems, a proper design would prevent accidents and help the evacuation of the
building with smoke removal systems that can be installed even on existing structures
[28].
1.3
Scope of the study
The objective of this thesis was the comparison of the performance among three
ventilation systems which are a centralized system, a decentralized apartment based
and room based with balanced airflow in dry rooms and exhaust from kitchen and
bathrooms. The systems were placed into three typical apartments of typical multi-
storey buildings in Denmark that were built before 2000. Also, it was assumed that
the systems were installed in relation to different levels of renovation. The study was
carried with dynamic simulations using the software IDA indoor Climate and Energy
(IDA-ICE 4.8 SP2) by EQUA [29]. In more details, the objectives were:
• Modeling apartment buildings corresponding to the relevant Danish building
typology.
• Implementation of centralized, decentralized apartment based and room based
ventilation systems in the IDA-ICE simulation models.
• Evaluation and comparison of the studied ventilation systems with respect to
energy consumption and indoor climate based on the limits of BR18[5], DS/EN
15251[1] and DS/ISO/TR 17772-2:2018 [2].

CHAPTER 2
Methods
In this chapter the selection of the different parameters for the simulation models
will be described, along with the steps that were followed to investigate the different
ventilation systems. The results for this thesis were obtained with dynamic yearly
simulations using the three different building types of Danish residential apartments
that can be found in Copenhagen region. The three categories of the investigated
ventilation systems were centralized, decentralized - apartment based and room based
with exhaust fans in kitchens and bathrooms. Moreover, the multi-storey apartment
buildings were simulated on different renovation levels as the buildings were assumed
to be constructed before the year 2000. The starting point for retrofitting was consid-
ered the change of window glazing to more energy eﬀicient, then additional insulation
was added on the short sides of the building with some improvements on the thermal
bridges and airtightness. Finally, as last step level of renovation was assumed the
installation of insulation in the whole building envelope and further improvement of
thermal bridges and airtightness.

Cooling
No mechanical cooling system was used in the simulation, so thermal comfort during

The assumption of

inflow air through the windows was tested to show which system was more dependant
on humans for cooling.
2.3.8
Indoor Air Quality (IAQ)
The limits of Category II of Danish Standards was used for the evaluation of indoor
climate of the apartments.
Relative Humidity (RH) ratio: Acceptable range of RH is between 25 and 60%
based on DS/EN 15251[1]. RH ratio below 25% turns the air too dry, while above
60% is too humid. Although this study was carried out with respect to building reg-
ulations, the range of RH ratio from 60 to 70% was considered acceptable, as it was
proved that the risk for mould grow is too low [34].
CO2: The limit was set at 1200 ppm based on DS/ISO/TR 17772-2:2018[2].
Operative temperature: Minimum limit was considered at 20oC and maximum at
27oC according to DS/EN 15251[1].
2.3.9
Ventilation systems
In this study three different systems were used to ventilate the apartments. It was
assumed that these systems didn’t use any heating or cooling coil to regulate the air
supply temperature. When the apartments were unoccupied a minimum airflow of
0.3 L/s/m2 was applied according to BR18 [5]. In the following subsections a more
detailed description of each system will be presented.
==PAGE:30==

2 Methods
2.3.9.1
Centralized (CEN) system
For all the three apartments of each block a central AHU was placed in the model
with SFP of 1500 J/m3 and a heat exchanger with heat recovery eﬀiciency of 85%.
The supply and exhaust airflows were in balance in every case.
The rooms were
ventilated according to occupants schedules (tables 2.4 and 2.5), meaning that a VAV
system was used according to the occupants position in the apartment to boost or
decrease the airflows. The required airflows were determined by DS/EN 15251 [1],
more specifically, for the dry rooms it was considered 7 L/s per occupant in the zone
to reach the limits of Category II[2] that was divided to the area of each room. In
the same way, the kitchen airflow was set at 20 L/s, while for the bathroom it was
used 15 L/s. Supply air temperature of the AHU was set at 18 oC.
2.3.9.2
Decentralized - Apartment based (DEC) system
Three different AHUs were placed in each apartment that operated with a return air
humidity control. A sensor was placed on the return side that measured the relative
humidity ratio of the whole apartment. The airflow of the apartments started to
grow proportionally for a range of relative humidity of 30% to 60%, keeping a balance
between supply and exhaust side. The specifications of each AHU were a SFP of 1000
J/m3 and a heat exchanger with heat recovery eﬀiciency of 85%.
2.3.9.3
Decentralized - Room based (RVU) system
The model of RVU that was used in the models was based on the proposed control
algorithm from the research of Smith, Jansen and Svendsen [35]. According to their
findings these units proved to be more suitable for use only in the dry rooms, as the
experiments showed that in the case of wet rooms, the amount of moisture that was
recovered inside the zone was too high [36]. This RVU was equipped with sensors that
measure temperature, relative humidity and CO2 concentration on the exhaust side,
while the supply side measures only inlet temperature. According to the incoming
signals the control algorithm regulates the airflow that its maximum value could be
up to 15 L/s [18]. RVU operates in three different modes according to outdoor or
indoor conditions regarding temperature, relative humidity and CO2 concentration.
It prioritizes the protection of heat exchanger from frost creation when outdoor tem-
perature is below 0oC, then the increase of moisture above 60% inside the zone will
trigger the second mode and as a last operation, RVU will control the CO2 level and
heat recovery based on the setpoints of cooling or heating season. The steps of first
mode for protecting the heat exchanger are the reduction of heat recovery eﬀiciency
and supply air temperature at 10oC and when outdoor temperature remains too long
below 0oC control algorithm boost the exhaust airflow by 15% more than supply air-
flow. The actions of second mode for the reduction of moisture level are the decrease
of heat recovery and boost of airflows when relative humidity ratio rises above 70%.
Third and last mode of RVU calculates the necessary airflow to reduce CO2 level and
==PAGE:31==

relief from high operative temperature during summer, while it reduces heat recovery
of heat exchanger when it’s not necessary. These units were placed in dry rooms,
whereas a central AHU was placed in the wet rooms with exhaust only fan. The
operation of the fan was connected with the schedules of these rooms and the airflows
were chosen based on the recommended values of DS/EN 15251[1].

Results
This chapter contains the outcome of simulation models for the three buildings types
equipped with the three examined ventilation systems of this study. The simulations
have been through three renovation levels, starting from the simple change of windows
and proceeding to more effective solutions to reduce the energy consumption of the
buildings by adding insulation to the building envelope, strengthen its airthightness
and improve thermal bridges. The presentation of the results was divided in three
sections for each building type, while a total overview for the most optimal ventilation
system was done in Discussion (chapter 4).

CEN and DEC systems were designed without heating coil in this study that resulted
in quite low supply air temperature during heating season for every ventilation sys-
tem. Their supply air temperature was lower than 16oC for 20 to 25% of heating
season with a minimum of 10oC for the different building types and above 18oC for
20% of heating season. The room based units of RVU systems reduced the supply air
temperature at 10oC when outdoor temperature was below 0oC in order to protect
the HEX from freezing.
The strategy of not using heating coil in this study was to reduce fan energy consump-
tion, but it resulted in low supply air temperature during heating season. This issue
is possible to cause discomfort to the occupants by creating cool draughts. Moreover,
the district heating energy demand would be increased to cover the heating setpoint,
as the supply air was in lower temperature. Thus, heating coil could be included in
CEN and DEC ventilation systems of this study, except the case of room based units
that were designed without it by their developer, to compare the energy consumption
of the building, with and without the use of heating coil.
The performance of the ventilation systems in preserving an acceptable level of indoor
climate was on the same level without any system presenting much worse results than
the others in each building type. DEC systems showed increased level of CO2 during

night in bedrooms when high moisture emissions stopped as they were produced from

DEC systems presented slightly better results on keeping RH ratio below 70%, but ev-

create mould with all these harmful effects on structures and human health.
Thermal comfort during heating season couldn’t be ensured for all ventilation systems
in RL1 and RL2, as there were some periods of time with operative temperature less
than 20oC, especially in RL1, when the minimum was less than 19oC in some cases.
The results were significantly improved in RL3 due to better insulation and airtight-

discomfort to occupants can be caused by the low supply air temperature due to the
absence of heating coil, by creating cool draughts in the rooms. Regarding heating
season, operative temperature higher than 27oC appeared for less than 5% of the
time, while mean operative temperature was higher in RL3 for every ventilation sys-

zones compared to the other systems, as it came up in every building type, due to
higher window airflow rate during the whole duration of cooling season.
There was no noise measurements for the ventilation units. In the case of CEN and

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The performance of each ventilation system was investigated with dynamic building
simulations for three different types of Danish buildings that were widely constructed
during the previous century. Also, three renovation levels were implemented in each
building type that represent different improvements in existing buildings to enhance
their airtightness and reduce energy consumption.
The performance of the exam-
ined ventilation systems was compared regarding fan energy consumption, heat re-
covery, energy frame of the building in yearly basis to fit Renovation class 1 and 2 of
BR18[5]. Also, ventilation systems were tested regarding their ability to preserve an
acceptable level of indoor climate with respect to the limits of Category II of DS/EN
15251:2007[1] and ISO/TR 17772-2:2018[2]. The fan energy of the CEN system was
the highest among the ventilation systems for every building type, as on average, the
fans of the DEC systems consumed 27% less energy per year than the CEN system,
while the RVU systems 48% less. The total supply and exhaust airflows of the CEN
and DEC systems were in balance, whereas the total exhaust airflow of RVU systems
was higher than the supply, because of the exhaust fan on the wet rooms. Total heat
recovery per year of the building was lower for RVU systems by 20.5% due to lower
total ventilation airflows. The fan energy had small contribution in energy frame of
the buildings, but the unbalance between total exhaust and supply airflow in RVU
systems created under-pressure in the apartments and increased the energy demand
of district heating. As a result, the RVU systems presented the highest energy frame,
even though the fan energy was the lowest among the simulated cases. DEC systems
presented the lowest energy frame, fulfilling the requirements of Renovation classes
of BR18[5] in 7 out of 9 cases. Supply air temperature of every ventilation system
was low during heating season due to the absence of heating coil in the AHU. Supply
air was in-between 10 to 16oC for 25% of heating season for every ventilation system,
while in CEN and DEC systems it was 18oC or higher for 25% of heating season and
for 50% in RVU systems. RH ratio results were slightly better on DEC systems, but
every ventilation system presented high RH ratio in wet rooms during cooking or
showering, as the maximum values were peaking at 92±5% in the kitchen and 100%
in the bathrooms. Moreover, retrofitting improvements improved the performance of
the examined ventilation systems in maintaining RH ratio between the acceptable
limits. Operative temperature was at similar level among the ventilation systems,
except RVU systems that presented slightly higher temperature during cooling sea-
son. In RL1 and RL2 the lower limit of 20oC wasn’t achieved by the ventilation
systems, as the minimum temperature could drop around 19oC, whereas this issue

during cooling season was ensured for the examined ventilation systems as the hours
with operative temperature higher than 27oC were less than 5% of the total hours of

ppm for 50% of sleeping time in DEC systems and for 6% in RVU systems, while CO2
