Abstract
In today’s fast­paced digital world, use of text­based passwords is still the most popular
authentication method. Many online platforms require users to be authenticated to gain
access to internal systems, which forces internet users to create more and more profiles
and the passwords that come with them. This leads to user preference of convenience in
the form of password reuse as well as the use of simple and easy to remember passwords.
The aim of this thesis is to analyse leaked credentials and create a targeted password
prediction system powered by AI that takes as an input user­specific data extracted from
the email address (name, nationality, gender, year of birth). Three modern LLMs are
selected (Google’s T5, Meta’s BART and LLama) and fine­tuned on a breached dataset.
The central part of this thesis are conducted experiments that aim to identify the best
performing model as well as investigation of the influence of the input data on the number
of guessed passwords.
The analysis of the breached credentials illustrates that most users create passwords
of length 8 composed of uppercase or lowercase characters and numbers. The results
of the conducted experiments illustrate that the best performing model is T5 across all
conducted tests. The best success rate in terms of guessed passwords was obtained for
inputs containing email address, name, nationality and year of birth with 54.45% correct
guesses.
This thesis contributes to the development of research of targeted password guessing and
brings awareness to the threats which arise when using weak or predictable passwords.

Introduction
Many online services enforce guidelines of password complexity by requiring a minimum
length and the inclusion of specific elements (digits, special characters, uppercase and
lowercase characters). These regulations aim to increase password strength in order to
make them less vulnerable against brute force or guessing attacks. However, even pass­
words compliant with complexity requirements might be easily guessable when common
phrases or personal information is included in the password. According to the Bitwarden
survey conducted in 2024 [1], 36% of respondents use personal data in their passwords
with 60% of them having disclosed that the same personal data can be found on their pub­
lic social media profiles. Furthermore, 25% of the survey respondents reuse passwords
across different systems. Such patterns pose a significant security threat which is con­
firmed by Google’s Threat Horizons Report 2024 [2] which observes that weak credentials
is the most common unauthorized access attack vector.
The National Public Data Breach in 2024 is among the biggest breaches in recent years,
exposing Personal Identifiable Information (PII) including social security numbers, home
addresses or phone numbers, totaling 2.9 billion records [3]. Other notable breaches
include the Cam4 Data Breach in 2020 leaking 10.88 billion records and the Yahoo Data
Breach in 2017, exposing 3 billion users [4]. The IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report [5]
estimates the global average cost of a data breach in 2024 to total at $4.88 million.
This thesis builds upon a study proposed in 2023, titled ”Your Email Address Holds the
Key: Understanding the Connection Between Email and Password Security with Deep
Learning” [6] by researchers from the Swiss Federal Technology Institute of Lausanne.
The study observes the amount of additional information an email address can carry and
leverages this observation to present the relationship between an email address and the
corresponding profile password. Their research utilized a deep­learning model to guess a
user’s password based on their email address. The analysis of the dataset identifies that
email addresses hold relevant information about the user. For instance, email domains
can hint to nationality and name or initials can be extracted from the username. The
results show that context­sensitive models predict passwords with higher probabilities
than standard password generator models.
1.1
Goals of the research
In this study, three state­of­the­art machine learning models are selected (Google’s T5,
Meta’s BART and LLama) and fine­tuned on a dataset built from data breaches. The aim
of this research is to analyze the impact of the inclusion of user related data on model
predictions. The primary objective is to assess the security of text­based passwords in
the context of expanding artificial intelligence capabilities.
The research questions of this thesis are defined as:
1. Which of the three state­of­the art LLMs (T5, BART and Llama) is the most effective
context­aware password predictor?
2. To what degree can fine­tuning pre­trained language models on breached creden­
tials improve the accuracy of password guessing based on additional context about
the user?

Background
This chapter presents different authentication methods and focuses on techniques and
tools used for password attacks, as well as mitigation strategies. Moreover, the theory
behind machine learning approaches used in this research is presented.
2.1
Authentication methods
The following authentication methods are commonly recognized in modern security sys­
tem design:
• Text­based passwords ­ a sequence of characters, including numbers and special
characters,
• Pass phrases ­ a sequence of words (a sentence),
• Graphical passwords ­ this type of password can have different forms, including but
not limited to image sequences (correct image sequence selection from a superset
of images) or ”draw a secret” designs (drawing of a glyph within a grid) [8],
• PINs ­ a short sequence of numbers (commonly between 4 to 6 digits),
• Biometric passwords ­ passwords using different biometric properties of the user
that are commonly face scans or fingerprints [9]. Biometrics passwords have been
gaining popularity in the recent years and are nowadays commonly used in smart­
phones,
• Time­based one­time passwords ­ passwords generated on demand which provide
one­time access and are valid for a limited period of time (often these are sent to a
peripheral device, such as an SMS message received on a mobile phone) [10],
Out of these, the most common authentication method for online services by a wide mar­
gin are text­based passwords which were first introduced as a digital system in 1961 as
a response to the need for multiple people using the same device [11]. Hoverer, his­
torically, first mention of using passwords can be found in the ancient times where the
Roman military invented a ”watchword”. The secret phrase was distributed every night
among soldiers, allowing them to detect enemies by challenging them to state the correct
password.
2.2
Attack vectors on text­based passwords and mitigation
techniques
One of the most common attacks vectors designed to gain unauthorized access is phish­
ing. Cybersecurity specialists commonly consider the weakest link in the security chain to
be human error, which is precisely what phishing is exploiting. Phishing is the technique of
tricking users into providing their passwords (in plain text format) by sending scam emails,
texts or phone calls. In most cases, the victim is prompted to enter their credentials into a
fake, but identical webpage controlled by the attacker or tricked into downloading a virus
hidden in the email attachment. In recent years, phishing is becoming increasingly so­
phisticated as well as attackers focusing on precise targeting. This is a consequence of
simplified access to AI which helps generate and tailor content used for the attack pro­
cess. As an experiment, ChatGPT can be requested to prepare the content of a phishing
email directing employees to update their passwords by clicking the provided link. The

done from the same IP address. This can be overcome by waiting a sufficient amount of

Commonly used, simple passwords can be cracked using predefined password lists, such
as rockyou.txt that contains almost 15 million entries and is constructed of the most
common passwords observed in leaked credentials list. The most common passwords
are considered to be 123456, password, 12345678 and qwerty. Breached data can also
be used for Credential Stuffing ­ a technique that takes advantage of password reuse.
The attacker obtains leaked credentials for a given profile and attempts to use the same
credentials for other systems. This can be mitigated by the users creating different pass­
words for each account, yet many users decide to reuse passwords or use simple pattern
templates for different services due to personal convenience. Nowadays, users have
access to solutions such as Bitwarden [12] or LastPass [13]. which are designed for cre­
dential storage with quality of life functionalities such as auto­filling credentials on login
screens.
If an attacker is in the possession of a password’s corresponding hash and wants to find
its plain text format, they may use a list of pre­computed hashes also known as a rain­
bow table. A great mitigation against cracking a password hash is using salting and
a resource­heavy hashing algorithm [14], for example Argon2Id [15]. Salting is the
process of appending a unique and randomly generated string to the password. Adding
salt constrains attack algorithms to guessing single passwords at a time, as the salt func­
tion effectively irreversibly distorts the hashing algorithm used for all passwords in the
breached system. Moreover, using a resource­heavy hashing algorithm further increases
the time needed to crack the password.
Social engineering plays an increasingly larger role in defining attack vectors. Social
media users are progressively more active on social media, revealing both personal and
public (such as current employment) details on large social media platforms including
LinkedIn, Facebook and Instagram. Attackers are provided with sufficient information to
create targeted phishing profile dictionaries or lists which include personal information
such as the target’s pets’ names, year of birth, or relatives’ names. This data can be
used to create password templates composed from combinations of the target’s personal
information.
The above methods of attack are largely mitigated by enabling (or enforcing) multi­factor
authentication (MFA) [16], [17], which requires users to confirm their access attempt on
a peripheral device within a short period of time. These factors can be: a) Knowledge
Factor, depending on ”Something You Know”, most commonly a text­based password; b)
Possession Factor defined as ”Something You Have”, such as a mobile phone with Au­
thenticator app generating a one time pass; c) Inheritance Factor, described as ”Some­
thing You Are” ­ biometrics data, most commonly a fingerprint or face scan. For many
services, Two Factor Authentication (2FA) using Knowledge and Possession factors is a
sufficient investment for a significant increase in user security, while also not adding ex­
cess overhead to the authentication process. However, some of the more sophisticated
phishing techniques are designed to extract the second authentication factor. One imple­
mentation of MFA are passkeys [18] which are gaining more and more popularity due to
it’s simplicity. This solution relies on the public­key cryptography and eliminates the need
for traditional credentials. When the user wants to authenticate to the service, the private
key is used to prove the identity.

Apart from attacks designated for cracking or obtaining access to a profile’s password,

increasingly difficult task for users to spot phishing attempts. In turn, many services en­

Organizations also implement mandatory password update cycles. Finally, knowledge
and awareness of cyber crime and safety in the online world is globally rising.

Password entropy
Password entropy describes how effective the password is against password guessing
attacks. This is calculated based on the character set of the password as well as the
length of it.
The entropy is calculated as follows and is represented in bits:
H[bits] = N ∗log2  (S)
where:
• H ­ entropy, represented in bits,
• N ­ password length,
• S ­ character set size,
The entropy score can be interpreted as low ­ up to 30 bits, medium ­ from 30 to 50 bits
and high ­ from 60 bits.
Time needed to brute force a password can be represented as follows:
Time[s] =
2H
guesses per second
Given modern GPU efficiency at around one billion guesses per second, passwords with
an entropy of at most 30 can be cracked instantly. Accordingly, passwords of entropy 40

2.4
Similarity score
In this thesis, similarity score calculations are applied in two contexts: similarity between
username and corresponding password (username similarity) and similarity between the
best guess and the real password (password similarity).
In order to represent similarity between two strings, three metrics are selected: Longest
Common Subsequence (LCS), Levenshtein ratio and Jaccard score.
The similarity score is presented as a floating point in the range 0.0 to 1.0. Two strings
are considered to have low similarity for scores in range [0.0, 0.3), medium similarity for
[0.3 ­ 0.6) and high similarity for scores above or equal 0.6.
Longest common subsequence
Longest common sequence measures longest subsequence present in both strings. LCS
is calculated as follows for two strings String1 and String2:
LCS Ratio = |Longest_common _substring|
max(|String1|, |String2|)
Levenshtein ratio
Levenshtein ratio is based on the Levenshtein distance which measures the minimum
number of operations on a character level (insertions, deletions, substitutions) needed to
turn one string into another. The ratio is calculated as follows for two strings String1 and
String2:
Levenshtein Ratio = |String1|  +  |String2|  − Levenshtein Distance
|String1|  +  |String2|
Jaccard score
Jaccard score measures similarity between two strings, where strings are represented as
sets of characters. The score is calculated as the size of the intersection divided by the
size of the union of the sets and is calculated for two strings String1 and String2 as
(N(String1), N(String2) represents sets of n­grams):
Jaccard Similarity = |N(String1) ∩N(String2)|
|N(String1) ∪N(String2)|
2.5
Machine learning and AI
Machine learning is a fast­growing field within Artificial Intelligence that focuses on algo­
rithms enabling computers to learn from data and perform tasks without explicit instruc­
tions. One of the key subfields of AI, Natural Language Processing (NLP), specializes
in processing human­like text input, allowing users to interact with computers using natu­
ral language. Large Language Models (LLMs) are designed for NLP tasks, with popular
examples including Gemini, GPT, LLama, BART, and T5. ChatGPT [22] is an example
of a powerful AI chatbot based on the GPT LLM, which utilizes NLP to transform the con­
text of the conversation into coherent responses to multilevel discussions. At the core
of these advancements are neural networks, computational models that mimic the hu­
man brain by using layers of neurons connected by weights. Deep learning, a subset
of machine learning, employs multiple layers of neural networks to handle complex and
multidimensional data.

2.5.1
Transformer­Based Architecture
Most of the LLMs use transformer­based architecture which is a type of neural network.
Transformers were introduced by Google in 2017 in the research paper ”Attention Is All
You Need” [23]. As the title of the paper suggests, the main concept behind transformers
is attention which allows the models to capture dependencies between words in the input
sequence. Transformers create attention maps which define the relation between words
in a sentence and thus they are effective for longer and more complex inputs. Moreover,
transformers process input non­sequentially which makes the training and output gener­
ation faster and offers parallelization with high scalability. This addresses limitations of
the previous architectures ­ RNNs (Recurrent Neural Networks) and LSTMs (Long Short­
Term Memory). The building blocks of transformers’ architecture are the encoder and
decoder components [24]. The encoder is responsible for processing the input which first
has to be tokenized ­ words or characters in an input sentence are mapped to tokens.
The decoder then uses the input representation created by the encoder to generate out­
puts. Encoders and decoders can be used as independent components and hence, the
following frameworks can be distinguished:
• Encoder only ­ models utilizing this architecture excel in tasks requiring deep un­
derstanding of the input without the need to produce new sequences. Common
applications are text classification, question answering or entity extraction. Popular
models built with encoder­only frameworks are BERT and RoBERTa.
• Decoder only­ used for auto­regressive tasks where output tokens are generated
as a left­to­right sequence. Models implementing this framework predict one token
at a time based on the tokens previously generated. This allows such models to
generate logical and contextual output and hence are often used for text generation
or for data forecasting. Some of the most used decoder­only models are GPT and
LLama.
• Encoder­decoder ­ this architecture is often refereed to as Seq2Seq (Sequence­to­
Sequence) models as they map one sequence to another. Those models are com­
monly used for translations, summarization or converting one form of media to an­
other (text­to­speech and speech­to­text). Popular models using encoder­decoder
architecture are T5 and BART.
2.5.2
Fine­tuning
Fine­tuning is a powerful technique used to adapt a pre­trained model to a domain specific
task. Pre­trained models are initially trained on a large dataset to gain general knowledge.
Fine­tuning offers many benefits as it is usually faster than training a model from scratch,
requires a smaller dataset, and the tuned models are capable of combining general knowl­
edge with domain specific data. Many state­of­the­art models are available for fine tuning,
such as GPT or Llama. Moreover, during the fine­tuning process, training parameters can
be specified to further customize the model. There exist different solutions that can be
used to download and fine­tune models, such as HuggingFace [25].

Related Work

3.1
Password guessing attacks
Researchers has been focusing on password security as well as different password guess­
ing techniques since the invention of the text­based passwords. The first research within
this domain is considered to be ”UNIX Password Security” [26] published in 1979 by
Robert Morris and Ken Thompson. The authors describe the threat posed by guess­
ing attacks when using weak and common passwords. The paper prompted adaptation
of better password security practices, introducing hashing algorithms as well as creating
the foundations for later work within this field.
The research paper ”A Systematic Review on Password Guessing Tasks” [27] presents
a deep­dive into different password guessing techniques developed from 2016 to 2023.
According to this study, the current state­of­the­art solutions within password guessing
are Markov Models, Probabilistic Context­Free Grammars (PCFG), PCFG and Markov
Hybrid Models, Neural Network­Based Models and Targeted Rule­Based Guessing.
3.1.1
Markov Models
Markov model is a statistical model that predicts the probability of future states taking
into the account only the current state. Markov models were the first solutions to ”smart”
password guessing.
”Fast dictionary attacks on passwords using time­space tradeoff” [28] is the first research
paper proposing the Markov model for password guessing tasks and was published in
2005. The proposed method noted a much higher success rate in password recovery
than the state­of­art tools used in early 2000. However, being memory­less models by
nature, they are considerably constrained. Regardless of that, the Markov Model is still
researched in the context of password guessing. One of the most recent studies ”Dy­
namic Markov Model: Password Guessing Using Probability Adjustment Method” [29]
(2023), enhances the standard model by dynamically adjusting the probability distribution
of passwords and hence, reducing repetition rates.
3.1.2
Probabilistic Context­Free Grammars (PCFGs)
PCFGs combine probability models with standard Context­Free Grammars and are mostly
used for string generation based on specified rules as well as the likelihood of occurrence
of each rule.
One of the most influential studies in this domain is ”Password Cracking Using Prob­
abilistic Context­Free Grammars” (2009) [30]. It introduced the use of the probabilis­
tic context­free grammars (PCFG) to enhance brute­force methods. The presented ap­
proach cracked significantly more passwords than the targeted rule­based guessing ap­
proach. Many research papers built upon this study. ”On Practical Aspects of PCFG
Password Cracking” [31] introduced a PCFG framework adapted to the task of password
cracking. The research ”SE#PCFG: Semantically Enhanced PCFG for Password Analysis
and Cracking” [32] further expands on the previous study and focuses on the semantics
and patterns within analyzed passwords.

3.1.3
PCFG and Markov Hybrid Models
This solution combines the strengths of PCFGs and Markov models by taking into the
account both the global structure and the local state. The research ”GuessFuse: Hybrid
Password Guessing With Multi­View” [33] utilized this technique which presents visible
advancements in password guessing.
3.1.4
Neural Network­Based Models
Since the advancement of AI and machine learning, many researchers apply neural net­
work models to analyze how users compose their passwords as well propose improve­
ments to the password cracking methods.
The study ”Fast, Lean, and Accurate: Modeling Password Guessability Using Neural Net­
works” [34] implements Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) for password guessing and
shows that neural networks are more effective in detecting patterns and guessing pass­
words than state­of­the­art solutions using PCFG.
”PassGAN: A Deep Learning Approach for Password Guessing” [35] research laid the
foundations for using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to generate new password
lists which can be used in brute­force attacks. The proposed solution matches the perfor­
mance of the RNN. Since then, several papers were published that built upon this study.
The most recent one ­ ”PagPassGPT: Pattern Guided Password Guessing via Genera­
tive Pretrained Transformer” [36], enhances password guessing by utilizing Generative
Pretrained Transformer (GPT) architecture to generate a new password set based on the
distribution and patterns discovered in the training set. It uses pattern guided guessing
and new method for limiting number of duplicates in the generated set. Compared to
previous research in this domain ­ ”PassGAN: A Deep Learning Approach for Password
Guessing” [37], the PagPassGPT guessed 12% more passwords and generated 25% less
duplicates.
The research ”PassTSL: Modeling Human­Created Passwords through Two­Stage Learn­
ing” [38] introduces a new solution that combines pre­training and fine­tuning to create a
two­staged learning framework. The results show that this approach outperforms all other
state­of­the­art password cracking methods.
3.1.5
Targeted Rule­Based Guessing
Targeted password guessing attacks take into the account user specific context, such as
Personally Identifiable Information (PII) that describes information that can directly link to
a specific person. Such data is, for example full name, email address, residence address
or data of birth.
Machine learning techniques are also utilized in studies researching targeted password
attacks that focus on guessing passwords based on specific personal information. A
research from 2016 named ”Targeted Online Password Guessing: An Underestimated
Threat” [39] accesses the effectiveness of using PII and and a profile’s leaked password
from other services in targeted attacks. The authors propose Targuess ­ a machine learn­
ing framework that operates on different sensitive information of the user: personal identi­
fiable information such as name or nationality, passwords from other services and different
combinations of this data. Furthermore, the study is comparing the ”normal users” and
the ”security­savvy users”. The results show that the greatest success rate (over 73%
against normal users) was obtained by using user’s sister password together with email
address and name and additional context such as gender or hobby.
The research paper ”Your Email Address Holds the Key: Understanding the Connection
Between Email and Password Security with Deep Learning” (2023) [6] focuses on email

address context, which more precisely means observing the amount of extractable per­
sonal data contained in email addresses. This research compares a general password
generation model (base model, auto regressive two­layer LSTM) and a context­aware
model which takes as an email address as input. The results show that the context­aware
model was able to identify patterns within an email’s domain and top­level domain. This
leads to a higher number of guessed passwords compared to the base model. The base
model produces guesses based on a probability distribution and responds with the most

The findings from this research influenced the development of this thesis. Based on the

retrieved data. This thesis performs various experiments on context­aware models and
does not take into consideration a general password generator. The dataset used in the
aforementioned study is Exploit.in and Anti Public. However, those sets are not publicly
available and this thesis instead utilizes the Collection #1­#5 subset which is smaller than
the dataset considered by the authors of the ”Your Email Address Holds the Key: Un­

research.
3.2
Users’ habits in password creation and management
Over the years, there have been several studies focused on analyzing users’ habits in
password creation, management and coping mechanisms in today’s world of increasing
dependency on various online services.
The research paper ”The Password Life Cycle: User Behavior in Managing Passwords”
(2014) [40] presents users’ password creation strategies and adaptation to new demands
(increased password length and complexity). It shows that many participants of this study
categorize their accounts based on various security considerations and reuse the pass­
words accordingly. The study shows that many participants link the frequency of using
a particular service with the need for a stronger password. Consequently, many users
have one password for infrequently used accounts but use stronger passwords for ser­
vices accessed more often. The study shows that 40% of the participants never change
their passwords if they are not required to do so. Moreover, when resetting a forgotten
password, most responders change their passwords to something similar to an already
existing password. The research also analyses password re­using strategies of the par­
ticipants. Many choose the same or alike passwords for services with similar content
(professional accounts, social media, shopping, etc). Most responders admitted to storing
their passwords physically, often as a note kept close to their computer or by using digital
media­ such as an Excel sheet, Word document, a txt note or an email sent to them­
selves. Moreover, the participants that were not using password managers explained that
copying and pasting the credentials from the manager is too time consuming. However,
the research was published in 2014 and modern password managers can now auto­fill
credentials. The research discusses potential solutions, such as using Single Sign­On,
improving password managers and rising awareness about cyber threats.
The research ”Why people (don’t) use password managers effectively” (2019) [41] studies

factors driving the participants into using different password management strategies. It
reviled that many users opt to use built­in browser tools out of convenience but often
chose weaker passwords than the group using external password managers. The primary
motivation for the second group are security concerns. Many responders chose not to use
any tools simply because they believe there is no need for it.
In the study ”So Long, And No Thanks for the Externalities: The Rational Rejection of
Security Advice by Users” [42], the author accesses security recommendations and seeks
explanation as to why those are often disregarded by the users. The analysis of the
cybersecurity guidelines reviled that they are usually overly complex and include much
overhead which often outweighs potential benefits. The study explains that the ”worst­
case harm” is not equal to the ”actual harm” meaning that by following complex security
advices, the gain is not significant compared to the amount of effort needed to be complaint
with the guidelines. Hence, users often choose simpler solutions instead of following
security advices.
”Who creates strong passwords when nudging fails” [43] research discusses the role of
different forms of nudging in choosing a password ­ images, reminders, colored mes­
sages. The results show that nudging did not influence password choosing behavior.
The participants that have higher cybersecurity knowledge use stronger passwords. The
takeaway from the study is that increasing awareness about the importance of cyber hy­
giene and the risks posed by cyber threats influences users’ choices and habits in the
cyber space.

Summary
There exist five state­of­the­art approaches within the targeted password guessing field.
Many of the mentioned papers were published in recent years, which demonstrates a
growing trend in the domain of password guessing as a relevant and fast­growing re­

advancements to the current state­of­the­art solutions were proposed that utilize deep
learning methods.
The reviewed research papers focusing on the users’ habits in password creation and
management, show how individuals handle the increasing number of online accounts.
These studies highlight the critical need for education and awareness about the impor­
tance of cyber hygiene and risks posed by cyber threats. Additionally, the authors discuss
how complex security guidelines impact user behavior and how technology can enhance
security without introducing unnecessary complexity.

4
Dataset Analysis
Data used in this project originates from Collections #1­#5 [44]. The set contains 2.2 billion
unique pairs of email addresses and passwords. This leak was discovered in the dark web
in January 2019 and includes over 845 gigabytes of data. The data is structured as email
address:password pairs in plain text. Many credentials included in this dataset were
found in the Yahoo data breach (2013 and 2014) as well as different LinkedIn breaches,
although the origin of the whole set is unknown. The set includes duplicated entries but it
is not clear if this indicates password reuse or multiple inclusions of data from the same
breach. In this work, username will refer to the local part, appearing before the @ symbol
which is a unique identifier on a domain level. A set of 1,078,419 entries were selected
where password length is within within the range of 4 to 16 characters and both emails
and passwords are constructed from Latin letters. There are no duplications of the email
address:password pairs in the set.
4.1
Methodology
Dataset analysis is split into three parts: password analysis, email address analysis and
evaluation of similarity between usernames and corresponding passwords. In order to
examine the content of usernames and passwords, 11 rules are applied to categorize the
content based on the characters that they are constructed from (uppercase, lowercase,
special characters and digits). Combining rule based analysis with an examination of
the content length, provides an overview of how users compose their credentials. More­
over, an important factor when analysing passwords is their entropy which indicates how
hard it is use brute force methods to crack the password. The entropy is computed from
password’s length and character space. Email address analysis includes reviewing do­
main names and top level domain names. Lastly, the similarity between usernames and
passwords is assessed by calculating similarity scores between the two strings. The sim­
ilarity score reflects users’ habits of creating credentials which use a common string of
characters in the username and the password.
4.1.1
Rule based analysis
Rule based analysis utilizes 11 rules composed to classify a string based on its con­
tent. The examined strings can consists of uppercase characters, lowercase characters,
special characters and numbers. Strings containing uppercase or lowercase characters
(exclusive) are grouped together since both cases have the same character space (26).
There are constraints regarding which special characters can be present in the usernames
but this is not reflected in this study and the character space for special characters is 32.
The rules are ordered according to the character space. The regex for each rule can be
found in Appendix A.1.

5.1
Technical overview
The programming language used in this project is Python, as it offers access to many

an application that can be used for building, maintaining and deploying machine learning
projects. It offers integration with different models as well as datasets and allows two
methods of access ­ through the web application or connecting to the public API. For this
project, all the machine learning scripts were developed with HuggingFace’s transformers
library for Python and all the utilized models were downloaded from HuggingFace to a
local environment. Being able to interact with the models locally was one of the project’s
requirements, as the dataset contains sensitive information.
5.2
AI data extraction
An email address is Personally Identifiable Information (PII) because it uniquely (within
a domain) points to an email address owner. Moreover, the username often contains
personal information as it is often constructed from a profile’s name and surname and/or
other data such as year of birth or a nickname. Moreover, the domain address can reveal
personal information. Among others, these could be a workplace via a company domain
or a country of residence via the domain name and country code (e.g. poczta.pl for
Poland or libero.it for Italy). Nationality may also be determined based on an entry’s
name/surname (if present in the username) or top­level domain name.
For example jan.kowalski95@freenet.de indicates that the profile’s name and surname
is Jan Kowalski. Based on the domain name freenet.de it can be deduced that Jan
lives in Germany, meanwhile examining the full name, the profile’s nationality could be
determined to be Polish. This data mining narrows down the profile’s nationality options
to either Polish or German. Other elements of the username can be used as well. The
name indicates Jan’s gender to be male, and the number 95 suggests the profile’s year of
birth to be 1995. This example shows that from a single email address, it may be possible
to extract personal information from the username’s content. These include full name,
nationality, gender, and year of birth.
In order to extract this information from dataset used in this project, meta-llama/Llama-2-7b
[46] was used. Listing 5.1 was given to the model to attempt to extract name, nationality,
gender and birth year from every username in the set.

The prompt is constructed to obtain the requested information (limited to the expected

ignore part of the prompt.
Additional automated post­processing was created to sterilize and unify the results. In
some cases, the model failed to determine the profile’s nationality even when the country
code was present in the email address. For these cases, the country code is mapped to
a nationality in a prepared lookup table based on the country code standard ISO 3166. In
many entries where the name and surname properties were not identified, the model set
”name” to be the username. This was not desired, but the behavior was not corrected as
it would require human moderation. The idea behind data extraction with AI use is to limit
the need for manual data retrieval. In consequence, some of the generated data may be
hallucinated. In addition, the year key is not specified in the prompt as exactly the year
of birth, so the model usually includes any number found in the username.
The model had the most difficulties with extracting year data. It managed to extract the
year only for 11% of the email addresses, nationality for 35%, gender for 58% and name

• top_k ­ parameter that filters top k tokens with the highest probability for the next
output. By default set to 50.
• top_p ­ a probability based filtering. It selects the most likely tokens from a proba­
bility distribution, until a predefined threshold is reached.
• do_sample ­ a boolean flag that determines whether the model should use sampling
for generating outputs. When sampling is enabled, the model selects the token
based on their probabilities which results in more diverse outputs. This is especially
important when the aim is to obtain multiple different answers per prompt.

desired results. Based on the above, three presets of parameters (presented in Table
6.2) can be created to define creative, deterministic and default (balanced) generation
behavior. The top_k was left as default (50) for all presets.

7
Experiments and Results Analysis
The test set contains 10,000 unique entries. For every input, the model generates 1,000
guesses. A common drawback for all the models is that the generated output contains
duplicated guesses. As part of processing the output, duplicates are removed with respect
to the order of unique guesses. The best guess is selected by finding a string that has the
highest similarity score between itself and the real password.
Terminology used in this chapter:
• username score ­ term used to describe the similarity between a username and the
corresponding password,
• password score ­ denotes a similarity between the guess and the real password,
• best guess ­ describes the guess that is the most similar (the highest similarity score)
to the real password.
The experiments are divided into three main parts. Phase One and Phase Two focus on
fine tuning the models on original data (email address:password pairs) as well as the
AI­complemented dataset. The aim of the two first phases is to select the best performing
model. The evaluation is focused on the following metrics:
• Number of guessed passwords ­ a primary metric taken into account when selecting
the best model. Ultimately, the aim is to guess as many passwords as possible.
• Analysis of the guessed passwords ­ the guessed passwords are divided into dif­
ferent categories based on similarity between the password and the corresponding
username. This provides a good overview on how well the model behaves for a
variety of cases. At this point, the content or the length of the password is not as
important.
• Distribution of password similarity scores ­ this metric presents similarity scores be­
tween the best guess and the real password. It aims to measure how close the
model is to guessing passwords.
• Distribution of best guess index ­ best guess index indicates which guess attempt
out of all results generated for each prompt was the best. This provides a better
insight into how many guesses are needed to guess the password or generate the
best guess. The models often produce duplicates within 1,000 guesses per prompt.
Duplicates guesses are removed while keeping the order of the items. The best
guess index is selected out of the ordered unique guess attempts.
• Number of unique guesses ­ depending on the generation settings, the response
might contain duplicates. The number of unique guesses shows how diverse the
output is.
• Average generation time per prompt ­ generation speed is essential for bigger datasets
and when requesting a greater number of guesses.
In order to visualize the results, three graphs types are generated for every model and
aim to compare the results for different output generation presets:
1. Histogram of the profile count for each username score. The scores are presented
in bins of size 0.1. Bins below 0.4 are disregarded because the test set contains
AI­Based Password Guessing Attacks
37
==PAGE:46==
only entries with scores higher or equal to 0.4. The aim of this plot is to present how
many passwords are guessed depending on the connection between username and
the corresponding password.
2. Histogram of profile count for each password score. This graph is used to show the
distribution of guessed passwords and analysis of distance between the best guess
and the actual value.
3. Density plot of the best guess indices. The density plot peaks appear at points of
relative likelihood of a common best guess index. The plotted values include best
guess indices in the context of the whole set and indices of exact guesses. The set
containing exact guesses is a subset of all best guesses. It is important to note that
the models did not always generate 1,000 unique guesses, therefore models using
certain generation settings might not reach the index 1,000.
Phase One and Phase Two eliminate one model respectively, which results in Phase
Three experiments using the best model with optimal generation parameters. The final
phase is dedicated to observing the impact of each key (name, nationality, gender, year)
included in the AI­complemented dataset. The model selected from previous stages is
tuned and tested on datasets with all possible combinations of the included keys, further
split into a non­filtered dataset (all keys including ”unknowns”) and filtered dataset (any
entries with at least one unknown key are ignored). Figures presenting evaluation and
training loss during the fine­tuning process for all experiments can be found in Appendix
A.

when it comes to the number of guessed passwords as well as the distribution of the

guesses.
Unlike T5 and BART, the Llama model obtained the most correct guesses for the default
configuration. However, the number of guessed passwords for Llama is less than 1% of
the whole set (performing at a fraction of the other models’ efficiency). In the creative

of all three models. Regardless, even with a high number of unique outputs the model
guessed only 42/10,000 passwords. Hoverer, Llama performed slightly better for pass­
words with lower similarity to the username than the other models. The Llama model has
the widest range of best guess indices compared to T5 and BART. For all three models,
deterministic generation settings produced visibly the worst results.

8
Conclusion
This chapter concludes the study on leaked credentials and the influence of additional
context use in targeted password guessing using LLMs. The research questions are
evaluated and discussed in the context of future work within this domain.
8.1
Summary
The aim of this research is to analyze user password creation habits and assess the
strength of passwords created with elements of their corresponding email addresses by
implementing a targeted password guessing tool. Three modern LLMs (Google’s T5,
Meta’s BART and LLama) were selected and fine­tuned on credentials obtained from
data breaches. The data used in the experiments is a subset of Collections #1­#5 and
contains 1,078,419 rows. Dataset analysis revealed that most of the included passwords
are between six to ten characters long, constructed of uppercase or lowercase characters,
and numbers. Analysis of the dataset email addresses shows that the most commonly
occurring server domains are yahoo, gmail and hotmail, with a majority of Russia­based
top­level domains. This research focuses on the similarity between usernames and their
corresponding passwords. This connection is measured using the similarity metrics Lev­
enshtein ratio. The average similarity of the whole dataset is medium (0.67).
Additional data was extracted from the email addresses using AI. The Llama model was
asked to retrieve name, nationality, gender and year. Even though the results are not
always accurate, very minimal post­processing is performed to observe the efficiency of
AI automation. Post­processing focuses on extracting the nationality information from
the top­level domain in case the model failed to do so and unifying the formatting of the
response.
The models generate password guesses based on provided context about the user. In
order to assess the performance of the tuned models, three experiments were conducted.
The primary metric used for selecting the best model is the number of guessed passwords.
The evaluation also focuses on the number of unique guesses, distribution of the best
score index, and the analysis of guessed passwords.
In Phase One, all three models were fine­tuned on data containing email address:password
pairs. The T5 and BART models produced very similar results while the Llama model per­
formed significantly worse and was excluded from further tests. The goal of Phase Two
was to identify which of the two remaining models could guess the larger amount of pass­
words when fine­tuned on the AI­complemented dataset. The T5 model performed best
in both Phase One and Phase Two, and thus was selected as the sole model for the final
phase. In Phase One, T5 guessed 5,270/10,000 passwords using creative generation
settings. In Phase Two, T5 guessed 5,406/10,000 using default settings.
In Phase Three, the influence of each key included in the AI­complemented dataset is
studied. According to the findings, the variation of the model trained on the dataset that
contains email address, name, nationality and year keys, produced the best results com­
pared to other combinations of selected keys (5,445/10,000 guessed passwords). The
impact of the ”Unknown” values in the dataset was measured and the model guessed
62.1% when tuned on full set (with ”Unknown”) and 64.3% on filtered set.
For all models and experiments, the number of unique guesses per prompt was the high­
est for creative settings and the lowest for deterministic. This was expected as for the

creative preset, the number of tokens taken into the account when generating the results
is the highest which allows the model to explore more combinations and consequently
come up with more diverse results.
The performed experiments, as well as the comparison with the PagPassGPT ­ password
generation model and content of the (”rockyou.txt”) dataset commonly used for brute­
force attacks show that the user context can have a positive impact on the number of
guessed passwords. This matched the results presented in the ”Your Email Address
Holds the Key: Understanding the Connection Between Email and Password Security
with Deep Learning” [6] research paper.
The results of this study show the importance of raising awareness on threats posed
by use of weak and predictable passwords. In the fast­growing digital world with rapid
improvements within Artificial Intelligence, it is more important than ever to educate people
about online hygiene and consequences of cyber attacks.
8.2
Discussion of the research questions
This section revisits the three research questions presented in the introduction (1.1).
Regarding the first question ­ ”Which of the three selected models is the most effective
context­aware password predictor?” ­ all three models were fine­tuned equally and on the
same datasets and subsequently evaluated based on the number of guessed passwords.
The T5 model produced the best results across all experiments.
To address the second question ­ ”To what degree can fine­tuning pre­trained language
models on breached credentials improve the accuracy of password guessing based on
additional context about the user?” ­ the research conducted extensive experiments to
identify the impact of user­related data (email address, name, nationality, gender and year
of birth). This additional information about the user was extracted from a given email ad­
dress with the use of AI. The best results were obtained with the exclusion of the ”gender”
key. Moreover, the results were compared to passwords included in the rockyou.txt set
and the passwords generated by PagPassGPT. The proposed method correctly guessed
more passwords than both those approaches.
The aim of the third question ­ ”How do different generation parameters affect the per­
formance of fine­tuned model password prediction?” was to find optimal generation pa­
rameters for the selected models. Three presets were created with varying degrees of
randomness and diversity of output. These are the creative, default and deterministic
configurations. The best generation settings were selected taking into account only the
number of guessed passwords. For T5 and BART, models based only on email address
inputs, the creative settings generated the best results, whereas models based on ad­
ditional context (AI­complemented dataset) performed better in the default preset. The
Llama model was used only in Phase One and generated the best results using default
settings.
8.3
Future work
Further research could explore automated social engineering using AI to find additional
target profile data. There exist models which could simulate social engineering by con­
ducting simple research on a given profile by crawling email address search results and
extracting data based on the results. This solution could produce more accurate results
than the simple data extraction performed in this study. The models used in this study
are in ”base” size due to limited resources, however using checkpoints with larger num­
ber of parameters could produce better results. Furthermore, fine­tuning the latest and

accurate presentation of the capabilities of modern models.
The presented work could be further improved by creating a measurable metric for com­
paring the username similarity score with password strength. The entropy score metric
used in the study does not reflect real password strength when considering additional
context. The Levenshtein ratio used in this thesis has considerable limitations when pre­
senting username similarity.
For instance, password having strong connection to the
corresponding username such as johnsmith@mail.com:smith, will be determined as
”medium” similarity while the link is obvious. Moreover, the presentation of results could
be standardized to be compatible with state­of­the­art solutions, and with other recent
research within this field.
