Abstract 
 
This thesis reviews electricity makert designs and imbalance schemes implemented in India, 
Pakistan , Nord Pool and South Africa.It applies them to a set of 4 wind sites and 1 price zone 
located in 5 countries around the world to see the revenue impact due to deviations from 
schedule, and makes recommendations to the implementation of imbalance scheme in South 
Africa. The comparison between the imbalance schemes revealed the following; The Nord 
Pool imbalance schemes is designed for markets where wind energy has an influence on the 
energy price which might not work in markets where wind energy is a price taker like in South 
Africa. There are similarities in the generation mix  as well as the penalty schemes applied in 
India and South Africa. Firstly, both countries are heavily reliant on coal to meet their daily 
electricity needs and have big ambitions and commitments towards netzero emissions As a 
result, renewables are starting to play an integral part in the power generation sector. 
Secondly, the imbalance model in India is similar to that of South Africa as they both charges 
for deviations from the forecast as a way to incentivise renewable energy generators to 
improve their forecasting. The main difference between the India and South Africa imbalance 
rules is that the direction of the imbalance is taken into consideration , positive and negative 
imnalances are not treated eqaully. This is due to the grid operator’s struggle to keep the  grid 
frequency stable.  
 
There are lessons to be learnt from each country, however no single country’s imbalance 
models were found to be suitable for South Africa due to the uniqueness in market designs 
and contribution of renewables in the total energy mix.  
 
South Africa has no functioning intra-day market or allowance of schedule revisions by 
generators closer to real time when more accurate information of forecasting could be 
avaibale. This alone brings a disadvantage to market bidders. Accordingly, this paper 
recommends that the imbalance rules make allowence for multiple schedule revisions after 
the day-ahead gate closure for up to at least 2 hours before real time. Further to this, in order 
to create a level of transparency and fairness across all generation technologies as well as 
market participants,  harminization of imbalance prices should be given a priority as this 
doesn’t favor generators with higher power purchase agreement contract prices. Furthermore, 
consideration by the grid operator on balancing responsibility by renewable power plants need 
to take into account market maturity, state of the power system as well as the level of 
penetration of renewable energy in the power system.

Chapter 1 
1. Introduction 
 
1.1. 
Background and Context 
Wind power currently represents a substantial percentage of the generation mix across the 
globe and this share will increase exponentially in the coming years as more and more 
countries around the globe are undertaking climate change mitigation efforts to fulﬁll 
renewable energy targets as part of their commitments under the Paris Climate Agreement 
of 2015. The integration of renewable energy generation in power systems leads to new 
challenges that include long-term security of supply and an increased need for balancing 
and network stability. While hydro power has existed in many countries for decades, new 
renewable technologies such as onshore wind and solar PV are phased-in into the electricity 
system. The use of variable renewable energy sources has, however, several implications 
for the electricity supply system. Countries with high shares today have adapted new 
allocation and system control mechanisms over the past decade to ensure system stability 
as well as cost efficiency of supply. In European countries, the power supply systems have 
undergone substantial changes: changes in market design, and to some extent, changes in 
network operations. Among other measures, intraday markets have been introduced, and 
the requirements for the integration of renewable energy plants have widely increased. While 
these experiences hold relevant lessons for countries following them in the phase-in of 
renewable energy sources, they cannot be transferred directly to power systems that are not 
based on market trading of electricity and ancillary services as system operation as well as 
remuneration of electricity generation may be based on an entirely different set of rules.  
1.2. 
Scope and Objective  
 
The objectives of this thesis is to study imbalance penalty schemes implemented in India, 
Pakistan, the Nord Pool and compare them to that implemented in South Africa, and make 
recommendations for consideration in the South African imbalance penalty scheme for it to 
be fair across all market participants, to consider ground conditions and not be one that 
unfairly penalizes renewable energy generators but also incentivizes them to implement 
technologies that provide the most accurate forecasting.  
The objective is to check how the South African forecast penalty scheme is aligned to the 
forecast scheme being implemented in other markets

 Chapter 2 
2. Literature Review 
This chapter gives a descriptive background of electricity markets, in general This chapter 
will give a detailed overview of the electricity design, operation and timelines as well as an 
analysis of the imbalance rules employed in the various markets which will be used in the 
quantitative analysis of revenue impact of wind power plants.  
 
2.1. 
Integrating Wind Power  
 
Wind power has speciﬁc regulations in comparison to other generation technologies. Firstly, 
it is subject to support schemes in order to make it economically competitive. Secondly it 
presents some unique challenges and benefits to grid balancing authorities and energy 
market operators. Its variability must be accounted for in day-ahead and intra-day markets. 
Because it’s a free “fuel” with zero CO2 emissions, it lowers the overall cost of energy in 
organized electricity markets. Traditionally, market designs have usually been created for 
conventional generation units. For example, the day-ahead (DA) market gate closure usually 
takes place 12-15 hours prior to the dispatch day, in order to pre-schedule conventional 
generation units. However, there is still a lot of weather forecast uncertainties which affects 
wind power’s predictability. On the other hand, intraday (ID) markets that have gate closure 
times closer to real time usually present low liquidity [1], the shorted time horizon and 
reduced volumes attract a narrower range of market participants and this in turn introduces 
uncertainties in price. However, forecasting as these time horizons is more accurate than at 
day-ahead and liquidity has been seen to improve in recent years [2]. Power system have 
the special characteristic that supply, and demand should be equal in each instant and 
market participants such as Wind Power Producers (WPPs), are incentivized to participate 
actively in the balancing arrangements, by sending accurate schedules to the Transmission 
System Operator (TSO) and by participating in the provision of balancing services. The 
focus of this thesis is on the comparison of charges imposed on wind power plants in 
different markets when they deviate from their production schedules and the impact thereof 
on revenue and renewable energy integration in electricity systems.

2.2. 
Market Timeline 
In electricity systems, two targets must be met: First, demand and supply must match 
precisely, otherwise system stability is endangered. Second, generation should be 
scheduled and dispatched at the lowest cost, preferably based on a market mechanism [3]. 
The presence of variable RE causes difficulties in meeting these goals, as their generation is 
variable due to dependency on the wind and solar radiation at any given moment and only 
partially predictable because of uncertainties in weather forecasts. 
To understand the imbalance costs, this section gives a review of the workings of power 
markets, especially the very short-term part. Spot power trading is generally defined as the 
trading of power in the wholesale market from day-ahead until just before delivery, which is 
referred to as gate closure. In response to changing power systems due to the increasing 
penetration of intermittent sources of power generation such as wind and solar, trading of 
power has transitioned from the traditional day-ahead trading to shorter time horizons.

2.3. 
 The Day-Ahead Market 
 
In electricity markets, the initial generation schedule is typically determined one day 
ahead of delivery, either through zed scheduling in a centralized market or by means 
of internal production planning of market participants in a decentralized market. The 
day ahead market enables the trading of electricity one day in advance of the 
production. The following day is divided into 24 separate trading periods of 1 hour. 
Participants that want to trade power on the day-ahead market must submit their 
bids to the electricity pool or system operator by a certain deadline. In South Africa 
this deadline is 10:00 each day [5], the generators must submit their forecasted 
generation schedules to the system operator. The outcome is a day-ahead schedule 
of all controllable resources, whose sum should match expected demand.  
 
2.4. 
The Intraday Market 
 
The intraday market enables trading of electricity during the day of operation. It facilitates the 
fine adjustments of possible imbalances related to the submitted day-ahead schedule close to 
the hour of operation. The intraday market opens after the day ahead market closes and 
adjustments can be made until hours or minutes prior to the operating hour [Mazzi and 
Pinson,2017] [6],One advantage of the ID market is the accurate prediction of power volume 
compared to the DA market, which is important for renewable generators as the weather 
patterns can still change significantly between the day-ahead predictions and actual 
production. Intra-day markets are however not very widespread in emerging markets and 
vertically integrated utility structures due to several reasons such as where electricity markets 
may not have reached a level of market maturity where intraday markets are considered 
necessary. These markets may still be in the early stages of development, with a limited 
number of participants, lower trading volumes, and less complex market rules and regulations. 
In such cases, the need for intraday markets may not be perceived as high, as the existing 
arrangements for trading electricity may be considered sufficient to meet the market 
requirements. The other contributing factor could be grid infrastructure and generation 
capacity, they may have limitations in their grid infrastructure and generation capacity, may 
face challenges in terms of reliable and efficient power supply, which can affect the need for 
intraday markets. For example, if the grid infrastructure is not well-developed, it may be difficult

to manage and balance electricity supply and demand in real-time, which can reduce the need 
for intraday markets. 
 
Using a simulation model, Holttinen (2005) [7] estimates that generators could reduce the 
balancing costs by 30% by trading on an hour-ahead market as opposed to a day-ahead 
market. This supports the theory that wind power forecast accuracy is improved as you get 
closer to real time. In the intraday market, electricity is traded on the delivery day itself, 
which enables market participants to correct for shifts in their day-ahead nominations due to 
better renewable feed-in forecasts, demand changes or unexpected power plant outages. 
 
In summary, the limited prevalence of intraday markets in emerging electricity markets can 
be attributed to a combination of factors, including market maturity, grid infrastructure, 
market design and regulation, market participants and trading volumes, and market 
integration. These factors may vary depending on the specific characteristics of each 
emerging electricity market, and the development of intraday markets may require 
addressing these challenges and creating an enabling environment for their establishment. 
 
2.5. 
The Balancing Market 
In the early phase of the energy transition, variable RE were usually exempt from any form 
of centralized scheduling or dispatch by the system operator but were allowed to operate 
purely based on resource availability. But today, variable RE installations in most countries 
with liberalized markets are obliged to participate in the wholesale markets, like conventional 
generators. In addition, they are increasingly exposed to ‘balancing responsibility’, i.e., they 
are commercially responsible for any deviations (‘imbalances’) between the generation 
schedules or forecasts submitted by them and metered production, under consideration of 
any sales or purchases in the wholesale market [8]. Consequently, variable RE operators 
may be incentivized to improve the quality of their generation forecasts, engage in intraday 
trading and establish innovative ways to reduce variability. The choice of imbalance pricing 
rule applied in each country is a partly determined by market structure and other issues.  
2.6. 
Imbalance Penalties 
The amount of energy generated by a wind facility in any given time frame is variable as it is 
driven by the weather. Therefore, the amount of energy generated is not always exactly as 
the amount of energy scheduled during the same time and system operators must schedule 
generators to serve the load. The difference between the energy schedules and the actual

energy used or generated in an hour is referred to as the imbalance energy that needs to be 
managed by system operators. The energy imbalance tariff/penalty is charged to generators 
for deviating from their schedule and is designed to promote system reliability by 
encouraging better scheduling and by discouraging unfavourable operating practices such 
as intentional deviation from a schedule. Hiroux & Saguan (2010) [9] argues that, as any 
other player, WPPs should face the market risk. Otherwise, the risk will be assumed by the 
TSO. Rivier Abbad (2010) [10] agrees that allocation of balance responsibility to WPPs will 
have a positive impact. The allocation of balance responsibility to WPPs will lead to 
construction of better forecasting tools because WPPs will face the costs related to deviation 
from forecast. WPPs can designate those responsibilities to other companies that offer 
access to the market and forecasting services. 
The Council of European Energy Regulators (CEER) (2010) [11] also suggests that balance 
responsibility should provide the same incentives to wind generation as any other type of 
generation. Due to the intermittency of renewable energy generation as well as its increasing 
share in electricity systems, balancing responsibility is being assigned to all technologies in 
different markets as a way to incentivise generators to improve forecasting tools and reduce 
imbalances. The choice of imbalance pricing rule applied in each country is partly 
determined by market structure and other issues.

Electricity Market Overview  
 
2.7.1. South Africa Electricity Market Overview 
 
South Africa has a diverse mix of energy sources for electricity generation but relies heavily 
on coal for about 90% of the country’s energy needs. The current power utility Eskom 
operates as a monopoly in the sense that it controls the generation, transmission, and a 
large portion of the distribution of electricity in the country. Wind energy contributes about 
(3.4GW) 3% of the of the total electricity generated in the country and this is procured 
through PPAs between Eskom and IPPs which define the terms of electricity procurement 
including price and quantity. Concentration of the market around a few substations in the 
East and West coastal provinces of the country which are home to the strongest resource 
potential has created bottlenecks and slowed down wind power integration and this 
inadequacy in transmission infrastructure is expected to take years to be resolved. Despite 
plans to decommission old power plants and integrate more renewables, coal is expected to 
remain dominant in the energy mix up to 2030 and possibly beyond as the country as face 
rolling blackouts due to plant failures resulting in deficits in power generation. Eskom as a

2.7.2. India Electricity Market Overview 
 
Similar to South Africa, the electricity sector in India is dominated by state-owned utilities 
and relies on coal for roughly 51% of its electricity generation as of March 2023 and 56% of 
the country’s GHG emissions. The country currently meets about 38% of its electricity needs 
with renewable energy, with wind making up a share of 10.3%. [13]. High resource potential 
makes wind energy a key source for India’s power sector transition with targets to scale up 
renewable energy from 43.8.6 GW [12] in 2023 to 140 GW by 2030[13]. India currently 
operates a day-ahead as well as real time electricity market. On the day-ahead market, 
power is traded in 96 blocks of 15-minute durations each and bidding is done from 10:00 to 
12:00 hours on the day before dispatch. Market participants are allowed 16 revisions of the 
day-ahead schedule submitted every hour for 1.5 hours [14].  
 
2.7.3. Pakistan Electricity Market Overview 
 
Pakistan has a diverse energy mix with thermal generation, oil and gas taking up the 
majority share of the total energy generation in the country. The country has a high potential 
of generating power from hydro wind and solar, the share of renewables is currently still 
small, wind accounts for 4.8% of the installed capacity and solar around 1.4% [15]. The total 
share of renewables is expected to increase to about 50% by 2050 [16] as the country 
transitions to more environmentally friendly renewable sources to reduce the use of fossil 
fuels. The National Electric Power Regulatory Authority NEPRA is responsible for overall 
regulation of the power sector and established a market operator as an entity responsible for 
establishing and administering the wholesale market and among other responsibilities, the 
administration a payment system for the Imbalances of Market Participants and other market 
charges. Pakistan has a competitive bilateral trading market which enables bulk power 
consumers to purchase electricity directly from competitive the distribution companies or 
independent power producers [17].

Chapter 3 
 
3. Methodology 
The thesis, thus far, has commenced by giving a literatue review on electricity market 
operation, challenges faced by grid operators with the increasing share of renewables in 
electricity systems as well as the different market timelines that exist within each of the 
markets anlysed.The remainder of the report is encapsulated sequentially in the following 
chapters: The thesis will continue from the literature given above to present a review of the 
imbalance penalty shcemes in different markets in Chapter 5. To get an appreciation of the 
impact of the different forecasting regimes in the markets reviewed in chapter 5. In chapter 6 
the imbalance penalty formulations will be applied to five wind sites located in South Africa, 
Pakistan, Italy, South-Western USA and the Nord Pool trading zone DK1 and present the 
results and discussion. Based on the finding from chapter 6, recommendations and 
conclusions are made in chapter 7. 
3.1. 
Data collection, quality and assumptions 
 
The forecasting data used is based on the state-of-the-art forecasting tools available in each 
of the countries and represents their performance levels. In all the wind sites assessed, wind 
power plants are under balancing responsibility to the SO. The revenue impact will be 
quantified using the different imbalance schemes and compared based on forecasting 
accuracy and capacity factors.  
To simulate the 16 revisions allowed for in India, the data was converted from 1-hour to 15-
minute measurements though linear interpolation between 2 hours and the measurements 
persisted by 1.5 hour as predictions. The monthly average energy price from the Nord Pool 
has been used to calculate the revenue for power generated in all markets, this is to ensure 
that there is a fair comparison between the different markets. This is also taken as the PPA 
energy rate in South Africa, India and Pakistan to which the imbalance calculation is based 
on. 
The Nord Pool published imbalance prices for January 2023 have been used for DK1 
calculations as per their imbalance rules. The requirement for additional ancillary services

4.1. 
Imbalance Pricing Calculation in India 
The Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (CERC) introduced the provisions for 
forecasting of solar and wind power under the Indian Electricity Grid Code (IEGC) in May 
2010 to solve management issues emerging from intermittent solar and wind power. The 
CERC Deviation Settlement Mechanism (DSM) Regulations, defined a market mechanism 
for holding generation companies accountable for their power supply commitments, and 
implemented to maintain grid discipline and security, in line with the grid code. 
The CERC has introduced several amendments to these regulations and most recently the 
CERC has issued the DSM and Related Matters Regulations, 2022 which seek to ensure, 
through a commercial mechanism, adherence to the schedule of injection of electricity into 
the grid, taking care of the security and stability of the grid. These regulations apply to all 
grid-connected regional entities and other entities engaged in the interstate purchase and 
sale of electricity, where the state-level regulations do not exist [11].  
Penalties are calculated per unit (or kwh) in percent error, where percent is relative to the 
installed capacity of the plant. If the error reaches above a certain limit, then the generator is 
charged at a fixed defined rate for deviation settlement. The rules allow for 16 revisions to be 
made to the 24-hour generation forecast, this allows for more accurate forecasting closer to 
real time, so renewable projects revise the schedule up to 16 times a day subject to one 
revision for each time slot of 1.5 hours and the imbalance settlement calculation is then 
based on the revised forecasts. The objective of these regulations, which apply to all 
renewable projects, is to minimize scheduling deviations in order to maintain grid stability 
and security.

The Nordic Balancing Model is a unique system used by Nord Pool, the energy market 
operator in the Nordic electricity market to manage the balance between electricity supply 
and demand in real-time, ensuring the stability and reliability of the power system. The 
Nordic electricity market comprises five countries - Denmark, Finland, Norway, Sweden, and 
Estonia - and operates under a common set of rules and regulations. In the Nordic power 
market, all participants are held responsible for imbalances they cause in the system or 
contractually delegate such imbalance to a chosen representative (Balance Responsible 
Parties). All generation and loads are under financial balancing responsibility towards the 
TSO, including wind power generators. The same gate closure and rules apply to all 
generation plants regardless of technology and the same imbalance prices apply to wind 
power as other generators [Error! Reference source not found.]. In the past, the Nordic

7. Recommendations and Conclusions  
In this thesis, imbalance schemes implemented in various markets were studied and applied 
on five wind farms to determine the level of revenue loss that would be experienced by each 
generator and make recommendations for consideration in the South African imbalance 
penalty scheme .The wind energy market in South Africa is highly competitive on tariffs and 
the implementation of an imbalance penalty is an additional burden on developers as it 
affects the bidding tariff. While the rationale behind the imbalance regulations is understood, 
it is equally critical to take into consideration the market conditions and the implications 
thereof on future projects and the overall government policy targets to decarbonise the 
electricity sector to reduce emissions.  
7.1. 
No Penalty for over-injection 
For a country that has been battling with rolling blackouts due to generation deficits 
consistently since 2018, penalizing renewable energy generators for over-injection 
deviations from schedule is counterproductive and unfair as the power is still sold to the 
utility at the PPA tariff. The generators could minimise this risk through curtailment, however 
there are very few instances that such would be achieved at zero revenue loss to the wind 
farm under a “take-or-pay” PPA. This thesis recommends that no penalties be imposed for 
over-injection, while generators have the ability to curtail their generation output, doing so 
would come at significant revenue loss as no payment would be received for the energy 
deviation from

7.2. 
Allowing forecast revisions closer to delivery time 
The penalty bands proposed by Eskom are arbitrary bands, not linked to any benchmark 
study on the error range that is achievable in forecasting wind power in the country. The 
results shown in Figure 17  show no significant improvement in the accuracy and imbalance 
costs even with 6 -hourly revisions of the forecasts intra-day. Wind power generation 
forecasts provide better accuracy closer to real time as seen with the persistence forecast 
applied in Figure 15  and Figure 16. Allowing revisions to the day-ahead forecast as applied 
in India would improve the accuracy and reduce the cost borne by wind power plants as this 
would allow them to adjust their schedules as new information becomes available (improved 
weather forecast and incorporate of most recent actual measured data). The grid operator 
would also realize reliability and economic benefits in using forecasts closer to real time to 
avoid using expensive “hot start” thermal generation units and ancillary services. A shorter 
time-horizon would lower the overall operating costs for the grid operator.

7.3. 
Harmonisation of imbalance prices 
 
In any electricity market, the revenue is a function of the DA price and the imbalance price 
due to the difference between what was forecasted in the DA market and what was 
produced. The imbalance costs will always reduce the revenue proportionally due to forecast 
errors. In South Africa, the imbalance settlement is calculated at a different price for each 
generator as the formula uses the project’s PPA tariff, an example of this is that two wind 
farms with an equal imbalance of 10 MW and the same forecast variation ratio or error will 
incur different costs for the same imbalance volume. This introduces a level of unfairness 
amongst generators and raises questions regarding the true cost of balancing the grid. This 
is something that would not exist if the imbalance price was linked to the true cost of 
balancing the system which would translate to a single imbalance price for all generators. 
The Nordic single-price balancing model gives an example of this. The imbalance cost is 
linked to the direction of the imbalance and depending on their position, market players can 
either earn additional revenue for the excess power produced or incur a penalty depending 
on the direction of the overall system balance. This gives a much more realistic view of how 
and if the imbalance affected the overall power system in anyway. Imbalance price 
harmonisation should be one of the key considerations for assigning balancing responsibility 
to renewable power plants and this should reflect the cost of balancing and not on arbitrary 
penalties imposed by the transmission system operator.

Harmonised imbalance pricing will create a level of transparency and fairness across all 
generation technologies as well as market participants. Unbundling of the power utility plays 
a key role in achieving this, as it would come with an establishment of an Independent 
Transmission System and Market Operator which is decoupled from the generation owened 
by Eskom. This will in turn ensure that Eskom does not continue to implement rules that 
favor its own generation over private prower producers, a level playing field in the electricity 
trading market, increase competition and drive down the cost of electricity for consumers.  
 
7.4. 
Industry consultation 
Despite numerous iterations of the imbalance rules in India, CERC and the grid operator 
have made it a point to ensure the inclusion of renewable energy industry association in the 
implementation of imbalance rules and continuously monitors its effectiveness in achieving 
the intended objectives. This not only gives the market participants a platform to influence 
the market rules but also a platform to give insight into some of the challenges faced and 
provide recommendations. This is another key lesson that can be taken from the India 
approach, South Africa introduced imbalance rules to renewable energy PPAs for the first 
time in 2020 and no industry consultation process has been undertaken to date.  
7.5. 
The impact of the predictability and capacity factor on revenue 
 
From the results presented in Table 6 it can be observed that there is a relationship between 
the capacity factor of a wind farm and the imbalance cost. From this it can be concluded that 
to quantify the revenue impact due to imbalance cost, both the predictability and wind 
potential at the site need to be taken into consideration when making a decision to build a 
wind farm as greater errors lead to higher penalties. However, due to the fixed tariff structure 
in the South African market, predictability tends not to play a role in decision making, it is 
made solely on the site’s wind potential. The introduction of imbalance penalties challenges 
this approach and IPPs need to invest in prediction tools that can provide the best forecast 
accuracy prior to the construction and pre-operational phase of the wind farm to reduce the 
impact on revenue.
