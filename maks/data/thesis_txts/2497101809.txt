Summary
In this study frost resistance of concrete with high content of fly ash was investigated.
For years it has been a common understanding that for a concrete to resist frost attack
it must have sufficient strength and air void structure. However, recent studies indicated
that concrete with high content of fly ash performed worse than a concrete without fly
ash, given they had same strength and air void structure. For this study several concrete
mixes were made in order to test this.
At first it was investigated, how well a concrete with 50% fly ash and 50% Portland
cement and a concrete with 100% Portland cement performed in a freeze-thaw test. For
the freeze-thaw [DS/EN 12390-9, 2019] was used. The two types of concrete mixes had
varying air content but approximately same strength, the ones with fly ash a bit higher.
Using the [ASTM, 2016] the air void structures were analysed. Based on the 56 cycles
scaling measured from the freeze-thaw test and the spacing factor from the air void struc-
ture analysis a clear tendency was observed. The concrete with 50% fly ash and 50%
Portland cement did in fact scale more than the concrete with 100% Portland cement
even with a similar spacing factor. So even when the concrete with fly ash had more
strength and same air void structure it did worse in the freeze-thaw test. From this
it does seem evident that a concrete with high content of fly ash perform worse when
exposed to frost than a concrete with pure Portland cement.
It was also investigated if the interaction between concrete and de-icing chemicals could
be measured and observed. The hypothesis was that chloride interact with concrete
and change the surface permeability.
For this a chloride migration setup was used
[NT Build 492, 1999]. Eight difference concrete mixes were used for this. Four with
100% Portland cement and varying water-cement ratio and four with fly ash content of
10%, 20%, 30% and 50%. From the chloride migration test the resistance was measured
through the samples before and after the test. This revealed a change in resistance.
For the concrete with 100% Portland cement they all gained an increase - became less
permeable. For the concrete with varying fly ash content, a tendency of them gaining a
decrease in resistance - became more permeable. From this it does seem evident that a
concrete with fly ash becomes more permeable and possibly lose strength in the surface
when exposed to chloride.

I dette studie er betoners frostbestandighed ved højt flyveaske indhold undersøgt. I
mange år har det været en general opfattelse at betoner udelukkende skulle have en høj
styrke og tilstrækkelig luftpore struktur for at kunne modstå frost. Nyere studier har
dog vist tendenser, hvor betoner med højt flyveaske indhold har vist at klare sig dårlige
end en beton uden flyveaske, hvor begge havde sammenlignelig styrke og luftpore struk-
tur.
I dette studier er der derfor udført adskillige beton blanding for a undersøge dette. Til
at starte med blev det undersøgt, hvordan en beton med 50% flyveaske og 50% Port-
land Cement og en beton med 100% Portland cement klarede sig i en frost-tø test. Til
dette blev [DS/EN 12390-9, 2019] anvendt. De to typer af beton blandinger havde ib-
landet varierende luftindhold, men sammenlignelig styrke.
En anelse højere styrke i
dem med flyveaske. Til luftpore strukturen blev der anvendt [ASTM, 2016]. Ud fra
de målte afskalninger af beton efter 56 cyklusser fra frost-tå testen og afstandsfaktoren
from luftpore analysen viste der sig en klar tendens. Betonen med 50% flyveaske og 50%
Portland cement afskallede mere end betonen uden flyveaske, selv ved en sammenlignelig
afstandsfaktor. Så selvom betonen med flyveaske havde højere styrke end betonen uden
flyveaske, klarede den sig dårlige i frost-tø testen. Ud fra dette virker det evident, at
betoner med højt flyveaske indhold klarer sig dårligere når udsat from frost-tø end en
beton med 100% Portland cement.
Det er ligeledes undersøgt om interaktionen mellem beton og afisnings kemikalier kunne
måles og ses.
Hypotesen var at klorid interagere med beton og efterlader en per-
meable overflade.
Til at undersøge dette blev der anvendt en klorid migration test
[NT Build 492, 1999]. Otte forskellige beton blandinger blev der lavet. Fire med 100%
Portland cement og varierende vand-cement indhold og fire med flyveaske indhold på
10%, 20%, 30% og 50%. Ud fra klorid migrations testen blev modstanden igennem be-

100% Portland cement kunne måles en tilvækst i modstand – de blev mindre permeable.
For betoner med flyveaske viste sig en tendens af fald i modstand – de blev mere per-
meable. Ud fra disse resultater var det evident, at betoner med flyveaske bliver mere
permeable og muligvis mister styrke i overflade ved udsættelse af klorider.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the construction industry concrete is one of the most used materials. Its strength,
versatility, durability and low cost makes it material suitable for many types of structures.
Cement is a main component of concrete and therefor the production of cement has
gained a lot of attention the last couple of years. Since it is becoming more and more
evident that the global climate is changing and that for the worse, the world has started
to pay more and more attention to the energy consumption in every sector. As of 2018
a report suggested that the production of cement was to account for as much as up to
8% of the global CO2 emissions [Lehne and Preston, 2018]. In 2015, the world leaders
agreed to 17 goals to create a better world [UN, 2015], several of these goals involving
the climate. At least two of these are directly connected to the construction industry,
making it crucial that the industry must take its part in making the world a better place.
The goals directly related are #9 and #11, see Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: United Nations world goals #9 and #11 [UN, 2015].
The focus has lead to many different approaches as to how the construction sector can
reduce its total CO2 emission. One of them is by improving durability of constructions
where cement is used, i.e. make the concrete last longer.
For many years fly ash has been used to replace some of the cement in concrete. The
fly ash works as a supplementary cementitious material (SCM) which if not used, would
probably have to be landfilled. When used in concrete, the fly ash usually accounts
for approximately 15%-25% of the binder content [Thomas, 2007], but even higher
==PAGE:14==
2

amounts can be used. By replacing some of the cement with fly ash, the total CO2
emission is thus reduced. The use of fly ash has proven to both reduce the heat de-
velopment at early age [Mehta, 2004] and also reduce the chloride ingress in concrete
[Luping and Löfgren, 2016]. However, nothing comes for free. Recent studies conducted
at Technical University of Denmark (DTU) provides evidence that concrete containing
high amount of fly ash are less durable in some cases. Especially in relation to frost
resistance of concrete [Hasholt et al., 2019].
When exposed to de-icing chemicals concrete tends to scale.
The liquid penetrates
the surface and expands when freezing.
This is very unfortunate in cold climates
such as Denmark.
De-icing chemical is commonly used during winter and the rela-
tive low concentrations (around 3%) have shown to be damaging to the concrete sur-
face by studies [Verbeck and Klieger, 1957]. According to [Valenza and Scherer, 2007a]
two main parameters are used to design a concrete to resist frost scaling: strength
and air void structure.
Thus suggesting if a concrete has at least 40 MPa and an
air content of approximately 3%-7%, it has sufficient resistance to frost attack.
In
[Valenza and Scherer, 2007a] several studies has tested concrete with up to 58% fly
ash, has indicated that scaling is greatly reduced at a 28-day strength of 40 MPa
[Bilodeau and Malhotral, 1992]. However, the air void structure may have been incor-
rectly determined. As it was found by [Hasholt et al., 2019], concrete with high content
fly ash can distort the air void structure analysis, as some of the fly ash particles are
hollow. This can possibly lead to a false picture of the air void structure. Therefor the
concrete in [Bilodeau and Malhotral, 1992] may have been deemed adequate based on
false air void structures. Thus it is found relevant to compare two types of concrete with
varying air content. First type with 100% Portland cement and the second with 50%
Portland cement and 50% fly ash. If the two according to [Valenza and Scherer, 2007a]
have similar strength and a comparable air void structure, such as the spacing factor,
they should scale the same from a freeze-thaw test such as [DS/EN 12390-9, 2019].
In the same study by [Hasholt et al., 2019] it was concluded that the misleading air void
structure could not be the only reason as to why concrete with high amount of fly ash
suffers more from frost scaling. In the study it was found that for the concrete with
high amount of fly ash, the surface had deteriorated unexpectedly. Through thin section
analysis and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) the surface was seen not to suffer from
internal cracking as what is usually associated with frost damage [Bilodeau et al., 1994].
So if it was not the internal cracking that had caused the greater amount of scaling then
what had? As the SEM photo’s revealed a surface which appeared to have changed den-
sity/permeability, it is thus suggested by [Hasholt et al., 2019] that the greater amount
of scaling is caused by a combined action from frost attack and a chemical degradation.
Since the concrete during the freeze-thaw test had only been exposed to saline solutions
it is relevant to assume that the chemical degradation is due to the salt, NaCl. Thus
it is found relevant to test if concrete with high amount of fly ash suffer greater from a
chemical degradation due to NaCl, than a reference concrete with pure Portland cement.
This degradation can possibly be measured in an accelerated chloride migration test

2.1
Hydration of Portland cement
The first and probably most crucial part of a concrete life is the early phase where the
cement undergoes hydration. In this phase the cement reacts in contact with water
and forms the gel solid holding aggregates together in a stable matrix. The cement
itself, in this case Portland Cement, consist of 4 main clinker materials: tricalcium
silicate (Alite, C3S), dicalcium silicate (Belite, C2S), tricalcium aluminate (Celite, C3A)
and tetracalcium alumino ferrite (Ferrite, C4AF). The amount of each clinker minerals
varies from the type of cement and have an impact on the final strength and durability.

2.3
Fly ash
Fly ash is a pozzolan. Pozzolans can come from many different sources. Some of these
are granulated blast-furnace slag, vulcanic glass, calcined clay, fly ash or silica fume
[Mehto and Monteiro, 2006]. In Denmark the most commonly used is fly ash, as this is
bi-product available from the burning of coal.
Fly ash is a fine powder of mainly spherical glassy particles and contains high amounts
of SiO2 and Al2O3 [DS:450-1, 2012]. These are produced as a bi-product when burn-
ing coal at temperatures between 1200-1600 ◦C [Dansk Bentonforening, 2019].
Most
fly ash particles are solid spheres of glass, however, a small amount of fly ash does
as well appear as cenospheres (hollow) and plerospheres (multiple number of spheres)
[Mehto and Monteiro, 2006]. The fly ash particles are roughly the same size as cement
between 1 µm and 100 µm in diameter. In Denmark the fly ash is categorised based on
the content and quality according to [DS:450-1, 2012].

When concrete comes into contact with dissolved sodium chloride the concrete will start
absorbing this liquid. This takes place through two processes; capillary suction and
diffusion. This is commonly referred to as a transport of matter. First step is the cap-
illary rise which is the drive of a pressure gradient within the dry concrete pores and
the outside water sources. This process saturates the concrete. As the samples used in
this study are all pre-saturated, either naturally or by vacuum, the focus is not on this
mechanism in this study.

The chloride migration coefficient Dnssm
Usually the density and a measure of how difficult it is for the chloride to migrate through
a concrete, is measured as a coefficient of migration. This can be obtained through a
non-steady state chloride migration which is used in this study. By creating an electrical
potential in the axial direction on a concrete specimen, it forces the ions through the

In order to compare different concrete mixes, ideally they should all have reached the

comparing a reference Dnssm of the 28 days matured mixes may not have reached
the same degree of hydration, comparing a mix without fly ash to one mix with fly

2.6

Many studies have shown the phenomenon of concrete scaling when exposed to a freezing
media. However, the mechanisms behind are still very much discussed and no real agree-
ment seem to have been found [Valenza and Scherer, 2007b]. But what does seem to be
agreed on, is the fact that it does happen. In a study by [Verbeck and Klieger, 1957]
scaling was shown to be dependent on the concentration of the solution exposed to the
surface. In Figure 2.4 from [Verbeck and Klieger, 1957] different types of salts and their
concentrations were tested on concrete, from where the amount of scaled concrete was
measured.

When using air entraining agent (AEA) in concrete with high amounts of fly ash a com-
bination of admixtures tends to influence and reduce the effect of one another. Small
amounts of the fly ash consist of unburnt carbon, which can be absorb the AEA and make
it inactive [Pedersen et al., 2008]. This is described in the study by [Hasholt et al., 2019].
This can cause a problem as the fresh air content of concrete might show a sufficient
amount of air, but in fact at a later hydrated state, the air content in concrete is less
than required.
According to [Valenza and Scherer, 2007a] it is often the understanding that for a con-
crete to be frost resistant it most have a satisfactory air content and a strength of at
least 40-45 MPa. But if using a concrete with high content of fly ash, the ”satisfactory
air content” one might think they have, may not be present after placement.
Through air void analysis it has been found that fly ash can distort the air void analysis.
By [Hasholt et al., 2019] it was shown that the cenospheres in fly ash could be incorrectly
accounted for as air in the air void structure analysis. As the cenosphere entrapped in a
glass particle it provides no contribution to the frost resistance. However, when an air
void analysis is conducted the cenosphere might be cut open and appear to be hollow.
This can lead to it being mistaken for an air void. Thus the difference between actually
entrapped air and the cenosphere can be misleading.
2.7
Hypothesis
Hypothesis A
It seems that the common understanding for a concrete to be frost resistance is that it
must have an air content of 3%-7% and a strength above 40 MPa [Valenza and Scherer, 2007a].
This regardless of the fly ash content. But as shown by [Hasholt et al., 2019] the pres-
ence of fly ash can interfere with the air structure analysis and possibly show an incorrect
amount of air in concrete. The hypothesis is thus, that a concrete with high content of
fly ash scale more than a concrete without fly ash, given they have a similar air void

structure and strength.
In a recent study by [Hasholt et al., 2019] similar strength properties were determined
for concrete samples with and without high content fly ash. These experiences can be
replicated and mixed with different air contents.
This will be tested by the same freeze-thaw test [DS/EN 12390-9, 2019] to get the
amount of scaled material after 56 days and by air void analysis of the concrete samples
using [ASTM, 2016] to get the air content and spacing factor.
Hypothesis B
As it was observed and suggested by [Hasholt et al., 2019] the surface of the concrete
changed density and permeability in contact with a saline solution. The hypothesis is
thus, by exposing a concrete surface to a chloride migration it should change its density
and permeability, which will result in a change in resistance through the samples. By
making samples with varying fly ash content it should be evident as to what the amount
of fly ash does to the resistance. Likewise samples with pure Portland cement and vary-

As it was experienced through thin section analysis and SEM in the study by

chemical composition it should be possible to see and compare this change. It is common
practice to observe and estimate the strength of concrete through thin section.
By

in the permeability could give an indication as to how much strength it has lost.

3.3
Conclusion
Hypothesis A is that a concrete with high content of fly ash scale more than a concrete
without fly ash, given they have a similar air void structure and strength.
In the results and discussion a thorough air void structure analysis of the concrete sam-
ples were presented. These provided an air content and a spacing factor. All samples, ex-
cept PC50-FA50-0,0, with high content fly ash had an air content above 3%. This air void
structure was analysed using [ASTM, 2016]. All samples with high content of fly ash had
a compressive strength of at least 50 MPa. According to [Valenza and Scherer, 2007a]
the requirement for a concrete to be frost resistant was to have a min. 40 MPa strength
and 3% air content.
The scaling of the concrete samples were measured using the freeze-thaw standard
[DS/EN 12390-9, 2019]. The measurement of scaled concrete after 56 cycles were used.
The results presented showed evidence of concrete with high content of fly ash scaled
more than a concrete without fly ash. Samples had a similar air structure in terms of
spacing factor, but samples with fly ash also had a higher compressive strength, yet they
scale more.

4.3 Conclusion

4.3
Conclusion
The hypothesis is, that by exposing a concrete surface to a chloride migration it changes
its density and permeability, which results in a change in resistance through the concrete.
This was tested by an accelerated chloride migration [NT Build 492, 1999]. Samples
with pure Portland cement and varying water-cement ratios and samples with roughly
the same strength and varying fly ash content were tested. The samples with pure Port-
land cement all showed an increase in resistance after the migration. The samples with
varying fly ash content showed a tendency of a decrease in resistance change after mi-
gration. The sample with 50% fly ash showed a decrease in resistance of approximately
50%, all assumed to be in the migration zone.
It is thus evident that the concrete does in fact change its resistance during a chloride
migration and concrete with fly ash loose more with the increase in fly ash content. This
change is very likely related to the change in permeability, leaving the concrete with fly
ash more exposed to frost scaling. Thus it is safe to conclude that hypothesis B is vali-
dated.
It was also tested whether the change in resistance/permeability could be observed
visually. This was attempted through a thin section analysis to see if the chloride front
could be found, after concrete had been exposed to a chloride migration. This test did
not provide any clear evidence. The test was conducted on a digital microscope using
image analysis. This distorted the image quality, changing the light settings. Local
variation in the paste was observed. It was questionable whether these originated from
the chloride front or poor mixing of the concrete from the beginning.

Future work
After this study it could be found relevant to investigate the following work:
1. Finish the air void structure analysis for part A. 2/3 of the required area are still
missing to be analysed according to [ASTM, 2016].
2. Do a the same chloride migration test and analyse the ingress zone using SEM.
Perhaps the ingress zone would be clear.
3. A chloride migration test where chloride is migrated all the way through the con-
crete. Afterwards do a compression test. This could provide the actually strength
lose in the surface - if any strength is present.
4. Do the migration test with samples of varying maturity, and see what effect this
has on the resistance. As it is seen with the migration coefficient changes with
hydration, perhaps the same happens with the resistance.
5. Attempt the freeze-thaw scaling using a different method / standard.
