Effect of stimulus parameters in cortical measurements of spectral discrimination
Abstract
Spectral and temporal cues are fundamental to speech perception, yet their cortical encoding remains—in
some aspects—an open question, particularly in individuals with hearing impairment. This study investigates
the effects of stimulus parameters on cortical measures of spectral discrimination, focussing on the acoustic
change complex (ACC) as an objective measure of auditory processing. Using electroencephalography
(EEG), we measured ACC responses to different spectro­temporal stimuli, including a novel stimulus—
discrete spectro­temporal modulation (D­STM)—, in both normal­hearing (NH) and hearing­impaired (HI)
participants. We systematically manipulated alternation rates, amplitude roving and contrast levels (CLs) to
assess the robustness of cortical responses.
Our results reveal remarkable differences in response strength between NH and HI participants, with HI
individuals exhibiting significantly weaker ACC responses, particularly at lower CLs. D­STM stimuli elicited
stronger ACC responses than traditional spectro­temporal modulation (STM) stimuli, and responses declined
as CL decreased. Importantly, when controlling for loudness cues via amplitude roving, we found a signif­
icant relationship between cortical responses and behavioural spectro­temporal modulation discrimination
thresholds measured by the audible contrast threshold (ACT) scores. These findings show the potential of
the ACC as an objective proxy measure of speech processing in populations unable to perform behavioural
tasks.

Introduction
Spectral and temporal cues play a crucial role in speech understanding, as they allow individuals to detect
and process acoustic cues necessary for comprehending speech, especially in challenging listening environ­
ments. These cues help to ‘group’ different sound sources based on their spectral content, rhythm, common
onset, and pitch changes, allowing the brain to selectively attend to and understand speech (Bregman, 1990;
Rabinowitz et al., 2013).
However, hearing loss often leads to a reduction in the ability to resolve these cues. Specifically, individuals
with hearing impairment experience reduced spectral and temporal resolution, which affects their ability
to perceive and understand speech, especially in noisy or complex auditory environments. That reduced
sensitivity to spectro­temporal cues makes it difficult for individuals with hearing loss to follow conversations
and distinguish speech sounds from background noise (Mehraei et al., 2014).
Several studies have investigated how hearing loss affects the processing of these cues and, consequently,
speech intelligibility. Zaar et al. (2023) explored how sensitivity to spectro­temporal modulation (STM) can
predict speech reception thresholds (SRTs) in hearing­impaired (HI) listeners, showing that STM detec­
tion thresholds correlate with speech understanding in real­world listening conditions, such as speech in
noise. Their findings also highlighted the importance of using individualised audibility compensation, which
enhances the predictive accuracy of the STM test for individuals with moderate­to­severe hearing loss.
Sohier et al. (2021) measured sensitivity to detect changes in spectral patterns (sinusoidally modulated in the
frequency domain), i.e., spectral processing, using the so­called spectral ripple noise (SRN) in HI individuals.
They found that behavioural discrimination of SRN was associated with speech perception, particularly in
noisy environments, suggesting that SRN­based tests may offer an objective measure of auditory processing
abilities.
Furthermore, the relationship between the behavioural discrimination of SRN and an objective cortical re­
sponse evoked by changes in the SRN frequency ripples—the acoustic change complex (ACC)—was also
investigated. The ACC is a cortical response evoked by abrupt transitions imposed on an ongoing sound
and characterized be a positive peak (P1) at ≈50 ms, a negative peak (N1) at ≈100 ms, and a positive peak
(P2) at ≈200 ms (see figure Figure 3.1), the so­called P1­N1­P2 complex (Martin and Boothroyd, 2000).
Previous studies have demonstrated that ACC responses are sensitive to spectro­temporal modulations
and their characteristics can be used to evaluate auditory discrimination abilities (Undurraga et al., 2021).
Sohier et al. (2021) findings showed that ACC responses to SRN were significantly linked to speech per­
ception abilities, particularly in noisy environments, proposing the use of SRN­based ACC measurements
as an objective tool for assessing auditory discrimination in the hearing­impaired population. However, in
their study, the level of the amplified stimulus at the ear­drum was not accounted for, and the observed
relationship between speech perception and the ACC response was no longer significant after controlling
for audibility.
The development and optimisation of objective physiological measurements that can help to predict speech
in noise performance are of great interest, not only because they can be utilized to improve hearing aid
fitting, but also because they can be utilised with populations that can not perform behavioural tasks, e.g.,
infants (Martinez et al., 2013).
This study investigates the relationship between behavioural and physiological measurements of supra­
threshold auditory processing in adult human participants, aiming to develop an objective electrophysiolog­
ical measurement that could be used as a proxy to assess speech processing in infants and/or patients
unable to perform a behavioural test. The behavioural test consisted of the audible contrast threshold (ACT)
(Zaar et al., 2024). The ACT measures the ability to detect STMs like those found in speech signals, and
it has been shown to correspond well with speech­in­noise performance (Saoji et al., 2009; Meehan et al.,
2024). The electrophysiological objective measure was a cortical ACC response evoked by supra­threshold
3
==PAGE:9==
changes (spectral and spectro­temporal) imposed on a continuous ongoing stimulus (Han and Dimitrijevic,
2015; Calcus et al., 2022).
To ensure reliable cortical responses, we investigated several stimuli in normal­hearing (NH) participants
and selected those that could elicit a reliable ACC response. That because neural synchronisation—critical
to obtain a reliable ACC response—depends on the sharpness of the transition imposed on the ongoing
stimulus. We therefore hypothesised that stimuli like those used in the behavioural ACT will evoke weaker
ACC responses, due to the ‘slow’ and continuous spectro­temporal modulations that needs to be detected,
than stimuli with instantaneous transition across frequencies. Stimuli included the traditional STM (Zaar
et al., 2024) (like the one used in the behavioural ACT), the SRN (Sohier et al., 2021) and a novel varia­
tion of the STM, here referred to as the discrete spectro­temporal modulation (D­STM) stimulus. Because
supra­threshold changes applied to the ACC stimulus can introduce other unrelated cues (temporal and/or
loudness changes, for example) which can also elicit and/or contribute to the response of interest (Dimitrijevic
et al., 2009; Presacco and Middlebrooks, 2018), we investigated the extent to which intensity changes—
affecting loudness—elicited ACC responses and modified the stimulus by applying a second order speech­
like (≤4Hz) envelope—amplitude roving—aiming to ‘adapt’ the sensitivity of cortical neurons responding
to intensity changes. This idea is motivated by previous studies suggesting that increasing the sound­level
variance decreases the responsiveness of neurons activated by this cue (Dean et al., 2005; Kvale and
Schreiner, 2004). We therefore hypothesised that employing a speech­like amplitude roving—increasing
the intensity variance—on the entire stimulus will reduce the contribution of loudness changes in the ACC
response.
Once we selected the stimuli evoking reliable responses (D­STM), we measured ACC responses in NH and
HI participants using several degrees of ‘spectral contrast’—the difference in modulation depth between the
spectral patterns of the stimuli utilized to evoke the response—, from high contrast, i.e., 0 dB (full spectral
modulation, easy to discriminate) to low contrast, i.e., ­12 dB (mild spectral modulation, barely discriminate),
with and without amplitude roving. In both behavioural and physiological measurements, we ensured that
audibility was accounted for by ensuring a minimum audibility level of 15 dB within the bandwidth of the
stimuli (Humes, 2007; Zaar et al., 2024).
Our results revealed remarkable differences between NH and HI participants. Specifically, we observed dis­
tinct patterns in cortical ACC responses and behavioural ACT performance between the two groups. More,
the effects of spectral contrast level (CL) and amplitude roving varied across participant groups, suggesting
important differences in how NH and HI individual process spectro­temporal cues.

Methods
2.1
Participants
The study included 46 participants, 18 NH (aged 20­44) and 28 HI (aged 27 to 81), recruited from DTU and
Rigshospitalet (Science­Ethics Committee for the Capital Region of Denmark H­1­2013­138). All participants
provided their informed consent prior to participating in the study and were compensated for their time. For
the purpose of this study, inclusion criteria required participants to have either normal (thresholds below
20 dB hearing level (HL) between 250 Hz and 2 kHz—the bandwidth of the tested stimuli); Figure 2.1a&b) or
impaired hearing (Figure 2.1c&d), but limited up to 80 dB HL within the stimuli bandwidth) as determined by
pure­tone audiogram (PTA) thresholds. Within this bandwidth, NH PTA thresholds ranged from 0 to 15 dB
HL, while for the HI group, thresholds ranged from 20 to 80 dB HL.
All PTAs were performed in a sound­proof booth using a calibrated audiometer (Affinity Compact version 2,
Interacoustics, Denmark). Thresholds were determined using the Hughson­Westlake rule, according to ISO
8253­1:2010 International Organization for Standardization, 2010 (Figure 2.1). Participants were excluded
if they had a history of neurological disorders, excessive cerumen, or any other condition that could affect
auditory processing.
2.1.1
Audible Contrast Threshold (ACT)
Participants completed the ACT test to assess their sensitivity to spectro­temporal modulations similar to
those found in speech. In this test, participants were asked to detect—by pressing a response button—a
modulated target sound burst from unmodulated sound bursts. The modulated sound consists of 1­s pink
noise (354­2 kHz) with imposed spectro­temporal modulations (2 cycles per octave spectral ripple and 4 kHz
temporal modulation ( see fig.2 in Zaar et al. (2024) and Figure 2.2a), perceived as ‘siren­like’ sounds. The
unmodulated noise bursts share the same bandwidth and duration but lack of any spectro­temporal modu­
lation (Interacoustics, n.d. Zaar et al., 2023, 2024).
The degree of spectro­temporal modulation, expressed in dB normalized contrast level (dB nCL), was varied
adaptively according to a 2­down 1­up Hughson­Westlake rule with a 2­dB step size until participants could
no longer distinguish modulated from unmodulated sounds. An ACT threshold of 0 dB nCL represents a
good average performance for young NH listeners, whilst a threshold of 16 dB nCL—the maximum possi­
ble spectro­temporal modulation—represents poor performance. The ACT threshold was obtained for all
participants. Note that the ACT accounts for audibility by applying ear­specific amplification based on the
corresponding audiogram (Zaar et al., 2023, 2024).
2.2
Electrophysiological measurements
2.2.1
Stimuli
Cortical ACC responses were evoked by imposing periodic transitions at a rate of either 1 or 0.5 Hz (see
Experiment Design). In addition to the cortical ACC response, we evoked the auditory steady­state response
(ASSR) by applying an amplitude modulation of 42 Hz to all stimuli (Picton et al., 2003).
Three stimuli were used in the physiological experiments, the STM (Zaar et al., 2024), the SRN (Sohier
et al., 2021), and a novel variation D­STM.
The STM (Figure 2.2a) consisted of sinusoidal modulations applied to both the spectral and temporal di­
mensions of a broadband noise stimulus. These modulations created dynamic patterns that varied over
time and frequency, simulating key features of speech. The SRN (Figure 2.2b) is a noise stimulus charac­
terized by spectral peaks and valleys arranged in a periodic manner. Unlike STM, SRN does not contain
temporal modulations, being useful for isolating spectral processing abilities without influence from temporal
cues. The D­STM (Figure 2.2c) incorporates discrete changes in the spectral pattern, similar to SRN, but
employing four instead of one spectral pattern. That is, there are four spectral transitions before the pattern
repeats. Thus, it can be considered as a hybrid stimulus that blends characteristics of both STM and SRN.

All stimuli had a band width of 1768.2 Hz (337.1 to 2105.3 Hz), but spectro­temporal modulations were
confined within 354 and 2000 Hz. Frequency components outside this range remained unmodulated to
reduce potential edge cues at the higher and lower end of the spectrum.
The temporal modulation for the STM stimuli was 4 Hz and the spectral modulation 2 ripples/octave. The
SRN and D­STM consisted only of 2 ripples/octave spectral modulations.
The transitions employed to evoke the cortical response depended on the stimulus type. When using the
STM, the ongoing stimulus alternated between the STM and an identical stimulus but without the spectro­
temporal modulation, similar to the ACT test (Figure 2.2a) . With the SRN, the ongoing sound alternated
to a similar SRN but with the phase of the spectral modulator shifted by 180° (Figure 2.2b). Finally, for the
D­STM the phase of spectral modulation was shifted by 90°, relative to the phase of the previous transition.
Therefore, a full cycle occurred after four transitions (Figure 2.2c).
For all stimuli, spectro­temporal modulations were conveyed either using a noise carrier (NC) or a tonal car­
rier (TC) (Zaar et al., 2024). The NC consisted of 2500 sinusoidals with constant amplitude and a random
starting phase, with equidistant carrier frequencies on the logarithmic scale. The TC consisted of 17 sinu­
soidals with their squared amplitude—energy—following a 1/f power spectral density (PSD) and a random
starting phase, with equidistant carrier frequencies on the linear scale.

Pilot phase 2
In the second pilot phase, we investigated the effect of alternation rate, stimuli and carrier type, contrast (the
modulation depth of the spectral ripples), and intensity on the ACC. We obtained ACC responses from 12
NH listeners, two of whom participated in the first pilot phase. In this second phase, we only included SRN

0.5 Hz at 0 dB CL using TCs and NCs.
The alternation rate was investigated by measuring ACC responses to D­STM stimuli presented either at 1
or 0.5 Hz using a TC at 0 dB CL.

unmodulated, but otherwise were identical to the D­STM.

2.2.3
EEG recordings
Electroencephalography (EEG) measurements were carried out both at DTU and Rigshospitalet. Record­
ings were carried out inside an electromagnetically and acoustically shielded booth. Stimuli and trigger
signals were generated using an open­source matlab interface “AEP­GUI” and delivered using a digital
sound card (RME Fireface UC). The output of the soundcard was amplified (Phonitor mini ­ SPL Audio) and
the sounds delivered to the participants via insert phones (Etymotic ER3A) Stimuli were calibrated so that
the SPL was 65 dB SPL (verified with a 2­cc G.R.A.S. artificial ear) when neither roving nor compensation
was employed. However, the overall SPL differed for each HI participant once the audibility compensation
was applied. The overall SPL decreased by ≈5 dB SPL when the amplitude roving was employed.
EEG recordings were obtained from 64 + 2 surface electrodes using a BioSemi ActiveTwo (Amsterdam, The
Netherlands) system. Sixty­four electrodes were placed in accordance with the international 10­20 system
and two additional electrodes were placed on the left and right mastoid (TP9 and TP10). The voltage offset
was often kept below 20 mV, and never exceeded 40 mV.
Recordings were obtained at a sampling rate of 2048 Hz at 24 bits/sample (31 nV LSB). The cut­off frequency
of the internal low­pass filter was 512 Hz (5th order sinc response).
A total of 160 epochs were obtained per condition (≈5.3 minutes at 0.5 Hz alternation rate, and ≈2.6
minutes at 1 Hz alternation rate).
2.2.4
EEG processing
All EEG analyses were carried out using an open­source python package developed in Python 3 “pEEGy”.
First, recordings were referenced to the vertex electrode Cz and inverted in polarity. Next, we down­sampled
the data to 500 Hz and poor electrode contacts (typically 1 or 2 electrodes containing consistently large DC
components) were automatically detected and removed from the analysis. Data were then filtered between
2 and 200 Hz for ASSR analyses, and between 2 and 30 Hz for ACC analyses (FIR Kaiser filter 65 dB ripple
in band and stop pass regions, and 1 Hz transition between pass and stop band). Afterwards, data were
split into epochs and weighted averaged (Don and Elberling, 1994). ASSRs were analysed by taking the
amplitude of the 42­Hz bin in the frequency domain (fast Fourier transform).
ACC responses were characterised by estimating the amplitude—defined as the absolute difference be­
tween N1 and P2—as well as the F­value—a proxy to the signal­to­noise ratio (SNR)—within the analysis
encompassing 50 to 550 ms (500 ms length). Note that the F = SNR + 1, and therefore, an F­value of 1
indicates the presence of noise only (SNR = 0).
ASSRs were characterized by the amplitude of the 42­Hz frequency component and the SNR, estimated in
the frequency domain as the ratio between the energy of the frequency of interest and the average energy
of the neighbouring frequency bins (± 2 Hz).
2.3
Statistical Analysis
All statistical analyses were conducted using R software (version 4.4.2). linear mixed effect (LME) models
were used to examine the effects of stimulus parameters on ACC and ASSR responses, with subject ID set
as the random factor. Post­hoc pairwise comparisons were performed where applicable, with multiple com­
parison adjustments (false discovery rate correction). To examine the relationship between ACC responses
and behavioural ACT scores, robust regression was applied to minimise the influence of outliers. In this
study a significance level of α = 0.05 was used.

Effect of alternation rate
After demonstrating that discrete transitions (across the stimulus frequency range) resulted in significantly
larger ACC responses than those produced by the STM, we investigated the extent to which ACC responses
are affected by the rate at which these transitions are imposed. We measured ACC using the D­STM with
transitions presented at either 1 Hz or 0.5 Hz at 0 dB CL. A LME model with the F­value as the dependent
factor, rate and channel as independent factors, and participant as the random factor indicated that rate was

N1 latency was shorter (by ≈9 ms) for the higher 1 Hz alternating rate, suggesting that the reduction of the
response is driven by the adaptation of a later source contributing to the ACC.

3.4
Effect of intensity cues and roving
One of the main challenges in analysing ACC responses is to disentangle the response components driven
by spectral and temporal cues from those driven by loudness changes that could occur simultaneously. This
distinction is important because speech perception relies on detecting spectral and temporal modulations.
However, the ACC response is also sensitive to sudden intensity changes, which can elicit or contribute to
the response independently of spectro­temporal cues.
To address the extent to which intensity—and loudness—changes contribute to the ACC, we examined
responses by imposing a 5 dB SPL amplitude increase (65 to 70 dB SPL) using the D­STM presented at
0.5 Hz alternating rate and 0 dB CL (Figure 3.3). These were obtained with or without spectral changes.
In the presence of intensity changes only—i.e., no spectral changes—the results demonstrated significant
ACC responses (Figure 3.3, only intensity condition), confirming that intensity changes, alone, can lead to
large ACC responses, and potentially bias the interpretation of responses to other cues. That is, responses
observed under certain conditions might not reflect true processing of a given cue, but instead a general
sensitivity to intensity changes.
Having confirmed that intensity cues evoked large ACC, we evaluated whether this could be reduced by
inducing neural adaptation to intensity changes. To do this, we introduced a speech­like amplitude roving to
the same stimulus and measured the ACC (Figure 3.3, third column). In line with our hypothesis, the results
showed that amplitude roving effectively reduced the ACC response to intensity changes that were previ­
ously dominated by this cue. Importantly, when both spectral and intensity were simultaneously presented,
ACC responses remained almost unaffected in the presence of amplitude roving (cf. first and last columns
in Figure 3.3).

3.5
Effect of spectral contrast
As spectro­temporal sensitivity is critical for speech perception and, in fact, it is what the ACT score repre­
sents, we investigated whether spectral sensitivity would also be reflected on the ACC response by adjusting
the CL in the stimulus. For the D­STM, the spectral contrast represents the depth of modulation in the fre­
quency domain, with higher CLs providing more salient spectral cues, i.e., easier to discriminate, than lower
CLs. To investigate this, we compared ACC responses to the D­STM using both 0 dB CL (full modulated)
and −6 dB CL, and employing both the NC and the TC.
The results (Figure 3.5) showed that reducing spectral contrast weakened the ACC responses. This effect
was more pronounced with the NC, where ACC responses were barely observed at −6 dB CL, whereas
responses remain detectable, but reduced, with the TC. These observations were confirmed by a LME
model with the F­values as the dependent factor, contrast (0 dB CL and −6 dB CL), carrier (TC and NC),
and channel as independent factors, and participant as the random factor. Here, the LME model indicated
that both carrier [F(1, 78.6) = 117.3, p < 0.001] and contrast [F(1, 78.6) = 6.3, p = 0.014] were significant
factors (see Figure 3.6a&b).
Similarly, the LME model applied to the latency, indicated that only the carrier [F(1, 55.5) = 33.7, p < 0.001]
was a significant factor. Here, the N1 latency was shorter for the TC than the NC (by ≈20 ms). Overall,
these results confirmed that ACC responses are sensitive to CLs.

also to make the stimulus more speech­like and closer to the ACT stimulus, without compromising neural
representation.
The results (Figure 3.7) revealed that ACC responses in the HI group are considerably weaker than those
in the NH group, and those decreased as the CL was reduced, for both groups but more pronounced in the

Discussion
This study demonstrated that D­STM elicited significantly stronger ACC responses than STM, indicating
greater cortical sensitivity to abrupt spectral changes. We found that slower alternation rates (0.5 Hz) pro­
duced larger responses than at a higher rate (1 Hz) and that, in agreement with the ability to reflect various
degrees of discrimination, the ACC response decreased with CL.
When intensity changes were presented in the absence of spectral cues, we found strong ACC responses,
which could mislead the interpretation of the ACC response. However, we demonstrated that the response
to this undesired cue was largely reduced by employing speech­like amplitude roving, without compromising
the response to the feature of interest—the spectral cue.
Employing our EEG paradigm in HI participants we found significantly weaker ACC responses than in NH
participants, especially at lower CLs. Furthermore, the strength of the ACC responses correlated with their
ability to discriminate spectro­temporal modulations, as measured with ACT, but only when amplitude roving
was employed, highlighting the need for controlling loudness cues. Despite cortical differences, midbrain
ASSR responses were well represented in all conditions, confirming that the stimuli were audible for all
participants.
4.1
Cortical representation of spectral and temporal cues
One of the key findings of this study was that D­STM stimuli evoked stronger ACC responses than the STM
stimuli Figure 3.2, suggesting that cortical responses are particularly sensitive to abrupt spectral transitions.
This is in line with previous work by Sohier et al., 2021, which demonstrated that ACC responses to SRN
were significantly correlated with speech perception performance, especially in noise. Because D­STM has
discrete spectral changes similar to those in SRN, we also expected that these transitions will evoke stronger
cortical activity compared to the smoother STM modulations.
Mehraei et al., 2014 have shown that spectro­temporal processing is critical for speech perception, particu­
larly in challenging listening environments. Because D­STM incorporates speech­like amplitude fluctuations,
it better captures key characteristics of natural speech. Importantly, these fluctuations had minimal impact
on neural response strength, which suggests that D­STM can serve as a more reliable proxy for speech
discrimination, independent of response magnitude.
In terms of temporal processing, our results demonstrate that ACC responses were significantly larger at an
alternation rate of 0.5 Hz compared to 1 Hz (Figure A.1). This is likely because slower transition rates allow
for a more stable neural representation before the adaptation effects become evident at higher alternation
rates reducing the response strength. Willmore and King, 2023 describe how neuronal adaptation adjusts
response sensitivity based on stimulus statistics, leading to reduced cortical responses to repeated acoustic
events. Our findings suggest that lower alternation rates mitigate adaptation effects and preserve the neural
response to spectral transitions.
4.2
Effects of intensity cues and the role of roving
One of the challenges in interpreting ACC responses is the potential confounding effects of sudden changes
in SPL that can evoke cortical activity, independently of the experimental variable (Figure 3.3). As shown
in section 3.4, our findings confirm that intensity changes alone were sufficient to elicit significant ACC
responses (Figure 3.4), demonstrating that loudness cues can strongly influence cortical responses.
Dimitrijevic et al., 2009; Martin and Boothroyd, 2000 demonstrated that N1 amplitudes systematically in­
crease with intensity, regardless of tonal frequency. This supports our observation that amplitude­driven
ACC responses can be large and may overshadow spectral contributions or lead to the wrong conclusion
that an individual can discriminate spectral changes while the response is in reality driven by a change in
loudness.
23
==PAGE:29==
Using the principle known as ‘adaptation to the variance´ (Willmore and King, 2023), in which the cortical
sensitivity to intensity decreases as its variance increases—here, implemented by means of speech­like am­
plitude roving—we demonstrated that ACC responses to intensity changes were reduced to almost chance
levels, while the cortical response spectral transition remained well represented. Thus, the interpretation of
the response to spectral changes is more accurate.
4.3
Effect of absolute loudness on the ACC
Aside of the ‘in between alternation’ intensity changes, another potential concern that may arise when in­
terpreting ACC responses is the absolute loudness of the stimulus, which is likely more variable in HI than
NH individuals. Since HI individuals have higher thresholds, reduced dynamic range, and lower tolerance to
loud sounds, it may be that their cortical responses were weaker due to a lower loudness rather than deficits
in spectro­temporal processing.
To address this potential confounding factor, we analysed the ACC responses in NH participants at 50 and
65 dB SPL (at 0 dB CL), with and without roving (Figure A.4). There were no intensity changes between
alternations.
We found no significant effect of presentation level or roving on ACC responses, as neither F­values nor
the latency were affected. We only observed a small but significant effect on amplitude [F(1, 65.38) = 5.4,
p = 0.023], in which amplitudes at 50 dB SPL were about ≈9% smaller than at 65 dB SPL. However, when
examining ASSR responses (Figure A.7), statistical analysis revealed a significant effect of both roving and
absolute loudness on ASSR amplitude and F­value. These results show that while absolute loudness does
not significantly affect ACC responses, it does reduce ASSR amplitude and F­value.
Furthermore, these additional findings argue against the possibility that lower stimulus loudness in the HI
group was the main factor influencing their reduced cortical responses. Instead, our results indicate that
as long as stimuli are audible, cortical responses should be predominantly driven by suprathreshold feature
changes rather than absolute level differences per se.
4.4
Reduced cortical sensitivity in hearing­impaired participants
Our results demonstrate that HI participants exhibited significantly reduced ACC responses compared to
NH participants, particularly at lower CLs (Figure 3.9). This indicates that individuals with hearing loss have
a reduced ability to encode spectro­temporal changes at the cortical level, especially when spectral contrast
is diminished. The findings align with previous research showing that HI individuals experience degraded
spectral resolution (Mehraei et al., 2014) leading to reduced neural encoding of fine spectral features.
The attenuated cortical responses in the HI group could be attributed to broader auditory filters, as a conse­
quence of sensorineural hearing loss. Broader tuning reduces the ability to distinguish fine spectral details,
which makes it more difficult to track spectral transitions, especially when the contrast is low.
Interestingly, the contrast­dependent decline in ACC responses was more evident in NH, than in HI group.
While NH showed higher cortical responses than HI participants across all contrast levels, their responses
at −12 dB CL were notably reduced. In fact, the decline in cortical responses was steeper in NH than in
HI (Figure 3.9), suggesting that NH have a greater neural dynamic range for encoding spectral contrast,
whereas HI listeners show a more compressed range. It indicates that hearing loss not only affects the
strength of the neural response, but also the ability to differentiate spectral cues—important for speech
perception—especially in complex auditory environments.

When amplitude roving was not applied we did not find a significant relationship between ACC amplitude and
ACT scores or between F­values and ACT scores (Figure 3.10). However, when roving was employed a sig­
nificant negative relationship was observed, where participants with lower ACT scores (better performance)
exhibited stronger ACC responses for both amplitude and F­value. When this analysis was extended to
include both NH and HI participants we again found a significant relationship between ACC amplitudes and
ACT scores and ACC F­values and ACT scores, respectively (Figure A.3), in agreement with Sohier et al.,
2021. This was expected as NH showed larger ACC responses and lower ACT scores, emphasizing the
link between the cortical and behavioural measures of spectral processing.
Beyond examining the relationship between ACC responses at 0 dB CL and ACT scores, we also explored
alternative cortical measures to determine their potential as objective markers of spectral discrimination.
Specifically, we assessed whether the difference between ACC responses at different CLs (∆ACCCL; e.g.
0 dB CL vs. −12 dB CL Figure A.5), could provide an objective marker of speech discrimination abilities in HI
individuals. This analysis also indicated a significant relationship between ∆ACCCL values and ACT scores
under the roved condition, and suggests that larger cortical differences across CLs—i.e., larger contrast
dynamic range—are associated with better behavioural spectro­temporal discrimination.
To further look into the relationship between cortical and behavioural measures, we also computed the ACC
thresholds for each participant (Figure A.6). The ACC threshold was defined as the lowest CL at which a
significant response was detected. If no significant response was found, the maximum CL (0 dB CL) was
assigned as the threshold, providing an objective estimate of an individual’s spectral discrimination ability.
However, unlike ACC amplitudes/F­values at 0 dB CL or the ∆ACCCL values, ACC thresholds did not corre­
late with behavioural performance in either condition (with vs. without roving). This implies that the threshold
at which a significant cortical response emerges is not a reliable predictor for one’s auditory discrimination
ability. Instead, measures that capture the strength of neural responses across CL, rather than the absolute
threshold based on binary detection (presence or absence) appear to be more closely linked to behavioural
spectro­temporal performance.
4.6
Clinical implications
Based on all the findings of this study, an objective test for assessing spectro­temporal processing could be
based on ACC responses obtained at a single contrast level (e.g. 0 dB CL). The strength of the response
(amplitude or F­value) could be then mapped to the corresponding ACT score. This approach could serve
as an objective alternative for populations that cannot perform the ACT test, such as infants. By using
a predefined function that related the ACC strength to ACT scores, clinicians could estimate behavioural
discrimination ability based only on EEG responses as a tool to diagnose suprathreshold discrimination
problems and to confirm whether the fitting of a hearing helps to improve access to speech information
without active participation.

Conclusion
This study investigated cortical representation of spectral discrimination and its relationship to behavioural
sensitivity in NH and HI individuals. Using the ACC as an objective measure, we examined the effects of
stimulus type, carrier, alternation rate, spectral CL and amplitude roving on cortical responses. Our findings
provide important information into neural encoding of spectro­temporal modulations and its implications for
speech perception.
First, we demonstrated that D­STM stimuli elicited stronger ACC responses than the traditional STM stimuli,
showing that abrupt spectral transitions result in a more robust cortical representation.
Second, our results confirmed that cortical responses to spectral contrast were significantly influenced by
carrier type and intensity cues. While TC consistently yielded stronger ACC responses, potentially being a
confounding variable when interpreting spectral discrimination measurements. However, applying speech­
like amplitude roving effectively reduced the influence of loudness cues while preserving the sensitivity to
spectral modulations.
Third, we found that ACC responses in HI participants were significantly weaker than those in NH, especially
at lower CL. This implies that hearing loss is associated with reduced cortical sensitivity to fine spectral dif­
ferences, which can reflect difficulties in speech perception. Importantly, the strength of the ACC response
was significantly correlated with behavioural ACT scores, but only when amplitude roving was applied, re­
inforcing the need of controlling for loudness cues in electrophysiological measurements.
Finally, our results highlight the potential of ACC measurements as an objective tool to assess spectro­
temporal discrimination in individuals who cannot perform behavioural tasks, such as infants or individuals
with cognitive impairments. By mapping ACC response strength to ACT scores, electrophysiological mea­
sures could serve as a clinical alternative for evaluating suprathreshold auditory processing.
In conclusion, this study provides strong evidence that ACC responses reflect individual differences in spec­
tral discrimination, particularly when loudness cues are accounted. The findings presented contribute to our
understanding of auditory precessing and show their potential for ACC measurements as a tool for assessing
speech processing.
