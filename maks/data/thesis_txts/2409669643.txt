Introduction 
 
Currently, Ireland has a modernizing view to implementing best state in practice in waste 
management, which is seeing more domestic treatments of waste with incineration, as well as 
more diversion of residual waste derived fuel (RDF) type wastes to heat processes such as 
cement production.  While being an island nation without neighbors, it may be seen as having 
not adopting waste incineration as much as, say for example a similar population-size country 
such as Denmark. In Denmark, as much as 40% of all waste is incinerated (Affaldsdatasystemet 
2015) while in Ireland only approximately 5% is incinerated domestically (Irish Environmental 
Protection Agency 2016). To reach EU recovery rates and avoid landfilling wastes are being 
exported to be treated abroad. Special wastes such as Industrial and other hazardous waste are to 
a larger degree than municipal solid waste (MSW) being exported, about 50% (Irish 
Environmental Protection Agency 2016). These are being treated, with recovery, e.g. recycling 
and incineration.  There is currently one operational commercial MSW incinerator in County 
Meath1, and as well there are a number of cement plants currently using MSW as a fuel.  In the 
South Western Planning Region (SR - one of Ireland’s three waste planning districts, comprising 
10 separate local authorities) a third plant as well as a partly purpose built landfill for receiving 
bottom ashes, have not met the planning restrictions, and it is not likely it will be open any time 
soon. The future of whether or not an incineration plant will be built in the waste planning region 
in IE which sees nearly 40% of the nation’s waste volume, the SR is in doubt (Irish 
Environmental Protection Agency 2012). In the light of the similarities in populations of Ireland 
and Denmark, and similarities with other EU sized states (e.g. Switzerland which has high waste 
to energy (Wte) also), why is not this waste being treated with waste incineration plants (WIP) 
and being exported? The answers are obviously very large differences in the way that Irish 
planners as well as the public view the pristineness of their local environments. Or is there a lack 
of clear scientific consensus operating in the country surrounding the use of this technology?  
For my thesis it was decided that it would be interesting to use specifically “Waste LCA 
Software” to calculate the impacts of treating Municipal Residual Waste (MRW) generated in 
the Southern Waste Planning Region, with Wte in the region, and the rest to other Wte and

cocombustion in elsewhere in Ireland (IE). The idea is to see the impacts of “domestic treatment” 
rather than “exports”, and so landfilling is also included.  
Apparently there is a national will especially amongst those who are working in waste, to 
see the go-ahead of Wte in IE. The trick is, making it as free from “impacts” as possible. The aim 
of “Life Cycle Assessment” is to gauge what the overall balance of these impacts are, and where 
the hot spots exist, and to offer some solutions based upon this.  
As well, with larger Wte capacity there is the requirement for an ongoing supply of 
waste. Having a large fleet of Wte in Europe which is over-sized (or in Ireland), is contrary to 
EU policy (European Commission 2017).  EU goals in fact are moving the opposite way to this 
requirement in that the goal is to remove waste, and to improve circularity. Therefore another 
purpose in this report was to estimate the “balance of waste volumes for fuel versus Wte 
Capacity” in IE as increasing EU recycling goals are reached, up to 2030.  
 
Goal 
 
The goal of this report was to create a model which represents a larger commitment to 
WtE in Ireland, which up till now has been exporting high volumes of waste overseas to 
numerous countries in the EU such as Holland and Estonia, including Denmark. The aim was to 
calculate the impacts from treating the wastes regionally on the basis that the wastes are no 
longer shipped overseas.

Aims 
 
The report set out to achieve a number of aims: 
 
1.  The overall concept in this paper has been termed as a “Planning Environmental LCA”. 
It uses conventional desktop and research modelling, along with the steps of LCA such as 
data collection (and the mainly familiar steps of undertaking an LCA) to reveal the 
impacts of higher Wte in Ireland. 
 
2. Recycling rates were studied as they impact the quantities of waste, and the rates were 
applied to look into whether Ireland has correctly sized the planning of WtE and 
cocombustion, and by performing a forecasting for MSW volumes up to 2030.  
 
3. Considering the “availability” of regionally representative data, both for the desktop 
modelling to investigate the availability of current waste data, e.g. export volumes, 
originating region of the waste, and more technically, the Irish Life Cycle Processes Data 
availability and level of detail. Using the above to build as much resolution into the 
model as possible. 
 
4. An aim was to forecast the impacts of a potentially less polluting Waste Management 
System (WMS) in the future, with technology such as utilization of heat from Wte in 
Ireland (i.e. in Denmark) since it has not been utilized at present, as well as increases in 
technology which allow for more use of bottom ashes as a road aggregate and therefore 
increase the circular economy.

Intended applications 
 
The intended application is to develop an “Environmental footprint”, such as “CO2 
footprint” or similar indicator from using both incineration and Bottom Ashes (BA) landfilling. It 
could therefore shed some light on the potential problems with treating the waste regionally, and 
assist planners and policy makers.  
Limitations due to choice of method, assumptions, impacts 
coverage  
 
This design concept in the study is “forecasting” and thus is based upon assumptions. A 
main one is for the general view it creates in the Wte “fleet” in IE. This is especially the case for 
planning of where to site Wte plants. This is squarely a job for local planning departments, such 
as the Irish planning agency - An Bord Pleanala, and the Irish Environmental Protection Agency 
(EPA), and involves Environment Impact Assessments and other “on the foot of the site” 
instruments (e.g. stakeholder participation, consensus). A limitation is it cannot claim to be 
“hard” decision tool for whether or not to locate a Wte plant in the Southern Region. It can on 
the other hand compare country-wide, EU, and Global impacts coverage, for example, if there is 
less coal burning impacts from avoided electricity in IE due to the added electricity generation 
from the Wte versus the impacts avoided in shipping waste to DK.  
Decision-context and reasons for carrying out the study  
 
The reason for the study is to generate a set of Life Cycle Impacts for a waste 
management system in Ireland and the SR, which treats higher amounts of waste with Wte. The 
impacts calculated are intended to be a further  guidance for what could need to be implemented 
if there is future changes to current policies, for example if there is no allowances for exports, 
and it should be a nation’s own responsibility, per say, to treat its wastes. To incinerate all of the 
post-consumer and other wastes in a WtE plant provides more electricity and potentially heat for 
industry and consumers, and therefore this would lead to the potential avoidance of primary 
produced electricity. This has large-scale consequences on energy supply in the region with some 
==PAGE:12==
5 
 
avoided production and savings in associated emissions from burning of natural gas, coal, oil, 
peat, etc. LCA looks to the International Reference Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) for showing 
a decision context (European Commission JRC Institute for Environment and Sustainability 
2010). In terms of the ILCD therefore it constitutes a decision context of “Situation B -
meso/macro-level decision support, especially for technology scenarios”. However, the avoided 
energy is minor in comparison to the requirement for domestic energy, since the amounts do not 
influence the amounts of fuels necessary to meet energy supplied. So, if it is likely that a 
decision will not be taken on the basis of the results, i.e. it is not interacting with the electricity 
production system, it will be decision context - C1. Therefore the results of the study will more 
than likely indicate that there is less impacts with treatment of wastes outside of the country. 
Thus a decision will not be taken to reverse the transportations of waste. However, recent papers 
published by the Irish EPA stress the importance of improving domestic handling of wastes 
(Irish Environmental Protection Agency 2016). This is partly driven by non-environmental 
related factors such as having long term control upon waste management, and having a long term 
solution which is not subject to any changes in the way waste moves across boundaries. If so, 
then therefore this will more likely lead to a decision to be taken to put up with the extra burdens 
of treating the wastes domestically.  
Recently,  the EU communicated that in justified cases, the cross-border shipments of waste 
could help to make optimal use of the waste-to-energy capacity already available in a number of 
Member States (European Commission 2017). Exporting non-recyclable waste for energy 
recovery to another Member State should not necessarily be seen as contradicting the so-called 
principle of proximity (i.e. using the nearest appropriate Wte plant). However, it further notes, 
that before opting for such approach competent authorities in the Member States should carry out 
a life-cycle analysis to ensure that the overall environmental impacts, including those related to 
the transport of waste, do not offset the sought benefits. In the light of such news, a decision may 
be taken on the basis of similar results as this.

Target Audience  
 
The target audience is those with whom the burden is to make the best overall decisions 
and design WMS which have the lowest environmental burden. This is seen as the job the WM 
industry, the Irish Environmental Agency, An Bord Pleanala, the SR, other planning regions, the 
Waste to Energy companies, and importantly the educators – society, schools and universities.  
Commissioner of the study and other influential actors 
 
There was no commissioner however since the study is performed using LCA software 
(EASETECH developed at The Technical University of Denmark (DTU), along with imported 
processes from other LCA software), it could be said the designers are influential actors, and it is 
the hope it can lead to improvements in the further design of the software and processes. 
Scope  
Types of deliverables 
 
This study comprises an LCA, and involved Data Collection, and was modelled on 
EASETECH software to generate the total flows of substances and to build the Life Cycle 
Inventory (LCI), a Life Cycle Inventory Assessment (LCIA), and interpretation of the results. 
Desktop waste characterization and recycling goals modelling was done for all of the waste 
regions in Ireland using the best available and confirmed reported data and spreadsheets 
developed to model the growth and recycling effects, using predictions.

Model Descriptions 
 
Scenario 1 
 
Year 2015 was designed as the first scenario (S1). It represents a legacy situation as the 
first Wte plant had been built in 2012, cocombustion plants were burning a fraction of the RDF, 
there was high traditional landfilling, and there was also high exports of waste. This scenario 
sees 3 directions for waste from the Southern Region: Landfilling from SR (in SR and Rest of 
Ireland (ROI)), WtE in Ireland, and Exports to Denmark. Steel and aluminium are sorted from 
the bottom ashes and recycled. Fly ashes are being shipped to Germany for treatment. Tables the 
flow fractions for scenario 1 are described more clearly in the Methodology Section in the LCI 
(Table 7). 
 
Scenario 2 
 
Year 2020 was designed to be the second scenario (S2).  The new Wte in Dublin of 
600000 T per annum has been built (2017), and there is cocombustion with a fraction of the 
RDF. Added to the Scenario 2 model, is a 200000 Tonne per annum Wte plant in the Southern 
Region. This scenario has 3 directions for the waste - Wte in SR, Wte in IE, and exports to 
Denmark. Landfill is now is modelled as a MSW incineration bottom ashes landfill. There is 
utilization of electricity being produced from the Wte plants, however no utilization for heat, 
(though, the RDF fraction is providing heat in the Cocombustion plants). There is 50% 
utilization of the bottom ashes as these can be used as a road construction aggregate (as is done 
in Denmark2). Steel and aluminium are sorted from the bottom ashes and recycled. Fly ashes are 
being shipped to Germany for treatment.  Tables of the flow fractions for scenario 2 are provided 
in the Methodology (Table 8).  
 
Scenario 3 
 
Year 2030 was designed as the third scenario. There were no changes in Wte capacity in 
IE from the Scenario 2 - 2020, however, there are now zero exports, and heat from Wte plants is 
being utilized as district heating.  Instead of 50%, there is now of 100% utilization of bottom

Functional Unit 
The functional unit used in modelling is:  
Functional unit 
Treatment of one year’s Household and Commercial MRW arising in the 
Southern Waste Planning Region of Ireland.  
Reference flow 
All inputs to fulfill the functional unit. 
 
The waste is the municipal Residual waste which arises within the South West Planning 
region. The reference flow refers to all the processes, materials, fuels, energy, and so forth 
needed to fulfill the functional unit.  
 
LCI modelling framework and handling of multifunctional 
processes 
 
Consequential LCI modelling was used to evaluate co-products from the waste-to-energy 
treatment such as energy, and metals. In order to evaluate shortfalls of waste from changing 
waste volumes in the future, the system was expanded to include impacts from coal burning in 
cement kilns. Consequential LCI and system expansion are recommended by the ILCD 
guidelines on LCI modelling choices for decision context - B (European Commission JRC 
Institute for Environment and Sustainability 2010).

Completeness requirements 
 
Within the boundary, it didn’t consider the full amount of waste originating from the SR. 
At the beginning, it was important to decide “which” categories of waste could be included to be 
studied. Numerous categories of regular waste are treated in WIPs: industrial wastes, such as 
pharmaceutical, and other manufacturing wastes, also sludge from Waste Water Treatment 
Plants (WWTP), along with numerous hazardous waste types. Inclusion of these wastes would in 
fact have over doubled the volumes of waste generated in the region in 2012-2014. It would 
reasonably mean higher amounts to Wte.  However, the composition of the waste was not easy to 
recognize from local authority data, and thus not easily modelled via the software 
“EASETECH”. In a longer study (included in further iterations) it should be possible to model 
any “novel flows” down to the specific industrial wastes coming out of  a specific region.  As it 
stands,  25% of Irish Wte was “Industrial” wastes (in 2015 and so is  probably far higher at 
present (Indaver Ireland Limited 2015)), and so it should be possible to model it in further 
==PAGE:20==
13 
 
studies. The decision was made here to limit the waste to the household and commercial waste 
that could recognized in higher detail down to sub-fractions. Also, for the following two reasons: 
1. The waste types were on the National Transfrontier Shipping Office (NTFSO) waste for 
exports shipping lists as the classification for “waste to energy” D5, and could be 
accounted for with accuracy, to best model exports specifically to Wte. 
2. Not all the impacts of applying the higher recycling rates could be accounted for as 
numerous “specific” recycling targets are in place too, such as for farm plastics, WEEE, 
and chemicals, in Ireland and in the EU.  It was necessary to match as accurately as 
possible the best data available on waste, with targets to accurately forecast the change to 
the volume of waste. 
 
Importantly to highlight in completeness, is the study doesn’t factor the virgin materials avoided 
with having a higher recycling rate, and greater circularity of materials generally from less Wte. 
Reuse in highest on the waste pyramid, along with “avoid”.  
 
Representativeness of LCI data  
 
One of the aims in the study was to make the study as representative of actual conditions 
as was possible through using representative data. Issues with this, is technological and temporal 
representativeness of the corresponding data, and whether or not technology has moved on since 
the data was produced. This was particularly so when modelling for IE since it is not as well 
covered, as other places e.g. Denmark. In saying, the majority of the processes used are either 
EU averages, or from Denmark, or others. Certainly this was not always a problem considering 
the trans-border shipments of waste, including treatments of fly ashes overseas from DK and IE. 
As well, wherever local data was available it was tried to be used. It was tried to be 
geographically representative of “best available geographical modelled data”. As is the case in 
all LCA, further inventory data collection, is a requisite to lay out a foundation for a full LCI of 
IE data (which is representative).  This discussion along with further analysis of how the data 
used influenced the final results and conclusions was continued in the Uncertainty Analysis 
section of the LCI.

Types, quality, and sources of required data and 
information with required precision and maximum 
acceptable uncertainties 
 
In order to assess the quality of the data, all of the processes which were modelled and 
the background literature, sources, and parameters were taken in an overview, examined, and 
also provided with a score (see the Data Quality section of the report).  The results of the Life 
Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA), was vital and raised issues with the quality of the data used. 
The required precision was not considered, since the study has future forecasts and is open to 
being imprecise; however maximum ranges of uncertainties were considered. 
Special requirements for system comparisons 
 
The comparison to be made between the scenarios in this study, as they are all distinct 
future predictions, is in discussing what the future Wte landscape “would be”, regards emissions 
in the future on an individual year.  This is unlike a traditional LCA which is used in order to 
make “decisions” in a specific product or system.  It is about comparing the systems in different 
years. One alternative would be to compare a year of Wte versus the impacts of recycling more, 
which would be feasible too but is not within the boundary conditions of the present study.

Data Collection 
 
The first stage was the widespread collection of data that is used in the model. The ways 
the main types of waste management data, for processes/stages, such as waste generation and 
Wte were collected and used, proceeds next in the following section which explains the main 
technology descriptions, and follows with an analyses of sources of uncertainty in the data.  
 
Technologies Description 
Generation Data 
 
In Ireland, waste data is compiled by the Irish Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) 
about every 4 years, therefore the most comprehensive where the data needs of this report 
covered, the Municipal waste fractions of household and commercial waste, was published in 
2012 (Irish Environmental Protection Agency 2012).  More broad estimations of waste amounts 
(such as industrial waste) and generation rates were made by the waste planning regions, 
including the SWR in 20143.  
Ireland is divided in three regions for waste management planning: Southern Region, 
Eastern Midlands Region and the Connacht-Ulster Region. Waste amounts must be submitted to 
Local Authorities each year. Any organization dealing with large amounts of waste reports to the 
local authority, and will be required to submit Annual Environmental Returns (AER) to the EPA. 
This is the case with the waste collection companies, landfill sites, and (as of 2012) the waste to 
energy plant in Co. Meath, run by the company Indaver.

same to 2017 (3% from 2012 to 2017). 
(Appendices: Inventory Notes: 3% Growth  estimated from available data from 2012 – 2014).  
 
0.5% growth rate estimated from 2017-2030 
 
Other growth rates found for the national value an increase of 6% since 2012 (Irish 
Environmental Protection Agency 2016). This would imply the national growth rate to be larger 
than the waste growth rate in the SR. The population growth patterns across the regions may be 
an important distinction to be considered. Central Statistics Office (CSO) data shows that while 
the capital Dublin may grow by 0.9 %, the population of all other regions will increase more 
slowly over the same period growing by between 0.2 % (Border and West) and 0.5 % (South-
East and South-West) (Central Statistics Office 2016). A waste growth rate for the SR of half the 
national average may be a reasonable approximation. As a conservative estimate, this was taken 
as the short term growth prediction and may not be very accurate for the long term, as there may 
likely be a levelling out, or a slower increase. Other more conservative estimations have been 
made up till 2030 by specific industry analysts working on forecast models of future EU waste to 
energy capacity (Eunomia 2016). In their predictions waste growth will occur in countries in the 
northern parts of Europe e.g. Ireland, UK, Denmark, France, etc., as long as there is net 
immigration, and the growth rate which is forecast for Ireland was 0.5% per year (Eunomia 
2016).  Summing up, the waste growths were modelled to predict higher growth from 2012 – 
2017 (3%), followed by slower growth thereafter up to 2030 (0.5%).

EU recycling goals from 2015- 2030 
 
Two main factors were considered in forecasting the MRW from the region. One, as time 
goes it is predicted that waste volumes may grow due to immigration and economic growth 
forecasts (as explained). Two, EU targets see an increase in the amounts of recycling, which 
results in a general lowering of residual waste as further recyclables in the waste is saved. 
Recycling goals, are based on the total fraction recovered, and  it is calculated with the equation: 
the total Recycled amounts of paper, metal, plastic and glass, divided by the Total Generated 
amount of paper, metal, plastic and Glass (European Commission 2011).

Methods of finding Uncertainty in the Modelling 
Modelling of Base Case 
 
The model did not originally account for the cocombustion of waste in cement plants, it was 
modelling all to the Wte process “as is” on EASETECH, with energy substitution resulting from only 
electricity (since in 2015 there was no modelled district heating as there is in 2020 and 2030). There 
were three initial things wrong with this approach: 
1. The assumptions in the model are that as much that up to 41% of the waste was heading to  
cocombustion plants (from reports) and it was not taking into account that the energy savings 
from this would be primarily from heat substitution from coal. In effect the amount of 
electricity substitution could be too high. 
2. It was not accounting for the impacts from the burning of coal in Scenario 3 where there is 
forecasted to be a  shortfall of RDF for cocombustion as a result of higher recycling targets 
lowering  the waste availability.  
3. Since there would be no way to model the “actual” emissions of burning RDF in a coal kiln, 
e.g. as opposed to the coal emissions  (doing so was beyond the scope of this study), 
accounting for the flows of all the waste in Wte plant, yet substitution of heat from coal, was 
however within the scope. 
Therefore the first change to the Base scenario 3 was to show the impacts of the cocombustion 
of the shortfall of coal in a combustion plant. However, the heat substitution modelled was the heat 
substitution from 73% of the energy content of the waste, and was modelled from avoided “small 
scale central heating”, which the emissions from burning of natural gas. This was in line with the 
==PAGE:46==
39 
 
goals of the report to visualize a potentially “cleaner” Irish Wte landscape in 2030. There were some 
issues it was felt which could be modified with some alterations to the substance transfers mainly 
with the purpose of reconciling the emissions of a “Heat from Coal” process occurring with the 
cement kilns, with the avoided emissions of the saved energy which were from “Heat from Natural 
Gas”. The approach taken divides the energy content of the waste in the Wte process into 41 % / 59 
%. 41% is immediately allocated to “Heat substitution in Cement Kilns”, and substituted with “Heat 
from Coal”.  The other 59% was allocated into “Electricity AND Heat”, and  should reflect the lower 
proportion of waste to Wte than was previously modelled. The energy substitution was then similar to 
previously, reflecting the efficiency of a combined heat and power Wte plant (73% heat, 22.9% 
Electricity).   
This result is in effect much like a Base Case Sub Scenario, as it was used as a way to “reel 
in” the assumptions, since the future modelling is generally showing “greening” of the WMS into the 
future (however it was best not to make exaggerated assumptions of a too overly green future). 
Higher Wte in Ireland, future utilization of Bottom Ashes in road construction, and the increased 
utilization of waste heat, are all examples, of greening. Finally, this was done for all three scenarios 
(e.g. the whole model), the emissions were then from the same energy division process, so there is an 
element of saved “heat from coal” from the substituted energy, across the model, and this was 
assumed to be more appropriate method since emissions are mainly confined to the process specific 
emissions of Wte, and not another kind of combustion-process-specific emissions.  
A measure of the success in the changed way the energy was allocated was to be generally a 
“lowering” of “saved” impacts17.

Scenario Uncertainty 
 
This type of model was a forecasting approach in which scenarios were unique due to the 
alterations over time and different years and aimed at showing an “evolving” waste treatment 
landscape, which meant it did not seem to be highly relevant or highly useful to test extensively with 
parameter uncertainty, as mainly it was the overall choice of the models used which had the highest 
potential uncertainty. This lent towards testing alternative futures using “Sub-Scenarios”, which here 
are scenarios with different large scale outcome differences. Initially, three sub-scenarios were tested

Data Quality 
 
Data quality was an important consideration, not alone for the reason of “incorrect data”, but 
for choosing appropriate data. At times data was required to be rerun several times with alternative 
processes. This was the first way of checking the data quality, in comparison terms of discovering 
what the impacts were with other processes. It was found this was sufficient in at least narrowing 
choices down to a range (and removing bad data conclusions).  It was the case with processes used, to 
use “sanity” checking, considering the substance flows which were behind the processes. To improve 
choices, the background data information such as pdfs online (for the LCA Process Data imported 
from Ecoinvent, along with background data on the EASETECH processes used) were checked and 
the “flows” (amounts of elementary flows of pollutants generally going to the environment) were 
assessed.  The Data Quality Report outlines how this method was effective in understanding how the 
level of technology for electricity production in Ireland was being modelled (in the processes). It 
revealed that the process “electricity from coal; IE” was not modelled with Denitrification or 
Desulfurization at the given time when the process was made. It is not known what the present level 
is but it may be compared with a process with “better” flows, and a lower and higher limit taken.  The 
other method used for data quality was in using the matrix of Weidema and Wesnas, which is a 
questionnaire for making an assessment of data quality (Weidema & Wesnaes 1996). There are five 
independent data quality indicators:  reliability, completeness, temporal correlation, geographical

correlation, and further technological correlation.  Using the matrix, a score was taken for all of the 
processes used. (Inventory Notes: Data Quality Matrix.)

Impacts Interpretations 
 
All the impact categories were examined and are next interpreted (leaving out Ionising 
Radiation). For better views of the impacts they are shown in individual figures after the 
interpretations (see Figure 7). Background data tables have been provided to assist in the discussion 
of the interpretation. The Tables referred to may be easily viewed in the Appendices (Life Cycle 
Inventory Data Tables). 
Global Warming- GW 
 
In S1 the highest impacts are as a result of landfilling, which is from oxidation and venting 
(Table 23). Transportation is higher in S1 due to transportation of exports and shipping (Table 24, 
Table 25, Table 26). Apart from S1, there is very high savings in GW impacts out of the avoided 
fossils in the electricity produced by the Wte plants in IE, which substitutes for common fuels used in 
electricity production (Table 27).  Notably, the waste volumes are approximately equal in S1 (30% 
IE, 30% DK) (Table 27). This can be seen by the savings from electricity substitution of the Irish 
electricity mix, which contains a large fraction of fossils (natural gas, coal, peat and oil), versus a 
cleaner modelled DK mix. Given that the DK energy is 69% wind power, the IE energy 29% Wind, 
the rest being a mix of “fossil” based in both Marginal energies this fraction is the explanation of the 
difference in the two GWP (Table 29).  In terms of energy Irish heat substitution and GW impacts the 
savings from utilization in Cement kilns is comparable to the energy saving achieved with heat 
substitution in DK (Table 28, Table 29). Recycling offers a negative contribution (Table 28, Table 
29). 
Ozone Depletion – OZDE 
 
In S1 high positive contributions from landfilling in the SR (Table 30, Table 31), are due to 
methane release from the waste on the landfill (Table 32). In S1, there is a large negative contribution 
(Table 33) from Wte ROI of the same scale as Wte DK, and is due to savings in CFCs releases 
(Ethane, Methane-Halon) (Table 34, Table 35). In S2, high negative contributions are coming out of 
Wte Plants SR and Wte ROI, and similarly are from savings from aluminium recycling (Table 36). 
Reduction in overall savings in S3 is from increasing recycling goals, and lowered Al output from 
Wte plants available for recycling plants. Increases in overall recycling should see benefits elsewhere, 
but it is not considered within the current boundary conditions.

Human Toxicity Cancer Effects – HTCE 
 
In S1, S2, and S3, the largest negative contribution to Human toxicity cancer effects is from 
the IE energy mix (Table 37, Table 38). The DK energy mix affords smaller savings (Table 39). The 
high savings are from the avoided Electricity from coal; IE, which contributed the heavy metal 
“Chromium”. Therefore, it is mainly the heavy metals which are the leading factor. The biggest 
positive contribution in S1 is from heavy metals associated with landfill leachate (Table 40, Table 
41). The higher levels of Wte capacity in IE in S2, S3, result in impacts of leachates lowering as the 
volumes of waste to landfill decreases. As will be further explained in the Uncertainty Analysis, a 
new “cleaner energy” sub-scenario was tested replacing the IE coal process with a “Marginal DK 
Process”, and the result was much less savings in Wte ROI and Wte SR, which showed that the 
“Electricity from Coal; IE” was dominant in contributions.   
Human Toxicity Non Cancer Effects – HTNCE 
 
For HTNCE it was all positive contributions for the net value due to steel recycling, from the

Irish Marginal Electricity Mix, which is greater than DK Marginal Electricity mix (Table 43, Table 
44). The transportation impacts decline as less waste is shipped to DK (in S1 to S3).  Due to a

up with Heat from coal (493), these benefits are cancelled out (Table 45). 
Particulate Matter – PM

contribution in IE was larger than DK, though volumes were equal in both places in S1 (Table 46). In 
particular, it was found that “Electricity from coal; IE” was the most contributory process in the Irish 
Marginal Electricity Mix process (Table 49), and sulfur dioxide was an influencing substance (Table

dioxide (Table 50). As with HTCE and the Irish Marginal Energy, “Electricity with IE coal” when 
replaced with DK coal was a highly changed result, and is explained further in the Uncertainty

Discussion of Uncertainty 
 
Sub-scenarios interpretations showed that there are differences in final outcomes when 
assumptions are tested. Examples are with Lower Recycling Targets and DK Coal.  However, major 
uncertainty is in the data, out of major gaps in the Life Cycle Inventory which have to do with using 
EU, DK, World, or Average data, to model, and not knowing exactly which technologies are in place. 
While this is fine where certain processes are concerned as they will be more likely to be comparable 
and used locally, the emissions specifically from electricity production is outdated, and there is also a 
vital need to find out exactly how things are run in other areas of the WMS, such as landfilling, 
handling of bottom ashes, and metals recovery. Therefore until such time as the LCI is updated by 
industries wide investigations, only approximations are possible.  
In testing the predictions of “future forecasting scenarios” only highly uncertain predictions 
are possible.  For example, it’s possible the LRT will be reached, however it is uncertain, and with 
==PAGE:80==
73 
 
regards to Wte in the Southern Region, the rising opposition from local groups and planners to Wte in 
the region has stalled decisions, and the SR Landfill Site (in Bottlehill,  County Cork), which was put 
in place to store BA is unused. Attitudes to landfilling in IE are similarly complex. Also, for Irish 
Marginal Electricity, it is assumed that coal usage will remain moderately high due to low prices but 
it is likely that there will be  (if there hasn’t already) technology improvements to reach the levels that 
are seen in Denmark with Denitrification and Desulfurization as being standard. All of the modelling 
done is therefore highly uncertain, and represents “predictions” of possible outcomes.  
Discussion and Conclusions 
What does the study reveal about total impacts of traditional 
WMS? 
As outlined in the recent EU Communication in early 2017 on the role of Wte,  the EU 
encourages countries that currently discard a lot of waste as landfill  to generate energy from some of 
it instead (European Commission 2017). While the modelling which was performed here made 
assumptions for e.g. landfilling as declining in 2020, higher domestic Wte, and lower transportation, 
in doing so, there were better impacts than for the “worse” scenario,  which had traditional 
landfilling, high exports, and low Wte IE (2015). There was not “one” process which was more 
contributory to this (e.g. landfill), but the overall waste management system. The modelling outlines a 
potentially “greener” future from 2015 to 2020, providing the rules in the “modelling” were applied 
in reality. In 2030, the decline in SR MRW volumes sees less need of transportation and shipping, 
and less recovery of energy. Transportation is always going to be an impact (with fossil fuels 
emissions), and if indeed waste can be reduced, the benefits can be gained. 
 
What does the study reveal about shortfall of RDF? 
Though the MSW generation is predicted to be positive from 2020 to 2030, the results show 
that with the increase in the recycling rate it gives declining volumes of MRW at the gate of WIP and 
cocombustion plants. Given the capacities described in the report with the existing capacity in 
Poolbeg Wte  and Meath Wte, it may allow there to be another WIP (such as Wte SR), however it 
would mean that in 2030,  cement cocombustion would face a shortfall, and need to supplement RDF 
with regular fuels (such as coal). Therefore, there may be a choice available, to either continue to 
==PAGE:81==
74 
 
supply the coal combustion plants, or to invest in another Wte plant.  Since burning RDF in cement 
plants replaces 100% coal (at 80% heating efficiency), while burning waste replaces 20% coal  (of 
22.9% efficiency, 73 % heat) 18 the rest less polluting fuels/energy,  it could be preferable to use 
waste in cement plants since it displaces more coal burning. As well, it may be that the anti-emissions 
standards for burning coal in cement kilns exceeds the standards in maybe older coal burning power 
plants in IE.  One reported advantage of using RDF in cement kilns, is that approximately 25% of the 
waste material content is recycled into production of clinker, increasing overall circularity of the 
waste. As well there is less bottom ash produced from the cement kiln waste incineration process 
(Saveyn et al. 2016). It is reported that waste incineration with limited energy recovery is still 
regarded as disposal. Therefore, with reuse of the clinker, and higher energy recovery than just 
electricity recovery, it is higher on the waste pyramid (Figure 14)  (European Commission 2017).  
One option, may be reduce the planned capacity of any further Wte plant e.g. Wte SR, to match the 
requirement in cocombustion plants, and it would need a further assessment.  
 
 
Figure 14: Examples of Wte  type stages on the waste pyramid (European Commission 2017). 
 
It is important to further investigate the total availability of waste going forward, as the 
modelling here used less waste than the total available.  A more detailed study should look at the 
availability of more of the combustible wastes from other sectors such as industrial and agricultural, 
to get a more accurate estimate of the total availability.  This would also aid in decisions on whether a 
further Wte plant would remove RDF from cement plants and increase coal burning. It could also be

==PAGE:82==
75 
 
considered to look into the potential to increase avoidance of coal in other industries besides cement 
Kilns, as coal appears to have the highest impacts in the present format/way it is modelled.  In 
agreement to the circular, there should be state aid for development of energy from renewable sources 
using waste or support for cogeneration and district heating installations (Saveyn et al. 2016). This 
can be observed in the model, as the highest savings to be achieved in Global Warming impacts 
results out of the avoidance of fuel used for energy, both for electricity and heating, if district heating 
was adopted by 2030.  Using waste should make a positive contribution to environmental protection, 
by development of such technological advances whereby benefits outweigh the negatives, and the 
waste hierarchy is used as a basis to choose the favorable technologies.  
 
What does the study reveal about Exports of waste?  
 
The base case showed that with a Wte plant in the SR, by 2030 the decline in the MRW 
resulting from increased recycling targets required that coal plants will replace RDF with coal, which 
is more polluting than burning waste. If there is a LRT, in 2020, 13% of waste is still exported, and in 
2030, 1% is exported. If there is not a further plant built in the SR then by 2020, 20% of waste is 
exported, in 2030, 8% is exported. Given the availability of waste in IE and the current capacity in 
both Wte and Cocombustion in IE, there’s the need to assess whether it would be more beneficial to 
continue to export the higher volumes of the waste, or to build a Wte SR (even a larger Wte plant to 
cover more waste). With no Wte SR, transportation impacts were highly positive in several Impact 
Categories, mainly due to shipping.  A more conclusive LCA study, which takes an in depth look into 
all these factors, is advisable.  In the recent circular it states that Member States should carry out a 
life-cycle assessment to ensure that the overall environmental impacts of options which include 
shipping of waste, do not offset the benefits (European Commission 2017). Therefore, further LCA is 
recommended here also. 
    
 
What is the view held on Wte and its role in the modern WMS? 
 
Recently there was news from the WIP plant in Poolbeg (modelled here in “Wte ROI”) that 
there was a leak from the Air Pollution Control System, and 11 construction workers were 
hospitalized (The Irish Times 2017). Episodes such as this, while being very unfortunate,  may be the 
==PAGE:83==
76 
 
unavoidable “cost” of the benefits of avoidance of untreated waste landfilling, and must to a small 
degree be accepted as a level of occupational risk. It reveals also, from the perspectives of an LCA 
study, that modelling is very site specific, and LCA data which is compiled over a number of years, in 
a certain location, can be considered as “representative” geographically, and temporally, for only 
those specific conditions. Thus, the more recent emissions evidence should be collected separately for 
an LCI for Ireland, in the same way as updating the Electricity Processes for IE, would lead to more 
certain results on Impacts from Electricity production. Returning once more to the introduction 
section of the report it began that there was difficult public perceptions of Wte in IE, many being 
opposed to it in principle. This has led to the suspension of plans to build a Wte plant in the Southern 
Region.  There are as well other concerns in academic circles, some with regards Bottom Ashes 
landfilling from Wte.  I completed an independent study while at DTU, upon the evidence that some 
post-consumer products which nowadays contain Engineered Nanomaterials (ENM), are reaching 
their End of Life (EOL), and may be entering the WIPs. These are treated and converted to Bottom 
Ashes and landfilled, and the leachates from the landfills are entering waste water treatment plants 
(WWTP)  and are possibly next released untreated to the environment due to there not being specific 
removal processes in the WWTP for lack of identification (SI: Leavitt 2017). Thus currently, the 
hazard identification for all ENMs (unconverted and converted by heat) in BA has not been 
thoroughly completed. Therefore, there is missing information on the Risk Assessment from the 
unknown behavior of ENMs. The point made here is that it is not wise to merely accept all Wte has to 
offer, without working continuously upon improvements. 
   
Ultimately, along with avoidance of untreated landfilling by safer and cleaner Wte options, it 
is the view held here that waste volumes must be reduced. It is the view that “cleaner technology” and 
“cleaner products” go hand in hand, that there is “no free lunch”, and that in the long run, the main 
focus should be upon reducing the vast quantities of waste the human race produce on a daily basis. 
This is helped by designing cleaner products with less packaging, and by being responsible 
consumers and putting “circularity” into our thinking.

Generation                         
Generation begins with the most up to date data – 2012. Growth trends noted for region from 
2012 to 2014. This rate is applied to 2017. The recycling rate as learned from the EPA via pc is 
applied to the data (SI: inventory 1.xls). This provides the generation volume for scenario 1. Data is 
next assumed to be increasing at a rate of 0.5 percent per annum from 2017 to 2030. In 2020 there is 
an assumption for recycling rates, and in 2030 another assumption, which lowers the amounts of 
MRW.

Waste Composition 
Modelling of Waste Generation in the Southern Region 
To enable a better profile of emissions from treatments of the waste types in the household 
and commercial wastes in the SR, detailed waste characterization results gained in a nationwide study 
(RPS 2009) were associated to different inputs choices available on Easetech. It was a case of 
matching the  numerous waste types in the material main fractions of paper, cardboard, metal, 
plastics, organics, composites, to  the material fractions in Easetech. In the study, it characterized 
both average household waste, and commercial industry sectors, e.g. Cinema, Hairdressor, etc. wastes 
(RPS 2009).  
In the present study for detailing the Household and Commercial Waste the same overall 
method was used both for HH and COM: 
1. Starting with percentages of the Waste Compositions (SI: Inventory 1.xls – Household: Table 
5 – Commercial: Table 6). This gives the total fraction of the main material types, e.g., paper, 
card, plastic, textiles which is used to break down the more detailed  items explained further 
on. Notably, there are more main fractions available for HH, there were 17 classifications, for 
Com there were 10.  
2. More detailed tables of results available in the same study were used to define further sub-
material fractions (for the waste generation process in Easetech).  It identified up to 62 
==PAGE:139==
132 
 
different waste fractions for household and commercial wastes, covered 8 household waste 
sampling locations (with 2-3 campaigns apiece) and 11 commercial waste sampling location 
(1 campaign apiece). The average composition of the material sub-fractions was obtained (SI: 
Inventory 1xls – Table 10 and Table 11) across all these different sampling episodes and this 
was the % used in the model.  
3. Detailed tables of fractions from Sampling Campaigns were recognized  to Easetech 
Nomenclature and inputted into the model.

Directive 2008/98/EC is the directive on Waste, and it contains the targets which are now 
recently being assessed, which provide the “staging posts” which countries should reach for recycling 
in mainly, 2020 and 2030. From personal communication to the waste statistical office of the Irish 
EPA it was understood that for verifying compliance with the target on municipal waste set out in 
Article11(2)(a) of Directive 2008/98/EC, Ireland were applying  choice (a) the preparation for reuse 
and the recycling of paper, metal, plastic and glass household waste (Personal communication). The 
calculation method in Annex I: The methods for the calculation of the target on municipal waste 
(Personal communication) was:

Total generated amount of paper; metal; plastic and glass household waste 
 
To find a baseline, the recycling rate that is being achieved was needed, since it would signal 
the losses due to contamination, and poor quality recycling. Ireland’s current rate of MSW recycling 
is 45% (Irish Environmental Protection Agency 2017) , meaning that 55% or so of recyclables are 
being lost to residuals. However based upon the data shown initially, the recycling should be around 
60%. However by personal communication19 it was learned that the contamination rate for dry 
recyclables was 26%, and at BB and CAS could be up to 10%. Once this was factored in, this 
lowered the apparent rate from 60% to 48%, not so far off the reported figure of 45%. If a 
contamination rate of 35% losses is chosen, this matches the loss rate overall to the EPA rate. There 
may be some differences may be between national and SR contamination rates. 
This first of all showed that the composition profile for wastes in the recycling and in the 
residuals could be used as a baseline. Next, it was possible to consider that 45% was the baseline for 
the first scenario, and that later targets could be applied to the waste showing how much was recycled 
versus how much recycling is transferred to the residuals due to contamination. Applying the target

Sub-scenario notes 
 
10% Lowered targets 
It was assumed that the targets overall were lowered by 10% each. How this effects the 
model, is that the final municipal residual (household and commercial) fractions, are bigger. This is 
because the model applies the “goal” recyclables recycling rate to the potential recyclables (paper, 
card, plastic, glass, metals), in the generation data, and calculates the difference between the 
recyclables stated in the compositional studies, and the value calculated by recycling rates, and adds 
or subtracts this from the MRW. It might be now be clarified that the reason for doing so,  was the 
type of data information that was available during the Inventory Collection phase. A leading source 
of information was of the “contamination” rate (learned from direct communications with Head of 
Statistics in the Irish EPA, Fionna MCoole). It was possible to adjust the recorded volumes of 
recycling in the Base year 2015, to get a better value for the quantity of MRW. Since the 
contamination rate is applied to recyclables which after sorting are in fact deemed unsatisfactory for 
recycling these are next added to the residual fraction. Therefore, when the targets are lowered, there 
are higher waste volumes. It was decided to try to do a scenario sensitivity analyses on 10% lower 
rates, for example in the situation where the “goals” haven’t been reached in the timeframe.  
 
Steps to modify:  
1. The new recycling rates are applied (SI: Inventory_1.xls, Lower Recycling Targets) and the 
new values for the SR for 2020, and 2030 are calculated.  
2. To find the % to exports, it is necessary to apply the new recycling rates for the EMR and the 
UCR to find the totals for household and commercial (Table 2 Inventory_1). As noted each is 
larger than the prior recycling rate value (Table 3 Inventory_1). 
3. The new waste volumes are divided appropriately to match the Wte SR, Wte ROI + 
Coocombustion, and Exports to Dk, which differ from the original scenario.
