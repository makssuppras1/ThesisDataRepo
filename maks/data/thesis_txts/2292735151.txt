ABSTRACT
 
 
Seismology as a modern science has been mainly studied with a purpose 
of produced waves analysis while traveling within the Earth. The seismic 
waves travel through different layers in the Earth and the velocities of 
these waves varies depending of the properties of the corresponding layer 
that the waves propagating through. The seismic wavesвҖҷ activities are 
recorded with seismometers to provide the initial data for geological 
analysis. Analyzing the amplitudes of the reflections related to the seismic 
waves, termed as AVO (Amplitude variation with offset) analysis has 
been widely used in seismic studies. Using AVO analysis has been 
proposed as a remarkable method to discover the major properties of the 
reservoir rocks such as porosity, density, lithology, pore fluid, and 
consequently the probable hydrocarbon detection. Knott-Zoeppritz 
presented a theory containing equations related to P-wave and S-wave 
velocities and densities with an assumption of two bounded layers which 
has been developed for reflection amplitudes versus the incident angle of 
the plane wave. A two-layer model of the Earth has been assumed to 
establish the analysis within the thesis. Considering known values for each 
layerвҖҷs parameters, P-wave velocity, S-wave velocity and density, a true

model have been modified using the main Zoeppritz equations to calculate 
an initial data for the model (shale over sand). 15 probable models have 
been made to determine best model based on the true data. To fine the 
successive model, using a MATLAB optimization function would give the 
best fitting model. The graphs and related interpretation have been carried 
out as well as the conclusion for the proposed problem.

 INTRODUCTION 
 
 
 
1.1 HISTORY OF SEISMOLOGY 
 
Seismology with current concept is a relatively modern science which has been 
studied approximately for 100 years, however, study of the earthquakes can be 
referred to the commencing time of science. The major objective of the studies is 
focused on the produced waves (mainly by earthquakes) and analyzing of the 
properties of the seismic waves traveling through a medium. The characteristics 
of the medium are in use of the seismic waves studies. (1) 
==PAGE:13==
 
10 
 
In 1800 Cauchy, Poisson, Stokes, Rayleigh and others started to develop a theory based on 
elastic wave propagation and explained the fundamental expected wave types including 
compressional waves, shear waves, body waves travelling through solid masses and the surface 
waves travelling along the free surface in solid materials. (2) 
The compressional waves and the shear waves are defined in the order of their arrival time. The 
compressional waves which arrive earlier are called primary or P-waves and the shear waves 
which arrive later are called secondary or S-waves, however, the theory was much ahead of its 
time because they hadnвҖҷt been observed then. The first instruments for seismic measurements 
were based on undamped pendulum, but the problem occurred when they couldnвҖҷt record the 
time consistently. They could only record the time in the beginning period of shaking. In 1890 E. 
Wiechert proposed an instrument using a viscous damping which could record the entire duration 
of an earthquake.By 1900 Richard Oldham first presented the observation of P-waves and S-
waves on seismograms. The arrival times followed precisely which led to organizing a travel 
time table. The travel time is described based on a function of distance from the earthquake. In 
1907 the first expanded travel time table constructed by Zoeppritz. Since about 50 years ago the 
travel times of seismic waves have been used to define the average velocity of earth based on 
depth. Determination of the density of the Earth is more complicated than the velocity, because 
the travel times of P-waves and S-waves do not have any direct impose on density. 
 K. E. Bullen formed a schematic density profile using a probable velocity changing versus 
density has been illustrated in figure (1). (2) 
In 1920 to 1930 a seismic survey has been accomplished for purpose of oil production industries 
in Mexico and United States. In the future, seismic studies proceed to work on the seismic 
==PAGE:14==

reflections from subsurface layers. A higher resolution of the data has been discovered by 
placing the measurement instruments in closer distances. In early 1900s a large number of 
seismic stations have been set up which recorded the data and the location of the major 
earthquakes. Further studies illustrated that the location of the earthquakes were not distributed 
randomly but they took place on specific identified belts illustrated in the following figure. 
During 1960s - 1970s by improvements in normal mode of seismology, more proper constraints 
such as EarthвҖҷs density on large scale structures have been provided. By 1970 the radial velocity 
and the density profile of the Earth has been quite proved, considering  the velocity 
discontinuities approximately close to a depth of 410km and 660km in the upper mantle. (2)

1.2 SEISMIC THEORY 
 
 
A body mass can be deformed in three ways illustrated in the following figure under the forces 
pushing from above called compression, pulling from above called tension and pushing from the 
sides called shear. The volume of the body changes due to tension and compression, but the 
shape remains the same. The shape of the body changes due to the shear force, but the volume of 
the body doesnвҖҷt change. (3) 
 
Figure 4 вҖ“ Deformation of a body under (from left to right) the compression, tension and shear forces (3) 
 
==PAGE:19==
 
16 
 
Volume of a body can be deformed in three dimensions. The force per unit area needed to 
deform a body is called stress. The consequent deformation by this force (stress) is called Strain. 
Strain and stress can be formed in materials and their relation can define the nature of the elastic 
material.

1.6 SEISMIC WAVES 
 
There are two types of waves in general; the longitudinal waves and the transverse waves.  When 
the particles oscillate this oscillation produce the waves, it should mentioned that the particles do 
not move within the wave. In the longitudinal waves the particle motions are along the wave 
direction. In fact, the movement is parallel to the wave direction. In the transverse waves the 
direction of the movement is perpendicular to the wave direction. The propagation of the waves 
along a rock has been illustrated in the following figure. 
==PAGE:30==

perpendicular to the direction (2) 
 
The seismic waves in the Earth can be divided into the following two categories. 
пҖӯ Body Waves which propagate through the entire of bulk medium. They can be 
subdivided into: 
пҖӯ P-wave is a longitudinal wave which propagates on the same direction of the 
particle motion and in seismic events, it has the fastest movement. P-wave is 
mostly reliable in geophysics studies. 
пҖӯ S-wave is a transverse (Shear) wave and recently has been a subject of studies in 
earthquake seismology in early 20s. 
==PAGE:31==

пҖӯ Surface waves which propagate on the surface of the Earth (Air- Earth interface). These 
kinds of waves arrive with significantly large amplitudes. Due to the damages mostly 
because of these waves, they are an important subject of study in Earthquake seismology. 
The wave motions which received by measurement instruments deployed on various stations on 
the EarthвҖҷs surface have been recorded are termed as seismograms.

AVO/AVA ANALYSIS 
 
 
 
2.1 AN INTRODUCTION TO AVO 
 
AVO refers to a method in which the change in amplitude of the seismic 
reflections has been analyzed versus the change in distance between the source 
and the receiver of the waves. AVO analysis helps determining some of the major 
properties of a reservoir rock such as porosity, density, lithology and the fluid 
content. (12) 
 
==PAGE:63==
 
60 
 
Knott (in 1899) (13) and Zoeppritz (in 1919) (14) worked on a theory of P-wave and S-wave 
velocities as well as the densities within two bounded layers which has been considered as an 
essential study in AVO theory. The studies led to an equation developed by Knott Zoeppritz for 
plane wave reflection amplitudes correlated to angle of the incident wave. In 1961, the Zoeppritz 
equation has been simplified by Bortfeld (15) in order to ease the concept of the relationship 
between the reflection amplitudes, the incident angle and the physical parameters (12). 
In 1955, Koefoed expressed the relationship of PoissonвҖҷs ratio and AVO variation across a 
boundary (16). Around 1980, Rosa and Shuey (17) made a significant progress (based on 
KoefoedвҖҷs article) (16) on estimation of the lithology through AVO analysis. Later on, Ostrander 
in 1982 (18), Allen and Peddy in 1993 (19) continued working on the practical interpretation of 
AVO. (12) 
AVO analysis today is broadly used in lithology, pore fluid and hydrocarbon detection 
 
2.2 PRINCIPLES OF AVO METHOD 
 
The seismic waves confront different layers with different velocities and densities, while 
traveling through the Earth. Due to the different properties of each layer, the energy of seismic 
waves is partitioned at layer boundaries. A part of the energy related to the compressional wave 
convert to a shear wave energy. Then a part of the energy of the compressional and shear wave 
will be reflected from and a part will be transmitted through the next layer boundary. The angle 
of the reflected incident wave affects the fraction of the energy of the incident. If the reflection 
==PAGE:64==
 
61 
 
amplitudes versus the incident waveвҖҷs angle would be studied, sometimes lateral changes in 
elastic properties of rock might be observed. For instant, the PoissonвҖҷs ratio may vary which 
leads a change in P-wave to S-wave velocity ratio. Consequently, analyzing the change would 
lead a change in fluid saturation of the rock. In an isotropic material the wave equations for plane 
elastic waves can be solve using the HookвҖҷs law and the equation of motion. Applying the 
SnellвҖҷs law, the solution for the plane waves and the equations of continuity for the vertical and 
horizontal components of strain and stress tensor at the layer boundary, the reflected and

2.8 AVO CLASSIFICATION  
 
The classification stands on reflection coefficient curves by Rutherford and Williams. It has been 
proposed for reflections from hydrocarbon saturated basins. In this classification the reflection 
amplitude decreases by the incident angle. The class one refers to high impedance sands (Dim 
spots), the class two refers to near-zero impedance contrast sand or almost the same impedance 
for a surrounded area of shale (Phase reversal), and the class three refers to low-impedance sands 
(Bright spots) In 1998 Castagna et al. discovered that in some cases on class three the anomalies 
for gas saturated may decrease gradually by offset. These specific class three anomalies have 
been expressed as class four anomalies of AVO. The significant specification of class four 
anomalies can be referred to hydrocarbons related large amplitude. (12)

2.9 AVO/AVA ANALYSIS OF SEISMIC DATA PROCESSING 
 
AVO analysis has been performed to determine the properties of rock and reservoir using the 
recorded seismic data. AVO is based on fitting gradients to the observed amplitudes in a range of 
offsets. During the analysis and processing the real amplitude variations related to lithology and 
fluid properties must be entirely preserved and the noise disturbances and the consequences of 
them should be precisely monitored. 
In 2001 (25), Yilmaz proposed a summary of the procedure of the data processing as follows, 
пҖӯ The relative amplitudes of the seismic data must be retained within the analysis in order 
to identify the amplitude variation with offset. 
==PAGE:92==
 
89 
 
пҖӯ In the data with a flat spectrum, the utmost broadest signal band must be maintained 
throughout the band. 
пҖӯ To determine the AVO attributes, pre-stack amplitude inversion must be exerted on 
common-reflection point (CRP) gathers.  
 
The true amplitudes of the seismic data may be interfered by different kinds of noises within the 
AVO analysis which should be removed by relative suppression processes. 
In 1993, Dey-Sarkar and Svatek have categorized the noises into three main types which make 
disturbances on pre-stack amplitudes. 
 Noise type 1 assigned to the type produced by the source which can be removed by Fourier 
transform methods and surface consistent calculation. Noise type 2 is related to the 
instrumentation within the process of the data. Noise type 3 is associated to the effects resulted 
from wave propagation through a viscoelastic Medium. (12)

2.11 FORWARD MODELLING 
 
Through forward modeling for the reflection seismic data, a geologic model of deeper layers is 
converted to a synthetic reflection seismic record.  This computation is done before and after 
getting the reflection seismic data. When synthetic seismic records are prepared, they can define 
if the final output data created by geologic target could be interpreted and also help to select 
proper field parameters. Creating them after getting the reflection seismic data helps to recognize 
certain reflections which are recorded on data field and express the geologic explanation and 
description of the processed reflection data. (29) 
Through forward seismic modeling a subsurface geologic model (acoustic impedance) is 
converted to a synthetic seismic record in accordance with (or considering) their dimensions. 
(Figures 37 and 38) The depths of the geologic model are changed to two-way transit time and 
acoustic impedance contrasts are changed to reflection amplitudes. Through direct observation it 
can be inferred how the geologic models and synthetic seismic records are related together. As 
mentioned above synthetic seismic data are computed before and after the seismic field data are 
recorded. (29) 
Through Synthetics it is possible to make an accurate relation between the recorded reflection 
and geologic interfaces and to confirm the consistency of the seismic responses which express 
the concept of the geologic model with the actual seismic data.
