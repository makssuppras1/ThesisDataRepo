Summary
Introduction:
Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) is among one of the deadliest
cancers.
A main contributing factor for the poor survival rate is the ability of the
cancer cells in NSCLC to hide from the immune system. Cancer cells can express a
transmembrane protein called Programmed Cell Death Ligand 1 (PD-L1), which enable
them to evade the immune system. Immune therapy has been developed in order to
re-program the immune system to recognize the cancer cells and initiate an immune
response against PD-L1 expressing cancer cells.
Prior to giving the treatment, the
proportion of cancer cells expressing PD-L1 must be established by a pathologist. It has
been suggested to apply deep learning to identify these cells. The aim of this project is to
use state-of-the-art object detectors and optimize them for detection and classification
of PD-L1 cancer cells and compare them to Visiopharm’s current method (U-Net).
Methods:
A number of variations of the RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN were applied
in an effort to improve performance, including change of anchors sizes, altering the non-
maximum suppression threshold, and changing the Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) of
RetinaNet. The output of Visiopharm’s U-Net was transformed so it could be compared
with the two proposed object detectors.
The data set was painstakingly created by
ourselves with the help of an annotation guide. The data in which the final metrics were
based on was quality controlled by a pathologist to ensure clinical acceptance.
Results:
RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN both improved performance when the size of
the anchors was reduced. RetinaNet’s Feature pyramid network (FPN) was changed
to include a shallower feature map, which further increased its performance. The final
models of RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN achieved a mean Average Precision (mAP) of
0.52 and 0.49 respectively on the test set. The baseline method U-Net showed similar
performance by obtaining a mAP of 0.49.
Conclusion:
Anchor size was found to be a crucial hyper-parameter that affected both
RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN’s performance significantly. This work has laid the foun-
dation for developing a method for assisting pathologists in assessing PD-L1 samples.

Resumé
Introduktion:
Ikke-småcellet lungecancer er en af de mest dødelige former for cancer
i verden. En af grundende til den høje dødelighed er, at kræftcellerne kan gemme sig
for kroppens immune system. Dette gøres ved, at kræftcellerne udtrykker membran-
proteinet Programmed Cell Death Ligand 1 (PD-L1), der beskytter kræftcellerne mod
immunforsvarets angreb. Immunterapi er blevet udviklet til at styrke immunforsvaret
i at genkende kræftceller og angribe dem. Før immunterapi kan gives, skal andelen af
kræftceller der udtrykker PD-L1 estimeres af en patolog. Til at genkende disse kræft-
celler er det blevet foreslået, at anvende deep learning til denne opgave.
Projektets
formål er at bruge de nyeste object detectors og optimere disse til at klassificeret og de-
tektere PD-L1 positive kræftceller, og sammenligne disse med Visiopharms nuværende
metode (U-Net).
Metoder:
En række modificeringer af RetinaNet og Mask R-CNN blev udført for at
forbedre deres ydeevne. Dette inkluderede ændringer af deres ankerstørrelser, ændringer
af Non-maximum supression threshold, samt en modificering af RetinaNets Feature Pyra-
mid Network (FPN). En konvertering af outputtet fra Visiopharms U-Net blev også
udført, så denne kunne sammenlignes med de to object detectors RetinaNet og Mask
R-CNN. Med hjælp fra en annoteringsguide blev et datasæt omhyggeligt udført. For
at sikre klinisk accept blev den del af datasættet, som vi evaluerede modellernes perfor-
mance på, kvalitetskontrolleret af en patolog.
Resultater:
Både Mask R-CNN og RetinaNets performance steg da ankerstørrelserne
blev reduceret. RetinaNets performance steg ydereligere ved at modificere FPN til at
inkludere en lavere feature map. De endelige modeller af RetinaNet og Mask R-CNN
opnåede en mean Average Precision (mAP) på henholdsvis 0.52 og 0.49 på testsættet.
Baseline metoden fra Visiopharm opnåede en sammenlignelig performance på 0.49.
Konklusion:
Ankerstørrelse viste sig at være en vigtig hyperparameter, der påvirkede
både Mask R-CNN og RetinaNets performance betydeligt. Arbejdet i dette projekt har
lagt kimen til en metode, der kan hjælpe patologer til at vurdere PD-L1 prøver.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Lung cancer is one of the deadliest types of cancer with 1.7 million attributed death an-
nually [7]. Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) accounts for 85% of all the lung cancer
diagnoses and in most cases when NSCLC is diagnosed the disease has already metas-
tasized to other organs in the body [41] [45]. This leaves patients with poor survival
prognosis and the 5-year survival rate is as low as 6% [31].
A main contributing factor for the poor survival rate is the ability of the cancer cells in
NSCLS to hide from the immune system. Cancer cells can express a small membrane
protein called Programmed cell Death Ligand 1 (PD-L1), which binds to the immune
system’s cells through Programmed cell Death 1 (PD-1) receptor. This interaction be-
tween the cancer cells and the immune system fools the immune system to believe that
the cancer cell is ”healthy”. This allows the tumor to grow and metastasize without
interference from the immune system. Immune therapy has been developed in order to
block the interaction and re-program the immune system to recognize the cancer cells
and initiate an immune response. Benefits from this treatment are only found in patients
with NSCLC who have PD-L1 expressing tumor cells. Prior to giving the treatment, the
proportion of cancer cells expressing PD-L1 must be established, because the treatment
depends on the proportion of PD-L1 expression cells [45].
In order to determine if a patient with NSCLC would benefit from immune therapy,
a small sample of the tumor is collected via a biopsy. The sample is then stained for
PD-L1 positive cells and a medical doctor specialized in pathology examines the sam-
ple through a microscope. The pathologist estimates a Tumor Proportion Score (TPS)
based on the proportion of PD-L1 positive cells in the tumor. The TPS helps determines
which kind of treatment the patient should have. Due to time constraints, the patholo-
gist cannot determine if each single cell is PD-L1 positive or negative, meaning that the
TPS score is a rough estimation of a larger overview of the tissue sample. PD-L1 stains
have been reported to be difficult to interpret and studies have shown low inter-observer
agreement between pathologists, further contributing to an uncertain TPS estimation
[44] [46].
Studies have investigated image analysis methods for calculating TPS in PD-L1 stained
slides using traditional image analysis methods [49] [24]. It has been proposed for future

CHAPTER 2
Clinical background
In this section an introduction to lung cancer, diagnosis and treatment are given. Further,
a description of the diagnostic challenges the pathologists face when determine and
classifying lung cancer is given.
2.1
Non-small cell lung cancer
Lung cancer is one of the most deadliest cancer worldwide with an estimated 1.7 million
deaths annually [7]. Lung cancer can be divided in to two main subgroups; Small Cell
Lung cancer (SCLC) and Non-small Cell Lung Cancer(NSCLC) where approximately
85% of the patients are diagnosed with NSCLC and 15% with SCLC [41]. When the
patient is diagnosed with NSCLC, over half the cases the disease has already metasta-
sized to other parts of the body, greatly reducing the chance of survival [45]. The 5-year
survival rate for patients with NSCLC is 6% [31] and majority of the NSCLC patients
are inoperable when diagnosed and curative treatments are sparse. Several factors can
cause NSCLC but the most common cause is smoking or being exposed to secondhand
smoke. Other risk factors are exposure to asbestos, certain chemicals, air pollution and
being genetically disposed [2].
2.2
PD-L1
Cancer is developed as a consequence of various genetic and epigenetic changes, that
make cancer cells geno -and phenotypically different from normal cells [45].
These
changes can be detected by the host immune system and induce an immune response,
thereby inhibiting tumor growth. However, cancer cells can develop mechanisms that
enable them to ”hide” from the immune system. They can express the transmembrane
protein Programmed Cell Death Ligand 1 (PD-L1) that is normally expressed on healthy
cells. PD-L1 binds to Program Death 1 (PD-1) on T-cells and prevents an immune re-
sponse [45]. Figure 2.1 left illustrate a healthy cell that expresses PD-L1 and an immune
response is prevented by binding PD-L1 to PD-1 on the T-cell. The image in the middle
of figure 2.1 shows a cancer cell expressing PD-L1 that interacts with PD-1 on T-cells
and thereby prevent an immune response. Studies have indicated that PD-L1 expression
on cancer cells is often associated with a poor prognosis [42] and approximately 20-30%

2.3
Treatment implications of PD-L1
The discovery of PD-1/PD-L1 interactions as a key factor in which tumours escape host
immune response has lead to the development of immune therapy that inhibits PD-1
and PD-L1 interactions between cancer cells and immune cells. Immune therapy agents
bind to the PD-L1 protein on cancer cells and thereby disrupt the signalling between
the cancer cell and T-cell, thus releasing the T-cells from their inhibitory interaction
with the cancer cell, and then activates an immune response (figure 2.1 right). Anti
PD-1/PD-L1 drugs have shown promising results with an increase of overall survival of
33% compared to conventional chemotherapy [51]. Benefits from this treatment is only
found in patients with NSCLC who have PD-L1 expressing tumour cells. Hence, prior
to an eventual intervention with immune therapy the amount of PD-L1 positive cancer
cells must be established.
2.4
Identification of PD-L1
To determine if patients have a tumor with PD-L1 expressing cells, a staining of the
tissue is carried out. The process starts with a biopsy of the suspected tumor, in this
case, lung tissue. The small sample is then processed into a formalin -fixed parafin-
embedded (FFPE) tissue block and cut into thin slices. The slices are then placed on
glass plates and immunohistochemistry (IHC) allows visualization of PD-L1 proteins
in the sample. IHC involves the process of selectively identifying a specific antigen by

2.5
Interpretation of histological findings
A pathologist analyzes the sample and gives a Tumor Proportion Score (TPS). The TPS
is the percentage of viable tumor cell showing membrane staining relative to all viable
cells present in the sample is calculated with equation 2.1 [33].
TPS =
PD-L1 positive tumor cells
Total of PD-L1 postive + PD-L1 negative tumor cells
(2.1)
As a slide can contain several hundreds of thousands of cells the pathologist due to time
constraints does not calculate if each single cell is a PD-L1 positive cancer cell or not.
The TPS is based on a overview of the sample looking for areas with PD-L1 postive
cells, hence a crude estimation of the proportion of PD-L1 positive cancer cell to the
negative cells is obtained. The TPS is divided into three groups, TPS<1%, TPS 1-49%
and TPS>50% and the groups determine which treatment the patient should have. Pa-
tients with TPS<1% will not have an effect with immune therapy blocking PD-1/PD-L1
interaction and other treatments are considered. The two other groups TPS 1-49% and
TPS>50% immune therapy blocking the PD-L1 - PD1 interaction is given [33]. The
images in figure 2.2 shows examples of different levels of TPS. PD-L1 staining is noto-
riously difficult to interpret as the stain color all cells with PD-L1 and PD-1 meaning
that immune cells such as T-cells and macrophages also show membrane staining as
they express PD-1 on their cell surface. These immune cells can also be present inside
a tumor making it difficult for the pathologist to get the true TPS as non-cancerous
immune cells could be included as positive cells in the TPS and thereby a misleading
TPS is obtained
A misleading TPS can be an issue, especially when it is close to the cutoff points
TPS<1%, TPS 1-49% and TPS>50% as it is on this basis of these groups the oncologist
decide the treatment. The challenges faced by the pathologists have led to several stud-
ies on inter-observer assessment agreement on PD-L1 samples. A study investigated the
inter-observer agreement between 24 pathologists from 15 different countries on scoring
PD-L1 staining of NSCLC. They were all certified pathologists with experience with
PD-L1 scoring in NSCLC but to ensure that they had the same basic knowledge a 1.5
day per-course in scoring PD-L1 samples was given. The study showed good agreement
between the pathologists’ ability to estimate the percentage of PD-L1 positive cells in

CHAPTER 3
Technical background
Artificial intelligence (AI) is a broad term that holds several subcategories. The exact
definition of AI has been widely discussed, but AI can be described as the effort to
automate cognitive functions, normally performed by humans [8]. A subcategory to AI
is Machine Learning (ML) that aims to learn from data. ML algorithm is presented
with several examples of a relevant task, and it then finds statistical structure in these
examples that allows the algorithm to make predictions based on these structures. The
key element in ML is to allow computers to learn without human assistance or inter-
ference and adjust actions accordingly themselves. A subfield to ML is Deep learning
(DL) that concerns algorithms that often involve tens or hundreds of successive layers
of representation that are learned automatically by exposure to training data [8].

3.1.1
Convolutional neuaral networks
A classical NN is not feasible for processing images, as large input data in a classical NN
result in a high number of nodes. This increases the number of learnable parameters,
and the model becomes slow and unstable [14]. Convolutional neural network (CNN) is
a NN that uses convolution instead of matrix multiplication. The convolutional opera-
tion greatly reduces the number of learnable parameters by using sparse connection and
parameter sharing. The parameter sharing means that instead of learning a separate set
of parameters for each location, CNN only learns one set of parameters, hence the same
feature can be detected at multiple image locations [14].
CNN’s main building blocks are the convolutional layer, the pooling layer, and a fully
connected layer (FC). The convolutional layer takes a subarray of the image called the
local receptive field and convolves this with a filter.

3.1.2
Training a deep learning network
When training a deep neural network, there are two essential goals: to find a set of values
for the weights of all the layers in the network that successfully map the inputs to the
correct targets. Secondly, the final model must be good at generalizing. Meaning that
when the network has been trained and is given a different data set than the training set,
it must be able to successfully map the inputs from this data set to the correct targets
as well. The training of a deep neural network is performed in repeated cycles. The first
thing in a training cycle is to do a forward pass of the data. The output of the network
is then measured in order to determine how far the prediction of the network is away
from the true target. This is measured by a loss function, that computes a score that
reflects how well the network has performed on a specific example. Essentially, the loss
function is used as a feedback signal to adjust the value of the weights in the direction
that lowers the loss score for the current example. This is achieved by an optimizer
that uses a backpropagation algorithm [39] that updates the weights to minimize the
loss function. At the beginning of training, the output is far from the target output
and the loss score will be accordingly high, however, as the weights are updated during
each training cycle the loss score decreases [8]. Figure 3.5 gives an overview of the steps
involved in training a network.
Figure 3.5: Flowchart for training a network. Figure from [8].
The most efficient way to make a model generalize and perform better is to train it
on a large amount of data, but data is often limited. Models can overfit if the dataset
is too small which makes the trained algorithm unable to generalize on new data. To
circumvent this, regularization is introduced. Regularization is a broad term that covers
different techniques that enable models to better generalize on new data. One of these
techniques is data augmentation. Data augmentation essentially creates ”fake” data by
manipulating the existing data set. Images are easy to create fake data from as images
are high dimensional and include a large variety of factors of variations. Operations
like flipping the image horizontally or vertically, rotation of the image, contrast and
==PAGE:30==
14

brightness changes can greatly improve the generalization of the model [14].
Early stopping is also a regularization technique where the training data is split into
a training set and a validation set. This validation set is not used to train the model.
By monitoring the performance of the model during training, signs of overfitting can be
detected. This is showed as an increasing validation loss during training. Training is
stopped when validation loss starts to increase. This reduces overfitting and improves
the generalization of the model [5].

3.4.2
Feature Pyramid Network
The Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) extracts feature maps from Conv2 to Conv5 in
ResNet50. This is done using a top-bottom pathway approach, where the deepest fea-
ture map (Conv5) is extracted first. The FPN convolves the feature map with a 1x1
convolution filter to reduce the channel depth from 2048 to 256 channels, thus creating
P5. To create the next layer, the previous layer from conv5 is upsampled two times by
using nearest neighbor upsampling. The feature map from conv4 is convolved with a 1
x 1 convolution filter to reduce the channel depth and the two feature maps from conv5
and conv4 are then added together to produce the next layer. This process is repeated
to extract the layers from conv3 and conv2. A 3x3 convolution is applied to the merged
layer to reduce the aliasing effect when added with the upsampled layer thus creating
P2, P3, P4 and P5 [28]. The scales for the different feature maps are the same as found
for Conv2-Conv5 in the ResNet50, thus being 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, 1/32 for P2, P3, P4 and
P5 outputs respectively. The process of extracting feature maps from the backbone with
FPN along with channel size and scales can be seen in figure 3.8.

3.4.3
Anchors
To capture objects present in the feature maps generated by the Backbone and FPN,
anchors are introduced. Anchors are basically bounding boxes that surround the objects
on the feature map. They are generated by assuming that each pixel in the feature
maps (P2-P5) is an anchor center. At each anchor center, a set of rectangular boxes
are defined with different sizes. Common sizes are 322, 642, 1282, 2562 pixels on P2, P3,
P4, P5 respectively. Note that the anchor sizes are given with respect to the size of the
input image. The actual size of the anchors places on the feature maps is divided by the
scale of the feature maps they are placed on, hence 322/4, 642/8, 1282/16, 2562/32.
Higher level feature maps contain grid cells that cover larger regions of the input image
due to the increase in scale and is therefore more suitable for detecting larger objects. On
the other hand, grid cells from lower-level feature maps are better at detecting smaller
objects. With these anchor boxes, the idea is to capture objects of any size and shape
in the image. An example of anchors applied to the input image from different feature
map levels is shown in figure 3.9. Anchors are placed at each pixel center on the feature
maps, meaning that the anchor stride effectively are 4, 8, 16, and 32 on P2, P3, P4 and
P5 with respect to the input image [28].

3.4.5
RetinaNet
RetinaNet is a one-stage object detector that does its prediction directly from the FPN,
eliminating the need for the RPN described in the previous section. RetinaNet combines
the accuracy of the two-stage detectors, like Mask R-CNN and the R-CNN family with-
out the compromise of increasing the inference time. This is achieved by a loss function
called focal loss that down weight the loss assigned to well-classified examples. This is
done to circumvent the foreground-background class imbalance problem associated with
one stage detectors, that the RPN in two-stage detectors normally deals with [29].
3.4.5.1
FPN and Anchors
Just like the Mask R-CNN, RetinaNet utilizes anchors to extract features from the
feature maps generated by the FPN. Unlike the Mask R-CNN, it uses P3-P7 feature
maps, where P7 is computed by applying ReLu followed by a 3×3 stride-2 convolution
on P6 [29]. The P7 feature map has a scale of 1/128 of the input image. At each feature
map predefined anchor sizes 322, 642, 1282, 2562 and 5122 pixels are placed on P3, P4,
P5, P6 and P7 respectively. RetinaNet uses the same aspect ratios for defining different
anchor shapes as Mask R-CNN (0.5, 1, 2). As an addition at each anchor center, anchor
size is varied with the scales 20, 21/3 and 22/3, thus resulting in 9 anchors at each center
[29].
3.4.5.2
Predictor head
The predictor head consists of a classifications subnet and a regression subnet and is
attached to each feature map of the FPN. They each consist of four 3 x 3 convolutional
layers with 256 filters, followed by ReLu activations. For the classification subnet a 3
x 3 convolutional layer with filter size K x A is used, where K and A are the number
of classes and number of anchors per center, respectively. At the regression subnet, a
3 x 3 convolutional layer is also added with filter size 4 A. The output of the classifi-
cation sub-network is a feature map with the dimensions (W, H, KA) where W and H
are proportional to the size of the input feature map from a given level. The output
of the regression sub-network is a feature map with the dimensions (W, H, 4A). Note
that parameters of the classifications subnets are shared across all pyramid levels [29].
This also applies to the regression subnet. Figure 3.15 illustrates the predictor head’s
structure. In contrast to the RPN used in Mask R-CNN the two classification subnets
are deeper than the ones in the RPN and only uses 3×3 convs [29].

between easy/hard examples. By adding a modulating factor (1 −pt)γ to equation 3.5
==PAGE:41==
3.5 U-Net

the loss function down weights easy examples and focuses training on hard negatives
examples [29], resulting in the equation:
FL(pt) = −αt(1 −pt)γlog(pt)
(3.6)
Equation 3.6 is essentially the focal loss which is used as the loss function for the classifi-
cation subnet. γ is a smoothness factor that adjusts the rate at which the easy examples
are down-weighted and if γ = 0 it becomes a cross-entropy loss with a class balancing
factor α [29]. An example of how the focal loss penalties difficult cases over easy ex-
amples can be showed by considering two different cases. If we have a prediction with
p=0.99 that does not match up with a ground truth box during training, equation 3.4
calculates pt=0.01. The modulating factor (1 −pt) then becomes ≈1 and the loss is
unaffected. However, if that same prediction was matched with a ground truth, equation
3.4 calculates pt=0.99 and makes the modulating factor (1 −pt) to be ≈0. As a conse-
quence, the loss is down-weighted and essentially focuses the training of the network on
the hard cases [29]. Suggested parameters for γ and α are 2 and 0.25 [29].
3.4.5.3
Post processing
When the network outputs the final predictions it selects the 1000 top predictions with
detector confidence of a least 0.05, from each FPN level. The top predictions from each
FPN level are merged together and NMS with a threshold of 0.5 is added to yield the
final predictions [29].
3.5
U-Net
U-Net differs from the two above methods by assigning a label to each individual pixel
in the input image (semantic segmentation), instead of outputting bounding boxes as
predictions (object detector). Furthermore, this method does not rely on anchors, FPN,
or RPN and multiple loss functions in its framework. It is a simple CNN network.
3.5.1
Architecture
The architecture looks like a ”U” thereby the name U-Net. The network consists of
three sections a contraction section, bottleneck section, and expansion section. The con-
traction section consists of blocks that take the input and applies two 3 x 3 un-padded
convolutions layers, each followed by a ReLu and then uses a 2 x 2 max pooling. At
each down-sampling step, the number of channels is doubled and the size of the input
is reduced by half. These 3 x 3 convolution and max-pooling blocks are repeated until
the bottleneck section is reached which mediates between the contraction layer and the
expansion layer. At this point, the upsampling operations start with an up-sampling of
the feature map followed by a 2 x 2 convolution that halves the feature map channels
==PAGE:42==
26
3 Technical background
and doubles the size of the feature map. The upsampled layer is concatenated with
the correspondingly cropped feature map from the contracting path. The network uses
un-padded convolutions so cropping is needed due to the loss of border pixels in every
convolution [38]. The concatenated feature map is then convoluted with two 3 x 3 convo-
lutional layers. At the final up-sampling layer a 1 x 1 convolutional layer is used to map
each 64-component feature vector to the desired number of classes [38]. A pixel-wise
softmax function is applied to the final output to estimate a probability for each pixel
to belong to the different classes. The loss function used for training the U-net is a
pixel-wise Cross entropy loss function [38]. The architecture of the U-Net can be seen
in figure 3.16.
Since the network uses un-padded convolution the output is smaller than the input
image. To ensure that the output has the same dimensions as the input an overlap tile
strategy is used. The overlap tile strategy enables the network to predict the pixels in
the border region of the image and the missing context is extrapolated by mirroring the
input image [38].

4.1.3
Data assumptions
As stated in the clinical background section, identifying PD-L1 positive cells can be a

4.4.3
Evaluation
Average Precision (AP) and mean Average Precision (mAP) are used as metrics to
evaluate the models. Calculating the metrics start by assigning each predictions from
their respective classes into three categories; True Positive (TP), False Positive (FP),
False Negative (FN). A TP prediction must satisfy that the predicted class matches
the class of a ground truth and the predicted bounding box has an IoU greater than a
certain threshold with the ground truth. If one of these two conditions is violated the
prediction is assigned as FP. FN occurs when a ground truth is present in the image and
the model fails to detect the object. Once the predictions from the model have been
assigned a category the precision can be calculated by equation 4.1.
Precision =
TP
TP + FP
(4.1)
The precision is a measurement of how well the distribution of correct predictions (TP)
is compared to the overall number of predictions (TP+FP). Recall is a metric that gives
a percentage of the total relevant results correctly classified by the model [50] and is
given in equation 4.2.
Recall =
TP
TP + FN
(4.2)
AP is based on the precision-recall curve where the curve is created by first sorting all
the predictions of one class based on their confidence scores. For each proposal, the IoU
for all the ground truth is calculated and if the proposal has an IoU above 0.5 with a
ground truth it is considered a TP. Only one prediction can be matched with a ground
truth and if another prediction also hits above the threshold it is ignored and considered
a FP. The precision and recall are calculated, starting with the predictions with the
highest confidence scores and goes through them in a descending manner. The precision
and recall curve is plotted and by taking the area under curve the AP is obtained [20].
Once AP for PD-L1 positive cells and AP for negative cells are calculated, the mAP is
simply defined as the mean of AP across the two classes. As an addition, we calculate
mAP for three different object sizes at:
• mAPS: Area < 162 pixels
• mAPM: 162< Area >322 pixels
• mAPL: Area > 322 pixels

4.5.2
Modified FPN
Recall from the theory section that Mask R-CNN places anchors on P2-P6 whereas Reti-
naNet uses deeper feature maps P3-P7. It is hypothesized that including P2 in the model
we are able to detect the small features that otherwise would fade out on the deeper
feature map P3. RetinaNet’s Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) is modified to include P2
and drop P7. P2 is added to the FPN of RetinaNet by upsampling P3 two times then
added with the feature maps from the Conv2 block in the backbone. This essentially
makes the FPN identical with the one described for Mask R-CNN in the theory section.
A version of RetinaNet is then trained for each anchors size configurations:
• Default anchor size [322, 642, 1282, 2562, 5122]
• Half of the default size [162,322, 642, 1282, 2562]
• Quarter of the default size [82, 162, 322, 642, 1282]
The three resulting models are trained for 50 epochs and mAP is calculated for the
validation set.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The work in this project has focused on detecting PD-L1 positive cells and negative
cells using RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN. The two methods were optimized with respect
anchors size, FPN modifications and NMS threshold. Finally, the two methods were
compared to the baseline method (U-Net) provided by Visiopharm.
This section is
devoted to the discussion of the results obtained in the experiments, limitations and
suggestions for future research.
6.1
Initial experiments
At the anchor size experiment in section 5.1, we saw that altering anchors sizes for
Mask R-CNN had an effect on the mAP on the validation set. mAP dropped when
anchors were changed to half size and increased again at quarter-sized anchors. This
demonstrates that Mask R-CNN is sensitive to anchor size changes. This was further
supported by the behavior found in the anchor selection experiment in section 5.3. We
investigated how anchors were selected by the RPN in relation to the size of the objects
present in the validation set for three versions of Mask R-CNN (default, half, and quar-
ter anchors). We observed at half size anchors that the majority of the proposals came
from P2 with 162 anchor size and a small fraction of the proposals came from P3 where
322 anchor size was used. One could assume that the proposals from P2 have such high
objectness scores that they would overrule the other proposals from P3, thus the final
proposals are collapsed around P2. As a result, only cells with a diameter of around
16 pixels were found, which did not encapsulate all various cell sizes in the data and
was reflected by the low mAP. The performance at default anchors showed that it was
good at detecting large objects, as the RPN chose anchors from P2 and P3 with sizes
322 and 642 that fitted the larger cells in the data. At quarter-sized anchors we saw
that most of the proposals came from P2, P3 and P4 where anchors at 82, 162 and 322
were placed. The proposals from P2 was not expected when comparing to the cell sizes
in the data, as almost no cells with a diameter of 8 pixels are present in the data. An
explanation of this could be small noisy objects in the P2 feature map that were detected
by the anchors. It could be assumed that the noise was filtered out in the final propos-
als as we got the highest mAP at this setting. This assumption was further supported
by the findings that the majority of the anchors came from P3 and P4 as these fitted
the distribution of the cell’s size the best. However, this assumption could not be verified.
==PAGE:68==
52
6 Discussion
RetinaNet behaved differently than Maks R-CNN, when the anchors were changed in
section 5.1. RetinaNet’s response to anchor size changes could be caused by its usage of
deeper feature maps than Mask R-CNN. RetinaNet with its default anchors could not
detect objects with an area under 162 pixels, reflected by mAPS=0 in table 5.1. If we
assume that the smallest objects were detected at RetinaNet’s shallowest feature map
P3, objects under 162 pixels in the input image would be under 22 pixels on P3. Thus,
the smallest anchor placed on P3 322/8=42 was possibly too large to detect the small
objects under 22 pixels. Reducing the anchors to half the size enabled RetinaNet to
detect the small objects, seen in table 5.1. The anchors at 162 would result in the anchor
to be the size of 22 pixels on P3, which is assumed to capture objects under 2 pixels
on the feature map better. Reducing the anchors further to a quarter size resulted in a
drop in mAP. It is believed that at this setting, anchors would start to be too small to
capture the objects on the feature maps. The reason for this is believed to be anchors
across all feature maps only cover 1x1 pixels (82/8=12, 162/16=12,... 1282/128=12) at
this setting. Even though RetinaNet applied anchors at three scales (20, 21/3, and 22/3)
on each feature map, the anchors at quarter size were too small to capture the objects.
In section 5.2 table 5.2 we demonstrated that RetinaNet with P2 included in the FPN
gave a higher performance at all settings (default, half and quarter) compared to the
ones obtained in section 5.1 table 5.1. It was especially mAPS that increased, which
supported our assumption that we were able to detect smaller objects by including P2
in RetinaNet. This could be due to the fact that including P2 with its smaller scale of
1/4 of the input image, would preserve small objects and thus enable the network to
identify these better than at P3 with the scale of 1/8. It was also demonstrated in table
5.2 that with default anchors, RetinaNet was able to detect objects smaller than 162
pixels, unlike the experiment in 5.1. The smaller scale on P2 resulted in objects under
162 pixels in the input image to under 42 pixels on the feature map. The increased
area of the feature could cause the network to detect these objects better. Comparing
RetinaNet response to anchor changes in sections 5.2 to Mask R-CNN response in sec-
tion 5.1, a correlation between them is seen. mAP dropped as anchors were reduced to
half size, which was also seen for Mask R-CNN. However, the drop in mAP was not as
significant as the one found for Mask R-CNN. Perhaps this drop was caused by the same
reason as Mask R-CNN, but due to coding challenges of tracking where anchors came
from in RetinaNet, this could not be verified. At quarter-sized anchors, we observed the
highest mAP for RetinaNet, just like Mask R-CNN in section 5.1. The similar behavior
is believed to be caused by the identical FPN for Mask R-CNN in section 5.1 and Reti-
naNet in section 5.2. Furthermore, RetinaNet with modified FPN achieved higher mAP
than Mask R-CNN. This could be due to the fact that RetiNanet placed anchors with
three different scales (20, 21/3, and 22/3) on each feature maps instead of one as Mask R-
CNN does. This enabled RetinaNet to capture objects that otherwise would be too big
for an anchor on one feature map and too small for the anchor on the deeper feature map.
NMS IoU was changed for Mask R-CNN, RetinaNet, and RetinaNet with modified FPN
in section 5.4 as we hypothesized that changing the anchors sizes resulted in a need for a

anchor scales. At low IoU threshold, we saw a slight drop in mAP for Mask R-CNN and

In these experiments we have observed that anchors are a crucial hyperparameter, that

The author of RetinaNet stated that with the focal loss they could achieve the same
accuracy as a two-stage detector like Maks-RCNN and still match the speed of other
one-stage detectors [29]. This was confirmed in our experiments, as RetinaNet and Mask

FPN of RetinaNet in section 5.2 made RetinaNet outperform Mask R-CNN. We also
experienced a faster inference time of RetinaNet than for Mask R-CNN. 1.5 seconds for
RetinaNet and 3 seconds for Mask R-CNN running a 512 x 512 pixels image. This is
also of interest as whole slide images can be above 112.000 x 85.000 pixels, so running
a network with half the inference time of the other would result in a large decrease in
inference time on whole slide images. Since we are not using the Mask predictor in
Mask R-CNN, we could remove it from the network, thus making the network more like
Faster R-CNN with FPN and RoIAling added, and possibly decrease the inference time.
However, inference time for faster R-CNN has been reported to exceed RetinaNet [26]
[37]. Thus, removing the mask predictor is not believed to reduce the inference time
significantly enough to be competitive with RetinaNet. Which is properly due to the
RPN in the two-stage detector Faster R-CNN.

Performance of proposed methods vs.
baseline method
In section 5.5.1 we compared the final models of Mask R-CNN and RetinaNet with the
baseline method U-Net, provided by Visiopharm. One can argue that the comparison
between U-Net and the two object detectors is like comparing apples and oranges. As
their respective outputs were so different. However, our objective was to only detect
and classify PD-L1 positive cells and negative cells, and not the need for obtaining any
morphological information about the cells. This enabled us to do the post-processing
step of U-Net’s predictions described in the method section 4.4.2.1 and thereby make
them comparable with the object detectors. If there was a need for morphological in-
formation of the cells, it would have been a different situation. Here we would only be
able to compare U-Net and the segmented mask by Mask R-CNN as shown in figure
5.7. As these are able to give us morphological information about each cell, like size,
shape, curvature, etc.. We did not calculate any performance measurements on these
segmented cells by U-net and Mask R-CNN, but was included as visual results to inspect

how the two methods compared to the ground truth and each other.
During the experiments, a higher mAP for both RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN on the
validation set was computed in sections 5.1 and 5.2, compared to the mAP for the test
set in section 5.5.1. When looking at the AP for the positive and negative classes we
can see that the biggest difference between the models evaluated on the validation set
and the test set is found for the negative class. An explanation could be that we ex-
perienced the same as observed in the anchor experiments when reducing the anchors
to half size. This could be caused by another cell size distribution present in the test
set than the validation set. When we observed the decline in mAP for Mask R-CNN at
half anchors, both APpos and APneg declined, however at the test set APpos declined a
little and a large drop in APneg was seen. This could indicate that we have not been
able to classify the negative cell very well and that the pathologist has made signifi-
cant changes in the test set. However, we also observed in the visual results in figure
5.6 (example D), that the test set contained images where negative cells seemed to be
smeared out. These were not seen in the training and the validations set, and all the
proposed models did not perform very well in that area. This could be the cause of the
significant drop in AP for the negative cell class. As these areas were not found in the
training set nor the validation set, one can ask how common these areas with smeared
out cells are. If we would have had images from more than two patients these areas
would not have affected our metrics that much. Still, as these cells are present in the
samples we need to address these cells. Future work should involve a pathologist’s ex-
pertise to determine how often these areas occur and if the even are included in the TPS.
As it was seen in the initial anchor experiments, determine the right sets of anchors was
a crucial hyperparameter, that needed tuning. Even though U-Net showed a slightly
lower mAP than the other models it has a major advantage of not using anchors in its
framework. This makes it easier to train with fewer hyperparameters to tune. This is of
interest as the potential end-users of these models are doctors and scientist without a
deeper understating of each hyperparameter. Alternatively, anchors could be calculated
automatically from the training set. The object detector YOLOV3 applies K-means clus-
tering [36] of the size of objects present in the training data prior to training in order to
define the optimal anchor boxes. A method eliminating anchors has also been proposed,
a recent paper presented an anchor free version of RetinaNet called Fully Convolutional
One-Stage Object Detection (FCOS) [43]. The method outperformed the anchor-based
object detectors RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN on the COCO data set [43]. It could be
interesting to look further into these methods and try to calculate optimal anchor sizes
using K-mean clustering for Mask R-CNN and RetinaNet and investigate how FCOS
performed on our data. Until the performances of these proposals been investigated,
U-Net is still the preferred method, because of the few hyperparameters, simplicity, and
its relatively good performance.

6.3 Limitations
55
6.3
Limitations
The process of generating the data set was time-consuming, due to the high complexity
of interpreting PD-L1 staining. Ideally, the training set, validation set, and test set
were created by a certified pathologist, but this was not possible.
We proposed an
annotation guideline in order to make consistent annotations. However, we decided that
it was important for us to get a certified pathologist to go through the test set, as we
wanted to make sure that the metric and result would be accepted among pathologists.
One could argue that since the inter-observer agreement has been reported to be so
low between pathologists in interpreting PD-L1 stained slides, how do we know if the
quality-controlled test set was correct. A different pathologist might have interpreted
the slides differently and we would have obtained another result. However, we decided
to disregard this matter as it is out of the scope of this project. In the annotation guide
presented in the method section, we did some simplifications to our data set. We made
these simplifications as we had no prior training in PD-L1 interpretation and getting
a pathologist to annotate the data was not an option.
This included a decision to
merge PD-L1 negative cells inside the tumor and cells outside the tumor into one class.
Furthermore, we also disregarded the identification of macrophages and considered them
as positive cells. The TPS is based on PD-L1 positive cells and negative cells inside tumor
areas and macrophages are classified as negative. With these simplifications, we cannot
calculate the true TPS with the current framework.
6.4
Future prospects
The project has raised the following perspectives for future research.
• As all the methods struggled to identify the negative cells found in the test set,
ways to better identify these areas must be established.
• To achieve a framework that is able to calculate the TPS we need to first establish
a method for identifying tumor areas. The data set we train the PD-L1 classifier
should be able to identify macrophages and count them as negative. This requires
a re-work of the training set and due to the complexity, the work should involve a
pathologist. This could also mitigate the problem stated above, as these negative
areas might be outside the tumor areas and thereby could be discarded.
• A comparative study between the TPS calculated by deep learning and patholo-
gist should be performed in the future to establish if deep learning can replace
pathologists doing this task.
• As anchor size is an important hyperparameter, robust methods for estimating
them as K-Means clustering should be investigated.
• Anchor free methods should be investigated in order to eliminate the use of anchors.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The work presented in this project aimed to implement state-of-the-art object detectors
RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN and optimize them for detection and classification of PD-L1
positive cell and negative cells in NSCLC samples. A secondary aim was to compare the
optimized versions of RetinaNet and Mask R-CNN with Visiopharm’s current method
(U-Net).
The data set was painstakingly created by ourselves with the help of an annotation
guide and the test set was quality controlled by a pathologist to ensure clinical accep-
tance of the test set the final metrics were based on.
The anchor’s size was proven to be a crucial hyper-parameter that affected the suc-
cess of the final predictions substantially. RetinaNet with our modifications of the FPN
achieved a mAP of 0.52, thus outperforming Mask R-CNN and the baseline method
U-Net, which both achieved a mAP of 0.49 on the test set. The potential end-user of
these models are doctors and scientists without prior knowledge of tuning the different
hyper-parameters. With this in mind, U-Net was still the preferred method, due to fewer
hyper-parameters that needed tuning and performance that was close to RetinaNet’s. As
future work, it is proposed to look into anchor free object detectors to eliminate the need
for this hyper-parameter.
Due to the simplifications made in the data, we were not able to create a model that
could calculate a TPS for the samples. However, we have created a data set that can
be used as a foundation for future work to obtain a more precise TPS in the clinic with
the help of deep learning.
