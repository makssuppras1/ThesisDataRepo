Abstract
In this thesis, photonic crystal nanobeam lasers are characterized experimentally in terms of spontaneous emis-
sion factor and threshold power. Experimental characterization of 2D line defect lasers are included for compar-
ison. The nanobeam lasers resemble external threshold powers in the range of 30 to 170 µW and spontaneous
emission factors in the range of β ∼[0.005, 0.06]. The β-factor has been shown to increase with the size of the
defect as well as the length of the BH-based active region. This finding is supported by FEM simulations, where
increasing the BH length has been found to shift high energy antinodes of the lasing mode out of the active defect.
An alternative method of estimating β has been proposed, relating the β-factor to the ratio of linear power slopes
well above and well below threshold. From analytical derivations it has been showed, that β is proportional to
the inverse of the slope ratio and a constant factor dependent on the mode. Through a comparison with the
β-factor extracted by rate equation fitting the slope ratio method was found to explain qualitative trends.

Motivation and report thesis outline
In a world with growing demands for efficient electronic devices for communication, the already vast power
consumption is set to increase. One of the main issues is data transfer on on-chip electrical interconnects, due
to larger power consumption and loss in form of heat generation introducing a bottleneck for further development
[1]. Replacing the electrical interconnects with optical interconnects is an option that will considerably lower
power consumption.
However, optical devices must have very compact design demanding and the use for
on-chip optical communication demands efficient controll of light in small areas comparable to the size of
the wavelength. Photonic crystal (PhC) nanolasers are good candidates, due to their size and low reported
thresholds. A significant quantity characterizing the performance of a nanolaser is the spontaneous emission
factor, β - a measure of the amount of spontaneous emission (SE) coupling to lasing mode. To reduce lasing
threshold and thus power consumption, devices with a large β-factor is desired. A specific candidate is the PhC
nanobeam (NB) laser, which in previous research have reported a value of β ∼0.97 [2].
This report covers an attempt of relating the design of photonic crystal nanobeam lasers to their performance
in terms of the β-factor and the threshold power, Pth.
By combining FEM simulations with experimental
characterization of fabricated nanolasers, the performance of devices is analyzed in relation to design parameters
of nanobeam cavities and the active region. Specifically, the study focuses on quantum well-based lasers realized
with buried heterostructures (BH) based upon group III-IV semiconductors.
Upon introducing the main theoretical concepts in chapter 2, the used experimental and numerical methods are
presented in chapter 4, after which the design of devices is covered in chapter 3. In chapter 5, the main results
are analyzed and discussed in relation to previous research. The main focus in the thesis is to understand how
the spontaneous emission factor relates to nanobeam design parameters as hole size, cavity length and the size
of the BH region. 2D photonic crystal line defect lasers are characterized as a reference.

Photonic Crystal cavities
The concepts introduced in this section are based on the material in [3].
In this section, the concept of photonic crystal (PhC) cavities is introduced. PhCs provide optical confinement
for QD- or QW-based active regions in micro- and nanolasers. Two types of PhC cavities are treated in the
project, the 2D line defect (LD) cavity and the 1D nanobeam (NB) cavity. The NB cavity serves as the main
focus, whereas 2D LD cavities are included as a reference.
Photonic crystals are dielectric structures with periodic variations in the refractive index or, equivalently, the
dielectric function, ε(r, ω) [3]. Analogous to the periodic potential in semiconductor crystals, a periodic dielectric
function gives rise to photonic bandgaps, similar to electronic bandgaps.
Figure 2.1 contains examples of
photonic crystals in one and two dimensions.
Figure 2.1: The left figure resembles a 1D PhC. A multilayer stack of varying material, indicated by colors, with
each their dielectric constant. The right figure resemles a 2D Phc. A dielectric slab with a triangular pattern
of airholes. In both cases the periodicity is defined through the lattice constant, a.
The periodicity in photonic crystals arises from the periodically varying dielectric function ε(r), with period a
referred to as the lattice constant. As light propagates through the PhC in a direction with periodic dielectric
function, the multiple scattering results in destructive interference disallowing light propagation within specific
ranges of frequency. In the 1D case in figure 2.1(left) the interference occurs in the direction normal to the
stacking of layers (ˆz). For the 2D Phc slab in figure 2.1(right) the destructive interference gives rise to forbidden
frequencies for propagation within the slab plane (ˆx, ˆy). Derivation of the prohibited frequency ranges relies
on assuming infinite structures in the periodic dimensions. In directions where the structure is finite, light
confinements governed by total internal reflection (TIR). The prohibited frequencies makes up the photonic
bandgap (PBG) between continuous bands of optical modes. Figure 2.2(left) contains an example of a photonic
bandstructure for a 2D dielectric slab with a triangular array of airholes.
In photonic crystals, light is mostly lost in finite dimensions, by light not being reflected internally. From snells
law, only light that incident on barriers below the critical angle fullfill the TIR criteria at the interface between
the PhC and the ambient medium.

Introducing a defect in the periodicity by removing a line of N airholes forms the line defect cavity. Line defect

Creation of light: The buried heterostructure
This section is based on Diode Lasers and Photonic Integrated Circuits, by Coldren and Corzine.
As we move toward a full description of lasers, it is essential to introduce how light is generated through an
emitter. This is done initially by considering a two-layer system with energies Ec, Ev and energy difference
Eg = Ec −Ev.
Figure 2.4: Top: E, k-diagram of Valence and conduction bands for an arbitrary semiconductor with bandgap
energy Eg. Bottom: Transition mechanisms in an arbitrary semiconductor described as a two-level system at
k = 0. Figures reprinted from [5].
The top figure in 2.4 represents the E, k-diagram with valence and conduction bands of an arbitrary semicon-
ductor. A valence electron is excited to the conduction band by absorbing a photon with energy exceeding
Eg. The excited electron leaves behind a hole in the valence band with the opposite charge. By neglecting
the small photon momentum p = hk this description considers only direct transitions for the photon energy
ℏω12 = E2 −E1. Electrons excited to the conduction band at energy E2 thermalize to the bottom of the
conduction band through emission of phonons.
The bottom figure illustrates the fundamental interaction processes between light and the emitter and their as-
sociated rates, Rx. The rate Rsp governs spontaneous emission, where an excited electron radiativly recombines
with a hole in the valence band, thereby emitting a photon with energy ℏωg = Eg. The phase and direction
of the spontaneously emitted photon is random. The second process is stimulated absorption (R12) where a
photon of energy ℏω12 ≥Eg is absorbed by exciting an electron to the upper level. The third process involving

light-matter interaction is stimulated emission (R21). An incoming photon stimulates the decay of an electron in
the upper level, emitting a second photon with equal phase and direction. Excited electrons can also recombine
in several non-radiative ways, including Auger recombination and recombination via defect states. These are
elaborated in section 2.4.
The active region of lasers covered in this project is implemented in the nanocavities as a buried heterostructure
(BH). BHs are two-dimensional quantum wells (QW) constructed by placing a semiconductor layer between two
layers of semiconductor with a larger bandgap energy. Reducing the thickness of the middle layer (∼100 −102
nm) results in discrete electronic states normal to the QW plane. In the QW plane electrons can move freely in
a continuum, assuming the structure is infinite in the plane dimensions. For a finite structure free movement is
a good approximation for structures with plane dimensions much larger than the thickness, as states are closely
packed in momentum-space.
The BH in the in the studied devices is contructed of five vertically stacked QWs. The well layers are of InGaAsP
(t = 7.4 nm) and with layers of InAlGaAs (t = 5.65 nm) forming the quantum barriers. This resembles a BH
with atotal thickness of 70.9 nm. Figure 2.5 contains a sketch of the NB cavity defect containing a 5QW BH as
seen from the (x, z)-plane.

Tapering of holes close to the defect improves the performance by rapidly increasing the quality factor and
spatial localization of the fundamental mode of the cavities, as demonstrated in [12]. The air holes acts as
reflective Bragg mirrors, but the profile of the cavity mode is continuous and will penetrate some distance into
the mirror. Without a tapered section the mode field terminates abruptly, leading to significant scattering losses
[12]. Tapering the holes results in the mode field smoothly adapting along with the local effective refractive
index. This is referred to as gentle confinement and will increase the Q-factor of the mode by shifting high
intensity field components to k∥states outside the light cone [13]. The remaining 14 wholes on either side of the
defect have the same radius as r6. In reference to the naming conventions in section 3.2, feature sizes for NB
cavities, are found in tables 5.1.1 and 3.2. For the NB devices, the cavity length (LC) is defined as the distance

4.1.1
Setup I - OSA
976 nm CW
Figure 4.1: Schematic of the experimental setup used to acquire input-output characteristics of the laser samples.
Figure reprinted from [15].
A schematic of the experimental setup for acquiring input-output characteristics is shown in figure 4.1.
An external LED is used to illuminate the sample to locate devices via an infrared (IR) camera before measure-
ments. The devices are pumped by a 976 nm CW laser operating at 1100 mA. After passing through a variable
attenuator, a 20 dB coupler guides 1 % of the light to a power meter, enabling us to monitor the initial pump
power. The remaining 99 % is guided through a polarization controller (PC), after which a beam collimater
couples light into a microscope. In the microscope a dichroic mirror guides the pump to the sample through an
18
==PAGE:21==
CHAPTER 4. METHODS
objective with 50x magnification and a numerical aperture of NA = 0.42. The minimal achievable spot size, is
calculated by through the airy disk approximation, D = 0.61λpump/NA, where D is the diameter of the inner
spot disk [16]. For a 976 nm pump this corresponds to a diameter of 1418 nm. Vertically leaking light from the
sample is collected through the same objective. The collected light is transmitted through the dichroic mirror
(transparent in this range) and guided to the OSA via a beam splitter (BS) and a beam collimator. A tiny
fraction is transmitted through the BS to the IR camera making the collection spot visible on the computer
monitor during measurements.
The PC and the piezo electric positioning stage (PECS) enables optimization of the coupling to the lasing
mode. The PC enables manual optimization of the signal. The variable attenuator included after the pump
laser enables variation of the input power to acquire input-output data.
As loss is introduced throughout the setup, the PM readings does not reliably reflect the actual power reaching
the sample. To gain a better, a portable PM is used to measure the amount of power below the objective at 0
dB attenuation. The value of this power is used as the reference maximum power, when measuring the signal
for varying attenuation. The power of light right beneath the objective we refer to as the external pump power,
Pp,ext. In reality, only a fraction of this power contributes to carrier excitation in the active region of the sample,
characterized by the pumping efficiency, ηp. The efficiency is affected by the numerical aperture (NA) of the
objective, reflection from the sample surface and mismatch between pump spot and active region area. A bad
overlap between pump spot and active region results in more light absorption outside the active region.
Notice the choice of using different OSA resolutions for different purposes. A low resolution (2 nm) is advan-
tageous for collecting as much power as possible and is thus used for acquiring the L-L curves. Inspections of
linewidth and peak wavelength favors measurements at high resolution (0.02 nm).
4.1.2
Setup II - Spectrometer
Setup II is a multipurpose setup built by Lin, Qualing for optical characterization of devices in his PhD thesis,
Exploring excitons in van der Waals heterostructures and their potential for lasing [17]. This presentation of
the setup, including some visual schematics, are based on his work.
Setup II differs from setup I on various parameters, most prominently by the use of an optical spectrometer in
place of an OSA. The spectrometer measures photon counts along the wavelength spectrum within a chosen
integration time. The other main difference is the use of a pump laser (CW) in the visible range, λpump = 637
nm (red). The setup operates in free space with guiding mirrors placed on an optical breadboard extended
upon the optical table, see figure 4.2(c). As in setup I, the pump is guided through various optical components
and coupled to the sample through a NIR objective (50x) with NA = 0.65 (spotsize ∼1µm [17]), after which
the objective collects the vertical signal to be guided to the spectrometer. Measuring the external power below
the objective is not possible for this setup. As a result the pump power is monitored by via a PM. As in setup
I, samples are placed on a PECS. The a spectrometer grating of 150 lines per millimeter is chosen to maximize
the wavelength range of the measured spectra. Two rotating filters enables manual continuous variation of the
pump power.

4.1.3
Considerations regarding the setups
The two setups are used depending on the task. For measuring input-output characteristics Setup I is preferred
due to enhances automatization including stable measurement at high powers. It is also superior to setup II
for accurate measurements of mode locations, due to better resolution (δλOSA ≥0.02 nm, δλSpec ≥0.2 nm).
Setup II is less time efficient as it requires manual variation and monitoring of the pump power. However, Setup
II is superior at very low pump powers due to lower noise allowing accurate measurements in the spontaneous
emission regime.
Comparing results across the setups is difficult and demands caution. Setup II pumps the device at higher
energies (637 nm) possibly increasing heating effects. In addition, the polarization of the pump is not optimized
for coupling to the cavities.

To further understand
the implications of increasing the defect length, the nature of these modes, and the spacing between them is

∆λ ∝λ2/2ngL

with increasing LC. The profiles of M1,2,3 has envelope valleys in the center, as depicted for M1 in figure 5.3(a,
right), whereas the mirror region contains one (M1), two (M2) and three (M3) envelope peaks. As a result, the
M1,2,3 modes are less affected by the local refractive index shift in the center for increasing LC. The observed
blue shift is thought to be a result of the electric field penetrating deaper into the low index holes. Additionally,
M1,2,3 are not tightly bound defect states and may exist closer to the PBG edge, where dispersion is more
significant. Figure 5.3(b) contains the line profiles in the vicinity of the first tapering air holes. The figure
illustrates how the |E|-profile for M0 is more smooth at the hole interfaces, indicating gentle confinement and
hence the higher Q-factor. For M0, explaining the non-monotomic behavior of Q when varying the defect length
remains unexplained.
The BH is introduced and varied in length by integer or half integer numbers of a = 440 nm, within three
unique defect lengths, LC = (1.04, 2.38, 3.7) µm, corresponding to the defect length in columns C3, C4-C5
and C6-C7 devices. For each LC the BH length is varied such that it fits in the defect, LC > LBH. The
introduction of the BH is seen to drastically decrease Q and increase Vp, as seen when comparing figures 4.8(a)
and (b). The decrease in Q follows from the cavity being optimized prior to insertion of the BH. Introducing
the BH modulates the refractive index locally in the defect, and thus the locations and radii of the holes must
be re-optimized to achieve gentle confinement, thereby increasing Q.

Figure 5.2 illustrates result from [10], where the confinement factor is extracted for a fine grid of varying LBH-
values in a 2D L7 cavity. Considering that the fundamental and 2nd order mode in the L7 cavity have antinodes
out of phase with the antinodes of the 1st order mode, the distinct bumps at specific BH lengths confirm, that
Γ (ΓBH) follows the antinodes. In addition, figure 5.2 yields a similar saturation of the confinement factor at
large BH lengths for the fundamental mode. The cause of this saturation can be explained by considering the
E-field line profiles in figure 4.8. Increasing the defect length forces the mode to spread out further into the
mirror hole region, causing a increase in the mode volume and a decrease in the field strength of antinodes
in the BH. The saturation is caused by the balance between the increasing amount of overlapping antinodes
and the decrease in the antinode field strength at LBH/LC ≈1. Considering the electromagnetic variational
theorem, the electromagnetic energy functional is minimized by the lowest frequency mode, often referred to
as the fundamental mode [3]. It can further be shown, that the electric field of the fundamental mode must
be concentrated in the high dielectric region to minimize the functional [3]. As the BH with ε = (3.39)2 is the
region with highest dielectric constant, this property of is not reflected in our results. This leads back to the
fact that cavities are optimized prior to including the BH. Furthermore, the cavities are optimized to maximize
Q and not the spatial confinement. Potentially, re-optimizing for confinement of M0, with the BH included, can
result in field profiles with strongest antinodes located in the BH. This is left as a question for further research.
These findings demonstrate that the definition of a fundamental mode must be done with care. For 2D LD
cavities, the fundamental mode refers to the lowest energy mode appearing in spectra. For the NB spectra,
modes exist at both higher and lower energies M0.

By inspecting figure 5.11 one will notice a significant discrepancy in Pout and Pth across the setups. When

Additionally, the signal appears much stronger for setup II measurements. In section 4.1 it was discussed how

efficiency will be higher for setup II. However, the large difference in signal power indicates, that the sample is
further from the objective in setup II. If this is not the case, the discrepancy in signal power can also indicate the
vast difference in signal-loss in the optical circuit before the signal reaches the detector. Although not proven,
setup II is assumed to introduce much greater attenuation due to the setup being in free space, where beam
divergence and small misalignments are significant. The apparent difference in threshold power is thought to
be a result of differences in the pumping process. In setup II the minimum attainable spot size (∼1 µm) of the
pump is smaller than for setup I (1.42 µm), with relative difference RD = 1.42/1 = 1.42. Visual inspections of
the spot sizes via the respective setup cameras suggest, that the the relative difference is substantially larger.
The result of this is discussed by considering the pumping beams as 2D Gaussian distributed with peaks at the
beam center. In this case and for equal pump powers, the intensity of the pump in setup II (power / area)
is larger than in setup I, thus increasing the pumping efficiency, ηp. The above as well as other unconsidered
differences complicates quantitative merging of the data across the setups. Qualitative differences can however
still provide important insights, and will be discussed in section 5.2.4.

changes simultaneously with Lc and LBH along a row of devices. The simulations further show, that Q varies
in nonmonotonically, indicating complex relationsship between Q, LC and LBH. Among unanalyzed effects
fabrication errors, this is a likely explanation of the nonmonotonic behavior of Pth. A more straightforward

This section the β-factor analysis is taken further by analyzing the distribution of spontaneous emission in
measured nanobeam device spectra. Spectra acquired with setup II are chosen to enable low-noise measurements
in the spontaneous emission regime. As mentioned earlier, the total amount of SE is shared between distinct
cavity modes including a background. The background mentioned previously exists in the spectra for all devices.
This result is also apparent in previous research of nanobeam lasers [26]. The background spectrum is thought
to be the signature emission of leaky cavity modes coupling to the active region [26].

Conclusion
In this report a numerical study was performed covering how the defect length and BH length influences modes
in nanobeam cavities. Increasing the defect length was showed to increase the mode volume of the lasing mode
monotonically. The spectral location of the lasing mode was found to red-shift for small variations of the defect
length, ∆LC = 4 nm, comparable to the fundamental mode of 2D line defect cavities. For large variations of
the defect length, ∼650 nm, the lasing mode was shown to blue-shift as a result of the mode rearranging to fit
additional antinodes in within the defect. When increasing the defect length, no clear trend was found for the
Q-factor. This remains unexplained. The inclusion of a BH was found to significantly impact modes in terms of
spatial and temporal confinement. The confinement factor of the lasing mode was found to increase when vary-
ing LBH length within a constant defect length before saturating in the limit of LBH →LC. The saturation of
Γ was discussed to arise from a balance between two opposing effects: (1) increasing LBH enhances overlap with
additional antinodes, while (2) the same increase shifts the mode further into the mirror region, reducing the
field strength within the BH. The Q-factor was found to increase with LBH, with indications of local optima for
certain values of LBH/LC. From these result we conclude, that increasing LBH within the cavity introduces a
tradeoff between the amount and strength of antinodes in the BH. Considering also the Q-factor, the combination
of results indicate that the ratio between the BH and defect length has a optimal value in the range of LBH/LC.
In chapter 4 we proposed a method of estimating the spontaneous emission factor, by relating it analytically to
the ratio of (Pout/Pp)-slopes well above and well below threshold. Through analytical derivation the β-factor
was defined as the product between inverse slope ratio, R−1
p
and a constant factor, depending on mode related
quantites like Γ, Q, vg, among other.Considering only the slope ratio, Rp, the method showed promise for
describing trends in β when comparing with βfit across many devices. Estimating β from Rp proved difficult
due to uncertainties across parameters as the Q-factor, the transparency carrier density and the radiative ef-
ficiency. Furthermore, it was discussed how sensitivity to power fluctuations and noise affected observations
below threshold influences the validity of the method. It is concluded that the method is reliable for observing
trends in the spontaneous emission factor. However, the reliability demands stable low-noise measurements
ranging from well below to well above threshold.
In chapter 5 it was investigated how the β-factor and threshold power depends on the design parameters as
hole size, defect length and BH length. For the characterized 2D L7 lasers, it was found that β decreases
monotomically for small and larger BH lengths. The trend roughly reflected results in [10], where the decrease
at large BH lengths was labelled to suppression of Γ for the lasing mode, relative to non-lasing modes. For the
nanobeam lasers, the results yielded a decrease in β for devices with longer BH’s and defects. The reduction of
β was labeled to a comination of simultaneously increasing the defect length and the BH length. The increase
in defect length introduces more non-lasing modes to share the SE, and the increase in BH length enhances
48

confinement of the mode, Meven, relative to the lasing mode. These results where supported by an analysis
of the distribution of spontaneous emission by inspecting measured spectra. Results of tracking the peaks of
emission in modes Meven and M1 way above threshold was found to support the trend of β when varying the
defect and BH lengths. Clear patterns in the results suggested, that the origin of the low β-factors in the
nanobeam devices can be divided into seperate channels of spontaneous emission. Including more modes in the
analysis, as well as the observed spontaneous emission background, is an interesting focus for further research,
as it may enable a comlete mapping between SE channels and the design parameters.
