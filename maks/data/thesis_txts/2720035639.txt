Abstract
In Denmark, most larger onshore wind farms and photovoltaic plants are placed in Jutland
and the Southwest part of Sealand – only few and smaller plants are developed in the
North Sealand region. The missing development in the region is a problem, as the primary
strategy for Denmark to reach the 2030 goal of reducing CO2­emission (”Danmark Kan
Mere II”, 2022) is based on the development of onshore renewable energy plants. The
available grid capacity in North Sealand increases the incentive to develop further in the
region. A case study is developed in the Municipality of Fredensborg in collaboration
with local actors to identify barriers. This analysis is further supported by semi­structured
interviews, surveys and Development Arena theory.
The characterisation of the identified barriers is diverse, ranging from technical, political,
social and environmental character. A selection of actions was developed to mitigate
these barriers and was tested on the case study. The proposed actions were based on
reviewing other renewable energy projects and include for example agrivoltaics and cit­
izen involvement. From the thesis analysis, it is difficult to conclude the prospects for
developing RE in North Sealand as a whole and must be evaluated case by case, but
none of the identified barriers indicates that it should not be feasible. The investigated
case site shows potential in relation to several parameters, e.g. free grid capacity and cit­
izens’ support. The performed economic assessment indicates that it should be possible
to fund actions to make the project attractive to the local community.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In the spring of 2022, the Danish government presented the reform package Danmark Kan
Mere II [1] as a consequence of the war in Ukraine and to become free of Russian fossil­
based energy. This reform package is to be seen in context with other initiatives to further
drive the green transition in Denmark ­ following the UN Sustainable Development Goal 13
­ Climate Action: ”Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts” [2]. One
of five key elements in the Danmark Kan Mere II was ”More Green Power”. This element
contains multiple actions within offshore wind and energy islands ­ as long­term strategies.
To accelerate the green transition in Denmark in the near future, the primary strategy is
to quadruple the current onshore wind and photovoltaic (PV) electricity production. The
government states that onshore wind and PV plants are the fastest and cheapest means to
deliver renewable energy (RE) to consumers. However, there are barriers to overcome to
ensure the necessary areas for constructing these plants, and the government states that
the construction of RE plants must play a greater role in physical planning in the municipals
across the country than it does today. The current state in Denmark is that most onshore
RE production is located in Western Jutland, where the concentration of wind farms is
significantly higher than the rest of Denmark [3], see Figure 1.2. PV plants (large utility­
scale on fields) are primarily scattered around Jutland, and Southern Sealand [4]. In The
Capital Region of Denmark and in North Sealand there are only a few RE production
plants presently constructed, and in the near future, the plan is to develop only a few PV
plants as well, see Figure 1.1. The insufficient state of the national transmission grid do
also force the near­future development of RE to areas with free transmission/distribution
capacity as a short­term strategy [5].
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the barriers to the lacking development of RE in
North Sealand through a case study in the Municipality of Fredensborg. The identified
barriers are further investigated to see if available solutions could solve the barriers to
promoting the development of RE in the region. The motivation for this thesis is to aid
initiators (e.g. landowners) who want to contribute to the green transition by starting the
development of a RE project. The selection of the specific case study is based on a
request from a landowner who wished to allocate land areas for the development of a RE
plant but did not know how to get started.
1.2
Problem statement
Today most onshore field­based renewable energy sources, in terms of wind farms and
PV plants, are located in Jutland, only a few plants can be found in the Eastern part of
Denmark and there are close to none in the North Sealand region. The unbalance is
a problem as the need for renewable electricity is high in North Sealand as the area is
densely populated. Achieving the common goal of reducing the emission of greenhouse
gasses requires every part of Denmark to take part in the green transition. This project
will investigate why the municipalities in this region haven’t developed any plants and how
renewable energy projects can be made more attractive for the municipalities and com­
munities to ensure the realisation of more renewable energy capacity in North Sealand.

1.3
Objective and Research Questions
The objective of this thesis is to develop an insight into the described problem statement by
applying recognised academic methods, see section 1.4, and from those findings propose
solutions to mitigate the barriers to support the green transition in North Sealand region.
Answering the following research questions will help achieve this objective.
It is hypothesised that there is a general intent for developing renewable energy sources,
such as wind farms and PV plants, in North Sealand among the major stakeholders;
landowners, developers and municipalities. But due to the complexity of discourses be­
tween stakeholders, the development progresses slowly. Also, due to the rapid devel­
opment of wind farm and PV plant concepts, the landowners and municipalities are not
aware of the available opportunities to make projects more attractive for a given context.
1) What are the current governing constraints for developing wind farms and PV plants
in North Sealand?
2) How will the constraints develop for wind farms and PV plants until 2030?
3) What is the process for developing a wind farm or PV plant in North Sealand – seen
from the perspective of landowner, developer, and municipality?
4) Which concrete actions can be made to make a project more attractive and how can
they be implemented?
1.4
Approach
The project will take a holistic view of the problem and include technical, environmental,
economical, and societal issues as well as specific stakeholder concerns. The project’s
foundation will be based on a case study supported by other methods to help answer the
research questions:
• Case Study ­ is used to generate an in­depth, multi­faceted understanding of the
complex situation of a RE project in a real­life context. The underlying approach for
the case study follows the method taught in the DTU Master’s course: Planning and
Development of Wind Farms (46200).
• Semi­structured interviews ­ with the purpose of collecting perspectives on RE projects
in terms of how the informants see the situation and which barriers to overcome to
promote the development. The following groups will be interviewed: industry ex­
perts, landowners, urban city planners and city council members.
• Surveys ­ with the purpose of gathering public opinions in relation to the case study
which will be used to determine the picture of the public perception of RE.
• Development Arenas ­ which has evolved from Actor­Network Theory, is used to
account for all actors, both human and non­human, and their mutual relationship to
describe the arena for the RE development in North Sealand. Likewise, the method
will be used to see how the actors interact and how translations will influence the
arena.
• Risk Quantification and Risk Management in Renewable Energy Projects ­ This risk
assessment approach was developed specifically for RE projects and was commis­

1.5
Limitations and Assumptions
To specify the scope of the thesis the following limitations have been set:
• This project will only address electricity production from wind or solar resources
produced from conventional onshore wind turbines (WTG) or photovoltaic panels
(PV) (Note: so when reading ”RE project” this refers only to wind and solar).
• Wind farms and PV plants are of a commercial character, field­based, and cover an
area of a minimum of 1 hectare.
• Only the conditions for developing wind and solar projects in Denmark and primarily
the North Sealand region are investigated.
In the scientific work the following assumptions are made:
• During the years 2021 and 2022, the world has experienced supply chain and in­
flation issues which have influenced the costs of materials. This makes comparing
previous RE projects with today/future projects inaccurate.
• During the fall of 2022 Europe was heading towards a potential energy crisis which
has influenced electricity prices and consumption significantly. This also makes
it difficult to predict business cases of new RE projects, as the current conditions
deviate from previous RE projects. It is assumed that the energy crisis will pass in
the near future, so the current extreme conditions can to some degree be neglected.
• Data will be collected primarily from a few selected actors related to RE and the
case study. The collected data should be verified by multiple similar actors, but to
keep within the limited timeline of the project, it is assumed that the collected data is
representative of developing the case study and proposing solutions to the barriers.

2
Literature Review
The review of the following topics presents the main trends in the literature. The topics
are chosen to serve as the needed background knowledge for supporting the thesis.
2.1
The public perception of renewable energy
Denmark has for decades pursued an encouraging policy towards renewable energy ­
especially wind energy has played a major role. This policy has been driven by the Danish
consumers’ demand for ”green energy solutions” [9]. In the 80s and 90s, it was very
popular in Denmark to join a wind turbine guild to support renewable energy technology
and buy green power ­ around 100.000 citizens had joined a guild when the support was
at its peak [10]. So historically, Danish consumers are aware of and support renewable
energy.
The general attitude of the citizens in North Sealand towards renewable energy is currently
supporting. In recent surveys by TrygFonden about the development of renewable energy,
45% of the respondents in North Sealand agreed to the question: ”There are areas in
my municipality where wind turbines can be installed”. In 2022 after the break out of the
conflict in Ukraine, 80% of the respondents in North Sealand agreed to the same question.
The national average response to the question was 75%. Also in the 2022 survey, 74%
of all respondents agreed that the expansion of renewable energy should happen as fast
as possible [11]. It is assumed that trend could be expected if asked about PV plants.
Even though a significant majority of the citizens support the development of renewable
energy were onshore wind projects equivalent to 102.5 MW blocked in the first four months
following the conflict in Ukraine due to complaints and other objections[12]. According to
Kristine Grunnet, branch manager at the organisation Green Power Denmark: “We are
looking into a future where protests and lengthy litigation are the biggest barriers to green
transition — not price and efficiency.”[13].
Denmark is densely populated, and it is difficult to find remote areas to install wind farms
and PV plants without impacting any neighbours. In most cases, a local resistance or
different interest organisations, e.g. Denmark’s Nature Conservation Association, will
form to stop the development.
2.2
Renewable energy technologies

2.2.1
Wind turbines generators (WTG)
A typical onshore wind turbine generator (WTG) structure consists of ­ from ground­up ­ a
concrete foundation, steel tower, nacelle (generator, gears, etc.) and on the nacelle is the
rotor (hub) placed, where the three blades are connected. The term ”hub height” refers
to the height from ground level to the centre of the rotor, and ”rotor diameter” refers to the
total diameter of the wingspan. The total height (also called ”tip height) of a wind turbine
is hub height + rotor radius. WTG models can have different configurations in terms of
hub height and rotor diameter depending on site conditions (e.g. wind resource). The
cut­in wind speed for WTGs is 3­4 m/s and a rated power generation wind speed of 10­12
m/s, and a cut­out speed of 25 m/s. WTGs must comply with International Electrotech­
nical Commission (IEC) standards to be allowed to be installed in Denmark. The current

==PAGE:16==
design lifetime of new WTGs is 25 years, and from 2030, the design lifetime is expected
to increase to 30 years due to design development [14].
The average rated power of new onshore wind turbines in Denmark has increased by
a factor of three since 2000. Hence, areas with an average wind speed of 7­8 m/s are
considered ”good” locations for the erection of wind turbines. The average capacity factor
of modern WTG has also improved during the last decades, so for a typical onshore WTG
is the yearly output expected to be around 2600­3100 full load hours ­ depending on WTG
model and site conditions [14].
Over the past decades have the height of onshore WTGs installed in Denmark increased,
and from 2015 have the average total height been approximately 140 m and with a hub
height of 85­90 m. This has primarily been driven by the fact that larger WTGs are more
effective and have a significantly larger output ­ hence a better business case. It is also
due to higher average wind speeds which can be found from the altitude of 100 m (>7
m/s) [14].
From screening well­known WTG manufacturers (Vestas, Siemens, Nordex SE, and En­
ercon) product catalogues, it is clear that the manufacturers focus on larger WTGs. The
smallest onshore WTGs within their portfolio start from 125 m in total height. This also
emphasises the general trend of WTG development where the size increases over time.
The largest Vestas WTG model currently being installed at Østerild National Test Center is
280 m high ­ and developed for offshore locations. However, smaller commercial WTGs
do exist. The manufacturer, EWT, offers commercial WTGs in the range of 61­100 m total
height [15].

2.2.2
Photovoltaic
The PV modules are divided into two distinct classes by application; residential and utility
(plant). The modules for large utility­scale plants are 2­3 m2 with a power density of 190­
220 Wp per m2 and have a lifetime of 30 years. A recent improvement of the PV module
design is the bifacial panels, meaning the PV module can absorb irradiation from both
the front and back sides of the panel. Some of the solar irradiation that hits the ground is
reflected and can then be absorbed on the back side of the panels. PV modules can be
assembled in different setups. Fixed, Tracking or East­West [14].
PV plants are normally ground mounted and typically range in size from 20­60 MWp, but

larger plants up to 300 MWp are also in development. The average annual solar irradiance
on a horizontal surface in Denmark is 1068 +/­ 33 kWh/m2/year (circa 1,000 full load hours
per year). When optimising the angle to the sun, a higher solar irradiance can be obtained.
This means in rough estimation that a PV plant with a capacity of 1 MWp will produce circa
1,000 MWh per year (including optimal tilt and losses in the system) [14].
The typical PV module structure has a height of 2­5 m above ground depending on the
structure system. Also, the row spacing variate depending on design and level of opti­

optimised fixed and standard east­west PV system a higher capacity per hectare can be
expected.
Fixed
A fixed mounting structure is cheap, simple to install and has lower operating costs com­
pared to tracker systems. The structure is set up with a certain tilt towards the equator
depending on the latitude of the site, see Figure 2.1. This allows the optimise production
conditions for the fixed structure. In Denmark is the optimal tilt angle approximately 39
degrees, but in practice, utility PV plants are typically installed with a 25­degree tilt to re­
duce the shadow effect between rows. It is possible to fit more fixed PV modules within a
given area than tracker systems. Resulting in more energy for the same area despite the
lower production per capacity (MWh/MWp) [14].

Tracker
A tracking system rotates the PV modules to optimise the angle relative to the fun for

of the tracking system is the improved electricity production in the morning and afternoon
when the consumption is high compared to midday. However, the more moving compo­

which is a drawback for large­scale applications [14].

2.3
Transmission and distribution grid
The electricity grids across Denmark can be divided into two categories; a transmission
grid or a distribution grid. The purpose of a transmission grid is to transport electricity
over long distances, whereas the purpose of a distribution grid is to transport electricity to
consumers within a given area. Due to the significant development of RE capacity in the
last years, the capacity of the transmission grid in Eastern Denmark is fully used, and if
new RE capacity is connected to the transmission grid, it will have to wait or be regulated ­
to the transmission capacity have been upgraded. The current plans indicate the upgrade
will be achieved gradually from 2024 to 2030 across Denmark [5].
The strategy to avoid the bottleneck effect of the transmission grid in certain areas is to
nudge developers to focus on areas with free capacity in either the local distribution or
transmission grid. The Grid Capacity Map (Danish: Kapacitetskort for elnettet) developed
by EnergiNet and Green Power Denmark shows where free capacity can be found and
designates specific transformer stations where to couple new RE capacity [22]
A RE plant can be connected both to a transmission grid and a distribution grid or just
one of them depending on the capacity of the RE plant and the grids. The required output
voltage from the RE plant needs to be 132­150 kV to be connected to the transmission
grid or 50­60 kV for the distribution grid (the precise voltage will be specified by the local
grid company). It is the local grid companies and EnergiNet, who are responsible for the
up­coupling of new plants. However, the estimated waiting time is long due to the limited
capacity of the authorities to connect new plants, see Table 2.3. The connection to the
transmission grid can take between circa 2 to 6 years and 0.5 to 3.5 years. This clearly
underlines the need to find sites close to the point of common coupling to minimise the
timeline for up coupling [22].

2.4
RE project process
The development of an RE project is typically divided into phases. In the following is the
project model from RE developer European Energy presented (a simplified version). The
approach is divided into nine phases and represents the common approach for a project
[23]:
• 1. Screening: The area is investigated and assessed for planning regulations.
The developer secures the land/project rights either by buying the land or making a
development agreement with the landowners.
• 2. Development: Conduct a number of studies/analyses and apply for permits to
realise the project. Submit an application for grid connection.
• 3. Power purchase agreements: If possible make power purchase agreements
with companies or other actors.
• 4. Engineering and procurement: Design and engineering of the actual RE plant.
• 5. Financing: Securing funding for the project.
• 6. Construction: After all rights, permits, funding and procurement can the con­
struction begin and finally connect the plant to the grid.
• 7. Divestment: In some cases is the plant sold to an investor.
• 8. Independent Power Sale: If the plant is not sold is the electricity just sold on the
energy market as an independent power producer.
• 9. Asset Management and operation: Operate and maintain the facility to the end
lifetime (where after the plant is removed).
Note: The case study involves aspects from phases 1, 2 and partially 4.

Methodology
3.1
Case study: Planning and developing of RE plant
As the focus of this thesis is on a specific geographical region and the number of exist­
ing cases in the region is very limited, it prevents an adequate cross­case method. To
understand how to develop RE projects in the region and how they will be received by
local stakeholders in context to the thesis scope a case study is made. The idea­with a
case study as a method is to gain a better understanding of the whole by focusing on key
parts. The case study is a qualitative and holistic method that can be formed and applied
in many ways [24].
In this thesis does the case study follow the approach taught in the DTU course Planning
and Development of Wind Farms (Course No. 46200)[25], which is based on assessing
the impact of several topics to evaluate the feasibility of a RE project at a specific site.
The topics to investigate in the case study are to some extent determined by the author,
who also has to prioritise the level of in­depth analysis. The approach of the case study
is reminiscent of the RE industry practice of conducting feasibility studies, which often
have a limited time frame (e.g. 3 weeks), so the detail level is to be balanced. The author
does also have to adjust the approach to the given case. How the approach is formed in
this thesis will be elaborated through the case study chapters, where arguments to stop or
pursue further findings will be presented to let the reader understand the ongoing choices.
The approach shall be seen as an iterative process, as the topic’s scopes depend on each
other.
It must be emphasised that the role of the author in the development of the case study is as
the project developer ­ similar to a project manager from the company European Energy.
The approach does contain an economic assessment to see if the business aspect of the
project is sustainable in a commercial sense, but for academic purposes, the findings of
the case study will be presented in a neutral understanding.
The case study is split into six parts:
1. Development area: To introduce the given area and set the boundaries for the case
study.
2. Environmental assessment: For the specific area which is an essential topic do
address for evaluating the feasibility of the case.
3. Societal context: To investigate social aspects of the case and see how it will fit into
the community.
4. RE plant layout options: Several plant options are designed to suggest how the site
area can be developed in relation to different considerations. For each layout is the
plant capacity (MWp) and annual energy production (AEP) estimated.
5. Economical assessment: Here the economic feasibility of the case is assessed.
6. Evaluation and Recommendation: Summarise and discussion of all results from
previous parts.

3.2
Economical feasibility analysis
A fundamental requirement for pursuing a RE project in a commercial setting, it needs to
be competitive in the energy market. There are different ways to assess the overall eco­
nomic feasibility of a RE project[26]. The economic assessment of the case study in this
thesis is based on a Discounted cash flow analysis [27], which is a standard approach for
evaluating investment projects where the future value of the investment is included. It is
based on assessing the costs and benefits of a project over its lifetime. This approach re­
quires a set of input parameters such as costs in terms of capital expenses (CAPEX) and
operational expenses (OPEX), but also the expected electricity prices (spot market prices
­ source: Nord Pool [28]) to calculate expected revenues. Several of these parameters
are often dynamic and changes over time. Most companies in the industry with detailed
knowledge and experience of these parameters treat these this information confidential,
as it describes their competitiveness. Therefore, it is necessary to estimate these input
parameters based on assumptions and available open sources. The common approach
to economic compare energy projects is by the levelized cost of energy (LCOE), which
is an expression of the average cost per unit of electricity over the lifetime of a project
(average DKK/MWh over the lifetime). In this way can different RE projects be compared
considering the total lifetime. Onshore and PV are considered technologies with the low­
est LCOEs, but the actual LCOEs vary from project to project.
The applied approach to estimate these input parameters is based on a comparison of
available material from existing similar RE projects, which are further adjusted from cur­
rent trends or expert predictions for how the parameters will behave in the future (the
approach taught in the DTU course Feasibility Study of Energy Projects (Course No.
46205)). It is important to emphasise that there is a high degree of inaccuracy associ­
ated with this method, as significant changes in e.g. the electricity price or inflation can
have a considerable impact on a project. As RE projects have a typical lifetime of 20­30
years, it is difficult to state which conditions that dominate so far into the future.

3.5
Development Arena
To understand and constitute the ”space” of developing RE in North Sealand is the theory
of Development Arena applied[33]. This theory has many similarities to the well­known
Actor­Network Theory by Law (1992) and Actor­world by Callon (1986), which forms the
fundamental methodologies for analysing heterogeneous processes that constitute tech­
nology and society in general. However, the Development Arena approach differentiates
by focusing more on a problem area that links actors ­ in contrast to actor­networks that
interfere within a certain problem space, so instead of investigating dislocated processes,
Barriers and opportunities to renewable energy development
17
==PAGE:26==
the aim is to identify where actors relate to a certain problem and interact with each other.
A development arena analysis provides a snapshot of the current situation, which can be
used as a kind of ”laboratory” for developing new concepts. The purpose of setting up a
laboratory is to reduce many complex processes and interactions to a set of frames limited
by certain situations ­ this is also called an ”arena”. The term arena is used to symbolise
a space where a concept has to compete, and these can be generated in many variants.
The authors behind the methodology characterised a development arena as ([33], page
190):
1 A number of elements such as actors, artefacts and standards that populates the
arena.
2 A variety of locations for action, knowledge and visions that define the changes of
this space.
3 A set of translations that have shaped and played out the stabilisation and destabil­
isation of relations and artefacts
Meaning that actors in an arena can be both human and non­human, an arena is not lim­
ited to one location, and translation can change the situation of an arena. By setting up
an arena, it is possible to observe interfaces and overlaps between actors and to capture
the conflicts arising. This also shows the potential of Development Arena as a tool, for
besides giving an overview of an actor­worlds, it is also possible to identify the contro­
versies among actors, and where a new concept really can have an impact in an arena
is to solve those controversies. This thesis aims to create a framework which can solve
multiple controversies within the determined problem area, meaning solving a number of
problems connected to very dissimilar processes held together by various linkages and
inter­dependencies among actors. To further understand Development Arena theory you
need to know the associated vocabulary and the definition of the terms, see Table 3.1.
In this thesis will Development Arena be applied in the following way. The first step is to
identify involved actor­worlds. What constitutes an actor­world, is a group of actors that
have a common concern within the investigated problem area, e.g. Landowners, who
wish to allocate areas to RE, and also share a collective agreement in relation to what
methods or means which should be applied to reach their goal ­ also called commitment
to action. Actor­worlds can share common concerns but have different commitments
to action or vice versa, e.g. two different commitments to actions could be landowners
who would accept to allocate areas for free ­ in terms of revenue ­ to support the green
transition and other landowners who seek to gain higher revenues on areas, so these
landowners would be divided into two actors­worlds sharing the same common concern
but with a different commitment to action. An actor­world is also defined by a number of
shared objects which act within the actor­world, e.g. connection to the grid or PV­cells.
Also, the definition of actors is not limited to humans but can also be non­human ­ refer
to a concept or technology such as the climate or environment [34]. The second step will
be to map all the actor­worlds to create an overview of the arena. In the overview, it will
be possible to identify where ­ or between who ­ the controversies emerge. Why these
controversies emerge shall then be analysed and described to be compared with findings
from other analyses.
The development arena is the product of actors’ initiative and is in continuous change as
actors ­ new or existing ­ interact, which leads to destabilising of the current situation and
shapes a new. These interactions between actors are called translations and are initiated
by translator­spokesmen.

How does Development Arena Theory differentiate from classical Stakeholder Analysis?
To conduct a Stakeholder Analysis requires to have sufficient knowledge of the stakehold­
ers, who often is well defined uniform organisations or individuals, whereas in Develop­
ment Arena Theory, the actors are more loss defined and can be comprised of multiple
organisations, individuals or both, and at the same time be of human and non­human
character. Also, in Stakeholder Analysis, the approach is to 1) identify stakeholders and
then 2) manage stakeholders (simply speaking), which again stands in contrast to the
more non­linear approach of development arena theory, where actors floats in and out of
the arena of interest and they cannot be controlled, but only influenced in a brief snapshot.

several other landowners. The goal is to develop a case that accommodates the needs of
the landowners, the developer, the local community and society ­ in a balanced way. It is
assumed the landowners and the developer are motivated by obtaining the best possible
business case, which often means the largest possible RE plants, as the increasing scale
will improve the cost/revenue­ratio. Also, society encourages the largest possible plant
to meet the increasing demand for renewable energy. However, the society does also
have to cherish other demands, such as protecting nature and transport infrastructure.
The local community will benefit from renewable energy like the rest of society but be
annoyed by the construction of a large technical facility in their nearby landscape. So the
case shall seek a compromise of developing the largest possible plant which takes other
societal and environmental considerations into account and has an acceptable impact
on the local community. The applied approach to developing the case is described in
section 3.1.
The initial reason for selecting this site for the case study is based on the request from the
owner of Rydlandsgård and from the considerations that the landowner wishes to start a
RE project’ and from a quick screening of the site shows large open areas, a limited num­
ber of neighbours and a short distance to distribution/transmission infrastructure, which
all together indicate positive conditions for developing a RE plant. If the common goal in
Denmark is to develop fire times as much onshore renewable energy within the next years
­ ’why not here?’. From an academic point of view, the site contains multiple interesting
elements regarding identifying barriers. The site is located centrally in the Eastern part
of North Sealand and has similar demography and characterisation to the municipalities
of interest. So to investigate the constraints and processes for developing RE plants in
a defined part of North Sealand, it is estimated the results from this case would support
answering the raised research questions, see section 1.3.

5.1
EIA approach in Denmark
If a project is assessed to have a significant impact on the environment, the project is
subject to environmental assessment requirements, and the EIA process is initiated. By
default all energy production plants are subject to an EIA. The legislation for EIA in Den­
mark is denoted ”LBK nr 1976 af 27/10/2021: Bekendtgørelse af lov om miljøvurdering af
planer og programmer og af konkrete projekter”[46]. The legislation framework is based
on European Union directives for environmental protection. The general purpose of the
framework is to protect the environment, and doing so by forcing the developer to in­
vestigate the impact of the concrete project and different alternative solutions. Another
important aspect of the EIA process is to provide the public insight and possibility to op­
pose the project. The legislation is administrated by the Environmental Protection Agency
(EPA) and the local municipal councils acts as authorities in managing EIAs. However,
there are exceptions to which governmental entity that acts as authority. In regards to RE
projects concerning wind turbine of 150m height or more the EPA acts as environmental
assessment authority on behalf of the local municipal council. For the case study, it is the
local municipal council who will be the authority handling the EIA process [47].
The EIA process starts after the project developer has submitted an application to the rel­
evant authority. The assessment process can be divided into six phases with the desired
output of a §25­permit [47]:
1 Public hearing (1): Collection of ideas and proposal from relevant authorities and
the public.
2 Scoping: Based on the first public hearing a statement specifying the scope of the
EIA is made.
3 Report: Developer drafts the EIA report containing mandatory subjects and speci­
fied subjects from the Scoping phase. The report is submitted to relevant authority
to ensure reports fulfils requirements.
4 Draft of §25­permit: Based on the EIA report the relevant authority drafts an §25­
permit.
5 Public hearing (2): The EIA report and drafted §25­permit i issued for public hear­
ing.

6 Decision: After the public hearing the relevant authority concludes their decision
based on the developed material. If the project is approved and an objection period
is passed without any inquiries, a §25­permit is issued to the developer.
The case handling time for a full scale EIA varies from municipal to municipal and from time
to time depending on the number of inquires. In Municipality of Copenhagen, the indica­

and governmental processes for handling EIAs for promoting the development, as the de­
mand for RE has increased due to the current energy and climate crisis. The experience
from previously years shows how many RE projects get rejected by the authority due to a
narrow sighted approach and how case processing time acts as a barrier for the develop­
ment of RE projects. The EU Commission goal is to streamline the approval process and
increase documentation requirements for environmental framework directives to shorten
the EIA process and ensure fewer projects get rejected due to insignificant environmental
considerations [49].

Critical environmental planning regulations

Coastal zone
The purpose of the coastal zone is to protect the remaining undeveloped danish shore­
lines to maintain nature and landscape values. Technical facilities, such as RE plants,
need to have a specific reason to be placed in the coastal zone to be given a land zone
permit (Danish: landzonetilladelse) by the relevant planning authority and have a clear
argument for why this facility cannot be placed elsewhere outside the coastal zone. The
planning authority will have to assess if the project is of ”national planning interest” [43].
If the project is to be continued, it has to be investigated if a land zone permit can be
obtained.
A significant parameter to investigate for obtaining a land zone permit will be the visual
impact on the shoreline. The coastal zone covers, by default, areas within 3km of the
shoreline, no matter the local topographical characterisations. Circa half of the case site
is placed within the zone, see Figure 4.4, and by investigating the area’s elevation, it is
observed that large parts of the site are at a lower altitude compared to the area between
the site and the shoreline. Meaning a facility at the site will not be visible from the shore­
line ­ depending on the height. The area north of Rydlandsgård is at a high altitude but
only partially within the coastal zone (Appendix B.4). So the visual impact on the nearby
shoreline will be negligible depending on the height of the RE plant.

be developed with no or minimal impact on the landscapes worthy of conservation. It will

Context of case study
As already uncovered in chapter 4, there are good conditions for developing a RE plant
(free grid capacity and sufficient area) at the case site and the citizens in the North Sealand
demand more renewable electricity [65]. In this section will the social tendencies be in­
vestigated to see if the case site in the near future would become a more or less attractive
project.
6.3.1
Transport corridor
The transport corridor has been a significant barrier to developing large areas in the Mu­
nicipality of Fredensborg. The transport corridor plan is part of the ’Fingerplan’, which is
a governmental plan for the capital region [42]. The Fingerplan is a piece of legislation
determined among the politicians in the Danish parliament and managed by the Danish
Planning and Housing Authority. As the focus on the green transition improves among
politicians and the awareness of limited areas to develop RE (where there is free grid
capacity) grows, the politician and the Danish Planning and Housing Authority start to
loosen the restrictions in the corridor. At the end of 2022, this has been expressed by an
initial screening and upstart for a large PV project in the transport corridor southwest of
Copenhagen [17]. What is more interesting is that the case site has also been identified
in a joint screening of The Danish Housing and Planning Authority, The Danish Energy
Agency and The Danish Environmental Protection Agency conducted in the fall of 2022
[66][67]. Currently, has none legislative changes or similar concrete been proposed in the
Danish parliament. Still, it shows an interest in using the areas in the transport corridor
for RE development.

7.1
Plant design
The plant design philosophy is based on studies of existing RE plants and general guide­
lines identified in the literature (see chapter 2, and the approach is to be considered con­
servative. The following features have been implemented in the design:
• Respect distance to public roads of 10 m.
• Respect distance to §3­nature of 5 m.
• From plant boundary to closest PV structures a distance of 10 m (5 m for hedge/fence
and 5 m for service road).
• PV squares divided in accordance with ”coherent” areas to create natural gaps for
service roads.
• Internal service roads 5 m wide.
• One WTG requires an area of 50 m x 50 m.
• Facility areas for transformer station etc. have been scaled in relation to the adjacent
energy­producing areas.
The layout designs follow natural curves and lines in the landscape, as well as possible,
and it has been imposed to have long straight lines to get a ”clean” visual expression.
Other considerations regarding plant design for each layout will be further elaborated
in the following sections, and it must be emphasised that this is only a very initial and
basic plant layout design ­ many other technical considerations should be implemented
in continued development work.
7.1.1
Wind and solar resources
The two open­source renewable energy resource tools are applied; Global Wind Atlas [68]
to estimate wind resources and Global Solar Atlas [69] to estimate solar resources. The
tools are provided by The World Bank Group (part of UN) to support the development of
renewable wind and solar energy globally. The tools allow the user to obtain site­specific
estimation of wind and solar resources and calculate annual energy production (AEP) for
specific WTG models or fixed ground­mounted large­scale PV systems.

7.1.2
Chosen RE technologies
In the proposed RE plant layouts, the chosen PV system is fixed ground­mounted, as
it is the most applied and Global Solar Atlas can generate an estimate of AEP of this
system. The PV panels are facing straight south and to minimise row distance are the tilt
25 degrees (below optimum tilt), but it is assessed the increased number of panels per
area will improve the overall energy production.
From a screening of WTG models across manufacturers the two most suited WTGs for
the site ­ based on total height. Both WTG models are for onshore use and appear in the
manufactures current product catalogue:
• Vestas V90­2.0 MW: Hub height 80 m, rotor diameter 90 m, total height 125 m[70].
• EWT DW61­900kW: Hub height 69 m, rotor diameter 61 m, total height 100 m[71].
The local regulation for the development of WTGs in the municipality ­ combined with the
high number of neighbours ­ makes it very unlikely that approval for erecting one WTG
at the case site can be obtained. However, due to the many advantages of a hybrid RE
plant, a WTG has been included in layouts 1 and 2, to see how it will affect the general
performance of the plant. The 100 m high EWT WTG is less productive, but due to the
height, it may be more realistic to get approved. In contrast, the 125 m high V90 is sig­
nificantly more modern and productive but the extra 25 m compared to the EWT causes
considerably more potential compensation claims.
7.2
Layout Options
In general, the main constraints for all layouts are that the owner of property no. 3 must
be engaged and accept the development of PV within the 200 m distance boundary. The
same goes for the owner of property no. 1 ­ the impact on that property is significantly less
compared to property 3. The use of areas around and close to Rydlandsgård (A) have to
be balanced, as people do live at the farm and surrounding the farm with PV structures
is not durable. Lerbergbjerggård (B) is not used for residential purposes and that has to
be guaranteed for the lifetime of the RE plant. The included cadastres for the layouts are
owned by 4­5 different landowners ­ including the owner of Rydlandsgård. For the project
to continue would also require the other landowners’ participation. Lastly, it is assumed
that all the planning restrictions, to some degree, will be possible to solve to obtain the
necessary permits. Each layout is designed to have various degrees of impact on nature,
planning regulations and neighbours.
The available placement options for a WTG is very limited, but somewhere between Ryd­
landsgård and Lerbjerggård, and as close to Helsingørmotorvejen (but minimum one total
height). The exact optimal placement has not been thoroughly investigated, and the sug­
gested location in Layout 1 and 2 shall only be seen as indicative. It would not be possible
to have more than one WTG, due to a required distance between WTGs of 2.5 total height
to avoid interaction (turbulence), to keep distance to neighbours. In term of wind resource,
it does not have significant influence if the WTG is moved several meters in any direction
from the suggested location.

Case Study Part 5: Economical
Assessment
In this chapter is the economic feasibility of the developed layout options calculated using
the Discounted cash flow analysis, see section 3.2. The results from the analysis are
discussed in chapter 13. The performed economical assessment shall be considered
”indicative”, as the included key parameters are mostly based on simple estimates.
Several of the input parameters in the analysis is based on financial or design decisions
made by the developer. These are often based on experience from other projects or mar­
ket trends. The International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) has collected economic
data from many RE projects and has made an overview of trends within the market (RE
Cost report)[73]. The following key input is based on the benchmark value estimated by
IRENA, and others are chosen by the author:
• Plant lifetime: 30 years
• WACC: 6% (Typical for RE projects [73])
• Valuation date (end of year): 2023
• Start of operation: 2024
• Electricity price (2023) ­ estimate: 374 DKK/MWh (Calculated as the average of the
period 2018­2021 [28])
• Inflation rate ­ estimate: 2.0% (The current inflation is exceptionally high and the
chosen value is based on an estimate from looking at the inflation development
over the past 20 years [74])
• Corporate tax rate: 22% (Denmark)
• Depreciation: 5%/year (straight line, 20 years ­ decided by the author and in accor­
dance to ”Afskrivningsloven” [75])
To estimate the CAPEX and OPEX of the proposed layout options were several sources
included. The two primary sources applied are the IRENA RE cost report and the Dan­
ish Energy Agency developed ”LCOE Calculator” [76]. From these two sources were the
CAPEX and OPEX direct linked to PV and WTG estimated, which are the most significant
costs of a RE project. The CAPEX of laying the cable trace between the plant and trans­
former stations (distribution grid) was also included and calculated based on distance.
As of 1, January 2023, EnergiNet has phased in new tariffs: Connection fee (Danish:
Tilslutningsbidrag) and feed­in tariff (Danish: Indfødningstarif) [77]. The connection fee
is a CAPEX cost and the feed­in tariff an OPEX cost. The connection fee depends if a RE
plant is connected to a distribution grid(s) or a transmission grid ­ due to the estimated

Case study evaluation
The case site contains several different planning regulations as stated in chapter 4. Es­
pecially, the transport corridor (Fingerplan) has been a major barrier to developing the
areas along Helsingørmotorvejen. However, a paradigm shift is in development, and The
Danish Planning and Housing Authority is looking into possibilities for using the reserved
areas for RE development. City council member, Mie Stattau (V ­ Municipality of Fredens­

like to get in contact with our planning manager to start a trial case” (Appendix C.3). This
clearly indicates the governmental planning authorities are heading in a more pragmatic
direction to utilise available areas. Still, the RE project developer shall expect to increase
the overall effort in applying for the necessary land zone permits in regard to all the other
restrictions. This is also emphasised by the urban planner, Nils­Erik Wandall: ”It is heavy
work and there are many rules and restrictions, especially here in the capital region where
the Fingerplan applies” (Appendix C.2).
The primary environmental issues identified in the analysis are the distance require­

nearby bird sanctuary.
The distance requirements do mainly concern the erection of
WTGs. It is possible to mitigate the mandatory four heights distance requirement ­ if only
one WTG (max 150 m high) is erected. However, the subsequent distance requirement

the affected residential properties are under 10 pcs. The exact compensation costs have
not been estimated as it would require a comprehensive investigation, but the accumula­
tive compensations to many properties are expected to be significant. The impact of the
RE plant on nature can be mitigated to a high degree. The development of a RE plant
will impact the visual expression of the landscape, and it would be up to the local politi­
cians to determine what is acceptable. The developer must also, as part of the EIA, make
visualisations to illustrate how the RE plant will look at the case site.
The public agenda is generally positive towards the development of RE within the Mu­
nicipality of Fredensborg. The results from the survey show public support for developing
a RE plant in the municipality and also to utilise areas along Helsingørmotorvejen. How­
ever, the support does not come without claims. Many respondents answered that they
wanted several features implemented such as hedges and actions to improve biodiversity.
In the political landscape, city council member Mie Stattau experience a more ambiguous

Development Arena Analysis
The defined problem area for the development arena analysis is development of onshore
RE projects with a geographical focus on the North Sealand region. In contrast to the case
study that focuses on a specific project, at a specific location, this analysis will investigate
the actor­worlds dynamics of RE projects in a more general understanding. The aim is to
characterise an arena where possible controversies can be discovered. An understanding
of the arena can assist in designing RE projects in a conceptual sense, as shaping RE
projects to meet and solve controversies can be an efficient way to mitigate barriers. The
methodology of the analysis and applied terms are explained in section 3.5.
10.1
Actor­world identification
The first step is to describe relevant actor­worlds investigated arena. Each actor­world is
described based on their Common concern, Commitment to action and Shared objects,
see Figure 10.1. An insight into relevant actors has been obtained in the Literature Review
and Case Study.
Figure 10.1: Identified Actor­worlds in relation to the defined arena: Development of onshore RE
projects in North Sealand region.
In the following paragraphs, each actor­world is further described. Important notice from
the actor­world identification is that the actor­worlds are not totally uniform, as the worlds
are comprised of multiple actors. Translations in the arena may split the current com­
bination of actors into new ones. Also, the approach to defined actors­worlds is from a
perspective relevant to this thesis, for the purpose of designing the future concept of RE
projects. Another notice should also be taken of how ”Legislation” is a shared object that
Barriers and opportunities to renewable energy development
59
==PAGE:68==
influences several actor­worlds. Although, it provides different constraints and ways to
mitigate controversies. Understanding and using legislation in the defined arena is cru­
cial for succeeding ­ no matter the perspective of the problem area.
Entrepreneurs
The ’Entrepreneurs’ consist of actors who initiate and own a RE project (or land), such
as the developer (e.g. European Energy), landowners and investors. Their goal is to de­
velop projects, and their concern is profit margin from those. Their commitment to action
within the problem area is to adjust their profit margin to finance actions that meet the
requirements or needs of other actor­worlds (or stop a project), so they need to balance
the economic aspects of a project. The shared objects within this actor­world are wind tur­
bines, PV and ”know­how”, and all these objects together are the product the actor­world
brings to the arena.
Neighbours
This actor­world comprises actors who own property close to a project site. They are
not included in the actor­worlds ’Common citizen’ or ’Opposing citizens’ because due to
the geographical proximity of a project to their home gives them certain rights following
current legislation, which raises concern about maximising their gain in case a project is
approved. The gain may not necessarily be economical but influence a project.
Common citizens
Normally in actor­world theory large groups of actors are broken down to create more pre­
cisely defined groups of actors. However, in context of the arena of RE development that
can be experienced today, it is assumed that the general population in Denmark under­
stand and support the need for more RE all over Denmark and are ready to compromise
their own needs to find solutions to benefit national interests. However, an internal conflict
between national interest and ”not in my back­yard” splits the actors. Maybe in the near
future, translations in the arena will break up this huge actor­world and re­configure the
actors in new worlds ­ this is one of the basic principles of actor­world theory. However,
this large group of actors’ common concern is a product of the actor­world ’Governmental
authorities’, but ’Common citizens’ have another commitment to action, as they need to
also act on their local interests.
Opposing citizens
This actor­world comprises actors with a strong focus on local environmental interests
and less on national interests. The actors could be local citizens and unions like The
Danish Society for Nature Conservation. The actor­world constitutes a significant role
in the arena due to their resistance to starting RE projects. The primary shared objects
within this world are ”Complains”, a legislative tool, and ”Social Media” which they use to
express their opinions and connect to actors within their world.
Local authorities
’Local authorities’ refer to the municipality administration (planners, surveyors, etc.) and
city council members, who practice the power to assess and approve RE projects. The
interplay between the actors in this world is complex ­ some are driven by technical con­
siderations and others by political considerations. Together they have to act within the
given legislation and listen to public opinions. The object that circulates with the actor­
world is the ”Permit” (e.g. EIA or land zone permit), which is an object that can affect the
other actor­worlds in the arena.
Governmental authorities

The actor­world ’Governmental authorities’ is even more complex, as it is a combination
of several large organisations such as the parliament, Danish Environmental Ministry,
Danish Energy Agency, and more, who are all connected by a similar commitment to
action and centred around the shared object ”Legislation”. Another important object which
is included is ”The grid”, as it is an object that has significant importance in the arena.
Depending on how the grid is applied, it can be the means to create translation which can
disrupt the order of the arena.

11.2
Opportunities to improve the realisation of a RE project
Many new ideas for improving RE plants are in development. Several concepts are be­
ing researched to document the effect, and for others, they have mostly been tested in
practice by developers. The fundamental paradox of many of these potential actions for
improving social acceptance or mitigating environmental impact ­ is the balance of costs,
as a RE project needs to be economically feasible. In practice, it may not be possible to
implement all actions or reduce the plant size to such a small area that no one would be
affected. The following proposed opportunities are based on a superficial review of cur­
rent research or experiences to provide an overview of the possibilities of which barriers
the opportunities could solve.
11.2.1
General framework conditions
Several of the identified barriers require legislative changes, and cannot be fixed by the
developer, municipality or landowner from implementing concrete actions in a RE project
­ besides selecting a site where the effect of the barriers is limited. Hence, the following
barriers 3, 4, 10, 12 and 13 are considered conditions for developing RE projects in North
Sealand.
Many of the larger estates in North Sealand ­ especially in the Municipality of Fredensborg
­ are owned by people, who are motivated by the recreational/leisure aspects of owning
land (barrier 15). In contrast, conventional landowners who are driven by a stronger
focus on generating revenue, may find RE projects more interesting as it is a source of
higher revenue per hectare compared to crops. To change this condition would require
changing the incentive or motivation of the leisure landowners. This may be done through
information and by actively seeking out the landowners and informing them of all the other
benefits of leasing land to a RE project.

11.2.2
Current changes within framework conditions for RE development
The development of the Grid capacity map (chapter 4), and newly introduced tariffs (chap­
ter 8) make RE projects in free capacity areas more attractive compared to other places,
due to the increased costs for developing in production surplus areas. These changes
in framework conditions indirectly mitigate the cost effect from barrier 14 but also the
general cost improvement of mitigating other barriers.
In the fall of 2022, the Danish Housing and Planning Authority made a screening of suit­
able areas for RE project (independent of this study) and identified the case site area
between Nivå and Humlebæk [81]. This shows that barrier 1 of utilising areas in the
transport corridor may in the near future change to the benefit of developing RE.
11.2.3
Design options
Agrivoltaics: One of the issues in barrier 2 is the particularly valuable agricultural land
and the fact that a large PV plant would take up a lot of this agricultural land. The con­
cept of agrivoltaics is rapidly developing, and many small­scale test facilities are being
established. The basics of the concept are to have a combination of PV panels and
crops in different configurations. Tests show that one hectare of combined wheat crops
and PV can produce 80% wheat and 80% electricity compared to the equivalent of one
hectare of 100% wheat or 100% PV [82]. See examples of the application of agrivoltaics
in Appendix G.1. The primary applied PV system with agrivoltaics is the tracker system,
where the farmer can control the pitch of the PV panels and park selected rows in a ver­
tical position when working between them. Another type is the elevated PV, where the
ground­mounted structure is extended, so the bottom of the PV panel is 2­5 m above
ground to allow free access to the land area beneath [83].
Landscape architect: The common approach today to mitigate the visual impact on the
landscape is to investigate how RE structures will look in the landscape (graphic visuali­
sations as part of the EIA process). The visualisation is then evaluated by the city council
and approved if found acceptable. Subsequently, the primary tool to mitigate the inves­
tigated impact is establishing a hedge around the RE plant to hide the structures. This
approach is insufficient to break barrier 7. A team of landscape architects have recently
proposed that it should be a standard to involve people with competencies in landscape
architecture to improve RE plant aesthetics appearance and to mitigate the impact gen­
erally [84]. An example can be seen in Appendix G.1, where an architect has proposed a
more aesthetic layout of a PV plant in Skibstrup, Helsingør [85].
Public access (recreational use): The planning regulations, Green and Coastal Zone,
are to protect or secure public access to nature. Public concern about RE plants is that
they occupy areas and limit people’s access, but in reality, now public access to areas
covered with crops field is already limited. By converting fields into RE plants, it is actu­
ally possible to create a concrete gain by allowing access to areas (Barrier 2). In relation
toBarrier 5, another initiative that the developers have started to implement in plant lay­
outs is recreational facilities. Between Kolding and Vejle a PV plant is under development.
Here the developer planned to construct hiking trials, shelter camps, a pedestrian tunnel
under the highway for access purposes and more, as part of the plant design [86]. This can
be realised due to the conversion of fields to RE plants and the funds that can be obtained
through the project. This creates a gain for the community and the indirect neighbours.
Biodiversity: The biodiversity in Denmark is critically threatened, and the issue has
caught many people’s attention (barrier 6). The primary cause is human activities, e.g.
occupation of areas for growing cities and farmland. The process of converting land ar­
eas from farmland to RE plants provides an opportunity to change this development. The

general conversation from monoculture to grass/meadow areas with more hedges creates
better conditions for biodiversity. But also concrete biodiversity­improving measures are
being implemented by developers in RE projects. At the PV plant Blangslev, Næstved, the

The actions included an analysis of the local ecosystem (flora and fauna) [87]. Based on

11.2.4
Process options
Design panel: Special consultant, Jesper Madvig, says ”Citizens must feel included ­
early on”. Barrier 9, involvement of citizens early in the process was the key suggestion
from several interviewed informants. In combination with design option public access and
recreational areas, the citizens could be invited to take part in designing those facilities.
After a potential site has been identified (screened) and the relevant landowners are on
board the project, it could be an idea to apply for volunteers in the local community to
join a ”design panel” to give them influence on how to utilise a given area ­ instead of
presenting a finalised design at a public hearing late in the overall process, so they feel
excluded. By doing that, local citizens are involved and can influence the project.
Active identification of local ambassadors: To solve barrier 11 of missing persistence
within local initiators, the developer or municipality could initiate campaigns in selected
areas for identifying local ambassadors. The performed survey in the Municipality of Fre­
densborg showed a large positive support for a RE project. An information campaign with
the right guidance and prospects of opportunities could maybe get local citizens to vol­
unteer to be part of a project ­ especially if they get motivated in the form of influence or
similar (E.g. design panel).
Offer ownership shares to citizens: In Jutland, a developer successfully reduced the
number of protests to zero and provided the necessary support so the city councils unan­
imously approved the RE projects. This was done by offering ownership shares [89].
Solving barrier 8 requires citizens to commit to projects to show the politicians that there
are citizens in support of it. A means for that could be to offer citizens, within a given
radius, the opportunity to buy shares in the RE plant ­ up to 50% of the total ownership.
This is also a way to finance the project.

In this chapter are risks related to the development of RE project in North Sealand inves­

focuses on managing risks in renewable energy projects and contains a Risk Identifica­

literature research. The focus will not be on the specific case but on RE projects in gen­

Technical. Each risk is qualitatively rated on probability and consequence on a scale of 1
(low) to 5 (high). The outputs along with the associated numbering are listed in Appendix

Discussion
The accumulated applied methods ­ case study, interviews, survey and Development
Arena analysis ­ resulted in the identification of several barriers to the development of RE.
The characterisation of the identified barriers is diverse, ranging from technical, political,
social and environmental character, and all are to some degree entangled. This also
emphasises the complexity of the problem statement and the reason why the primary
actors (industry, landowner, municipalities and government) have not solved the problem.
The sub­results from the analysis will be broken down and specified for how they have
contributed to answering the raised research questions and where there are gaps in the
analysis.
1. What are the current governing constraints for developing wind farms and PV
plants in North Sealand?
By developing the case study, it was possible to clarify concrete constraints for develop­
ing a wind farm, PV plant or hybrid plant. From the initial screening and environmental
assessment, planning regulations and the EIA approach were identified, which constitute
the main constraint as part of the Danish legislation on the area of planning and usage of
land areas (see chapter 4 and 5). One of the findings from this screening was the number
of different planning regulations presented at the case site. If this is the expected ”nor­
mal” for a given area has not been investigated, but from the statement of an interview
urban planner, Nils­Erik Wandall: ”there are many rules and restrictions, especially here
in the capital region where the Fingerplan applies”, it is to be expected in the Municipality
of Fredensborg. The many regulations make it difficult to determine if a site is realistic to
develop, as land zone permits are given based on an evaluation of the relevant authori­
ties. However, by investigating the principle basis of the regulations, possible mitigation
actions were proposed. If the impact of the actions is sufficient requires an assessment
by the authorities, which have either been investigated. In perspective to the general de­
velopment of RE in North Sealand, the severe degree of planning regulations does also
highlight one of the reasons for the lacking development.
2. How will the constraints develop for wind farms and PV plants until 2030?
Just here at the turn of the year (2022/2023) the constraints changed dramatically with the
implementation of a new tariff system and the opening of development in the transport cor­
ridor. The effects of these changes are still to be accounted for. However, the aim of the
changes is to improve the attractiveness of developers to develop projects in ­ among oth­
ers ­ North Sealand. This effect was simulated by estimating the incurred costs from the
new tariff system for the case project, and how the percentage of CAPEX would change
depending if it were located in a consumer­dominant area or a production­dominant area.
The cost related to the tariff Connection free would increase from approximately 2% to
7% of CAPEX (see chapter 8).
The Danish Parliament have also asked governmental authorities (see Table A.1) to pre­
pare suggestions for legislative changes or other framework conditions to support the
development of RE to meet the goal of ”Danmark Kan Mere II”. The outcome of this
initiative is yet to be revealed ­ besides the possibility of using areas in the transport cor­
ridor. But more measures are to be expected. This development in constraints is driven
by dynamics between the relevant actors, which were investigated in the Development
Arena analysis. The identified controversies could prove as suggestions to areas where
changing constraints would be observed in the coming future (see chapter 10). Espe­

3. What is the process for developing a wind farm or PV plant in North Sealand –

The processing of developing a RE plant was investigated by developing possible layouts
for a plant by considering different aspects; planning regulations, distance requirements,
neighbour consideration, annual production estimations, etc. The process showed that a
plant could take many different forms and account for specific needs if necessary. The
general process is in principle the same across Denmark, but it was shown that the rela­
tively high population density raised issues regarding fulfilling the requirement distance ­
especially for WTGs, due to the many neighbours. From the interviews, it also becomes
clear that the lacking experience and resources in the Municipality of Fredensborg can
complicate the development process. Several process­related barriers were identified
linked to different actors, and these barriers would have to be engaged by the relevant
actors (See chapter 11).
4. Which concrete actions can be made to make a project more attractive and how
can they be implemented? It was possible to propose actions to mitigate most of the
identified barriers. From reviewing how recently planned or developed RE projects have
implemented actions in benefit of mitigating barriers, several opportunities were identified
(See chapter 11). Other proposed opportunities are based on the statements from the in­
terviewed informants. The concrete actions were divided into design options and process
options and were applied to the case site to see the impact. The design options were
easy to implement and assess the impact. It was more difficult to concretize the impact
of the process options as these are abstract in a theoretical desktop case study.
Potential of investigated case site
Each of the developed layouts for the case site possesses advantages and disadvan­
tages. As recommended a combination of Layout 1 and 2 seems plausible when it comes
to harvesting the advantages of both. The final layout would require implementing the
proposed actions to some degree, as illustrated in section 11.3. Determining the ”bal­
ance” of capacity and costs vs. local gain would require the involvement of, and an open
discussion between, all relevant landowners, the city council and the citizens. The eco­
nomic margin for the project was estimated to be around 7% of CAPEX and OPEX, but
this would leave the project sensitive to market changes. Also, the estimated financial
indicator, LCOE, went from 266 DKK/MWh to 284 DKK/MWh, which is close to the upper
limit of 302 DKK/MWh (according to IRENA for PV). Besides investigating actions to mit­
igate barriers ­ as done in chapter 11 ­ actions for optimising the plant layout would also
be advantageous to investigate, e.g. AEP, CAPEX or OPEX cost reduction, etc. This will
improve the overall business case and secure funds for providing a local gain.

The uncertainties of the thesis results are related to the case study and the many esti­
mated parameters. The case study focused on the conditions in relation to the site at
Rydlandsgård. Several of the results from this study will be partially applicable and de­

to be available capital ­ based on the given input parameters ­ for realising the proposed
actions. The relative costs of the proposed actions should be investigated to qualify what
can be expected to be realised in a given project. A solution to this could be to develop
a key indicator in terms of a percentage of a given project’s CAPEX and OPEX based
on the evaluation of multiple projects, which should be reserved for local benefits, for
example, all future projects should implement a cost of 5% of CAPEX to construct nature
and recreational areas and 3% of OPEX to maintain these.

Conclusion
In the near future, the insufficient transmission grid in Denmark can put a limitation on
developing further RE projects in the outskirts of the country, and shift focus to areas with
free grid capacity ­ like in North Sealand. The thesis has investigated the barriers related
to the missing development of RE in North Sealand and identified opportunities to mitigate
these barriers.
A total of 15 barriers were registered with ranging characteristics. Some of the barriers
can be considered static conditions for RE projects, and cannot be solved by technical
or process initiatives. A selection of actions was developed and categorised as design
options and process options and tested on the Rydlandsgård case. The actions were
inspired by recent research and by reviewing existing RE projects across Denmark. The
design options include; the use of the agrivoltaics to reduce the impact on agricultural
land, the use of landscape architects to improve the aesthetics appears of plant layouts,
the creation of public access to areas to provide a gain to the local community and the
use of biodiversity increasing measures to improve the nature in the developing area. The
process options are of more abstract character and focus on citizen involvement.
Based on the highlighted results, it is difficult to conclude the prospects for developing RE
in North Sealand as a whole, as many parameters and conditions influence the processes
and must be evaluated case by case ­ but none of the identified barriers indicates that it
should not be feasible. The investigated case site shows potential in relation to free grid
capacity, wind/solar resources, sizeable area, and support from citizens and landowners.
The primary barrier here is planning restrictions, but if the flexibility improves for using
areas in the transport corridor and the authorities can see the potential of implementing the
proposed actions, it could be a feasible case that could supply 7,000­11,000 households
with renewable electricity. And it would improve the local biodiversity and create access
to new recreational areas, benefiting the local community.
It is recommended that future RE projects in North Sealand improve the focus on the
involvement of local actors and ensure a local gain. The primary goal of implementing new
actions to a RE project should be to change the general perception of a RE plant from
a ”technical facility” to a ”community improving asset” (more nature, more recreational
areas, more green electricity, more funds for local projects, etc.), so that communities
around North Sealand demand RE plants to gain the benefits. This will change the general
dynamic of finding suitable areas. The survey and interviews with local actors such as the
landowner and the city council member showed high motivation and willingness to start a
RE project.
The thesis did not succeed in developing a proper ”Solution Framework”. The initial aim
was to create a solution framework using a morphology matrix to provide an overview
and usability of the thesis findings. This was not achieved, as the complexity and en­
tanglement of barriers and opportunities were too difficult to match. It would result in an
over­simplification if forced into a framework with the purpose of providing ”easy fixes”
to identify problems in a given case. It is necessary to emphasise that the thesis find­
ings are based primarily on a case study in the Municipality of Fredensborg, and there is
no evidence that these findings can be fully transferred to other municipalities or cases.
Measures to account for this limitation of the results are proposed in recommended future
work.

14.1
Recommendation for future work
The proposed solutions from this thesis are only in a preliminary state and should be
further consolidated if to be applied in practice. Also, as the cause of the failed objective
to develop a solution framework, it is necessary to develop the framework further to make
RE projects more attractive. The following tasks are proposed:
1) Two or three extra case studies at different sites across North Sealand should be un­
dertaken to consolidate the identified barriers and to fully understand the mechanism of
developing RE projects in North Sealand.
2) In connection to the case studies it could be interesting to interview more city council
members, as they represent a link between all actors; landowners, urban planners, citi­
zens etc.
3) All the design and process options should be further investigated to understand the
underlying diversity sub­solutions, and the economic parameter for each of them should
be estimated. For example, the costs for establishing 1 hectare of nature with specific
biodiversity properties and different variants of nature.
4) All barriers and solutions should systematically be developed into a framework and
tested on the developed case studies.
Also, as mentioned, essential framework conditions are currently under review by the
authorities and the coming changes can have a huge impact on developing a RE project.
These should also be implemented in the overall solution to assist actors in promoting the
development of RE in North Sealand.
14.2
Evaluation of project plan and process
Reflecting on the project process reveals two significant deviations from the original project
plan. The first is in relation to the prepared timetable. The reserved time for conducting
interviews was far too short, and it created major delays and pushed other activities to
be handled within a short period. This also resulted in limited review phase of results
and written chapters. The second deviation is the planned model for technical problem
solving, where the objective was to develop a more solid solution framework as a final
product of the thesis. But this was not possible with the achieved results and is therefore
listed as future work.
The involvement of European Energy, the Municipality of Fredensborg and Rydlandsgård
positively exceeded all expectations, and the results are primarily based on their input from
them. Data generation by interviews was a significant tool for conducting the thesis.

A.2
Mitigation of impacts
The following actions can be implemented to mitigate the impact of the identified critical
environmental issues.
Minimising visual impact and strengthening recreational properties within the Coastal
and Green zone:
The two main concern within the coastal and green zone is the visual impact on the shore­
line and the recreational possibilities close to city zones. Given the distance to the shore
and the topographical characterisation of the area, it would not be an issue to construct
the necessary facilities for a PV plant. However, tall wind turbines may be of concern and
a more comprehensive study of how a given wind turbine model will impact the shoreline
visual must be made. But from an initial estimate, it is believed the visual impact will be
close to insignificant for a 125­150m high wind turbine, considering the distance and to­
pography.
As mentioned, the areas around Rydlandsgård and Lerbjerggård are not of a recreational
character, due to highway and fields used for agricultural purposes. In practice, will the
local citizen not lose any recreational areas if the fields in the case site were converted
from agricultural purposes to accommodate a RE plant. In contrast, it would be possible
to access the area to some extent by establishing hiking routes around the RE plant or
similar features. A recreational synergy effect with the coming nature area Nivå Engfjord
could be an interesting possibility. Initiatives like this must be clarified with the landowners
and if it is possible to set aside areas for this purpose.
Design RE plant to support the landscape values:
The terrain within the case site has natural curves, hedges, streams, etc., which determine
the landscape’s visual impression. By orientating constructions (PV rows and turbines) to
suit the landscape best possible, it is possible to mitigate the visual impact. In Appendix
B.4 Figure B.17 is the line of sight from public roads illustrated (roughly estimated from
Google Street View).
In the area west of Helsingørmotorvej and on the public road, it is possible to overview
the whole area. From the highway itself, it is only possible to see the area closest ­

reaching approximately 100m­200m. From Langstrup village, it is possible to overview
the western/Northern half of the area. To mitigate the impact of the RE plant, the layout
should favour the view from the public road to the north, as the view from the highway
and Langstrup is limited. To maintain the landscape curved should, the PV rows be run­
ning North­South and wind turbines should be placed as close to Helsingørmotorvejen as
possible not to block the view.
Most of the area east of Helsingørmotorvejen can be seen from the short stretch between
Rydlandsgård and the bridge crossing Helsingørmotervejen. From Hørsholmvej, running
along the eastern boundary, it is possible to observe approximately the eastern haft of
the area. To mitigate the impact of the RE plant, a sufficient distance to the Hørsholm
should be considered, as Hørsholm is a heavily trafficked public road providing a good
view to the eastern part of the case site. The viewpoint between the Rydlandsgård and
the bridge is estimated to be insignificant, as it is a short stretch with limited traffic. PV
rows can be oriented according to other requirements, and wind turbines should also be
placed as close to Helsingørmotorvejen as possible.
The use of hedges to camouflage the RE plant should also be considered, even though it
will limit the view depth in the landscape. But limiting the RE plant to be within the natural
landscape curves (see black lines in Figure B.17 will mitigate the overall impact.
Combined RE plant and agriculture
The required space per wind turbine is limited ­ meaning it will not take up significant ar­
eas for agriculture. The concrete foundation can be submerged below the terrain, so it is
possible to use the land close to the turbine tower for agriculture or similar. In contrast,
conventional PV plants require space, and if a high production output is expected, it will
require the use of large areas. However, ongoing research projects investigate the pos­
sibility of exploiting the land under PV panels for agriculture. Implementing a PV setup
where the loss of agricultural areas is decreased would be a solution to minimise the
impact of the loss of particularly valuable agricultural land.
Difficult environmental and political conditions for wind turbines (125m­150m)
The proximity of a bird sanctuary with several critically endangered birds and good habitat
conditions for bats are circumstances that cannot be changed. Different technical solu­
tions can be implemented to avoid bird and bat collisions, such as painted wind turbine
blades or speakers transmitting noise to scare off bats. The actual risk of a collision of crit­
ically endangered birds must be investigated to determine the required countermeasures
or a possible ban on wind turbines in the area. Also, the local municipal plan indicates
political resistance to larger wind turbines within the Municipality of Fredensborg. The
mitigation of this issue will require a more comprehensive dialogue with the city council to
uncover the possibilities.
The analysis of areas where wind turbines can be placed to comply with distance re­
quirements showed limited possibilities. However, complying with the requirements were
possible, but covering the required compensations to entitled neighbours may be an is­
sue. To mitigate this issue would be an internal aspect of economics and the possibility
of sourcing shorter wind turbines, as this will limit the number of affected neighbouring
households.

2) Compensation to direct neighbours has been standard in many developed projects, but
this does not apply for indirect neighbours ­ who live further away – and Jesper Madvig
believes this misalignment would be a problem in the future, as all local citizens need to
feel they gain something:
”There was an analysis of how these compensation schemes had worked. It
turned out that for the projects that went through, the citizens who had been
compensated and who lived close to the project were perfectly satisfied, while
it was the citizens further from the project who felt that their access to nature
had been ruined or they had to look at the project when they were out cy­
cling and were not compensated, they felt they lost something and were the
ones who were still upset afterwards. It is our opinion that local communities
should generally be compensated to a greater extent. This will, among other
things, ensure that the citizens who are not directly affected – and thereby not
compensated – will also increase their support for the projects.”
(Translated from: ”Der var en analyse af hvordan de her grønpuljer og bonu­
sordning havde virket. Det viste sig for de projekter der gik igennem, der var
de borgere som var blevet kompenseret, altså dem som boede tæt på projek­
tet at de var fint tilfredse, mens det var de borgerne i kanten rundt om projektet
der følte at deres adgang til naturen var ødelagt eller skulle kigge på projek­
tet når de var ude at cykle og ikke var kompenseret, de følte at have mistet
noget og var dem der stadig var sure efterfølgende. Det er vores holding at
lokalsamfundene generelt skal kompenseres i højere grad. Dette vil bl.a. be­
virke, at de borgere der ikke er direkte påvirket – og derved ikke kompenseres
– også vil øge deres opbakning til projekterne”)
