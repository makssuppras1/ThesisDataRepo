Abstract 
Asphalt pump tracks are paved circuits of rollers and banked turns that have emerged in the past 
decade as multifunctional sports facilities for cyclists, skateboarders, scooter riders, and other 
action seekers. Despite their growing presence, asphalt pump tracks remain a relatively unex-
plored area within civil infrastructure. They lack clear design and maintenance guidelines, with 
the current design and construction approaches being achieved empirically. This thesis provides 
an extensive overview of asphalt pump tracks, focusing on their unique geometrical and structural 
characteristics. The research includes a comprehensive literature review that not only covers the 
existing documentation on pump tracks, but also examines guidelines, specifications, and stand-
ards on related infrastructure design, such as BMX race tracks, skateparks, and similar. Addition-
ally, maintenance techniques for pump tracks and analogous facilities are reviewed to establish 
a knowledge base for practical applications. 
A novel methodology for evaluating the condition of asphalt pump tracks was developed, inspired 
by the findings in the literature and during field observations. This methodology was applied to 
assess the functional and structural condition of 20 existing pump tracks in Denmark and Latvia, 
using a set of 15 criteria. The oldest surveyed track was opened to the public in 2017, the newest 
in 2024. However, no correlation between the overall condition and age of the tracks was ob-
served. The most common damages across the inspected asphalt pump tracks are associated 
with insufficient surface water drainage and pavement-edge drop-off/embankment erosion. 
Furthermore, a mechanical model of the banked pump track turn was developed using the Finite 
Element Method (FEM) to investigate potential failure mechanisms in the material layers, aiming 
to correlate observed cracks on top of the banked turns with underlying structural issues. The 
created engineering model did not highlight any failures in material layers that would explain the 
documented cracking damage mechanism. Cracking on the berm platform was observed at half 
of the inspected tracks. The findings of this thesis provide a foundational reference for the future 
design, construction, and maintenance of asphalt pump tracks, promoting a more rational and 
systematic approach to their development.

1 Introduction 
This chapter provides the necessary information to understand the world of asphalt pump tracks. 
It introduces the fundamental terminology, explains what kind of infrastructure this thesis is about, 
outlines its history and evolution, and describes the current status of the design, construction 
process, and maintenance of asphalt pump tracks. Based on the background and motivation to 
conduct this study, the study objectives and scope are presented. 
1.1 
Background 
1.1.1 Deﬁnition 
 
Figure 1.1: Asphalt pump track in Hvidovre, Den-
mark. Aerial view. (Photo by Velosolutions) 
Figure 1.2: Asphalt pump track in Hvidovre, 
Denmark. Overall view. (Photo by Author) 
A pump track (Figure 1.1 & Figure 1.2) is a continuous loop of rollers and banked turns that can 
be ridden without pedaling or pushing. The term ‘pump’ originates from the method these tracks 
are meant to be ridden – forward movement is maintained by upwards (pulling) body movements 
when ascending and downwards (pushing) movements when descending. Such a continuous 
sequence of movements is referred to as pumping (see Figure 1.3) due to its apparent similarities 
with using a manual air pump. In other words, the human body’s center of gravity is continuously 
being changed in reference to a bicycle or other riding equipment.[1], [2], [3] 
 
Figure 1.3: How to ride a pump track. [4] 
The continuity of the track in the form of a loop is a fundamental feature of any pump track. The 
track has no start or end and can technically be ridden in both directions. Doing a full loop means 
no elevation change. This is ensured by constructing the tracks in rather flat areas. The width of 
the riding surface is usually around 1 to 3 meters. It is designed as a single trail, meaning no 
riders are pumping next to each other at a given time.

Rollers can also be called bumps and banked turns are usually identified as berms. Rollers and 
berms are the two backbone elements of any pump track (see Figure 1.4). They are rhythmically 
connected to each other in a circular entity. There may be multiple lines designed to provide var-
iation in a compact area. A set of rollers is often referred to as a rhythm section. Rhythm is defined 
as smooth repetitive oscillations (e.g. a sine wave). The better the oscillations, the better the flow. 
Flow is defined as a predictable rhythm, based on a regular pattern. It is usually said that all good 
pump tracks have a continuous, consistent rhythmic flow and no flat spots.[5], [6] 
 
Figure 1.4: Set of rollers going into a left-sided berm. (Photo by Author) 
Pump tracks are suitable for all sizes of bicycles, skateboards, longboards, rollerblades, scooters, 
pushbikes, mountainboards, even wheelchairs, and other wheeled sports equipment. 
Riders of all ages and skill levels can enjoy pump tracks and practice basic skills such as carrying 
momentum, improving balance, and building speed. As riders progress, the tracks offer options 
for more challenges without altering the construction. Hence the tracks allow beginners to learn 
how to ride while also serving as a training ground for professional athletes as riding can get 
physically and technically demanding.[3] 
Asphalt pump track is a term used for pump tracks whose riding surface has been paved with 
asphalt. Such tracks are the focus of this project. Other than the surface type, all pump tracks 
usually share the same characteristics, no matter if the track is made of dirt, crushed stone, wood, 
concrete, fiberglass, composites, or other materials.[5] 
In summary, the pump track is an innovative recreational sports facility that smoothly combines 
fun and skill building. Unlike other cycling infrastructure, pump tracks are designed to be used 
without pedaling. 
1.1.2 History and Evolution 
It is believed that the concept of pump track has merged ideologically from classical skateparks 
and practically from hard-pack BMX (bicycle motocross) race courses. Pump tracks are some-
times referred to as miniature BMX tracks. 
The development of skateparks started in the 1960s. Until now they are made to be used by 
rather experienced and professional riders. The complexity of features carries along a high risk 
of injuries. These are the reasons why the range of their target audience has been quite nar-
row.[7], [8] 
BMX racing has been around since the 1980s. It was integrated into the Union Cycliste Interna-
tionale (UCI), the official governing agency for sports cycling, as a discipline in 1993, and has

been an Olympic discipline since 2008. It features tracks that are considerably larger than pump 
tracks these days. They are designed to host a mass start of several riders lined up at a starting 
gate side by side at an 8m high start hill, therefore the tracks are much wider than pump tracks. 
Rhythm sections of a BMX course are usually designed to be technically challenging to accom-
modate professional racing. They also have a definitive start and finish and require pedaling. A 
typical BMX race course is seen in Figure 1.5. Tracks are usually 400m long and have 4 straights 
where speeds up to 60 km/h are reached.[6], [9] 
 
Figure 1.5: BMX race course. [10] 
Considering the constraints of skateparks and BMX racing, pump tracks appeared in the early 
2000s, built out of the soil in enthusiasts’ backyards. The rollers and berms were spaced in a way 
that made pedaling inefficient, forcing the riders to carry momentum using their body movements. 
Those backyard facilities could be constructed by hand, required less area and soil, and were 
less prone to injuries. Consequently, pump tracks were more attractive to adventure seekers of 
all ages and skill levels. The intensity of the skill building was found to be a big advantage over 
the typical bicycle rides. Riding a pump track contributes to skills such as: 
 Anaerobic fitness 
 Proprioception and bike control 
 Vision 
 Cornering 
 Pressure control 
and also creates a solid foundation for more advanced skills like manualing and jumping.[5] All of 
this has built a foundation for the continuous evolution of pump tracks over the last 20+ years. 
With every freshly built dirt pump track came not only excitement but also maintenance chal-
lenges. Many pump tracks started as a success but lacked maintenance activities, and became 
dominated by grass. Besides that, compacted soil is susceptible to breaking down due to weather 
and user-based erosion.[6] 
In 2012 the innovation came around – the first ever pump track with asphalt surface was con-
structed in Chur, Switzerland (Figure 1.6). The track is still operational. A few years before that 
there have been attempts to build in concrete, but asphalt worked out better. Several significant 
advancements came along with the introduction of asphalt [11], [12]: 
 Increased durability, safe from weather and vandalism with minimal maintenance required

 Due to the smooth and hard surface pump track can be ridden not only by bicycles but also

 Good grip and hard surface give more possibilities to speed up and perform tricks, i.e.

since then the event has been held annually on a different asphalt pump track for each edition.[13]

1.1.3 Modern Asphalt Pump Tracks 
Design 
Over the years of evolution, pump tracks have become not only an odd-geometry asphalt strip on 
a piece of land attractive to small groups of enthusiasts but very often also a significant architec-
tural element in existing urban and non-urban environments. Moreover, it not only stands as an 
addition to already existing sports and recreational infrastructure in the area but also serves a 
social function by providing a fresh air open space for people to interact with each other and be 
active. 
Thus, to satisfy the needs of a modern pump track, as of current design trends it features partic-
ular elements, such as (but not exclusively): 
 Asphalt paved track (usually 2 meters in width) in the form of a loop of rollers and berms 
 Rhythm sections with varying heights of roller tips 
 Berms with consistent configuration – same relative height (usually around 1 meter) and 
outer radius (usually around 4 meters) within a track, to ensure consistent flow 
 Colored safety lines on top of berm faces to help keep a safe trajectory 
 Safety barriers where necessary 
 Water drainage system 
 Track and territory lighting 
 Green adjacent areas 
 Benches, bicycle racks, trash bins, information boards, toilet facilities, and other landscap-
ing 
 Optional track element(s) for additional complexity – jump line, wall ride, grind box, etc. 
Having Velosolutions as a collaborator in this project and also being responsible for the design 
and construction of over 640 asphalt pump tracks all around the world, it can be said with great 
confidence that there are no two identical asphalt tracks built. Due to their dynamic configuration, 
they are all designed from scratch to align with the existing site terrain, possibly saving trees and 
other objects in the area. 
The asphalted circuit is designed in a way that the entire riding area can be well visible from any 
given point. There cannot be any flat areas on a track to ensure rainwater drainage as well as 
serve the track’s purpose. There are no specific vertical radiuses for the shape of rollers and berm 
faces defined at the design stage. Instead, that sort of geometry relies on experienced construc-
tion personnel. Normally the rollers are rather shallow with a casual inclination being around 10° 
to 20°, while berms at the top can reach an inclination of up to 70°. A typical asphalt pump track 
layout can be seen in Figure 1.7. This track consists of 7 rhythm sections and 7 banked turns. A 
sample section view of a set of rollers is given in Figure 1.8 and a banked turn in Figure 1.9.

Overall, the pavement structure of the pump tracks has so far been somewhat replicating the 
conventional road structures. The excavated natural subgrade is topped with a layer (ca. 300mm) 
of drainage sand, on which then the desired track terrain is made using dolomite aggregates, 
gravel, recycled asphalt, or similar. The final geometry is then topped with asphalt concrete. A mix 
with a maximum aggregate size of 8mm is usually used. Since there are currently no design 
requirements or guidelines for asphalt pump tracks, the composition of the substructure tends to 
be rather flexible. Therefore, it is usually chosen depending on the material availability at a specific 
location. A typical cross-section of the paved track is shown in Figure 1.10. 
 
Figure 1.10: 3D cross-section of a typical straight [18] 
The project phases of an asphalt pump track are similar to other casual construction projects. It 
is initiated by a tender or sometimes action is taken by communities, or pump track consultants 
sell it as a product. After an agreement for financing has been reached, the conceptual and de-
tailed designs are developed. When the necessary site investigations have been conducted and 
project documentation is approved, construction can be started. What differentiates pump tracks 
from other construction projects is that due to their uniqueness, the companies usually offer full-
service cycle, including planning, design, construction, maintenance, and even fund sourcing and 
marketing.

Construction process 
The construction process of an asphalt pump track depending on its size may last anywhere from 
a few weeks to a few months. It can be divided into 4 main stages [19], [20]: 
1. Site preparation (Figure 1.11) 
 Securing and fencing 
 Topsoil removal 
 Drainage system and lighting cable installation 
2. Raw shaping (Figure 1.12) 
 Shape and compact rollers, berms, and other track features with sub-base material 
 Lay and compact base 
 Any additional installations like geo-stabilizing webbing, etc. 
3. Asphalt works (Figure 1.13) 
 Asphalt laying and compaction 
 Surface painting where necessary (i.e. safety lines) 
4. Landscaping (Figure 1.14) 
 Greening and planting 
 Fitting information boards, seating, lighting, fencing, trash bins, etc.

Maintenance 
In general asphalt pump tracks are considered low-maintenance infrastructure with most works 
being grass cutting and maintaining landscaping and drainage systems. A formal maintenance 
plan from the contractor would usually be issued to the person in charge of the track. This includes 
information on how often the track should be visually inspected and what elements should be 
checked. However, these inspection schemes are rather simple, and systematic maintenance is 
usually not performed. Due to all asphalt tracks being relatively new (up to 12 years of age), they 
have not reached their end-of-life or a stage when complete restoration would be necessary. The 
need for maintenance also depends on the local weather conditions, how much use the track gets 
as well as the construction quality. Some of the typical damages reported by riders are water and 
sand/soil accumulation on track surfaces, slope erosion, and cracking in asphalt. 
1.2 
Motivation 
Asphalt pump tracks are riding tracks made up of a circuit of rollers and banked turns. Over the 
last decade, they have been getting increasingly popular – they are hotspots for all kinds of riders 
who seek a fun and action-filled sports experience. Pump tracks are layered structures consisting 
of an asphalt surface on top of a substructure, which defines the overall shape and geometry of 
the track. Due to their purpose, pump tracks are characterized by complex geometry and loading 
conditions. 
Despite their popularity, they lack comprehensive design and maintenance standards or even 
general guidelines; the current approaches have been achieved empirically and vary from project 
to project. Therefore, various construction materials are used, ranging from local natural or stabi-
lized soil to gravel, mineral aggregates, or recycled asphalt for base layers, topped with asphalt 
concrete surfaces. Moreover, their complex geometry makes it difficult to implement conventional 
construction equipment, as well as test methods for quality assurance, e.g., dynamic plate tests

2.1 
Design of Pump Tracks 
2.1.1 Geometry 
Apart from the key elements listed in Section 1.1.3 that are crucial for a modern asphalt pump 
track to make the track visit enjoyable, there is also a set of rules and properties that a pump track 
must comply with to ensure a good riding experience on the track itself. It is mentioned earlier in 
Section 1.1.1 that all good pump tracks have a continuous, consistent rhythmic flow, which is 
based on a perceptible pattern. However, guidelines for designing a good pump track (from both 
geometrical and structural perspectives) are not well-documented, unlike those for roads and 
other transport infrastructure subjected to traffic loads. Over the years, standards and guidelines 
have specified acceptable slope inclinations, curvature radii, widths, and many other parameters 
based on many years of experience with such conventional infrastructure. 
Meanwhile, the geometrical design of asphalt pump tracks has evolved primarily through trial and 
error. In the pump track industry, the principle “by riders, for riders” has been the driving force to 
build the first publically available tracks, and has therefore played a significant role in shaping 
pump track development until today. While there is no comprehensive documentation, a handful 
of professionals worldwide possess an intuitive understanding of what constitutes an effective 
pump track design geometry, backed by practical experience. Despite the lack of formal guide-
lines, there have been attempts to describe the optimal geometry for a successful track both 
qualitatively and quantitatively. 
Further down some of the geometrical requirements as suggested by pump track building profes-
sionals are listed. This does not mean that every pump track is designed based on these, but can 
at least be taken as a good starting point. 
Rollers 
It is stated in Section 1.1.1 that rollers are one of the backbone elements of every pump track. To 
make them rollable, they should follow something similar to a sine wave function – as smooth and 
round as possible, with no holes, bumps, or flat spots. 
Roller-straight ratio is a quantitative measure to follow to make rollers rideable. As a rule of thumb, 
they should be 1 unit tall and 10 units apart from each other (see Figure 2.1), meaning that if the 
relative height of the rollers is 0.5m, they should be 5.0m apart from each other, for instance. In 
reality, the existing tracks rarely follow a constant roller pattern but are rather made more chal-
lenging and appealing by following a variable pattern. Thereby, this ratio can be played around 
with, but in general, if the rollers become too tight, it is hard to maintain speed. On the other hand, 
if the rollers are made too far apart, it becomes hard to generate speed.[5], [21]

Other essential design elements that make the track rideable are [20], [21]: 
 Berm rollers. At both ends of each berm as marked in Figure 2.4 there should be a roller to

 All berms should also have a rest platform on top as well as a lower pavement as a curva-

 Track design layout should be such that it encourages a one-way flow naturally, to avoid

UCI Pump Track Guide [22] 
This guide is a short document defining the sport of Pump Track, accompanying and explaining 
the UCI regulations, as well as providing a set of guidelines to advise national federations as they 
attempt to integrate the sport. 
It is emphasized in the text that the guide does not contain specific dimensions for various track 
types as such tracks are not built to any particular standard. Instead, the track's size, feature 
spacing, and heights vary for each pump track. 
To accommodate racing events, UCI recommends a minimum track size of 800m2. For interna-
tional racing events, certain geometrical requirements are defined: 
 2.0m minimum track width  
 130cm minimum relative berm height  
 Variable roller heights from 30cm to a minimum of 80cm  
 Variable roller distances from 3.0m to 10.0m (this means a roller-straight ratio from 1:10 to 
at least 1:12) 
 The speed must be maintained by pumping, and not by gravity, meaning no vertical drops 
within the track are allowed 
 It is recommended to have some space around the track to set up an event (e.g. staging 
area for riders, place for spectators, etc.) 
Approval of the track from UCI is needed for the UCI Pump Track World Championships to be 
held.  
It is important to mention that the objective of this project is not to evaluate potential enhance-
ments to the geometry of asphalt pump tracks. Instead, it is presumed that the current geometries 
represent an optimal design in terms of rideability. The focus is to conduct a structural perfor-
mance assessment of these geometries to understand their effectiveness and longevity within the 
intended applications.

2.1.2 Structural Design 
Some paved pump track specifications are publicly available, and here are presented some in-

clay underneath the pavement. To mitigate this, the base material should have a high clay and

All stones in all features regardless of size should be angular with a good share of fine particles. 
Subbase material should be laid and compacted in 300mm thick layers – clay-based, high stone 
content, no organic material, and dry. The better the layers are compacted the more stable the 
features will be and the less likely there will be movement in the structure causing fractures or 
subsidence. 
Base material should be laid to a thickness of 150-200mm of Type 1 material (25–45mm in size). 
Again the higher compaction the more stable the structure. Limestone, dolomite, or granite-based 
crushed stone is the best material for the base. 
Asphalt aggregate size of 6mm or 8mm for wearing course; 6mm is smoother and more closed, 
while 8 mm provides a slightly rougher, more open course and can be grippier in wet conditions. 
The wearing course must be well compacted and sealed to ensure no loose stones within the 
asphalt can come loose as the surface wears down. The material should be an open-graded 
porous type asphalt which allows water to seep through the two layers, as such it is important that 
the base is sufficiently open-graded (and thick enough) to allow water to continue to disperse 
through the layer. Otherwise, the water will gather under the asphalt layer and with help from frost 
heave and natural settle is likely to fracture due to movement under the asphalt layer.

The side slopes of the track’s features should be as low and smooth as possible (1:3 minimum)

UCI Pump Track Guide [22] 
When it comes to structural aspects of pump track design, this guide does not provide any infor-
mation apart from mentioning the possible construction materials – asphalt, concrete, dirt, wood, 
or fiberglass. Asphalt is established as the best option following a recommendation from UCI 
Cycling Regulations that a pump track should have a compact, hard surface that withstands 
weather and erosion. Therefore, asphalt is recommended due to its various great advantages: 
 Durability – asphalt is arguably the least prone to wear and tear among other material op-
tions while still providing all the other necessary characteristics 
 Cleanliness – with a properly designed and constructed track the riding surface can be kept 
clean passively by the rainwater drainage system 
 Low rolling resistance and high traction – the seamless surface of asphalt provides a 
smooth yet grippy experience, allowing riders to maintain control in turns and maneuvers 
(grip to be considered when painting the asphalt surface) 
 All-weather suitability – asphalt can survive all types of weather conditions and still provide 
the desired surface properties 
 No maintenance is needed other than mowing the lawn from the side slopes 
Apart from general benefits, asphalt pump tracks are often compared specifically to concrete 
pump tracks. In that case, there are some additional advantages to the above listed [2], [12], [23]: 
 Repairs – cracks and imperfections can be sealed or patched up easily on asphalt while 
repairing concrete cracks is more complex and costly and may require extensive work. 
 Cost-effectiveness – concrete is generally a more expensive construction material than 
asphalt in terms of volume cost along with the extensive formwork required. 
 Adaptability – with asphalt adjustments to track layout are simpler, while with concrete it 
can involve significant demolition and reconstruction. 
 Noise reduction – asphalt has a higher sound absorption coefficient than concrete, resulting 
in a quieter riding experience and lower noise pollution. 
 Aesthetics – asphalt is considered to blend better with natural surroundings, while concrete 
may appear more industrial and stark.

Typical maximum sizes of aggregate used for asphalt bikeways are 5mm, 7mm, 10mm, and 
14mm. The finer the size, the more closed the asphalt surface is in terms of texture. Mixes of 
smaller aggregate size are used for surfacing layers, as these can be laid thinner than larger size

Conventional traffic roads are subject to loads of high magnitude and their number of repetitions.

sufficiently worked by its light traffic to reduce the air void content. This may result in the surfacing 
becoming prematurely brittle and more susceptible to raveling and cracking. Therefore it is im-
portant to use special asphalt mixes designed for light traffic applications. Such mixes are usually 
gap-graded and more bitumen binder than normal is added (5 to 7% by mass).

2.2.2 BMX Race Track Design 
While the tracks for BMX racing are generally made of materials other than asphalt, many tracks 
have start hills and corners surfaced with asphalt (Figure 2.9) or high-grip concrete. It is not strictly 
necessary as building the track entirely out of compacted soil can save money. However, the 
benefit of using asphalt or concrete in those key areas of the track is that the necessary amount 
of maintenance the track will need is reduced.[29], [30] 
 
 
Figure 2.9: BMX racing tracks with asphalted starting hill and turns [29], [30] 
UCI Regulations [31], [32] 
The design framework for BMX tracks developed by UCI features asphalt surfacing and hence is 
relevant to asphalt pump tracks. 
The structure of the race course rhythm sections is generally made of crushed stone surface and 
consists of layers: 
 Subbase – clay-based, dry, non-organic material with good compaction level. Built and 
compacted in 300mm layers to the necessary height. 
 Base – type 1 stone of 25/40mm fraction, scalpings, crushed concrete, or stone-based 
material which compacts well to give a sealed surface, laid to a compacted thickness of 
100 to 150mm. 
 Surface layer – limestone or granite crushed stone with fraction 0/10 or 0/6mm, laid to a 
compacted thickness of 100mm. 
Meanwhile, for Continental Championships, BMX Racing World Cup, World Championships, and 
Olympic Games, there is a requirement from UCI that the surface material of the turns and finish 
area must be asphalt, concrete, or bricks. Thereby the BMX Racing Track Guide issued by UCI 
specifies the cross-section for corners as (see also Figure 2.10): 
 Subbase – same as for rhythm sections. 
 Base – same as for rhythm sections. 
 Surface – asphalt in 2 layers. A 75mm thick binding course with an aggregate size of 32mm, 
and a 50mm thick wearing course with an aggregate size of 6mm. Alternatively, concrete 
can also be used, as long as the surface is prepared such that it offers a high level of grip. 
Paving stones with a high grip surface can be used as long as they are flat and installed to 
prevent gaps and holes between stones. However, such corners do require regular mainte-
nance to keep the stones in place.

2.2.4 Odd-shaped Pavement Structures – Automotive Proving Grounds 
Automotive proving grounds, also called automotive test tracks, are specially built facilities that 
vehicle manufacturers use for road vehicle testing. Often one of the features there is a circular

Infrastructure Maintenance 
Maintenance of asphalt pump tracks is important to ensure the functionality of the facility during 
its expected lifetime. A typical surfacing life expectancy of dense graded asphalt (or asphalt con-
crete, AC) is 10 to 20 years, while a fine gap-graded asphalt (FGGA, also called light duty asphalt) 
from 15 to 20 years.[26] Apart from functionality, economic considerations are also on the agenda 
of designers and contractors. Cost-effective designs of pump tracks should take into considera-
tion not only the initial construction costs but also those associated with the maintenance and 
rehabilitation of the structures. Maintenance and rehabilitation costs are directly or indirectly re-
lated to [26]: 
 Pavement loads 
 Environmental factors 
 Material properties 
 Construction practices 
 Pavement performance 
 Maintenance treatments 
If sufficient capital funds are provided for a well-designed and constructed track, then that nor-
mally helps to minimize ongoing long-term maintenance costs.[26] 
National Asphalt Pavement Association (NAPA, USA [27]) claims that a properly designed and 
constructed asphalt pavement will require minimal maintenance, assuming that an appropriate 
mix design is used. Providing proper drainage is also one of the most important factors affecting 
pavement performance. A significant advantage over concrete pavement is asphalt’s ability to be 
repaired quickly and inexpensively. Maintenance of an asphalt pavement is generally divided into 
two categories: 
1. Preventive maintenance – performed on a regular basis to remove vegetation and debris, 
improve drainage, and resolve any unsafe conditions.

2.3.1 Condition Monitoring 
Inspections shall be the basis for preventive and corrective maintenance.[43] This section pro-

Pavement Condition Index (PCI) 
The most widely used method for quantifying road pavement condition is the Pavement Condition 
Index (PCI). The PCI method was originally developed by the United States Army Corps of Engi-
neers as an airfield pavement rating system but is nowadays modified for road pavements and 
standardized by the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) [44]. It is a numerical 
rating that ranges from 0 to 100, with 0 being the worst possible pavement condition and 100 
being the best possible condition. It provides a measure of the surface condition of the pavement 
based on visual surveys, indicating the structural integrity and surface operational condition (such 
as roughness and safety). The PCI is used to determine maintenance and repair needs and to 
monitor the rate of pavement deterioration. 
The PCI for the entire pavement system (for relatively small systems) or for a representative 
sample (for larger pavement systems) is acquired taking into account the type of distress and its 
severity and quantity (Figure 2.15). It is then calculated from those three parameters using an 
iterative algorithm. 
Pavement distresses are external indicators of pavement deterioration caused by loading, envi-
ronmental factors, construction deficiencies, or a combination of all. For asphalt-surfaced roads 
distress types are: 
1. Alligator (fatigue) cracking  
2. Bleeding 
3. Block cracking 
4. Bumps and sags 
5. Corrugation (washboard) 
6. Depression 
7. Edge cracking 
8. Joint reflection cracking 
9. Lane/shoulder drop-off 
10. Longitudinal and transverse cracking 
11. Patching and utility cut patching 
12. Polished aggregate 
13. Potholes 
14. Railroad crossing 
15. Rutting 
16. Shoving 
17. Slippage cracking 
18. Swell 
19. Raveling 
20. Weathering (surface wear) 
 
The severity of a particular distress type is defined as low (L), medium (M), or high (H) according 
to the classification specified in the ASTM standard. 
Quantity of a particular distress type is measured as area in square meters or as length in linear 
meters, or by counting the number in case of the potholes.

 Pavement edge drop-off 
 Surface irregularities associated with tree roots or soil movements 
 Construction errors

of these methods could also be conveniently used for pump tracks. These investigations are con-

3 Inspection Methodology 
This chapter presents the methodology for condition evaluation of asphalt pump tracks, it consists 
of four parts. Firstly, a surveying approach is outlined based on simple principles. Presented next 
is the inspection procedure and datasheet, followed by a surveying analysis. Finally, the limita-
tions of the surveying method are discussed. 
3.1 
Surveying Approach 
Since no existing asphalt pump track inspection reports were found during the Literature Review 
in Section 2, it was apparent that a suitable way of surveying such tracks must be developed. 
Based on the background and literature review three basic principles have been established for 
the development of a comprehensive pump track surveying scheme: 
 Procedure must be suitable for any asphalt pump track as described in Section 1.1.3 Mod-
ern Asphalt Pump Tracks. 
 No tools are necessary for inspection; visual surveying is sufficient for executing the 
scheme (due to the complexity of the structure and to encourage more frequent surveying). 
 Surveying activities do not interfere with the daily operation of the track. 
Asphalt pump tracks have been constructed all over the world, meaning that local design prac-
tices, construction culture, material availability, and economic considerations always play a role.  
Therefore, the created surveying approach is not meant to check compliance with any regulations, 
safety standards, design specifications, etc. Instead, this methodology has naturally formed as a 
combination of the relevant condition monitoring practices discussed in Section 2.3.1, and my 
initial observations during the visits to some existing asphalt pump tracks. In other words, the best 
knowledge from theoretical research is taken and applied to the unexplored medium of pump 
tracks. Once a European Standard or similar will be released for public use asphalt pump tracks, 
then the investigation can become more targeted. Meanwhile, the inspections conducted within 
this project are a rather custom approach based on my theoretical and practical research. 
As a result, a simple inspection scheme was developed. It consists of 15 criteria, all grouped into 
three categories based on their role within the pump track – functional, structural, and additional. 
Again, as mentioned in the Literature Review, such criteria and categories are inter-related. For 
instance, insufficient surface water drainage may be a structural error but it also highly affects the 
functionality of the track, surface wear may be an indication of a poorly designed asphalt mix but 
it also interferes with the functionality.

1. Sediments, leaves, debris, etc. on riding surfaces and fall zones

4. Surface water drainage errors 
Structural: 
5. Longitudinal cracking on the berm platform 
6. Longitudinal or transverse cracking on other parts of the track 
7. Surface settlement on the berm platform 
8. Unwanted deformations on other parts of the track

3.2 
Inspection Procedure and Datasheet 
In practice the track inspection procedure consisted of several steps: 
1. Desk research of the pump track I plan to visit. 
2. Planning the trip to the location of the track. 
3. Arriving at the track location and getting acquainted with the surroundings. 
4. Initial visual inspection of the track to identify the main problem areas. 
5. Capturing damages and the track in general in photos. 
6. Taking any necessary notes for later reference. 
7. Taking any necessary measurements of the track. 
8. Have a chat with the users of the track if any are present. 
9. Pumping a few laps of the track myself. 
10. Processing the acquired inspection data and photos. 
The measurements done on some of the pump tracks were the width of the paved surface within 
the rhythm section, the arc length of the curved berm from top to bottom, and the inclination of

3.3 
Surveying Analysis 
Conducted pump track inspections are meant to be beneficial for each individual pump track, and 
also for pump track as a type of infrastructure in general. 
Firstly, an average score for each pump track can be drawn as a whole as well as per category 
(functional, structural, additional), taking into account all of the 15 criteria used in the scheme. 
This score will also range from “0” to “2”. That way one track can also be compared to another. 
And certainly, since surveying shall be the basis for preventive and corrective maintenance, the 
acquired results must be linked to maintenance activities that the track condition demands. There-
fore, each score rating is also an indication of whether the action has to be taken to address the 
problem or not. As a general rule, the inspection scheme scoring system is designed in a way 
that: 
 “0” corresponds to a desired condition of the element mentioned in the criterion. 
 “1” means that the condition is acceptable but it may become “2” and require rehabilitation 
in the near future. 
 “2” means that the problem restricts the element from serving its intended purpose com-
pletely or to a very high extent, and preventive or corrective maintenance has to be done 
to go back to normal. 
Secondly, the scores can be averaged among a batch of tracks for each criterion separately. Then 
the most problematic damage types of asphalt pump tracks in general can be identified. Those 
can point out not only the aspects where more maintenance is generally required but also make 
us reconsider some existing design and/or construction techniques to avoid the problem in the 
first place. 
3.4 
Surveying Limitations 
The criteria in the inspection scheme were created from the relevant infrastructure (pump track 
and other) condition monitoring knowledge collected during the Literature Review, Section 2.3.1. 
However, slip resistance (grip) is a parameter that would be very valuable to investigate on asphalt 
pump tracks but it has not been done in this project. It is generally measured as a friction coeffi-
cient of the asphalt surface and special equipment is required to determine that. Conventional 
grip testing devices that are towed behind a motorized vehicle are not suitable for the geometry 
of asphalt pump tracks. Instead, small portable equipment should be used. The Portable Skid 
Resistance Tester (Figure 3.3), also known as the British Pendulum Tester, is one of the options. 
It is used to test the frictional resistance of roads, their horizontal markings, and other surfaces. 
The Micro GripTester (Figure 3.4) is another, more novel option. It is a portable, compact,

lightweight push mode continuous friction measuring device designed for areas unsuitable for 
larger friction testers. It was designed as a replacement for the classic British Pendulum and is 
not affected by curves or inclines. Therefore, the Micro GripTester would be a very appropriate 
device for use in asphalt pump tracks. It is already being used to investigate skidding accidents 
in NASCAR racing and for other similar applications.[49], [50] 
By obtaining the grip data from different asphalt pump tracks and linking it to riders’ feedback and 
the statistics of accidents, the most suitable coefficient of friction for asphalt pump tracks could 
be determined. That can then be used to compose a guideline for the most suitable asphalt mix 
as currently the design and construction practices and materials vary around the world, conse-

properties are directly related to the pavement surface texture, therefore the surface properties

5 Engineering Model 
This chapter presents a numerical model of a banked turn of an asphalt pump track. The purpose 
of the analysis is to give insight into whether the observed structural damages from the previous 
sections were related to failures in material layers. 
5.1 
Introduction 
During the condition surveys, cracks in the asphalt surfacing on the berm platforms were the first 
distinctive damage observed (Figure 5.1). Although repeatedly occurring on different pump tracks 
in all the investigated areas, there was no clear explanation for that sort of damage type. There-
fore, it was decided that this problem is worth studying in more detail. The developed engineering 
model can also serve as a template for future studies and projects. 
 
 
Figure 5.1: Longitudinal cracking on the berm platform 
Before building up the model of the banked turn, intuitively the main concern associated with the 
cracks in asphalt discovered on many berm platforms was the compaction level of the upper base 
layer underneath the asphalt surface. The stiffness of an unbound material depends on the com-
paction level of that material. Considering the complex geometry (refer to Figure 2.5) of the berm 
when the base layer sharply goes from a nearly leveled platform to a drop of up to 65° steep as 
measured during track inspections, it may be tricky at these key parts of the track to achieve a 
satisfactory compaction level of the base material with just the plate compactor (following the 
same construction method as for the asphalt compaction seen in Figure 1.15). Consequently, this 
may lead to settlements in the base layer near the berm edge later in the lifetime of the pump 
track, causing the asphalt surface to crack, which facilitates further water ingress and makes the 
problem even worse if the formed cracks are not treated. 
Many pump track contractors nowadays follow a more advanced method when the berm platform 
is piled up and compacted to the necessary height at a considerably smaller plan radius than 
necessary. Then it can be appropriately compacted from the top. This way the vibratory plate can 
utilize its full compaction force normal to the surface and does not have to be applied from the 
side at the steep banking. For large enough platforms roller compactors are used. When the 
compaction is done, the excess material is carefully excavated in thin layers until the desired 
geometry, i.e. the plan radius and inclination of the berm, are achieved. This procedure is visually 
shown in Figure 5.2. Then the track is ready for the asphalt surfacing.

For the sake of this model, a homogenous compaction level (hence also stiffness) throughout 
each of the structural layers is assumed. This technically corresponds to the more advanced ver-
tical compaction and excess material excavation method described in the previous paragraph. 
However, in practice, the compaction level of the base layer could still be non-uniform, which is 
not considered in this investigation. Two cases are investigated: 
1. Damage in early-stage construction, assuming that the asphalt is flexible (i.e., hot). 
2. Damage in late-stage construction, assuming that the asphalt is relatively stiff (i.e., cold). 
5.2 
Modeling Framework 
Due to the limitations of standard pavement models (see e.g. [52]), the Finite Element Method 
(FEM) is chosen as a numerical tool to simulate the banked turn. FEM has the capability of solving 
a wide variety of engineering problems and is currently one of the few widely accepted numerical 
tools used by design engineers.  
The model is prepared in Abaqus, a powerful finite element analysis (FEA) software application 
developed by Dassault Systèmes (France). It is a tool used for modeling and analyzing mechan-
ical components and systems. It is particularly useful in the field of civil engineering for simulating 
the behavior of structures under various physical conditions. In the context of asphalt pavement, 
Abaqus can be utilized for Finite Element Modeling (FEM) to predict and analyze the performance 
of pavement structures. It can be used for: 
 Modeling pavement layers: Abaqus allows detailed modeling of different pavement layers, 
including the asphalt layer, base, subbase, and subgrade. This helps in understanding how 
each layer contributes to the overall performance of the structure. 
 Analyzing material behavior: Abaqus can model the viscoelastic behavior of asphalt, which 
is important for predicting how the pavement will perform under various loading conditions. 
 Evaluating pavement distress: Abaqus is used to evaluate common pavement distresses 
such as rutting and cracking. 
 Improving pavement design: by predicting the pavement’s response to loads, Abaqus helps 
in designing more durable and cost-effective pavement structures. 
Abaqus provides a comprehensive platform for the FEM of asphalt pavements by predicting the 
pavement’s response to various stresses and environmental conditions.[53] Within this project, 
Abaqus is taken as a proven solution for conventional asphalt pavement applications, with the 
unconventional geometry of a pump track berm being the main challenge in this case.

5.3 
Model Geometry and Material Properties 
The cross-section of the model (Figure 5.3) was built up to comply with two requirements that 
have arisen during the literature review and track inspections, to model the berm as realistic as 
possible: 
 The relative height of the berm must be 130cm to comply with UCI regulations for interna-
tional racing events. 
 The inclination at the top of the banked turn should be 65° according to the maximum angle 
identified during pump track visits. 
Since these turns are entirely handmade on a site with no pattern, the distance from the berm 
peak to the edge of the pavement at the bottom was measured to be approximately 3.0m on most 
tracks. So, the 2225.0mm radius of the vertical curvature was derived from 3.0m horizontal dis-
tance, 1.3m vertical distance, and 65° inclination at the top. The platform was also extended to 
2.0m width to be able to accommodate the intended plate compactor load on the platform at the 
berm peak. Such a configuration is considered enough to observe the model’s response to the 
intended load. 
 
Figure 5.3: 2D cross-section geometry of the modeled pump track berm (all dimensions in mm) 
The layer thicknesses are chosen according to the latest design specifications used by Velosolu-
tions. Asphalt thickness is 80mm throughout the model, while the base layer has varying thickness 
depending on the geometry throughout the track but no less than 250mm. 300mm drainage sand 
is laid on top of a leveled subgrade. The subgrade layer is substituted with an elastic foundation 
equivalent to 10.0m depth of a 50MPa subgrade material (foundation stiffness per area 5MPa/m). 
The whole cross-section is modeled as a deformable solid (single part), which is then extruded to 
a depth of 1.0m to add the 3rd dimension. The part is then partitioned into cells to obtain the 
necessary layers and accommodate the intended plate compactor load (Figure 5.4).

6 Conclusions & Recommendations 
6.1 
Conclusions 
As a rather unknown topic, to start with this thesis presented an extensive overview of the asphalt 
pump track as a relatively new type of civil infrastructure. To investigate the asphalt pump tracks 
further, Literature Review not only discusses their geometrical and structural design records and 
practices but also provides a detailed examination of related infrastructure design. That includes 
guidelines, specifications, standards on cycling lanes (low-volume pavements), BMX race tracks, 
skateparks, and other unconventional facilities. To build a knowledge base for the practical part 
of this project, a review of the maintenance activities and techniques for pump tracks and other 
relevant infrastructure objects is conducted. 
The second highlight of this project is the condition evaluation of existing asphalt pump tracks. In 
total 20 tracks across Denmark and Latvia have been surveyed and analyzed. To do so, a new 
methodology for inspecting asphalt pump tracks was developed, inspired by the thorough litera-
ture review, field observations, and expert knowledge from external contributor. The method is 
suitable for evaluating the functional and structural condition of any asphalt pump track based on 
the assessment of 15 criteria. A quantitative and qualitative analysis of the generated data is then 
conducted. 
Last but not least, a mechanical model was developed as a part of this research by employing a 
Finite Element Method (FEM), to give an insight into whether the observed structural damages 
could be related to any failure mechanisms in the material layers. 
Findings from Literature Review 
 Despite cycling and other action sports and their infrastructure being a significant industry 
worldwide, there is a lack of detailed and thorough design, construction, maintenance, etc. 
documentation, compared to other conventional civil infrastructure such as light duty pave-
ments. 
 There is a significant difference in the asphalt pavement thickness between asphalt pump 
tracks and other surfaces intended for usage by the same type of wheeled vehicles. For 
instance, asphalt is paved to a thickness of 3 to 4cm on cycling lanes in Denmark and 
Latvia, while on pump tracks it is at least 8cm throughout the track. With 12.5cm it is even 
more for the banked turns of BMX race tracks. 
 The complex geometry of pump tracks makes it difficult to implement conventional con-
struction equipment, as well as test methods for quality assurance. 
 The desired coefficient of friction on the track surface (grip) should be taken as a starting 
point when deciding on the most suitable asphalt mix design for an asphalt pump track. 
 Since asphalt pump tracks are built worldwide, there is a wide range of site-specific char-
acteristics, so design and construction, as well as maintenance methods, have to be differ-
entiated to align with the local conditions. 
Findings from Track Inspections 
 Many of the observed damages around asphalt pump tracks are unique, therefore the in-
spection schemes for other conventional pavement structures cannot be fully applied to 
pump tracks but is still a solid starting point. 
 No obvious correlation between the overall condition and age of the tracks was observed. 
At this point when all the pump tracks are relatively new structures, the damages found are 
associated with design and construction methods rather than with aging effects, fatigue, 
etc.

 The most common damages across the inspected asphalt pump tracks are associated with 
insufficient surface water drainage and pavement-edge drop-off/embankment erosion. 
Cracking on the berm platform, which has been the focus of the numerical investigation, 
has been observed at half of the inspected tracks. 
Findings from Engineering Model 
 As far as the created engineering model was able to resemble the banked pump track turn, 
the results confirmed that the field observed structural damages are not related to failures 
in material layers. 
 Abaqus is a suitable platform for modeling the unconventional geometry of the banked 
pump track turn. However, the unique shape must be explored in more detail to make the 
model more realistic. 
6.2 
Recommendations 
This section provides a set of design and maintenance recommendations for practical applica-
tions and future development of a systematic asphalt pump track design guideline. These have 
resulted from the conducted research and findings. 
Design 
 Based on the literature review findings – reconsider the necessary asphalt thickness for 
pump track pavement. Design the asphalt mix based on the desired coefficient of friction 
on the track surface (grip). 
 Based on track inspections – revise the design and construction techniques based on the 
damage types discovered during the track condition evaluation. 
 Based on the literature review – consider the treatment of the granular base layer by ap-
plying a thin coat of emulsified asphalt. This will enhance the adhesion of the asphalt layer 
to the base and stabilize the base layer itself. 
 Based on expert suggestion – the entire base layer or the upper part of it should be mixed 
with 4-5% cement/lime to help facilitate the compaction process and improve the material 
workability when constructing inclined berms. As discussed in this project, insufficient base 
layer compaction might contribute to the crack formation on the berm platforms. 
 Based on expert suggestion – consider using fiber reinforced asphalt concrete (FRAC) for 
the pump track surface paving. 
 Based on feedback from external contributor Velosolutions – to tackle the difficulties of 
getting the desired asphalt mix from asphalt plants at the right temperature at the right time, 
establish their own portable asphalt production plant that could be used at a construction 
site. That way the asphalt could be prepared on demand and there would be more flexibility 
with regard to the desired asphalt mix, temperature, and other properties. This way the 
damages associated with surface roughness and cracking could also be possibly reduced. 
Maintenance 
 The inspection methodology for asphalt pump tracks developed within this project can be 
implemented as a basis for the condition monitoring of existing tracks. If the condition is 
properly evaluated according to the created scheme, the results will indicate whether any 
maintenance activities should be carried out or not. 
 Generally, if the criterion is scored ‘2’, rehabilitation procedures should be done to get it 
back to score ‘0’. For example, any sediments or other objects should be removed from the 
track surface and fall zones, any cracks in the asphalt pavement or unsealed construction 
joints should sealed with a suitable sealant, any damaged additional equipment should be 
fixed if possible or replaced, etc.
