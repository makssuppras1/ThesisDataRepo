2. Abstract  
 
Funnel-web and widow spiders are among the few spiders worldwide that can cause serious envenomings in 
humans. The mainstay treatment of spider bite envenoming is antivenom, consisting of polyclonal antibodies 
derived from the plasma of hyperimmunized animals. While these antivenoms save lives, some products 
have suboptimal safety, efficacy, and availability. To address these disadvantages, the development of 
recombinant antivenoms based on human monoclonal antibodies targeting key spider toxins may present a 
promising solution.  
The aim of this thesis project is to discover human monoclonal antibodies that target key toxins from two 
different spider species using antibody phage display technology. One of the toxins is δ-hexatoxin from the 
funnel-web spider, Atrax robustus, and the second toxin is α-latrotoxin from the black widow spider, 
Latrodectus tredecimguttatus. Though the venom of both spiders consists of numerous toxins, δ-hexatoxin 
and α-latrotoxin, respectively, appear to be responsible for the main clinical effects of envenoming observed 
in humans. 
The first part of this project focused on recombinant expression of δ-hexatoxin, as native material is scarce. 
Recombinant δ-hexatoxin was produced using an Escherichia coli periplasmic expression system. After 
cleavage from a fusion protein, recombinant δ-hexatoxin was purified using RP-HPLC. Unfortunately, only 
the first pilot batch yielded recombinant δ-hexatoxin, while the larger batches yielded a truncated fragment. 
The activity of the full-length recombinant δ-hexatoxin was assessed using patch clamp electrophysiology, 
indicating a native confirmation of the toxin. Due to time constraints, optimization of recombinant expression 
and purification was paused at this point. The amount of purified full-length recombinant δ-hexatoxin was 
insufficient for phage display selection. 
In the second part of the project, an antibody phage display library consisting of 1.6 x 1010 human antibody 
fragments was successfully employed to accumulate phages displaying human scFv antibodies that bind 
α-latrotoxin. The genes encoding the scFvs were subcloned and expressed in E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells and their 
binding characteristics were then evaluated through ELISAs. Based on ELISAs and DNA sequencing, six unique 
monoclonal scFvs were identified, expressed, and ranked based on their binding to α-latrotoxin through 
concentration-normalized ELISA experiments.  
Three of these scFvs were sent to collaborators at the company Seqirus in Australia and their neutralizing 
ability is currently being assessed in vitro. Thereafter, the most promising scFvs could be converted into 
human monoclonal IgG antibodies, expressed in mammalian cell cultures, and tested for neutralizing ability 
in vivo.  
The results of this project demonstrate that phage display technology can be utilized to discover human 
monoclonal antibodies that bind key spider venom toxins. Currently, the process of phage display selection 
is being repeated with minor modifications to allow for the discovery of more unique scFvs binding 
α-latrotoxin. This project represents the first steps toward the development of a recombinant human 
antivenom against spider bite envenoming.

3. Resume  
 
Tragtspinder- og enkeedderkopper er blandt de få edderkopper i verden, som kan forårsage alvorlige 
forgiftninger i mennesker.  Den traditionelle behandling er modgift, bestående af polyklonale antistoffer, 
som udvindes fra plasma fra hyperimmuniserede dyr. Til trods for at disse modgifte redder liv, er brugen af 
dem forbundet med en række problemer herunder suboptimal virkning, begrænset tilgængelighed og risiko 
for bivirkninger. Udvikling af en rekombinant modgift baseret på humane monoklonale antistoffer rettet mod 
essentielle edderkoppetoksiner er en potentiel løsning på disse udfordringer.  
Formålet med dette kandidatspeciale er at identificere humane monoklonale antistoffer, rettet mod to 
essentielle toksiner fra to forskellige edderkoppearter, ved hjælp af fag display teknologi. Disse toksiner er 
δ-hexatoksin fra tragtspinderedderkoppen, Atrax robustus, og α-latrotoksin fra den sorte enkeedderkop, 
Latrodectus tredecimguttatus. Hos begge edderkopper består giften af en blanding af forskellige toksiner, 
men δ-hexatoksin og α-latrotoksin menes at være årsag til de alvorlige kliniske effekter i mennesker.  
Den første del af projektet fokuserede på rekombinant ekspression af δ-hexatoksin, idet toksinet er svært at 
anskaffe fra edderkopper. Rekombinant δ-hexatoksin blev produceret som et fusionsprotein ved hjælp af et 
periplasmisk ekspressionssystem i Escherichia coli. Desværre viste det sig at kun den første ekspression og 
oprensning havde resulteret i et toksin af rette størrelse, hvorimod senere oprensninger viste sig at være et 
trunkeret fragment. Aktiviteten af det rekombinante δ-hexatoksin blev undersøgt ved hjælp af ’patch clamp’ 
elektrofysiologi, hvilket indikerede en korrekt konformation af toksinet. Grundet begrænset tid blev 
optimering af rekombinant ekspression og oprensning indstillet, og mængden af det oprensede 
rekombinante δ-hexatoksin var desværre utilstrækkelig til at fortsætte med fag display selektioner.  
I anden del af projektet blev et antistof fag display bibliotek bestående af 1.6 x 1010 humane 
antistoffragmenter brugt til at akkumulere fager, der udtrykker humane scFv antistoffragmenter, som binder 
α-latrotoksin. De kodende sekvenser for disse antistoffragmenter, blev subklonet ind i E. coli BL21 (DE3) celler 
og efterfølgende udtrykt. ELISA blev anvendt til at evaluere fragmenternes binding til α-latrotoksin. ELISA og 
DNA-sekventering ledte til identifikation af seks unikke monoklonale scFv-antistoffer. De seks kloner blev 
udtrykt og rangeret på baggrund af deres binding til α-latrotoksin baseret på koncentrationsnormaliserede 
ELISA eksperimenter. Tre af antistoffragmenterne blev sendt til en samarbejdspartner i Australien, hvor de 
vil blive testet for deres evne til at neutralisere α-latrotoksin in vitro. Derefter vil eventuelt lovende 
fragmenter kunne konverteres til IgG antistoffer, som kan udtrykkes i mammale cellekulturer og herefter 
testes for deres evne til at neutralisere in vivo.  
Resultaterne af dette projekt demonstrerer, hvordan antistof fag display teknologi kan anvendes til at 
identificere humane monoklonale antistoffer, der binder essentielle edderkoppetoksiner. I øjeblikket 
gentages fag display selektioner mod α-latrotoksin for at se, om yderligere unikke antistoffragmenter kan 
identificeres. 
Projektet repræsenterer et lille skridt på vejen mod at udvikle verdens første rekombinante humane modgift 
mod edderkoppeforgiftning.

5. Introduction 
5.1 Spider bite envenoming 
5.1.1 Epidemiology of spider bites 
The majority of the 48,000 recorded species of spiders are venomous [1–3]. However, most pose little danger 
to humans, since spiders are rarely aggressive in addition to their venom production and means of injection 
(mouthparts) being limited by their size [2,4]. Moreover, since spiders predominantly feed on invertebrates, 
their venoms are evolutionary optimized to target their preys’ physiology, which is often significantly 
different to that of humans [2,4].  
Whilst most spider bites in humans only induce minor effects, some spiders constitute a substantial public 
health concern as they can result in significant pain, systemic envenoming, or dermonecrosis [5].  Worldwide, 
two important clinical syndromes are caused by spider bite: latrodectism caused by widow spiders 
(Latrodectus spp.), and loxoscelism caused by recluse spiders (Loxosceles spp.) [6]. An additional two groups 
of spiders with local distributions can cause serious envenoming: The Australian funnel-web spiders (Atrax 
spp., Illawara spp., and Hadronyche spp.) and the armed spiders, also known as Brazilian wandering spiders 
(Phoneutria spp.) [5]. Different factors can play an important role towards the severity of envenoming as 
specified in table 1. Furthermore, spiders are able to bite without injecting venom, which has been termed 
as a “dry bite” [7]. 
Spiders have pharmacologically complex venoms that generally feature over 100 components [4].  Typically, 
the primary purpose of the venom is to rapidly subdue prey by incapacitating their nervous system [3]. The 
chemical complexity of spider venoms ranges from salts and small organic compounds to linear cytolytic 
peptides, disulphide-rich peptide neurotoxins, enzymes, and large presynaptic neurotoxins. Often these 
components act synergistically to enhance venom potency [3,4].

The epidemiologic analysis of spider bites is confounded by several factors: the extensive differential 
diagnosis of dermal bite-like lesions (potentially caused by recluse spiders), suspected versus definitive spider 
bites, and lack of expert identification of the biting spider. In addition, there are no national or international 
registries for spider bites as it is not a notifiable disease [2], which makes it difficult to gauge the extent of 
the problem. However, some reports on suspected spider bites do exist. From 2000 to 2008 the American 
Association of Poison Control Centers received more than 23,000 reports on possible spider bites from widow 
spiders alone [8]. In Brazil, the Brazilian Information System for Notifiable Diseases (SINAN) recorded more 
than 17,000 suspected spider bites with 18 deaths attributed to spider bites in the period 1990-1993 [9]. 
Finally, in Australia it has been estimated that redback spiders alone are responsible for at least 5,000 
envenomings in humans per year, making it the most common type of envenoming in Australia [10].  
In the following section, emphasis is placed on the funnel-web and widow spiders, as the work in this thesis 
has been conducted on toxins from the Sydney funnel-web spider (Atrax robustus) and the Mediterranean 
black widow spider (Latrodectus tredecimguttatus).  
 
5.1.2 Funnel-web spiders   
Epidemiology, clinical manifestations, and treatment of funnel-web spider bite envenoming     
Australian funnel-web spiders (Atracidae family: genera Atrax, Hadronyche, and Illawara) are medium to 
large spiders with parallel fangs [5] that inhabit the east coast of Australia (figure 1). Of the 40 described 
species [1], six are known to have caused severe envenoming in humans with a rapid onset of life-threatening 
effects [5,11]. Severe systemic envenoming is characterized by neuromuscular excitation, including 
paraesthesia and fasciculations, pulmonary edema, and massive autonomic stimulation/excitation with 
generalized diasphoresis (excessive sweating), hypersalivation, hyperlacrimation, and hypertension. Without 
antivenom, further effects like neuromuscular paralysis, coma, hypotension, and multi-organ failure might 
follow [10].  
Australian funnel-web spiders are arguably the most lethal spider in the world with 13 confirmed deaths from 
the male Sydney funnel-web spider (A. robustus) before the development of antivenom in 1981 and with no 
recorded deaths since [11]. However, due to the low number of human envenomings and ethical 
considerations, no systematic randomised control trial of the efficacy of antivenom exist [5]. The funnel-web 
antivenom is produced in rabbits immunized with crude venom from male A. robustus spiders, as the male 
venom is more potent [11–14]. The antivenom is used to treat all severe cases of envenoming from funnel-
web spiders irrespective of the specific species [5,15]. A systematic review of recorded clinical cases by 
Isbister et al. in 2005 [11] showed that the tree-dwelling funnel-webs (H. cerberea and H. formidabilis) 
presented the highest rate of severe envenoming following a bite (75% and 63%, respectively), whereas only 
17% of bites from A. robustus were severe. Funnel-web antivenom was administered in 75 patients with 
three adverse reactions seen – two early allergic (one mild, the other severe requiring adrenaline) and one 
case of serum sickness [11]. Notably, the incidents of funnel-web spider bites will likely increase in future, 
since the human population on the east coast is growing [15].

δ-hexatoxin – the toxin affecting primates    
The toxicity to humans following a funnel-web spider bite is derived solely from a small peptidic neurotoxin, 
the δ-hexatoxin of 4.8 kDa [16]. The toxin displays great homology between funnel-web species, 
underscoring the similar clinical presentation when envenoming has occurred [15,17]. δ-hexatoxin induces 
spontaneous repetitive neuronal firing and prolongation of action potentials by binding to receptor site 3 of 
Nav channels, slowing the inactivation of the channels and causing a hyperpolarizing shift of the voltage 
dependence of activation [17–19]. 
The structure of δ-hexatoxin from A. robustus was identified by solution nuclear magnetic resonance in 1997 
[20]. The toxin adopts the inhibitor cysteine knot structure [21] with four disulphide crosslinks involving 
cysteine residues in both the N- and C-terminal as well as three consecutive cysteines in the middle of the 
sequence [22]. Notably, the δ-hexatoxin only affects insects and primates, but not other vertebrates except 
at high doses [16].  
Throughout the thesis δ-hexatoxin will be used to denote δ-hexatoxins as a group irrespective of origin (i.e. 
spider species), whereas when referring to δ-hexatoxin from a specific species the rational nomenclature for 
naming peptide toxins from spiders introduced by King et al. [23] will be employed (e.g. δ-HXTX-Ar1a from 
A. robustus).  
 
5.1.3 Widow spiders  
Epidemiology, clinical manifestations, and treatment of widow spider bite envenoming     
Latrodectus or widow spiders have a worldwide distribution with 31 recognized species throughout the 
Americas, Africa, Europe, Asia, and Australia [1]. They are generally glossy black with varying red markings 
(figure 2) [6]. Female widow spiders are bigger than males and are responsible for most cases of envenomings 
[5]. The resulting clinical syndrome is called latrodectism and is characterized by diaphoresis and especially 
local and regional pain developing gradually that last for hours to days. In case of systemic envenoming, 
which occurs in about one third of cases, non-specific symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, headache, and 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
fatigue are common, but latrodectism is rarely life-threatening [5]. The clinical manifestations of latrodectism 
are characteristic, however, a substantial variation is present between species with local pain radiating from 
the bite site typical in Australia, whereas back and abdominal pain predominates in America [5,24]. For 
L. tredecimguttatus myocardial injury has been reported, but it is considered extremely rare [25].

The diagnosis of latrodectism is purely clinical combined with the patient’s history of spider bite. No analytical 
assays exist to detect widow spider venom [5]. Treatment for latrodectism include antivenom, calcium 
gluconate, and analgesic agents among others, however, evidence of effectiveness are scarce, and treatment 
is often based on anecdotal experience [24]. The use of antivenom is debated [24,27,28], and has somewhat 
depended on its availability [29], reported success in retrospective studies [30,31], and the perceived risk of 
adverse reactions [32–34]. On the other hand, randomized controlled trials of antivenom support the safety 
of its use with acute reactions in about 5% of cases, including anaphylaxis in 1-2%, and delayed reactions or 
serum sickness in up to 10% of cases [32,33]. Regardless, most studies have not been able to find a significant 
reduction in pain, which is the primary target of treatments [24,35].  
Five antivenoms are currently being produced against venom from widow spiders as seen in table 2. The 
European widow spider (L. tredecimguttatus) antivenom is no longer in production [24,29]. Case reports and 
in vitro and in vivo studies indicate that widow spider antivenoms are likely to exhibit cross-species 
neutralizing capabilities [29,36–38]. In the US, the black widow antivenom is used to treat envenoming 
caused by bites from all North American widow spiders, and similarly, in South Africa, the antivenom against 
L. indistinctus venom may be used to treat latrodectism from the bite of L. geometricus [39].  
Interestingly, the redback antivenom has mainly been administered intramuscularly in contrast to the other 
antivenoms against widow spiders that are administered intravenously [40]. This is due to a recommendation 
from the manufacturer as they believe it to cause fewer allergic reactions [31,41].

α-latrotoxin – the vertebrate-specific toxin causing latrodectism  
The molecular composition of widow spider venom has only been extensively characterized for 
L. tredecimguttatus [47], with the addition of a recent second α-latrotoxin DNA sequence from L. hasselti 
[48]. The venom contains four paralogs of latrotoxins with 30-60% amino acid sequence identity and similar 
domain organization. However, only α-latrotoxin affects vertebrates and is vertebrate specific [49]. The gene 
for α-latrotoxin from L. tredecimguttatus encodes a precursor protein that undergoes proteolytic cleavage 
during venom maturation, resulting in the active α-latrotoxin of 131 kDa that appears to be responsible for 
the clinical effects in humans [50,51]. Limited variability (>93% nucleotide identity), conserved cysteine 
residues, similar lengths, and posttranslational processing signals of α-latrotoxin sequences imply that all 
Latrodectus species express a form of α-latrotoxin with strong functional similarity to the ortholog from 
L. tredecimguttatus [47,48]. This supports the similarity of clinical manifestations observed from bites from 
various widow spiders [48].  
Similar to δ-hexatoxin, α-latrotoxin is used in the remainder of the thesis to denote α-latrotoxins as a group 
irrespective of specific species, whereas α-LTX-Lt1a refers to the α-latrotoxin found in the venom from 
L. tredecimguttatus.  
The three-dimensional structure of α-LTX-Lt1a was determined by Orlova et al. in 2000 by cryo electron 
microscopy with a resolution of 18 Å and 14 Å, for a dimer and tetramer, respectively [52]. The α-LTX-Lt1a 
monomer consists of three tandem domains: the wing, the body, and the head region, where the former is 
hypothesized to be the receptor-binding domain whereas the body and head make up a transmembrane 
channel [49,52]. α-latrotoxin monomers assemble into stable dimers that, facilitated by divalent cations, 
have the ability to form tetramers spontaneously [52]. α-LTX-Lt1a has played a fundamental role as a tool in 
the study of vertebrate release of neurotransmitters [49,53] despite some unresolved questions about its 
mechanism of action [54].  
α-LTX-Lt1a binds to multiple membrane proteins on presynaptic neuronal and neuroendocrine nerve 
terminals and induces spontaneous massive neurotransmitter release through vesicle exocytosis via both 
Ca2+-dependent and independent mechanisms [50,55,56]. How this release causes the clinical effects seen in 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
humans remains unclear [5]. Currently, neurotransmitter exocytosis induced by α-LTX-Lt1a is believed to 
involve an initial step of α-LTX-Lt1a binding to extracellular cell surface membrane proteins. Three 
structurally distinct receptors have been identified to this date: (i) neurexin 1α, a neuronal protein with a 
single transmembrane domain [57], (ii) latrophilin 1 or CIRL (calcium-independent receptor of α-latrotoxin), 
a G protein-coupled receptor [58,59], and (iii) receptor-like protein tyrosine phosphatase σ [60]. α-LTX-Lt1a 
causes extracellular Ca2+ dependent and independent release of neurotransmitters, which is illustrated in 
figure 3 but likely true for all α-latrotoxins: (1) The toxin binds on the cell surface by binding to its receptors. 
(2) Once α-LTX-Lt1a has bound, it forms cyclical tetramers, followed by membrane insertion and non-
selective cation channel formation [52]. (3) Pore formation results in influx of Ca2+, which triggers the release 
of vesicles. (4) Receptor-mediated signalling takes place through the latrophilin receptor, which involves PLC, 
production of IP3 and diacyl glycerol (DAG), resulting in release of stored Ca2+ and activation of PKC, which 
affect vesicle release as well [54]. (5) In the absence of extracellular Ca2+, exocytosis requires Mg2+, resulting 
in formation of α-latrotoxin channels. Some secretion may be non-vesicular [54]. Prolonged nerve terminal 
stimulation can result in depletion of synaptic vesicle pools and functional blockade of synaptic transmission 
[61].

5.2 Antivenoms 
5.2.1 Traditional antivenom production  
Antivenom serotherapy was discovered in 1894 simultaneously by two French scientists, Phisalix and 
Calmette [62–64]. Even today, 125 years later, antivenoms are still based on the same principle of 
animal-derived antisera, which is illustrated in figure 4. Antivenom consists of concentrated 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
immunoglobulins, primarily from horses that have been hyperimmunized with one or more venoms over a 
period of several months [65–67].  
 
 
Figure 4: Schematic overview of traditional antivenom production. (A): The spider is milked to collect its venom. (B): The horse is 
immunized with venom over a period of several months. (C): Blood is drawn from the horse when the antibody titre is sufficiently 
high. (D): Immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibodies are purified from the plasma using different precipitation techniques. (E): After 
concentration and formulation the antivenom is bottled and ready for distribution. Figure inspired by work from Jonas Jürgensen.   
 
Traditionally, antivenoms consisted of crude antisera from immunized animals, but over the years following 
the discovery of antibodies, the production has been refined. Today most antivenoms are comprised of 
antigen binding fragment (Fab) or F(ab’)2 fragments (figure 5). The Fc moiety is removed from the 
immunoglobulin G (IgG) under the assumption that it reduces the risk of adverse reactions and the smaller 
size increases the volume of distribution [65]. However, Fab fragments are cleared from the body much faster 
compared to IgGs due to rapid renal clearance [68]. IgG molecules are usually purified by caprylic acid 
precipitation, but can also be extracted by column purification, increasing purity but also cost [65,68].   
 
 
Figure 5: Overview of the three antibody formats commonly used in plasma-derived antivenoms. The formats F(ab’)2 and Fab are 
obtained by enzymatic cleavage in the hinge region of IgG with pepsin or papain, respectively. The F(ab’)2 fragment contain two 
antigen-binding regions, whereas Fab is a monovalent fragment. VH: Heavy chain variable domain; VL: Light chain variable domain; 
CH: Heavy chain constant domain; CL: Light chain constant domain.    
 
Most of the 10 available spider antivenoms are monovalent and cover the medically relevant spiders: widow 
spiders (Latrodectus spp.) (table 2), funnel-web spiders (Atracidae family), recluse spiders (Loxosceles spp.), 
and Brazilian wandering spiders (Phoneutria spp.) [69]. As previously stated, the use of antivenom to treat 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
spider bite envenoming, besides funnel-web spider bite envenoming, is debated due to the apparent lack of 
efficacy and risk of adverse reactions [24]. Antivenoms are associated with several inherent drawbacks, most 
of which have been well documented for snake antivenoms but less so for spiders. Nonetheless, because of 
the similar manufacturing process, many of these drawbacks are expected to be true for spider antivenoms 
as well. Therefore, experience from snake antivenoms will be included in the following, where the different 
drawbacks of antivenom are discussed.  
Adverse reactions are due to the heterologous nature of the proteins in the antisera, which can result in an 
immune response when administered to the patient [70]. Early adverse reactions to antivenom include 
pyrogenic (i.e. urticaria (hives), nausea, fever, and vomiting) and anaphylactic reactions requiring treatment 
with adrenaline [70,71]. A late adverse reaction, such as serum sickness one to two weeks post treatment, 
can also occur [70]. Few studies have investigated the safety of spider antivenom, but for the redback and 
the funnel-web spider antivenom, studies report that around 5% of patients develop acute reactions [11,33]. 
This is in sharp contrast to the experience from most snake antivenoms, where the incidence of early 
reactions vary between 3% to 88% depending on the antivenom [70]. This discrepancy could be due to the 
greater amount of antivenom injected in order to neutralize a snakebite, which is an issue since only a subset 
of antivenom antibodies are therapeutically relevant. Studies of different snake antivenoms found that only 
between 5 and 40 percent of antibodies are specific against venom antigens [72–74]. Similar studies have 
not been conducted for spider antivenoms, but considering the similar manufacturing process, it is likely also 
an issue in spider antivenoms.  
For snake antivenoms, discrepancy between toxicity of venom antigens and their immunogenicity have been 
reported, resulting in an unbalanced antibody content with a majority of antibodies targeting immunogenic, 
but non-toxic, high molecular mass components [75–79]. Considering the small size of δ-hexatoxin in 
funnel-web spider venom, this is presumably also an issue, resulting in administration of multiple vials of 
antivenom to ensure neutralization. In addition, batch to batch variations are also reported for snake 
antivenoms due to individual differences in the immune response of the production animals [80].  
Lastly, and particularly for spiders, the production of antivenom depends on a very labour-intensive process 
of venom collection through “milking” of a large number of spiders as each spider only produces small 
amounts of venom [69]. Both in 2015 and in 2017, the Australian Reptile Park that is responsible for collecting 
and milking venom from male A. robustus spiders used for antivenom production, urged the public to catch 
Sydney funnel-web spiders they come across and bring them in to secure a continuous supply of antivenom 
[81,82].  
 
5.2.2 Development of a recombinant human antivenom 
Animal derived anti-sera have saved countless lives for more than 120 years. However, as explained in the 
section above, the treatment has inherent drawbacks. To address these issues, researchers have adopted 
different approaches to improve current antivenoms [83], including innovative immunization strategies, 
identification of small molecule inhibitors to target enzymatic toxins [84,85], and the development of 
recombinant antivenoms based on monoclonal human antibodies [78,85]. In this thesis, the latter of these 
approaches was pursued.  
Figure 6 shows the principle behind the development of a future recombinant antivenom against spider 
venom consisting of human monoclonal IgG antibodies discovered by employing antibody phage display 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
technology. The monoclonal antibodies are targeting the medically most relevant toxins in the venom in 
question, increasing the therapeutic content in the antivenom with potential increased efficacy. The 
antibodies are produced by mammalian cell cultivation, abolishing the use of animal immune systems, thus 
also dismissing the batch to batch variation. In addition, the need for venom procurement is reduced. The 
main advantage though of such a recombinant antivenom is its compatibility with the human immune 
system, limiting the risk of adverse reactions upon administration [85]. The development phase of 
recombinant antivenoms is expensive but in an article by Laustsen et al. in 2017 [86], it is argued that 
producing recombinant snakebite antivenoms in the future could be a cost-competitive alternative to 
existing antivenoms.  
The discovery pipeline depicted in figure 6 has already yielded promising results, as Laustsen et al. in 2018 
showed that a cocktail of three human monoclonal IgGs had the ability to neutralize dendrotoxin-mediated 
neurotoxicity of D. polylepis (black mamba, snake) whole venom in vivo [87].  
The same methodology can be applied to the field of spider bites. Given that less medically relevant toxins 
in spiders venoms are essential to neutralize compared to snake venoms and given the more laborious venom 
extraction procedures required for current spider antivenom production, producing a recombinant 
antivenom based on human monoclonal antibodies may be a promising and feasible possibility [69,88].

5.3 Antibody phage display technology  
Phage display technology is the foundation of much of the work performed in this thesis, and thus an 
introduction of the method is warranted. Antibody discovery is gaining traction in almost all areas of modern 
medicine, including fields like oncology and autoimmune diseases. Often one of two different antibody 
discovery approaches are used: hybridoma technology and antibody phage display selection [89,90]. In its 
basics, antibody phage display selection is used as an easy-to-perform, robust, and inexpensive method, 
generating antibody reagents selected from large combinatorial phage-antibody libraries [91,92]. 
Phage display technology was invented in 1985 by Smith, who inserted foreign DNA fragments into gene III 
(encoding a phage coat protein) of a M13 phage, creating a library of phages that displayed foreign peptides 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
on their surfaces [93]. In 1990, McCafferty et al. fused genes encoding single chain variable fragments (scFvs) 
to gene III [94]. This allowed for selection of phages displaying antigen-recognizing antibody fragments and 
links a protein (phenotype) to its equivalent gene (genotype) through a phage. Since then, different antibody 
formats have been used in the construction of antibody-displaying phage libraries, which includes heavy-
domain antibody fragments (VHs), heavy-domain camelid and shark antibody fragments (VHHs), and Fabs 
among others [92]. Large phage display antibody libraries can be constructed by cloning genes encoding an 
antibody fragment into phage genomes. Different types of libraries can be constructed either from synthetic 
sequences or from a natural source. Of the latter, two types of libraries can be constructed: naïve or 
immunized, depending on whether the donors, from which the antibody genes were isolated, have been 
immunized with an antigen or not. The naïve libraries can generally be used for discovery of a wide range of 
antigens; however, the discovered antibody fragments may have lower affinities compared to using an 
immunized library. Different techniques can be used for in vitro affinity maturation of antibody fragments 
from naïve phage libraries, which entails diversification of the sequences of the antibody fragments before 
new phage display selections, for instance by chain shuffling or mutagenesis [92].  
In this research project, all phage display selections were performed by employing the IONTAS phage display 
library, which is a filamentous M13 bacteriophage system. The library is a human antibody phagemid library 
of 4 x 1010 clones displaying scFvs. The scFvs are fusion proteins of the variable regions of the heavy and light 
chains of immunoglobulins, connected with a short linker peptide [95]. They were constructed from B 
lymphocytes collected from 43 non-immunized human donors [91].  
The M13 bacteriophage only infects E. coli that express the F pilus as the phage coat protein binds to the tip 
of the F pilus for adsorption to the bacterium [96]. The phage contains a genome of single-stranded DNA that 
consists of nine genes encoding 11 different proteins [92]. Five proteins are coat proteins, whereas the 
remaining six proteins are involved in replication and assembly of the phage. Upon infection of its host, the 
phage establishes a chronic infection, where it continuously releases new phages. Figure 7 is a schematic 
representation of the M13 bacteriophage displaying a scFv on its surface. 
 
 
Figure 7: Schematic representation of the M13 bacteriophage. The single-stranded DNA contains nine genes, encoding 11 proteins 
including five coat proteins. Gene III is fused to a gene encoding a human scFv. The resulting fusion protein, Gene 3 Protein (G3P)-
scFv is displayed on the surface. Figure inspired by work from Jonas Jürgensen.   
 
Each phage normally incorporates three to five copies of Gene 3 Protein (G3P), and thus there is a risk of a 
multivalent display of antibody-G3P fusion proteins if the antibody genes are cloned directly into the phage 
genome. Instead, phagemids, which are phages with smaller genomes that are not carrying the genes 
necessary for propagation, can be used to display antibody genes. Phagemids contain 1) an antibiotic marker 
for selection and propagation of the plasmid, 2) the gene encoding the antibody-G3P fusion protein, and 3) 
phage origin of replication. To produce functional phages displaying antibody-G3P fusion proteins, E. coli 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
infected with a phagemid vector is superinfected with a helper phage, M13KO7 [97]. The helper phage 
contains the complete M13 genome encoding all the phage proteins necessary for capsid production, 
assembly, replication, and budding. The M13KO7 phage is constructed with a low-copy origin of replication, 
which means that in the case of a superinfection in E. coli, the high copy number phagemid will out-compete 
the helper phage during packaging, resulting in majorities of phage particles carrying phagemid DNA [97]. 
The phagemid particles will, in most cases, incorporate G3P encoded by the helper phage genome over the 
G3P-scFv fusion. Therefore, the majority of phagemids will not be displaying an antibody fragment (bald), 
and if displaying, usually only a single copy (monovalent display). With the IONTAS phage display library bald 
phages can be rendered non-infectious, as they are sensitive to trypsin digestion [98].  
Figure 8 summarizes the consecutive steps in an antibody phage display experiment: 
 
 
Figure 8: Overview of the consecutive steps in an antibody phage display experiment. Phages are panned against immobilized 
antigens (A), after which unspecific binders are washed away (B). Remaining phages are eluted (C) and are used to infect E. coli cells 
(D) before amplification (E). The amplified phages can be used in a new round of panning (F) to enrich for high-affinity binders. The 
selection rounds are evaluated by polyclonal ELISA or a plate test (G). Figure inspired by work from Jonas Jürgensen.   
 
The first step is the addition of an antibody phage display library to a vial or well coated with antigen 
(panning) to allow the phages displaying antibody fragments with the greatest affinity to the immobilized 
antigen to bind (A). Next the rest of the phages are washed away (B) before the bound phages are eluted by 
addition of trypsin or acid (C). The eluted phages are used to infect E. coli cells (D) and helper phages are 
added to allow for amplification of the infective phages in E. coli (E). The steps are usually repeated once or 
twice with the amplified phages from the preceding round of selection (F) to accumulate phages displaying 
high affinity antibody fragments. Lastly, the antibody binders are evaluated through polyclonal enzyme-
linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) or plate tests (G).

When performing phage display experiments, especially antigen presentation is important to consider. For 
solid-phase panning it can be conducted via direct coating (adsorption) or via a capture system such as 
streptavidin-biotin. The risk of direct immobilization is that the antigen can undergo conformational changes 
upon binding to the surface of the well/vial, which can lead to a distorted or denatured antigen. Antibodies 
selected against such an antigen can result in accumulation of phages with low affinity to the antigen in its 
native conformation. Some areas of the antigen can also be less exposed than others, if the antigen has a 
preference to how it binds to the surface [92].  
A biotin-streptavidin capture system can in some cases overcome some of these issues. Using this method, 
the antigen is chemically conjugated to a biotin molecule via a linker. Biotinylation is, however, challenging 
to control, as some antigens can be over-biotinylated, which may prevent phage binding through decreasing 
the available surface areas, resulting in fewer possible epitopes [99]. Over-biotinylation can also lead to e.g. 
antigen aggregation through altered physical-chemical properties of the antigen. Conversely, the benefit of 
biotinylation is that the antigen is lifted from the surface of the well/vial by first coating with 
streptavidin/neutravidin which captures the biotin molecule, thus allowing for better display of the antigen. 
Deselection steps must be taken to avoid selecting phages with affinity against streptavidin/neutravidin [99].

6. Project aim 
 
The overall aim of the thesis is to discover human monoclonal antibody fragments against key spider venom 
toxins using antibody phage display technology. This could be the foundation for developing the first 
recombinant antivenom based on human antibodies to treat spider bite envenoming.  
The research has focused on two key toxins, δ-hexatoxin and α-latrotoxin, from funnel web and widow 
spiders, respectively, as these spiders are of great medical concern.   
The main objectives of the thesis were: 
i. 
To isolate or recombinantly express δ-hexatoxin from A. robustus and H. infensa. 
ii. 
To discover human scFvs against δ-hexatoxin and α-latrotoxin from L. tredecimguttatus using 
antibody phage display technology.  
iii. 
To evaluate the binding properties of the discovered scFvs.  
Additional work performed in relation to this project, but not covered by the main objectives, will not be 
presented in the thesis report. This includes a side project with the aim of crystallizing α-LTX-Lt1a to 
determine the crystal structure of the toxin at a high resolution. Unfortunately, this side project did not yield 
any promising results within the master thesis period.

8.2. Methods 
8.2.1 Venom fractionation using RP-HPLC 
220 µg of crude lyophilized venom from male and female A. robustus spiders was dissolved in 22 µL of water 
and fractionated using reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) (Shimadzu 
Prominence HPLC system, Japan). The venom was loaded onto a Phenomenex Aeris C18 column 
(250 x 4.6 mm, 3.6 µm) and eluted with 5% solvent B (0.043% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) and 90% acetonitrile) 
in solvent A (0.05% TFA) at a flow rate of 0.8 mL/minute for 5 minutes, followed by a linear gradient of 5-20% 
solvent B over 5 minutes, then 20-40% solvent B for 40 minutes, 40-80% solvent B for 5 minutes, 80% solvent 
B for 2 minutes before lowering the amount of solvent B from 80-5% in 3 minutes and finally 5% solvent B 
for 10 minutes. Absorbance was monitored at 214 nm and 280 nm using an SPD-10AVP UV-VIS detector 
(Shimadzu, Japan) and reported as arbitrary units (Au). Individual peaks were collected manually, and 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
collected fractions were dried on a model 2-25 vacuum rotary evaporator (Martin Christ, Osterode am Harz, 
Germany) and dissolved in 22 µL of water.  
 
8.2.2 MALDI-TOF MS 
Fraction masses were verified by matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry 
(MALDI-TOF MS) using a model 5800 TOF/TOF System (AB Sciex, Washington D.C., USA). 
α-Cyano-4-hyroxycinnamic acid (CHCA) was used as the matrix. RP-HPLC fractions were mixed 1:1 (v/v) or 
2:1, 1:2, 1:5, and 1:10 (fraction:matrix) with CHCA (7.5 mg/mL in 50/50 acetonitrile/H2O, 0.1% TFA) on a 
MALDI steel target plate. MALDI–TOF spectra were collected in reflector positive and linear mode 
(2000 shots).  
 
8.2.3 Recombinant expression of δ-HXTX-Ar1a and δ-HXTX-Hi1a and initial purification 
Plasmids each encoding a synthetic gene of 165 base pairs (bp) were designed and ordered from Invitrogen 
(by Thermo Fischer Scientific). Figure 12 gives a schematic representation of the vector construct and fusion 
protein. The genes consist of a KpnI recognition site, TEV recognition site, the gene encoding the peptide 
δ-HXTX-Ar1a or δ-HXTX-Hi1a respectively, and a SacI recognition site. The vector was a variant of the 
pLIC-MBP vector (available from Addgene), which encodes a MaIE signal sequence (MaIEss) for periplasmic 
export, followed by an N-terminal His6 affinity tag, a maltose binding protein (MBP) fusion tag to enhance 
solubility, and a tobacco etch virus (TEV) protease recognition site immediately preceding the target peptide 
sequence [100].  In order to have a TEV recognition site there, a glycine was added in the N-terminal end of 
the toxin sequence. As this is the simplest amino acid, it would hopefully not interfere with the correct folding 
of the toxin and thus function. The plasmids were transformed into E. coli competent BL21 (DE3) cells carrying 
a T7 RNA polymerase under the control of the lacUV5 promotor, which can be induced using either isopropyl 
β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) or autoinduction medium.

(C). (A): The vector-coding region includes a MaIEss for periplasmic export, a His6 affinity tag, a solubility-enhancing MBP fusion tag, 
and the coding sequence of the δ-hexatoxin (either δ-HXTX-Ar1a or δ-HXTX-Hi1a), with a TEV protease recognition site inserted 
between the fusion tag and the peptide coding regions. Key restriction sites and the ribosome binding site (RBS) are displayed. (B): 
The fusion protein produced in the cytoplasm is directed to the periplasm, where the MaIEss is removed. The mature fusion protein 
is purified from the soluble E. coli lysate. (C): After affinity purification of the fusion protein, it is cleaved with TEV protease to yield 
the recombinant δ-hexatoxin. Figure adapted from [100].

The lyophilized plasmid DNA was reconstituted in DNAse-free water. 50 µL of competent BL21 (DE3) cells

A mini-purification of test expression pellets were performed for sodium dodecyl sulphate-polyacrylamide 
gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) analysis in order to test which colonies expressed the highest quantity. 
Glycerol stocks were used to inoculate 5 mL LB with 100 µg/mL ampicillin. All cultures were grown for 3 hours 
at 37 °C 200 RPM and then induced with 5 µL of 1 M IPTG. The cultures were cultured for 5 additional hours, 
before 1 mL of sample was taken and centrifuged at 6,000 xg for 10 minutes. Supernatant was discarded and 
each pellet was resuspended in 240 µL 20 mM Tris-Cl, 200 mM NaCl pH 8.0, and 0.6 mg/mL lysozyme. The 
resuspended pellets were incubated at 37 °C for 15 minutes at 200 RPM. 40 µL was used as a sample of whole 
cell extract and, the remaining was centrifuged at 20,000 xg for 10 minutes, after which 40 µL of the 
supernatant was taken as a sample of soluble cell extract. The remaining supernatant was discarded, and the 
pellet resuspended in 200 µL of SDS-PAGE running buffer of which 40 µL was used as a sample of the insoluble 
cell extract. To all samples 3x SDS-PAGE loading dye was added, and the samples were boiled at 95 °C for 
10 minutes. 10 µL of each sample was loaded onto 12.5% SDS-PAGE gel that ran at 100 V for 10 minutes and 
then at 140 V for approximately 1 hour and 15 minutes. The gels were stained with 40% methanol and 10% 
acetic acid in water with 0.25% Coomassie Blue R-250 for a minimum of 2 hours, before being destained O/N 
in 10% methanol and 10% acetic acid in water.  
To test optimal conditions for expression, combinations of four medias (ZY, TB, DN, 4xYT) with IPTG induction, 
autoinduction, or no induction was tested for δ-HXTX-Ar1a and δ-HXTX-Hi1a at two different temperatures 
(24°C, 200 RPM, 24 hours) or (37 °C for 3 hours, 17 °C for 21 hours, 200 RPM) in 24-deep well plates with 
5 mL of inoculated culture in each. 5 µL 1 M IPTG was added after 3 hours for the IPTG induced conditions.  
For larger scale expression 50 mL of LB media with 100 µg/mL ampicillin in a 250 mL Erlenmeyer flask was 
inoculated with a thawed 250 µL glycerol stock. The cultures were incubated O/N at 37 °C with shaking at 
180 RPM. 1 L of DN media with 100 µg/mL ampicillin and 200 µL anti-foam was inoculated the next day in a 
5 L baffled flask with 50 mL of the starter culture. The culture was growing at 37 °C with shaking at 200 RPM 
until an optical density at 600 nm (OD600) of 0.8-1.0 was reached. At that point 1 mL of 1 M IPTG was added 
to each culture and the temperature was reduced to 17 °C for the remaining hours of incubation (in total 
24 hours). The cultures were next centrifuged at 5000 xg for 20 minutes at 4 °C, after which the supernatant 
was discarded, and the cell pellet weighed. The pellets were dissolved in 15 mL of TN buffer per gram of 
pellet. Cells were lysed twice using a cell disruptor at 25-27 kPa (TS Series Cell Disruptor, Constant Systems 
Ltd.) at a temperature below 6 °C. The cell lysate was centrifuged at 41,000 xg for 30 minutes at 4 °C. 10 mL 
of 50% Ni-NTA resin slurry was added to a column (5 mL resin bed volume). Excess liquid was drained of and 
the resin was washed with 10 bed volumes of water. Five bed volumes of 100 mM NiSO4 was added to the 
resin and incubated for a minimum of 30 minutes. The excess NiSO4 was washed off with five bed columns

of water. The resin was equilibrated with 10 bed volumes of TN buffer before the soluble lysate was added 
to the charged resin and flowed through by gravity alone. The column was washed with five bed volumes of 
wash buffer (TN buffer + 15 mM imidazole) before the His-tagged protein was eluted and collected with two 
times 10 mL of elution buffer (TN buffer + 250 mM imidazole).  
A Millipore Amicon Ultra-15 10 K concentrator was prewashed with 10 mL of TN buffer and centrifuged at

three to four times. The fusion protein was mixed with an equal amount of 2x redox buffer. Four different

pH 8.3). 100 µL of 1 mg/mL TEV protease was added to each sample and incubated O/N at room temperature 
or for 2-3 days at 4 °C.  
Small samples for SDS-PAGE analysis were taken from each step of the expression process (un-induced 
culture, whole cell lysate, soluble extract, insoluble extract, flow-through, wash, elution, and cleavage). 
 
8.2.4 Purification of δ-HXTX-Ar1a and δ-HXTX-Hi1a by RP-HPLC 
All solvents used for HPLC were filtered through a 0.22 µm filter before use. TEV protease and His6-MBP were 
precipitated by adding 0.05% (final, v/v) TFA to the samples from the TEV protease cleavage reactions, 
followed by centrifugation at 14,000 xg for 20 minutes at 4 °C. For smaller sample volumes an analytical grade 
C18 column (Vydac 218TP; 250x4.6 mm, 5 µm) was used for the HPLC, whereas for greater volumes a 
semi-preparative C4 column (Phenomenex Jupiter; 250x10mm, 10 µm) was used. The columns were washed 
and equilibrated in 5% solvent B, before a blank run was performed. A gradient profile of 5-50% solvent B in 
solvent A over 45 minutes to elute the peptides of interest, followed by 50-80% solvent B over 5 minutes to 
elute any remaining MBP, TEV protease, or other contaminations. Flowrate was 1 ml/minute for C18 columns 
with 1 mL injected or 3 mL/minute for C4 with 5 mL injected. The fractions of interest were collected 
manually. Fractions were analysed to identify the desired product based on mass and to asses purity by using 
MALDI-TOF MS. Peaks of interest was pooled and lyophilized by freeze-drying. NanoDrop spectrophotometer 
was used to measure the protein concentration.  
 
8.2.5 Biotinylation of α-LTX-Lt1a  
Two vials of 200 µg of lyophilized α-LTX-Lt1a from Alomone labs was each reconstituted in 200 µL 
1x phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) by pipetting up and down carefully before a short spin. 400 µg of 
α-LTX-Lt1a was biotinylated using a toxin to biotin molar ration of 1:3. Biotin (No-Weigh NHS-PEG4 biotin) 
was thawed for 30 seconds before a hole was punctured in the foil of the tube and 170 µL of milliQ water 
was added. The biotin was mixed by pipetting up and down and an appropriate volume was added to the 
eppendorf containing the toxin with subsequent mixing. The sample was incubated for 30 minutes at room 
temperature. The Vivacon 500 concentrator was used to concentrate the biotinylated toxin (cutoff of 2 kDa). 
100 µL of the biotinylated toxin was transferred to a column, which was filled by PBS afterwards (in total 
around 500 µL). The column and collector tube were centrifuged at 14,000 xg at 4 °C for 8 minutes and then 
the content was mixed up and down and the remaining toxin was added as well as PBS. The flow-through 
was discarded. These 8 minutes cycles were repeated until three rounds of filling with PBS had been washed 
through the column. The biotinylated toxin was transferred to a new tube and 2x100 µL of PBS was used to 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
rinse the column to collect residual biotinylated toxin. The protein concentration was measured using 
NanoDrop spectrophotometer. 
 
8.2.6 Phage display selection 
Day one: A cryostock of TG1 cells were taken out from the -80 °C freezer and streaked on a minimal media 
plate. The plate was incubated for a minimum of 24 hours at 37 °C before being transferred to the fridge, 
where it was used for up to one week.  
Day two: For each selection one MaxiSorp vial was coated with 250 µL of 10 µg/mL of streptavidin in PBS and 
placed in the fridge at 4 °C covered with parafilm O/N.

plate (<1 week old) and incubated O/N at 30 °C and 280 RPM. 
Day three: Streptavidin solution was removed from the vial by pipetting and the vial was washed three times 
with 4 mL PBS. The vial was coated with 250 µL of 10 µg/mL biotinylated α-LTX-Lt1a (antigen) in 3% non-fat 
dried milk in phosphate-buffered saline (M-PBS) and incubated at room temperature for one hour. 
Meanwhile, per selection 25 µL of streptavidin coated Dynabeads was washed twice with 50 µL of 3% M-PBS 
before being blocked with end-over-end rotation in 25 µL 3% M-PBS for one hour at room temperature. Per 
selection 50 µL of IONTAS phage display library λ was blocked by mixing with 150 µL of 3% M-PBS and 
incubating at room temperature for one hour with end-over-end rotation. The antigen/M-PBS solution was 
pipetted out of the vial and it was washed once with phosphate-buffered saline with tween 20 (PBS-T) and 
thrice with PBS before being blocked with 4 mL 3% M-PBS at room temperature for one hour. The 3% M-PBS 
was removed from the Dynabeads and they were resuspended in 50 µL of 3% M-PBS and 200 µL of the 
blocked λ-library was added and incubated for one hour at room temperature before the deselected phage 
solution was transferred to a new tube. The vial was emptied for 3% M-PBS and washed thrice in PBS. 250 µL 
of the blocked and deselected phage solution was added to the vial which was incubated for one hour at 
room temperature. The phage solution was then pipetted out and the vial was washed six times in PBS-T and 
six times with PBS. 250 µL of 100 µg/mL trypsin in phage elution buffer was added to the vial and incubated 
at room temperature for 15 minutes. Earlier on in the day 2xYT media in an Erlenmeyer flask was inoculated

timed so the culture reached an OD600 between 0.4-0.5 before 5 mL of TG1 culture was aliquoted to a 50 mL 
falcon. The eluted phage solution was pipetted up and down 4 times before 250 µL was used to infect 5 mL 
of TG1 culture and incubated one hour at 37°C with 150 RPM. 100 µl of the uninfected TG1 cells were plated

2xYT media was plated on a 2xYT-GA plate (termed dilution plate). The rest of the phage infected culture was 
centrifuged for 5 minutes at 4,400 xg. The media was decanted, and the pellet of cells were resuspended in 
the remaining supernatant and plated on a 2xYT-GA plate (termed harvest plate). The 2xYT-GA plates were 
incubated O/N at 30 °C and the 2xYT-GK at 37 °C.

a cryotube and mixed with end-over-end rotation for around 10 minutes. The OD600 was measured and the 
culture was used to inoculate 5 mL of 2xYT-GA media to an OD600 of between 0.05-0.1. The remaining of the

harvested cells were stored at -80 °C. The 2xYT-GA culture was incubated at 37 °C at 280 RPM until an OD600

9. Results and discussion 
9.1 Isolation of δ-hexatoxin from A. robustus and H. infensa 
Three strategies were explored in order to obtain sufficient amounts of isolated δ-hexatoxin from funnel-
web spiders in Australia for subsequent phage display experiments in Denmark: (i) isolation of native toxin, 
(ii) recombinant expression of toxin, and (iii) chemical synthesis of the toxin, with the majority of time spent 
on recombinant expression.   
 
9.1.1 Fractionation of crude venom from A. robustus and H. infensa  
As our collaborator in Australia was unable to collect spiders for milking of venom, only limited amounts of 
crude venom were available. Regardless, isolation of native δ-hexatoxin was attempted, as it would make it 
possible to do ‘co-elution’ of recombinantly expressed or synthesized δ-hexatoxin with native toxin by 
RP-HPLC (i.e. same column, flow rate, HPLC system). A similar retention time would indicate that the 
recombinant or synthesized toxin adopts the native fold, which is important for activity.  
Crude venom from female H. infensa, and female and male A. robustus specimens was fractionated using a 
C18 analytical column on RP-HPLC to resolve the venom into fractions. The venom profiles of female and 
male A. robustus venom were compared to determine which peaks were more predominate for male spiders, 
see figure 13A, as they are known to contain more δ-HXTX-Ar1a than females [12,14]. All fractions with peak 
signals higher for males than females and that eluted at 15 min or later were initially analysed using 
MALDI-TOF MS with CHCA matrix, which did not reveal any mass matching the expected for δ-HXTX-Ar1a 
(average oxidised mass of 4848 Da). Eventually all fractions from both H. infensa and A. robustus were 
analysed using MALDI-TOF MS with CHCA matrix followed by the reductive matrix 1,5 DAN to try to sequence 
some fragments [101] with no success.  
A possible explanation is that the fractions contain multiple peptides, thus complicating identification. Also, 
there could be an issue with recovery of δ-hexatoxin (e.g. binding to tubes) or with ionizing.

9.1.2 Recombinant expression and purification of δ-HXTX-Ar1a and δ-HXTX-Hi1a  
The second strategy to obtain δ-hexatoxin was to recombinantly express the toxins using a periplasmic 
expression system according to Saez et al. 2017 [100]. This expression method had proven useful for 
disulphide rich small toxins in the past [100] and was fully integrated in the laboratory. The δ-hexatoxins have 
four disulphide bonds involving both the N- and C terminal, see figure 14.

To potentially discover cross-reactive scFvs by phage display selection later in the project, recombinant 
expression of δ-hexatoxin from both A. robustus and H. infensa was attempted.  
The designed plasmids encoding for the fusion proteins (see figure 12 in the method section 8.2.3.) were 
transformed into E. coli competent BL21 (DE3) cells. The periplasm was used as a compartment for 
recombinant protein expression because of its oxidative environment favouring disulphide bond formation 
[104].

IPTG seemed to improve the solubility of the expressed fusion protein.  
The purification of the recombinant toxin involved two steps. First, the released fusion protein from the lysed

9.1.3 Chemical synthesis of δ-HXTX-Ar1a 
The third strategy to obtain δ-hexatoxin involved chemical synthesis. One advantage of chemical synthesis is 
that it is possible to add a linker and biotin moiety N-terminally, which would circumvent the need to do so 
later and, in that way, control that all toxins have one biotin molecule attached. This can help antigen-
presentation when performing antibody phage display selections as discussed in the introduction section.  
In 2003, Alewood et al. described their successful chemical synthesis and oxidative refolding of δ-HXTX-Ar1a 
[19]. This is no trivial task as the peptide contains four disulphide bonds. The chemist, Zoltan Dekan, from 
the Alewood research group started the synthesis but was facing some challenges and to this date has not 
been able to deliver any toxin.

Subconclusion on 9.1 
Three strategies of obtaining δ-hexatoxin were explored. (i) isolation of native toxin (ii) recombinant 
expression of toxin, and (iii) chemical synthesis of the toxin.  
(i) No native δ-hexatoxin was isolated from crude venom from either A. robustus or H. infensa. Although, 
fractionation can be reattempted if additional crude venom is obtained. (ii) Recombinant expression was 
unsuccessful for δ-HXTX-Hi1a. A pilot batch of expression yielded a small quantity of recombinant 
δ-HXTX-Ar1a with the right mass and function demonstrated by a patch clamp electrophysiology assay. 
However, subsequent batches resulted in a truncated peptide. Further optimization of expression and 
purification is needed. (iii) Chemical synthesis of δ-HXTX-Ar1a was initiated by a chemist, Zoltan Dekan from 
the University of Queensland, but no toxin has been delivered yet.   
The work on δ-hexatoxin was discontinued, since the quantity of recombinant δ-HXTX-Ar1a was insufficient 
to carry out antibody phage display selections. 
 
9.2 Discovery of antibody fragments against α-LTX-Lt1a 
The focus of the remaining part of the thesis project is shifted towards discovery of human monoclonal 
antibody fragments against commercially acquired α-LTX-Lt1a from L. tredecimguttatus using phage display 
technology.   
 
9.2.1 Phage display selection 
α-LTX-Lt1a was reconstituted and biotinylated as described in the method section. Due to its size, it was not 
possible to use MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry to investigate the level and distribution of biotin molecules 
(share of α-LTX-Lt1a molecules with zero, one, two, three or more biotin attached). A biotin to toxin mol ratio 
of 3:1 was applied to biotinylate α-LTX-Lt1a. This ratio was used to increase the proportion of α-LTX-Lt1a that 
had at least one biotin attached. Given the size of α-LTX-Lt1a (131 kDa), having multiple biotin molecules 
attached is unlikely to block all possible epitopes, as biotin and linker has a mass of 588 Da.  
scFv-displaying phages from the IONTAS phage display library λ with a phage diversity of 1.6 x 1010 [91] were 
used to select scFvs that bind biotinylated α-LTX-Lt1a. Three rounds of selections were performed according 
to [87] to enrich the pool of phages displaying scFvs with high affinity to the toxin, which can be seen in figure 
19.

In each round of phage display selection, high affinity phages against α-LTX-Lt1a were accumulated and 
amplified, while the lower affinity phages were eliminated. Too few panning rounds allow too many low 
affinity phages to remain in the output, while too many panning rounds result in a loss of diversity and a 
possible accumulation of binders with an amplification bias. Additionally, too many panning rounds might 
not yield the scFvs with the highest affinities as the phages that replicate most efficiently in E. coli might not 
necessarily be the ones displaying the scFvs with the highest affinity for the toxin [92].  
Panning against α-LTX-Lt1a with the IONTAS phage display library κ with a phage diversity of 2.4 x 1010 was 
also attempted, but only resulted in a low number of selected phages and was, therefore, not continued to 
the second round.

The test showed an increase in CFU/mL for the phages of more than 10-fold between the second and third

be less important compared to smaller antigens. Since even more colonies were observed for directly coated 
α-LTX-Lt1a compared to streptavidin bound biotinylated α-LTX-Lt1a, coating directly with α-LTX-Lt1a seemed

selected colonies. Nonetheless, ELISA has several limitations. One of these is that the absorbance level 
reflects not only on the affinity of the respective scFv to its antigen, but also how well it is expressed in 
solution. Concentration-normalized ELISA in section 9.3.3 corrects for this.   
 
9.2.5 Sequencing of monoclonal scFvs 
In order to identify unique monoclonal scFvs, the scFv-encoding genes were amplified by PCR and sent for 
sequencing. Sequences were aligned in Jalview using multiple alignment. The complementarity-determining 
regions (CDR) were identified and all CDR regions were analysed, however, usually the heavy chain CDR3 
region is the most variable. Only six unique monoclonal scFvs were identified. 31 monoclonal scFvs turned 
out to be identical, which is not so surprising as they all showed similar absorbance signals in ELISA, see figure 
21B. The 31 identical monoclonal scFvs were approximately evenly split between stemming from panning 
round two and round three. Despite one scFv being predominate, it is still likely not over selection, as only 
7% of the tested colonies had an absorbance above the selected threshold in monoclonal ELISA.

9.3 Expression and purification of scFvs 
In order to eventually test the identified scFvs for their neutralizing ability in vitro, the six unique monoclonal

9.3.1 Optimization of periplasmic extraction protocol   
Initially, the scFv that produced the highest signal in the previous ELISAs (TPL020_02_G9) was expressed in 
50 mL cultures and purified using poly-prep chromatography columns to optimize the protocol for 
periplasmic extraction.  
Periplasmic extraction can be conducted following different methods. It often involves osmotic shock, 
potentially combined with the addition of ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) and lysozyme. The role of 
EDTA is to destabilize the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria like E. coli by chelating Mg2+ ions, thus 
allowing the lysozyme to enter the periplasm and hydrolyse the peptidoglycan cell wall; subsequently, the 
periplasm is released and leaves the cells as spheroplasts. Osmotic shock is used to weaken the outer 
membrane by adding a hypertonic solution, in this case a Tris-EDTA-sucrose buffer (TES), after which the cells 
are harvested and resuspended in a hypotonic solution (5 mM MgSO4 buffer) to partially disrupt the outer 
membrane while keeping the inner membrane intact [106,107].  
In the initial protocol the hypotonic solution (1:5 dilution of TES buffer) was added to the cell mixture after 
10 minutes incubation with the hypertonic solution (TES buffer). This protocol was compared to the one by 
Malherbe et. al. [107], where the hypertonic and hypotonic addition was separated by a harvesting step, and 
also to Martin et. al. where lysozyme was used [105]. The comparison of the three methods can be seen in 
figure 22.

from an identical protein chain. This monomer to dimer transition is in an equilibrium [108].

to release the produced scFv and/or a major of part of the produced scFv was aggregated due to misfolding.

9.3.2 Purification of scFvs using ÄKTAprime plus 
Since there were only six scFvs to be expressed, it was decided to directly focus on large-scale expression and 
purification using HisTrap columns on a ÄKTAprime plus system. The advantage of this system is the ability 
to vary the imidazole concentration, possibly resulting in more pure extracts of the scFvs without other 
proteins present.

in the French press under higher temperatures, which could affect the stability of the scFvs.  
The last issue to be discussed is whether some of the scFvs are prone to aggregate. The aggregated scFv can 
be separated from the soluble scFv by introducing a second purification step after IMAC involving a size 
exclusion column that could separate the aggregated protein from the monomeric scFv and the dimeric scFv 
as well as all other proteins present. This would allow the assessment of the extent of aggregation and 
dimerization.

10. Future research and perspectives  
10.1 Assessment of the discovered scFvs 
Six unique scFvs with varying affinity to α-LTX-Lt1a were discovered in this project, with the scFv 
TPL020_02_G9 presenting the most promising characteristics. The next step will involve the testing of the 
scFvs in vitro for their ability to neutralize the function of α-latrotoxin. Different assays to assess the toxicity 
of α-latrotoxin and its neutralization by antibodies have been employed in the literature including 45Ca2+ 
uptake assay in PC12 cells [61,110] and isolated chick biventer cervicis nerve-muscle preparation [48], but 
they would require a lot of time, expertise and resources to set them up in this laboratory. Instead, the 
Australian antivenom producer, Seqirus, has agreed to a collaboration. Three of the scFvs, TPL020_01_A5, 
TPL020_02_G9, and TPL021_03_D1 have been sent to Seqirus, where they will be tested for their ability to 
bind and neutralize α-latrotoxin from the redback spider (L. hasselti). The scFvs have not been selected for 
their cross-reactivity to α-LTX-Lh1a, but given that 93% of the amino acid residues of α-LTX-Lh1a are identical 
to α-LTX-Lt1a [48], cross-reactivity is possible. The importance of cross-reactive antibodies and how to 
discover them will be discussed in the following section.  
 
10.2 General perspectives 
10.2.1 Strategy for developing recombinant antivenoms  
The work of this thesis project aims to take the first steps towards designing and developing the first 
recombinant antivenom, consisting of human antibodies, against spider bite envenoming. This “bottom-up” 
approach involves the definition of monoclonal antibodies, followed by the recombinant expression of this 
known anti-toxin cocktail. This approach, however, requires in-depth knowledge of what toxins in the target 
venom need to be neutralized. Therefore, an alternative approach (“top-down”) could instead use 
recombinant DNA technology to express a polyclonal pool of antibodies of unknown composition. This would 
come with the advantage of not needing to elucidate the exact venom composition and toxicity profile as 
well as purifying each single toxin. Julve Parreño et al. explored this approach in 2017, where they used 
transgenic plants to produce recombinant polyclonal antibodies against snake venom [111]. In the case of 
antivenom against spider bite envenoming, the “bottom-up” approach explored in this project is likely the 
most suitable and moreover feasible. First of all, knowledge on spider venom components already exist for 
the spiders with medical importance to humans [4]. Secondly, medically important spiders most often only 
have one toxin with importance to human health, as in the case of α-latrotoxin for widow spiders and 
δ-hexatoxin for funnel-web spiders [16,50], thus in-depth knowledge of their venoms is likely not required 
for antivenom development. In relation to developing the recombinant antivenom, many aspects must be 
considered including efficacy, safety, manufacturability, and more [112].  
 
10.2.2 Broadening the neutralization capacity of monoclonal antibodies  
To simplify the manufacturing process, it is an advantage to have as low oligoclonality as possible. Therefore, 
it would be beneficial to broaden the neutralization capacity of each monoclonal antibody to cover ortholog 
toxins in closely related species. The ultimate goal, for widow spiders for instance, is to identify antibodies 
that can neutralize all α-latrotoxin irrespectively of which widow spider it came from. This is of importance, 
because some regions have multiple Latrodectus species and in addition, it would benefit regions of the world 
where specific antivenoms to treat envenoming from local Latrodectus species are not available. For funnel-
web spiders there is a significant geographical overlap between the species that cause severe envenoming 
==PAGE:55==
55 
 
[6]. The existing antivenom raised against male A. robustus venom is being used to treat all suspected severe 
envenomings from funnel-web spiders [5,6,11,15,113], which suggest the possibility of discovering cross-
reactive antibodies, but also the need for recombinant antivenom to have the same broadly neutralizing 
property.   
To fulfil such an ambition, several orthologs of a toxin are likely needed during the discovery process of cross-
reactive monoclonal antibodies. In Tropical Pharmacology Lab, the experience is that cross-reactive 
monoclonal antibody fragments against snake toxins are not discovered by chance but are a result of rational 
choice of which toxins to be used as antigens in each selection round of phage display. Experiments showed 
that antibody fragments discovered in phage display where the same snake toxin was used as antigen in all 
three rounds, could not recognize closely related toxins from other species in the same genus. If, however, 
the antigen was changed between the selection rounds, e.g. by using toxin A in round one and three, but 
toxin B in round two or vice versa, a panel of antibody fragments was discovered that could not only recognize 
toxin A and B, but also other related toxins. Furthermore, this was confirmed with in vitro functional assays 
of cell cytotoxicity [114].  
For widow spiders, two murine monoclonal antibodies raised against α-LTX-Lt1a, the IgG 4C4.1 and the Fab 
FM1, have both shown efficacy in vitro and for FM1 also in vivo in neutralizing the activity of whole 
L. tredecimguttatus venom [61,110]. However, 4C4.1 was not able to bind α-latrotoxin from other 
Latrodectus species [48]. Furthermore, it was shown to exert its effect through the prevention of 
tetramization of α-LTX-Lt1a prior to its insertion into cell membranes, but incapable of reversing α-LTX-Lt1a 
pore formation once the tetramer had been formed [115]. On the other hand, FM1 was able to reduce the 
severity of symptoms and prevent lethality of envenoming from crude L. tredecimguttatus venom in mice in 
rescue experiments [61], but has not been tested against α-latrotoxin or whole venom from other 
Latrodectus species. Polyclonal redback spider antivenom have been shown to successfully neutralize the 
activity of a variety of Latrodectus venoms both in vitro and in vivo [36,37]. α-latrotoxins are highly 
homologous, but do not have identical primary structure. Taken all together, it is perhaps possible to discover 
a single monoclonal antibody that can abolish the main effects of widow spider venoms following a bite, 
although it requires identifying an epitope on α-latrotoxin that is indispensable for all its functions and 
discovering an antibody targeting this epitope. Alternatively, a mixture of antibodies of low oligoclonality 
could be envisioned.  Likely, as mentioned previously, different α-latrotoxins need to be employed in the 
different selection rounds of the phage display selection discovery process. To do so, some hurdles must be 
overcome with the lack of availability being the main one. Only α-LTX-Lt1a is commercially available of the 
α-latrotoxins. Also, the full primary sequence is only known for α-LTX-Lt1a and α-LTX-Lh1a, making it difficult 
to infer the full extent of variability for α-latrotoxin.  
Looking to the case of funnel-web spiders, none of the δ-hexatoxins nor any crude venom are commercially 
available, which was also the reason why two of them, δ-HXTX-Ar1a and δ-HXTX-Hi1a, were attempted to be 
recombinantly expressed and purified in this project. Most likely it is crucial to use at least δ-HXTX-Ar1a as 
antigen in phage display, since bites from male A. robustus spiders are the most severe of them all. As 
depicted in figure 14, the two δ-hexatoxins are not identical. In fact, they share only 81% identity and 86% 
similarity, making the difference between them greater than for α-latrotoxins. Nevertheless, they are 
predicted to have the same fold due to conserved cysteines. In addition, the current polyclonal antivenom 
appears effective against all severe funnel-web bites according to case reports, as previously mentioned 
[11,113].

10.2.3 Selection of antibody format 
To effectively neutralize the effect of spider venom, it is important to consider which antibody format to use. 
There is no definitive answer as it depends on the characteristics of the toxin(s) that need to be neutralized 
and its effect in humans. Furthermore, each format present different pros and cons in terms of 
pharmacodynamics, pharmacokinetics, formulation, immunogenicity, and expression [116].  
In this project, an scFv phage display library was employed as a convenient in vitro method to discover 
antibody binding domains before conversion to a full IgG molecule. Human IgGs have the advantage of a long 
half-life in the body (21-28 days) due to its size (no glomerular filtration) and its constant Fc region that binds 
to the neonatal Fc receptor, which facilitate recycling [116]. Neurotoxins like δ-hexatoxin and α-latrotoxin 
act systematically and IgG antibodies could therefore hopefully intercept and neutralize the toxins before 
they reach their targets in the peripheral nervous system. This would be especially important for envenoming 
after a funnel-web spider bite where systemic envenoming progresses very fast, likely due to the low 
molecular mass of δ-hexatoxin. Nevertheless, a single-domain antibody that rapidly penetrates the tissues 
might also be an advantage. This could also hold true for widow spider bite envenoming where intense local 
pain is a common feature, indicating toxins acting locally. These single-domain antibodies could be from 
libraries of heavy-chain fragments found in camelids, potentially immunized, or be libraries of engineered 
human heavy domains. None of these formats have been tested against spider toxins yet. In the end, the 
different formats must be evaluated on an individual basis and a combination of single-domain and IgG 
antibodies might turn out to be the most effective treatment.  
 
10.2.4 Monoclonal antibodies for diagnostics  
Finally, it is also worth a discussion whether the discovered antibody fragments can be used for other 
purposes than treatment. Even if the discovered antibody fragments in this project cannot neutralize the 
effect of crude L. tredeciumguttatus venom, they might be useful for diagnostic purposes. In particular if the 
antibodies are capable of binding other α-latrotoxins as well. The diagnosis of latrodectism is clinical and no 
analytical assay currently exist to detect widow spider venoms in blood, urine, or at the bite site [5]. For 
Loxosceles spiders, diagnostic tests are of interest particularly to distinguish between dermonecrosis caused 
by a spider bite from other causes. In a research setting, sandwich-ELISA using polyclonal antibodies has been 
used to detect venom antigens from Loxosceles species [117]  and Phoneutria nigriventer [118], which 
showed high specificity and sensitivity. Similarly, it could be envisioned that a test for injection of venom 
from widow spiders would be of use in the research setting to help bring clarity on how many of suspected 
widow spider bites are in fact spider bites. This may pose clinically relevant as well as having an impact on 
public opinion given that the reputation of spider bites often exceeds their potential harm [7]. Developing 
point-of-care testing is probably unnecessary as differing between different Latrodectus species does not 
impact treatment and use of antivenom should be based on a clinical assessment.

10.3. Commercial and innovative perspectives 
The work presented here is, at its core, applied research, where the emphasis falls on how an innovative 
approach to antivenom development can eventually make a difference to the victims of animal envenoming. 
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of the discovery and development of human monoclonal 
antibody fragments against spider venom toxins. Future antivenom comprised of such human monoclonal 
antibodies is hypothesised to alleviate many of the disadvantages associated with the traditional antivenoms. 
However, without focus on commercialization, such an antivenom is unlikely to ever reach the market due 
to the high development costs. Discussed below are some brief deliberations on commercialization and 
protection of the work commenced in this thesis.  
Prior to commercialising a biotechnological product, an extensive development phase is typically required. 
To commit to such risk and cost, it is often necessary to seek protection of intellectual property rights e.g. by 
patents. In short, for something to be patentable, it must be (i) eligible for patent protection, (ii) novel, non-
obvious (in the US) or involve an inventive step (in Europe), and (iv) useful (in the US) or have industrial 
applicability (in Europe) [119,120]. Products, procedures, and uses can in principle be patented.  
A patent is not a right to make or sell an invention, but rather a right to exclude others to do so [120]. In the 
case of the work presented in this thesis, the approach and methods used for discovering and developing 
antibodies are not novel or inventive in itself. Instead the specific antibodies to be used in an antivenom is 
likely patentable, which means the sequences and their use could be protected. Whether it would be worth 
pursuing is another question given the cost of patenting and the low profit margin of antivenoms. On top of 
this, it is important to determine whether there is freedom to operate so other patents are not infringed. 
Professionals usually conduct such an analysis, but with access to Google Patents, it is possible to make an 
initial search oneself.  Based on a search on Google Patents involving the terms “antibody”, “spider venom”, 
“black widow”, and “antivenom” in different combinations, no patents were identified that would seemingly 
limit the freedom to operate. The patents found were mostly related to recombinant toxins (or parts of it) to 
be used for immunizing vertebrates to raise antibodies that could be used for diagnosis or treatment of 
loxoscelism. In addition, BLAST was used on the nucleotide sequences of the six discovered scFvs without any 
matches, indicating novelty given that the sequences have not previously been published.  
An alternative to obtaining a patent on the sequences of antibodies to be used in an antivenom is to keep 
them a trade secret. Copying the antivenom would be very laborious without access to the sequences or a 
high-quality phage display library.  
The market of antivenoms is very fragmented, and it is difficult to estimate the full market potential and 
potential return on investment. In the end, it is going to be a cost benefit analysis. Obviously, the business 
case does look better if the antivenom has broad applications, which an antivenom against widow spiders on 
all continents would have, including regions where no antivenom are currently available. Despite widow 
spider antivenom being a relative niche product, five antivenoms are on the market today, indicating the 
willingness of some governments to prioritize this type of medicine regardless of high production costs. In 
order to succeed in commercialising future antivenoms, collaborations are required to go through product 
development, clinical testing, regulatory approval, manufacturing, distribution, and marketing. The 
Australian antivenom company Seqirus has, as previously mentioned, agreed to collaborate, which illustrates 
the interest and potential for innovating the antivenom field.

11. Conclusion  
 
The overall aim of this thesis project was to discover human monoclonal antibody fragments against key 
spider venom toxins using phage display technology. This research could help create the foundation for 
future development of a recombinant antivenom against spider bite envenoming.  
The research was centred on two key toxins, δ-hexatoxin and α-latrotoxin, from funnel web and widow 
spiders, respectively, as these spiders are of great medical concern.  The project had three specific objectives.  
The first objective was to either isolate or recombinantly express δ-hexatoxin from two funnel-web species, 
A. robustus and H. infensa. Crude venom was scarce, and the isolation of native δ-HXTX-Ar1a and 
δ-HXTX-Hi1a was unfortunately unsuccessful. Thus, plasmids for recombinant expression were designed 
encoding δ-HXTX-Ar1a and δ-HXTX-Hi1a as fusion proteins to be expressed in an E. coli periplasmic expression 
system. A patch clamp electrophysiology assay of the purified recombinant δ-HXTX-Ar1a demonstrated that 
this strategy was successful but required further optimization and repetition to produce higher yields of the 
full-length recombinant toxin. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time a δ-hexatoxin has been 
expressed recombinantly.  
The second objective was to discover human scFvs against δ-hexatoxin and α-latrotoxin using antibody phage 
display technology. As recombinant expression of δ-HXTX-Ar1a only yielded a small quantity of toxin, phage 
display was only employed on α-LTX-Lt1a. Given that only six unique scFvs were discovered, the discovery 
process is currently being repeated with some modifications to hopefully allow for the discovery of a wider 
range of antibodies that bind α-LTX-Lt1a.  
The final objective was to evaluate the binding properties of the discovered scFvs. The scFvs were expressed 
and purified after optimizing a periplasmic extraction method for E. coli. ELISAs were used to rank the scFvs 
according to their affinity to α-LTX-Lt1a where especially one scFv showed great promise. Unfortunately, this 
particular scFv turned out be difficult to express and purify, requiring further optimization.  
While the work presented in this thesis has been successful in some respects, and a proof of principle for 
each of the objectives has been established, further optimization is required to exploit the full potential of 
this discovery pipeline. In vitro evaluation of the discovered scFvs by our collaborators will hopefully elucidate 
their potential use, and the repeated phage display discovery process will likely result in the identification of 
additional unique scFvs that bind α-LTX-Lt1a. Furthermore, phage display selections on δ-hexatoxin are 
planned, following the chemical synthesis of δ-HXTX-Ar1a in Australia. Together these findings have the 
potential to pave the way towards the first human recombinant antivenoms against spider bite envenoming.
