Summary (English)
Waste to Energy (WtE) plants provide a way to produce carbon-neutral energy
and reduce the amount of waste being landﬁlled. An interesting design for small-
scale applications is a Combined Heating, Cooling and Power (CHCP) plant,
based on waste gasiﬁcation and SOFCs. In the current study, the economic
attractiveness of such a project was investigated for two medium sized city-
business hotels located in the city of Thessaloniki in northern Greece.
The
electrical capacity of the plant was set at 100kWe.
In the beginning, the model of the plant was constructed in the Dynamic Net-
work Analysis (DNA) software. Speciﬁcally, the power generation unit (PGU),
consisting of the gasiﬁcation and the SOFC module, as well as the heat re-
covery unit (HRU), consisting of the Space Heating (SH) and Domestic Hot
Water (DHW) heat exchangers (HXs) and the absorption cooling module, were
integrated in the DNA model. Then, after deﬁning a number of operating pa-
rameters, the operation of the plant was simulated. The maximum theoretical
capacities of the heating and cooling production in the SH&DHW and the ab-
sorption cooling modules were derived.
In addition, special focus was given on the design of a syngas cleaning system,
which is capable of realizing the strict cleaning requirements regarding the var-
ious contaminants in the syngas, so that the performance of the components
downstream the gasiﬁer is not compromised and the reliability of the plant is
assured.
Then, the optimum operational strategy was obtained by forming and solving an
operational costs minimization problem using the data provided for the hourly
==PAGE:3==
ii
energy demand of the selected hotels and the General Algebraic Modeling Sys-
tem software (GAMS).
The obtained operational costs, along with the capital costs, were used to per-
form an economic analysis of the proposed plant (CHCP/P-EX), as well as the
pre-existing plant (P-EX) for the sake of comparison.
The results of the economic analysis showed that only the investment on Hotel
1 was favorable, due to its higher energy demand with respect to electricity,
heating and cooling, yielding an NPV of -3,452,905 efor the proposed CHCP/P-
EX plant and -3,498,432 efor the P-EX plant.
In the case that the capital
investment of the proposed CHCP plant is subsidized, the investment on Hotel
2 becomes attractive too, yielding an NPV of -1,739,631 efor the CHCP/P-EX
plant and -2,024,013 efor the P-EX plant, while in the case of Hotel 1 the NPV
for the CHCP/P-EX plant is higher than before, speciﬁcally -2,826,675 e.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nowadays, the need for a transition from fossil fuel-based energy systems to
more environmentally sound solutions, so that the global green house gas (GHG)
emissions are reduced, seems to be rising to new heights. This is fueled by the
doubt on whether the target set by the Paris Agreement (global warming of
2.7 ◦C above pre-industrial levels) will be reached.
The utilization of municipal solid waste (MSW), an abundant and free resource,
for the purpose of energy production in Waste to Energy (WtE) plants could
prove invaluable. Not only as a carbon-neutral energy production method and
a way to prevent GHG emissions from landﬁlling (1 ton of equivalent CO2 is
saved per each ton of waste combusted rather than landﬁlled [1]), but also as a
way of preserving landﬁll space (reportedly 80 % to 90 % of waste reduction in
volume [2]) and saving land that would otherwise be allocated for such purpose.
Speciﬁcally, in gasiﬁcation-based WtE plants, MSW is processed in order to
yield a fuel gas called syngas via the gasiﬁcation process. These plants oﬀer
solutions such as the widely known Integrated Gasiﬁcation Combined Cycle
(IGCC), where the syngas is burned in the process, or the Integrated Gasiﬁcation
Fuel Cell Cycle, which is based on the electrochemical reaction of the syngas in
solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs), generating electricity at high eﬃciency. Due to
the high operating temperature of the SOFCs (700 ◦C to 1000 ◦C), the energy
content of the high temperature exhaust gas can be retrieved in energy recovery

systems for the production of heating or cooling energy, thus increasing the
overall energy utilization.
The resulting plant is a WtE Combined Heating,
Cooling and Power (CHCP) plant, which is based on waste gasiﬁcation and
SOFCs.
An interesting ﬁeld of application for a small-scale, WtE CHCP plant, based on
waste gasiﬁcation and SOFCs, is the hotel sector in Greece for two main reasons.
Firstly, the climate of the country is the Mediterranean climate type, which is
characterized by mild winters and hot summers, creating a potential interest
for such an investment, due to the evenly distributed combined heating and
cooling energy demand around the year. Secondly, the hotel sector in general is
comprised by medium sized hotels (around 100 rooms) of the city-business type,
since these operate all-year round and are equipped with systems that cover the
whole range of the hotel’s seasonal energy needs.
The main goal of this project is to investigate the economic attractiveness of a
WtE CHCP plant for hotels, based on waste gasiﬁcation and SOFCs, providing
the main portion of the annual electrical, heating and cooling load of selected ho-
tels in Greece, while aided by the pre-existing energy system. To reach this goal,
a thermodynamic model of the plant is built and consequently simulated in the
Dynamic Network Analysis (DNA) software. In addition, the optimal operation
strategy is obtained, while utilizing the available units of both the CHCP and
the pre-existing plant, so that the operational costs are kept minimum during
every single hour of the plant’s annual operation.

Chapter 2
Design of the CHCP plant
The Waste-to-Energy (WtE) CHCP plant, which is investigated as a possible
solution for covering the main portion of the energy needs of a hotel, while aided
by the pre-existing plant during periods of high demand, consists of two main
units: a power generation unit (PGU) and a heat recovery unit (HRU).
The PGU, which is the preceding unit with respect to the energy ﬂow, includes
a municipal solid waste (MSW) gasiﬁcation module, followed by a solid oxide
fuel cell (SOFC) module. The gasiﬁcation of the MSW produces syngas, which
is used as a fuel together with atmospheric air, from the SOFCs to generate
electricity. After the mixture of air and non-reacted syngas at the outlet of
the SOFCs is burned in a catalytic burner, the resulting exhaust gas enters the
second unit, the HRU.
The HRU consists of two separate modules, connected in parallel: one that
produces useful heat for space heating (SH) and domestic hot water (DHW)
purposes and one that produces cooling energy.
The ﬁrst fulﬁlls its role by
means of heat transfer from the exhaust gas to the utility water of the SH and
DHW circuits, via two separate heat exchangers connected in series. The second
does so by heat transfer from the exhaust gas to the refrigerant of an absorption
chiller in its generator (a heat exchanger). The exhaust gas ﬂow can be split at
any ratio between the two branches, so that heating and cooling energy can be
generated according to the hotel’s needs.

2.1
The gasiﬁcation module of the plant
2.1.1
Theory of waste gasiﬁcation
In general, gasiﬁcation is deﬁned as the partial oxidation of waste, which occurs
when the oxidant is supplied at a lower amount than that required for its sto-
ichiometric combustion. The product of this process is syngas, which is a fuel
gas that consists mainly of hydrogen (H2) and carbon monoxide (CO), lesser
amounts of water vapor (H2O) and carbon dioxide (CO2), and a small amount
of methane (CH4), while it can also contain atmospheric nitrogen, if air is used
as the gasifying agent (medium for providing oxygen). However, it also carries
various contaminants, such as particulate matter and tars, as well as sulphur-,
chlorine-, alkali- and nitrogen compounds (H2S, HCl, NaCl and KCl, NH3 and
HCN), which create the need for cleaning the syngas to a varying extent (de-
pending on the application) before its use [3]. At the bottom of the gasiﬁer, i.e.
the reactor where the gasiﬁcation takes place, an amount of ash remains, which
includes non-volatile substances that did not go through gasiﬁcation.
The waste gasiﬁcation process includes the following steps:
1. Drying: The wet solid waste is dried up to certain degree, before entering
the gasiﬁer. This process is carried out at temperatures up to about 160 ◦C
[3].
2. Pyrolysis:
A thermo-chemical process occurs at temperatures up to
about 700 ◦C, which results in light gases (e.g. H2, CO, CO2, CH4, H2O,
NH3), tars and char. Tars is a mixture of various condensable organic com-
pounds with a molecular weight higher than that of benzene [4], while char
is the carbon rich solid residue of the waste [3]. There are many deciding
factors that aﬀect the resulting composition, quantities and characteris-
tics of the produced chemical species, the most important of which are
the composition and structure of the waste (Kawaguchi, as cited in [3]),
the conditions of temperature, pressure and gasiﬁer atmosphere, and the
heating rate provided by the speciﬁc gasiﬁer type (de Souza-Santos, as
cited in [3]).
3. Oxidation-Reduction chemical reactions: In an auto-thermal gasi-
ﬁcation process, the necessary heat for the endothermic reactions of the
process, is provided internally by a series of partial oxidation reactions that
occur in the reducing environment of the gasiﬁer. So, through the partial
oxidation of the lighter gases and char of the previous step, i.e. H2, CO,
lighter hydrocarbons and C in char, a series of endothermic reactions can

occur, including gasiﬁcation reactions involving steam and carbon dioxide,
as well as the thermal cracking of tars and lighter hydrocarbons [3]. For a
full list of the reactions, the reader can refer to the study by Arena et al.
[3]. All the aforementioned reduction reactions are equilibrium reactions,
although in reality the ﬁnal composition of the products depends on re-
action rates and the presence of catalytic eﬀects instead of the chemical
equilibrium after an inﬁnite amount of time (various sources, as cited in
[3]).
There are a number of operating parameters that aﬀect the waste gasiﬁcation
process, which are the following [3]:
• The Equivalence ratio (ER), which is the ratio between the oxygen
content in the gasifying agent and that required for stoichiometric com-
bustion. Values close to 0 refer to pyrolysis conditions and values equal
to or greater than 1 refer to complete combustion conditions. While lower
values of ER lead to greater amounts of char and tars, resulting in in-
eﬃciency in the energy extraction from the waste feedstock and possible
problems from condensation of tars, respectively, on the other hand, higher
values of ER lead to reduction of the H2 and CO content and thus, to a
lower syngas heating value. In Fig. 2.1, the eﬀect of ER on the com-
position of wood-derived syngas is shown, signifying the importance of
selecting a proper value of the parameter with respect to the performance
of an application downstream the gasiﬁer. In the current case study, this
parameter was not investigated.
• The reactor temperature, or to be more precise, the temperature pro-
ﬁle along the diﬀerent sections of the gasiﬁer has an impact on the state
of the bottom ash and, up to a certain degree, on the tar content in the
produced syngas (Devi et al. and Arena et al., as cited in [3]).
• The residence time of gases and waste, which can vary depending
on the gasiﬁer type and design.
• The physical properties and composition of solid waste aﬀect the
composition of the syngas and the ash produced by the gasiﬁcation process,
as well as the requirement of pre-treatment of the feedstock, before it is
supplied to the gasiﬁer. So, the properties of the solid waste, which are of
interest regarding the gasiﬁcation process, are its elemental composition,
its ash content and composition, its volatile matter content, its content
in contaminants (e.g. S, Cl, Na, K, N and heavy metals), its moisture
content and its bulk density and size.
It should be noted that the volatile matter content aﬀects the fraction of
the species that migrate to gas phase, aﬀecting the amount of light gases

The moisture content of the waste and its particle size play an important

the size of the waste particles was not investigated.

2.2
The absorption plant module
The absorption (cooling) plant or absorption chiller is a refrigeration system,
which is based on a heat-driven, vapor absorption cycle in order to deliver cool-
ing energy, in contrary to the more common mechanical/electrical-driven vapor
compression cycle, which is utilized by the electric chillers. Despite the fact
that the energetic eﬃciency of the former is considerably lower than the latter,
typically ranging from 0.5 to 0.9 compared to greater than 3 [9], the utilization
of waste heat for its operation, if available, oﬀers a signiﬁcant advantage over
the electrical energy need of a compressor in an electric chiller.

aqueous solution of LiBr (Lithium bromide) or that of NH3 (Ammonia), but the
focus will be put on the former, since that one will be used in the absorption
plant of the current study. The driving force of the vapor absorption cycle is the
concentration of LiBr in the aqueous solution, which varies from high, giving
the attribute "rich" (or "strong") to the solution, to low, giving it the attribute
"poor" (or "weak"). In Fig. 2.2, the diagram of an absorption cooling plant is

Initially, the "weak" LiBr solution is pumped from the absorber outlet at low
pressure to the generator inlet at high pressure. Then, in the generator, thanks
to the heat provided via heat transfer with a hot medium, water is removed
from the solution through boiling, leaving a "strong" LiBr solution behind,
which is then throttled to the low-pressure absorber through a valve. The water
vapor, which was produced in the generator, proceeds to the condenser, where it
rejects heat to the cooling water ﬂowing at the secondary side, becoming liquid.
Afterwards, the pressure of the liquid water is lowered to the low pressure level
of the evaporator, as it is forced to pass through an expansion valve. Since
the pressure is lower, the vaporization temperature of water is also lower than
before, which enables the transition from liquid to vapor state. This takes place
in the evaporator and the result is the removal of heat from the water in the
secondary side, which is actually the generated cooling energy. Due to the low
pressure in the absorber, water vapor enters the component, where it dilutes
the "strong" LiBr solution coming from the generator, in a process where heat

Modelling and simulation of the plant’s op-
eration in DNA
After the general layout of the investigated WtE CHCP plant was previously in
chapter 2, the PGU and HRU need to be speciﬁed further with respect to the
components that comprise them and the various operating conditions, so that
the plant can provide electricity, heating and cooling to the hotel. The plant was
modelled using the Dynamic Network Analysis (DNA) software, which simulates
the steady state operation of the CHCP plant.
To elaborate on the way that DNA works, when the various components of an
energy system are connected with each other in such way that energy ﬂows in the
system, in the form of chemical energy contained in the fuel, and eventually out
of the system, having been transformed in a diﬀerent, useful form in the process
(e.g. electrical, thermal or cooling energy), they create a thermodynamic system
network. This network can be described by a system of equations, stemming
from the energy and mass balance for the various components in the network,
which can be solved provided that a suﬃcient number of operating parameters
(e.g. temperature, pressure, mass ﬂow) are given as input by the user, depending
==PAGE:25==

on the type of the component and its connections to other components in the
network. Running the simulation gives a solution to the system of equations
and thus, the values of the unknown operating parameters at various nodes of
the network and at a steady state of the operation of the energy system.
In the following sections, the DNA model of the investigated CHCP plant is split
in four parts, which will be analyzed separately: the gasiﬁcation and SOFC mod-

Chapter 3
Syngas cleaning system
The product gas of the gasiﬁcation process of a solid fuel contains numerous
compounds, usually in either solid or gaseous phase, which in most applica-
tions with integrated gasiﬁcation are considered contaminants, since they have
detrimental eﬀects to the performance of components downstream the gasiﬁer.
However, the extent of those eﬀects varies considerably depending on the nature
of the application that utilizes the produced syngas, which may be either heat
(and/or electricity production) or conversion to other types of fuels or chemicals,
like synthetic natural gas (SNG), liquid hydrocarbons of variable chain length
(e.g. waxes, diesel fuel, oleﬁns e.t.c) via the Fischer-Tropsch (FT) method, liquid
methanol via synthesis methods and more. Moreover, and in the case of stan-
dalone electricity or combined heat and electricity production, the tolerance
limits of the equipment involved in each process towards the aforementioned
contaminants depends on whether syngas is combusted and the produced ﬂue
gas fed to a gas turbine or it undergoes electro-chemical reactions in a fuel cell
stack.
==PAGE:45==

3.1
Contaminants in the produced syngas and
their impact on the plant’s equipment down-
stream the gasiﬁer
In general, the contaminants in the fuel gas produced by gasiﬁcation of a cer-
tain type of feedstock diﬀer from the ones that would be found in the ﬂue
gas produced by its complete combustion, since the ﬁrst process takes place
in a reducing environment, where the amount of oxidant is lower than what is
required for stoichiometric combustion, whereas the second takes place in an
oxidizing environment, where the opposite situation occurs. Speciﬁcally, most
of the species retain their less-oxidized forms when the gasiﬁcation reactions
take place: CO, H2, H2S, NH3 or HCN instead of CO2, H2O, SO2 and NO or
other oxides, respectively, as it is pointed out by Arena et al. [3]. Of course,
not all of the compounds mentioned above have purely, or any, negative eﬀect
to the equipment included in a system such as the one in question: H2, CO and
NH3 act as a fuel for the SOFCs, but CO may cause carbon deposition on a
catalyst’s surface under speciﬁc conditions, thus leading to its fouling; on the
other hand, H2S is a poison for a great number of catalysts.
Moreover, the amount of contaminants that may be found in the gasiﬁcation
product gas depends, apart from the type of gasiﬁer used and the operating
conditions of the gasiﬁcation process itself, on the levels of all the diﬀerent
kinds of impurities contained in the feedstock. For example, the level of alkali
species in municipal waste is similar to what is found in biomass, since around
40 % of the household solid waste (the predominant type of municipal waste
considered in this study) is comprised of organic matter and food waste, and
is generally quite high compared to coal, another common solid fuel used in
gasiﬁcation processes. On the other hand, coal naturally contains a higher level
of sulfur species compared to biomass or municipal waste.
In the following subsections, the various contaminants are separated in diﬀer-
ent categories depending on their nature or the chemical composition of the
compounds that they form. For each contaminant category, the way they af-
fect the system downstream the gasiﬁer and the imposed tolerance levels for
the ones that should be removed from the gas stream are presented, along with
estimations of their concentrations in the syngas.

3.1.1
Tars
Tars are condensable organic compounds that vary from primary oxygenated
compounds to heavier deoxygenated hydrocarbons and polycyclic aromatic hy-
drocarbons (PAHs), which are classiﬁed as primary, secondary and tertiary tars
in an increasing molecular weight order (Stevens, as cited in [14]). The primary
and tertiary tars can condensate even at high temperatures, causing fouling and
clogging in the equipment of systems based on gasiﬁcation, while the secondary
tars can deactivate catalysts used in fuel gas upgrading [14]. Regarding SOFCs
speciﬁcally, Aravind [4] mentions that tars can probably cause carbon deposition
on the anodes of the fuel cells.
The tar species in the produced syngas depend on the characteristics of the
feedstock which is used in the gasiﬁcation process, as well as the operating
parameters of the gasiﬁcation itself, e.g. temperature, pressure, the type and
amount of the gasifying agent, the feedstock residence time in the gasiﬁer e.t.c
(Torres et al., Devi et al. as cited in [14]). With increasing temperature and
reaction time, the overall tar content in the syngas is reduced, but the tar
yield composition demonstrates a gradual transition from the primary to the
tertiary tars, which are diﬃcult to remove [14]. According to [4], gasiﬁcation
temperatures of around 1000 ◦C yield mostly tertiary tars, while low gasiﬁcation
temperatures of around 500 ◦C yield mostly primary tars.
Also, the type of the gasiﬁer plays an important role in the resulting tar con-
tent in the syngas. According to the study by [15], an air-blown circulating
ﬂuidized-bed biomass gasiﬁer gives a tar content of 10 g/m3, while Aravind [4]
mentions that ﬁxed-bed gasiﬁers yield a tar content in the syngas depending
on their design: updraft ﬁxed-bed gasiﬁers produce tar concentrations in the
range of 20 g/Nm3 to 100 g/Nm3, while downdraft ﬁxed-bed gasiﬁers produce
signiﬁcantly lower tar concentrations, in the range of 0.01 g/Nm3 to 1.5 g/Nm3
(Stevens, as cited in [4]), owing to their design, which forces the volatile prod-
ucts of the pyrolysis step to pass through a high temperature combustion zone.
The advantage of a downdraft ﬁxed-bed gasiﬁer regarding the tar content in the
produced syngas was demonstrated experimentally with the Viking gasiﬁer, de-
veloped by the Mechanical Engineering Department of DTU, where the resulting
tar content in the wood-chips-derived syngas was less than 0.015 g/Nm3.
In the current case study, no secondary systems for tar removal will be con-
sidered, since it is assumed that tars will be greatly diminished primarily, i.e.
inside the gasiﬁer. In order to achieve that, a down-draft ﬁxed bed gasiﬁer will
be used and an appropriate gasiﬁcation temperature, after the pyrolysis zone of
the reactor, will be selected.

3.1.2
Particulate matter
The term particulate matter refers to particles whose size varies from < 1 µm
to > 100 µm and the distribution of the sizes of particulate matter contained
in the produced syngas can vary depending according to the feedstock and the
gasiﬁcation process (Hoﬀmann, as cited in [14]).
It is comprised mainly of
inorganic compounds, such as compounds based on alkali metals, alkaline earth
metals, silica and other metals, as well as residual solid carbon (Szemmelveisz
et al., Gustafsson et al., as cited in [14]). The impact of temperature on the
particulate matter composition is important, since some compounds, such as
alkali-based ones, will solidify and coagulate to form particles at temperatures
below 600 ◦C (see section 3.1.3).
Particulate matter can cause fouling, corrosion and erosion in components down-
stream the gasiﬁer (Newby et al., Stiegel and Maxwell, Seville as cited in [15]).
In addition, according to Aravind [4], particulate matter with sizes that range
from the sub-micron level to a few microns could have a detrimental eﬀect on
the performance of SOFCs, since the sizes of the pores of the catalysts in the
anodes lie in the aforementioned range and thus, blockages may occur.

was required.

3.1.3
Alkali compounds
Compared to coal, biomass-based feedstock contain alkali metals in higher con-
centrations. The alkali metals with a more pronounced presence in those feed-
stocks are potassium and, to a lesser extent sodium.
Alkali react partly with other ash components of the feedstock and thus create
non-volatile compounds that remain in the ash at the bottom of the gasiﬁer.
Some alkali compounds (chlorides, hydroxides, sulfates) though, melt or vaporize
at temperatures higher than 600 ◦C, forming aerosols or vapors, respectively, and
thus leave the gasiﬁer (Smeenk et al., Dayton et al., Turn et al. as cited in[14]).
As the temperature of the syngas drops below 600 ◦C downstream, the alkali
compounds that exist in vapor phase may go to liquid or even to solid phase.
Speciﬁcally, they might solidify and coagulate to form particles [4] or they might
condense on ﬂy ash particles contained in the syngas or chemically react with
them. Direct condensation on surfaces of components, such as ducts or heat
exchangers, is also possible, thus causing corrosion or ash fouling [18](Smeenk
et al., Trembly et al., as cited in [14]).
Additionally, alkali compounds may have a negative impact on the performance
of the SOFCs’ electrodes, since it has been reported that they can poison fuel
reforming catalysts (Stevens et al., as it was cited in [4]), but the eﬀect of those
compounds on the SOFCs has not been studied thoroughly [4]. However, it has
been observed that, when several hundreds of ppms of impurities, such as Na2O,
were present in the structure of the anode material of SOFCs, their performance
was degrading at a faster rate compared to the case, where the impurities did
not exceed a few tens of ppms [4].
From the above, it can be claimed that the fraction of the alkali metals, which
escapes the gasiﬁer through the formation of vapors or aerosols, can be removed
from the gas stream by strategically placing the particulate matter cleaning
devices in a way that the condensation of the aforementioned gaseous alkali
compounds occurs just before the syngas enters the devices, provided that the
temperature of the gas at that point has been brought down and below 600 ◦C.
For example, such a placement would be somewhere in the vicinity of the steam
generator component in the gasiﬁcation module (SG) of the DNA model (see Fig.
2.3), where the temperature at its syngas side ranges from 760 ◦C at its inlet, to
450 ◦C at its outlet. In reality, the aforementioned temperature range could be
served by two separate HXs, with the particulate matter cleaning device placed
in between them.
Consequently, it is assumed that no dedicated cleaning device will be required

3.1.4
Sulfur compounds
Under the gasiﬁcation conditions, the sulfur contained in the feedstock is mainly
converted to hydrogen sulﬁde (H2S) and, to a lesser extent, to carbonyl sulﬁde
(COS) [14].
Sulfur compounds have the tendency to corrode metal surfaces
[15] and, in addition, they act as poison for catalysts used to upgrade or clean
syngas, leading to their deactivation [14].
More importantly, experimental results from the operation of SOFCs fed with
H2S-contaminated syngas have shown that even concentrations of 1 ppmv can
have a serious eﬀect on the performance of SOFCs with Ni/YSZ (Ytria-stabilized
zirconia) anodes, due to poisoning of the catalyst via the adsorption of H2S
molecules on its active sites, thus hindering the electro-chemical reaction of fuel
molecules. [4].
Consequently, for the current study, it was assumed that the tolerance limit for
H2S will be set at 1 ppmv.

DNA simulation results for the syngas composition and on a dry basis, from the
readjusted molar fraction of H2S from Tab. 2.9, giving: Cin, H2S = 682 ppmv.

Consequently, for the current study it was assumed that setting the tolerance

in this area, will be prioritized in the current study.
2. Favorable sulﬁdation reaction
The eﬃciency of the sulﬁdation reaction should be as high as possible, al-
lowing only very low concentrations of H2S in the eﬄuent syngas (<1 ppmv
for the current study). It is controlled by two parameters—the thermody-
namics and the kinetics of the reaction; the ﬁrst determines the capability
of a metal oxide adsorbent to remove sulfur at speciﬁc reaction conditions
(i.e. temperature, pressure, contaminated gas composition) and is mea-
sured by calculating the partial pressure of H2S (which is dependent on its
concentration) over the sorbent at equilibrium state, whereas the second
dominates the rate of the reaction. Therefore, the most suitable sorbents
for desulfurization are the ones that demonstrate the most optimum bal-
ance between these two parameters [28].
3. Not favorable or very slowly progressing side reactions
Some metal oxides face stability issues, since they may be reduced to their
respective elemental forms if they are exposed to a reducing atmosphere
under high temperature conditions. Such a reducing atmosphere could be
provided by the syngas itself, due to the presence of reducing agents such
as H2 and CO in high concentrations. The reduction of metal oxides is un-
desirable because it lowers the desulfurization eﬀectiveness of the sorbents
and may even cause loss of sorbent material due to volatilization of the
metal elements. Carbon deposition on certain metal oxides is another side
reaction, which could eventually lead to sorbent passivization (see more
in section 3.3.2.2).
4. High sulfur sorption capacity
In general, the capability of a solid sorbent to attain high amounts of
sulfur, before it becomes saturated and needs to be regenerated, oﬀers low
down-time and operational costs, owing to the less frequently occurring
regeneration cycles and the slower aging of the sorbent from the repeating
sulﬁdation-regeneration cycles.
5. Ability to be suﬃciently regenerated
Via a sorbent’s regeneration, it should be possible to revert it to a state,
==PAGE:65==
54
Syngas cleaning system
which is as close as possible to the one it had just before its latest sulﬁ-
dation.
6. Strength and durability for a considerable number of sulﬁdation-
regeneration cycles
Even after a considerable number of sulﬁdation-regeneration cycles, the
sorbent should be able to retain its capability to adsorb H2S to such a
degree, which is as close as possible to its fresh state4 capability. In other
words, the sorbent should age well over the passage of multiple desulfur-
ization cycles.
7. Limited or no sulfate formation during regeneration
For a given set of sorbent regeneration conditions, speciﬁcally oxygen con-
centration and temperature (in the case of oxidative regeneration), two
diﬀerent sulﬁdized metal oxide sorbents will revert to their pre-sulﬁdation
condition with a diﬀerent degree of success, due to the fact that the extent
of metal sulfate formation will vary between the two cases. So, depend-
ing on the oxygen concentration of the regenerative gas stream and the
operative temperature, it is possible that the metal sulfate (M2(SO4)y),
which could be formed by the reaction of the excess supplied O2 with the
produced SO2 and the regenerated metal oxide (MxOy), may be stable.
In such a case, after a number of desulfurization cycles, the sorption per-
formance will decay to the point of a practically complete deactivation of
the sorbent, due to the cumulative substitution of metal oxide with metal
sulfate5. Hofbauer et al. refer to the spalling of the sorbent by the large
sulfate anions as the reason behind the decrease in its sorption capacity
and its loss of reactivity [27].
Such a sulfate formation reaction, as the one mentioned above, is sug-
gested by Garcia et al. [29] for Mn and Cu, who experimented with the
regeneration of Mn/Cu mixed oxides, after subjecting them to sulﬁdation
using a gas with a composition of 0.5 % (vol.) H2S / 10 % (vol.) H2 / bal-
ance N2. After conducting thermobalance tests, they reported that, under
the temperature of 720 ◦C and 3 % (vol.) O2, manganese sulfate (MnSO4)
was formed, while, on the contrary, no copper sulfate (CuSO4) was de-
tected in the regenerated sorbent sample.
Garcia et al.
also reported

8. Low costs of purchase, preparation, etc.
Among many factors, the cost of purchase of a speciﬁc metal (oxide) is de-
termined by the costs of mining and reﬁning in order to acquire the metal
element, that are aﬀected, in turn, by the accessibility of the deposits
and the degree of concentration of the metal element in them, making the
mining process either more or less economical. In any case, the cost of
purchasing a speciﬁc metal is subject to ﬂuctuations following the supply
and demand law.
The market price of minerals, including metals, is aﬀected, as many other
commodities, by the interplay between supply and demand levels and their
elasticity to changes in price. In general, an increase in the demand of a
mineral will be met by the subsequent increase in its supply at higher
production costs, which will eventually lead to a higher market price,
when equilibrium between supply and demand is achieved (elastic supply).
However, if the supply (production rate) of a speciﬁc mineral is limited by
its scarcity in the earth’s crust and no additional deposits are discovered,
then a grow in demand will lead to a greater increase of its price than in the
previous case, accompanied only by a small increase in supply (inelastic
supply) [30]. Another usual case of inelastic supply is that of minerals
which are not acquired as the main economic product of mining a speciﬁc
ore, but as by-products of another main product; e.g., cobalt (Co) is largely
produced as a by-product of nickel and copper, accounting for around 50 %
and 35 %, respectively, of the total cobalt production [31].
Overall, metals are not quite as abundant as non-metals and some of
them are experiencing high degrees of reserves depletion, such as tin, zinc,
copper and chromium, which could sustain a continuous increase in their
price, if the demand does not decline and/or no signiﬁcant reserves are
discovered [30].

carried out, limits the capability of TiO2 to prevent Zn vaporization. For this
reason, and mentioning the ﬁndings of a study by Sasaoka et al., Ohtsuka et al.
suggested that, desulfurization using ZnO-based sorbents should be performed
at temperatures below 600 ◦C [34].

removal, but they are thermodynamically limited, so that they are not able

forming zinc ferrites (ZnFe2O4), they oﬀer the same advantages as titanium
oxide, but they are less eﬀective at it (Meng et al., as cited in [32]); however, as
opposed to the inactive TiO2 with regard to the sulﬁdation reaction, iron oxides
aid their zinc counterparts in a zinc ferrite sorbent during sulﬁdation, oﬀering,
in fact, a greater sorption capacity and improved regeneration properties to this
sorbent, compared to one consisting of pure zinc oxide (Tamhankar et al., as
cited in [14]).

3.3.3
Chlorine compounds
Regarding the removal of chlorine from the gas stream, most of which evolves
from the solid to the gas phase as hydrogen chloride (HCl) under the waste gasi-
ﬁcation conditions, a common solution is the use of a sorbent that acts upon
HCl exclusively. This is usually done with the aid of alkali and alkali earth
metal based sorbents. The preference to these substances stems from thermo-
dynamic calculations, which have indicated they could perform satisfactorily
when they are used in HCl cleanup duty at temperatures greater than 500 ◦C
[32]. Speciﬁcally, hydrogen chloride is adsorbed to the solid sorbent’s surface
through chemisorption in a process called dechlorination, which is represented
by one of the three chemical reactions below,
MO + 2 HCl −−→MCl2 + H2O
(3.26)
MCO3 + 2 HCl −−→MCl2 + H2O + CO2
(3.27)
M2CO3 + 2 HCl −−→2 MCl + H2O + CO2
(3.28)
, where M is alkali or alkali earth metal elements. When the metal oxide sorbent
MO is exposed to an environment that has a suﬃciently high concentration of
CO2, it becomes carbonated, resulting in MCO3 or M2CO3 [32]. As it will be
pointed out further below, the gasiﬁcation product gas has such a high concen-
tration of CO2 that it enables the carbonation of the metal oxide sorbents.

5.1
Capital costs
The total capital cost of the investment can be estimated by combining cost
functions for the various pieces of equipment that comprise the plant, which
have been derived from correlations of real cost data from manufacturers given
as a function of a "size" parameter, allowing cost scaling in relation to the
equipment capacity. In most of the cases, such functions give estimates of Free
on Board (FOB) costs, which means that they reﬂect the cost of manufacturing
the speciﬁc piece of equipment, including loading charges onto shipping means
(e.g. a ship, railcar, or truck), but excluding freight or unloading charges [42].
However, so as to estimate the total capital investment (TCI) cost, the FOB
costs need to be expanded in order to include freight and unloading charges
of the equipment (delivery costs), as well as costs related to the installation
of the equipment, investment on utilities (e.g. auxiliary equipment for eﬄuent
treatment or process water in a chemical process plant e.t.c), investment on
oﬀ-sites (e.g. auxiliary buildings such as oﬃces, guardhouses, warehouses and
the required personnel, roads, plant service vehicles e.t.c) and working capital
(e.g. raw materials for the plant start-up and their transportation cost, money
to meet payroll when starting up e.t.c) [42]. In addition, since cost data come
from various sources and refer to many diﬀerent years, such cost values have to
be brought up-to-date utilizing cost indexes, the most popular of which is the
==PAGE:121==

Chemical Engineering Plant Cost Index (CEPCI), due to the fact that it has
been constantly updated on an annual basis from 1957 till now (2018).
5.1.1
Cost scaling law
The cost of a piece of equipment is related to a parameter which is characteristic
of its capacity, and thus, given a known base cost cB of equipment with capacity
qB and a cost scaling factor f, the equipment cost cE of capacity qE may be
calculated through the following power law [42]:

fact that such functions were formed by correlated data.

5.2
Operational and maintenance (O&M) costs
The CHCP system based on waste gasiﬁcation and solid oxide fuel cells is ex-
pected to bear the main part of the annual electrical, heating and cooling load
of the selected hotels after its introduction to the pre-existing system, while
the latter remains on standby, ready to cover the rest of the energy demand
at any instance of the plant’s operation. Knowing the hourly demand for the
three energy components over the course of a year for the selected hotels and
having assumed a speciﬁc level of electrical, heating and cooling energy capacity
for the CHCP plant in question, ﬁnding the most optimal operation strategy
of the new combined system requires solving an optimization problem, where
the operational costs of the plant should be minimized at every single hour of
operation on an annual basis, while being possible to use units of the old and
the new plant; provided that the energy demand of the hotel is always met. The
layout of the optimization problem is presented in section 5.2.1.
In section 5.2.2, the methodology for determining the operational cost factors
for the electricity/fuel which is purchased or sold (in the case of electricity), is
presented.

5.2.2.1

The cost of the heating oil was derived from the spot price for purchasing heat-

5.2.2.2
PGU operational cost cf−pgu
The operational cost of the PGU is not related to the waste feed that needs
to be supplied to the gasiﬁer, but to the acquisition of fresh sorbent for HCl
removal.
The municipal waste is provided to the plant by the municipality’s garbage
collection service and it is expected that it would not bear any extra costs
beyond the garbage collection fee, which is already paid to the municipality by
the hotel owner. In fact, it could be claimed that there are eligible grounds for
a reduction in the amount of money paid for such a fee, owing to the fact that
the hotel contributes to the reduction of the volume of waste that is received by
the landﬁll and thus, avoids the environmental impact of opening new landﬁlls.
However, such a fee reduction was not considered in the current study.
As a non-regenerable sorbent, NaAlO2, which is used for HCl removal from
the syngas, introduces a cost that is related to its replacement with fresh ma-
terial, once the speciﬁed breakthrough level of HCl concentration in the gas
stream is reached. So, a relation that links the fuel energy input rate to the
PGU, ˙Fpgu, wet−HHV (or simply ˙Fpgu), with the delivered equipment cost of the
sorbent cDEC will be derived.
The calculation procedure and the relevant data are presented in the Appendix

5.4
NPV calculation
A number of deﬁnitions and considerations regarding the economic analysis
methodology are given below.
All of the prices are considered "end-of-year", which means that they attain the
values it is assumed they would have at the end of the year.
The base year of the project, t = 0, is the year when the investment is completed
(ready to operate) and priced (investment cost). The pricing is done at the last
day of that year.
The economic lifetime of the project Tlife is the time, at the end of which, the
investor is interested in knowing if the project will be economically viable or
not.
5.4.1
Real and nominal prices
The diﬀerence between real and nominal prices is that real prices do not take
into account changes in price due to inﬂation, while nominal prices do, being
equal to the price one has to pay at a speciﬁc point in time. For example, a
price of a speciﬁc product in a future date is expected to be higher than in the
present, simply because of inﬂation. Consequently, since the investor would be
interested to know this kind of price, all prices in the economic evaluation in
this study will be transformed and used on a nominal basis.
The nominal prices in the years succeeding a base year of reference, in which
the price of an item is known, can be calculated by multiplying the reference
price with an inﬂation index ii,
Pt, nom = Pt, real · iit
(5.35)
, where t denotes the current calculated year and Pt, real is the real price, which
is constant at every year t and equal to the price at the reference year. In order
to ﬁnd the inﬂation index at the next year t + 1, the following equation is used,
iit+1 = iit · (1 + it+1)
(5.36)

remains constant every year, Eq. 5.35 combined with Eq. 5.36 gives:
Pt, nom = Pt, real · (1 + i)n
(5.37)
In Eq. 5.37, n is the number of years starting with n = 0 for the reference year
until the year t is reached.
5.4.2
Free and discounted cash ﬂows - NPV
Cash ﬂows represent the movement of money that enters or exits the project (in
this case the proposed multi-generation energy plant) for a speciﬁc time period.
In this case, annual cash ﬂows are considered for each year of the project’s
economic lifetime.
Speciﬁcally, these cash ﬂows are categorized as follows:
• Revenues (REV) are the cash ﬂows going into the project and in the
current case study is the amount of money which is earned by selling
electricity at a price rate of cel−ex (e2017/kWh) on an annual basis.
• Capital investment expenses (CAPEX) is the one-time cash ﬂow
coming out of the project in the base year t = 0 and, in the current study,
it is the TCI cost of the total investment, as it was presented in section
5.3.1.
• Operational expenses (OPEX) are the cash ﬂows coming out of the
project and reﬂect the associated O&M costs. In the current case study,
it is the amount of money that is required for the operation of the plant
on an annual basis, speciﬁcally for purchasing electricity from the grid at
a cost rate of cel (e2017/kWh), purchasing heating oil for the boiler at
a cost rate of cf−boiler (e2017/kWh) and operating the PGU of the new
plant cf−pgu (e2017/kWh), as well as the amount of money needed for its
maintenance on an annual basis.

Discussion and conclusions
In the following, a deeper interpretation of the study’s results is attempted, as
well as a discussion on the challenges encountered during various parts of the
study and the possible impact of some assumptions or simpliﬁcations that had
to be made during the design process of the investigated WtE CHCP plant.
The syngas cleaning system
Due to the strict requirements regarding the concentration of contaminants in
the produced syngas, a hot gas cleaning system was used, which created the need
of using adsorbents as the only solution for eﬀective syngas cleaning. The author
was restricted in the use of sorbents which have been experimentally tested and
breakthrough curves were provided in the respective studies.
Moreover, the
necessity to use the aforementioned sorbents in the current case study under
diﬀerent conditions than the ones used in the experimental studies and the lack
of knowledge regarding some material properties of the sorbent, introduced the
need to make assumptions in order to ﬁnally obtain the values of two necessary
parameters: the breakthrough time of the dechlorination/desulfurization process
in the ﬁxed-bed reactors and the pressure drop along the reactors.
It is rather important to highlight the signiﬁcance of breakthrough time, since
a wrong estimation of its value could potentially lead to signiﬁcant errors in the
OPEX of the investigated WtE plant, considering that the cost factor cf−pgu
==PAGE:155==
144
Discussion and conclusions
is related to the replacement of the non-regenerable sorbent NaAlO2 with fresh
material after the breakthrough level has been reached.
If the syngas cleaning system becomes unavailable or underperforms, the reli-
ability of the total plant is at stake, since the performance of the SOFCs and
other equipment downstream the gasiﬁer will certainly be compromised. On
such an occasion, the PGU of the investigated plant would have to shut down
and, depending on the frequency of such events and the total downtime, the
increase in the operational costs of the plant would be rather signiﬁcant. In
any case, increased maintenance costs would be expected in order to keep the
system in a good operational condition. These facts were not taken into account
in the current study.
The design and economic assessment of processes and systems related to the
disposal of the spent non-regenerable sorbent used in syngas dechlorination, the
regeneration system for the regenerable sorbent used in syngas desulfurization
and the cleaning system of the candle ﬁlter were not taken into account in the
current study.
The characteristics of the energy demand of the hotels
The derivation of the hourly energy demand for each day and each month of the
whole year for Hotels 1&2 was performed based on a typical daily proﬁle in Greek
hotels, which were investigated in the study [41]. This led in a repeating pattern
consisting of one to two energy load proﬁles (the latter if weekdays were treated
separately from weekend-days), which spanned over a whole month and in some
cases, more (see section 4.2). A more realistic approach, which would aﬀect
the solution of the operational costs minimization problem and the obtained
operational strategy (see section 5.2.1 in chapter 5), would be to use the data
acquired from the energy audit, which was performed in the hotels in [41] and
made it possible to clearly deﬁne the electrical and heating load proﬁles, as well
as the base load and load variations.
In general, variations in occupancy, seasonal climate and usage of the hotel’s
premises for other activities could result in diﬀerent load proﬁles, but these
parameters are usually diﬃcult to predict and they could present a subject for
sensitivity analysis.
Regarding the procedure of deriving the load proﬁles, the base electrical load
(e.g. lighting) during summer months was distributed to the hourly intervals of
a total electrical load proﬁle, which demonstrates peaks on the hours of the day
when cooling load is high, and thus, there was a possibility of costing a greater
part of the electrical energy with the electrical grid cost factor, when the PGU
electrical capacity was surpassed.
==PAGE:156==
145
The results of the economic analysis
In the current study, the investigated hotel cases (Hotel 1&2) involved two
medium-sized Greek hotels of approximately 130 rooms (the majority of the ho-
tels in Greece have less than 50 rooms [40]), but with diﬀerent total conditioned
area and energy demand with respect to electricity, heating and cooling, yield-
ing a speciﬁc energy consumption of 298 kW h/m2 for Hotel 1 and 210 kW h/m2
for Hotel 2 . Also, both hotels were business hotels located in the city centre of
Thessaloniki (northern Greece region), which is translated to all-year operation
and equipment for both heating and cooling. It is considered that the factors of
energy demand level and hotel type were deﬁnitive in the results of the economic
analysis.
In the base scenario, where capital subsidies are not available, the economic
attractiveness of the CHCP/P-EX over the P-EX plant for the case of Hotel 1
was realized owing to the higher energy demand of that hotel compared to Hotel
2, leading to a greater extent of utilization of the WtE CHCP plant and thus, to
greater savings from substituting the costly operation of the P-EX plant, owing
to the consumption of electrical energy from the grid and heating oil, for the
more economical one of the proposed WtE CHCP plant.
On the other hand, increasing the power capacity would not be beneﬁcial for
Hotel 2, as it could be observed in the current case study, where, despite the
longer operation of the PGU of the CHCP plant during time periods where
excess electricity could be sold to the grid, the revenue was not high enough to
change the NPV considerably. Thus, subsidizing the high CAPEX of such an
investment is crucial.
However, in a hypothetical scenario where subsidy of 30 % of the CAPEX are
included, the investment on the investigated CHCP/P-EX plant for Hotel 2
becomes more attractive compared to the P-EX plant, due to the increase in
the NPV.
Environmental beneﬁts
Apart from the potential attractiveness of such a WtE CHCP plant from the
investigated private economic perspective, a socio-economic evaluation of the
project would include the environmental beneﬁts from the avoided greenhouse
gas emissions and preservation of landﬁll space, which are some of the main
advantages of WtE systems [3]. It is also quite possible that in the future such
environmental beneﬁts will be linked to the private economic evaluation of this
project, by means of ﬁnancial incentives for systems that provide environmen-
tally sound waste management solutions and GHG emissions reduction, such as
the ones oﬀered by the investigated WtE CHCP plant.

Discussion and conclusions
Conclusions
The investigation regarding the economic attractiveness of an 100 kWe Waste-
to-Energy CHCP plant, aided by the pre-existing energy plant of the Hotels
1&2, concluded that, without subsidies, only the investment on Hotel 1 was fa-
vorable, due to its higher energy demand with respect to electricity, heating and
cooling. Speciﬁcally, greater savings were obtained, which were associated with
substituting the costly operation of the P-EX plant, owing to the consumption
of electrical energy from the grid and heating oil, for the more economical one of
the proposed WtE CHCP plant. Taking subsidies into account, the investment
on such as plant was favorable for both Hotels 1&2, signifying their importance
in an investment project with high CAPEX such as this. The type of the two
hotels, categorized as business-city rather than tourist seasonal, was considered
a deﬁnitive factor for the economic attractiveness of such an investment, since
they operate all-year round and need to be equipped with systems suﬃcient
enough to cover the whole range of the hotel’s seasonal energy needs.

The purpose of this calculation is to determine the modiﬁcation factor that
should be applied on the waste feed mass ﬂow, so that the energy content

biomass feed per unit of time. The same amount of moisture content is assumed
in both cases, i.e. 5 % (w/w), which is equal to what is assumed in the case
study.

can be found from the linear relation LHVwood−biomass [kJ/kg] = −213, 13 ·

feed per second (same as what is delivered by the wood-based biomass feed),
