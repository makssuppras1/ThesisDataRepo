Abstract
Machine learning and computer vision are increasingly used in large-scale industrial produc-
tion to enhance automation and efficiency. Meliora Bio aims to optimise its second-generation
bioethanol production line by integrating advanced technologies to increase production yield
and reliability. A critical component of this process is a chute filled with straw at the very
start of the pipeline, which is responsible for maintaining a vacuum seal for production sta-
bility. Currently, the fullness of the chute is monitored by ten side-mounted sensors, which
regulate the rate at which straw is fed. However, these sensors are prone to measurement er-
rors and disruptions, leading to inefficiencies. This thesis explores an alternative approach
for straw-level measurement with machine learning and computer vision, and the goal is to
create a model framework that obtains higher level accuracy than the current sensors. A data
pipeline was developed to collect and curate datasets to train and test supervised models. A
reference framework was also made with AprilTags, which allowed for live-overlay plotting
of predictions and going from bounding box pixel values to straw levels and vice versa. Two
distinct machine learning methodologies were investigated: an object detection approach us-
ing YOLOv11 to detect straw bounding boxes (YOLO-S) and three image regression methods,
ConvNeXtV1, ConvNeXtV2 and ViT, applied on cutouts of the chutes that were made with
a secondary YOLOv11 model (YOLO-C). Results showed that the YOLO-S and ConvNeXtV1
models achieved the highest accuracies on our test set - 89% and 98.5%, respectively - when
measured within a ±10% threshold of the annotated values, significantly improving upon the
SCADA sensor’s accuracy of 23.33%. However, the YOLO-S model was the fastest, averaging
620ms per frame on our lowest-performing CPU (Intel i5 5300U), and demonstrated the high-
est and most consistent accuracy across our custom robustness tests, achieving 94.35%, com-
pared to ConvNeXtV1’s 92.87%. Based on our findings, Meliora Bio would benefit by using
our framework with the YOLO-S model.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Today, driven by the green transition, many companies are focused on innovative biotechnol-
ogy. It is a huge industry with various applications, ranging from medical to agricultural,
with many others in between. In healthcare, biotechnology is currently being used to prevent
and treat various diseases, such as diabetes [1] and as nutritional supplements. In agriculture,
biotechnology is used to genetically enhance the resistance and yield of crops and plants [2]. In
the wider industry, many companies are also interested in replacing fossil fuels with biofuels
for a more sustainable world.
In pursuit of sustainable bioethanol, prebiotics, and lignin production, Meliora Bio operates a
state-of-the-art biorefinery that upcycles agricultural straw into high-value bioproducts. The
operation started in 2020, and they are one of two biorefineries in the world operating at this
scale. Their focus is second-generation biological products, bioproducts created from non-food
biomass, such as straw, typically limited to heating fuels. Most of the straw is used in the pro-
cess, and only a small percentage of the straw is left as a waste product. As Meliora Bio is part
of the Kalundborg Symbiosis, the remaining waste product is turned into a product stream for
the biogas plant, where it is turned into biogas, thus reducing the environmental impact of the
process [3].
Due to the recent start of operations, Meliora Bio is continuously exploring methods to op-
timise and increase the effectiveness of its production line, both via internal processes and
through collaborations with various universities and educational institutes. They work fast
and are not afraid to experiment. It allows them to explore and incorporate state-of-the-art
technologies into their production line. In modern production lines, computer vision has be-
come an integral part of the process, increasing quality control, automating visual processes,
and enabling systems to integrate visual information into their autonomous tasks. When com-
bined with machine learning, the system’s capabilities are further increased, allowing it to
learn various tasks from data collected during production. By leveraging these methods, Me-
liora Bio hopes to increase the robustness of its production line, avoid disruptive shutdowns

and reduce the mental load of its operators. This thesis is the first step toward Meliora Bio

1.1
Problem Definition
In many complex industrial processes, maintaining smooth, efficient operations in the process-
ing pipeline presents significant challenges. A critical component in Meliora Bio’s production
line is the straw chute, where straw is initially gathered and regulated before being fed into
subsequent stages of production. This production is prone to disruptions and inefficiencies
when straw levels in the chute fluctuate unpredictably. Currently, the plant relies on a sensor-
based system for straw level monitoring. However, these sensors are frequently disrupted by
dust accumulation and physical disturbances, resulting in delays and occasional shutdowns
due to inaccurate readings. The sensors send an analogue signal to the production line, which
controls the speed at which straw is fed into the chute.
This thesis explores the integration of real-time computer vision and machine learning to en-
hance straw level monitoring at Meliora Bio, offering a more accurate and reliable alternative
to the existing sensor-based system. By leveraging live-feed video analysis, this project aims
to deliver a robust, real-time monitoring solution that integrates seamlessly into Meliora Bio’s
existing framework, advancing the plant’s operational efficiency.
1.2
Goals
The milestone goals of the thesis are:
• Survey the literature and background of state-of-the-art computer vision and machine
learning methods, both in general domain literature and a production setting.
• Create a framework for data collection, annotation and model selection & tuning.
• Collect and process evaluation metrics for the current SCADA sensor setup.
• Develop two modelling approaches for predicting the straw level:
– An object detection model to predict bounding boxes around the straw in the chute.
– A straw-level model that directly predicts the straw level in the chute.
• Create a live overlay for inference, validation and performance evaluation, capable of
converting between bounding box pixel values and straw level percentages.
• Evaluate both approaches’ robustness and computational efficiency based on the criteria
set forth by Meliora Bio and recommend which approach to use.

The criteria Meliora Bio set forth are:
• A computer vision model that can predict the straw level in the chute with higher accu-
racy than the current sensor setup.
• Minimum speed of one prediction per second.
• The model needs to be robust to changes in the chute environment, camera position and
other artefacts that might appear.
• It should be possible to see what the model predicts in real-time.
• The model needs to be able to run autonomously, i.e. without the need for human interfacing,
for the entire duration of multiple productions.
• The predictions of the model need to be able to interface with the rest of the production at
Meliora Bio, specifically by outputting an analogue signal in the 4-20 mA range.
1.3
Limitations and Delimitations
Several limitations and delimitations are affecting the work on the thesis.
Data There is no dataset to work from, and all data must be collected and annotated by hand.
This is typically a timely and expensive effort, as it often takes trial and error to reach a
final dataset and will thus limit the amount of data that can be used in our modelling.
Generally, the more varied data one can collect, the better the performance and generali-
sation of the machine learning model. Additionally, Meliora Bio has no measurement of
its sensor accuracy.
No Existing Framework There is no existing machine learning framework at Meliora Bio, which
means we have to develop it ourselves. While it poses challenges regarding integration
into an existing system, it allows us to model and experiment with our setup freely.
Time Scope The time scope of the project limits every aspect, including research, data collec-
tion, model exploration, implementation and evaluation. Some aspects that might have
been worth exploring more, such as deeper model exploration, have been left out due to
these limitations.
Limited Experience in Electronics As part of the project constitutes converting a digital sig-
nal to an analogue signal, our limited experience within the field of electronics might
prove a hindrance in this task.
Finally, even though there are several potential use cases for computer vision and machine
learning in the production process at Meliora Bio, this thesis will only explore the problem of
predicting the straw level in the chute. Although much of this framework could potentially
apply to those other problems as well, that will be left for future work.

1.4
Outline
This thesis contains six chapters, including the introduction and an appendix. The outline of
the thesis is as follows:
2. Background & Literature Here, we explore the background and literature of computer vi-
sion in production settings and typical use cases and give a general overview of con-
volutional neural networks, object detection and the You-Only-Look-Once model, and
state-of-the-art classification & regression models.

Image recognition has been a long-standing task in computer vision, with applications ranging
from medical diagnostics, such as cancer cell detection, to autonomous vehicles, such as cars
and drones. Since the late 20th century, the use of deep learning models, especially convolu-
tional neural networks (CNNs) [4, 5, 6, 7, 8] and more recently transformer models [9], have
significantly advanced the field with new opportunities that surpass prior methods in recogni-
tion tasks. This chapter reviews the foundational literature that underlies the methods applied
in this thesis and covers the following topics:

state-of-the-art image models: the Vision Transformer and ConvNeXt.

a similar problem, but the model predicts a continuous variable instead of a categorical vari-
able, i.e. the problem is considered a regression problem. Finally, object detection is the task of
finding the location of specific objects in an image, and this often includes the need for object

State-of-the-art (SOTA) deep learning models generally consist of three main modules: the

2.2
Level Monitoring Using Computer Vision
Level monitoring is crucial to many systems used worldwide, particularly in the process con-
trol industry. Most commonly, water level monitoring and control systems have been inte-
grated into wider systems, such as hydro power plants [15, 16], river and stream water level
monitoring [17, 18, 19] and chemical process flow [20, 21, 22].
While none of the work mentioned above directly deals with straw level monitoring, the tech-
niques and methods they apply to monitor and measure water and other liquid levels in differ-
ent circumstances warrant an investigation. Common for most of the papers is that a solution
for remote level measuring was needed, and due to the pre-established camera surveillance at
the locations, gathering images was relatively easy, similar to this thesis. However, the chal-
lenges connected to data collection often arise during annotation, as it is a tedious and time-
consuming process.
In his thesis, Automatic Recognition of Water-Levels with Machine Learning [15], Moreg˚ard cre-
ated and implemented a system to automatically recognise water levels using high-quality im-
ages captured from a surveillance camera. To do so, Moreg˚ard implemented a simple CNN to
classify the water level within a hydropower plant into 40 classes, each representing a specific
water level. He collected data from 35 days of measurements, with a dataset of ∼10000 im-
ages, manually pre-processed and labelled. Each image was taken from a parallel angle to the
water gauge, which indicated the level. He describes the iterative process of finding the best
model configuration for the problem by adjusting parameters and conducting tests, constantly
measuring the changes in validation accuracy. Using this method, he achieved an accuracy of
> 91% in water level monitoring.
In their paper, Deep Learning and Object Detection for Water Level Measurement using Patterned
Visual Markers [18], Filho et al. investigate the possibility of replacing pressure and ultrasonic
sensors that measure water levels in rivers with a camera-based deep learning setup. While
accurate, the sensors are susceptible to damage and costly to replace. Instead, they use black
stainless steel bars to create high contrast areas in the images, which can determine the water
level in each image, getting around issues of low visibility and other interference in the im-
ages. They tested classification, regression, and object detection to determine the number of

bar plate in a swimming pool. For classification, they predicted discrete classes 3 to 7, and for

the water level. They used a ResNet50V2 backbone for this purpose. Another approach was

control the speed at which the straw is fed into the chute.
2.3
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional neural networks have evolved significantly since the foundational work of Le-
Cun et al. [4] with LeNet-5 and Krizhevsky et al. [5] with AlexNet, which relied on single, sim-
ple convolutional blocks in each layer of a relatively simple architecture. The main idea behind
Background & Literature
Page 8 of 111
==PAGE:21==
DTU & Meliora Bio
this type of model was to move away from globally connected networks, typically seen in fully
connected neural networks, to a network consisting of local receptive fields. By convolving
a weight filter over an input image or feature image, the resulting output of the convolution
will only have information from its local features. Furthermore, since all features share the
same weights, which are stored in the filters, the total number of stored parameters is reduced
significantly compared to a fully connected network. Multiple filters are stacked, creating con-
volutional layers, with the input of each layer being the previous layer’s output, as illustrated
in Figure 2.2. Typically, various pooling operations are used to down-sample the feature maps
from previous layers, extracting only the most important features [23]. Early models used a
standard fully connected neural network at the head, taking the flattened feature map from
the last convolutional layer as input. This head is shaped after the task at hand.
These early models achieved impressive results, but as networks grew deeper, they encoun-
tered the problem of vanishing gradients. In deep networks, the gradients of the loss function
are back-propagated from the output layer to the earlier layers via their gradients, but with
each layer, the gradient is multiplied by a value less than one, causing it to shrink exponen-
tially. This complicates the training of deep models, as earlier layers fail to receive signifi-
cant updates, limiting the model’s capacity to learn complex features. In 2015, He et al.[6]
introduced the notion of shortcut connections together with the publication of the Residual
Network (ResNet) architecture. It marked a major turning point in how model architectures
were created. ResNet’s design allows gradients to bypass certain layers by taking skip connec-
tions, enabling the training of much deeper networks and alleviating the vanishing gradient
problem. This innovation of residual learning through shortcut connections inspired the devel-
opment of many subsequent models, as it allowed for a new way to efficiently handle gradient
flows through deeper networks, e.g. ConvNeXt[7], YOLO[8] and even transformer models like
BERT[24] all utilize shortcut-connections. While using shortcut connections to mitigate van-
ishing gradients is one benefit, they are also used for better feature reuse, as in DenseNet[25],
or help preserve spatial information from the encoder to the decoder in U-Nets[26]. These
developments have enabled models to perform highly nuanced feature extraction, achieving
remarkable accuracy in various computer vision tasks.

2.4.1
The You-Only-Look-Once Model
The You-Only-Look-Once (YOLO) model, introduced by Redmon et al. in their seminal 2015
paper, ”You Only Look Once: Unified, Real-Time Object Detection”[27], represented a ground-
breaking shift in object detection methodology. Unlike traditional two-stage detection models,
YOLO introduced a unified, single-stage network that combined bounding box regression with
object classification into a single, efficient process. YOLO is a SOTA deep learning model for
object detection, optimised for real-time applications [28]. It is renowned for its high speed
and object localisation accuracy, even within complex environments and low-resolution data
[29].
Traditional two-stage object detection consists of two sequential phases,
1. Region Proposal Network (RPN): Generates a set of region-of-interest (RoI) boxes
in the image that may potentially contain objects, referred to as candidate object
proposals.
2. Classifying the RoIs: The classification part classifies the candidate object propos-
als into specific object categories and refines their bounding box locations.
Notably, two-stage networks have longer inference times as the RPN introduces extra
processing time, and phase two classifies each of the proposed RoIs.
Single-stage object detection models refer to models that directly predict object classes and
their corresponding bounding boxes from input images in a single evaluation.
The actual performance of the two object detection methods varies, and depending on the
problem at hand and the importance of accuracy vs. inference time, either one can be a poten-
tial candidate model [30, 31].
Since the YOLO model’s first introduction, the entire framework around it has been continu-
ously refined and optimised, aiming to maintain SOTA performance. As of 2025, development

2.4.1.A
Oriented Bounding Boxes
In some object detection tasks, such as when working with aerial imagery or autonomous nav-
igation, simply identifying an object within an image might not be sufficient. In these cases,
the object’s orientation within the image is equally important to the location. Thus, many re-
searchers have worked on incorporating Oriented Bounding Boxes (OBBs) into deep learning.
The foundational work started in 1983 when Godfried Toussaint proposed an algorithm to find
the minimum-area enclosing rectangle for a convex polygon [37]. It formed the theoretical
basis for detecting oriented objects. In 2018, Ding et al. [38] introduced the Rotated Region-
of-Interest (RRoI) Learner and Warper. This approach introduced a network that regresses
the offsets of rotated ground truth RRoIs relative to horizontal ones, which are subsequently
warped to extract rotation-invariant features. In addition to improving algorithms for handling
rotated boxes, specialised loss functions have also been developed to optimise OBB regression.
For example, Pixels-IoU (PIoU) [39], from 2020, which directly optimises the Intersection-over-
Union (IoU) between predicted and ground truth OBBs, and Probabilistic-IoU (ProbIoU) [40]
from 2021, which models boxes as Gaussian distributions and uses a probabilistic approach
to enhance bounding box representations. It effectively accounts for the inherent uncertainty
with object location and orientation, offering a more robust solution to bounding box regres-
sion.
Building upon these developments, modern object detection frameworks have integrated OBB
representations to enhance detection accuracy and robustness. Notably, the YOLO model has
been extended to support OBBs, enabling it to handle rotated objects more effectively. This in-
tegration involves modifications to the model’s architecture and loss functions to accommodate
the unique challenges posed by OBBs. Currently, YOLOv11[8] uses ProbIoU as its bbox regres-
sion loss (see section 2.4.1.B), and represents its bounding boxes with corner coordinates. The
Ultralytics YOLO framework takes a point-based OBB representation as input but internally
uses an angle-based OBB representation.

2.4.1.C
Non-Maximum Suppression with ProbIoU
In the Ultralytics YOLO framework, NMS is implemented for OBB with ProbIoU. The function
is called nms rotated , and takes in a batch of bounding boxes, computes the ProbIoU for
all pairs of rotated boxes resulting in a N × N matrix. The N × N matrix is reduced to an
upper triangular matrix for computational purposes. Then, like normal NMS, the function
finds indices where the max IoU is below the threshold T , ensuring these boxes are kept. Then,
the function returns the coordinates of those bounding boxes.
2.4.2
Fiducial Markers: AprilTags
Fiducial markers can be 2D symbols or tags often printed on a sheet of paper and stuck on a
surface of interest to add the possibility of accurate localisation, tracking and more. See Fig-
ure 2.5 for examples of what AprilTags look like. They allow for easily distinguishing between
objects or locations in the environment. Fiducial markers have a border and a payload area
that contains information about the tag. AprilTags are, as presented in Edwin Olson’s and
the team behind AprilTags’ work [42] from 2011, a visual fiducial system based on 2D planar
targets. The tag detector is robust to lighting variation and occlusions and produces accurate
localisations of the tags. AprilTags work in multiple stages.
Line Segmentation The image is processed to detect strong gradients that indicate possible
tag edges. Pixels with similar gradient directions and magnitudes are clustered, isolating
potential line segments.
Quadrilaterals Extraction The algorithm searches for groups of line segments that form quadri-
laterals. A recursive depth-first search identifies candidate quadrilaterals, constrained by
proximity (within twice the previous line’s length plus 5 pixels) and counter-clockwise
ordering.
Homography Transform For each detected quadrilateral, a Direct Linear Transform is applied
to compute a 3×3 homography matrix, used to map 2D points in homogenous coordinates
from the tag’s coordinate system to the real-world coordinates.

AprilTags and fiducial markers are widely used in robotics and augmentation, e.g. in simulta-
neous localisation and mapping (SLAM) of robots as discussed by Lee et al. in their compar-
ative study paper of different SLAM-based methods with AprilTags from 2023 [43]. Fiducial
markers are also often used in the coordination of aerial drones. In 2020 Mateos [44] extended
the use of AprilTags from their simple 2D planar representations to 3D, aiming to improve pose
estimation accuracy of AprilTags in field robotics, especially for use in complex swarm robotic
applications, and in 2023 Hulek et al. [45] applied AprilTag detection to enable vision-based
positioning on low-cost drones.

with the transformer architecture.

The Vision Transformer (ViT) is split into several parts, as illustrated in Figure 2.6. The flow
of a Vision Transformer model typically consists of a series of steps. First, the input image
is divided into patches of equal size. Then, the patches are flattened and go through a linear
projection. The projections then get positional embeddings before being sent through a Trans-
former Encoder. The output from the Transformer Encoder is sent through a fully connected
neural network, the head, which then produces the final prediction from the model [9].
Patches Images are split into equal size patches and flattened. A trade-off has to be made
between speed/accuracy when selecting patch size. Smaller patch size results in more
patches and typically higher accuracy, whereas larger patch size is more efficient at the
cost of some accuracy.
Linear Projection In NLP, words and sentences are commonly projected onto a vector embed-
ding to process them effectively in the models. In ViTs, Dosovitskiy et al. follow a similar
pattern as in [47], projecting the 2D patches into flat linear projections of fixed size ready
to go into the Transformer. These are called patch embeddings, and the linear projection
is trainable alongside the model [9].
Positional Embeddings gives the transformer information about the position of each patch
relative to the entire image. Positional embeddings are most commonly treated as learn-
able parameters trained alongside the model.

The Transformer Encoder is the main backbone of ViTs and consists of multiple layers. An
illustrated overview can be seen in Figure 2.7 (left). Within each layer of the transformer, there
are typically four blocks through which the input goes, with the two main ones being a multi-
head attention block and a fully connected neural network. In between each of those, there is
a layer normalisation block [48].
LayerNormalisation is a method used to stabilize and improve the training of neural net-
works. It was introduced in 2016 by Ba et al. [48] and found great success in Recurrent
Neural Networks and, more recently, in Transformers. Layer normalization works by
summing the inputs to the neurons in a layer on a single training case and calculating
layer mean and layer variance. These are then used to normalize the input features of the
layer, mitigating the problems of vanishing and exploding gradients.
Self-Attention is the primary driving force behind Transformer models. It works by calculat-
ing Keys, Queries, and Values from the input. These are calculated using weight matrices,
which are learned during training. The Queries and Keys are multiplied to get a score
for each input for each key. By applying a Softmax to the scores, they represent an im-
portance score for each input related to one another. This importance score is multiplied
by the calculated Values matrix, resulting in the output of the attention block. See Fig-
ure 2.7 (right) for a visual overview of the attention block. Multi-Head Attention sends
the input through multiple attention blocks in parallel.

2.5.2
ConvNeXtV1
After the introduction of ViTs, most deep learning researchers pivoted away from convolu-
tional networks to transformer-based architectures for image tasks. However, in 2022, Liu et
al. beat the performance of ViTs with the introduction of the first ConvNeXt [46] model, a
purely convolutional network architecture. In their work, they reevaluated the design of a
pure CNN by gradually modernising a ResNext model [50], applying some of the learnings
from ViTs to convolutional networks. Refer to the original paper for more information regard-
ing the specific improvements made.
The ConvNeXtV1 model consists of four main stages and a stem. The stem is the input layer
of the network and is where the first ViT design is used, as the input is ”patchified” by applying
a strided convolution. Then, each of the four stages is a series of ResNeXt blocks, wherein the
input goes through grouped convolutions. Additionally, the second design taken from ViTs is
an inverted bottleneck layer in place of the large fully connected layer in the ViT. Finally, Con-
vNextV1 utilises large kernel sizes (7 × 7) to increase the receptive field of the model, similar
to the global receptive field in ViTs. Other small changes to the model architecture include
swapping to GELU activation functions [51], using fewer activation functions and normalisation
layers, and using LayerNormalisation, similarly to ViTs.
All the changes Liu et al. made resulted in a new model that they dubbed ConvNeXtV1. It beat
the performance of transformer models at the time using purely convolutional methods. An
illustrated overview of the model can be found in Figure 2.8. Note that the figure illustrates
the architecture of the ConvNeXtV2 model; however, the only architectural difference between
the models is the introduction of a new normalisation, as explained in section 2.5.3.
2.5.3
ConvNeXtV2
The primary contributions of the ConvNeXtV2 model [7], by Woo et al., are two-fold. The
first is a new pre-training method utilising a fully convolutional masked autoencoder (FCMAE)
in a self-supervised manner, allowing the model to learn valuable representations of images
and increasing its generalisation performance on other tasks. The second improvement is the
introduction of Global Response Normalization, which helps combat feature collapse during the
model’s training. Feature collapse can be seen when the cosine distance between features be-
comes smaller and smaller as the input goes through the layers of the model.
Fully Convolutional Masked Autoencoder (FCMAE) It has proven beneficial to pre-train su-
pervised models via a self-supervised method, dubbed Masked Autoencoders [52], wherein
large parts of the images are masked, and the models then predict the masked parts based
on the remaining context. In ConvNeXtV2, the authors introduced a fully convolutional
masked autoencoder, working with sparse convolutions in the encoder, which prevents the

model from learning shortcuts that allow it to copy and paste information from masked
regions [7].
Global Response Normalisation During the training of ConvNeXtV2, Woo et al. discovered
that the features in the later layers of the models would collapse, becoming redundant by
not providing the model with diverse information. To combat this, they introduce global
response normalisation, which consists of three steps: global feature aggregation, feature
normalisation, and feature calibration [7]. This response layer is added to the residual
blocks after the GELU activations.

3.2.5
Pre-processing
Pre-processing
Raw data collected from sensors, cameras, and other sources, including
labelling procedures, often can not be applied directly to a machine learn-
ing framework. If done incorrectly, it can negatively impact model train-
ing and reduce the accuracy of desired outcomes. Pre-processing ensures
that raw data is transformed into a structured and standardised format, facilitating efficient
feature extraction and improving model performance.
This subsection describes the pre-
processing employed for our datasets and models. We focus on methods to normalise input
features and handle artefacts to optimise data quality and consistency.
YOLO
The YOLO model, implemented through the Ultralytics [8], handles many pre-processing steps
natively within its codebase. These include converting to RGB, scaling pixel values to the range
[0, 1], and normalising using predefined ImageNet mean and standard deviation values. For
YOLO models, data class imbalance is not deemed an issue as the dataset contains only one
class label. The framework requires input data to adhere to a specific format: image files
paired with corresponding text files containing labels and bounding box coordinates. This for-
mat is detailed further in section 3.2.5.C.
Straw Level Models
Common for all straw level models is a custom-made data loader with several features built in
that are useful for this project. These include the ability to convert the dataset to a classification
or a regression setting, setting the number of classes, extracting manual features, applying
augmentations, normalising the images (see section 3.2.5.A) and balancing the dataset (see
section 3.2.5.B). The data loader returns a batch of normalised RGB images and their belonging
annotations.

3.2.5.B
Balancing the dataset
There is a significant class imbalance as seen in Figure 3.4. To combat this, a functionality
was built into the data-loader of the straw-level models to balance the dataset, which can be
enabled or disabled, such that the classes with significantly more samples are downsampled to
a maximum of 400. This limit was selected based on the distribution of the training data. This
leaves 5544 frames in the training data after downsampling, and the downsampled distribu-
tion can be seen in Figure 3.4. We chose to downsample instead of oversample for two reasons:
downsampling allows for faster model training and experimentation, and upsampling gener-
ally involves creating some synthetic images. This can introduce bias in the dataset based on
the generation method utilised. One popular method for upsampling, SMOTE [57], has re-
cently been shown not to be as effective for SOTA classifiers as initially thought [58], which
backed our reasoning for choosing downsampling instead.

3.2.5.D
Creating a Robustness Dataset
Apart from collecting data to account for data drifting and chute rotation, as described in
section 3.2.2, we combined the two robustness datasets, resulting in 1150 frames in vertical
and rotated chute positions. Afterwards, we applied six different transformations in various
combinations. This dataset aims to test the models in semi-realistic settings that might make
inference difficult. The selected transformations are as follows:
Lens Flares were added at random positions on the image. The lens flares are a selection from
[59], wherein Dai et al. collected 7000 real and synthetic lens flares to train machine-
learning models to remove lens flares from images.
Dust was added as an overlay of the images, simulating a dirty camera lens. Five different
dust overlays were collected from onlygfx and applied at random, one at a time.
Scratches were generated using OpenCV. They are meant to simulate damage to the camera
lens but also provide small blocked areas of the image.
People were overlaid at random positions near the chute, resulting in a partially blocked view
of both windows and fiducial markers.
Partial Blurred Areas were added at a varying amount and sizes to simulate low visibility.
The partially blurred areas are circles with varying radii, blurred via Gaussian blur.
Random Augmentations where a random amount of brightness was added to the images and
a random chance of adding a random amount of Gaussian noise.
Specific information about how the transformations are made, parameters used and examples
of resulting images can be seen in section 9.3.1 in the appendix.

Modelling
This part of the thesis describes the modelling of the straw level predic-
tors. The general method for training deep neural networks described
in the blog ”A Recipe for Training Neural Networks” by Karpathy [60] was
followed. The first step is understanding the data, as described in sec-
tion 3.2. Then, an end-to-end training and evaluation framework should be built, such that
empirical experiments can be done and measured properly. Then, using the framework, model
selection can be done by adding and removing features, modifying architecture, swapping out
optimisers, and other bigger changes to the models and training. Finally, the hyperparameters
can be tuned to squeeze out as much performance from the model as possible.

We take two separate approaches to predicting the straw level. In the first approach, we train
a YOLO model to predict bounding boxes around the straw in the chute. Then, we convert
the predicted bounding boxes to a straw level by mapping the coordinates of the top line in
the bounding box to a straw level with the fiducial markers (explained in section 3.5.1.C). The
second approach uses a classifier or regressor to predict the straw level from the images di-
rectly. To make this method robust to the rotation and translation of the chute, we also train a
YOLO model to predict bounding boxes around the chute itself. The images are then cut out
and rotated to the bounding box before being sent through the straw-level model.
In section 3.3.1, an explanation for the general training framework with training processes
and optimisers will be given. The YOLO models are elaborated on in section 3.3.2 and the
straw-level models in section 3.3.3.
3.3.1
Training Framework
The training framework is a classic deep-learning training loop. We iterate over all images in
the dataset in mini-batches to minimise noise and stabilise training [61]. Each mini-batch is
sent through the model, and it makes its predictions. These predictions are then scored via
a loss function (see section 3.4.1). From the loss, we perform backpropagation in the form of
gradient descent. We test several optimisation strategies, including Stochastic Gradient De-
scent (SGD), Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam) [62], and the variant of ADAM with weight
decay (AdamW) [63]. See section 3.3.1.C for more. This entire process is repeated multiple
times. Each repetition is referred to as an epoch.
3.3.1.A
Early Stopping
We implement early stopping to prevent our models from overfitting. Early stopping involves
measuring whether performance has increased over the last several epochs. In practice, we
save the model that achieved the best performance on our test dataset during training, along
with the most recent model.
3.3.1.B
Transfer Learning
In recent years, utilising Transfer Learning has become increasingly popular when training
deep neural networks [64]. The process involves training a model on a huge general dataset,
such as ImageNet, and then applying the learned weights to a different problem set. This way,
the model gets to build good representative general features that are useful in a multitude of
problems and apply that knowledge to a different, more specific problem set. Fine-tuning the
model on the new dataset quickly achieves greater performance than training a model from
scratch would have, especially if the amount of data in the new dataset is limited. We utilise
transfer learning by taking models that have been pre-trained on the ImageNet dataset and

the image with bilinear interpolation [67] fitting the chosen model’s input size. The straw-level
models are trained on a cutout of the chute based on the manual annotations.
During inference, we use the YOLO model trained to predict OBBs around the chute on the
same annotations and use those bounding boxes to create the cutouts. However, due to the
variability of the images and predicted bounding boxes from the model, the input images to
the straw-level models might have differences compared to the images they have been trained
on, affecting the performance of those models. Additionally, having to run two models to be
able to do inference introduces a large overhead in computation time and complexity.
Late in the project, we explored an alternate method leveraging AprilTag detection to simplify
the process and enhance robustness to address these challenges. See section 3.5.1 for details.
Combining the tags for the upper and lower chute windows makes it possible to generate a
precise cutout of the entire chute. This is achievable because the orientations of the upper and
lower chute windows are consistent and aligned in the same direction. This approach elimi-
nates the need to deploy multiple models, enabling a more efficient detection of both the chute
and straw level within a unified framework as the two processes can run in parallel on different
threads. Furthermore, this method ensures consistent chute crop-outs, as the tags are on the
chute itself. It reduces variability in the input images provided to the straw-level model across
consecutive frames and improves system reliability. Additionally, this method can account for
some of the image distortion by ensuring that the top and bottom windows are roughly equal
in size.
When creating the AprilTag cutouts, we warp the image back into a square dimension, by con-
ducting a Homography transform based on the four AprilTags forming a quadrilateral around
the chute. By defining those four points as the source points and then four points based on the
desired size of the image as the destination points, we use OpenCVs getPerspectiveTransform
and warpPerspective functions to warp the images such that each chute window is roughly
equal size. Under the hood, the functions calculate the 3x3 transformation matrix, M, by solv-
ing a series of linear equations to get the transformation coefficients, first to map from the
source vectors to the basis and then from the basis to the destination vectors. By multiplying
each point in the original image with the transformation matrix, we can get our warped image,
which in our case is a cutout of the chute where each window is roughly equal size [68]. For
visual examples of the annotated cutouts and the AprilTag cutouts used for the straw-level
models, see Figure 3.6. Unfortunately, while this method can equalize the sizes of the chute
windows and works well for images where the chute is vertical, it does not account for the ro-
tation distortion at the edges of the image, which is very prevalent in the rotated image dataset.

3.4.2
Cross Validation
To get an estimate of generalisation performance and avoid relying on lucky runs, we employ
k-fold cross validation [69]. This method splits the training dataset into k equal-sized folds and
then uses k −1 folds for training and the last fold for validation. This repeats k times until each
fold has been used as the validation set once. By averaging over the validation performances,
we get an estimate of the model’s performance. Similarly, we also test each of the models on
the test dataset to get an average of model performance on completely unseen data. We employ
4-fold cross-validation for all models and train each model for 20 epochs, for the straw level

1. Remove noise from the image by applying a Gaussian filter.
2. Find the intensity gradients of the image by running a Sobel kernel over the image
horizontally and vertically, and then calculate the gradient direction from those.
3. Remove spurious edge responses by applying a Non-maximum Suppression where
non-local maxima in the direction of the gradients are discarded.
4. Apply a double threshold by marking potential edges as a strong candidate if their

and th = 200.
5. Final edges are determined by conducting a blob analysis around weak edge candi-
dates. If a strong candidate is in the 8-connected neighbourhood pixels of the weak
candidate, the weak candidate is marked as strong and can cause neighbouring weak
candidates to be preserved.

to an objective function, f , we talk about the black-box model that needs to be optimised, and

approximate f . The surrogate model provides a predictive mean and an uncertainty estimate,
enabling informed decision-making regarding the next evaluation point. In this thesis, we use
a Gaussian Process defined by a mean function m(x) and a covariance function k(x,x′) (Matern
kernel), which captures the correlations between function values at different input points,

and as new observations are incorporated, the GP is updated to approximate the objective
function better. Lastly, an acquisition function is used to quantify the utility of sampling at
a given location in the surrogate model, e.g. the Expected Improvement function selects the
hyper-parameter configuration with the highest expected improvement over the current best
configuration. Then, the objective function is evaluated with the newly chosen configuration
and the process restarts.

In this final methodology section, we summarise the process of utilising
our models in the live-streaming domain, detailing how the system oper-
ates, from capturing images from the live feed to generating predictions
and accurately converting them into a straw percentage level while simul-
taneously displaying a live overlay on the feed.
Our system incorporates two distinct models: one designed to predict the bounding box of
the straw using a YOLO architecture and then converts the bounding box to a straw level, and
another that directly predicts the straw level. A unified framework is essential for converting
between pixel values and percentage levels to align these two methods. Specifically, the YOLO
model requires a mechanism to translate pixel-based straw-level predictions into percentage
values, while the straw-level model necessitates a method to convert percentage-based predic-
tions back into pixel values corresponding to the straw-level on the chute.
To achieve this, we adopt a unified solution by leveraging AprilTags. AprilTags provides a
reliable and precise reference framework that can accurately go between straw-level pixel and
percentage values.

3.5.1.A
Inferring missing tags
When detecting AprilTags, a few methods are applied to account for any tags that might not
have been found, even after the measures applied in section 3.5.1. For straw level tags, if we,
e.g. find tags 1 and 3, then due to the relatively small distance between neighbouring tags,
we assume that tag 2 is in the middle of these two tags. By linear interpolation, we infer the
position of the tag. The same method applies to non-corner tags in the upper and lower chute
windows. If the missing tag is a corner tag, we require two tags from the same side of the chute
as the missing tag and the corresponding corner from the other side, i.e. if tag 22 was missing,
we would need two tags from the left side and tag 26. Then, to infer the position of tag 22, we
find the intersection between a line created by the two points (x1,y1) and (x2,y2) from the same
side and a line created by a point (x3,y3) that is perpendicular to the first line. The intersection
between the lines is the coordinates for the inferred tag.
In the framework, we ensure that the positions of all tags that have been detected or inferred
are for the next frame. If the average error between the location of the newly detected tags

and the ones stored exceeds 10 pixels, the tags will be reset and re-detected. We call this
functionality carry-over.
3.5.1.B
Delimitations of our tag setup
In general, the tag connection heuristic when drawing the overlay is based on an adjacency
list of two disjoint graphs. The first graph is the straw-level lines, and the second is the chute
cutout. This way, we can control how tags are connected with lines when drawn. However,
it also causes some disadvantages. Suppose tags are neither found via our recursive detection
algorithm nor via inferring tags from neighbouring tags. In that case, some tags might not be
drawn on the overlay, as the adjacency list explicitly states which tags are to be connected.
3.5.1.C
Going from pixel-values to straw-level
Importance: Needed to report a percentage value for the straw level.
Object-detection models output bounding boxes that represent the predicted location of the
straw within the chute. To convert the bounding box into a straw percentage, the top-left and
top-right corners of the bounding box are used to define a line.
To determine the corresponding straw level, we calculate the intersection between two lines:
• The line formed by the two top corner points of the bounding box.
• The regression line derived from the centre positions of all detected tags that make up
the straw-level ruler.
This intersection provides a precise mapping of the bounding box location to a specific pixel
value along the ruler line. Once this intersection value is determined, we identify the closest
tags located directly below and above it on the ruler. A linear interpolation is performed using
these two tags to calculate the straw level as a percentage value.
3.5.1.D
Going from straw-level to pixel-values
Importance: To create an overlay of a predicted straw level by converting it into the appropri-
ate pixel values.
While regression models directly return the desired value — a decimal between 0 and 1, repre-
senting how full the chute is — this output can be challenging to visualise or interpret directly
in certain contexts. The first step is determining which two tags on the ruler correspond to the
percentage range. For example, if the percentage value is 35%, we locate the tags that represent
30% and 40%. Then, we can use the centre positions of those two tags and linearly interpolate
them to find the pixel value corresponding to the input straw level percentage.

Implementation details
When arguing for the validity of using the AprilTags as our reference framework for the straw

4.2.2
Straw Level Models
During the straw-level model selection, we focused on the accuracy and training curves of the
various experiments we conducted with cross-validation. The following will go through the
overall model selection results and end up with the final model. All reported results are the
mean of the four models, one for each fold, on the test dataset.
4.2.2.A
Model Selection
This section covers only the best parameters and models. We report full results in section 9.7
in the appendix. The configurations of the best models are in Table 4.7. All three models
achieved high accuracies, with both ConvNeXt models outperforming the ViT by more than
3%. One of the first things to note is that the regression models performed significantly better
than the classification models. Early results showed that classifiers performed 10-30% worse
than regressors. Secondly, using larger image sizes resulted in sizeable gains for most models:
for ConvNeXtV1, swapping from 384x384 to 672x208 image size was a 5% gain in accuracy.
For ConvNeXtV2, increasing image size initially resulted in a loss in accuracy of -9%. Still, it
ended up being a 13% increase over the 384x384 image size when combined with more hidden
layers in the head. Balancing the datasets also benefitted both the ViT and the ConvNeXtV2
models, resulting in a 1.01% gain for both. However, this was not the case for the ConvNeXtV1
model, where it resulted in a 5% decrease. Augmentation and edge features only proved ben-
eficial for the ViT model. Results on heatmaps were inconclusive, as the training time of the
models with heatmaps included was too long to train on all four folds within the time limit
on the HPC servers at DTU, and we chose not to include them in the models. For the head,
using sigmoid was a 2% increase for ConvNeXtV2 and ViT, but not for ConvNeXtV1, where
it was a 2% decrease. It is important to note, however, that even though introducing sigmoid
to the ConvNeXtV2 model led to increased performance, it also caused the model to be com-
pletely unable to learn sometimes, crashing the training. Most notable for ConvNeXtV2, in
terms of accuracy, is that increasing the number of hidden layers in the head from zero to three
increased the accuracy by 21%.
Conclusively, both ConvNeXt models showed strong performance with their respective config-
urations and were chosen to proceed with further hyperparameter tuning. We continued with
ConvNeXtV1, trained on the unbalanced dataset, and ConvNeXtV2 on the balanced dataset.

We test the accuracy of our models in a live overlay setting. For the YOLO-S model, this means

mance of the machine learning models on the test set shows that they can solve the problem of

precision of the models. Even though both models boast high accuracies, the instances where
they are wrong tend to be quite spiky, jumping more than 30% from one frame to the next. We
elaborate more on what measures can be applied to account for such spikes in section 4.5.1.
In conclusion, the trained object detection and regression models are all performing better
than the SCADA sensors. However, when looking at the test data, some deviations in straw

4.4
Robustness
We test our models for robustness in four cases: data drift, chute rotation, upper and lower
precision, and noisy interference, which are of interest to know whether or not the framework
is suitable for use in production at Meliora Bio based on the criteria set forth.
4.4.1
Data Drifting
We test the models’ capabilities on data drifting by testing on the vertical set of images in the
robustness dataset, as described in section 3.2.2. Figure 4.9 shows the performance of the two
models on the vertical dataset. Both models show strong performance on the dataset, with the
YOLO-S model gaining 4% accuracy, up to 93.29%, while ConvNeXtV1 drops 2%, down to
96.16%, still beating YOLO-S in terms of raw accuracy. The sensors achieved an accuracy of
33.57%, which is better than on the test set; however, not nearly enough to beat the accuracy
of the models. One thing to note is that both models show fewer spikes in their predictions on
this dataset than on the test dataset.
4.4.2
Chute Rotation
To test the models’ invariance to the rotation of the chute, we test performance on the rotated
set of images in the robustness dataset, as described in section 3.2.2. Figure 4.10 shows the
results thereof. Here, we see that both models achieve above 90% accuracy. YOLO-S hits
95.23%, an even further increase from the vertical dataset, while ConvNeXtV1 falls to 91%

Smoothing and clipping
During testing, we noticed that the models were prone to large spikes in their predictions from
one frame to the other. The straw level controls the speed at which the production line runs
at Meliora Bio, and thus, a large spike might speed up or slow down the production unwont-
edly. To combat these spikes, we investigate the results of smoothing out the predictions as
described in section 3.5.2. We test smoothing on the combined dataset of Vertical data drift
and Rotated. In addition to smoothing, we test an alternate approach, wherein we clip the
models’ predictions based on the last frame’s prediction. This way, predictions are unable to
spike from frame to frame. We test smoothing with {1, 2, 5, 10, 15} (s) weights and clipping to
a max change of {0, 0.5, 5, 10, 15} (%) between each frame.
We report the findings of testing the different smoothing methods in Table 4.11. As evident
from the table, when compared to Table 4.9, the only positive impact that our operations had
was on the YOLO-S model with clipping at 0.5% and 5%. With clipping at 0.5%, it achieved
the highest increase in accuracy with +0.7%, and 5% clipping yielded the greatest decrease
in MAE −0.12%. If we look at the raw YOLO-S predictions compared to the predictions from
the two clipping operations, we get Figure 4.13. It is evident from the plot that the clipping
operations have helped in reducing the magnitude of the spikes. However, using these values
for clipping does not remove all apparent spikes.
In conclusion, we achieved two insights into the smoothing and clipping. While smoothing
reduced the magnitude of the sporadic spikes, it also decreased the accuracy and increased the
MAE for most configurations. The only configuration that improved its performance was the
YOLO-S model with 0.5% clipping, increasing accuracy by +0.7%.

Chapter 5
Discussion
This chapter will analyse our methodology and results and discuss challenges and how they
were overcome. Additionally, we will put our work into a broader perspective by comparing it
to other works from the literature and commenting on why some specific decisions were made.
5.1
Why YOLO?
Before tuning any model configurations, we decided to use the YOLOv11 model as our exclu-
sive object detector. This decision was guided by an extensive literature review of SOTA object
detection models and was based on considerations of computational efficiency, real-time in-
ference, and practical deployment feasibility. While Faster-RCNN has long been a standard
object detection model in computer vision, and transformer-based models such as DEtection
TRansformer (DETR) and Vision Transformers (ViT) have emerged as competitive alterna-
tives, YOLOv11 remained the most practical choice for our specific application.
Why not Faster-RCNN?
As highlighted in section 2.4.1, Faster-RCNN, like all two-stage detectors, first generates re-
gion proposals through an RPN before classifying objects within each proposal. While this
approach can provide higher accuracy, it comes with higher inference time as multiple eval-
uations per image have to be made. This makes it less suitable for the real-time straw-level
detection problem. Although high accuracy is always desirable, our exact straw-level monitor-
ing problem does not require fine-grained detection and classification. Instead, we believe that
it benefits from fast and continuous estimation, and the added complexity from Faster-RCNN
was not justified. Additionally, Karbouj et al., in 2024 [30], found that even the older YOLOv5
version consistently outperformed Faster R-CNN in a screw head detection and classification
problem with better AP50-95 while offering twice the amount of FPS and less than a third of
the training time. This further reinforced our decision to prioritise single-stage detection mod-
els over the traditional two-stage architectures.
Discussion
Page 69 of 111
==PAGE:82==
DTU & Meliora Bio
Why not DETR or RT-DETR?
DETR, on the other hand, employs transformers for object detection, eliminating the need for
region proposals and anchor boxes. However, transformer-based detection models require sig-
nificantly more computational resources due to their self-attention mechanisms, which scale
quadratically wrt. input length. Since straw level monitoring with object detection, with the
current setup, only involves continuous tracking of one bounding box, YOLO’s ability to per-
form fast made it a far more suitable choice for us to make. While Lv et al. [73] introduced
RT-DETR, an optimised version of DETR designed for real-time applications, Ultralytics [74]
reported that YOLOv11 still outperformed RT-DETR on the COCO dataset in terms of both
mAP50-95 and speed. Given these findings, we determined that YOLOv11 was still the supe-
rior option for our use case. Additionally, while transformers excel at capturing global context,
we explored their applicability in a regression-based straw-level prediction context using ViT.
However, as discussed in section 4.2.2.A, this approach yielded subpar performance metrics,
particularly when compared to our other models.
Ultimately, our goal was to deploy a high-accuracy model and a fast one that could seamlessly
integrate with Meliora Bio’s production system. YOLOv11 provided the best trade-off between
accuracy, speed, and implementation feasibility for the object detection part. Additionally, at
the start of the project, we were uncertain how much time we could allocate to training our se-
lected models, as we first needed to set up the data pipeline. Thus, the ease of implementation
of our chosen models was also an important factor. With the Ultralytics package in Python,
training, validation, and inference with the YOLOv11 model worked seamlessly.
5.2
Why ConvNeXt and ViT?
When selecting which models to use for the direct straw-level predictions, we based our deci-
sion on a couple of factors: model type, top-1 performance on ImageNet classification, model
size, and availability of pre-trained models.
Literature has shown that ViTs can perform exceptionally well on image tasks, beating typi-
cal convolutional-based models [9]. Due to their self-attention mechanisms, they also tend to
generalise better under distribution shifts or on out-of-distribution data, [75] than convolu-
tional models do, which we theorised might happen due to the small changes in camera and
environment over the long temporal scope of the deployed models. Additionally, the base-
line ViT model was recently shown to be Pareto-optimal in efficiency across multiple metrics
[76]. However, ViTs typically require large amounts of training data to reach this performance,
which is why we opted for pre-trained models. Moreover, one of the benefits of ViTs is their
global context, which allows them to find global image features and thus learn representations
of more abstract images and objects. This is not as relevant in our case, as we are using chute
Discussion
Page 70 of 111
==PAGE:83==
DTU & Meliora Bio
crop-outs, and the information relevant for the models, i.e., the top line of the straw, is a local
feature.
These potential issues with ViTs lead us to explore a purely convolutional model. Convolu-
tional models are excellent learners of local features, like textures, edges and colours, and the
straw exhibits unique properties in the images, which are beneficial for this exactly. Addi-
tionally, due to using chute crop-outs as the input, the models would not need to learn to be
translation-invariant, a problem which is inherent to the nature of the design of convolutions
[77, 78]. ConvNeXtV1, specifically, beat the performance of ViTs [46], which was part of the
decision to use this model. Additionally, ViTs are slower than purely convolutional models
during inference and scale worse as image size increases due to their attention mechanisms, so
we also looked to include a fully convolutional model. Initial tests also confirmed this, with the
ViT being 24% slower than the ConvNeXtV1 model on a low-powered CPU (i5-5300U) during
inference.
Finally, while we considered the inference speed limitations during model choice, we chose
models of adequate size, as we were unsure how complex the problem would be, opting for
slightly larger models. This was also based on our more conservative approach to choosing
our YOLOv11 models, so we believed we had more room to investigate slightly larger models
here. The trade-off of choosing larger models meant that the straw-level models were quite a
bit less computationally efficient than smaller models would have been; however, they were
also more likely to reach high performance. It is possible that smaller variants of these models
could have reached high enough accuracy for Meliora Bio, but this has been left for the future.
How to reduce the model size, or alternative models that are more efficient, is presented in
section 6.5.
5.3
Data
The dataset collected proved to work well for our purpose of straw-level monitoring, as seen
by the accuracy scores in Table 4.9. In addition, the models appear robust under both data
drift and chute rotation. If time allowed, we would have liked to collect more training data
over a larger period to increase the generalisation of the models. As presented in section 3.2,
our data showed large class imbalances when looking at annotations. This was an expected
occurrence, as the current system that controls the production already tries to ensure that the
straw level is always around 50%, which is why the edge cases, as a result, will have fewer
samples. Interestingly, the model that showed the best performance on our test set among our
trained straw-level models was ConvNeXtV1. It was trained on the unbalanced dataset, as we
found that on the balanced dataset, the test accuracy decreased by 5%. This was, however, only
the case for ConvneXtV1, and both ConvNeXtV2 and ViT gained accuracy by balancing the

dataset. Gathering more data, in general, is a good idea, as 1) it allows for the training on more
varied data, and 2) balancing the dataset afterwards would result in a larger dataset, which
could prove beneficial for further model development. This has been left as a consideration
since the timeline of the project did not allow for further data collection. It would also be ben-
eficial to the generalisation of the models to include images of the chute in different rotations
and positions since the distortion of the chute is different, depending on its location in the im-
age.
Additionally, for the straw-level models, as the regression models proved to be superior to
the classification models, we could give the models more granularity by training them on the
fullness values calculated from the straw bounding boxes instead of the fullness scores we
annotated in 5% increments. This was not done, as only later in the project were we able to
implement the functionality to convert straw bounding boxes into fullness scores.
The ConvNeXt models were pre-trained on ImageNet, which consists of 14 million images var-
ied across 22,000 classes, and YOLOv11 was pre-trained on DOTAv1 as explained in appendix
section 9.5. While these datasets provide the models with a good opportunity to learn gen-
eral features across vast data, they are not entirely representative of our datasets concerning
straw-level monitoring. This meant we had to collect a varied and sizeable dataset to ensure
the models could appropriately transfer to our use case.
Finally, it might have proven beneficial to annotate the images multiple times, preferably by
different people, and then take an average of those annotations to ensure labels matched the
real straw levels as closely as possible.
5.4
Why AprilTags for Live Overlay?
Initially, for our live-overlay setup, we planned to use object detection to identify the land-
marks, which previously were handwritten digits placed on the chute, as seen in Figure 3.2a.
We specifically thought about using the YOLOv11 model trained on the full images overlaid
with handwritten digits, which we did test. Unfortunately, during the preliminary experi-
ments, the model exhibited inconsistent and unreliable predictions, frequently failing to detect
most digits. To improve the detection rate, we experimented with Slicing Aided Hyper Infer-
ence (SAHI) [79] to enhance detection resolution. SAHI increased the number of evaluations
per image, significantly impacting the inference time without yielding sufficient improvements
in detection accuracy - why we deemed it insufficient for our purpose.
Following these challenges, we explored alternative approaches. With guidance from our tech-
nical supervisor at Helix Lab, we considered ArUco tags but ultimately opted for AprilTags
Discussion
Page 72 of 111
==PAGE:85==
DTU & Meliora Bio
due to our prior experience with the fiducial marker system. The advantage of AprilTags is
that they are less susceptible to false positives, as they only register a tag if it is explicitly
present in the image. If, in turn, a false positive does occur, it is much easier to handle, as
each tag id has a predefined location in relation to all its neighbours. In contrast, a YOLOv11
model trained to recognise multiple digits exhibits more considerable inherent uncertainty if
not appropriately trained, increasing the risk of false positives.
Additionally, using a YOLO model would require a substantially large and varied dataset with
handwritten digits, requiring even more annotation time. In contrast, the AprilTags, despite
having to be placed physically on the chute, do not need to be trained, as they are based on
a coding and decoding system. This robustness made AprilTags the preferred choice for our
application.

5.5.1
Model Selection
YOLO Models
The YOLOv11 model demonstrated high and consistent performance across both the test and
robustness sets, as shown in Table 4.9. However, compared to the manually annotated ground
truth data, an error of approximately 3-25% was observed in certain cases. While some of
these accuracy differences can be attributed to the inherent uncertainty in our annotations,
given that they were created manually, they do not fully account for the observed deviations.
Part of this discrepancy may be due to the tuning process of the models. As illustrated in
Figure 4.4a and Figure 4.4b, training stopped shortly after the hyperparameter tuning phase.
The hyper-parameter tuning was performed over 30 epochs, during which the models achieved
strong performance metrics. However, the models chosen as the final ones stopped at 34 epochs
(YOLO-S) and 48 epochs (YOLO-C) during their long run of 300 epochs with a patience factor
of 100 for early stopping.
This naturally leads to the question of whether the models received sufficient training to fully
converge during tuning. Depending on image size and learning rate, a more optimal model
could require more epochs to converge and achieve even better model performance. Stopping
the training prematurely means that some of the models did not fully explore the parameter
space, potentially missing out on subtle but important improvements.
However, the trade-off between additional training and diminishing returns is an important
consideration. Since the top-5 performing models for both YOLO-S and YOLO-C already ex-
hibit satisfactory mAP scores, further increasing the number of epochs may not necessarily
lead to substantial performance gains. Nevertheless, if the pre-configured epoch limit was too
Discussion
Page 73 of 111
==PAGE:86==
DTU & Meliora Bio
restrictive, the models may have been prematurely constrained by a decision bias, resulting in
suboptimal tuning. Given that larger images typically provide more detailed features for the
model to learn from and that learning rates can help avoid local optima, models with larger
image sizes and lower learning rates may have more potential than initially anticipated. Since
each model took approximately 2.5 hours to train during the 4-fold cross-validation, increasing
the number of epochs from 30 to 45 warrants further investigation. Such an adjustment might
offer a more comprehensive understanding of the models’ capabilities without significantly
increasing training time (by only 1.5 hours per fold). It might allow for further refinement,
possibly enhancing the final model’s performance and reducing any inconsistencies in the test
cases. Optimising the training process could be critical in achieving more reliable results if
these findings are to be applied in practice.
Beyond the number of epochs, the hyperparameter space is another key factor influencing per-
formance. When analysing the top-5 performing models for YOLO-S and YOLO-C, we find
that further restricting the hyperparameter space could improve the efficiency of the Bayesian
Optimization (BO) algorithm. For YOLO-S, Table 4.4, it is evident that the model has con-
verged onto a very consistent image size around 224 −256, and all top-performing models use
a batch size of at least 25. Since the current batch size restriction is set at 32, exploring batch
sizes beyond this limit could facilitate better model convergence. In contrast, for YOLO-C,
we observe a broader range of image sizes and batch sizes. However, an interesting pattern
emerges in the learning rate: while models ranked top 2-5 employ similar learning rates and
achieve comparable performance metrics on the test set (Table 4.5), the top-performing model
achieves distinctly superior results with a learning rate that is an order of magnitude smaller.
This suggests that fine-tuning the learning rate further by restricting the range of the parame-
ter from which the BO algorithm can sample could unlock additional performance gains.
Finally, choosing the YOLOv11 variant was crucial in balancing accuracy and efficiency. Based
on a study by Jegham et al. [29], we initially decided to proceed with the small ( s ) variant,
as it demonstrated performance only slightly below the m model while offering significantly
faster inference times. The performance gap between the two variants was minimal, making
the

Regarding data augmentation, our results showed that they were not beneficial to the Con-

crease the generalisation of deep learning models, with the important distinction being to keep
in mind when augmenting data, that the augmentations have to be logical relative to the data.
For instance, it would not make sense to flip a digit recognition dataset, as digits are typically
only written in one direction. Similarly, we only applied data augmentation methods aimed
at simulating changes in lighting, environment, and camera settings during the training of the
straw-level models. It might have been more valuable for the models to include geometric
augmentations, such as translation, rotation and zoom. Interestingly, in the robustness tests,
we found that the Augment noise transformation, which added a random brightness, and had
a chance of adding Gaussian noise to the image, increased the accuracy of both YOLO-S and
ConvNeXtV1. It might be worthwhile investigating whether adding this transformation as part
of the pre-processing would increase model performance.
Similarly, we did not find a significant gain in accuracy from utilising manual features. Gener-
ally, deep learning models learn to generate high-level feature representations during training,
often surpassing the capabilities of manually engineered features. However, this does not mean
that such features are completely worthless. Other ways of adding features to the model, such
as a Fourier transformation, might be worth exploring.
Regarding optimisers, we expected ADAM to perform better generally due to its adaptive gra-
dient estimation method requiring less tuning of hyperparameters. Often, a well-tuned SGD
might be better at generalisation. However, vigorous hyperparameter tuning and introducing
an annealing learning rate schedule to the training loop would be required to test this. As the
model performance was already sufficiently good, we did not focus on this aspect of model
training.
Finally, we were uncertain as to whether or not to include a sigmoid in the model head. It
would have the benefit of limiting the output range to between 0 and 1 as a model feature in
itself. However, due to the shape of the sigmoid function, it is harder to reach the edges of the

5.5.2
Robustness and Prediction Smoothing
The models showed promising results in robustness to data drift and rotation. They indicated
that all three models showed signs of invariance to data drift.
Both performances of the ConvNeXt models showed decreased accuracies on the rotated dataset,
which was expected. Due to the large distortion in the chute caused by its position in the im-
age, the upper chute window is more affected by the barrel distortion. This effect is enhanced
further in the AprilTag cutouts, as shown in Figure 3.6d. Both ConvNeXt models have not
been trained on rotated images, neither in the data itself nor through data augmentation. In
contrast, the YOLO models have been trained using a rotation data augmentation, which would
make them more invariant to the rotation itself, but not the added distortion.
We investigated some more extreme occurrences where the models could not predict the cor-
rect straw level. We looked at three distinct frames where the models showed signs of being
overly sensitive to situations where dust build-up against the upper and lower chute windows
introduced a visual ambiguity that they were incapable of accounting for, even presenting
a challenge for us during annotation. One of the main reasons for this is the training data.
Frames with high dust build-up are rare in our training data, and thus, when presented to
such cases, the models lack the experience necessary to make correct predictions. A robust fix
would be to record more data that has dust occurrences, labelling them and adding them to
the training pool.
Because we experienced these prediction spikes, we tried to investigate multiple methods of
alleviating the large deviations. We implemented one-second smoothing to ensure that spo-
radic prediction behaviour was dampened. However, no apparent improvement from the raw
predictions was evident in our attempt to do so. It is important to note that smoothing the
outputs works as a form of phase-shifting of the predictions, delaying the actual prediction.
This is more evident when the smoothing is stronger. We also looked at clipping the values
for each prediction based on the previous frame. While, in theory, clipping should make our
respective models have smaller MAEs, we only observed this behaviour for the YOLO-S model.
This is based on the idea that within a tenth of a second, the straw level should not be able to
move more than 0.5%. The idea itself should be valid based on the physical constraints of the
Discussion
Page 76 of 111
==PAGE:89==
DTU & Meliora Bio
straw behaviour. However, as evident, the threshold should be treated as a hyper-parameter
specifically tailored to each model.
Additionally, the dataset in which we conducted the tests contained all 24 frames per second.
In a deployed setting, this will not be the case, as the models’ processing time prevents them
from being able to predict every single frame. This means that the clipping values would need
to be adjusted once deployed with this in mind. Lastly, we tried to implement a simulated
mini-ensemble model to dampen the deviations more. The general idea was to create a new
YOLO model that both predicts chute and straw bounding boxes, but as we did not have such
a model tuned, we tried to simulate it by taking the average of the YOLO-S and ConvNeXtV1
predictions. The outcome of the simulated case did not prove to increase accuracy, although
it did work as a smoothing function. The two models act as each other’s counterbalances by
reducing the prediction deviation with an averaged value. On the other hand, if both models
predict incorrectly, as seen in Figure 4.12, the ensemble-like method will not reduce spikes.
5.5.3
Comparing the Models
Overall, both models show strong performance across the board.
They both appear to be
largely unaffected by data drift and chute rotation, which indicates that the models are ro-
bust to the most common changes that might happen during a production run. Additionally,
the results on the noisy datasets show that the models can still perform well, even in some of
the worst-case scenarios. Both models achieved accuracies around 90% in all cases, with an in-
crease between 50% and 70% compared to the SCADA sensors, substantially improving upon
the 19% to 33% of the current sensor-based monitoring system at Meliora Bio.
Regarding data drifting and rotation, it was expected that YOLO-S would outperform Con-
vNeXtV1, as it has significant data augmentation included as part of its training, whereas the
ConvNeXtV1 model does not. Additionally, since we selected our models by looking at the per-
formance on the test dataset, it is possible that they were slightly over-fitted through selection
bias, resulting in a loss in performance on the robustness datasets. In terms of processing time,
YOLO-S far outperformed ConvNeXtV1, which was especially evident on the slowest CPU, be-
ing more than twice as fast (0.62s vs. 1.62s). Given its significantly smaller size and that the
images only need to be sent through a single model instead of two, this was expected.
Apart from the accuracy differences, it is also worth noting that the two approaches we adopted
during this project have their pros and cons. With the YOLO-S model, we only care about the
model finding the straw bounding box no matter what the input is. It does not need to predict
a straw level directly based on some reference point but merely a straw bounding box. Thus,
as long as the distinction between the straw and the background in the chute is visible in the
frame, the YOLO-S model can predict our desired bounding box. It is then possible, with the
Discussion
Page 77 of 111
==PAGE:90==
DTU & Meliora Bio
current overlay setup, as long as the two closest tags above and under the top of the bounding
box are found, to report the predicted straw level. This is something the ConvNeXtV1 model
lacks, as it constantly needs the whole chute to infer where the straw level is. For the Con-
vNeXtV1 model, each prediction is relative to all other information in the input frame. Since
the model is trained on full cutouts of the chute, the model will not be able to extrapolate to
single window straw level detections. On the other hand, assuming that some of the tags are
unavailable or can not be detected by our overlay setup and the entire chute is visible, then the
ConvNeXtV1 model is able to report an estimated prediction of the straw level. In contrast, the
YOLO-S model will be stuck at the conversion between the bounding box and the percentage
value and will thus fail.
5.6
Perspective to Existing Literature
While we could not find related work that directly predicts straw level in real-time, much pre-
vious work has gone into water level monitoring and machine learning, a problem which has
similar solutions to the methods we apply, as described in section 2.2. Because the problems
are different, direct comparisons must be made with a grain of salt; however, it is still useful to
see if we fall in the same ballpark range.
In contrast to our results, in their work on water level monitoring in rivers, Filho et al. [18]
found that using a classification model achieved a better MAE than using a regression model.
This could indicate that it would also be possible to reach high accuracy with classification
models in our problem. However, since the real straw level variable is continuous, whereas the
problem Filho et al. were trying to solve was discrete, sticking to a regression model, especially
when combined with more granular fullness values, as discussed in section 5.3, might still be
the best option. Overall, our methodology resulted in models with higher accuracy than the
ones in Filho et al., but their dataset is considerably smaller, with only 214 images. While
we did a small investigation into manual feature engineering with edges and heatmaps, other
methods could potentially extract additional information and possibly lead to requiring less
data to achieve similar performance. This could be features such as a Fourier Transformation
of the images to extract information about edges and textures in the frequency domain.
Similarly, the work done by Moreg˚ard in his thesis on water level monitoring in a hydropower
plant [15], also achieved a high accuracy using a simple CNN. This could indicate that simpler
models could potentially solve the straw level monitoring problem as well. However, one of the
main advantages of the dataset Moreg˚ard collected is the temporal spread. As his entire dataset
was collected over 35 days, there is a much larger variability present in the data compared to
ours, which was collected over 2 days. Unfortunately, we could not collect more data due to
the extensive process of preparing the chute environment and model selection. A training
Discussion

dataset of data collected over a more extended period would likely lead to higher accuracy and
robustness. In addition, the increased variability in data features might also result in a smaller
dataset that performs on par with our dataset, as each sample, on average, would contain more
new information for the model to train on.

This chapter covers some of the work that has been left for the future due to the project’s time
limitations. We cover digital to analogue signal conversion, warning systems for production,

6.1
Digital to Analogue Signal
To interface and seamlessly integrate with the production at Meliora Bio, the digital output
signal of the model needs to be converted to an analogue signal in the form of a milliamp (mA)
signal. The model currently outputs a digital signal in the range of 0% to 100%, but the pro-
duction line expects an analogue signal in the range of 4 to 20 mA.
Our plans for implementing this part of the project was to send the digital signal over an
HTTP request to an Arduino or similar hardware, which would then receive the signal and
send it through a Digital-to-Analogue Converter (DAC), which would be hooked into the pro-
duction system. DAC with milliamps does not appear to be a common use case, but we did
find and order two copies of electrical components which we intended to use for this purpose:
the DFRobot Gravity: I2C 4-20mA DAC Module (Arduino Compatible), which can take an I2C
input from the Arduino, and output an mA signal.
This approach should work well in theory. However, in practice, we could not get the DFRobot
components working, no matter which configuration we tried. We suspect the components
were defective. We still believe this would be the way to go if the digital signal cannot be
directly used by communicating with the OPC UA server.

6.3
Blowback Identification
Sometimes, during a production run at Meliora Bio, a blowback can happen in the chute, caus-
ing the production to halt. This happens when the chute gets emptied while production is
running, as the vacuum seal caused by the straw is torn up. This typically happens if the sen-
sors read that the chute is fuller than it is and straw is not fed into the chute fast enough. When
this happens, production has to be restarted, and a new vacuum seal has to be formed. If it is
possible to collect video of this happening, either real or synthetic, it would be possible to train
a model to identify blowbacks and incorporate them into a warning system in production.
6.4
Model Drifting
Model drifting often occurs when a small shift in the data occurs over a long period. For Me-
liora Bio, this could be minor interference in the images, loss in quality, change in lighting due
to the season, or other similar effects. To combat this, a self-supervised learning method could
work in which the model incorporates some of the images it has predicted into its training
dataset and then periodically retrains itself. This would ensure that the model always has an
up-to-date internal data representation. Data cleaning must also be implemented to ensure
model performance stays up to par.
6.5
Optimising Model Size
One of the drawbacks of the ConvNeXt model is its size, requiring a GPU of significant power
to make predictions in real-time. Even though Meliora Bio only needs a readout of the fullness
every second, the more predictions the model can make, the more information it can use to
smooth out its predictions, avoiding unwanted spikes in the fullness measurements. Both the
YOLO and ConvNeXt models are optimised to be run on GPUs; however, due to their much
smaller size, the YOLO model is less affected by CPU bounds.
One way to optimise the size is to use knowledge distillation, in which smaller models are
trained on the outputs of the bigger models, typically leading to the same, or in some cases
even better, performance as the bigger models [82]. Another approach could be to use models
specifically built for inference on CPUs, such as MobileNet [83] or EfficientNet [84].

Building Upon the Framework
While the framework we have built is created explicitly for straw-level monitoring, it can be
utilised in other settings by making minor modifications. The data collection method works
well, and the annotation program can be extended to create other types of labels. Model se-

6.7
Chute Cutout Accuracy and Accounting for Distortion
The current method of creating AprilTag cutouts worked well in combating the barrel distor-
tion in the images where the chute is roughly vertical; however, the further away from the
centre of the image the chute was, the worse the distortion effect got, especially for the April-
Tag cutouts. The current integration of AprilTag cutouts only accounts for the four corner
points of the chute; however, we do have information from eight different tags on either side
of the chute. One could perform a transform by cropping the chute into a series of sub-squares
and performing homography and warping transformation on these sub-squares such that they
have a format with the same width but different heights. All the sub-squares would be verti-
cally stacked to create the chute cutout. This could help account for some of the distortion in
the image; however, since the warping operation from OpenCV might take some time, using
multiple of these operations per image might be infeasible for this pipeline.

Investigate What the Models See
If we look into improving the models, it would be beneficial to know what the models learn
as their feature representations. For this purpose, techniques such as GradCAM [85] could be
used. This way, we can ensure that the model has not taken any shortcut learnings [86] during
training, and could potentially increase the robustness and generalisation of the model.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to develop an autonomous framework for a straw-level monitoring system
using computer vision and machine learning at Meliora Bio. To be of use, it had to achieve
a higher accuracy than the current SCADA sensor monitoring system, which has an accuracy
of 23-33%. Additionally, predictions need to happen in real time, and the model needs to be
robust to any changes in environment and camera position.
We developed a process for data collection, annotation, and pre-processing, and we created
four separate datasets to train and evaluate the models, including evaluation metrics for the
SCADA sensor system. We created two distinct approaches for straw-level monitoring, one
using a YOLOv11 model to predict bounding boxes around the straw in the chute, denoted
YOLO-S, and converting those bounding box positions into a straw level using AprilTag mark-
ers physically placed on the chute itself. The second approach uses a combination of a YOLOv11
model trained to predict bounding boxes around the chute itself, creating cutout images of the
chute and sending those cutouts further through a ConvNeXtV1 regression model.
We used transfer learning by utilising models pre-trained on large datasets and fine-tuning
them on our training data. To find the best model configurations, we performed model se-
lection via 4-fold cross-validation and hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian Optimisation in
an iterative process. We developed a live overlay, so that model results could be visually and
quantitatively evaluated in a production setting.
Results showed that both approaches led to models that far outperformed the SCADA sensor
system implemented at Meliora Bio, reaching an accuracy of 89% for the YOLO-S model and
98.50% for the ConvNeXtV1 model on the test dataset. While the ConvNeXtV1 straw-level
model achieved the overall highest accuracy, the YOLO-S model was more robust to data drift,
rotation and noise. On robustness evaluations, YOLO-S had an accuracy of 94.29%, beating
ConvneXtV1’s accuracy of 92.87%. YOLO-S was significantly more efficient in inference speed,
requiring only 0.620 seconds per prediction on the slowest hardware we tested, in contrast to
Conclusion

==PAGE:96==
DTU & Meliora Bio
ConvNeXtV1’s 1.62 seconds. This makes YOLO-S satisfy the limits of the real-time criteria set
forth by Meliora Bio.
Our work is relevant to Meliora Bio in their pursuit of optimising and increasing the effective-
ness of their production line. Our framework enables them to replace the sensors and use the
computer vision and machine learning method we developed to get accurate real-time read-
ings of the straw level in the chute. This will prevent disruptions to the production and take a
mental load off of the operators, as they will no longer need to watch it as closely. Additionally,
the framework is extendable, such that it can work to solve other problems at Meliora Bio that
could benefit from similar solutions.
Due to the project’s time limit, certain aspects have been left for future work. We were un-
able to directly interface with the production via analogue or digital signals as it proved to be
more challenging than anticipated, as both the Digital-to-Analogue Converters we bought were
defective. Moreover, testing the framework’s autonomous capabilities during multiple sequen-
tial production runs was down-prioritised due to the complications of our analogue signal.
Additionally, the ConvNeXtV1 model was slower than the required real-time limitation on the
slowest CPU tested, and optimisation would be needed if it were to be used in production with
a low-powered CPU.
In conclusion, we researched relevant methodologies for machine learning and computer vi-
sion integration for straw-level measurement at Meliora Bio and created a framework capa-
ble of collecting and annotating data for model training and testing. Through the frame-
work, we successfully developed two supervised approaches to monitor the straw level in the
chute. With a customised live overlay, we visualised and evaluated the performance of both
approaches and found that both outperformed the current SCADA sensors. The YOLO-S ap-
proach proved most resilient to data drift, chute rotation and noise and was more than twice
as fast as the ConvNeXtV1. Thus, we recommend that Meliora Bio implement our computer
vision and machine learning framework with the YOLO-S model.

9.3.1
Robustness Dataset
The noisy datasets were created by adding transformations to the images. Overlays are avail-
able on ScienceData. The post-transformation images can be seen in Figure 9.5. The specific
parameters are as follows:
Lens Flares inserts a lens flare overlay, selected from [59], at a random position on the image,
padded to the same image size as the whole image.
Dust is added to the images by inserting a random dust overlay, resized to fit the chute on top
of the chute frame.
Scratches are generated with open-cv. A total of 60 scratches are generated. Each scratch
follows a bezier curve, where white circles are drawn, and has a max length of 150, end
point brush size between 0 and 1, and mid brush size range of 2 to 5.
People are added via cutouts of an anonymous worker at Meliora Bio. The cutouts are inserted
at random positions near the horizontal image center, with a max distance of 250.
Partial Gaussian Blur is added as circles of sizes between 50 and 500, that have been blurred
with a standard Gaussian blur, with a kernel size of 15, and σ = 5. Between 1 and 5
blurred circles are added at random positions on the image.
Random Augment increases brightness by a random amount between 10 and 50 and has a
50% chance of adding Gaussian noise to the image, with µ = 5 and σ =

9.3.3
Straw Level Models
One of the configurations of the straw level models was to add data augmentation to a random
subset of images during training. We tested augmenting 0%, 25%, 50%, 75% and 100% of
images. If an image is selected for augmentation during training, it would randomly select
which augmentation to apply from the list of chosen augmentations. All augmentations used
are default implementations from Pytorch transforms v2. The settings used are:
ColorJitter with brightness=0.25, contrast=0.25, saturation=0.5, hue=0.05
JPEG with quality=(1,100)
GaussianBlur with kernel size=5 and σ = (0.1,5)
GaussianNoise with µ = 0.0 and σ = 0.1.
RandomEqualize with p = 1.0
RandomPosterize with bits=np.random.randint(3,5) and p = 1.0.
RandomAdjustSharpness with sharpness factor=np.random.uniform(1.0, 2.0) and p = 1.0.

9.5
Pre-training Models
9.5.1
YOLO
We use pre-trained YOLOv11 ( n and s ) models trained on DOTAv1[87, 88, 38], which has
15 different classes split among 1411 training, 458 validation and 937 testing images.
The DOTAv1 data-set is a large-scale dataset for object detection in aerial images, allowing for
oriented bounding box object detection. See Figure 9.8.
Figure 9.8: Examples of Annotated Images from the DOTAv1 dataset. Figure taken from DOTAv1.
9.5.2
Straw Level Models
The following is a break-down of how each of the selected straw level models have been pre-
trained. All three models have built in features to be able to take input images in different
sizes. For the Vision Transformer, the image size must be a multiple of the patch size. For
the ConvNeXt models, any image size will do, as they are fully convolutional models. The
input weights are interpolated at the start of training in order to fit the new image size, if it is
different from the pre-trained image size.
The Vision Tranformer (ViT) used is a custom TIMM version of a vision transformer. The
Appendix
Page 99 of 111
==PAGE:112==
DTU & Meliora Bio
model card can be found here. It has a width of 640, an mlp width of 2560. It has 10
heads, and a depth of 12. The ViT has been pre-trained on ImageNet12k, with moderate
augmentation, and then finetuned on ImageNet1k with high augmentation. An image
size of 384x384 was used during training.
The ConvNeXtV1 model used is a small image classification model. The model card can be
found here. It has been pretrained on ImageNet12k and finetuned on ImageNet1k. An
image size of 384x384 was used.
The ConvNeXtV2 model is an image classification model. The model card can be found here.
It has been pre-trained using FCMAE, a self-supervised pre-training method, as de-
scribed in section 2.5.3. It was then first finetuned on ImageNet22k, and afterwards
ImageNet1k. It used an image size of 384x384.
