Abstract
This project aims to study in depth the thermodynamical processes involved in an hydraulic
accumulator during operation. The heat losses from the gas chamber to the surroundings deteriorate
the thermal performance of the device and decrease its ability to recover hydraulic energy effectively,
affecting the hydraulic pitch systems capability of controlling safely and precisely the wind turbines.
An initial evaluation of the thermal time constant model is made and compared with exper-
imental results for a 6 liter accumulator, showing that the current estimation techniques for the
thermal time constant are not suited for the application studied, over predicting heat loses. Further-
more, it is shown that the assumption of a constant value for the parameter can provide extremely
accurate results, provided that the compression ratios of the process are known in advance. For
varying compression ratios, dynamical effects play an important role and the accuracy of the model
decreases.
To study the thermal processes, a simpliﬁed CFD model of the accumulator is developed. The
results show that the main heat transfer losses are associated with temperature diffusion in the solid
parts of the accumulator, making up to 20% of the total heat loses. It is also shown that the heat
transfer processes and the thermal time constant are tightly connected to variations in gas mass,
in rate of change of volume, compression ratios and orientation of the device. Comparison with
experimental results validate the CFD model accurately.
A enhanced model for predicting the thermal time constant is proposed, based on a critically
damped second order differential equation. The model provides an extra 3-7% accuracy when
predicting pressures, compared to the actual implementation. The model, however, requires ﬁne
tuning of the parameters for speciﬁc cases and limits its implementation. It is concluded that
the best solution, in terms of accuracy and computational efﬁciency, is to provide an invariant
thermal time constant value based on experimental or CFD data. It is also concluded that the linear
condensed models based on the thermal time constant have an implicit accuracy limitation, since
no ﬂuid ﬂow mathematical expressions are solved.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
One of the keys of making wind turbines efﬁcient in generating energy from wind power relies
on having a robust and precise pitch control mechanism for the blades. These systems are frequently
used due to their ability of providing exceptional real time control capabilities and also due to their
response in high risk situations like emergency stops, or when extra energy is needed to make high
speed pitch angle changes in the blades. When these situations occur, the hydraulic pitch control
system has to be absolutely reliable, since any mishandling of the situation might jeopardize the
integrity of the whole turbine. To be able to handle the enormous amount of stored power that
the system has during the aforementioned situations, hydraulic accumulators are installed in the
system, as seen in Figure 1.1. The function of these is to effectively store the extra energy that is
needed by the hydraulic system by accumulating pressurized ﬂuid, due to the compression of a gas
in a separate compartment of the accumulator’s assembly.
(a) Turbine Hub
(b) Hydraulic Components Layout
Figure 1.1: Hydraulic accumulator integration in a pitch control system of a wind turbine
In an ideal situation, all the energy stored in the accumulator could be delivered without thermal
losses in the gas side during compression, but in reality, the process is not adiabatic. The gas
experiences pressure and temperature variations as the chamber’s volume changes time, and heat is
transfered to the solid parts of the accumulator, and consequently to the environment, either through
convection, conduction or radiation. The complexity of these phenomena has been, in several
occasions, studied and modeled with empirical correlations that provide fairly accurate results for

very speciﬁc situations, but little is known about truly happening in the gas of the accumulator. A
better understanding of the thermo and ﬂuid dynamics of the gas can lead to improved models that
are able to predict more accurately the performance of the device. The improved models can lead
to several optimizations in the pitch control systems of the wind turbines, for example by reducing
the size or the number of accumulators used per blade.
1.1
Thesis Objective
This project is made in collaboration with Fritz Schur Energy (abbreviated FSE), a leading
Danish company that specializes in hydraulic control systems for wind turbines. Their interest
is to develop and include an improved accumulator mathematical model in their simulation and
design platform, which can lead to more accurate predictions of the behavior of the hydraulic
systems. The goal of this project is to study in detail the thermodynamical processes involved in an
accumulator in operational conditions and how these are affected different factors initial pressure
and temperatures, compression speeds and orientation. The end result of the study is to present a
mathematical expression (or expressions) that model accurately the dynamical behavior of the heat
transfer processes in the accumulator.
1.2
Literature Review
The conception of the hydraulic accumulator as it is known nowadays (piston or ram moving
inside a cylinder) was made by William Armstrong in 1858, where a ram is arranged vertically in
a closed cylinder and loaded by a dead weight ballast, with energy able to be stored by upward
movement of the ram and recovered on its descent (Gibson and Pierce [2009]). The gas charged or
closed accumulator was then invented by Mercier [1943], who ﬁled the patent for the ﬁrst bladder
type hydraulic accumulator to be used in variable pitch propellers for the aircraft industry . His
design was based on a rubber bag which was ﬁlled with the compressible gas, and encapsulated in
the accumulator casing, where the hydraulic ﬂuid entered and compressed the bladder increasing the
pressure and storing the energy. In later years, designs for diaphragm and piston type accumulators
were developed, following different application demands.
Hydraulic systems made their way into the wind turbine sector in the 1990’s with the de-
velopment of the variable speed wind turbines (Wilmshurst [1988]). The need to increase the
energy harvesting capacity of the constant speed wind turbines used in the 1980’s gave birth to
the development of hydraulic control systems that regulate the pitch of the blades in the turbine,
consequently increasing the efﬁciency of the devices and making them safer when high winds
and overspending occurred. Hydraulic accumulators made their way into the control systems as
devices for energy storage and supply. When emergency stops or high speed pitching of the blades
happened, hydraulic accumulators were able to store compressed ﬂuid when the system pressure
increased and provided pressurized oil when extra energy was needed. Smaller accumulators were
used as dampers of high frequency movements on the systems.

1.2. LITERATURE REVIEW
3
The mathematical modeling of gas charged accumulator has been greatly simpliﬁed by assuming
ideal gas characteristics or using overestimated safety factors to size the accumulators. Also the
use of polytropic models has been widely used to describe the gas side behavior of the accumulator
(Hansen and Andersen [2012]), but has been shown that the polytropic exponents are usually
dependent on the speed of the process and can be considerably higher than the usually recommended
for engineering purposes (Beachley [1973]).
Speciﬁc studies on the behavior of the heat transfer phenomena of closed gas charged hydraulic
accumulators were made by Otis [1973], who dropped the use of the polytropic model pV λ
and developed a convective model which is based on a thermal time constant, derived from an
energy balance between the gas and the environment. His modeling also included the use of
real gas equations, since the pressure and temperature conditions were aggressive enough to turn
down the ideal gas assumption (the used equation of state was Benedict-Webb-Rubin, which was
successfully validated against experimental results for nitrogen by Jacobsen et al. [1973]). Otis and
Pourmovahed [1985] developed an algorithm to integrate the resulting equation on time and study
the properties of the gas side of the accumulator. Further investigation and validation of this was
made by Otis and Pourmovahed [1990], where experimental correlations for the prediction of the
thermal time constant were developed using data from live experiments from a 2.5 liter piston type
accumulator and several temperature, pressure and orientation setups, providing precise results
when the mathematical model was implemented. These studies established constant values for the
thermal time constant, depending on the accumulator’s size and initial conditions of the system.
Svoboda et al. [1978] developed a model that takes into account the transient phenomena inside
of the gas domain during compression-expansion cycles, assuming that the main heat transfer
process involved at low pressures and low frequencies is conduction through the gas. This lead
to the development of a mathematical description based on transient heat conduction models in
solids, that related the intrinsic properties of the gas to their equivalents in a solid. This model
provided a detailed insight into the temperature gradients inside the domain and also into how the
overall heat transfer of the gas with the environment evolves in time. Bouchard [1980] used this
heat conduction approach to develop an expression of the thermal time constant model of Otis
[1973], which reﬂected a temperature and pressure dependence and therefore provided a dynamic
way of describing the thermal time constant, and was successfully validated against experimental
results, having better agreement than the ones considering a constant value of the time constant.
In depth investigation of the behavior of hydraulic accumulators, both analytically and experi-
mentally was made by Rupprecht [1988] in his PhD. work. It included several experimental studies
regarding the thermal efﬁciency of hydraulic accumulators of different types (bladder, piston and
membrane) and sizes, while being subjected to a vast range of compression expansion cycles
(square signals, sawtooth, triangular, etc.), and consequently derived expressions and tables for
the prediction thermal time constant for different sizes of accumulators and several sets of initial
conditions. Rotthäuser [1993] in his PhD. work, also studied the thermal behavior of hydraulic
accumulators, analyzing the efﬁciency of compression-expansion cycles and also emphasizing the
importance and beneﬁts of computational modeling and solving of the thermodynamical prob-
lem, based mainly on the thermal time constant model. Also provided correlations for predicting

Faulkner and Smith [1983], Kornhauser and Smith [1993] and Faulkner and Smith [2008]

Hansen and Rasmussen [2013] proposed a model for an hydraulic accumulator to be used in a
wind turbine control system, based on the thermal time constant approach, and using an empirical
correlation for its determination based on the work by Rotthäuser [1993]. The model delivered
good results for speciﬁc cases, where compression expansion ratios are relatively small, but has
important limitations at high rate of volume changes. They demonstrate that the Redlich-Kwong-
Soave equation of state provides an accurate prediction of the gas properties, and conclude that
the main source of error for the prediction of pressure and temperature in the gas side is due to
inaccurate heat transfer modeling of the domain. They suggest that the heat balances in the piston
and accumulator casing, as well as in the oil should be included to improve the insulating properties
of the accumulator and be able to reach the peak temperatures and pressures after the compression
and expansion procedures. The study made in the aforementioned article signiﬁes the starting point
and motivation for the project described in this thesis.
More recently, and following the studies of Pourmovahed et al. [1992] regarding thermal
regeneration systems for hydraulic accumulators, Juhala et al. [2011] used the aid of commercial
multiphysics software, coupling ﬂuid dynamics and heat transfer, to study the inﬂuence of placing
a heat regenerator inside the gas chamber of the accumulator, in order to increase the energy
efﬁciency of it, and reduce the inﬂuence of heat losses during compression-expansion cycles. The
model was successfully validated with experimental results, and moreover showed that the inclusion
of a heat regenerator is indeed beneﬁcial for a better energy storage in the gas phase.
1.3
Problem Description
A general layout of an hydraulic accumulator is presented in Figure 1.2. The oil side has an
input port from where the hydraulic oil can ﬂow in or out. A ﬂoating piston separates the gas side
from the oil. The gas is entrapped in the other half of the cylinder and is compressed by the piston
when the oil starts ﬂowing into the accumulator.
When oil enters the accumulator, the gas is compressed and its temperature and pressure rises.
Equivalently, when the hydraulic system demands pressurized oil, the ﬂuid leaves the accumulator
and the gas expands, decreasing both its temperature and pressure. Heat transfer processes start
between the gas and the solid parts of the accumulator. Convection from the gas to the piston and
the shell of the accumulator, conduction through the solid parts, convection and radiation to the
environment. Consequently, the energy stored in the gas when it is compressed decreases over time

The mathematical modeling of a gas charged accumulator is often based on the thermal time

in the accumulators in detail to be able to predict more accurately the behavior of the thermal time

Thesis Outline

STAR-CCM+ Model
In this chapter a detailed description of the computational modeling of the problem using CFD
software is presented.
4.1
Model Setup
For the modeling of the accumulator, the use of two softwares was implemented. For the CAD
modeling and meshing, COMSOL Multiphysics was used due to its powerful and ﬂexible mesher
and STAR CCM+ was then used for solving and post-processing of the results.
The main objective of the modeling is to develop a simple geometrical representation of the
accumulator, that takes into account the main features of the real component and that provides
accurate results, comparable to the real life behavior of the device.
A Note About COMSOL
The decision of using the two softwares in conjunction, and not one standalone package, was
taken due to problems encountered when using COMSOL to solve the CFD problem. COMSOL
multiphysics does not have real gas models included by default, so the RKS real gas model had
to be coded and compiled into a shared library to be included in the model. This brought several
convergence problems that did not allow solutions to be obtained. The built in solvers in COMSOL
had a hard time solving the highly convective ﬂow in the gas. Moving the solving portion of the
problem to STAR CCM+ provided robustness and results.
4.1.1
Work-ﬂow
Mesh generation is carried out initially in COMSOL Multyphysics, where the initial volume of
the accumulator, its size, the number of elements per domain and overall mesh reﬁnement can be
setup depending on the needs of the simulation. Once the mesh is generated, it is exported to a
NASTRAN type ﬁle, that can be read by STAR CCM+ which will carry on with the simulation.
This integration is done through a Java macro where the mesh is imported, manipulated, divided in
regions and boundary and initial conditions are applied, ﬁnally proceeding to solve the problem.

• Simpliﬁed Geometry: Many geometrical details were not included to make the domain as

• Constant Natural Convection with the Ambient: The accumulator interacts with the outer

procedure in the accumulator, the outer walls of the cylinder remain fairly close to the initial
conditions and since the difference with the outer temperature is relatively small (below
40◦C), the contribution of the radiation to the heat transfer remains fairly small compared to
the convective heat transfer, and can be safely neglected(Incropera et al. [2011]).

From Figure 4.2 it can be seen that the overall values of the non dimensional parameters
suggest that indeed the ﬂow is turbulent, since the mean Reynolds number in the experimental
runs is O(104) and the mean Grashof number is O(1011).
• Laminar Flow in Oil Domain: The viscosity of the hydraulic oil is a relatively high (be-
tween ) viscous ﬂuid and its temperature is not expected to change dramatically during
the compression-expansion processes (it is expected to be around 30-40 °C due to the heat
generated by the pumping system and friction heating), which implies that ﬁrst, the Reynolds
Number of the domain is between 700 and 1200, making the ﬂow inherently laminar, and
second that the body forces in the domain are less important than the viscous ones, ruling
out the effects of buoyancy or turbulent natural convection.
4.1.4
Solver Setup
The transient simulation is solved by a ﬁxed step implicit solver, with a time step of 0.001 s,
and ﬁrst order discretization in time. The inner solver steps are performed in a segregated manner,
where the momentum, pressure, energy and turbulence equations are solved one after the other.
The inner solver loops until the residuals of the main quantities fall below a predeﬁned tolerance
(below 10−4). The differential equations are solved by a multi-grid method.

CFD Model Validation
In order to ensure that the model represents the real component, subject to real life conditions,
a validation was carried out using the experimental data from Hansen and Rasmussen [2013].
The data obtained from this test runs includes the piston position over time and temperature and
pressure of the gas domain. The validation was carried out with data from 19 selected runs from
the previous study. For each of the experimental test runs, the following procedure was followed
• Calculate the initial temperature, pressure and volume from the experimental data and feed
to the model as initial conditions.
• With the experimental piston position, calculate the piston velocity over time and feed to the
corresponding morphing and moving domains in the simulation model.
• Setup time reports of the pressure and temperature in the gas domain and simulate.
• Compare results with experimental data and calculate maximum and root-mean-square errors
of the tracked quantities over time

simulated results obtained with the CFD model, visually ﬁt accurately the experimental curves.

not adequately reached, but still the decay in the pressure for the simulated result resembles the
experimental accurately. Figure 5.3(a) and Figure 5.3(c) show results were the simulated data
matches with higher degree of accuracy the experiments. In general, the average maximum error
in pressure between the experimental and simulated results is of 5%, and the average overall

repeatable and reliable results for the range of conditions used in the previous experimental studies.
The errors that can be seen between the experimental and the simulated data can be explained by
different factors:
• Reduced Model: The nature of the model is expected to introduce some errors since only
a fraction of the real domain is actually used in the simulation. Assumptions of radially
symmetric physical phenomena might not hold not valid when, for example, the sources of
body forces change direction (meaning a change of the positioning in the accumulator, which
could be horizontal, vertical, inclined or changing position over time.). Also, the simpliﬁed
geometry used in the computational model can generate local differences in how the ﬂuid
really behaves or how the heat transfer processes are truly carried out.
• Uncertainties in the Experimental Data: The simulations are initialized with the real values
of the temperature and pressure of the gas domain, and the compression of the domain is
modeled by inputting the measured velocity of the piston over time to the corresponding
moving and morphing domains (piston and its associated boundaries and interfaces with
its neighboring domains). The problem with these real measured data is that the signals
have a degree of noise which induces errors in the model. A noisy velocity signal of the
piston in the model can induce numerical errors and convergence problems. The downside
of ﬁltering these signals is that, if done in excess, data can be lost and therefore the real
signal of the position of the piston over time cannot be faithfully reconstructed. This can
lead to under-predictions in the gas temperature and pressure, as seen in Figure 5.3(e) and
Figure 5.3(f).
• Computational Domain Deformation and Degraded Cell Quality: The ﬂuid domains in the
computational model are subjected to compression and expansion, and the discrete elements
change in shape over time. This deformations can degrade the quality of the elements and
introduce numerical errors in the solution, specially when cells have high aspect ratios.

6.2.3
Heat Transfer In the Boundaries
Heat losses from the gas to the surrounding parts of the accumulator and from these to the
environment are the main focus of the project. With the CFD model, the boundary ﬂuxes in the
walls of the gas domain can be integrated to know the amount of heat transfered between bodies.
The resulting rate of heat loses are presented Figure 6.7(a). It can be seen that the heat losses follow
==PAGE:54==

the dynamics of the compression-expansion cycle. Heat loss increases as the gas is compressed due
to the continual increase of temperature and the momentum induced in the gas due to the piston
movement. After the compression, the heat transfer gradually decays due to a sustained decline
of the gas temperature and the increase of temperature in the solid bodies of the accumulator.
During the expansion, the heat transfer decreases gradually as the gas gets colder. At around 20
seconds into the simulation the heat transfer process reverses, now the gas receives heat from
the hotter boundaries. When the expansion ﬁnalizes, the gas keeps receiving heat from the solid
boundaries, and the magnitude of the heat transfer diminishes as time passes, due to the gas and the
solid boundaries reaching slowly thermal equilibrium. Most of the heat is transfered through the
cylinder shell boundaries. This responds to the fact that it has the biggest heat transfer area (which
is between 10 and 2 times higher than the one of the piston or the top boundary) and high ﬂuid
velocities because of natural convection effects (as seen in Figure 6.6(a)). The heat losses through
the piston and top boundary are smaller but account for 15% of the total. It can be seen that for the
top boundary the heat transfer process does not experience an inversion when the expansion begins.
This is caused due to the small temperature difference between the gas and the boundary. In the
piston, a small recovery can be seen after the compression. The lowest temperatures occur near this
boundary so the temperature difference is high enough to be able to recover some of the heat.
The heat transfer coefﬁcients associated to each boundary (Figure 6.7(b)) show that after the
compression, the highest heat transfer coefﬁcient is experienced in the cylinder wall, while the
lowest is in the piston. The temperature difference between the piston and the cold gas at the
bottom of the domain is the smallest between the boundaries, which results in lower heat transfer.
On the top boundary, the higher gas temperatures are found, which increases the heat transfer
coefﬁcient. After the expansion, the piston has the highest values of h, consequence of the higher
temperature difference between the now colder gas at the bottom of the gas domain and the piston.
Still, although having a reduced heat transfer coefﬁcient, the highest amount of heat is transfered
through the cylinder walls due to the large heat transfer area.

It has been shown that the heat leaves the gas domain towards the solid domains during and

and the colder gas receives heat from the adjacent boundaries. During these processes, a very small
==PAGE:56==
44

amount of heat is loss to the environment. The energy is therefore stored in the solid domains.
The problem is that not all the energy is recovered by the gas when the expansion ﬁnishes. As
mentioned in Section 6.2.3, Heat Transfer in the Boundaries, the heat transfered through the piston
and to the top ﬂange of the domain is practically not recovered when the expansion takes place.
This losses account for 15% of the total.
If the system has enough time after the expansion, (Figure 6.9(a)), it reaches thermal equilibrium
and the amount of energy lost and recovered can be quantiﬁed. Figure 6.9(b) shows that in the
long run, when the system reaches equilibrium, the gas ends up losing an unrecoverable amount of
energy of 5.91 kJ. The amount of energy recovered by the gas would be the difference between the
maximum energy lost, 19.52 kJ and the ﬁnal steady value, which yields 13.62 kJ, meaning that the
recovery efﬁciency is of 69%. This value of the efﬁciency of the accumulator matches the ones
in Pourmovahed et al. [1992], Rotthäuser [1993] and Juhala et al. [2011], where recovery rates
between 55% and 70% are standard for gas charged accumulators for different conditions.
It can be concluded that the main source of energy loss of the gas domain and what avoids
a more efﬁcient energy recovery is the diffusion of temperature in the solid domains. Energy is
stored due to an increase in temperature and proportional to the heat capacitance of the solid (which
is considerably higher than the one of the gas). The temperature diffuses through the solid body,
which experiences a small overall increase in temperature, although it holds a large amount of
energy. Then, this energy is held in the mass of the accumulator and only a part of it is given back
to the gas during the expansion process, due to reduced heat transfer dynamics and temperature
differences between the gas and the surrounding domains.

6.3.2
Compression Velocity
The variations of the internal energy of the gas are directly proportional to the rate of change in
volume, as stated in Equation 2.2. Therefore, it can be expected that increases in the compression
velocity will lead to higher internal energy generation, and consequently elevated pressures and
temperatures on the gas. In Figure 6.14(a) and Figure 6.14(b) it can be seen that the increased
compression velocity leads to a higher peak in the gas pressure and temperature respectively. Faster
piston velocities compress the gas in shorter time, which increases the molecular interactions in
the gas chamber and therefore higher temperatures are developed. Figure 6.14(c) shows that the
effect of the compression velocity affects the dynamics of τ during compression and expansion.
The slower the velocity, the less momentum is transmitted. Also, the internal energy generated in
the gas is decreased leading to a smaller temperature difference between the boundaries and the gas.
This make the system more thermally isolated during the compression and expansion, reﬂected in
the increased value of the thermal time constant. This results are in accordance with the ﬁndings of
Otis and Pourmovahed [1990], reﬂecting that the piston velocity (and therefore the compression
velocity) has a clear effect in the overall thermal time constant for slow rate of changes in volume.

7.1
Proposal
Based on the experimental and simulated results derived from the CFD study, a dynamic model
of τ can be proposed.
The behavior of the thermal time constant suggest that it resembles a critically damped system.
During the compression phase, τ increases with proportionally to the rate of change in the gas
volume. This means that the system is isolating. This is a consequence of the phase shift that the
heat losses have with respect to the temperature difference between the gas and the surrounding
boundaries, as seen in Figure 6.11. The average temperature of the gas increases faster than the
temperature near the boundaries, which prevent the initial heat transfer. As the piston moves
and induces momentum into the ﬂuid and the temperature has increased fast enough, τ reaches
a maximum and starts decreasing. The heat losses damp the isolation effect and τ exponentially
decays until the compression ﬁnish.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
The thermodynamical processes involved in an hydraulic accumulator during operation were
successfully studied by the use of a CFD model. The results obtained from this model had a high
level of agreement with the experimental results for a wide range of cases. The results demonstrate
that the heat losses in the gas side of an accumulator are mainly associated with heat transfered
to the solid parts that is later diffused into their bodies. The heat loses to the environment are
relatively small for the conditions simulated, and have no meaningful effect in the system. There is
also clear evidence that the there are important temperature gradients in the gas that make the heat
losses not uniform throughout the boundaries.
It has been proved that the thermal time constant of the gas varies in time. It is proportionally
dependent on the gas mass in the chamber, and is affected by changes in the initial conditions,
orientation, compression velocity and compression ratio. The dynamics of τ are deﬁned by the
ﬂuid ﬂow of the gas. Convection is the main heat transfer process between the gas and the solid
parts and it is affected by the piston movement, which induces momentum into the ﬂow. The non
linear behaviors of the heat transfer coefﬁcients and thermal time constant are generated by the
ﬂuid motion and the convective processes.
A mathematical model for τ was developed based on a critically damped second order system
formulation. The model is able to replicate the behaviors of τ obtained from the CFD model, and
is able to predict gas pressure with good accuracy. However, there is lack of robustness associated
to the extra effort needed to ﬁne tune the model’s parameters for different cases. A constant τ
approach, with an optimized value of the thermal time constant, can deliver the same levels of
accuracy. This makes a constant τ approach the best one in terms of precision and computational
efﬁciency. It is clear, however, that the condensed models have a limitations and their accuracy is
dependent on the operational conditions modeled.
Recommendations and Future Work
As for the CFD modeling, it would be interesting to develop a full 3D model of the accumulator
and include the geometrical details omitted and compare with the obtained results of this project.
Especially, the inclusion of forces generated due to the rotation of the accumulator in the hub of a
61
==PAGE:74==
62
CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSIONS
turbine. It is expected that the gas would develop curious ﬂow patterns that might lead to different
temperature distribution in the domain and changes in the heat transfer patterns. It would also be of
interest to adapt the CFD model to different sizes and compare them with experimental results and
see if the dynamics of the system are scalable.
It would be also interesting to focus in the real device and think about how to improve the
way the accumulator is manufactured and decrease the heat losses. Could be through concentric
cylindrical shells, with smaller thicknesses and varying thermal conductivities, that can be stacked
in a way that the device is able to withstand the pressure demands and lose the smallest amount of
heat. A suggestion could be to isolate with a low thermal conductive material the piston and the top
ﬂange of the gas chamber and run tests to see the changes in the heat losses.
The focus should be shifted from the development of dynamical condensed models to think of
ways to make the system more isolated. The thermal time constant model is simple to implement,
and with the right values of τ, provides good levels of accuracy. Investigations should be carried
into what can be made to increase this time constant of the gas and reduce the heat losses. The
inclusion of foams (Pourmovahed et al. [1992]) or heat sinks (Juhala et al. [2011]) in the gas
chamber are proving to be efﬁcient for this purpose. From these ideas, several studies regarding the
kind of material and its shape and positioning in the gas chamber can be derived and, supported
with appropriate CFD models and experimental data, provide insights into how to improve the
thermal efﬁciency of an hydraulic accumulator.
