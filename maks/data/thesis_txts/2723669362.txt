Abstract
This CFD study analyzes the flow field, flame propagation, and emissions of a
spark ignited CFR-engine at 1100 RPM for a range of spark timings. The engine uses
a lean premixed air/methane mixture at an air/fuel equivalence ratio λ = 1.4. The
CFR engine has a modified piston head to increase the compression ratio to 15. The
study uses a 0D model to implement the jet of unburned piston crevice CH4 ejected
from the piston ring end gap during the power stroke. The flow field is analyzed for
a shrouded and a plain intake valve. The shrouded valve provides a stable swirling
flow while the plain valve provides an unstable tumbling flow. The combustion is
analyzed for the shrouded valve. The flame is convected by the swirling flow, and some
trends in the heat release rate can be directly mapped to the geometry of the engine.
The modification to the piston crown and the protruding inlet valve are particularly
important. The same combustion model tuning was used for all spark timings. The
heat release rates are captured better for the later, lower pressure spark timings.
The Metgalchi-Keck correlation used in this study, as well as other ways to predict
laminar flame speed, is unsuited for lean fuels at high pressures, and the modelling
of the combustion is concluded to need separate tuning for different spark timings
due to the different conditions. The cycle averaged burned emissions are predicted
well, but the time resolved unburned hydrocarbon emissions are overpredicted. This
is likely due to a combination of underprediction of temperature and overprediction
of cylinder mass due to uncertain boundary conditions. The 0D ring end gap model
has a 30% lower mass flow than intended, but the difference in emissions caused by
the end gap jet as well as the position of the gap are significant.

INTRODUCTION
2
Introduction
2.1
Background
With the advancement power-to-X technologies and improvements in infrastructure for gas
fuel, liquid natural gas (LNG) engines are becoming an increasingly popular way to reduce
pollution and greenhouse gas emissions in the marine industry [1]. However LNG is mostly
methane (CH4), which itself is a potent greenhouse gas with a global warming potential
(GWP) 84-86 times that of CO2 on a 20-year scale [2]. Thus, avoiding unburned methane
escaping into the exhaust is crucial, as this can completely eliminate the environmental
benefits of using LNG [3]. One known source of unburned hydrocarbons is residual gas
stored in tiny spaces, so called crevices, in the cylinder. The crevices can be small regions
left by tolerances, spark plug threading, valve seating, gaps between the piston rings and
the piston, etc. [4]. In a four-stroke engine, gas enters the crevices during the compression
stroke when there pressure is high. Here it remains unburned and it is then released from
the crevices when the pressure drops during the exhaust stroke. It is thus of great interest
to capture the physics of crevice flow and to visualize the crevice regions. Doing so may
help improve engine geometry to reduce emissions from unburned hydrocarbons.
2.2
Project description
A Cooperative Fuel Research (CFR) engine has been used to investigate methane combus-
tion and the emissions of unburnt methane by the section of thermal energy at DTU. To
better understand the physics inside the CFR engine, a CFD model of the engine should
be built. It was decided a deeper understanding of the CFR engine can be gained by
examining different configurations of valve geometry and spark timings. This will provide
insight about the flow and the locations of unburned methane. To create the model, it is
necessary to accurately capture the combustion. Additionally, a description of the shape
and direction of the flame propagating inside the engine should be investigated, providing
further insight into the engine characteristics. The goals of the project are therefore:
1. Create a CFD model of the CFR engine that matches the motored conditions.
2. Use this model to explain the flow for different valves and the flame propagation for
different spark timings
3. Illustrate the crevice trapped unburnt methane and how it affects emissions.

Physical Setup
The physical experiments were run on a Cooperative Fuel Research (CFR) engine from
1948. The piston was modified in 2008, adding an extra part to the top of the piston head
to increase the compression ratio [5].
The experiments this study uses for benchmarking were conducted by Mads Carsten Jes-
persen from the section of thermal energy at the department of Civil and Mechanical
Engineering at DTU. At the time the CH4-experiments were run, it was not possible to
measure the instantaneous pressure at the intake. The setup was later rebuilt for experi-
ments using NH3, at which point a pressure sensor was installed in the intake. Thus, the
time-resolved inlet pressure is available only for the experiments using NH3 as fuel.

COMPUTATIONAL MODELLING
4
Computational Modelling
4.1
Software
The simulations were set up and run using commercial CFD code STARCCM+ which is
developed by Siemens Digital Industries Software. It uses the finite volume method to
solve partial differential equations on a collocated grid. Most of the early work was done
using STARCCM+ v. 17.04.008-R8, however all presented results were run using STAR-
CCM+17.06.007-R8 and STAR-CCM+18.02.008-R8.
Additionally, a special license for
the add-in "STAR-CCM+ In-Cylinder Solution" was used to aid in the construction of the
engine and allow for the use of some combustion models which are not natively available
in STAR-CCM+. The geometry was processed using SolidWorks 2022.
4.2
Geometry
An high quality X-ray scan of the engine geometry was kindly provided by P. Pal at
Argonne National Laboratory (ANL). ANL have previously used this scan to do CFD
of the engine [6]. The custom piston head was drawn in accordance with the technical
drawings in the 2008 project by A. Bjerre and M. Gøttrup [5]. The rest of the piston and
the valves themselves were measured from equivalent parts to the ones in the engine using
calipers at room temperature. Thermal expansion is not considered. The custom piston
head is fastened to the old piston head using 6x M5 Phillips flat head screws countersunk
so as to not protrude from the top of the piston head. The screw heads are countersunk
deeper than flush with the piston surface. This has been neglected and the top of the
piston is thus perfectly flat. The piston and valves can be seen on Figure 4. The scan of
the spark plug did not match the spark plug volume used in the experiments. The volume
of the spark plug used in the experiments was measured by filling the spark plug with
drops of ethanol using a pipette. To match the volume of the physical spark plug crevice,
a large amount of the spark plug geometry was removed from the scan. The geometry of
the pressure sensor inserted into the pressure tap is unknown, and the internal geometry
of the pressure tap was simply approximated as a cylinder as it appears in the scan.
To achieve the correct compression ratio, the volume of the pressure tap was reduced to

Two different turbulence models were used in this project, RANS modelling and LES
modelling. These will described very briefly in the following section.
4.4.1

time-averaged such that the mean flow, which is typically of interest, can be described.

consequence of the non-linear convective term in the NS-equation. The Reynolds’ stress
is a symmetrical tensor, adding 6 additional equations. This causes a closure problem.
To solve this problem, Boussinesq proposed modelling the Reynolds’ stress analogously to
the viscous stress by introducing the turbulent eddy viscosity, µt, as a parameter. This
parameter is purely mathematical, and allows the Reynolds’ stress to be described by
the properties of the mean flow using the velocity gradients.
This is called the eddy
viscosity approach to turbulence modelling. There are many ways to approximate the
eddy viscosity, but two common ways are the two-equation models, k −ω and k −ε, which
use the turbulent kinetic energy k and a measure of the dissipation rate ω or ε. These
properties are modelled by introducing two additional equations expressed in terms of
the mean flow. These will not be covered in this report. This makes RANS-modelling a

4.5.1
Combustion Basics
In the following section a brief description of combustion will be given. Focus will be on the
structure of premixed combustion. The purpose is to provide some background motivating
why the combustion models used are designed as they are.
Combustion is a chemical processes where the fuel (CH4 in this case) reacts with oxygen
to release heat. Mixing of fuel and oxidizer at a molecular level is required in addition to a
sufficiently high temperature to initiate the chemical processes. In a spark ignited engine,
like the CFR engine as modelled here, the fuel and oxygen are mixed into a homogeneous
composition by the time the spark plug fires. A spark plug uses electrical energy to ionize
the gas between the spark prongs causing a sudden local rise in temperature and pressure.
This initiates the reaction and creates an initial flame kernel. The flame kernel is initially
laminar and later becomes turbulent [12]. The main fuel converting reaction occurs in
the inner layer of the flame and will provide the majority of the heat to the flame. On
both sides of the flame the composition rapidly converges to the two meta-stable states:
Burnt and unburnt. The flame will continue to persist until conditions no longer allow for
the flame to persist. Either there is no more reactants, or the pressure and temperature
becomes too low.
In premixed combustion a thin flame separates the unburnt and burnt gas. The flame
propagates with velocity sL called the laminar flame speed.
The laminar flame speed
increases with temperature and decreases with pressure. In Figure 11 a schematic of the
conditions in a flame is shown. The flame is divided into 3 zones: The preheat zone, the
inner layer, and the oxidization layer. In the inner layer the main fuel consuming reactions
occur which release the majority of the energy. The temperature here is denoted T0. In the
preheat zone the heat is diffused ahead of the flame, preheating the unburnt gas. In the
oxidization layer oxidization of CO occurs. The thickness of the flame is denoted lF. The
flame surface is defined as the area defined by an isosurface of temperature T0. Turbulent
combustion is characterized by a disruption of the flame by the turbulent flow. The effect
of turbulence on the flame depends on the turbulent length scale, the turbulent velocity,
the laminar flame speed sL, and the flame thickness lF. Turbulence can have multiple
effects on the flame:
Page 14 of 113

4

1. Turbulence can wrinkle the flame surface increasing the flame surface.
2. Turbulence can increase the heat transfer away from the inner layer e.g. ahead of
the flame in the preheat zone.
3. Turbulence can increase the diffusion of radicals away from the inner layer.

4.5.2
Combustion Models
There are two common approaches to combustion modelling, reaction models and flamelet
models. Reaction models work through energy and species transport equations, and use a
chemical mechanism to estimate combustion by scaling elementary chemical reaction rates
based on the present temperature and pressure in accordance with the Arrhenius law. The
chemical mechanism contains information related to the reaction rate constants at different
operating conditions for a number of reactions, depending on the desired complexity vs
computational cost. In principle, this requires the flame thickness to be resolved on the
grid but models exists that scale the reaction rates with an approximation of the laminar
flame speed. This removes the necessity to resolve flame thickness as the reaction rates
are artificially modified. Additionally the turbulent corrugation of the flame needs to be
resolved as well but turbulent flame speed closure models can be included to scale the
reaction rate based on the modelled turbulent flame speed.
Flamelet models do not model chemical reactions directly but solely based on the laminar
flame speed. Additionally a modification of this is used to account for the increase in fuel
reaction rate by the turbulent corrugation. Fuel and oxygen species are tracked to ensure
Page 17 of 113
==PAGE:26==

4.5. Combustion
enough is available for the combustion. A simple fuel oxidization reaction is then used
in conjunction with a reaction rate to obtain the amount of fuel and oxygen used and
the amount of combustion products are produced. Usually flamelet models also include
models to handle emission formation.
This project has primarily focused on flamelet models.
ECFM-3Z
In this section a brief introduction to the ECFM-3Z model will be given. It should be noted

The Extended Coherent Flame Model (ECFM) models the flame propagation occur-
ing in the inner layer and the post-oxidization occuring in the oxidization layer separately.
These are called flame propagation reaction mode and post-oxidization reaction mode
respectively. First, a description of the flame propagation reaction mode will be given
followed by the post-oxidization reaction mode and the emission models.

is only necessary to calculate the area of the turbulent flame and the laminar flame speed.

and no chemical reaction rates need to be calculated. As described in subsubsection 4.5.1
at a small enough scale the combustion can be considered laminar. The ECFM model

Ignition models
In this section, a brief introduction to spark plugs will be given followed by a description
of the models used to capture the physics of spark modelling. This serves to motivate why
the models are designed as they are and to highlight the disparity between the current
understanding of physical sparks and what is actually applied to the computational domain.
Sparks Theory
In a spark ignited engine the air/fuel mixture is in a metastable unburnt state. A spark
plug converts electrical energy into heat locally destabilizing the mixture and initializing
a high temperature region where reactions are enabled. If the spark is strong this initial
region is strong enough to sustain a propagating flame. In this section a very brief overview
of the flame initialization around a spark plug will be given. Spark plugs have 3 phases:
the breakdown, arc and glow phase. Upon spark initialization the gas between the spark
plug prongs acts as an insulator. In pre-breakdown state the spark voltage ionizes the gas
in the spark gap. Accelerated electrons collide with gas molecules generating radiation and
additional electrons which are again accelerated causing an avalanche effect. The number
of electrons increases rapidly and the temperature remains comparatively unchanged [18].
Once sufficient radiation is present to liberate photo electrons on the cathode surface,
large increases in current in conjunction with a a drop in voltage occurs. This is called
breakdown. The breakdown phase lasts ∼O(ns) and increases gas temperature rapidly up
to 60000 K and then drops as the spark diameter increases. The breakdown phase contains
the highest power output but due to its short duration the total energy deposited is larger
in the later phases. After the breakdown phase, the arc phase begins. The breakdown
phase has increased the electrical conductivity in the gas, now plasma, and hot molten
spots on the cathode from which thermionic emissions can occur. During the arc phase
the plasma in the spark channels is ∼5000K and is quasi-steady [18]. It usually lasts a
few ms. Lastly the glow phase occurs. It differs from the arc phase in that the center and

4.7
Time-step
During a cycle the engine exhibits vastly different conditions. Intake and exhaust stroke
differ from the compression stroke which differs from combustion and expansion stroke.
This motivates a time-step that changes depending on which of the following 3 things
dominate the conditions in the engine. 1. When valves are open large flow velocities are
present necessitating a small time-step to ensure the numerical stability and accuracy of
the simulation. 2. After spark initialization combustion occurs which requires a small
time-step to resolve the flame propagation. 3. When none of the previous occurs. The
time-step used can be found in Figure 15. The base time-step is 10 ms. Upon valve intake
valve opening there is a huge pressure drop over the narrow valve gap of 0.1mm. The
time-step is therefore briefly reduced to 0.1 ms mostly to aid numerical stability. During
the intake stroke the time-step is 2.5 ms. During combustion the time-step is initially 1 ms
but increases to 2.5 ms during the later part of the combustion where the flame has been
quenched. During exhaust valve opening the time-step is briefly reduced to 1 ms. Note
the time-step is reduced to 1 ms upon valve closing. This is solely to ensure numerical
stability. The valve curtains close suddenly when the gap is 0.1 mm. This can cause
numerical instability. The PISO-algorithm is used to reach convergence at each time step.

4.10.3
Convergence: Conclusion
The mesh convergence analysis suggests that the difference between the 1 mm and 0.25
mm mesh is minimal. This in principle indicates that using a 1 mm mesh could be viable.
In this project a mesh size of 0.5 mm was used. This was done for two reasons. First,
the 0.25 mm mesh took over 6 weeks of wall-clock time and was therefore only complete
late in the project. The mesh size chosen was therefore based on convergence between the
1 mm and 0.5 mm mesh. These show a significant difference. That the 0.25 mm mesh
reverts back to the 1 mm mesh could not be known a priori. Second, investigating the
mesh at TDC illustrates that at a base size of 0.5 mm the vertical discretization between
valve head (bottom of valve) and the piston is discretized by 2 prism layers on either face
and 10 hexahedral elements (see Figure 9). At double mesh size this would be discretized
by approximately 5 hexahedral cells. It was deemed too low and that even though the
final AHRR did not show sensitivity, this coarse of a discretization might change dynamics
of both the flow and the combustion. Therefore the 0.5 mm mesh was chosen for the
simulations.
The time-discretization during the intake stroke showed very small sensitivity when mea-
sured based on in-cylinder pressure.
This indicates a larger time-step could be taken.
However the flow across the valve face showed local CFL numbers of up to 50 during
the early part of the intake stroke. This indicates that the flow and turbulence levels
in cylinder could be poorly resolved. Additionally it is necessary to maintain numerical
stability. During the combustion a time-step of 1.0 µs was used. Thereby an error of 4
to 8% is accepted compared to the time-step of 0.5µs. Due to the exploratory nature of
the project this error was accepted. During the project many different combustion- and
spark models were used each of which had to be tuned. A large number of simulation
had to be run in the combustion range and thus the reduction in computational cost of
using 1 µs was deemed more important than the increased accuracy. It is important to
note that while a significant discretization error is observed here combustion modelling

it is cooled by the cold intake gas. While not the same fuel, 440K is within the ranges

actual volume in the cylinder as monitored during simulation varies when the valves move
further into the domain displacing the volume in the cylinder causing a difference between
the cylinder volume equation and the simulation. This is to be expected, as the equations
do not account for this. When the intake valve is not open the cylinder volume matches
perfectly. It is thereby concluded that the cylinder volume in the simulation is accurate.
6.1.2
Cylinder Pressure
From Figure 30, it can be seen that the pressure matches well prior to intake valve opening.
Afterwards, the experimental curve suddenly changes slope and the pressure is overpre-
dicted. This is consistent for all available datasets. As the pressure at this time is below
atmospheric, this can not be explained by leaks. Varying the inlet pressure and tempera-
ture, as well as the initial and wall temperatures in the cylinder, it has not been possible to
match this trend. The intake stroke ringgap crevice jet was not large enough to make up
this difference either. After IVC at about 200 degCA, the motored pressure matches the
experimental pressure well, but slightly underpredicts. However, looking at Figure 31, it
can be seen that the simulation catches up to the experiment later, slightly before the de-
sired time of ignition at 350 degCA. This could be because of leaks in the real experiment.
However, the 0D model suggests that piston ring leaks are not significant at this crank an-
gle indicating leaks should be found elsewhere. It could also be that the wall and initial gas
temperature used in the CFD model is not representative of the real cylinder conditions.
Additionally the inlet pressure boundary condition used was from a different experiment
so its possible that it is not similar to the pressure at the inlet in this experiment.

Uncertainty Sources
The following section will provide an overview of the sources of error related to the simpli-

9.1.1
Cylinder Volume
The CFR engine on which the experiments were run is from 1948. It is therefore expected
to be worn. This could increase the bore slightly or otherwise affect the shape of the
crevice regions. The scan obtained from Argonne National Laboratory is based on a 2008
CFR engine and therefore is likely less worn.

Inlet Pressure Boundary Condition
Early in the project it was established that obtaining a pressure curve that fit the experi-

is from a different experiment using ammonia. It is therefore possible that this pressure
is not representative of the pressure at the boundary during the methane experiments to
which this project is compared.

this project. Further, benchmarking the accuracy of the flow in the cylinder is difficult.

the experiment are available:

pressure and temperature in the cylinder affects the laminar flame speed and the flame

9.2.1
Numerical Errors
At IVO the pressure difference over the valve is 0.6 bar initiating a sonic flow that lasts

Premixed combustion is a complicated and sensitive problem that requires many approxi-

show that they are unavoidable but they can be diminished and that their presence do not
dilute the conclusions of a project like this.

9.2. Modelling errors
Spark Modelling
The different spark timings used in this report use different settings for the FI spark model.
The justification is the FI-spark model does not attempt to accurately model the dynamics
of the spark as the ISSIM model does. However, as the spark dynamics depend on the
operating conditions, the FI-model has to be tuned separately. The FI-model is only used
to initialize a flame kernel at a time and size that ensures the combustion will grow as seen
in the experiments, essentially skipping the first steps of the combustion process, missing
the shape of the starting profile. As the starting profile can not be used for benchmarking,
the FI model has some inherent uncertainty, as it may offer too much control, allowing the
user to artificially accelerate the combustion by initializing an excessive flame kernel.
Flame Surface Density
The flame surface density equation greatly simplifies modelling of flames but also adds
several error sources that can adversely impact the simulation accuracy.
First, the corrugation of the flame by turbulence is a well known phenomenon.
The
flame surface density equation assumes the total flame area increases in the presence of
turbulence (see Equation 9).
Assuming the turbulent production term in Equation 9
accurately converts turbulence to flame surface still means the flame surface area is only
as accurate as the turbulence levels predicted in the engine. When using a k−ϵ turbulence
model a series of constants are used which are determined based on empirical data. In some
cases these require tuning as they are not universal for all turbulent flow. Some research
suggests modifying these is necessary for accurately capturing the turbulence in cylinder
[27]. Since no experimental data was available for comparison this was not investigated.
Second, the flame surface density approach simulates flame corrugation using a scalar.
Flame corrugation can occur at many scales and cause many shapes that might interact
with other parts of the flame surface changing local conditions affecting reaction kinetics,
influencing the flame propagation. When using the FSD approach (or any flamelet model)
the conditions ahead of the flame are effectively uniform along the entire flame front. This
can especially be a problem for lean combustion due to its strong response to pressure and
temperature deviations (see subsubsection 9.2.3 & [26]). The accuracy of this approach
Page 93 of 113
==PAGE:102==

9.2. Modelling errors
can be disputed but its a practical option and it has shown good results [6][28][29][30]. It is
therefore not likely to be a large source of error for the flame propagation and heat release
in the engine. It is however a notable short-cut distinguishing the physics simulated from
the real-world physics.
Third, reaction rates are assumed to be captured using a laminar flame speed and thickness.
This can be difficult to estimate, as discussed in section 9.2.3.
Wall Quenching
Wall quenching is a process whereby the preheat layer and inner reaction layer are cooled by
the wall sufficiently that the chemical reactions are no longer occur. In all flamelet models,
including the ECFM, the flame is quenched by the wall via two mechanisms. First, gas
temperature is lowered by the colder walls which lowers the laminar flame speed. This
reduces the fuel consumption through the ω equation see Equation 15. However this will
only slow down reaction and not reduce FSD. FSD destruction near walls is done by the
correction term C in Equation 9. This effectively switches off all FSD production terms
within some distance of any wall, allowing the flame to fizzle out. This is an unrealistic
way to prevent combustion occurring near walls as it is completely independent of the
temperature of the wall.
Complex Chemistry and Turbulent Combustion
The difficulty estimating a laminar flame speed can be overcome by directly simulating the
chemical reactions on the mesh using a chemical reaction mechanism. Thereby it is not
necessary to use a table or a correlation that includes built in errors. It does bring some
issues.
Estimating flame propagation using a chemical mechanism is very difficult, as the mech-
anisms designed for a range of operating conditions and are validated experimentally.
Measuring flame propagation at pressures upwards of 100 bar is experimentally difficult
and thus no mechanism could be found that is validated for the range of operating condi-
tions present in this study. Therefore even ignoring turbulence and assuming the thin inner
reaction layer is resolved, errors will be present. This is illustrated in Wang et. al. [26]

where a large discrepancy between flame speeds are observed for several different chemical
mechanisms.
The thin inner reaction layer needs to be resolved when using a chemical mechanism. This
is not practical for full scale engine simulations. Therefore STAR-CCM+ offers a thickened
flame model. Within a zone it changes the reaction rates such that they comply with those
found a user selected laminar flame speed approximation. It effectively overlays a flamelet
model onto the chemical mechanism. Therefore it should not be expected that the HRR
changes significantly between using a flamelet model and the Complex Chemistry with a
chemical mechanism.
Another issue is that using a chemical mechanism, is it requires resolving the turbulent
effect on the flame front. As described in subsubsection 4.5.1 this requires a mesh size
down to and possibly less than the Kolmogorov length scale which, as of this project,
is computationally unfeasible.
Therefore it is necessary to use a model that based on
empirical data further modifies the reaction rates ahead and behind the flame. In STAR-
CCM+ a turbulent flame speed closure model is available [9].
The modification is a
function on a turbulent flame speed model which in turn are functions of laminar flame
speed approximations. Again this means the accuracy of the AHRR flame propagation
will likely not differ from that found using a flamelet model.
On the other hand using a chemical mechanism does entail simulating more of the physics
as opposed to modelling it. Post-combustion reactions using ECFM-3Z are rather rudi-
mentary and the auto-ignition performed through a TKI table could be seen as an ad-hoc
approach. Using detailed chemical kinetics is more complete and these parts in particular
would be more accurately modelled. During this project it was attempted to use the well
established chemical methane/air mechanism GRI-Mech 3.0 [31] in addition to a turbulent
flame speed closure model, but it proved difficult to get the flame to propagate properly.
It was therefore decided to stick with flamelet models as is the most common for premixed
combustion.
9.2.3
Laminar Flame Speed Approximations
The laminar flame speed (LFS) is a function of fuel type, air/fuel ratio, pressure and
temperature and needs to be accurately approximated. The combustion process with the

FSD approach is highly sensitive to the laminar flame speed as it dictates the immediate
fuel consumption rate as well as the production of flame surface (see Equation 9). As the
flame develops in the cylinder the pressure and unburnt temperature varies significantly.
The laminar flame speed approximation has to accurately report the laminar flame speed
under a wide range of conditions. Especially since the earlier spark timings show much
higher pressure and corresponding unburnt temperature than the later spark timings. In
this project 3 different approximations were used. The Metghalchi correlation presented in
subsubsection 4.5.3 and 2 precomputed laminar flame speed tables. One table was based
on a n-heptane/methane chemical mechanism. This table was added as it had shown good
results for previous projects at the department.
Secondly, a precomputed table based
on the GRI-Mech 3.0 [31] methane mechanism was used. It is made and distributed by
Siemens using the table generator built into STAR-CCM+.
Approximating the laminar flame speed is notoriously difficult under engine conditions due
to the difficulty of running experiment at these pressures and temperatures [26][32][33].
Therefore experiments are usually conducted at lower pressures and temperatures and
then extrapolated. Most correlations and chemical mechanisms have therefore not been
validated for unburnt temperatures above ∼1000 −1100 K and pressures above ∼50 bar,
above which the early spark timings in this project exists. Alternatively the LFS is ap-
proximated by numerical simulation using detailed chemical mechanisms. In Wang et. ak.
[26] 7 different established methane combustion mechanisms were compared under engine
conditions along with the results found from 14 different power-law LFS approximations.
The numerical simulations was performed using CHEMKIN-PREMIX in 1D. AMR was
used to refine the thin inner reaction layer down to 0.17 µm. The 7 different mechanisms
showed significant difference in laminar flame speed. At 30 bar and 846 K the reported
LFS ranged between 28 and 38 cm/s. At 70 bar and 1042.5 K the reported laminar flame
speeds are between 42 and 60 m/s. This indicates that despite the laminar flame speed
being a well established concept simulating it is very difficult even under very favorable
conditions compared to a full size engine. Additionally, the slope of the laminar flame
speed with increasing temperature and pressure were not all in agreement. Therefore even
if tuning one mechanism such that it accurately reports the laminar flame speed at one
spark timing e.g. 350 deg, it will not scale correctly to other spark timings with a much

9.2.4
Combustion modelling errors: Conclusion
Throughout this project, a strong aversion to tuning the combustion and flame speed
models for the different spark timings was upheld. In the version of STARCCM+ used
at the start of the project, there was no simple way to tune the laminar flame speed.
The Metghalchi correlation was therefore chosen to model the laminar flame speed, as it
produced the best results out of the box. Later in the project, it became clear that tuning
is a necessity to match a wider range of engine conditions. There is thus potential accuracy
gains in switching to a different laminar flame speed approximation, and tuning to match
the results. However, one should still be careful when tuning the models with limited
available experimental data for validation. For instance, underestimating the turbulence
levels but changing the turbulence scaling in the combustion model could help predict
more correct heat release rates, even if the flow is not properly modelled. However, with
proper validation, once a model is tuned it should still be useful for parametric studies
of things such as changing geometry, or similar changes that do not change the operating
conditions significantly.

9.3.2
Combustion and Emissions
The heat release rates presented in section 7 show an increasing deviation from the ex-
perimentally reported values. The errors increase with earlier spark timings and corre-
spondingly increasing cylinder pressure and temperature. The same combustion model
and laminar flame speed was used for all cases. The increasing deviation is likely due
to exceeding the valid range for the flame speed correlations without applying additional
corrections by tuning.
The methane emissions reported for the 330 and 350 deg spark timings show a large
difference compared to the experiment. The 350 deg case overpredicts CH4 emissions and
330 deg case underpredicts CH4 emissions. The total heat release for the 350 deg case is
slightly larger than the experiment and the 330 deg case show significantly larger total heat
release compared to the experiment Table 5. The simulation methane mass in cylinder is
4% lower for the 350 deg case. It would therefore be expected to have emissions smaller
than those found in the experiment. For the 330 deg case the mass is 4% larger however
the AHR is 40% larger. It would be expected that the CH4 emissions are smaller than
the ones found in the experiment. However the emissions for the 350 deg spark timing are
found to be 3 times larger and for the 330 deg spark timing the emissions are 4-5 times
lower.
The higher CH4 emissions observed for the 350 deg spark timing in the simulation compared
to the experiment is likely caused by one or more of the following error sources.
1. The methane mass in cylinder pre-combustion could be larger in the simulation
compared to the experiment. This would allow the same amount of energy to be
released from the fuel consuming reaction and still maintain methane concentrations

2. Cylinder wall temperatures are too high. The AHRR measure includes heat release

resulting in higher methane concentration post-combustion. The wall temperature
is set to 440 K. In Pal et. al. [6] wall temperature between 413 and 520 K are used.
The flame temperature is roughly 2300 K. The sensitivity in heat loss behind the
flame between walls at 400 or 550 K is limited. Lowering wall temperatures would
lower cylinder temperature thereby lowering laminar flame speed and ultimately
reaction rate. Therefore increasing the laminar flame speed would likely be needed
to compensate.

the exhaust is ill defined and the heterogeneity of methane in the outlet this close
to the pipe bend is a source of large uncertainty. It is not known exactly where the
methane probe located in the exhaust. This motivates using 6 probes. While the

The sensitivity of methane emissions to errors in the simulation is large compared to the
other emissions. For the 350 deg spark timing 91% of the methane is consumed during the

the experiment with a concentration of 66395 ppm methane, the experiment shows that

metric. This would increase product emissions and AHR by 8% which is well within the
physical modelling error. However it changes methane emissions by about 300%. This
inherently makes estimating unburnt fuel in emissions difficult.

Conclusion
Analysis of the flow field, flame propagation, and emissions of a modified spark ignited
CFR-engine is performed using a computational model for a range of spark timings. The
engine is fuelled using methane at an air/fuel ratio of 1.4 at a compression ratio of 15. A
0D crevice flow model is implemented to account for the jet of unburned methane escaping
from the piston ring end gap, as described by Namazian and Heywood [20]. The analysis
was carried out using the realizable k-ε turbulence model, the FI-spark ignition model, the
ECFM-3Z combustion model, and the Metgalchi-Keck correlation for laminar flame speed.
The analysis shows that the type of inlet valve used has a significant effect on the flow field
after valve closure. A shrouded valve will provide a stable swirling flow, and a plain valve
will provide an unstable tumbling flow that quickly decays after valve closure. The mixing
of the assumed perfectly homogenous intake gas with the pre-intake residual gas in the
chamber reaches close to the same level of homogeneity by the time of ignition, regardless
of the type of valve. Initial LES-results back this up. Using the shrouded valve, trends in
the experimentally observed heat release rate are replicated in the CFD-simulation. The
flame is convected by the swirling flow, and it is concluded that some trends can be directly
mapped to the geometry of the engine. The indentation in the piston and the protruding
intake valve seem particularly important to the flame propagation.
The experimental
data is matched well for the late spark timings where the pressure and temperature is
lower, but does not match the early spark timings well. The Metgalchi-Keck correlation is
unsuited for lean fuels, particularly at very high pressures and temperatures. Other ways
to model the laminar flame speed are likewise unverified for those operating conditions, and
should be tuned to experimental values. As the experimentally observed range of pressures
and temperatures is large, it is concluded that it is necessary to tune the modelling of the
combustion separately for the different spark timings. The cycle averaged burned emissions
are predicted well for the spark timings with accurate heat release rates, but the time
resolved unburned methane emissions are overpredicted. The most likely error source for
this is a combination of underprediction of heat loss and overprediction of cylinder mass
due to uncertain boundary conditions. The time resolved unburned hydrocarbon emissions
match the experiment qualitatively for the 350 degree spark timing, which also matches

FUTURE WORK
11
Future Work
The following list compiles the suggestions for future work based off this project.
1. Verify in cylinder mass of the experiment. The findings in this project suggest that
the mass in cylinder reported for the experiment based on the mass-flow meters is
not accurate. Having correct mass in cylinder is vital to accurate combustion and
emission estimation. Removing this source of uncertainty is deemed very important.
2. Further investigation of combustion models under more controlled conditions. Quan-
tifying the error caused by various combustion models e.g. ECFM-3Z, G-equation,
Complex Chemistry with premixed closure models in a constant volume case could
be very useful for setting them up for engine conditions. Reducing variables will
help fairly evaluate their performance and under which conditions they work well.
Similarly attempting to use different laminar flame speed approximations could also
help better illuminate the error caused by this approximation.
3. It is doubtful that the air/methane mixture is well mixed when it reaches the inlet
boundary from this simulation.
Conducting a simulation including the upsteam
mixing could help determine how well mixed the air/fuel is both during the intake
stroke and during the compression stroke when the spark timings are approached.
4. A focused the investigation of unburnt crevice trapped gas under just one spark
timing. The conditions of the 350 deg spark timing lend themselves best to LFS
approximations which is also represented in the accuracy of the HRR for this spark
timing. Conducting a study of ways to reduce crevice trapped gas based only on this
spark timing could reduce time spent on flame propagation for other cases. Ideas
such as chamfering the top of the piston is a well-known method.
Investigating
whether the CFD model presented in this project can capture this could go a long
way to proving or disproving the predictive power of the model.
5. Investigate the use of LES to better capture the flow and consequently the combus-
tion. During this project it was attempted to use LES in the hopes it would better
capture the turbulence and combustion. Also the diffusion of the λ = 1.4 gas ejected
into the cylinder from the ring gap post-combustion would be better captured.

A.1
ISSIM
The Imposed Stretch Spark Ignition Model models a simplified inductive ignition circuit
to estimate the time-resolved energy and FSD release during the spark glow phase. The
breakdown phase energy release is neglected due to its short duration. The ISSIM model
estimates
1. Whether a spark is succesfully formed.
2. A delay from spark circuit initiation until breakdown, arc and glow phase.
3. The size and intensity of FSD deposited on the computational domain.
4. Energy in the electrical circuit dictating the spark life-time.
The ISSIM model was originally made to work with the ECFM combustion model using the
FSD equation as its main driver. Complex Chemistry does not use a FSD equation and thus
this step is omitted when using it with ISSIM. This section will detail the ISSIM model
first in general and then finally describe the differences when using ECFM or Complex
Chemistry. The Electrical Circuit is modelled as a inductive ignition system an example
is shown in Figure A.1.

A.2. Lancaster Experiments
A.2
Lancaster Experiments
The CFR engine used in the Lancaster experiments uses an IVO timing of ∼10 deg BTDC
[24]. The CFR engine experiments at DTU use an IVO timing of ∼26 deg ATDC. This is
significant, as the CFR engine at DTU builds a large pressure difference across the valve
during the intake stroke before the valve opens, leading to sonic flows at IVO which is not
present in the Lancaster experiments. Additionally, the engine head geometry described
by Lancaster does not match the DTU CFR engine. The cylinder, stroke, and bore is the
same, but the position of the spark plug and pressure tap, as well as the geometry of the
piston crown, is not. It is unknown whether other differences are present. The DTU CFR
engine experiment used for benchmarking in this report runs at 1100 RPM, whereas the
Lancaster CFR engine is run at 1000, 1500, and 2000 RPM. Velocity and turbulence data
was gathered in a 2mm radius sphere located at the same position as the hotwire used in
the Lancaster experiments. Using the realizable k-ε turbulence model, the velocity for the
shrouded and plain valve can be seen on Figure A.3 and Figure A.4 respectively.
The turbulent fluctuations reported by Lancaster are computed from 45 degree crank angle
mean values for single cycles, meaning large bulk flow quantities are included as if they
were turbulent fluctuations. Performing a similar signal processing on the CFD data and
adding contribution from the turbulent kinetic energy or subgrid turbulent kinetic energy,
the results using LES and the realizable k-ε can be seen on Figure A.5. The turbulence
data is only available for the 1500 RPM Lancaster experiment. The turbulence level is
positively correlated with RPM, so if not for the different valve timing, the CFD data is
expected to underpredict the experiment. However, the large velocity gradients at IVO
in the DTU CFR engine makes comparing turbulence difficult between the two cases.
Results without including the modelled turbulence can be seen on Figure A.6. In general,
the trends match the experiment, but the magnitude is off.
