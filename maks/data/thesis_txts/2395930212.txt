Abstract
This project studies Edison-type nickel-iron and nickel-zinc batteries with the aim of optimising the
battery concept for use in large-scale energy storage applications. This will be done by developing and
analysing battery cells in order to overcome some of the challenges, the technology faces.
The Edison battery itself dates back to the early 20th century, but it never gained signiﬁcant traction.
The concept is however relevant today, as the increasing production of renewable energy calls for cheap
and reliable storage technologies capable of storing excess energy produced and delivering this energy
when solar and wind energy production is minimal. Since the iron or zinc electrodes used in Edison
type batteries are much cheaper than more widely used electrode materials, and even the old Edison
batteries were highly durable and long-lived, the battery concept has potential for large-scale energy
storage applications.
The Edison battery concept is based on a nickel oxy-hydroxide/nickel hydroxide positive electrode and
either iron or zinc as the negative electrode in an alkaline electrolyte. The Ni-Fe battery has a cell
voltage of 1.37 V, while the Ni-Zn battery has a cell voltage of 1.73 V. The relatively high voltages give
the batteries high energy densities, but since they are based on aqueous electrolytes, the high voltages
also lead to corrosion and self-discharge of the cells. The technologies face several other problems;
the Ni-Fe batteries have poor high-power performance due to passivation of the iron electrodes, while
the Ni-Zn batteries deteriorate due to dendritic growth of zinc when the battery is charged. This
project reviews the current studies related to solving these problems, as well as the thermodynamic
and electrochemical theories necessary to understand their origins.
Experimentally a number of nickel-zinc battery cells are manufactured and tested, and it is found that
it is impossible to simply charge a pure nickel electrode due to the existence of native oxides. Instead
the active nickel hydroxide must be provided, either as a precipitated coating on the electrode, or as
a powder in a pocket-plate electrode. The manufactured cells were all prone to gas evolution during
charge, which reduces the eﬃciencies of the cells to around 75-80 %. The cells however had very low
energy densities due to low amount of active Ni(OH)2 relative to the total mass of the cells.
It is found that the Zincaslot bright zinc-plating additives from the electroplating industry can be used
to inhibit the dendritic growth of zinc, and possibly also minimise corrosion of the electrolytes. This
discovery may enable the production of cheap and long-lived nickel-zinc batteries, however it has also
been observed that the eﬀect of the additives vary with the charge-discharge cycles. Further analysis
is thus needed in order to ensure stable performance throughout 5000+ cycles.
While working nickel-zinc battery cells are developed in the project and possible solutions have been
proposed, there is still signiﬁcant work to be done before actual batteries can be produced in practise.
Furthermore the competition on the market is increasing, as other technologies such as lithium-ion
and redox-ﬂow batteries for large-scale storage are also undergoing extensive research at the moment.
It is however believed that the Edison battery concept has potential to oﬀer a cheap and long-lived
solution once the proposed solutions in this project have been validated, and eﬃcient battery cells with
reasonable energy densities have been demonstrated.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Electrical energy can be stored chemically in batteries. This has been known for more than 200 years,
and batteries are widely used in many aspects of the modern world today. Batteries come in a range
of diﬀerent sizes and shapes, from microscopic to the size of a football ﬁeld and almost anything in
between. Many diﬀerent types of batteries are available, and each type comes with speciﬁc advantages
and disadvantages. This project focuses on the “Edison battery”, which usually denotes an alkaline
battery using nickel and iron electrodes - here however it will also include alkaline batteries using nickel
and zinc electrodes, since the two systems are very similar.
The simplest way to characterise batteries is to distinguish between primary and secondary batte-
ries. Primary batteries can be used once, while secondary batteries are rechargeable. Most portable
consumer batteries are primary batteries, common examples include zinc-manganese dioxide batteries
(e.g. Duracell Plus Power batteries) or lithium button cells. Common examples of secondary batteries
include the lead-acid battery of a car, or the lithium-ion battery of many smartphones and laptops.
The Edison battery concept is a type of secondary batteries.
A simple battery cell consists of a positive and a negative electrode submerged in an electrolyte and
possibly separated by a membrane allowing ion transfer. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1, where a
zinc and a copper electrode are submerged in aqueous solutions of their respective sulphate salts.
Electricity is produced by redox reactions on the electrodes; the zinc electrode is oxidised and zinc
ions are dissolved in the electrolyte on one side, and on the other side the copper ions are reduced
and deposited on the copper electrode. This causes electrons to ﬂow in the electric circuit from the
zinc electrode to the copper electrode, driving an electric current. In a secondary battery, the redox
reactions are reversible, which is not the case for primary batteries. A more thorough introduction to
the electrochemistry of the battery will be discussed in chapter 2.

Introduction
1.1. History of the Edison battery
There are many diﬀerent types of battery cells, consisting of diﬀerent electrode materials and electro-
lytes. Of the secondary batteries, the most common types include lead-acid, which is cheap, rugged
and safe, lithium-ion (Li-ion), which has a high energy and power density, vanadium redox-ﬂow, which
oﬀers relatively cheap and eﬃcient large-scale energy storage, molten sodium-sulphur (Na-S), which
has a promising high energy density and an interesting setup consisting of liquid electrodes and a solid
electrolyte, as well as several nickel-based types [2]. The nickel-based cells are all similar given that
they utilise the same reaction on the positive nickel electrode and an alkaline electrolyte, and they
can all be considered relatives of the “Edison battery” concept. The ﬁrst nickel-based battery was the
nickel-cadmium (Ni-Cd), which in the 1990s was gradually replaced by nickel-metal hydride batteries
(Ni-MH) due to the toxicity of cadmium. Ni-MH batteries show good performance, and they are for
example used to power electric or hybrid vehicles such as the Toyota Prius. However Ni-MH battery
electrodes are usually made from signiﬁcant amounts of rare-earth metals, and the Prius has thus been
described as “the biggest user of rare earths of any object in the world” [3]. The expensive rare-earth
electrodes can be replaced by cheap and abundant metals such as iron or zinc in order to create bat-
teries that are both cheap, simple, lightweight, eﬃcient and durable. Thomas Edison was also aiming
for these characteristics in the beginning of the 20th century, but the Ni-Fe and Ni-Zn batteries have
had their share of problems along the way, some of which this project can hopefully help address.
1.1
History of the Edison battery
The ﬁrst rechargeable battery was the lead-acid battery invented by Gaston Planté in France in 1859 [4].
Many diﬀerent scientists improved the battery during the late 19th century, and the battery was used
to power nearly all electric vehicles built from 1890-1910 [2]. In the 1880s the ﬁrst secondary alkaline
batteries using nickel oxide electrodes were discussed by Desmazures in France [5], and Dun and
Hasslacher in Germany [6]. In 1890 Jungner from Sweden and Michalowski from Germany described
methods for preparing nickel oxide to be used as electrodes in these batteries [7], and in 1899 Jungner
invented the ﬁrst nickel-cadmium battery [8]. Jungner also worked on a nickel-iron battery, but due
to decreased eﬃciency and increased gassing, he focused on the Ni-Cd design [9]. Meanwhile in the
United States, Thomas Edison was also working on a similar battery design, and both Edison and
Jungner wrote a number of patents regarding the technology in 1901 [10,11], which resulted in several
patent suits over both nickel-cadmium and nickel-iron batteries [7].
Edison started selling his nickel-iron battery in 1903, aiming at the automotive industry [12]. When he
started his research in the 1890s, most vehicles were powered by steam or electricity, since the gasoline-
powered car was still too unreliable (Karl Benz built the ﬁrst car, the Benz Patent-Motorwagen in
1886 [13]). The electric cars were mainly powered by lead-acid batteries, but Edison realised that
these batteries had two major issues: they were very heavy, and the acid electrolyte caused the lead
electrodes to corrode, which shortened the life of the battery. He thus began testing a range of metals in
non-acidic electrolytes in order to ﬁnd the ideal combination of lightweight, stable electrodes [12,14].
The result was the nickel-iron battery, sold as the Edison Alkaline Storage Battery, which Edison
claimed to be far superior to the lead-acid batteries [9, 12]. The cathodes consisted of black nickel
hydroxide powder mixed with graphite to increase the conductivity, but it was found that the graphite
oxidised and caused swelling, which caused leaks and loss of capacity, and engineers found that the
batteries did not actually outperform the lead-acid batteries despite being lighter [7,12]. Edison tried
to solve this problem by replacing the graphite with nickel ﬂakes in 1908 and was ready to produce
the improved battery in 1910. However a year earlier Henry Ford introduced his Ford Model T car,
which was the ﬁrst aﬀordable, mass-produced car, and this made the gasoline internal combustion
engine the standard for the automobile.
Edison was thus too late with his batteries to aﬀect the
automotive industry apart from special commercial vehicles such as small trucks or cars used in cities
for deliveries [12].
Despite the failure to capture the automotive market, Edison still found use of the batteries which were
produced at the Edison Storage Battery Company in New Jersey from 1903-1972 [9]. The batteries were
mainly used as backup power for rail-road crossing signals or to power lamps in mines [12], and they

1.2
Applications
There are many proposed applications for Ni-Fe and Ni-Zn batteries. As mentioned, Edison originally
wanted to use his Ni-Fe battery to power electric cars, but he was superseded by the combustion engine
in the Ford Model T. In the 20th century, mainly the Ni-Fe battery has been used as a durable and
reliable power source in mines (e.g. mining vehicles and miners’ lamps), in railway cars and signal
systems as well as emergency lighting [32]. Recently the company Iron Edison have started creating
nickel-iron battery systems for oﬀ-grid and backup energy sources for personal use - these batteries are
shown in ﬁgure 1.5(a) [20].
Like nickel-iron batteries, modern nickel-zinc batteries are very abuse tolerant and can deliver high
current densities compared to equivalent Ni-Cd, Ni-MH and Li-ion technologies. This makes Ni-Zn
batteries suitable power tools such as drills and circular saws as well as garden and lawn tools such
as hedge trimmers and electric lawn mowers [21]. Nickel-zinc batteries have also been sold as AA
consumer batteries as an alternative to the primary batteries that make up more than 90 % of the
market, as seen in ﬁgure 1.4(a). Recently attempts have been made to create nickel-zinc batteries for
light electric and hybrid electric vehicles. Nickel-zinc batteries are smaller, lighter and cheaper than a
corresponding Ni-MH battery - this is seen in ﬁgure 1.5(b) where the standard Toyota Prius Ni-MH is
seen below an equivalent Ni-Zn battery from PowerGenix.
ZincFive, who have acquired PowerGenix, continue to make batteries for small and hybrid electric
vehicles as well as providing anti-idling power for heavy-duty trucks (e.g. to avoid the truck engine
running at idle to power a refrigerated trailer) [29]. They also produce uninterruptible power supplies
(UPS) for data centres and traﬃc signals.

batteries with a reasonable energy density. One of the main issues with the batteries is a high rate
of self-discharge as well as hydrogen evolution upon charge, discharge and on stand. This is due to
a low hydrogen overpotential on the iron electrode, and the hydrogen evolution (gassing) means that

high-rate capabilities of the batteries signiﬁcantly, they are also more costly to manufacture, and this

1.3.2
Nickel-zinc batteries
While nickel-zinc batteries potentially oﬀer a high cell voltage, a high energy density and low cost,
there are some problems with the technology. One of the biggest issues has been the short cycle life of
the batteries, which is due to a number of factors. As the battery is discharged, zinc is dissolved in the
electrolyte. During charging, this zinc is re-plated onto the zinc electrode, however this may result in
dendrite growth as well as “shape change” of the electrode. Dendrites are formed as zinc preferentially
plates onto electrode high points rather than uniformly on the electrode surface. As the dendrites
grow during cycling, they may reach the nickel electrode and short-circuit the battery, rendering it
useless. Another problem arises when the zinc is deposited away from its original location, causing the
electrode to change its shape, and ﬁnally the uneven deposition may destroy any initial high surface
area of the electrode, compromising the high-rate capabilities of the battery. Other problems include
passivation of the zinc electrode during discharge, which lowers the capacity. This occurs when the
solubility of zinc ions is reached, and zinc oxides or hydroxides precipitate onto the zinc surface. Finally
the low redox potential of zinc in the highly alkaline electrolyte may lead to hydrogen production and
subsequent corrosion of the zinc electrode [21]. Many diﬀerent solutions to these problems have been
proposed, including advanced charging mechanisms, electrolyte additives, tough separator membranes,
and doping of the electrodes with various materials.
In 1982 Himy and Wagner patented a way of charging the nickel-zinc battery by using pulsed charges
in order to minimise shape change [52]. The eﬀect has been studied by Woumfo and Vittori to ﬁnd
optimum pulse and wait times [53] and in 2004 Phillips patented a charger which included both a
charge and a shorter discharge pulse [54]. The charge pulses can have higher current densities than a
constant charge since the wait times allow ions to diﬀuse towards the electrode, so problems with the
concentration overpotential can be avoided (will be elaborated in section 2.2.2). The larger current
density creates more nucleation sites on the zinc electrode and thus causes a more uniform deposition
and minimises dendrite growth and shape change.
Much attention have been given to the electrolyte composition, which is based on an alkaline solution
(usually potassium hydroxide) with a smaller amount of a weak acid (usually boric acid). One of the
ﬁrst breakthroughs came with Eisenberg’s electrolyte formulation from 1993 [23]. He realised that
there needed to be a much larger excess of hydroxide in the solution than previously used, as well as
a lower concentration of alkali ﬂuorides. He did not understand the reason why, but he observed that
it had signiﬁcant beneﬁcial eﬀects on the cycle life of the batteries, and his formulation has been the
basis of many subsequent patents. In 2010 Philips and Mohanta claimed a patent regarding a number
of additives in the electrolyte [55]. They found that using phosphoric acid instead of boric acid had
beneﬁcial eﬀects which could be due to zinc phosphates being less soluble and thus causing less shape
change. They also included ﬂuoride to “improve performance”, but it is not clear why. Silicates are
added to reduce the solubility of zinc, and diﬀerent additives such as tetrabutylammonium hydroxide
or indium sulphate are used to reduce zinc corrosion. In 2015 Kim and Shin showed that SnO in the
electrolyte could also suppress zinc dendrite growth [56].
Various organic additives have also been researched. The non-toxic and cheap polyethylene glycol
(PEG) has been studied and proven to reduce the corrosion of zinc by Ein-Eli et al. in 2003 [57], and
in 2006 Cohen-Hyams et al. showed that PEG DiAcid 600 was even more eﬀective [58], while Zhou et
al. could prove that the addition of imidazole (IMZ) and PEG-600 had a synergistic eﬀect at inhibiting
the corrosion. In 2013 Banik and Akolkar showed that addition of PEG molecules to the electrolyte
could also suppress dendrite formation during Zn electrodeposition [59] - this was however not aimed

1.3.3
Related technologies
Zinc, iron and nickel are widely used materials in other types of batteries as well. As mentioned, nickel is
commonly used as the positive electrode in both nickel-cadmium, nickel-metal hydride and even nickel-
hydrogen batteries which utilise pressurised hydrogen. Ni-Cd batteries are gradually being faded out
due to the toxicity of the cadmium, while Ni-MH batteries are used in both consumer electronics and
electric vehicles, and Ni-H2 are especially used in satellites [71]. These technologies all utilise the same
reaction mechanisms of the nickel electrode as the proposed Ni-Fe and Ni-Zn technologies.
Iron is not as widely used as nickel, and the main application has been Edison’s battery from the
beginning of the 20th century with several modiﬁcations along the way.
Recently however a new
iron-based battery technology has been proposed. While the Edison batteries have been based on
the oxidation of iron to state II or state III, the new “super iron” batteries involve a highly oxidised
iron(IV) positive electrode. Iron(IV) is known to be very unstable, but in 1999 Licht et al. managed
to produce a stabilised chemistry, using metal hydride negative electrodes and and alkaline electrolyte
to achieve high capacity batteries [72]. These still have some issues with reversibility however.
Zinc is one of the most commonly used battery materials, as it is the negative electrode material in
conventional primary alkaline batteries. These use MnO2 as the positive electrode. However recently
scientists have discovered a way to make the discharge reactions reversible and thus creating a secondary
Zn-MnO2 battery cell. The speciﬁc chemistry is somewhat disputed as some claim the battery acts
like a lithium-ion cell, where the zinc ions are intercalated into the manganese dioxide [73], while
others claim that a chemical conversion occurs similarly to lead-acid batteries [74]. Regardless of the
chemistry, a secondary “alkaline” battery will potentially be a cheap alternative with a high energy
density.
Zinc is also used in two entirely diﬀerent types of batteries, both the zinc-bromine ﬂow battery and
the zinc-air battery. In the zinc-bromine ﬂow battery, zinc bromide salt is dissolved in an electrolyte,
and reduction and oxidation occurs on inert electrodes [75]. In the zinc-air battery, oxygen from the
air is used to oxidise zinc, which gives the cell a high theoretical energy density [76].
Zinc is a desirable battery material due to its very low reduction potential (it is very “un-noble”) and
low price, and this is also why it is interesting as electrode material in the Edison type batteries.

Electrochemistry of the batteries
Since batteries work by converting chemically stored energy into electricity, this chapter will be devoted
to discussing the electrochemical reactions taking place. This will involve the thermodynamic laws and
principles governing both the battery reactions, but also other processes such as corrosion. The battery
cell will be reviewed, including the properties of the electrolyte and the electrode materials, and the
speciﬁc electrochemical problems related to nickel-iron and nickel-zinc batteries will be discussed.
2.1
Thermodynamics
Batteries consist of electrodes immersed in (typically liquid) electrolytes, and the reactions that are
used to store energy are governed by thermodynamics. The positive electrode will undergo reduction
during discharge, and the negative electrode will undergo oxidation (and vice versa during charge),
and this is what drives the electric current. When a metal is immersed in an ion-containing aqueous
solution, it will establish an equilibrium with its ions, and the metal will be slightly charged. For metal
“A”, which can be oxidised to oxidation state z, the following equilibrium will be established:
A ←−→Az+ + ze−
The equilibrium depends on the metal. Some metals such as zinc are easily oxidised, and the equi-
librium lies on the far right of the equation.
Other metals such gold are not easily oxidised, and
the equilibrium is further on the left side of the equation (gold is considered a noble metal). This is
illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1. It is seen that the oxidation or reduction reactions occurring on the metals
will cause the metals to be charged. Since zinc is easily oxidised, it will be negatively charged, while
the reduction and deposition of gold ions will cause the gold to be positively charged.

2.1.2
Corrosion of metals
Corrosion is closely related to the operation of batteries, since the same thermodynamics that govern
the battery cell also govern corrosion of metals. Corrosion involves a pair of redox reactions and a
transfer of electric charge. Typically one of these reactions is the reduction of oxygen in water, while
the other is the oxidation of a metal. The most well-known type of corrosion is the red rusting of iron
in contact with water (and oxygen), which occurs due to the oxidation of iron to Fe(III) along with
reduction of oxygen, and the red/brown FeOOH is then formed [81]. The corrosive properties of a metal
are typically evaluated with respect to their standard reduction potentials, and the nobler the metal
(higher reduction potential), the less prone the metal will be to corrosion. The Pourbaix diagrams are
also important in evaluating the corrosion properties, since these reveal the thermodynamically stable
species at a given pH and potential. For example zinc has a very low standard reduction potential,
but it is often used for corrosion protection of steel (galvanisation) since the corrosion product of zinc
in atmospheric air is the natural conversion coating Zn5(OH)6(CO3)2, which passivates the surface
and prevents further corrosion [83]. This would be evident from a Pourbaix diagram of zinc in water
including carbon. Carbon is not included in the diagram in ﬁgure 2.4(a), but here it is seen however
that at very high pH, the stable species is the ion ZnO2

Another approach is the use of corrosion inhibitors. These usually work by manipulating either the
cathodic or anodic corrosion reactions. It has been found that corrosion of zinc in alkaline solutions
can be minimised by using small amounts of sodium sulphide as well as a range of organic inhibitors
including 4-biphenylcarbonitrile, 1-chloro-3-ﬂuorobenzene and triphenyl-chloro-methane [87]. Sodium
sulphide is known to work as an oxygen scavenger, reducing the amount of oxygen available in the
solution and thus decreases the cathodic reduction of oxygen [81]. Reducing the cathodic reaction will
reduce the “consumption” of electrons, which means that the anodic oxidation of zinc will be reduced
as well.
Another inhibition mechanism is to increase the overpotential required to reduce hydrogen. Although
the reduction is thermodynamically possible below a potential of −0.059×pH, a certain overpotential
is required to actually start the reaction (this will be elaborated in section 2.2.2). For some metals
such as platinum, the overpotential is very low, but for other such as zinc it may be substantial.
Without this high hydrogen overpotential zinc would not be a useful electrode material as the very
low reduction potential would just result in hydrogen evolution. It is possible to increase the hydrogen
overpotential by doping with elements such as indium or bismuth (as well as lead and mercury), either
by doping the electrode itself or by dissolving the elements (or oxides/hydroxides of said elements) in
the electrolyte. Increasing the hydrogen overpotential will minimise the cathodic hydrogen evolution
reaction and thus minimise the overall corrosion [64,65]. In the patent literature, bismuth is typically
used in Ni-Fe batteries, while indium is preferred in Ni-Zn batteries [51,55].
It is important to be aware of the operating mechanisms of the corrosion inhibitors in batteries, as some
inhibitors work by adsorbing to the surface in order to block a certain reaction. This is very unwanted
for battery electrodes, since this will also likely block the charge/discharge reactions occurring on the
surfaces. These inhibitors are known as “interface inhibitors” and should be avoided or kept to a
minimum in order to maintain a high performance of the battery cell.
The nickel electrode may also corrode due to the high potential of the NiOOH, which will cause the

2.2
The battery cell
By combining a material with a high reduction potential, such as lead oxide (PbO2), with a material
with a low reduction potential, such as lead (Pb), it is possible to create a battery cell with a potential
diﬀerence equal to the diﬀerence in reduction potentials. Under standard conditions this PbO2-Pb
battery would thus have a voltage of 1.82 V - commercial lead-acid batteries which utilise said electrodes
do not operate under standard conditions however, and the cell voltage is typically around 2.1 V [4].
The battery cell is also referred to as a galvanic cell or voltaic cell, named after Luigi Galvani and
Alessandro Volta who experimented with the early versions of batteries in the late 18th century [88].
It is preferable to have a reasonably high cell voltage, since this parameter is related to the energy
and power capabilities of the battery. It is seen from table 2.1 that both iron and zinc are quite easily
oxidised with reduction potentials of -0.44 V and -0.76 V respectively, while NiOOH has a reasonably
high reduction potential of 0.49 V, making it possible to construct Ni-Fe or Ni-Zn cells with decent
cell voltages. It is also seen that lithium has a very low reduction potential of -3.01 V, which is why
it is widely used in e.g. lithium button cells and Li-ion rechargeable batteries.

2.2.1
The electrolyte
The electrolyte is a material with mobile ions to carry electrical current, and it can be a liquid, gel,
or a solid polymer or ceramic [89], but in this project only liquid electrolytes will be considered.
The simplest electrolytes to fabricate are aqueous liquid types, these are used for example in lead-acid
batteries, where sulphuric acid H2SO4 is used, and in Edison type batteries where typically the alkaline
potassium hydroxide KOH is used.
In order to carry an electrical current, the electrolyte must have a good conductivity. Pure water
cannot be used as an electrolyte as it has a poor conductivity [83], and salts are thus dissolved in water
due to the dissociation into positive and negative ions (e.g. K+ and OH–) capable of carrying electrical
charges. The conductivity σ is the reciprocal of the resistivity, and it is measured in units of S/m or
S/cm. The conductivity of electrolytes is much lower than in metals (≈6 orders of magnitude). For
aqueous electrolytes, the conductivities are usually in the range of 0.10-0.55 S/cm [90].
The conductivity depends on both known factors such as the salt concentration, the size and charge of
the ions and the viscosity of the solution, but other more complicated factors such as the hydration of
the metal ion, the interaction between diﬀerent ions and the dissociation of the solution must also be
taken into consideration [83]. First it is important to consider that ions in aqueous solutions will be
hydrated, i.e. associated with a number of water molecules. This occurs because of the strong dipole
moment of the water molecule which is attracted to the charge of the ion. This is illustrated in ﬁgure

2.2. The battery cell
Ohmic polarisation
The ﬁnal polarisation, which negatively aﬀects the battery performance, is called the Ohmic polari-
sation. This is related to the internal impedance of the battery cell, including the resistance of the
current collectors, electrodes, electrolyte etc. This impedance or resistance causes a voltage drop ac-
cording to Ohm’s law; ∆V = IR. This voltage drop can be described as the Ohmic overpotential
ηr, and it will decrease the cell voltage linearly with the current. It is thus important to have a low
internal impedance, which can be obtained by using highly conductive current collectors (such as Cu)
and minimising the thickness of the active electrode material.
The eﬀect of the diﬀerent types of polarisation on the cell voltage is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.11. Since all
overpotentials are dependent on the current density, the realisable cell voltage decreases as the current
is increased. This causes a fundamental problem for batteries regarding energy versus power, since the
energy is proportional to the cell voltage, while the power is proportional to both the current and the
voltage. Increasing the power thus results in a loss of energy (and vice versa). This will be elaborated
further in section 3.
Figure 2.11: The eﬀect of diﬀerent overpotentials on the cell voltage at increasing current. From [90].
Crystallisation polarisation
The crystallisation polarisation is related to the activation energy required for ions to deposit and
crystallise on a metal electrode surface.
This is due to the crystal growth mechanisms; the most
energetically favourable deposition/nucleation site is at steps in the electrode crystal lattice. Generally
however, atoms are deposited at random locations on the surface, and they need a certain mobility
to be able to diﬀuse to the lattice steps, otherwise they will nucleate and form new islands on the
surface. At the equilibrium potential the lattice positions and the adsorbed atoms will be in thermal
equilibrium, but during deposition of metals (such as during charging of the Zn electrode), there will
be a surplus of adsorbed atoms relative to the equilibrium state, and this induces the crystallisation
overpotential ηk [83]. It normally very small (≈10 mV), and furthermore it is not generally considered
for batteries, since dissolving electrodes are usually avoided - it will however be brieﬂy treated here,
since the Ni-Zn battery is known to have problems with the zinc electrode. During discharge zinc is
dissolved in the alkaline electrolyte and then redeposited during charge. The crystallisation process is
very important, since there have been many problems with zinc growing in dendrites [21]. R. Winand
has studied the relationship between the crystal growth method and the deposition parameters and
has introduced the Winand diagram as shown in ﬁgure 2.12. This diagram relates the mass transfer
(measured as the current density relative to either the diﬀusion limiting current or the concentration of
metal ions to be discharged) and the intensity of inhibiting species to the crystal structure. The crystal
structure is classiﬁed in four groups: Field orientated crystal growth (FI, e.g. whiskers and dendrites),

Hydrogen and oxygen overpotentials
The overpotentials for hydrogen and oxygen production are important for aqueous electrolytes. They
are actually activation overpotentials, but they will be treated separately because the eﬀect is opposite
that of the activation overpotential of the cell-related reactive species. While it is beneﬁcial to have a
very low activation overpotential for the charge transfer of the “active” reactions, for example in the
Ni-Zn battery:

it is beneﬁcial to have a very high activation overpotential for the “inactive” or parasitic reactions,
which are mainly the production of hydrogen and oxygen:
2 H2O −−→O2 + 4 H+ + 4 e−

One potential issue with the nickel hydroxide electrode is the poor conductivity of the active layer.
Pure α-Ni(OH)2 has been found to have a conductivity ranging from 10−13 to 10−17 S/cm, and it is
thus an electrical insulator. The conductivity of β-Ni(OH)2 is higher but it is also an insulator. It
has been found that the absorption/intercalation of water can increase the conductivity to as much as
10−4 S/cm, and the oxidation to NiOOH also results in a 50x increase in conductivity [96]. But it is
evident however that highly conductive current collectors must be utilised in order to be able to reduce
the resistance of the battery, and only thin layers of Ni(OH)2 can be used. It is also possible to dope
the electrode with e.g. cobalt to increase the proton conductivity [96], increase the oxygen evolution
overpotential [37] and improve the charge acceptance and thus reversibility of the electrode [32].
The synthesis of Ni(OH)2 can be achieved by chemical or electrochemical precipitation, sol-gel synthesis
or electrochemical oxidation of nickel. The electrode can then be manufactured in numerous ways.
One of the cheapest methods is to produce the electrode by roll-bonding, where the nickel hydroxide is
mixed with graphite for increased conductivity, a PTFE binder and a benign mineral oil as a solvent,
and then formed into a sheet and roll-bonded [97]. Other electrode types include; sintered electrodes,
where the Ni(OH)2 powder is compacted under high temperatures onto a substrate of either nickel
or nickel-plated mild steel netting, perforated nickel (or plated) foil or a nickel-ﬁbre mat, tubular
or pocket electrodes, where Ni(OH)2 powder is blended with cobalt, graphite and/or nickel ﬂakes
to increase conductivity and then placed in perforated steel tubes or pockets, and nickel composite
electrodes, nickel-coated graphite ﬁbres or nickel ﬁbre is sintered to a compact mat and coated with
Ni(OH)2 [37].
Since the nickel electrodes are similar for both Ni-Fe, Ni-Zn, Ni-Cd and Ni-MH battery types, the
main focus of this project will be on the iron and zinc electrodes and less on the manufacturing of the
nickel electrode, as this technology is more mature.

2.3.4
Electrolyte and membranes
The electrolyte for both types of batteries consist of high levels of “free alkalinity” in the form of
hydroxides in order to both achieve a high conductivity and to ensure thermodynamic stability of the
electrodes as per the Pourbaix diagrams. This is mainly achieved by KOH, but other alkali hydroxides
such as NaOH or LiOH (or a combination) are also used. A buﬀering agent is used to maintain the pH
during charging and discharging as these reactions consume OH–. Diﬀerent buﬀering agents can be
used, but they will diﬀer from Ni-Fe and Ni-Zn batteries. Ni-Fe batteries may utilise carbonate such as
K2CO3 [99], while other patents do not use any buﬀer [100]. Ni-Zn batteries typically use borate (e.g.
boric acid H3BO3) or phosphate (e.g. phosphoric acid H3PO4 or trisodium phosphate Na3PO4) [55].

2.4. Electrochemical plating
Various additives are used in the electrolytes. For the Ni-Fe system, the main additive is sulphur in
the form of e.g. K2S or Na2S, which will reduce the passivation of the iron electrode and thus increase
the active electrode area [46,100]. For the Ni-Zn system, a range of other additives are needed since
the zinc electrode has problems with both corrosion and dendrite formation. The dendrite formation
can be avoided by using organic additives such as PEG or PEI as described in section 1.3.2, but this
project will utilise organic additives known from the zinc-plating industry, which has made it possible
to produce bright zinc coatings. Other additives include ﬂuoride, which has been shown to increase
the performance of the cell [23] and indium salts such as In2(SO4)3 to reduce the corrosion of zinc.
The membrane/separator is not a critical component for this project. For some Ni-Zn battery concepts,
the separator is used to stop zinc dendrites from reaching the nickel electrode and short-circuiting the
cell as described in section 1.3.2, but since this project aims at eliminating the dendritic growth, the
only purpose of a membrane or separator will be to physically separate the nickel and zinc electrodes
to avoid contact during handling of the batteries. Typically polyoleﬁns such as polyethylene or po-
lytetraﬂuorethylene (PTFE) are used, but other materials may be considered [68, 100]. However the
experiments in this projects will not feature a separator or membrane, since the simple test set-up
makes it possible to avoid contact between the electrodes.
2.4
Electrochemical plating
The manufacturing methods used in this project will involve electrochemical plating (electroplating)
of zinc and nickel. This method is used because it is possible to achieve very thin layers of electrode
material, which is important since only the surfaces of the electrodes are active in the electrochemical
reactions of the battery cell. It is thus useful to minimise the amount of “bulk material” to keep both
weight, volume and cost to a minimum.
Electroplating is very similar to the operation of a battery. The plating process takes place in an
electrolyte containing ionic species of the material to be plated (typically cations). An electric circuit
is established, with the sample to be plated acting as a cathode (in case of plating of cations) and with
anodes facing the sides to be plated in order to establish an electric ﬁeld through the electrolyte. This
ﬁeld will cause the cations to be electrically attracted to the sample, and they will subsequently be
reduced on the sample and form a coating of pure metal.
Electroplating is governed by Faraday’s Laws.

2.5. Challenges in this project
Acid zinc electrolytes
The acid zinc-plating electrolyte contains around 30-55 g/L zinc in the form of ZnCl2 or ZnSO4,
conducting salts such as KCl, NH4Cl or AlCl3 as well as acetate boric acid as a buﬀer [83]. Furthermore
organic additives are used to achieve better coatings; polyoxyalkylated naphthol is used to control the
material distribution while aldehydes and aromatic ketones can be used as levelling agents to produce
bright coatings.
One of the main advantages of the acid zinc electrolyte is the high current eﬃciency, which is close to 100
% even at high current densities (6 A/dm2). Alkaline electrolytes have signiﬁcantly lower eﬃciencies,
which means that the acid electrolyte achieves the highest plating rate of around 1.5 µm/min.
Alkaline, cyanide free electrolytes
Alkaline, cyanide free electrolytes have a lower current eﬃciency than acidic electrolytes. This is due
to the activation overpotential for the hydrogen evolution reaction, which appears to decrease with
increasing pH. In acidic baths it is thus possible to deposit zinc without producing hydrogen, while
hydrogen production will decrease the current eﬃciency in alkaline baths. Alkaline electrolytes yield
a better material distribution however, and the pretreatment process is less demanding. The alkaline
zinc bath contains zincate ions, and the electrodeposition occurs through a sequence of steps [83]:

Challenges in this project
The key challenges in this project will be to develop eﬃcient and cheap batteries with a long cycle life.
For Ni-Fe batteries, the main challenge is to limit hydrogen evolution on the iron electrode, as this
decreases the eﬃciency of the battery, causes a high self-discharge rate and causes a need for venting
and maintenance (electrolyte reﬁll) of the batteries. It is believed that this challenge can be overcome
by doping the iron electrode with certain elements to increase the hydrogen overpotential. Studies have
already shown that bismuth and sulﬁde are eﬀective [46,49]. Additionally it is necessary to increase the
high-rate performance of the batteries, since the batteries are aimed at storing renewable energy for
the national energy grid, meaning that high-rate use can be expected. This can potentially be achieved
by using electroplating methods to create high-surface-area electrodes with good conductivities. Other
studies have achieved extremely high surface areas by the use of a range of nano-particles [38–40,42],
but these approaches will be too expensive for large-scale batteries.
For Ni-Zn batteries there are several challenges. The high nominal cell voltage of 1.73 V causes a
risk of unwanted hydrogen or oxygen evolution as well as corrosion and self-discharge of the battery,
and it is important to avoid this by manipulating the overpotentials. It has been shown that certain
organic additives such as PEG or IMZ may inhibit corrosion of zinc [57,58]. Furthermore it has been
found that the zinc electrode may passivate during discharge due to supersaturation of zinc ions in the
electrolyte, which lowers the capacity. The main challenge however is to increase the cycle life of the
Ni-Zn batteries in order to make them suitable for large-scale energy storage applications. The limiting
factor is the growth of zinc dendrites during charging of the batteries, which short-circuit the cell if

3.2
Charging rate
The charging/discharging rate is usually denoted the “C-rate” and expressed as e.g. “1C” or “C/5”.
The C-rate is related to the nominal capacity of the battery, and it can be thought of as the amount of
times the battery can be charged/discharged in an hour [93]. For a battery with a nominal capacity of
500 mAh, a 1C charging rate will correspond to a current of 500 mA, while a C/5 rate will correspond to
a current of 100 mA - it should take 1 or 5 hours to charge the batteries completely at the two charging
rates, respectively. The C-rate is thus a parameter that can be used to compare the performance of
batteries with diﬀerent capacities.
The charging/discharging rate has a big impact on the performance of the batteries. This is due to
the overpotentials associated with the electrolytes as discussed in section 2.2.2.

3.4
Speciﬁc energy [Wh/kg]
The speciﬁc energy of the battery is another important parameter, as this parameter expresses how
much energy the battery can store per mass (or per volume). For some applications such as electric
vehicles, where the weight must be kept as low as possible, it is important to have a high speciﬁc energy,
and for other applications (e.g. smartphones) a low volume and thus high energy density [Wh/L] is
desired.
The amount of energy stored electrically in batteries (or capacitors) is given by the charge stored
multiplied by the voltage diﬀerence between the electrodes: E = QV . The SI unit is the Joule, J,
but for battery applications it is more common to use the Watt-hour, Wh. The speciﬁc energy (or
energy density) is thus found by dividing the energy with the mass of the battery (or the volume).
The theoretical speciﬁc energy Es,cell is found by simply multiplying the speciﬁc capacity (in Ah/kg)
with the cell voltage:
Es,cell = Qs,cellVcell

the higher cell voltage at 1.73 V results in a higher speciﬁc energy of 372 Wh/kg.
As mentioned above, the theoretical speciﬁc capacity (which is used to calculate the speciﬁc energy) is
always higher than what is practically achieved, and this is thus also the case for the speciﬁc energy.

3.5
Speciﬁc power [W/kg]
The speciﬁc power is a measure of how quickly the battery can release the stored energy, as power
simply means the energy released per unit time. The SI unit for power is the Watt, 1 W = 1 J/s. The
power output is the product of the voltage and current:
P = IV
(3.5)
and the speciﬁc power is then the power divided by the mass (or volume) of the battery. The speciﬁc
power is not an intrinsic material value such as the speciﬁc capacity or energy, since the power depends
on the discharge rate. It is however possible to compare batteries (and other power storage technologies)
under similar conditions. While many diﬀerent technologies exist, it has so far not been possible to
develop a type of energy storage which has both a high speciﬁc energy and speciﬁc power.
Fuel
cells have some of the highest energy densities, but cannot deliver high amounts of energy rapidly.
Supercapacitors (or Double Layer Capacitors) on the other hand are able to deliver high power, but
cannot store large amounts of energy [2]. This can be illustrated in a Ragone plot such as shown in
ﬁgure 3.4. Most technologies are pushing further towards the upper right-hand corner of the Ragone
plot (where internal combustion engines exist), and new technologies such as hybrid supercapacitors
emerge by combining a high-power electrode from supercapacitors with a high-energy electrode from
Li-ion batteries [105].
One of the problems with high-power batteries is that to increase the power output, the current must
be increased (since the voltage is determined by the cell potential). However a higher current will
cause increased polarisation and associated overpotentials - both activation, concentration and Ohmic.
These overpotentials will cause a voltage drop and decrease the eﬃciency of the battery, and the voltage
drop will lead to a loss of energy since E = QV .

Cycle life
The cycle life of a battery speciﬁes how many times a battery can be charged and discharged (1 cycle
= 1 charge + 1 discharge). This is an important parameter for large scale batteries, since for example
batteries used to store renewable energy will likely be charged and discharged daily, and in order to be
a viable investment the batteries should last at least 10-20 years. For batteries coupled to windmills,
it would be ideal to have a battery life similar to that of the windmill. The batteries in this project
should thus aim for a cycle life of ≈4000 −8000 cycles.

important to state both the depth of discharge (DOD, which is typically about 80 % of the nominal
capacity) as well as the end-of-life capacity (which is usually when the fully charged battery retains 80

Chapter 4
Large-scale electricity storage
This project focuses on storing excess renewable energy at times when there is excess production of
wind/solar energy for the grid to accommodate. The discrepancy between production and demand
is not limited to renewable energy however, and the need for large scale energy storage has existed
for some time. The main reason for this is that the grid itself cannot store energy, and the produced
electricity must be used immediately. The electricity grid can thus be described as the largest “Just-
In-Time” supply system in the world [117]. JIT systems are (among other things) characterised by
being able to adjust the supply quickly to meet a changing demand, however this is a problem for
coal and nuclear power plants. They typically operate at an optimum load range, and operating them
below this range or ramping the load up or down will cause increased fuel use and emissions [117]. By
looking at the demand cycle during a normal day such as shown in ﬁgure 4.1, it is clear that generators
will be operating below optimum load during night-time and will be ramped up and down a few times
every day, with increased emissions and fuel use as a consequence. By using batteries (or other storage
mechanisms), it is possible to charge these devices during night time during low demand and keep
the generators running at an optimum load, and during peak-hours (around 5 pm) the devices can
be discharged in order to smoothen the curve (“peak-shaving”) and avoid ramping up the generators.
Furthermore the batteries can act as back-up systems in case of generator outages and thus increase
the reliability of the electricity grid.

the demand, and the red curve illustrates how the curve can be smoothed by incorporating an electrical
energy storage device. From [106].

4.2
Types of grid-scale storage
One of the reasons why so many diﬀerent storage solutions exist is that many diﬀerent types of grid-scale
storage are required. The types of applications can generally be divided into two groups; energy storage
and power storage. The names may be misleading since energy is stored in both cases - “power storage”
devices are able to deliver the stored energy faster however (higher power output). Energy storage
applications typically involve long charging and discharging times, with one charge-discharge cycle
per day, while power storage applications are more rapid and can be charged/discharged in seconds
to minutes and go through many cycles per day.
Batteries are typically used for energy storage
applications, while supercapacitors and ﬂywheels are most commonly used for power applications.
However there is signiﬁcant overlap between the two groups. The “power storage” applications include
[117]:
• Frequency regulation: Generators operate at a set frequency of 50 Hz, but if the generator
load is increased or reduced (due to large ramps in demand), the generators will decelerate or
accelerate slightly, respectively. This can damage both the generators and consumer electronics.
By quickly injecting or extracting power to the grid from a storage device, the frequency can be
kept constant.
52
==PAGE:63==
Large-scale electricity storage
4.2. Types of grid-scale storage
• Transmission system stability: Events such as lightning strikes or generators going on- or
oﬀ-line can cause generators to come out of sync with the grid oscillations (cause a phase shift).
This can lead to unstable oscillations and collapse the system, if the oscillations are not damped
by injection or extraction of fast pulses from a storage device.
• Rapid/operating reserve: If a generator unit fails, it is necessary to restore the lost capacity
instantly to avoid a blackout. Traditionally utilities have operated their generators signiﬁcantly
below their maximum capacity in order to accommodate potential failures, but incorporating a
rapid storage system would solve the issue and allow the utilities to operate the generators at
higher capacities.
• Power quality and reliability: It is possible that small voltage sags or spikes can occur during
the duration of 1-2 cycles (2-4 ms), and these can be destructive to sensitive electronics such as
microprocessors on assembly lines with loss of production and data processing as a consequence.
Again fast storage devices can be used to counter the sags and spikes and ensure reliable, high
quality power.
• Renewable generation smoothing: The rapid ﬂuctuations associated with renewable energy
(due to clouds passing over a PV array or a wind gust ramping up the power output of a wind
turbine) increase the problems mentioned above, and storage devices can be used to even out
these ﬂuctuations and ensure a stable power delivery to the grid. This storage application is
diﬀerent to the key application of the Edison batteries, which is to actually store the excess
renewable energy produced and release it to the grid upon demand.
Although these “power storage” applications are usually handled by high-power-density devices such as
supercapacitors, they can also be handled by (some) batteries. Since battery system can also be used
for “energy storage” applications, it is possible to have a single storage system capable of performing
multiple tasks - it is not necessary to have a separate system for each of the mentioned applications.
Apart from the mentioned need to store excess production of renewable energy, “energy storage”
applications also include:
• Power ﬂeet optimisation: As mentioned in the introduction and ﬁgure 4.1, the daily energy
demand has peaks and troughs. By storing energy during low demand and injecting it during
high demand, it is not only possible to avoid ramping up/down the generators and save fuel,
but it may also be possible to beneﬁt economically, as the electricity prices are lower during low
demand than high demand. This application is also known as “peak shaving” or “load levelling”.
• Transmission and distribution facility upgrade deferral: Electricity utilities need to ope-
rate well below maximum capacity in order to be able to accommodate e.g. the loss of a power
line or an increased load. Gradually increasing loads require regular upgrades of the infrastruc-
ture, but by incorporating storage devices it is possible to defer these upgrades and thus utilize a
greater amount of the installed capacity by operating the storage devices during peak demands.
• Black start services: In order to restore power after a black-out, it is necessary to restart
the main (fossil fuel/nuclear) generators, but this can require a lot of energy and power. This
is usually provided by a “black start diesel generator” but could also be provided by a storage
device.
These types of applications all require diﬀerent amounts of power, and the discharges last over diﬀerent
time scales.

4.3
The role of Edison-type batteries
Considering only the key application of the Edison-type batteries, which is to store excess renewable
energy, it is possible to narrow down the competition. High-power technologies are unable to store
the large amounts of energy required, meaning it only makes sense to compare diﬀerent battery sys-
tems (e.g. lead-acid, lithium-ion, sodium-sulphur, redox-ﬂow and Edison) as well as pumped hydro,
compressed air and fuel cells (i.e. production of hydrogen/synthetic natural gas). Hedegaard and
Meibom studied the speciﬁc energy scenario in Denmark in 2012 and compared diﬀerent technologies
with respect to diﬀerent timescales [126], and they found that for storing energy on an hour-to-hour
or day-to-day basis, all the mentioned types are suitable, while only compressed air, pumped hydro
and fuel cells (and possibly redox-ﬂow batteries) can store energy during several days of excess (or
deﬁciency) or even during the seasonal changes in wind. While (traditional) batteries are unable to
store several days worth of energy, a scenario where renewable sources generate excess energy several
days/weeks in a row still seems quite distant, and it is thus still relevant to look at battery technologies
for day-to-day renewable energy storage. Furthermore it is possible that battery storage solutions are
moving from centralised energy utilities providers towards the end users, meaning that smaller-scale
systems (excluding pumped hydro and compressed air) are more desirable. Ibramhim et al. have made
another comparison study of storage technologies, and have taken a range of factors into account, inclu-
ding; storage capacity, power, depth of discharge, discharge time, eﬃciency, cost, durability, autonomy,
self-discharge, operational constraints, reliability, environmental aspects as well as a few others [127].
They found that for medium-sized systems (100s of kWh), lead-acid batteries are preferred, ahead of
Li-ion, while compressed air, pumped hydro, redox-ﬂow and fuel cells are either too ineﬃcient or too
expensive. Going up to larger systems (10s of MWh) compressed air and ﬂow batteries are better, but
the authors note that both systems are still technologically immature. In a study made by Sandia Na-
tional Laboratories in 2011 [128], pumped hydro and sodium-sulphur batteries are also viable solutions
for daily discharges - this is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.8(a). Other aspects are important to consider as
well however. First of all both pumped hydro and redox ﬂow batteries have a low energy density and
require large uses of land, this is shown in ﬁgure 4.8(b) - and pumped hydro also requires an elevated
reservoir, which can be diﬃcult to achieve in Denmark. Compressed air storage also require speciﬁc
geological formations to store the air such as salt caverns, and large amounts of water are needed to
dissolve the salt formations (around 8 m3 per m3 excavated). Large amounts of water are also used
to cool the air before storing it, and when the air is released it needs to be re-heated, which is usually
done using natural gas - leading to air pollution of NOx and CO2 [109]. Due to these issues, it is still
relevant to be looking at cheap and eﬃcient battery systems.

Preparation of the Ni electrode

1. The surfaces are cleaned with dishwashing liquid to remove any oil, grease and dirt left over from
the manufacturing process.
2. The surfaces are cleaned with ethanol.
3. The subjects are then kept in de-ionized water to avoid contamination between the following
cleaning steps.
4. The steel surface are degreased anodically at a voltage of 5 V for 2 minutes. This helps remove
any residual oil or organic substances.
5. The subjects are weighed in order to determine the increase in mass after plating.
6. Anodic degreasing is performed again for 30 s in case the surfaces were contaminated by the
scales.

performance [130]. None of the tests led to signiﬁcant mass increases of the nickel electrode, however
the cell potential was measured at 1.75 V, indicating that some of the nickel has been oxidised to
NiOOH. It was also found that the cell voltage prior to charging was almost 1 V, which for pure Ni
and Zn electrodes should be only around 0.5 V based on their standard reduction potentials and the
Nernst equation. This indicates that the nickel surface has already passivated and formed an oxide
layer, which raises the potential of the Ni electrode. This passivation layer may have destructive eﬀects
on the Ni electrode - Seghiouer et al. have reported that a very thin layer of NiO, Ni3O4 and other non-
stoichiometric oxides makes it impossible to oxidise the nickel substrate, even at high potentials [131].

5.1.4
Discharging the cell
The cell was discharged using the ACM Gill AC apparatus, however it was not possible to obtain useful
data. When the instrument was set to run a negative current, the cell potential would immediately drop
below 0 V. This further substantiates the theory that a dense layer of native oxides have hindered the
oxidation of nickel to NiOOH, which can be reversibly reduced to Ni(OH)2 upon discharge. The current
supplied to the electrodes during charge has thus entirely gone to hydrogen and oxygen production
instead of charging the electrodes, which is rather disappointing. The reason the potential dropped
even below 0 V is due to the fact that the instrument and software used have not been designed with
battery testing in mind. The “discharge” was performed galvanostatically, meaning the instrument
would draw a constant current through the circuit, and if the battery cells are not able to produce this
current, the instrument simply lowers the potential until the speciﬁed current is achieved - even if it
means a cell voltage below 0 V, probably resulting in production of oxygen on the zinc electrode and
hydrogen on the nickel electrode, which provides the speciﬁed current. It would be optimal to be able
to solely drive the current from the battery cell, but that has not been possible with the set-up used.
In order to use the potentiostat for measurements, it will thus be necessary to use suﬃciently charged
electrodes to produce an electric current.

Precipitated Ni(OH)2
A method of electrochemically manufacturing a ﬁlm of Ni(OH)2 is mentioned by Hall et al. [96,132].
A nickel substrate is immersed in a solution of 0.1 M Ni(NO3)2 and connected to a rectiﬁer as the
cathode - an inert metal (e.g. titanium) is used as anode. A low current density of 2.5 mA/cm2 is
used, and the treatment is carried out for 10 minutes. This will cause reduction of hydrogen at the
nickel surface:
2 H2O + 2 e−−−→H2 + 2 OH−
The nickel ions in the solution will then react with the hydroxide to form Ni(OH)2, which has a very
low solubility, and it will then precipitate onto the surface.
This method was tested on both a pure nickel coated sample, a NiC coated sample as well as com-
mercially available nickel foam. The foam was introduced due to its high surface area, which is a very
useful electrode property. The surface of the foam is shown in the microscope image in ﬁgure 5.16(a).

5.5
Improved electrolyte
In order to test the inﬂuence of the electrolyte of the cell, a diﬀerent electrolyte was fabricated. While
the ﬁrst, simple electrolyte was based on the 1993 patent of M. Eisenberg [23] and only consisted of
KOH for high alkalinity and H3BO3 for buﬀering (plus ZnO and some plating additives to investigate
dendrite growth), this electrolyte would be somewhat more complex. The basis was the 2010 patent
by J. Phillips [55], and would include:
• 2 M NaOH and 6 M KOH for high alkalinity.
• 0.1 M Na3PO4 as a buﬀering agent.
• 0.3 M KF to improve the performance [55].
• 5 mM In2(SO4)3 to inhibit corrosion.
Furthermore the Zincaslot plating additives (similar concentrations as previously) as well as 0.06 M
ZnO would be added to the electrolyte to investigate the deposition of zinc.
A solution was prepared using 400 mL de-ionized water, and again it was found that the temperature
increased as the hydroxides were added.
It was not possible to obtain complete dissolution of all
species, so the electrolyte was ﬁltered (this was also mentioned in the patent). This led to a warm,
saturated electrolyte however, and upon cooling some crystals formed in the container. The exact
composition of the electrolyte is thus somewhat uncertain.

electrolyte (Electrolyte 2), both during charging and during discharge. Since the electrodes are similar
and both cells were stirred throughout the experiment, it is likely due to to an increased activation
polarisation, i.e. something is hindering the redox reactions at the surfaces. This is highly unwanted,

increased potentials necessary to charge the electrodes may also cause production of oxygen and/or
hydrogen alongside the charging reactions. Furthermore it is seen that a voltage “barrier” must be
overcome in Electrolyte 2 at the beginning of each charge cycle - the potential increases to a peak and
then reaches a steady level. This indicates that a certain overpotential is required to actually start the
charging reactions, which is also unwanted as this too wastes energy.

One of the problems encountered with Edison type batteries is a high corrosion rate of the electrodes
and thus self-discharge. As described in section 2.1.2, the corrosion reactions are similar to the discharge
reactions of the electrodes, apart from the fact that the electrons produced by the redox reactions are
used to reduce oxygen or hydrogen instead of driving an electric current.
It is thus important to
minimise corrosion if the batteries are to be stored over extended periods of time.

steels in seawater, which corrode at a current of 10-15 µA/cm2 on average [81]. The corrosion rate
in electrolyte E2 cannot be determined, as it is unknown when the cell was completely discharged,
however it must be larger than 5.44 µA/cm2.

discharge. This is also expected, since the electrolyte (or another supplier of H2O) is required for
the cathodic reduction of water or oxygen (reactions (2.10) and (2.11)). Without these reactions, the
anodic oxidation of the zinc electrode cannot take place. Access to water is also needed to reduce the
NiOOH electrode, since the electrode is cathodically reduced, while water is oxidised to form oxygen
gas (reaction (2.16)).
It is found that the cell with electrolyte E1 retained 82 % capacity while the other cell with electrolyte
E2 retained 85 % capacity. It does not make sense to calculate the corrosion currents, as the corrosion
rate cannot be assumed to be constant. This is due to the gradual drying of the electrodes as they
are left out of the electrolyte, and it would be expected that the corrosion rates would decrease as the
availability of H2O decreased.

electrodes. These are the potentials of the electrodes (measured by a reference electrode) when no
potential or current is applied, and thus allows for investigations of a single electrode rather than a
battery cell.
The OCPs were measured by submerging the charged electrodes into each electrolyte in separate
containers. Saturated calomel electrodes (SCE) were used as a reference electrodes to evaluate the
potentials of the diﬀerent electrolytes - these were all calibrated against silver/silver chloride reference
electrodes prior to the tests to ensure reliable measurements. The calomel electrode has a reduction
potential of +244 mV relative to the standard hydrogen electrode, which means that the measurements
must be adjusted in order to compare with e.g. Pourbaix diagrams. The potential was then measured
for each electrode in each electrolyte for a duration of 60 minutes without stirring.
The results for the nickel electrodes are shown in ﬁgure 5.36(a), where it is seen that the potential is

The main results of the tests are:
• Very stable charging and discharging voltages were possible.
• Even after 66 hours of testing, the full capacity of the cells was not reached.
• The overpotential encountered during charging decreases with cycling.

Chapter summary
In this chapter, a number of diﬀerent tests have been carried out in order to investigate Ni-Zn battery
cells.
While it was initially thought that a cell could easily be built using pure nickel and zinc,
it was found that the nickel electrode was a bit more complicated to manufacture due to a native
oxide layer preventing charging of the electrode. It was found that precipitating Ni(OH)2 onto either
NiC or nickel foam substrates led to operational cells, but since the precipitated layer was very thin,
the relatively thick Ni-coated steel samples led to low speciﬁc capacities and energy densities. The
charging of the electrodes lead to a very spongy dendritic growth of zinc if no additives were present
in the electrolytes, but it was found that the Zincaslot additives from the zinc-plating industry greatly
improved the quality of the deposited zinc layer. It was however evident that the additives underwent
some reaction during the charge-discharge cycles of the cells, and the long-term performance of the
additives is yet unknown. Two diﬀerent electrolytes were tested, and it was found that the simpler
electrolyte chemistry was actually preferred over the newer, more complex electrolyte, as it resulted
in higher capacities, lower overpotentials, less self-discharge and better zinc depositions. Finally a
commercial nickel-electrode was tested and characterised, and it was found that the pocket-plate
design used was capable of storing more active material and thus achieving a larger capacity than the
electrodes manufactured in the project.

6.1
The nickel electrode
Five diﬀerent types of nickel electrodes have been tested in this project; pure nickel, nickel with “baked”
NiO, NiC and nickel foam with precipitated Ni(OH)2 and a commercial pocket-plate type electrode.
It was clear that simply oxidising nickel to oxidation state (III) and thus forming a suﬃciently thick
layer of NiOOH was not possible, and this is probably due to the formation of a native oxide on the
surface of the nickel. This means that simple electroplating processes alone are insuﬃcient to produce
working electrodes. It was thought that a deposition of thermally “baked” NiO on top of the nickel
substrate could relieve the problem, as the conversion of Ni(NO3)2 to NiO would also release oxygen
and nitrous oxide as well as some of the bound water, and this would create a more porous structure
which might more easily be converted to NiOOH by oxidation. This was not the case however, as the
more porous structure disintegrated in the electrolyte and furthermore was unable to oxidise to form
NiOOH.
Based on these failed attempts, it was clear that it was necessary to fabricate the active Ni(OH)2
beforehand in order to achieve a functional electrode. One way of doing this is to precipitate Ni(OH)2
onto a sample surface by immersing the sample in a solution of Ni(NO3)2 and connecting it to an
electric circuit as a cathode. This will form a thin layer of Ni(OH)2 on the sample, which is able to
be oxidised reversibly to NiOOH. This treatment works well with electrodes coated in the NiC coating
as well as nickel foams, however plain nickel has shown problems with adhesion. Since the achievable
coating thickness is very thin, it is desirable to use as thin samples as possible to maximise the ratio of
active/total mass of the battery. In this project only relatively thick steel plates have been used, but it
would be interesting to see how thin the substrates can be made without compromising the mechanical
integrity. In this regard, the nickel foam has been interesting since the thin mesh-structure allows for
a high surface-to-mass ratio, leading to a reasonably high speciﬁc capacity. The NiC and nickel foam
electrodes had similar volumes, but the mass of the foam was only around 1.5 g, while the NiC coated
steel samples weighed around 20 g. However the adhesion of the Ni(OH)2 on the nickel foam was not
as good as onto the NiC coating, and some detachment of the precipitate particles was observed in the
cells.
Both the cells containing nickel foam and NiC electrodes with precipitated Ni(OH)2 were functional,
delivering a capacity around 15-20 mAh. This is by no means a large capacity, and considering the
mass of the NiC electrode alone is around 20 g, the result is far from the theoretical speciﬁc capacity
of the Ni-Zn cell of 215 mAh/g. The low speciﬁc capacity is partly due to the high mass of the thick
underlying steel sample, which illustrates the importance of using thinner substrates. The mass of the
active Ni(OH)2 was only 0.062 g, indicating that it would actually be possible to achieve signiﬁcant
speciﬁc capacities if it is possible to minimise the amount of inactive material (also including electrolyte,
electronics etc.).

types, for example a pocket-plate electrode, where the active material is stored as powder in metal
“pockets”. This solution was seen in the commercial Ni-Fe battery, where both the iron and nickel

6.4
Practical uses
This project has focussed on manufacturing functional Ni-Zn cells in order to determine possible
solutions to the main problems they face.
The cells are still at a very initial stage, and there is
quite some way to the practical large-scale applications described in chapter 4. However the project
has shown that the cells are capable of energy eﬃciencies close to 80 %, which is very reasonable
comparing to other technologies. In chapter 3 it was found that typical round-trip eﬃciencies are in
the range 60-90 %, which show that the Ni-Zn batteries have potential as an eﬃcient storage solution.
It may be possible to increase the eﬃciency even further by ﬁnding a way to further increase the
oxygen/hydrogen evolution overpotentials on the electrodes, as gas evolution was observed during
charging of the electrodes. If this can be avoided, the eﬃciency will increase. It is also important
to note that the measured eﬃciency is found at relatively small charge/discharge currents, and the
eﬃciency will decrease at higher currents since the overpotentials are proportional to the current. It
is thus very important to ﬁnd ways to minimise these overpotentials.
Assuming that it is possible to decrease the overpotentials, the Ni-Zn technology (and Ni-Fe as well) has
potential as a large-scale storage solution, primarily due to the low cost, simplicity and (presumably)
high eﬃciency. This project has shown that it is possible to limit the dendritic growth of zinc by adding
plating additives, and that these additives may possibly also inhibit corrosion, which means that the
overall cell is very simple with only three “constituents” to the electrolyte apart from water (KOH
for alkalinity, H3BO3 for buﬀering and Zincaslot additives for corrosion and dendrite inhibition). To
proceed to the next stages of this project, it is ﬁrst necessary to validate that this cell composition
also works after 5000 and preferably even 10000 charge-discharge cycles. If this is the case, the cells
deﬁnitely have practical use, and it is just a matter of ﬁnding suitable, high surface area electrode
designs capable of delivering high power outputs, while still maintaining a high eﬃciency. The electrode
kinetics can be further evaluated by utilising e.g. cyclic voltammetry to investigate the reversibility
of the reactions as well as the activation polarisation of the hydrogen/oxygen evolution reactions on
the electrodes, chronopotentiometry to investigate the diﬀusion of the reactive species, which could
be important for highly porous electrodes, and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy which can be
used to investigate the impedance of the electrodes and system by treating the diﬀerent components
as elements in an equivalent electric circuit [90].
While the Ni-Zn batteries have shown some promising results, it is critical to increase the capacity of
the cells in order to be competitive. As mentioned, it is not necessary to be “best in class” when it
comes to speciﬁc capacity or speciﬁc energy, as the actual mass/volume of the batteries are not the
most important parameters. But the capacities of the tested cells have simply been too low. This is
partly because this project has been focussing on proof-of-concept, but it is clear that the next stages
must evaluate the prospects of increasing the speciﬁc capacities by making the electrodes thinner or
using powder-based electrodes (i.e. increasing the amount of active material relative to the total mass).
By making thinner electrodes and basing the manufacturing on electrochemical methods, it would be
interesting to see if the manufacturing steps are able to be turned into an automated reel-to-reel
process, as this would help lower the cost of the batteries.
If the speciﬁc capacity of the batteries is too low, more material will be needed to store a certain amount
of charge, which in turn will increase the cost of the batteries. If this cannot be achieved, the technology
will be outperformed by Li-ion, molten Na-S or ﬂow-batteries, which all have competitive speciﬁc values
and are subject of a lot of research projects at the moment. Na-S batteries have been used to store wind
and solar energy in Japan since 2008 [142], and the Danish wind turbine manufacturer Vestas have
recently collaborated with various partners to ensure stable energy production; on November 28th a

Chapter 7
Conclusions and Outlook
In this project Edison type Ni-Zn battery cells have been manufactured and tested. The project aimed
to optimise both Ni-Fe and Ni-Zn batteries, however the problems related to the Ni-Zn technology
proved to be more challenging than anticipated, and the focus was shifted to speciﬁcally tackle these
problems. To do this, a range of nickel-zinc battery cells were manufactured and tested in order to
investigate various parameters.
The original Edison battery was invented in the early 1900s by Thomas Edison in the United States
(alongside Waldemar Jungner in Sweden), and it consisted of a nickel oxy-hydroxide positive electrode
and a negative iron electrode in an alkaline electrolyte. However due to the similar chemistries, other
alkaline nickel-based batteries may also be regarded as Edison-type (such as nickel-zinc or nickel-
cadmium). The Ni-Fe and Ni-Zn batteries were never able to capture a large market share, but they
have recently received renewed attention due to their potential applications and low cost. A literature
review was thus initially conducted to get an overview of the technologies, the problems faced and
potential solutions. The main problems for the Ni-Zn batteries are dendritic depositions of zinc during
charging of the batteries, which decrease their lifetime, high corrosion rates leading to self-discharge
of the batteries, as well as gas evolution during charge, leading to both a lower eﬃciency and the need
of venting (and increased maintenance of) the batteries. The Ni-Fe batteries also have problems with
corrosion and gas evolution, and furthermore they are known to perform poorly at high discharge rates
due to passivation of the iron electrode, and this is worsened at low temperatures. Several diﬀerent
solutions to all these problems exist, and commercial Ni-Zn and Ni-Fe batteries are available - however
they are yet to be seen as large scale storage solutions e.g. coupled to wind or solar farms, which is
the ultimate goal of this project.
A thorough review of the theory behind batteries was performed in order to fully understand the
underlying thermodynamic principles governing the operation of the batteries.
When (secondary)
batteries are charged, an electric current is used to drive a pair of reversible redox reactions, leading
to oxidation of the positive electrode (nickel) and a reduction of the negative electrode (iron or zinc).
The diﬀerence in reduction potentials of the positive and negative electrodes will then drive an electric
current upon discharge, whereby the positive electrode will be reduced and the negative electrode
oxidised.
The reduction potentials can be calculated using the Nernst equation, which is derived
from the change in Gibbs free energy of the reactions. The thermodynamic analysis also reveal why
the batteries have problems with corrosion and self-discharge, since the nominal cell voltage of the
batteries is above that necessary to split water. Finally the electrochemistry of the battery cell was
reviewed, including the properties of the electrolyte and the various polarisations and corresponding
overpotentials encountered, lowering the eﬃciency of the batteries.
In order to be able to compare the Edison type batteries with other battery types, a chapter was
dedicated to studying the diﬀerent parameters deﬁning the performance of batteries. It was generally
found that Edison type batteries have potential as low-cost, large-scale solutions due to the low material
cost and high cycle-life of the batteries. However they are not completely superior to other technologies,
101

as e.g. sodium-sulphur or zinc-air batteries have larger energy densities, nickel-metal-hydride batteries
have larger power densities while lithium-ion batteries have higher eﬃciencies. It is clear that the
competition is tough, and many diﬀerent parameters can be optimised for each battery type with
respect to the particular application. For the Edison batteries aiming for large-scale applications, it
is important to keep the cost down while ensuring a high cycle life (i.e. a high Speciﬁc Value) and
high eﬃciency. In order to keep the cost down, it will be beneﬁcial to maintain reasonable power and
energy densities.
Since the Edison type batteries are aimed at large-scale energy storage, it was important to consider
the national energy production and demand. It was seen that the daily energy demands peak every
afternoon, while it is lower at night, and due to the increased fuel use caused by ramping a coal
power plant up or down, it is desirable to use batteries (or other types of energy storage) to “shave
oﬀthe peaks” by discharging in the afternoon and charging at night. Furthermore, with an increasing
amount of renewable energy generation, which is ﬂuctuating by nature, batteries can be used as “buﬀer
systems” to store excess renewable energy production and discharge when there is no sun/wind. Other
applications for large-scale energy storage have also been discussed, as well as the existing technologies
including pumped hydro which is the most widely used technology today. While many solutions exist,
it is believed that the Edison type batteries could play an important role - provided it is possible to
solve the problems encountered.
In order to solve the problems mentioned, a range of experiments have been carried out. A number of
diﬀerent nickel electrodes were manufactured and tested in two diﬀerent electrolyte with and without
zinc-plating additives.
It was found that the nickel electrode was more diﬃcult to fabricate than
anticipated, but it was possible to precipitate Ni(OH)2 onto the surface of both NiC coated electrodes
and nickel foam, which both gave useful results, with increasing capacities after each charge-discharge
cycle. Furthermore a commercial nickel electrode from a Ni-Fe battery was tested and characterised
in order to ﬁnd the composition. This electrode was based on a pocket-plate design with Ni(OH)2
powder placed in metal pockets, and based on the XRD analysis it was found that the powder probably
also consisted of graphite for increased conductivity. Due to the high amount of active material in the
electrode pockets, this electrode showed a much higher capacity than the fabricated electrodes, which
only had active material (Ni(OH)2) on the surface of the electrodes. All electrodes showed some gas
evolution upon charge, which is a problem that has yet to be solved, and this decreases the round-trip
eﬃciency of the cells to around 75-80 %. The gassing must be eliminated, both in order to increase
the eﬃciency, but also to avoid pressure build-up in the cells as well as drying out of the electrolyte.
Two diﬀerent electrolytes were manufactured based on patents from 1993 and 2010. The older, simpler
electrolyte was initially used without any additives, which resulted in signiﬁcant dendritic growth, and
a very spongy and mechanically unstable zinc deposition. By adding additives from the zinc-plating
industry, it was possible to minimise the dendritic growth and achieve homogeneous and mechanically
stable depositions with good adhesion. The results were not consistent however, and diﬀerent appea-
rances were observed as the tests continued. It was also observed that the electrolyte changed color
during the tests, which indicate that the additives may degrade over time.
Additionally it was found that the newer and more complex electrolyte (with added plating additives)
performed worse than the older electrolyte. The overpotentials encountered during both charge and
discharge were higher, leading to poorer eﬃciency. The zinc deposition also showed poor adhesion,
which could be due to co-deposition of indium from the electrolyte. Furthermore the electrodes cor-
roded much faster in the newer electrolyte, which is thought to be due to interference between the
corrosion inhibitors in the electrolyte (indium) and the plating additives, which may also have corrosion
inhibiting properties.
Finally the potential uses of the Edison type batteries have been discussed. It is clear that the capacities
achieved in this project have been too low for practical uses, but it is believed that the technology
does have potential once a higher speciﬁc capacity is achieved and it has been demonstrated that the
additives prolong the lifetime of the batteries to more than 5000 charge-discharge cycles. It is also seen
that there are many other competing large-scale storage technologies available (and currently being
researched), which means that it is necessary to develop functional batteries as quickly as possible in
order to be able to enter the market before it saturates. Otherwise the batteries may end up similarly

due to the emergence of alternative solutions before the technology was fully developed.
Further studies
Based on the ﬁndings in this project, it is evident that the Edison type batteries have potential, however
there is still much testing to be done to investigate diﬀerent parameters. First of all it is necessary
to test the cells for an increased amount of cycles (preferably +5000 charge/discharge cycles). This
should be done by automated equipment which can stop the charge/discharge cycle as soon as the cells
reach a certain potential in order to ensure that no overcharging occurs and the cells are discharged to
a certain depth of discharge. This would clarify how many cycles are needed for the cells to reach their
full capacity, as it was found that the capacity was continuously increasing during at least the ﬁrst 60
cycles. It would also reveal if the additives degrade over time, and how this would aﬀect the battery
performance as well as the deposition of zinc. If the additives do degrade over time, and this leads to
dendritic depositions of zinc, it will be necessary to develop a diﬀerent approach to this problem.
It is also necessary to increase the speciﬁc capacity of the manufactured cells, i.e. increasing the amount
of active material relative to the total mass of the cell. This could either be done by testing how thin
the electrodes can be manufactured, or by testing diﬀerent powder-based electrodes, e.g. the pocket-
plate type found in the commercial Ni-Fe battery. It would be interesting to see if it is possible to
make the electrodes thin enough for reel-to-reel plating to be a feasible manufacturing method, as this
would help keep the manufacturing cost down. These precipitate-based electrodes have the advantage
of large interfaces between both the active material and the electrode and the active material and
the current collector, which helps lower the resistance and diﬀusion polarisation of the cells. If this
approach does not lead to signiﬁcant improvements of the speciﬁc capacity however, it is necessary to
focus on the powder-based electrodes instead.
Once high-capacity electrodes are manufactured, it is necessary to study the overpotentials of the
electrode and electrode-electrolyte interfaces. It is important to minimise the activation and Ohmic
overpotentials to increase the round-trip eﬃciencies of the batteries. The kinetics could be evaluated
using cyclic voltammetry, chronopotentiometry and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy in order
to get a better understanding of the various reactions and processes occuring in the cells.
While low activation overpotentials are required for the cell reactions, it would be advantageous to
increase the activation overpotentials for the oxygen and hydrogen evolution reactions in order to
decrease the corrosion of the electrodes and thus self-discharge of the cells. For Ni-Fe cells it has
been shown that bismuth is an eﬀective dopant in achieving this, and it would be interesting to see
if the zinc (or nickel) electrodes could be doped by similar elements. Increasing the hydrogen/oxygen
overpotentials would also reduce the gas evolution during charging of the cells and thus solve two
problems at once.
Since existing products already exist, it would also be interesting to test, disassemble and characterise
other batteries, including the Ni-Zn batteries produced by ZincFive and the Ni-Fe batteries produced
by Encell and the Iron Edison Company. It is possible that this could lead to new ideas on how to
optimise the cell design, keeping their intellectual properties and patents in mind of course.
Finally, while both Ni-Fe and Ni-Zn technologies have shown potential as large-scale storage solutions,
it would be advantageous to focus solely on one of the two technologies. The chemistries are similar, but
there are still slight diﬀerences in the composition of the electrolyte, where e.g. sulphides are needed
in Ni-Fe batteries to reduce passivation, while dendrite-inhibiting additives are needed in the Ni-Zn
batteries. It is thus diﬃcult to “kill two birds with one stone”. This project has focussed primarily on
the Ni-Zn technology and proposed possible solutions to some of the challenges, and it would thus be
favourable to follow up on these results and investigate whether or not the proposed solutions are able
to ensure a long cycle life, high eﬃciency and useful speciﬁc capacity.
