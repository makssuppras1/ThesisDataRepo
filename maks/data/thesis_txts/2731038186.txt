Preface
This master thesis was prepared at the Department of Electrical and Photonics
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements
for acquiring a master degree in Photonics. This thesis reports on a method of tapered
optical fiber fabrication process using chemical etching, with optical, mechanical, and
fluidic analyses of the fiber fabrication process. Such fibers are designed for large or
controlled neural stimulation using evanescent waves by modifying the geometry of the
fiber end.
Chapter 1 lays the foundation by delving into the project’s background. It sum-
marizes prior research, identifies challenges. Chapter 2 is devoted to elucidating the
theoretical foundations, focusing on the properties of polymer optical fibers and the
characteristics of light propagation in different optical fibers. Chapter 3 offers a closer
look at the practical aspects of our project. It encompasses optical simulations of the
fiber, mechanical analyses of the implantation process, and validation of the fiber fab-
rication by chemical etching. Chapter 4 provides an in-depth look at the process of
fabricating optical fibers via the thermal drawing method, followed by a detailed inves-
tigation of the chemical etching process and the problem that has been encountered. In
Chapter 5, in vitro characterization was carried out for the fabricated optical fibers, and
the optical fibers were verified to have neural stimulation capabilities through illumina-
tion maps. Chapter 6 summarizes the entire project and discusses the issues identified
during the process.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
This thesis encompasses the design, fabrication and characterization of single con-
ical structured neural implants (optical fibers) based on 2 different polymer materials
(Poly(methyl methacrylate) and Polycarbonate) for deeply stimulating the neural cavity
to dissolve the brain dynamics. Such novel implantable neural interfaces are developed
with the rapid advances in optogenetics, an emerging field that combines optical and
genetic approaches to study the behavior of cells and even individual animals[1].
The neuromodulation driven by electrical neural interfaces has been successfully uti-
lized in neural prosthetic systems such as cochlear implant, deep brain stimulation (DBS)
system, artificial retina[2], for their outstanding signal quality regarding spatiotemporal
resolution and signal-to-noise ratio(SNR)[3]. However, a handicap for the better devel-
opment of electrical neural interfaces is the long-term stability. A chronic recording
experiment on macaque monkeys was carried out for 3-4 weeks using microwire arrays,
illustrating a decrease in signal-to-noise ratio and the number of detectable neurons over
time[4]. The mechanical mismatch between the biological tissue and implants, and the
long-term biocompatibility of material, are the main reasons to cause the instability,
preventing people from gaining a deeper understanding of interneural behaviors. To
break the hurdle, optogenetics has been received a lot of concerns in the last 2 decades
due to the precious control of nerve cells without long-term effect by light at specific
wavelength and photosensitive proteins. Optogenetics can be employed not only for the
study of neurological diseases, but also for the treatment of diabetes[5], cancer[6], drug
rehabilitation[7], and post-stroke depression (PSD)[8].
The main mission of tools in optogenetics is to manipulate the neural activity (Exci-
tation or inhibition), while, some of them integrate other capabilities such as imaging[9],
drug delivery[10][11]. The neural activity can be detected by Transcranial Magnetic Stim-
ulation (TMS)[12], Electro-encephalography (EEG)[13], functional Near Infrared Spec-
troscopy(fNIRS), and functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging(fMRI)[14],nevertheless,
these methods either require wearing a large amount of electrodes or require highly ex-
pensive instruments. This project focuses on the development of tapered fiber optic
devices relying on wet etching method, which allows for a faster and less burdensome
fiber optic device manufacturing process. To simulated the neurons optically, the deliv-
ery of light is achieved by evanescent field in the tapered region, and the illumination

1.2
proir work
Optogenetics, also referred as optical stimulation plus genetic engineering, is based
on the principle of using photosensitive channel proteins to control specific neuron, tis-
sue, organ[15] or organism at microsecond level by light. The new field was first in-
troduced by Karl Deisseroth et al in 2005, in which he used Channelrhodopsin-2, an
algal protein, in combination with high-speed optical switching technology to achieve
modulation of mammalian neurons[16]. The stimulation tools, photosensitive protein
and the transporters are key elements in optogenetics. O.Shimomura et al. found the
green fluorescent protein(GFP) from aequorea victoria in 1962[17]. This protein is able
to emit light at the wavelength of 508nm or 395nm (minor peak at 479nm) as the region
of excitation is ultraviolet(UV)[18], then it has been investigated widely for biolumines-
cence research, such as cell ablation, bacteriuria screening, etc. Photosensitive proteins,
such as oCHIEF, can be activated by light with wavelengths in the range of 450nm -
470nm￿in some neurons it can respond to high-frequency light that accelerates the rate
of channel closure and the deactivation has decreased in response to continuous optical
stimulation. For photostimulation tools, optical fibers and laser source are commonly
used in combination in vivo experiments, while light emitting diodes (LEDs) are used
extensively for in vitro experiments.
For LED based nerve tools, Cao et al prepared a polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) -
encapsulated LED that can stimulate deep nerve cells, but it is not conducive to LED
heat dissipation because of the strong thermal insulation of the material, which can
easily cause thermal damage to tissues[19].
For fiber-optic based nerve tools, mainly can be categorized into laser coupled and
LED coupled. In 2008, R.Adamantidis et al. achieved the first experimental behavioral
control of rodents in a free-moving state with the combination of a laser-coupled fiber-
optic probe and a pipe casing, allowing not only stimulation of the cortical surface
but also neuromodulation and drug delivery.[20]. This avoids repeated implantation of
optical fibers and reduces the burden on the experimental body. In 2013, Kwon er al.
fabricated LED-coupled fiber optic probe that used an optical array to achieve multi-
point light stimulation of neurons in different depths. However, the effective irradiation
range is narrow because of the low coupling eﬀiciency between LED and optical fiber[21].
In 2014, F.Pisanello et al. demonstrated a multipoint-emitting optical tapered fibers
which allows for selective light stimulation in awake mice[22]. In 2017, P.Ferruccio et
al. offered a new method to illuminate large brain volume via a single tapered optical
fiber by adjusting the light incident angle[23]. It is easy to see that neural implants are
moving toward miniaturization, minimally invasive, and multifunctionalization.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical concepts
2.1
Polymer optical fiber
Since R. Maurer et al. from Corning Inc. achieved Kao’s idea in 1970 and success-
fully developed a silica optical fiber with a loss of 20 dB/km[24], optical devices using
silica as the transmission medium have emerged from time to time. It has also employed
in the biomedical field due to the low attenuation and high immunity to electromag-
netic interference (EMI). However, silica is restricted to the drawbacks of brittleness,
high hardness[25], and non-biocompatibility of the material, there are risks of fracture
residue and rejection. Therefore, polymer materials as alternatives are applied in this
field.
•
PMMA: In 1968, DuPont first manufactured the polymer optical fiber of methyl
methacrylate (PMMA) with a high loss. In the 70s, the optical loss of PMMA fiber can
be reduced to the theoretical limit around 106dB/km at 570nm. Peng et al. fabricated
a PMMA fiber sensor being applied in humidity sensing. Until now, PMMA has been
the most popular material for the fabrication of polymer optical fibers because of its
ability to be directly doped in the synthesis of preforms from monomers.
•
PC: In 1987, A.Tanaka et al. developed a polycarbonate (PC) core optical fiber with
a loss of 0.8dB/m at 765nm[26]. In 2007, M.Nakazawa et al. demonstrated a hollow
PC core fiber for endoscopic application[27]. PC can self-degrade in living body and is
suitable for being implanted.
•
CYTOP: In 1988, Asahi Ltd. introduced a fully amorphous and transparent poly-
mer that is fully fluoridated substance, CYTOP[28]. A.Mokhtar et al. manufactured a
CYTOP optical fiber for respiratory monitoring[29]. The transmittance of this material
is much better than PMMA, and the refractive index is relatively low, yet the manufac-
turing process is highly diﬀicult.
•
ZEONEX: This material has very good drawing properties and has a high refractive
index. However, the synthesis conditions of this polymer are harsh and not suitable for
industrial production and application. In 2016, G.Woyessa et al. fabricated and char-
acterised the FBGs of Zeonex POF for the first time[30]. In 2022, M.Sen characterized
a Zeonex fiber with the loss from 0.018 cm−1 - 0.0345cm−1 in the range of 0.3THz to

2.2
Light transmission theory
2.2.0.1
Light transmission in a step index fiber
In a single mode step-index fiber, the transmission of light in the fiber can be
described in terms of total reflection in geometric optics, which is shown in Figure.2.2.
Figure 2.2: Light propagation in a step index fiber.
The rectangular region shows the core of the fiber with refractive index n1 and
the outer part is the cladding with refractive index n2. Rays 1, 2, and 3 illustrate the
trajectories of the light at different incidence angles. Ray 2 is the configuration at an
angle of incidence of acceptance angle, where the total reflection occurs at the interface
of core and cladding. If the incident angle is smaller than the acceptance angle, the total
internal reflection happens which shows as ray 1, on the contrary, it will refract into the
cladding like ray 3.
2.2.0.2
Light transmission in a tapered fiber
Figure.2.3 gives a illustration of light propagating in a tapered fiber. Assume the
maximum core radius is a, the tip radius is b, the tapered angle is δ, an is the incident
angle at the interface of core and cladding, n is the number of how many times the light
reflecting inside the core.

insertion. The critical load factor is employed to evaluate the strength to against the

4.2.1
Sample preparation
To ensure a good etching quality, the sample preparation steps are necessary to
conduct to make the fiber suitable to be etched. The details are demonstrated as follows:
1.Fiber tip polishing
The fibers were cut into segments by blade, which results the fiber tip a high degree
of roughness. This not only leads to non-uniform etching, but also to a reduction in
fiber coupling eﬀiciency during optical characterization. Therefore, it is necessary to
polish the two ends of the optical fiber. Since the fiber diameter is so small, the fiber
is fragile when polished directly. Thus, a ferrule was employed to polish the fiber tip.
After cleaving the fiber with blade, the fiber was inserted into a ferrule, leaving a small
portion of the fiber then adjusting ferrule diameter to fix the fiber for polishing. The
fibers were polished in a figure-eight motion on the Diamond Lapping(Polishing) Sheet
(THORLABS,Inc) with the grid of 1µm, 0.3µm and 0.1µm, respectively.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
6.1
Discussion
In the early stages, this project aimed to develop a recipe that could allow stable
fabrication of tapered optical fibers. However, the shape of the product could not be ef-
fectively controlled by changing the etching conditions during the etching process. After
trying different solutions, none of them could avoid the aggregation and swelling of the
polymer material. In this regard, there are a few reasons summarized below that may
cause such a result:
Residual stress: In this project, even though both kinds of fibers were annealed
prior to chemical etching, there was still pressure residue. In addition, during the thermal
drawing process, three fiber diameters were set, experiencing different pressure histories
for the entire section of fiber. While continuously trying chemical etching, it was found
that some PMMA fibers were very well etched initially. The reason for this should be
that the section of fiber was not located in the transition stage where the fiber diameter
becomes smaller when it was drawn.
Hydrophilicity: The tapered region was found to be too long after the chemical
etching process. Since the length of the target fiber is not long in the first place, the
uneven distribution of tension due to hydrophilicity can lead to contact with the solution
for even twice the target length of the fiber.
Chemical vapor: The etching solutions selected for this project are mostly organic.
Therefore, it has strong volatility. To test this hypothesis, the optical fiber was wound
with TPFE thread seal tape and chemical etching was performed under this section of
the fiber, and it was found that the wound portion of the fiber would also be etched.
This phenomenon cannot prove that the chemical vapor has a significant effect on the
etching effect, but it can corroborate the effect of hydrophilicity.
6.2
Future work
For the project itself, in the future these two different optical fibers will be sub-
jected to in vivo experiments to verify their performance for neural stimulation. For the
