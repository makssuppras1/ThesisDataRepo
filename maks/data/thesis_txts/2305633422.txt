Abstract 
The European fisheries management is currently in a process of a fundamental change in the handling 
of commercial fisheries catches with the implementation of the Common Fisheries Policy of 2013. 
One of the main objectives of the Common Fisheries Policy of 2013 is to end the practice of discarding 
in the EU by 2019. However, for such changes to be successful it is vital to ensure stakeholder 
acceptance and prudent to consider possible means to verify compliance of the new regulation. 
Remote Electronic Monitoring (REM) with Closed-Circuit Television (CCTV) has been tested in a variety 
of fisheries worldwide for different purposes and is currently considered as one possible management 
tool to ensure compliance with a European ban on discards. 
This study investigates the opinion of Danish fishers and fishery inspectors concerning the 
implementation and possible effect of the Landing Obligation in the European fisheries, as well as the 
opinion on the use of REM with CCTV as a mean to ensure compliance with the regulation. Finally, the 
study analyzes whether target species and fishing area have changed for vessels that have 
participated in a trial on Catch Quota Management verified by REM with CCTV. 
In this study, changes in target species and spatial distribution cannot solely be ascribed to 
participation in a trial on Catch Quota Management verified by REM with CCTV. There is disagreement 
as to whether REM with CCTV is an appropriate control measure among stakeholders, whereas the 
Landing Obligation is viewed as both difficult to control and comply with. It is shown that REM can 
help in the implementation of the Landing Obligation as one of few measures that to a large degree 
can verify compliance on-board fishing vessels with the obligation to land all catches.

Introduction 
Fisheries is a major contributor to the world food supply and an important source of income for many 
communities (FAO 2007; Allison et al. 2009). However, stock depletion and overfishing has been a 
continuous problem in world fisheries, with more notorious examples including the collapse of the 
Californian soupfin shark fishery and the cod stock of Newfoundland (Hutchings & Myers 1994; 
Jennings, Kaiser & Reynolds 2001a; Pauly et al. 2002). An unstable situation was also seen in the 
1990s and early 2000s, were the status of cod (Gadus morhua) stocks were poor in the European 
fisheries, with low quota settings unable to resolve the problem (Kraak et al. 2013; Ulrich et al. 2015). 
The situation was probably worsened by the European fisheries management which did not promote 
incentives to counter the unsustainable situation (Kraak et al. 2013). As only landed catches were 
deducted from quotas, the true quantity of fish removed from stocks was larger than the allocated 
quota and selectivity was not sufficiently encouraged (Hedley, Catchpole & Santos 2015). In light of 
these challenges, plans were developed in the beginning of the 2000s to counter the decline in the 
cod stocks (Horwood, Brien & Darby 2006; Kraak et al. 2013; Ulrich et al. 2015). In 2007, the Danish 
government proposed a results-based approach to the fisheries management, as an alternative to the 
very technical regulations, in order to reverse the declining trend and achieve sustainable stocks. The 
suggested approach was based on the idea of making the actual catch at sea count against the quota, 
instead of only having the landings count against the quota. In effect the proposal meant a change 
from Landings Quota to a Catch Quota Management (CQM), which would require Fully Documented 
Fisheries (FDF) if compliance were to be ensured (Dalskov & Kindt-Larsen 2009; Kindt-Larsen, 
Kirkegaard & Dalskov 2011). In 2008 the first Danish trial with FDF verified by Remote Electronic 
Monitoring (REM) with Closed-Circuit Television (CCTV) was launched (Dalskov & Kindt-Larsen 2009; 
Ulrich et al. 2015). Danish trials with FDF verified by REM with CCTV have been conducted on a yearly 
basis since then, with 2016 currently set as the final year. In conjunction with the development on 
FDF, the pressure for a change in fishing practices in the European Union (EU) have increased, not 
least due to public demand and the Fish Fight campaign, aimed at ending discards in the EU (Havstein 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
2013; Fernley-Whittingstall 2014; Sigurðardóttir et al. 2015). Discards are the part of the catch that is 
returned to the sea for economic, legal or personal reasons (Alverson et al. 1994). Discarding can be 
due to incidental catches of seabirds or marine mammals, catch being damaged, catch of non-target 
species, illegal catch e.g. due to quota being exhausted or high-grading. High-grading is the act of 
discarding relatively small but legal specimens in the hopes of acquiring larger and more valuable 
specimens in a later catch (Jennings, Kaiser & Reynolds 2001b; Batsleer et al. 2015; Damalas 2015; 
Gullestad et al. 2015). The practice of discarding is generally viewed as unsustainable, unethical and a 
waste of resources, which has led to attempts at limiting or ending the practice (Diamond & Beukers-
stewart 2009; Petter Johnsen & Eliasen 2011). Measures for this include increased gear selectivity, 
effort restrictions, quota limitations, temporal and spatial restrictions, transferability of quotas and 
discard bans (Macher et al. 2008; Gullestad et al. 2015; Sigurðardóttir et al. 2015).  
Discard bans have been in place in Iceland since 1977, Norway since 1983 and the Faroe Islands since 
1994 (Gullestad et al. 2015; Vidarsson et al. 2015). The Landing Obligation in the Common Fisheries 
Policy (CFP) of 2013 will mean that a discard ban will come into effect in the EU as well (European 
Parliament and Council of the European Union 2013). To ensure compliance with a discard ban it is 
necessary to have CQM. Accordingly, the CFP of 2013 is ushering in FDF and the means to obtain this 
as a subject that must be considered in the future fisheries management of the EU (Mangi et al. 2015; 
Ulrich et al. 2015).  
How the discard ban and change in the fisheries management will be recieved is important. Research 
shows that compliance with a regulation is positively affected if it is supported by the involved 
stakeholders (Nielsen 2003; Nielsen & Mathiesen 2003). Hence, stakeholders’ view on a discard ban 
and control measures for FDF are important aspects for a successful implementation of the new CFP. 
This thesis seek to contribute with insights into fishers’ and fishery inspectors’ opinion and 
expectations regarding the Landing Obligation, as well as on the use of REM with CCTV as a means to 
verify catches. Additionally, the thesis will examine changes in target species and fishing patterns for 
demersal trawlers that have been in a CQM scheme verified by REM with CCTV.

General Background 
The European Quota System 
The fisheries management of the EU has been governed by the CFP, also sometimes termed “the 
Basic Regulation”, since 1983. The CFP has had major revisions in 1992, 2002 and 2013 (European 
Parliament 2015).  A central management tool is the setting of Total Allowable Catches (TACs), which 
often are referred to as “the quotas”. TACs are the quantity of a fish or shellfish stock which can be 
caught and landed each year. The European Council sets the TACs for the stocks and allocate the TACs 
among the Member States of the EU (European Commission 2015a). 
The road leading to the Landing Obligation 
The former CFP have been criticized for creating wrongful incentives in the fishing practices and being 
unable to promote sustainable fisheries  (Daw & Gray 2005; Sissenwine & Symes 2007; Khalilian et al. 
2010; Reiss et al. 2010; Symes & Hoefnagel 2010; Havstein 2013; Kraak et al. 2013; Hatcher 2013; 
Salomon, Markus & Dross 2014; Msomphora & Aanesen 2015). In 2008, a UK vessel was filmed while 
discarding large amounts of fish after leaving Norwegian waters, where discarding is illegal. The 
incident increased the public awareness on discard practices and the pressure to end the practice 
grew (Borges 2015). Following this incident and with the next revision of the CFP beginning to emerge 
in the horizon, the European Commission issued a Green Paper in 2009 to address the perceived 
shortcomings of the CFP of 2002. Five overall failures of the CFP were stated in the Green Paper 
(Sissenwine & Symes 2007; EU Commission 2009): 
 a deep-rooted problem of fleet overcapacity 
 imprecise policy objectives resulting in insufficient guidance for decisions and implementation; 
 a decision-making system that encourages a short-term focus 
 a framework that does not give sufficient responsibility to the industry 
 lack of political will to ensure compliance and poor compliance by the industry 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
Prior to the final CFP document of 2013, a number of proposals and negotiations took place to 
influence the process, including “Fish Fight”. “Fish Fight” was a large public campaign initiated in 2010 
by Hugh Fernley-Whittingstall, that used social and traditional media together with a petition to put 
pressure on politicians to end discards in the EU (Fernley-Whittingstall 2014; Salomon et al. 2014). 
The CFP of 2013 seeks to counter the above mentioned failings of the previous CFP as well as meeting 
the public demand for a change in fishing practice (Salomon et al. 2014; European Parliament 2015). 
Although the setting of TACs remain the main instrument to regulate the fisheries in the CFP of 2013, 
a major change in the CFP was the introduction of a gradual Landing Obligation on all species for 
which TACs are set (European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2013; Mortensen et al. 
2015). This will in effect change the management from landings quota to catch quota, whereby the 
full catch at sea has to be landed and counted against the TAC (Mortensen et al. 2015; Plet-Hansen et 
al. 2015). If followed by a loosening on the technical regulations on the individual fisheries, CQM can 
move the fisheries management towards a results-based management. A results-based management 
will be in line with the recommendation in the European Commission’s Green Paper as a way to 
increase the industry’s responsibility for a sustainable fisheries management (EU Commission 2009; 
Mortensen et al. 2015). Additionally, because bycatch has to be retained on-board and landed under 
the Landing Obligation, bycatches will take storage room and be deducted from quota shares. This 
should create incentives for a more selective fishery and thereby a reduction of bycatch (Hatcher 
2013; Sigurðardóttir et al. 2015). 
The Landing Obligation 
Article 15 in the CFP of 2013 state the specifics for the obligation to land all catches of species under 
TAC. The first fisheries became subject to the Landing Obligation 1 January 2015 and by 1 January 
2019 all fisheries in EU waters or conducted by vessels sailing under the flag of an EU Member State 
will be subject to the Landing Obligation.  
Some catches can be exempted from the Landing Obligation. These are (European Parliament and 
Council of the European Union 2013; Salomon et al. 2014):  
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
 Species for which the survival rate after discarding is high according to scientific advice 
 Species that are prohibited to fish 
 Species for which there are no TAC 
 Catches under the de minimis exemptions 
The de minimis exemptions can be applied if scientific advice state that increased selectivity in the 
specific fishery is very difficult to achieve or if the costs of handling unwanted catches are 
disproportionate compared to the amount of unwanted catches. The acceptable discard ratio for de 
minimis exempted fisheries is 7% in 2015 and 2016, 6% in 2017 and 2018 and 5% from 2019 and on. 
Discards under the de minimis exemption will not count against the TAC but full documentation of the 
catch is required.  
Members States shall ensure adequate documentation in accordance with the principle of efficiency 
and proportionality to secure compliance with the Landing Obligation. Measures to secure such 
documentation are listed as observers, CCTV (Closed-Circuit Television) and “others” in Article 15 
(European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2013). In order to ensure some flexibility, 
Member States can derogate up to 10% from the annual TAC on a year-to-year basis. Landings beyond 
the original TAC will then be deducted from next year’s TAC. (European Parliament and Council of the 
European Union 2013; Salomon et al. 2014).  
 
Implementing the Landing Obligation 
At the time of writing, the European Commission has established a delegated act for the demersal 
fisheries which will be subject to the Landing Obligation in the North Sea and Skagerrak as of 1 
January 2016 (European Commission 2015b). Despite advice from the North Sea Advisory Council 
(NSAC) calling for an introduction of the Landing Obligation on a species-by-species manner, the 
European Commission has, in line with the Joint Recommendation of the Scheveningen Group, 
decided to introduce the Landing Obligation both on a species-by-species and fisheries-by-fisheries 
manner (European Commission 2015b; c; Scheveningen Group 2015). The introduction of the Landing 
Obligation in this manner is not viewed as superior to a solely species-by-species introduction by 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
STECF, DTU Aqua and the Danish Fishermen Association (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2014a). However, the 
Scheveningen High Level Group believe that a species-by-species introduction will be in violation with 
the CFP, which is why the introduction is done on a fishery basis as well (European Commission 
2015b; Scheveningen Group 2015). Accordingly the following catches, caught by trawl or beam trawl, 
has from 1 January 2016 been subject to the Landing Obligation in the North Sea, Skagerrak, Kattegat 
and ICES division area IIa (European Commission 2015b; c): 
 catches of saithe (if caught by a saithe targeting vessel), plaice, haddock and deep sea prawn – 
if caught as a bycatch – when caught by trawl with a mesh size equal to or larger than 100 mm. 
 Catches of Norway lobster, common sole and deep sea prawn – if caught as bycatch -  for trawl 
with a mesh size of 80-99 mm, except for ICES area IIIa where the mesh size is 70-99 mm and 
all catches of haddock is subject to the Landing Obligation as well. 
 All catches of deep sea prawn by trawl with a mesh size of 32-69 mm. 
 All catches of plaice and all bycatch of deep sea prawn caught by beam trawl with a mesh size 
equal to or larger than 120 mm. 
 All catches of common sole and bycatch of deep sea prawn caught by beam trawl with a mesh 
size of 80-119 mm.  
This also implies that a number of species, for instance cod, will be subject to the Landing Obligation 
in the North Sea and Skagerrak later than 2016, but no later than 2019.  
Another important notion is that discarding will not end when the Landing Obligation is in full effect in 
2019. Since the Landing Obligation applies to all species under TAC, discarding is allowed for species 
without a TAC (Salomon et al. 2014). In effect, this will divide unwanted catches into two overall 
groups: bycatch, which is discarded, and bycatch, which previously could be discarded, but have to be 
landed with the Landing Obligation. It is the handling of the last group – what ICES currently refers to 
as “unwanted catch” – that the fisheries, fish produce industry and fisheries management need to 
adapt to. “Unwanted catch” is in this thesis referred to as “keepscards” as it is fish that is unwanted 
and previously would have been discarded but now has to be retained. This catch proportion should 
be distinguished from the unwanted bycatch than can be discarded legally after the full 
==PAGE:14==

implementation of the Landing Obligation. Figure 1 gives a visualization of the difference in catch 
handling and landings after the Landing Obligation is introduced.

A major contributor to keepscards is undersized fish. Undersized fish is fish that prior to the Landing 
Obligation would be discarded because they were below the Minimum Landing Size (Larsen et al. 
2013). To distinguish that such undersized fish has to be landed, the Minimum Landing Size (MLS) is 
renamed to Minimum Conservation Reference Size (MCRS) under the CFP of 2013 (Larsen et al. 2013; 
European Commission 2016a). 
Current Danish implementation process of the Landing Obligation 
The following section is based on minutes from the Danish AgriFish Agency. To ensure a successful 
implementation of the Landing Obligation, the Danish AgriFish Agency has held meetings in 2014 and 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
2015 on the introduction of the Landing Obligation with representatives from the Danish Fishermen 
Associations (DFPO and DPPO) and DTU Aqua (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2014b; c, 2015a; b). A topic 
often raised in these meetings have been concerns from the fishing industry on whether the practical 
implementations of the Landing Obligation will come in a form that ensures an equal playing field 
between all fisheries and all areas. The view of the industry is basically, that exemptions from the 
Landing Obligation shall be applicable to all types of fisheries and all Member States in order to avoid 
unfair competition or advantages between national fisheries or gear types due to the legislation 
(NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2014b; c; d, 2015a; b). Due to this, the Danish fishing industry have advocated 
for an introduction of the Landing Obligation in a species-by-species manner, which, as mentioned in 
the previous section, is not how the regulation will come into effect (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2014a).  
Simultaneously with this process, a trial on the impact of the Landing Obligation on the Danish 
industrial fishery and a trial on the impact of the Landing Obligation on the Danish fisheries in the 
Baltic Sea were carried out in the autumn of 2014. The conclusions from the trial in the industrial 
fishery were (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2014e): 
 Vessel self-sampling estimates and AgriFish Agency samplings are roughly equal overall 
 The requirement for all catches above 50 kg to be registered within a 10% tolerance limit is 
seen as unreasonable 
 There are some issues on the definition of the target species for some vessels since their 
catches are close to 50/50 between Norway pout and sprat 
 Overall conclusion: The AgriFish Agency must give guidance and allow the individual vessel to 
adjust its fishery to the Landing Obligation on its own  
The conclusions from the trial on the fishery in the Baltic Sea were (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2015c): 
 The Landing Obligation on cod seems to be of no significant concern at the moment 
 The Landing Obligation on plaice can become a problem since large quantities of undersized 
plaice and flounder have been registered. These catches risk depleting vessel storage capacity 
and it is recommended to exempt flounder and plaice from the Landing Obligation in the Baltic 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
Sea. Instead, unwanted catches of plaice or flounder shall be discarded swiftly in order to 
ensure a high survival chance 
After the introduction of the Landing Obligation in January 2015 for the industrial fisheries and in the 
Baltic Sea, the Danish Fishermen Association has been pleased with the Danish AgriFish Agency’s 
policy of mainly acting as advisors on the Landing Obligation, instead of initiating full enforcement 
straight away. However, the industry has raised the following issues (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2015d): 
 The quota levels are seen as too low 
 The requirement for the logbooks to be filled in a haul-by-haul basis is seen as a nuisance, 
especially for smaller vessels 
 The regulations on the storage of unwanted catches are seen as confusing 
Regarding the quota levels, the Danish AgriFish Agency respond that the quota level is due to the 
stock assessment for the Baltic Sea and that quota levels would have been lower if the extra quota 
due to the Landing Obligation had not been added. Additionally, the Agency clarify the regulations 
regarding storage of unwanted catches (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2015d). The initial response to the 
industry’s concern on the requirement for reporting in the logbooks is that although the requirement 
is a national decision, several other Member States have the same requirement and the requirement 
is beneficial for the data collection used for the stock assessments (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2015d).  
Possible uses of the landed bycatch 
The requirement to land bycatches, including undersized fish, rather than discarding it presents 
challenges but also new market opportunities. Fish, that previously had to be discarded, will now 
enter the fish produce industry and thereby add to the available resources, provided that the Landing 
Obligation is implemented with adequate quota uplifts (Haugan & Laugesen 2015; Ravensbeck et al. 
2015). Commissioned by the Danish AgriFish Agency, the consulting company, COWI, made a report in 
2015 on possible added-values created by the Landing Obligation (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 2014f). The 
report is only based on the Danish demersal fisheries because the proportion of bycatch in the pelagic 
fisheries are seen as negligible (Haugan & Laugesen 2015).  COWI identified four possible scenarios 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
for the handling of the bycatch taken ashore. Only one of these scenarios is legal at the time of 
writing (Haugan & Laugesen 2015): 
1) Taking keepscards ashore as whole fish and selling it for non-human consumption, that is 
for fishmeal or fish oil (legal) 
2) Taking keepscards ashore as whole fish and sell it for human consumption (illegal) 
3) Taking keepscards ashore as ensilage and sell it for fishmeal or fish oil (illegal) 
4) Taking keepscards ashore as ensilage and sell it as bioenergy (illegal) 
According to the report, only scenario 2, where keepscards are sold for human consumption could 
give added revenue to all vessels. Scenario 1 would only add to the revenue, if vessels have spare 
storage which can be used to store the keepscards. The reason for the difference in added value 
between scenario 1 and scenario 2 is the limited price received for fish sold for fishmeal     1.5 
DKK/kg) compared to fish sold for human consumption (the price per kg for human consumption 
varies greatly depending on species. Lowest average price in the report is plaice at 4.49 DDK/kg and 
highest is Norway lobster at 62.56 DKK/kg) (Haugan & Laugesen 2015). Storing keepscards as ensilage 
had a negative impact on the revenue for small (12-18 meters in length) and medium-sized (18-24 
meters in length) fishing vessels. Large vessels (>24 meters in length) however, was estimated to 
increase their revenue if scenario 3 became an option (Haugan & Laugesen 2015). Ensilage has been 
used in the Danish fisheries in the 1960s and 1970s but was prohibited because no methods to verify 
the ensilage content existed (Larsen et al. 2013). Today however, the use of quantitative DNA testing 
and REM can alter this situation (Larsen et al. 2013). 
In addition to the above mentioned uses marketing of keepscards for animal food (livestock, 
aquaculture or pet food), fish bait, cosmetic products, pharmaceutical products or fertilizers may also 
be possible (Viðarsson et al. 2016). However, catches must be handled appropriately according to the 
intended use. Experiences from Iceland support that adapting the handling of fish produce to 
optimize the use of all landings is possible, but it takes time to achieve (Vidarsson et al. 2015; 
Viðarsson et al. 2016).

Remote Electronic Monitoring with CCTV 
The use of REM with CCTV have been tested in various fisheries worldwide, such as the hook and line 
groundfish fishery in Canada (McElderry, Schrader & Illingworth 2003), demersal gillnet fishery and 
the Eastern tuna and billfish fishery in Australia (Evans & Molony 2011; Piasente et al. 2012), mixed 
species fisheries in England (Marine Management Organisation 2013a; b, 2014) and Scotland (Marine 
Scotland 2011, 2013; Needle et al. 2015), mixed demersal fisheries in the Netherlands (van Helmond, 
Chen & Poos 2015; van Helmond et al. 2016), tuna purse seine fisheries in the Atlantic, Indian and 
Pacific Ocean (Ruiz et al. 2015), trawl and set net fishery in New Zealand (McElderry et al. 2007), 
groundfish trawl in the US (McElderry et al. 2008) and demersal fisheries in Denmark (Dalskov & 
Kindt-Larsen 2009; Dalskov et al. 2012; Kindt‐Larsen et al. 2012; Mortensen et al. 2015; Ulrich et al. 
2015). In the Canadian case, the use of REM with CCTV has moved beyond the initial testing as CCTV 
has been a requirement since 2006 for all vessels wishing to operate in the fishery if they are not 
carrying an on-board observer on fishing trips (Stanley et al. 2011).  
Several studies have found the cost of REM with CCTV to be lower than a similar coverage with on-
board observers (McElderry & Turris 2008; Kindt-Larsen et al. 2011; Needle et al. 2015). Ongoing 
validation between the two methods is advised to ensure optimal estimations (Needle et al. 2015). 
In 2015, the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) commissioned a report on the use of REM with CCTV 
(Course 2015). In the report, the benefits and shortcomings of REM with CCTV are compared to other 
traditional monitoring systems. Assuming hardware replacement every 5 years and 90% funding of 
hardware costs by the European Maritime and Fisheries Fund (EMFF), the cost of fitting fishing vessels 
with REM with CCTV and monitoring with a 10% video audit is estimated at (Course 2015): 
 All UK fishing vessels (£29.95m per year) 
 All UK fishing vessels above 10 meter in length (£6.38m per year) 
 All UK fishing vessels above 15 meter in length (£3.17m per year) 
In the report, the total spending on fisheries control in the UK for the financial year 2013/2014 is 
reported at £20.13m.  
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
The estimations based on UK vessels are extrapolated to the entire EU fishing fleet in the report, 
whereby the following cost estimations are made (Course 2015): 
 All EU fishing vessels fitted with REM with CCTV and 10% video audit – cost: £409m / 567m € 
per year 
 All EU fishing vessels above 10 meter in length with REM with CCTV and 10% video audit – 
cost: £88m / 122m € per year 
 All EU fishing vessels above 15 meter in length with REM with CCTV and 10% video audit – 
cost: £39m / 54m € per year 
Exclusion of fishing vessels smaller than 10 meters in length will reduce the costs associated with REM 
and CCTV markedly. Further cost reductions are possible by lowering the percentage of video audited. 
It should be noted that a requirement for REM with CCTV based on vessel length will impact the EU 
fisheries differently, because the largest part of landings in countries like Greece and Romania are 
caught by small scale vessels (<12 meters in length) compared to the countries bordering the Baltic 
and North Sea, where landings predominantly come from larger vessels (STECF 2015). 
Danish trials on Catch Quota Management and Remote Electronic Monitoring  
The first Danish trial using REM with CCTV on-board fishing vessels took place in 2008/2009 and 
covered six commercial fishing vessels (one gillnetter, one Danish seiner and four trawlers). The trial 
focused on the catches and discards of cod (Gadus morhua) and participating vessels were given a 
100% increase in cod quota for participation. The additional quota was given as an incentive to 
participate and because all catches of cod would be counted against the quota (Ulrich et al. 2015). 
The main purpose of the trial was to test the feasibility of REM systems as a tool for FDF in the Danish 
fisheries. The REM system was found to be a reliable, versatile and a cost-effective tool (Dalskov & 
Kindt-Larsen 2009; Kindt-Larsen et al. 2011). Additionally, it was found that participating vessels 
landed a higher proportion of small sized cod than similar non-participating vessels (Kindt-Larsen et 
al. 2011). A subsequent trial was launched in 2010 in a collaboration between DTU Aqua and the 
Danish AgriFish Agency (at that time termed the Danish Directorate of Fisheries) (Dalskov, Håkansson 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
& Olesen 2011). This trial used the same REM systems as in 2008/2009 and focused on 1) the 
feasibility of a CQM verified by REM systems to account for all catches in order to reduce discards, 2) 
the potential to increase the scientific data and 3) the encouragement for a more selective fishing. 
Estimated discards were divided into two groups: Cod and others, meaning that the discards of cod in 
kg was accounted for, whereas the discard of all other species was aggregated into one large pool 
(Dalskov et al. 2011; Plet-Hansen et al. 2015). Seven vessels participated in the 2010 trial, all of which 
were demersal trawlers (Dalskov et al. 2011). The cod quota uplift for trial participation was 30% in 
the 2010 trial and has remained at this level in the subsequent extensions of this trial, which took 
place annually from 2010 to 2016 (Ulrich et al. 2015; Bergsson 2016a). In all, five demersal trawlers 
have continuously participated in the trial from 2010 to 2014 and six demersal trawlers have 
continuously participated from 2011 to 2014. No demersal trawlers have entered the trial later than 
2011 (Dalskov et al. 2012; Plet-Hansen et al. 2015). The trial was extended in 2015 and 2016 as well 
but some of the discard estimation conditions and the REM system has changed. Since 2015, the 
discards of haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus), whiting (Merlangius merlangus) and saithe 
(Pollachius virens) have been estimated on a species level by video audit. In October 2015 hake 
(Merluccius merluccius) was included in the estimates on a species level as well (Bergsson 2016a). 
Since 2015 a new REM system, provided by Anchor Lab, has been used. The new REM system has 
enhanced features compared to the previous system which include infrared camera to record when 
lights are off and the REM data is uploaded directly to a secure server at the Danish AgriFish Agency 
office in Copenhagen. The upload of data automatically begins when the CDF vessel is within range of 
WiFi or 4G/3G network (Anchor Lab 2016a; b; c; d). Additionally, the video audit unit at the AgriFish 
Agency has developed a system for automatic calculation of fish weight based on length and a grid 
overlay that can add distances in cm to the video screen. The grid overlay has been fully implemented 
in the REM system in 2016, whereby length measures can be done based on the number of pixels on 
the footage and a tool to measure fish which have a curved or skewed position on the discard band 
has also been added (Bergsson 2016b).  
The quota system for demersal fisheries in Denmark has been based on Individual Transferable 
Quotas (ITQ) since 2007 (Andersen et al. 2007; Petter Johnsen & Eliasen 2011). This means that 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
although the overall fisheries management in Denmark is based on the CFP, Danish fishers can rent, 
buy or swap quota from other Danish fishers. This gives Danish fishers the possibility to acquire 
additional quota instead of stopping their fisheries when a certain quota is exhausted, an option also 
applying to vessels participating in CQM verified by REM (Petter Johnsen & Eliasen 2011; Dalskov et 
al. 2012). Whether the fishers end their fisheries or acquire additional quota is then up to the quota 
prices and the economic reasoning of the individual quota-holder. 
Perhaps the most illustrative result so far from the Danish trials on FDF is illustrated in figure 2. The 
figure, presented in Ulrich et al. 2013, show the percentage of the landing sizes of cod from the North 
Sea per trip (Ulrich et al. 2013). For the vessel presented in this figure, the landed percentage of cod 
in sorting class 5 (the smallest sorting class) per trip, increase markedly after participation in the FDF 
trial.

Critique has been raised as to whether the Danish trials on FDF select for certain vessels because it is 
more attractive to enter the trial for vessels which (Msomphora & Aanesen 2015): 
 Already have low discard levels, since these vessels will gain the most from implementing 
CQM rules 
 Have large cod quotas, since these vessels will gain the most from the 30% increase in cod

==PAGE:22==

 Are willing to change target species, since the additional cod quota may make it more 
profitable to target cod 
The latest experiences from the Danish trials on FDF are published in the article by Ulrich et al. and 
the report by Plet-Hansen et al. (Plet-Hansen et al. 2015; Ulrich et al. 2015). Ulrich et al. use the data 
collected in the trials from 2008 to 2014. Additionally, data from on-board observers is used to 
compare the discard levels based on REM with CCTV, observers and the fishers’ self-sampling reports 
in the electronic logbook (eLog) (Ulrich et al. 2015). The study shows that discards are reduced overall 
for vessels participating in the trial. The study also finds, that reporting in the eLog is improved to a 
level where eLog reporting can be used as a source of information (Ulrich et al. 2015). Additionally it is 
shown that smaller, legal cod catches are more likely to be landed for vessels participating in the trial. 
However, uncertainties remain, especially regarding discard estimates. On-board observer estimates 
are higher than those reported in the logbooks which in turn are higher than those reported by video 
auditors. The discard estimates by the three different methods overall result in the following pattern 
(Ulrich et al. 2015): 
Discard estimateOn-board Observer > Discard estimateSelf-sampling  >  Discard estimateVideo audit 
Possible reasons for this are stated as different measures for the weight of baskets used for discards 
and impaired camera vision under certain conditions which reduce the ability of video auditors to 
estimate discards (Ulrich et al. 2015). 
Plet-Hansen et al. use the trial on CQM with REM and CCTV data collected from 2010 to 2014 for 
vessels staying in the trial from their year of entry until 2014 (Plet-Hansen et al. 2015). In the report, a 
comparison of on-board observer discard estimates between vessels participating in CQM with REM 
and CCTV and vessels not participating in CQM with REM and CCTV is done. The same difference in 
discard estimates between on-board observers, self-sampling and video viewers is found as in Ulrich 
et al. 2015, which was to be expected since a large amount of the data is the same. Based on on-
board observer estimates, the report finds that discards are markedly lower for demersal trawlers 
participating in CQM with REM and CCTV in the North Sea compared to those not participating in 
CQM with REM and CCTV. The report investigates the change in malfunctions on video footage and 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
finds that the number of video footage with errors influencing or rendering discard estimation 
impossible is declining over the years. The highest average video error percentage per vessels is 
13.0% in 2011 declining to the lowest average video error percentage per vessels at 2.6% in 2014 
(Plet-Hansen et al. 2015). This indicate that the reliability of the REM system increase as user 
experience grow. Finally, the report looks into possible choke species under a Landing Obligation 
(Plet-Hansen et al. 2015). Choke species are species that act as bottlenecks under a discard ban, 
because their quota tend to be depleted faster than the quota of other target species. With the 
Landing Obligation implemented, vessels must either stop fishing or acquire additional quota if their 
quota limit has been reached for a species (Hatcher 2013; Sigurðardóttir et al. 2015). The report 
identifies cod and whiting as two species that could act as choke species in the Danish North Sea 
mixed demersal fisheries (Plet-Hansen et al. 2015).

Thesis Objectives 
The primary aim of this thesis is to investigate stakeholder opinion towards the Landing Obligation 
and the use of video documentation in fisheries management. Additionally, some of the critique 
raised against the Danish trial on CQM verified by REM with CCTV is explored and behavioral effects 
that may be ascribed to participation in the Danish trial on CQM verified by REM with CCTV are 
examined. Because the main bulk of participating vessels were demersal trawlers and beause discard 
rates tend to be larger for this gear type than for other gear types (Jennings et al. 2001b; Kennelly & 
Broadhurst 2002; Quirijns et al. 2014; Hedley et al. 2015), the thesis focusses on demersal trawlers.  
In line with the above, the following investigations and questions are examined in the thesis:  
1. A qualitative study on fishers’ view on the Landing Obligation and on the use of video 
documentation as a control measure. 
2. A qualitative study on fishery inspectors’ view on the Landing Obligation and on the use of 
video documentation as a control measure. 
3. Is there a shift in target species after vessel participation in the CQM with REM and CCTV 
trial?  
4. Is there a shift in the spatial distribution of fishing effort after vessel participation in CQM 
with REM and CCTV? 
In the rest of the thesis, the Danish trial on CQM with REM and CCTV, beginning in 2010 and still 
running at the time of writing, will be referred to as “Camera Documented Fisheries”  CDF). 
Findings in this thesis will contribute to the EU Horizon 2020 project DiscardLess headed by DTU Aqua 
on the issue of a gradual elimination of discards in the European fisheries (Frangoudes et al. 2015).

Material and methods 
Interview questionnaires for fishers 
Interviews regarding the opinion on the Landing Obligation and the use of video documentation as a 
control tool were conducted from 7 October to 9 November 2015. A questionnaire was developed 
prior to the interviews (please go to Appendix G for the interview questionnaire in Danish and 
Appendix I for English version). Fishers willing to participate went through the questionnaire together 
with the interviewer, who would note the reply to each question. Interviews were not voice recorded. 
The questionnaire was divided into three overall sections: Camera documented fisheries, camera 
documented fisheries with the Landing Obligation and the Landing Obligation. The main focuses in 
the interview questionnaire were on the camera documented fisheries by itself and on the Landing 
Obligation by itself. The design of the questions was a balance between three overall goals: 
 Incorporation of the listed topics from the H2020 DiscardLess project (Frangoudes et al. 2015) 
 Enable Danish fishers to elaborate on their view on FDF with video documentation and the 
Landing Obligation 
 Keep the duration of the interview at a level where fishers would be willing to participate for 
free 
Questions relating to the positive or negative effect on the marine environment and the fisheries 
were close-ended but allowed for an open-ended explanation. All other questions were open-ended. 
The advantage of open-ended questions is that it does not set a limit for the answers to the question 
(Schuman & Presser 1979; University of Waterloo 2016). A disadvantage to open-ended questions 
include difference in response detail between participants and more difficulty in statistical 
comparison between responses (Schuman & Presser 1979; University of Waterloo 2016).  The 
interview questionnaire was developed in collaboration with Søren Q. Eliassen, Department of 
Development and Planning, Aalborg University and Lotte Kindt-Larsen, Section for Ecosystem based 
Marine Management, National Institute for Aquatic Resources, DTU Aqua, who both have experience 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
on conducting interviews with fishers (Kindt-Larsen et al. 2015; Mortensen et al. 2015). Additionally, a 
fishery consultant from the industry went through the questions with the purpose of enhancing the 
wording and the scope to the target group. Based on feedback from these collaborators it was 
deemed that a higher proportion of responses would be achieved if fishers were contacted directly by 
telephone or face-to-face rather than by mail.  
Of 44 fishers approached from 7.-9. September 2015 at the 24th International Fisheries Exhibition 
DanFish held in Aalborg, Denmark, 14 interviews were conducted face-to-face. Three fishers were 
approached for an interview at Strandby Fish Auction’s Exhibition stand, five fishers were approached 
at the Danish Fishermen’s Association exhibition stand, four fishers were approached at Port of 
Hanstholm’s Exhibition stand and 32 fishers were approached at the Danish AgriFish Agency’s 
Exhibition stand. All 14 fishers willing to participate in an interview were approached at the Danish 
AgriFish Agency’s Exhibition stand, where the interviews were conducted as well. To get responses 
from more fishers and due to practical issues, interviews at the DanFish Exhibition were conducted by 
two interviewers. Kristian S. Plet-Hansen conducted ten interviews and a deputy fisheries inspector 
from the Danish AgriFish Agency conducted four interviews.  
In order to reach additional fishers, eight interviews were conducted by telephone from 14 October to 
9 November 2015. Fishers interviewed by telephone had given their contact information and declared 
their willingness to participate in a telephone interview at a local meeting on camera documented 
fisheries where the Danish AgriFish Agency, DTU Aqua, the Danish Fishermen’s Association and local 
fishers in Hanstholm participated, held in Denmark on 18 August 2015. All eight telephone interviews 
were conducted by Kristian S. Plet-Hansen.  
Interview time ranged between 25 to 45 min. for both interviews conducted face-to-face or by 
telephone. Difference in time range of interviews mainly depended on the number and length of 
elaborations made by fishers to clarify their responses to the questions. In all 22 fishers have been 
interviewed. With the total number of registered fishers in Denmark being 4796 in 2014 (Danmarks 
Fiskeriforening Producent Organisation 2015), the interviews represent    0.5% of Danish fishers, 
implying that the findings shall be seen as solely qualitative.  
==PAGE:27==

All responses to the open-ended questions were put into categories based on the similarity of the 
answers. These categories give the basis for the presentation of the data and can be seen in the tables 
of the result section. Responses of particular note have either been given their own category, been 
quoted in the text or both.  
Questionnaires for fishery inspectors 
Questionnaires regarding the opinion on the Landing Obligation and the use of video documentation 
as a control tool were developed and distributed among fishery inspectors from the Danish AgriFish 
Agency (please go to Appendix H for the questionnaire in Danish and Appendix J for English version). 
Questions were developed to mimic the interview questions for fishers, but with management 
specifics related to the achievement of an appropriate fisheries control. Questions relating to the 
rating of control measures and the positive or negative effect on the marine environment and 
fisheries were close-ended but allowed for an open-ended explanation. All other questions were 
open-ended. The questions were pilot tested on a Senior Fisheries Officer from the Danish AgriFish 
Agency who work with REM and CCTV in the fisheries control. The practical distribution of the 
questionnaires was discussed with a Chief Officer in the Danish Fisheries Control. Based on the 
feedback from these collaborators it was deemed that a higher proportion of responses to the 
questions would be achieved if fishery inspectors could receive and respond on the questionnaire by 
email. The Chief Officer in the Danish Fisheries Control and Kristian S. Plet-Hansen facilitated the 
distribution of the emails to all relevant sections and personnel in the Danish AgriFish Agency. In all, 
30 fishery inspectors answered the questionnaire. With the total number of relevant fishery 
inspectors in Denmark being 95 at the time of writing, the questionnaires represent 31.6% of Danish 
fishery inspectors.

Change in landed species  
Data on landings from the North Sea and Skagerrak was obtained through eLog reports, which have 
been mandatory for all vessels above 12m in length since 2012, and vessels above 15m in length prior 
to 2012 (European Commission 2016b). Reported landings for seven commercially important species 
(saithe (Pollachius virens), plaice (Pleuronectes platessa), cod (Gadus morhua), whiting (Merlangius 
merlangus) , haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus), hake (Merluccius merluccius) and Norway lobster 
(Nephrops norvegicus)) were compared before and after commencement of vessel participation in the 
CDF trial. These species were chosen because the Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark have 
suggested these as the main target species for the Danish demersal fisheries (NaturErhvervstyrelsen 
2015e). Although sole (Solea solea) is mentioned as well, this species is excluded because it mainly is 
caught by beam trawlers (ICES 2015a) and landings of sole were negligible for the investigated 
vessels. 
The comparison was done to investigate whether demersal trawlers participating in the CDF trial 
changed their target species due to the extra quota on cod given for participation in the trial. Nine 
demersal trawlers were identified which entered the trial in 2010 or 2011, had no prior participation 
in a FDF trial and remained participants in the CDF trial until 2015. Landings of each of the seven 
species were compiled per fishing trip for each of the nine participating vessel. Similarly, the same 
analysis was performed for a control group of six vessels not participating in a FDF trial. Control 
vessels were selected by; 1) identifying Danish vessels with similar fishing gear and mesh size as 
vessels participating in CDF. 2) Selecting the vessels that land the largest amount of cod from the 
North Sea without participating in a FDF trial during the years in question. Control vessels had to 1) 
have VMS data before 2010, 2) land cod caught by bottom otter trawl with a mesh size ≥ 120 mm 
from the North Sea, and 3) begin their fishery no later than 2008 and continue until 2015 in order to 
be suitable for a comparison with the CDF vessels. It turned out to be difficult to have a large control 
group due to these constraints. Vessels suited for one criteria did often not fulfill another criterion. 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
For instance, some suitable vessels participated in another FDF trial during the years in question, 
whereas others did not have VMS data in 2009 or ended their fishery before 2015. Consequently, the 
control group consists of six vessels. The CDF trial mainly took place in the North Sea and Skagerrak. 
Landings from other areas are not included in the analysis (Dalskov et al. 2012; Ulrich et al. 2015).  
The timeframe of the analysis is from 2008-2015. Years before trial participation are 2008 and 2009 
and years after trial participation are 2011-2015. Exclusion of 2010 from the comparison was done for 
two reasons: Some of the vessels CDF vessels entered the trial in April or May 2010, whereas all CDF 
vessels had entered the trial by 1 January 2011. At the same time, the TAC set for cod in 2010 was the 
highest within the timeframe 2008-2014 (ICES 2015b). The high TAC on cod for 2010 could potentially 
influence the comparison. 
The hypotheses are:  
 H0: Reported landings per trip of the seven species during the years 2008-2009 are the same 
as during the years 2011-2015. 
 H1: Reported landings per trip of the seven species during the years 2008-2009 are different 
than during the years 2011-2015. 
A Shapiro-Wilk test for normal distribution was performed for each data group. The first test did not 
support a satisfactory normal distribution of the data. Various transformations of the data were 
investigated, and based on a Box-Cox Power transformation λ-value of around 0.2 for the data, 
combined with the plotted distribution of the data, it was deemed that square root and log10 +1 
transformation should be the most appropriate methods for transformation. However, these 
transformations also proved unable to induce normal distribution to the data. This could be due to 
the essentially non-random “sampling” method that commercial landings represent. Not only do 
fishers actively target certain areas and species, they also filter the catch before landing it (the 
practice of discarding). After the attempts to induce normal distribution had failed for almost all of 
the data groups (61 groups out of 63 possible), the non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test was 
selected for the analysis. The Mann-Whitney U test can be used on data which is not normal 
distributed. The test compares the medians of unmatched samples by rank and can be used on 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
samples of uneven size. In order to carry out the Mann-Whitney U test it is necessary to have 
sufficient data points, which meant inserting up to four zeros in cases where landings of a species 
occurred less than four times over the years before or after CDF (Fowler & Cohen 1990).  
The statistical analysis is done for landings per trip in order to get sufficient sample points. To account 
for changes occurring on a yearly basis, the total landings per year and the change in total landings 
per year since 2008 is plotted for the CDF and control vessels. A standardized plot of the change in 
landings since 2008 is plotted as well. Standardization was done according to the following formula:

Spatial distribution of fishing areas 
The VMS data from the nine demersal trawlers, participating in the CDF trial and the control group of 
six comparable vessels, was also used to investigate possible changes in fishing areas after the CDF 
trial began. VMS (Vessel Monitoring System) is a monitoring system that provides data on speed, 
course and position to the fisheries management. The system has been mandatory for all EU vessels 
above 12 m in length since 2012 and vessels above 15 m in length prior to 2012 (European 
Commission 2016b). 
The VMS data for the fifteen demersal trawlers were analyzed to distinguish fishing activities from 
steaming or drifting/port time based on the speed profile of each vessel. Analyses were done using 
the VMStools package for R (Hintzen et al. 2012). Analyses were done for 2009 as the year before 
onset of CDF trial and 2011 as the year after onset of CDF trial. Areas where vessels trawled were 
plotted on a map for the North Sea, Skagerrak and the Western Baltic Sea based on a grid overlay. 
Each VMS position assessed as a fishing activity was assigned to a grid cell. All grid cells where a 
fishing activity occur, are colored with a grey color bar for 2009 and a red color bar for 2011. To assess 
the effort, the time difference between reported positions was calculated. Grid cells with repeated 
VMS positions and/or high fishing effort duration get a darker coloration in the plots than grids with 
low counts of VMS positions and/or low allocated fishing effort. The total area where fishing activity 
had occurred was calculated based on the number of grids associated with a fishing activity. A grid cell 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
was set at 0.1o by 0.05o, or approximately 62km2. By multiplying the number of grid cells with the area 
covered by each grid cell, an approximation of the total fishing area for each vessel and vessel group 
per year was calculated.

Non-responders

do that oh no! But we do not land cod of sorting class 5 at 15 DKK pr. kg when we can continue fishing 
after sorting class 3. Does it say “Idiot” on my back?! But oh no! You are not allowed to do that.”

General trends in responses, camera documented fisheries 
All fishers who have participated in CDF can say something positive about the idea and although the 
issue of constant surveillance is raised as a negative effect too for this group, the CDF fishers have 
more detail on their critique of CDF than non-CDF responders. All non-CDF responders state they are 
against the use of REM with CCTV, whereas the majority of CDF responders are for the use, including 
a risk-based approach. Free selection of gear is not seen as an adequate benefit for participation in 
REM with CCTV for the majority of responders. Storage of bycatch as ensilage (three out of 22 
responders) and quota uplifts (14 out of 22 responders) are the main benefits seen as adequate for 
having REM with CCTV on-board. 
Fishery inspectors ranked at-sea control and on-board observers as more accurate control measures 
than REM with CCTV. Fishery inspectors’ main issues regarding REM with CCTV are the constant 
surveillance and possibilities to practice non-compliance by avoiding video detection. The groups for 
and the group neither for nor against the use of REM with CCTV are almost equal in size (12 out of 30 
being for and 13 out of 30 being neither for nor against). 
General trends in responses, the Landing Obligation 
Fishers mainly point to storage issues, choke species and confusion on the regulations as the main 
issues with the Landing Obligation. 86% of the responding fishers do not believe the Landing 
Obligation will have a positive effect on the marine environment and 68% believe the effect of the 
Landing Obligation will be negative for the marine environment. 73% of the responding fishers do not 
believe the Landing Obligation will have a positive effect on the Danish fisheries and 64% believe the 
effect of the Landing Obligation will be negative for the Danish fisheries. 
Fishery inspectors mainly point to the economic feasibility of the fisheries as the challenge for the 
fishers with the Landing Obligation. The main challenge for the fishery control with the Landing 
Obligation is stated as controlling and enforcing the regulation by 83% of the fishery inspectors.

Discussion 
Change in spatial distribution 
Three out of ten interviewed CDF participating fishers stated that they changed fishing grounds 
slightly more after they entered the CDF trial. All three believed they had changed the way they 
conducted their fishery, due to their participation in the CDF trial. A Dutch FDF pilot study on CQM 
verified with REM and CCTV showed an overall change in spatial distribution of larger vessels towards 
the north after they started to participate in FDF. This change was not reported for non-participating 
vessels in the Dutch study. The change coincided with large increases in landed cod and some 
participating skippers stated they had shifted their fishing area to target cod, because of the cod 
quota uplift given for trial participation in the Dutch study (van Helmond et al. 2016). 
Taken as a whole, the fishing activities for vessels participating in the CDF trial cover a larger area in 
2011 than in 2009. However, summing the change in the covered fishing areas for the vessels 
individually leads to a calculated decrease in fishing area. This means that the vessels participating in 
CDF have decreased their individual fishing area, but the vessel group as a whole has spread out. The 
total days spent at sea decreased with 12% for CDF participants from 2009 to 2011. The control group 
also cover a larger area in 2011 than in 2009, but unlike the CDF vessels, the summed change in 
fishing area for individual control vessels also increase, meaning that control vessels have not spread 
out compared to each other as CDF vessels have. Additionally, control vessels have a total increase in 
days spent at sea of roughly 31%. This suggest that the increase in fishing area for control vessels is 
linked to the change in days at sea. 
Overall, three spatial groups of CDF vessels can be made:  
1) Vessels mainly operating in the Norwegian trench (CDF vessels no. 1, 4, 5, 6, 7 and 8) 
2) Vessels mainly operating in the central North Sea area (CDF vessels no. 2 and 3) 
3) Vessel mainly operating in the Norwegian trench but switching to Skagerrak after CDF begins 
(CDF vessel no. 9). 
==PAGE:71==
71 
 
None of the control vessels exhibit the same pattern as the majority of CDF vessels that mainly 
operate in the Norwegian trench, although some spatial overlap occurs in Skagerrak. The control 
vessel with a spatial distribution closest to the Norwegian trench is control vessel C2, but the northern 
distribution is more westward towards the Shetland Islands and the vessel also operate a lot in the 
central North Sea. Control vessels are more similar in spatial distribution with CDF vessels no. 2 and 
no. 3, although three of the control vessels mainly operate in Skagerrak (control vessels C1, C3 and 
C6). CDF vessel no. 9 stand out with a particular change in fishing area. CDF vessel no. 9 show the 
opposite trend of the rest of the CDF vessels and has the biggest change in fishing area, both 
quantitatively with a change in covered km2 of around 60% and in the geographical area. Vessel no. 9 
begin to concentrate its effort in Skagerrak after participation in CDF and increase its landings of 
plaice at the same time.  
Overall, the CDF vessels tend to maintain their fishing area in roughly the same area, although there is 
a slightly more north and westward distribution of the fishing areas. The control vessels also have a 
more westward and northern distribution pattern in 2011 than in 2009, especially for control vessel 
C2. The control vessels generally maintain a more southern distribution than the CDF vessels and do 
not target the Norwegian trench as the CDF vessels. Two of the control vessels increase their fishing 
area in the western Baltic, which also is seen for one CDF vessel (CDF vessel no. 3).  
A distinctive change in spatial distribution among CDF vessels is only apparent for CDF vessel no. 9, 
were cod landings decreased. Unlike the findings in the Dutch study, the Danish CDF vessels cannot be 
said to have changed their main fishing area.  
To establish whether some changes in spatial distribution can be associated with CDF, further analysis 
of fishing effort and distribution will be necessary. Firstly, the change in spatial distribution is not 
uniform for all vessels participating in the CDF trial in this study. Secondly, several factors influence 
the distribution of fishing areas and separating these factors is beyond the scope of this thesis. 
Thirdly, Norway operates a system of Real Time Closures to enforce its discard ban (Gullestad et al. 
2015). Fishers operating in Norwegian waters are likely to have changed fishing grounds in order to 
comply with the Norwegian regulations, regardless of their participation in CDF. As the investigated 
CDF participating vessels had substantial fishing activity occurring within the Norwegian EEZ, change 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
in fishing grounds may very well be due to the Norwegian regulations rather than participation in the 
CDF trial. A future analysis of the spatial distribution of CDF vessels compared to similar and 
contemporary Norwegian vessels might clarify the possible spatial effect of CQM verified by REM with 
CCTV. 
Change in landed species 
Two out of ten non-CDF fishers stated in the interview that they believe the Danish trials on FDF are 
skewed. They stated that the increase in cod quota makes the participating vessels change their 
fishery in order to target cod, which is found to be the case for large vessels the in the Dutch study 
presented above (van Helmond et al. 2016). Msomphora & Aanesen found that Danish vessels 
participating in the Danish CDF trial mainly get their income from cod and plaice, whereas Danish 
vessels not participating in the CDF trial get their main income from Norway lobster (Msomphora & 
Aanesen 2015). It is speculated in the article whether this difference occur prior to vessels 
participating in CDF or whether the trial have induced such a change, which would be in line with the 
critique raised by the above mentioned fishers. 
Overall, changes in landings per trip occur for both the CDF vessels and the control group. Likewise, 
the landings per year also fluctuate for CDF vessels and control vessels. Neither CDF or control vessels 
have decreased landings per trip of plaice and increased landings per year of plaice occur for both CDF 
and control vessels. The TAC on plaice in the North Sea and Skagerrak has increased in the timeframe 
of the analysis, especially from 2011 and forth (ICES 2015c). The increases in plaice landings may to a 
large degree be explained by this overall increase.  
Landings per trip of Norway lobster increase for one control vessel, decrease for one control vessel 
and are stagnant for four control vessels. However, for these four control vessels landings per year of 
Norway lobster is almost non-existing. One of the control vessels with substantial landings of Norway 
lobster have slightly decreasing landings per year, whereas the other control vessel with substantial 
landings of Norway lobster have large fluctuations in the landings between the years. Landings per 
trip of Norway lobster are stagnant or decreasing for CDF vessels and the landings per year tend to 
decrease or be almost non-existing for CDF vessels. The two CDF vessels mainly fishing in the central 
==PAGE:73==
73 
 
North Sea have as good as zero landings of Norway lobster for all years, which resemble the majority 
of control vessels that also operate in the central North Sea. The six CDF vessels mainly operating at 
the Norwegian trench and CDF vessel no. 9 have decreasing landings per year of Norway lobster, 
although fluctuations occur for CDF vessel no. 1. During the timeframe of the analysis, the Danish 
landings of Norway lobster decreased much more in the central and northern North Sea, as well as in 
the Norwegian Deep (ICES 2015d; e), than in Skagerrak (ICES 2015f). CDF vessels have substantial 
fishing areas outside of Skagerrak meaning that the decrease in landings of Norway lobster may be 
largely ascribed to reduced quotas at their main fishing areas, rather than because of a deliberate 
choice to shift away from fishing Norway lobster.   
Although the landings per trip of saithe increase for several CDF vessels but remain stagnant or 
decrease for control vessels, the landings per year of saithe reveal that CDF vessels mainly operating 
in the Norwegian trench have much larger catches of saithe than control vessels. Additionally, the 
standardized landings per year of saithe remain roughly stagnant, except for CDF vessel no. 9, which 
has decreased landings per of year of saithe, haddock and hake. For both vessel groups it is not 
apparent that saithe have been targeted more or less during the years in question, except for CDF 
vessels no. 9, where landings decrease.  
The change in landings per trip of whiting show significant increases for both control vessels and CDF 
vessels when comparing 2009 to 2011. However, this should largely be seen as a result of very low or 
zero landings occurring before 2010. In several cases, the landings of whiting are still minute after 
2010, meaning that the change in landings of whiting might be due to better reporting, rather than 
fishers increasingly targeting whiting.  
The changes in landings of hake and haddock both per trip and per year are mainly interesting for CDF 
vessel no. 9. This vessel mainly operated in the Norwegian trench prior to CDF participation and 
although a bit lower, its landings of hake and haddock resembled the other CDF vessels operating in 
the Norwegian trench in 2009. After CDF participation, CDF vessel no. 9 has declining landings of hake 
and haddock and begins to resemble control vessels and CDF vessels no. 2 and 3 more. This coincides 
with a shift in fishing area from the Norwegian trench to Skagerrak for CDF vessel no. 9. CDF vessel 
no. 9 has a decline in all landings of the investigated species, except for whiting and plaice. For 
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
whiting, the landings are quite minute, but the landings of plaice increase markedly after CDF 
participation. This change in landings does however not seem to support the notion that trial 
participants will change their fishery to target cod. It seems more likely that CDF vessel no. 9 has 
changed it fishery due to some other factor and is directing its fishery towards plaice, rather than cod.  
Landings of cod increase per trip and per year for seven out of nine CDF vessels with CDF vessels no. 
5, 6, 7 and 8 having a steady increase from 2008 and onward. Increased landings per trip of cod also 
occur for three out of six control vessels. Although the landings per year do not increase as much 
among control vessels from 2008 to 2015, there is no overall decrease in landings either. Four CDF 
vessels have a larger increase in landings of cod, than the control vessels. Because the reasoning for 
the cod quota uplift in the trial was that cod catches would be subject to a CQM scheme, the quota 
uplift was roughly set to correspond to the estimated discard level prior to trial participation. Vessels 
were still allowed to discard undersized cod meaning that the difference in the quota management 
was that these discards would count against their quota (Dalskov et al. 2011). Because the data used 
in this analysis is based on the vessels’ landings, the discard of undersized cod is not accounted for. It 
therefore implies that if a CDF vessel has an increase in cod landings the vessel must have done one 
or more of the following; 1) Had an overall increase in cod quota, 2) discarded less than the expected 
30% quota uplift or 3) acquired additional cod quota through the Danish ITQ system. During the 
timeframe used for the analysis, the cod quota has fluctuated but there have not been an overall 
increase from 2008 and forth (ICES 2014, 2015b; g). It is therefore likely that particular increases in 
cod landings among CDF vessels have been brought to by either additional cod quota acquired from 
other Danish vessels or a lower discard than the quota uplift given for trial participation. Whether the 
vessels already had lower discards than the quota uplift prior to the CDF trial have not been 
investigated in this thesis, but have been speculated by others (Msomphora & Aanesen 2015; van 
Helmond et al. 2016). A voluntary system could have selected for vessel owners who already believe 
they comply with the rules, have a generally positive opinion towards the fishery management or 
believe there is an economic gain in participation. Likewise, it is plausible that the cod quota bonus is 
more favorable to vessels already holding large cod quotas. The total landings per year of cod are 
larger for five of the CDF vessels in 2009, than for any of the control vessels. Additionally, merely 16 of 
==PAGE:75==
75 
 
the vessels participating in the Danish CDF trial accounted for about 30-40% of the total Danish 
landings of cod from the North Sea, during the years 2010-2014 (Plet-Hansen et al. 2015). This does 
suggest that the fishers who found it attractive to participate in the CDF trial were, and are, operating 
on vessels with substantial quotas on cod.  
There are some trends in line with the findings in Msomphora & Aanesen with CDF vessels increasing 
their landings of cod and plaice (Msomphora & Aanesen 2015). However, some of the control vessels 
display similar changes in their landings. As stated in the previous section, the spatial distribution of 
the Danish CDF vessels does not change, unlike the large vessels participating in the Dutch FDF study 
(van Helmond et al. 2016). Considering that: 
 An overall shift in fishing area only occur for one CDF that have decreased landings of cod  
 The change in landings also occur among control vessels  
 Change in landings coincide with overall changes in the TACs 
it seems more likely that in the Danish CDF trial, no shift in target species has been induced by the cod 
quota uplift. The trial setup is likely to have selected for certain vessel types and characteristics, which 
had certain target species prior to trial participation. 
The CDF trial is currently continuing with a cod quota uplift as benefit for participation. However, the 
discard estimations have been extended to include haddock, whiting, saithe and hake in 2015 and 
2016. Because no added quota is given on these species it could be of interest to focus on the catches 
and discards of these species in future studies using the Danish CDF trial data. Especially the landings 
and discards of haddock are likely to be of interest, because this species could legally be discarded in 
2015 but has to be retained and landed as of 1 January 2016.

Fishers’ opinion on REM with CCTV 
There is a more positive opinion towards the use of video verification in the fisheries management 
among fishers who have tried such a control measure. This may be explained by the voluntary 
participation in the Danish trials using REM with CCTV and therefor a positive opinion prior to 
participation. All fishers who have participated in a trial with REM and CCTV can say something 
positive about the concept, mainly pointing to quota uplift, public goodwill and more sustainable 
fisheries. In contrast, 60% of non-participants do not have anything positive to say. Both groups view 
the issue of constant surveillance as problematic, but trial participants raised the issue in a less harsh 
manner, which is the reasoning for the division between responders pointing to surveillance as a 
problem and responders stating that the surveillance is a criminalization in the result section. Critique 
of REM with CCTV is generally less harsh from trial participant than from non-participants. Non-
participants state either that the REM with CCTV is a waste of time, can be misused, is a 
criminalization of the industry or that the trials are skewed. In contrast the critique from trial 
participants point to “the equal playing field” by stating that all EU vessels should have REM with 
CCTV, that they do not like being under surveillance or a more specific detail like inadequate feedback 
on trial results. One trial participant even responds in a humoristic manner that the negative with 
REM with CCTV is that they cannot high-grade. There is a markedly division between trial participants 
and non-participants regarding their general opinion towards video documentation and the idea of 
making video documentation compulsory in cases where noncompliance is believed to occur. All non-
participants are generally against the use of REM with CCTV, except for one who is undecided. The 
majority of trial participants are generally for the use of REM with CCTV, although a third is against. 
The use of REM with CCTV in a risk-based approach is generally more acceptable for trial participants 
than non-participants but some non-participants did find the idea acceptable. Responses on bonus 
possibilities for video documentation clearly favor quota uplifts as a compensation, although the 
possibility to store bycatch as ensilage and free selection of gear is mentioned as well by trial 
participants. However, all non-participants do not see free selection of gear as a sufficient benefit and 
a large group of (40%) of non-participants do not see quota uplifts as a sufficient benefit. Some 
responses to the question were quite passionate as to why no benefit could mitigate the nuisance of 
==PAGE:77==
77 
 
having REM with CCTV on-board. The responses on bonus possibilities are somewhat in line with the 
report by Hedley et al., 2015. There, the authors state that due to the voluntary nature of the trials in 
FDF it is difficult to infer how FDF would work if it would become compulsory for all vessels, without a 
quota premium (Hedley et al. 2015). Based on the responses from this survey, although the number 
of responders only allow for a qualitative estimate, it does seem as if the implementation of a 
compulsory FDF with REM and CCTV will be met with serious opposition by Danish fishers. Although 
quota uplifts could meliorate the opinion towards such an approach, there is no reason to believe 
that all Danish fishers will find such a benefit adequate. Other incentives to take on REM with CCTV 
could include the possibility to store bycatch as ensilage, a certain certificate with some resemblance 
to the MSC certificate or loosening of other regulations like gear restrictions or effort limitations. 
Loosening of regulations imposed on the fisheries is possible if REM with CCTV is used because REM 
with CCTV allow for a result-based management, meaning that only the imposed effect of the 
fisheries is controlled (Mortensen et al. 2015). Using REM with CCTV is therefore an opportunity to 
place the responsibility for sustainable fisheries at the fishers. However, it seems likely from the 
responses presented in this study, that some Danish fishers will oppose the use of REM with CCTV no 
matter what benefit is offered.  
Fishery inspectors’ opinion on REM with CCTV 
The fishery inspectors’ responses regarding REM with CCTV bears some resemblance to the responses 
from the fishers who had not participated in a trial using REM with CCTV. One in six fishery inspectors 
have nothing positive to say about REM with CCTV, and almost 25 % state that video documentation 
only allows for full documentation in principle. Almost a third of fishery inspectors point to the ethical 
dilemma in such a level of surveillance, as did several of the fishers. A critique of REM with CCTV 
specific for fishery inspectors, is that video surveillance can be bypassed. More than half of the fishery 
inspectors either point to this issue or see it as problematic that video documentation is portrayed as 
a more effective control tool than they believe it is. There is a range of technical critique of REM with 
CCTV among fishery inspectors with doubts being raised on the robustness of the camera systems. 
The amount of material being gathered, the time needed to review video footage and the cost of 
==PAGE:78==
78 
 
operating a fisheries controlled by REM with CCTV are all raised as issues. However, other fishery 
inspectors state the lower cost and better level of control as the possible gains from REM with CCTV. 
This suggests quite divergent perceptions of the outcomes of the Danish trials on REM with CCTV 
among fishery inspectors. One responder stated that he has never heard anything about CDF being 
used for anything but video footage was actually used in two cases in 2011 to document non-
compliance, meaning that the actual uses of the system is not known among all fishery inspectors. 
Generally speaking, fishery inspectors view at-sea control and on-board observers as superior control 
tools compared to REM with CCTV. The opinion on at-sea control might be somewhat influenced by 
the responders. Because several responding fishery inspectors perform at-sea control in their daily 
work, they might be inclined to favor this control measure. Additionally, REM with CCTV is ranked as 
the third best option out of seven possible control tools and has the same median ranking as at-sea 
control, meaning that REM with CCTV score a top ranking quite often among the responders. The 
overall opinion on video documentation among fishery inspectors is quite divided. One in six fishery 
inspectors state they are against the use of REM with CCTV and two almost equally large groups are 
either “for” or “neither for nor against” the use of video documentation. It should be noted that the 
large group of fishery inspectors who state “neither for nor against” to the question on their general 
opinion towards REM with CCTV, may be influenced in their response by their position as government 
officials. Because the final decision on the use of REM with CCTV is political, some responders may 
find it inappropriate to take position for or against the control measure.  
Stakeholder Acceptance of the Landing Obligation 
The responses regarding the Landing Obligation are somewhat less divided, especially among the 
fishers, than was the case with REM and CCTV. Overall, the responses are quite antagonistic towards 
the regulation. Fishers do not only fear the economic feasibility of their sector with the Landing 
Obligation, they are also to a large extend confused and feel harassed by the bureaucracy of the 
regulation, although some of the responders are less worried of the impact of the regulation. This is 
highlighted by the responses on fishers’ current preparations for the Landing Obligation. Half of the 
responders have not made any preparations for the Landing Obligation, but the reasoning behind 
==PAGE:79==
79 
 
range from responders not being worried, to responders who are so frustrated that they have given 
up on adapting to the regulation. Roughly 2/3 of the interviewed fishers believe the Landing 
Obligation will have a negative effect on the Danish fisheries. 19 out of 22 fishers believe that the 
Landing Obligation will have no positive effect on the marine environment. More than 2/3 of the 
fishers even believe the effect of the Landing Obligation will be negative for the marine environment. 
This negative effect on the marine environment is believed to occur due to the increased mortality for 
fish that would have survived discarding (but will be taken ashore with the Landing Obligation) and 
the decreased food availability for organisms that feed on discards, be it seabirds, other fish or 
benthic organisms. Studies have pointed to the reduced food availability as a potential negative effect 
of reduced discards, especially for seabird populations (Catchpole, Frid & Gray 2005; Heath et al. 
2014; Sardà et al. 2015). The effect of reducing the food source from discards depend on several 
factors and the potential increase in selectivity and reduced fishing mortality are likely to mean a net 
positive effect on fish stocks (Catchpole et al. 2005; Condie, Grant & Catchpole 2014; Heath et al. 
2014).  
The fishery inspectors’ opinion on what will be the main challenges for the fishers with the Landing 
Obligation are much in line with the concerns raised by the fishers, that is difficulties in catch 
handling, storage issues, quota settings, bureaucracy and ultimately the economic feasibility of the 
fisheries. As for the effect of the Landing Obligation the largest concern among fishery inspectors is 
that they do not believe the regulation can be enforced. A majority of fishery inspectors either state 
that the intention with the regulation is good or that it should end discarding. At the same time, more 
than 80% state that they cannot see how the regulation can be enforced. Though the majority of 
fishery inspectors believe the Landing Obligation will have a positive effect on the marine 
environment, a large group of responders are doubtful as to what will happen. Concerns are also 
stated towards the impact of removing the “free” food source that discards act as. Fishery inspectors 
are however less certain as to whether this effect will be negative than the responding fishers were. 
As for the fisheries, a bit more than a third of fishery inspectors seem to have confidence in the 
adaptability of the industry and the effect of a better public image. Roughly another third of the 
fishery inspectors believe the Landing Obligation will have a negative effect on the Danish fisheries, 
==PAGE:80==
80 
 
mainly due increased complexity in regulation without allocated subsidies to mitigate negative 
impacts and/or facilitate investments in more selective gear.  
In all, there is a notable resentment towards the Landing Obligation among the responding fishers and 
the regulation is to a large extend not perceived as meaningful. Among the fishery inspectors there is 
serious concern as to how the control agencies are supposed handle the Landing Obligation and 
doubts as to whether the regulation will be accepted by the Danish fishers.  
The survey only has responses from a small number of Danish fishers. Nevertheless, it gives reason for 
concern that the dislike for the Landing Obligation is so profound among the responding fishers. This 
is further emphasized when held in line with the clear doubt, which is expressed by the fishery 
inspectors, on whether the regulation will actually be complied with. Compliance behavior among 
fishers have been found to be influenced by whether regulations are seen as meaningful and 
legitimate (Hønneland 2000; Nielsen & Mathiesen 2003). In a Danish study, it was found that if fishers 
begin to violate regulations, it can create a shift in the attitude towards a regulation, were non-
compliance risk becoming the norm, rather than compliance (Nielsen & Mathiesen 2003). An 
interesting notion is, that the study also reports fishers to find it morally wrong to discard catches that 
are dead (Nielsen & Mathiesen 2003). Based on the interview responses in this thesis, the opinion 
towards discards seems to have changed profoundly over the last 12 years.  
In essence, there are two possibilities to counter non-compliance (Jennings, Kaiser & Reynolds 2001c; 
Catchpole et al. 2005; Petter Johnsen & Eliasen 2011; Msomphora & Aanesen 2015):  
 Having enforcement of the regulations at a level where non-compliance is very likely to be 
caught, whereby the cost of cheating will be higher than the perceived cost of compliance  
 Convincing the fishers that the regulation is meaningful and creating incentives which make 
compliance more attractive than non-compliance. 
Such incentives could be to legalize the storing of bycatch as ensilage for some vessels. Indeed, three 
fishers actually stated that they saw the possibility to store bycatch as ensilage as an acceptable 
benefit for having REM with CCTV on-board. These responses also acknowledge the need for 
documentation if keepscards are to be stored as ensilage. Verification of the actual content is difficult 
==PAGE:81==
81 
 
with ensilage, but it could be possible to use REM with CCTV as a tool for verification. However, 
according to COWI, the majority of vessels are unlikely to gain added revenue from ensilage. For 
these, the optimal options seem to be a loosening of regulations concerning fish for human 
consumption (Haugan & Laugesen 2015), at least until a new market opportunity arises. If so, it is 
important that the marketability of keepscards remain at a level where avoidance of undersized fish is 
preferably. Otherwise there is a risk that the market for keepscards could undermine the objective of 
achieving a exploitation rate at the maximum sustainable yield (Catchpole et al. 2005; European 
Parliament and Council of the European Union 2013; Sardà et al. 2015). This is particular important 
for undersized fish because quotas are set in weight but fishing mortality also effect the ecosystem in 
numbers. Utilization of keepscards is therefore meaningful, both as an incentive and from an ethical 
point of view, but it has to remain favorable to target specimens above MCRS. 
Remote Electronic Monitoring and the Landing Obligation 
Based on the responses regarding the Landing Obligation there seems to be a need for control 
measures if compliance is to be ensured. The three measures mentioned in the CFP of 2013 for this 
purpose are on-board observers, CCTV or others (European Parliament and Council of the European 
Union 2013). Looking at the more technical criticism raised by responders in the present study, the 
shortcomings portrayed for REM with CCTV focus on the cost of running a REM system with CCTV, a 
cumbersome workload with review of the video footage, malfunctions and that the system is 
inaccurate and does not ensure actual full documentation. All of these points are valid, but must be 
held in comparison to the alternative control measures. First of all, the workload in reviewing the 
video footage can be reduced by doing samples, as has been and currently is the case in the Danish 
CDF trial (Ulrich et al. 2015). Hereby a certain percentage of the total video footage is reviewed. This 
percentage can be lowered or raised depending on factors like available funds and the level of control 
deemed adequate. If non-compliance or suspicious behavior occur in the sampled footage, the 
amount of reviewed data can be increased to comprehensively document potential non-compliance. 
Fitting REM with CCTV onto vessels and recording all fishing activities does therefore not 
automatically mean reviewing all the video footage. It is therefore more accurate to compare the 
==PAGE:82==
82 
 
level of control with those obtained with current control measures, like at-sea control and on-board 
observers. With such a comparison both the cost and the workload involved in reviewing video 
footage seems less cumbersome and is likely to be cheaper (Kindt-Larsen et al. 2011; Course 2015; 
Mangi et al. 2015; Needle et al. 2015). At the time of writing, the company providing the REM system 
currently used in the ongoing Danish CDF trial are looking into the use of a new camera system that 
can comprise video data with up to 80% without losing quality. Such a system would lower the 
required network for video upload, whereby either a cheaper network could be used or more data 
could be transmitted using the same network as before (Bergsson 2016b). Furthermore, the cost of 
reviewing video footage would be kept low if mainly part time workers, like students, are employed to 
go through the video data. After initial training in the system and recognition of species in question, 
student workers could audit video supervised by fully educated staff like a fishery inspector. Part time 
workers would also deal with an issue on whether personnel for video audit can maintain accurate 
estimations for a full work week. To validate the estimations by video audit, double check of random 
video sequences can be performed, as has been the case in the Danish FDF trials (Dalskov & Kindt-
Larsen 2009; Ulrich et al. 2015). Additionally, although the technique is still in its infancy, there is 
potential for achieving full review of all video footage in the future. A Scottish project is investigating 
the possibility of using machine learning to craft a system for automated image analysis (French et al. 
2015; Needle et al. 2015). The technique is still vulnerable to dirt on camera lenses, distortions in the 
field of video view and periods where large quantities of fish occur on the discard belt (French et al. 
2015). However, if fruitful, further development could enable video audit to be done mainly by 
computer software, supported by trained personnel in order to determine the course of action in 
cases where the computer software is unable to distinguish or if malfunctions occur.  
It is likely that malfunctions in a REM system will continue to occur. However, improvements, which 
counter some of the failures, have already taken place. In the Danish CDF trial, REM data was 
originally collected manually and transported in portable hard drives (Dalskov et al. 2012; Ulrich et al. 
2015). Since 2015, the REM system used for the trial has uploaded REM data directly to a secure 
server at the Danish AgriFish office in Copenhagen and the current camera system allow for video 
recording in the dark (Anchor Lab 2016a; b; c; d; Bergsson 2016b). The new system has made data 
==PAGE:83==
83 
 
gathering swifter, eliminated malfunctions due to damaged hard drives and led to better positioning 
of cameras, because staff at the AgriFish Agency could view the recorded area and instruct 
technicians on adjustments while the cameras were being placed (Anchor Lab 2016b; Bergsson 
2016b). Additionally, inaccuracy in the determination of fish length has been reduced with the grid 
overlay described in the section on Danish trials on CQM and REM in the General Background. 
As to the inaccuracy of species determination and that REM with CCTV does not ensure actual full 
documentation, these points have been raised in other studies as well, especially whether it is 
possible to distinguish species accurately enough in mixed fisheries (Hedley et al. 2015; Mangi et al. 
2015). It is probably true that CCTV can be bypassed and non-compliance occur regardless of its 
presence. However, this holds true for control measures like at-sea control or landings control as well, 
since these measures only allow for limited or no presence during the handling of catches at sea. The 
fishery inspectors’ concern as to how a Landing Obligation can be verified highlight these limitations. 
Current Danish fishery control uses at-sea control, landings control and self-reported catches in the 
eLog to perform a risk-based management (Council of the European Union 2009; European 
Commission 2011). With the majority of fishery inspectors fearing that the Landing Obligation cannot 
be enforced properly, it seems like these methods are believed to be insufficient when it comes to the 
verification of catches. Using on-board observers is likely to ensure better data and a higher degree of 
full documentation than REM with CCTV, because observers can perform additional measurements 
which are not possible for a video system and are less likely to have blind spots (Mangi et al. 2015). As 
presented by Ulrich et al. there does seem to be a discrepancy between data from video audit 
compared to observer samples (Ulrich et al. 2015). The reason for this discrepancy is yet to be 
discovered but it does to some extent support the critique of REM with CCTV being inaccurate and 
unable to perform full documentation.  However, self-sampling estimates are at the same time 
reported to be generally higher than estimates based on REM with CCTV. Although it might be 
possible to bypass a REM with CCTV system, it may be more valid to ask: is it profitable to bypass the 
system?  If the perceived effort it takes to bypass the control measure is greater than the perceived 
gain from non-compliance, it would make more sense simply to comply with the regulation. That self-
sampling estimates in the Danish CDF trial are generally higher than estimates from video audit (Plet-
==PAGE:84==
84 
 
Hansen et al. 2015; Ulrich et al. 2015) could be due to compliance being generally perceived as more 
beneficial than non-compliance. If so, self-sampling verified by REM with CCTV has fulfilled its 
purpose, regardless of whether the REM system can be bypassed or not.  
One of the biggest reservations against the use of REM with CCTV is the ethical dilemma it presents. Is 
the measure too much of a “Big Brother is watching” concept and a violation of privacy rights? The 
strong resentment towards the surveillance level is a weakness for REM with CCTV (Mangi et al. 
2015). Rather than making REM with CCTV compulsory it may be more favorable to establish a 
voluntary reference fleet where self-reported catches are verified by REM with CCTV. As in the 
Norwegian reference fleet, the benefit for vessels could be quota uplifts (Mangi et al. 2015), which 
from the responses in this survey also seem to be the benefit favored by most fishers. Depending on 
the number and specifics of the participating vessels, such a reference fleet could cover a substantial 
proportion of Danish catches. In the report by Plet-Hansen et al. 16 of the vessels participating in the 
Danish CDF trial, besides accounting for 30-40% of cod landings from the North Sea, also account for 
more than 50% of the total Danish landings of saithe and around 30% of total Danish landings of 
haddock and hake from the North Sea, during the years 2010-2014 (Plet-Hansen et al. 2015). From 
this follows, that not only do these 16 vessels account for significant amounts of the Danish landings 
of cod, but also of the landings of saithe, hake and haddock from the North Sea. If these vessels were 
to continue their participation in a CQM scheme verified by REM with CCTV, a substantial amount of 
the induced fishing mortality on cod, hake, haddock and saithe from Danish vessels in the North Sea 
would be verified by video documentation. Using the data obtained from such a reference fleet in the 
stock assessments could enhance the accuracy of the quota settings, especially if followed by a 
continuation of on-board observer schemes in order to calibrate data obtained from REM with CCTV 
and allow for a reliable comparison between the reference fleet and the rest of the fishing fleet. 
Another approach could be to make REM with CCTV mandatory for certain vessels, either based on 
vessel length, gear type or in a Danish context; based in the vessels percentage of the total quota. The 
strength of using the size of the vessel ITQ as the threshold is that a relatively large proportion of 
catches could be covered without necessarily having to fit REM with CCTV onto a large number of 
vessels. However, such an approach might be hard to manage. In an ITQ system, a vessel owner could 
==PAGE:85==
85 
 
be below the threshold for mandatory REM with CCTV at the beginning of a year and then rent or buy 
additional quota during the year to an amount that would bring the vessels quota share above the 
threshold or vice versa. Using vessel length as a threshold for mandatory REM with CCTV is another 
option. The strength of using vessel length is that it deals with the issues on fitting cameras onto small 
vessels (Ulrich et al. 2015) and that larger vessels are more likely to have larger quotas, thus covering 
a relatively large proportion of the total catches without having to document as large a number of 
vessels. However, this approach might lead to an increase of vessels just below the threshold for 
mandatory video documentation. Using gear type as a criterion for REM with CCTV would have the 
advantage that it could focus on gear types were bycatch issues are more frequent. However, vessels 
may swop gear type during a year which will impose practical issues on when REM with CCTV is 
required and on whether vessels may adopt non-compliance by changing gear type while at sea. 
For all these approaches to the use of REM with CCTV it can be said that they may be in violation of an 
equal playing field between fishers, because one group will be imposed a stricter control measure 
than other groups, especially if Member States adopt different approaches. However, a uniform 
approach for the entire EU will still affect the fisheries differently due to regional differences in vessel 
type, target species and national regulations.  
Finally, the thesis survey investigated fishers’ opinion on another approach to the use of REM with 
CCTV, namely a risk-based approach. Making it compulsory for vessels under suspicion of non-
compliance to be controlled by REM with CCTV for a period until compliance had been assured would 
avoid the fitting of REM systems onto all vessels and dealing with issues of whether it is fair to single 
out certain vessels or types of fisheries for mandatory video documentation. An issue with this 
approach is whether the system should be taken down or just shut off when the vessel has been 
cleared from suspicion. This is a particular concern since the initial costs of fitting the REM with CCTV 
is reported to be a major cost of using the systems (Hedley et al. 2015; Needle et al. 2015). One CDF-
responder point to this as an obstacle to a risk-based use of REM with CCTV. Additionally, there is 
great division between CDF and non-CDF responders on this question, meaning that although this 
approach might be more acceptable than imposing general requirements for REM with CCTV, the 
concept of a risk-based mandatory fitting of REM with CCTV is still likely to be met with opposition.  
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
In all, there is a lot of division on whether or not video documentation is an appropriate control tool. 
Some of the opposition can be mitigated with the right incentives and the degree of mandatory 
coverage. Although a decision not to use video documentation in the fisheries management is less 
controversial, it cannot be said to be the same as maintaining the status quo, due to the 
implementation of the Landing Obligation. If REM with CCTV is ruled out as a control measure it will 
be necessary to ensure compliance by some other mean. Creating economic incentives for 
compliance might be possible but it is a delicate balance to maintain the incentives for gear selectivity 
in such a case. On-board observers have the potential to ensure compliance with the Landing 
Obligation, but the extent of such a control measure is likely to be very costly and making on-board 
observers mandatory might very well be seen as an intrusion just like the use of REM with CCTV.

Conclusions 
Change in spatial distribution 
At the investigated spatial scale in this thesis, it is not evident that CDF participation has led to a 
change in the spatial distribution of the vessels in question. One CDF vessels stand out in its spatial 
distribution change, both compared to CDF and non-CDF vessels. Because it is a single case, it seems 
more likely that other factors caused this change in spatial distribution than participation in CDF. That 
the spatial distribution of fishing areas for CDF vessels remain the same in the first year of trial 
participation indicate that no change in target species has occurred for CDF vessels. 
Change in landed species 
Although the landings of some species increase for CDF vessels from 2008 to 2015, this is not found to 
be a phenomenon exclusive to CDF vessels. Furthermore, a subsequent decrease in the landings of 
other species is not found to be evident enough to support a change solely related to a change in 
target species due to participation in the CDF trial. Based on the findings in the analyses of landings 
and spatial distribution, it is not found that the extra cod quota given for trial participation can be said 
to have made participating vessels change their target species. It is possible that some vessels see CDF 
participation as an opportunity to adapt their fisheries and focus more on some species, but the 
notion is not apparent as a general rule. Differences between vessels participating in the Danish CDF 
trial and non-participating vessels are probably due to factors prior to CDF participation and factors in 
the trial setup that have made participation more favorable for some vessels. 
Questionnaires 
The responses from interviews and questionnaires point to great uncertainties regarding the 
perceived effect of the Landing Obligation and indeed as to whether the Landing Obligation will be 
complied with. Not only does it seem like there is serious doubt as to whether the regulation is 
meaningful in the Danish fishery community, the regulation is largely seen as impossible to enforce by 
the fishery inspectors. This poses a real danger of a situation where the Landing Obligation will have 
==PAGE:88==
88 
 
no real effect. The use of REM with CCTV has been reported as a mean to ensure compliance and 
document whether fishers exploit the common marine environment in a sustainable manner. 
However, the use of REM with CCTV is likely to be met with opposition from the fishing community. 
Because fishery control currently is done by at-sea and landings control and the majority of fishery 
inspectors believe the Landing Obligation cannot by enforced properly, it seems like these methods 
are unable to ensure adequate control. The use of on-board observers would need to be mandatory 
and enlarged if this method is chosen as a control tool of the Landing Obligation. This in turn will most 
likely create issues regarding the funding of such a control scheme and it is an open question whether 
a mandatory on-board observer requirement will be greeted positively by the fishing community. 
Instead of extending and enhancing control measures, another option would be to create incentives 
for compliance. This method would have the benefit of more or less removing the need for control 
measures because it will be in the best interest for the individual fishers and the industry to comply 
with the Landing Obligation. It is however important to ensure, that such incentives are fashioned in a 
manner were they can provide rule compliance without decreasing improvements in gear selectivity. 
There are many different approaches and combinations of control measures which could come into 
play as means to verify compliance with the Landing Obligation. Ultimately, it is a political decision 
which of these methods should be applied in the fisheries management. It is important that the 
chosen measure is able to ensure the best possible compliance with the Landing Obligation if the 
regulation is to have a chance of proving its effect on the fisheries and the marine environment.  
So how can Remote Electronic Monitoring help in the implementation of the Landing Obligation?  
As one of few control measures, and arguable the most cost-efficient, that to a large degree can verify 
compliance on-board fishing vessels under an obligation to land all catches associated with a TAC.

Future Studies 
1) A statistical study of the spatial distribution of fishing effort before and after CQM with REM 
and CCTV, possibly with a comparison of Norwegian vessels. 
2) Quantitative study of fishers’ view on REM with CCTV and/or the Landing Obligation. Using the 
qualitative feedback to the open-ended questions to create a mainly close-ended 
questionnaire, in line with the recommendations by Schuman & Presser (Schuman & Presser 
1979).  
3) Landings and discard estimations of other species than cod, using the data obtained directly 
from the CQM with REM and CCTV trial. Especially the catches and discards of haddock could 
be of interest since this species has been under full documentation in 2015, where it could be 
discarded, and in 2016, where it became subject to the Landing Obligation.
