Project Description: 
This project investigates how a new interdisciplinary framework for early stage IoT Innovation can be 
developed as a design tool in order to continuously evaluate how specific design choices in a technical 
solution proposal affect the business value assumed to be realized through IoT. A study of existing 
methods revealed a need for new interdisciplinary frameworks.  
Based on well-established methods from respectively the technical- and business-oriented domain 
together with observations and experience of current practices from my work at FORCE Technology I 
propose a framework that uses industry-recognized canvases, models and standardized system 
architectures which integrates and reveals their mutual influence to a cross-domain development team 
of engineers, designers, business developers and management. The framework consists of a number of 
design activities combined in a four-step framework to be applied in industry and used consecutively 
or partially depending on the project maturity.

Executive Summary 
 
Internet of Things (IoT) is considered as one of the main future business drivers for Danish 
manufacturing companies as it facilitates new service offerings, products and revenue streams to 
support global competitiveness. However, balancing technology-, people- and business perspectives in 
the process of designing new digital products can be a difficult task to handle, which often tempts a 
siloed development approach from either a technology driven- or business/market driven perspective. 
As a consequence, many promising IoT projects fail to be realized and deployed due to the late-stage 
discovery of the interrelationship and mutual influence between the business model and the IoT system 
architecture.   
 
Much prior work has been done in order to integrate user- and contextual perspectives in technology 
design processes whereas research shows a need for new tools to include business perspectives at an 
early stage when companies engage in IoT driven innovation. 
 
In this project, I investigate how an interdisciplinary framework for early stage IoT Innovation can be 
developed in order to continuously and iteratively evaluate how specific technical design choices in the 
solution proposal affect the business value assumed to be realized through IoT. To support the 
development, a delimited literature study of current practices of IoT innovation within the two domains 
were conducted. The final framework is inspired by their mutual use of standardized reference models, 
canvases and architectures which is formed into a four-step design cycle framework: 
 
1 
Inquiry & Analysis: Identifying the Business Opportunity 
2 
Synthesis: Designing an IoT concept    
3 
Simulation: Reference Architecture Mapping 
4 
Evaluation: System Architecture Analysis 
 
Finally, the framework is put into industry context by exemplifying and evaluating the effect and 
usability through a case study with Sjørring Maskinfabrik. Results show that important IoT system 
architecture characteristics can be identified and assessed to show their possible effect on the business 
model, which provides a well-justified basis for decision making in a cross-domain development team.    
 
The objective of this project is to contribute to the design methodology in the gap between technology 
and business development, especially in an IoT context. The framework is designed to be used in an 
industry context and facilitate a common language in complex IoT projects for development teams 
consisting of multiple stakeholders of engineers, designers, business developers and management 
personnel.

1.1 Why is this work relevant? 
Companies are currently facing a challenge of complex change-processes as a result of digital 
transformation. Especially small-to-medium sized enterprises (SME’s) have to find ways to cope with 
the tasks of discovering technology driven business value, designing digital solutions as well as 
validating, implementing, operating and maintaining new technology systems. Often this task requires 
vast resources and competencies within business development, design, engineering and operations 
management that SME’s struggles to lift by themselves. A recent study from Deloitte show that 60 
percent of Danish companies already have on-going IoT initiatives but ranks “Lack of convincing 
business model” as the largest barrier for actual implementation. “Handling of new technology” is 
ranked as number 2 (Deloitte, 2015). McKinsey (2016) is accessing a GDP growth rate in the Nordic 
countries of 0,9-3,1 percentage point derived from digitalization in companies by 2025 (McKinsey, 
2016) – of which 99 percent of Danish companies are categorized as SME’s1.  
  
The emergence of IoT provides SME’s with new types of valuable assets like data, service etc. but also 
sets new standards for transparency that especially production SME’s have to comply with in order to 
maintain global competitiveness. Larger associations are emerging where companies agree on rule sets 
for their suppliers e.g. the CDP network including Walmart, Tesco and Target etc. which requires access 
to CO2 emission data from their 11,500 global suppliers in order to be approved2. This switches SME’s 
interest in IoT from nice-to-have to need-to-have. 
 
In order to realize the potential of IoT driven digital transformation there is a vast need for supporting 
companies with tools that enables interdisciplinary analysis between technology design and business 
value in the early stages of IoT innovation. With the ability to accelerate the understanding of the 
interrelationship between technology design choices and business value, companies will be able to reach 
a higher maturity level early in the design process, reducing uncertainties and development cost, thus 
ensuring more IoT driven value to be realized in the future.   
 
1.2 How can the framework be used? 
The framework is proposed as a toolset for integrating respectively business- and technical perspective 
in the early-stage development process when designing new IoT driven services or products. 
Mainly it is meant to be used as a common ground framework where stakeholders from several domains 
in a development team find themselves comfortable working in. Primarily three scenarios are proposed 
where using the framework could be beneficial: 
 
• 
As a tool for investigating the effects of technical design choices on the business model assumed 
to be realized through IoT innovation, once a concept has been suggested by a design team. It 
enables detailed analysis of how technical design choices in an IoT system might affect the 
desired realization of value expected from IoT innovation.  
 
• 
It can also be used for decision making in a scenario where multiple technology concepts and 
architecture scenarios are suggested that all meet the requirements initially made and/or derived 
from contextual investigation through the design process.  
 
• 
Lastly it can act as cross-domain strategic development tool providing a common language 
between business viewpoints and technical viewpoints using standardized terminology and 
tools from both worlds and combining them for better mutual understanding.

1.3 Contextual Setting: FORCE Technology – IoT, Data & Services Innovation 
In order to understand the origin of the identified challenges on which this project has been built, it is 
appropriate to include a brief contextual framing.  
FORCE Technology’s business unit of IoT, Data & Services Innovation (IDSI) supports companies in 
IoT driven innovation in the early-stage development phases. IDSI offers services within business 
development (Getting started), design of IoT products (Designing products with IoT) and IoT 
architectures and technical development (Building products with IoT). Furthermore, IDSI offers 
maintenance services after market-entrance (Troubleshooting IoT) (fig. 1).     
 
 
Fig. 1: IDSI service areas. 
Through my work as a consultant at FORCE Technology I have participated in various projects of 
exploring the potentials of digital transformation though IoT for Danish SME’s. Companies often 
engage in these projects anchored in a concrete operational challenge that has been identified with the 
desire to assess how IoT will be able to alleviate this pain. Often companies don’t consider any derived 
value proposition exceeding the specific challenge which can be beneficial in order to build a solid IoT 
driven business case. Projects often progress as 1) identifying the challenge, 2) proposing a technical 
solution (see fig. 2).  
 
 
Fig. 2: IDSI design process (DIPS). 
However, I argue that the significance of the technical design choices is often neglected as a business 
influencer as it can have important effects on the business model. As an example, choice of battery will 
have a significant impact of the lifecycle of the device or choice of wireless communication protocol 
will have an effect on who receives data, thus depending on partnerships. Currently, FORCE 
Technology do not have appropriate tools that can identify these interrelationships in early stage IoT 
innovation projects. As a result, many companies fail to validate IoT projects from a business 
perspective in order to get management approval to continue product development.  
The case study presented in chapter 7 is anchored in such a former project with Sjørring Maskinfabrik 
on which I worked as a project manager from FORCE Technology that exemplifies this challenge. In 
the project it was desired to monitor vibrational data from the company’s excavator bucket product and 
access it remotely. The solution proposal suggested either wireless transmission of data through 
Bluetooth, using the construction machine as a gateway, or transmitting data directly into the cloud 
using a narrow-band communication protocol. The question arose: Which one is better? The 
development team agreed that this would have to be investigated more in-depth before proceeding with 
the project.

fast analysis and understanding of the two scenarios mentioned, which would result in faster realization

1.4 Delimitations 
Some delimitations of the project are worth mentioning: First, it is worth considering the starting point 
of which this project was built, being the industry challenges observed and experienced through my 
work at FORCE Technology. The initial aim of the project was to contribute to the application-oriented 
design methodology with new practices that could be directly adopted in companies. Contributing to 
industrial practice have obviously shaped the format of processes in the framework e.g. the four step 
guide and the use of well-known, mature canvases and standardized architectures. This work does not 
consider how other types of canvases, business models innovation tools and architectures available 
would work in order to achieve the overall goal. This proposes further work where e.g. a systematic 
comparison of using other tools in the framework as well as a comparison study of the performance of 
the framework against other tools (e.g. Aagaard et al’s tool for IoT Digital Business Model Innovation 
(2018)).  
 
Furthermore, contextual biases should be considered as my observations and experiences are solely 
derived from different consultancy projects at FORCE Technology. The framework might not provide 
the same support in other industrial settings. As an example, the framework relies on an impartial 
facilitator, e.g. a FORCE Technology consultant. In some cases, companies will manage early stage 
IoT innovation internally which have other dynamics that needs to be met. Future investigations on 
using framework in different industry context is encouraged. 
 
Lastly, the project does not provide results of how the framework performs when applied to 
development team context as the case study was conducted as “desktop research”. The key conclusions 
and take aways presented from the case study in section 7.6 is conducted through an analysis of the 
needs and solution proposal already known from earlier project. The case study is delimited to focus on 
how important IoT system architecture characteristics can be identified and used for analyzing its effect 
on the business model. I strongly encourage further work in investigating the perceived usability and 
value from industry stakeholders.

2 Literature study of current practices 
 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
14 
This section gives a brief overview of current theoretical notions and practices of how to develop 
complex technical systems in the specific context of IoT by exploring current literature on IoT 
development approaches.  
In order to structuralize the study, I first describe how IoT innovation is approached in literature from 
a technical perspective, primarily based on the conceptual framework of Systems Engineering including 
fundamental definitions, approaches and tools. This will provide a foundation for understanding the 
historical perspective of the siloed approach to systems development between the engineering and 
management domains.  
Furthermore, in order to give perspective to the technical practices of IoT development, I briefly review 
IoT innovation from a business perspective, mainly anchored in business model design approaches with 
special emphasis on digital business models. This includes a quick historical perspective on the 
antecedent of business models as well as fundamental notions, application, approaches and state-of-the-
art methods used in industry. Furthermore, I describe some of the challenges in digital business model 
design that literature has identified. 
By outlining how the two approaches to IoT innovation is mentioned in literature I seek to identify gaps 
and useful overlaps or mutual relationships in methods, processes and specific tools in the ways of 
thinking about- and executing IoT development across the two domains. 
All activities described above are included in this project in order to seek and analyze relevant literature 
in regard to the learning objectives (p. 3). 
2.1 IoT Innovation from a technical perspective 
From a terminology standpoint, academia struggles to provide a clear understanding and scope of how 
the notion of IoT is defined. Haller (2010) points out that the IoT domain has suffered from inconsistent 
usage and understanding of the meaning of the IoT term. The term is very much depending on in which 
domain and context the contextual framework is described. However, in literature four main definitions 
of IoT can be described according to reference (Lee et al. 2017):  
 
• 
IoT as intelligent objects 
• 
IoT as an extension of the internet 
• 
IoT as a global network infrastructure 
• 
IoT as an interaction of information 
 
Even so, identifying an appropriate level for working with- and discussing IoT can be a challenge as 
these perspectives are overlapping most of the time (Aagaard et al., 2018). Nevertheless, one definition 
that seems to meet widespread agreement is that of IoT as a complex system (or ecosystem) including 
several interdependent layers of human, organizational and technical elements all working together in 
a mesh (Uckelmann et al., 2011). With focus on the technical perspectives, the definition of IoT as a 
system constructs a need for understanding how complex systems development are engineered and 
managed which can be best found in the field of Systems Engineering. As this is a comprehensive set 
of traditions, methods and tools I distinguish between three categories of Systems Engineering literature 
as follows:      
 
• 
Systems Engineering mindset: The mindset(s) that argue the justification for the Systems 
Engineering tradition as well as the fundamental building blocks and definitions.  
 
• 
Systems Engineering processes: Works which present the overall processes of Systems 
Engineering. 
 
• 
Systems Engineering tools: Specific tools and methods defined for Systems Engineering in 
literature.  
==PAGE:15==
 
 
15 
 
In the following section I strive to briefly summarize how Systems Engineering is described in literature 
in the categories above. 
 
2.1.1 
Systems Engineering mindset(s) 
Systems Engineering is a process that can be used to develop something that is too complex to be 
designed and viewed as a monolithic entity (Lightsey, 2010). Often applied in development projects of 
technical systems and products to be able to grasp upon the system as a whole and understanding the 
interaction between the external world and the internal components of the system. It is used as a 
framework for developing complex projects in an efficient way that meets the project/programme’s 
objectives and requirements, minimize rework, early error detection and ensure effective 
communication between the stakeholders of the project. In academic practices this is mentioned as the 
Systems Engineering Mindset or Viewpoint (Kossiakoff et al., 2003).    
 
 In general, Systems Engineering is derived from the sociological field of Systems Thinking as the study 
of the interdependency and mutual influence between entities that hence form a system (Goh, 2010). A 
key aspect of Systems Thinking is “…to recognize the circular nature of most systems where Causal 
Loops can map the circular nature of cause and effect and demonstrate aspects of a system over time”. 
(Ibid.). Often associated with the rapid technology development during World War 2 and academically 
described in the decades after (Kossiakoff et al., 2003), Systems Engineering was used as an appropriate 
framework to recognize and study the cause/effect relationship between the elements of a system e.g. 
subsystems and technical/non-technical components as well as guiding the process of development in 
a complex context. In contrast to Systems Thinking, Systems Engineering is also taking into 
consideration the aspect of meeting stakeholder requirements, mitigating risks, measure outcomes and 
solve problems in complex environments (fig. 3). Therefore, it is often applied to large scale industry 
projects.  
 
 
Fig. 3: Systems perspectives (Kossiakoff et al., 2003) 
 
Several cultures and flavors of Systems Engineering processes and frameworks can be found throughout 
literature (e.g. Lightsey, 2001, Richmond, 2001, Goh, 2010). One example is the wide deployment of 
Systems Engineering in military programmes where it is used as a standard framework (e.g. DoD) for 
performing technical- and strategical defense projects (Lightsey, 2001). Other examples are modified 
processes that provides the appropriate support in large infrastructure processes, where requirement 
management is a key factor and a complex task (Kossiakoff, 2003, Holt et al., 2014). 
 
2.1.2 
Systems Engineering Processes 
The processes used in Systems Engineering is anchored in the viewpoint that the central objective is 
always the system as a whole which means that “…the Systems Engineer is always the advocate of the 
total system in any contest with a subordinate objective” (Kossiakoff et al., 2003, p. 27). 
With such a broad task of systems development it is needed to break down into processes often 
sequenced of designing, developing, integrating and testing the system. As the field has grown rapidly 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
16 
over the last two decades some sequential processes have been accepted as de facto standards. 
Especially the V-model as shown in fig. 4.  
 
 
 
Fig. 4: Examples of Systems Engineering processes. V-model in the lower left corner (Kossiakoff et al., 
2003)  
 
For IoT development purposes, the V-model seems to start showing a positive effect when applied in 
IoT research projects (Fernendez & Pallis, 2014, Morkevicius et al., 2017) as system requirements are 
put as the anchor point for the technical system design. Going “down the V”, Design choices are 
decomposed (white boxed) down to the lowest level of functionality. Going “up the V”, then 
recomposed (black boxed) and validated at all system levels in order to check that system requirement 
can be reached.    
 
2.1.3 
Systems Engineering tools 
Managing such a broad landscape in systems development requires a division in different system 
domains in order to comprehend the requirements in different human-, technical- and political levels 
and abstractions of the system. Kossiakoff describes the domains as a Rubik’s Cube of different domain 
faces (fig. 5) which all need to be taken into consideration.

At a high level, this provides a tool set of diagrams that can be used for mapping information at a

The evolvement of using UML models for Systems Engineering purposes formalized a subset of 
Systems Engineering called Model Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) making use of the notion of 
Systems Architectures (Rechtin, 2010). This forms a structural guideline and visual representation for 
managing system components, their relationships, governing their design and evolution over time 
(Ibid.) This applies strongly to IoT development and has been greatly accepted as a standardized 
framework for IoT development in the last decade. MBSE driven IoT innovation can be seen in research 
by e.g. Papke (2017), Taha et al. (2018) and Hause et al. (2016, 2018). The latter as a contributor to 
INCOSE3 official guidelines.  
 
2.1.4 
Architectural Views and Viewpoints 
In relation to UML, in MBSE literature researchers are often referring to the Architectural Views and 
Viewpoint, which is described in Raymond (1995) but also Rozanski & Woods’ white paper on the 
benefits and challenges of applying these to software architectures (2005). Raymond argue that large 
information systems (at the time) often lack a toolset in order for systems architects to make decisions 
across different system domains. Prior to this a common approach would be to include the entire system 
architecture into one all-encompassing model, which makes it difficult to navigate in. Rozanski & 
Woods argue that this pain can be alleviated by instead introducing a set of architectural layers that can 
be used according to the system level the development team is trying to work on. They define the 
distinction between Architectural Views and Viewpoints as follows (Ibid.): 
 
• 
“A view is a representation of one or more structural aspects of an architecture that 
illustrates how the architecture addresses one or more concerns held by one or more of its 
stakeholders”. (Rozanski & Woods, 2005) 
 
• 
“A viewpoint is a collection of patterns, templates and conventions for constructing one type 
of view. It defines the stakeholders whose concerns are reflected in the viewpoint and the 
guidelines, principles and template models for constructing its views”. (Ibid.) 
 
Summed up, the notion of Architectural Views and Viewpoints provides representations of the system 
from multiple views to assist the stakeholders in understanding system concepts and functionality 
(Kossiakoff et al., 2003). Several frameworks of different sets of viewpoints can be found in literature 
(Raymond, 1995; Shames, 2004; Rozanski, 2005; Woods, 2008; Kossiakoff et al., 2003; IoT-A, 2013) 
as well as standards (IEEE, ISO/IEC, INCOSE) and other associations e.g. DoD, NATO etc. A good 
example which seems to apply well to IoT development processes could be Department of Defense’s 
Architectural Framework (DoDAF) as it includes both functional viewpoints as well as cross domain 
viewpoints (fig. 8).

2.2 IoT Innovation from a business perspective   
Approaching the research landscape of IoT from the business perspective, literature is often centered 
around a historical- and comparative perspective of how technology offers a broad spectrum of 
opportunities for transforming traditional businesses models into digital business models. Much 
research has been made in order to structure the identification and capturing of the business value which 
is too comprehensive to mention here. However, much research has a specific focus on the challenges 
of designing digital business models in the era of IoT (e.g. Sun et al., 2012; Gassmann, 2014; 
Westerlund et al., 2014; Hui, 2014; Chan, 2015; Aagaard et al., 2018).  
 
Before moving more into different perspectives in literature on IoT business model design I argue the 
importance of understanding the fundamental objectives of business models. Research made by Amit 
& Zott (2015) shows that the business model concepts has rich theoretical roots that can be seen in 
different research streams. Overall, business models strive to describe the heuristic logic that connects 
business potential with the realization of economic value (Ibid.). Business models was broadly 
democratized by Alexander Osterwalder et al. in the book “Business Model Generation” (2010) which 
operationalized business model design in simple building blocks for anyone to use. Much like technical 
systems architectures, the framework provides a reference model of fundamental business components 
needed to be present and fulfilled in order to transform potential into customer value. In parallel with 
business model frameworks being recognized as the industry standard for mapping business systems in 
the last decade, researchers identify that the emergence of digital technologies also creates new 
challenges, needs and opportunities in business value creation. In the following I will be presenting two 
research tendencies within designing business models for IoT, derived from this literature study: 1) A 
need for new IoT business model frameworks and 2) Technology as an actor in digital business models. 
 
2.2.1 
A need for new IoT business model frameworks  
One current literature stream has already suggested that business model design is an inevitable 
component in modern business development for companies to create a dynamic overview of how to 
create value through their operations (Chan, 2015). Several frameworks have been developed with the 
purpose of making it easy to integrate business model design into operational business innovation tools 
that can be used in companies. IoT is already widely considered as an integrated part of future business 
operations (Ibid.) as it enables any article to be connected and talk to each other. Abdmeziem and 
Tandjaoui (2014) categorizes the basic IoT paradigm in a business context in three domains: 1) Things 
oriented (sensors, devices and things), 2) Internet oriented (middleware, the enabler of digitalization) 
and 3) Semantic-oriented (Knowledge). However, this mostly applies to siloed “single company” IoT 
systems where knowledge is only utilized internally.  
 
As all things now are potentially connectable, Chan elaborates this paradigm, and argues that the nature 
of combining digital technology with physical objects will often result in collaborations between 
partners of vastly different industries. Collaboration and partnerships will thus become a key asset of 
future businesses where the interdependency of knowledge and products between partners needs to be 
activated and structured for commercial exploitation (Dijkman et al., 2015).  
 
The new business model opportunities disrupt traditional business value chains that many current 
business model innovation tools encompass. A new literature stream argues that new business model 
tools must be developed in order to capture and commercialize the new opportunities that IoT and 
digitalization and general makes possible in the future. Research by Mansour et al. (2018) provides a 
comparison of seven industry-recognized business model innovation tools’ usability for IoT ecosystems 
including Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder et al., 2010), DNA-model (Sun, 2012), The Business 
Model Cube (Lindgren, 2013), The Business Model Navigator (Gassmann, 2014), Value Design Model 
(Westerlund, 2014), BM type for IoT model (Turber, 2014) and 3DCM Model (Chan, 2015). The 
conclusion of the study shows the easy-to-use tools falls short on taking into consideration important 
IoT characteristics, while specialized tools have better success exposing these characteristics but falls 
short on maturity and usability (Mansour et al., 2018).

Also, research emerges where new contributions with IoT specific business model tools are suggested 
(Dijkman et al., 2015; Chan, 2015; Aagaard et al., 2018; Henriquez et al., 2019). The characteristics of 
the new proposals are primarily the focus on the new elements needed to be taken into consideration in 
IoT enabled business models (fig. 9) and how digital technologies should be taken into account to create 
new business value, which will be covered in the following section.

2.2.2 
Technology as an actor in digital business model tools 
Another main argument in literature is recognizing technology as a mutual influential active in business 
models. Aagaard et al. (2018) mentions the main critique of Osterwalder’s Business Model Canvas as 
the absence of technical features of the IoT architecture as the technical components, sub systems and 
processes of an IoT driven business model design creates new interdependencies, constraints and 
opportunities in the business system. This is mainly due to the fact that the IoT paradigm had not been 
coined at the time when the tool was invented but Aagaard et al. argues that a contribution that enables 
the integration of IoT architecture perspectives into already exiting frameworks can be useful as 
companies already have adopted these and find them comfortable to work with.  
 
A main argument that Aagaard et al. present is that mature and recognized business model tools do not 
cover the interdisciplinary complexity of IoT systems which makes it challenging for businesses to 
comprehend and stimulate IoT driven business development. The core challenge in this, is discovering 
the effect which specific technical design choices have on the building blocks of the business model. 
As a result, business models might change radically when technology is applied to the idea in order to 
realize the desired business value as the parameters will change according to system design choices. 
 
Aagaard et al.’s IoT Business Model Innovation Tool combines the two most adopted business model 
tools in the world (Ibid.) Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder, 2010) and The Business Model 
Navigator (Gassmann, 2014) with a definition of the IoT architecture in four layers: 1) The Device, 2) 
The connectivity, 3) The Cloud and 4) The Application (fig. 10).

The expansion of Osterwalder et al. and Gassmann’s existing business model tools holds an interesting 
ability to capture and incorporate the effect of IoT architectural design choices on the business model, 
although the analysis of the technical components is still maintained at a high-level viewpoint. This is 
assumably to facilitate an easy-to use framework that can be used in companies.  
 
2.2.3 
Main findings 
• 
Companies widely recognizes the use of standardized business model innovation tools that 
facilitates the mapping of how business capabilities can be transferred in customer value. Most 
adopted is Osterwalder’s Business Model Canvas (2010) and Gassmann’s Business Model 
Navigator (2014). 
 
• 
Mature business model innovation tools do not cover the interdisciplinary complexity of IoT 
which makes it challenging to comprehend which effect technical design choices will have on 
the IoT driven business model expected to be realized (Aagaard et al. 2018). One suggestion is 
to incorporate system architectural aspects into existing mature business model innovation tools 
(Ibid.). 
 
• 
Business literature has a vast emphasis on the importance of managing and commercializing 
new cross-domain partnerships which will be needed in IoT driven business models of the 
future (Chan, 2015).

4 Building Synthesis 
 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
27 
Based on the literature study of current practices of IoT innovation, in respectively the technical domain 
and the business domain, the following synthesis to be used when proposing a novel interdisciplinary 
framework is derived:  
 
1) There is relationship in how business systems and technical systems are mapped using standardized 
reference models (ie. business model innovation tools and system architectures) in order to understand 
the individual components that need to be (or usually are) present in order for the system to work. It 
also provides users with the ability to understand how entities relate to other entities of the system. 
However, current models fail to include perspectives from the other domain, which results in a siloed 
development process between business innovation and technical innovation. As a consequence, 
technical design choices are not assessed before at a late maturity stage where changes can be costly 
and complex. The already established use of reference models in both domains can be leveraged in a 
new framework in order to create an environment that both engineers, business developers and 
management stakeholders are comfortable working with. However, it is important to recognize that an 
all-encompassing model is not appropriate for development purposes. 
 
2) Following this, a new framework ought to introduce the use of Views and Viewpoints that enables 
different perspectives of the IoT system for different groups of expertise to work with.  
 
3) Furthermore, the framework should facilitate a translation, or pathfinder, of the cause/effect 
relationship between technical elements in a system architecture to business perspectives in the business 
model. This can be done by making use of appropriate views and viewpoints. This is important to be 
able to backtrack in the system and identify which technical elements are linked to which business 
model parameters in order to change them.  
 
4) Also, there is a need for including an assessment tool that provides stakeholders with the ability to 
analyze the characteristics of components in the system architecture and how they might affect the 
business model. In order to do so, the functionality of each technical component of the system 
architecture must be described in way so non-technical stakeholder are able to understand it.     
 
5) Lastly, the framework can appropriately be developed as a design process that facilitates the 
sequential, yet agile stages of a design process. To be able to use the interdisciplinary tool, it is necessary 
that a business idea and a solution proposal (concept) must be present at a certain level of quality. Using 
the Basic Design cycle scaffold of Analysis (business idea), Synthesis (Solution proposal), Simulation 
(architectural modelling) and evaluation (assessment of technical design choices effect on expected 
business model) quality and progress are ensured.     
 
 
In the following chapter I describe the considerations of how to incorporate the above-mentioned 
synthesis into a framework that improves interdisciplinary early stage IoT innovation. Also, I present 
considerations in how to apply the framework in the industrial context of FORCE Technology (see also 
section 1.3).

5.1 Idea and concept 
Based on the synthesis requirements I developed a proposal for an interdisciplinary technical- and 
business model design thinking framework that can be utilized as a design thinking tool in early stage 
IoT innovation. The framework strives to provide a design process which facilitates a common ground 
for cross-domain understanding of the interrelationship between technical, functional and 
operational/business parameters surrounding an IoT system.  
The framework combines recognized approaches that are already used in three domains in a 
standardized, controlled process: 
 
Systems Architectures 
From the domain of Systems Engineering (MBSE), as often found in technical development, the 
framework makes use of the conceptual apparatus surrounding System Architectures, where concepts 
are modelled in a standardized representation of a system. Inspired by the arguments of enabling the 
benefits of Views and Viewpoints in system development as argued in Raymond (1995), Shames 
(2004), Rozanski (2012) and put into perspective by Woods (2011) and Gressler et. Al (2018), the 
process provides two different abstraction levels of the IoT system, which can be used according to the 
development perspective. In order to facilitate this, the framework uses the IoT-A Architectural Refence 
Model (2013) as well as the ISO/IEC 30141 IoT Reference Architecture (2018).   
 
Business Model Design 
From the domain of Business Model Design the framework suggests a way to meet the need for new 
business model innovation tools that recognize technology as a dynamic actor in digital business 
models, as described in literature. Also, the framework includes the use of two well-known business 
design tools: The Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder et al., 2014) and The Business Model Canvas 
Ibid., 2010). Making use of domain-recognized notions and terminology found in these models, 
processes and tools creates a comfortable environment for a cross-domain development team of e.g. 
engineers, business developers and management stakeholder that they can all identify with.     
 
Design Thinking     
In order to administrate the desired agile and iterative nature of the process, the framework makes use 
of Design Thinking process concepts. More specifically, the framework is suggested to be carried out 
in an agile design cyclical process in four steps. The benefits of doing so lies especially in the need for 
product designers to structure continuously acquired knowledge into the decision-making process as 
argued by Roozenburg & Eekles. The approach is inspired by the Basic Design Cycle as described in 
their work (1995).  
 
Furthermore, the framework provides a novel contribution of a translating-method between system 
architecture and business model by using a SWOT analysis in step 4. 
 
5.2 Using the conceptual apparatus of Systems Architectures 
In order to understand the approach of including System Architecture structure into the framework I 
find I necessary to prioritize further emphasis on explaining the two frameworks that I adopt for this 
purpose: The IoT-A Architectural Reference Model and ISO/IEC 30141 IoT Reference Architecture in 
this section. 
 
The IoT-A Architectural Reference Model (ARM) is the final deliverable of the project The Internet of 
Things – Architecture (2013) co-funded by the European Commission within the Seventh Framework 
Programme, which took place from 2007-2013. The aim of the project was to establish a standardized 
reference base for the IoT domain in order to support horizontal scalability and interoperability for a 
future, and expected fast growing, IoT. At the time, when the IoT-A project was on-going, the emerging 
field of interconnected smart objects showed no- or little interoperability capabilities as most systems 
where developed to solve industry-specific challenges. As a consequence, more and more isolated 
systems where introduced with a vertical approach resulting in a set of siloed “Intranet of Things”. 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
30 
Furthermore, this also resulted in a large and heavy development process alongside risk-full investments 
for companies as all development is based on a per-case basis. 
A problem that stands out, is that “the smartness of new IoT applications can only reach its pinnacle 
whenever full collaboration between those vertical silos can eventually be achieved”. (IoT-A, 2013, p. 
35).  
The project group propose a visualization matrix with different views of an IoT system that stands on 
the shoulders of the Views & Viewpoints methodology in technical system development (Rozanski, 
2005). The work is derived from exhaustive analysis of current State-Of-The-Art to identify any 
possible functionalities, mechanisms, components and structures that an IoT system can be built from. 
 
The ARM suggests two different models: 1) The IoT Reference Model and 2) The IoT Reference 
Architecture: 
 
The IoT Reference Model: Provides a common understanding of what components and relationships 
any IoT system consists of regardless of scale, technology, functionality and application by suggesting 
a visualization of the technical components and their interrelationship in the IoT system. This could 
encompass, but not be limited to, physical entities, virtual entities, hardware device(s), communication 
technology, services, resources etc.  
 
The IoT Reference Architecture: Companies that want to IoT enable products or services in (part of) 
their business can be supported by The IoT Reference Architecture that provides a reference for building 
compliant IoT architectures. It provides perspectives at different levels of the system (e.g. Information 
view, functional view etc.) which encompasses the aspects of importance to specific stakeholders. This 
could include, but not be limited to, access and communication with system, ensuring daily operations 
of system and data security. The IoT Reference Architecture focus on an abstract set of mechanisms 
and organizational needs rather than the concrete application components. 
 
The ARM thus provides the highest level of abstraction level of the building blocks and mechanisms of 
IoT systems in different views and viewpoints. This is beneficial as it provides the development team 
with the possibility to isolate a specific topic that needs to be talked about. An example could be one 
software engineer and one hardware engineer from the development team talking about how data is 
being transported and communicated in the system. In another scenario the management team of a 
company needs an overview of the needed vendors of their future IoT system. Here, the project manager 
is now able to show a landscape of functionalities that needs to be fulfilled internally in the company 
or outsourced e.g. cloud service provider, customer service etc. 
 
5.2.1 
The IoT Reference Model (RM)

5.2.1.1 
RM Domain Model 
The Domain Model visualizes the individual concepts that any IoT system consists of as well as their 
capabilities and interrelationships. To map this, the domain model is visualized in an UML4 
representation in the IoT-A (fig. 14). The domain model contains the following concepts: Physical 
Entities, Digital Artifacts, Virtual Entities, Devices, Resources, Services and Users.  
 
 
 
Fig. 14: The IoT Domain Model (IoT-A, 2013) 
 
Physical Entities: Represents the “Thing” in “Internet of Things”. This could be defined as the physical 
object in the real world that you want to have represented in the digital world by a Virtual Entity. In 
short, physical entities can be anything from cars, lamps and toys to assembly lines, machinery and 
living organisms (plants, humans etc.). 
 
Digital Artifacts and Virtual Entities: Digital artifacts live only in the digital world. Virtual Entities 
are digital artifacts that represent a Physical Entity in the virtual world. These are categorized as either 
passive (e.g. databases) or active (e.g. algorithms).   
 
Devices: The hardware devices in an IoT system mediates the link between the physical entities and 
virtual entities. The device needs to contain at least some computation, communication and storage 
capabilities. The device usually is bundled with sensors that sense conditional parameters of the 
physical entity, actuators that actuate an event e.g. “close window when too cold”. Tags are objects used 
to identify the physical entity like e.g. RFID.

Resources: Resources are components in the IoT system that provide some kind of functionality. 
Resources are mostly identified as software with an objective to perform a certain task that the system 
is able to utilize. Resources can either run on the system network (Network Resources) or on the IoT 
system hardware (On-device Resources). An example of the latter could be sensors that pick up sensor 
data or a Bluetooth component that enables wireless communication via the Bluetooth protocol.  
 
Services: The IoT-A quotes Brown’s definition (in IoT-A, 2013) of services in architectures stating that 
services are the mechanism by which needs and capabilities are brought together. The more technical 
oriented definition of a service in systems architecture are somewhat broader and more complex. The 
simplest understanding of the definition is a standardized interface to access complex resources, often 
bundled in an offer e.g. an alarm service, when temperature sensor data is too high. Another example 
could be a cloud platform service including many cloud facilities like Amazon Web Services or 
Microsoft Azure.  
 
Users: The user component is defined as entities that uses the IoT system. This could be a Human User 
that access the system via an interface like e.g. a computer. Human Users can come in different scales, 
like an organization, or as many different types like customers, maintenance/operational staff and 
developers. Users can also be non-human and refer to some kind of digital artifact that needs to interact 
with the physical entity through the system like different services, applications etc. 
 
5.2.2 
The IoT Reference Architecture (RA) 
A system architecture needs to map out many different things. As stated in IoT-A this could include 
functional elements, interaction between these elements, information management, operational features 
and deployment of the system (IoT-A, 2013). This is best done by providing a matrix of system Views 
and Viewpoints as mentioned earlier in section 2.1.4. IoT-A adopts the concept of Views and 
Viewpoints, although these are mostly suitable for specific use-case architectures and not reference 
architectures. The Functional View is considered to be the most generally informative view that covers 
all of the functional components of the IoT system and thus is described and defined in great detail.  
 
In this project, I have chosen to refer to- and visualize the Functional View as it is formulated and 
structured in the ISO/IEC 30141 IoT Reference Architecture standard (2018) which utilizes the IoT-A 
work as a guideline and body of knowledge. The two IoT RA’s are similar although the version in the 
ISO standard has been reworked for greater simplicity with less technical terminology (fig. 15).  
 
 
 
Fig. 15: The IoT RA Functional View. Left: IoT-A (2013). Right: ISO/IEC 30141 (2018). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
34 
ISO (The international Organization for Standardization) and IEC (The International Electrotechnical 
Commission) form a specialized system for worldwide standardization (ISO/IEC, 2018). Standards are 
considered a recognized approach by industries as a structure for defining agreed ways of doing 
something. Standards can either be De Facto standards that emerge from acceptance of a custom or a 
certain way of doing things over time without It being regulated centrally (often exemplified with the 
QWERTY keyboard) or emerging from committees and voluntary organizations like e.g. ISO. Often 
these are developed with a number of contributors. In the case of ISO/IEC 30141 IoT Reference 
Architecture, the standard is built with the IoT-A body of knowledge as a contribution.  
 
5.2.2.1 
RA Functional View 
The ISO/IEC 30141 IoT Reference Architecture (RA) consists of four different views: 1) Functional 
View, 2) Systems Deployment View, 3) Networking View and 4) Usage View. For the purpose of this 
work, I have chosen to use to Functional View (fig. 16) as this is proposing a representation of the 
different functional components necessary to form an IoT system. This view is differentiated from the 
RM that represents the necessary technology concepts needed in an IoT system. Both models/views are 
mandatory for any type of IoT system and can be used independent of applications, technology types 
and other contextual parameters.  
As I am using the Functional View in great detail, I find it appropriate to highlight the definitions of the 
functional groups and inherent components. As the standard is accessible only by purchase,

Physical Entity Domain (PED): As also described in the RM this represents the area of the “thing” in 
IoT. Usually being an object that a user would like to acquire data information from. These are identified 
by a tag with a unique ID in order to differentiate between physical entities in the IoT system.  
 
Sensing & Controlling Domain (SCD): The domain where physical parameters are captured and 
bridged from a physical- to a digital representation by sensors actuators and controlling units. 
• 
Sensing: The function that read sensor data from sensors. Not to be confused with the actual 
sensor. 
• 
Identification: Function that enables identification of components in the SCD e.g., sensors, 
actuators etc. 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
35 
• 
Actuation: Function that initiates an action e.g., making sure that data packages are shipped off 
every 60 minutes. 
• 
Control services. 
 
Operations & Management Domain (OMD): Functions that are responsible for managing the IoT 
system, making sure that the system is operating and running as intended. OMD consists of two types 
of functional components: 
• 
Operational Support Systems (OSS): E.g. monitoring and reporting of system operations. 
• 
Business Support Systems (BSS): Responsible for business aspects e.g. subscription 
management, accounts, billing etc. 
 
Application & Service Domain (ASD): To be considered as “the brain” where application and service 
logic is being managed in the system. Here, the requests to the different service providers are being 
executed once a state has been reached (e.g. “when temperature from sensor reaches above x, request 
alarm service and sent to user that subscribes to this service along with a print of temperature 
measurements from database”). The ASD is separated into four functional components: 
 
• 
APIs & Portal: Different controlled ways of accessing the IoT systems by users (e.g. human 
user though web portal, digital user through API).  
• 
Business Services: Functions that support the business process flow (e.g. what causes high 
temperature? Any additional tasks needed to be performed (e.g. call service technician)? 
• 
Analytic Services:  Provide analytical services e.g. “air conditioner has been running for x 
hours, service of x and y components are needed in x days”. 
• 
Application Support: Functions that the system can use for configurability and scalability 
towards a specific application e.g. if system is applied to many air conditioners in a shopping 
mall to control heating.  
 
Access & Communication Domain (ACD): The functions necessary to access and communicate 
in/with IoT system. This could be communication data from sensors to the cloud but also internal 
communication in the system. 
• 
Discovery and access control: Functional components that manages external and internal 
access capabilities ie. controls who get access to what. 
• 
Data Flow: Preparation and transfer of any kind of data e.g. cleaning, sorting, reducing etc. 
 
User domain (UD): The functions that enable users to access the capabilities of the IoT system via a 
user interface. 
 
Furthermore, the RA proposes a set of cross-domain capabilities that is relevant in any of the before 
mentioned domains: 
 
• 
Connectivity: Protocols in an IoT system usually connect entities from more than one 
functional domain alone thus relevant across domains. 
 
• 
PII protection: Personal Identity Information (PII) needs to be protected in any domain of an 
IoT system. 
 
• 
Security: Security policies needs to be an integrated part of the entire system design in order 
to maintain trustworthiness. 
 
• 
Resilience: The system should be able to have some sort of recovering ability if a break down 
should occur. 
 
• 
Interoperability and Dynamic Composition: These functions provide system capability of 
integration with other systems and dynamically change elements in an easy, comfortable way.

5.3 Applying framework to the industry 
The framework should, from the very beginning, be targeted as being application oriented towards being 
used as a problem-solver in an industry setting. The contribution should consider the limitations of time, 
budget and resources that can challenge the execution of complex IoT innovation projects in companies. 
Thus, a method that can facilitate progress and incorporate insights gained in a sequential but delimited 
process will be appropriate. Using an already known design methodology, the basic design cycle 
(Roozenburg and Eekels, 1995) encompasses this in a way that is comfortable industry stakeholders to 
work in.   
 
Even through the framework is aimed towards early-stage development it is important to define what 
this exactly means in an industry context and where on the development timeline the framework is 
applicable. FORCE Technology defines their services in three sections of the development process: 
Business Development, Product Development and Market Delivery, as seen in fig. 17. In general, this 
project argues that these phases are much interrelated and should be treated as such in project execution, 
Nevertheless, using the context of FORCE Technology’s service areas, the framework proposal is 
suggested to be applied as interdisciplinary support in FORCE Technology’s service offerings between 
Business Development and Designing product with IoT (fig. 17).

5.4 Other considerations 
During the framework development several considerations was made in regard to which format the 
framework could have. Some of them are still not settled. 
It is yet unclear how much, and how, stakeholders from the company should be involved in conducting 
the process and how the activities will be carried out. One suggestion could be to do it in a collaborative 
co-design setting where participants sit down in e.g. a workshop to carry out the system architecture 
analysis. This would provide diverse inputs and attitudes which could strengthen the outcome. 
However, the process and results might also be affected by the rules of democracy in a negative way as 
the assessment of the mutual affect between system architecture design choices and business model 
needs to be done by stakeholders with a certain cross-domain expertise. Another approach would be to 
use the framework in IoT design assessment made by independent experts/specialists like. e.g. a trained 
consultancy group that separated the risk of biased analysis as much as possible.

6 Framework Proposal 
 
 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
38 
In the following I describe my proposal of a framework that facilitates the bridging of the 
interrelationship between business design and technical design in IoT innovation. The framework is 
able to identify and analyze the mutual influence the two domains have on each other when designing 
an IoT system (fig. 18). The framework structure draws upon well-known methodology in design work, 
from which I specifically use the four steps in The Basic Design Cycle as a process scaffold. More 
specifically as described by Roozenburg and Eekels (1995) (see also Methodology chapter 3).  
 
 
Fig. 18: Framework Proposal: Interdisciplinary Technical- and Business Model Design Thinking 
Process for Early Stage IoT Innovation. 
  
The four steps of The Basic Design Cycle (Inquiry & Analysis, Synthesis, Simulation, Evaluation) have 
a high level of identity with the objectives in the steps of the proposed framework. In order to create a 
more suitable understanding of the different activities in each step, the steps have been renamed 
accordingly which are made clear in the following sections. 
 
6.1 Step 1: Inquiry & Analysis – Identifying the Business Opportunity 
In most company cases, the desire to engage in innovation comes from either a good idea, an identified 
business/market opportunity or simply a challenge/requirement that needs to be met through change. In 
order to align a good starting point for this framework it is proposed to identity these ideas and map 
them through a standardized and well-known tool so the outcome will be uniform and accessible in a 
format that is cross-domain appropriate and suitable for the further work in the process.  
 
6.1.1 
Purpose and activities 
As most companies will have different starting points and maturity levels it is important to investigate 
and identify this thoroughly. For this work, I am proposing to use well known tools that most 
management employees will recognize and already be comfortable in using as well as provide a simple 
understanding of the current situation and desired improvement goals to stakeholders from other 
domain, e.g. technical personnel.   
The key tool in step 1 is primarily The Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder et al., 2010). Often, 
companies analyze business models and value creation at strategy level using a variety of different tools, 
methods and processes. Often resulting in documents, notes and oral decisions from meetings are 
scattered and stored different places and in different formats where it can be challenging to understand 
and align internally. To facilitate a common ground for debating and deciding on concrete value 
objectives, improvement goals and how to get there, The Business Model Canvas is estimated as an 
appropriate tool with the right abstraction level and complexity to be used at this stage. 
Other tools are useful to support the Business Model Canvas. For this work, I have chosen to include 
the Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder et al., 2014) as value proposition is a key component in the 
Business Model Canvas which needs to be identified in a structured way. The canvases can be found in 
template form in appendices A6.1.1.1 and A6.1.1.2 as well as in filled out version through the case 
study in appendix A7.2.1. The objectives and detailed description of the canvas frameworks will not be 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
39 
mentioned here. However, I find it appropriate to briefly sum up the building blocks of the individual 
tools:  
 
The building blocks of The Business Model Canvas: 
 
1. Customer Segments: Which customer Segment(s) is the organization serving? 
2. Value Propositions: What value does the company bring to the customer? 
3. Channels: The value is delivered to the customer via certain communication, distribution and 
sales channels. 
4. Customer Relationships: What are the relationships that are established with each customer 
segment? 
5. Revenue Streams: Where does the revenue occur? 
6. Key Resources: The key assets required to offer the elements mentioned above. 
7. Key Activities: The things needed to be performed. 
8. Key Partnerships: Which partnerships are needed to be established? 
9. Cost Structure: The costs and expense structure. 
 
The building blocks of The Value Proposition Canvas: 
 
Consists of two larger blocks: 1) The Customer Profile which describes a specific customer segment. 
2) The Value Map that describes the features of a certain value proposition in your business model like 
e.g. an idea for a new IoT enabled product.  
 
The Customer Profile: 
 
1. Customer Jobs: What jobs and tasks are the customer trying to get done in their work? 
2. Pains: Hazzles and negative outcomes related to customer jobs. 
3. Gains: What gains and benefits would customers like to achieve?  
 
The Value Map: 
 
1. Products and services: A list of one or more products and services the value proposition is built 
around. 
2. Pain Relievers: How is the product alleviating customer pains? 
3. Gain Creators: How is the product creating gains for the customer? 
 
It is important to emphasize that the canvases must be filled out according to a desired to-be situation 
thus exemplifying what value propositions and business model structure are expected that the company 
will be able to realize through IoT innovation.  
 
6.1.2 
Outcome 
The outcome of step 1 is an overview of value proposition and business model structure that is expected 
to be realized through IoT intervention in the company. This overview is provided in the Value 
Proposition Canvas and Business Model Canvas (fig. 19).

(Osterwalder et al. 2014). Left: Templates. Right: Filled out examples. 
 
6.1.3 
Considerations and limitations 
It is important for the development team to consider the different digital maturity levels that companies 
are situated in. Some companies might possess a high level of digital and technological identity 
including the necessary vision, strategy, company resources etc. Other companies might be in the very 
beginning of digital transformation. This must be taken into consideration in order to figure out which 
level of complexity that will be appropriate when an IoT concept is proposed and accessed. It might be 
necessary to simplify the value proposition in focus in order to address simple improvement goals with 
IoT. This also sparks a discussion if the process is suitable for all types of company cases at all different 
maturity levels. This is discussed further in chapter 8.  
 
6.2 Step 2: Synthesis – Designing an IoT concept    
Based on the inquiry and analysis work performed in step 1 it is now clarified how IoT enabling of a 
product or service could provide customer value as well as how a business model could be built around 
the idea. In step 2 an IoT solution proposal is designed based on this. 
 
6.2.1 
Purpose and activities 
Based on experience from working on company cases at FORCE Technology I have observed a 
tendency that most companies are able to identify potential value propositions and hypothesize a digital 
business model based on an idea where there is no actual IoT concept or design proposed yet. Often the 
work is derived from notions such as “Data collection from…” or “Smartification of…” or 
“digitalization of…”. In step 2 a specific IoT concept is designed in order to meet the pain relievers and 
gain creators identified in step 1. 
 
Much additional design work is required in order to design a solution proposal that fits the needs and 
requirements of the stakeholder sphere surrounding a design space. These activities will not be 
mentioned here, even though this work should be prioritized with high importance and it is expected 
that the solution proposal is based on thorough design work. There are several methods that can be used 
to present a good solution proposal. The following acts as a guideline for what an IoT concept design 
should include as a minimum in this framework. 
 
• 
Concept Description 
The IoT concept should consists of a detailed concept description clarifying the scope the proposal 
is based upon. This will systematically explain the concept in a simple framing that maps out how

6.2.2 
Outcome 
The outcome of step 2 is a full concept description as mentioned above which can function as a 
foundation for the further work.  
 
6.2.3 
Considerations and limitations 
The concept description must include as much information of the solution proposal as possible in order 
to provide the best basis for analyzing the concept in the following steps. However, it can be difficult 
to reach the ideal level of detail at this stage of the development process as many unknowns are still to 
be investigated and settled. As a result, concepts at this stage often consists of many hypothetical 
features and functions based on assumptions that require validation before it can be possible to access 
the feasibility. This is particularly the case with the BOM as it is not appropriate to choose specific 
technical components at this stage. Although this creates an amount of uncertainty in the further work, 
I still argue that a lot of value can be derived from even highly unvalidated concept descriptions as it 
provides a foundation for testing assumptions in general through other types of design work. This 
process itself will also act as an assessment of the feasibility, durability and viability of the technical 
components and concepts in the system.     
 
 
6.3 Step 3: Simulation – Reference Architecture Mapping 
Drawing upon the arguments concerning a need for a common understanding of the essential building 
blocks of the IoT (IoT-A, 2013), the solution proposal is now mapped in reference models and 
architectures provided by the IoT-A RM and the ISO/IEC RA in step 3.  
 
6.3.1 
Purpose and activities 
As it is the case with the tools used in step 1, using the RM and RA provides the development team 
with an industry-recognized and standardized way of mapping out the proposed IoT design in a 
comfortable layout that can be understood across technical and non-technical domains. The mapping is 
carried out in three sub-steps: First, the design is mapped in the RM Domain Model in order to identify 
individual technical component roles in the system as well as their interrelationship. Secondly a tool is 
needed in order to drive the conversion from Domain Level to Functional View. Lastly, the IoT concept 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
42 
is mapped in the RA Functional View describing which functional building blocks are coming into play 
in the proposed design.      
 
6.3.2 
Mapping IoT concept: Domain Model 
First, the concept is translated into the IoT-A RM Domain Model (fig. 20) based on the technical 
concept description as well as the proposed BOM developed in step 2.  
             
  
 
 
Fig 20: RM Domain Model. Reference (left) and filled out example (right). 
 
Mapping in the Domain Model should be considered as a guideline for what technical components an 
IoT system consists of. Thus, the significance and presence of components will differ from system to 
system. In fig. 20 is shown an example from the case study that will be further explained in section 7.4. 
 
6.3.3 
Switching abstraction level – from Domain Model to Functional View 
Both the viewpoints visualized in the RM and the Functional View of the RA provide significant 
perspectives for understanding how the technical design influences the expected business model to be 
realized through an IoT system. The RM outlines which technical concepts, as found in the concept 
description, the system consists of. The RA maps which functional components are activated in- and 
around the IoT system. 
 
In order to bridge knowledge around the technical system architecture (RM) into functional components 
(RA) a mediating tool is proposed consisting of the following parts: 
 
• 
Domain: The technical component as listed in in the RM (Sensor, Service etc.).  
• 
Function in System: Describing which function the concepts/components have in the system. 
• 
Electronic Component/Example: Explaining which electrical component is used or could 
potentially be used that will meet the requirements for providing the needed functionality. 
• 
Supplier/Example: Shows who will be supplying the component (development house, EMS 
etc.) 
• 
Functional Group/Component (RA): To which group/component in the RA the technical 
concept (RM) is associated.  
 
 
It is important to note that the Functional View is not/cannot be directly derived from the Domain Model 
but is a product of a collection of all the identified and mapped considerations like concept description 
and Domain Model as well as other contextual parameters (e.g. company structure etc.). This needs to 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
43 
be considered when filling out the tool. An example of how the mediating tool is operationalized can 
be found in appendix A7.4.2 and section 7.4. 
 
6.3.4 
Outcome 
Once the abstraction levels have been analyzed and bridged it is now possible to visualize the affected 
functional groups- and components of the IoT system in the RA Functional View (fig. 21). 
 
               
 
  
 
 
 
 
Fig 21: RA Functional View. Reference (left) and filled out example (right). 
Mapping of the IoT system in the Functional View provides an abstraction level that has as a close 
interface with the operational jobs and tasks needed to be taken care of by an IoT system owner. It 
enables an analysis of which system architectural groups that activates different types of activities, 
resources, partnerships and assets needed to operate the system. The identified affected groups and 
components will be collected and used for further analysis in step 4.  
 
6.3.5 
Considerations and limitations 
Even though the IoT ARM and the ISO/IEC RA standard provide easy, cross-domain understanding of 
IoT systems the development team should be aware that the mapping of the IoT concept should be 
conducted, or at least supported, by trained stakeholders that provide documented skills in IoT system 
architecture design. This could potentially limit the flexibility of using the RM and RA internally in a 
cross-domain development team. However, the work executed in step 3 will act as a foundation for the 
analysis done in step 4 and therefore needs to be conducted in thorough detail. The work is therefore 
not suitable for a collaborative environment like e.g. a workshop, at first.    
 
 
6.4 Step 4: Evaluation – System Architecture Analysis 
Step 4 is the key novel design methodology contribution in the framework used to analyze and 
understand how the technical system architecture of the design proposal influences the business model 
expected to be realized through IoT innovation. 
 
6.4.1 
Purpose and activities  
The purpose of step 4 is to provide the bridging between the system architecture and the business model. 
It is here that the causal effect of certain technical design choices is translated into effect on the business 
model. As it is not possible to derive precise cause-effect relationships and results between system 
architecture design choices and business model perspectives it is necessary to provide a mediating tool 
that can control this process of cognitive evaluation in a design team.  
As mentioned in chapter 3, I argue that this can be achieved through executing a SWOT analysis of the 
functional groups/components of the RA. Once this is done, it enables a simple visual representation of 
the system architecture’s potential effect on Business Model Parameters using color coding of the 
Business Model Canvas building blocks. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
44 
6.4.2 
SWOT analysis 
In order to have the prerequisites to conduct a SWOT analysis it is suggested to supplement with a 
detailed and understandable description of each RA functional component of the system as well as what 
parts/concepts of the RM the component are associated with in order to enable backtracking and 
troubleshooting. The (slightly expanded) SWOT analysis in step 4 is proposed as follows (an example 
of a filled-out version can be found in section 7.5 and appendix A7.5.1): 
 
• 
Functional Group/Component (RA) 
o The individual functional group/component in question. 
 
• 
Description  
o A detailed description of what the functional component is, looks like and does in the 
system. 
 
• 
System Associates (RM)  
o Which RM parts/concepts is the RA functional component associated with? 
 
• 
Strengths  
o Which strengths does the functional component have that is associated to the business 
model?  
• 
Weaknesses  
o Which weaknesses does the functional component have that might affect the business 
model?  
 
• 
Opportunities  
o Which opportunities does the functional component enable that is related to the 
business model? 
 
• 
Threats  
o Which threats to the business model can be identified that are caused by the functional 
component?  
 
• 
Possible business model effect  
o What effect caused by the functional component can be expected to be seen in the 
business model? 
 
• 
Business Model Canvas Threats/Change/Opportunity  
o Converting the possible business model effects into traffic light color coding that can

6.4.3 
Outcome 
The outcome of step 4 is an in-depth analysis of the possible effect that different design suggestions in 
the system architecture might have to the business model expected to be realized though IoT innovation. 
The initial Business Model Canvas filled out in step 1 can now be color coded according to the results

Considerations and limitations 
It is in general important to consider the role of stakeholders in step 4 as the assessment of the causal

On the positive side, the assessment in step 4 is still built on thorough prior analysis in step 3, which

As mentioned in section 6.2.1, thorough design work is needed before conducting step 2 in order to 
develop an appropriate IoT solution proposal which can be described in the concept description in this 
step. An example of this work could be as conducted in the previous project with Sjørring Maskinfabrik. 
The solution proposal is therefore built based on this work. Fig. 25 shows a visual representation of the 
concept proposed. To access the full concept description please see appendix A7.3.1. 
 
 
Fig. 25: The SmartAttach – simple system architecture overview. 
 
7.3.1 
SmartAttach - key outcomes from step 2 
The SmartAttach solution concept proposes two design alternatives of how to transmit data wirelessly 
from the bucket into the cloud (fig. 25). One way is to use the construction machine’s existing telematics 
system6 as a gateway by connecting the bucket to the construction machine via Bluetooth. From here, 
the telematic system forwards data to the cloud via GSM. The telematic system is provided by a 
company called DCT. Another way is to transmit data directly from bucket using a narrow-band 
communication protocol, e.g. NB-IoT or Sigfox.

7.4.1 
SmartAttach - key outcomes from step 3 
The SmartAttach system consists of a sensor-box with a unique ID (tag), which is placed on the bucket 
(PED). The device wakes up when movement is detected and logs vibration orientation data (SCD) 
which will be transmitted wirelessly (Connectivity) and should work with many stakeholder systems, 
machines and products (Interoperability). The system will monitor operational status and faults (OSS) 
and use data processing and analytical services from the cloud provider (ASD). The data can be accessed 
by different human- and digital users from APIs and controlled access environments (ACD) as well as 
human users can access data through a user interface on a computer or other device (UD). Last but not 
least, the system should process data in a secure way (security). 
 
This overview of the activated functional groups and components provides a more appropriate 
abstraction level for further understanding of which jobs and tasks that needs to be done in order to 
make the IoT system work. It also provides the development team with transparency in which 
operational activities are taking place in the SmartAttach functional groups, which specific 
electrical/technical components and concepts are doing the job and who is the supplier of these.

7.5 Step 4 
An analysis is done on the possible effect that the proposed system architecture might cause to the 
business model expected to be realized through the SmartAttach concept. The analysis is based on the 
parameters as described in section 6.4.2. Fig. 29 shows an extract of the table in which this is done. Full 
assessment can be found in appendix A7.5.1.  
 
 
Fig. 29: Extract of table – System Architecture/Business Model Analysis 
7.5.1 
SmartAttach – key outcomes from step 4  
From the analysis, one particular finding stands out: The two scenarios of how to execute wireless data 
transmission (see appendix A7.5.1) each have individual effects on the complexity level and feasibility 
of the business model. The first scenario, where the construction machine is used as a gateway, the 
SmartAttach only uses Bluetooth as wireless communication protocol. As a result, the complexity level 
of the ACD, SCD, Connectivity and Interoperability functional groups decreases as Bluetooth is an 
industry recognized standard with high market availability and interoperability. This could have a 
positive impact on the key resources and activities needed to be conducted and price of device might 
drop. However, this scenario requires that OEM and telematic system provider (DCT) are included in 
the system as data co-owners. This partnership structure will result in increased complexity level in the 
Key Partnerships of the business model as well as change the activities and needed resources. On the 
other hand, providing data in third party partnerships might also result in an unexpected revenue stream 
opportunity. 
The second scenario, where data is being transmitted directly to the cloud from the SmartAttach, raises 
the technical complexity level of the SCD, Connectivity and Security functional groups. This could 
have a negative effect on the key activities and resources needed for the system to operate as Sjørring 
Maskinfabrik will be in charge of reliability of data collection (network coverage etc.). On the other 
hand, this scenario is isolated from inclusion of third-party suppliers and partners, which will lower the 
complexity level of Key Partnerships in the business model.      
 
Once the analysis has been conducted, the results are directly transferred from the far-right column of 
the table named BMC threat/change/opportunity (fig. 29). The results are transferred to the Business 
Model Canvas using color coding which provides a simple visual representation of the analysis. The 
example in fig. 30 shows the Bluetooth/gateway architecture scenario. For detailed color-coded 
Business Model Canvas please see appendix A7.5.1.1

7.6 Conclusions and key takeaways 
To sum up, conclusions and key takeaway are listed: 
 
• 
The framework for interdisciplinary technical- and business model design thinking supports 
decision making in the early stages of IoT innovation projects by providing a tool that help a 
cross-domain development team of e.g. engineers, management and business development 
employees understand how technical design choices in an IoT system might affect the desired 
business model expected to be realized from an IoT intervention. It enables the possibility of 
conducting agile analysis of concrete technical solution proposals down to the level of specific 
functionality and electrical components, modules, protocols etc. It also provides an overview 
of which technical concepts are associated with specific functional groups as wells as who 
would be supplying them in an IoT system. The SWOT analysis mediates the understanding of 
the effect on the business model certain technical design choices that can be expected.   
 
• 
Furthermore, the system supports a need for new digital business model tools that incorporates 
technology as an active stakeholder (Aagaard et al, 2018). The framework identifies the 
interrelationship, and act upon the mutual effect, between the business design of how the 
Sjørring Maskinfabrik’s SmartAttach provides customer value and the technical systems design 
needed to realize it. The framework incorporates well known methods, tools and practices from 
both domains using Value Proposition Design (Osterwalder et al, 2010), Business Model 
Design (Ibid, 2014) and systems architecture Views and Viewpoints (Raymond 1995, Shames, 
2004, Woods, 2011, Rozanski 2012, Gressler et. Al, 2018) in a design thinking process inspired 
by the basic design cycle (Roosenburg & Eekles, 1995).  
 
• 
Step 1 facilitates a common baseline for understanding the initial business idea and/or company 
challenge the idea is spawned from. By using well known tools it formats the input in a well-
structured and standardized way which can be used for further work in the process. 
 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
54 
• 
Step 2 provides a guideline of how to conduct an IoT concept description suggesting all the 
necessary parameters that should be included in order to analyze the concept further in the 
process. 
 
• 
Step 3 makes use of industry recognized IoT reference models and architectures derived from 
the IoT-A work (2013) and the ISO/IEC 3141 standard (2018) to give a simple and 
understandable overview of the solution proposal system architecture. Using both the Domain 
Model (RM) and the Functional View (RA) it is mapped which functions are activated in the 
system, how and by who. 
 
• 
Step 4 analyses how the functional components and groups of the proposed system architecture 
will be expected to affect the business model. The SWOT analysis evaluates the capabilities 
and criticalities of each functional group, including different functionality scenarios, and sums 
them up by assessing its effect on specific building blocks of the Business Model Canvas.

8 Discussion 
 
 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
56 
As a reflective practice, certain aspects of the proposed framework and the process call for discussion. 
First, how the framework supports the, in literature, identified needs for new interdisciplinary tools. 
Furthermore, some reflection is needed on how the framework was empirically evaluated in this project. 
Also, the framework in itself has some challenges that is appropriate to mention in this section. Lastly, 
it should be considered how the framework can be contextualized in an industry setting. This I strive to 
address in the following sections.     
 
8.1 Addressing the needs for new IoT innovation tools 
As identified in section 2.2.3, current tools used in respectively technical- and business-oriented IoT 
development seem to be insufficient in including important characteristics from both fields which can 
appropriately be leveraged and combined in early stage IoT development. The proposed framework was 
developed based on the identified domain gaps, mainly being the ability to make the IoT system 
architecture easy to understand for non-technical stakeholders and clarify how technical design choices 
directly affect operational parameters of the IoT system, hence affecting the business model. It is yet 
unclear if stakeholders actually achieve this. Furthermore, the framework does not address what should 
be done in the case that a system architecture has a negative or unwanted influence on the business 
model. However, the framework facilitates a way to backtrack and perform troubleshooting in the 
system architecture in order to identify the technical design choices which can then be changed or 
reconfigured in a more appropriate way. In the case study this would e.g. mean that the wireless 
communication of data could be re-routed to not include a partnership with the OEM but to the end-
user instead.  
 
8.2 Empirical analysis and evaluation method 
Another important aspect to address is the empirical evaluation method used to contextualize the 
framework. The exemplification with Sjørring Maskinfabrik is derived from a project conducted at 
FORCE Technology in the spring/summer of 2020, which failed to provide the support in choosing 
which wireless communication protocol should be used for further development. For this project I chose 
to conduct a hypothetical “desktop” case study with the Sjørring Maskinfabrik project applying the 
framework in order to analyze how it could alleviate the pains from the previous project. As a result, 
this project does not provide any insights and feedback from stakeholders which challenges the 
validation of the usability for now. With hindsight, another approach could provide better understanding 
of the performance and usability of the framework. An example could be to conduct a comparison of 
the outcomes on measurable parameters (ideas generated, challenges identified, time spend etc.) or 
performing qualitative interviews with various stakeholders to discuss the potentials of the framework. 
 
8.3 Using the framework in industry 
The purpose of the framework is to provide an applicable toolset that can be used by industry in order 
to support successful realization of IoT projects. More specifically, the framework was initially aimed 
at supporting the challenges met in the industry by FORCE Technology (see section 1.3).  
Fig. 31 shows an initial assessment of the applicability in each of the three IDSI service areas divided 
in eight industries which forms the IDSI market service strategy of 2021. Furthermore, it can be seen 
how the IDSI industries apply to the five general serviced industries in FORCE Technology (above) as 
well as how the clusters and research programmes that IDSI is involved in are focused by industry 
(below). This provides an overview of industry potential that also can be used outside of FORCE 
Technology context. For example, it is assessed that the framework would be beneficial in industrial 
IoT projects in several industries, which is often covered in the MADE research programme. 
 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
57 
 
Fig. 31: Assessment of framework applicability in IDSI context. 
 
However, several aspects need to be considered when applying the framework to an industry context: 
Firstly, the framework supports more specialized and detailed IoT development that allows stakeholders 
to operate on a deeper technical level. This also sets a requirement of maturity and quality of both 
business idea and solution proposal which delimits the usability at even more immature stages. In 
contrast, Aagaard et al. (2018) propose a tool for introducing simple characteristics of the IoT system 
architecture into business model innovation tools, which can be used at any maturity level of the concept 
and be more appropriate for a larger group of development teams and companies. As a result, the tool 
will most likely not be appropriate to use in some early stage IoT innovation projects at first but will 
however be of vital importance to some cases.   
 
Secondly, trained personnel are needed in order to ensure the quality and appropriate level of detail in 
step 2, 3 and 4. This limits the flexibility of companies being able to use the framework themselves. 
Thorough introduction to the framework would be needed which suggests that the framework is 
facilitated, or at least supported by consultants or specialists.  
Thirdly, it needs to be recognized that it can be challenging to reach the desired level of detail at this 
stage in IoT innovation projects. Most companies will have to incorporate system component vendors 
and supplier in order to fully understand the system architecture components. As a result, concepts at 
this stage often consists of many hypothetical features and functions based on assumptions that require 
validation before it can be possible to access the feasibility. However, I argue that much value can be 
derived from even highly unvalidated concept descriptions as it provides a foundation for testing 
assumptions in general through other types of design work. 
 
Lastly, it is yet unclear if the framework is best conducted in a workshop format or a guideline. On one 
hand, the outcome quality is very dependent on the framework being conducted consecutively as it 
ensures high quality input in all of the phases. On the other hand, if this can be ensured by the company 
itself from leveraging the appropriate competencies, the framework could be used partially, at the most 
simplified level, solely using step 4.  
 
8.4 The framework as a whole 
Some challenges of the framework steps have been identified, which is appropriate to address. In 
general, the framework does not provide a static frame, where certain inputs provide certain outputs of 
how system architecture components affect certain building blocks of a business model. It is more 
suitable to consider the framework as an analysis apparatus for how to conduct thorough cognitive 
analysis to identify potential criticalities that technical design choices can expect to cause to the business 
case.  
 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
58 
The activities in step 1 does not provide new contributions to the field but exploits the use of 
Osterwalder’s Business Model Canvas (2010) and Value Proposition Canvas (2013). IoT-A argues that 
already industry adopted tools can be used to create a comfortable and recognizable environment for 
stakeholders to work in. However, it is of vital importance that the business model canvas is being filled 
out anchored in the future to-be situation ie. the business model expected to be realized. If the analysis 
is based on a company’s current business model, the tool will not be able to operate in its current form. 
 
Step 2 suggests a guideline of which elements should be made clear in a solution proposal. This work 
requires thorough design work which this project is delimited from including. However, it is often the 
case that several concepts are suggested that will meet the specified requirements. In our case study 
with Sjørring Maskinfabrik, one concept was proposed including two possible ways of transmitting 
data. In this case the framework can provide a good comparison tool to assess and decide which concept 
to move forward with based on their potential effect on the business model. 
 
As mentioned earlier, the activities in step 3 are depending on the maturity level as well as which 
competencies and levels of expertise the development team contributed with. In order to ensure high-
level quality, the framework is constructed in a way that the system architecture of the solution proposal 
is mapped through the use of standardized reference architectures. In a case where a development team 
is able to provide a detailed, filled out Business Model Canvas and Functional View architectural 
representation of the solution proposal, some steps can be skipped. However, this limits the possibility 
of easily backtracking in the system architecture and identify root-causes, change components etc.     
 
Step 4 is the key element and novel contribution to the framework, translating functional parameters of 
the system architecture into potential business model opportunities and threats through a SWOT 
analysis. The analysis is therefore conducted based on the expertise, attitudes and level of experience 
present in a development team, which makes it vulnerable towards bias and contextual influence in 
general.

9 Conclusion 
 
 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
60 
This project proposes a new framework for interdisciplinary technical- and business model design 
thinking in early stage IoT innovation using reference models, canvases and architectures as key 
enablers. Findings from the case study show that the framework enables specific technical design 
choices to be analyzed at component-level to show possible effects on business model building blocks, 
thus identifying critical parameters at an early stage that was neglected or not possible to see to 
development teams before.   
 
The proposal is derived from knowledge gained in this project from four phases consisting of 1) a 
literature study of the current practices of IoT innovation from respectively a technical- and a business 
perspective in order to understand and mapped the individual mindsets, processes and tools currently 
used in the two domains; 2) a synthesis developed by inferring relationships, discrepancies and needs 
within the two domains; 3) framework development and 4) an exemplification and evaluation of the 
framework applied to practice through a case study.  
Findings from the literature study revealed that technology development have historically been treated 
in a siloed development culture from either a technical- or business-oriented perspective. Mature and 
adopted tools in the two domains e.g. The Business Model Canvas and System Architectures fall short 
on the ability to include important characteristics of IoT systems by not considering the interrelationship 
between business mechanisms and technical design choices. However, literature also shows that are 
much similarity in the way both domains make use of standardized models and architectures from the 
fields of MBSE and Business Model Innovation frameworks which can be leveraged.  
The novel framework proposes a Design Thinking process that includes industry recognized 
development tools: Osterwalder’s Business Model Canvas and Value Proposition Canvas as well as the 
IoT-A Architectural Reference Model and the ISO/IEC 30141 IoT Reference Architecture. In order to 
bridge the understanding of how the system architecture affects the business value, a SWOT analysis 
evaluates the Strength, weaknesses, Opportunities and Threats of each individual technical design 
choice up against the business model.  
 
The framework is conducted in four, design-cycle inspired, steps: 1) Identifying the business 
opportunity and standardizing the results in a Value Proposition- and Business Model Canvas, 2) 
Designing and IoT concept through a suggested concept template, 3) Simulating the system by mapping 
in two standard architectural models and 4) Evaluating how technical design choices affect the business 
model assumed to be realized through the IoT concept.    
 
The framework contributes to practitioner-oriented design methodology supporting the current gap 
between technology- and business design practices in IoT innovation. This project opens up for further 
research to further assess the performance of the framework in different project contexts. Especially by 
analyzing the usability at different maturity levels of IoT innovation. 
 
Additional work is needed in order to further validate the framework as the findings in this project is 
based on a hypothetical case study analyzing the differences up against the outcomes of an already 
finished project in FORCE Technology. Empirical analysis of the usability and performance in context 
with different development teams is needed. By conducting the additional research, it is my expectation 
that the potential value of the framework can be conclusively validated.

12.8 Appendix A7.3.1 – Sjørring Maskinfabrik SmartAttach: Concept Description 
 
Concept description: Sjørring Maskinfabrik SmartAttach 
 
The Sjørring SmartAttach is an IoT enabled attachment (bucket) for construction machines. The concept 
enables the bucket to collect, process and communicate sensor data collected on the bucket itself. When 
a bucket is idle on e.g. a construction site and/or not attached to a construction machine the system is 
in deep sleep with ultra-low power consumption and not logging, processing or communicating. When 
the bucket is attached to a construction machine, or a construction machine with an already attached 
bucket starts the engine, the system wakes up by detecting movement (vibration/acceleration). Once 
awake, the system starts advertising for Bluetooth pairing with nearby devices e.g. the construction 
machine’s telematic system or an operator smartphone. When paired, the operator will be able to access 
bucket information like e.g. ID number, dimensions, weight and type. This will enable the operator in 
easier, or even automatic reconfiguration of necessary machine parameters e.g. axel weight distribution.  
Furthermore, once awake the system will log vibration data and direction (x/y/z axis) as long as the 
system is running (ie. “engine on”). Data is transmitted wirelessly (see note) directly from bucket. When 
an operator detaches the bucket from the machine or turns off the engine the system will go back into 
deep sleep after not having detected movement for 15 minutes. 
Data can be accessed through Sjørring’s own data platform “AttachSense” or incorporated into 
stakeholder’s existing ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) systems and software via API. 
 
Note: 
Wireless communication version 1 
The sensor box placed on the bucket will utilize the already established Bluetooth pairing with the 
machine’s telematic system and transmit data via Bluetooth, thus using the machine telematic system 
as a gateway for shipping data into the cloud as the telematic system is already transmitting operational 
data from the machine via GSM.     
 
Wireless communication version 2 
The sensor box placed on the bucket has a build-in wireless communication module that transmits data 
directly from the bucket by using a suitable narrow band wireless communication protocol. Examples 
could be Narrowband IoT, LoRa or Sigfox. 
 
Impact for OEM 
 
Gain creators 
OEMs are competing on a global market where price, quality and brand are important parameters for 
construction machines and equipment. Therefore, being able to offer premium, reliable and future 
proven products that can alleviate as many operational pains for their customers as possible are key 
competitive factors. Furthermore, as the construction industry is maturing more and more towards 
sustainability at an organizational/strategy level, suppliers need to contribute with better, more 
optimized machinery. Being able to access data from the bucket will extend OEM’s knowledge of the 
machine’s operational parameters. Also, data can be used for more detailed root cause analysis of 
damage in warranty claims (i.e.: What is causing the machine to fail?).  
 
Pain relievers 
IoT enabled bucket will provide extended knowledge on machine usage and health condition by 
accessing parameters from a third-party supplier. The partnership with Sjørring Maskinfabrik will 
provide data anchored knowledge without having to deal with technical implementation other than 
providing Bluetooth pairing and implementation of bucket data into construction machine’s PLC7 
and/or telematics system.  
 
7 Programmable Logic Controller. Used for automation of industrial machinery  
==PAGE:81==
 
 
81 
Also, the physical architecture of the sensor box enables wireless communication and is retrofittable so 
already sold products can be included. Furthermore, the hardware is optimized to run on ultra low-
power consumption extending the battery lifetime to up to eight years, which is consistent with the 
average lifetime of a bucket.  
 
Impact for OED 
 
Gain creators 
Fleet management in construction companies is moving more towards calculating Total Cost Of 
Ownership (TCO) of assets. Here, both the expenses for purchasing, upgrading and maintaining 
(CapEx), as well as the on-going operating expense (OpEx) of an asset’s lifespan is calculated. 
Therefore, it is necessary for OED’s to support the requirements that construction companies have to 
be able to do so. The system enables dealers to do premium service offerings by being able to access 
data around a bucket (and machine’s) health condition. Repairs and maintenance checks can be done 
event-based instead of routine based reducing maintenance costs for OED and downtime for 
construction company. Also, if additional service is needed this can be detected and carried out in the 
same service visit.  
 
Pain relievers 
Data acquisition and knowledge about usage history can be used to eliminate doubts in warranty claims 
where it is unknown if the root cause of the damage is covered by the warranty/insurance. Currently 
OED’s normally accepts any damage in products that are claimed under the warranty, in order to 
maintain a high level of trust and service, even though it is presumed that many damage-cases are not 
covered under the warranty due to inappropriate use. Also, by accessing data the service technicians 
have a better opportunity to prepare and perform the necessary maintenance needed when driving to 
customer. Furthermore, OED’s will also benefit from the pain relievers that OEM’s get like the ability 
to retrofit device, no/low device maintenance etc. 
 
    
Impact for construction companies 
 
Gain creators 
Using IoT enabled construction machine buckets will support construction companies in the overall 
strategy of reducing operational costs. Operational data from the bucket will provide knowledge on how 
operators’ driving skills are affecting fuel consumption, number of load cycles, stress and wear of 
machinery, stress concentration on lifting arms etc.   
 
Pain relievers 
The concept will provide knowledge of the where-abouts of movable assets like e.g. buckets. Although 
the system does not provide detailed geographical location capabilities through e.g. GPS, users are able 
to combine data of which machine the bucket was last attached to as well as a time stamp of when it 
was last active (i.e. detached or engine shut off). This will indicate at which construction site the bucket 
presumably can be found, which is a sufficient level of detail to construction companies (Aarsleff, 2020) 
    
Technical description: 
The device consists of a low power System on Chip (SoC) ESP32 microcontroller with integrated WiFi 
and Bluetooth for communication. For this setup an ESP32 PICO KIT evaluation board is used. 
Furthermore, an ISM330DHCX module with accelerometer and gyroscope is connected to the 
microcontroller along with a microSD card for local access to stored data if needed. A Li-on battery is 
powering the device. The system is placed in a plastic casing, compliant with IEC IP67 i.e. dustproof 
and liquid proof.

Usage scenario: 
The following scenario is based on a hypothetical case where Aarsleff A/S (construction company) 
owns and operates an excavator produced by Volvo Construction Equipment (OEM) with a Sjørring 
Smart Attach excavator bucket, both bought through Volvo Entreprenørmaskiner Danmark (OED) with 
whom Aarsleff A/S also have signed a “gold” service contract with on both products. Asger is an 
employee at Aarsleff A/S operating the excavator on the construction sites. Søren is the operations 
manager at Aarsleff A/S.  
Furthermore, Volvo Entreprenørmaskiner Danmark is offering Aarsleff A/S access to operational data 
from the excavator in their online platform Volvo Active Care. The data is accessed via the machine’s 
Telematics System which is provided by a company named DCT.  
  
Device is attached to the excavator bucket on the upper rear surface where minimal contact with 
material being dug in occurs e.g. sand, gravel, water, clay, stone etc. (see figure x).

Asger gets in the cockpit and drives the excavator over to the bucket located on the construction site. 
He attaches the bucket to the excavator using a quick coupler8. The vibration/acceleration from the 
attachment wakes up the sensor box on the bucket, which starts to advertise for Bluetooth pairing with 
machine and initiates logging of vibration/movement data. Asger can see from his cockpit display that 
a bucket is asking for Bluetooth pairing, which he accepts. He immediately gets information on the 
buckets ID, dimensions, weight and model/type, which he uses for recalibrating the excavator to fit the 
bucket dimensions. This time is it necessary to redistribute the weight more on the rear axles. At the 
end of the day, Asger turns the engine off and the bucket goes to sleep.  
At the weekly staff-meeting, Søren is presenting statistics on fleet performance. It shows that excavator 
3 and 7 have done more load cycles than machine 4, which indicates that the buckets can be filled up 
more before unloading to save time and fuel. Søren also detects that two buckets have not been active 
for a week at the site. These can then be transported over to the nearby construction site where they are 
needed. 
Meanwhile the service manager at Volvo Entreprenørmaskiner Danmark is getting a notification that a 
bucket has reached a runtime of 4.000 hours and needs to be serviced. From the data he can see that 
some large acceleration peaks have been detected which could indicate that the bucket have been 
exposed to shocks, hitting hard surfaces like rocks etc. Usually this causes damage to the BOE9 so he 
also decides to bring a new blade. He is also working on a warranty case where a customer has reported 
a bucket with cracked welds and wants it repaired, which should be covered by the warranty. By 
comparing vibration data with data on how the bucket was oriented he can see that the bucket has been 
turned upside down simultaneously as large vibrations have occurred. This could indicate that the 
bucket was used to lift or move something heavy like a container. The manager engages in a dialogue 
with the customer in order to identify the root cause of the damage.
