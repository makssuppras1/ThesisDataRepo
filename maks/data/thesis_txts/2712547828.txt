Abstract
The small community in the Faroe Islands aims to become carbon neutral by 2030. Many
governmental and industrial plans have been laid out to achieve this, especially through
electrification. The Faroe Islands boasts abundant salmon production, but production re-
quires many boats and other fossil fuel users which are more difficult to electrify. More
sustainable means of production and transport may be possible through a P2X system.
In the case of using P2MeOH, methanol can be produced from H2 from excess power
production, CO2 and CH4 from the excess of processed biomass, and CO2 in air. The
methanol may be used as an alternative fuel for local industrial transport and fishing ships,
and O2 from the electrolysis can be used for aeration in recirculating aquaculture systems.
H2O from the production of methanol or other sources can be converted back to power.
Furthermore, it is possible for waste heat to heat relevant processes. The project is con-
ducted in collaboration with SMJ Consulting engineers. It focuses on the possibility of
using excess windmill production and other energy for a reversible P2X system in the
Faroe Islands. The project includes a modelling section and a feasibility study of the sys-
tem with varying price structures of P2X, along with relevant research and data collection.
The results are that it is possible to generate up to 78.7 tons/y of methanol, which can
have a levelized cost of 3.24 DKK/kg.

Introduction
1.1
The Faroe Islands
The Faroe Islands is a small archipelago of 18 islands in the Northeast Atlantic. According
to Hagstova, the population of the Faroe Islands was 52,109 people in 2020. It is a part of
the Danish kingdom, but the native language is Faroese, which is a derivative of Old Norse
from the Norsemen that arrived 1200 years ago. The Faroese people are a very traditional
type of people, with multiple survived traditions and a strong sense of community. In
recent years it has seen a travel boom due to its beautiful tall mountains, steep cliffs, and
mysterious vibe. Figure 1.1 shows the map and location of the Faroe Islands.
Figure 1.1: Map of the Faroe Islands. [11]
The Faroese government’s (Løgtingið) website facts and figures show that the Faroese
people have their own parliamentary democracy and several independent industries. The
main industries in the Faroe Islands are fishing and aquaculture, shipping and offshore
services, tourism and prospects for petroleum in the Faroese area [22].
The fishing industry has been a part of the Faroe Islands for a long time. Already in the
middle ages, dried fish was being produced and exported. However, from the mid 19th
century, there was a shift from the Faroe Islands being a peasant society to being a fishing
society [22].
According to Løgtingið, with a consistent population increase the 1960s started to need to
develop and to modernize commercial fishery. The production on land and on sea sped
up with deep-frozen fish as the leading product, creating more jobs. Today, fisheries and
aquaculture have a high value in the society, being 95% of the total export income and
20% of the total Faroese GDP, which totaled at 18,708 million DKK in 2017 [22].

As a global interest in decreasing emissions to the furthest extent has peaked, Faroese

1.2
Environmental plans
The policy document Energy and Climate Policy 2021-2030 was released in 2021 by the
Environment Agency in the Faroe Islands and the Ministry of Environment, Industry and
trade. This policy has its basis on “Orkupolitisk ætlan – hvussu vit røkka málunum fram
til 2025 og 2030” (Energy policy plan – how we can achieve the goals by 2025 and 2030)
and “Veðurlagspolitikkur Føroya 2020-2030” (Faroese Climate Policy 2020-2030) along
with political goals the current coalition have set. It is also an aim to increase the energy
independency in the Faroe Islands. It also replaces the previous 2009 policy.
According to The Environment Agency and Ministry of Environment, Industry and Trade,
the Faroe Islands has an interest with the energy and weather field for the next 10 years,
which include increasing the renewable electricity production, while simultaneously de-
creasing greenhouse gasses on land and sea. As one can see from ??, a commitment
has been made to the UN’s 1992 Climate Change Agreement and 2015 Paris agreement
to reduce greenhouse emissions, as well as to attain IPCC neutrality goal for 2050. As
well as this, the Faroe Islands together with other Nordic countries has agreed to “Vision
2030”, where it was a goal to have the Nordic countries be the most sustainable and most
collaborative area for 2030. On top of all of this, the Faroe Islands wish to set their own
goals, which are to be notified to the UN [30].
Since the last policy was published, The Environment Agency and Ministry of Environ-
ment, Industry and Trade shows that the goal to decrease greenhouse gases by 20%
from 2005 to 2020 fell short, and rather increased in that time frame. To ensure better
performance, more specific goals for the time frame 2020 to 2030 must be set and met,
ensuring to decrease the oil use and greenhouse gases by at least 30%. This needs to
be prioritised with political decisions that make a positive difference to respect interna-

To ensure meeting goals for 2030, the main focus must be to have a shift in the source

The current track for carbon neutrality by 2030 is shown in a case presented by The
Environment Agency and Ministry of Environment, Industry and Trade, which decreases
greenhouse gasses significantly by removing the use of fossil fuels and increasing the
renewable energy infrastructure. This will mainly be done with the increase of wind energy,
but also using pumping systems and water storages to combat peaks and dips in energy
availability. Solar panels will also increase the green energy in the summer, while the tidal
stream energy might be an option if it becomes a technically and financially competitive
energy source. Sea heating also may be an option.
Current estimates from The Environment Agency and Ministry of Environment, Industry
and Trade are that changing the energy system to fit the goals will cost 4-5 billion DKK.
This considers the development of wind energy, pump systems and energy storage, along
with the strengthening of the network with batteries, synchronous compensators and other
system units. These changes will decrease the oil import from 286,000 tons (2019) to
approximately 110,000 in 2030, assuming complete electrification. This will save approx-
imately 600-1000 mil DKK/year once in use. This is based on the prices at the time of
the release of the report, where the oil price to produce electricity is 3.60 DKK/kg or 0.80
DKK/kWh, as set by SEV, the energy provider in the Faroe Islands. Contrary to this, the
wind energy price for SEV is 0.21-0.25 DKK/kWh with 100% energy.

1.3.1
Electrification of heating, transport and industry
According to The Environment Agency and Ministry of Environment, Industry and Trade,
in the transport sector, all land vehicles, including cars, buses, trucks, and varying work
machines should be electrified and encouraged to do so through improved infrastructure
and regulations. This includes ensuring electric or hydrogen busses from 2025, and set-
ting a ban on purchasing or renting oil-driven vehicles from 2023. Furthermore, public sea
transport should turn to electrical drift the next time they are replaced. The decreased oil
usage will affect the CO2 emitted, as shown in Table 1.2. Total oil used in vehicles in 2020
was approximately 35 ktons. Transferring this to renewable energy will save 112 ktons
CO2/year.
In heating, it is an aim to ban all oil firers in new houses from 1. January 2023, and from
January 1. 2022, support schemes are to be made for private citizens who wish to change
their oil firers to a more sustainable choice. Likewise, houses should set out to insulate
better from 2022. Approximately 60 ktons of oil are burned to heat each year. Using
renewable energy, this will save 190 ktons of CO2/year.
Faroese industries use approximately 9% of all oil use, which equates to approximately 27
ktons. The biggest uses are the machines in building industry or feed production. These
will be more feasible with cheaper electric energy or other support schemes.

1.3.2
Reduce emissions on sea and on land
According to The Environment Agency and Ministry of Environment, Industry and Trade,
reducing emissions on sea and on land are a vital part of the policy, and are mainly sec-
tioned by reducing F-gas emissions, city planning, the sea and air industry, CO2 fees for
all oil use, and nature with CO2 binding. F-gasses are a strong greenhouse gas from
industrial use, such as what is used as a coolant in fishing ships (HFC and SF6). This is
to be reduced 75% by 2030 and 85% before 2036. With a shift to natural coolants such
as CO2 and NH3, which are also more energy efficient than other cooling systems, the
emissions will decrease by 90 ktons CO2 units.
City and town building, including road networks, energy networks etc. should be sustain-
able and regard the UN’s sustainability goals. Decreasing the energy necessary to travel
and heat will decrease the need to create energy. This planning should include closer
living quarters, better road connections, better walking and biking conditions, especially
for children and youth etc. Furthermore, city planning should include the possibility of
geothermal units, district heating, and access to electricity charging stations. Likewise
towns and neighbourhoods should collaborate for decentralized energy systems, such
as solar energy and geothermal units. Environment and nature should be considered on
land and at sea with the consideration of expected population growth.
The sea and air industry should ensure international regulations and recommendations,
such as from UN’s IMO and ICAO, for emissions and equipment. IMO approved a plan for
trade ships over 400 BT in 2011 for air pollution as seen in Annex VI for MARPOL. Likewise
they approved a plan in 2018 to cut emissions of greenhouse gasses for ships by 50% in
2050, with 40% less CO2 per ton goods from 2008 to 2030, and 70% from 2008 to 2050.
This can be done in the Faroe Islands through shifting to renewable energy sources for
ships to run on. Systems can be electrified, and this should be supported financially in
the Faroese government. Flight emissions totaled at 44 ktons CO2 in 2019, although not
registered in the Faroe Islands. ICAO wishes for the energy use in air travel to decrease
2% per year up to 2050. The EU ETS helps with systems to decrease emissions of CO2,
while CORSIA helps with marketing schemes for CO2. This does not apply to flights
between the Faroe Islands and Denmark as it is considered domestic travel [30].
Finally, to improve carbon neutrality, focus can be placed on how nature binds CO2.
Plants, algae and other beings remove CO2 from the air for growth, which will also de-
crease the CO2 pollution. However, they also release CO2 once they die and break down.
Therefore one challenge is to store these, for example through tree growth, wetlands and
uncultivated land. This also has the added benefit of being generally good for nature life,
such as for birds. As the Faroe Islands have a wet and cool climate, most of the earth
is soil and peaty soil, which contains much organic material, with the estimate that most
area under 300 m in the Faroes contains organic material. Wetlands in particular are great
storages for CO2 due to their lack of oxygen. Faroese authorities should aim to protect
wetlands and to limit pasturing in uncultivated land, which affects the intake of CO2 by
ruining new growth. The intake, storage and release of CO2 should be better regulated
to see future improvements, with support schemes to cover this [30].

Solar energy
While wind energy is a great source of energy for many months of the year, the summers
in the Faroe Islands are not very windy nor rainy. To meet energy demands, there needs
to be approximately 60 MW of solar energy in the summer. Each MW of solar energy
requires 6000 m2 of area and will supply 650 MWh per year. Large areas will be dedicated
for the majority of this, while it will also be possible to set up smaller solar energy panels
on roofs with correct procedures, and such regulations and procedures, price structures
etc for up to 11 kW of solar energy are from 2021 and valid from 01.01.2022. Likewise
these dates will reflect medium sized solar energy plants for the roofs of public buildings
and industrial buildings. Finally, 2022 will also mark when an offer will be made with clear
regulations and access to land. 60 MW will be 40 GWh of energy, which will save 9 ktons
of oil and 27 ktons of CO2. Solar panels will cost approximately 15,000 DKK/kW [30], [12].

Hydrogen and P2X

hydrogen and P2X solutions, with the advice to further analyze these in connection with
the total energy and transport systems, with this being the responsibility of Umhvørvissto-

1.3.4
transport
As of 2020, out of the 301 ktons of oil being used in the Faroe Islands, 84 ktons are
being used for fishing ships, 53 ktons are being used for other ships, and 35 ktons are
being used for industry [14]. These account for 28%, 18%, and 12% of the oil usage,
respectively. Furthermore, as of 2020, there are currently 208 electric cars [26].
Faroe Ship
After a meeting with Faroe Ship, a shipping and logistics company, the following informa-
tion was received. According to Faroe Ship, currently approximately 20 chauffeurs driving
approximately 20 vehicles are using their services across the Faroe Islands. These mainly
consist of Skania vehicles (90%) and Volvo vehicles (10%). There are 4 smaller vehicles,

Background
2.1
Power to X
In recent years, the shift to more renewable energy sources has been possible through a
high increase in wind turbines and solar panels. However, while wind and solar energy
can produce much power, they fluctuate in generation hours by hour, day by day. Multiple
storage technologies exist, including batteries, hydropumps, and thermal storage. Power
to X is a rising technology which may be one component in the solution to reach the
climate goals by 2030.
2.1.1
What is Power-to-X?
According to The Danish Ministry of Climate and Utilities, Power-to-X (often denoted P2X
or PtX) is a process that uses excess electric power to generate an “X” with the basis
of hydrogen. With P2X, renewable energy sources will be able to, rather than waste the
unused energy, both store it and use it to produce fuels and chemicals that otherwise
require fossil fuels to produce, meaning a reduction in CO2 emissions. Likewise, excess
heat from the process can be used in district heating, allowing for less energy waste.
Many fuels/chemicals can be produced, such as hydrogen, methanol, or ammonia, as e-
fuels. Power to X is possible through use of electrolysis of water (H2O), which produces
hydrogen and oxygen (H2 and O). Hydrogen can then further be chemically synthesized
with CO2 or N to create either methanol or ammonia, respectively [20].
With these e-fuels, the otherwise necessary use of fossil fuels can be replaced where
electricity may not be feasible or even an option. The Danish Ministry of Climate and
Utilities states how this is especially true when it comes to bigger vehicles such as heavy
trucks, big ships and aviation, as simply storing energy with batteries may be too expen-
sive and require far too large batteries. Moreover, Kibsgaard and Sustainability states
how P2X can procure necessary chemicals for feedstocks, medicines, plastics and more.
2.1.2
Power-to-X obstacles
Although P2X has many positive sides, according to The Danish Ministry of Climate and
Utilities, direct electrification will always be the most optimal and cost-effective way to
power transport and industry. This analysis concludes that approximately 70% of wind
energy can be converted into useful energy for an electric vehicle, but only 30% will be

Electrolysis
According to The Danish Ministry of Climate and Utilities, electrolysis is completed by
using electrolyzers. They produce hydrogen and oxygen from water and electricity without
emitting carbon. [7] states that electrolyzers fundamentally have two electrodes, an anode
and a cathode, as well as an electrolyte. The electrolyte lets ions through it, while blocking
the conduction of electrons. Either cations move from the anode to the cathode or anions
move from the cathode to the anode when there is a potential difference between the
electrodes. The reaction direction depends on the type of the electrolyzer.
Electrolysis creates hydrogen and oxygen at a 2:1 ratio from water and electricity without
any carbon footprint, because there is no carbon in the reaction. Water and hydrogen will
have a molar ratio of 1:1. The overall reaction at standard conditions (25°C and 100 kPa)
is:

Different types of electrolyzers based on electrolyte type
There are varying types of electrolyzers, which all have different properties, applications
and costs. According to Dincer and AlZahrani, the most suitable electrolyzer technology
may be based on a checklist containing the type of application, the operating conditions,
the cost, the environmental impact, the type of energy resource, and the commercial
availability. The three main types are alkaline electrolyzers, proton exchange membrane
electrolyzers, and solid oxide electrolyzers.
Alkaline electrolyzers (AE) are built to contain a liquid alkaline solution (often KOH or
NaOH) which is separated by a diaphragm. Proton exchange membrane electrolyzers
have a membrane electrode assembly (MEA), which is entirely submerged in water. Here
a membrane electrolyte conducting protons is surrounded by two electrodes. The elec-
trodes usually contain two layers, where the one facing the electrolyte is coated with a
porous catalyst, while the other thicker layer is a supportive construction for the MEA.
PEMs are generally the most promising electrolyzer type with the highest performance
due to the lowest electrolyte thickness, along with the simplest manufacturing method [7].
SOECs are the newest type of electrolyzer. The cathode, oxygen ion, electrolyte is often

Pyrolysis is a process that heats biomass or other organic materials anaerobically. The
lack of oxygen means that there is no combustion of material. The three main products
that come from pyrolysis are bio-oil, bio-char, and syngas. Bio-oil is a liquid organic mix-
ture, and can be used as a boiler fuel or even further treated to become a renewable fuel.
Bio-char is a solid product that is great for farming, especially in the case of absorbing
and retaining water, nutrients and agricultural chemicals [27].
Pyrolysis can be driven by itself once it is started, as the energy within the products can
fuel the reactions, while the syngases can combust as well driving the reaction [27].
Depending on the operating conditions, especially the temperature and heating rate, the
composition of the resulting products will vary. Biomass pyrolysis often occurs at 500°C.
Under this temperature, with a heating rate of about 1000°C/s, a yield of approximately
60-70 wt% will become bio-oil, 15-25% becomes bio-char, and the remaining 10-15%
becomes syngas. Lower heating rates will increase the bio-char production [27].
2.5
Gasification
Gasification is a similar process to pyrolysis, in that it is heats biomass to break it down.
Gasification is a thermochemical process that converts solid or liquid feedstock into gaseous
fuel of chemical feedstock. This is done without combustion. It adds hydrogen to the
biomass and make the energy content higher while removing carbon from the biomass.
This makes the gases that are released have a much higher hydrogen to carbon ratio.
Gasification typically includes drying, pyrolysis, partial combustion and finally the gasifi-
cation itself [6].
2.6
Carbon capture, utilisation and storage (CCUS)
CCUS is another way to work towards the climate goals. CO2 is abundant in our air, and
CCUS includes varied technologies that can balance this out.
According to IEA, CO2 can be captured where there are high concentrations of emissions,
such as near factories or industries that burn fossil fuels or biomass, or just in general from
the atmosphere.
CO2 once captured can then be compressed and transported via piping or shipping,
whereafter it can be used in the circular economy or stored. In the case of storage, it may
be trapped in porous geological reservoirs, surrounded by impermeable layer of rocks
[18].
There are multiple CCUS facilities in operation around the world, especially for natural gas
processing applications. There has been a large increase since 1970s or 1980s, when
the technology first began, to now, more than quadrupling in MtCO2 since 2000 to over
40 MtCO2. CCUS may also be retrofitted to already built facilities [18].
There are multiple CO2 capture technologies, including chemical absorption, physical sep-
aration, oxy-fuel separation, membrane separation, calcium looping, chemical looping,
direct separation, supercritical CO2 power cycles. The most used technologies are chem-
ical absorption and physical separation. Chemical absorption uses the reaction between
CO2 and a chemical solvent. Physical separation, which is mainly used in natural gas
processing for the production of methanol, ethanol, and hydrogen, is a process that can
be based on several things. These include adsorption, absorption, cryogenic separation,
and dehydration and compression [18].
Once the carbon dioxide is captured and transported, it can be used for a range of pos-

3.2.3
CO2 from air
Of the 5 types of CC technology (post-combustion, pre-combustion, oxy-fuel combustion,
chemical looping combustion and direct air capture), not all are viable options. As of the
report by Agency and Energinet, chemical looping is low on the development stage, and
has only been proven with gas as an input fuel, which this project avoids. Pre-combustion
has high investment costs and is also energy intensive. Post-combustion also gets ex-
pensive, although in the post separation methodology. Finally, oxy-fuel becomes costly in
the oxygen production stage. This now leaves direct air capture as the only viable option.
Ergo, in this project, DAC (direct air capture) will be considered. More than it being the
only viable option, it can also be located close to the production of methanol. This is
due to the fact that there is no CO2 heavy flue gas. Furthermore, as the energy use is
not as large scale as in other countries, it will be of less importance to have large scale
solutions. In direct air capture, the CO2 will be captured by absorption by using solvents,
usually CaCO3 and air conductors [1].
Mainly two types of DAC are considered to be most relevant for the near future. These are
solid adsorption & low temperature regeneration, and liquid absorption & high temperature
calcination. In the adsorption process, ambient air passes through and the CO2becomes
chemically bound to a filter, whereafter CO2 free air comes out. The filter is then heated
and the CO2 is collected.
In the absorption process, CO2 from air is absorbed in a a circulating caustic solution such
as potassium hydroxide. Hydrated lime binds to the the CO2 to create limestone CaCO3.
It is then calcinated, releasing concentrated CO2.

50 ktons of biomass is expected to yield 2.7-4 million m2, but as the capacity of the plant

the biomass is estimated between 50% to 70%, where almost all of the remaining gas
consists of CO2. This means there is a range of CH4 content from 2.7-5.6 mill m3/y, and a
CO2 content from 1.62-4 mill m3/y. Considering a CH4 molar density of 44.9 mol/m3, and
a CO2 molar density of 41.7 mol/m3, the ranges are then 3.8-8.0 mol CH4/s and 2.1 - 5.3
mol CO2/s.
These gasses can be partially oxidized and then used in the synthesis. In this case the
low CH4 value of 3.8 mol/s will be used to stay conservative. Again, the partial oxidation
of methane is:
CH4 + 1
2 O2 −−→CO + 2 H2.
(3.9)
The available O2 from the system will come from the electrolysis, and you need 2 moles
of CH4 for each mole of O2. If the available O2 is higher than half of the produced CH4,
then the value will be dictated by the half value of the available CH4. Otherwise, the CH4
will be limited to only twice the amount of the available O2. The resulting molar values of
CO and H2 are 1:1 CH4:CO and 1:2 CH4:H2.
Further processing
Because biomass does not completely become biogas, there is always some left. This
is currently used for fertilizer, but it is possible to treat it further. The remaining 40 ktons
(from 100 ktons planned) go to fertilizer indicating a gas yield of 60%.
The 40% remaining biomass can be dried and pyrolysed, and then gasified. This gasifi-
cation uses high temperatures of above 700°C with little oxygen or steam and converts
the biomass into CO, H2, and CO2, without ever combusting the biomass. Following this,
the syngasses can be again transformed into methanol [2].

Economic analysis
5.1
Methodology of analysis
In this section, the costs have been estimated using especially technology data sheets
from the DEA. In the cases where prices in the technology data catalogues are displayed
in EUR, the conversion rate of 7.45 DKK/EUR has been used, as this is the reciprocal of
the stated conversion rate to EUR used in such cases.
Again, the cost analysis can be especially split into several sections:
1. Electricity price for excess electricity
2. Cost of electrolysis components and process
3. Cost of carbon capture components and process
4. How much CO2 and CH4 is available from biomass
5. Methanol creation from H2 and CO2
6. Storage and transport
5.1.1
Excess electricity
The electricity price is rated at somewhere between 0.21-0.25 DKK/kWh, as stated by The
Environment Agency and Ministry of Environment, Industry and Trade. In the economic
analysis, the average value of 0.23 DKK/MWh will be used. The excess electricity for this
analysis will be utilized mostly in the electrolysis, although some of it is also necessary in
DAC and possibly in the biogas plant. The excess electricity will be utilized as much and
efficiently as possible.
5.1.2
Electrolysis
In the case of hydrogen production via either AEC or PEM electrolysis, one can expect
several components, including a pump and AC-DC converter prior to the electrolyser stack
itself, followed by a heat exchanger and a dryer. Furthermore, the inputs into the system
will require pure water at 25°C, 1 bar and electricity at 400 V. For this, it may be necessary
to account for a water purifier (required water purity of 5 µS/cm), and also perhaps a
transformer. In the output stream there will be H2 pressurized at up to 35 bar along with
waste heat at 50°C. It may be necessary to upgrade these streams, and to do this one
may need to account for a compressor and a heat pump. These are included in the DEAs
cost estimation [2].
Electrolyzers are an excellent option for a relatively quick response in the case of usage,
with a cold start up time less that 2 hours, and and an idle, or warm start up time at only
1-5 minutes.
The DEA has estimated CAPEX requirements based on previous reports and manufac-
turers, with the above mentioned temperature, water purity and pressure. CAPEX based
on the size of electrolyzer systems is not expected to vary much due to the fact that elec-
trolyzer systems are built from building blocks, and the main additional costs are due to
an increase in the amount of units. Therefore, the prices are displayed per energy.
In 2030, it is expected that specific investment will be between 3353 and 4247 DKK/kW,
depending on if it is closer to a 100 MW or 1 MW electrolyzer, respectively. This value

will be decided based on a linear projection between the sample data, using the excess
power as the data point. The operational and maintenance cost will also be approximately
2-5% of this value yearly. Of the specific investment, the equipment will be approximately
90-95% of the cost, and the remaining 5-10% will be installation costs. A plant such as
this would be built in 1/3 of a year with a technical lifetime of approximately 30 years.
Storage and transport of O2
Considering how the electrolysis part of the system will be on site, i.e. near the methanol
synthesis process, the storage and transport of the H2 will be considered negligible, as it
will be used immediately in this analysis. However, the O2, if it is to be transported and
stored will have associated costs.
The storage costs of O2 will be considered negligible, both due to the fact that the smolt
production locations need to ship them internationally, and that the physical inputting into
cylinder stores will be less intensive than shipping the O2.
Considering the usage of O2 in smolt production facilities, which will be spread across
the country, the transport will likely be done by trucks, rather than pipes. Because the
smolt production facilities have their own units for aeration and degassing, the storage
and costs will not be considered for CO2, only the transport itself.
Using the technology catalogue for carbon capture, storage and transport, for a truck with
capacity 30 tons CO2, the fixed cost will be approximately 28.31 DKK/tCO2 in 2030, with
a variable cost per km of 1.04 DKK/t CO2/km. These trucks travel both ways, but return
empty. With an estimated driving range from varying smolts being 80 km, the total cost
per truck can be found by multiplying by 30 tons and 80 km and summing the value, giving
1185.3 DKK per truck. Based on the given available CO2, it is then possible to find the
amount of trucks required per year as well as the yearly cost of transport.
It is also assumed that the costs of transporting O2 will be the same, due to the fact that
the gasses have similar properties. The costs associated will be calculated in the same
manner.

DAC

Storage and transport

would not match the possibility of carbon capture. Furthermore, storing CO2 would lead
to other options, such as using for other purposes, putting it into organic matter, or even
shipping it out of country.

6
Discussion
From the previous sections, it is apparent that with more methanol, there is a higher in-
vestment cost, but a lower methanol fuel price/levelized cost. In each of the scenarios, the
largest investment cost was the investment cost for the methanol, although the variable
costs were not. Over 20 years, the ”largest” methanol cost became the least costly, with
the lowest levelized cost or methanol price in DKK/kg. Furthermore, the total amount of
the methanol in each of the three scenarios was 36401, 78778, and 36401 tons/y. The
amount of oil that needs to be replaced is upwards of 173 tons of oil per year, although
part of this value (35 ktons) is from undefined vehicles. It is likely that in the case of
smaller vehicles or busses, hydrogen would be a preferred fuel in the case of the use of
FCEVs, rather than methanol. Methanol would likely be saved for ships and larger trucks.
Scenario 2 has a close value to that of the required use for fishing ships, at 84 ktons/y.
Institute states how the cost of methanol is often lower than the equivalent cost of LNG,
for example. The cost to fuel is lower by the amount of energy. Methanol requires about
twice the space of oil, though, and will have a similar energy density to LNG.
Methanol is 5.5 kW/kg, meaning that for scenarios 1, 2, and 3, the prices per kW are
21.79 DKK/kW, 17.80 DKK/kW, and 25.81 DKK/kW, respectively. Considering how these
values used a likely and average lifetime of 20 years, it is likely that the methanol price
should be lower per power content.
Furthermore, there is potential for more analyses, for example for biogas from aqua and
boats, which has been shown in figures, but not been quantified, by further using the
biomass via for example gasification. In this case, one could expect that with higher yield,
it would be possible to create even more methanol, lowering the price.
The capacity factor of the components of the plant can also be varied to see if there
are cheaper ways to get the same amount of methanol. For example, in scenario 2, the
available CO2 is close to 3/4 of the input of H2 for the methanol synthesis, rather than
being close to 1/3 as the other two scenarios. The value would have been closer if either
the DAC or the CO2 from fish had not been considered or both had been lowered, which
would likely have saved some more costs.
From using only the excess amount of electricity it does not seem that all ships and heavy
transport can be replaced with methanol. This does not mean that it needs to be disre-
garded. While the hydro pumping is the current consideration, but has not been included
in this project, it is quite costly, at 1-1.3 billion, while there still seems to be nothing to
do with the excess energy over longer than a year. The methanol scenarios ranged at
investment costs from approximately 800 MDKK to 2 BDKK, and make use of reducing
the emissions without requiring the electrification of old ships and vehicles, or buying new
ones, as they have the opportunity to retrofit.

Conclusion
This project considered power to methanol, and it is discovered that one may be able
to create fuel at approximately 3.2-4.7 DKK/kg methanol. Furthermore, with the current
power supply, one can expect to be able to produce somewhere between 36 ktons and
79 ktons, based on existing and new technologies, i.e. electrolysis, biogas, and DAC.
Although the entire oil use in transport can not be covered, the possibilities of retrofitting
while still being able to fuel a significant portion of the transport is valuable. Furthermore,
the costs are variable and may even be lower than hydropumping, which although creates
balancing for the grid does not replace the existing infrastructure, where there will need
to be something new. Therefore it is still of value. Finally, there is potential for more
analyses, for example for biogas from aqua and boats, which has been shown in figures,
but not been quantified, by further using the biomass via for example gasification. In this
case, one could expect that with higher yield, it would be possible to create even more
methanol, lowering the price.
