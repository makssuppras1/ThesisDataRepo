Abstract
Music appreciation is generally reported poor in Cochlear Implant (CI) users. Their
performance judging in spectral cues is reported to be unsatisfactory. However, temporal
cues such as rhythm and tempo are perceived without much diﬃculty. Studies have also
indicated that these temporal descriptors help them understand the emotional content in
the music. In this study, a model is proposed which is based on pulsation cues produced
by drums as they are devoid of melodic content and tonal pitch. Under the proposed
model, it was assumed that Normal Hearing (NH) listeners will simulate the CI response
for both emotional content and preference of the music.
In the ﬁrst experiment, 12 NH listeners’ emotional response and preference was
recorded for 28 drum and piano clips. In relation to that, the second experiment aimed
to obtain perceived pulsation value through a tapping experiment. The third experi-
ment, in which 16 NH listeners performed emotion-preference rating task and tempo
reproduction tasks for drum and piano clips which were set at speciﬁc values of 60 bpm,
120 bpm and 150 bpm.
It turned out the pulsation from drums from NH listeners was successful in modelling
the CI response for piano. However, it was also studied that pulsation is not the only
cue which governs the emotional and preference in CI users. In addition to pulsation,
there is the musical mode which help CI users understand emotion in music and guides
their preference.

Introduction
1.1
Music Emotion Processing - A biological basis
Humans are predisposed to emotional learning and processing music. The biological basis
of social development and ﬁne-tuning for auditory perception indicates that these two
happen in conjunction and their association begins at the infancy stage. Evidence shows
that infants discriminate happiness, sadness and anger from face expression (LaBarbera
et al., 1976) and integrate visual information from faces or images to discriminate happy
and sad tones in vocal expressions (Walker-Andrews and Lennon, 1991). The innate
propensity to process various dimensions in music is also observed during infancy (Tre-
hub, 2003). For instance, infants from 2 to 6 months exhibit a preference for consonance
over dissonant music (Trainor et al., 2002, Zentner and Kagan, 1998) and are able to
recognise familiar melodic patterns and discern the relationship of tonal elements in mu-
sical pattern (Chang and Trehub, 1977). From 7-9 months infants categorize auditory
sequences on the basis of rhythm and tempo.(Trehub and Thorpe, 1989) and are able to
classify tonal stimuli based on the spectral structure in musical timbre (Trehub et al.,
1990). The emotional learning and its association with music are consolidated in early
developmental stage. Around 2-3 years of age, children correctly recognise happy and sad
emotions by talking about them as well as identify them in situations (Wellman et al.,
1995). In relation to music emotion recognition, studies have shown that at the age of
three, children are able to distinguish between happy and sad tones in the music of their
own culture. (Kastner and Crowder, 1990). The recognition of happiness in music occurs
at the age of four (Cunningham and Sterling, 1988) however, identiﬁcation of sadness,
threat, and anger in music develops at the age of 6 or later (Dolgin and Adelson, 1990,
Terwogt and Van Grinsven, 1988). Children at ﬁve years of age, discriminate happy from
sad music based on diﬀerences in tempo (fast versus slow) while at six, they can dis-
tinguish the emotions based on changes in mode (major versus minor) similar to adult
listeners (Dalla Bella et al., 2001).
This account on biological basis of music emotional processing asserts that sensitivity
towards emotions emerges primarily for happiness, sadness, fear and anger. These four
emotions are often identiﬁed as basic emotions. The nature of basic emotions has always

1.2
Association of music with emotion and preference
The aﬀective dimension of music reﬂects that music not only conveys emotion but also
elicits emotion and inﬂuences the emotions of listeners as well as induces emotions.
(Gabrielsson and Lindstr¨om, 2001, Hunter and Schellenberg, 2010). Several features of
music such as pitch, melody, rhythm, tempo, loudness, consonance and timbre have
an impact on our music enjoyment, preference, mood and emotional response. Studies
have provided evidence that our emotional response is largely governed by these musical
characteristics (Juslin and Laukka, 2004). One of the most intriguing studies was carried
out by Balkwill and Thompson (1999) proposed a model based on various psychophysical
dimension for music emotion perception. A psychophysical dimension is a characteristic
or property of musical structure which is perceived by the listener. For e.g., speed of
the pulse is mapped as tempo, sound intensity is a mapping of loudness etc. These
psychophysical dimensions function as universal cues in emotion perception in music
and are independent of prior music experience or enculturation. The study credits the
fact that listeners use psychophysical cues to identify emotions expressed by musical
structure.
Although various musical characteristics loudness, timbre, tempo, pitch height such
as and its relation with emotion response has been studied (Balkwill and Thompson,
1999, Balkwill et al., 2004, Gundlach, 1935, Ilie and Thompson, 2006, Juslin, 1997), the
available evidence advocates that tempo and mode are the strongest determinants of
emotional content in music (Hevner, 1935, 1937, Juslin and Laukka, 2004). Both tempo
and mode are readily associated with happiness and sadness in music and this association
may also be evident across varied musical cultures (Balkwill et al., 2004, Fritz et al., 2009)
There is a direct relationship of tempo and mode with music expression. Generally, fast
tempo and major mode are associated with happiness and slow tempo and minor mode
are attributed to sadness.
It is a well-observed fact that listeners like some musical pieces over others or a cer-
tain type of music piece is preferred over others or a certain music piece is disliked by
listeners. This labels preference as one of the most aﬀective responses to music. Liking or
Disliking of music is primarily driven by factors of consonance-dissonance, familiarity or
previous exposures to music piece and emotion conveyed by the music or mood induced
by the piece (Hunter and Schellenberg, 2010, North and Hargreaves, 1997).
The following account takes a step further to delve into the subtle concepts of rhythm
and tempo.

1.3
Temporal Organisation of Music
The analysis of acoustic stimuli is broadly divided into components on perceptual level:
Spectral (pitch) organisation and Temporal organisation. The reference for this organi-
sation comes from the modular model of music processing (Figure 1.1) which provides
a cognitive architecture of music processing.
The model has been proposed specially for the study of music deﬁcits in brain-
damaged patient but also provides guidelines for understanding music processing in
people without any damage to music faculty of the brain. The pitch organisation for
analysis of acoustic stimuli has been extensively studied while temporal organisation
remains an area of interest in understanding music faculty. (Peretz and Coltheart, 2003).
This model helps to understand the temporal organisation of music faculty with main
focus on analysis of rhythm and its perceptual aspects, primarily tempo. The model
was useful in understanding the concepts of rhythmic organisation, emotion expression
analysis and motor activities such as tapping which form the cornerstone of this study.

1.3.1
Rhythm
Rhythm (derived from the Greek word, ”rhythmos”) means ordered movement or form
or a way of ﬂowing, which is marked by succession of elements which has a pattern.
This ordered quality of succession in rhythm is readily perceived in dance, music, poetry
and song. Another characteristic trait of rhythm perception is anticipation. This aspect
can be well-understood from one of the modern deﬁnitions of rhythm given by Martin
(1972) which states ”Inherent in the rhythmic concept is that perception of early events
in a sequence generates expectancies concerning later events in time (p.503)” . In order
words, the presentation of earlier events, (such as notes in music or seasons in a year)
are characterised by a pattern. This pattern in time leads to expectations that the later
events will be characterised by the same pattern. (Fraisse, 1982).
In music, rhythm is the temporal pattern created by the music notes as it evolves over
time. Research on psychological perception of rhythm shows that is the time between
the notes, or Inter-onset interval (IOI) which determines the perceived rhythm and not
the duration of notes (Krumhansl, 2000, Tan et al., 2017). The fundamental elements in
perceptual rhythmic organisation which are Pulse and Tempo.
The ability to anticipate rhythmic stimuli and synchronising movements with regular
sound sequences demonstrates that there exists a strong link between rhythm perception
and production. The accuracy of synchronisation ranges between 150 ms or 100 bpm to
1600 ms (37 bpm) (Fraisse, 1982, Krumhansl, 2000).
1.3.2
Pulse and Tempo
Pulse is the subjective mapping of beat. It is the pulsation that makes us kinesthetically
respond to music. Motor activities such as tapping the feet, dancing, marching are the
response because of the sensation of the pulse embedded within the rhythmic aspect of
music.
Tempo (from Latin ’tempus’ meaning time) is the rate at which the beat ﬂows
through time. In other words, it is the rate at we perceive the pulse in a music piece.
In music, tempo refers to the speed or pace of a given music piece i.e., how slow or
fast is the song. It is measured in beats per minute (BPM). Tempo is one of the crucial
dimensions of music and is one of the factors which governs the overall framework of a
musical piece. On conceptual level, rhythm and tempo are independent of each other.
Tempo refers to the perceived elements per unit time (beats per minute), which means
that absolute duration of each element is perceived. In contrast, rhythm is based on
relative time as it is the organisation of the absolute duration of diﬀerent values of the
duration of perceived elements per unit in time. However, this independence holds only
for a certain range of tempi values as shown in Figure 1.2. Musical rhythms can only
be perceived within time span of 200 ms (300 bpm) to 1000 ms (60 bpm). Time events
below 200 ms are perceived as single continuous event whereas intervals greater than
1000 ms are heard as separate events and appear disconnected from one another. This
implies if the tempo slows down below 60 bpm both rhythm and melody are lost from
the music piece (Fraisse, 1982, Krumhansl, 2000, Tan et al., 2017).

Periodic pulse and tempo both form an attribute of our physical activities. Periodic
spontaneous physical activities such as rocking and beating of the heart (72 bpm) have
their own tempo which is referred to as Spontaneous tempo (also called personal tempo
or mental tempo). It is measured by natural of tapping falls in the range 380 to 880 ms,
600 ms (100 bpm) being the most representative value. On the other hand, motor ac-
tivities such as walking (120 bpm) are characterised by Preferred tempo. It corresponds
to the speed of sounds that appear to be most natural and people are able to synchro-
nise their physical response with the sound. Preferred tempo is generally measured with
Metronome and its measure centres around 600 ms within the range of 500 ms (120 bpm)
to 700 ms. Thus, the measures of spontaneous and preferred tempo have comparable
frequencies and are often associated (Fraisse, 1982).
The comprehensive studies conducted by Fraisse (1982) on the concepts of sponta-
neous and preferred tempo provide evidence that preferred tempo was usually located
around 100 bpm or 600 ms. However, the phenomena of preﬀered tempo was reconsid-
ered in several studies thereafter which contradict this value. van Noorden and Moelants
(1999) presented the resonance model for tempo perception based on the physical reso-
nance of human body. The model provides compelling evidence that preferred tempo is
located at a signiﬁcantly faster speed and is 120 bpm or 550 ms. (Moelants, 2002).
1.4
Music perception in CI users
With music emotion association and rhythmic organisation as biological basis, this rela-
tionship plays a crucial role in social and inter-personal development. For people without
any hearing impairment, it is a pleasant integrated experience.
However for people with post-lingual deafness, this scenario around them is rather

diﬀerent as task of comprehending emotion both in communication and music is de-
manding. This leads to lack of music enjoyment and impaired social participation. In
cases of profound hearing impairment which involves damage to the inner ear, cochlear
implantation serves to restore hearing. Cochlear implant is a prosthetic device which con-
sists of an array of electrodes that are inserted inside the cochlea. The implant bypasses
the damaged parts of the cochlea. The electrodes directly stimulate the auditory nerve
ﬁbers by converting the acoustic information from environmental sounds into electrical
pulses. This information coded in spectral and temporal domain is then transmitted to
the auditory cortex where they are interpreted as sound.
Cochlear implantation has been beneﬁcial among people with hearing loss as it has
ameliorated the scenario of social withdrawal, isolation, frustration, stress and impaired
memory. A considerable amount of studies have been carried out to study emotion in
language including rhythm and speech prosody perception in CI users. While speech
comprehension in CI users remains successful, music still remains a challenge. Due to
the complex nature of music over speech, music perception is generally poor and its
enjoyment has been reported to unsatisfactory by CI users (McDermott, 2004, Zeng,
2004).
1.4.1
Spectral aspects - Timbre, Pitch, Melody, Scale
Studies have conﬁrmed that CI users exhibit tremendous diﬃculty in perception of spec-
tral aspects of music. (Cooper et al., 2008). Pitch perception in CI users has been reported
to be poor. Spectral limitation in CI device is due to small number of channels and phys-
ical placement of the electrodes which leads to limited and poor frequency resolution of
the CI systems. (Caldwell et al., 2015). Pitch information is extracted from the temporal
envelope due to which many spectral cues from information are lost. Studies on musical
Timbre perception has shown that CI users primarily rely on temporal cues, both tem-
poral envelope and ﬁne structure than spectral envelope and ﬁne structure cues (Kong
et al., 2011, Prentiss et al., 2016). But accuracy in identiﬁcation of musical instruments
is a challenge for CI users (McDermott, 2004, Zeng, 2004). Perception melodic pattern
identiﬁcation and melodic contour change is a diﬃcult task for CI users. However, tune
identiﬁcation or recognition of familiar melodies is easier for melodies with rhythmic
pattern than less rhythmic ones (McDermott, 2004, Schulz, 1994). Due to lack of per-
ception of tonal pitch, CI users perform worst at scale detection tasks (Cooper et al.,
2008).
1.4.2
Temporal aspects - Rhythm and Tempo
CI users perform tempo discrimination and rhythmic tasks with ease and rely on rhyth-
mic cues for identifying familiar melodies (Gfeller and Lansing, 1991, Gfeller et al., 1997,
Kong et al., 2004) Rhythm recognition and reproduction tasks have revealed that per-
formance of CI is comparable with NH listeners (Schulz, 1994). Their performance is
comparable with NH listeners on beat detection and simple rhythm detection tasks is
comparable with NH listeners. However, for complex rhythmic patterns the performance

deteriorates (Gfeller et al., 1997, Jiam and Limb, 2019, Kong et al., 2004). This ease of
extraction of rhythmic component could be due to the preservation of temporal envelope
information in implant processing (Jiam and Limb, 2019, Zeng, 2004). CI users rely more
on temporal cues than pitch cues for perception of emotion in music and beneﬁt from
them (Ambert-Dahan et al., 2015, Caldwell et al., 2015) as compared to NH listeners
who rely on pitch and melodic content. In addition, detection of isochronicity in beat

synchronicity performance has been best for drums as compared to piano (Phillips-Silver
et al., 2015). There has been a considerable amount of evidence suggesting that there is
a strong reliance on temporal cues than spectral cues by CI users in music perception
which helps in pitch related tasks and identifying some aspects of timbre (Cooper et al.,
2008, Innes-Brown et al., 2013).
Despite the eﬀorts involved in music listening, research on CI and music appreciation
has shown that many people with profound impairment consider music enjoyment as a
major motivation for getting an implant over the beneﬁt of improved speech perception
and communication (Gfeller et al., 2002, Looi et al., 2012). Research surveys indicate sur-
prising facts that many CI users continue listening to music after implantation, some of
them ﬁnd music equally or more enjoyable than before implantation and about one third
of them want to undergo implantation just for being able to listen to music. (Kohlberg
et al., 2014). Reports from CI users show that listening to music has enhanced their
quality of life in social setting. (Ambert-Dahan et al., 2015, Innes-Brown et al., 2013).
One of the studies was carried out by Vannson et al. (2015) proposed a novel approach
for improvement of music perception and enhanced music enjoyment of CI listeners. The
strategy was based on the stream segregation phenomena (Bregman, 1994) which states
that rhythmic cues can accentuate the degree of segregation of two auditory streams.
The technique adopted in this study involved presentation of treble and bass clef of piano
samples (Vieillard et al., 2008) to opposite ears. The separation of two parts or musical
streams was suppose to provide laterlization or localisation cues that might supplement
music information and thus improve perception and recognition of emotion in music in CI
users. It was hypothesized that the Dichotic presentation of two musical streams would
lead to improvement in three parameters: clarity in music (Clear or Unclear), emotion
conveyed by the music (Happy or Sad) and preference of music (Like or Dislike). The
results from this study indicated that the proposed listening conﬁguration with later-
isation cue improved clarity in music perception. Statistical analysis showed that the
diﬀerence between listening conﬁgurations (Monoaural and binaural) were statistically
signiﬁcant. However, the presence of laterisation cues due to stream segregation did not
prove helpful in improving judgment of emotion in music and rating the preference of
music at a signiﬁcant level. Therefore, laterisation through listening conﬁguration can be
employed for clarity improvement but does not help in judgment of emotions in music
and rating its preference. This implies that CI users rely on some other cues for the

• Does pulsation inﬂuence the preference of the music of CI users?
• Is Pulsation the only factor in rating emotional content and determining the pref-
erence of the music?

Model framework
The model proposed in this study is based on psychophysical cue, pulsation. The model
is constructed from the response data from NH listeners and then implemented to sim-
ulate the CI response.
The construction of model involves three steps:
• Establishment of model: The ﬁrst step in model construction is based on the fact
that listener uses spectral, temporal and spectro-temporal cues available from the
musical piece are employed to derive musical information. This step serves two pur-
poses: Firstly, it conﬁrms that the response parameters under examination emotion
and preference are judged similarly across NH listeners. Secondly, it shows that
pulsation from the musical piece plays a role in judgement of emotion in music and
its preference. To fulﬁll the goals, Experiment 1 was designed.
• Implementation of model: The starting point of this step utilises two facts. First,
CI users exhibit no diﬃcultly in tasks involving rhythm and tempo (as discussed in
subsection 1.4.2) forms starting point of the model. And second, rhythmic patterns
and beat perception are easily comprehended in percussion instruments, particu-
larly in drums. There were two sub goals in this step:
– Firstly, to examine if the perceived pulsation from drums and piano is same
or diﬀerent. It is hypothesised that the perceived sensation will be same for
both musical instruments.
– Secondly, beat perception is better in drums than piano. This suggests that
sensing the pulsation and reproduction of tempo for drums clips is easier than
piano.
In order to achieve the above two goals, Experiment 2 was designed.
• Working of the model: Lastly, having proved that drum is a better prototype than
piano. The response of NH listeners for drums was used to simulate CI emotional

and preference rating for piano clips. It is assumed that CI users perceive similar
pulsation in piano as NH perceive for drums and that NH listeners will judge
emotional content and rate preference based on the perceived pulsation from drums
as the only available cue.
The model based on pulsation was employed to address the ﬁrst two questions of this
study. It was investigated if the perceived pulsation in music inﬂuences the judgment of
emotions and preference of CI users. Under this premise, the ﬁrst hypothesis is proposed

able to model the emotion and preference data from CI users.
It was further examined if pulsation is the only factor that is suﬃcient to rate the
emotional content and preference. Under this argument, second hypothesis was proposed
stating that the perceived pulsation matches the tempo at which the musical piece
is presented which forms the second hypothesis. Elaborately, this means that if the
presented music clip was at 60 bpm, the participants were able to perceive 60 beats per
minute and tap at the same rate. Similarly, for tempo values 120 bpm and 150 bpm. For
this purpose, Experiment 3 was designed.

Materials and Methods
Participants
All participants involved in this study were normal hearing listeners. They all either went
through an audiometry or a standard screening test which comprised of a questionnaire
(which consists of general background of the test person, hearing status and medical
history, noise exposure, musical training and family history regarding hearing loss) Oto-
scopy, Typanometery, reﬂex measurements, digit span test and Audiometery test. The
age of all participants ranged between 22-32 years. They all gave formal written consent
and were reimbursed for their participation.
Apparatus
All experiments for this project were carried out in listening booths of psychoacoustics
lab of Technical University of Denmark, Lyngby campus. The experiments were con-
ducted in a double-walled sound insulated booth. All experiments ran on a computer
and were implemented in Graphical User Interface (GUI) of MATLAB. The participants
were seated in front of a computer with instructions of the experiment displayed on GUI.
The response of the participants was recorded either through the use of mouse or key-
board depending on the experiment performed. The stimuli were music pieces in MIDI
format which were converted into WAV format using MATLAB. The music ﬁles were
played from the MATLAB during the experiment and converted to analog using RME
Fireface soundcard. The output signal from soundcard was fed to Sennheiser HDA-650
headphones for presentation of the music stimuli. The level of the sound was calibrated
at 70 dB SPL by following the instructions provided under HEA-Info Wiki library on
Hearing systems internal website. The computer in the listening booth was equipped
with a set of ﬁles for the calibration procedure. The ﬁle HD650 MAG 48k current.mat
contained the latest calibration data for Sennheiser HDA-650 headphones. The ﬁle was
implemented in the MATLAB script designed for audio playback for the experimental
setup. To set the target stimuli level, the given calﬁlter.m was implemented. The func-
tion uses the data in HD650 MAG 48k current.mat to compensate for the headphone

of the function include sampling frequency (fs) at 48000 Hz, name of the headphone

3.1
Establishment of Model
To conﬁrm that emotion and preference are judged similarly across NH listeners and
examine the inﬂuence of pulsation in judgement of emotion in music and its preference
the following procedure was carried out. The response parameters under examination
were observed for two groups of NH listeners for the 28 piano clips. The ﬁrst group
comprised of 11 NH listeners from Vannson et al. (2015). Their data for emotion and
preference response was obtained for 28 piano clips. The second group comprised of 12
NH listeners who participated in this study. Their response data was obtained for both
28 drum and 28 piano clips as described in the next section subsection 3.1.1). The raw
data from NH listeners Vannson et al. (2015) both for emotion and preference response
was scaled. The rating scale to record response variables in two studies was diﬀerent.
In Vannson et al. (2015), the rating scale ranged from 1 (happy, like, clear) and 100
(sad, dislike, unclear) and in the current study 0 (happy, like) and 1 (sad, dislike). The
diﬀerence in the response ratings was 0.01 units.
3.1.1
Experiment 1 - Emotion and preference rating of the music
Participants
Twelve NH listeners participated in this study (10 males, 2 females, age range 22-29
years, mean 25 ± 2.32 years). Six of the participants indicated that they had received
formal education in music for 8 years or more and remaining six did not have any prior
training in music. All the participants in this experiments were right-handed.

==PAGE:26==
CHAPTER 3. MATERIALS AND METHODS
Stimuli
The musical stimuli were chosen from the stimuli set of 56 music excerpts that conveyed
four emotions Happy, Sad, Scary and Peaceful
(Vieillard et al., 2008). They all were
composed by professional musicians. Out of 56, Twenty eight piano clips were chosen
for this study. Fourteen of the clips were composed to convey happiness and fourteen
were composed to convey sadness. The duration of each musical excerpt was approx-
imately 10s to 12s. They were downloaded from http://www.brams.umon-treal.ca/
plab/downloads/Emotional_Clips.zip. and were in MIDI format. The music clips
did not have any lyrical content and were composed such that their musical structure
conveyed emotions. The original sampling rate of all piano clips was 41,000 Hz. All the
twenty eight piano excerpts were converted into drums (conga drums) using MATLAB.
Conga drums have been used since they are played with two hands in a similar manner
as piano. This has been done to preserve the homogeneity in terms of localisation cues.
The sampling rate of all piano and drums clips was 48 kHz. All the musical stimuli were
presented binaurally at 70 dB SPL.
Procedure
In this experiment, there were two tasks performed by the participants. For each music
clip, the participants were asked to rate the emotional content of the music as happy or
sad and rate the preference of the music as like or dislike. Before the beginning of the
main experiment, the participants were given verbal instructions to explain the tasks
assigned to perform the experiment. The participants were speciﬁcally instructed to rate
the emotion of the music based on the musical structure and the content of the piece
and not the emotion evoked by the music. However, the rating of the preference of music
was a subjective aspect.
A brief training session was conducted before starting the main experiment. There
were three musical excerpts in the training session which were chosen from the stimuli
set of scary and peaceful excerpts (Vieillard et al., 2008). The three clips composed in
piano to convey fear were converted into drums clips and used in the training session for
drums. Similarly, three excerpts from peaceful ones were used in piano training session.
The clips in training session were not reused in the actual experiment and were only
used so that the participants were familiarised with the experimental setup and musical
stimuli. The duration of training session was approximating 5 minutes and after which
the main experiment was conducted.
The participants began the experiment by following instructions on GUI by clicking
on it. After each music piece ﬁnished, the instructions on the GUI asked them to rate the
excerpt using two continuous rating scales which were implemented as sliders on the GUI
interface. The ﬁrst slider was labeled as happy/sad and the participants were asked to
rate the degree to which they thought that the music was composed to convey happiness
or sadness. The second slider was labeled as like/dislike, the participants were asked to
indicate the extent to which they liked or disliked the piece. The response was indicated
by moving the slider with the mouse. The subsequent music piece started only when

the response for both emotion and preference for a particular piece was validated by the
participants. The order in which the music pieces were played back was randomised.
All the participants were tested for both drum and piano clips. They ﬁrst performed
the emotion-preference task for drum clips and then piano pieces. The drum session
always preceded piano session so that the participants do not receive any sort of priming
or melodic cues from piano clips. The duration of experiment for each condition (drum
and piano) was 20-25 minutes.
3.2
Implementation of model
In order to examine the uniformity in perceived pulsation from drums and piano is same
or diﬀerent, the pulsation was recorded for 28 drums and 28 piano clips from 12 NH
participants through a tapping task to reproduce tempo of the music as described in
subsection 3.2.1. For each music sample, the most frequent value of the pulsation was
obtained by taking the mode across pulsation data of 12 NH listeners. The value hence
obtained was attributed as the tempo for that particular stimulus in bpm. This process
was carried out for all 28 drum and 28 piano samples.
The following procedure was carried out to conﬁrm that beat perception is better in
drums than piano. The most correct tempo value of all 28 drums and piano clips was
taken and a lower and higher limit was set to get a range of tempi values. This limit was
taken as 50% of the most correct tempo value. The concepts of tempo and rhythm are
quite subtle and their production is strongly linked with motor regions of the brain. Due
to this ﬁne yet complex distinction, it was was suspected that some participants might
have reproduced rhythm instead of tempo. Thus, a narrow range of 50% was selected
to get rid of all the values that did not correspond to the tempo. The number of people
who reproduced the tempo of a particular music piece within this speciﬁed range were
listed as number of people who agreed on a certain tempo for a particular drum and
piano piece.
3.2.1
Experiment 2 - Tempo Reproduction
Participants
Twelve NH test subjects who participated in emotion preference rating experiment were
recruited for the tempo reproduction experiment. Since, the hypothesis was centered on
pulsation cues from drums, the tempo was recorded only for the 28 drum excerpts. Later,
discrepancy was realised while designing the follow up study as mentioned in section 3.4
and it turned out that it was necessary to acquire tempo data from piano clips. However,
out of 12 NH subjects only 3 were able to participate in this experiment. Thus, in order
to maintain the uniformity in the sample number, 9 NH listeners from the participant
set of Experiment 3 were asked to reproduce the tempo of piano clips.

Stimuli
All twenty eight drum and piano clips were used for this experiment to record pulsation
by NH participants.
Procedure
In the tempo reproduction experiment, the task was to reproduce the tempo of the drum
clips (for both drums and piano clips) by tapping on any one of the keys of the keyboard.
Before the main experiment began, the participants were given verbal instructions
to elaborate on the nature of the experiment and the task assigned to them. The par-
ticipants were speciﬁcally instructed to reproduce the tempo of the music and not the
rhythm. They were further instructed to press only a single key of their choice on the
keyboard by tapping with one hand to replicate the real-life scenario where tempo is
produced by tapping with one hand or foot. Due to the constraints in MATLAB GUI
interface and experimental design, the participants were requested not to press SPACE
bar, ENTER and shift keys for tapping.
The participants began the experiment by clicking on a push button to start the
track on GUI. A two second silence was inserted between the click and start of the track
after which the music clip began. While the track was being played, the instructions
on the GUI displayed ’Start tapping in 3,2,1 seconds’. The timer to record the tempo
started only after the ﬁrst three seconds of the music piece were lapsed. This was done
so that participants were able to comprehend the music piece and get the underlying
pulsation. However, the participants were told that they could start tapping at any point
regardless of the start of the timer and prior instructions that appeared on GUI.
Visual feedback was provided to participants at the time of tapping in order that the
they were ensured that the tempo was being recorded. The participants had to manually
begin the next clip by clicking on GUI. The order in which the music pieces were played
was randomised. The procedure was repeated for all 28 music excerpts.The duration of
experiment for each condition was 15-20 minutes.

Statistical Analysis
Data analysis was carried out using JMP 14.1.0. For experiment 1 and 2, analysis was
carried out using linear models with least squares for emotion and preference as continu-
ous response data. The main eﬀects of following independent variables were considered:
tempi or pulsation (in bpm) as continuous variable and mode as nominal variable (major
or mode) and their two way interaction eﬀects. Due to technical reasons, participants
and their interaction with tempi and mode were not included as random eﬀects.
For Experiment 3, Bivariate ﬁtting was used to analyse the relationship between
speciﬁc tempi values 60 bpm, 120 bpm and 150 bpm (in bpm) as continuous variable
and reproduced tempi or pulsation (in bpm) as continuous variable. Linear models using
least squares was used analysis of emotion and preference as continuous response data for
drums. The main eﬀects of following independent variables were considered: reproduced
tempi or pulsation (in bpm) as continuous variable and mode as nominal variable (major
or mode) and their two way interaction eﬀects.
A new response variable, called Sensitivity structure or S was obtained to demon-
strate the relationship between given tempo and their perceived pulsation. The derived
response S is a nominal variable with labels as Half, Same, Double, Other i.e., if given
Tempo = Pulsation, it was labelled as Same, similarly if Tempo = 2 · Pulsation, it was
labelled as Double.

5.1
Establishment of model
The model based on pulsation supports the premise that the aﬀective responses, emotion
and preference, are judged similarly across all NH listeners. Results from Figure 4.1
demonstrate strong correlation between the judgment of happy - sad emotion by two
diﬀerent groups of NH listeners who were presented the same piano clips. As expected,
NH listeners in both the groups were clearly able to distinguish the two emotions. The
clustered response pattern along the ﬁtted line on opposite sides also lends support
to the dichotomous nature of the happy - sad emotion. The model establishment is
further corroborated from evidence which states that people make similar judgments
in identiﬁcation of musically expressed emotion and that there is an innate propensity
to interpret unfamiliar styles of music and listeners easily adapt to them (Balkwill and
Thompson, 1999, Castellano et al., 1984, Kessler et al., 1984). In addition, the weak
correlation for preference of the piano clips from Figure 4.2 indicates that liking and
disliking music is invariant of the musical properties, primarily musical mode (major or
minor). Liking for music piece is largely governed by parameters such as consonance,
familiarity with musical piece and mood of the listener (Hunter and Schellenberg, 2010)
Furthermore, results from Figure 4.3 indicate that pulsation rendered by piano is
sensed similarly across all NH listeners. The ﬁndings are also consistent with the well-
established evidence that emotion association in music particularly happiness and sad-
ness is strongly linked with tempo and mode of the music (Dalla Bella et al., 2001,
Hevner, 1935) and are key determinants in distinguishing happy and sad songs. How-
ever, the tempo and mode do not play a key role in liking or disliking a music piece.
As noted from Figure 4.4 preference for the music is invariant of tempo or the mode
(major or minor) of the music. NH listeners prefer both happy and sad songs. It still
remains an intriguing question that people exhibit liking for sad sounding music. It has
been conjectured that the cathartic eﬀect created by sad sounding music helps people
to connect with their feelings and studies have also indicated that sad sounding music
conveys ambivalent feelings. (Hunter and Schellenberg, 2010)

5.2
Implementation of model
Figure 4.5 shows the correlation between pulsation of drums and piano values. It can
be noted that out of 14 piano clips which are composed in major mode, for 10 clips
the perceived pulsation is same regardless of the instrument. For three of the piano
clips composed in major mode, the pulsation for drums is perceived as twice as for the
pulsation of piano clips. The discrepancy for music stimuli composed in major mode
where the pulsation rate for the piano is 100 bpm and that for drums is 150 bpm. The
ratio of drums:piano is 1.5. The variation can be reasoned by looking at the music score,
shown in Figure A.1 for this particular music clip with stimulus name as G 05. It can be
noted that the inherent complexity in composition of the music itself caused diﬃcultly in
tapping task. For the remaining 14 piano samples composed in minor mode, for 7 of them
the perceived pulsation is same for the two instruments whereas for the remaining 7 the
pulsation for drums is perceived as twice as for the pulsation of piano clips. The above
account suggests that the perceived pulsation for drums and piano is diﬀerent. Thus,
the proposed hypothesis that the perceived pulsation for the two instruments would be
same is contradicted.
This diﬀerence in perceived pulsation can be attributed to the availability of musical
cues to the listener. In case of piano samples, in addition to the rhythmic cues, the listener
relies on tonal pitch, melody, harmony while listening to the drums only rhythmic are
utilised.
Although piano music pieces were perceptually pleasant and it was easier to rate
emotion and preference than drums, the participants reported that they had a great
deal of diﬃculty in performing the tapping task for piano clips. Figure 4.6 and data from
Table 4.2 and Table 4.1 indicate that there was an overall agreement in performing tempo
reproduction task for drums than piano. This overall agreement by the NH listeners over
drums demonstrates that that it was easier to perceive pulsation and reproduce tempo
for drum clips than the corresponding piano clips. This agreement served as a direct
mapping to understand ease of perception of pulse in drums over piano.
The diﬃculty at tempo reproduction in piano clips can be addressed from task speciﬁc
and task general resources in cognitive processes and the theory of divided attention.
(Reisberg, 2015). The temporal and pitch organisation of music are processed in parallel
both in peripheral and central auditory system. In piano clips, the complexity is rendered
by presence of both spectral (pitch, scale, melodic, tonality) and temporal (rhythm and
tempo) cues whereas in drums only rhythmic cues and pitch is present. The task of
tapping involves listening and then reproducing tempo. In drums, the task is one-to-
one mapping since it involves perceiving rhythmic cues (pulsation) and reproducing it.
However, in piano the task of tapping becomes many-to-one-mapping. Since there is
presence of many cues and they all are processed simultaneously, it becomes diﬃcult to
rely on a single cue (perceived pulsation). It can be argued that presence of melodic,
tonal coding and rhythm analysis will facilitate the tapping task and performance would
be better in tempo reproduction task for the piano (opposite in emotion preference task)
however contrasting pattern was reported.

It is conjectured that the overall discrepancy in tapping could also be attributed to
duration of music pieces. The piano clips used in this study had been speciﬁcally designed
to convey emotions. During the course of this study, no evidence or any other research
study was encountered that employed same musical pieces in tapping experiments to
reproduce the tempo of the music excerpts. But in either case, the model based on drum
pulsation works well to understand the tempo reproduction phenomena.
5.3
Working of Model
The aim of this study was to examine if CI users rate emotional content based only
on perceived pulsation in the music and also if pulsation plays a role in determining
their response for preference of music. It was hypothesised that underlying pulsation
embedded in rhythmic cues from drums will determine their response for emotion and
preference.
Evidence suggests that CI users perceive and identify happy sad emotion in piano
music pieces quite well, however the accuracy of judgement and the extent to which the
emotion is judged correctly is less than NH listeners (Ambert-Dahan et al., 2015, Vannson
et al., 2015). The perception of emotion is attributed to rhythmic cues and the residual
spectral cues that might facilitate this phenomena. The results indicate that pulsation
rendered by piano does play a role in judging the emotion in music. This is observed in
from Figure 4.7a that emotion response of the CI users for piano excerpts in Vannson
et al. (2015) study follows the same trend as the NH listeners in this study. However, the
emotion response of CI users is accurately modelled by implementing pulsation from the
drum clips as shown in Figure 4.7b. Thus, the model proposed based on drum pulsation
cues was able to simulate NH response for happy and sad for drum clips as CI response
for piano clips. Since CI users are able to synchronise their body movements to drums
(Phillips-Silver et al., 2015), the model will hold good if performance of CI users for
drum clips is comparable with the NH listeners for drums.
It was reported by NH participants that it was diﬃcult to judge emotional content
in drums than in piano. This was due to the absence of melodic cues and tonal pitch.
It would be interesting to observe the consistency of this aspect by having CI users rate
emotional content of the drum clips.
However, the success of the model was partial as in addition to the pulsation, reliance
on mode (major and minor) was also found crucial in inﬂuencing judging the emotional
content of music pieces. Studies lend support in favour of poor perception of musical
mode in due to the absence of tonal pitch (Cooper et al., 2008), and that CI users rely
heavily on tempo than on mode in musical emotion perception (Caldwell et al., 2015).
It could not be ﬁrmly established the musical mode plays role in music emotion
perception by CI users. It might be possible that both pulsation and mode supplement
information about musical emotion content which help CI users rate the emotion content.
Another explanation comes from reliance on culturally determined cues. Before the onset
of hearing loss, CI users cultivated both acoustic cues and cultural cues to comprehend
music. With post-lingual deafness, there might have been continued reliance on cultural
37
==PAGE:51==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSIONS
cues to compensate for the diﬃculties in processing acoustic cues. (Ambert-Dahan et al.,
2015, Gingras et al., 2014) The motivation to be able to listen to music again and feel its
pleasantness might also be one of the explanations of reliance of mode for music emotion
perception.
As observed in Figure 4.8a for piano excerpts, the preference rating by NH listeners
in terms of liking and disliking is independent of both the mode and pulsation rate.
The response pattern is relatively a plateau which indicates that the preference of music
is not inﬂuenced by mode or tempo of the music piece. Instead, liking is governed by
consonance, familiarity and musical emotion both conveyed and induced by the music
as discussed in section 5.1 However, the preference response of CI is inﬂuenced by both
tempo and musical mode. A closer look at the observations reveals that CI users prefer
or like music pieces having faster tempo and composed in major mode and tend to
dislike music pieces that have slow tempo and are composed in minor mode. The results
lend support to this observation that preference of CI users is inﬂuenced both by tempo
and the mode. The preference response of CI users for piano clips resonates with NH
response for preference for drum clips as shown in Figure 4.8b. This indicates that both
NH listeners and CI users rely on pulsation and musical mode for rating the preference
of music. Thus, the hypothesis that underlying pulsation embedded in rhythmic cues
from drums will determine the preference is not supported. For music samples, with
simple rhythms and faster tempo CI users are able to make use of both cues for music
perception and appreciation. This might be the reason for inclination of CI users towards
liking songs composed in major mode and having fast tempo It is not known if preference
of CI users is governed by other parameters such as consonance or dissonance, familiarity
or exposure to a music piece similar to NH listeners.(Hunter and Schellenberg, 2010).
5.4
Discussions for Speciﬁc tempo values
The second question aimed by this study was to examine whether pulsation was the
only factor which inﬂuenced rating emotional response and preference of the music. To
investigate the validity of the stated premise, it was hypothesised that the perceived
pulsation matches the tempo at which the musical piece were presented.
The results indicate that pulsation does play a role in inﬂuencing response for emotion
and preference as shown in Figure 4.9 and Figure 4.10 respectively. This is consistent with
the evidence which indicate that tempo plays a key role in determining emotion in music
(Dalla Bella et al., 2001, Kamenetsky et al., 1997, Kratus, 1993). However, the results
also highlights the role of mode in emotion judgment and preference determination.
This implies the underlying hypothesis is not supported which emphasised the pulsation
cue as the only factor in determining the response variable. In experiment 3, only the
tempo values were manipulated while the mode was left unchanged. Although there is no
perception of scale or musical mode in drums stimuli, the eﬀect persists and inﬂuences
the response variables.
In addition, studies have also lent support to the claim that fast tempi are attributed
to happy songs and slow ones are associated with sad ones (Hevner, 1935). However, the

results indicate discrepancy in this judgment since not all songs at tempi values 120
bpm and 150 bpm were judged as happy and the ones at 60 bpm were rated as sad. This

in piano samples leaving the tempo values unchanged and then converted into drums
stimuli. In such a case it would be intriguing to observe if mode plays a key role in
rating, as in adults changes in mode have a larger impact than changes in tempo change
in making emotional judgements (Dalla Bella et al., 2001)
As noted from Figure 4.10, pulsation did inﬂuence in determining the response for

ones in minor mode. The proposed model can then be reﬁned with the inclusion of mode
along with pulsation and then examined if CI response can be modelled as intended.
The results obtained through tempo reproduction task are in agreement with es-

production (Fraisse, 1982, Krumhansl, 2000). As observed from Figure 4.11 there exists

rhythmic cues in rating emotion and preference, it proved useful in understanding the
perceived pulsation and reproduced tempo.

Conclusions and future work
In this study, a model based on pulsation of drums was proposed to understand the
emotion and preference response by CI users. The use of rhythmic and pulsation cues
in judgment of emotion in music and rating the preference form the focal point of this
project. The model constructed using response data of NH listeners was successful in
showing that drum pulsation from NH listeners hold well in modelling CI response for
piano clips. However, the success of the model was partial as it was observed that in
addition to pulsation, musical mode also had an impact in determining the emotion and
preference of music. This was further conﬁrmed in the study by manipulation of tempo
values which proved that pulsation was not the only factor in determining the aﬀective
response of both NH listeners and CI users. Additionally, the model was useful in getting
insights into the phenomena of tempo perception and its reproduction of NH listeners
and can be extended to understand the same phenomena in CI users. Modiﬁcations in
model with the inclusion of musical mode could form an intriguing future research work
that would help simulating CI response more accurately.
