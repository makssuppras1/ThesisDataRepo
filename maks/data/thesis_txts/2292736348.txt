Abstract
KEY WORDS:
PLC - Conveyor belt - C++ - Timers - GUI
Nowadays, automated systems have a signiﬁcant impact all over the industrial sectors. PLC’s (Pro-
grammable Logic Controllers) and robots are a basic part of these systems. This project intends to integrate
both under a single application.
In DTU, at building 326 a conveyor belt was installed and controlled from a Siemens PLC on the fall
semester of 2014. The station included the controller, the pneumatic system as well as detections sensors, a
barcode reader and ﬁve different actuators.
Then, the aim of this project was to expand the plant and combine it with two robotic arms (UR10). One
of them is used for grabbing blocks and leaving them on the belt, and the second to pick them up from the
belt and sort them in a table. Besides these new functionalities added, the whole plant had to be monitored
and controlled from the PC under a Linux OS.
Along this project the different communication protocols available for the PC-PLC and PC-UR10 were
assessed. Then, once established a TCP/IP communication in parallel with both systems, the control strategy
was deﬁned. The main obstacle of working with this plant was how to track the blocks which must be picked
up, just with the components were already installed. For this purpose, a control strategy using timers was
implemented and satisfactory achieved.
Besides the control strategy, a MySQL database was developed in order to store all the information of
the plant, and check from there all the blocks which are detected along the conveyor.
Finally a GUI was built under a C/C++ application. From this interface, the user could monitor, control
and check the code or even the internal variables of the program. The results obtained, proved the integration
of the conveyor and the robot was satisfactory achieved, but with room for future improvements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since the 1940s decade when General Motors created the ﬁrst automation department, this industry has
increased exponentially. The reasons are multiple and varied. It allows the companies to save labor, as well
as an important increase on the quality, the productivity and the precision of the material produced.
During the last couple of decades, one of the main foundations of the actual automated systems are based
on PLC’s (Programmable Logic Controller). They were ﬁrst introduced on the 70s to substitute the relay
logic systems in complex industrial installations. Basically, PLC’s are digital computers, nowadays, capable
of almost establish any kind of communication and control many different platforms and systems. From
measure a thermistor, to control a pneumatic systems, all done within the same platform. This is possible
due to the ﬂexibility of these controllers. They are designed for multiple arrangements, by dividing the PLC
in racks, where different modules can be installed, monitored and control at almost real-time.
The ﬁrst PLC was designed by MODICON, today part of the French company Schneider Electric SE.
Though, nowadays the Modicon PLC’s have lost their space in the market, which is basically capitalized by
the German Siemens AG and the American Allen Bradley, part of Rockwell Automation Inc.
The Siemens automation platforms are grouped under SIMATIC brand, which comes from SIemens
and AutoMATIC. Under this trademark, the German company offers a varied range of products, specially
focused on HMI’s (Human Machine Interface) and PLC’s. Within the different versions of Siemens PLC’s,
the most well-known is the SIMATIC S7-300 platform. It is the most common across the automation industry.
Other famous versions are the S7-200 which is being replaced by the S7-1200. In the incoming years the
S7-1500 will substitute the SIMATIC S7-300.
Due to huge impact of these controllers in the actual industry, this project aims to develop an application
under the S7-300 PLC and two robotic arms are controlled. In the next section, an explanation about how
was the previous setup of the plant will be presented, as well as reasons why some changes are required, and
ﬁnally what are the main goals of this project.
1.1.
Background
The conveyor plant, located in DTU building 326, was constructed using some equipment donated by the
Danish energy company DONG A/S. That included a S7-300 PLC, its different modules (see Fig.1.1(a)), as
well as pneumatic valves, actuators, a conveyor belt, two AC motors... With all this equipment, the student
Gauti Gunnarson built the station shown in Fig.1.1 in the fall semester of 2014.

To solve this important problem, the control for all the sensors along the belt will be based in the
descendant ﬂags. From positive to descendant ﬂag, all the timers in the S7 code have to be decreased
around one second.
Also the timing for pulling back the actuators had to be reduced down to 150 ms in order to handle a
fast recovery to the initial position and avoid obstructions in the sorting line.
Actuator timings
Apart of the sensor ﬂags, the main problem of the plant was in the timing control of the actuators.
When the user manually stopped the station, the timers of the PLC were still running an the actuators
pushed the blocks even if they were not in the required position. Then, they weren’t sorted and hence,
lost.
The GUI is a basic part of this project and it was clear since the beginning that from there, the station
had to be stopped and started. But it couldn’t mean that the detected blocks were lost. To solve this,

MySQL database
In a plant which is processing different products, blocks in this case, is basic to store all the information.
It is not only a matter of keeping the data, it is also useful to prevent future failures or losses. For instance, if
one of the blocks is not detected at the end of the line but it was inserted, the user can realize that something
happened in between.
Then, for this project, all the processed blocks should be stored in a database. There are two spots were
the data should be registered, one at the barcode reader and the next at the robot detection sensor. The ﬁrst
would represent the initial point where all the blocks are processed and the second to the picked up blocks.
If the user inserts one of the desired blocks on the line and the robot fails to pick it up, the user should
know the reasons why the system failed. For that, the time and the block number are basic. For instance, if
many blocks are detected within few seconds at the barcode, it could happen an obstruction when sorting
them because of the physical layout of the plant. If this scenario takes place, the only way to ﬁgure out the
reason, is by looking at the order and the time sequence of each block under the barcode reader.
The way to store all this information as an organized collection, is done by using databases. This
chapter is going through the procedure to create a database which can handle all the data required for the
implemented plant.

Results
When the GUI was ﬁnished, the real testings were carried out. In this ﬁnal stage of the project many
scenarios which were not considered at the beginning, showed the need of more coding. This mainly
happened after the terminal implementation. The possibility to check all the internal variables from there,
gave the chance to ﬁnd out many errors and solve them.
The results of this project can be split in ﬁve different sections:
PLC code improvements
UR10 control using timers
Database implementation
GUI design
Obstruction problems
The ﬁrst design phase of this project referred to the PLC code in the SIMATIC platform. Initially, the
sensor control was changed to be able to sort consecutive blocks. The carried out improvements leaded to
the scenario shown in Fig.7.1, where the blocks can be close down to 1 cm, and the plant is still able to sort
them both.
Figure 7.1: Sorting two consecutive blocks separated by 1 cm
However, these control strategy designed for the actuator timings has some limitations. As explained in
previous chapters, the control is done by using 50 ms timers, and hence the total time is the sum of 100 ms
ﬂags. Initially, the best idea was to decrease these timers as much as possible, in order to be very precise.

Then, the application concept and all the features implemented proved to work as demanded, but there
were some problems regarding obstructions. There are three possible scenarios which can lead to that. First,
when the blocks are being sorted on line number two, once in a while they bog down. The reason is the line
has not a smooth slope, and that leads to random obstructions. The second case affects to a block which is
being sorted when another is passing by just in front of the same line. This was one of the assumptions at the
beginning and nothing can be done to solve it. That’s why, with the current conﬁguration it is something
must be considered when working with the plant. If there is a high concentration of blocks in the sorting line,
obstructions should be expected. The third case happens when a block is passing by the sensor structure at

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
From the goals stated at the beginning of the project all were satisfactory achieved. First the communi-
cation through TCP/IP from the PC to the PLC, proved to be very stable and all the initial problems that
appeared at the design phase were solved. Moreover, the PLC code using SIMATIC software was improved
and it is able to keep the trace of the blocks even when the plant is stopped.
Regarding the robot control strategy with timers, it was an idea which at the beginning casts doubts
on its reliability, but the results were really satisfactory. Being able to track the desired blocks without
using a camera, proved to be feasible solution under the normal circumstances. The problem is that when a
obstructions happens, the application cannot be aware of that and solve it by itself.
Apart of the robot control and the improvements in the PLC code, it is important to highlight the GUI
designed. The initial requirements covered the idea of having a functional interface where the user could
manipulate the station. This was satisfactory achieved and even with many more functionalities. It is also
signiﬁcant to remark that the GUI is user-friendly. This means, the station is just not showing the state of
the plant, it is aware of it, and guides the user to manipulate it correctly, preventing any malfunction. One
example of this, is the Insert button. When the robot is moving the user should be aware that the button
shouldn’t be triggered again, but just in case, the button is blocked until it has located back in the initial
coordinates. Then, the implemented GUI helps the user to understand the core idea behind it, and avoids
many scenarios where the system could be messed up.
Besides this, the database is fully accessible from the GUI and has perfectly ﬁlled the expectations stated
at the beginning. Also the terminal is one of the strongest points of the GUI. As mentioned before, this
project is focused on educational purposes, and it may be possible that some other student could use this
thesis as a starting point for any other application. For this purpose, the terminal is really useful, specially
because it allows the user to checks the program variables. During the testing phase, using the terminal gave
the chance to improve the application and solve many scenarios which were not considered before. Hence,
for future developments could be an important tool for understanding and creating more functionalities.

CHAPTER 9
Future work
Even though the application results are really positive, there is still room for improvements. As a future
work it would be interesting to make the plant work completely by itself, that means reducing all the possible
obstructions. On one hand, one of the improvement could be the installation of a new structure for holding
the sensors at the picking place. It is basic to avoid obstructions at that location, if they take place, the plant
would be completely inoperative. On the other hand, another improvement could be the replacement of
the second sorting line. With these two simple and small changes, the reliability of the plant can be highly
increased.
Even though the modiﬁcations explained above would be carried out, there would be still the problem of
having an obstruction when one block is sorted and another is passing by in front of the same line. In this
case as mentioned in previous chapters, nothing can be done to solve with the current conﬁguration. As a
future work, I would suggest to combine the actual plant with a robotic arm, but in this case with a camera.
In that way, it would be possible to detect the problem, stop the system before having a critical situation,
and then if possible, solve the obstruction. This strategy would lead to a much more stable and independent
system, able to work completely by itself no matter what scenario could take place.
There is also room for improvements in the timing control of the actuators. As explained in the previous
chapter, the timing control could vary around -200 ms. However, no obstructions have been observed due to
this reason, it should be improved with a new control strategy. The new design should be done inside the
SIMATIC platform and without using extremely short timers. One possible solution could be to set timers in
the order of the seconds. Then, when the belt is stopped, retrieve the consume time and store it. And ﬁnally,
when the user starts the belt again, set a timer with the time left till the picking place.
