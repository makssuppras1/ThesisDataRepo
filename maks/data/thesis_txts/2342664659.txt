Abstract
Sigma Designs Technology is in need of a new configuration deployment and status
acquisition infrastructure for their Z-Wave series, as they have an ever increasing
volume of analogue devices for each new chip release. All these devices are spread
across the chip and only a few configuration bits needs to be transferred and verified.
In this thesis, a proposal on an infrastructure from Sigma Designs has been designed
and two other solutions have been designed using different aspects. Sigma Designs
proposal were an infrastructure formed as a ring which is built of latches to form
a shift-register. The second design was also a ring, but built with another protocol
to minimize the area and power, while the third design were formed as a tree to
secure shorter paths between the analog unit and the initiator. An analysis were
performed in order to determine weakness’ and strengths’ between a ring and a tree
topology. The analysis was backed by synthesis and simulations of the designs, which
showed that a ring structure requires less area and uses less power compared to a ring
structure thus the tree is superior in noise reduction.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This master’s thesis is performed at the Technical University of Denmark in collabo-
ration with Sigma Designs Technology Denmark ApS. The topic is on development
of a new configuration deployment and status acquisition infrastructure (CDSAI) for
Sigma Design’s Internet of Things device series: ”Z-Wave”. The newest chip is built
of a host system consisting an ARM processor and several digital signal processors
(DSP). The chip has a large group of analogue and mixed signal processing compo-
nents which are configured and monitored from the host system.
The thesis presents three different design solutions. The first is a redesign of the
suggested infrastructure for Sigma Designs ZW700, soon to be released. This is done
for analytically purposes in order to determine specification for the final designs and
comparison of the designs.
The two other designs use a ring and a tree network
topology. The tests in this project show that these have different advantages with
respect to latency, power, noise injection and area. Each solution has been made as
a toolbox of modules, in order to build infrastructures for new Z-Wave chips in the
future.
In the following, the use of Sigma Designs Z-Wave series will be present.
Next,
the problem in the target system is described and why Sigma Designs needs a new
configuration deployment and status acquisition infrastructure for their future chips.
Comments are given on their old infrastructures and in the end, an outline is given
of the entire thesis.
1.1
The Z-Wave series
The Z-Wave series consists of many different devices, each containing a system-on-
chip (SoC). All devices communicate using the z-wave protocol and are backward
compatible with older versions of the protocol. The series is used in more than 1500
internet of things (IoT) devices. Sigma Designs collaborate with 350 companies to
develop these devices for home automation. The group of companies go under the
name Z-Wave Alliance. This amount of devices and contributing companies make
the Z-Wave series the most popular IoT chips on the market today. Typical IoT
products running on a wireless Z-Wave network are lightning devices, HVAC systems,
TV remotes and various kinds of sensors. With a getaway, it can all be connected to

The Z-Wave protocol uses a less used frequency band around 1GHz while many other

1.2
Problem Overview
The Z-wave series run user specific applications. Therefore a custom amount of config-
uration is needed for the analogue components. Under run-time, the components can
be re-configured, control registers updated or status registers verified. Sigma Designs
have foreseen trends in the number and functionality of analog components for each
new release and where they have an ever increasing amount of control bits and the
components requires more status acquisition.
The control registers and the status registers of the analog components inside the
analogue domain have to be written and read. This is done by the host system which
resides in the digital domain. It involves a lot of problems such as wires crossing
digital/analog domains with different voltage levels and digital synchronous logic in-
side the analog domain which results in noise injection. To minimize the physical
resources between the core system and the analogue and mixed signal components a
communication network is introduced. Reasoned, instead of routing bundles of wires
from the host system to each analog component, a communication network would im-
ply resource sharing. Now two modules are defined, the master module and the slave
module. Figure 1.1 describes the overview of the communication infrastructure with
an unspecified communication network topology. One master module is always con-
nected to the network and n slave modules. The host system can handle more than
one communication infrastructure, but communication infrastructures never shares
the same master module and slave modules. Parts of the network are, or can be
within the analogue domain as well as the digital domain indicated by the dashed
line.
The first infrastructure Sigma Designs used was parallel wires from one slave module
to one master module. This solution has a high resource overhead and the wires are
not used often as the latency bounds are very low. Newer techniques were needed

This section explains how the configuration deployment and status acquisition infras-
tructure are build and works. First, the interfaces of the master and slave modules
are described accordingly to Figure 1.1. and then how the communication network
who deploys the configuration bits and how the status bits are acquired works.

fully controllable from software. In that way it can run any protocol. For the specific
master module a set of registers are used in order to send and receive data in 12-bit

design space and the digital part of the module is shown on Figure 2.1. When do is
not asserted the data packet will always bypass the module without modifications. A
read is completed when the do-signal is asserted and the D.ctrl signal is deasserted.

2.1 Communication Infrastructure
7
The suggested configuration deployment and status acquisition infrastructure for
Sigma Design’s latest Z-Wave chip is simple and uses a ring network structure. The
ring is built of latches where data values are shifted through. The master module
sends out configuration bits on the ”head” register and receives status bits on the
”tail” register. When the do-signal is asserted every slave module will be written and
read or read only. The do-signal and the signal used to clock the latches are also
routed to memory mapped registers. Note that the master module shifts in dummy
data with the wr control bit ”low” for the slave modules who are not written. It means
that every slave on a do-assertion is always read. Figure 2.2 gives a simplified exam-
ple of a ring-structure. The shift-register is built of latches and clocked with a signal
called move, going in the opposite direction. The reason for calling it shift-register
is that two latches form a register and latches are used instead, to make the critical
path shorter. Figure 2.3 shows an example which have three move transitions and
every second latch holds the data and every other leads data through(transparent).
The data moves from left towards right and the clock in the opposite direction. A
slave module is coupled between two latches by re-routing the signals from the output
of the first latch to the input module. Likewise, the output of the slave module is
connected to input of the second latch. Two latches will have to separate each slave
module or the same data package will be written to two slaves if the latch in between
is in transparent state.
The ring can be shortened, extended or changed by a pair consisting of a fork module
and a merge module. Only one ring per head/tail set is complete or active at any
time, while all other paths are inactive. In Figure 2.4, an example is shown where
three different paths can be chosen. The active path is going through three latches
and the grey marked paths and latches are inactive. In general, the local registers of
the slave module is used to configure its internal mechanism, but s_ctrl on the figure

CHAPTER 3
Asynchronous design
principles background
This chapter presents the general design principles and explains how asynchronous
circuits differ from synchronous clocked circuits and the advantages of asynchronous
design. Most of the chapter concerns asynchronous pipelines, as they are the basic
building blocks of asynchronous designs. From the problem description in Section 1.2,
it is an obvious choice to build an asynchronous transport media as there are different
clock domains on the chip. It is also a good choice to avoid synchronization problems
across the infrastructure, as asynchronous circuit are clock-less. Instead of a clock,
handshakes are used and therefore draw evenly currents compared to synchronous
circuits, as synchronous design have a high frequency spike in the spectrum of the
clock frequency.
3.1
Fundamentals of asynchronous pipelines
Instead of having a synchronous signal to clock every register and therefore transport
the data forward in the pipeline, an asynchronous Muller pipeline stage consists of a
controller to make the backward and forward handshake. The controller switches a
latch to hold the data or makes it pass through. A piece of a Muller pipeline is shown
in Figure 3.1. It shows four stages and between some stages there is combinatorial
logic. Note that the request signal is delayed to meet the handshake agreement. This
is usual done with a chain of inverters. A Muller pipeline stage controller consists
of a C-element and a NOT-gate. Most important to note, is the Muller C-element,
which is the synchronization element of the controller. It has similar function as an
AND-gate, but only changes state if both inputs are the same. Figure 3.2 describes
the truth table and the symbol of the gate. The data request and acknowledge signal
bundle between the stages are called channels or links.
In order to achieve a successful handshake, it is agreed that the data is always ready
at the same time or before request is asserted and it stays valid until acknowledge
goes high. The requesting side asserts ”req” and the receiving side acknowledges by

An asynchronous pipeline is typically described by the simpler notation showed in
Figure 3.4 where 6 stages is depicted and the arrows describe the links. Each stage
is marked by tokens and bubbles. A token is either a valid token or an empty token.
The valid token corresponds to an assertion of the ”req” line and the empty token
corresponds to a deassertion of the ”req” line. This means that a valid token is al-
ways followed by an empty token and vice versa. In between the tokens, there are
bubbles. This means a token is free to move to that stage. In asynchronous circuits,
it is critical to respect the agreed handshake protocol. Wrong designs suffer from two
types of hazards, the static and the dynamic. A static hazard is when a bit flickers
from high to low to high. A dynamic hazard is when the bit is supposed to go low,
but go low, high and low. The result of a hazard could be deadlock of the circuit or
wrong data transitions. To avoid hazards, asynchronous controllers are described as
a state transition graph (STG) which are visualized via Petri nets. A program like
WorkCraft can synthesize Pretri nets and generate the gates for the asynchronous

Next, examples are given of two different asynchronous pipelines Mousetrap(2-phase

3.2
Mousetrap pipeline
Mousetrap stands for minimal-overhead ultrahigh-speed transition-signaling asyn-
chronous pipeline. The main advantages are that it have a static spread of one, a low
propagation delay of a D-latch and the pipeline itself can be implemented by the stan-
dard library. The standard library for designing a layout for a chip does not include
the C-element. Because forks, merge or multiplexer units introduces C-elements for
the synchronization if they are needed. The static spread means that each existing
latch in the pipeline can hold a valid token and that no empty tokens exist. It uses
the handshake format 2-phase bundled data which is similar to the Muller pipeline
that uses 4-phase bundled data. The only way it differs in the handshake is that for
each transition of the request signal the data changes.

3.3
Dual-rail pipeline
The 4-phase dual-rail (1-of-2 encoding) is an extended version of the Muller pipeline.
The request signal is encoded into the data bits expressed in two signals: a false bit
and a true bit. The two bits are described as d.f and d.t, where they evaluates as a
low bit and a high bit. Figure 3.6 shows a stage with the two data/request signals and
the acknowledge signal. There are three legal combinations of the data and request
bits. A request high transition is encoded as {d.f, d.t} = {1, 0} or {d.f, d.t} = {0, 1}
and it means either a zero or a one on that data bit line. {d.f, d.t} = {0, 0} indicates
a request down. The combination {d.f, d.t} = {1, 1} is illegal and if used one of the
bits can be wrongly evaluated at the next controller in a pipeline, as one of them will
arrive before the other.

Topology analysis
This chapter covers an analysis of a ring topology and tree topology transportation
network with respect to latency, resources and power.
The power drain directly
results in noise injection into the analog domain. In the ring, the data are transported
parallel with shift-registers and in the tree the data are transported serially by an
asynchronous pipeline protocol. This makes the ring superior to the tree and vice
versa with respect to certain properties. The reason for only comparing these two
topologies is that other types of topologies are not viable, for example the parallel
wires spend too much resources on wires, as described shortly in section 1.2. Secondly,
mesh topologies will not favor the ring or tree topology as there is only one master
in the configuration deployment and status acquisition infrastructure.
4.1
Latency
The time to exchange data with one slave module versus the total number of slave
modules of the communication network using a ring topology and a tree topology are
showed in Figure 4.1. If the network consists of more than 35 slave modules, the serial
asynchronous tree outperforms the shift-register ring. The ring is difficult to analyze
since sub rings can be constructed to sustain a lower latency, while from the figure it
can be concluded that to perform equally to the ring, sub rings must not consists of
more than 35 slave modules. To generate the analysis it is presumed that the delay
d is equal to the delay of the data path between each slave module in the ring and
it is equal to the delay between nodes of the tree. Gate delay are neglected. and the
latency of the tree is doubled, since the data also need to reach back to the master

path down through the tree is log2(n + 1) including the master module and the 12

4.3
Power drain and noise
High instantaneous power draw inside the analog domain affect neighboring circuitry,
as the power supply becomes unstable and the analog components are sensitive to
this, as the supply have varying voltage levels. This differs from digital circuit which
have a high level and low level only thresholds. Both the shift-register ring and the
serial asynchronous tree act asynchronous with respect to power charge, since they
are not clocked synchronously. The move-signal clocks the latches asynchronously as
the signal ripples through the latches in the opposite direction of the data. Though,
a high power spike will be generated if all the 12 bits of a latch changes from zero’s to
one’s when clocked. In the asynchronous 1-to-n pipeline protocol in the tree, only one
signal changes at any time due to the handshaking nature of the protocol. Therefore
the tree must be better in decreasing the noise injected into the analog domain.

Design
This chapter covers the design of three solutions for the configuration deployment
and status acquisition infrastructure aimed for Sigma Designs Z-Wave series. Section
5.1 covers the design of a network formed as a ring, where data is shifted around with

Instead of using latches in the ring, an asynchronous pipeline is used. A good choice

keep the amount of wires and control logic low.
The mousetrap controller which
uses bundled data handshake protocol has been chosen from the fact, that it can
be implemented without using C-elements and it only uses one latch instead of two.
This argument holds for the entire solution, as no C-elements are used in any of the
modules. All modules are similar to the ones on the Shift-register ring, but modified
slightly, because the delay of the request signal have to match to each module. The
data packet and acknowledge signal can use the data packet and move signals ports
of the components from the shift-register ring.
The section below, concerns the master module and the control of how the pipeline

check if the pipeline is full, the signal runs from tail to head through each mousetrap
stage and the req signal is part of the handshake protocol.
When doing a write/read or a read only cycle the entire pipeline is filled with data,
then the do is asserted and data shifted through one Mousetrap stage by reading one
word at the tail of the master module, before do is deasserted again. For gaining
maximum speed it is better to empty the pipeline and fill it again, instead of reading
one value from a full pipeline and then load a new value. The reason for this is that
the latch of all the Mousetrap stages are transparent when the pipeline is empty and
makes the data pass through much faster than taking one value out and the rest have
to ripple down through the pipeline. This is described by the equations in Section
3.2.

for transporting the configuration bits to the slave module and one for transporting
the status bits back to the master module. Figure 5.9 shows the master module as
the root node connected to four slave modules coupled with two trees. To support
four slave modules, three router modules and three merge modules are needed. On
the outgoing tree, the router modules choose which route the data should flow, to
reach the specific slave module. A router can only lead the data in one direction
out of two output ports and on the returning tree, the merge modules merges the
two incoming data paths from the slaves. These two modules are explained in their
related subsections further down in this section.
From the analysis in Chapter 4 it was found that tree topologies’ longest path is
log2(n) and for a ring it is n. This makes the path delay of the tree much smaller
than the ring for large values of n. For the ring to maintain a small latency it is pos-
sible to extend or shorten it, while the tree cannot be modified in length. However,
the tree always maintain low latency, which is good as the tree cannot communicate
in parallel, like the ring topology can. The tree must only initiate communication

5.3.2

The outgoing serial data stream are encoded in two different commands. These are
shown in Figure 5.10. The wr bit specifies whether it is a write/read command or a
read command only. On a read only, the data bits of the bit stream are not used.
The first bits of the bit stream describe the route of the data when it is led down
through the tree from the master to the slave module. The second part of the bit
stream describes the original data packet format, where the wr bit are sent first, then
the address bits and lastly the data bits. The value k of the routing bits describes
the number of routers the data stream have to pass through in order to reach the
slave module. Note that the values are different among the slave modules, as they
can be placed in different length from the master module and the trees also varies in
size among from different chips.

scribed in Section 3.3. The most accounting fact choosing this pipeline, is because of
its delay insensitivity. It means that no timing delays have to be measured out like

5.3.4
Slave controller module
The architecture and the interface of the slave control module are shown in Figure
5.14. It consists of four blocks: The ”Bit_extractor” converts the serial data to par-
allel, to feed the original slave unit from Section 2 and the ”Bit_generator” converts
the parallel data to a serial stream. All control of these is managed by the controller.
Notice, that the address and wr bit outputs from the slave module is not needed
in this communication network. The dashed line separates the analog and digital
domains, where fewer wires are let into the analog domain compared to the original
slave module.
The ”Bit_extractor” converts the serial data stream from the asynchronous 1-to-4
pipeline into the data package format word, fed directly to the slave module. Sec-
ondly, it raises a control signal when a fifo-buffer inside is full, this happens when
a command is received. The asynchronous fifo pipeline have 23 stages, to hold the
12 valid tokens and the empty tokens in between. The 12 tokens represent the data
packet format. To check that the pipeline is full, it secures that the fifo has the combi-
nation: valid token, empty token, valid token and so on. On a write/read command all
23 stages are checked and a read only command the first 7 stages are checked(4 valid
and 3 empty). Basically, the acknowledge signal of each stage is AND-ed to assert the
signal full_fifo, where there is a NOT-gate on the acknowledge line from the stages
with empty tokens. The reason for testing every second stage for an empty token is
because, in practice when looking at a stage it can not be detected if it is a bubble or
a token. Fx if only one bit was loaded into the pipeline all acknowledge signals would
be asserted, which means that each stage has a valid token. And this would be wrong.

5.3.5
Merge module
The merge module is used to merge the pipelines coming from the slave to the master
module. Secondly, it is used inside the ”Bit generator” of the slave controller module.
The merge module is built of a controller shown on the STG graph in Figure 5.18(b)
and its interface is shown in Figure 5.18(a), which is 1-to-2 encoded as described
previously. The controller is simple and when a request data bit from input port 0
the token will take one of the left going paths and when a request data bit from input
port 1 the token will go right to perform a full handshake before returning to initial
stage. To secure that the controller is working correctly there can only arrive data
to one of the inputs of the merge module and it will have to do a full handshake on
one of them before it can receive a new token on the other. Collision of data on the
input ports might end up in a deadlock or other unpredictable behavior.
5.3.6
Summary
The section covered how a serial communication infrastructure was designed. It uses
two different asynchronous pipelines in the communication network, where the net-
work is formed as a tree topology, to deploy the configurations bits and acquire the
status bits.
Only three modules are designed and needed for the Implementer to
build infrastructures. A router module to control the flow of the data to each slave
controller module, a merge module to merge incoming path for the data going back to
the master module, and the third module is the slave control module, which control
and communicates to and from the original slave module. Mainly, STG diagrams for
controller and structural overview diagrams have been used to present the modules
design.

Tests
This chapter covers test of the three infrastructure designs, including a comparison
of these. Two test networks of each design are synthesized in Synopsis Design Vision
with a HVT 90nm library delivered by ST Microelectronics. Gate-level simulations
have been made, where Design Vision does an estimated calculation on the wires
length. The ring structures in the tests consists of n different paths, where each path
is a chain of 2k slave modules for k = 0, 1, .., (n −1). Latch modules are placed in
between and further one slave module is used to control data flow to each of the paths.
The number of slave modules in the networks are then given by 1 + 20 + 21 + ..2n−1.
Two different values of n have been chosen for test networks, which is n = 4 and n = 8.
The tree networks for the serial asynchronous tree are generated as equally weighted
trees with each leaf as a slave module. This means a total of 6 different networks are
tested, two for each design. The network consisting of 4 paths are shown in Figure 6.1
for the two parallel designs and the corresponding serial asynchronous tree in Figure
6.2. Notice that they have the same amount of slave modules. The grey edges are
control paths to the join/fork modules and routers/merge modules in between the
slave and master modules.
6.1
Synthesis
The synthesis tool Synopsis Design Vision is developed to optimize on synchronous
logic. It tries to optimize on the datapaths between registers, to obtain the highest
clock frequency.
In all three designs, there are no synchronous clocks, but small
changes have been made on the designs to meet all hold times. The wr signal which
clocks the two rows of registers inside the slave unit was not defined as a clock and
therefore it was redesigned. Now it latches data on rising edge of wr and latches out
on falling edge. With this modification, no hold time violations where produced in
the slave units. When no hold time violations are experienced in simulations and
simulation completes successful, it means that the designs must obey timing rules.
All propagation delays of each datapath loop including gates and wires are larger
than the hold times for the serial asynchronous tree. Note that it was not necessary
to insert delay d1 and d2 of the serial slave control module seen in Figure 5.17(b).
Unfortunately, the synthesis tool removes all timing paths on the signals looping back
in to the same datapath, as it can not optimize on a infinite long datapath. It expects
registers in between. On the serial asynchronous tree networks, it was not a problem

6.6
Noise injection
Noise is generated from instantaneously current draws which affect the VCC supply
from where the design is coupled. Usually it does not matter for a pure digital design
voltage threshold, but analog design units are connected to the same power supply in
this case and analog design are prone to noise from an unstable power supply. There-
fore, the theory is, when drawing less instantaneous power, noise will be low. To test
for noise, we say that the largest power charge will be due to the wires connecting
the modules of the infrastructure. Reasoned, that they are long as the modules are
spread far across on the chip.
Since a layout have not been developed for the designs, the length of wires between
modules are only estimated by the gate-level synthesis, which will think they are
shorter because no analog components are placed on the chip. It will think all mod-
ules of the design are placed in a cluster. No qualified guesses have been made of
the distance between the modules. An evaluation is made on the amount of toggles
on the wires between the modules. Then the toggles of the three designs will be
compared in order to favor the one which generates less noise. All noise simulation

6.7
Sources of Error
By investigating the shift-register ring, it was seen that the two latches have been
implemented as a register instead. This implies faster runtime, but most importantly,
it worsen the noise due to the fast propagating clock. It is important to note that
the clock propagate fast because wires are short and therefore have no delay.
The manual placement of delays on request lines for the parallel asynchronous ring
design affects the runtime as not every pipeline stage needs such long delay. To avoid
hold time violations a delay of 1.5ns where needed. Secondly, it affects the noise of
the design as value will ripple though the pipeline almost synchronously due to the
matched delay.
From the start, the period of the do-signal asserted was 30ns, which is long as it
should only be long enough to reach all slave modules. It for example affects the
runtime of the full load test case with 1000ns for the shift-register ring. The periods
are clearly visible on Figure 6.6.
Each ring network is converted to a tree with a script and the streams of data pack-
ages are translated to single data packets commands to be sent down through the
==PAGE:60==
54
6 Tests
tree. Therefore the the slave module who controls the fork and merge modules are
not needed. Likewise the commands to control it is not needed, but these are not
removed in the translation. This of course affects the power and runtime simulations.
The slave unit, which is used in all tree designs should use the same amount of power
for each network. It does for the two ring structures, but in the serial tree it uses
four times more energy. The unit should have been synthesized independently and a
”Don’t touch” command should have been placed on the unit when synthesizing the
entire design. The reason for the extra power spent could be that the logic of the tree
is much more dense, so that the synthesis tool choose to use different components
from the standard libraries, though this is not showed in the area report.
The hypothesis was that the power consumption of the wires where the majority,
due to the long length. Thus this is not accounted for in the power simulations.
6.8
Summary
This chapter covered testing of the three communication infrastructure designs and
two networks instances for each were used for simulations. It was seen, from the
section regarding latency, that the ring performed worse than the tree, when sending
data packet stream where a low amount of slaves were written for a longer chain.
Secondly, the ring structure had constant runtime not depending on the number of
slaves written in a stream. Under the area section synthesis results showed than the
area of the ring versus the tree had an area increase of 160%. Thus the transportation
network of the tree was smaller than for the ring. Because of the large of amount
of area for the slave modules, it was fair to say that the size of the infrastructures
increase linearly. For the power simulations a high amount of leakage current for
all designs where observed and the tree used much more power than the ring, which
is obvious as the design were larger in area and results in more leakage. For the
test on the large network with 256 slave nodes the tree also had a larger power
consumption, but it were cheaper to transfer data packages in the tree with respect
to switching power. Under the noise simulations, the toggles were measured on the
wires connecting the modules of the network. High instantaneous toggle count were
measured for the two ring structures, reasoned that they act closely as a synchronous
pipeline. The tree had an even increasing toggle count which means low noise. This
is due to the handshaking nature of the asynchronous protocol where only one signal
toggles at any moment.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
This chapter discusses different aspects of the three designs.
First, the different
deviations between parallel and serial bit transfer are discussed, then a comparison of
the two ring infrastructures are given. The synthesis of the designs are discussed and
the adaptations in to other technologies. A few comments are given on the simulations
and in the end, it is discussed which design has lowest implementation time.
7.1
Parallel to serial
The two ring structures transports 12 bits in parallel and the serial asynchronous
tree transports one bit in serial. It means that at certain points these are difficult to
compare, especially when the wires are not accounted for as the wires will be a large
amount of the overall power consumption. Obviously, the tree generates lower noise
and of course the transportation network is smaller. It would have been interesting if
the two ring structures were redesigned to a 1-bit data line and compared to the tree.
Thus the tree still outperforms the current ring structures in situations with respect
to latency and noise.
7.2
Shift-register versus Mousetrap
The idea behind replacing the latch pairs of the shift-register with a mousetrap
pipeline stage was to decrease the area. Unfortunately, the synthesis tool converted
the two latches of the shift-register into a register, therefore the area reports are te-
dious to compare. A register is not double the size of a latch, but smaller. The size
of the mousetrap transportation network is about 10% smaller with respect to area
and power. Thus, the runtime is much higher for the mousetrap infrastructure and
it makes lower in noise.
7.3
Synthesis
No layout have been made for any of the designs, therefore it affects all tests. The
latency tests results are not critical since no specification are given on this matter.
However, it affects the power and noise. Design Vision generates the SDF-file used for
==PAGE:62==

simulation with an estimated guess of wires length. The wires are short as possible
as the tool thinks all the logic are supposed to be placed close. In reality all modules
of the infrastructure are spread across the chip.
The mousetrap pipeline was chosen for the parallel asynchronous ring for the rea-
son that less latches were used and because no c-element was used. For the serial
asynchronous tree, no c-elements were used either, as they were redefined to use
standard gates in the VHDL. The simulations did not give any hold time errors and
functioned as intended. When this design of the tree is ported to another technol-
ogy than the 90nm library, it might not be functioning as intended, because hold
times might not be met. This means that it does not have the robustness property
as claimed in Section 3.1. If the modules are implemented with library containing
c-elements the property would hold. Another issue are the two delays inside the slave
module of the tree, in the synthesis’ of the networks they were not necessary to use,
but for a different network or technology they might be needed.
7.4
Simulations
Acquiring a 0.5 switch ration on each data wire was secured by changing the bits
of the next value flowing through the pipeline, but it is difficult to predict the best
infrastructure and test cases for a certain system. The belief behind the test cases
was to see how the system react to full load of all the slave modules and low load. On
average you can say the tree performs greatly in both cases, but the ring structures
wins on full load and performs equally on low load. So it really depends on the target
system and the technology library is also a factor, a high leakage is noticed for the
tree, but is the cheapest in switching power.
7.5
Power and Noise
The hypothesis from Section 4.3 was that the wires connecting the modules, were
the larges part of the power consumption. Likewise it must be the most noisy part
of the designs. As the layout of the designs have not been made, the wires are not
recorded to have a realistic length. From this we can say that the results regarding
power consumption are not valid. It is fair to say though, that if the wires connecting
the modules are close to the equal length, the noise evaluation of the test section are
valid and comparable.
Two thirds of the power consumption are due to leakage currents running through
the leaky transistors. The leakage will vary with respect to the cells used to build the
designs of. In this case a HVT 90nm library are used and this one are the delivers
the less leaky cells available for synthesis of the project. Possible better ultra low
power libraries are available for certain technologies. Secondly, gating or power down

7.6
Implementation time
From working on the synthesis, it is believed that the designs are not each equally easy
to synthesize. All three designs have in common that they do not use a synchronous
clock, therefore the synthesis tool have difficulties to optimize the designs. The shift-
register ring and the serial asynchronous tree did not require any special tweaks in
order to be synthesized and the timing rules will be obeyed naturally due to the fashion
of the designs, while the parallel asynchronous ring requires manually treatment of
the request signals in order to obey timing rules. With respect to fork/merge module
pairs, it is simpler to assemble the serial asynchronous tree as it consists of simpler
modules. The master interface of shift-register are interface with fewer cycles than
the interface of the serial asynchronous tree, in case they are controlled direct from
software controllable registers.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to realize Sigma Designs original idea for a configura-
tions and status deployment infrastructure which were a shift-register ring. Secondly,
it was to design other infrastructures and compare these. The two new ideas were the
parallel asynchronous ring which were similar to the shift-register ring and the last
solution transferred the bits serially down through a tree and the results back again,
also using an asynchronous protocol. This one is called the serial asynchronous tree.
The most important design object behind the solutions was to design a low power
network which generates less noise.
The topology analysis showed that the latency of the tree would be lower for trans-
portation of single data packages. This have proven to be correct through the tests,
but if streams of data packages are transferred, the two ring structures might perform
better.
For larger networks, the analysis showed that more wires were needed to connect
the components of the tree compared to the ring structures. The tests showed that
even though the shift-register ring and the tree uses same amount of toggles on the
wires, the tree still generate much less noise. It is difficult to imagine a better design
with respect to noise as the toggle almost increase with a constant slope.
From the tests it is concluded that the smaller designs in case of area results in
a lower power consumption, but we saw it was due to high leakage currents. If the
leakage currents were low enough, the tree would actually transport data packages
with a lower switching power.
Sigma Designs should consider to use one of the ring infrastructures for the lowest
power, in fact the parallel asynchronous ring requires 10% less area and consumes 10%
less energy. If they prefer the lowest noise they should chose the serial asynchronous
tree, but it gives a penalty of 160% more area and power. The large increase in area
are mainly due to the controller of the slave module.
It was more difficult to make good synthesis of the parallel asynchronous ring, there-
fore the shift-register ring and the serial asynchronous tree are the easiest to imple-
ment with respect to power and noise. The tree has less modules and all interfaces

8.1
Future work
The controller of the serial asynchronous tree was a large part of the overall area.
It could be good to investigate this more. To lower area and power consumption,
a 2-phase protocol could have been made for the 23 4-phase pipeline stages loading
input data, then 12 2-phase stages was sufficient and a controller to convert from
4-phase to 2-phase was needed on the input interface. As described earlier the area
would explode if a 2-phase router where to be designed. Thus, the back-going tree
could easily have been a 2-phase handshake protocol.
To acquire better results from the power simulations, a layout should have been made,
even though it is difficult to predict the distances between the modules. The conse-
quences of a layout would also have given more precise latency results of the different
designs. Another factor is to give better power results were to implement clock-gating
and power down areas, that it is known to exist for certain analog modules on Sigma
Designs chip.
