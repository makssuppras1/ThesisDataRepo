ABSTRACT 
Energy harvesting of human body energies has in the past years experienced a rising 
interest because it can be used to power wearable applications directly on the human 
body. This project investigates a design model for a permanent magnet rotational energy 
harvester with and without core material. It is done through calculations, simulations 
and tests of two prototypes. 
 
It is found out that a design model with core material has too much core losses resulting 
in too much cogging torque in order for the energy harvester being able to spin by hu-
man body motions. 
 
It is also found that a coreless design model is the best suited to be used for harvesting 
energy from the human body motions and that rectangular magnets gives the best power 
output. A prototype is made and tested which shows peaks of the open circuit voltages 
of almost 1 V and an output power of almost 1 mW.

INTRODUCTION 
1.1 
Background 
Energy harvesters can be defined as devices which collects energy from the surround-
ings and converts it into electrical energy[1]. Besides harvesting energies from the envi-
ronment, they can be used for harvesting the energies of the human body. It becomes 
interesting when the harvested electrical energy can be used directly on the human body 
for wearable applications. Wearable applications can be defined as electronic devices 
with a low and self supplying power consumption and a size small enough to be inte-
grated in clothes or attached directly on the human body [1]. The power for the weara-
ble applications is often supplied by an internal battery. Batteries need to be recharged 
or replaced over time which can be challenging or impossible in some cases [2] and 
they take up a lot of space in the wearable application. 
 
In order to reduce maintenance time of wearable applications and increase their lifespan 
it is desirable to harvest the energy for the wearable applications directly from the hu-
man body resulting in a decreased or completely removed requirement of batteries. This 
can be done with energy harvesters which can give the wearable applications a source of 
power for a longer period of time than if they are powered by batteries only. The human 
body produces kinetic and thermal energy and it can be a challenge to harvest kinetic 
energy because the motions of human body limbs are unpredictable and volatile. 
 
1.2 
Problem statement 
The purpose of this project is to design a permanent magnet rotational energy harvester 
for human body motions. It will be powered by eccentric weights which will rotate 
when subjected to the accelerations of human motions. It should be suited to be used 
together with wearable applications. The following elements will be treated in the re-
port. 
 Design and simulation 
- Design theory and equations will be developed and simulations will be 
performed for a design model of an axial flux permanent magnet ma-
chine with core material. It is desired to investigate if it is suitable to use 
==PAGE:20==

core material in small electrical machines used in combination with hu-
man motions and wearable applications. 
- Simulation models for a coreless design model of an axial flux perma-
nent magnet machine will be developed and investigated in order to 
compare different magnet dimensions and how they affect the possible 
power output of the machine when used as an energy harvester for hu-
man motions. 
 Prototype and test 
- A prototype of the core design model will be produced in order to test 
and verify the core losses from the simulations. 
- A prototype of the coreless design model will be produced as a proof of 
concept and in order to test and verify the simulations when used as an 
energy harvester. 
 
1.3 
Method and delimitations 
The work in this report is limited to the design, simulation and production of two axial 
flux permanent magnet machines. The power output of an energy harvester is intended 
to be delivered to a maximum power point tracking converter which secures a stable DC 
output voltage and maximises the power output of the energy harvester by varying the 
load [2]. 
 
It is expected that it will be possible to make a converter, that the control circuit will 
consume power so that it is able to be powered from the energy harvester alone [3], that 
the overall efficiency of the converter can reach around 70 % and that it has a suitable 
energy storage depending on the power output and the wearable application it will be 
used in combination with. The converter will not be further covered in this report. 
 
 
1.4 
Related work 
A coreless axial flux permanent magnet machine is produced and tested in [4]. They 
make the stator with planar coils on flexible copper-clad polyimide. It is done with UV 
light exposure and metal etching. The rotors are double sided meaning that there is one 
on each side of the stator. It is rotated by an eccentric weight on each rotor which is 
moving when subjected to accelerations from human motions.

Reading guide 
This report is devided into three parts. The first part is a chapter about selected wearable 
applications and their power usage, human body energies and human body accelera-

The second part is about a design model for the permanent magnet rotational energy 
harvester with core material. The core model will be designed from theory, equations 
and their belonging calculations. The core losses will be calculated, simulated and tested 
in order to see if they are too large to be used in combination with human motions and 
wearable applications.

already existing design from [4]. A prototype will be produced and tested as a proof of 
concept and in order to verify the simulations.

ENERGY HARVESTING 
2.1 
Wearable applications 
This section describes different types of wearable applications and their power usage. 
As stated in section 1.1 a wearable application is an electronic device with a low and 
self supplying power consumption and a size small enough to be integrated in clothes or 
attached directly on the human body. It ranges from sport gadgets and fashionable tech-
nology to vital medical devices and sensors. All of the wearable applications are de-
pendent on a power supply which is often done with batteries. As mentioned in section 
1.1, batteries need to be changed or recharged over time. If energy is harvested directly 
on the human body, it could make it possible for the wearable application to run until it 
malfunctions. In some cases it could completely eliminate the need of a battery if the 
wearable application only needs power while the person is moving. In Table 2-1 the 
power usage of selected wearable applications are listed and sorted according to the 
power usage. It shows that wearable applications have a wide range of power usages. 
According to Delta [5] the power usage for wearable applications is normally a few mW 
or less but can also be seen in the few W range. The list will be used in section 3.2.1 in 
order to choose a power level for the energy harvester design models.

2.2 
Human body energies 
As stated in section 1.1 and 2.1, it is desirable to harvest the energy for the wearable 
applications directly from the human body. The body produces heat and kinetic energy. 
The heat from the body can be harvested and converted into electric energy by using, as 
an example, micro-thermoelectric coolers as done in [12]. They use thermoelectric 
coolers (Peltier elements) on the back of a digital wristwatch in order to power it solely 
from the harvested heat energy. Thermal energy is a continuous source of power. 
 
The kinetic energy from the body can be harvested with, as an example, piezoelectric 
elements as they are exposed to pressure. It can also be harvested electromagnetically 
when exposed to body limb movements. In [13] an energy harvester is made as a cylin-
der with a magnet, coil array and a magnetic spring. It harvests the motions of the hu-
man body while walking. Kinetic energy is unpredictable and is not a continuous soruce 
of power. The need of batteries is larger for kinetic energy harvesters and is nessesary if 
it is supposed to be used in combination with a wearable application which needs con-
stant power. 
 
If too much energy is harvested from the body, it will be noticed by the person wearing 
the energy harvester, which is not desired. If too much heat is harvested, the person will 
feel cold and if too much kinetic energy is harvested, the person will feel resistance 
when moving the body limbs which can lead to exhaustion. It may be desired in some 
cases for kinetic energy if it is used in relation with a wearable sports application where 
the resistance can be used for a more intense training. For this project, it is desired to 
harvest kinetic energy passively, without the person noticing. Therefore the wearable 
application for this project should be in the lower power usage range as listed in Table 
2-1.

CORE DESIGN MODEL 
This chapter contains theory, equations and calculations for the core design model made 
in this project. Furthermore, simulations are performed in order to estimate the core 
losses in the core design model and a core loss prototype is made in order to verify the 
core losses from the theory and the simulations. 
 
3.1 
Design and theory 
3.1.1 
Design considerations 
This section contains the choices made before the equations for the core design model 
are made. 
 
3.1.1.1 
General design choices 
As stated in section 2.2, it is chosen for this project to design an energy harvester for 
kinetic energy and that it will be a permanent magnet rotational energy harvester. For 
the core design model, the energy harvester is made as an axial flux permanent magnet 
machine with an eccentric weight placed on half of the back side of the rotor as ex-
plained and sketched in section 2.2 Figure 2-1. This is done because an axial flux ma-
chine can be thinner compared to radial flux machines and will be ideal to place at the 
wrist or generally when attached to the body it can be less protruding and noticeable. 
The axial flux machine is also easier to wind and easier to fit with a higher number of 
poles [24] which will increase the output power at lower rotational speeds such as in 
direct drive wind turbines which are made with a large number of poles because of the 
lack of a gearbox. 
 
Only one rotor and one stator will be used for the core design model as it will simplify 
the mechanics for the energy harvester compared to if the energy harvester has two ro-
tors as it will in chapter 4. This can be done due to the presense of the core material 
which will conduct the magnetic field through the coils in the stator. When core materi-
al is used the energy harvester might have problems with the cogging torque being too 
high making the energy harvester hard to spin and it might also create problems with the 
weight of the energy harvester making it hard to harvest energy passively, without the

Core design model

person noticing. It might also cause problems with core losses due to eddy currents and 
hysteresis. It will be investigated in sections 3.2-3.4. Lastly it is chosen to design the 
generator with the number of phases 𝑎𝑝ℎ= 3 as it is a common way to set up machines.

The generator is wound with one coil around each tooth. This means that each slot con-
tains windings from two different coils. It is decided to design a fractional slot machine 
because it can reduce cogging torque, making it easier to spin. A fractional slot machine 
is defined in [25] as a machine with a number of slots pr pole pr phase which is not an 
integer number. Cogging torque is in [25] defined as the interaction between the perma-
nent magnets and the teeth. If this torque is higher than the mechanical input torque cre-
ated by the eccentric weight being moved by the human motions, the energy harvester 
will not turn. It is important to take it into concideration because the eccentric weight 
most likely will not create large mechanical input torque values. The cogging torque 
will be simulated and tested in section 3.3 and 3.4 as it is difficult to calculate numeri-
cally. 
 
In [26] a series of finite element analysis were made for selected pole/slot combinations 
for a permanent magnet radial flux machine. The results can be seen in Table 3-1 and 
the proportions between the different pole/slots combinations are considered to be usea-
ble for an axial flux machine aswell [24]. The magnitude of the cogging torque will vary 
depending on the size of the machine but the table gives a good impression of which 
combination will have the lowest cogging torque. A machine with 16 poles and 15 slots 
will result in a low cogging torque, but because the energy harvester is supposed to be 
small and it can be difficult to produce a small machine with a high number of poles and 
slots it is not used for this project. In order to achieve a balance between a low cogging 
torque and a low number of poles and slots, it is chosen to use the combination with 
𝑝= 10 poles and 𝑎𝑠= 9 slots. 
 
The generator will be wound as illustrated in Figure 3-1, where each phase is denoted 
by A, B and C. Each phase containing three coils denoted with numbers 1-3. This 
pole/slot combination introduces a winding factor of 𝐾𝑤= 0.945 which is gotten from 
the winding design tool in the computer program MotorSolve [27]. This means that the 
output voltage of the generator will be decreased by 5.5 % due to a slight phase shift 
between the three coils in each phase. 
 
It would also be a good candidate to use a combination such as 4 poles and 6 slots as it 
also has a low cogging torque. This combination is not investigated in [26] and it cannot 
be used practically as this pole/slot combination was patented in 1995 with the patent 
number US5382853 A. 
 
It is also possible to reduce cogging torque with skewing of the stator teeth or the rotor 
magnets, changing the magnet arc, making bifurcated teeth, varying magnetisation of 
the magnets, introducing semi-closed slots and shifting the position of the magnets. It 
will not be treated in this project as it can reduce performance of the machine further 
according to [26].

The core material is chosen to be soft magnetic steel S355 J2 as it does not have a good

back material as it will increase the size of coreback making the energy harvester heavi-
er according to equation 3.19. The relative permeability in the core material for soft

The magnet material is chosen to be NdFeB N35 because it is easy and fast accessible

3.3 
Core loss and cogging torque simulations 
This section contains a description and the results of the core loss and cogging torque 
simulations for the core design model. As stated in section 3.2.2 it is desired to simulate 
the core losses in order to verify the estimated core losses from the calculations of 
𝑃𝑐= 35.4 mW. The simulations are performed in the computer program MagNet[32] 
which is using finite element analysis. 
 
The dimensions of the generator from the calculation results in Table 3-3 are used to 
create a 3D-model in MagNet. It is done with scripting in MATLAB [22]. An example 
of automating MagNet with MATLAB can be seen in appendix C below the headline 
MagNet_script_no_core_t.m. This is the code used for the coreless simulations in sec-
tion 4.2 but the script for the core loss simulations look similar and are not included. 
The model can be seen sliced in half in Figure 3-7. It is made without copper windings 
as it is only desired to get the results for the core losses. The core material is not lami-
nated as the core loss prototype in section 3.4 will be produced with no core lamina-
tions. A new user material is made in MagNet as soft magnetic iron S355 J2 based on 
the meassurements from Figure 3-5. The magnet material NdFeB N35 does not excist 
in MagNet so the material named NdFeB 32/31 is used instead as it is the American 
version of NdFeB N35 which is the Chinese standard. The mechanical rotation speed of 
the rotor, also called motion component, is set to 𝜔𝑚= 11.3 
rad
s , taken from section 
3.2.1. The speed is set to be constant in order to get the core loss results at steady state, 
just as the core model is designed and calculated in section 3.1 and 3.2. 
 
The stator and rotor of the model are enclosed individually by virtual air components 
which again are enclosed by normal air components. In virtual air, torque will not be 
calculated. This means that torque will only be calculated on the entire surface up 
against the virtual air components. It can be seen in figure Figure 3-8 that the airgap is 
sketched with lines and it shows that it is devided into four sections as a consequence of 
the air enclosures. Closest to the stator and rotor is the virtual air and in the middle of 
the airgap is the normal air. The acting torque will be calculated for normal air which 
will be for the entire planes between the normal and virtual air. It is done because it is

difficult and less precise to calculate torque values close to sharp edges like the corners 
of the teeth and magnets. This is due to higher flux density values or eventually satura-

The simulations need to be performed in 3D because the energy harvester is an axial 
flux machine. These machines cannot be simulated in 2D because when seen from the

symmetry the entire model is simulated. It would be possible to simulate only half of 
the generator in order to save computation time. It requires setting the correct boundary 
conditions in order for MagNet to mirror the generator and get the correct results. It is 
chosen to simulate the entire model.

hysteresis loss will not be included which might cause a difference between the calcu-

the core loss prototype in section 3.4.3 where the hysteresis losses will be included.

3.4.3 
Test of core loss prototype 
This section contains the tests of the core loss prototype. As explained in section 3.3 the 
core losses consists of both eddy current and hysteresis losses. In the simulations made 
in section 3.3 and 3.4.2 only the eddy current losses are simulated. 
 
The core losses will be tested by measuring the cogging torque acting on the stator disc 
during no load and while it is being exposed to a rotating magnetic field generated by 
the rotor magnets spinning at steady state speed. The method originates from [40] where 
the relation between torque, power and rotational speed shown in equation 3.41 is used. 
This method is also used for core loss tests in [41] and in [39]. According to [39] the 
hysteresis losses causes an offset in the measured cogging torque but according to [40] 
it is the total core losses, meaning both from eddy currents and hysteresis, which causes 
the offset. In this project the offset is considered to be caused by the total core losses. 
The reason why some papers neglect the eddy current losses can be because they as-
sume that they are small when the generator is operated at low frequencies. The offset in 
==PAGE:67==
 
Core design model 
65 
the measured torque will be used in combination with the relation from equation 3.41 to 
get the core loss results. This can be done because when using equation 3.41 on the 
cogging torque without an offset it means that the average resulting power will be zero 
and no core losses are present. When an offset is introduced the average resulting power 
will not be zero and this is therefore the core losses. This is also seen for the eddy cur-
rent loss simulations in section 3.3 Figure 3-10 and Figure 3-11 where the cogging 
torque is shown with and without eddy current losses. 
 
The friction losses will not be a part of the measurements as the torque will be measured 
at the stator side which does not rotate and the friction losses from the rotor is not af-
fecting the torque profile on the stator side. It will also be present as an offset if the 
torque is measured on the rotor side according to [39].

CORELESS DESIGN MODEL 
This chapter contains description of the design choices, simulations and tests made for 
the coreless design model. 
 
4.1 
Design choices 
This section contains the choices made for the coreless design model. For the coreless 
design model, the energy harvester is also made as an axial flux permanent magnet ma-
chine as the core design model described in section 3.1.1.1. The coreless design model 
will use two rotors, one on each side of the stator and both with an eccentric weight 
placed on half of the back side of them as explained and sketched in section 2.2. Two 
rotors are needed because no core material is present to conduct the magnetic field 
through the coils in the stator. 
 
The magnet material is chosen to be NdFeB N45 because it is easy and fast accessible 
from the magnet retailer Magnetz og Magnordic ApS and because it is more powerful 
than NdFeB N35 which was used in the core design model from chapter 3. It has a rem-
nant flux density of 1.32 T. It is convenient that is is more powerful when there is no 
core material to conduct the magnetic field as there is in the core design model. The 
material will be used for the coreless prototype in section 4.3. 
 
A design theory section will not be made for the coreless design model as it is for the 
core design model in section 3.1. This is because they are developed in similar ways. 
The things needed to be changed for the new design would be the flux path in equation 
3.22 which is used for calculating the height of the magnets. The flux path would in-
stead be through four airgaps, four magnets and two pieces of surrounding air resulting 
in a larger height of the magnets. The rest of the design would be very similar. Calcula-
tions will not be performed for the coreless design model as well. The use of two rotors 
is inspired from the design model used in [4] where they also made an axial flux perma-
nent magnet energy harvester with two rotors. The coreless design model is based on 
the core design model from chapter 3. The number of poles is for the coreless design 10, 
the number of slots is 9, the thickness of the airgaps is 1 mm and the weight of the ec-
centric weight is 10 grams as in chapter 3. The number of windings is chosen to be 400

CONCLUSION 
5.1 
Results 
Throughout this report two design models have been made of a permanent magnet rota-
tional energy harvester for body motions. It was desired to design the energy harvester 
so it could power various wearable applications in order to reduce the need of batteries 
in wearable applications. It was the intention to power the energy harvester with eccen-
tric weights which would make it rotate when subjected to human motion accelerations 
 
A core design model has been presented by a section containing equations and theory. 
Calculations were performed in order to get the specifications of the generator. They 
showed problems with high estimated core losses so simulations and tests were made of 
a core loss prototype which veryfied the problem with the core losses. It was found that 
the core losses and therefore the cogging torque were too high to allow the generator to 
be powered with an eccentric weight subjected to human motion accelerations. It was 
also stated that it would make it more feasible if the stator was skewed in combination 
with a laminated core. 
 
A coreless design model has been presented based on an already existing rotational en-
ergy harvester. Simulations on selected models were performed in order to find the best 
performing design and shape of the magnets. The best performing design was a rectan-
gular magnet with the size of 5 mm x 18.5 mm x 2 mm. As a proof of concept and to 
verify the simulations it was chosen to produce the design with the magnet dimensions 
of 20 x 4 x 2 mm. It was not the best performing model but it would make the energy 
harvester easier to produce, cheaper and friendlier to the environment due to the recy-
clability of magnets. The main body of the energy harvester was 3D-printet. Tests were 
performed on the energy harvester and it was able to output a peak voltage of almost 
1 V and a simple load test showed a power output of 0.96 mW and a power density of 
13 
μW
cm3. The open circuit voltage was considered to be on the lower side when fed to a 
converter which was set to have the requirement for the open circuit voltage of 1 V due 
to the active mosfet rectifier often used in maximum power point tracking converters. 
The tests did not match the simulations which gave a higher open circuit voltage. This 
could be caused by frictional losses and the fact that the tests were performed by walk-
==PAGE:90==
 
Conclusion 
88 
ing in place and not real walking. It was the coreless design model showing the biggest 
potential to be used as an energy harvester for human body motions and power wearable 
applications. 
5.2 
Perspectives 
The coreless energy harvester from chapter 4 is most suited to be used on the upper 
body as it is designed for the accelerations and frequencies for the limbs on this part of 
the body. If used on the lower body on the ankle or knee it will be exposed to larger 
accelerations and frequencies. It will make the open circuit voltage larger and when 
loading the generator it will most likely result in larger copper losses due to a higher 
input power. It would make it possible to overload the energy harvester but also to har-
vest more energy. 
 
The coreless energy harvester can also be used in other places than the human body like 
vibrations from machines in the industry. These vibrations would expose the energy 
harvester to larger accelerations and frequencies [46] than what is present on the human 
body. This would have a higher risk of failure and it would be unsure wether or not the 
energy harvester would spin at high frequencies making the magnets static infront of a 
coil resulting in no power output. 
 
It is advisable not to use the coreless energy harvester in combination with vital weara-
ble applications such as pacemakers due to the lack of power when no movements are 
present. Even if batteries are used to store the energy when movements are present it 
will be risky as it is difficult to predict how and when a human body will move. 
 
If it is assumed that a blood sugar monitor will be carried in a bag which will be moving 
regularly, the coreless energy harvester will have a large potential to be used for charg-
ing the internal battery of a blood sugar monitor considering the low average power 
usage of 25.5 μW from Table 2-1. This will make it possible to extend the battery to 
last for more than 1000 blood sugar tests [7]. It might even extend the battery to always 
be fully charged, making it possible to do an infinite number of tests or until the blood 
sugar monitor or energy harvester malfuncitons. Even though the blood sugar monitor is 
not getting exposed to movements every day, it should be able to be fully charged be-
cause the power usage for a blood sugar monitor during a test is 3.3 mW which means 
that the battery will be charged when exposed to movements during the use of it. This 
requires the energy harvester to output more than 3.3 mW as an average which should 
be possible according to the calculations in section 3.2. 
 
The energy harvester can be used on boats. Larger ships often have emergency boats 
with emergency equipment on board. This equipment contains batteries which need to 
be fully charged at all times. Batteries experiences a small self-discharge when not in

from discharging and ready to be used in case of an emergency. The size of the energy

5.3 
Future work 
This project was limited to cover the design, simulation and test of two axial flux per-
manent magnet machines. The energy harvester has a volatile voltage curve and in order 
to use it with wearable applications it would be desireable to design a maximum power 
point tracking converter to stabilise the output voltage and control the loading of it. This 
could also lead to a test of the entire efficiency of the energy harvester and converter 
combined depending on the scale of the accelerations and frequencies. This would make 
it easier to predict the useability of the energy harvester in other environments than the 
human body. 
 
Further simulations with hysteresis losses and a higher precision than what was per-
formed in the core design model in section 3.3 could be made in order to get a closer 
estimation of the real core losses and cogging torque. It would also be interesting to 
simulate models with skewing of the stator teeth and a laminated core to see if could 
potentially be used for energy harvesters. 
 
The coreless prototype in this project was made in larger scale than intended. It would 
be interesting to see if it is possible to scale the energy harvester down to a size which 
can be integrated in smaller wearable applications such as smartwatches.  
 
The coreless prototype was not the best performing of the investigated coreless design 
models but it was the fastest, easiest and friendlist to the environment. It would be in-
testing to improve the performance of the design further while still considering the pos-
sibility of reusing old magnets in other pole combinations and placements. It would also 
be interesting to see the influence of the number of windings. If the number of windings 
is increased the copper layer thickness would also increase. The open circuit voltage 
would rise as a consequence of this but the magnetic flux would have to travel further in 
the air and the copper resistance is increased. In this way the optimal number of wind-
ings could be found in the same way by evaluating the ratio

It would be interesting to investigate with simulations how the coreless design model 
would perform by arranging the magnets in a Halbach array. This is done in [47] by 
varying the magnet placement angles relative to the stator in order to achieve a higher 
airgap flux due to less fringing between adjacent magnets meaning more flux going
