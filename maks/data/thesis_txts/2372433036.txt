Abstract
The electricity generation expansion planning is one of the most complicated decision
making problems in power systems, which determines the technology, capacity and
location of new generation units to be built. This thesis proposes an efficient solution
model based on Benders decomposition to solve a network constrained generation expan-
sion planning problem incorporating unit commitment constraints under uncertainty.
The developed stochastic generation expansion planning decision making model for
power systems determines future conventional investment decision in new conventional
and renewable capacity, while enforcing unit commitment constraints of all existing and
candidate conventional generating units.
Furthermore, this model provides a tool to compare the generation expansion decisions
with and without enforcing each unit commitment constraint. Consequently, this tool
allows the evaluation of the importance of each of those constraints. Formulated as a
two stage stochastic programming problem model the first stage refers to the generation
expansion plan and the daily day ahead market scheduling, whhereas the second stage
represents the real time operation to adjust wind power imbalances under each scenario.
Wind power production is expected to contribute strongly to electricity generation,
despite its high variability. This thesis considers wind power production as the source
of uncertainty, which is modeled through a suitable set of scenarios.
The wind power production is the only source of uncertainty considered in this thesis,
which is modeled through a suitable set of scenarios.
Due to computational issues, Benders decomposition is implemented to decompose
the original problem to a number of smaller problems. The proposed Benders approach
allows decomposing the original problem, which is mixed integer linear, into a mixed
integer linear master problem and a set of linear continuous subproblems, one per
scenario and day.
Finally the established model is recast as a mixed integer linear
programming problem.
In the end, resulting models are implemented and solved in
GAMS. Moreover, numerical results from a case study provide evidence of the usefulness
of the proposed approach. In conclusion, consideration of hour-to-hour ramp rate limits
is crucial for reaching an optimal generation mix.
Index Terms— Network constrained generation expansion planning, Impact of unit
commitment constraints, Wind power uncertainty, Stochastic programming, Two-stage
model, Benders decomposition, Multi-cut, Impact of operational detail.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The power sectors worldwide are transforming from a dominant fossil fueled towards a
low carbon electricity generation mix. As regions promote policies and measures for the
utilization of renewable energy technologies, these technologies become a greater part of
the global energy mix [1] . Wind power is expected to contribute strongly to electricity
generation from renewable energy sources in the coming decades, despite its variability
and limited controllability [2]. Issues regarding the integration of wind generation into
the electrical grid arise due to the unpredictable nature of output uncertainty caused by
weather, i.e. wind speed, dependent planning [3].
The electricity generation expansion planning is one of the most complicated decision
making problems in power systems, which determines the technology, capacity and
location of new generation units to be built. In the literature, it is common to ignore
the unit commitment constraints of the conventional generation units, e.g. ramp limits,
minimum production requirements, or minimum up and down times, within the generation
expansion decision making. However, under wind uncertainty, the dispatch of conventional
generators becomes more volatile, so that unit commitment constraints are often active.
In other words, the growing use of renewables pushes thermal generators against operating
constraints, e.g. ramping limits, minimum output, or operating reserves, thus changing
the energy production plan and optimal capacity mix [4].
Taken together, as more variable and uncertain wind power is being integrated into
power systems, the unit commitment constraints cannot be ignored. The exclusion of
these constraints may result in inefficient generation expansion planning decisions in
wind integrated power systems. This thesis presents an optimization based approach
to address the generation expansion planning problem of a large scale, central power
system with high wind penetration, taking unit commitment and operational constraints
specifically into account. The impact of these operational constraints is analyzed.
Firstly, this thesis gives a short overview over electric energy markets in general. Then
the concept of generation expansion planning is discussed, with a subsequent review of
relevant works in the literature. After the research questions at hand and thereby, the
contributions of this Master thesis are described, the underlying modeling assumptions
are documented.
Secondly, the mathematical model is described, formulated, and
illustrated through a simple example.
Then an illustrative case study is introduced,
modeled, and solved.
The results of this case study are presented and the impacts
of the individual constraints are briefly evaluated.
In a next step, the necessity of
decomposing the proposed model is explained along with the concept of the applied

the well-functioning of the decomposed model is validated. Finally, the impacts of the

1.2
Energy markets
Electric energy systems are operated so that the power demand is supplied at minimum
cost while enforcing appropriate security margins. Mathematical programming models
provide an appropriate framework to address these optimization decisions in a precise
and formal manner [5].
Electricity markets encompass the production, transmission, distribution, and supply
of electric energy.
Typically, the day ahead market and the balancing market, as
well as the futures market are parts of the electricity market. The day ahead market
schedules production and supply for the 24 hours of the following day, whereas the
balancing market closes several minutes prior to power delivery on an hourly basis. The
futures market serves as medium-term contracting for trading energy from one week to
several years in advance at rather stable prices compared to day ahead and balancing
prices [6]. In these marketplaces, power generation supply and electricity demand are
balanced according to an appropriate market clearing tool, usually the Merit Order [7].
The integration of power from renewable sources with low production costs pushes the
Merit Order curve, additionally the injection of power from renewable production is
often prioritized over conventional sources. Hereby, the commitment of conventional
production facilities and system requirements change, commanding flexibility and adap-
tation.
Key agents in an electricity market include flexible, inflexible or stochastic producers,
consumers, retailers, the independent system operator, and the regulator.
Flexible
producers provide energy and reserve, e.g. backup power, whereas inflexible producers
provide just energy, which lack the flexibility to adapt the production to real time
operating conditions. Stochastic producers provide energy on a stochastic basis, e.g.,
wind power units or solar power plants. Consumers require energy and may have the
capability of adapting to real time operating conditions, e.g. through load shedding.
Retailers buy energy in the market to sell it to the respective clients for a profit. The
independent system operator runs the market and uses an appropriate market clearing
procedure to derive production and consumption quantities, reserve levels, and market
clearing prices. The regulator is an independent entity in charge of ensuring an efficient
and competitive working of the electricity market [6]. The regulator in the United States
is the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, while in the European Union, regulators
are country based, see [8].

1.3 Generation expansion planning

1.3
Generation expansion planning
Generation expansion planning is the problem of determining the type, quantity, and
timing of power plants to be built in order to meet increasing demand for electricity over
an extended time horizon [3]. The increasing penetration of stochastic power units, e.g.
wind power generation, in the generation mix introduces new dynamics and uncertainty
into the power markets, forcing power system planners and policy makers to revisit the
question of how much operational detail must be captured to adequately assess long
term planning options [4].
The production from the renewable technologies wind and solar power is intermittent
and non dispatchable.
Hence, problems arise because supply and demand must be
balanced everywhere at all times in a power system. In terms of investment planning
this means that there must be sufficient backup and transmission capacity installed in the
system to accommodate all possible situations, for example when output from installed
renewable capacities is low and demand is high, or on the opposite when output from
installed renewables is high and demand is low [9]. Consequently, the need derives for
more flexibility in the system. The flexibility provided by conventional generation is
limited by a variety of constraints, e.g. ramp rates, minimum power output, start up
and shut down costs, minimum or maximum up and down times etc. General generation
expansion planning often relies on highly simplified approximations for operational cost
that ignore details such as plant startups, minimum loads, operating reserves, and
ramping limits. These details are typically reserved for unit commitment and economic
dispatch models that are run a few hours or days before actual operation to determine
the power production schedule, see [10] [11] [12] [13] [14] [15] [16].
In traditional capacity planning these details have often been ignored because of
predictable and fairly slow time dynamics of load and production patterns. However,
with increased quantities of renewables, the net load requirement for conventional thermal
power plants is less predictable. Thus, more operational characteristics are required
within planning models [4] [17]. However, it is highly debatable how much operational
detail needs to be introduced into the long term planning models to adequately secure
the validity and the robustness of the investment decisions [18].
On the one hand, long term expansion planning models should embody all operational
constraints that influence planning decisions. On the other hand, too much operational
detail may result in an intractable model.
Thus, an appropriate trade off between
modeling accuracy and computational tractability needs to be found [6].
In depth analysis is essential for making informed decisions about the construction
of new electricity generation facilities, which usually last and impact the power system
for many years. Moreover, investment decisions involve high costs, are often made under
high uncertainty, and generally require risk control [6]. Generation expansion planning
mitigates investment and production cost. The former is dictated by the number and
types of units of each power plant to build over the planning horizon. The latter depends
on the quantity of electricity generated by each plant, and the specific costs associated
with such generation. Investment decisions must consider future uncertainties, which will
affect operational costs. At the same time, investment decisions must satisfy additional

requirements, including satisfaction of electricity demand, power generation reliability,
energy resource limitations, financial budgets, maximum carbon emissions, and the
minimum required electricity generation proportion for the renewable energy, and so
on [3]. Investment models or generation expansion planning models for long term power
system planning focus on system adequacy, which refers to the long term ability of the
power system to meet its peak demand. The short term unit commitment models and
economic dispatch models quantify the effects on system security, which refers to the
short term ability of the power system to maintain the system balance. With growing
shares of renewable power supply, not only system adequacy, but also system security
issues become a concern for power system planning. Forecast errors for renewable power
production, together with other drivers of system imbalances such as equipment outages
and demand forecast errors, increase the need for operational flexibility within the
system, i.e. capacity which can be rapidly regulated upwards or downwards to keep total
injection in balance with outtake, and specifically also the need for operating reserves,
i.e. capacity which is reserved on system level to cover imbalances [17]. When facing
small shares of renewable power generation, the required operational flexibility might
still be found in the existing conventional generation portfolio. This might no longer be
the case when facing higher shares of renewable production, and operational flexibility is
to be ensured by means of operating reserve requirements. Consequently, these system
security issues are expected to influence the power system operation, but in second
instance also the power system planning. Neglecting these issues in planning models is
then expected to result in a suboptimal power system planning, and an underestimation
of the technical barriers and integration costs of renewable energy.
1.4
Literature review
The following section analyzes different approaches to generation expansion planning
models in the literature. The literature review focuses on the modeling of generation
expansion planning problems as well as the consideration of unit commitment constraints.
A variety of model characteristics are identified, e.g. inclusion of uncertainties, network
representation, form of modeling of investment decisions, representation of settlements
in the markets, time horizon, as well as inclusion of energy policies and investment in
different types of technologies. The results are summarized in table 1.1.
In [3], a dynamic generation expansion planning problem is formulated as a two-stage
stochastic mixed integer linear program minimizing either the expected cost or the
Conditional Value at Risk for a central planner. The model determines type and number
of power plants to be constructed over each year of a planning horizon and is thus a
dynamic investment model. The stochastic program consists of two-stages: investment
and operation. Demand and fuel prices are sources of uncertainty, which are in turn
used to generate trees that accurately represent the uncertainty space. Neither network
constraints nor unit commitment constraints are considered.
In [19], a generalized disjunctive programming model for dynamic investment planning
in different energy sources is presented. The work considers the use of renewable and non
==PAGE:23==
1.4 Literature review
5
renewable sources. Furthermore, demand development, revenues, as well as operation,
start up, and amortization costs of new energy facilities and the availability of fossil
fuels reserves are represented. The dynamic investment model aims at maximizing the
net present value for a central decision maker over a time horizon of 20 years. The
model takes start up cost and general operation cost into account, but neglects specific
unit commitment constraints and the transmission capacity of the power system. Fossil
fuel reserves are the source of uncertainty in the conduction of a scenario analysis. The
development of the energy matrix is assessed and planned under different circumstances
represented by the scenarios.
In [20], a generation expansion planning framework is introduced based on robust
optimization, which portrays an optimal investment plan that mitigates inherent planning
uncertainties.
The robust optimization methodology presents a worst case optimal
solution which is totally or partially immunized against the uncertain variables. The
robust optimization problem is formulated as a mixed integer linear program. Forecasted
electricity load demand, estimated investment and operation costs are modelled as
sources of uncertainty.
The dynamic investment problem spans the time horizon of
several years, while neglecting transmission effects and specific unit commitment con-
straints. The model is formulated as a single-stage and as a two-stage program. Benders
decomposition is applied to ensure problem tractability.
In general, the generation expansion problem is tackled in two different ways: adopting
a centralized approach or considering a market framework. On the one hand, considering
a centralized approach, a central planner, e.g.
the independent system operator, is
assumed to determine the generation expansion plan that is most efficient for the
system as a whole. The central planner determines the generation expansion plan that
results in an optimal operation of the entire electric energy system and in an efficient
supply of demands. This way of tackling the generation expansion problem represented
is also known as a command and control approach.
On the other hand, a market
framework assumes that agents determine own generation expansion plans with the aim
of maximizing expected profits. The strategic behaving agents recover their investment
costs after building the production facilities by selling the respective production in a
market [6].
An example for strategic generation expansion planning is found in [21]. A network
constrained, large scale stochastic programming problem is formulated as a mixed integer
linear model, with rival offering and rival investment as sources of uncertainty. The
static investment decisions are made for a strategic producer for a target year. Hence,
the model identifies the investment options most beneficial for the strategic producer.
The market based approach is represented through a bilevel model: the upper level
considers both investment decisions and strategic production actions and the lower level
corresponds to market clearing. Different technology investment options can be selected,
including base load units and peak units, as well as the respective siting of the units.
To limit computational effort, current power system expansion planning tools typically
consider limited detail on the technical constraints of power systems. With growing
flexibility and balancing needs, this is likely to underestimate the technical barriers
and integration cost of large shares of renewable generation [17]. Generation expansion

1.5
Research questions and contributions
The research questions at hand are the following:
• How do unit commitment constraints impact generation expansion decisions?
• How important is each unit commitment constraint for correct generation expansion
planning?
Considering the works analyzed in the literature review and summarized in table 1.1,
the contributions of this thesis are the following:
1) A centralized generation expansion planning model is proposed.
This static
investment model is network constraint, able to represent the two settlements in the
day ahead and the balancing market, and incorporates operational detail in the form
of relevant unit commitment constraints. The model will select the best investment
options in wind and thermal technologies. The resulting model is a risk neutralized
mixed integer linear stochastic program with the uncertainty inherent in wind power
production modeled through a set of scenarios.
2) The formulated and possibly intractable mixed integer linear model is recast into
a decomposed model using Benders decomposition.
3) An example of small size is comprehensively analyzed and results of the impacts
of the unit commitment constraints are provided for a larger case study.
1.6
Assumptions
• Following a common approach in the literature, the generation expansion plans are
made at a single point in time. The investment planning considers a single future
target year and establishes the optimal investment for that year, i.e.
a static
investment planning approach is considered.
A dynamic investment approach
would lead to a more complex model. Note that this static approach constitutes an
appropriate trade off between modeling accuracy and computational tractability.
Therefore, the considered time horizon is one target year with representative days
on an hourly basis.
Moreover, the commissioning of old units is not considered within the model for
the sake of simplicity.
• Consumers are represented as inelastic loads, and thus the social welfare is maximized
by minimizing the total cost.
• A central planner, e.g. the independent system operator, aims to determine the
generation expansion plan most beneficial for the system as a whole, maximizing
the overall social welfare.
• The model is risk-neutralized.
==PAGE:30==
12
1 Introduction
• Investment costs are annualized and production costs do not change. The considered
market is energy only, i.e. there is no compensation for energy provision and no
shut down cost.
• The wind power production costs are assumed to be zero.
• The supplied energy is required to meet the demand in each time period within
the day ahead planning.
In the real time operation, load shedding and wind
curtailment can occur.
• The investment model is network constrained. However, during the considered
planning horizon, there are no changes in the system topology, i.e., the existing
transmission lines remain the same throughout the planning horizon and cannot
be extended within this model. A direct current network representation of the
transmission network is embedded within the investment model, which is linear,
simple, and appropriate for the generation expansion planning.
The network
constraints adequately represent the effect of locating new units at different nodes,
i.e. siting of facilities, as well as the impact of transmission congestion.
• The unit commitment constraints, that are regarded and analyzed for the impact
on the generation expansion decision, include start up costs, minimum production
limits, and hour to hour ramp rate limits.
Active power losses, maintenance cost and fixed cost, minimum up and down times,
or fast starting units are not included in the proposed model.
• The only source of uncertainty within the model is wind power generation, which is
modeled through a set of finite scenarios for probable wind patterns to approximate
the probability distribution of the unknown future wind speeds.

2.1
Modeling description
Figure 2.1: Two stage stochastic decision framework of the proposed generation
expansion problem with unit commitment constraints.
The proposed model is a two stage stochastic programming problem. The stochastic
programming problems is formulated as a decision tree, see figure 2.1, with each node
in the tree representing a decision point.
Many problems for planning under uncertainty can be formulated as two stage
stochastic programming problems with fixed recourse. In the two stage framework, the
first stage decisions are made here-and-now prior to the resolution of uncertainty, while
the second stage decisions are postponed in a wait-and-see mode after the uncertainties
are revealed [32]. The first stage provides the deterministic generation expansion plan
and daily day-ahead scheduling decisions. At the first stage the electricity day-ahead
market, its constraints and rules are taken into account, while the second stage models
==PAGE:32==
14
2 Model
the real-time operation to adjust wind power imbalances under each scenario of wind
realization. Thus, the second stage considers the actual power system, its operation, and
physical limitations for each scenario. There is uncertainty about the way in which the
information of wind forecasts evolve from the day-ahead planning stage to the real-time
planning.
The uncertainties inherent in wind power production and its forecast are
represented through a number of discrete realizations, constituting distinct scenarios.
In order to make informed investment decisions, the operation of the market under
many plausible future working conditions needs to be represented carefully. Thus, the
scenarios need to capture most conditions. As there are many possible future operational
states, the number of different scenarios to consider in the investment planning problem
is vast, when assessing possible investment options. Taken together, the problem is a two
stage recourse problem, since the initial decisions of investment planning and day-ahead
scheduling are made under uncertainty of actual wind realization followed later by a
second decision in the real-time operation, which allows corrective, i.e. recourse, action
to be taken in light of the actual wind power production [33]. Within the multi-stage
stochastic programming formulation, there is recourse associated with the investment
plan. All decisions are made up-front, and cannot be modified as uncertainty about the
future is resolved. The objective is to find an optimal decision at the root node that
performs well on average under all possible scenarios [32] [34].

3.1
Motivation
The linear optimization model developed in this master thesis is solved using CPLEX
12.6.3.0 under GAMS 24.7.4 on a Lenovo ThinkPad intel CoreTM i5-5200U with two
processors clocking at 2.70 GHz and 4 GB of RAM. The computational times obtained for
direct solutions of the model are collected in Table 3.1. Mixed-integer linear programming
problems are routinely solved provided that the number of integer variables is sufficiently
small, typically below a few thousands. However, these problems are typically hard to
solve and can become numerically intractable [5]. The CPU time of the proposed model
increases with the complexity, the number of time intervals, and the number of scenarios
of the considered system. The increase in CPU time is significant and the problem will
become intractable for a big model. Even with a small number of scenarios and days,
the optimization problem can be large and difficult to solve in reasonable CPU time.
When increasing the number of days and scenarios in the six-node system, the model
cannot be directly solved anymore.
In order to reduce the solution time, a decomposition technique can be applied
[38]. Decomposition techniques are generally able to handle nearly intractable expansion
planning problems and can solve the given large-scale problem by decomposing it into
smaller and simpler subproblems using an iterative scheme [5].
Decomposition techniques are based on the idea that problems can be decomposed

into their component parts. These smaller parts are tackled independently and then

the breaking up of a mathematical programming problem into separate smaller problems
that are easier to solve separately and then reintegrate to get an overall solution [33].
Stochastic programming problems lend themselves to this type of solution because they
are formulated as decision trees with each node in the tree representing a decision point,
see figure 2.1.
Instead of solving in a centralized fashion using branch-and-cut techniques, mixed-
integer linear programming problems can be decomposed separating complicating and
non-complicating variables. Such decomposable structure can be usually exploited com-
putationally. When applying decomposition one tries to split a very large mixed-integer
programming problem smaller mixed-integer and linear programming problems that are
each quicker to solve than the original. Although the problems might need to be solved
several times to arrive at the solution, this is still likely to be quicker than directly solving
the original large problem [5]. The advantage of this scheme is that the subproblems are
each easier to solve than the original problem.
This situation often arises in practice, particularly, in long-term multiperiod investment
planning problems. Investment decisions are integer while operation decisions are con-
tinuous and often separable by a time period [5].
When applying decomposition to
stochastic programming problems one tries to split a very large mixed-integer programming
problem, i.e. the deterministic equivalent of the stochastic programming problem, into
smaller mixed-integer and linear programming problems that are each quicker to solve
than the original. Although the problems might need to be solved several times to arrive
at the solution, this is still likely to be quicker than directly solving the original large
problem.
3.2
Benders decomposition
The deterministic equivalent of the proposed stochastic program is a mixed-integer linear
programming problem with a specific structure, see figure 2.1.
For each scenario, a
subproblem is included that describes the second-stage decision associated with the
corresponding wind scenario realization for each representative day. These subproblems,
representing the real-time operation, are linked by the first-stage decision variables.
Optimization problems characterized as stochastic programs can be solved using Benders
decomposition procedure [34] [5]. Among these methods, Benders decomposition [39],
also called the L-shaped method, has become the major approach to tackle stochastic
programming problems.
This method takes advantage of the special decomposable
structure of the two-stage stochastic programming model and generates duality cuts
based on the subgradient information iteratively [32].
The Benders decomposition algorithm was first applied to mixed-integer problems
to separate integer from continuous variables [39]. Benders decomposition procedure is
also applied to stochastic two-stage linear programs, known as the L-shaped method [9]
[34].
==PAGE:55==
3.3 Application
37
Within a stochastic minimization problem, upper bounds are obtained by fixing the
first-stage variables and optimizing the second-stage decisions for each scenario. Lower
bounds are obtained by solving a master problem in the space of the first-stage variables,
which incorporates lower bounds on the expected second-stage costs.
Convergence
of the algorithm is achieved by improving the lower bound with the master problem
which adds successively dual information of the second-stage costs obtained from the
upper-bounding subproblems, which in turn potentially leads to first-stage decisions
that improve the upper bound.
Lower bounds on the second-stage costs are added
to the master problem in the form of cuts determined by the dual multipliers of the
subproblems. In the classical Benders decomposition algorithm, one cut is generated at
every iteration [38] [34].
A flowchart for Benders decomposition algorithm is shown in figure 3.3. The algorithm
terminates when the gap between upper and lower bounds becomes smaller than a
prespecified tolerance. Benders decomposition algorithm starts with an initial master
problem and then sequentially adds constraints that correspond to the optimality of the
subproblems [33].
3.3
Application
The proposed generation expansion problem with unit commitment constraints is a
promising candidate for decomposition, since the structure is decomposable due to
complicating variables.The problem is easily divided either into investment decision and
operational decisions for each day or into the first stage problem and a number of second
stage problems for each of the scenarios and representative days.
The generation expansion problem including unit commitment constraints involves
integer investment decisions and continuous and integer operating decisions.
It is
convenient to process these two sets of decisions separately, due to the different nature
of integer and continuous variables.
Furthermore, the second-stage problems are independent of each other and are only
linked by the choice of the first stage variables. So the only information that needs to be
passed to each of the second-stage problems is the first stage decision. Then the second
stage problems can be solved independently [33]. When fixing the first stage variables
that appear in the second stage constraints, the second stage (subproblems) become a
linear program.
So there are two options for decomposing the proposed model identified, see table
3.2. The first option separates the generation expansion decisions from the operational
decisions in the day-ahead market and the real-time, whereas the second option disjuncts
the first-stage decisions from the second-stage decisions. In the first option, only generation
expansion decisions are fixed, resulting in 2|GC| + |KC| complicating variables, i.e. as
many complicating variables to be fixed as twice the number of candidate conventional
generating units and the number of candidate wind farms. This approach leads to a
subproblem for each representative day, |D|. Thus, only a small number of variables
needs to be fixed, but at the same time, only a small number of subproblems is generated

problem [5].
Although the proposed model does not have a strictly convex solution
space, due to its integer character, Benders decomposition is still applicable: If the
number of scenarios or days is large enough, the objective function convexifies. The
objective function of an expected value stochastic programming problem convexifies as
the number of scenarios increases. The underlying reason is that the objective function
represents the expectation over a number of scenarios. Thus, as the number of scenarios
increases, the diversity of objective functions increases, while the weight of each single
scenario decreases. This causes a smoothing effect leading to the convexification of the
expected value objective function. This convexification allows the implementation of
Benders decomposition [21]. Briefly, the proposed problem is sufficiently convexified by
considering a large enough number of scenarios.

from the subproblems (second-stage) back to the master problem (first-stage) about how
to make the first-stage decision in such a way as to be optimal. This is a sensible way
to add constraints to the master problem, a less constrained version of the complete
problem, in order to arrive at sensible estimates of the solution without solving the
entire problem. The constraints are added to the problem in such a way as to improve
the chance of arriving at the optimum solution before all the constraints are added. The
optimality cuts create an outer linearization of the solution space, because these cuts
place linear lower bounds on the value of the solution. As more optimality cuts are
added these will approximate the shape of the original solution space more and more
closely, but always remain outside of it at some points until all the constraints have been
added [33].

problem. The new master problem is then solved with the added cut constraint and the
resulting first-stage decisions are again fixed [32].

The work in [32] presents a multicut version of the Benders decomposition method
for solving two-stage stochastic programming problems, with only continuous recourse
(two-stage) variables. The main idea is to add one cut per realization of uncertainty to
the master problem in each iteration. Briefly, as many Benders cuts as the number of
scenarios are added to the master problem in each iteration. While both the standard
and the multicut versions of the Benders decomposition method can solve large-scale
stochastic programming problems, significant savings in CPU time can be achieved by
using a multicut algorithm.
The standard Benders decomposition algorithm only returns one cut per iteration
to the master problem. For large-scale problem, its convergence might be slow and the
algorithm might need many iterations to reach a predefined optimality tolerance, see
figures 3.5 and 3.6. To speed up the algorithm, the dual variable vector is decomposed
by scenarios to return as many cuts as the number of scenarios at each iteration.
While the multicut method provides more cuts to support the recourse function from
below and most likely reduces the number of iterations, more variables and constraints
are introduced in the master problem in each iteration, which potentially slows the
computation down by making the master problem quickly more complex [32] [38]. On
the one hand, the single cut method tends to have information loss due to the aggregation
of all dual information of all scenarios into one optimality cut in the master problem
at each iteration. On the other hand, the multicut method uses all dual information
per scenario and places an optimality cut for each scenario in the master problem. [42].
Using dual information from all scenarios sends more information than a single cut to
the master problem and therefore, fewer iterations are needed. This property enables the

of iterations has an immediate consequence on the size of the first-stage problems to be

the convergence rate, i.e. improvements in the objective function per iteration, and the
size of the master problem. Adding more constraints per iteration allows more freedom
in approximating subproblem cost at each scenario since fewer scenarios are aggregated
together. However, there is a trade-off since adding many constraints means the master
problem grows rapidly in size and takes longer to solve. Thus, having a great influence
on the performance of Benders decomposition [9] [43]. In general, the unicut algorithm

4.4
Impact of minimum production requirements
The minimum output level constraints are relaxed by setting the left-hand sides of the
subproblem equations (3.2e) and (3.2f), as well as of the master problem equations
(3.10b) and (3.10s) to zero. This relaxation leads to the correct investment decisions,
but underestimates the total expected cost of the system. Furthermore, this version
leads to a reduction of expected reserve deployment, wind spillage and load shedding
due to the increased flexibility assumed to be provided by generating units producing
below the minimum output level.
On the other hand, only enforcing production limits, while neglecting start up
cost and ramp rates, leads to an underestimation of capacity investments while also
underestimating the total cost and neglecting the necessity for wind curtailment, when
assuming generating units not restricted by ramp rate limits
4.5
Impact of ramp rate limits
Omitting the subproblem equations (3.2g), (3.2h), (3.2i), and (3.2j), as well as the
master problem equations (3.10e), (3.10f), (3.10g), and (3.10h) leads to an error in
generation expansion decisions of conventional and renewable candidates. Furthermore
the deployment of reserves in the real-time operations increases while load shedding is
reduced and wind spillage is assumed to be unnecessary. All generating units are assumed
to be extremely flexible producers, which is a misrepresentation. This misrepresentation
results in schedules for the generating units which are infeasible working conditions of
generators. The generating units will not be able to adapt as flexibly as expected in the
simplified model.
On the contrary, only considering ramp limits, while omitting start-up cost and
production limits, shows no error in investment decisions. Again, the expected cost
is underestimated, mainly due to ignoring the costs incurred by starting up units and
ignoring minimum output levels of all generating units. Less wind curtailment is deployed
in real-time operations. Nonetheless, this result implies that the ramp rate limit constraints
are the most important unit commitment constraints in order to arrive at correct capacity
investment decisions.
4.6
Summary
Taken together, the results from the case study show that individual unit commitment
constraints impact the optimal generation portfolio mix. Moreover, neglecting these
constraints will lead to faulty investment decisions.
Within this case study, ramp rate limitations are shown to have the most impact
on proper generation expansion planning, while start-up cost and minimum production
requirements seem to be less influential.
One needs to note, that the input data may be responsible for this assessment. In a
system of generators with relatively higher start-up costs, these constraints may become

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
5.1
Concluding remarks
Many of the most commonly used generation planning models have been formulated in
a way that neglects the chronological sequence of demand and the mixed integer nature
of generating units.
The introduction of variable renewable power has consequences
for the effectiveness of existing generation planning models. Where unit commitment
characteristics are not accounted for in the models, changes in how units are committed
and dispatched cannot be anticipated, leading to portfolios unsuited for variability.
This thesis seeks to determine the relative importance of utilizing unit commitment
in the planning of generation on systems with variability from wind power production,
providing critical information to decision-makers and ensuring truly rectified recommen-
dations relating to the capacity expansion planning.
The illustrated case study demonstrates that the incorporation of unit commitment
constraints affect the investment decisions for an optimal future generation capacity
mix. Within the case study, ramp rate limits have the biggest impact on the generation
expansion decisions, whereas minimum output levels and start up costs are less influential
factors.
The generation expansion planning model with incorporated unit commitment con-
straints introduced in this thesis, can be applied to a variety of power systems to
assist in the planning of optimal generation mix including renewable power providers.
The provided tool is able to analyze the impact of the operational constraints for all
generating units.
The outcomes are highly system specific.
The importance of the
individual unit commitment constraints depends on the specific characteristics of the
examined power system.
The computational burden of the proposed stochastic mixed-integer linear model is
found to be high and perhaps excessive for many applications. The applied and validated
Benders decomposition helps reducing the computational cost for bigger models, while
still containing the operational details.
The unit commitment constraints, that are regarded and analyzed for the impact on
the generation expansion decision, include start up costs, minimum production limits,
and hour-to-hour ramp rate limits. Other unit commitment constraints which can be
analyzed for future analysis include for example minimum up and down times, or fast
starting units. Moreover, the impact of active power losses, maintenance cost and fixed
cost may be analyzed further on.

5.2
Outlook
Not only additional operational details can be incorporated into the model, but also
the joint generation and transmission expansion planning problem may be the topic of
future research. A dynamic investment approach can also be considered. However, the
trade-off between computational effort and more precise representation of more complex
models needs to be balanced.
Another method for convergence improvement in Benders decomposition is the adaptive
multicut, see [33] [9] [32]. The adaptive version of Benders multicut is based on dynamically
changing the number of cuts added to the master problem as the computations progress.
An adaptive approach to adding Benders cuts may enhance the solution time and storage
requirements of the decomposed model, potentially leading to improved convergence
rate and reduced computation time. Furthermore, old and inactive cuts can be removed
leading to shorter solution times on many problems, see [43].
