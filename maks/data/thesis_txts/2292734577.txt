Abstract
Firstly, the theory of the tight-binding method and its connection with the ﬁnite-diﬀerence discretiza-
tion of the Hamiltonian is detailed followed by a description of Green’s functions and their connection
with the density of states.
Following this, oscillations in clamped, pre-tensioned membranes are described.
Then the electronic properties of such a membrane made from graphene are elucidated along with the
properties of plasmons in such membranes.
Secondly, numerical simulations of atomic chains and grids using the ﬁnite-diﬀerence method are
presented and benchmarked against known analytical results such as the quantum well and harmonic
oscillator.
The last part of the thesis presents numerical tight-binding simulations of oscillating graphene mem-
branes in the shape of circular disks and hexagons.
The distribution of strain in the membranes is visualized and quantiﬁed, and found to be quite low
on average (3.6 %), supporting the use of a simple oscillating membrane model.
Then the electronic eigenstates are found from the Hamiltonian and plotted on the graphene mem-
brane. The states are similar to the acoustic eigenstates, as expected.
Next, the density of states and local density of states are plotted and the appearance of zigzag edge
states at 0 eV and a region of linear dispersion around 0 eV (Dirac cone) conﬁrms the simulations.
Finally, the dispersion of plasmons in the oscillating membranes is shown. It is found that the plas-
mons exhibit highly dispersive behavior, in some cases shifting as much as 0.2 eV as the membrane
deforms. An approximate power law dependence of the dispersive shift vs. amplitude is found for
small deformations.

Introduction
A little over half a century ago a revolution happened in electronics. The technology went within
a relatively short period from consisting of large, messy, point-to-point wired systems with vacuum
tubes to integrated circuits (ICs) with transistors; ﬂat silicon wafers with all components soldered
onto the board. This revolution ultimately enabled the world of consumer electronics we know today,
hand-held devices, laptops etc.
For light to really make its way into our everyday, consumer electronics it has to undergo a similar
revolution. Today photonics is primarily coupled with the world of electronics in the form of optical
ﬁbers (for example across the bottom of the atlantic ocean, with erbium-doped ampliﬁers along the
way to keep the signal alive), but eﬀorts are being made to make optical analogues of many of the
electronic components and to integrate them all on a chip. In some cases these photonic components
are predicted to far outperform conventional electronics and also to enable completely new phenomena
[1].
In this quest, graphene is foreseen to play a prominent role, due both to its ﬂatness and crystal
structure (a monolayer of graphene is a true 2D material with a hexagonal lattice structure) and its
electronic moldability - graphene exhibits highly tunable electronic properties (the addition of just
a few dopant electrons can make a noticeable impact on the fermi level [2]), which in turn makes
it possible to tune the interaction between graphene and light. For example, due to Pauli blocking,
photons of energy less than 2ϵF are not absorbed [3], so in principle absorption of photons can be
turned on and oﬀby electrostatic gating.
Especially interesting is the coupling of light to graphene in the form of plasmons - collective os-
cillations of charge. Plasmons in graphene provide a means of highly conﬁning light to nanoscale
dimensions at THz frequencies [4].
Recently, the idea that graphene plasmons could be controlled not with doping or gating as is most
often done [5] but instead via acoustic vibrations which could utilize the high degree of control
exhibited in graphene nano-electromechanical systems (NEMS) [6] has been investigated theoretically
(within classical electrodynamics) [7] and experimentally ([8] accepted by Nature Communications).
The present thesis is an extension of the work in [7] wherein plasmons in circular graphene membranes
were calculated classically, and dispersive behavior over time during oscillations was observed. This
classical treatment ignored the atomistic details of the graphene membranes, an approach which is
justiﬁable in the limit of large membrane sizes, but which becomes gradually less justiﬁable as the
membrane size shrinks and atomistic details become important.
The open question is then: will the observed, classically described behavior survive the transition to
a quantum mechanical treatment? To answer this question a quantum mechanical treatment within
the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) has been carried out in the present work, the results of
which have been added to the abovementioned work [7] and submitted to the journal Nanoscale. The
submission is currently under review.

2.4
Acoustic oscillation modes of circular graphene membranes
A continuum elastic model of graphene is often adopted [16] and here a particularly simple idealized
drumhead model will be applied where the oscillating circular disk is treated as a pretensioned circular
membrane. Only small amplitudes will be considered where nonlinearities and bending stiﬀness of a
monolayer graphene can be ignored [17].
Standing waves on a string is a familiar concept. If the model is extended to two dimensions and
the oscillations are conﬁned to a circular domain, then the oscillatory modes of a ”drumhead”-like
membrane are obtained. Assuming the oscillations of the membrane are time harmonic and driven

relation Ωmn = jmnv/R and are Ω01/2π = 49.7 GHz, Ω02/2π = 114 GHz and Ω11/2π = 79 GHz.
Since the plasmonic resonances of interest in this thesis are mostly in the range 24-240 THz (0.1 - 1
eV), it is safe to assume that the acoustic vibrations do not aﬀect them much - they oscillate so fast
that they just see a static background.
When the graphene nanostructure has the same symmetric properties as the graphene crystal it can be
considered elastically isotropic in the small amplitude limit [19]. So in the context of small amplitude
oscillations of graphene hexagons, it is fair to consider graphene as elastically isotropic, whereas for
the circular disks, it is an approximation, and in principle it is necessary to take into account the
elastic tensor. But in the small amplitude limit where the c-c bonds generally are stretched very little,
it should be a fair approximation to assume that the shape of the membrane would not be altered
much by including elastic anisotropy so it is considered to be elastically isotropic.
A more rigorous approach could include minimizing the free energy functional at each displacement
to ﬁnd the shape of the membrane, taking the elastic tensor of graphene into account [20].
The hexagons considered are of approximately the same size as the circular disks, and the approxima-
tion is made that the hexagons have the same acoustic modes as the disks. This is of course not the
case, and in principle it would be necessary to solve Eq. (2.30) for a hexagonal domain. One argument
supporting this approximation is that the limit of n →∞for an n-gon (polygon with n sides) is a
circle. So the acoustic modes for an n-gon will converge toward the acoustic modes of a circular disk
for n →∞. So the circular disk modes are closer to the true modes of a hexagon, than the modes of
for example a pentagon or a square membrane.
2.5
Electronic modelling of graphene membranes
In this chapter the electronic properties of graphene membranes are explored.
The tight-binding
model described in Section 2.2 can also be used for graphene.
The carbon atoms in graphene are arranged in a hexagonal lattice. The pz orbitals (perpendicular to
the graphene layer) form a π-band and the hopping between neighboring pz orbitals is modelled by
the tight-binding description i.e. excited states are formed as the electrons jump between neighboring
pz orbitals. The in-plane sp2 hybridized orbitals form a σ-band and it is assumed to not contribute
signiﬁcantly to the electronic properties of graphene at the low energies considered in this thesis [21],
i.e. it is assumed to be well separated in energy from the π-band.
The out-of-plane displacement occuring as the membrane oscillates causes strain in the membrane,

some amount of hybridization will take place). However, in the present thesis it will be assumed that
the reorientation of the orbitals is of minor importance compared to the stretching of the c-c bonds
[22]. For small amplitudes where the graphene membrane can be assumed locally ﬂat, this is a good
approximation.
The stretching of the c-c bonds is implemented using a simple model, which assumes an exponential

2.6
Plasmons in graphene
For an account of the classical theory of plasmons, the reader is referred to [24] or the author’s B.Sc.
thesis [25]. Here, only a quantum mechanical description of plasmons will be touched upon.
Plasmons are collective oscillations of charge. In nanosized graphene structures, the plasmons are
basically localized density oscillations in the sea of electrons.
In 2D systems such as graphene, the resonant frequency of the plasmons scales as the square-root of
the momentum ℏk [26] such that ω ∝
√
k. Using the phenomenological replacement k ∝1/L where L
is a characteristic size of the nanostructure (for a circular disk, it would be the diameter of the disk)
gives ω ∝1/L. So as the characteristic size of a graphene nanostructure is increased, the resonant
plasmon frequency redshifts.
There is a momentum mismatch between the incoming light and the graphene plasmons, as shown
in Fig. 2.4. There exists several schemes for overcoming this mismatch (including the Kretschmann
scheme and the grating coupling scheme [27]) but in the case of a subwavelength-sized graphene
nanostructure the coupling is mediated via the small size of the structure itself - it lends momentum
on the order of the inverse characteristic length scale 1/L (L being the diameter in the case of a
circular disk) to the light allowing coupling from free space to the graphene sample. It can actually
couple a wide range of wavelengths since its spatial fourier transform confers signiﬁcant momentum
contributions ∆k up to 2π/L [28] (consider the small sample as a defect/deltafunction, the Fourier
transform of which is composed of an inﬁnite number of sine waves [29]).
In extended unpatterned graphene, the coupling can also be facilitated by surface acoustic waves
forming a diﬀraction pattern which acts as a grating and provides the momentum required to couple
to the plasmonic modes [30, 31].
The ﬁrst step in ﬁnding the plasmonic resonances is to calculate the polarizability matrix (non-
interacting density response function) within the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) where only
electron-hole pairs are taken into consideration [21]. The RPA was developed in the 1950’s during
studies of screening and the collective behavior of electrons and it accounts for weak screening and is

with the incoming wave and thus the phase diﬀerence depends on the position of the electron. But
the position of the electron is generally random, so the out-of-phase response is considered to average
out when a large number of electrons is considered [33].

Simulations of graphene
In this chapter the electronic properties of graphene membranes shaped as hexagons and disks are
investigated. Focus is especially on the behavior of plasmons in such membranes.
The tight-binding model (Section 2.2) is implemented for graphene by giving all the c atoms coordi-
nates corresponding to arrangement in a hexagonal lattice and then computing the distance between
them to determine whether they are nearest neighbours and thus should have a non-zero hopping
matrix element. The method outlined in Section 2.5 has been employed to take into account the
eﬀect of out-of-plane bending on the electronic properties of the graphene membrane.
4.1
Reﬂections on experimental realisability
It is not the main focus of this thesis to engage in discussions on the experimental realisability of the
setup described herein but a few short comments will be made in the following section.
To excite the acoustic modes in the membrane a laser could be used. Speciﬁcally, the frequency of
a pulsed laser could be modulated to a higher frequency and then used to excite the membrane into
an acoustic mode. A laser Doppler vibrometer (LDV) could then be used to probe the mechanical
oscillation modes, while a measurement of the optical scattering would give information about the
plasmonic modes [7].
Exciting the asymmetric (11 etc.) modes would require some asymmetric method of excitation and
might prove somewhat diﬃcult to carry out. Also, the 11 mode has more abrupt changes in curvature
(compared to the 01 and 02 modes) which might confer large perturbations on the electronic wave-
functions, and only small oscillation amplitudes are possible before exceeding the 20 % strain limit
locally.
The 01, 02, 03 acoustic modes are more easily excitable, since they are rotationally symmetric (i.e.
the resulting contour could actually be generated by a surface of revolution). The 01 mode is akin to
a bubble which has already been investigated [43] (though not in the context of plasmons). It is also
more likely to be examined in other contexts, since it is also similar to a suspended layer with a load
on it. Since the 02 mode is rotationally symmetric and the separation in energy is larger for lower
order modes, it was therefore, in the context of oscillating membranes, deemed more interesting to
investigate the 02 mode than for example the 03, 04 etc. modes.
The disks and hexagons naturally would have to be part of a larger system, and as such the truncation
at the edges used in the present simulations is not completely realistic. The biggest issue in not
including the surrounding system in the simulations is that edge states can arise and blur the picture
(another issue is that the boundaries are fully reﬂective). Edge states appear everywhere a graphene
sheet is truncated with zigzag edges, whereas edges with an armchair conﬁguration suppress the edge
states [44]. The zigzag and armchair edges have been illustrated in Fig. 4.1 for a graphene nanoribbon.
For this reason a graphene hexagon with only armchair edges has also been simulated in order to
suppress the formation of edge states, and thus give a result more realistic for the situation where the
graphene device is included as part of a larger system. Additionally, since the armchair edges do not

a graphene sheet in between two slabs of SiO2, and subsequently etching out a circular (or hexagonal)

4.2
Strain in an oscillating graphene membrane
It is natural to question whether or not the oscillating membrane model described in Section 2.4 is
applicable to a real-life graphene membrane. As mentioned it for example doesn’t take into account
bending stress and the nonlinear elastic response of the material, and the calculations have there-
fore been restricted to small amplitude oscillations. An often quoted ﬁgure for the stretchability of
graphene is 20 % [46], so the simulations were stopped when any one of the c-c bonds reached that
limit.
In Fig. 4.2 the amount by which each c-c bond in the structure has been stretched is shown in a
histogram. Clearly, most of the bonds are stretched only by a few percent and the average stretching
in both cases is just 3.6 % justifying the use of the simple oscillating membrane model.
In Fig. 4.3 the spatial distribution of the stretch is shown. The stretching primarily occurs in a ring
around the central hill, leaving the top of the hill and the outer parts mostly unstretched.

4.3
Electronic eigenstates
In Fig. 4.4 the electronic eigenstates of a ﬂat graphene hexagon of sidelength 2 nm (approximately)
are shown. The distance between two opposite vertices (a sort of diameter) is approximately 4 nm.
The radius of the spheres centered at the site of each carbon atom is the amplitude squared of the
local value of the wavefunction, while the color indicates the sign (red: positive, blue: negative).
These are the electronic eigenstates, but they could just as well represent the oscillatory, acoustic
eigenstates since both involve solving a wave equation on a circular domain and thus have the same
solution, namely a Bessel function in the radial direction with some number of nodes, modulated by
a cosine in the azimuthal direction as shown in Section 2.4.
Inspection of the eigenstates show that they are indeed the solutions of the 2D waveequation on
a membrane, with for example the 11 modes (states number 2 and 3 in Fig. 4.4) having a dipole
moment, and the 21 modes (states number 4 and 5 in Fig. 4.4) having no dipole moment (resembling
a quadrupole).
The fact that there are degenerate pairs stems from the high symmetry of the hexagons, as described
by group theory [13] which also dictates that the states can exhibit maximally a 2-fold degeneracy.
States 7 and 8 in the ﬂat case are not degenerate although they are very similar. It seems that state 8
cannot be created from superpositions of rotations of state 7, and vice versa. Therefore, they are not

by 60◦and 120◦respectively would give state 3).

ﬁnite DOS at 0 eV, and as seen in the LDOS (Fig. 4.9b) this is again due to states at the corners of
the hexagon. Upon examining the LDOS plots it seems quite clear that at higher doping levels, the

more electrons excited into the conduction band and they can roam more freely). This trend should
also have been present in the LDOS of the small hexagon Fig. 4.8b, but it was not evident due to
ﬁnite-size eﬀects.
Even for the large hexagon there are some ripples in the DOS both for the ﬂat case and at max amp,
which is due to the fact that even though it is large, it is still ﬁnite (consisting of approximately 3000
atoms). In an attempt to determine the origin of the ripples, additional simulations were conducted
where the graphene sheet was pushed beyond the 20 % stretching limit (plots not included in the
thesis). It was observed that the ripples in the DOS at max amp grew substantially larger, which
might be indicative of the generation of pseudomagnetic ﬁelds leading to Landau quantization of the
DOS [49]. It should be noted though, that the broadening parameter has been chosen unnaturally
large here (ℏ/τ = 200 meV, about a factor 10-100 too high) which as mentioned makes the plots look
much more smooth than they should. Reducing ℏ/τ would cause the appearances of large ripples
due to ﬁnite-size eﬀects. It was therefore concluded that the ﬁnite-size ﬂuctuations are too large and
blur out any features which might be due to Landau quantization. To positively say anything about
pseudomagnetic ﬁelds arising from strain in the membrane, it would therefore be necessary to plot
the DOS for a much larger structure (or, as in Section 4.1, embed it in an inﬁnite graphene sheet via
for example a patched Green’s function technique [45]). In this regard, it could also be interesting to
look at the spatial distribution of the induced pseudomagnetic ﬁeld, and compare it with the LDOS,
as was done in [43] where pseudomagnetic ﬁelds and Landau quantization was found in a gas-inﬂated
graphene bubble and good correlation with the LDOS was observed.

In Fig. 4.12 the plasmonic spectrum for a ﬂat hexagon of sidelength 2 nm has been plotted and the
plasmonic eigenstates at the peaks in the spectrum are also shown. The blue line represents the
largest plasmonic resonance and the orange line the 2nd largest. They are mostly degenerate in the
shown spectrum, as expected due to the symmetry of the hexagon.
The plasmonic eigenstates bear resemblance to the electronic eigenstates shown in Fig. 4.4 (and thus
also the acoustic eigenstates) but are still fundamentally diﬀerent. Since the membrane is clamped
at the edges the acoustic modes are forced to be zero at the edges, and thus one could say that they
generally reside closer towards the middle of the membrane (the amplitude is usually larger towards
the middle than near the edges). Similarly for the electronic eigenstates, they are also forced to be
zero at the edges due to the truncation (c.f. an inﬁnite quantum well). But the plasmonic modes can
extend into the surrounding dielectric (in the simulations this is basically vacuum since the system
is truncated at the boundaries) and that’s the reason why many of the plasmonic eigenstates seem
to live closer to the edge compared with the acoustic and electric eigenstates. This can be seen more
clearly in the supplementary material of [50] where the plasmonic modes of a ﬂat hexagon are shown
including their extension into the surrounding dielectric.
Some of the plasmonic eigenstates in Fig. 4.12b clearly have a dipole moment (for example states 1
and 1), while others exhibit higher-order moments (for example states 6 and 6 resemble a quadrupole).
The obtained modes match quite well with the results of [50], although it should be noted that their
hexagons with a sidelength of 10 nm were far larger than the ones in the present thesis, where a
sidelength of 2 nm has been used.
In Fig. 4.13 the plasmonic spectrum for a hexagon of sidelength 2 nm at max amplitude has been
plotted, along with the mode proﬁles at the peaks. The modes have shiftet substantially compared
to Fig. 4.12. For example modes 3 and 4 are no longer degenerate, but have shifted away from each
other due to the perturbation (in Fig. 4.12 they were modes 3 and 3). The quadrupole modes have
also shifted, from 6 and 6 down to 2 and 2.
According to the multipole expansion [51], the electric ﬁeld of a monopole falls oﬀas 1/r2, the dipole
ﬁeld as 1/r3, the quadrupole ﬁeld as 1/r4 etc. In the absence of free charges (monopoles), the dominant
contribution in the far-ﬁeld will thus come from the dipole term. If probing with light in the far-ﬁeld,
the incoming wave will approximately be a plane wave due to the deep subwavelength size of the
graphene membrane. The only modes which can be probed are then modes with a dipole moment (in
order to probe modes without a dipole-moment it is necessary to have some form of variation/gradient
in the light across the sample, so that it matches the desired mode). Another theoretical method
exists in which only the dipole-active modes are found by ﬁrst calculating the induced charge, then
the polarizability from applying an external electric ﬁeld, and ﬁnally the absorption cross section
[21]. If only the dipole-active modes are wanted, then this method is preferred, since all the other
modes tend to make the spectrum a bit crowded. But the other modes can be probed by for example
electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) [52], or near-ﬁeld optical methods such as scanning near-
ﬁeld microscopy (SNOM). EELS has developed a lot recently and measurements can achieve a spatial
resolution below 1 nm and energy resolution below 0.1 eV [53] and because plasmons dominate the
loss spectrum [54] it is a robust method for ﬁnding the plasmonic resonances.

breathing mode curves like a snake is probably not accurate, more than likely the case is that two
breathing modes are crossing each other. In this regard it should be noted that the two green dots at
the very top of the plot probably are not placed exactly at the top of the peaks, but rather somewhere
at the tails of the peaks (the peaks assumed to be just outside of the simulated range).
On the other hand, the degenerate x- and y-dipole modes (orange) are completely isolated, so it
should be easy to probe them. Due to their complete isolation in the spectrum, it is natural to expect
that the average contribution of those modes is equal to the total average of the modes (purple), since
there are no other modes which can contribute in the given part of the spectrum. The only reason it
does not appear so in the plot is that the orange dots are just the value at the peak manually picked
out via inspection of the mode proﬁles. The fact that the peak has a certain width is not taken into
account, since it would constitute a lot of manual work for a relatively small gain. On the other hand,
the total average (purple) does take the width of the peaks into account, so when the peak does not
shift much between consecutive times, the tail of the peak from all other times will contribute to the
peakvalue. Therefore, in the region around 0.25 eV, the average of the x- and y-dipole modes is quite
a bit below the total average, whereas in the region around 0.2 eV, where the peak moves a lot (and
the peaks from consecutive times don’t overlap much) it is almost equal to the total average.
As mentioned, this could be remedied by including the whole peak instead of just the peak value in
the orange dots. Alternatively narrowing the peaks from the beginning would also help, but this
would require a higher resolution in energy, otherwise the peaks might disappear in between sampling
points. Also, making the peaks more narrow implies lowering ℏ/τ and lowering it a lot might be
unphysical, constituting an unrealistically low decay rate at the considered doping level.
Quite a few modes which were outside the plotted range of the spectrum in the ﬂat case get redshifted
and enter the spectrum as the hexagon deforms during oscillation. Many also seem to become ampliﬁed
considerably (compare the y-axis scale in Fig. 4.12a with the one in Fig. 4.13a). Consider for example
the lowest energy peak (the dipole modes at all times) which becomes more than doubled in height.
This could be attributed to the fact that as the hexagon deforms, it squeezes the modes into less
space.
Plotted in Fig. 4.17 is the shift in energy relative to the energy at t = 0 (ﬂat) for a hexagon of sidelength
2 nm. The purple ﬁlled area marks the region between ±26 meV corresponding to the value of kBT
at room temperature. To be able to measure the shift in energy and eliminate the possibility of it
just being due to thermal ﬂuctuations, the curve thus needs to move out of the purple ﬁlled area.
Marked also is the region between ±3.2 meV (red) which is the FWHM of the peaks. For a peak to
be clearly resolvable, it has to shift more than its FWHM, but since the thermal ﬂuctuations are far
larger than the FHWM of the peaks it becomes irrelevant here. At lower temperatures the FWHM
(which is proportional to the decay rate) might become the main limiting factor.
For the most part, the shift in the x- and y-dipole modes will be unmeasurable at room temperature.
Due to thermal ﬂuctuations, they only become resolvable very close to the max amplitude.
On the other hand, the breathing mode is highly dispersive, with a peak shift of approximately 0.1
eV, so it should undoubtedly be detectable by EELS (especially if performed with a TEM [55]).
It could be interesting to determine the dependence of the shift on the amplitude, but no real con-
clusion can be drawn from the plot. From a theoretical viewpoint it seems plausible that it could
be linear for small amplitudes, since it might be possible to apply ﬁrst-order perturbation theory
to calculate small shifts. Inspecting the shape of the lines, it seems more likely that higher-order
perturbation theory might be necessary to catch the behavior at least somewhat accurately. This can
be investigated by plotting it with log-log axes which can help determine whether the dependence
is linear or higher-order. Therefore the absolute value of the shift in energy has been plotted with
log-log axes in Fig. 4.18.

5
Conclusion and outlook
Tight-binding simulations have been carried out for armchair terminated hexagons and circular disks
with mixed edges. The hexagons were included as an easy way of eliminating edge states, which would
not be present if the disk was embedded in a large (inﬁnite) graphene sheet, or in some other way
connected to a larger system.
The DOS and LDOS of the hexagons and disks was simulated, and as expected the absence of edge
states on the armchair terminated hexagons led to the absense of states at 0 eV (approximately,
since it was discovered that at each of the 6 corners of the hexagons, there are 3 atoms in a zigzag
conﬁguration, leading to a small DOS at 0 eV).
Via quantum mechanical simulations it was established that plasmons in oscillating graphene mem-
branes exhibit dispersive behavior; a clear dependence of the plasmon resonant energy on the phase
of the oscillation was observed. This conﬁrmed the results of previous classical calculations [7]. A
shift of up to .2 eV was observed for the breathing mode of a 4 nm diameter circular disk and a shift
of approximately 0.1 eV for the y-dipole whereas the x-dipole showed almost zero dispersion. The
hexagons also exhibited signiﬁcant shifts and additionally a much cleaner and more easily interpretable
spectrum due to the absense of edge states.
The shift in energy vs. amplitude was plotted on both normal axes and log-log axes. In the log-
log plot, the shift was observed to be almost linear for small amplitudes, indicating an approximate
power-law dependence.
Given additional computational resources and time it would be interesting to increase the timeresolu-
tion of the plasmon dispersion simulations, such that the plasmon modes would be more clearly spaced
and easy to follow. This would especially be helpful in the higher energy regions, where hybridization
occurs abundantly and causes a ﬂattening of the spectrum.
Implementing a fast fourier transform would decrease the simulation time signiﬁcantly allowing larger
structures to be simulated.
It could also be instructive to carry out simulations calculating the absorption cross-section [21] which
only ﬁnds the dipole-active modes, and compare with the present results.
Considering the approximate power-law dependence of the energy shift on (small) amplitudes, it could
be interesting to apply low-order perturbation theory to the membranes, in an eﬀort to get an ap-
proximate analytical solution to compare the simulations with.
Due to the similarity of the acoustic, electronic and plasmonic eigenstates it might be possible to
perform an eigenfunction expansion, which might elucidate some features of the coupling between the
modes [56].
Future investigations on oscillating graphene membranes could also entail quantum mechanical sim-
ulations of the membrane’s interaction with a nearby dipole emitter.
A couple of improvements could be made to the method used in order to make the simulations more
realistic. For example in the regime of large amplitudes it could be illuminating to introduce a more
accurate elastic model [20] for the graphene membranes which would allow for more precise modelling
of the shape of the membranes during the oscillations.
Including also the eﬀect of the reorientation of the orbitals via for example the pi orbital axis vectors
(POAV) method [57] would aid in making the simulation results more realistic for the out-of-plane
42
==PAGE:52==
bending the membrane undergoes during the oscillations.
It would also be more realistic to incorporate the membrane in a larger system for example via a
patched Green’s function [45] or via connecting it to leads [9] since this is the kind of environment it
would ﬁnd itself in as part of a nanoelectronic device.
Including the above-mentioned factors to make the simulations more realistic would have some impact
on the plasmonic resonances, but hopefully the eﬀect would not be big enough to alter the overall
conclusion of this thesis, namely that the plasmons exhibit a dispersive behavior as the graphene
sheet is deformed, and deformation (and oscillations) of graphene membranes thus oﬀers a means of
controlling plasmons.
