Abstract
Introduction. The production of beer results in the generation of brewer’s spent grains (BSG),
which is a highly abundant, available, and low value by-product. Current applications of BSG
are limited to animal feed or landﬁlling. Increased research has been performed to utilize this
resource including fractionation of the valuable, large protein content in BSG, which have many
industrial applications. However, no large-scale utilization of BSG has been established. The
objective of this project was to develop sustainable technologies for protein extraction from
BSG.
Materials and methods. Current strategies have been employing alkaline and enzymatic pre-
treatment in order to solubilize the protein present in BSG. Water and enzymatic pretreatment
at pH 6.25 were in this study compared to alkaline and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8. Process
conditions such as temperature (30-135 ◦C), reaction time (0.25-24 h), raw material to a solvent
ratio (2.5-6.67% w/v), and pH (pH 6.25 and 8) were studied in order to evaluate the inﬂuence
of these variables on the amount of solubilized protein.
Results. The highest amount of solubilized protein (11.67g/100g BSG) measured in the liquid
fraction was observed after 24 h alkaline pretreatment using 2.5% w/v. Alkaline pH had a pos-
itive eﬀect on solubilization of protein. Furthermore, the highest amount of solubilized amino
acids (5.2g/100g BSG) was observed after 24 h enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8 using 2.5%
w/v. The addition of proteases signiﬁcantly increased the amount of solubilized amino acids.
The lowest amount of insoluble protein (10.12g/100g BSG) measured in the solid fraction was
observed after 24 h water pretreatment at 60 ◦C using 2.5% w/v. However, only 2.76g solubilized
protein/100g BSG was measured suggesting that a relative large amount of protein was neither
present in the solid fraction nor completely solubilized.
Conclusion. This study demonstrated that 24 h water pretreatment at 60 ◦C and 2.5% w/v is a
potential sustainable technology, that could extract protein from BSG. The amount of insoluble
protein fraction after this pretreatment was equal to non-sustainable methods using alkaline and
enzyme. However, further studies are required to optimize the process conditions in order to
maximize the protein extraction yield.

Summary
A growing global population combined with other factors such as emerging economies in devel-
oping countries and urbanization have increased the demand for protein. Currently available
resources will not be able to meet the need, and exploration of alternative resources are there-
fore required. Extraction of protein from BSG, a lignocellulosic biomass by-products from the
brewing industry with a large protein content, could be exploited in order to solve this issue.
A number of studies have investigated alkaline and enzymatic pretreatment for extraction of
protein from BSG. This approach have been successful, however there a disadvantages related
to the use of alkaline. There is a generation of a substantial amount of waste water from this
process, and no large scale process for protein extraction from BSG has been developed using
alkaline pretreatment. This highlights the need for the development of a sustainable technology
for protein extraction from BSG.
The objective of this Master’s project was to subject BSG to water pretreatment using tempera-
tures from 30-135 ◦C, reaction time from 1-24h, and 2.5-6.67% w/v ratios. Alkaline pretreatment
and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8 and pH 6.25 were included in the study. Water pretreat-
ment is regarded as sustainable technology due to almost no environmental impact of the process
satisfying the criteria for green and sustainable processing. Water has successfully been used to
extract protein from other types of biomass, such as green tea leaves.
The highest amount of solubilized protein (11.67g/100g BSG) measured in the liquid fraction
was observed after 24h alkaline pretreatment using 2.5% w/v. Alkaline pH had a positive ef-
fect on solubilization of protein. Furthermore, the highest amount of solubilized amino acids
(5.2g/100g BSG) was observed after 24h enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8 using 2.5% w/v. The
addition of proteases signiﬁcantly increased the amount of solubilized amino acids. The lowest
amount of insoluble protein (10.12g/100g BSG) measured in the solid fraction was observed after
24h water pretreatment at 60 ◦C using 2.5% w/v. However, only 2.76g solubilized protein/100g
BSG was measured suggesting that a relative large amount of protein was neither present in the
solid fraction nor completely solubilized. It is discussed that the protein could be removed from
the solid fraction, but not completely solubilized due to linkage with carbohydrates or lignin in
the soluble fraction.
In conclusion, a sustainable technology using water at 60 ◦C for 24 hours was developed to extract
protein from BSG. However, optimization is necessary in order to obtain a amount of solubilized
protein that can meet the amount measured in the liquid fraction after enzymatic pretreatment
at pH 8.
This project contributes to future studies focusing on sustainable technologies for
protein extraction from lignocellulosic biomass such as BSG.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
A growing global population combined with other factors such as emerging economies in devel-
oping countries and urbanization have increased the demand for protein. Currently available
resources will not be able to meet the need, and exploration of alternative resources are therefore
required. Extraction of protein from lignocellulosic biomass by-products from the agricultural
industry could be exploited in order to solve this issue.
There is a number of diﬀerent applications for protein, and especially the nutritional value has
increased the demand. They function as building blocks for components in every cell, necessary
for generation of new and repair of old cells. Furthermore, proteins are transporters, enzymes,
and signals such as hormones. The daily dietary intake of protein is recommended to 0.6-0.66
g/kg body weight for an average adult, which must be supplied in order to meet metabolic
demands (WHO, 2007). In addition to the nutritional value, proteins can be used for the pro-
duction of diﬀerent compounds such as pharmaceuticals and chemicals.
Currently available resources for protein are major crops such as wheat, soybean, and maize.
However, it is estimated that in 2030 the amount of protein generated from these resources will
not be able to meet the global demand. An alternative to the traditional resources is the usage
of protein fractions from lignocellulosic biomass by-products from diﬀerent industries such as
the agricultural. Extraction of protein from by-products would provide more protein to meet
the demand from the global community, and it would also lead to optimal exploitation of the
primary raw materials by the valorization of the by-products. A challenge for processing these
types of resources is to develop technologies that can extract the protein from the by-product
with high yields and be cost-competitive. Besides aiding in solving the need for protein and
more optimal use of the world’s resources, utilization of by-products from agricultural or similar
industries as raw materials may also help to solve environmental problems, that would have
been caused if the by-products were disposed of (Sari et al., 2015).
Brewer’s spent grain (BSG) is an abundant by-product of the brewing industry with a large
protein fraction. Around 20 kg of wet BSG is generated from the production of 100 L of beer
leading to an average annual global production at approximately 39 million tonnes of BSG. Cur-
rent applications including use as feed and disposal as landﬁlling are limited, and BSG could,
therefore, be utilized as a non-traditional protein resource. In the recent years, research has
been put forward that explored BSG as a raw material rather than a waste product. However,
due to diﬃculties such as technological feasibility, environmental eﬀectiveness, and economical
aﬀordability no industrial scale utilization of BSG has been established. The success of fraction-
ation of the proteins in BSG heavily depends on engineering solutions that can combine high
yield of soluble protein with an aﬀordable and environmentally friendly process (Lynch et al.,
2016).

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Barley grains
Barley (Hordeum vulgare L.) is a major cereal crop and ranks as the fourth most important
cereal in the world after wheat, maize, and rice. The crop is produced primarily in Europe
and Russia due to the temperate climate found in these regions. However, the crop is highly
adaptable and is therefore also found in Africa and other regions with a subtropical climate. The
annual global production of barley was 136 million tonnes in 2007 (www.fao.org). The grains
are predominantly used as feed for animals, but the second largest use of the barley grains is
for the production of alcoholic beverages. Furthermore smaller amounts (<2%) are used for
production of various types of food (Akar et al., 2004; Gupta et al., 2010).
Figure 1 illustrates the composition of a barley grain. It can be divided into four main parts
(from inside-out): the embryo, the endosperm, the bran composed of the aleurone, testa and
pericarp layer, and the husk.
Figure 1: The composition of a barley grain. From the inside out: the embryo, the endosperm,
aleurone layer, testa, pericarp layer, and the husk (Niemi, 2016).
The embryo is the most important part of the grain.
It is the fertile part responsible for
generation of new seeds. The embryo is mainly composed of starch and stores protein and lipids
to initiate growth. The endosperm is located next to the embryo, and it is largest part of the
grain. The walls of the endosperm cells have a large fraction of β-glucan (70%) and a smaller
fraction of hemicellulose (20%). The function of the endosperm is to store energy in the form of
starch. When in need for energy for example during germination, the starch can be hydrolyzed
to mono- and disaccharides, primarily maltose and glucose. Surrounding the embryo and the
endosperm are multiple protective layers.
The inside layer, the bran, consist of three parts
(from inside to outside): an aleurone layer, the testa, and a pericarp layer. The aleurone layer
is composed of a two-to-three cells thick layer. The wall of an aleurone cell has a larger fraction
of hemicellulose (67%) and a smaller fraction of β-glucan (26%) and phenolic compounds. The
aleurone layer stores many proteins and is important for expression of enzymes such as β-
glucanases and proteases, that can degrade endosperm during germination. Surrounding the
aleurone layer is the testa. This layer is comprised of a large fraction of hydrophobic cutin that

layer surrounding the testa. Water is in large parts not able to diﬀuse through the pericarp

against external stress factors and deﬁnes the interior and exterior of the grain. The outermost

1.2
Generation of brewer’s spent grains
Grains are a fundamental ingredient in the production of alcoholic beverages such as beer. The
choice of grain can inﬂuence the alcoholic strength and taste of the ﬁnal product. Barley is
a well-suited grain for the production of beer due to its structure and composition, and it is,
therefore, the most common grain used for this purpose (Gupta et al., 2010).
Initially, the barley grains are converted to barley malt. Malt is a general term for grains that
have been through an incomplete germination process. To accomplish this, three steps constitute
the malting process: (1) steeping, (2) germination, and (3) kilning (Figure 2).
Figure 2: An overview of the malting process. The barley grains are steeped in water, and hereafter
transferred to another vessel, where the grains are exposed to air to begin germination. The germination
is stopped by kilning, which leads to the generation of barley malt. The ﬁgure is adapted from German
Brewers Association (2018).
The malting process starts by steeping the grains in cold water. The grains absorb water and
as a result, the moisture content is increased from approximately 12% to 45%. This activates
the production of enzymes from the protein present in the aleurone layer. Hereafter, the grains
are transferred to another vessel and exposed to an air temperature between 15 and 21 ◦C. This
promotes germination and further activation of enzymes. Especially β-glucanases, proteases,
and amylases are synthesized, and these are secreted to the endosperm, where they start to
degrade starch and protein necessary for the growth of the embryo. The germination process
is stopped by kilning. The grains are exposed to higher temperatures at approximately 40-
60 ◦C, and as a result, the moisture content is minimized to avoid contamination, inactivate the

of the endosperm. The malted grains are stored until used for brewing (Mussatto et al., 2006;
Gupta et al., 2010).
In a conventional brewing process, the ﬁrst step is the mashing of the barley malt which results
in the production of a liquid, referred to as the wort. Initially, the barley malt is mechanically
milled and transferred to a large mixing vessel, a mash tun. Water is added, and the temperature
of the process is increased from 37 to 78 ◦C (temperatures can vary between breweries). The
high temperature initiates germination, which results in the synthesis of new and activation of
previously synthesized enzymes. The enzymes catalyze the release and degradation of starch -
particular degradation of starch present in the endosperm of the grains into fermentable and
non-fermentable carbohydrates. These carbohydrates are essential in order to produce alcohol
in the following stages of the brewing process. Furthermore, soluble protein is also liberated
from the grains and degraded to smaller peptides and amino acids by proteases. At the end of
this stage, there are two parts: a liquid wort consisting of water, solubilized carbohydrates and
protein and a solid undegraded part of barley grains. The solid part is referred to as brewer’s
spent grains (BSG), and it is constituted mainly of the coverings of the grains (the bran and
the husk). The wort and BSG are separated mechanically in a lauter tun. The wort is ﬁltered
through the BSG, which lies at the bottom of the tun. In order to convert the carbohydrates
into alcohol, the wort is transferred to a fermentation tank. Yeast is added, which initiates
alcoholic fermentation (Mussatto, 2014; Lynch et al., 2016; Nigam, 2017). Figure 3 illustrates
the production of beer from barley malt with a focus on the mashing process.
Figure 3: An overview of the brewing process. Barley malt is transferred to a mash tun, where
water is added. This result in production of wort and BSG, that are seperated in a lauter tun. The wort
is transferred to a fermentation tank, and yeast is added in order to produce beer. The ﬁgure is adapted
from Lynch et al. (2016).
Thus, BSG is obtained as a by-product of the mashing process, one of the ﬁrst processes in beer
production. The use of barley malt generates a substantial amount of BSG, and it is actually
the most signiﬁcant by-product from the brewing process, comprising 85% of total by-products

1.3
Characteristics of brewer’s spent grains
BSG is a lignocellulosic biomass, which is rich in ﬁber such as cellulose, hemicellulose, and
lignin. These polymers come primarily from the undegradable coverings of the original barley
grain; the husk, pericarp layer, and testa. Furthermore, a large part of insoluble protein and
small amounts of starch originating from the endosperm are also present in BSG depending on
conditions employed during mashing (Guido and Moreira, 2017).
Even though the above mentioned is common for BSG, the biomass is an highly heterogeneous
material, and the chemical composition of diﬀerent lots of BSG may diﬀer signiﬁcantly. Varia-

1. INTRODUCTION
1.3.1
Carbohydrates
BSG is composed of a large fraction of carbohydrates, approximately 50%. This fraction is
mainly consisting of the two polysaccharides cellulose and hemicellulose. Cellulose consists of β-
(1,4)-linked glucose residues, whereas hemicellulose consists primarily of arabinoxylan, which has
a backbone of β-(1,4)-linked xylose residues, which is common in cereals. However, the xylose
residues can be substituted with arabinose.
Small amounts of starch and β-glucan are also
present in BSG. Thus, the most common monosaccharides present in BSG are xylose, glucose,
and arabinose. Hemicellulose and cellulose are derived from the cell walls of the coverings of
the barley grain. The cell walls are comprised of bundles of cellulose, so-called microﬁbrils,
which form a complex matrix with cross-links between diﬀerent microﬁbrils, arabinoxylan, and
lignin. The microﬁbrils are water-insoluble and resistant to enzymatic degradation, and they are
therefore not broken down or solubilized during the malting or mashing process. Furthermore,
arabinoxylan is somewhat able to resist degradation by enzymes and consequently composes a
large fraction of BSG. Starch, the main form of energy storage present in the endosperm and
most abundant polysaccharide in barley grains, is almost completely solubilized into wort during
the mashing process. Only a small amount of starch (2.7-2.8%) and β-glucan remain in BSG
(Vieira et al., 2014; Niemi, 2016; Lynch et al., 2016).
1.3.2
Protein
Protein is the second largest fraction, and it varies from 14.2-36.2% depending on diﬀerent
parameters such as the choice of grain type and conditions applied during malting and mashing.
The proteins are located inside the aleuronic cells and in the starchy endosperm fragments.
In malting, barley proteins are partially degraded into soluble amino acids and peptides by
endogenous barley proteases, but it has been estimated that approximately 65 to 75% of the
proteins in barley grains are retained in BSG. Since the temperature during mashing is gradually
increased from 37 ◦C to 78 ◦C, the proteins in BSG can also be somewhat denatured. Despite
degradation of some proteins and due to nearly complete solubilization of starch, the protein
content of BSG increases compared to the content in the barley grains (from 10-15% to 14.2-
36.2%) (Celus et al., 2006; Niemi, 2016).
There are four main classes of protein present in BSG. These are hordeins, glutelins, globulins,
and albumins. Hordeins are the most common protein (35-55% of the total amount of protein)
together with glutelins, whereas globulins and albumins constitute around 10%. Hordeins are
storage proteins in barley, and during mashing, disulphides bridge are formed leading to protein
aggregation, which can make protein solubilization diﬃcult. Alkaline conditions with enzymatic
degradation are often necessary in order to solubilize hordeins. There are four classes of hordeins;
A, B, C, and D. All are present in the barley grains. However, only A, B, and C are present
in BSG. Hordein D are degraded during the malting process. Glutelins are structural proteins
and also extractable under alkaline conditions. Globulins are a class of protein that can either

1.3.3
Lignin, lipids and minerals
Lignin is a poly-phenolic macromolecule with a complex structure. The amount of lignin present
in BSG varies from 11.5-19.6% (Table 1). In barley, lignin is present in the husk and pericarp.
It is a polymer, that is formed by the three monomers; p-courmaryl, coniferyl, and sinapyl
alcohol. Lignin is important for maintaining the structure of the cell and it provides rigidity to
the wall. Furthermore, it is an important factor in water transportation within the cell due to
its hydrophobic nature. Lignin can also aid in the protection from microorganisms, as ligniﬁed
cell walls are resistant to enzymatic attacks from pathogens. Lignin content is enriched after
mashing (Niemi, 2016; Lynch et al., 2016).
Smaller fractions of lipids and minerals are also found in BSG, 4.5-13.3% and approx.
1%,
respectively. The majority of lipids in barley are located in the endosperm and embryo, as their
role is to provide nutrients and energy for the generation of new seeds. The main fatty acids are
linoleic acid (55%), palmitic (22%) and oleic acid (13%). Silicon, phosphorus, and calcium are
some of the must abundant minerals in BSG (Mussatto, 2014; Niemi, 2016).

1.4
Current applications of brewer’s spent grains
With an average annual global production at 39 million tonnes wet BSG and a current market
value of 35 euro per tonne (when sold as feed), BSG is a highly abundant, available throughout
the year, low-value by-product. Current applications of BSG are very limited, and there are
only a few options for the valorization of the by-product available for breweries (Lynch et al.,
2016).
Today, the most common option for the valorization of BSG is to sell the grains as feed to
farmers, mainly cattle farmers. BSG has been used in a signiﬁcant amount as nutrition for
dairy cattle, however, urbanization of breweries have challenged this application. Farmers have
to be in close proximity to the breweries due to the high moisture and carbohydrate content,
which make BSG well suited for the growth of microorganisms. It has been estimated that
BSG should be supplied to the farmers within three days, or shorter during high-temperature
seasons, of generation. The transportation of the BSG from breweries to farmers is, therefore,
hindering this application. Another restriction is the large production of BSG which exceeds
the amount of BSG, the farmers can incorporate in a feed regime. BSG cannot alone be used
as feed due to a low energy content. In the end, the low proﬁt, risk for microbial destruction,
and a large amount of BSG produced have driven the need for other applications (Xiros and
Christakopoulos, 2012; Mussatto, 2014; Nigam, 2017).
Another current application of BSG is disposal as landﬁlling. This option is relatively simple
to perform, and it is not hindered by microbial growth.
Disposing BSG as landﬁlling has
been accepted as an environmentally friendly strategy for conversion of BSG into other useful
agricultural compounds, and it is viewed as an alternative to conventional landﬁlling.
For
composting purposes, BSG can be supplemented with another material such as poultry manure
to increase the nitrogen level. Even though it is a common practice, if left uncontrolled this
application can lead to environmental problems (Xiros and Christakopoulos, 2012; Nigam, 2017).
1.4.1
Sustainability in the brewing industry
Handling waste streams from the production of food and beverages are a challenging and urgent
problem due to environmental and societal reasons. It is expected that in the coming years a
larger number of initiatives and legislative orders will pressure the industries to focus on and
re-think the current approach related to disposal of waste streams (Garcia-Garcia et al., 2017).
For breweries, legislative and societal drivers have already impacted the processes within the
companies. A concept, the Green Brewery-concept, has been developed. This focus on reducing
the processes’ impact on the environment and shifting the energy supply towards 100% renew-
able resources. Moreover, as a part of this concept handling waste streams in a sustainable
manner could be incorporated. Breweries are already changing their strategy and employing a
sustainable approach, and investments are made in this ﬁeld. Viewing BSG as a raw material as
opposed to a waste product has also lead to increased scientiﬁc research, that focus on ﬁnding

1.5
Potential applications of brewer’s spent grains
In the recent years, many potential biotechnological applications have been explored as alter-
natives to the current applications of BSG. However, none of these applications have been
utilized on an industrial scale yet. Nevertheless, it is still promising to ﬁnd a biotechnological
application for BSG that can be transferred to a large scale. Characteristics such as chemi-
cal composition (high protein, arabinoxylan, and phenolic content), low value, abundance, and
availability throughout the year drive the scientiﬁc interest. Research in BSG valorization has
been active in the past years (see reviews Mussatto et al. (2006); Xiros and Christakopoulos
(2012); Mussatto (2014); Lynch et al. (2016); Nigam (2017)), and the following sections sum-
marize the areas where most eﬀorts have been made; a substrate for microbial products, food
application, materials, and fragmentation. Figure 4 gives an overview of these possibilities along

1.5.1
Substrate for microbial products
The carbohydrate and protein content of BSG makes it a potential substrate for the cultiva-
tion of microorganism and for the production of value-added products. Microorganism such
as fungi and bacteria have been able to utilize the BSG as a substrate and grow successfully.
Furthermore, physical properties such as particle size, water-holding capacity, and density also
allow for microbial growth from BSG. Therefore, a large number of studies have been performed
to evaluate the production of diﬀerent compounds of industrial interest from microorganisms.
These diﬀerent compounds are spanning from enzymes (α-amylases, cellulases, hemicellulases
and many other) to saccharides (xylitol and arabitol) and biofuel (bioethanol and biogas), to
mention some. Also, organic acids (lactic acid) and antioxidants have been produced from BSG
using microorganism (Mussatto et al., 2006; Xiros and Christakopoulos, 2012; Mussatto, 2014;
Nigam, 2017).
1.5.2
Food application
Incorporation of BSG into diﬀerent food products have been investigated in order to re-use the
grains. The major components of BSG are dietary ﬁbers (approx. 60%) and protein (approx.
20%) (Table 1). These are common nutritional components, but the high amounts present in
BSG result in the possibility to boost certain products with ﬁbers and protein. Furthermore,
the grains already have food-grade status. It is, therefore, a very attractive application, since it
is fairly easy to implement. Research studies have introduced BSG as a baking ﬂour supplement
in diﬀerent bread products such as bread, buns, muﬃns, and cookies, as well as directly into
snack product. Even a small bakery has utilized BSG as a constituent in their bread production.
However, there are limitations to this application. The ﬁnal product can have unwanted ﬂavor,
color, texture, and aroma that impact the acceptance from the consumer.
This limits the
application, and less than 20% of BSG can be successfully incorporated into food products
(Lynch et al., 2016).
1.5.3
Materials
Processes for integration of BSG into material such as bricks and paper have also been developed.
BSG is suitable for this application due to the high amount of ﬁber (hemicellulose, cellulose,
and lignin) and low ash content. Furthermore, BSG has been integrated into the production of
business cards and coasters. However, an increased interest in utilizing the nutritional value of
BSG has limited the scientiﬁc interest in this application (Mussatto et al., 2006).
1.5.4
Fragmentation
Lignocellulosic biomass such as BSG has been exploited by breaking down the biomass into
components such as mono- and disaccharides, proteins and phenolic compounds.

1.6
Protein extraction from brewer’s spent grains
Extraction of protein from BSG is of major interest due to the increasing global demand for pro-
tein and limited success of other applications of BSG. The protein hydrolysates can be used in
many industries, such as food, feed, pharmaceutical, and chemical. However, it can be diﬃcult
to extract protein from BSG. Conditions employed during malting and mashing can aﬀect the
structure of the protein, which can have a negative eﬀect on the solubilization of the protein.
A number of studies have since 2007 been published in relation to this subject. Chemicals have
been employed in every study to extract protein from BSG, but adding enzymes to the process
have also been applied to obtain a higher yield of solubilized protein (Xiros and Christakopoulos,
2012). Table 3 gives an overview of the studies that have been published within this research

extractions, employing alkaline and enzymatic pretreatment.
Protein extraction can be inﬂuenced by a number of process conditions such as weight-volume
ratio (% w/v), choice of solvents, the concentration of solvents (pH), temperature, and reaction
time. Even though the proteins in BSG are mainly insoluble during malting and mashing, they
can be solubilized by optimizing the mentioned process conditions. Table 3 shows that protein
can be extracted with high yields through alkaline extraction with the addition of proteases.
The location of protein within and the structure of the grains can also aﬀect the extraction of
protein from BSG. A large amount of the protein is present within the aleuronic cells, which
are somewhat intact after malting and mashing. In order to release the protein from the cells
diﬀerent mechanical and enzymatic methods have been employed. Kemppainen et al. (2016)
employed a physical method to break down the cell walls. Steam explosion is a rapid method
to destroy the cell walls without a major impact on the molecular structure of the proteins.
Furthermore, the addition of another class of enzymes, namely carbohydrates, have also been
performed by diﬀerent studies. However, the eﬀect of this is debatable, and studies have re-

1.6.1
Alkaline pretreatment
Alkaline pretreatment is a chemical method that can be used to extract protein from various
types of biomass. The method has a dual eﬀect by (1) disrupting the cell wall, and (2) extract-
ing the protein. The cell walls in BSG are constituted of cellulose, arabinoxylan, and lignin.
Alkaline is able to break down linkages between lignin strands and glycosidic bonds between the
polysaccharides. Hence, the cell walls are degraded. Hordeins and glutelins, the major protein
classes in BSG, are mainly water-insoluble, and most studies have, therefore, used alkaline pre-
treatment (Table 3).
There are a number of advantages using alkaline pretreatment. It is relatively easy to perform,
and a large number of diﬀerent alkalines are available with diﬀerent properties and price. Alka-
line pretreatment often results in a high yield of solubilized protein especially due to eﬀective
disruption of the cell walls. However, alkaline may aﬀect the properties of the protein and limit
the following application of the recovered protein. The pretreatment is often performed under
high pH conditions, that can result in molecular cross-linking and rearrangements, that can re-
sult in the formation of unusual amino acids or destroyed amino acids. Furthermore, employing
alkaline is not sustainable, since it leads to the formation of waste streams that need treatment
before disposal. The alkaline needs to be washed thoroughly from the product resulting in the
need for extra equipment and this leads to the generation of a substantial amount of waste
water. Other components in BSG are also extractable during alkaline extraction. This includes
arabinoxylan, lignin, and smaller phenolic compounds, such as ferulic and p-coumaric acids.
This could potentially challenge the recovery of the solubilized protein (Mussatto et al., 2007;
Xiros and Christakopoulos, 2012; Sari et al., 2015; Niemi, 2016).
1.6.2
Enzymatic pretreatment
Enzymatic pretreatment is a method that has been used to solubilize diﬀerent components in
biomass. The method can be employed to solubilize protein from BSG using speciﬁc enzymes,
proteases. In general, high speciﬁcity and the need for mild reaction conditions have increased
the interest of this method. Proteases are able to break down the peptide bonds between amino
acids. Degradation of these bonds are extremely slow without the presence of proteases or other
reagents, and employing this method has, therefore, been widely used to extract protein from
BSG.
Enzymatic pretreatment has many advantages and is regarded as a gentle method. In general,
there is a requirement for mild process conditions related to pH and temperature. The processes
are normally conducted at the optimal pH and temperature of the enzyme described by the
manufacturer. Mild conditions result in a minimal risk of destructing the amino acids or altering
the chemical properties of the protein hydrolysates, which allow for nutritional application of

1.6.3
Water pretreatment
As an alternative to the two above mentioned methods, water pretreatment has been investigated
for the extraction of protein. The interest in this method is driven by the disadvantages related
to the other methods. Most important, solely using water fulﬁll the criteria for a sustainable
technology. There is a limited environmental impact due to the utilization of a by-product from
another industry and no generation of toxic waste products. Furthermore, no expensive enzyme
or alkaline are necessary.
Water extraction of protein can be performed at diﬀerent temperatures.
It is often divided
into three classes: (1) below 100 ◦C, (2) subcritical (a temperature between 100 ◦C and 374.2 ◦C
under pressure), and (3) supercritical (T = 374.2 ◦C, P = 22.05 MPa). Especially sub- and
supercritical extraction are becoming a more widely used tool to process cellulosic biomass.
High temperatures can cause the peptide bonds to break between amino acids without the
assistance of for example proteases.

system, water remains a liquid, and the properties of the molecules change. One of these changes
is the disruption of hydrogen bonds and formation of clusters of concentrated molecules. These
molecules have an ionic product, which results in the formation of hydroxonium, H3O+, and
hydroxide ions, OH–. This change in structural property gives water the ability to be utilized

2. HYPOTHESES & AIM
2
Hypotheses & Aim
Due to an increasing interest in ﬁnding alternative protein resources and promising results in
regards to water pretreatment for extraction of protein from other types of biomass, the following
hypothesis is suggested: water pretreatment could be performed in order to extract protein from
BSG. Without the addition of an alkaline, the process would be viewed as a ’green’, sustainable
technology. Furthermore, the process could be cost-competitive if the use of protease is avoided.
Driven by the increasing global demand for protein and the need for sustainable methods, the
aim of this project is therefore to subject BSG to water pretreatment at temperatures between
30-135 ◦C. Optimization for the production of a high fraction of soluble protein after water
pretreatment is performed in relation to process conditions such as reaction time, temperature,
and weight-volume (% w/v) ratio.

3. THEORY BEHIND ANALYTICAL METHODS
3
Theory behind analytical methods
In order to evaluate the eﬀect of the pretreatment in regards to protein extraction, it is important
to be able to quantify the diﬀerent components such as protein, carbohydrates and lignin before
and after pretreatment. A number of diﬀerent methods can be employed for this purpose, and
the following sections describe the theory behind methods used in this study.
3.1
The RCDC™protein assay
The RCDC™(reducing agent and detergent compatible) protein assay is a colorimetric assay for
the quantiﬁcation of protein in a liquid sample. It is a sensitive assay, that can detect quantities
between 0.2-1.5 mg/mL. The assay is modiﬁed from the Lowry method ﬁrst described by (Lowry
et al., 1951). The principle is a two-step reaction, that will lead to a characteristic blue color
development. First, the formation of a cuprous-protein complex, and secondly, an oxidation-
reduction reaction with Folins reagent.
Protein reacts with cupric ions (Cu2+) at alkaline pH, which leads to the formation of a cuprous
(Cu1+)-protein-complex (Figure 5). The addition of the Folins reagent, an oxidizing reagent,
leads to the oxidation of the cuprous ions back to cupric ions. Furthermore, aromatic amino
acids such as tyrosine and tryptophan are also oxidized. Following this oxidation reaction, the
Folins reagent is reduced to the compound molybdenum blue. This color absorb light at 750 nm.
The protein concentration can be calculated from the amount of Folins reagent that is reduced
to the blue color. A larger amount of reduced Folins reagent is correlated to the amount of
cuprous (Cu1+) that is oxidized, leading back to the amount of protein present in the sample to
produce cuprous-protein-complexes.
Figure 5: Principle behind RCDC assay. Under the presence of an alkaline, cupric ions reacts with
peptide bonds, and together the ions and protein form a cuprous-protein-complex. Subsequent, Folins
reagent is reduced by reaction with the cuprous-protein-complex, which yields the color molybdenum
blue.
3.2
Phenol-sulfuric acid assay
A number of colorimetric assays have been developed to determine total carbohydrates in a
liquid sample. The phenol-sulfuric acid assay is one of the most common assay due to its sensi-

tivity and simplicity. The method was ﬁrst described by Dubois et al. (1956), and many papers
have since been published to optimize the assay. The principle behind the assay is a two-step
reaction, that leads to the development of yellow color from the reaction between carbohydrates,
phenol and sulphuric acid.
In the presence of sulfuric acid and any type of carbohydrate (mono-, di-, oligo- or poly-
saccharides) a series of dehydration reactions occur, and hereafter furan derivatives such as
furfural and hydroxy methyl furfural (HMF) are formed. By the addition of phenol, the furan
derivatives undergo condensation reactions with either phenol or themselves, and the formation
of a yellow color occur (Figure 6). The color will absorb light at 480-490 nm depending on the
composition of the carbohydrate present in the liquid sample. Samples with a high amount of
pentose such as xylose should measure absorption at 480 nm, and samples with a high amount
of hexose for example glucose should measure absorption at 490 nm (Guo et al., 2014).
Figure 6: Principle behind phenol-suluric acid assay. Carbohydrates such as xylose and glucose
react with sulfuric acid and undergo a number of dehydrations, that leads to the formation of furan
derivatives, furfural and HMF. After the addition of phenol, yellow color can be observed.
3.3
The Dumas method
Determination of chemical elements such as carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen in organic sam-
ples can be important in order to evaluate for many diﬀerent reasons. The Dumas method is
available for this determination, and it was ﬁrst described by Jean-Baptiste Dumas in 1826.
Today, automated instruments apply high temperatures exceeding 1000 ◦C in order to quantify
the diﬀerent elements simultaneously. This method has become often-used for quantiﬁcation
of nitrogen-containing protein in food and non-food samples due to advantages such as rapid
measurement and high sensitivity.
In generel, samples are transferred to a combustion tube, where the temperature is increased
up to 1200 ◦C using oxygen. During the combustion process, three important reactions occur;
(1) carbon is oxidized to carbon dioxide, (2) hydrogen is oxidized to water, and (3) nitrogen
to nitrogen oxides. The gasses are passed over to a reduction tube, where nitrogen oxides are

Advantages of the Dumas method is related to sensitivity, speed of detection and level of au-
tomation. It is relatively easy to operate the equipment and the only part that is not automated
is the sample preparation.
Furthermore, when compared to the Kjeldahl method, a similar
method for measurement of chemical elements, there is a considerable advantage in relation to
speed. For Dumas it takes a few minutes compared to 1 hour per measurement for Kjeldahl.
There is no use of toxic chemicals. However, the method is related to a high cost, and it does
not give a measure of true protein, since it measures all nitrogen present in the sample. A factor
of 6.25 have been adapted by many in order to obtain the amount of protein in the sample (Jung
et al., 2003).
3.4
Quantiﬁcation of lignin
Due to the characteristics and function of lignin in the plant cells, many ﬁnd it important to be
able to quantify this compound. A large number of methods for quantiﬁcation of lignin exist,
and one of most common methods for quantiﬁcation of lignin from solid biomass is a two-step
acid hydrolysis to determine acid-soluble lignin and acid-insoluble lignin, also referred to as
Klason lignin.

==PAGE:28==

In this method, decrystallization and partially depolymerization of polysaccharides such as cel-
lulose and hemicellulose in BSG into oligomers are obtained by the addition of an acid, often
72% sulfuric acid. Hereafter complete depolymerization are obtained by exposing the samples
to high temperatures above 100 ◦C. The remaining solid residue represents the Klason lignin,
which can be determined by gravimetric analysis. The acid-soluble lignin is quantiﬁed by the
UV absorption at 215 and 280 nm according to Goldschmidt’s equation (Golschimdt, 1971).
A disadvantage of the gravimetric analysis is the possibility that other compounds such as non-
extracted proteins and polysaccharides is measured together with the lignin. This could lead to
an overestimation of lignin present in the sample. However, furan derivatives stemming from
carbohydrates might interfere with the absorbance measured for determination of acid-insoluble
lignin leading to an underestimation. In the end, an universal method to determine lignin has
yet to be developed (Moreira-Vilar et al., 2017).
3.5
HPLC
High-performance-liquid-chromatography (HPLC) is a analytical technique that can be used to
separate, identify and quantify diﬀerent constituents in a liquid sample. The chromatographic
technique is based on the principle, that diﬀerent constituents of a sample are separated due to
diﬀerent aﬃnities for a mobile and stationary phase used during separation. The technique was
employed in this study in order to obtain the proﬁle for solubilized amino acids and carbohy-
drates.
In general, HPLC is based on a stationary phase, which is composed of one or more solid ab-
sorbent materials; often a column with small porous balls inside. A mobile phase is pumped
through the stationary phase. The mobile phase can be diﬀerent solvents depending on the
type of HPLC. The sample is automatic injected and transported through the stationary phase
by the mobile phase by applying a high pressure which allow for fast analysis. Constituents
interacts diﬀerently with the stationary phase, and they will therefore not be transported at the
same speed through the column. Those constituents, that have a stronger interaction with the
stationary phase, will exit the column later than the constituents with weaker interactions.
This method is widely used due to its sensitivity to high and low concentrations and measure-
ment of virtually all type of compounds. However, there can be interference between diﬀerent
metabolites which makes quantiﬁcation troublesome and time-consuming.

tube and freeze-dried using FreeZone 6 freezedryer (Labconco, USA). After 3 days, the solids
were collected, gravimetric analyzed and stored at −20 ◦C until further analysis.

5.7.5
Insoluble carbohydrates
Determination of insoluble carbohydrate proﬁle was carried out by modifying the procedure de-
scribed by Sluiter et al (2008). 300 mg DW BSG obtained as the solid fraction after extraction
protocol was dispersed in 3 mL (4.92 g) 12 M (72%) H2SO4. Samples were incubated at 30 ◦C,
250 rpm for 1 hour using an Innova 44 incubator-shaker (New Brunswick Scientiﬁc Co., USA).
Samples were then diluted to 1 M H2SO4 with 84 mL milliQ, and further autoclaved in an Cer-
toclav MultiControl 2 autoclave (CertoClav, Austria) using following program; (1) reach 121 ◦C,
(2) hold at 121 ◦C for 1 hour, and (3) reach 80 ◦C. After autoclavation, samples were left to cool
down slowly to room temperature. The samples were ﬁltered using a DURAN Filtering Appara-
tus vacuum assembly (Duran, USA) equipped with membrane 542 (pore size 2.7 µm, Whatman,
USA). The liquid from the sulfuric acid hydrolysis was collected for two diﬀerent analysis; (1)
determination of insoluble carbohydrate proﬁle using HPLC, and (2) determination of insoluble
lignin.
For determination of insoluble carbohydrate proﬁle, 20 mL of liquid sample obtained after sul-
furic acid hydrolysis was neutralized with the addition of 0.92g CaCO3 in order to obtain a pH
between 5.8-6.3. CaCO3 was added in small amounts due to the formation of CO2. Samples
were centrifuged for 10 min at 5000g and 4 ◦C to precipitate the salt. The supernatant was
transferred to new 15 mL tubes, and the pH was measured again to approximately 6.3-6.8. The
samples were frozen at −20 ◦C. After freezing, the samples were left to reach room temperature.
Hereafter, the samples were centrifuged using the previous conditions (10 min, 5000g, 4 ◦C).
The samples were ﬁltered using 0.2 µm syringe ﬁlters (Minisart syringe ﬁlters, Sartorius, USA),
and the pH was measured again (max. 7). The liquid was saved for HPLC analysis.
Standards containing cellobiose, glucose, xylose, galactose, arabinose, and mannose, had been
prepared in milliQ water. Furthermore, two diﬀerent 10 mL carbohydrate mix containing glu-
cose, xylose, galactose, arabinose, and mannose, with 0.348 mL 72% H2SO4 had been subjected
to the same sulfuric acid hydrolysis as the samples. The carbohydrate mix was neutralized in
the same manner as the samples using CaCO3 to reach a pH around 6.5. Approx. 1.5 mL of
standards and sample were transferred to HPLC vials. The insoluble carbohydrate proﬁle were
determined using Dionex UltiMate 3000 HPLC (temperature: 50 ◦C, mobile phase: degased
milliQ, ﬂow rate: 0.4 mL/min, injection volume: 15 µL) equipped with an Aminex HPX-87P
carbohydrate analysis column (9 µm particle size, 7.8 X 300 mm) from Bio-Rad Laboratories
(UK). See Appendix E.1 for preparation of standards and Appendix E.2 for preparation of
carbohydrate mix of this experiment.

6.1
Separation of liquid and solid fraction
In order to ﬁnd the optimal method for separation of the liquid and solid fraction after pretreat-
ment, a number of diﬀerent membranes were tested for vacuum ﬁltration, which was compared to
centrifugation. The results obtained from testing diﬀerent membranes can be found in Appendix
F and the results for comparison of centrifugation and vacuum ﬁltration are found in Appendix
G. Parameters such as ﬁltration time, recovered volume, and soluble protein was evaluated.
In the beginning of this part of the project, only M1, M2, M4, and M5 were available. After wa-
ter pretreatment, M4 resulted in the highest amount of SPY, however there were issues related
to the performance of this membrane. The membrane seemed to be blocked, and the liquid
was only ﬁltered very slowly from the solid leading to a ﬁltration time up to 1 hour or even
longer. Due to a limited number of vacuum ﬁltration assemblies, this was not acceptable. This
was also observed for the M5 membrane after water pretreatment. After alkaline pretreatment,
M1 resulted in the highest SPY compared to M2 and M4. However, the M1 membrane broke
shortly after the vacuum was applied leading to non-optimal separation. Small particles of the
solid fraction ended up in the liquid fraction after moving through the hole in the membrane.
A lower amount of recovered volume was also observed since the vacuum did not work properly
after the membrane broke. The M1 membrane is a standard membrane used for this purpose,

and it is unknown what caused the membrane to break. The M2 and M4 membranes seemed
to be blocked, similarly to the M4 and M5 membranes after water pretreatment. It was not
excepted to have these issue with membranes used for vacuum ﬁltration, and the M3 membrane
was, therefore, not included in this project until December. The M3 membrane is not commonly

Comparison of centrifugation and vacuum ﬁltration showed that the maximum SPY was ob-

capacity, and the liquid was therefore separated more eﬀectively from the solid using vacuum
ﬁltration, which could inﬂuence the result. It was diﬃcult to keep the grains in the bottom of
the tube during transfer of supernatant after centrifugation. In the end, vacuum ﬁltration was
chosen for separation of the liquid from the solid fraction.

6.7
Composition of the liquid and solid fraction after protein extraction
During pretreatment, other components in the biomass can be solubilized and therefore be
present in the liquid fraction. It is important to follow these changes in the composition of the
liquid and solid fraction in order to evaluate the eﬀect of the pretreatment on other components
than protein. To sum up on the previous sections, the determined fractions of soluble and in-
soluble protein, carbohydrate and lignin were compared to the loss and recovery of DW BSG.
Table 9 gives an overview of this.
For water pretreatment at 105, 120, and 135 ◦C, the sum of the components from the diﬀerent
analyses mostly add up to the amount of BSG solubilized and recovered. The majority of the
protein is detected in the liquid or solid independent of reaction time (comparison between sum
of SPY and IPR to the amount of protein present in NT BSG determined after Dumas equal
to 22.9g protein/100g BSG ± 4.61). More than 75% (=IPR/22.9×100%) of the protein remain
insoluble, whereas the soluble fraction is mainly composed of carbohydrates. Furthermore, the
amount of solubilized carbohydrates increases with the temperature. At water pretreatment at
105 ◦C, 40% of the soluble fraction is carbohydrates. This number increases to 65% at 120 ◦C
and more 70% at 135 ◦C. Lignin remains insoluble during water pretreatment at 105, 120, and
135 ◦C independent of reaction time.
For water pretreatment at 60 ◦C, the majority of the insoluble fraction has been determined by
individual analyses. Lignin remains insoluble during water pretreatment at this temperature in-
dependent of reaction time and w/v ratio. The amount of insoluble carbohydrates is independent
of reaction time constituting approx. 50% of the insoluble fraction. 45% (=IPR/22.9×100%)
of the protein remains insoluble after 24h. Determination of the soluble fraction proved more
diﬃcult. 50-65% of the soluble fraction is not determined by the individual analysis. Especially,
a fraction of protein remains undetermined, when comparing the amount of protein detected in
the soluble and insoluble fraction to the amount in NT BSG.
For enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8, >85% of the insoluble fraction has been determined. The
solid composition remains identical throughout 24h of pretreatment and between the two w/v
ratios at 13-17% protein, 50% carbohydrate and 24% lignin. The lignin content is increased
mainly due to solubilization of protein and carbohydrate compared to NT BSG. The soluble
fraction consists of 55-65% protein/amino acids and 40% carbohydrate for 2% w/v. For 4%
w/v, a large soluble fraction remains undetermined.
For alkaline pretreatment, the sum of the components from the diﬀerent analyses mostly add
up to the amount of BSG solubilized and recovered. Lignin remains mainly insoluble. 40-50%
(=SPY/(SPY+IPR)×100) of the protein is solubilized. The amount of soluble carbohydrates
increases with reaction time, from 15 to 20% for 2.5% w/v and 10 to 15% for 4% w/v. The
amount of solubilized amino acid is independent from reaction time, where as the highest amount
of solubilized protein is observed after 24h alkaline pretreatment using 2.5% w/v.

7. DISCUSSION
7
Discussion
With an increasing global demand for protein ﬁnding alternative resources are critical. Further-
more, optimized utilization of the world resources has lead to increased focus on re-use of waste
streams from especially the food industry. This thesis focused on developing sustainable tech-
nologies for protein extraction from BSG, an abundant, low value by-product from the brewing
industry with a high protein fraction. Current methods have focused on the extraction of protein
from BSG using alkaline pretreatment, which requires treatment of waste streams, before it can
be released into nature. The focus have been to explore alternatives to alkaline pretreatment
which could be used for protein extraction. Furthermore, pretreatment can be a very expensive
process due to addition of enzymes, and lowering the cost is sought after in order to implement
a large-scale application of BSG.
7.1
Pretreatment of BSG for protein extraction
Pretreatment was performed under a large number of diﬀerent process conditions in relation to
temperature, reaction time, w/v ratio, and pH. In the following sections, the diﬀerent process
conditions’ eﬀect on protein solubilization are discussed.
7.1.1
Inﬂuence of temperature on protein extraction
Temperature have been described as important process condition for the extraction of protein.
The majority of studies focusing on protein extraction from BSG have used a temperature be-
tween 50 and 60 ◦C. These temperatures were often applied due to the common use of enzymes
to aid in the solubilization of protein (Table 3). This project evaluated a large span of temper-
atures for water pretreatment from 30 to 135 ◦C.
Temperatures at 30 and 45 ◦C were quick to be discarded due to microbiological problems. It was
expected that it could be a problem during this project. BSG has a high moisture, carbohydrate,
and protein content, that makes the grains well-suited for growth of microorganism. It could be
very diﬃcult to control microbial growth in an industrial setting with large scale reactors. One
could add bacteriostatic agents such as sodium azide, but the main purpose of this project was to
develop sustainable technologies, hence avoid the use of chemicals. However, water pretreatment
at these low temperatures has beneﬁts related to low process cost and minimal alteration of the
properties of the protein, and the temperatures were therefore included in the study. In the
end, thorough determination of the liquid and solid fraction obtained after water pretreatment
at 30 and 45 ◦C were not performed. Too many factor limited the probability of employing these
temperatures for protein extraction in an industrial setting, even though IPR was the lowest
reported in this project. It is debatable, that the results for IPR underestimates the insoluble
protein content after water pretreatment at these low temperatures. According to the results
from determination of IPR, 79% (=IPR/22.9×100%) of the protein is solubilized during water
57

pretreatment at low temperatures, which is very eﬀective. However, the solid fraction obtained
after pretreatment was not homogenized before analyzing the protein content with the Dumas
method. Later, the importance of this homogenization will be discussed. The experiments could
have been repeated using the M3 membrane for separation, freeze-drying of solid sample, and
homogenization before analyzing insoluble protein content in solid sample. Due to diﬃculties
related to microbial growth, the decision was made not to repeat these experiments.
During water pretreatment at temperatures ≥60 ◦C, no smell of microbial growth were observed,
and it was concluded that the problems related to microbial contamination were reduced. The
largest SPY and SAAY was 2.76g/100g BSG (Figure 15) and 2.27g/100g BSG (Figure 16), re-
spectively. This level of solubilization of protein was observed after 24 h of water pretreatment
at 60 ◦C using 2.5% w/v. In general, the SPY is between 1.09-2.76g/100g BSG (Figure 15) and
SAAY between 0.43-2.27g/100g BSG (Figure 16) for water pretreatment at temperatures ≥60 ◦C.
Compared to the protein content in NT BSG, temperatures ≥60 ◦C did not solubilize the protein
fraction eﬃciently based on the sum of SPY and SAAY. The highest percentage of solubilization
is 22% (=(SPY+SAAY)/22.9×100) for water pretreatment at 60 ◦C. However, the results for
IPR showed another correlation between temperature and solubilization of protein. For high
temperatures ≥100 ◦C the minimum IPR was 14.7-15.5g/100 g BSG suggesting that these tem-
peratures did not have a positive eﬀect on protein solubilization, whereas water pretreatment at
60 ◦C and 2.5% w/v resulted in a IPR at 10.3g/100g BSG leading to 55% (=IPR/22.9×100%) of
the protein content solubilized. Even though IPR for 6.67 and 5% w/v were lower, these samples
were not homogenized before analyzing the protein content with Dumas method. The results
should be validated by repeating the method using homogenized samples. The sensitivity of the
Dumas method is discussed later. Water pretreatment at 75 ◦C did not seem to solubilize any of
the protein, since IPR was between 19.8-23g/100g BSG. There was a larger variance in IPR at
90 ◦C, and two diﬀerent methods were used for sample homogenization. The experiment should
be repeated using the same method for sample homogenization in order to conclude the eﬀect
of this temperature on protein extraction.
In the, 60 ◦C was the most optimal temperature applied during water pretreatment for solu-
bilization of protein and amino acids from BSG based on SPY, SAAY, and IPR. Employing
this temperature for protein extraction could be beneﬁcial for the potential applications of the
recovered protein. Higher temperatures might aﬀect the protein in a number of ways, which
could explain the low solubilization of protein at these temperatures. First, a high temperature
could potentially cause changes that would decrease the solubility of the protein. Furthermore,
production of rare toxic compounds such as lysioalanine are avoided at lower temperatures to-
gether with racemization, which is the change from the L-form into D-form for the amino acids.
Racemization aﬀects the nutritional value, limiting the application of the recovered protein. In-
creasing the temperature during water pretreatment up to 135 ◦C did not have a positive eﬀect
on protein or amino acid solubilization. Low temperatures at 30 and 45 ◦C should be avoided
due to the risk of microbial contamination.

Temperatures between 50-60 ◦C is commonly applied during alkaline pretreatment (Table 3), and
the inﬂuence of temperature on protein solubilization during alkaline pretreatment was there-
fore not investigated. Ammonium carbonate volatilizes at 58 ◦C. In principal, the ammonium
carbonate should decompose to NH3 and CO2 at this temperatures minimizing the use water
and time for treatment of the waste streams, since ammonium carbonate is toxic for the environ-
ment. However, this factor was not studied in this project. The level of ammonium carbonate
present in the solvent after pretreatment is therefore unknown. The pH was stable throughout
24 h of pretreatment leading to the possibility that only a low level of ammonium carbonate
were decomposed. A possibility could therefore be to investigate alkaline pretreatment with am-
monium carbonate at higher temperatures to minimize treatment of waste streams. However,
there are disadvantages combining high temperatures with alkaline pretreatment in relation to
properties of the protein. These are similar to water pretreatment at high temperatures, and
the potential application of the recovered protein is limited by this factor. The inﬂuence of
temperature on protein solubilization after enzymatic pretreatment was not investigated. Sev-
eral studies have already investigated this eﬀect and reported the most optimal temperature for
enzymatic pretreatment with Alcalase 2.4L was 60 ◦C (Table 3).
7.1.2
Inﬂuence of reaction time on protein extraction
Reaction time aﬀects protein extraction notably, and it was important to investigate this pro-
cess parameter. The majority of studies focusing on protein extraction from BSG decided for
reaction time ≤6 h (Table 3). The objective was is to ﬁnd the shortest reaction time that
would lead to the highest amount of solubilized protein. It is argued that it could be necessary
to employ longer reaction time for water pretreatment, since no aid is applied in the form of
enzymes or alkaline. It would be expected that increasing the reaction time would lead to an
increased amount solubilized of protein, until no more protein could be solubilized due to struc-
tural properties of the grains.
Water pretreatment at temperatures ≤60 ◦C were performed up to 24 h. The long reaction time
during water pretreatment with temperatures 30 and 45 ◦C was not beneﬁcial, since there was
a high risk of microbial growth. For 60 ◦C, SPY did not show a signiﬁcant increase after 6 h
leading to the possibility that no more protein was solubilized after 6 h of pretreatment (Figure
11 C). However, SAAY and IPR was solely determined after 1h and 24 h. Comparison of the two
reaction times showed a signiﬁcant increase in SAAY (Figure 16) and decrease in IPR after 24 h
(Figure 11 F). It could have been interesting to include SAAY and IPR for water pretreatment
at 60 ◦C after 6 h to determine, if the pretreatment could be stopped at this hour. This would be
beneﬁcial in order to minimize the risk of microbial growth and decrease the cost of the process.
For water pretreatment at temperatures 75 and 90 ◦C, the reaction time spanned from 1 to 6
h. SPY increased until 4 h for 75 ◦C and 6 h for 90 ◦C for 4 and 2.5% w/v, but not for 6.67
and 5% w/v (Figure 12 A and B). There was no diﬀerence in IPR between 1 and 6 h for water
pretreatment at 75 ◦C (Figure 12 C) suggesting that the majority of the protein was extracted

7.1.3
Inﬂuence of w/v ratio on protein extraction
The volume of solvent can inﬂuence protein extraction, and it was therefore investigated in
order to determine an optimal w/v ratio for protein extraction in this project. A minimum
volume of solvent is necessary in order to recovery any liquid after the pretreatment, since the
grains can take in the solvent due to the low moisture content of dried NT BSG used as starting
material. A larger volume of solvent can drive the solubilization of protein into the liquid, since
the concentration in the biomass exceeds the concentration in the solvent. However, it is not
preferable just to add an excessive amount of solvent due to increasing cost of the process.
Especially for alkaline and enzymatic pretreatment it is important to decrease the volume of
solvent when compared to water pretreatment. Furthermore, an excessive amount of solvent
can also aﬀect downstream processes, since the solubilized protein would be present in smaller
concentrations. Potential environmental issues might also be raised from an excessive use of
solvent. In the end, it was important to establish a w/v ratio optimal for the process conditions
explored in this study. This project included four diﬀerent ratios to test; 6.67, 5, 4 and 2.5%
w/v.
For water pretreatment, there is no clear trend that suggest one w/v ratio results in the highest
solubilization of protein. At temperatures ≥100 ◦C, there are no trend leading to the possibility
that the w/v ratio aﬀect the solubilization of the protein (Fiugre 13 A, B and C). 2.5% w/v
result in a higher SPY than the other ratios, but the amount of solubilized protein is low. At
lower temperatures IPR is determined from diﬀerent methods, and it is therefore not possible
to properly investigate the inﬂuence of the w/v ratio. At 75 and 90 ◦C, SPY is higher for 2.5%
w/v (Figure 12 A and B).
For alkaline pretreatment and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8, the maximum SPY was found

7.1.4
Inﬂuence of pH on protein extraction
Commonly carried out at a pH between 8-10 (Table 3), this project investigated the eﬀect of
no pH control (water pretreatment and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 6.25) and pH control at
approx. 8 (alkaline pretreatment and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8) on protein solubilization.
pH has previously been described as very important for the extraction of protein yield, because
the pH can inﬂuence the characteristics of the protein and the cell wall of the grains.
During this project, it was experienced that pH could also be used as an indicator of microbial
growth. pH increased during water pretreatment at 30 and 45 ◦C (Figure 11 D and E). This
is opposite as to what was expected.
Treimo et al. (2008) states that protein extraction of
BSG without any pH control yields a rater acidic solutions at approx. pH 5. This is due to
the cleavage of peptide bonds. Free carboxyl acid (-COOH) residues is liberated resulting in a
decrease in pH. The increased pH observed under water pretreatment at 30 and 45 ◦C could be
due to microbial contamination. At water pretreatment at 60, 75, 90, and 105 ◦C and enzymatic
pretreatment at pH 6.25 a stable pH is observed between 6.3-6.5, whereas at water pretreatment
at 120 and 135 ◦C a decrease from approx. 6.2-5.7 is observed. It is not clear why there is a
decrease in pH at these high temperatures, when the amount of solubilized protein is higher at
60 ◦C. It could be due the the length of the soluble peptides being shorter at higher tempera-
tures. This could be investigated by determination of the size of the solubilized protein.
For alkaline pretreatment and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8, the pH is stable throughout 24
h. However, in this case the pH is aﬀect by the w/v ratio. BSG notably reduced the pH after
the addition of the solvent, which was also observed by Niemi et al. (2013). The highest pH
during the process was observed for 2.5% w/v, and for SPY there was a positive correlation
between SPY and lowered the w/v ratio, indicating a positive correlation between SPY and pH.

However, this correlation was not observed for IPR.
In order to investigate the inﬂuence of pH during enzymatic pretreatment, the two pretreat-

SPY and SAAY was
signiﬁcantly higher for enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8. From the determination of IPR, there

loosening the matrix composed of cellulose microﬁbrils, hemicellulose and lignin. This would
ease the access for enzymes to cleavage the protein into smaller peptides and amino acids. This
is supported by SPY and SAAY but not IPR. The pH was also thought to decrease during the
enzymatic cleavage of the bonds between the peptides which result in the liberation of -COOH
residues. However, a drop in pH was not observed for enzymatic pretreatment at any pH. This
could be due to the concentration of hydrolyzed protein to be to low to cause any signiﬁcant
decrease in pH.
In the end, there was a signiﬁcant larger amount of solubilized protein and amino acids at a
shorter reaction time for alkaline pretreatment in comparison to water pretreatment. Further-
more, enzymatic pretreatment at pH 8 had a signiﬁcant larger amount of solubilized protein
and amino acids compared to enzymatic pretreatment at pH 6.25. This could be due to the
increased pH caused by the alkaline, which aﬀect the properties of the protein and cell walls.
However, increasing the reaction time for water pretreatment could limit the inﬂuence of pH
control on protein extraction, which makes the alkaline and thereby controlling the pH of the
process redundant.
7.2
Individual analysis of components and overall mass balance
It was important to investigate the solubilization of protein, carbohydrates and lignin, which
together represent the three most important components in BSG constituting more than 80%.
Furthermore, it was important to determine the diﬀerent components individually in both frac-
tions obtained after protein extraction, i.e. the solid and liquid fraction.
To start, it was important that composition of the BSG used in this project had been deter-
mined. Due to large variance between diﬀerent breweries (Table 1), it would not be optimal to
compare the results obtained in this study with reported values from other studies or an average.
This could inﬂuence the conclusions. Furthermore, the BSG used in this project consisted of
30% barley. The remaining is undetermined due to protection of company’s recipe.
After pretreatment with the conditions mentioned in Table 9, it was possible to account for
the majority of the protein in either the solid or liquid fraction. However, water pretreatment
at 60 ◦C after 24 h, more than 20% of the protein is unaccounted for. This could be due to
actual losses (protein remaining on membrane or ﬁltering apparatus) or sensitivity of the RCDC
assay and Dumas method. During the project, the results from Dumas were troubled by large
variance in control samples (NT BSG). The Dumas method was previously used to determine
the protein content of NT BSG used in this study (18.9g protein/100g BSG, Table 1), but it

Carbohydrates are the largest group of constituents in BSG, and they can play an important

in the cell walls protecting the protein inside. It was therefore sought to investigate the inﬂu-

pretreatment. The result showed that highest temperature explored in this project lead to the
largest solubilization of carbohydrates. Increasing the temperature resulted in a larger fraction
of carbohydrates released. However, increased solubilization of carbohydrates did not have a
positive correlation on the solubilization of protein during water pretreatment. This could be
due to the structural changes caused by high temperatures that decreases the solubilization of
protein. For alkaline pretreatment and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 6.25 and pH 8 carried out

possibility that the solubilization of protein not necessarily was correlated to the solubilization
of carbohydrates but to the pH 8 (i.e. the use of alkaline as catalyst). This should also be
noted that there is a high sensitivity related to the phenol-sulfuric acid assay, where thorough
mixing is very important for the color development, or actual losses (carbohydrates remaining

carbohydrate content was solubilized (=SCY/50.48×100%). Even though there was no positive
correlation between solubilization of carbohydrates and protein extraction at high temperatures,
there might be one in relation to lower temperatures. The remaining protein that were present in
the solid after pretreatment might remain insoluble due to the carbohydrates. It could therefore
be interesting to investigate the structure of the solid fraction after pretreatment and placement

7.3
Experimental design and limitations
The strengths of this project lie in the large number of process conditions evaluated and de-
termination of the three main components in BSG; protein, lignin and carbohydrates, in both
the solid and liquid fraction. The process conditions; temperature, reaction time, w/v ratio and
pH, were all included because they have been described as important process conditions that
can aﬀect the solubilization of protein (Sari et al., 2015). Furthermore, water pretreatment at
high temperatures exceeding 100 ◦C had not been performed using BSG before. The project
was therefore considered novel, and the result could contribute to information about the eﬀect
of subcritical water pretreatment on lignocellulosic biomass.
Limitations of the study included: 1) poor statistical strength of the determination of IP, SAA,
SC, IC, and IL; 2) variation in homogenization of samples before analyzing IP using the Dumas
method; 3) the late discovery of M3 membrane for vacuum ﬁltration; and 4) limited investigation
of the inﬂuence of reaction time under alkaline and enzymatic pretreatment.
Furthermore in the beginning of the project, the idea was to subject BSG to water pretreatment
at temperatures near 200 ◦C. It was planned to perform these experiments using an oil bath or
small scale reactor (volume = 1.8 L). However, due to unforeseen delays related to the installment
of the equipment, it was not possible to perform water extraction at these high temperatures.
It was therefore decided to perform water pretreatment at temperatures 105, 120, and 135 ◦C.
In the end, these temperatures did not result in a signiﬁcant amount of solubilized protein. A
number of factors could have inﬂuence the result. Most importantly it was not possible to apply
any mixing, shaking, etc. to the reaction, because the bottles were heated using an autoclave.
The grains would lie in the bottom of the bottle, which resulted in a lower surface area of the
grain that could react with water. If the reactor had been used, it would have been possible to
apply mixing with a motor, which might have improved the yield of soluble protein.
In order to optimize pretreatment of BSG, it is suggested to investigate the eﬀect of the pre-
treatment on the diﬀerent classes of protein found in BSG. Hordeins, glutelins, globulins, and

7.4
Comparison of previous studies extraction protein from BSG
The majority of previous studies focusing on extraction high yields of protein from BSG used
alkaline pretreatment with or without the addition of an alkaline protease. Temperatures be-
tween 40 and 60 ◦C, pH from 8 to 10, and reaction time up to 6 h is the most common process
conditions previously employed (Table 3). It can be diﬃcult to compare the results due to dif-
ferent methods and calculations used by diﬀerent studies.
The majority of the studies used Kjeldahl’s method (Celus et al., 2007; Treimo et al., 2008, 2009;
Vieira et al., 2014; Rommi et al., 2017) to determine the solubilization of protein (see section
3.3 for diﬀerences between Kjeldahl and Dumas). Coming across an advice from Treimo et al.
(2008) correlated well with experience of issues related to the Dumas method in this study: vari-
ations in particle size of samples can signiﬁcantly aﬀect the reproducibility in nitrogen analysis.
Treimo et al. (2008) was the only ones to describe any homogenization before analyzing the
insoluble protein fraction. This issue is very important, because the majority of the studies did
not measure the amount of solubilized protein. The yields were calculated as the mass protein
recovered in solid relative to mass protein in initial biomass. In this project, no homogenization
and diﬀerent methods for homogenization of samples before analyzing total protein content in
solid sample did have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the results. No homogenization lead to a lower
IPR, which might be an underestimation of the true amount. Vieira et al. (2014) measured the
soluble protein yield to approx. 7g protein/100g BSG after alkaline pretreatment. In this study,
the amount of solubilized protein after alkaline pretreatment was 11.67g protein/100g BSG after
alkaline pretreatment.
It can be diﬃcult to compare this study with previous published study. The calculations leading
to the yield of extracted protein are not always transparent, and protein in the liquid is often not
determined or data is not shown. The lowest IPR (10.12g insoluble protein/100g BSG) in this

8. FUTURE WORKS
8
Future works
In the future it could be interesting to optimize water pretreatment at 60 ◦C to increase protein
solubilization. Based on the obtained results, this condition could be promising as a sustainable
technique for protein extraction from BSG. In order to optimize the yield, the placement of
”moved” and remaining insoluble protein could be determined.
If retained inside the cells
protected by carbohydrates and lignin, a pretreatment step could be evaluated before protein
extraction with water at 60 ◦C. A physical pretreatment such as ball milling would not have a
negative environmental impact, and it could reduce the rigidity of the cellulose microﬁbrils and
thereby increasing the yield of protein in the following pretreatment.
If water pretreatment at 60 ◦C could be optimized further, the pretreatment could be part of a
bioreﬁnery strategy for utilization of BSG. The biomass is a very well-suitable by product for
this purpose due to its current low value, abundance, and composition. A bioreﬁnery concept
using BSG has multiple beneﬁts including (1) creating a valuable utilization of a waste product
from an existing process, which is cost competitive, (2) reducing the waste management related
to beer production, and (3) generation of protein to meet global food demand. The overall idea
behind the bioreﬁnery strategy is to re-use all constituents of the BSG including the protein
fraction.

9. CONCLUSION
9
Conclusion
In this thesis, diﬀerent methods for pretreatment of lignocellulosic biomass were performed in
order to solubilize the large protein fraction present in BSG. Process condition related to tem-
perature, reaction time, w/v ratio, and pH were evaluated in order to determine the inﬂuence
of these conditions on protein solubilization. The focus of the project was to develop sustain-
able methods, and water pretreatment was therefore investigated. Alkaline pretreatment and
enzymatic pretreatment at pH 6.25 and pH 8 were performed in order to compare sustainable
and non-sustainable methods for protein extraction. The following conclusions are based on the
results obtained during this project:
1. 60 ◦C was the best temperature for solubilization of protein from BSG based on IPR.
Higher temperatures did not result in an increased yield of soluble protein due to potential
changes in the structure of the protein decreasing its solubility. Low temperatures should
be avoided due to the risk of microbial contamination.
2. Long reaction time was necessary for protein extraction during 60 ◦C water pretreatment.
For alkaline pretreatment and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 6.25 and 8, it was diﬃcult to
state the most optimal reaction time, but it is suggested to be shorter than necessary for
water pretreatment. In addition, long reaction time had a positive eﬀect on solubilization
of amino acids.
3. There was no clear diﬀerence between the diﬀerent w/v ratios examined in this project.
For water pretreatment, low v/w could be used due to the concentration force between
the solid and liquid. There are no negative cost eﬀects or environmental impact related to
increasing the volume. For alkaline pretreatment and enzymatic pretreatment at pH 6.25
and pH 8, it could beneﬁcial to use a higher w/v ratio due to cost.
4. pH aﬀected protein and amino acid solubilization by decreasing reaction time. A signiﬁcant
larger amount of protein was solubilized faster for alkaline pretreatment compared to water
pretreatment.
In conclusion, 24 h water pretreatment at 60 ◦C and 2.5% w/v was found to be the best conditions
for a sustainable technology, that could extract protein from BSG. The amount of insoluble
protein fraction after this pretreatment was equal to non-sustainable methods using alkaline
and enzyme. Optimization of water pretreatment is necessary in order to increase the amount
of solubilized protein.
