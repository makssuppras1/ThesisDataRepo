interact. This happens when there is a strong coupling between the structure and the
surrounding medium. This is often the case when the structure is a thin plate. Examples
of strong coupling and weak coupling are (see ’¼ügure 1.1):
Strong coupling: Sound and vibration in a car.
Strong coupling: Diaphragm in a microphone.
Weak coupling: Noise barrier by a motorway.
Weak coupling: Concert hall.

Unfortunately, it is computational heavy to calculate structural-acoustic coupling dir-
ectly.
It is, however, possible to approximate the solution by modal expansion.
It is
straight forward for undamped systems, hence closed air cavity. For damped systems it is
possible to use modal expansion if the solution is rewritten in state-space form.
This thesis will describe a computationally cheap approximate method to calculate the
structural-acoustic coupling for an open air cavity. Hence ’¼ünd a state-space formulation
which allows to solve an eigenvalue problem (EVP) and modal expansion for an open
acoustic domain. This will be done by altering the modal expansion, for the undamped
coupled problem to also work for the damped structural-acoustic coupled problem.
The modal expansion for damped coupled problem is investigated in the following steps
1. Understanding the theory of modal expansion method for

2.1.2
Structural damping
The structural damping used in this thesis will be the viscous damping model "General
proportional damping", also known as Rayleigh damping. Viscous damping is assumed
to be proportional to velocity. Structural damping is included in the EoM by adding a
velocity part,
[M]{ ┬©D} + [C]{ ╦ÖD} + [K]{D} = {f},
(2.20)
where [C] is the damping matrix.
The most popular model of viscous damping is the general proportional damping
model. It uses a linear combination of the mass and sti’¼Ćness matrix as the viscous damping
matrix,
[C] = ╬▒[M] + ╬▓[K],
(2.21)
where ╬▒ is the mass-proportional damping coe’¼ācient and ╬▓ is the sti’¼Ćness-proportional
damping coe’¼ācient.
The general proportional damping model is popular due to mathematical convenience
when performing modal analysis. To be able to easily perform modal analysis the EoM
has to be decoupled. This is possible if the damping matrix is a linear combination of the
mass and sti’¼Ćness matrix. [4, p. 322] At lower frequencies the mass proportional term is
dominant, while at higher frequencies the sti’¼Ćness proportional term is dominant.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
This report studied the modal-superposition method, for both undamped acoustic do-
main, and damped acoustic domain. This was done with the modal-superposition method
in state-space formulation.
First the theory was described for the structural and acoustic domain separately, and
then the coupling. To try and avoid numerical troubles with singular matrices when solv-
ing the EVP, a scaling was implemented. Further work could be to study the e’¼Ćect of
di’¼Ćerent scaling methods.
Applying the direct solution in the numerical analysis, seemed to comply with the
theory. It would have been optimal to have this con’¼ürmed using a FEM program. Unfor-
tunately this was not accomplished, thus another suggestion for further work.
When applying the modal-superposition in state-space form, the numerical solution
did not agree with the direct solution, especially for closed air cavity. The reason for
this is most likely errors in the code. Again an area to study further. Even though the
implementation of the modal-superposition method failed, it illustrated the purpose of it:
computational time saving when solving.
To further study the importance of the damping and coupling terms when performing
the modal expansion in the modal-superposition method, a segregated approach was ap-
plied. This approach did not seem to work. Part of the reason for this is the coding error
for the modal-superposition method, as the segregated approach uses the same assembly
code.
