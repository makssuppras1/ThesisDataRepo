Abstract
This project shows how a practical THz detector can be constructed based on plasma creation
through THz induced cold-ﬁeld electron emission from metal antennas. The detection yields a trans-
verse 2-D image of the incident electric THz ﬁeld, which can be recorded with a commercial camera
optimised for visible wavelengths. The complete detection method has never been demonstrated
before and has the potential to reduce the cost of high-ﬁeld THz image detectors by an order of
magnitude.
The THz detection relies upon a strong THz ﬁeld enhancement in the time domain. This project
demonstrates a novel design of metal antennas that have a time domain ﬁeld enhancement factor
of 300 upon irradiation with short THz transients. The enhancement is obtained by coupling 2
split-ring resonator structures to a common capacitor-like element. The strong ﬁeld generates a
tunnel current from the metal antennas to free space where the electrons are accelerated and used
for plasma generation of noble gases.
It is found that Ar and He plasmas give strong light emission intensities through glow discharge. In
addition, they seem to decrease the eﬀect of unwanted nonlinearities in the entire system consisting
of metal antennas on a dielectric substrate. The latter is optimised for durability over time as well
as the generation of an intense light emission by using a Si wafer with a thin layer of SiO2 on top.
Metal antennas on top of the SiO2 layer can preferably be made from Au or Cu.
During the investigation of substrates and antennas, interesting data is collected that can eventu-
ally aid the understanding of THz-induced nonlinearities in some materials.
It is demonstrated that the light emission from antennas optimised for diﬀerent resonance fre-
quencies can be used to determine the relative content of diﬀerent frequencies in the incident THz
transient. Moreover, it is shown that further optimisation of the system can easily be done by re-
arranging antennas in a denser mesh on the substrate surface as well as making the distance from
antennas to camera lens shorter.

Resume
Dette projekt viser, hvordan en praktisk THz detektor kan konstrueres på grundlag af plasmaskabelse
gennem THz-induceret kold-felt elektronemission fra metalantenner. Detektionen giver et transverst
2-D billede af det indkomne elektriske THz felt, hvilket kan optages med et kommercielt kamera som
er optimeret til synlige bølgelængder. Den komplette detektionsmetode er aldrig tidligere blevet
blevet publiceret og har potentiale til at reducere prisen på THz detektorer til høje feltstyrker med
en størrelsesorden.
THz-detektionen er afhængig af en stærk THz feltforstærkning i tidsdomænet.
Dette projekt
demonstrerer et nyt design til metalantenner som har en tidslig feltforstærkningfaktor på 300 under
bestråling med korte THz transienter. Forstærkningen opnås ved at koble 2 split-rings resonatorer til
et fælles kondensator-lignende element. Det stærke felt frembringer en tunnelstrøm fra metalanten-
nerne til frit rum, hvor elektronerne accelereres og anvendes til plasmageneration i ædelgasser.
Det konstateres, at Ar og He plasma udsender det kraftigst observerede lys gennem rekombina-
tionsmekanismer i plasmaet. Desuden synes de at mindske eﬀekten af uønskede ulineariteter i hele
systemet bestående af metalantenner på et dielektrisk substrat.
Sidstnævnte er optimeret til at
kunne modstå det høje elektriske felt over tid samt at bidrage til maksimal lysemission. Dette gøres
ved brug af en Si-wafer med et tyndt lag af SiO2 på toppen. Metalantenner på toppen af SiO2-laget
kan fortrinsvis være fremstillet af Au eller Cu.
Gennem undersøgelserne af substrater og antenner er interessant data indsamlet som i sidste ende
kan hjælpe forståelsen af THz-inducerede ulineariteter i nogle materialer.
I projektet påvises det, at lysemissionen fra antenner optimeret til forskellige resonansfrekvenser
kan anvendes til at bestemme det relative indhold af forskellige frekvenser i det indkomne THz signal.
Endvidere vises det, at yderligere optimering af systemet let kan gøres ved at arrangere antennerne
i et tættere mønster på substratoverﬂaden. Ligeledes kan en bedre virkningsgrad opnås ved at gøre
afstanden fra antenner til kameralinsen kortere.

Chapter 2
Introduction
Terahertz (THz) waves1 is a term widely used to describe the gap in the electromagnetic spectrum
that resides between the electronic and photonic regions. Human beings experience it as a part of
the warmth they feel from very-far-infrared (FIR) radiation, because THz is generated from almost
any natural source of heat. Due to technical challenges with controlled generation and detection of
THz signals, these waves have remained unexplored for decades longer than e.g. IR-, visible and UV
light. However, during the last two decades, THz technology has been the subject of a heavy increase
in attention from both scientiﬁc and business communities. It is aided by new advancements in the
ﬁeld of optical science that moves down in frequency from the IR regime as well as novel microwave
developments, which move up in frequency [1]. The outcome of this project is a contribution to the
general development of THz detectors, which is based on principles from both optics and microwaves.
The developed detector has the potential to bring down market price for ultra high THz ﬁeld imaging
devices2 by more than 95%.
This project is presented in a very detailed way and is aimed for an audience with limited knowledge
in the ﬁeld of THz technology. Readers that already have ﬁeld-speciﬁc knowledge will therefore want
to skip some sections. Hence, chapters that contain a lot of background information will initially
give information on which of its sections that include the central results.
This project contains two introductions: A general introduction to THz technology in terms
of science, business and applications. A second introduction will go through the aforementioned
categories again, but with emphasis on the developed THz detector. Assessments of the THz market
and speciﬁc business opportunities are by no means exhaustive. They constitute only the ﬁrst steps
towards a possible commercialization. Readers interested in only the scientiﬁc part of this project
may read Sec. 2.4 and then skip to the next chapter.

2.2
Introduction to THz science
The name terahertz originates from the wave frequencies it encapsulates. Although no standard
deﬁnition of the exact THz frequency band has yet been established, a commonly used deﬁnition is
0.1 THz (λ = 3mm) to about 10 THz (λ = 30µm) [1]. In popular science terms, this is the transition
region from microwaves to far infrared waves - see Fig. 2.3. The upper limit of the THz band is
largely determined by the atmospheric absorption of the speciﬁc application in question [4].
THz possesses a range of special properties. Due to a very low photon energy, it is considered
non-ionizing and therefore harmless to living cells under normal irradiation conditions.
Due to
their high conductivity, metals cannot be penetrated by THz.
While metals, due to their high
conductivity, cannot be penetrated by THz, many other materials that are opaque to IR-, visible-
and UV-light are penetrable by THz. These include e.g. clothing, paper, cardboard, plastics, paint,
dielectrics and insulators [5], [6]. Consequently, THz is used for noninvasive inspection in a growing
number of places. One example is security inspection of cargo in some airport scanners3. Antique
paintings, which are sometimes painted on top of several other paintings, can be inspected by looking
at the THz reﬂection from each layer of paint [7]–[9]. Many molecular systems have chemical and
structural "ﬁngerprints" in the THz region. They arise due to vibrational and rotational modes

Quantitatively, THz marks the 2 orders of magnitude large spectral transition from the electronics
regime to the optical regime. One of the main challenges working with THz is therefore to make
controlled use of the properties that are associated with each regime.
In the optical regime, photon energies are much larger than the thermal energy at room tem-
perature, kBT = 25meV. This means that optical components can manipulate and detect photons
by using clearly deﬁned energy transitions between quantum mechanical states upon photon im-
pingement.
Think e.g.
of an incident photon that knocks an electron across the band-gap of a
semiconductor. At energies around kBT and below, the energy levels of the relevant states in almost
all solids are lying so closely that it becomes impossible to distinguish them from each other. Hence,
the eﬀect of individual, incident photons with energies kBT cannot be told apart from e.g. small
mechanical deformations of the components or the like [11]. Thus, in order to measure incident
photons on a surface at THz frequencies, the photon ﬂux needs to be substantial. Even then, special
precautions must be taken to enhance the S/N ratio. Bolometers are examples of equipment that
overcome this issue in various ways, but at a price in the range of $25,000 and up for an instrument
[12].

Due to the indistinguishable energy states corresponding to sub-THz photon energies, the quantum
mechanical nature of light cannot be exploited and is therefore usually not accounted for in the system
designs made by e.g. electrical engineers [13]. In this regime of low photon energies, light is called
radio- and microwaves, and it can be captured and manipulated through the use of e.g. antennas.
In this project, both the electrical (classical) and the optical (quantum mechanical) properties of
a system with THz as the main driver will be exploited.

2.4
The underlying patent and work principles
This project is based on the international patent WO2015028029A1 [2], issued on August 29, 2014.
The patent inventors are Krzysztof Iwaszczuk and Peter Uhd Jepsen, and the patent applicant is
the Technical University of Denmark. The patent is entitled "Detection of terahertz radiation" and
it is schematically depicted in Fig. 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Schematic outline of the underlying patent. Adopted from [2, Fig. 1].
The complete system (10) consists of a possible substrate (35) and a metallic layer (30), which is
illuminated by an incident THz wave (20). Upon irradiation, the metallic structure emits an optical
signal (40), which can be detected by an optical detector (50).
The metallic layer might be free-standing or placed on a substrate with some non-vanishing trans-
parency to the THz wave. It has been shown experimentally that gold has advantageous properties
when it comes to high photo-luminescence and very low oxidation over time [1], [3], [21], [22]. In
essence, the metallic layer consists of an array of structures smaller than the wavelength of the in-
cident THz wave. The array is furthermore periodic in two dimensions. Given that the interaction
between THz wave and metal is strong, the array will be able to squeeze the THz wave down to
sub-wavelength dimensions at certain points in space and time. These periodic arrays of artiﬁcially
engineered structures that obtain properties not found in any material in nature are generally called
meta-materials. Hence, this patent describes the use of a THz meta-material to work properly.
The emission of an optical signal builds on the realization that a strong THz pulse incident on
the metallic structures can lead to cold-ﬁeld electron emission and subsequent generation of a light-
emissive plasma.
In [3], it is shown that the emission is described well if one assumes that the
incident THz ﬁeld reduces the electric potential close to the metallic surface to allow for an eﬀective
tunnelling of electrons out of the metal. The tunnelling eﬀect is highly nonlinear with the electric
ﬁeld strength [23]. As a consequence, this detection method is sensitive to the peak electric ﬁeld
instead of the average photon ﬂux measured in current THz imaging devices. A note of caution is
however that the strong THz ﬁeld itself might lead to destructive eﬀects such as migration of metal
atoms away from the metallic structure [24], [25]. Due to the requirement of strong ﬁelds, only a
pulsed THz system will be considered in this project unless stated otherwise.
The electron tunnelling process happens over a time scale which is comparable to the oscillation

The business opportunity
The THz detector put forward here builds on novel principles, but has so far only been demonstrated
for ultra high THz peak ﬁeld strengths (UH-THz) on the order of hundreds of kV/cm. An estimate
from the author and supervisors is that there are between 50 and 100 research groups worldwide
that are currently working with such high THz ﬁelds. An estimate for the same metric in private
industry remains unknown.
A few hundred customers - at best - worldwide does not depict the business opportunity adequately
due to ongoing market growth in various THz application ﬁelds. First of all, the developed detector
could become a vital part of many THz imaging systems, albeit not for inspection of human beings
due to the high ﬁelds. For instance, some sources have suggested that focused, UH-THz might have
the ability to untangle double-stranded DNA [26], [27] and cause unpredictable cell mutations over
time. While the use of UH-THz on living cells in situ is currently not feasible, UH-THz imaging
of biological samples in vitro - like skin cancer biopsies - could be realised well by the use of the
developed detector. Other types of biomedical imaging with active systems should be considered as
well.
As for non-invasive inspection in the transportation sector, the spectroscopic use of THz for
detection of explosives and illegal drugs currently catch the most attention as described in Sec.
2.2.
The knowledge about chemical characterization of mentioned substances through UH-THz
imaging is currently limited.
The reason here-fore is that UH-THz sources and imaging devices
have historically been disproportionately more expensive than equipment for spectroscopic testing
at much shorter wavelengths compared to the obtainable information. The UH-THz imaging detector
demonstrated here could potentially bring down the cost on the detector side from $25,000-$50,000 to
<$1000, hereby enabling a variety of new applications. With an expected drop in price for UH-THz
sources throughout the next decade, one must expect research in UH-THz imaging to accelerate and
novel uses to emerge. The 2014-2019 projection in Tbl. 2.2 for science and research reﬂects such
expectations8.

3.1
THz generation
One of the ways to generate THz radiation is through the second-order nonlinear process called
optical rectiﬁcation1 [29], [30]. The eﬀect is present in media that are non-centrosymmetric i.e. they
have no center of inversion [31]. Such media include GaP, GaAs, ZnTe, CdTe and LiNbO3. For the
setup in this project, lithium niobate (LiNbO3) was used. Figs. 3.1 and 3.2 depict LiNbO3 from two
diﬀerent angles to show its rather complex crystal structure, which plays a vital role in its optical
properties.
Non-centrosymmetric crystals are simply crystals in which the chemical bonds between the involved
atoms possess certain asymmetries. This leads to stationary solutions for the Schrödinger equation
where some electrons see a parabolic potential, but only very close to their equilibrium point. When
a weak light ﬁeld is incident on the crystal, electrons start to oscillate harmonically in the parabolic
potential. This is denoted the linear regime, since the induced polarisation PL of the crystal2 can
be approximated closely with a power series expansion to ﬁrst order in the incident electric ﬁeld

3.2
THz detection
One of the standard methods for detecting THz is through electro-optic (EO) sampling [33]. This
is an optoelectronic technique, which allows one to sample the time spectrum of a signal much
faster than using any pure, electronic technique. The obvious advantage to recording the direct time
spectrum of a signal is that the Fourier transform reveals both frequency and phase information.
Recordings of the frequency content of an incident THz pulse might be easier through resonant
structures, but it can be hard to directly determine the relative phases of many frequencies of a
broadband signal - like a THz transient - in order to obtain the spectrum in time.
EO sampling relies on the Pockel’s eﬀect, which states that the birefringence of some speciﬁc
media can be modulated by an externally applied, electric ﬁeld. More speciﬁcally, the Pockel’s eﬀect
is the linear electro-optic eﬀect, such that the modulation of the refractive index is proportional to
the applied ﬁeld. Many of the nonlinear crystals mentioned in Sec. 3.1 possess high electro-optic
coeﬃcients, and crystals such as ZnTe and GaP are widely used [30].
The principle behind EO-sampling is shown in Fig. 3.5. An EO crystal, in this case GaP, is placed
in the optical path at the point in space where the focus from both the NIR and THz pulse overlap.
In this project, the NIR was split oﬀbefore reaching the LiNbO3 crystal. It was subsequently led
to meet the THz pulse at the position of the EO crystal.
When the THz passes through the GaP crystal, it induces a birefringence that is proportional to
its ﬁeld value. The NIR is conﬁgured in a linearly polarized state, and if it passes through the GaP
crystal at the same time at the THz, it will turn into an elliptic state. The ellipticity can be tuned by
varying the time diﬀerence between the THz and NIR. A second, important property of EO crystals
is that the phase velocity of the THz and the group velocity of the NIR must be approximately the

3.3.1
Substrate choices
All of the above numbers are for linear material responses to the incoming THz ﬁeld. It was reported
in [38] that ﬁeld strengths of >250 kV/cm, which are comparable to those used in this project, could
cause a nonlinear response in the form of impact ionization in Si. This has not been conﬁrmed, but
is somewhat supported by the fact that the Si breakdown ﬁeld is 200 kV/cm in the continuous wave
(CW) case [39].
For SiO2, the breakdown ﬁeld is about 8 MV/cm [40], so 1 order of magnitude larger than for Si,
but about 1 order of magnitude lower than the strength of the enhanced ﬁelds in this project, which
will be shown in Sec. 4. The impact ionization happens when the strong ﬁeld causes a conduction
band electron to collide with a bound electron, which subsequently generates two free electrons
that each multiply in the same way again. The eﬀect happens over a time scale comparable to the
duration of the incident THz pulse, and the eﬀect is completely reversible given that the dielectric
does not suﬀer from any other kind structural damage [3]. In addition to a lower breakdown voltage,
Si has a higher electron mobility than SiO2. Therefore, impact ionization could very well happen in
Si without happening in SiO2.
The possible generation of additional free carriers in Si will inevitable change the conductivity and
therefore also the refractive index. However, since a change in refractive index in Si due to impact
ionization at ﬁeld strengths on the order of 300-500 kV/cm has not been thoroughly documented, this
project assumes a linear dielectric response everywhere unless very close to the metallic antennas.
SiO2 is assumed to have a linear response at all points in space and time.

3.3.2
Metal choices
In addition to the considerations about impact ionization, reﬂections and absorption loss - which
largely guide the choices of substrate material - the eﬀects associated with a choice of antenna metal
must also be taken into account. As mentioned in Sec. 2.4, Au has advantageous properties when
it comes to very low oxidation over time [1], [3], [21], [22].
However, in this case, the gaseous
environment around the antennas can be engineered in such a way that signiﬁcant oxidation should
not happen for any choice of metal. This can be obtained e.g. by creating a plasma of a noble gas.
In addition to oxidation, the metal’s ability to withstand strong ﬁelds must be taken into account.
It has been reported in [43] that two same-sized Au dipole antennas placed on a HR-Si substrate
with various gap sizes between adjacent tips undergo permanent deformation when exposed to ﬁelds
on the order of 200 kV/cm. The dimensions of the Au antennas were comparable to those used in
this project. The degree of deformation is determined by the gap size and the exposure time by
looking at the change in THz transmission at the separated dipole antenna resonance (0.6 THz) and
the resonance in the case where an electrical conduction path has somehow been established between
them (0.33 THz). The gap sizes vary from 2.5 µm to 10 µm. It is shown that a 2.5 µm gap leads
to a strong dipole antenna connection in less than 5 minutes. For 5 µm, the eﬀect happens over the
course of 10 minutes, but in a less pronounced way. 7.5 µm is only slightly aﬀected after 1 hour,
and 10 µm is not aﬀected even over the course of 16 hours. Recent, unpublished results from DTU
show that the deformation is hugely decreased on a SiO2 substrate compared to that of Si. This
underscores the need for a Si/SiO2 heterostructure substrate.

have tunnelled out of the metal antennas and subsequently interact with the surrounding gas as well
as bombarding the opposite antenna tip. The two former eﬀects are similar in the sense that they
both describe the possible migration of metal atoms away from their original lattice positions. This
can be caused through momentum transfer from conduction electrons to the lattices via scattering

3.3.3
Fowler-Nordheim equations
When an electric ﬁeld is present at the surface of a metal antenna, it works to decrease the value of
the electric potential outside the metal. If the ﬁeld is very strong, the potential decreases and takes
a roughly triangular form. It does not alter the work function inside the metal, but it decreases the
thickness of the potential barrier at the metal/air interface so much that electron tunnelling out of
the metal may occur. This is termed cold-ﬁeld emission (CFE) and it is approximated with a large
equation family called the Fowler-Nordheim-type (FN) equations. The concept is shown in Fig. 3.9.
Figure 3.9: A system of a metal in air. Conduction electrons in the metal are modelled as a free
electron gas. r is a distance, and U an electric potential. a) No external ﬁeld present close to the
metal surface. No electron tunnelling can occur. b) CFE emission concept with a triangular work
function locally around the metal surface. The external ﬁeld allows for electron tunnelling out of
the metal.

3.3.4
Gas choice
Subsequent to the CFE, electrons are accelerated in the enhanced ﬁeld close to the antenna edges.
When they collide with the atmospheric atoms/molecules in a gas form, they put them into exited
states. Some of these states correspond to ionization of the gas, which leads to the creation of a local
plasma close to the antennas. The aim of the plasma creation is that it will have a glow discharge in
the visible range (400-800 nm), since this is where most commercial image sensors are most sensitive.
The UVA range from 300-400 nm should also be detectable, although with a lower eﬃciency.
Tbl. 3.4 shows the 5 ﬁrst ionization energies for He, Ar and N in atomic from. These were the 3
gases tested in this project and they were primarily chosen due to availability on site. In addition,
the following reasoning was done:
Since He and Ar are noble gases, they do not form molecules to remain stable. N, however, forms
N2 and therefore has ionization energies that are slightly higher than what is shown in Tbl. 3.4
[45]. Despite the fact that it forms N2, N was chosen due to low values of the ﬁrst two ionization
energies. Furthermore, it was easily available in large quantities and had been used in comparable
experiments in [3]. For a prolonged use, N might be unfeasible in combination with Cu or Al due to
oxidation.
He and Ar both solve the problem of possible metal oxidation due to their low reactivity. They
are relatively cheap, but have higher thresholds for ionization than N2. From [3], it is known that

Chapter 4
Simulations
One of the most challenging parts of this project was to design an antenna with an optimal response in
time. Luckily, due to the scale invariance of Maxwell’s equations, concepts from classical microwave
engineering could be used for inspiration in a down-scaled version. However, classical antenna designs
primarily focus on control of individual frequencies. This gives a fundamentally diﬀerent approach
from a time-optimised design, as the latter seeks to make a response that is broad in frequency to
exploit the inverse time/bandwidth relationship given through Fourier transformation.
One point to notice is that only the antennas will be designed only with the electric ﬁeld in mind.
The main reason is that the substrate materials have permeabilities close to 1, so the magnetic
antenna response will in all cases be thought of as insigniﬁcant compared to the electric response.
It will be shown in the very end of this chapter that such design thinking could be inadequate for
development of antenna structures beyond those investigated in this project.
When an incoming ﬁeld interacts with an antenna, it starts to drive a current in the metal. The
current consists of electrons moving close to the metal surface to prevent the ﬁeld from penetrating
into the bulk. Due to their coulomb repulsion, the electrons tend to spread out along the metal
surface. At the antenna corners, where the amount of surface area per volume is larger than on
straight parts, the electron density is naturally higher. The concept is shown in Fig. 4.1.
Figure 4.1: The concept of carrier redistribution in metal in response to an incoming ﬁeld with diﬀer-

A high time response is equivalent to collecting as many electrons as possible in a small antenna
volume at the same time. For a narrow-band incoming signal, this is eﬃciently done in a resonant
structure since most of the incoming energy can be captured through one or a few frequencies. In
the case of this project, the incoming signal is very broadband with frequencies starting at 0.1 THz
and possibly continuing all the way to 2 THz1. The most energy is therefore captured if many of the
incoming frequencies can be used in a multi-resonant antenna. Seeing as diﬀerent frequencies move
electrons around in the antenna in diﬀerent ways, the challenge is to design a structure that allows
all frequencies to drive electrons to the same spot at the same time. This, in turn, could potentially
create such strong ﬁelds that it damages the antenna itself.

4.1.1
Electric dipole
The simplest and most well-described antenna design is the electric dipole antenna. It is highly
resonant in a narrow frequency band. In [3], it was simulated to give a ﬁeld enhancement of β = 32
over a 0.05THz bandwidth. Placing two dipole antenna tips adjacently with a gap of 2.5µm reportedly
gives β = 62 [43]. Both concepts are shown in Fig 4.2(a-b). In the shown cases, the antennas do
not taper to get a sharp tip. From the previous arguments, this could increase the surface area
density and allow for an even higher ﬁeld enhancement. Tapered tips are successfully used in CFE
for scanning tunnelling microscopes (STM) both for a static ﬁeld and recently in a pulsed THz ﬁeld
setup [48]. The tapering is only limited by the resolution of the structure fabrication (here, it is
about 1.5µm) and the metal’s ability to withstand the high electric ﬁeld.
4.1.2
Bowtie antenna
One type of gap creation from two tapered structures is the bowtie design shown in Fig. 4.2(c).
The bowtie has been under scrutiny for many years in the nanometer regime since it can be used
as an aperture-less near-ﬁeld system. In a 2004 paper [49], it was suggested that it could be only
approximately understood as a coupled dipole. More recently, its behaviour has been well-described
in the language of plasmonics through the coupling between light and surface plasmons (called surface
plasmon polaritons) in the gap [50]. The essential point of interest is that the small gap is able to
conﬁne the incoming light below its far-ﬁeld diﬀraction limit and therefore enhance the electric ﬁeld.
4.1.3
Polariser grid
In Fig. 4.2(d), a linear polariser is shown in a conﬁguration where all the incoming ﬁeld frequencies
below the dipole resonance are reﬂected due to the free movement of electrons.
An interesting use of a polariser grid as a ﬁeld enhancing device has been demonstrated in [51].
Here, the end-to-end metal strips on the polariser were replaced by individual dipole antennas with
dipole-to-dipole gaps slightly larger than the antennas themselves. It was shown that β > 40 if the
resonance of the plasmonic coupling in the gap was tuned to the transmission resonance of the entire
sample.
Taking the polariser and turning it 90◦with respect to the incoming, linearly polarised ﬁeld, the
ﬁlter eﬀect seeming disappears. This is shown in Fig. 4.2(e). However, looking very close to the
dipole strips, electrons are pushed from one dipole side to the other in a non-resonant way like it is
depicted in Fig. 4.1(b). Two adjacent dipole sides couple in a capacitor-like way and all frequencies
are therefore enhanced in the gaps. The lack of resonance, though, limits the strength of the ﬁeld
enhancement. Calculations made by one of the supervisors in earlier works showed a β on the order
of 10 −20 for nanometer-sized gaps between the dipoles.
Fig. 4.1(f) depicts an exotic variation of the grid polariser that is approximately independent
of the incoming ﬁeld polarisation. The structure has not been tested, but could potentially carry
characteristics from both a broadband and resonant response.

4.1.4
Fractal structure
A fractal structure is one that joins several dipole antennas in a pattern. One example is shown in
Fig. 4.1(g). For each dipole antenna, a new, smaller one is joined perpendicularly to the end. This
leads to several ways that the currents can run around in the antenna. The design is taken from [52]
where several resonance frequencies are shown to exist. Problematically, all frequencies do not reach
maximum ﬁeld enhancement at the same time nor in the same place. The largest ﬁeld enhancement
happens between the two capacitor-like plates on both the left- and right side while other frequencies
are enhanced at the antenna tips.
4.1.5
Split-ring resonator
Giant electric ﬁeld enhancement in resonant antennas has been shown to work for single split ring
resonators (SRR) with nanometer-sized gaps [1], [25], [53]–[56]. The concept is shown in Fig. 4.1(h).
The SRR is simply a folded dipole antenna that for allows ﬁeld enhancement at both tips like
illustrated in Fig. 4.1(a), but with the addition of plasmonic coupling between the two ends. In fact,
a ﬁeld enhancement of β = 104 has been shown in a 100nm gap [53].
Most reports on the use of SRRs for modulation of electromagnetic ﬁelds are concerned with
geometries in which the incoming magnetic ﬁeld points through the ring. This can lead to a powerful,
inductive eﬀect and it makes the SRR work much like a LRC circuit [56]. In the cited case of [53],
the magnetic ﬁeld was parallel to the side of the SRR and hence had a negligible contribution to
the overall functionality of the SRR. Naturally, since the THz was incident from free space, it had
not been possible to direct light straight onto the SRR and have the magnetic ﬁeld point in the
direction of propagation unless the free-space mode had been altered through a waveguide structure
just before incidence on the antenna. The only option to have a part of the magnetic ﬁeld penetrate
the SRR center in a free space setup would be to rotate the SRR about the incident, electric ﬁeld
vector.
4.1.6
Comparison
The literature study shows that several antenna properties should be investigated numerically, but
it can already at this point be decided that the ﬁnal structure needs to have a gap that allows
for plasmonic coupling. The gap size has been shown to be eﬀective even at sizes larger than the
antenna itself, which is reassuring when considering the possible antenna degradation in small gaps
as described in the theoretical sections. In contrast to the fractal structure, the antenna must have
a single gap that collects the eﬀect of all the enhanced frequencies in one place, just like the single
SRR. On the other hand (in line with the fractal structure design), it could be an idea to create
several current paths that are spaced far enough that the currents do not interfere most of the time.
Looking at the rather low β-values for polariser-like designs, it is clear that the antenna must posses
strong resonances. Thus, in this project, it is not sought to make a design that necessarily enhances
the entire, incoming bandwidth of up to 2THz.
A combination of the above-mentioned traits are incorporated into the design in Fig. 4.1(i). With
few minor modiﬁcations, this design is the one that ended up in the laboratory for experimental
investigation. It combines two single SRR circuit loops into a double SRR. The aim is to have both

The investigation of diﬀerent antenna designs was carried out through simulations in the Microwave
Studio (MWS) software package from the Computer Simulation Tool (CST) suite. The choice of
software was largely based on the supervisors’ work experience as well as the required programing
time needed to make useful simulations in diﬀerent software packages. MATLAB was considered to
have too few templates to work from, even though the author had the most experience working in
this environment. Since the author was new to COMSOL, it was rendered too complicated to set

experience and know-how of how to correctly set up right mesh for the antenna designs.

also been reported to make larger error in its calculation of the right ﬁelds [57]. The mesh becomes
especially important when the antenna features are much smaller than the wavelength of the incoming

Equivalent circuit
To round oﬀthe simulation section, it is relevant to show that the dSRR works like it was designed to.
Namely, it was designed to have current ﬂows that are dominated by two resonance frequencies charge

a capacitor-like element with plasmonically coupled tips. The currents should interfere constructively
at the point in time when the maximum THz ﬁeld drives charges to the tips.
In Fig. 4.57, two current-measuring loops were introduced. The resulting currents in time for a
200kV/cm incident ﬁeld are found in the top graph of Fig. 4.67. The currents are approximately a
factor of 2 larger than what was measured for the double dipole antenna. The blue, vertical lines
show that at the points in time when the two currents have equal magnitude, the maximum ﬁeld
enhancement occurs. Both currents are determined from the right-hand rule, so when the left current
is positive, it runs away from the top tip. Likewise, when the right current is negative, it runs away
from the top tip. It means that when the currents have opposite signs, they both charge up the tip
with either electrons or holes.
In the capacitor-picture, the capacitor reaches maximum charge when the currents cross. For a
circumference ratio of 1.5 between the two loops, it happens that the maximum ﬁeld enhancement
takes place exactly at the time when the maximum charge has accumulated at the tip, so the full
"potential" of the circuit is exploited. In other words, the two current resonances interfere positively
at the right point in time.
The proposed circuit only works for time proﬁles that look similar to the one that has been used
in the simulations. Fortunately, this is often the case for standard LiNbO3 setups.
The right description of this circuit might not be a simple one, as has been mentioned in earlier
chapters. To exemplify, the simplest model can be built as a LRC circuit. The resistance would
be equivalent everywhere and come from the ﬁnite width of the metal. The capacitance would be
modelled as a parallel plate of the gap with air as the dielectric, and the inductance would be the
equivalence of two, parallel-coupled solenoids of diﬀerent sizes with a single winding each.

designed vacuum chamber was made. The chamber was not needed to make simple investigations
of the samples, but seeing as the end goal was to move closer to a design for commercialisation, it

principle be only a few hundred µm thick if it had been feasible with all the diﬀerent connections
that went to it.

6.1
Light collection
A diﬀerent reason to make the vacuum chamber as short as possible - apart from the fact that
compactness was nice - was that it allowed for better light capture. The basic reasoning was that the
plasma glow discharge was completely unstimulated, so when a photon was emitted, it came out in
a random direction. Only photons emitted in the direction of the light detector would be captured.
Therefore, if the chamber sides were considered non-reﬂective (worst case scenario), the most light
could be captured if a collecting lens or the camera itself could be brought close to the plasma.
Fig. 6.3 shows a sketch of the problem. Catching the emission from antennas close to the center
of the sample (P1) is only limited by the extend of the entire chamber, whereas emission close to
the chamber side (P2) suﬀers from the 2-step design that was necessary to hold the small sample in
place and still ﬁt the larger tube connectors. Due to rotational symmetry, only the distance r from
the sample center is relevant.
The problem was solved numerically in MATLAB for discrete steps of 160µm and is shown in Fig.
6.4. The calculation considers the emission solid angle and checks for each point which cylinder edge
sets the limit. An average of 2.25% of the light can be caught within a radius of 2.5mm from the
center sample. As will be seen in the experimental chapter, this radius was not a limiting factor.

Overview and conclusions
To get an overview, the experiments with the old antennas that are presented in this chapter can
be found in Tbl. 8.1. Readers only interested in the ﬁnal results can read this section and skip the
next three.
The incident THz ﬁeld peaked at 0.79THz, so most useful experiments were done for 0.75THz
antennas. It is noted when bT-, sT- and I-antennas are used, but since it is shown that the unipolarity
is only 8%, all three antenna types will be compared as if they were the same.
The experiments showed that Au on Si/SiO2 maximum photon emission Pmax is larger than that
of Au on Si by a factor of 2. Moreover, the decay time constant tc is 3 times larger for Au on Si. One
sample with Au on Si for 0.45THz was tested, but no light emission was detected. It is shown that
the relative noise ﬂoor for Au on Si lies around ET Hz/Emax = 45%, which is equal to the relative
content of 0.45THz to 0.75THz in the THz pulse.
There seems to be no inﬂuence on tc from the THz intensity. Experiments run at ET Hz/Emax =
100% gave the same time constant as ET Hz/Emax = 55%. The decay is locally conﬁned to the focus
of the THz spot. Moving the focus laterally after having seen severe decay in one spot made photon
emission go back to Pmax. The decay is permanent, so when illuminating a spot 12 hours after its
decay, it had not returned to Pmax. N2 was seen to enhance the light emission by 328%, which was
expected from [3]. Using Ar increased Pmax by an order of magnitude and made the time decay
disappear. He was used at a much stronger ﬁeld than other gases and Pmax is hence incomparable
as the exact intensity dependence on ﬁeld strength for He with Au on SiO2 is unknown. However,
the time decay also disappears for He.
Intensity dependence on ﬁeld strength for Au on Si in air and Ar shows that I / E4
T Hz with small
deviations. Since diﬀerent experiments were run at diﬀerent days with ﬁeld strengths varying from
63 kV/cm to 190 kV/cm, no decimals on the proportionality order can be trusted and it is hence
considered to be 4 for all samples. For Au on SiO2 in air, the order lies between 7 and 8, but more
experiments need to be done to corroborate this result.
The time decays are ﬁtted in reasonable regions with exponential functions of the type y(t) =
A·exp(−tc ·t) to get comparable time decay constants tc. A and tc are ﬁtting parameters. However,
in air, there seems to be two decay regimes: One that follows a power law during the ﬁrst 10-15
minutes and one that is exponential after that. In N2, the decay is much closer to exponential the
entire time.
In some experiments, irrespective of the gas, there was a small, initial increase in light emission
over the ﬁrst 2-8 minutes. Afterwards, there was a peak and then a decrease. It is speculated that
the increase eﬀect comes from "sample cleaning" where plasma ions initially sputter the sample

For this experiment, there was a decrease in light emission over time, which was non-reversible.

a decay time constant tc = 0.13 could be found. The THz beam was then moved to the closest, new
spot where the light emission was maximum. This gave the beam spot size of 120µm. In comparison,
one full wavelength on the Si/air interface is c/(neff,Si · 0.59THz) = 202µm. Hence, the beam was
either relatively close to being diﬀraction limited or the sample was not placed perfectly in the beam
focus point.
When the second time constant could be determined to be tc = 0.16, the THz beam was moved
back to the ﬁrst spot. Here, the light emission continued from where it ended before moving the
THz beam. Moving back and forth showed that this was indeed the case for both spots. Changing
the gas pressure shortly decreased the light emission, but this was reversible.
When looking closely at the time constants after illuminating 4 diﬀerent spots, it was evident that
they increased over time. This suggests that the changes that were taking place to alter the light
emission happened both outside the focus of the THz beam as well as inside. The change associated
with oﬀ-focus THz illumination was diﬀerent than those happening in focus. This can be seen from
the fact that eﬀects outside the THz beam focus did not alter the initial light emission of any new
spot, but changed the time constant.
Fits were made as A · exp[−tc · (t −t0)], where t0 was the start time of any new spot. A and tc
were ﬁtting parameters. As discussed in Chapter 8, an exponential decay is not a perfect ﬁt, but it
gave useful, comparable time constants to use across experiments. A better model with r2 ⇡1 in all
experiments was A·exp[−tc·(t−t0)]+C, where C was a third ﬁtting parameter. It was believed that
C would represent an emission baseline that the exponential decay converged to. However, ﬁtting
C without limits yielded values orders of magnitude below zero, so the interpretation was unknown.
This led to uncertainty in the time constant interpretation, so C remained unused.

9.2
THz mode proﬁle extraction in pictures
In Sec. 8.4, it was shown how a THz mode proﬁle (MP) was extracted through pixel averaging. For
very low light emission rates, the picture contrast was enhanced well by taking log10 to each average
picture before subtraction. However, this meant that a back-transformation had to be applied to
get a picture of the actual MP. This back-transformation gave bad results for pictures that had the
edges of the sample holder slightly in focus, as these edges blew up and suppressed the contrast of
the MP itself.
The intensity of the incoming THz ﬁeld scales as E2
T Hz, but it was shown in Fig. 9.12 that the
light emission scales as E4
T Hz. This means that - to interpret any mode proﬁle image as a THz
intensity distribution - the square root of all pixel values had to be taken after having run the pixel
averaging procedure.
Some measures were taken to give acceptable contrasts without having to make a log-transformation.
1) the edges of each picture were removed as saturated pixels usually resided there. 2) a 2-D adap-
tive noise-removal ﬁlter (weiner ﬁlter) was introduced. The ﬁlter determined all pixel values from
the average of their 4 nearest neighbours. This removed the so-called "salt and pepper noise", where
single, stand-alone pixels in the picture would be saturated without it being part of the MP.
Figure 9.25: Example of a MP calculation. The top left corner shows the subtraction of the picture
with no incident THz and one with incident THz.
The horizontal, white line indicates the row
that has the highest sum of pixel values. The row is shown in 2D in the upper right corner. Here,
Lorentzian and a Gaussian are ﬁtted to the data. The Lorentzian ﬁts the best, so it is used to
determine the width of the MP by indicating where its function value intercepts the mean background
noise plus one standard deviation. The same procedure is carried out for the column in the MP
picture that has the highest pixel sum. The Lorentzian widths are used as axes for an ellipse that
encircles the MP. A close-up of the MP is shown in the lower right corner.

To quantify the MP in a picture, the following scheme was introduced:
1. All rows and all columns were summed up individually. The row with the highest sum value
rmax was considered to pass through the MP center horizontally, while the maximum column
sum indicated the line cmax that passed through the MP center vertically.
2. rmax and cmax were both ﬁtted with a Lorentzian of the form
Y =
P1
(X −P2)2 + P3
+ C
(9.1)
as well as a Gaussian. Fitting parameters were P1, P2, P3 and C. In all cases, the Lorentzian
ﬁt was the most accurate. The reason is unknown.
3. The center position for the Lorentzian at rmax was considered the x-position of the MP center.
Similarly, the Lorentzian center for cmax was considered the y-position of the MP center.
Therefore, the actual MP center was not necessarily determined to be residing on either the
rmax nor cmax line.
4. For each Lorentzian, the width was determined as the point where the Lorentzian value was
equal to the mean background noise plus one standard deviation hereof. The latter two numbers
were found from averaging areas in the picture without any features, usually close to the picture
edges.
5. The MP was taken to be elliptical with its two axes equal to the full widths of the two respective
Lorentzians. The MP "strength", which was compared to PMT measurements, was a sum of
all the pixel values inside the MP ellipse.
An example of the above steps can be found in Fig. 9.25. The white ellipse encircles the mode
proﬁle, which is cut out and shown in detail in the lower right corner. The proﬁle is for 0.43THz
where λ = c/(neff ·0.43THz) = 323µm. This is equal to the horizontal width of the orange center of
the MP ±20µm, so the picture reﬂects the actual THz MP intensity distribution to a high precision.

Chapter 10
Conclusion and outlook
This project has demonstrated for the ﬁrst time that it is possible to make a practical THz detector
based on plasma creation through THz induced cold-ﬁeld electron emission from metal antennas.
In speciﬁc, strong incident THz transients are detected by observing plasma glow discharge with a
commercial camera optimised for visible wavelengths.
The detection mechanism has been demonstrated to work for THz ﬁeld strengths greater than
or equal to 100 kV/cm using a US$4,500 camera. With a US$350 camera, the detection has been
demonstrated to work for THz ﬁeld strengths starting at 350 kV/cm. It has not been investigated if
the cheaper camera works for weaker THz ﬁelds.
The complete THz detector setup was fabricated at a price around US$1000. With the optimal
design and fabrication process, this number can be brought down below US$100. Hence, the detector
has a great market opportunity for THz imaging with strong THz transients. The current market
for such detector is limited to less than 1000 potential customers, but the overall THz technology
market is expanding rapidly and the developed detector ﬁts well into many existing and novel THz
applications for cargo inspection and biomedical imaging.
The THz detection relies upon a strong THz ﬁeld enhancement in the time domain. This project
has demonstrated a novel design for metal antennas that have a time domain ﬁeld enhancement
factor of 300.
The antennas work by having 2 coupled metal loops in which the THz induced
currents interfere constructively around a point in time where they have both charged a capacitor-
like element. The strong ﬁeld leads to cold-ﬁeld electron emission from the antenna edges, which is
governed by a Fowler-Nordheim-type equation. The emission is strongly nonlinear with the electric
ﬁeld strength in the time domain and theoretical predictions show that the emission current has at
least doubled in comparison to other time domain antenna designs found in literature.
The antennas are arranged in a periodic fashion on substrates that suﬀer from degradation due
to nonlinear eﬀects induced in the high electric ﬁeld. The exact nature of the degradation is still
unknown. However, material optimisation has found it advantageous to use either a SiO2 substrate
or a Si/SiO2 heterostructure with SiO2 between the antennas and Si. The antennas have been proven
to work well for Au and Cu, whereas Al degrades severely over 10-20 minutes.
Subsequent to the cold-ﬁeld emission, electrons are supposedly accelerated into the keV regime,
depending on their scattering interactions with the surrounding gas. Their kinetic energies are large
enough that they can ionize the gas to diﬀerent ionization states and hereby create the potential for
==PAGE:132==
114
Conclusion and outlook
a strong light emission upon plasma discharge. The light emission from atmospheric air has been
compared to that of N2, He and Ar. It shows that the use of air and N2 leads to a strong decay in
the light emission over time, whereas He and Ar tend to cause a much weaker decay. It is not yet
understood what drives the decay, nor is it understood which parameters inﬂuence the decay the
most.
The light emission intensity from the antenna sample grows with the 4th power of the incident
THz ﬁeld strength. Moreover, interesting eﬀects have been observed through the time variation
of the light emission, which could eventually lead to an understanding of the nonlinear eﬀects in
the substrate. For instance, some substrates make for an increase in light emission intensity over
the ﬁrst 5 minutes of THz illumination. Afterwards, the emission decays in what looks like 2 time
regimes: One that follows a power law and is dominant up until approximately 15 minutes from
the beginning of the THz illumination. Afterwards, the light emission decay seems to follow an
exponential function.
It has been shown with mode proﬁle pictures of the THz beam that diﬀerent incident THz fre-
quencies lead to diﬀerent light emission intensities. This has the potential to map discrete values of
the THz frequency band by having antennas optimised for diﬀerent frequencies placed in diﬀerent
regions of the sample. It has also been shown that the antenna design allows for a very dense mesh
on the substrate surface to optimise the total electron emission of the entire sample.
Apart from making further optimisation of the antenna and substrate design, future work could
be focused on inducing photo-luminescence from organic molecules using the high-energetic electrons
from the antennas. Photo-luminescent molecules can potentially convert more of the electrons to
photons than the impact with gas molecules does.
At this point, approximately 105 photons are detected with the camera per incident THz pulse.
It has been shown that only about 2% of the created photons go to the camera, but with only a
very thin layer of gas around the antennas, this number could in theory be brought close to 50%
based only on geometrical considerations. Moreover, determining the polarisation of the incident
THz from the light emission intensity has been tested in this project, but without success. It has
been argued from simulations and literature reviews that further antenna optimisation should make
such functionality work.
On the THz detector commercialisation side, a next step is to create a viable business model that
allows the detector to be made into product prototype. This could either be done through private
early stage investment into a spin-oﬀcompany or by licensing the technology to existing market
players.
