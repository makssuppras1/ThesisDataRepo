Abstract  
The master thesis investigates the carry-over phenomenon which is commonly observed in public 
organizations projects and its effective management through the application of a data management 
reporting tool. The existing knowledge gap concerning the influence that the key stakeholders’ risk 
perspectives exert on the generation of this phenomenon triggered the research concerning this 
particular topic. The case organization which is analysed for this purpose is the Danish municipality of 
Gentofte. Moreover, the presented findings constitute qualitative data that were collected through the 
execution of five interviews with the key stakeholders. The thematic qualitative analysis was utilized 
for the analysis of the interview data. The findings pinpointed that the disparate stakeholders’ 
perceptions with regards to the importance of the carry-over phenomenon and the defective reporting 
processes throughout the entire municipality are the two major factors that lead to the accumulation of 
excessive budget at the end of the financial year. Therefore, the potential benefits stemming from the 
application of Power BI; a data visualization reporting tool, in Gentofte municipality operations were 
examined. More specifically, its utilization can lead to the enhancement of the internal reporting 
processes through their standardization and increased transparency. Additionally, its contribution to the 
alignment of the involved stakeholders’ perspectives concerning the risk of carry-over proved to be 
eminent. Nevertheless, the successful application of this tool necessitates the utilization of supporting 
practices.   
 
Keywords: Carry-over phenomenon, Project portfolio management, Internal reporting processes, Power 
BI

1 Introduction 
The most common way that public administrations and government agencies have been handling their 
multi-year investment portfolios includes just an initial delineation of the budget allocation throughout 
the projects lifespan and disregards the effective forecast of the future payment patterns. This fact 
empowers the uncertainty behind the outlining of the true reasons for the accumulation of excessive 
budget at the end of the year. Furthermore, the accurate estimation of the requisite resources for the 
completion of public projects becomes almost infeasible (Lienert et al., 2009). 
Lienert et al. (2009) pinpointed that one of the major issues lies in the fact that in case budget surplus 
eventuates at the end of the fiscal year, it is extremely difficult to get rid of the carry-over through end-
of-year spending sprees due to the strict expenditure regulations. At the same time, it is often not 
possible to assert that this amount will be proved useful in the upcoming years.  
Then the increasing volume of the carry-over which is the amount of budget transferred to the next 
financial year can render the confrontation between the public administration plans concerning 
expenditure and the decisions of the financial manager inescapable. This volatile situation can 
potentially have a severe negative impact on the management of each project and ultimately of the 
whole portfolio (Lienert et al., 2009).  
However, if the project budgeting is properly performed in a realistic manner and existing regulations 
minimize the risks associated with the one-time spending of the remaining budget then the carry-over 
which will be transferred to the upcoming fiscal years will be relatively insignificant (Lienert et al., 
2009). 
A fundamental requirement for the effective management of the projects cash flow and hence the carry-
over phenomenon is the successful implementation of the project portfolio management practice. As 
Dettbarn Jr. et al. (2005) underline, this particular practice constitutes the medium for the organizations 
to coordinate the projects of their portfolio through the continuous assessment of the relevant resources; 
both monetary and human. In this way, it is rendered possible for the organization to minimize the risks 
associated with the projects expenditure and their budget management. A common pitfall that can result 
in the deficient budget allocation and therefore in the accumulation of excessive budget at the end of 
the year is the limited knowledge of the key stakeholders concerning both the available practices and 
the characteristics of the dynamic decision environment of the portfolio (Marinsuo, 2013). 
Amongst the major challenges with regards to the successful application of project portfolio 
management especially in public sector organizations is the great influence that the political interests 
exert to the project prioritization which can lead to the confusion of the engaged stakeholders 
perspectives and responsibilities (Elonen & Artto, 2003). This has also a huge impact on the decision-
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
making process as the project planning is strongly influenced by predetermined directions that originate 
from extramural factors and not from the organization itself (Martinsuo, 2013).  
According to Martinsuo and Lehtonen (2007), the information availability and the knowledge sharing 
regarding the different types of project data among the key stakeholders are crucial for the effective 
coordination of the organizational portfolio. A strong focus should be placed on the prompt 
identification of the various risks and uncertainties that are mainly related to the cost and schedule 
changes that can occur throughout the project life cycle (Martinsuo, 2013).  
Biedenback and Müller (2012) highlighted the important role of project portfolio management practice 
as the medium for getting the key stakeholders acquainted with the essential project information with a 
view to rendering the organization capable of adapting to the dynamic project environment. An 
extremely crucial prerequisite in regard to the successful implementation of PPM is the creation of 
robust and transparent communication bonds between the engaged stakeholders (Shehu & Akintove, 
2010).  
The performance management practice is described by Armstrong and Baron (1998) as an expedient to 
perform an evaluation of the implementation of the PPM practice based on the level of fulfillment of 
the established objectives such as the budget distribution among the various projects.  
The application of project risk management can actively contribute to the budget and cash flow 
management of the different projects and therefore to the effective management of the carry-over 
phenomenon. Nevertheless, according to Willumsen et al. (2019), the subjective value perceptions that 
each stakeholder possesses comprise a fundamental element for the capitalization of the risk 
management practice for the sake of value creation. Thus, the contextual adjustment of the PRM 
practice to the applied circumstances and the alignment of the distinct value perspectives should be 
taken into consideration.  
As Aven (2012, 2016) highlighted, the transparent information sharing among the entire organization 
constitutes an extremely useful medium for the accomplishment of the successful application of PRM 
at the organizational portfolio level. In addition, risk reporting together with the standardization of the 
various internal processes can play a decisive role in the enhancement of the communication links 
among the key stakeholders and ultimately lead to the effective implementation of PRM (Willumsen et 
al., 2019). 
According to Olsson (2001), it is imperative that a portfolio-oriented mindset should be followed 
concerning the risks and uncertainties identification process in the context of the organization. Thus, it 
is crucial that every project phase is taken into account and all the engaged stakeholders are actively 
involved in this process. Moreover, the effective risk analysis process strongly depends on the 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
availability of the risk-related data for each one of the different projects of the organization. In addition, 
the establishment of an association between the risks of the various projects is essential.  
Shafiri and Bagherpour (2016) pinpointed the great importance of the precise and prompt estimation of 
the project cash flow for the successful management of the project budget in the long term. Additionally, 
the cash flow forecast can have a great impact on the decision-making process due to its contribution 
to the effective handling of potential financial abnormalities that can occur throughout the project life 
cycle. The required steps for the achievement of an efficacious cash flow management process include 
the clear definition of the project lifespan, the division of the project to distinct activities and the 
assignment of cost elements to each one of them and the detection of the risk-related factors that are 
associated with them before the execution of their analysis. The credibility of the utilized inputs should 
be verified throughout the whole procedure.  
The literature search uncovered the fact that limited knowledge exists concerning the optimization of 
the projects budget and cash flow management processes and therefore the effective management of the 
carry-over phenomenon through the application of particular processes or tools for the alignment of the 
key stakeholders perspectives. 
1.1 Research Question  
The major aim of this thesis is the contribution to the literature through the execution of a case study 
with the Danish municipality of Gentofte and the application of a data management reporting tool for 
the purpose of addressing the carry-over phenomenon.  
Hence, the following research question was formed: 
How can a data management reporting tool improve the budget and cash flow management processes 
and therefore contribute to the effective management of the carry-over phenomenon through the 
alignment of the distinctive risk perspectives of the involved parties? 
The research methodology which was followed is the single-case study that was analysed based on 
semi-structured interviews through the application of the qualitative thematic analysis (see chapter 4).

2 Case Introduction  
This particular chapter includes the introduction to the case organization which is the municipality of 
Gentofte and the under-investigation department of Gentofte Ejendomme (GE). Then, the primary 
challenge that the municipality is facing is presented. The last section is devoted to the introduction of 
the data management reporting tool; Power BI that the municipality is planning to apply to its 
operations. 
2.1 Gentofte municipality organisational structure  
The municipality of Gentofte is a medium-sized suburban Danish municipality which is located north 
of the capital area of Copenhagen. Moreover, this modern municipality consists of the nine following 
districts: Charlottenlund, Dyssegård, Gentofte, Hellerup, Jægersborg, Klampenborg, Ordrup, 
Skovshoved and Vangede (Gentofte Kommune, n.d.). 
The organizational structure of the Gentofte municipality comprises multiple levels as shown in Figure 
1. In particular, the top authority is the municipal council which consists of nineteen people and is 
appointed every four years through elections. Furthermore, it constitutes the authority which is 
responsible not only for the communication of the political aims and objectives to the whole 
municipality but also for the ultimate decision making concerning the implementation of actions that 
serve the political vision of the municipality. The major of the municipality is the one who reports 
directly to the municipal council and also transfers the outcomes of its members' decisions to the 
municipal director. Moreover, the next level of the organizational structure comprises four divisions. 
Each one of these divisions has its own director and all of them report to the municipal director 
(Gentofte Kommune, n.d.). 
2.2 Gentofte Ejendomme (GE) 
Additionally, each division constitutes of multiple departments. The focus of this thesis is placed on the 
Gentofte Ejendomme (GE) department which is under the “Teknik og Miljø” (Technology and 
Environment) as this is a cross-functional department that undertakes and manages the construction and 
maintenance projects of the entire municipality. Therefore, they fulfill the needs of the other 
departments of the municipality in terms of construction and renovation works. This particular 
department consists of four sub-departments (as shown in Figure 2); “Constructions”, “Operations”, 
“Services”, “Internal services” that operate under the supervision of the GE division manager. The 
under investigation subdepartment is the one named “Constructions” due to the fact that this is the one 
specializing in handling all the processes concerning the construction and renovation projects that take 
place within the context of the municipality. Moreover, each one of these is administrated by one 
department manager who reports to the aforementioned division manager of GE. What is more, among 
the main responsibilities of the department manager is to coordinate the project managers of GE.

2.3 Current Processes and Main Challenge  
The aim of this chapter is to present the current processes that are taking place at the GE department 
concerning the management of the various construction and renovation projects. Additionally, the major 
challenge that GE is facing is presented.  
To begin with, in terms of the financial planning of the projects the PMs are utilizing the financial 
management sheet (Økonomi Styring (ØS) ark). In particular, this is the tool they use in order to keep 
track of the three different budget categories related to each project and more specifically the “original”, 
the “allocated” and the “actual spent” budget throughout the different years that comprise the life cycle 
of each project. The ultimate aim of this process is the prediction of the expenditure which is associated 
with the municipality projects. Moreover, they are constantly communicating with the FCs in terms of 
reporting to them the actual amount of funds that are needed for the execution of each project. Then, 
the FCs register this particular amount to PRISME which is the financial accounting system of the 
municipality. Afterwards, the FCs communicate with the PMs in order to ask for their estimations 
regarding the amount of budget which will be transferred to the next year. The aforementioned process 
is depicted in Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3: Reporting process between PMs and FCs 
 
Furthermore, the aforementioned parties are reporting to the department manager. More importantly, 
the financial controllers provide the department manager with the financial status of each project and 
the project managers report the project status concerning its various phases and their stage of 
completion. The department manager is authorized to give the final approval for each project according 
to the reports provided by the FCs and the PMs. Then these reports are handed to the division manager 
of GE who performs the prioritization of the projects comprising the portfolio based on both the status 
of the projects and the political interests that are communicated from his seniors. In addition, there is a 
reporting procedure between the GE department and the internal clients; the other departments of 
Gentofte municipality that assign construction and renovation projects to GE. In particular, the 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
department manager regularly updates them with regards to the projects status. Finally, GE is 
responsible for the provision of project updates to the steering group which is a party that consists of 
members throughout the municipality such as department and division managers and political 
representatives. More specifically, the project managers report to this group information concerning the 
expenditure of the projects. Also, the department manager communicates with them with a view to 
extracting insights in terms of the requirements and the needs of each project.  
The major challenge that Gentofte municipality and more specifically the GE department faces is 
indissolubly related to the carry-over phenomenon which represents the unspent amount of the project 
budget that is transferred to the next financial year. Figure 4 constitutes a visualization of the carry-over 
phenomenon. In particular, this occurrence is quite common due to the strong political influence that is 
exerted on the prioritization of the projects. Therefore, political interests can impel the postponement 
or the pause of some projects and the commencement of others. This ultimately leads to the 
accumulation of unutilized budget from the “frozen” projects which cannot be deployed by the active 
projects during the current year.  
Hence, there is a great need for GE to be able to accurately estimate the carry-over which is associated 
with the various projects of the municipality with a view to optimizing not only their financial 
management but also the management of the portfolio with regards to the required resources. Thus, GE 
is considering utilizing a business intelligence (BI) data reporting tool in order to enhance the reporting 
processes that take place in the context of the municipality with the ulterior aim of improving the budget 
and cash flow management of their projects.

2.4 Introduction to Power BI 
Power BI comprises an “interactive data visualization and analytics tool for business intelligence (BI)” 
(Branscombe, 2017) which specializes in the creation of reports that include visualizations of a wide 
range of data and renders possible their sharing across the entire organization through a common 
platform. One of the major advantages of Power BI is the presentation of data in a way that can be easily 
communicated and comprehended throughout the various engaged departments and stakeholders of the 
organization (Wright, n.d.). 
In addition, Power BI provides the opportunity to depict both the past and the current state of the 
visualized data and apart from that perform estimations for the future through the application of “what-
if” scenarios. In this way, the organizations are rendered capable of forecasting the future state of their 
data by taking into account the different possible scenarios. Hence, it can greatly contribute to the 
optimization of crucial decision-making processes of the organizations (Wright, n.d). Furthermore, 
Power BI comprises a way to not only detect patterns and valuable latent insights in a big pool of data 
but also to question their validity and accuracy (Branscombe, 2017). An additional advantage concerns 
the fact that it can provide real-time updates of the visualizations based on the alterations of the source 
data. Moreover, the usage of this particular tool does not require the expertise of a data analyst and thus 
can be employed by all the organizational stakeholders without any prior experience (Rouse, 2015).  
To sum up, Power BI is an extremely useful tool that can be utilized by any kind of organization for the 
sake of visualizing and effectively communicating organizational data.

3 Literature Review  
The literature review section firstly introduces the carry-over phenomenon and the existing carry-over 
management practices. Then, the links between the project portfolio management practice and the 
optimization of carry-over management are presented. Next, the impact of risk management project 
portfolio management and hence carry-over management is investigated. Lastly, the examination of the 
literature findings with regards to the influential relationship between project cash flow management 
and effective finance management takes place. 
3.1 Carry-over  
The first step followed was the research of the existing literature concerning the carry-over definition 
and the phenomena related to this financial term. Then, the literature on the impact of the carry-over 
generation especially towards public organizations was reviewed together with ways of managing it in 
a gainful way. 
3.1.1 Carry-over definitions  
Although limited definitions for the carry-over exist in the literature, one of the most ubiquitous refers 
to it as “the right to use any unspent appropriation beyond the time period for which it was originally 
granted” (Lienert & Ljungman 2009, p.2). In terms of the public portfolios, carry-over has to do with 
the budget which is transferred from one financial year to the next one due to various reasons that 
hindered the progress of some of the projects. Additionally, this term is often utilized in order to 
represent the transition among two consecutive fiscal years. Moreover, public sector budgeting 
constitutes a faulty procedure which ultimately leads to the generation of budget carry-over. This 
particular fact can have negative consequences on how effectively the expenses are allocated among 
the various budget assets (Lienert & Ljungman, 2009). 
On the other hand, Fisher (1975) introduces carry-over as an incentive that can potentially contribute to 
the avoidance of the overspending phenomenon at the end of the financial year by rendering possible 
the transfer of the non-used budget to the following year. According to the same theory, this budget 
surplus can constitute an indicator of either efficient budget management throughout the fiscal year or 
inadequate planning due to the retention of the budget which was not so important until the year 
completion. 
3.1.2 End-of-year spending phenomena  
One common prevention practice of the carry-over generation is the so-called end-of-year spending 
sprees phenomenon which takes place when it is certain that the available budget for a particular 
financial year will not be utilized by the originally planned activities. This particular phenomenon is 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
based on the interpretation that any untapped asset comprises a lost chance for spending (Lienert & 
Ljungman, 2009). 
According to the International Monetary Fund reports, end-of-year spending is a customary practice 
among the government administrations. However, this tactic is far from optimal and in most cases leads 
to the extravagant spending of taxpayer dollars. Furthermore, according to the National Bureau of 
Economic Research Working Paper a surge of federal agencies spending is usually observed in the last 
week; 52nd of each fiscal year. Another interesting finding indicated that the spending which occurs 
during this week is almost five times greater than the weekly average of the previous weeks throughout 
the year (Liebman & Mahoney, 2013). 
Although this phenomenon is extremely common in many countries and it has direct consequences on 
the financial management of the public agencies’ portfolios, only a limited amount of literature exists 
concerning its causes and potential risk prevention measures (Hurley et al., 2014). 
A similar phenomenon to the aforementioned “end-of-year sprees” is the “use it or lose it” practice 
which mainly describes the inclination of the United States government agencies to disburse the whole 
portion of the non-utilized budget when the end of the financial year comes closer. This incentive is 
mainly steered by the peril that the remaining budget will end up at the Department of the Treasury and 
additionally the budget for the following year will be distinctively decreased (Fichtner & Michel, 2016). 
According to the Missouri state auditor report, which was published in 2012, the yearly budget practices 
can have incontrovertible consequences to the agencies spending patterns. Also, it pointed out that the 
“use it or lose it” phenomenon is observed to some degree. An interesting finding pertained to the fact 
that during the period between 2009 and 2011 a considerable number of state agencies spent an amount 
greater than a quarter of their total revenue fund disbursement within the last two months of each 
financial year (Schweich, 2012). 
Hence, it can be easily concluded that the aforementioned end-of-year spending phenomena lead the 
public organizations to improvident expenditure during the last months of the fiscal year. So, instead of 
serving their purpose of mitigating the carry-over generation they actually disturb the well-balanced 
budget management of the organizations in a negative way. So, it would be imperative to look at the 
carry-over management practices which can contribute to the deflection of these phenomena. 
3.1.3 Carry-over management practices  
To begin with, unspent budget does not constitute a beneficial condition for the person who is 
responsible for the management of the annual budget of an organization. What is more, the looming 
risk concerning the reduction of the future budget allocations due to the incapability of spending all the 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
budget for the current year impels the budget managers towards the prevention of the generation of 
carry-over (Lienert & Ljungman, 2009).  
However, Lienert & Ljungman (2009) highlight the fact that the effective management of carry-over 
can lead to more robust expenditure management initiatives such as the decisive cost reduction. 
Moreover, the precise carry-over estimation can comprise a powerful tool for the formulation of an 
accurate overview of the actual demand for resources. 
Fichtner & Michel (2016) highlight the fact that the establishment of midyear or quarter budget checks 
could actively contribute to the significant minimization of the spending outbreak that takes place at the 
end of each financial year.   
A complementary action plan which could lead to supplementary limitation of the “last minute” 
spending is proposed by Fichtner & Michel (2016) according to which every public agency should be 
authorized to generate a limited amount of carry-over equal up to five percent of the total budget and 
move it to a rollover account. In addition, during the following financial years, it will be capable of 
transferring only fifty percent of any unspent balance to the aforementioned account. A limitation was 
also introduced concerning the rollover funds which have to be spent during the next two years. In this 
way, the delay of depleting rollover funds will be considerably decreased. In the long run, the 
advantages of using this plan will be much greater than the temporary costs. In particular, the public 
agencies will not be concerned in terms of how to consume any outstanding resources at the end of each 
year with a view to avoiding implications on the budgets of the upcoming fiscal years (Fichtner & 
Michel, 2016). 
Fichtner & Michel (2016) highlight the fact that two potential risks stem from the established budget 
regulations for public agencies in the vast majority of the countries worldwide; either a considerable 
portion of the budget will be rendered untapped or the available budget for the upcoming years will be 
decreased based on the unspent amount of the current year.  
As illustrated from the above sections, the carry-over phenomenon has an overwhelmingly negative 
impact on the overall financial planning of public agencies portfolios. More importantly, it can lead to 
the balance deprivation of the budget allocation and management of the portfolio through the common 
end-of-year spending phenomena. Moreover, portions of the available budget might be granted 
unavailable due to the transfer of funds to the next fiscal year. It is also significant to pinpoint the fact 
that there are literature gaps in terms of the efficient way of managing the carry-over for the sake of 
successful portfolio management. Hence, it was deemed necessary to explore the potential influence 
that project portfolio management has on the management of the carry-over in public organizations.

3.2 Project Portfolio Management 
In this section the investigation of the literature concerning project portfolio management takes place. 
More specifically, a particular focus is placed on the factors behind the successful application of the 
PPM practice and its further effect on the optimization of the relevant budgeting and planning activities. 
The ultimate aim is the revelation of the impact that this practice has on the effective management of 
carry-over. Also, further attention is placed on the application of PPM in the public sector with a view 
to identifying the influential factors with the greatest effect on its implementation. Lastly, the 
performance management practice was studied as a means to achieve the success of PPM.   
To begin with, project portfolio management is widely known as a dynamic decision process during 
which multiple projects are continuously undergoing examination and revision. This particular practice 
promotes the constant assessment, prioritization and selection of projects that share mutual strategic 
goals (Martinsuo & Lehtonen, 2007). 
Moreover, through the portfolio management, the organizations projects are being coordinated 
according to the available resources that are being reviewed on an ongoing basis with a view to 
minimizing not only the expenditure but also the risks associated with the various projects and at the 
same time fully capitalizing the benefits stemming from the project implementation (Dettbarn Jr. et al, 
2005). Additionally, according to Munson and Spivey (2006), portfolio management is a technique that 
helps the enterprise maintain control over its projects and operations in the long term.  
Although a plethora of guiding rules exist concerning the selection criteria and processes of the projects, 
the resources allocation throughout the various projects, the portfolio alignment with the overall 
organization strategy and the success criteria of the portfolio implementation, it is a norm that the 
majority of the enterprises are facing huge difficulties with the control of both the “resources sharing” 
among the different projects and the continuous alterations that occur inside a portfolio (Engwall & 
Jerbrant, 2003). 
In many cases, the lack of knowledge both in terms of the environment of the portfolio and the practices 
that the key stakeholders should apply leads to the defective resource allocation (Martinsuo, 2013). This 
problematic situation can seriously affect the management of carry-over not only through the inefficient 
budget allocation but also through the flawed capitalization of the available experts.   
3.2.1 Objectives of project portfolio management 
The major aim of project portfolio management is the value enlargement of the portfolio with regards 
to the organizational strategic objectives and the attainment of strategic alignment and balance across 
the various projects. Hence, project portfolio management could be characterized as efficient when the 
aforementioned goal dimensions are being achieved. According to another study, this particular 
==PAGE:23==
15 
 
efficiency depends on the extent to which the combination of individual projects contributes to the 
achievement of the whole portfolio objectives (Martinsuo & Lehtonen, 2007). 
According to Anderson (2008), the major portfolio management objectives comprise the clear definition 
of goals, effective risk management and the ongoing review of the portfolio performance in financial 
terms. In order to achieve these goals, a few fundamental steps should be followed; “strategic 
considerations, individual project evaluation, and portfolio selection” (Gabriel et al., 2006). 
Furthermore, risk management practice can play an extremely important role in the project portfolio 
management because of the existence of high risks such as the carry-over generation throughout the 
portfolio and their direct correlation to the portfolio’s financial outcome (Pereira & Veloso, 2009). 
3.2.2 Challenges concerning the implementation of PPM  
One of the major challenges that organizations face concerning the implementation of project portfolio 
management is the limited interest and commitment from the side of the involved stakeholders and 
more importantly the organization leaders. According to Martin and Nicholls (1987) “commitment” is 
defined as the readiness of each member to devote to the organization to a greater extent than the one 
initially agreed through the recruitment contract.  
Fowler and Walsh (1999) highlighted that the active engagement of the upper management to the project 
portfolio management is profoundly significant especially in case the portfolio requires and influences 
a big portion of the organizational resources. One of the most common consequences of this condition 
is the neglection of crucial activities for the implementation of portfolio management (Shehu & 
Akintoye 2010). 
Cooper et al. (2000) pinpointed several challenges related to the management of portfolios. More 
specifically, the dynamic nature of the decision environment, the numerous interdependencies across 
the various projects, the different stages in which each project is currently at, the uncertainty of the 
available and required information and the scarcity of the essential resources are amongst the major 
ones. 
Another common pitfall that is commonly observed during the implementation of PPM is the non-
utilization of a project prioritization method which actively contributes to the “portfolio coordination” 
through the assignment of clear roles and responsibilities among the key stakeholders (Elonen & Artto, 
2003). However, one major challenge which arises from the project prioritization is when this is done 
based on political criteria and hence decision-making is dominantly influenced by the political interests. 
This particular situation can lead to the inefficient management of the portfolio especially in case the 
uncertainty of both the project available resources and outcomes is quite high (Cooper et al. 2000). 
==PAGE:24==
16 
 
3.2.3 Factors leading to effective project portfolio management  
The unambiguous definition of the portfolio goals in regard to the projects scope, schedule, resources, 
budget, information sharing among the key stakeholders and the upper management engagement 
constitute the most significant contributory factors for the effective management of an organization 
portfolio (Martinsuo & Lehtonen, 2007). Moreover, the effectiveness of the project portfolio 
management can be strongly influenced not only by the strategic management of the projects but also 
by the learning and resource sharing throughout the various projects. Additionally, based on the same 
research findings intel accessibility comprises an extremely important factor concerning the project 
portfolio management efficiency chiefly when it has to do with the information needed by the decision-
makers. This particular factor proved to be of greater importance than the goals definition or even the 
systematic decision-making that also constitute highly substantial elements (Martinsuo & Lehtonen, 
2007). 
In terms of the goal definition influential factor, it is associated vicariously to the portfolio management 
efficiency through both project management efficiency and also through the achievement of the 
individual project goals. Concerning the project managers, the individual project goal definition has to 
be broadened to a portfolio extent (Martinsuo & Lehtonen, 2007). 
In particular, according to Matinsuo & Lehtonen (2007), the relation of the “systematic decision-
making” influential factor to the portfolio management efficiency is quite complex. What is more, they 
proved that the factor with the dominant impact on portfolio management efficiency is project 
management efficiency. This strong influential relationship lies in the fact that the major elements 
concerning the way of handling single projects should be embraced into the methodology of portfolio 
management. 
It is important to highlight the fact that the major element of the “single-project management” which 
contributes to the portfolio management efficiency is information sharing and availability among the 
key project stakeholders. Therefore, the upper management of the organizations should devote a lot of 
their focus on the establishment of a robust linkage between the single-project management capabilities 
and the ultimate goal of portfolio management efficiency. In the same context, it is profoundly crucial 
that a flexibility of resources and knowledge sharing exists across the different projects that comprise 
the portfolio (Nobeoka & Cusumano, 1995, 1997). Nevertheless, Matinsuo & Lehtonen (2006) 
underline that the individuality of each project should not be eliminated mostly in terms of optimizing 
the utilization of its resources with the aim of achieving its goals. Furthermore, the provision of the 
essential resources for the needs of single projects can play an important role at first for the acceleration 
of the project execution phase and ultimately for the effective management of the portfolio as a whole. 
The aforementioned dilemma troubles all the specialists of the portfolio management field and its 
difficulty of comprehension from their side renders it an insoluble task (Martinsuo, 2013).  
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
Another aspect which is relevant to be mentioned is the fact that portfolio managers should be strongly 
focused on the potential risks and uncertainties which may arise throughout the lifetime of the portfolio 
as the most common of them like schedule and cost alterations are often occurring before the 
commencement of the portfolio analysis events (Martinsuo, 2013). Therefore, extreme attention should 
be given to the budget excess which can occur from year to year. According to Christiansen and Varnes 
(2008), the key changes that are necessary for the optimization of portfolio management can have a 
diverse impact on the “logic” of the PPM system in terms of emphasizing mostly on political and 
spontaneous processes rather than reasonable ones.  
One of the major mistakes that are being made during the portfolio management process is the 
individual project selection together with the resources allocation are being operated according to the 
portfolio standards and hence wrong assumptions are generated such as the belief that one pool of 
resources which is under the complete control of the organization services all the projects (Artto et al, 
2004). 
To sum up, the knowledge and information availability together with the sharing of the requisite 
resources comprise the most influential factors for the implementation of effective project portfolio 
management. In addition, the devotion of the upper management to the alignment of the different project 
needs in terms of schedule and cost constitutes a crucial element towards the efficient management of 
the portfolio budget and therefore the effective carry-over management.  
3.2.4 PPM in the public sector  
It is crucial to have a look at the specificities of the application of PPM in the public sector in order to 
understand the objectives and value perception associated with project portfolio management.  
Firstly, among the major goals of public organizations that they are trying to achieve through the 
effective implementation of project portfolio management is the maximization of the benefits towards 
both the directly influenced stakeholders such as the members of a community or a municipality and 
the circuitous affected agents (Duarte & Reis, 2006).  
Similarly, according to Baker and Solak (2014), public sector success is interwoven with the provision 
of social benefits and entitlements. However, Stentoft et al. (2015) defined political agents and the 
collaboration with partners as the major influential factors for the efficient implementation of portfolio 
management.  
At the start of the 1980s, the public sector embraced the widespread known New Public Management 
(NPM) technique which was initially used in the private sector. The main reason behind the adoption 
of this practice was the huge need of the public administration for prompt and positive results with 
==PAGE:26==
18 
 
regards to the management of public affairs. This was the time when project portfolio management was 
introduced to the public sector (Young et al, 2012). 
Also, Young et al. (2012) pinpoint the fact that the analysis of the success criteria of PPM to the public 
sector can be proved a quite demanding task due to the complicated environment in terms of the 
assortment of aims of the public organizations and also the diversity of the engaged stakeholders. 
Martinsuo (2013) highlights that project portfolio management is interlinked with a couple of distinct 
tendencies. In particular, it is quite common for public organizations to execute the decision-making 
process which is part of the project portfolio management based on political interests and predefined 
directions. Therefore, the selection of the projects which constitute the portfolio is lacking the essential 
planning attention and is mainly influenced by exogenous factors that are not directly controlled by the 
organization itself. Hence, the extreme importance of taking into serious consideration the social aspects 
of portfolio management for the sake of achieving efficient portfolio performance is emphasized 
(Martinsuo, 2013). 
Another propensity of the portfolio management practices is the connection of the management 
strategies of the individual projects to the competencies and the way of working of both the project 
managers and the upper management of the organization. These particular stakeholders and their 
practices can determine the outcome of the portfolio management process as their decisions can be 
proved either beneficial or pernicious for individual projects (Martinsuo, 2013). 
Therefore, we can reach the conclusion that the application of PPM in the public sector is strongly 
driven by pre-determined political incentives and heavily influenced by the various stakeholders who 
are engaged in the management of the organization portfolio.  
3.2.5 Success of project portfolio management  
This subsection looks at the success criteria associated with the application of PPM with a view to 
understanding the influential factors behind the implementation of this practice.  
It is well established that portfolio management success is indissolubly related to the organizational 
context. Therefore, elements such as the complexity, the dynamics across the various functions and the 
engaged stakeholders and the way an organization is managed constitute crucial factors that actively 
contribute to the achievement of portfolio management success (Martinsuo 2013). 
Martinsuo (2013) pinpointed the necessity of the familiarization with the dynamics of risks, 
uncertainties, and changes that are associated with the portfolio of projects. Additionally, it is 
imperative that they are being taken into consideration during the project portfolio management 
practice. In the same context, Olsson (2008) emphasizes that it is quite common that the various projects 
of the portfolio are linked to mutual risks which can contribute to the generation of crucial issues for 
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
the whole portfolio, such as the excessive budget accumulation which can give rise to the carry-over 
phenomenon. Due to this fact, portfolio managers should not neglect them. Moreover, Petit and Hobbs 
(2010) also emphasized the importance of devoting a lot of focus on the tracking of the aforementioned 
elements and especially the high uncertainties due to their high impact on the management of the 
portfolio. Furthermore, their study together with Biedenback and Müller (2012) present PPM as a 
medium to comprehend and familiarize with the external information which is essential for both the 
decision-making process and the customization of the portfolio in order to follow the dynamic 
environment.  
In addition, Martinsuo (2013) investigated the influential relationship that exists between people and 
organizations both internally and externally. Despite the fact that this particular influence has been 
investigated throughout the years in terms of introducing novel decision-making processes, appointing 
both resources and responsibilities to the individual projects and involving upper management to the 
portfolio management, a knowledge gap which requires further research still exists concerning the 
influential interrelationships between the key stakeholders. In other words, a lot of the focus of the 
portfolio managers is assigned to the prioritization of the projects while less attention is being paid to 
the way stakeholders exert influence to each other with a view to managing any relevant type of 
resources for the sake of the project prioritization (Patanakul & Milosevic, 2008). Hence, the budget 
allocation can be neglected in a way that leads to the defective management of the projects funds and 
therefore the generation of carry-over.  
Another important requirement stands for the definition of the benefits that the organization aims to 
achieve through portfolio management and more importantly the provision of sufficient monetary and 
human resources with the purpose of analysing the portfolio data for the sake of decision-making (Shehu 
& Akintoye 2010). 
Furthermore, Shehu and Akintoye (2010) highlight that one of the most crucial elements with regards 
to the successful practice of portfolio management is the establishment of effective communication 
between the key stakeholders. The interchange of relevant to the various projects information across 
the different involved parties with the ultimate goal of controlling beliefs or decisions is inextricably 
associated with the achievement of effective communication. Moreover, the bigger the magnitude and 
the frequency of the change the greater the necessity of clear cross-functional communication in terms 
of the detection of the influential factors, the expected payoff and the proposed plan together with its 
potential outcome (Shehu & Akintoye 2010). 
The distinct approach of Gareis (2004) justified that “The Project Portfolio Database” constitutes a 
useful tool for the purpose of implementing project portfolio management in an efficient way. In 
particular, the significance of this tool stands for its capability of compiling data from the different 
projects that comprise the portfolio and hence rendering possible their comparison. The utilization of 
==PAGE:28==
20 
 
such a tool can assist an organization to identify the interconnections across the various projects and 
the distinct organizational levels. In this way the project needs in terms of budget can be identified early 
on and therefore it can contribute to the achievement of effective carry-over management. Nevertheless, 
it can be a quite challenging task, especially in large organizations to accumulate all the project data as 
they are usually spread out the various organizational units (Elonen & Artto, 2003). 
The ultimate aim of the focus on the existing interdependencies across the various projects would be 
the maximization of the group success objective instead of the individual project success. According to 
Patanakul & Milosevic (2008), it is best for the portfolio management to be applied to the operational 
level in order to make it easier for the project managers and all the other key stakeholders to associate 
the planning and coordination of the individual projects with the portfolio as a whole. Furthermore, the 
ulterior goal lies in the effective management of multiple projects through the adjustment and control 
of their distinct schedules and the efficient allocation of the needed resources; monetary or human. In 
this way, the life of the stakeholders and especially the project managers would become easier as the 
workload will be distributed according to the collective needs of the projects (Adler et al., 1996). In 
addition, the project delays would be mitigated with greater ease and this fact could decisively 
contribute to the aversion of budget transfer to the upcoming fiscal years. 
It is of great significance to highlight the fact that the “alignment, planning, coordination and execution” 
of the individual projects of the portfolio should be applied as precisely as possible otherwise the 
challenges that can be created from a defective project could potentially affect adversely firstly the rest 
of the projects and ultimately the portfolio as a whole. Therefore, the cross-functional coordination of 
each project is imperative to constitute a high priority of the portfolio managers. In addition, the lack 
of competencies and essential knowledge related to the portfolio management practice, risk 
management practice and financial control can be proved pernicious for the implementation of portfolio 
management in the construction industry (Milosevic et al., 2009) (Parr & Williams, 2004). 
Abraham (2003) states that the traditional success approach of project portfolio management 
particularly in the construction sector dictates that extreme focus should be placed on the timely 
fulfillment of the planning and execution phases of the projects. Therefore, successful organizations are 
characterized by their capability to complete the aforementioned phases without major delays and at the 
same time achieve to follow the initial budget plan and also deliver the required quality to the client. 
Hence, in case the delivery of some projects is delayed this will generate multiple challenges to the 
portfolio management practice (Abraham, 2003). Among these challenges is the alteration of the budget 
allocation among the various projects of the portfolio. 
Inferentially, we can see that the successful implementation of PPM and therefore the effective 
management of the projects budget and ultimately of the carry-over strongly depends on the 
understanding of the uncertainties related to the various projects and the effective communication 
==PAGE:29==
21 
 
among the key organizational stakeholders. Also, the establishment of robust interconnections across 
the projects of the portfolio constitutes a decisive factor in the optimization of the management of the 
projects budgets.  
3.2.6 Performance management  
This subchapter looks at the concept of performance management for the reason that it constitutes a 
medium for the achievement of the effective project portfolio management and thus can ultimately lead 
to the optimization of the budget management of projects. Therefore, research was conducted on the 
literature concerning the success criteria of this practice and its application in the construction sector. 
To begin with, the main focus of the performance management systems is placed on the effective 
management of the various processes that are related to PPM. Every successful organization is aware 
of the fact that a robust performance management system can constitute the difference between success 
and failure. In particular, the organization is given the opportunity to track its progress concerning the 
achievement of the set goals and objectives, establish a “quantitative decision-making” context, develop 
practices for the purpose of assessing its employees in a more effective and objective manner and last 
but not least enhance the quality of the provided services (Kazaz et al., 2019). 
Moreover, performance management comprises a management tool which includes multiple elements 
such as pre-defined objectives, targets, key performance indicators, rewards, continuous assessment, 
etc. with the ultimate goal of boosting the commitment of the key stakeholders and at the same time 
help them find their latent capabilities in order to become more efficient (Kazaz et al., 2019). 
Performance management can be also described as a procedure that acts as a medium for the 
organization to assess the results of the portfolio implementation by taking into consideration the degree 
to which the objectives and targets were fulfilled. An additional more specific definition for 
performance management for business exists and pinpoints it as “a strategic and integrated approach” 
which serves the purpose of maximizing the organizational effectiveness through the enhancement of 
not only the employees’ efficiency but also the advancement of the various parties cooperative 
capabilities (Armstrong & Baron, 1998). 
A deeply related practice to the aforementioned concept is the performance measurement through which 
the actual performance level of the organization is revealed by evaluating its effectiveness concerning 
the most vital activities that take place inside an organization and more specifically during the 
management of the organization portfolio. Such activities can be the management of the portfolio 
budget and cash flow but also the schedule management of the individual projects. Moreover, the 
fundamental principles of this particular practice are based on the successful identification and handling 
of the challenges that may arise throughout the projects lifetime (Kazaz et al., 2019). 
==PAGE:30==
22 
 
Furthermore, it has been proved that performance measurement and assessment should occur on an 
ongoing basis based on the initially defined key performance indicators and objectives. In this way, a 
meticulous and impartial juxtaposition of the available data can take place (Kazaz et al., 2019). 
3.2.6.1 
 Performance management in the construction sector 
After investigating the general application of performance management in the context of an 
organization, it is indispensable to have a closer look at the performance management practice in the 
construction industry.  
To begin with, it is essential for organizations that specialize in the construction sector to execute 
performance measurement in the context of the projects that comprise their portfolios with a view to 
finding solutions concerning the accomplishment of their objectives. The established targets include the 
schedule, budget and work distribution that are associated with each project (Kazaz et al., 2019). 
Furthermore, it is fundamental for construction-oriented organizations to adopt robust performance 
management systems with a view to adjusting to the continuously varying environment of the 
construction sector. Additionally, the contribution of the performance management practice to the 
attainment of the desired project goals and objectives is prominent (Kazaz et al., 2019). 
Moreover, the performance measurement practice which is applied for projects constitutes an indicator 
of the degree that each individual project conforms to its objectives. The traditional project management 
practice introduces success as a condition that is achieved when the key performance indicators; quality, 
cost and time reach their optimal state. In particular, the organization has to deliver to the client a high-
quality project, with the least expenditure and in a timely manner. Hence, the major requisite for the 
successful implementation of a project is the correspondence between its management and the pre-
defined feasible objectives. A couple of common pitfalls related to project performance are both the 
determination of utopian objectives and the inefficient management of the project (Navon & Sacks, 
2007). In case the former constitutes the cause of the performance divergence then the objectives should 
be reconsidered through the update of the historical database whereas if the latter is the case then it is 
the project manager’s responsibility to take steps closer to the performance target state (Kazaz et al., 
2019). 
Currently, plenty of methods exist in regard to the project performance measurements with the ulterior 
aim of rendering the project managers and the rest of the engaged stakeholders capable of getting control 
of the following challenges that are commonly observed during the management of projects (Kazaz et 
al., 2019):  
• 
Identification of the major factors that lead to both time and cost overrun and the project 
activities that are related to them  
==PAGE:31==
23 
 
• 
Constant tracking of the budget allocation and expenditure  
• 
Estimation of the final budget deviation in case budget overrun or carry-over is generated at the 
initial stages of the project  
• 
Detection of potential defective practices early enough in order to ensure their timely 
adjustment 
 
To sum up, it is evident from the literature findings of this chapter that successful project portfolio 
management is indissolubly associated with the optimization of management processes of the 
organization such as the schedule and budget management of the various projects of the portfolio. This 
is mainly achieved through the familiarization of the key stakeholders with the latent uncertainties and 
risks related to the projects and also through the empowerment of the communication and the 
information sharing across the organization. Although there is a missing link between the success of 
the portfolio management and the optimization of carry-over management, it can be deduced that this 
situation can affect indirectly in a positive way the management of carry-over through the optimization 
of project budgeting and planning. Finally, the performance management practice constitutes one of the 
suggested practices from the literature for the optimization of PPM, the basis of which is the continuous 
assessment of the organizational performance in terms of the accomplishment of its major objectives.

3.3 Project Risk Management  
As it was concluded in the previous section, the effective management of the carry-over heavily depends 
on the key stakeholders’ comprehension of the various risks concerning both the financial and the 
scheduling activities related to the various projects of an organizational portfolio. Therefore, it was 
deemed necessary to investigate the literature concerning the impact of risk management towards the 
effective management of portfolios and thus the optimization of carry-over management.  
3.3.1 Value creation through project risk management  
The topic of this particular subchapter is associated with the way that risk management adds value to 
projects. Additionally, it points to suggested risk management practices that lead to the successful 
implementation of project and portfolio management. 
To begin with, Willumsen et al. (2019) highlight that the major contribution of risk management to 
projects is the value enhancement. Additionally, it should be noted that the involved stakeholders’ value 
interpretation constitutes a key factor concerning the value creation through project risk management 
as its perception is commonly subjective (Willumsen et al., 2019).  
Furthermore, according to ISO 31000 (2018), the fundamental principle of risk management is the 
“creation and protection of value”. Therefore, it comprises an extremely important practice that should 
not be implemented as a “tick-the-box” process. Due to the indistinct nature of value creation, it is 
broken down into two components; content and process. The former determines “what is value” and 
the latter “how value is created” (Lepak et al., 2007). The content of the value creation is based on the 
fact that each stakeholder owns a distinct perspective in terms of the various project-related factors 
gravity (Krane et al., 2012). The value creation process rests upon the concept that value comprises the 
driving force for the attainment of valued goals. Hence, project risk management generates value for 
various organizational processes such as project outputs and outcomes and thus decisively contributes 
both to the project success achievement and the capitalization of plenty strategic benefits (Willumsen 
et al., 2019)  
However, according to Willumsen et al. (2019), the establishment of an immediate relation between 
project risk management and project success can be proved quite puzzling due to miscellaneous reasons. 
In particular, the underlying uncertainty with regards to the expected impact of project risk management 
to project success is one of the main factors (Besner & Hobbs, 2012). Additionally, it is extremely 
difficult to identify if the only influential factor in terms of the project uncertainty generation is the 
project risk management or if supplementary determinants exist (Bannerman, 2008; Besner & Hobbs, 
2012).  Moreover, project risk management contributes to value creation through the actuation of crucial 
processes in the organization (Oehmen et al, 2014) that can play a decisive role in the determination of 
the project outcome and ultimately in the achievement of project success (Besner & Hobbs, 2012). So, 
==PAGE:33==
25 
 
this circuitous relation empowers the adversity of associating directly PRM to project success. 
Furthermore, the distinct perceptions of the various stakeholders in terms of what is important for the 
project advancement render the value measurement of project risk management even more complicated 
(Laursen & Svejvig, 2016).  
Willumsen et al. (2019) came up with an important finding in terms of the project stakeholders’ interests 
concerning the value generated through project risk management; project managers commonly pay 
closer attention to the project objectives while the project owners focus mainly on the strategic 
objectives. Therefore, there is a divergence between each stakeholder’s comprehension of value and 
risk.  
One concept which is widely recognized by the risk management experts as a fundamental “value-
adding” practice is the transparency generation (Aven, 2012, 2016). According to the risk management 
community, transparency in terms of risk exposure can play a decisive role concerning both decision-
making and the avoidance of any pitfalls throughout the project life. Hence, these particular conditions 
can potentially foster the effective application of project risk management. In particular, the boundless 
internal information sharing can facilitate effective PRM at the portfolio level. In addition, high levels 
of transparency contribute to the further mitigation of risk exposure. However, excessive transparency 
can engender management attention which in most cases leads to risk attraction (Willumsen et al., 
2019). 
On the other hand, according to Willumsen et al. (2019) findings, low transparency can be also proved 
beneficial for the protection and preservation of strategic outcomes in the boundaries of the organization 
and therefore comprise leverage for the communication and negotiations with the clients.  
The study of Willumsen et al. (2019) came up with beneficial conclusions in terms of the usefulness of 
risk reporting in portfolio management. More specifically, this particular project assessment method 
contributes to the enhancement of the communication of the divergent parties that constitute 
management and additionally empowers the learning process which is based on the insights gained from 
the completed projects. Ultimately, risk reporting actively participates in the value creation for 
enterprises, projects and their strategic outcomes.  
An additional concept which is proved to be extremely valuable for project management is 
standardization. In particular, through standardization, it is possible to establish effective and 
continuous communication between the involved project stakeholders such as the project managers, the 
senior management and the financial managers. Moreover, flexibility is perceived as a crucial tool 
towards the achievement of the effective communication between project managers and upper 
management which leads to efficacious and optimized decision-making (Willumsen et al., 2019). 
==PAGE:34==
26 
 
According to Willumsen et al. (2019), it is imperative to link value creation with the subjective value 
perception that each stakeholder holds. Hence, project risk management should be adjusted to the 
existing conditions and aims otherwise it will not be an adequate value creation method. 
To sum up, the benefits of project risk management in terms of value creation and contribution to the 
optimization of project and portfolio management practices such as the budget and schedule 
management of the various projects is strongly dependent on the subjective value perceptions of the 
key stakeholders.  
3.3.2 Requirements for efficient risk management  
The risk management practice is mainly based on the concept that the project context can be 
reconsidered when unexpected incidents occur and also aims at providing a robust framework for the 
re-development of the project management process (Jaafari, 2001). 
In most organizations, risk management is performed intuitively and based on experience by the key 
stakeholders who are trying to establish a harmonic relationship between identified risks and 
opportunities. Thus, the need for systematic models for the execution of risk management that conforms 
with the aforementioned traditional way is evident (Kähkönen & Artto, 2000). According to Olsson 
(2001), the identification of uncertainties in the context of single projects can be proved quite defective 
when they are part of a bigger organizational portfolio. For this reason, a more portfolio-oriented 
viewpoint should be used in two ways. In particular, all the project phases should be taken into 
consideration irrespective of time or stage of implementation. Additionally, it is imperative that the 
functional organization is assimilated in the project risk management process with a view to involving 
all the relevant stakeholders throughout the multiple layers of the organization. 
Also, Olsson (2008) pinpoints the deficiency of the individual project perspective which is adopted 
from most of the existing risk management practices. Therefore, he underlines the necessity of moving 
towards a more collective mindset which will take into consideration the various interactions between 
the distinct projects that constitute part of an organizational portfolio (Olsson, 2008). Another crucial 
point which is touched upon by Ward and Chapman (1995) is the fact that high priority is given to the 
risks mainly originating from the nature of the project while the potential risks connected with the 
project management procedure are neglected. 
Concisely, among the major requisites for the successful application of risk management is the adoption 
of a broader portfolio perspective and the involvement of all the engaged stakeholders. In this way, 
everyone who is contributing to the management of the individual projects will become capable of 
identifying any potential risks or uncertainties that may arise during the projects’ lifecycle.  
==PAGE:35==
27 
 
3.3.3 Portfolio risk analysis methodology 
This subchapter serves the purpose of presenting both the requisites and the suggested steps towards 
the implementation of risk analysis in the portfolio context and also its potential benefits for the 
organization. 
The fundamental concept where the practice of portfolio risk management is based on is the fact that 
an element which is considered as a risk for a particular project can act as an opportunity for one or 
even multiple other projects of the same portfolio that are in a different stage of their lifecycle (Olsson, 
2018). 
Among the main prerequisites for the implementation of effective analysis of the risks that the portfolio 
is exposed to is the accessibility to both the data concerning the risks that each individual project is 
facing and the project-related data that can lead to the discovery of analogous and identical projects. In 
addition, the competence and ease of not only identifying but also analysing this kind of data are of 
great significance (Olsson, 2018). 
According to Olsson (2018), the recommended steps of a successful portfolio risk analysis include 
firstly the examination of the existing project-related issues. Then, the analysis of each individual 
project deficiencies together with the risks-associated data concerning the rest of the projects should 
take place. This particular step has to be applied to every project. Next, the analysis should comprise of 
the risk-data related to all the projects of the organizational portfolio.  
In terms of the benefits deriving from the application of portfolio risk analysis, Olsson (2018) highlights 
that this particular process could constitute the organization aware of the multiple uncertainties that are 
linked to the project environment and the interrelationships between the risks associated with the 
different projects. Hence, the provision of feedback and internal knowledge will be available concerning 
other organizational projects and applied risk practices. What is more, the visualization of the different 
risks is rendered possible which can ultimately aid both the prompt risk identification and mitigation 
but also the deduction of specific risk patterns.  
In conclusion, risk management practice can play a decisive role in the successful implementation of 
project and portfolio management under certain conditions such as the engagement of all the involved 
stakeholders and the consideration of a portfolio context. As soon as the prerequisites are fulfilled, risk 
management practice can lead to the efficient identification of risks throughout the organizational 
portfolio and to their timely mitigation. Lastly, it can contribute to the organizational learning 
concerning the different risks and uncertainties that may arise.

3.4 Cash flow management  
The last section of the literature review is devoted to the investigation of the influence that cash flow 
management can exert on the management of the carry-over phenomenon through the effective budget 
management. Therefore, it is deemed necessary to focus both on the factors that can determine the 
accuracy of project cash flow predictions and on the recommended cash flow management practices.  
3.4.1 Importance for construction projects  
This particular subsection looks at the impact of cash flow management on the budget management of 
construction projects with a view to understanding the deeper connections between this particular 
practice and the generation of carry-over.  
To begin with, Sharifi and Bagherpour (2016) define the project cash flow as the amount of money 
which comprises of the expenditure, the project cost, and the accumulated revenues. In other words, it 
constitutes the difference between the spent and the allocated budget throughout an exact period of the 
project lifecycle. It is crucial to highlight how important the timely and accurate forecast of the cash 
flow is, in particular, during the tendering stage since faulty estimations at this phase could render the 
project vulnerable to either paucity or budget excess in the long term.  
According to Al-Issa and Zayed (2007), project cash flows are mainly affected by two major factors; 
cash preservation and project span. In particular, the former one concerns the adequate cash inflow 
which can guarantee the availability of the needed funds for the project owner in case the contractor is 
proved incapable of carrying through the project and therefore requires some kind of further aid for 
completing some parts of the project. In terms of the latter factor, the cash circulation is directly 
influenced by the alteration of the project duration especially in the case of big projects. 
As we already saw from the previous sections of the literature review, construction projects are 
commonly characterized by a high degree of uncertainty and intricacy. What is more, this kind of 
projects are linked to various risks mostly due to their costly and resource-demanding nature and also 
because of the scarcity of valuable information concerning the budget estimation. It can be easily 
deduced that the inefficient management of these projects from a financial perspective can lead to their 
failure. Therefore, extreme focus should be placed on the project cash flow management. However, due 
to the previously mentioned reasons, the precise forecast of the project cash flow has been proved a 
quite challenging task throughout the years (Sharifi & Bagherpour, 2016). 
Furthermore, Sharifi and Bagherpour (2016) pinpoint that project cash flow constitutes a critical 
influential factor for the project especially during the execution phase and before its completion. 
Another element that proves its indisputable significance is the impact of its forecasting on the decision-
making process conducted by the upper management. Hence, the less attention is paid to the forecast 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
and the management of the project and portfolio cash flow the greatest is the probability for the 
organization to fail to encounter economical nature disorders and “negative” cash flows during the 
projects’ life span (Kaka & Price, 1993). Similarly, this can have a direct negative influence on the 
accurate prediction of the amount of funds that could be transferred to the next financial year.     
From the above, it can be deduced that the cash flow management practice is quite challenging as it can 
be affected by factors such as the availability of funds or the project duration that rarely remain static 
throughout the project duration. Additionally, the successful prediction of the projects cash flow inside 
an organizational portfolio is indissolubly connected to their successful management and therefore the 
control of the carry-over phenomenon.  
3.4.2 Cash flow management practices 
After investigating the significance of cash flow management for the effective handling of the projects’ 
budget, it is imperative to take a close look at elements and practices that lead to the efficacious cash 
flow management application. 
Sharifi and Bagherpour (2016) highlight the fact that an effective cash flow management process 
comprises various steps. First of all, the project time horizon should be defined as this can play a 
decisive role concerning the next steps. Afterwards, the assignment of cost elements to each individual 
activity of the project should take place through the utilization of the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) 
tool. Then, it is of great importance to track down the various influential risk-related factors that can 
have an impact on the project cash flow and subsequently classify them as “deterministic” and 
“probabilistic” elements. In terms of the probabilistic analysis, the risk identification comprises the 
fundamental step based on which the robustness of risk management with regards to cash flow 
management can be achieved. However, in case this crucial part of the process is not carefully executed 
then this may result in the neglection of some risk factors that can, later on, evolve into grievous issues 
that the organization will not be capable of resolving. After becoming aware of the uncertainties that 
can influence the project cash flow, the subsequent step constitutes the risk analysis. This particular part 
of the project contributes to the quantification of the effect that the identified elements have on the cash 
flow. It is crucial to highlight the gravity of the credibility of the information that comprises the input 
for the risk analysis and ultimately for the cash flow management process.  
One of the major organizational challenges associated with the project cash flow is the variance that is 
often observed between the real and the predicted cash flow. According to Zayed & Liu (2014), a 
profoundly effective tool for the regulation of the financial flows of an organization is the cash flow 
diagram. The aim of this diagram is the provision of a clear view of the project cash flow throughout 
its life span to the financial department of the organization. Through the utilization of this tool, valuable 
conclusions can be deduced with regard to the financial state of the project and its sub-processes. In 
==PAGE:38==
30 
 
particular, the key stakeholders will be able to promptly determine if the project is exceeding or falls 
short of budget and identify the processes which are running ahead or behind schedule. Hence, effective 
cash flow management can be performed through the deterrence of excessive expenditure and project 
delays. 
Hence, from the aforementioned literature findings, it can be concluded that the successful 
implementation practices of cash flow management strongly depend on the timely familiarization of the 
stakeholders with the various risks and uncertainties related to the availability of funds for the 
organizational projects.   
3.5 Literature Review Inferences  
In conclusion, the analysis of the above literature concerning the carry-over phenomenon and its 
correlation to project portfolio management, risk management and cash flow management contributed 
to the attainment of valuable insights concerning its effective management. Although, it also led to the 
detection of literature gaps in regard to the under-investigation topic.  
More specifically, among the major deductions is the fact that the inefficient management of the carry-
over can potentially lead to the disorder of the budget distribution among the projects of the public 
organizations portfolios. Moreover, the robust internal communication and the knowledge sharing 
across the organization proved to be strong influential factors with regards to the optimization of the 
portfolio management practice and hence the enhancement of the financial planning of projects 
activities. In addition, it was found that the active engagement of all key stakeholders to the tracking 
process of risks in a portfolio context constitutes an imperative requisite for the successful 
implementation of project portfolio management. Furthermore, it was concluded that the effective 
management of carry-over is connected to the precise cash flow forecast through the analysis of the 
several ambiguity factors that are related to the projects.  
However, a literature gap was identified concerning specific processes oriented towards the key project 
and organizational stakeholders with the aim of achieving their perspectives’ alignment regarding the 
potential challenges that can arise during the project. The optimization of budget and hence carry-over 
management practices are heavily dependent on the attainment of the aforementioned objective.

4.1 Data Collection  
The Data Collection section outlines the reasons why the execution of interviews was selected as the 
medium for data collection and then details the process concerning both their preparation and their 
implementation. 
4.1.1 Interviews as the data collection method  
The data collection method which was implemented in the context of this thesis was the conduction of 
interviews. This specific method was deemed appropriate for the purpose of gaining valuable insights 
into the distinct perspectives of the key stakeholders of the municipality with regards to the budget and 
cash flow management processes that are currently followed. Moreover, in this way, it was rendered 
possible to gain a deeper understanding of the challenges that the municipality is facing in terms of the 
carry-over phenomenon through the collection of the subjective viewpoints of the interviewees.  
4.1.2 Interviewees selection 
It was fundamental that the selection of the interviewees was based on their level of influence and 
involvement in terms of the aforementioned project and portfolio management processes and the related 
challenges. Therefore, the stakeholders who were selected for the conduction of the interviews were 
two of the project managers, the department manager, and the division manager of GE together with 
one of the financial controllers of the finance department. In particular, it was imperative to interview 
the project managers due to their major participation in the planning of the different project phases and 
in crucial reporting processes. It was also important to have the financial controller’s viewpoints due to 
his big role in the financial coordination of the municipality projects and his active cooperation with 
the PMs. Also, the department manager was chosen due to her role both as a moderator for the PMs and 
a communication link between them and the division manager. Finally, the division manager was 
selected as an interviewee not only because of his decision-making power but also due to his broader 
perspective regarding the municipality processes.

into consideration. A short description of the themes of the interviews follows:

Budget and cash flow management

5.1.1 Association with the different parties 
This particular subtheme looks at both the perceptions of the importance of the carry-over phenomenon 
and the responsibilities of the different parties towards it.  
To begin with, the financial controller argued that although carry-over does not affect directly the 
project managers, the upper management is extremely concerned if the financial fixed ceiling is reached 
in terms of the budget spent during each financial year. So, he advocated that this is mainly a “political 
and upper management problem” and the latter party is the one which should explain to the former one 
the reasons behind the potential anomalies regarding the annual expenditure. Thus, as he claims, “the 
blame will be placed to the division manager as he is the head of the GE department”.  
One of the project managers argued that in many cases they need to be badgered in terms of delivering 
an accurate estimation for the carry-over. He also alleged that the carry-over identification and 
management do not comprise a big part of the PMs’ daily operations and responsibilities as they have 
plenty of other tasks to work on. Furthermore, he highlighted the fact that the prioritization and 
allocation of the project funds do not constitute a theme in his work. As he mentioned, “I am not really 
concerned if the money is being spent on the current or the next fiscal year”. 
The second project manager who was interviewed claimed that carry-over is not a great concern for her 
as she has a budget for every project which is allocated throughout the various months of the project 
life. Moreover, she stated that although she has a good understanding of the fact that the estimations 
==PAGE:52==
44 
 
that she generates constitute an extremely important part for the whole municipality in terms of its 
economy robustness, she argues that due to the high uncertainty which is associated to the project delays 
it is quite difficult to forecast early in the year the magnitude of the carry-over for the next year. 
Concerning the impact of the inaccurate carry-over forecasting, she mentioned that “there are no direct 
consequences for us, but it is the upper management that gets the hit”.  
Additionally, the project manager claimed that she is not totally sure which is the problem associated 
with the carry-over phenomenon. According to her, the fact that it is quite tough to comprehend the 
process concerning all the political work together with the focus alteration of the municipality 
throughout the years lead to the generation of confusion among the various parties. Furthermore, she 
highlighted the fact that she is concerned regarding the carry-over only during the meetings with the 
financial controllers who labeled them as the main responsible for the handling of carry-over. Finally, 
she mentioned on the same line as the first project manager that the PMs are not so concerned if the 
budget is spent in one year or another and also that they are not directly affected by the inefficient 
management of the carry-over but it is the upper management that is mostly influenced negatively. 
The department manager expressed her willingness to render herself more involved in the management 
of the carry-over as she purports to have a distinct view on the projects which is mainly based on her 
deeper political understanding. What is more, in her opinion the project managers tend to analyze the 
projects in a more optimistic way in terms of what they can actually achieve and also concerning the 
identification of the carry-over in the early phases of the project.  
The division manager stated that the divergence in the comprehension of the projects finances in many 
cases leads to a situation where the project managers have a great amount of money left at the end of 
the year which they have to either spend in a relatively small amount of time or needs to be transferred 
to the next year as carry-over. Additionally, he emphasized that the change of culture inside the 
municipality would be quite important in order to assist the project managers in their tasks of performing 
more accurate forecasts in terms of budget and carry-over estimates. 
5.1.2 Carry-over management  
This subtheme looks at the potential ways of managing and handling effectively the carry-over of the 
municipality projects from the interviewees’ perspective.  
Firstly, the financial controller believed that the Power BI integration will have a decisive effect on the 
tracking of the cash flow and expenditure throughout the projects lifecycle.  
The first project manager advocated that the constantly changing pace of the projects together with the 
unsteady cash flows constitute a big nuisance in terms of identifying the exact amount of budget which 
has to be transferred to the following year. Thus, he argued that the utilization of a steering tool for the

This particular subtheme looks at the current forecasting processes concerning carry-over prediction 
which are utilized at Gentofte Ejendomme and their margin for improvement according to the

Firstly, the financial controller highlighted the great importance that the integration of a “forecasting

the achievement of a precise forecast. Nevertheless, the year-ahead predictions comprise a truly 
challenging task according to him. Lastly, he noted that “it is profoundly important to be able to forecast 
both budget and schedule ahead of time” as the upper management needs to have access not only to an 
updated current status but also to some credible estimates for the next years.

6 Discussion  
The next step after the presentation and quotation of the interviews findings is the in-depth analysis of 
these insights with a view to identifying and analysing the focal areas. In particular, the first focal area 
which was observed is related to the stakeholders’ perceptions concerning the carry-over phenomenon 
and more importantly its importance and management practice. The second focal area is associated with 
the information sharing and the distinct perspectives and needs of the key stakeholders. Next, the 
subsequent part of the discussion looks into the potential benefits of Power BI application for the 
confrontation of the various challenges stemming from the implications of the investigated focal areas. 
Also, the existing limitations of this tool are presented at the end of the chapter.  
6.1 First Focal Area 
This particular chapter comprises of three subchapters. The first one discusses the interviewees’ 
perceptions of the carry-over phenomenon and their importance for the municipality processes. Next, 
the carry-over management practices that are applied in the context of the municipality and more 
specifically in the GE department are investigated. The third subsection looks at the cash flow 
management processes which are utilized. The ultimate aim of this chapter is to come up with the 
implications that the aforementioned factors have for Gentofte municipality. Additionally, in each of 
the three subsections, the relevant literature review practices and findings are compared to the 
interviews findings.  
6.1.1 Perception of carry-over importance 
To begin with, we should highlight the fact that all the stakeholders argued that the project managers 
are not really engaged with the carry-over identification and further management. In particular, the 
financial controller mentioned that the management of carry-over constitutes mainly an upper 
management concern as they have to report the financial condition of the projects further up to the 
hierarchical scale and more importantly to the politicians.  
Furthermore, both project managers agreed on the fact that carry-over does not comprise a big priority 
across their daily tasks. This particular condition leads to the divergence of their apprehension of the 
great importance of the accurate carry-over forecasting compared to the rest key of the stakeholders. 
More specifically, they cannot comprehend why it is flawed for the municipality to end up with an 
excessive amount of money at the end of the year. Additionally, as they admitted they are not so 
concerned if the available allocated funds are going to be spent the current or the next financial year. 
Furthermore, they are focusing on the carry-over mostly when they have to prepare for the update 
meetings with the financial controllers.  
==PAGE:62==
54 
 
An additional declaration which strengthened this belief came from the division manager who argued 
that the lack of understanding of the projects finances from the PMs side is one of the main drivers 
towards the accumulation of excessive budget amounts at the end of the fiscal year. The direct 
consequence of this particular condition is either the overspending during the final months of the year 
or the generation of the carry-over which is transferred to the next year. Therefore, it can be easily 
deduced that the PMs are placing quite limited focus on the carry-over phenomenon and at the same 
time they apportion the responsibilities for its accurate prediction to the financial controllers.  
What is more, both PMs highlighted the high uncertainty which is associated with the forecast of the 
carry-over early on. Moreover, two important points which are concluded from their statements are not 
only their attempt to stay clear of the direct consequences that the inaccurate carry-over forecast can 
have to the department and ultimately to the municipality as a whole but also the assignment of the 
responsibilities regarding this particular matter to the upper management. This argument is reinforced 
by the department manager’s claim that the project managers follow a really optimistic approach 
concerning the project goals which can be achieved.  
This particular situation reflects the major challenge that Martin and Nicholls (1987) (see chapter 3.2.2) 
emphasized concerning the impact that the limited involvement from the upper management in the 
portfolio management can have on the increase of both the commitment and the interest of the 
stakeholders. As we can see the negative consequences of the aforementioned condition that Shehu and 
Akintove (2010) (see chapter 3.2.2) pinpointed are partially applied to this case as the management of 
carry-over is given less than the requisite attention. Hence, the focus should be given mainly on the 
project managers with a view to empowering their perspective towards the importance of carry-over 
management.  
In this way, it would be possible to align the management of the carry-over with the way of thinking 
and working of the engaged stakeholders and more importantly the PMs. This kind of alignment can 
play a decisive role in the successful outcome of the whole portfolio management due to its great impact 
on the decision-making practices of the municipality as Martinsuo (2013) highlights (see chapter 3.2.4). 
6.1.2 Carry-over management practice  
It is relevant to emphasize the PMs’ concern regarding the contractors’ progress concerning the 
scheduling of the projects which can have a strong influence on the carry-over prediction. In the same 
context, the construction works and the payments freezing increase the uncertainty related to the 
accurate carry-over forecasting. Moreover, the continuous alteration of the projects pace combined with 
the constantly changing projects cash flows and the payment plan absence for some projects comprise 
supplementary factors that influence negatively the project managers’ performance and make them even 
more tentative in terms of the carry-over forecasting. Hence, this argument can be empowered by the 
==PAGE:63==
55 
 
statement of Martinsuo (2013) (see chapter 3.2.4) regarding the great influence that exogenous factors 
such as strong political interests can exert over the decision-making process related to the management 
of public organizations portfolios. A direct consequence of this situation is the inconsistent change of 
vital for the PPM process parameters such as the budget inflow and the schedule of the various projects 
that could lead to the defective forecast of the carry-over and ultimately to the problematic planning of 
the municipality portfolio.  
Moreover, the financial controller noted that a crucial factor that triggers the limited involvement of the 
PMs in the forecasting process is the inefficiency of the tool which is currently employed for this 
specific purpose at the GE department. Hence, it is of great importance to highlight his argument 
concerning the significance of engaging the PMs with “the money being spent”. In accordance with that 
are the statements of the PMs that the way currently the carry-over forecasting is performed; through 
the constant reviewing of the payment plan, does not help them either enhance their comprehension in 
regard to the carry-over prediction importance or improve the accuracy of their carry-over estimates. 
Hence, a salient need is observed in regard to the familiarization of the key stakeholders with the various 
risks and uncertainties that are associated with the prediction of the carry-over phenomenon through the 
utilization of a more dynamic tool.  
According to Petit and Hobbs (2010) (see chapter 3.2.5), the prompt detection of uncertainty factors 
such as the variation of the budget distribution among the different projects of the portfolio and the 
probable freezing of some projects due to exogenous reasons can play a determining role to the financial 
management of the portfolio. During the identification process of the aforementioned factors, the 
municipality should work based on a portfolio-oriented mindset as suggested by Olsson (2001) (see 
chapter 3.3.2) and therefore take into consideration all the project interrelationships irrespective of the 
stages they are currently in. What is more, it is imperative that all the key stakeholders participate in 
this process even though they do not possess risk management skills.  
What is more, the remark of the financial controller with regards to the absence of a proper forecasting 
method for the prediction of both expenditure and carry-over associated with the various projects of the 
GE portfolio is worth mentioning. It is also fundamentally crucial to emphasize the fact that distinct 
perspectives combined with expertise, experience and historical analysis are essential requisites for the 
execution of an accurate forecast. On the other hand, the PMs argued that user-involvement comprises 
a much more crucial factor compared to the actual forecasting for the effective management of both 
carry-over and the project budget and schedule in general. Therefore, this perspective is based on the 
fundamental rule of the active engagement of all the key stakeholders to the management of the projects 
and especially the participation of the internal clients and the actual users who mainly define the 
requirements and needs of each project.

Additionally, it would be relevant to highlight the argument of Lienert and Ljungman (2009) (see 
chapter 3.1.3) in terms of the negative effect that the ineffective management of the annual budget can 
have for the finance department and the importance of mitigating the risk of excessive budget 
accumulation at the end of the year. Moreover, as described in the same chapter (3.1.3), the 
establishment and utilization of powerful cash flow management initiatives for the sake of the accurate 
carry-over estimations can provide the municipality with a powerful tool for not only the optimization 
of their resource management but also the communication of the carry-over phenomenon criticality 
among the key stakeholders. 
The model that Fichtner et al. (2016) (see chapter 3.1.3) proposed concerning the prosecution of 
frequent budget audits twice or three times per fiscal year could be proved beneficial for the more 
efficient management of carry-over for both the PMs and the financial controllers as the former would 
be kept informed for the exact project finances throughout the whole year. This practice could also keep 
the PMs interest high regarding the carry-over generation issue during the portfolio lifetime and not just 
at the end of the financial year as it happens now.  
6.1.3 Cash flow management  
Concerning the cash flow management which is currently performed at GE, the majority of the 
stakeholders agreed on the fact that there is no certain process followed and the PMs further admitted 
that the establishment of a robust connection between the project schedule and budget for the 
enhancement of both cash flow and carry-over management will not be proved so beneficial for them 
directly. However, as Sharifi and Bagherpour (2016) pinpoint (see chapter 3.4.1), the accurate forecast 
of the portfolio cash flow comprises a medium for the timely tracking of potential financial 
abnormalities that can occur throughout the projects lifetime and thus can have a decisive effect on the 
success of the decision-making processes driven by the upper management. Therefore, there is an 
imperative need for more focus to be placed in this particular direction.  
The proposed cash flow process from Sharifi and Bagherpour (2016) (see chapter 3.4.2) could be proved 
extremely advantageous for the municipality. In particular, the initial definition of an explicit time 
horizon for each project and the utilization of the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) with a view to 
assigning cost values to the various project processes can help both the PMs and the financial controllers 
gain a good understanding of the projects finances. The next step which lacks the attention of the 
Gentofte municipality includes the identification and classification of the risk factors that are related to 
the different project phases. From the interview findings, it can be deduced that the focus which is 
placed on the timely prediction of the common risks of carry-over and schedule alteration of the projects 
is quite limited. Therefore, the delayed track of these factors leads to negative implications such as the 
accumulation of excessive amounts of money at the end of the year. 
==PAGE:65==
57 
 
Additionally, the utilization of the cash flow diagrams suggested by Zayed and Liu (2014) (see chapter 
3.4.2) could be proved extremely advantageous especially for the financial department of the 
municipality. More specifically, through the provision of a distinct view of the projects cash flow 
concerning the whole duration of their life cycle, not only the financial controllers but also the project 
managers would be able to come to quick inferences regarding the financial state of the projects. Hence, 
they will be capable of reporting promptly to the upper management any excess or shortfall of budget 
associated with the various projects and their phases.  
 
To sum up, the analysis of the first focal area led to the identification of the root causes which provoke 
the ineffective management of the carry-over. In particular, the root causes comprise the PMs’ 
perception of the carry-over importance, the defective current carry-over and cash flow practices and 
the ambiguous nature of the projects. It was concluded that the perception of PMs is mainly 
characterized by a tendency to place limited focus on the carry-over phenomenon and abnegate any 
responsibilities regarding carry-over management. This situation is indissolubly connected to the 
problematic forecasting practices that are currently utilized. Hence, among the direct implications are 
both the limited comprehension of the importance of the carry-over phenomenon and the development 
of inaccurate estimations that lead to the accumulation of excessive budget at the end of the financial 
year. Figure 7 represents the aforementioned interrelationships. Furthermore, the comparison with the 
literature review findings resulted in the detection of the most important factors behind the above root 
causes. More specifically, the non-utilization of practices for the sake of the timely risk identification 
and the accurate cash flow prediction together with the limited engagement of some stakeholders in the 
process of budget management are among the major ones. Figure 8 depicts the result of the comparison 
with the literature review practices and its relation to the root causes.

6.2 Second Focal Area 
This chapter comprises of two subsections. In particular, the first section analyses the information 
sharing processes as described by the interviewees with a view to identifying the major communication 
challenges. The second one looks at the distinct needs and perspectives of the key stakeholders of GE 
in order to detect the potential issues stemming from them. The relevant parts of the literature review 
are also presented in order to identify suitable practices.  
6.2.1 Information Sharing 
First of all, it is crucial to underline the concerns of the financial controller that the project managers 
are often not fully prepared for the meetings they have together. Therefore, the cost estimates regarding 
the different project phases which are provided from the PMs side are not the most accurate as possible. 
This particular factor influences directly and negatively the FCs’ capability of having the projects 
finances under control due to the unprecise nature of the estimations.  
Moreover, we should highlight the statements of the PMs concerning the outdated payment plans and 
financial data that constitute the basis for the budget and carry-over estimations that they deliver to the 
FCs. The access to the current and updated financial information is a vital element the absence of which 
leads to the defective communication between these two parties. In the same context, Martinsuo and 
Lehtonen (2007) highlight (see chapter 3.2.3) that information accessibility constitutes an essential 
factor for the successful management of the organizational portfolio. Additionally, according to Shehu 
and Akintove (2010) (see chapter 3.2.5), one of the major determinant factors for the accomplishment 
of the effective management of the organization portfolio is the establishment of effective 
communication between the key stakeholders through the unimpeded internal information sharing. 
Moreover, the concept of transparency generation could constitute a powerful tool for the municipality. 
In particular, Willumsen et al. (2019) pinpoint (see chapter 3.3.1) that transparency in the context of 
risk exposure can decisively contribute to the improvement of decision-making. According to this 
concept, the flexible sharing of information inside the municipality could minimize the chances of risk 
exposure such as the generation of carry-over.   
Another important fact that it was deduced through the findings is the need for readiness in terms of the 
answers that the upper management of GE provides to the politicians with regards to the budget and 
cash flow management. A supplementary element that is also of great significance is the updating 
process concerning the project status throughout its lifecycle that takes place between the division 
manager of GE and the upper management of Gentofte municipality. It is quite obvious that there is a 
lack of automation which constitutes this particular reporting process more enduring than it should. So, 
we can conclude that a communicational link is missing in the form of either a flexible tool or a 
procedure that will enhance the reporting process at the upper scales of the municipality. The 
==PAGE:69==
61 
 
standardization tool could render the communication of these parties much more flexible and further 
lead to the optimization of decision-making (Willumsen et al. 2019) (see chapter 3.3.1) 
An additional point which is extremely crucial for the internal operations of the whole municipality is 
the information sharing in the form of updates for the projects between GE and the other departments 
of the municipality; internal clients. There is observed a need for a timely and more accurate update 
process according to the division manager’s claims. What is more, it is profoundly imperative for the 
GE department to take into consideration the user-influence and user-involvement factors while 
executing this update process. In this way, the constantly changing needs and demands of the users will 
not be neglected and hence it is much more likely that the project outcome will satisfy them. 
Furthermore, another angle of the information sharing focal area is linked to human resources 
management with regards to the information concerning the project managers’ schedule. More 
specifically, the efficient allocation of resources among the various projects, in terms of experts such as 
project managers, depends to a great extent on the precision of the schedule information that is provided 
to the people responsible for handling the human resources of the department and in particular the 
department and division manager. The timing of this information provision is also of extreme 
importance and together with its accuracy can strongly facilitate the project scope definition. The 
aforementioned challenge of human resources allocation is becoming even harder due to the existence 
of strong political influence (Cooper et al. 2000) (see chapter 3.2.2).  
6.2.2 Distinct needs and perspectives  
To begin with, an issue that was mentioned by both the project managers and the division manager is 
the need for optimization and streamlining of the different processes taking place at GE. In particular, 
it is obvious from the interviews findings that misalignment issues occur quite often across the 
municipality between different parties such as the financial controllers and the project managers. The 
fact that the aforementioned stakeholders “do not speak the same language” may result in crucial 
miscommunication issues between them. Additionally, the limited political understanding from the PMs 
side also hinders the accurate internal information flow across the department and ultimately has led to 
the generation of significant carry-over amounts during the previous years. Moreover, a lot of confusion 
is created among the different key parties due to this paucity of comprehension concerning the political 
work and its constant alterations throughout the years. It is relevant to mention the argument of Cooper 
et al. (2000) (see chapter 3.2.2) regarding the negative impact that the political interests can exert over 
both the project prioritization and the decision-making processes that relate to the organizational 
portfolio.

6.3 Power BI application  
This chapter looks into the potential application of Power BI to the operations of Gentofte municipality 
and more specifically the possible benefits of its implementation for the optimization of the 
management process of the municipality portfolio. Next, the limitations concerning its application are 
elaborated.   
6.3.1 Benefits from its potential implementation  
To begin with, the integration of Power BI tool into the daily reporting processes of the municipality 
can be proved truly advantageous both in terms of the enhancement of the management practices 
concerning the budget and carry-over and also with regards to the improvement of the information flow 
and the communication between the key stakeholders. This can be achieved through the confrontation 
of the challenges related to the previously analysed focal areas (see chapters 6.1, 6.2). For the purpose 
of attaching robustness to the argumentation, visualizations were created based on sample data.  
6.3.1.1 
Contribution to the first focal area  
In terms of the carry-over phenomenon, Power BI can provide the opportunity to visualize the carry-
over prediction not only for the next fiscal year but also for the rest of the portfolio lifespan. More 
specifically, as depicted in Figure 11 the breakdown of the different budget categories; “original 
budget”, “actual spent budget”, “remaining budget”, “expected expenditure”, “carry-over transferred 
from the previous year”, “expected carry-over for the next year”, among the different years that 
comprise the lifecycle of the project will be possible. The carry-over can be calculated based on the 
aforementioned elements by taking into consideration the current month of the year. So, the carry-over 
for the next year will be equal to the difference between the original and the actual spent budget in case 
the project is in the last month of the year, as the information regarding the exact expenditures for the 
whole year will be available. In any other case, such as in the start or the middle of the financial year, 
the carry-over estimate should be based on the difference between the original and the expected 
expenditure, which is based on the allocated budget, as the actual annual expenditures will not be known 
at that point. This particular distinction will give credence to the carry-over estimations and additionally 
will make the PMs more confident towards the carry-over phenomenon and provide more accurate 
numbers to the FCs during their meetings.

Moreover, Power BI provides the valuable possibility of visualizing the distribution of the carry-over

phase. For instance, the delay of the design phase of a project can lead to the postponement of the

the FCs will be able to report to the upper management these highly important insights concerning the

the municipality portfolio. Furthermore, it will be rendered feasible to perform continuous budget audits 
throughout the whole lifetime of the projects. Hence, the effective management of the carry-over can 
benefit significantly as constant monitoring of the different budget categories will take place.

6.3.2 Limitations  
Although Power BI could have a favourable impact on the challenges that the municipality is facing, 
the success of its application is strongly dependent on additional factors. More importantly, Power BI 
is a data visualization reporting tool, therefore the credibility and robustness of the generated visuals 
heavily rely on the source data where they are based on. Hence, the integration of a reliable forecasting 
methodology into the existing ØS ark tool (see chapter 2.3); which comprises the data source for Power 
BI, constitutes an imperative task. This factor will actively contribute to the achievement of accurate 
estimates in terms of the carry-over generation and the projects cash flow prediction.  
Moreover, the encouragement of the key involved stakeholders to utilize this particular tool in their 
daily working life is essential in order to fully capitalize its potential benefits. This requires the active 
engagement of the upper management by means of providing incentives to the employees with a view 
to convincing them in terms of the practicality of Power BI for the reporting processes of the department 
and the municipality as a whole.  
Furthermore, the employment of risk identification and risk analysis techniques prior to the utilization 
of Power BI is fundamental. In particular, all the risk factors related to each one of the projects 
comprising the municipality portfolio should be detected and evaluated in terms of their severity 
towards the cash flow and budget management of the portfolio. This major process should be also 
integrated into the existing ØS ark in order to achieve effective risk reporting through Power BI. 
In addition, the utilization of this particular tool should work as a fortifier for the facilitation of the 
current face-to-face meetings between the key stakeholders and not substitute them. Finally, a structured 
reporting process should constitute the base for the capitalization of Power BI with a view to avoiding 
the lurking pitfall of neglecting crucial reporting steps due to its easiness of use.  
 
To sum up, it can be deduced that Power BI can actively contribute to the effective budget and carry-
over management of the municipality projects through the advancement of the reporting processes 
among the major stakeholders. More specifically, its visualizations will assist not only the tracking of 
the carry-over throughout the whole portfolio lifetime but also the detection of the phases with the 
greatest distribution to its generation. What is more, the observation of the projects cash flow will be 
performed in a more accurate way through the comparison of the forecasted with the actual cash flow. 
Therefore, the alignment of the stakeholders’ perspectives regarding the criticality of the carry-over 
phenomenon will be achieved. Additionally, the standardization and transparency of the reporting 
processes between the associated parties will be rendered possible through the utilization of this tool. 
However, the successful application of Power BI will be determined to a great extent by the support of 
accurate forecasting and risk analysis processes which will increase the correctness of the source data.

7 Limitations  
This chapter looks at the limitations concerning the research and the analysis which was conducted 
through this thesis.  
In particular, although the investigation of the single case study of Gentofte municipality led to valuable 
insights regarding both the causes and the effective management of the carry-over phenomenon, it is 
still restricted within the context of one particular public organization. Therefore, the stakeholders’ 
dynamics and their risk perspectives might differ from one organization to the other and therefore the 
integration of Power BI into the organizational reporting processes would probably require a slightly 
different approach.  
Furthermore, the time scarcity concerning the timespan of the thesis project led to the execution of a 
limited number of interviews with the key engaged stakeholders of the GE and finance departments. 
Thus, the interviewing of the other associated parties like the internal clients or the political 
representatives was not rendered possible and their viewpoints were not taken into consideration.  
An additional limitation stands for the fact that the analysis of the findings and the research was 
conducted by solely one person. Hence, the likelihood of disregarding significant findings is higher 
comparing to the case where more than one investigator is involved. This particular fact had also a 
negative influence on the internal validity of the performed analysis. However, in the case of this thesis, 
the methodology concerning the data analysis is not only clearly presented and communicated but also 
validated by external stakeholders of the municipality.  
Lastly, the particular fact that Gentofte municipality is currently at the preparatory stage of applying the 
Power BI integration constitutes the accomplishment of the pragmatic validity of both the analysis of 
the findings and the proposed application actions impossible. This would require the actual application 
of the data visualization reporting tool from the municipality and its subsequent assessment.

9 Conclusion  
The objective of this thesis was the investigation of the carry-over phenomenon that takes place within 
the context of the management of the public organizational portfolios. The research was mainly driven 
by the limited existing knowledge concerning the effective ways of managing this particular 
phenomenon through the alignment of the key stakeholders’ risk perspectives for the purpose of the 
successful implementation of project portfolio management. The benefits of the application of a data 
visualization reporting tool; Power BI for achieving the aforementioned purpose were examined.   
Therefore, the literature review inaugurated the concept of the carry-over phenomenon and its 
implications to the financial management of the projects of public organizations. The influence of the 
effective project portfolio management, project risk management and cash flow management practices 
to the carry-over phenomenon was then investigated. Among the major deductions was the fundamental 
importance of the unhindered internal communication and knowledge sharing among the organization 
for the financial management of the projects. Additionally, the literature argues that the active 
involvement of the key stakeholders to the risk identification process together with the accuracy of the 
project cash flow constitute decisive factors for the effective management of the carry-over.  
The thesis researched the carry-over phenomenon and its relation to the key engaged stakeholders’ risk 
perspectives through a case study with Gentofte municipality. Moreover, the focus was placed on the 
main stakeholders who participate in the management of the municipality projects and in particular the 
PMs, the FCs and the upper management of the responsible department. The data collection was 
conducted through semi-structured interviews and the thematic qualitative analysis was applied for the 
analysis of the extracted data. Among the major findings that resulted from the analysis was the great 
influence that both the stakeholders’ perception regarding the carry-over phenomenon and the projects 
ambiguity exert on its effective management. Additionally, it was concluded that the defective reporting 
processes that are utilized in the context of the municipality constitute the alignment of the distinct 
stakeholder risk perspectives a difficult task. Furthermore, the findings were discussed in connection 
with the literature review and the available practices.  
Then, the potential benefits of the utilization of Power BI from Gentofte municipality were discussed

practices.

of having a clear budgeting view throughout the years? 
a. How can this view influence the projects’ prioritization? 
b. How can this view lead to the more efficient management of carry-over?  
i. 
Which is the exact process that you follow in these cases?  
 
I hope to get a current overview of the project in the portfolio, instead of it being up to a month 
old. Currently, it’s the financial controllers that go around interviewing the different project 
managers and hereby giving the information on. Currently, there is no transparency for the 
project managers where the information is going, and hereby I hope the Power BI will increase 
their own interest and caretaking of the data they provide in terms of the financial and 
scheduling aspects. In addition, it will ease the communication in the different meetings among 
the internal stakeholders, as it’s always an easy task to give the current data for how the 
individual project is going through an iPad, etc. and providing the intel the other departments 
of the municipality need. 
Also, I hope it will ease the resource management of the personnel, by giving me an overview 
of the different project managers schedule, and being able to see what their scope of projects 
are when they are going to finish the projects they are currently working on and hereby being 
more efficient in resource management. This is a hard task but gives me a helping hand in the 
process.  
Also, in terms of the liquidity and the carry-over PowerBI will help to get an easier overview 
of where the money is going to be paid, and hereby getting a better overview in terms of the 
money at disposal, and the money they need to transfer to the next years. This is also related to 
the particular problem, concerning having money in the bank, as right now we actually pay 
money to have the funds stored in a bank. I hope that Power BI will give a better overview of 
the money needed, and hereby being able to relocate the funds in the bank, and maybe putting 
them in a different stock or obligation to have the money stored there, instead of paying the 
bank, and thereby improving the municipality economy.  
Also, it will provide us with just a more automatic approach to the different aspects of 
controlling the portfolio of projects. 
7. What is the reporting process towards you from the department manager and the financial 
controllers? 
 
The department manager and I have a coordination meeting every week. Here she comes with 
the projects in her portfolio, and I have the rest in my head. Here we discuss the projects and 
their progress. Here we talk about the projects that are not going so well, and not concerning 
the “green” projects. After this, I talk to the relevant persons if something needs to be done 
from my side. 
I get information from my “department managers”, and then I give the information on to my 
boss, along with the other division managers in “Teknik and miljø”. This is how the information 
is passed upwards in the system. Every time the information needs to go upwards, it needs to 
be shorter and more precise to the case. Then there is the board, where we have the 
interdisciplinary cases and talk about with the other division managers. Here, I also hope that 
Power BI gives a more streamlining in terms of the information that gets passed along to my

controllers happens through the department manager, as she has to approve the projects. She 
needs to be responsible for this relation, in order for her to streamline the way the finances of 
the projects are looked at. She has the final saying before it gets to me. 
We had a problem before, with Charlotte going around and asking the project managers in the

8. What is the reporting process towards upper management? 
 
Every week we have a meeting in “Teknik og miljø” with the division managers and the 
director, and every second week we have this in addition to a meeting with all divisional 
managers and technical directors of Gentofte municipality. Here we go through the portfolio of 
the projects, and also work with the interdisciplinary problems.  
Also as mentioned before in the previous section, we do a lot of meetings upwards and 
downwards, but in general, a lot of different meetings to ensure a robust communication and 
doing a “collision control” of all the different projects on a municipality scale, to check if there 
are any clashes, and avoiding this. 
9. How do you handle human resource management in terms of the available Project 
Managers for the different projects of the portfolio? 
a. Have you encountered any scarcity issues in terms of human resources throughout 
the years? 
 
The department manager and I form the groups of the projects based on personality profiles. It 
is important to have a sense of what the different project managers are good at, and ensuring 
their competencies are at use in the projects, also based on the stakeholders they have to 
collaborate with. A lot of communication concerning what the needed preferences are in terms 
of what is acquired to do the job, and to see who is compatible. Also, it is also about seeing if 
the right personnel are available for the tasks I want to put them on. If they are not, it is about 
restructuring so they can get on the right tasks. There is also a particular “phenomenon”; if you 
set a time for completion, it will almost always be finished at that time, but if you don’t, it will 
take longer for it to be completed. I use this for optimizing the duration of the projects.
