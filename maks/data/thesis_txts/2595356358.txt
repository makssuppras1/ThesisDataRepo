Abstract 
The Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (DVFA) and Technical University of 
Denmark (DTU) are doing a joint research project to develop predictive models to 
determine safe temperature and shelf life combinations for ready-to-eat (RTE) products. 
The objective of this project was to quantify the inﬂuence of temperature and composition 
of the RTE salads on the growth of Listeria monocytogenes during refrigerated storage (2-
10℃). In challenge tests, the growth of L. monocytogenes on fresh-cut red cabbage, 
iceberg lettuce, mixed salads, and mixed chicken salads at 5℃ for 14 days was accessed. 
The logistic model with delay and square root models were used to describe the behaviors 
of L. monocytogenes using all experimental results combined with literature data. Results 
indicated that it would take 5-7 days for a 2 log CFU/g increase for chicken and mixed 
chicken salads at 5℃. L. monocytogenes on chicken reached the highest population levels 
(Nmax 9.3 log CFU/g) at 5℃. It is apparent that L. monocytogenes can grow faster at higher 
temperatures and in salads with a larger content of chicken. The growth rate in mixed salads 
with 30% (w/w) chicken was 0.039 per hour and approximately same as chicken alone 
(0.037 per hour). Bias and accuracy factors were performed to evaluate the reliability of 
predictive models. Results also revealed that the secondary model, derived based on the 
primary growth models for chicken (Growth rate (μ, 1/h) = (0.0056 temperature (℃) + 
0.0019)2) would be an excellent reference (50% confidence interval) to overpredict the 
shelf life of mixed chicken salads during storage at 2-10℃. 
 
Key words: RTE salads, Cooked chicken breast, L. monocytogenes, Temperature, 
Predictive model, Growth rate, Shelf life.

Chapter 1 Introduction  
1.1 Background and objectives 
Listeria monocytogenes (L. monocytogenes) is a Gram-positive, facultative anaerobe 
microorganism that can grow from 0 to 45℃.  This bacterium is widely found in the 
environment. Meat, vegetables, fruits, or dairy foods are known to be vehicles of 
transmission of this microorganism. L. monocytogenes poses a threat to the safety of food 
production because it can survive in refrigeration temperatures, a wide pH range of 4-9, 
and at high salt concentrations.  As a result, L. monocytogenes is considered a major 
challenge in the food factory (Churchill et al., 2019; Matthews et al., 2019).  
 L. monocytogenes are responsible for human Listeriosis, which is a potentially dangerous 
infectious foodborne disease. Almost every L. monocytogenes strain can result in human 
Listeriosis because of the complex interaction between the pathogen, food, and host (EFSA, 
2018). As per information provided by EFSA (2014), 1642 cases of Listeriosis were 
reported in 2012 in the EU with a high mortality rate of 17.8% (Stratakos et al., 2015). 
Affected individuals may experience a range of symptoms from mild flu-like symptoms to 
the severe systemic illnesses which can be potentially lethal for developing fetuses. 
Pregnant women, newborns, the elderly, and the immunocompromised people (YOPI) 
belong to the most vulnerable group (Mclauchlin et al., 2004) 
According to the Europe Commission, ready-to-eat (RTE) products are foods, which are 
consumed without reheating or any other treatment to eliminate or reduce hazardous 
microorganisms to an acceptable level before direct human consumption. RTE products, 
such as salads and cooked meats, are important for the food industry, and are generally 
gaining market popularity as convenience foods. L. monocytogenes is of particular concern 
in RTE products due to its ability to grow at refrigeration temperatures and survive in food 
for prolonged periods under unfavorable conditions (Ziegler et al., 2019). Different 
approaches to the management of L. monocytogenes have led to formulation of different 
regulations, which vary from country to country. The EU food safety regulations aim to 
==PAGE:8==
Technical University of Denmark

limit L. monocytogenes to <100 CFU/g at the end of the shelf life of a food product (EC 
2073/2005) to minimize the risk of Listeriosis.  
The Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (DVFA) is part of the Ministry of 
Environment and Food, which is responsible for food safety and health from farm to fork 
in Denmark. One of the current campaigns of DVFA is on investigating the safety and shelf 
life of RTE food products sold in supermarkets or corner stores. They wanted to enable 
storage of RTE products at a refrigeration temperature (0-10℃) of the producers’ choice 
rather than prescribing a fixed refrigeration temperature. To facilitate this and the setting 
of a realistic and safe shelf life for RTE products at different temperatures, DVFA and 
DTU are doing a joint research project to come up with models to predict safe temperature 
and storage time combinations for a range of RTE products. In this study, the focus is on 
examining and modelling the maximum growth rate of L. monocytogenes in a mixed salad 
with cooked chicken breast. Different RTE salads and individual components were 
artificially contaminated with 1000-10000 CFU/g of L. monocytogenes. All samples are 
stored at 5℃ for 14 days, and the extent of growth of L. monocytogenes and changes in pH 
were determined. Growth curves of L. monocytogenes in similar RTE salads and 
ingredients and at different temperatures were collected from the literature. The objective 
of the present study was to quantify the inﬂuence of temperature and composition of the 
RTE salads on growth of L. monocytogenes during refrigerated storage (2-10℃). 
The following are the specific objectives of this project: 
• 
To examine the growth potential of L. monocytogenes in RTE mixed salads.  
• 
To develop mathematical models to allow for improved estimates of safe shelf-life 
periods during storage at refrigeration temperatures. 
• 
To collect literature data about the growth of L. monocytogenes in RTE products stored 
aerobically and in plastic bags, derive the growth rates by fitting the data to the logistic 
with delay primary model, and compare the derived growth rates at 5℃ with 
experimental results.

1.2 Characteristics of L.  monocytogenes 
1.2.1 Biology and reservoirs of L. monocytogenes  
Listeria is a member of phylum firmicutes. According to Doyle et al. (2019), there are 19 
species belongs to the Listeria genus. L. monocytogenes is one of the members in the first 
clade, which also include Listeria ivanovii, L. innocua, L. seeligeri, L. welshimeri, and L. 
marthi. In this clade, both L. monocytogenes and L. ivanovii have been classified as 
opportunistic pathogens. These bacteria are Gram-positive, non-spore-forming motile rods, 
which are facultative anaerobes and capable of growth with and without oxygen. L. 
monocytogenes is a nonhost-specific pathogen (Wirtanen et al., 2016). It is ubiquitous and 
can be found in multiple ecological sites throughout the environment.   L. monocytogenes 
is the only human-pathogenic Listeria species of significant public health concern, since L. 
ivanovii infections are primarily confined to animals (Doyle et al., 2019). 
L. monocytogenes has been isolated from many kinds of foods such as raw or processed 
meats and vegetables (Beuchat and Brackett,1990), soft cheese, raw milk, RTE foods. It 
can grow on the surfaces with food debris in factories, and may as a psychrotroph be able 
to grow in refrigerated foods with an extended shelf life (Doyle et al., 2019). The 
contamination levels range from 0% in baked foods to 16% in RTE foods. For example, 
the numbers of L. monocytogenes in green salads at 15℃ for 6 days increased 3.34 log 
CFU/g. Besides, it is much easier to find L. monocytogenes in raw food than heat-treated 
goods. (Matthews et al., 2019). In terms of fresh meats, L. monocytogenes can be 
symptomatically or asymptomatically carried by food animals and this can lead to 
contamination of animal carcass during slaughter (Doyle et al., 2019). During cheese 
making, L. monocytogenes is concentrated about 10-fold in the curd. Although, in that case 
its growth will be suppressed because of the presence of lactic acid bacteria starter cultures.  
Moreover, L. monocytogenes is frequently found in facilities producing raw and RTE 
products (Doyle et al., 2019). From Hoffman et al. (2003), the results also indicated that 
the raw fish was possibly contaminated from the source (i.e., the harvest environment) 
rather than the processing environment. In general, L. monocytogenes is found widespread.

1.2.2 The intrinsic and extrinsic factors that influence the growth of L. 
monocytogenes 
A microorganism is an organism, meaning it has metabolism like humans, particularly 
concerning catabolism and anabolism. Thus, the growth kinetics of L. monocytogenes is 
influenced by the interaction of intrinsic and extrinsic factors, especially affecting the lag 
and log phases of the growth curve (Buchanan et al., 1989). The intrinsic factors include 
pH, water activity (aw), antimicrobial structures, redox potential, while extrinsic factors are 
comprised of environmental factors such as the gaseous atmosphere, temperature, relative 
humidity, and processing factors. Lastly, the implicit factors, these are characteristics of 
individual strains of L. monocytogenes, affect the growth as well (Carlin et al., 1995) 
The pH range for the growth of L. monocytogenes is thought to be 5.6 to 9.6 (Doyle et al., 
2019). Cells may survive at pH below 4.3 but do not multiply (Matthews et al., 2019). 
Growth at low pH values is influenced by the incubation temperature and other extrinsic 
as well as intrinsic factors such as presence of organic acids, which can inhibit the growth 
of L. monocytogenes. For example, the L. monocytogenes grows well on the meats where 
pH is around or above 6 and its growth would be reduced where pH drops to near or below 
5 (Glass et al., 1989). Experiments showed that the presence of up to 0.1% acetic, citric, 
and lactic acid in the medium inhibits the growth of L. monocytogenes, with inhibition 
increasing as the incubation temperature decreases (Doyle et al., 2019). However, the anti-
listeria activity varies from acids to acids; citric acid and lactic acid are less harmful at an 
equivalent pH than acetic acid (Matthews et al., 2019).     
L. monocytogenes can grow from 0 to 45℃, with growth slowing at lower temperatures. 
One experiment on RTE salads indicates that there is a considerable increase in growth at 
8℃ compared to 5℃ (Ziegler et al., 2019). Reducing the storage temperature will extend 
the lag phase and reduce the growth rate (Francis et al., 2001), which means the reduction 
of storage temperature would affect the bacterial activities and extend shelf life. Moreover, 
L. monocytogenes also will be inactivated by exposure to temperatures above 50℃ (Doyle 
et al., 2019).  
==PAGE:12==
Technical University of Denmark 
11 
 
L. monocytogenes grows optimally at water activities (aw) above 0.97, and it will survive 
at aw as low as 0.83 (Matthews et al., 2019). An inverse relationship exists between the 
thermal resistance of L. monocytogenes and the aw of the medium in which it is suspended, 
which means its heat resistance increases as the water activity of food decrease. It is worth 
mentioning for food supply chains which depends on low aw and thermal treatment for food 
preservation (Matthews et al., 2019; Doyle et al., 2019). 
L. monocytogenes can grow in the presence of 10 to 12% sodium chloride and can rise to 
a high level in moderate salt concentrations (6.5%). Lower temperature increases the 
survival of the bacterium is a high salt environment (Matthews et al.,2019; Doyle et al., 
2019). With regard to sodium nitrite, it displays a significant bacteriostatic activity against 
L. monocytogenes so that it can be applied on cured meats for protection against this 
microorganism (Buchanan et al., 1989). The effect of salt concentration on reducing the 
growth of L. monocytogenes is less than that of the gaseous environment and temperature. 
For processing factors, the growth of L. monocytogenes is impeded by modified 
atmosphere packaging (MAP) (Marshall et al., 1991). MAP method is popularized by 
reducing oxygen and increasing carbon dioxide concentrations, which can increase shelf 
life via the inhibition of aerobic spoilage bacteria (Beumer et al., 1996). But the 
effectiveness of MAP decreased with increasing temperature (Marshall et al., 1991).  The 
study from Francis et al. (1998) showed that CO2 concentrations of 5% to 10% did not 
significantly inhibit growth of L. monocytogenes by using a surface model system at 8℃ 
for 14 days, however, increasing the CO2 concentration to 20% reduced the rate of growth.

1.3 Listeriosis around the world  
1.3.1 Characteristics of disease 
Listeriosis, caused by L. monocytogenes, is an illness, which has been identified in both 
humans and animals. Listeriosis in animals is mainly caused by the oral-fecal route (Ryser 
et al., 2007). For humans, the majority of cases are foodborne, i.e., linked with the intake 
of contaminated foods (McLauchlin et al., 2004). Unlike infection with other foodborne 
pathogens such as Salmonella, which rarely result in fatalities, Listeriosis is associated with 
a mortality rate of approximately 20-30% (Goulet et al., 2001).  It is likely to be of 
increased public health concern due to its clinical severity and high mortality rate. 
In terms of the high-risk groups, the most at risk populations include pregnant women, 
newborns, the elderly, and the immunocompromised groups. The study from Hitchins et 
al. (1996) estimated that 15% of populations was at high risk for contracting Listeriosis. It 
was reported that most of the cases was predicted to occur in the elderly population (≥ 65 
years old) (48% of cases) followed by the pregnant population (41% of cases) and the 
healthy population < 65 years old (11%) (EFSA, 2018).  
There are two forms of Listeriosis (Allerberger et al., 2010). The first form is non-invasive 
Listeriosis, which develops as atypical febrile gastroenteritis. It is a mild illness with flu 
like symptoms appearing within 4 to 48 hours. The estimates of the infectious dose range 
from 1.9×105 CFU/g to 1×109 CFU/g. (Matthews et al., 2019). The second form is a severe 
illness called invasive Listeriosis. The mechanism by which the organism causes invasive 
Listeriosis, is by its ability to enter the host’s cells, grow inside the cells, and pass directly 
into nearby cells. The cell-to-cell transmission reduces the bacterium’s exposure to 
antibiotics and circulating antibodies; moreover, this membrane penetrating ability allows 
L. monocytogenes to cross to the brain and placenta. Determination of the infectious dose 
of L. monocytogenes obtained from human volunteer would be unethical. The results from 
animals test shows >100 CFU/g of L. monocytogenes in foods would be responsible for the 
outbreak. However, actual epidemiological data points to infectious dose was much smaller 
than it (Matthews et al., 2019). 
==PAGE:14==

The clinical manifestations for the invasive Listeriosis include stillbirth, septicemia, and 
meningitis. In a review of the literature, the most common signs and symptoms among 
neonates with early Listeriosis included respiratory distress, fever, and neurologic 
abnormalities (Mylonakis et al., 2002). The median incubation period of this acute 
Listeriosis would be from under 3 hours to 3 months, which makes it difficult to get 
accurate food histories and examine suspect foods.  
As technology develops, serotyping has proven its value over many years. There are 13 
serotypes of L. monocytogenes that can cause disease, but more than 90% of human isolates 
belong to three serotypes: 1/2a, 1/2b, and 4b (Doyle et al., 2019). Moreover, it has been  
proven that the L. monocytogenes strains, which are responsible for a specific Listeriosis 
outbreak, all had identical pulsed-field gel electrophoresis (PEGE) patterns (Matthews et 
al., 2019). Nowadays more source tracking and outbreak investigation is done using whole 
genome sequencing (WGS), which can effectively be used to trace a Listeriosis outbreak 
back to a food processing facility (Stasiewicz et al., 2015). 
1.3.2 Outbreaks and recalls 
Listeriosis cases are monitored by an active surveillance program, and it comprises 
sporadic and epidemiologic investigation of Listeriosis outbreaks (Doyle et al., 2019). 
Sporadic Listeriosis is proved to be a rare disease that annually causes 7.4 cases per million 
people in the United States (Matthews et al., 2019). 
Consumption of uncooked chicken was first identified as a risk factor for sporadic cases of 
Listeriosis in the United States in 1985 (Doyle et al., 2019). The first documented 
foodborne Listeriosis outbreak was in 1981. The epidemic strain was isolated from a sealed 
coleslaw container, made from white cabbage that had been contaminated by an intruding 
flock of sheep and stored in an unheated shed, which provides an excellent growth 
environment for L. monocytogenes (Schlech et al., 1983). However, it was not until 1985, 
prompted by the outbreak of Listeriosis in USA linked to inadequately pasteurized soft 
cheese, that L. monocytogenes became a big concern of the food industry (Goulet et al., 
2001). From Table 1, some typical cases show that Listeriosis poses a threat to consumers 
in the EU, USA, and Canada. Among outbreaks in the EU, it can be seen from the Table

1.4 L. monocytogenes and RTE food products  
According to the EC 2073/2005 regulation, the definition of ready-to-eat food (e.g., RTE 
salads, fruits, vegetables) stipulates that these are foods intended by the producer or the 
manufacturer for direct human consumption, without the need for cooking or other 
effective processing steps to eliminate or reduce microorganisms to an acceptable level. 
RTE food products present a challenge in management of human health risks and food 
safety due to the absence of heat (or any) treatment before consumption. The risk is likely 
to be increased if the RTE foods are exposed to marginal abuse temperatures or have 
extended refrigerated storage (Doyle et al., 2019). L. monocytogenes is a significant 
problem in RTE products because of its frequent presence in the environment and ability 
to grow at refrigeration temperatures (Ziegler et al., 2019). For different catalogues of RTE 
products, it seems like that seafood salads have the highest rates of positive samples for 
prevalence of L. monocytogenes. However, the results vary from regions and composition 
(Gombas et al., 2003). 
1.4.1 Regulations and guidelines  
The Chinese National Food Safety Standards (GB 29921-2013) only includes a limitation 
for L. monocytogenes on meat products, where the acceptable level is absence in 25 g and 
with no maximum acceptable level based on testing of 5 samples. The United Kingdom 
and the United States have “zero tolerance” (in 25-gram-samples) for L. monocytogenes 
(Matthews et al., 2019). 
The EU has taken another approach by dividing RTE products into products that are able 
or not able to support the growth of L. monocytogenes (EC 2073/2005). For products that 
are unable to support the growth of L. monocytogenes (except those intended for infants 
and particularly medical purposes), a maximum of 100 CFU/g is allowed during their shelf-
life. For RTE products which can support L. monocytogenes growth, the legislation 
demands the absence of L. monocytogenes in 5 samples of 25 g before the product leaves 
the immediate control of the producer, and allows for the content of a maximum of 100 
CFU/g for products placed on the market during their shelf-life. Also, for RTE products 
==PAGE:18==
Technical University of Denmark

intended for infants and medical purposes, legislation requires the absence of L. 
monocytogenes in 10 samples of 25 g during the entire shelf life for the products. 
1.4.2 Survival and growth of L. monocytogenes in RTE foods  
Many foods extrinsic and intrinsic factors will determine the survival and growth of L. 
monocytogenes. As mentioned before, L. monocytogenes can survive dry conditions and 
exhibit growth at a water activity as low as 0.92. It is also psychotroph with an ability to 
grow at temperatures from 0℃ to 45℃. The populations of L. monocytogenes increased 
by 1-1.5 log cycles by day 12 of storage at 8℃ for some natural vegetables. When stored 
at 4℃, L. monocytogenes remained in the lag phase for the first 10 days after but numbers 
slightly increased between days 10 to 12 (Francis et al., 2001).  The higher growth potential 
of L. monocytogenes at 8℃ compared to 4℃ emphasized the effect of temperature and 
showed that it is better to store RTE vegetable products at low temperatures at the retail 
level (Ziegler et al., 2019). A study from Burnett et al. (2005) showed the growth rate of L. 
monocytogenes also depends on the composition of RTE products. In other words, the food 
matrix has a tremendous impact on the growth potential of L. monocytogenes. This has led 
to many studies that have investigated how food ingredients influence the growth of L. 
monocytogenes. Lokerse et al. (2016) reported that in many fruits and vegetables, the 
growth of L. monocytogenes did not exceed 2 log CFU/g after 10 days at 7℃.  However, 
Galia melon, which is commonly used in RTE fruit salads, was shown to stimulate the 
growth of L. monocytogenes resulting in 3 log increases that were much higher than those 
found for the other tested fruits. On the contrary, anthocyanins, an antioxidant, inhibit the 
growth of L. monocytogenes in red cabbage (Beuchat et al., 1986). Besides, there was no 
observed growth on celeriac, carrot, and corn salad products. As carrots are known to 
contain a carrot phytoalexin called 6-methoxymellein which is inhibitory to the growth of 
survival of some fungi and bacteria (Francis et al., 2001).  
L. monocytogenes is a poor competitor compared with other microorganisms such as  
enterobacteria and pseudomonads (Matthews et al., 2019; Carlin et al., 1996). Thus, in the 
presence of high populations of background microflora, many foods such as soybean 
sprouts would not support the growth of L. monocytogenes because of the interactive 
==PAGE:19==
Technical University of Denmark 
18 
 
competition (Francis et al., 2001). In addition, lactic acid bacteria, as a competitive flora, 
have been documented to also decrease the number of L. monocytogenes. But one research 
study showed that Lactobacillus only slightly inhibited the growth of L. monocytogenes 
even if their concentration was 100 times that of L. monocytogenes in meat products 
(Beumer et al., 1996). Also, a high dry matter content seems to have a growth-inhibiting 
effect on L. monocytogenes in pasta products (Lokerse et al., 2016).  
One of the mitigation factors is disinfection, which can affect the initial numbers of and 
growth of L. monocytogenes. Research from Carlin et al. (1996) showed that the 
disinfection of endive leaves with hydrogen peroxide reduced the background microflora 
by approximately 10 to 100-fold, resulting in the rate of multiplication of L. monocytogenes 
was higher than on undisinfected products surface, likely due to the removal of the 
competing microflora. In the challenge tests with L. monocytogenes along with different 
strains of pseudomonads and Enterobacteriaceae on leaves of endive also showed that the 
inoculated population of P. fluorescens grew slightly to reach 108 CFU/g after 7 days at 
10℃ while inhibiting the growth of L. monocytogenes, which is one of a powerful proof 
that L. monocytogenes is a weak competitor.  
 1.4.3 Food preservation methods to control L. monocytogenes in RTE foods  
It is hard to eliminate the L. monocytogenes during the food processing or in the 
environment because the microorganism is ubiquitous in raw materials or environment and 
capable of survival and growth over a wide pH and temperature range, and in atmospheres 
of varied oxygen content.  Use of multiple hurdles is the best approach to achieve L. 
monocytogenes control in food. (Farber and Peterkin 1991; Matthews et al., 2019). 
Mild thermal treatment (3 min at 50℃) for cabbage is great extending the sensory shelf-
life by controlling enzymatic graying, but turned out to stimulate the growth of L. 
monocytogenes, likely due to the inactivation of the myrosinase glucosinolate system (Ells 
et al., 2010).  Harrison and Carpenter (1989) reported that 2.5-3.8 log cycle reductions of 
L. monocytogenes were observed following microwave heating treatment (temperature 
above 73.9℃) of chicken breast. Also, they found lower population increases of L. 
==PAGE:20==
Technical University of Denmark 
19 
 
monocytogenes in vacuum-packaged compared to film over-wrapped samples during 
storage at 4℃.  
There are some approaches, which may be considered for controlling L. monocytogenes in 
meats, such as the use of postprocessing pasteurization, antimicrobial agents, reduced 
storage temperature, reformulation of products. (Glass et al., 1989). One study revealed 
that heating sausage at 51.7℃ for 4 h after fermentation but before the drying process killed 
most L. monocytogenes. Thus, suitable postprocessing can be responsible for the presence 
of L. monocytogenes in final products (Glass et al., 1989). The use of bacteriocin-producing 
Pediococcus acidiiactici can control postprocessing L. monocytogenes contamination of 
Frankfurters. The efficiency was dependent on the cell concentration, which means a high 
level (107 CFU/g) of Pediococcus in sausage may better to inhibit the growth of L. 
monocytogenes compared to the lower level at 4℃ for 60 days. Moreover, incubation at 
15℃ with added Pediococcus acidiiactici into Frankfurters under aerobic or anaerobic 
conditions restrained the growth of L. monocytogenes more significantly than incubation 
at 4℃ by the end of 15 days (Berry et al., 1991).

2.2 Storage of products and monitoring of storage temperature 
Aliquots of 5 g of all samples were placed in polyethylene plastic bags (VWR Sterile 
Sample Bags). Samples of chicken, cabbage, iceberg lettuce, mixed chicken salads and 
mixed salads were incubated at 5℃ for 14 days. Also, temperature was logged using a 
thermometer during the storage period.  
2.3 Culturing and preparation of inoculum for challenge tests 
The detection and enumeration of L. monocytogenes followed the protocol outlined in EN 
ISO 11290-2 (Rollier et al., 2019). Total plate counts were performed according to ISO 
4833. For enumeration of L. monocytogenes, Palcam agar plates (CM0877, Oxoid, 
Hampshire, UK) with the Listeria enrichment selective (SR0150, Oxoid) were prepared by 
following the manufacturer’s instructions.  
For enumeration of the total viable count, Tryptic Soy Agar (TSA) plates (30 g/L of Tryptic 
Soy Broth (TSB), Merck Darmstadt, Germany) and 15 g of Agar (SigmaAldrich, Spain) 
were prepared. All plates were left at room temperature overnight, then the Palcam agar 
plates were stored in the refrigerator, and TSA plates were kept in the dark place.  
Inoculum of L. monocytogenes for challenge test were prepared according to the EU 
Community Reference Laboratory (CRL) for L. monocytogenes technical guidance 
document on shelf-life studies for L. monocytogenes in RTE foods (Beaufort et.al.,2014).  
Three L. monocytogenes strains LM 568, LM 08-5578, LM EGD-e were taken from the -
80℃ freezer and plated out on TSA plates separately. The clinical strain L. monocytogenes 
08-5578 (Gilmour et al., 2010) was isolated in Canada in 2008 during a Listeriosis outbreak 
with ready-to-eat meat products. The L. monocytogenes EGD-e strain was originally 
isolated from rabbit in 1926 (Murray et al., 1926). The last strain, L. monocytogenes 568, 
is an environmental isolate from a shrimp processing plant (Hefford et al., 2005). 
All plates were incubated at room temperature (25℃) for 48 hours. Next, one colony from 
each strain was transferring into 10 mL TSB solution (30 g/L TSB, Merck) and afterward 
incubated at 5℃ for 72 hours. To determine the number of cells in the precultures, phase 
==PAGE:25==
Technical University of Denmark 
24 
 
contrast microscopy at 1000 x magnification was used to observe the number of bacteria 
per ml, where one bacterium per view field corresponded to 1×106 CFU/mL. An equal 
volume from each preculture was then mixed and diluted in FKP to achieve an approximate 
concentration of 10000 CFU/mL in the working inoculum.   
The bags were inoculated with 1% (v/w) of the working inoculum. The bacteria were 
carefully mixed with the content of the bag to ensure uniform mixing. 
2.4 pH and growth of L. monocytogenes during storage of samples 
To better understand the changes of physicochemical properties about chicken and mixed 
chicken salads, the pH meter (HQ 411D, Hach) was used for the measurement of pH in the 
uninoculated control sample during the storage. Each sample was homogenized using a 
blender for 15 seconds. Taking 2 g of samples into 25 mL baker, adding 20 g peptone water 
(FKP) (Demineralized water composed of 1 g/L of Bacteriological peptone (Thermo Fisher, 
England) and 8.5 g/L sodium chloride (Merck KGaA, Denmark). The pH meter was 
calibrated prior to each use.  
In order to predict the growth of L. monocytogenes during storage, samples were analysed 
7 times during 14 days of storage period. Control samples (i.e., not inoculated with L. 
monocytogenes) for each product were analyzed on first day (day 0). In addition, all 
experiments needed to repeat three times with different bath of samples. 
During the sampling days, all samples were diluted with FKP to achieve an initial dilution 
of 1:10 and subsequently homogenized by stomacher for 30 seconds. Additional decimal 
dilutions were performed as needed using FKP. Volumes of 0.1 mL from suitable dilutions 
were spread plated on Palcam and TSA agar and left to incubate at 25℃ for 2 to 3 days 
and then enumerated as shown in Figure 3.

2.5 Data analysis 
2.5.1 Experimental results calculation 
All experiments were repeated in three biologically independent trials with triplicate 
samples to avoid systemic errors, meaning that plates counting and pH measurements were 
done in triplicates within each of experiments, either. Taking average was the best way for 
the pH results as there was little difference between parallel experiments. 
Colonies were enumerated following incubation of agar plates and converted to log 
(CFU/g), which is the total number of survivors at each time point. If the bacteria (no 
colonies) could not be detected on the agar plates, < 2 Log (CFU/g) was applied to the 
record. What is more, more than 300 were used to calculate uncountable plates.  
2.5.2 Literature results extraction and calculation  
Initially, all scientific literature data were collected using DTU Findit databases, Google 
scholar (English version); and the combined data base for predictive microbiology 
(Combase) as appropriate. The literature studies were used to gather data that could be used

to predict the growth rate of L. monocytogenes in chicken, iceberg lettuce, red cabbage and 
RTE salads during the storage at temperatures ranging from 2 to 10℃. The objective of 
present study was to perform a systematic review and logistic analysis of growth of L. 
monocytogenes in different type of products from literature study, for predicting and 
comparing the growth rate of L. monocytogenes with laboratory results. A total of 19 
studies were initially collected that they reported on the growth of L. monocytogenes in 
products related to the present study. As shown in Table 12 and 13, the product 
characteristics, storage situations, L. monocytogenes test strains and model conditions are 
summarized.   
In selecting the relevant studies, which could be used in the modelling work, the following 
criteria were used: 1. To come from original study. 2. To be obtained by using well 
characterized L. monocytogenes strains 3. The storage period was no more than 25 days. 4. 
The initial pH values of chicken and RTE salads were between 5.5 and 6.5 5. Storage was 
in an aerobic environment (not modified atmosphere packaging). 6. Chicken was included 
in RTE salads. As a result, only 8 of the total studies were evaluated and used in the 
prediction modelling and comparison with observed data.  
The third step consisted of the preparation of the date for modelling. All available data (L. 
monocytogenes concentration versus storage time) were selected from the literature. When 
all data were presented as graphs, the curve or line were uploaded into Plot Digitizer 
(Website was shown in Reference) followed by selection and extraction to Excel. It should 
be noticed that all parameter units had to be unified (e.g., conversion from days to hours).

Using the data from the experiment and the literature, results for the different types of 
salads※ were plotted in the form of μ versus temperature, and square root μ versus 
temperature (Figures 7-10).  As drawn in Figure 8 (left one), the curves of growth rate for 
cabbage would be the best simulation with the highest credibility because the R2 was the 
closest to 1. The plots for iceberg lettuce or shredded lettuces exhibited the smallest 
correlation coefficients, however, this could be due to the limited growth potential of L. 
monocytogenes on these foods. Furthermore, all slopes and intercepts (b) were positive, 
which means the growth rate and square root growth rate would increase as temperature 
increased. It should be noted that the minimum growth temperature is only a hypothetical 
value and often drops below 0. 
Next, it was examined whether the primary growth models (Table 7) and the developed 
secondary models (Figures 7-10) for RTE salads  and chicken only could be used to predict 
the growth rate of each other, i.e., can the chicken model be used to predict the growth rate 
of L. monocytogenes in RTE salads at different refrigerated temperatures. To look at this, 
the bias factor was calculated and provided it would be close to 1, the results between 0.75 
and 1.25 would indicate the achievement of 50% confidence interval. Based on the primary 
and secondary model results, both chicken and RTE salads models were fitted to the data 
and used to calculate the bias and accuracy factors shown in Table 8. The bias and accuracy 
factors outcomes (1.05 and 1.11 for RTE salads and 0.90 and 1.14 for chicken for the Bf31 
and Af31, respectively) calculated by the primary models showed that the primary chicken 
model fitted better to RTE salads than the other way around. It would be the case that the 
content of chicken determines the growth rate in RTE salads. In other words, the chicken 
content contributed significantly to the growth of L. monocytogenes in mixed chicken 
salads. In contrast, the results obtained from the secondary models were not acceptable 
with bias and accuracy factors ranging between 0.62-27.51 and 12.98-27.51, respectively, 
meaning that the current data set could not be used to produce a reliable secondary model 
to predict the effect of temperature on the maximum growth rate of L. monocytogenes.

Chapter 4. Discussion 
4.1 pH changes during storage of chicken and mixed chicken salads at 
5℃ 
The pH results of the study indicated that each sample did not change significantly(p>0.05) 
during 14 days at 5℃. The situation was analogous to studies from Glass et al. (1989) for 
sliced chicken in which the pH changed from 6.35±0.48 to 6.36±0.51 with 5 log CFU/g 
initial inoculation level as well as stored at 4.4℃ for 14 days. Besides, the pH was observed 
to decline to 6.08±0.53 at week 4 in Glass et al. (1989) study. With the growth of bacteria 
and suitable temperature, the main ingredients of chicken (proteins, and fats and 
carbohydrates) will be decomposed during storage resulting in a decrease in pH, however, 
the low carbohydrate content and buffering capacity of chicken could be the reasons why 
there was no significant decrease in pH. For pH tests on cooked chicken breast, Studies by 
Rodriguez et al. (2003) have revealed that the pH of cooked chicken breast is 6.31 provided 
by the manufacturer. The small difference of initial value would due to the different heat 
treatment or product origin. 
The addition of chicken to RTE salads increases pH, as shown in Table 3, 20% mixed 
chicken salads results were higher than 10% mixed chicken salads. During the storage of 
mixed chicken salads, a slight increase (about 0.7 units) in pH for 14 days was observed. 
Compared to the results published by Söderqvist et al. (2017), where the baby salads with 
chicken showed 0.3 units increase during storage for three days at 8℃, it is difficult to 
make prediction.  
The neutral pH and high water-activity may enhance the growth of L. monocytogenes 
(Francis and O' Beirne 1998), The results, according to Petran et al. (1989), also showed 
the optimum pH for L. monocytogenes growth is 7. All average pH of these four samples 
was lower but close to seven, so the pH was favorable and for the growth of L. 
monocytogenes was considered as suitable.

Technical University of Denmark

4.2 Total viable count on RTE products 
The total viable count, gives a quantitative estimate of the concentration of microorganisms 
such as bacteria, yeast in a sample. In addition, TVC can be a useful indicator to predict 
the shelf life of products and distinguish spoilage during storage. The TVC results on 
iceberg lettuce and red cabbage were similar and lower than TVC in the salads containing 
chicken meat. The counts increased from the same initial 4.2 log CFU/g to 7.8 and 7.1 log 
CFU/g for iceberg lettuce and red cabbage, respectively. In the meanwhile, other authors 
found the similar counts for TVC of iceberg salads (from 3.52 ±0.08 to 7.01±0.03 at 8℃) 
(Tucci et al., 2019), lower numbers for cabbage (from 4.9 log CFU/g to 6.7 log CFU/g) 
(Ziegler et al., 2019). The meat has been regarded as the most perishable products because 
its moist, nutritious surface would be the best platform for the growth of an extensive range 
of bacteria (Stanbridge and Davies, 1998). As a result, the chicken samples reach 9.4 log 
CFU/g at the end of storage period, resulting in a TVC which was much higher than the 
TVC in the vegetables. 
The 20 and 30% mixed chicken salads supported the highest level of TVC for 14 days. The 
numbers were even higher than the results of chicken only samples. This may be due to the 
effect of vegetables, which may have contained and allowed the growth of commensal 
bacteria (Lund, 1992). Additionally, it might be due to the specific microbiota of plants 
engaging in complex interactions with pathogens such as L. monocytogenes (Brandl et al., 
2016).  
4. 3 Impact of food matrix on the growth potential of L. monocytogenes 
In the experimental part of this study, the growth of L. monocytogenes on RTE products 
has been investigated. Results showed that the growth of L. monocytogenes on chicken 
samples surpassed the initial inoculum, and 5.2 log CFU/g increase was observed for 14 
days at 5℃. Other workers have obtained similar results, for example, a 5.9 log CFU/g 
increase was observed for sliced chicken after 14 days at 4.4℃ (Glass et al., 1989).  
==PAGE:44==
Technical University of Denmark 
43 
 
The behavior of L. monocytogenes in the vegetables indicated a much lower growth 
potential compared to the chicken and mixed chicken salads. The colony number increased 
1.7 log CFU/g and 1.4 log CFU/g for iceberg lettuce and red cabbage, respectively, at 5℃ 
after 14 days. Likewise, Szabo et al., (2003) obtained an increase of 1.1±0.37 log CFU/g 
at 4℃ for RTE iceberg lettuce after 14 days. Similarly, growth of approximately 1.5 log 
CFU/g was found at 8℃ for 12 days in the Francis et al. (2001) study. Smaller than detected 
values may be explained by the use of different packing materials and storage temperature. 
For red cabbage, most of the comparative values are reported for white cabbage, Yoon et 
al. (2014) found growth of L. monocytogenes of approximately 1.5 log CFU/g at 15℃ over 
a period of 60 hours. Generally, vegetables of this type contain fewer nutrients, which may 
contribute to the lower growth rates of L. monocytogenes. Presence of other bacteria can 
also lead to growth inhibition as discussed above. Also, crucifers such as red cabbage 
contains the antimicrobial allylisothiocyanate which could further reduce the growth of L. 
monocytogenes. 
The growth of L. monocytogenes in mixed chicken salads was higher than the vegetable 
type salads. Coincidentally, L. monocytogenes in mixed chicken salads increased 3 to 5 
logs higher than the celery salads at 5℃ within 30 days (Sahu et al., 2017).  Erickson et al., 
(1993) published that there were more than 5 log CFU/g growth for home-style chicken 
salads held at 4 ℃ for 10 days. Also, they indicated that the chicken salads recipe supported 
L. monocytogenes growth at refrigerated temperature. It was expected that the growth of L. 
monocytogenes in 30% chicken salads would be higher than 20% chicken salads, however, 
this was not the case.  
Based on Table 4, the L. monocytogenes population significantly increased to numbers 
above the 2 log CFU/g criteria after 5-7 days for chicken, 20% and 30% mixed chicken 
salads. It indicated that they become unsafe to eat more quickly than the other salads 
without chicken. Moreover, the consistent result obtained from Erickson et al. (1993) 
showed that the microbial safe shelf life (contamination level: >6 log CFU/g with 3 log 
CFU/g initial inoculum) of refrigerated chicken was 5 to 7 days.

shelf-lives of 6-7 days in the refrigerator at 5℃. In addition, the composition of vegetables 
varied from the experimental salad to real products. Moreover, the shelf life can be 
increased when the higher mayonnaise and other acidulating agents are used.

4.4 Growth rate of RTE products 
The calculations of the maximum growth rate and lag time showed there was an apparent 
variance between different bathes experiments, especially for iceberg lettuce and 20% 
mixed chicken salads. It might because raw materials were purchased at different times 
and could have been of different initial quality.  
Compared with all literature results in Table 7, it is apparent that L. monocytogenes could 
grow faster at a higher temperature for cooked chicken breast and mixed chicken salads. 
This is in agreement with the results from Carpenter et al. (1989) that L. monocytogenes 
increased faster at 10℃ than at 4℃ on processed poultry for 4 weeks. Besides, De Cesare 
et al. (2018) showed consistent results where the μ of RTE salads was 0.03 at 12℃ and 
0.01 at 4℃. Other factors will contribute to differences in growth rates such as the gaseous 
atmosphere in the packages.  Beumer et al. (1996) observed that vacuum packaging 
==PAGE:46==
Technical University of Denmark 
45 
 
reduced the growth rate of L. monocytogenes compared to the products which were film 
overwrapped.  
There were not excellent linear correlations (R2＜0.9) between growth rate or the square 
root transformed growth rate and temperature for RTE foods except for cabbage. This 
might because the range of the temperatures are not equally spread out through the 
temperature range of interest (2-10℃). However, the tendency of growth rate verse 
temperature was the same as in other studies. It has been shown that up to 42℃ the growth 
rate rises as the temperature increases (Duh et al., 1993).  
The residual sum of squares and root mean square error results are indicators of “the 
goodness of fit”. Table 5 shows that the primary growth curves described the cellular 
concentrations perfectly because RSS and RMSE were close to zero. For bias and accuracy 
factors, the results from Tables 6, 8, and 9 revealed that, in line with the hypothesis, that 
chicken breast plays a decisive role on the maximum growth rate of L. monocytogenes in 
30% mixed chicken salads. The same results were reported by Little et al. (2007), who also 
highlighted that RTE salads containing meat or seafood had a higher risk of being the cause 
of Listeriosis. The secondary model of chicken for prediction of the μ as a function of 
temperature: y=(0.0056x+0.0019)2, where y means the maximum growth rate and x is 
temperature. It tended to overpredict the growth of L. monocytogenes in chicken and RTE 
salads compared to observations. This may be an advantage in the prediction of safe shelf 
lives for such products at different temperatures, as it will be a fail-safe prediction, i.e., 
predicting it taking few hours to reach dangerous levels than is the case. 
Data from the literature was used to build the secondary models. In general, the validity of 
the comparison between studies can be challenging, as many factors such as composition, 
the process condition, packaging methods, or storage temperature would influence the 
outcome, i.e., growth rate of L. monocytogenes. In addition, the differences can be due to 
variation among the strains used in different studies or the difference in the way of 
enumerating the bacterium.

Chapter 5. Conclusion and Future Perspectives 
In this master thesis, the pH changes, total viable count, and the growth of L. 
monocytogenes at 5℃ within 14 days storages have experimentally been investigated in 
chicken, red cabbage, iceberg lettuce and mixed salads and mixed chicken salads. 
Moreover, the influences of temperature and composition of RTE salads on the growth of 
L. monocytogenes were compared with literature data. Firstly, the pH was used to monitor 
the physicochemical changes in samples. Obtained results showed that there was no 
significant difference (p＞0.05) for chicken and mixed chicken salads, where pH stayed 
around neutral ranging from 6.37±0.04 to 7.25 ±0.02 during storage for 14 days at 5℃. 
This neutral pH provided an excellent environment for the growth of L. monocytogenes 
and other bacteria.  
Concentrations of L. monocytogenes and TVC increased more in chicken and mixed 
chicken salads than in the vegetables alone. The growth of L. monocytogenes depended on 
the temperatures and content of chicken and could be modelled using the logistic model 
with delay.  With the addition of vegetables, 20% and 30% mixed chicken salads had 5.8 
log CFU/g and 5.6 log CFU/g increases in the total viable counts, respectively, which were 
higher than in the vegetables alone. The multiplication of L. monocytogenes on chicken 
samples was 5.2 log CFU/g when stored at 5℃ for 14 days because of the suitable growth 
environment on meats. After that, the estimated shelf life (time to a 2 log CFU/g increase) 
for chicken, 20%, and 30% mixed chicken salads were estimated to be five to seven days 
at 5℃. The results of predicted shelf life on RTE salads at 5℃ agreed with the survey of 
the declared shelf life for similar salads in Danish supermarkets indicating current practice 
for setting the shelf lives at this temperature are safe.   
Last but not least, the calculation with logistic model with delay showed that the growth 
rate of L. monocytogenes in chicken alone was the same as the 30% chicken salads, and 
chicken determined the growth of  L. monocytogenes in the salads with chicken.  Besides, 
the lower chicken content slightly decreased the growth rate of L. monocytogenes in mixed 
chicken salads as observed when compared the growth rates for chicken alone with 
==PAGE:48==

observations for 20 and 30% mixed chicken salads at 5℃. However, the difference in 
maximum growth rate was small enough that a secondary model could be developed which 
has bias and accuracy factors close to one, when the secondary chicken model was used to 
predict the growth rate of L. monocytogenes in 30% mixed chicken salads. As such, the 
secondary model for the growth of L. monocytogenes in chicken alone (Growth rate = 
(0.0056×Temperature+0.0019)2) could be useful to provide a fail-safe estimate for the safe 
shelf life for RTE salads at different temperatures. 
L. monocytogenes does not produce evidence of spoilage, even when present in high 
numbers. To ensure the food safety for customers, the aim for producers therefore is to 
keep L. monocytogenes levels to a minimum and eliminate the organism wherever possible. 
This project has shown that the growth of L. monocytogenes in 10, 20 and 30% mixed 
chicken salads stored at 5 ℃ for 14 days will potentially lead to dangerous levels after 7 
days. When looking further ahead, additional studies are necessary to be carried out. 1: 
Focus on studies of the growth rate at different storage temperature such as 10 ℃ in order 
to complete the secondary models with more balanced data. 2: Changing the proportion of 
chicken composition to confirm if the higher chicken content, the higher growth rate the 
RTE salads will have. 3: Based on the facts that most of RTE salads on the sales are under 
modified atmosphere packaging, it would be crucial to test the growth rate under different 
gas compositions.
