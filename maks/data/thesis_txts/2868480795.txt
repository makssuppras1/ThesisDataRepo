Abstract
Introduction AI models often struggle to generalize to out-of-distribution data, particu-
larly in PET imaging, where variations in scanners, acquisition protocols, and radiotracers
introduce distribution shifts that can significantly impact model performance. Since acquir-
ing large, diverse datasets for every possible variation is costly and challenging, there is a
growing interest in leveraging style transfer techniques to harmonize imaging data. With
this in mind, we set out to develop a latent diffusion model (LDM) capable of performing
style transfer from the [15O]H2O tracer to [18F]FDG as a proof of concept for applying LDMs
to tracer conversion in whole-body PET imaging. The quality of the style transferred images
was evaluated across two downstream tasks, namely PET-to-CT cross-modal translation and
brain age prediction.
Methods This study first presents a patch-based Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoder
(VQVAE) for compression of 3D PET images into a compact and efficient latent space. The
latent representations were utilized for training an LDM which incorporated a novel, region-
based adaptive instance normalization mechanism to facilitate style transfer by aligning the
H2O images to the FDG style distribution before serving as input to the LDM. Multiple
training schemes and loss configurations, including a multi-term loss function, were explored
to optimize the balance between preserving anatomical content and achieving accurate style
transfer. The model was further conditioned using classifier-free guidance to enhance con-
textual consistency during denoising.
Results The VQVAE achieved notable Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio, Structural Similarity
Index Measure, and Multi-Scale Structural Similarity Index Measure scores of 40.5, 0.98 and
0.99 on the FDG test set and 38.43, 0.98, and 0.99 on the H2O test set while compressing
the data to 0.78% of its original size. Although inconsistent quantitative metrics made it
difficult to assess, we ultimately succeeded in producing FDG-like images with the LDM
from a visual standpoint. However, we identified several areas with room for improvement.
We aimed to enhance the signal in the brain relative to the rest of the body, obtain a clear
separation between organs in addition to alignment of organ-specific histograms, all of which
were not fully achieved. In the subsequent downstream tasks, we demonstrated that the best
synthetic CT volumes were obtained from the H2O data and not the style transferred data,
as reflected in the DICE score for bones, which was 0.34 for H2O and 0.24 for the style
transferred data. The results for brain age prediction showed that neither the H2O data nor
the style transferred data could be used for reliable brain age prediction (R2 = 0.012 and
R2 = 0.002, respectively). The FDG data yielded the best results with R2 = 0.106.
Conclusion In this thesis, we demonstrated the feasibility of style transfer between PET
tracers using LDMs and highlighted the challenges of preserving anatomical fidelity while
achieving satisfactory tracer conversion using a patch-based approach. Future work should
explore a downscaling and super-resolution approach as an alternative to patch sampling.

Introduction
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) is a popular imaging modality in modern medicine.
With the injection of a radiopharmaceutical, PET scans provide detailed images of metabolic
and functional processes within the body. Different radioactive tracers, the most widely used
one being [18F]FDG [1], allow clinicians to diagnose, monitor, and evaluate a wide range of
diseases, including cancer, cardiovascular disease, and neurological disorders [2].
As the demand for more accurate and efficient interpretation of complex PET images in-
creases, Artificial Intelligence (AI) has emerged as a powerful tool in this domain.
By
leveraging advanced deep learning models, AI can significantly enhance the diagnostic ac-
curacy and efficiency of PET scans, reducing human-induced errors and optimizing clinical
workflows. AI models are being greatly investigated for their potential integration into dia-
gnostic pipelines for PET imaging, particularly in tasks such as imaging enhancement, tumor
delineation, and the characterization of pathological subtypes. These models are especially
valuable in the early detection and classification of diseases, enabling timely interventions
that can lead to improved patient outcomes [3]. In the context of image enhancement, AI has
proven its ability to improve the quality of PET images, facilitating more accurate clinical
diagnoses. By applying these advanced models, patients may benefit from reduced radiophar-
maceutical dosage and shorter scan times, while clinicians are provided with higher-quality
images, enabling more reliable diagnostic decisions [4]. AI has demonstrated promising res-
ults in tumor delineation, such as the delineation of [18F]FDG PET/CT-based gross tumor
volume in head and neck cancer, where differentiating healthy metabolic activity from ma-
lignant tissue in close proximity is a challenging task for clinicians [5].
Despite these advancements, the effectiveness of AI models in medical imaging is heav-
ily dependent on the availability of large, diverse, and high-quality datasets for accurate
training [6]. This need for extensive datasets presents a significant challenge, where data
acquisition is not only constrained by privacy concerns but also by the high cost and work-
load associated with data annotation, which requires the expertise of trained clinicians, such
as radiologists or nuclear medicine specialists. This challenge is further compounded by the
variation in scanners and protocols used across hospitals, leading to distribution shifts that
complicate data sharing and hinder model generalization as models trained on data from one
site may struggle with out-of-distribution data from another. To address these limitations,
generative AI has been explored not only for creating synthetic training data but also to
modify real data to better align with established training distributions, seeking to improve
the generalizability of existing models.
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) were among the first models used to generate syn-
thetic medical data to address the challenges of limited datasets in healthcare. Early studies,
such as Middel et al. [7], demonstrated how GANs could generate realistic chest X-rays, and
Islam et al. [8] applied GANs to generate brain PET images, demonstrating their potential
to produce high-quality synthetic medical data. In addition, task-driven generative models
have been explored to further enhance generation of labeled data. Zhang et al. [9] intro-
1
==PAGE:12==
duced TD-GANs with task-specific objectives for unsupervised domain adaptation in X-ray
segmentation. This method allowed the generation of synthetic X-ray images aligned with
segmentation tasks, reducing the need for manual expert labeling by creating useful labeled
images in a new domain. Despite this, GANs come with significant challenges, such as train-
ing instability and limited diversity in the generated images [10].
These limitations have led to the rise of Diffusion Models (DMs), such as Denoising Diffusion
Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) which have been shown to surpass GANs in generating more
realistic and diverse medical images as well as being easier to train. For example, Müller-
Franzes et al. [11] demonstrated that their diffusion model, Medfusion, outperformed vari-
ous GANs in terms of synthesizing fundoscopies, chest X-rays, and tissue microscopies and
Khader et al. [12] showed that DMs were able to generate high-quality MRI and CT images,
significantly improving anatomical accuracy and slice consistency compared to GANs. Addi-
tionally, Pinaya et al. [13] demonstrated the potential of Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) in
brain imaging, particularly in generating synthetic MRI images, emphasizing how diffusion
models can maintain anatomical consistency and improve image quality, even when operat-
ing within a low-dimensional latent representation.
DMs have also found applications in image-to-image translation and style transfer for medical
image generation. This process involves transforming images from one modality or contrast
mechanism to another while preserving the anatomical features. Recent studies have shown
that image-to-image translation between two modalities is possible: Xie et al. [14] explored
a joint probability diffusion model utilizing the joint probability distribution between noisy
PET images and noise-free MRI images to synthesize new PET images from the ultra-high-
field MRI images. Additionally, Yu et al. [15] applied an image-constrained diffusion model
to synthesize PET images from structural MRI data. Their model used anatomical details
from MRI scans as constraints to guide the generation of functional PET images, ensuring
that the synthesized images maintained anatomical accuracy. Recent developments, such as
the Adaptive Latent Diffusion Model, have demonstrated the ability to achieve high-quality
style transfer between T1- and T2-weighted scans, with and without contrast enhancement
[16] and the Harmonization framework through Conditional Latent Diffusion (HCLD) effect-
ively achieved harmonization between MRI scans acquired from different scanners, success-
fully mitigating site-related variations through style transfer techniques utilizing Adaptive
Instance Normalization (AdaIN) [17].
The success in style transfer within the realm of
MRI using LDMs paves the way for similar applications in other imaging modalities such as
radiopharmaceutical tracers in PET imaging.

1.1
Thesis aims
This project aims to develop an LDM capable of performing style transfer from the [15O]H2O
tracer to [18F]FDG in PET imaging. The quality of the synthetic images will be evaluated
both quantitatively and qualitatively across two downstream tasks. In both tasks, the out-
puts of the LDM will serve as input to models trained exclusively on FDG data to assess
whether these models can perform as accurately as they do with real FDG data. The tasks
include a PET-to-CT translation model and a brain age regression model.

Background and Related Work
2.1
Positron Emission Tomography
Positron emission tomography (PET) is a functional imaging modality used to visualize and
quantify dynamic physiological processes, such as metabolism, blood flow, and molecular
activity within the body. This is achieved through the administration of different radioact-
ive tracers, which are specifically selected based on the biological processes that are being
investigated.
Figure 1: Line of response [18].
PET operates by detecting gamma photons emitted indirectly by the positron-emitting ra-
diopharmaceutical (Figure 1). As the radionuclide decays, it emits positrons, which interact
with nearby electrons, leading to a matter-antimatter annihilation event. This interaction
produces two 511 keV gamma photons that are emitted in nearly opposite directions. These
annihilation photons are detected by the PET scanner’s array of detectors, which record the
coincident photons and define a line of response (LOR). The system then reconstructs these
events into detailed, quantitative images that represent the distribution and concentration
of the tracer within the tissues [19].
The radioactive tracers are synthesized by binding a radioactive isotope to molecules natur-
ally involved in the physiological processes in the body. For example, in cancer detection,
fluorodeoxyglucose ([18F]FDG), a glucose analog labeled with the isotope fluorine-18, is
commonly used. Since cancer cells require large amounts of energy for rapid growth, they
consume more glucose than normal cells. FDG scans highlight areas of increased glucose
metabolism which enables the identification of tumors and metastases [20]. [15O]H2O is an-
other tracer commonly used in PET, particularly for imaging blood flow. It is produced by
bombarding nitrogen-14 atoms with deuterium in a cyclotron, and it is rapidly distributed
throughout the body following intravenous injection, making it ideal for perfusion studies in
tissues such as the brain and heart [21].

2.1.1
Standardized Uptake Value
The standardized uptake value (SUV) is a semiquantitative metric used in nuclear medi-
cine to assess the uptake of radiotracers in tissue and aid in differentiating between normal
and abnormal metabolic activity. Defined as the ratio of tracer concentration within a spe-
cific region of interest to the overall body concentration, SUV offers a straightforward, but
approximate, measure of tissue activity without requiring complex kinetic modeling. It is
widely applied in PET imaging, where SUV plays a key role in oncology for tumor detection
and staging. Here, an SUV threshold of around 2.5 often indicates malignant tissue. How-
ever, SUV varies depending on factors such as dose calibration, scan time, body composition,
and glucose levels [22, 23]. Among the different methods of SUV normalization, the most
commonly applied is SUV Body Weight (SUVbw). This method adjusts for differences in
body size and amount of injected tracer. The formula for calculating SUV normalized by
body weight is:

2.2
Patch Sampling
Patch sampling is particularly advantageous for improving memory efficiency when working
with large volumetric data. Processing entire images can be computationally infeasible, so
dividing the images into smaller patches allows for more efficient use of resources. This ap-
proach also enables models to focus on learning finer details within localized regions of the
image [26].
TorchIO [27] is an open-source Python library for data preprocessing, augmentation, and
sampling. Patch sampling involves extracting a predefined number of 3D patches of the
same size from the individual 3D volumes (images). The patches are then fed into a queue,
which can be shuffled when full. The batch size determines how many of the patches from
the queue are processed simultaneously. When the queue is empty, a new set of patches
is loaded to continue the process. TorchIO’s LabelSampler can be employed to extract the
patches. This sampler utilizes a label map and corresponding label probabilities as input
parameters. The label map can be based on segmentation masks. The predefined label
probability map provides a flexible way to guide the sampling process by determining the
regions of the images from which patches should be extracted. As a result, the LabelSampler
randomly samples patches with the center voxel corresponding to a labeled region, following
the probability distribution specified by the label probabilities. This sampling strategy is
particularly beneficial, as excluding patches centered on undesired regions during training
can enhance the model’s performance and focus on relevant features. Additionally, the patch
probabilities can be adjusted to focus more on specific regions of the images where model
5
==PAGE:16==
performance may be suboptimal in reconstructing certain signal components.
During the inference phase, the patch sampling process differs from that used during train-
ing, as the images needs to be fully reconstructed. To achieve this, the sampler is required
to extract patches covering the entire image volume. For this purpose, TorchIO’s Grid-
Sampler and GridAggregator can be utilized, allowing for the extraction of patches with a
user-specified size and a defined overlap between patches to ensure dense inference across
the entire volume. By aggregating the reconstructed patches along with their corresponding
locations, the GridAggregator is able to stitch the patches together, resulting in a fully re-
constructed volumetric image. The training and inference processes are illustrated in Figure

The GridAggregator offers three distinct overlap modes, each with its own advantages and
limitations. The crop overlap mode addresses overlapping regions by cropping a portion of
the overlap, ensuring that each patch contributes only to its designated region within the
overlap, which effectively prevents blending in the overlapping areas. The average overlap
mode enables smooth transitions between patches by averaging the voxel intensities in over-

lapping regions, thereby mitigating boundary artifacts. However, this approach may result
in a slight loss of fine details due to the uniform blending. In contrast, the hann overlap
mode applies a Hann window function where the overlapping voxel values are weighted and
normalized. This prioritizes the central regions of the patches.
2.3
Autoencoders
An autoencoder (AE) is a neural network model composed of two primary components: an
encoder and a decoder [28]. Given an input x, the encoder maps it to a latent representation,
capturing a compressed version of the original input.
The latent representation can be
viewed as a feature space that encapsulates essential characteristics extracted from the input.
The decoder then seeks to reconstruct the original input from this latent representation,
producing x which is an approximation of x. Figure 3 illustrates the model architecture.

The goal of the AE is to compress the input data into an efficient, lower-dimensional rep-

space, substantially reducing computational demands compared to working directly in the
full input space [30]. The dimensions of the latent representation are set within the model
architecture, and careful consideration is needed when defining this latent space. The goal is
to identify an optimal size for the latent space that maintains sufficient information for ac-
curate reconstruction to the original space while preserving a sparse, efficient representation
of the input.

2.3.1
Variational Autoencoders
Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) are an extension of the traditional AE given that it still
consists of an encoder-decoder structure. However, instead of outputting a single point in
the latent space, the encoder now outputs a distribution (mean µ and standard deviation
Σ). The decoder then samples a point from this distribution and reconstructs the input
(Figure 4). Given that the VAE models a continuous and smooth distribution it has the
ability to both interpolate between latent representations and create new samples belonging
to the original data distribution - something that the traditional AE lacks because of its
unstructured latent space [31].
Figure 4: Variational Autoencoder architecture [29].
The objective function also changes from only a reconstruction loss to a combination of that
and the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence. This combination is called the Evidence Lower
Bound (ELBO) which is expressed as [29]:
LELBO = Eqϕ(z|x) [log pθ(x|z)] −DKL (qϕ(z|x) || p(z))
(2.3)
The term qϕ(z|x) denotes the approximate posterior distribution of the latent variable z
given the input x, which is parameterized by the encoder’s learnable parameters ϕ. The
decoder, parameterized by θ, models the likelihood pθ(x|z), which represents the probability
of reconstructing the input x from the latent variable z. Eqϕ(z|x) [log pθ(x|z)], measures the
reconstruction accuracy of the VAE by assessing how well the decoder reconstructs x from
z. The second term, DKL(qϕ(z|x) || p(z)), is the KL divergence, which regularizes the latent
space by encouraging the approximate posterior qϕ(z|x) to align with the prior distribution
p(z), which is typically a standard normal distribution N(0, I).
When training a VAE, backpropagation requires computing gradients with respect to the
model’s parameters. However, z, being a randomly sampled variable, does not allow the
flow of gradients. The reparameterization trick provides an elegant workaround. Instead of
sampling z directly from the distribution, we replace the randomness in z with a new inde-

backpropagation to flow through them [32].

2.4
Diffusion Models
The development of diffusion models (DMs) can be traced back to the foundational work
by Sohl-Dickstein et al. [37], who applied concepts inspired by thermodynamics to deep
learning. They introduced the idea of gradually transforming a complex data distribution
into a simpler, Gaussian distribution through a controlled diffusion process. This forward
process operates in a sequence of small, reversible steps, where noise is systematically added
to data, progressively "diffusing" it toward a Gaussian distribution. The reverse process
then reconstructs the original data by successively removing noise. Ho et al. [38] made a
substantial contribution to the field with DDPMs by simplifying the reverse process and
introducing an optimized training objective that made it feasible to train DMs on large-scale
datasets.
2.4.1
The Forward Process
Mathematically, the forward process is modeled as a Markov chain, a stochastic process
where each state in the sequence depends only on the preceding state [39]. This means
10
==PAGE:21==
that the state of the data, xt, at any time step, t, is influenced solely by the state from the
previous time step, xt−1. This process is depicted in Figure 6. During each time step, a
small amount of Gaussian noise is added to xt−1. Here, x0 represents the original, unaltered
data point, x1 is the data after the first noise addition, and xT is the final, fully diffused
state. With a sufficiently large T, the distribution of xT will approximate pure Gaussian
noise. Each step of the forward process is defined by the probability distribution q(xt|xt−1).
In the DDPM framework, the forward diffusion process is deterministic in that it produces a
Gaussian distribution in the final state and does not involve any learnable parameters [40].
Additionally, [41] mentions that the approach, where noise varies across steps, stabilizes
model training and improves performance by balancing two priorities: it enables the model
to learn from both highly represented data and "low-density" regions - those with fewer
examples in the training data. To achieve this balance, diffusion models use a sequence of
noise levels, determined by a variance scheduler. Each time step, t, introduces Gaussian
noise scaled by a factor, βt, with the noise level increasing as t progresses. The variance
scheduler ensures that β1 < β2 < · · · < βT, where each βt remains between 0 and 1.
At each timestep, the Gaussian noise added is characterized by both a mean µt and variance
Σt, both dependent on βt:
• Mean µt: The mean of the noise added at time step t is given by µt = √αtxt−1,
where αt = 1 −βt. This scaling ensures that the noise resembles a scaled version of
the previous step, with a small βt resulting in minimal deviation from xt−1.
• Variance Σt: The variance of the noise added at each time step is defined as Σt = βtI,
where I is the identity matrix. A higher βt leads to increased noise, while a small βt
results in minimal added noise.

2.4.2
The Reverse Process
The reverse process is where the actual learning takes place, as the model learns to recon-
struct clean data from noisy inputs. By training on progressively noisier data, the model
learns to denoise pure Gaussian noise into an image. This eventually enables the generation
of new data by initiating the reverse process with a sample drawn from a pure Gaussian

distribution.
In theory, the reverse process could be defined as simply the opposite of the forward diffusion
process. While the forward process is modeled as q(xt|xt−1), the reverse process would ideally
be represented as q(xt−1|xt). However, directly computing q(xt−1|xt) is intractable due to
the complex dependencies involved. To address this, the reverse process is approximated
by a neural network, denoted pθ(xt−1|xt), which learns the reverse transitions. Rather than
directly predicting x0 from xT, the model learns to predict the noise present in xt and
then removes a fraction of this noise, based on the variance scheduler, to approximate xt−1.
This approach allows the model to refine each step by iteratively denoising the data, and
recovering the structure of the original data distribution [42].

Classifier-Free Guidance
Proposed by Ho et al. [43], Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) challenges Classifier Guidance by
eliminating the need for a separate classifier when guiding a DM. In CFG, an unconditional
DM, ϵθ(xt, t), and a conditional DM, ϵθ(xt, t, c) are trained simultaneously. During training,

During inference, the model output is a combination of the unconditional and conditional

2.4.4
Latent Diffusion Models
DMs typically operate directly in pixel space and this approach comes with a significant
drawback in that it is computationally expensive, which makes both training and inference
slow. To address these challenges, Rombach et al. [30] introduced LDMs, which reduce
12
==PAGE:23==
computational costs while retaining the quality and flexibility of DMs. LDMs achieve this
by splitting the training process into two stages [44]:
• Perceptual Image Compression: In the first stage, an autoencoder is trained to
map the high-dimensional data into a lower-dimensional latent space that is percep-
tually equivalent to the original data. The compression reduces the computational
burden while preserving essential features.
• Latent Diffusion: In the second stage, a DM is trained on the compact latent space
learned by the autoencoder, instead of the high-dimensional pixel space, which makes
training significantly more scalable.

2.6
Related Work
Style transfer, originally popularized by Gatys et al. [58], introduced the ability to separate
and recombine the content and style of images using convolutional neural networks (CNNs).
While initially applied to artistic domains, this concept has since been adapted across diverse
fields, including medical imaging, where the ability to transfer within and between modalities
holds significant promise. This section reviews relevant work categorized into three groups:
(1) PET-to-PET style transfer, (2) MRI-to-MRI style transfer and cross-modal translation,
and (3) non-medical style transfer methods.
PET-to-PET Style Transfer with Paired Data Style transfer within PET imaging
has been explored to enable conversion between tracers or harmonization across datasets.
For example, Shen et al. [59] developed the Variable Augmented Invertible Network (TC-
INN) to facilitate a bidirectional mapping between [18F]FDG and [18F]DOPA images. The
model is optimized using a combined forward and backward loss function to minimize differ-
ences between the style transferred and reference images. They demonstrated high fidelity
and highlighted invertible neural networks’ potential in training cases where paired data
is available. Additionally, Zhou et al. [60] introduced a Segmentation-Guided Style-based
Generative Adversarial Network (SGSGAN) to perform style transfer from low-dose to high-
dose PET images. This approach integrates the tasks of synthesizing high-dose PET images
from paired low- and high-dose inputs and segmenting regions of interest (ROIs) from the
synthesized images, which are then compared to those of the reference high-dose images.
The combination of leveraging AdaIN for style modulation and focusing on ROIs allowed
SGSGAN to generate high-dose PET images effectively. In the realm of multi-tracer conver-
sion, the 3D Unified Anatomy-aware Cyclic Adversarial Network (UCAN) by Zhou et al. [61]
enabled multi-tracer synthesis for Alzheimer’s disease patients, achieving tracer translation
between [18F]FDG, [11C]UCB-J, and [11C]PiB. The model takes as input a combination of
17
==PAGE:28==
the source tracer PET volume, a paired MR volume for anatomical guidance, and a one-hot-
encoded target tracer domain label. They demonstrated the feasibility of multi-tracer style
transfer on a limited cohort of 35 patients, each of whom underwent all three PET tracer
scans as well as corresponding MR imaging.
MRI Style Transfer and Cross-Modal Translation Kim et al.
[16] introduced an
Adaptive Latent Diffusion Model (ALDM) for style transfer between various MRI contrast
mechanisms, such as T1, T2, and FLAIR while emphasizing the preservation of 3D ana-
tomical structures.
By operating on perceptually meaningful latent representations, the
model optimizes computational efficiency and it integrates novel Multiple-Switchable Spa-
cially Adaptive Normalization (MS-SPADE) layers for dynamic adaptation to target styles
which enables one-to-many style transfers without the need for separate models. Wu et
al. proposed the HCLD, a method designed to address site-related variability in unpaired
multi-site brain MRI studies. The framework uses a two-stage approach, starting with a 3D
autoencoder to encode MRIs into a latent space, followed by a Conditional Latent Diffusion
Model (cLDM) to perform style harmonization, before the decoder reconstructs them again.
The diffusion model translates latent representations of source MRIs from one scanner type
into target-style harmonized MRIs from another type of scanner. The method introduces
latent map fusion through AdaIN and employs loss functions tailored for content and style
preservation. Their model was evaluated on over 4,000 MRIs from multiple datasets and
demonstrated superior performance compared to GAN-based and slice-level harmonization
methods.
While style transfer and cross-modal image translation are distinct tasks - style transfer
focuses on altering the appearance of images while preserving their semantic content, and
cross-modal translation involves generating data in one modality from another - we ex-
amined cross-modal methods as they share conceptual similarities. Armanious et al. [62]
introduced MedGAN, a GAN-based framework tested on multiple tasks, including PET-to-
CT translation, where the generator applies a chain of encoder-decoder blocks (U-blocks) to
progressively refine the output. However, unlike many other frameworks, MedGAN operates
on 2D slices rather than full 3D volumes, which simplifies computation but limits its ability
to fully capture volumetric context. In another approach to cross-modal image synthesis, Li
et al. [63] proposed PASTA, a diffusion-based framework for MRI-to-PET synthesis. The
architecture features a dual-arm symmetry, with a conditioner arm encoding task-specific
representations from MRI data, and a denoiser arm performing reverse diffusion to recon-
struct PET images. PASTA employs Adaptive Group Normalization (AdaGN) to integrate
structural, temporal, and clinical data for pathology-aware synthesis. The model also incor-
porates so-called Cycle Exchange Consistency (CycleEx) to enforce bidirectional consistency
between MRI and PET domains as well as meta-based pathology priors to prioritize clinic-
ally relevant regions for Alzheimer’s disease.
Non-Medical Style Transfer Methods Style transfer techniques in medical imaging often
draw inspiration from foundational methods developed in non-medical contexts. Notable
examples include the introduction of AdaIN by Huang et al.
[46] which revolutionized

Data and Materials
The two datasets utilized in this study were acquired using a Biograph Vision Quadra
PET/CT Long-Axial FOV scanner at Rigshospitalet, Denmark.
All images were stored

dataset, referred to as the FDG dataset, consists of 3,545 whole-body PET images acquired

dimensions of [440, 440, 531] and a voxel size of [1.65mm, 1.65mm, 2.00mm]. In addition to

3.2
Preprocessing
3.2.1
The FDG Dataset
The starting point of the preprocessing was to crop the images and convert the datatype to
obtain a new dataset with the original number of images that took up less storage and was
faster to load.
The FDG images in the original dataset contained a large amount of empty space. This
was mitigated by smoothing each image with a Gaussian kernel and creating a binary mask
where all voxels with intensity above 0 were assigned to 1 and the rest to 0. Then the
center of mass (COM) was computed and used as the center point for cropping. We cropped
with a predefined amount of voxels in each direction chosen to [x, y, z] = [COM±190,
COM±135, z]. The head of the patient is often positioned very close to the edge of the
image so the z-dimension was intentionally left uncropped to ensure that no part of the
head was inadvertently removed. The cropping dimensions were determined from visual

inspection of the training dataset. The segmentation masks were resampled to [1.65mm,
1.65mm, 2.0mm] to comply with the PET images before cropping using the same COM and
cropping dimensions. Since the segmentation masks only contained values from 0 to 117,
these files were simply converted from float64 to uint8. To convert the PET images from
float32 to uint16, Eq. 3.1 was utilized.

3.2.1.1
Normalization
The normalization process consisted of two key stages:
SUV Normalization
The FDG data was normalized to SUV using the formula from
Section 2.1.1. The primary objective of SUV normalization was to ensure that variations in
tracer distribution due to patient size and dose were accounted for. Additionally, all SUVs
above 10 were clamped at 10.
Intensity Normalization
After SUV normalization, the PET images were scaled to
a range between -1 and 1. The intensity normalization process was based on predefined
thresholds that mapped SUVs to specific intervals within the range of [-1;1], using a piecewise
linear transformation. The process included the following mappings:
• SUVs in the range [0, 1] were mapped to [-1, 0].
• SUVs in the range (1, 4] were mapped to (0, 0.5].
• SUVs in the range (4, 10] were mapped to (0.5, 1].
This approach ensured that the entire data range was mapped to a consistent scale while
enhancing the distinction between low SUVs, which make up a large proportion of the data
while preserving the relative differences between low, medium, and high SUV regions. The
mappings are illustrated in Figure 8 below.

3.2.2
The H2O Dataset
The preprocessing of the H2O dataset closely mirrored that of the FDG dataset, involving
similar steps of cropping, resampling, and normalization.
The segmentation masks were paired with their respective PET images based on the ac-
quisition time of the PET image and its corresponding CT image on which the mask was
based. Then, the matched H2O images and segmentation masks were resampled to the same
voxel size of [1.65mm, 1.65mm, 2.0mm] as the FDG dataset and then cropped to the same
shape of [380, 270, 531] using their COM, which ensured consistency across both datasets.
As opposed to the FDG dataset, the H2O data was not compressed to uint16 because of the
limited amount of images.
3.2.2.1
Normalization and Augmentation
Multiple normalization methods were tested on the H2O data, the first one being mean brain
normalization where the images were normalized using the mean intensity of the brain re-
gion, which was identified through the segmentation masks.
23
==PAGE:34==
The other normalization methods were all quantile-based. This method involved dividing
all voxel intensities in an image by a predefined quantile value of the intensity distribution.
Normalization was performed using four different quantiles: 80%, 90%, 95%, and 99%. To
evaluate the effectiveness of these normalization techniques, we compared the intensity dis-
tributions across different ROIs (See Appendix 8.2).
After quantile normalization, the PET images were clamped to a maximum intensity value
of 4 and then scaled to a range between -1 and 1 similar to the FDG data. The following
mappings were used for the H2O data:
• Intensity values in the range [0, 0.3] were mapped to [-1, 0].
• Intensity values in the range (0.3, 1.5] were mapped to (0, 0.5].
• Intensity values in the range (1.5, 4] were mapped to (0.5, 1].
The primary objective was to transform the H2O intensity histogram to closely resemble the
FDG histogram (Figure 9). By aligning the intensity distributions using these empirically
selected thresholds, the transformation aimed to standardize the representation of intensity
values across the two modalities, facilitating better comparability and downstream processing
in subsequent model training.

3.2.3
Patch Sampling Strategy
Training a DM directly on whole-body 3D PET images is computationally infeasible due
to their high dimensionality and large memory requirements. To address this, we employ
an LDM, which significantly reduces computational costs while maintaining image fidelity.
However, training an LDM on the entire volumetric scans is equally impractical. Therefore,
a targeted patch sampling strategy was necessary to prioritize regions of interest and enable
efficient training while omitting the need for excessive downsampling.
This approach was implemented using a LabelSampler from TorchIO [27] detailed in Section
25
==PAGE:36==
2.2, which excluded patches centered around background voxels, as these patches contrib-
uted limited meaningful information to the training process. To ensure that patches could
be sampled near the image boundaries, we padded each image by half the patch size in each
direction for each dimension. This padding was important for anatomical structures, such as
the brain, located near an edge in the volume. During both training and validation, patches
were randomly sampled from the data. The LabelSampler allowed for dynamic adjustments
to sampling probabilities, enabling organ-specific focus. For instance, the brain is underrep-
resented in patches from the FDG dataset as the scanner’s FOV often starts in the middle of
or below the brain. By increasing the sampling probability for the brain, we ensured that a
higher proportion of patches centered on brain regions were included in the training process,
improving the model’s ability to capture relevant anatomical features.
3.3
Models
To perform style transfer between the FDG and H2O datasets, we propose to use a Latent
Diffusion Model (LDM) (Section 2.4) following a 2-stage pipeline. The stage 1 model is a
Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQVAE) (Section 2.3) which enables the cre-
ation of compact and effective latent space representations for patches extracted from the
PET volumes. The stage 2 model is a UNet-based Diffusion Model (DM) which operates
in the latent space and handles the style transfer. Before the H2O latent codes undergo the
diffusion process, we decouple the content of the latent code from the style with the use of
Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN), effectively overlaying the H2O content with the
style of FDG. This coarsely aligns the latent code with the target FDG distribution before
the LDM refines it to obtain a style transferred latent code. Because of the shared latent
space, the VQVAE ensures that the style transferred H2O latent codes can be reconstructed
in the style of FDG. The overall pipeline of the process is illustrated in Figure 11.

The VQVAE was first trained on the full FDG dataset for 7 epochs, taking approximately
56 hours. It was then fine-tuned on the H2O dataset for 60 epochs, which required around
33 hours. A detailed overview of the model configuration is shown in Appendix 8.3.
The model employs a structured loss function that incorporates multiple components (Eq.
3.2) to ensure accurate patch reconstruction and effective utilization of the latent space rep-
resentation. An L1-loss between the original and reconstructed patches was applied as the
reconstruction loss (Lrec). The use of a VQ regularization loss (Lreg), adapted from the
MONAI framework [67] to fit our architecture, was employed as well. This VQ utilizes an
EMA to learn the codebook parameters. The codebook size, commitment cost, and smooth-
ing epsilon were inspired from [67].
The adversarial loss (Ladv) is incorporated by setting up a simple discriminator network,
composed of three 3D convolutional layers. The first layer applies 32 filters with a Leak-
yReLU activation to capture initial features, downsampling through a stride of 2.
The
second layer doubles the filters to 64, further halving the spatial dimensions and stabilizing
training with batch normalization, followed by another LeakyReLU activation. The final
28
==PAGE:39==
layer reduces the output to a single feature map, reshaped to provide a prediction score that
classifies the input image as real or generated. This adversarial loss encourages the gener-
ator to produce realistic reconstructions by computing the binary cross-entropy between the
discriminator’s predictions for reconstructed images and a target label that classifies them
as real.
The perceptual loss (Lperc) is applied, by leveraging a ResNet-10 model pretrained on Med-
icalNet’s 23-dataset collection [68], with its final layer removed.
The perceptual loss is
computed by passing both reconstructed and original patches through the ResNet-10 model
and extracting features from various layers. We then calculate the L2 norm between cor-
responding features in each layer, capturing both fine-grained and structural details of the
patches. The training objective is shown below:
LVQVAE = Lrec + Lreg + Ladvλadv + Lpercλperc
(3.2)
where λadv and λperc are scalars that determine the weighting of each loss component. As
the loss function incorporates an adversarial component, the VQVAE can theoretically be
classified as a VQGAN. However, to maintain clarity and consistency, the model will be
referred to as the VQVAE for the remainder of this study.
The design of the latent space, including both the downscaling factor and the number of
channels in the latent dimension, was guided by insights from previous work. Specifically,
we took inspiration from the approach presented by Pinaya et al. [13] regarding the size
of the latent space representation. Furthermore, we drew insight from Rombach et al. [30]
to guide our approach in defining the number of latent channels relative to the degree of
downscaling applied to the input data.
Group Normalization (GN) was chosen for our VQVAE due to its suitability for small batch
sizes. Unlike Batch Normalization, which relies on batch statistics and struggles with small
batches, GN normalizes groups of channels independently of the batch dimension, ensuring
stable performance regardless of the batch size [69]. This property makes it particularly
well-suited for our application.

All experiments (for both the VQVAE and the LDM) were performed on one of three GPU

The experimental framework for the VQVAE was designed to balance computational ef-
ficiency with meaningful model evaluation. All experiments were performed on a subset of
the full FDG dataset, consisting of 1500 training images and 150 validation images. The

and computational efficiency. Larger networks may capture more intricate data patterns,

3.3.2
LDM
The LDM was designed under the hypothesis that an LDM trained solely on FDG data
would be able to denoise an H2O image that had been corrupted to the extent that its style
was lost but still had its content preserved, transforming it into an FDG-like image with the
original H2O content.
The LDM utilized the VQVAE as its stage 1 model and was trained on the entire FDG
dataset for 121 epochs. A detailed overview of the model configuration is shown in Ap-
pendix 8.5. Before training and inference, we extracted eight patches from each image in
the FDG dataset and precomputed their latent codes, which served as input for the LDM.
The model is based on the UNet architecture proposed by Pinaya et al. [13] and incorpor-
ates Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) with the encoded segmentation masks to allow spatial
conditioning.
All LDM iterations utilized MONAI’s DiffusionModelUNet [67]. The general architecture
of the diffusion model is illustrated in Figure 14. Like a classic UNet, it comprises suc-
cessive downsampling layers, a middle block (or bottleneck), and upsampling layers. The
downsampling layers use 3D convolutions with stride for spatial resolution reduction, while
the upsampling layers utilize interpolation with nearest neighbor followed by a convolution
for refinement of the features. All layers incorporate ResNet blocks, where each block ap-
plies a sequence of operations twice: group normalization, SiLU activation, and convolution.
Additionally, all layers except the first and the last include self-attention blocks which are
changed to cross-attention blocks when the model is conditioned on spatial information. We
also employ sinusoidal positional encoding [70] for time embedding, which is added to the
input of each ResNet block to condition the network on temporal information.

3.3.2.1
Precomputing Style Statistics for Latent Representations
Unlike previous work that uses randomly selected volumes [17] to compute style statistics
for AdaIN, we adopted a dictionary-based approach, where the mean and standard deviation
were stored based on the combination of anatomical structures present in each FDG patch.
This dictionary was then used to retrieve the relevant style statistics corresponding to the
anatomical region of a given H2O patch and applied in both AdaIN and the computation of
LS.
First, the pretrained VQVAE was loaded to serve as the encoder for generating the latent
representations. Each image in the FDG training set was processed using the same grid-based
sampling method employed during inference, which produced overlapping patches. For each
encoded patch, we computed the mean and standard deviation across spatial dimensions
(depth, height, width) for each latent channel, resulting in channel-wise mean and standard
deviation vectors for each patch. The specific label combination for the patch (i.e., unique
organ labels present in the patch) was also recorded. Patch-level means and standard devi-
ations were then grouped by the unique label combinations. When a new label combination
was encountered, we initialized a new group in an HDF5 file otherwise we appended to an
existing group. The groups ended up containing a collection of patch-level statistics for the
corresponding unique label combinations, meaning multiple means and standard deviations
per label combination. As a result, we computed the median of the means and standard
deviations, yielding representative channel-wise style parameters.
After processing all patches, we also aggregated the style statistics across all label com-
binations. This step produced an "all" group representing the average style, calculated as
the mean of the means and mean of the standard deviations from all groups, respectively.
This global group served as a fallback for training and inference cases where specific label
combinations were unavailable.
3.3.2.2
Hyperparameter Optimization and Fine-tuning Strategy Comparison
The fine-tuning strategy comparison was conducted with several hypotheses in mind, the
overall one being that the model would be able to denoise an H2O image from a semi-noised
state to an FDG-like image with content from the original H2O image. We expected that
using a model pretrained on FDG as a robust initialization would improve performance when
training on H2O data. At the same time, we recognized the challenge of balancing content
preservation with effective style transfer, as the model could lose important FDG charac-
teristics during adaptation to the H2O data. Additionally, we hypothesized that the model
might lose spatial orientation during style transfer and that providing more spatial con-
text would help preserve anatomical structures. These hypotheses guided the experimental
design, which systematically evaluated different model architectures, training objectives, and
34

fine-tuning approaches, inspired by previous work [13, 17, 43], with an overview found in
Table 2. All model iterations were trained until convergence using the Adam optimizer.
Batch size was adjusted for each individual experiment and we used a learning rate that
reduced on plateau. We utilized the precomputed style statistics presented in Section 3.3.2.1
for AdaIN and computation of LS.
During inference, all models incorporate AdaIN, and the patches undergo 5 noise steps,
reaching a noise level of 30, followed by 6 denoising steps. The number of noise and denois-
ing steps, as well as the maximum noise level, were selected based on preliminary tests on the
validation set, using visual assessment as the sole criterion. The baseline model consisted of
the VQVAE, where AdaIN was applied to the latent code after encoding and before vector
quantization and decoding. The baseline did therefore not incorporate diffusion at any stage.

3.4
Evaluation
3.4.1
VQVAE
The performance of the model iterations was evaluated using a comprehensive set of quantit-
ative metrics and qualitative assessments. Quantitatively, MSE, PSNR, SSIM, and MS-SSIM
(Sections 2.5.1 - 2.5.2.1) were calculated to assess reconstruction quality. Additionally, the
models were evaluated by comparing the means of mean SUV and the standard deviations of
mean SUVs across various organs in the reconstructed images to those in the original images
by utilizing an eroded mask of the organs as illustrated in Appendix 8.6. Both the mean
SUVs and their standard deviations were considered in the evaluation, however, greater em-
phasis was placed on the mean SUVs to ensure that the distributions were centered correctly
before assessing differences in spread.
3.4.2
LDM
The quantitative evaluation of style transfer success relied on two metrics: KL divergence and
KID (Sections 2.5.3 - 2.5.4.1). KL assess the alignment of the distribution of style transferred
images with the distribution of the target FDG images, while KID evaluates the similarity
of extracted features from the two sets of images. Furthermore, a qualitative assessment was
conducted to evaluate how well the style transferred images aligned with the characteristic
features of the FDG images shown in Figures 17-20. This assessment focused on several key
aspects: the preservation of a relatively high signal in the brain compared to other organs,
the presence of homogeneous signals in visceral organs, the visibility of anatomical struc-
tures such as organs and the spine, and a reduction in signal intensity within the vein where
the tracer was injected. Additionally, intensity histograms for selected organs were analyzed.
For KL divergence, a precomputed histogram of the FDG images was utilized as the rep-
resentation of the target distribution. To compare this with the style transferred images, a
histogram was generated from the style transferred outputs. The KL divergence was then
computed as the relative entropy between these two histograms.
36
==PAGE:47==
The KID was adapted using inspiration from work of Jayasumana et al. [55], who used
CLIP embeddings instead of Inception embeddings. However, given the focus on medical
imaging, MedicalNet’s ResNet10 model [68] was employed for embedding the images, as it
is trained on a variety of medical datasets and assumed to be more adept at capturing the
domain-specific features relevant to medical imaging. To avoid confusion, our version of KID
will henceforth be referred to as KMD. To compute the KMD, feature representations from
the third layer were first extracted for a fixed set of 35 FDG images. Similarly, the style
transferred images underwent the same feature extraction process. The similarity between
these two sets of feature representations was then assessed using the approximation of the
squared maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) with a radial basis function (RBF) kernel (Eq.
2.27).
3.5
Downstream Tasks
To evaluate the efficacy of the style transferred H2O volumes, we designed two downstream
tasks which were chosen to assess the utility of the style transferred data in scenarios where
FDG-trained models could be applied, specifically in predicting CT volumes and brain age.
Initial experiments indicated that the style transferred images did not utilize the full intens-
ity range. To address this, a post-processing step was introduced that involved clamping
intensities below -0.99 and above 0 before rescaling to span the full range of [-1, 1]. Below,
we outline the methodologies and evaluation metrics for each task.
3.5.1
CT Volume Prediction
The first downstream task for this study involved a transfer learned UNet model trained
to predict synthetic CT volumes from FDG volumes [71]. This model was finetuned using
the intensity normalized FDG volumes from the training set presented in Section 3.2.1.
The objective was to evaluate whether the style transferred H2O volumes improved the
model’s CT prediction performance compared to the original H2O volumes on both the
validation and test sets. Organ segmentations were utilized for organ-specific evaluation.
The following inference metrics were used to compare the original and synthetic CT volumes:
the DICE score was calculated for regions with Hounsfield Unit (HU) values greater than
100, corresponding to a bone mask, and the mean absolute error (MAE) was measured across
the entire volume as well as for specific organs.
3.5.2
Brain Age Prediction
For the second downstream task, we trained a regression model in the form of a 3D CNN,
following the exact setup proposed by Jonsson et al. [72], for the prediction of a patient’s
age from their brain volume. The model was trained exclusively on the intensity normalized
FDG training set.
We cropped the images to [128, 128, 96] using the brain’s COM as
reference point and excluded all images where the brain was partly or entirely missing. This
resulted in a filtered dataset of 811 images. We assessed the model’s performance on the
style transferred images by measuring its MAE and comparing it against its performance
on the FDG and H2O validation and test sets. Due to the exclusion of activation in the
37
==PAGE:48==
last layer, the model output could both be negative or unreasonably large, so we elected to
clamp the outputs to [0; 120] before computing the MAE.

4.1.5
Experiment 4: Increased Sampling Probability for Brain-centered Patches
Previous experiments revealed challenges specific to the brain, where reconstructed images
struggled to accurately capture fine details. These limitations were observed both visually
(Appendix 8.7) and in the quantitative evaluation of the mean SUV (Table 6). This disparity
could arise from the anatomical position of the brain relative to other organs. Unlike most
organs, which are closely located and often clustered within the central body cavity, the
brain is anatomically isolated in the cranial region. As a result, when patches are sampled
with an even distribution, the likelihood of the brain being included in patches centered on
other organs is relatively low. Moreover, when the brain is included in such patches, it is
often positioned near the patch’s edges, unlike other organs, which are more likely to appear
closer to the patch center due to their proximity to one another. This uneven representation
can limit the model’s ability to learn fine-grained features for the brain during training. To
address this, we adjusted the sampling strategy during training by significantly increasing the
probability of selecting patches centered around brain voxels. Specifically, the probability
of sampling a brain-centered patch was increased by a factor of 10, while the remaining
probabilities for other organs were adjusted accordingly. The model trained with increased
sampling probability for brain-centered patches is referred to as Model 5.

Figure 20: Original and reconstructed images from the FDG validation set for models trained with and
without increased sampling probability for brain-centered patches.
The quantitative metrics shown in Table 5 and 6 exhibit minimal differences between the
models, with the exception of the SUV values of the brain, which show a significant im-
provement in Model 5.

Discussion
The advancement of AI in medical imaging is largely hindered by data scarcity, a funda-
mental challenge that limits the development of robust and generalizable models. This issue
is further compounded by inconsistent practices, imaging protocols, and equipment across
hospitals. These inconsistencies lead to significant variability in data quality and format,
making shared data integration and collaboration across institutions extremely challenging.
The lack of standardization not only hampers the pooling of resources but also diminishes
the effectiveness of AI models trained on specific datasets, which can fail to perform reliably
when applied to data from different institutions.
With these issues in mind, we aimed to expand the understanding of LDMs in whole-body
PET imaging - an area that remains underexplored - by investigating their application in
style transfer from images acquired with the [15O]H2O tracer to images resembling [18F]FDG
scans. In the process, we sought to develop a reliable framework for applying diffusion mod-
els in whole-body PET imaging; a framework with the potential to be extended beyond
this specific application, enabling style transfer between other tracers or harmonization of
imaging data across different scanners, ultimately contributing to standardized and versatile
solutions in medical imaging.
To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first to employ LDMs for style transfer
in whole-body PET imaging, specifically transforming images from one tracer to another.
This novel application demonstrates the potential of LDMs to address the aforementioned
challenges that AI faces in medical imaging. Another key contribution of our work is the in-
troduction of a dictionary-based AdaIN approach which utilizes precomputed style statistics
based on localized regions. By combining our AdaIN approach with an LDM, we generated
FDG-like images while preserving the underlying anatomical structures. The effectiveness
of the style transfer was evaluated through two downstream tasks designed to test whether
the style transferred outputs of the LDM could be utilized as input for models trained ex-
clusively on real FDG data. These tasks included a UNet for cross-modal translation of
PET to CT images and a CNN-based brain age regressor trained to predict a patient’s age
from their brain volume. The first downstream task yielded unexpected results, with the
original H2O data proving to be the best input for CT translation, outperforming the style
transferred data. This could suggest that the less sharpened reconstructions produced by the
style transfer model, compared to the high-quality original H2O data, may have influenced
the results, particularly since the UNet was trained on high-quality FDG data. The age
prediction model demonstrated better performance on the real FDG data and the results
suggested that neither the H2O nor the style transferred data could be reliably used for
age prediction with this particular model. It is important to note that this approach to
brain age prediction was purely experimental. Unlike other methods focusing on MRI data
[73], our work focused on cropped PET images, and it remains unclear whether this is a
valid method. We also evaluated how well the outputs of two additional model iterations
performed on both downstream tasks.
We selected the best-performing model based on
quantitative metrics (FDG-L w/o AdaIN) and the runner-up when considering both metrics
59
==PAGE:70==
and visual assessments (TL-L-It3). The results are not included, as they were similar to
those of FDG-L-CFG.
With the suboptimal performance observed on the style transferred images, it raises ques-
tions about the feasibility of our approach for the intended task.
Although the AdaIN
operation successfully adjusted the signal distribution toward visually FDG-like images, it
also significantly shifted the image intensities toward the lower end of the intensity range.
Furthermore, the limited success also brings attention to how to appropriately balance and
prioritize content versus style in our unpaired data setup. The fact that the H2O data was
derived from the first 100 seconds of the dynamic scan provided a unique opportunity to
observe what the model perceived as content and style (e.g., the high signal in the injection
vein). Otherwise, this distinction would primarily be limited to intensity differences in the
organs. We observed that transfer learned models tended to retain the signal in the vein,
whereas this signal was significantly reduced in the LDM iterations solely trained on FDG
data. Furthermore, the AdaIN approach was expected to reduce the signal in the lungs
completely which was not achieved. These observations highlight a fundamental challenge
in clearly distinguishing content from style. If the signal (e.g. in the lungs) differ in the
two datasets and are classified as style for evaluating a successful style transfer, the model
perceiving it as content challenges this criteria. Incorporating CFG into our models was an-
ticipated to enhance style transfer performance. However, the CFG model trained with the
multi-term loss showed the opposite effect, both visually and in terms of the KL divergence,
when compared to TL-L-It3. This outcome again highlights the challenges with content and
style as guidance during diffusion resulted in suboptimal performance for this model.
The VQVAE demonstrated improved performance on both the FDG and H2O test sets
following transfer learning with the H2O data. While this improvement was anticipated for
the H2O data, it was unexpected that our model also exhibited enhanced performance on
the FDG test set after being trained to generalize across both tracers. A possible explana-
tion to this is that the preprocessing step for H2O effectively aligned its distribution to the
FDG data (Figure 9), enabling the VQVAE model to achieve improved generalization to this
distribution. It is also worth considering whether the model was sufficiently trained on the
FDG data prior to transfer learning but this aspect was not explored due to time constraints.
Previous studies have highlighted the inherent challenges of performing style transfer within
PET imaging despite some degree of success. Shen et al. [59] demonstrated satisfactory
results in style transfer from DOPA to FDG using an invertible neural network (INN), while
Zhou et al. [61] showcased the feasibility of multi-tracer style transfer with an anatomy-
aware CycleGAN. Both studies acknowledged the difficulty of the task, even under condi-
tions more favorable than ours. Specifically, they utilized paired data and focused on brain
images, which present fewer complexities compared to unpaired, whole-body images. Closer
related to our problem, Zhou et al. [60] developed a segmentation-guided style-based GAN
and achieved state-of-the-art results in synthesizing high-dose whole-body PET images from
low-dose ones. Again, their study benefited from the use of paired data, which facilitated
better alignment of image content between source and target domains. Shifting from GANs
60
==PAGE:71==
to LDMs, but still relying on paired data, Kim et al. [16] introduced MS-SPADE blocks to
enable style transfer across various brain MRI techniques. Their method surpassed existing
generative approaches, achieving style transfer to multiple MRI contrast mechanisms from
a single source MRI image.
To the best of our knowledge, the study by Wu et al. [17] is the most closely related to
our work. They also developed an LDM for style transfer using AdaIN, focusing on har-
monizing unpaired T1-weighted MRI brain images across different acquisition sites. Our
work still differs in several aspects as we adopted a patch-based framework due to the large
size of our images, which made it impossible to work with the entire volume at full resolu-
tion. Additionally, our dataset presented greater variability, compared to theirs. Wu et al.
[17] demonstrated superior performance over other approaches, including 2D GANs, which
neglect volumetric information, as well as 3D GAN methods, which highlights the potential
applicability of their 3D LDM-based method to other domains.
One of the key strengths of this study is the well-optimized shared latent space provided
by our VQVAE, enabling high-quality reconstructions for both FDG and H2O images. This
provided a strong foundation for exploring and refining our LDM by providing a unified and
effective compression framework.
The use of AdaIN requires a mean and standard deviation from a target style image which,
by Wu et al. [17], was obtained from a randomly selected volume. We speculated that using
volume-wide statistics might not represent local patch-based statistics in our setting, and
thus opted to identify candidate patches which shared the same combination of anatomical
structures. The precomputed style statistics facilitated faster training of the LDM iterations
with the multi-term loss by eliminating the need to load and encode an FDG patch along-
side the H2O patch during training and inference. This approach also mitigated the reliance
on precise anatomical alignment between the datasets, accounting for variations in patient
positioning introduced by differing imaging protocols.
The large size of the FDG dataset was a significant asset, providing a robust foundation
for training and reducing the need for additional augmentations to enhance variability. In
contrast, augmentations were applied to the smaller H2O dataset. The preprocessing of the
FDG dataset optimized the VQVAE training by enabling faster data loading, while the ex-
perimental VQVAE setup, which utilized only a subset of the full dataset, further accelerated
optimization. Consistency across the two datasets was ensured by acquiring both using the
same scanner, with the only differences being the tracer used and the patients involved.
When performing style transfer, a major challenge came from the fact that the datasets
used in this study were unpaired. Two distinct training strategies were employed depending
on which dataset we used. For the FDG dataset we had ground truth data which allowed
us to employ the standard training objective described in Eq. 2.13, as we wanted the model
to learn the fundamental FDG distribution. However, when training the LDM on the H2O
data, the ground truth reference FDG data was derived from adaptive instance normalized
61
==PAGE:72==
H2O images rather than actual FDG scans. Therefore, we employed the multi-term loss
function and evaluated different loss term weightings to optimize the balance between con-
tent retention and style transfer, aiming to ensure that the generated images maintained
meaningful structures while achieving FDG-like characteristics. For all models, regardless
of training data, our underlying assumption was that by adding sufficient noise to the H2O
images during inference - without removing too much content - the subsequent denoising
process using the LDM would produce images resembling actual FDG scans that retained
characteristics specific to the patient from whom the H2O image originated.
The metrics used for evaluation were inspired by comparable work in the literature [30,
13, 17], which made use of (MS-)SSIM, PSNR, and Wasserstein distance. However, the
consistency of these metrics varied significantly between the stage 1 and stage 2 models.
While the chosen metrics for the VQVAE corresponded reasonably with the visual quality
observed in the reconstructed images, it is worth noting that the VQVAE training followed
a supervised learning approach, in which cases of misalignment between visual inspection
and metrics are less common. In contrast, the results of the metrics applied to the more
complex, unsupervised setup used for the subsequent LDM training yielded outcomes that
were less consistent and more challenging to interpret. The KL divergence did not generally
align with what we saw in the images. For example, it showed that FDG-L w/o AdaIN was
the best performing model in terms of aligning the distributions, but from visual inspection,
the images did not appear as FDG images. We also computed the Wasserstein distance
(WD) between the FDG validation set and the style transferred images for all model itera-
tions and this metric showed similar trends. This begs the question if any distribution-based
metric would align with the perceptual interpretation for our case. However, while others
have reported the same issue as we did [73], Wu et al. [17] did not encounter discrepancies
between their qualitative evaluation and quantitative metrics, including WD. In addition to
the KL divergence, we explored other metrics used in generative AI and arrived at a modi-
fied version of the KID-inspired metric, CMMD, inspired by Jayasumana et al. [55]. They
showed that FID in many cases does not align with visual perception and that a kernel-based
method with CLIP-feature extraction performed more consistently. Therefore, we deemed
it appropriate to use MedicalNet’s ResNet for feature extraction. Nevertheless, the KMD
for all style transferred images was almost unchanged, which made it a questionable evalu-
ation metric. An explanation could be that the ResNet most likely could not distinguish the
sets of style transferred images from each other. Subsequent to our results, we discovered
a study that also employed MedicalNet for this metric [73]. Their findings similarly high-
lighted challenges in the alignment of quantitative results and visual assessments, even with
a larger pretrained model. However, they discovered that further increasing the model size
improved alignment with visual quality. We encourage future work to explore the impact of
using a larger MedicalNet model and/or finetuning the model on the data for the evaluation
metric.
An entirely different approach to evaluate the quality of generative models is by assess-
ing how well their outputs perform as inputs to models trained on real data from the target
distribution. For example, Wu et al. [73] employs this strategy by training a segmentation
62
==PAGE:73==
model to evaluate the quality of generated brain MRIs. By comparing the segmentations
from real data with those of synthetic data, they effectively identify differences in perform-
ance across multiple GANs and DMs. However, it is important to acknowledge that there
isn’t an obvious downstream task to directly evaluate the success of style transfer. This
lack of a clear benchmark posed a challenge in assessing the practical value of our model’s
outputs. For this reason, we sought to explore two distinct downstream tasks to approach
the evaluation from different perspectives.
While the patch sampling approach enabled us to work with full-resolution images and
circumvent the information loss typically associated with downscaling, it introduced several
drawbacks. These drawbacks were not particularly evident during the development of the
VQVAE, as we achieved noteworthy results for direct image reconstruction. However, during
the style transfer process, the approach struggled to maintain consistency across patches.
The potential loss of global information in a patch based framework has been addressed,
among others, by Kim et al. and Muñoz-Ramírez et al. [16, 74]. We suspected that the
LDM lost orientation when a significant amount of noise was added to the input during the
forward process as we didn’t provide the model with any spatial context. For this reason,
we experimented with CFG, conditioning the model on the embedded and encoded seg-
mentation masks. The comparison of results between FDG-L and FDG-L-CFG (Figure 26)
highlights the impact of CFG. Notably, major anatomical structures, such as the spine, are
better preserved after style transfer with FDG-L-CFG. While other methods for providing
the model with context certainly exist, time restrictions prohibited us from further testing
if any methods could provide the model with higher-quality contextual information than
the pretrained ResNet trained alongside the LDM could. One potential approach involves
training a separate, dedicated end-to-end AE specifically for reconstruction of CT images
or segmentation masks. Its encoder would be tailored to extract meaningful features from
the input, which would serve as conditioning for the LDM. With feature maps of matching
dimensions, an alternative approach could involve experimenting with concatenation instead
of cross-attention to integrate the contextual information into the LDM. A completely dif-
ferent alternative to avoid patch sampling altogether would be to downscale the images and
train a super-resolution model like the one proposed by Georgescu et al. [75] for upscaling
of 3D medical images. This approach would involve reducing the resolution of the images
to make them manageable for the stage 1 model, performing style transfer with the LDM,
and then using a super-resolution network to upscale the outputs afterward. Such a method
could maintain the overall structure of the data while bypassing the need for patch sampling.
We encourage future research to explore this approach.
While this study demonstrates promising results, it is important to acknowledge the lim-
itations that may affect the broader applicability of the findings. First, all images used in
this study were acquired on the same scanner and were all attenuation-corrected (AC). As a
result, the generalizability of the findings to non-attenuation-corrected (NAC) data or data
acquired from different scanner types remains uncertain, however, we expect the results to
carry over. In addition to this, we only tested conversion from a single tracer to another,
and as images from different tracer types can vary considerably it remains unclear how our
63
==PAGE:74==
method would perform when applied to other tracer combinations.
Future work should
explore style transfer from other "static" tracer types similar to FDG. Additionally, site har-
monization between scanner types could also be a relevant focus for exploration. Second,
the dataset of H2O images used in this study was very sparse. This limitation may very
well have impacted the training of the style transfer model, as smaller datasets can restrict
the ability of the model to capture the full range of variability in H2O imaging. Lastly, the
fact that the static H2O data was acquired from the first 100 seconds of the dynamic scan
emphasizes that this study serves as a proof-of-concept since the first 100 seconds do not
hold clinical relevance.
This work demonstrated the feasibility of style transfer for whole-body PET imaging us-
ing LDMs. However, there are several avenues for future research and development that
could refine and extend the methods presented here. While paired CT images were used
to generate segmentation masks for our application of AdaIN, alternative methods exist for
performing adaptive instance normalization without relying on segmentation masks. If the
ultimate goal is to generate CT images for attenuation correction, such approaches could
eliminate the dependency on original CT scans. Future research could explore methodologies
that bypass CT usage entirely.
An alternative to AdaIN could be the incorporation of SPADE, which offers an opportunity
for further exploration by replacing adaptive instance normalization with spatially-adaptive
normalization to utilize segmentation mask information more effectively and hopefully en-
hance the fidelity of the style transferred images.
The goal would not be to generate a
completely new FDG image solely from an H2O-based segmentation mask, which would be
more in line with the original work by Park et al. [76]. Instead, future research could fo-
cus on integrating the H2O image itself in conjunction with the segmentation mask. This
approach could better leverage the functional information present in the H2O image while
maintaining anatomical consistency through the spatial guidance that this normalization
method provides.
There remains room for further exploration in our approach to both AdaIN and the LDM.
One direction involves investigating alternative AdaIN methods, such as basing the dic-
tionary on the percentage-wise contribution of each label rather than relying on unique
combinations or testing how a global approach compares to our more localized implementa-
tion. Additionally, a more comprehensive exploration of the LDMs hyperparameters could be
conducted to identify configurations that maximize both performance and generalizability.

Conclusion
This study explored the application of latent diffusion models for style transfer between the
[15O]H2O tracer and [18F]FDG in whole-body PET images acquired from the same scanner.
A patch based approach was employed allowing us to preserve the full resolution of the im-
ages and our VQVAE provided an effective compression framework, achieving high-quality
reconstructions of the image patches with room for improvement in the brain-region. The
LDM incorporated a novel AdaIN approach using a precomputed dictionary which optim-
ized the training and inference pipeline by eliminating the need for simultaneous loading of
FDG patches. We explored multiple training strategies utilizing both the FDG and H2O
datasets and concluded that a model trained solely on FDG combined with CFG exhibited
the most promising results. While the visual assessment indicated that the generated images
resembled FDG-like characteristics, challenges arose in evaluating the quantitative metrics,
as they did not demonstrate consistent alignment with the visual observations. This dis-
crepancy highlights the need for further refinement of evaluation methods to better capture
the underlying quality of the generated images. This study served as a proof of concept,
exploring the feasibility of performing style transfer between tracers in whole-body PET
imaging using LDMs. Future research could investigate a super-resolution approach as an
alternative to the patch-based framework used in this study.
