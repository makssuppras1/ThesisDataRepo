Abstract 
 
Unconventional resources have become significant prospects to counterbalance the conventional 
resource deficit. Specifically, in the field of natural gas exploration, shale gas has drawn considerable 
attention, as it presents huge potential worldwide, and with recent technological advancements, the 
recovery rate have been significantly enhanced. 
Shales are geological formations rich in clay minerals that form from the compaction of silt and clay-
sized particles. Some, or occasionally all, of the oil and gas formed in the shale can remain trapped in 
the ultra-low permeable matrix, thus forming shale gas or light tight oil reservoirs. For all their 
abundance, a few of these shale deposits can be developed as hydrocarbon resource plays.  
Shale plays have created the current natural gas boom in the United States and according to the US 
National Energy Information Administration they produced 47% of the total US daily production by 
the end of 2013.The two main technologies that contributed heavily in the development of shale gas 
production are extended-reach horizontal drilling and multistage hydraulic fracture stimulation. By 
combination of the two techniques commercial rates of production can be achieved. 
Shale reservoirs contain a wide spectrum of hydrocarbon fluids ranging from low GOR black oils to 
volatile oils and rich to lean gas-condensates. At the nano pore scale of liquid rich shale reservoirs 
capillary forces play an important role in phase behavior which can affect the PVT properties of a 
hydrocarbon mixture. 
Black oil simulations are a useful tool to predict production performance of a reservoir well. The 
phase and volumetric properties extracted from PVT data can be used to calculate a black-oil model 
simulation case for liquid rich shale reservoirs. 
The purpose of this Thesis project is to investigate the behavior of hydrocarbon mixtures originated 
from shale plays and their depletion performance in a shale gas production well. 
The first part is to study the effect of capillary pressure in the phase equilibria of hydrocarbon fluids 
due to capillary pressure. An algorithm that can formulate a phase envelope under the effect is tested 
on several types of fluids. 
Changes were observed for the entire phase envelope except the critical point. In presence of capillary 
pressure the bubble point is suppressed until the critical point. The critical point remains unchanged, 
and for the dew point, variations increase from the critical point towards the cricondentherm in 
retrograde condensation region and decreases after the cricondentherm in all the normal condensation 
region. 
The second part consists of a mechanistic simulation of a typical liquid rich shale gas using a black oil 
model in a commercial simulator. The main aim of this is to study and analyze first order effects and 
main characteristics that can give an insight of the important processes and expected production 
performance of the reservoir.

The base case model description including reservoir data, production test PVT data, well data and 
PVT model data was taken from previous investigations. The black oil tables used as input parameters 
in the mechanistic simulation were generated using a commercial simulator software. Depletion 
performance is compared with previous work in order to interpret the results. Sensitivity analysis of 
basic parameters such as matrix permeability, critical gas saturation, residual oil saturation and 
maximum relative permeability for oil and gas is also presented. 
Results show that production performance is affected significantly by variations in rock permeability 
below 0.1 mD. For low permeable formations such as a shale, production performance for volatile oils 
becomes highly dependent on flowing bottom hole pressure. The producing GOR variation Rps of 
shale oil wells is a function of rock permeability, flowing BHP, oil PVT properties and gas-oil relative 
permeability curves. Producing GOR with constant flowing BHP is oscillating intensively around a 
constant value. Furthermore volatile oils will exhibit lower producing GOR than near-critical oils. 
The research can be expanded for other type of fluids such as gas condensates, different flow regimes, 
depletion schedules and other measuring parameters such as the level of under saturation of the fluid. 
It is proposed that numerical gridding is performed in terms of optimizing the current model.

1 Introduction 
 
In the last century, hydrocarbon consumption worldwide has grown exponentially, forcing the oil and 
gas industry to exploit different types of petroleum resources to meet the growing demand. In recent 
years the world reserves of fossil fuels has been considered to be declining due to limited discoveries 
of new resources, however these numbers refer to conventional resources based on already 
implemented technologies.  
Unconventional resources, including heavy oil, tight sands, coalbed methane, tar sands, gas-hydrates, 
shale oil, and shale gas, among others, have become significant prospects to counterbalance the 
conventional resource deficit. Specifically, in the field of natural gas exploration, shale gas has drawn 
considerable attention, as it presents huge potential worldwide, and with recent technological 
advancements, the recovery rate has been significantly enhanced. (Monalisa Mallick and Manoj 
Achalpurkar, 2014) 
 
1.1 Natural Gas and unconventional resources 
 
The development of unconventional gas reservoirs such as shale gas, gas condensate and shale oil, has 
gained significant interest over the last decade. Producing natural gas from these resources has a low 
environmental impact as well as easiness of transportation to distribution centers via pipelines. 
Therefore companies around the globe are aggressively adding unconventional hydrocarbon resources 
to their portfolios as natural gas is poised to enter a golden age. Successful exploitation of these 
unconventional plays can only be achieved by profitable development accordingly with an 
environmentally acceptable manner. (A. Orangi, 2011) An integrated approach in planning these 
activities is required and simulation for modelling Shale gas production is an essential tool for the 
future of the industry. 
 
1.1.1 
Types of natural gas from unconventional reservoirs 
 
Unconventional gas as a term refers to gas resources that have been traditionally considered difficult 
or costly to produce. Two important categories are presented below: 
 
1.1.1.1 Shale gas 
 
Natural gas contained within and produced from rock classified as shale formations is termed as 
‘shale gas’. These formations are typically rich in organic matter acting as source rock for gas 
migrating to other formations. Therefore part of this gas is trapped or remains close to the source rock,

in this case shale. These formations are characterized by low permeability which causes limited ability 
of gas flow when compared with a conventional case. (Fatih Birol, 2012) 
 
1.1.1.2 Tight gas 
 
This is natural gas typically found in shale as well as other low permeability formations. It is 
classified as gas that cannot be produced with an economically viable manner without the use of 
technology to stimulate flow towards the well such as hydraulic fracturing. 
 
1.2 Shale reservoir characteristics 
 
Exploration and assessment of hydrocarbon reserves in the oil and gas industry followed so far a more 
or less conventional path before depletion: search for hydrocarbon source rocks, find specific 
reservoir rocks where hydrocarbons can accumulate, identify how a trap is formed and drill a testing 
well. (Tom Alexander, 2011) 
However revolutionary practices are taking place nowadays in the E&P industry. Rock types of little 
interest in the past, notable only as source rocks, are considered today as potential reservoirs. Organic 
rich shales are of main focus for unconventional resource plays. 
 
1.2.1 
Geological Description 
 
Shales are geological formations rich in clay minerals that form from the compaction of silt and clay-
sized particles. These fine sediments are deposited to the bottom of lakes or seas, having then buried 
over the course of millions of years. (Fatih Birol, 2012) 
It is considered the primary source rock for most of the conventional hydrocarbon deposits in the 
world and consists 60% of the earth’s sedimentary crust. As shales form from mudstones they are 
often referred as mudstones, nevertheless they differ from other mudstones and clay stones in terms of 
structure. Shales are laminated, finely layered and fissile, which means they are prone to break or split 
along their laminations. (Tom Alexander, 2011)

1.2.2 
Organich rich shale formations 
 
For all their abundance, a few of these shale deposits can be developed as hydrocarbon resource plays. 
Organic-rich shale formations form under specific conditions such as high levels of organic matter 
and low levels of oxygen which differentiating them from other shale deposits. The total organic 
carbon (TOC) can give an estimation of the resource potential of a shale, the higher the TOC the 
richer the content in organic carbon. However there is an upper limit of 10% TOC as higher values 
usually consist the resource immature for development (Tom Alexander, 2011).  
The organic-matter-rich shales produce usually small-chain hydrocarbons (gas or oil) through 
biogenic or thermogenic processes, which is expelled because of high pressures through natural 
cracks and stored in porous reservoir rocks. (Monalisa Mallick and Manoj Achalpurkar, 2014)  
These conditions were prevalent during various geologic ages, including the Devonian Period when 
the climate was warm, sea levels were high and much of the Earth was covered by tropical seas. Thick 
deposits of organic-rich sediments were formed not only in the Devonian but in other periods as well. 
Organic-rich shales from the Precambrian Period through modern times have been identified (below 
left).  
However, to meet thermal maturity criteria, most gas shale plays focus on sediments from a geologic 
time range that begins in the Ordovician and extends through the Pennsylvanian Period. Organic 
matter, which consists mostly of remains of plants and animals, settles to the bottom of lakes or 
oceans and becomes food for other animals and bacteria. However, in anoxic environments, anaerobic 
bacteria, which are less efficient consumers than their aerobic counterparts, are the only bacteria able 
to consume organic matter. In this way sediments are able to retain much of their original organic 
material. Today, the Black Sea is a close analog for conditions needed to create shale resource plays 
thus anoxic conditions allow sediments to accumulate with high concentrations of organic matter. 
(Tom Alexander, 2011) 
As more material accumulates and underlying ooze becomes compacted, the sediments are buried 
deeper and subjected to increased pressure and temperature. Laminations can also develop. In deep 
marine environments, these processes can take place very slowly and encompass very long periods of 
time. For this reason accumulations of a few centimeters may take thousands of years. The weight of 
the overlying sediments expels fluids and compacts the mudstone, which are steps in the process of 
lithification. The organic material slowly and partially cooks and is transformed into kerogen, the 
insoluble material from which hydrocarbons, both oil and gas can be generated.Different organic 
material generates different kerogen types. When exposed to heat and pressure, each kerogen type is 
more prone to generate specific products which are oil, wet gas, dry gas and non-hydrocarbons. 
Type 1, algae kerogen has high hydrogen to oxygen (H/O) ratio which is rich in lipids when 
immature. This type is oil prone kerogen. Type 2, liptinitic kerogen is the most common type, is 
typically found in marine sediments and it seems to originate from zooplankton, phytoplankton and 
bacteria. Type 2 may develop oil as well as gas. Type 3, humic kerogen has a low H/O ratio and 
typically originates from carbohydrates and lignin. It is mostly found in coastal sediments and as a 
rule is gas prone. (Fabricius, 2001)

During the processes of burial and maturation, kerogen passes through a range of temperatures and 
pressures. First is the oil window, in which liquid petroleum may be generated from oil-prone 
kerogen, or wet gas from gas-prone kerogen. This stage of maturation is referred to as katagenesis. 
With deeper burial, the kerogen passes into the dry gas window. Through the process of metagenesis, 
gas is generated by the conversion of remaining kerogen and the transformation of heavier 
hydrocarbons created during katagenesis. 
This transformation typically increases pressure within the rock, resulting in part of the oil and gas 
being expelled from the shale and migrating upwards into other rock formations, where it forms 
conventional oil and gas reservoirs. The shales are the source rock for the oil and gas found in such 
conventional reservoirs. Some, or occasionally all, of the oil and gas formed in the shale can remain 
trapped in the ultra-low permeable matrix, thus forming shale gas or light tight oil reservoirs. (Fatih 
Birol, 2012) (Tom Alexander, 2011) 
Shales that are rich in organic materials and that have been subjected to temperatures and pressures in 
the dry gas window are prime targets for gas shale exploration. However, just because sediments pass 
through the maturation stages does not necessarily mean they are reservoir quality rock. Using 
geochemical, petrophysical and geomechanical properties derived from a variety of sources, 
geologists and engineers determine the feasibility of proceeding with gas shale exploration. (Tom 
Alexander, 2011) 
 
1.2.3 
Porosity and permeability 
 
Shale gas reservoirs present numerous challenges to analysis that conventional reservoirs simply do 
not provide. The first of these challenges is the dual porosity nature of these reservoirs. Similar to 
carbonate reservoirs, shale gas reservoirs almost always have two different storage volumes for 
hydrocarbons, the rock matrix and the natural fractures (Gale, 2007). Due to plasticity of shale 
formations, these natural fractures are generally isolated or closed due to the pressure of the 
overburden rock (Gale, 2007).  
The fundamental difference of an ultra-tight shale or resource shale gas bearing formations with 
conventional reservoirs is the magnitude of rock permeability. Ultra tight shale reservoirs can have 
permeabilities that range from 0.01 to 10 mD while resource shale gas formations can drop down to 
less than 0.001 mD in the nanolevel scale. (Guowen Lei, 2014) (Monalisa Mallick and Manoj 
Achalpurkar, 2014) 
Consequently, their very low matrix permeability, usually on the order of hundreds of nanodarcies 
(nd), makes un-stimulated, conventional production difficult, if not impossible. In addition the 
unconventional shale gas formations are generally characterized by nanometers sized pores where the 
average pore-throat diameter ranges between 5 to 100 nm. The commercial viable shale-gas reservoirs 
tend to have a porosity of 4 to 6%. (Nelson, 2009)

1.3 Shale Gas Production 
 
Several technologies have been developed over the years to increase the flow from the low 
permeability reservoirs such as liquid rich shale (LRS) formations. Nevertheless, it is widely accepted 
that the two main technologies that contributed heavily in the development of shale gas production are 
extended-reach horizontal drilling and multistage hydraulic fracture stimulation. 
Although experiments took place even back in the 19th century, the first official fracture stimulation 
was performed in the Hugoton gas field in Grant County, Kansas, in 1947 by Stanolind Oil. On the 
contrary horizontal drilling was performed much later in the 80’s as a more effective way to remain in 
the pay zone area by reaching longer distances in order to enhance production. (Beckwith, 2012)  
By combination of the two techniques commercial rates of production can be achieved. Evolution in 
this technology largely explains the increase of gas production in the United States since 2005. 
Hydraulic fracturing is initiated by injecting a fluid, known as fracturing fluid, at high pressure into 
the well so that the targeted shale rock formation is reached far below the surface. As a result, 
fractures or fissures, a few millimeters wide in the rock, are created. These can extend up to tens or 
even hundreds of meters long away from the wellbore. Since the fractures tend to close again after the 
pressure is released, small particles such as sand or ceramic beds are added to the fracturing fluid to 
fill the fractures and keep them open. These particles act as prop pants for the fractures allowing the 
gas to escape towards the well and flow in commercial viable rates. (Fatih Birol, 2012) 
In multistage fracturing a series of fractures is created in different sets of intervals for approximately 
every 100 meters along a horizontal wellbore. This technique has played a key role in unlocking 
production of shale gas and light oil in the US.A schematic is shown in Figure 3. Schematic of a 
hydraulic fracture scheme coupled with a horizontal well.

1.4.1 
Classification of fluids 
 
Reservoir fluids are typically classified in the following categories: natural gas (wet gas, dry gas), gas 
condensate, volatile oil and black oil (Yan, 2010) (McCain Jr., 1989). The classification depends on 
the location of the initial reservoir temperature and separator (surface) condition in a P-T diagram, 
relative to the phase envelope of the fluid. 
For natural gas systems the initial reservoir temperature is always greater than the cricondentherm. 
During depletion the reservoir temperature will remain in the single phase region. The path that 
follows production to surface, will define the classification wet or dry gas. If the surface conditions 
are within the two phase region as temperature and pressure is reduced, then liquid will form and the 
case of wet gas is observed. For dry gas the path will remain in the single phase region without liquid 
forming in the surface. (Yan, 2010)

1.4.2 
PVT sampling, testing and calculation of fluid properties 
 
In many cases what is produced at the surface is not in the same physical state as when it was stored 
in the reservoir underground. When a simulation model of a production scheme is to be constructed 
with a commercial simulator there are numerous parameters to be considered. The physical state of 
the liquid or vapor substances as well as the composition can vary significantly along with 
temperature, pressure and volume of the fluid. Collecting PVT data to incorporate the behavior of the 
fluid in the model is very crucial. The method and procedures of sampling, testing and analyzing PVT 
data is of great importance in order to achieve production output closer to reality. 
Inaccurate fluid characterization leads to large deviations of fluid in place volumes and recovery 
estimations become highly uncertain (Nagarajan, 2006). 
Traditionally for wells in both conventional and unconventional regions ‘production’ PVT data are 
collected. These refer to: 
1. Primary separator gas composition (yspi),  
2. Producing test gas-oil ratio (Rptest) or oil-gas ratio (rptest),  
3. Stock-tank oil gravity γAPI, and  
4. Reservoir temperature (TR).  
Source: (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012) 
Historically, for oil reservoirs, these data were sufficient to build a PVT model using correlations, and 
the test producing GOR provided a good estimate of the in-situ solution GOR (Curtis Hays Whitson, 
2012). As gas condensates undergo severe phase changes as the dew point is reached the production 
PVT data is not sufficient enough to provide valid correlations.  
For this case of gas condensates and highly volatile oils the trivial approach for fluid characterization 
is considered the fluid sampling at reservoir conditions. This is achieved by either direct open hole 
==PAGE:22==
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
 
Page 22 of 116 
 
formation tester samples or by re-combination of the separator gas and oil samples. In most cases 
estimations of the in-situ reservoir fluid are excellent (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012). 
The main objective of fluid sampling is to collect a sample which represents the in-situ composition at 
the time of sampling. (Reudelhuber, 1957) suggested that sample collection, early in the production 
stage, can give more consistent description of the in situ composition in the reservoir in general for oil 
reservoirs. In the same way (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012) proposes early sampling for LRS reservoirs 
as the best strategy when the drawdown is relatively low. 
The laboratory tests conducted on the samples for the construction of the PVT tables include: 
1. Constant composition expansion (CCE) 
2. Differential liberation (DL) 
3. Constant volume depletion (CVD) 
4. Multi-stage separation (SEP) 
 
The most trivial tests are CCE and DL both used for gas and oil. 
 
1.4.2.1 Constant composition expansion 
 
CCE is a test conducted in a cell housed in a thermostat of certain temperature (Reservoir temperature 
condition). The high pressure single phase fluid sample, either gas(gas condensate) or oil, is 
introduced into the cell with no discharge performed during the experiment, therefore the composition 
remains constant. By expansion the system pressure is reduced in stages with sufficient time intervals 
for thermodynamic equilibrium to be reached. Due to difference in compressibility for gas and oil the 
bubble point (in case oil is tested), or dew point (in case gas is tested) can be noticed by a break in the 
pressure vs total volume plot. (Yan, 2010)

though gas is more mobile. The mobility of the gas is considered in the differential liberation test, 
however as another extreme it assumes that only liberated gas will leave from the reservoir. The 
single phase fluid sample is loaded and pressure is reduced. When lower values than the bubble point 
pressure are reached the formed gas is discharged. Expansion is conducted again in the remaining oil 
until emerging equilibrium gas is discharged again. The process is repeated until atmospheric 
pressure. (Yan, 2010)

1.4.4 
Petroleum mixtures of shale reservoirs 
 
A large number of shale reservoirs contain a wide spectrum of hydrocarbon fluids ranging from low 
GOR black oils to volatile oils and rich to lean gas-condensates. Thus, it makes it necessary to 
characterize the fluids accurately to study their impact on the production from these shaly formations. 
(A. Orangi, 2011) 
In one specific region, part of Montney formation, the reservoir fluid can grade from a rich gas (50 
Bbl/MMcf condensate) to a light crude system (3,350 scf/Bbl), from west to east, with a coincident 
rise in elevation of 100 m. There are no ‘traps” holding the fluid in place other than the very low 
permeability of the reservoir. Large flow potential gradients exist from the westerly, down-dip, gas 
rich portion of the reservoir, toward the easterly more liquids rich region. Liquid and gas analyses are 
recombined at various, observed, condensate/gas ratios and subsequently input into a compositional 
simulation model. (Frank Kuppe, Steve Haysom and Glen Nevokshonoff; Ltd., Seven Generations 
Energy, 2012) 
The 2,430 meter long, Montney horizontal lateral, located at 9-12-64-4W6M, straddles a transition 
zone that penetrates myriad hydrocarbon phases and compositions, the aggregate of which cannot be 
represented by a single phase envelope. These types of wells could be classified as either “Gas” or 
“Oil” if using conventional criteria, depending upon which liquid/gas ratio was used to recombine 
fluids. 
It is very well understood from the above that oil can be stored in the low permeable layers of the 
shale formation along with gas, therefore these reservoirs are known as liquid-rich shale (LRS) gas

1.4.4.2 EOS Modelling and production performance of Liquid Rich Shales 
 
As oil mixtures contain multiple components in their heavy end (C7+) it is practically impossible to 
investigate them all in detail. On the other hand, to use EOS to calculate oil properties an estimation 
of the components in the plus fraction and their critical properties is required. The process is called 
characterization. The third step which is called lumping is the opposite of characterization where 
pseudo components are generated for further calculations. In a pseudo component, many components 
are grouped together.  
Pederson method is very popular for characterizing where a list of pseudo components with their 
composition and critical properties is generated. The minimum input required is mole fraction, 
molecular weight and specific gravity of the plus fraction (e.g. C7+). (Yan, 2010) 
In general EoS is currently the most popular way to generate PVT properties for fluid samples. They 
are correlating tools to generate smooth PVT tables for black oil simulators (Yan, 2010). 
==PAGE:27==
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
 
Page 27 of 116 
 
 
For a black-oil table to be created with the required input for the simulation, such as FVF for the 
phases and viscosities, an isothermal flash calculation usually is performed. The required inputs are 
the calculated composition and critical parameters from characterization process. The appropriate 
EOS (e.g. SRK) and the method of depletion (e.g. CCE or DL) is chosen as well as temperature and 
pressure stages. The output will contain the required black oil properties table. 
Black oil tables generated with EOS equations can be used for LRS cases. The black-oil tables include 
extrapolated saturated properties out to a critical condition, allowing flexible and consistent estimation 
of the in-situ reservoir fluid system for a given LRS well. (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012) 
In simulations numerical gridding is particularly important for LRS wells which exhibit highly-
localized gas-oil flow where severe oscillation of rates and GOR is endemic if not enough grids are 
used (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012). 
The most important performance parameter for LRS cases is the liquid yield, known as producing 
OGR or the opposite GOR. It is affected directly by the solution gas-oil ratio and the oil phase 
properties (Bo, μo and Rs). In general for LRS reservoirs: 
 
For constant bottomhole pressure (BHP) producing GOR (Rps) remains constant for extended 
periods of time (months, years). 
 
For a gas condensate reservoir the oil recovery is very low. The reservoir oil phase that 
develops through condensation will not be produced. 
 
The producing OGR variation of LRS oil wells is a function of flowing BHP, fracture 
geometry and gas formation volume factor. Relative permeability and oil PVT properties have 
little to no effect on liquid yield of LRS gas condensate wells. 
Source (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012) 
 
1.4.4.3 Liquid Rich Shale and Conventional Depletion Performance 
 
Conventional and ultra-tight shale reservoirs differ fundamentally in the magnitude of rock 
permeability. In order to compare the different cases of depletion correctly the appropriate parameters 
must be compared. 
For gas condensates simulations producing OGR is fairly constant with pressure and independent of 
rock permeabilities (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012) (Guowen Lei, 2014). For permeability higher than 
10 mD (conventional region) the production OGR follows closely the solution OGR curve. For 0.05 
to 3 mD large OGR variations are noticed (Guowen Lei, 2014). 
For higher permeability wells (k>10 md) production OGR curves vs pressure collapse simutaneously 
showing characteristics of conventional depletion performance for near critical oils. On the other 
hand, in an ultra-tight case (k<0.002 md) producing OGR remains constant at low levels with 
increasing average reservoir pressure. For intermediate permeabilities large variations of OGR vs 
==PAGE:28==
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
 
Page 28 of 116 
 
pressure exhist. (Guowen Lei, 2014)It has also been reported in the same study that cumulative 
revenue of 10 years is 10 times higher in conventional cases when compared with ultra tight cases of 
low permeability (Guowen Lei, 2014).  
For moderate oils it has been tested that high permeabilities (>0.01) the producing GOR remains 
practically constant for producing gas rate of 1000 scf/day. For low permeabilities the producing GOR 
increases with decreasing permeability (Guowen Lei, 2014). 
In general depletion performance of liquid rich conventional reservoirs has strong correlation of 
producing GOR and recovery factor (oil and gas) with average reservoir pressure. It has been 
investigated that the use of average reservoir pressure and recovery factors is of little value in 
characterizing ultra-tight reservoirs. The depletion is localized to the inner fraction volumes and a 
limited distance away from the fracture tips and in combination with the total drainage area the 
average pressure depends strongly by them. Furthermore, cumulative oil or gas production can be 
independent of outer drainage volume that may be undepleted. The cumulative production remains 
unaffected by the assigned drainage area and the recovery factor is of little importance. (Guowen Lei, 
2014) 
Conventional depletion performance is independent of rock permeability, well completion, and 
flowing bottom hole pressure (Guowen Lei, 2014). Ultra tight reservoirs deviate considerably from 
conventional performance as oil recovery and producing GOR are strong functions of flowing bottom 
hole pressure, fracture geometry, gas and oil PVT properties and gas-oil relative permeability curves 
(Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012). For the reasons described above average reservoir pressure and 
recovery factors may not be able to describe ultra-tight performance (Guowen Lei, 2014). 
In order to monitor more accurately the performance of an unconventional well the term of 
dimensionless GOR is introduced (rpd). It is somewhat describing oil recovery efficiency, defined as 
the ratio of initial GOR to producing GOR (rpd=Ri/Rp). 
The magnitude of oil recovery efficiency (rpd) represents the fraction of oil recovery obtained in a 
LRS reservoir when compared with a conventional reservoir after having produced the same amount 
of gas and it is a strong function of the degree of under saturation. (Curtis Hays Whitson, 2012)

1.5 Capillary pressure and confinement effects in reservoir fluids.  
 
Studies have shown that behavior of fluids under confinement deviates from that of bulk fluids due to 
increased effect of capillary, structural, electrostatic, van der Waals and adsorptive forces. The 
elevated interactions along molecules of the fluids plus the influence of the pore walls to the 
molecules may alter physical properties such as critical properties, density, viscocity, surface tension 
etc. (Kanda, 2004) (Chen, 2008) (Moore, 2010) (Travalloni, 2010)  
A very crucial aspect of fluid analysis for shale reservoirs is to understand the thermodynamics and 
phase equilibrium of hydrocarbon fluids in confined spaces such as pores at nanometer scale. 
The major effect in confined spaces is the high capillary pressure between the oil phase and the gas 
phase. At the nano pore scale of liquid rich shale reservoirs and tight formations, capillary forces play 
an important role in phase behavior which can affect the PVT analysis (T. Firincioglu, 2013). Not 
accounting for increased capillary pressure in small pores can lead to inaccurate estimates of ultimate 
recovery, and of saturation pressures (B. Nojabaei and R.T. Johns & and L. Chu, 2012). 
To understand the capillary pressure and the other effects on phase equilibria previous studies 
included approaches from molecular dynamics simulation, statistical mechanics based modeling to 
simle modification of critical properties in classical equations of state (EoS). 
Udell (1992) showed for a phase equilibria curve that bubble point pressure decreased significantly 
compared with the bulk fluid saturation pressure in small pore sizes. It was assumed for under 
saturated liquids, that a stable bubble can be formed only if the size is equal or smaller of the pore. In 
a similar project Brusllovsky (1992) presented a simulation of the capillary pressure influence on the 
dew- and bubble point pressures for natural multicomponent systems. He reported that the surface 
curvature between the components increased when pores became smaller, therefore bubble point 
decreased and dew point increased. Brusllovsky confirmed Ping (1996) who has presented previously 
that capillary pressure and adsorption increased the dew point pressure. 
Shapiro and Stenby (2001) generalized the Kelvin equation for multicomponent systems. They 
concluded that capillary forces dominate in the neighborhood of a dew point; adsorption plays the 
main role at a distance from the dew point, since the thickness of the adsorbed film decreases very 
slowly when the distance to the dew point increases. While the effect of capillary forces disappears 
close to the critical point (due to reduction of the surface tension), the adsorbed layer becomes 
especially thick close to this point. (A.A. Shapiro and E.H. Stenby, 1992) 
Firincioglou (2013) studied the effect of confinement. Conventional PVT cell measurements are 
designed to measure bulk fluids without consideration of spatial confinement. However in PVT cells 
gas phase reveals at the bubble point pressure point with equal gas and liquid phases (Figure 9). 
 
==PAGE:30==
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
 
Page 30 of 116 
 
 
Figure 9. Comparison of phase pressures in PVT cell vs. confinement (T. Firincioglu, 2013) 
 
This assumption can be valid for conventional reservoirs where capillarity is insignificant because of 
big pore radii. Due to capillary and surface forces, in confined environments gas-liquid interfaces are 
curved. A pressure drop is created between gas and liquid pressure which affects the bubble point. 
(Firincioglu, 2012) This is an important consideration when the pore radii decrease in the nano level 
scale. 
Results have shown that confined fluid behavior has a positive impact on oil production for black-oil 
reservoirs. There is an extension of the under saturated region and gas is released later in the 
production. It is not just the magnitude but also the distribution of the bubble point suppression that 
can influence the depletion as non-uniform capillary pressure can influence the multiphase flow. (T. 
Firincioglu, 2013) 
Even though Zhilin (2007) reported that interfacial phenomena increased the dew point pressure, 
Firicionglou (2012) concluded that surface forces have less impact than capillary forces for pores 
larger than 1nm. 
Nojabei et al. (2013) and Pang (2012) calculated the phase envelope in the presence of capillary 
pressure by solving fugacity equalities coupled with a capillary pressure equation. Results from both 
studies showed that the phase envelope variates everywhere except at the critical and the 
cricondentherm point. Furthermore the changes of the saturation points were significant only at the 
order of tens of nanometers for the pore radius. 
Sandoval et al. (2015) calculated as well the phase envelope in presence of capillary pressure by 
solving a modified version algorithm of Michelsen (1980) coupled by the Young-Laplace capillary 
pressure equation. The research confirmed Nojabei et al. (2013) and Pang (2012) that critical and 
cricondentherm points were the only elements not changed by the capillary pressure effect.

In overall, the capillary effect plays the most significant role in affecting the PVT properties of a 
confined reservoir. The present study concentrates on the effect of capillary pressure in the phase 
envelope of reservoir fluids from shale formations. Fluid samples from unconventional fields are used 
to investigate the effect by applying the algorithm developed by Sandoval et al. (2015).

1.6 Overview and Development of computerized reservoir simulators. 
 
In the Petroleum Industry several tools are used for optimizing hydrocarbon production and reservoir 
simulators are one of them. 
Reservoir Simulators are used widely in the oil and gas industry in order to provide valuable 
information about how to develop a field and introduce production strategies for the future of the 
company’s assets. In other words the reservoir simulator objective is to simulate the exploitation of a 
real reservoir by avoiding real life trial and error tests (Pettersen O. , 2006). In such a way the optimal 
solution can be selected before production is put in action.  
In order to create models that simulate production, input data are required to run the simulation. The 
physical parameters are provided by real data such as borehole logging data, lab experiments or 
theoretical properties from literature. Furthermore assumptions are necessary in many cases due to the 
complexity of the different scenarios and luck of data. A compromise must be made in order to 
simulate the field’s performance in reasonable computing time and results to be delivered in sufficient 
timing, considering the appropriate cost and the amount of given resources. 
Several companies have developed simulators for creating models for production forecast based on 
similar principles and calculations. The most widely used simulation package that is currently used is 
the Schlumberger Eclipse by Schlumberger. 
 
1.6.1 
Basic principles 
 
The basic principles of reservoir modeling involve the optimization and simulation of oil production 
by computing with mathematical techniques the conservation equations (energy, mass, momentum) 
that govern the fluid flow of the reservoir. In Eclipse isothermal conditions are assumed therefore no 
flow of energy taking place and energy conservation equations are not considered (Pettersen O. , 
2006). It is an assumption that is common for reservoir simulators. 
 
1.6.2 
Compositional and black oil simulations 
 
The primary goal of reservoir simulation is to predict future performance of a reservoir and find ways 
and means of optimizing the recovery of the hydrocarbons. Mathematical models of petroleum 
reservoirs have been utilized since the late 1800s. A mathematical model consists of a set of equations 
that describe the flow of fluids in a petroleum reservoir, together with an appropriate set of boundary 
and/or initial conditions. (Petroleum res simulation) 
A simulation is based on conservation of mass, momentum and energy equations that describe fluid 
flow in a petroleum reservoir. In the simulation of flow in the reservoir, the momentum equation is 
==PAGE:33==
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
 
Page 33 of 116 
 
given in the form of Darcy’s law (1856). It is an empirical law that indicates a linear relationship 
between the fluid velocity relative to the solid and the pressure head gradient. 
In a compositional model a finite number of hydrocarbon components are used to represent the 
composition of reservoir fluids. These components associate as phases in a reservoir. 
The term black oil refers to the fluid model, in which water is modeled explicitly together with two 
hydrocarbon components, one (pseudo) oil phase and one (pseudo-)gas phase. This is in contrast with 
a compositional formulation, in which each hydrocarbon component (arbitrary number) is handled 
separately. (Bell, 1989) 
It is assumed that the hydrocarbons (HC) components are divided into one gas component and one oil 
component in a standard condition stock tank were no mass transfer occurs between the different 
components. 
The model is described under the assumptions that the flow process is isothermal (i.e., at constant 
temperature), the components form at most three phases (e.g., vapor, liquid, and water), and there is 
no mass interchange between the water phase and the hydrocarbon phases (i.e., the vapor and liquid 
phases).

1.6.2.1 Grid 
 
A reservoir in reality is a continuous region of space with flow-dependent parameters that change in 
time constantly. A simulator which is a computer program cannot perform computation in a 
continuous way. For this reason the reservoir has to be subdivided in a finite number of discrete 
elements and the continuous time in different discrete time steps. 
The specific set of the discretized finite volume elements or cells is called a reservoir grid. The 
reservoir is discretized in different grid cells according to the finite volume method in order to avoid 
reference to a coordinate system creating a 1D, 2D or 3D grid structure of the total reservoir. As the 
formation and geometry of a real reservoir is typically very complex, the approximation of the grid 
towards the real case requires large number of cells. This approach is limited by computing time or 
CPU power and a compromise is held depending on the target of the project. 
The grid is sort of the schematic of the investigation and its characteristics and properties have to be 
defined. For each grid cell values of the petro physical properties (permeability, porosity) are 
determined usually as an average ignoring the variations. Changes along with time development are 
calculated with general relations to fluid pressure. It is very common in these cases that 
compressibility is constant. 
A fractured porous medium is a medium that is intersected by a network of interconnected fractures, 
or solution channels (cf. Figure 2.2). Such a medium could be modeled by allowing the porosity and 
permeability to vary rapidly and discontinuously over the whole domain. Both these quantities are 
much larger in the fractures than in the blocks of porous rock (called matrix blocks). A hydraulic 
fracture can be treated with the same principals of a natural fracture adjusting geometries to the 
specific investigated case.

1.6.2.3 Equilibration  
 
Phase equilibrium of the fluid is assumed be achieved instantly at no-flow conditions and is calculated 
by fugacity parameters through the EOS (equations of state) equations (CONWIconsult, 1995). The 
initial state of pressures and saturations for each grid are calculated from the simulator under this 
assumption. The datum of the different phases is provided by the user and the calculation is performed 
by fluid weight vs. depth gradients. 
 
1.6.2.4 Well specification 
 
The movement of the flow that goes in and out of the system is determined by the way the injection or 
producing wells operate. The Depth of open well contact with the reservoir and position of the wells 
within the grid are defined (well cells) as well as, production and injection rates. 
During the field’s development in time all the changes that may occur can be simulated as well as the 
operating constraints. 
In general boundary conditions and constraints of the system are defined before the user starts to 
implement commands in order to request results.

1.7 Scope of Master Thesis project 
 
The main purpose of this work is to investigate the PVT behavior of typical hydrocarbon mixtures 
found in liquid rich shale gas reservoirs and to study their depletion performance using mechanistic 
simulations. 
The thesis project is divided in two parts. 
The first part is to study the effect of capillary pressure between gas and oil in the phase equilibria of 
hydrocarbon fluids due to the confined space of the nanoscale-sized pores within shale formations. An 
algorithm that can formulate a phase envelope under the effect is tested on several types of fluids, 
classified as gases or oils from major USA resource plays, collected from previous studies with 
readily available PVT data. Variations in the phase envelope due to the effect of capillary pressure are 
analyzed and the magnitude of influence is discussed. 
The second part aims at the analysis of first order effects and main characteristics that can give an 
insight of the important processes and expected production performance of a shale reservoir. It 
consists of a mechanistic simulation of a typical liquid rich shale fluid using a black oil model in a 
commercial simulator. An appropriate depletion scenario is selected based on a previous work which 
uses typical fluid data from the Eagle Ford Field. Depletion performance with varying input 
parameters is investigated and compared with previous works in order to interpret the results.  
Guidelines and suggestions for expanding the research regarding shale gas production modeling and 
optimizing the base case model are proposed for the future.

condensation region is located. The dashed line represents the phase envelope with the capillary 
pressure effect and the continuous line the phase envelope without the effect.

For the near critical fluid V1 used in the simulation a warning was given in high pressures of negative 
compressibility. In black oil models, FrontSim checks for positive compressibility of each single 
reservoir fluid as the PVT data is read (the formation volume factor must be a monotonically 
decreasing function of pressure assuming all other variables are held fixed). However, FrontSim also 
checks that oil-gas mixtures have a positive total compressibility even when there is mass transfer 
between the two phases.For example, it is well known that oil sometimes swells when the pressure 
increases, apparently in violation of physical principles. This swelling occurs because gas is 
dissolving in the oil. Provided that the reduction in the volume of gas is greater than the increase in 
the volume of oil, the total volume of the oil-gas mixture reduces as the pressure increases, and the 
paradox is resolved 
The reason for the warning could be the lack of enough pressure stages above the saturation pressure 
for the initial Rs or not enough Rs nodes greater than initial Rs in the black-oil PVT tables which do 
not provide accurate extrapolation in higher pressures.

Considerations and future perspectives 
 
Sensitivity analysis of different aspects was conducted for this research and it can be expanded in 
terms of model consistency and more parameters of depletion performance.  
For instance the drainage area and prediction time can be extended to capture other flow regimes such 
as the boundary dominated flow. In this study volatile oils where tested from near critical to lower 
GOR types. Nevertheless, other type of fluids especially gas condensates should be also tested that are 
present in shale formation to monitor their behavior as well.  
Cases of smaller scale (covering the region around a single hydraulic fraction) can help to investigate 
in more detail time evolution of concentration profiles for different flow regimes. 
Furthermore, having longer periods of times under single flow situation may serve to assess or discard 
possible effects due to sorption at the high pressure range. Effect of under saturation can also be 
investigated to see the behavior when initial reservoir pressure is high above or close to the saturation 
pressure. In addition initialization in the two phase region can be tested in case reservoir pressure is 
located below the saturation pressure. 
Different relative permeability tables can be also assigned for the grid spacing and for the fractures to 
approach in more accuracy flow variations due to geometry. Since little research has been conducted 
for relative permeability gas-oil fluid systems, investigation in this direction would be beneficial in 
order to provide more consistent relative permeability tables for simulations. Furthermore tables with 
water-oil-gas can be tested since connate water may be present in the matrix or free water inside the 
fractures. 
For all the parameters of production performance there are fluctuations with depletion time that could 
be occurring from the way numerical computations are handled rather than physical explanation. 
Various parameters can create convergence problems and several ways to eliminate such effects can 
be tested. A denser grid refinement will provide more accurate computations. It is possible that the 
high range of oscillation of producing GOR at constant FBHP can be eliminated with increasing the 
number of grid cells. Dual porosity/permeability models can also be used which are a different way 
and perhaps a more proper way to address numerical problems where a fracture media inside the 
matrix is present.  
For the near critical fluid V1 negative compressibility warning, a different software to produce black 
oil tables might be considered to generate enough pressure stages above the saturation pressure in 
order to avoid some complications faced in the work.

4 Conclusion 
 
4.1 PVT analysis under capillary pressure effect 
 
In shale formations with nanoscale pore sizes, capillary pressure due to confinement of space may 
have an impact in production profiles. The phase envelope of pressure temperature diagram shifts and 
values of saturation lines (dew point and bubble point) change that can lead to different contribution 
for oil production. 
Results from Eagle Ford in-situ fluids show changes in saturation pressure due to capillary pressure of 
the gas-oil interface. Changes were observed for the entire phase envelope except the critical point. 
The shift to higher temperatures for the cricondentherm point was also observed. 
In presence of capillary pressure the bubble point is suppressed and the bubble point pressure 
variation becomes larger further away from the critical point towards lower temperatures. For the dew 
point, variations increase from the critical point towards the cricondentherm (retrograde condensation 
region) and decrease from the cricondentherm and after. 
In general, variations were larger in the bubble point region for oils and larger in the dew point region 
for gases.  
 
4.2 Simulation of the horizontal oil well 
 
Production performance is affected significantly by variations in rock permeability below 0.1 mD. 
This is characteristic for low permeable formations fluids. Above the value 0.1 mD reservoir wells 
exhibit a conventional performance regime where production performance parameters show identical 
trends irrespective of the permeability change. Producing GOR becomes independent of flowing 
bottom hole pressure and is more or less kept stable. 
For shale oil wells, oil and gas production rates are relatively lower than high permeable formation 
oils with higher percentage of deviation for oil production. For low permeable formations such as a 
shale, production performance for volatile oils becomes highly dependent on flowing bottom hole 
pressure. 
The producing GOR variation Rps of shale oil wells is a function of rock permeability, flowing BHP, 
fluid PVT properties and gas-oil relative permeability curves. 
Producing GOR and oil recovery efficiency factor rpd are directly dependent on flowing bottom hole 
pressure in low permeable regions. Specifically producing GOR with flowing BHP becomes highly 
sensitive in changes for the low permeable regions. In shale oil wells producing GOR with constant 
flowing BHP is oscillating intensively around a constant value. Oil recovery efficiency rpd will 
typically exhibit values below 1from early depletion stages for volatile oils. 
==PAGE:87==
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
 
Page 87 of 116 
 
Lower solution GOR volatile oils exhibit higher oil production rates with lower FBHP and lower 
producing GOR profiles than near critical high GOR volatile oils. The level of FBHP variation will 
determine the amount of producing GOR decrease. 
Variations of oil-gas relative permeability curves have the least impact on production parameters. The 
greatest impact in production performance from variations of the relative permeability table end 
points has the critical gas saturation and residual oil saturation. 
The maximum relative oil-gas and gas-oil relative permeability variations have a very low impact on 
production performance when compared with changes of critical gas saturation and oil residual 
saturation. In general oil production seems to be affected more than gas production when any of these 
parameters differentiates. 
This research can be expanded for other type of fluids such as gas condensates, different flow 
regimes, depletion schedules and other measuring parameters such as the level of under saturation of 
the fluid. For instance, different initial reservoir pressures above the saturation pressure or two phase 
region initialization for cases of initial reservoir pressure located within the phase envelope. 
In terms of optimization of the model denser gridding refinement may be applied to eliminate 
potential abnormalities and artificial fluctuations in the results. For assigning rock properties in 
eclipse the option of dual porosity or dual permeability could provide closer approach to reality. 
Finally, for near critical fluids more accurate PVT black oil properties simulators can be applied.
