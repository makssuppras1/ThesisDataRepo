Abstract 
Brown seaweed Fucus vesiculosus has a high potential as a source of natural antioxidants 
due to a high diversity of bioactive compounds in its composition. In this study, bioactive 
compounds from Danish Fucus vesiculosus were extracted with and without ultrasound 
pretreatment using water and ethanol as solvent. The extracts were characterized with 
respect to composition of bioactive compounds and in vitro antioxidant properties. 
Pretreatment with ultrasound did not improve extraction efficacy in the present study. 
Ethanol extract showed higher total phenolic content, higher radical scavenging activity 
and higher reducing power than water extract. Besides, larger content of phenolic 
compounds and pigments was found in ethanol extract compared to water extract.  
Furthermore, ethanol and water extracts were added to skin care emulsions to assess their 
efficacy in inhibiting lipid oxidation in dark and room temperature condition. Oxidation 
during storage was evaluated. It was found that both water and ethanol extracts could 
improve oxidative stabilities in skin care emulsion. Ethanol extract at 1 mg/g of emulsion 
showed the highest potential as antioxidants in skin care products. The higher efficacy of 
ethanol extract over water extract in skin care products was related to high total phenolic 
content, high radical scavenging activity high reducing power.

1. Introduction 
Seaweed has been widely consumed in the world, especially in Asia. In 2014, about 
28.5 million tons of seaweeds and other algae were harvested. They are used in direct 
consumption or further processing for food, in which traditionally in Japan, Korea and 
China, or for use as fertilizer and in pharmaceuticals, cosmetics and other purposes (FAO, 
2016). 
In recent years, brown algae have aroused the interest of many researchers due to the high 
variety of bioactive compounds and nutritional value (Holdt &Kraan, 2011). Studies have 
been reported polyphenols in brown seaweed species collected in Denmark showed the 
highest antioxidative activity in Fucus compared to other brown algal species, such as 
Sargassum and Laminaria  (Farvin, Holdt, &Jacobsen, 2010). Besides, Connan et al. (2004) 
also found that Fucus vesiculosus is one of the algae that contains the highest amount of 
phenolic content among eight brown algae spices. Therefore, Fucus vesiculosus (bladder 
wrack), a brown algae with high antioxidative activity, is the target species of this project. 
Skin care products, which are used to maintain skin smoothed and moistened, are widely 
consumed by consumers globally. Natural oils rich in unsaturated fatty acids are usually 
added to the skin care products as active ingredients since they can improve and strengthen 
skin. However, these unsaturated oils are susceptible to lipid oxidation and leads to 
physical deterioration and oxidative instability. Therefore, it is important to inhibit lipid 
oxidation and extend the shelf life of skin care products by application of natural 
antioxidants. In this study, Fucus vesiculosus extracts were characterized and applied to 
facial cream to assess their efficacy in limiting lipid oxidation. Figure 1 illustrates the 
concept of the project.

1.1 Hypothesis, aims and objectives 
The aim of this study is to develop high value active ingredients derived from F. 
vesiculosus and apply to skin care products to produce oxidative stable skin care products 
in the cosmetic industry.  
This study investigated the antioxidative activity of extracts derived from Danish F. 
vesiculosus and applied it in skin care products in order to improve the oxidative stability 
of the skin care products upon storage. 
This thesis tested the following three hypotheses: 
 Pretreatment with ultrasound can affect the extraction efficacy from F. vesiculosus. 
 Different solvents can be used to extract bioactives from F. vesiculosus and they 
can affect the composition and antioxidant activity of the extracts. 
 F. vesiculosus contains a wide range of antioxidants which can improve the 
oxidative stability of the skin care products by limiting the lipid oxidation during 
storage. 
The hypotheses lead to three main objectives: 
 Application of ultrasound pretreatment in seaweed extraction and determination of 
the difference in antioxidative properties between extracts with pretreatment and 
extracts without pretreatment. 
 Use of the different solvents to produce extracts with high antioxidant activity by 
characterization and evaluation of the antioxidant effect by determining DPPH 
scavenging activity, metal chelating activity, reducing power and total phenolic 
content. 
 Application of the extracts in facial cream and determination of the oxidative 
stability of the facial cream during storage in dark at room temperature.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 Brown algae 
2.1.1 Biology 
Seaweeds can be classified into three main categories based on pigmentation: brown, red 
and green. Brown seaweeds are usually large, and range from small 0.3 m to thick, leather-
like seaweeds of 3 m long to giant 20 m long (FAO, 2003). Brown algae are typically 
associated with the northern and cool water. The optimal growth temperature was 17.5 °C 
(Strömgren, 1977). The most common brown algae in the North Sea are Fucus vesiculosus, 
Fucus serratus and Saccharina latissimi. 
Fucus vesiculosus is commonly known as bladder wrack.  It has a leather-like surface with 
gas vesicles. The air vesicles are produced annually to make the frond float upwards and 
they usually appear in pairs. The species is found intertidally on rocky shores in a wide 
range of exposures. The sizes of Fucus vesiculosus differs in male and female. Male size 
can be up to 1.5 m while female size ranges from 15 to 20 cm. The growth rate is 0.48 cm 
per week (White, 2008). A F. vesiculosus plant comprises a holdfast, which is a root-like 
structure for adhesion to the seabed; a stipe, which is the connection between the holdfast 
and the leaf-like blades, and the blades, with air bladders. At the tip of the blades areas, 
there are receptacles with conceptacles. Figure 2 demonstrates the structure of a mature 
Fucus vesiculosus.

2.1.2 Utilization and potential 
Seaweeds are rich in a wide range of bioactive substances, such as polysaccharides, 
proteins, lipids, vitamins, pigments, and polyphenolic compounds (Holdt &Kraan, 2011). 
Extracts from F. vesiculosus have great potential in application in food systems to improve 
the oxidative stability (Wang, et al., 2009). It can also be applied in skin care products to 
inhibit lipid oxidation (Poyato et al., 2017). Compared to green and red seaweed, brown 
seaweed has the highest concentration of phenolic compounds (Holdt &Kraan, 2011). 
Fucus vesiculosus is highly antioxidative due to the presence of polyphenolic compounds 
called phlorotannins (Koivikko, Loponen, Honkanen, &Jormalainen, 2005; Wang et al., 
2012). The high content of phlorotannins can inhibit lipid oxidation by radical scavenging 
and metal chelating (Farvin &Jacobsen, 2013; Wang et al., 2012, 2009). Besides 
antioxidant properties, the functionality of phlorotannins includes anti-aging and anti-
inflammatory activity and UV protection, which makes these compounds well suited in skin 
care products (Dutot et al., 2012; Schoenwaelder, 2008). There is seasonal variation in the 
phenolic content in F. vesiculosus and the highest phenolic content is during summer 
(Connan et al., 2004). Therefore, F. vesiculosus from summer has the highest antioxidant

activity and extraction of these compounds and application to skin care emulsions are of 
high interest. 
 
2.2 Lipid Oxidation 
Lipid oxidation affects the quality of fat-containing products in terms of color, flavor, odor, 
appearance, texture and shelf life. It is a detrimental process in food systems. Lipid 
oxidation is affected by numerous internal and external factors such as fatty acid 
composition, content and activity of antioxidants, irradiation, temperature, oxygen pressure, 
surface area in contact with oxygen, and water activity. There are three major oxidation 
reactions: autoxidation, photo-oxidation and enzymatic oxidation. Autoxidation is the 
focus in this chapter as this project investigates the oxidative stability based on different 
ingredients added to skin care products. The skin care products will be stored in a dark 
place where the photo-oxidation is minimized. For enzymatic oxidation, which mainly 
happened in biological systems, thus is not considered in the present study. 
 
Free radicals and reactive oxygen species 
Reactive oxygen species (ROS) has a high ability to react with organic molecules, 
including food constituents. It includes both free radicals, which refer to molecules having 
an odd electron, and species that are not free radicals such as singlet oxygen 1O2, O3, 
hypochlorite.   
The reactions of ROS in food are generally harmful, leading to lipid peroxidation and 
damage to other food components. The main reaction induced by ROS in food is lipid 
peroxidation by initiating chain reaction in autoxidation.

2.2.1 Autoxidation Mechanism 
Autoxidation is a free radical chain reaction and a primary pathway for the formation of 
lipid hydroperoxides. It involves three main steps: initiation, propagation, and termination. 
In order to start autoxidation, unsaturated fatty acids (LH), initiators (In⦁) and oxygen are 
required. In the initiation, unsaturated fatty acids (LH) lose a hydrogen atom to form an 
alkyl radical (L⦁) in the presence of initiators such as light, trace transition metals or free 
radicals: 
LH →L⦁+ H⦁ 
In the propagation, alkyl radical (L⦁) reacts rapidly with oxygen (O2) to form peroxide 
radical (LOO⦁), which then react with another LH to form hydroperoxide (LOOH), a 
primary oxidation product: 
L⦁+ O2 →LOO⦁ 
LOO⦁+ LH →LOOH + L⦁ 
In termination, the free radicals generated throughout the autoxidation can polymerize to 
form dimers, trimers and polymers. However, termination is not very important in food 
and skin care products rich in unsaturated fatty acids. It is because before significant 
termination reaction has occurred, these products are already rancid. (Frankel, 2005) 
The susceptibility of food to lipid oxidation depends on a number of factors which includes 
the level of polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) present and levels of pro- and antioxidants. 
The hydrogen atom bound to the carbon atom separating the C=C bond is the easiest to 
lose. Therefore, PUFA containing multiple C=C bond are the most susceptible to 
peroxidation (Bartosz &Kołakowska, 2011).  Linoleate was found to be 40 times more 
reactive than oleate, and linolenate was 2.4 times more reactive than linoleate (Frankel, 
2005).  
 
==PAGE:18==
8 
 
Volatile secondary oxidation products 
Hydroperoxide has no effect on the sensory quality of oil as it is odorless and tasteless. 
However, it can further react with ferrous ions (Fe2+) to form lipid alkoxyl radical (LO⦁) 
and leads to the formation of secondary oxidation products by β-scission.  The secondary 
oxidation products includes aldehydes, ketones, alcohols, organic acids, and short-chain 
hydrocarbons. One example of the main aldehydes generated by lipid peroxidation is 4-
hydroxy-2-nonenal, which is formed from degradation of arachidonic and linoleic acids. 
Most of the secondary oxidation products are volatile and responsible for off-flavor of the 
oxidized edible oils and other food products. They have an impact on flavor at extremely 
low concentrations, often below 1ppm (Frankel, 1998).  Rancidity is the result of the 
presence of secondary oxidation products. 
They are mainly volatile aldehydes with different unsaturation degrees (Petersen et al., 
2012). Hexanal, pentane, and 2,4-decadienal have been suggested to be used as indicators 
to determine the extent of oil oxidation (Bartosz &Kołakowska, 2011). Hexanal is often is 
associated with rancid and oxidized aromatics in food (Chambers IV &Koppel, 2013).  
 
Role of transition metals 
Transition metals such as iron or copper can serve as catalysts to initiate the formation of 
free radicals via electron transfer: 
M(n+1)+ + LH →Mn+ + L⦁+ H+ 
It can also catalyze the decomposition of hydroperoxide in both their reduced and oxidized 
form. These two reactions may operate as a cycle so even trace amount of metal ions can 
generate radicals effectively: 
Mn+ + LOOH →M(n+1)+ + LO⦁+ OH− 
M(n+1)+ + LOOH →Mn+ + LOO⦁+ H+ 
In products like food and skin care products, transition metals are naturally present and can 
act as prooxidants, resulting enhanced lipid oxidation and reduced shelf life (Decker et al., 
2010).

2.2.2 Skin Ageing and skin care products 
Skin ages naturally and it is due to two main factors: intrinsic and extrinsic ageing. UV 
radiation is the main cause to extrinsic ageing. UV exposure causes physical changes to 
the skin due to alterations that occur in the connective tissue via the formation of lipid 
peroxides, cell contents, enzymes, and reactive oxygen species (ROS). Lipid 
hydroperoxides can be metabolized to form secondary oxidation products which damage 
the extracellular matrix while ROS are credited with involvement in the loss of skin 
elasticity. Moreover, collagen, elastin, and hyaluronic acid decrease during aging process 
due to the enzymatic action of collagenase, elastase and hyaluronidase. This causes loss of 
strength and flexibility in the skin, resulting in visible wrinkles. Therefore, inhibition of 
enzymes activity including collagenase, elastase and hyaluronidase can help anti-aging 
(Widowati et al., 2016). 
Facial cream and skin care products are used to slow down the process of skin ageing. Skin 
care products, like facial cream, has widely been used in daily household basis. Natural 
oils rich in n-6 and n-3 unsaturated fatty acids such as linoleic and α-linolenic acid (ALA), 
are applied in skin care products as the active ingredient. They contain oiling, softening, 
smoothing and protective properties which is functionally important to make the skin 
smooth and properly moistened (Zielińska &Nowak, 2014). Antioxidant properties derived 
from plant extracts have been proposed for anti-elastase, anti-collagenase and anti-
hyaluronidase activities, and thereby being capable of restoring skin elasticity slowing the 
wrinkling process (Ndlovu et al., 2013; Thring et al., 2009). Addition of plant extracts 
might give additional functionality of skin care products to improve elasticity and slow 
down skin aging. 
 
2.2.3 Lipid oxidation in skin care products 
Facial cream contains natural oils rich in unsaturated fatty acids. For example, almond oil, 
one of the natural oils commonly applies to skin care products, contains 57.5-78.7 % oleic 
acid and 12.0-33.9 % linoleic acid. These are the unsaturated fatty acids which represent 
about 90% of the fatty acid content (Roncero et al., 2016).  
==PAGE:21==
11 
 
However, unsaturated fatty acids are susceptible to lipid oxidation due to the presence of 
C=C bond. This leads to deterioration of the quality of the skin care products. Lipid 
oxidation can produce undesirable odors, color and flavors. Limiting the lipid oxidation is 
important to maintain the quality of the skin care products. Storing the skin care products 
in a cool, dark and dry place is the common strategy to keep the quality of the products. 
However, it could be difficult to achieve in a practical situation. Due to globalization, many 
skin care products are sold globally, which involves long transportation and the storage 
conditions are not well controlled. Therefore, addition of antioxidant, which would be 
discussed in section 2.3, is an effective way to limit lipid oxidation even under extreme 
conditions. 
Facial cream is an oil-in-water (o/w) emulsion. An emulsion is one phase dispersed 
throughout another phase in which it is immiscible, such as oil and water. In o/w emulsion, 
oil is dispersed in the aqueous phase. The oil and aqueous phases are separated by an 
interphase composed of amphiphilic compounds i.e. emulsifiers. Emulsifier are surface 
active molecules which can interact with the oil-water interface and stabilize the emulsion. 
As emulsions are thermodynamically unstable, it is important to have stabilizers to 
maintain their physical structure and prevent gravitational separation, flocculation and 
coalescence. 
 
2.2.4 Factors affecting lipid oxidation in emulsions 
There are several factors influencing lipid oxidation in emulsion systems. The first factor 
is the fatty acid composition in emulsion. The levels of unsaturation of the lipids present 
in the food or skin care emulsion highly influences the degree of lipid oxidation. Second 
factor is the water activity. In low moisture foods, the mobility of antioxidants is limited, 
resulting in high lipid oxidation rates compared to the rate at intermediate water activities 
(Labuza et al., 1972). Low water content increase the concentration of transition metal and 
thus lead to higher oxidation rate. However, a further decrease in water will significantly 
lower the reaction due to diffusion limitations. The other factor is the type of emulsifiers 
used. For example, protein-stabilized emulsions are highly sensitive to environmental 
conditions, such as the pH value, ionic strength and temperature (Gu et al., 2004). Besides, 
==PAGE:22==
12 
 
the presence of prooxidant, such as transition metals, also affect the rate of lipid oxidation 
as discussed in section 2.2.1. Moreover, droplet size distribution can also affect rate of 
oxidation. Lipid oxidation is more susceptible in interfacial area in the emulsion. It is 
because the pro-oxidant concentrations are high in the aqueous phase and thus the oxidation 
is usually initiated in the aqueous phase or the interphase instead of the oil phase. Therefore, 
lipid oxidation is related to the total interfacial area, which is determined by the size 
distribution of the droplet. The smaller the droplet size, the larger the interfacial area. Small 
droplets could result in a higher lipid oxidation rate. However, Berton-Carabin et al., (2014) 
stated that there are different contracting results of the droplet size on lipid oxidation. Other 
factors such as thickness and charge of interfacial boundary; metal binding capacity of the 
emulsifiers also affect the effect of droplet size on oxidation (Jacobsen, 2016). Last but not 
least, the surface charge of the droplets can strongly influence on the interactions between 
prooxidant metals in the aqueous phase and the lipids in the oil phase. Interfacial charge 
can either attract or repel prooxidant metals. A negatively charged surface might thus 
attract positively charged transition metals such as iron located in the water phase. Hence, 
more iron would be associated with the lipid surface, unsaturated fatty acids or lipid 
hydroperoxides and enhance lipid oxidation (McClements &Decker, 2000). 
 
2.2.5 Determination of lipid oxidations 
Physical and chemical analytical methods and sensory tests can be used to assess the 
oxidative stability. Physical methods such as droplet size distribution can be used in the 
emulsion system as the droplet size is related to initiation of oxidation. Chemical methods 
includes determination of hydroperoxide value (PV); secondary volatile oxidation products 
by gas chromatography (GC) based mass spectrometry (MS). PV measures lipid oxidation 
by evaluating the initial stage of lipid oxidation (figure 3). It requires extraction of oil and 
removal of solvent in emulsion system and the accuracy can be affected by the color of the 
sample during spectrophotometer measurement, which is color dependent. GC-MS 
analysis is another chemical method to determine the secondary volatile oxidation products 
(figure 3), such as aldehydes, ketones and alcohols. Some aldehydes could be used as 
marker of secondary oxidation. Hexanal is often used for emulsion samples rich in n-6 fatty 
==PAGE:23==
13 
 
acids while propanal is often used in emulsion samples rich in n-3 fatty acids. Besides, 
fatty acids composition and tocopherol content can change due to lipid oxidation. They can 
be used as parameters to identify to lipid oxidation (Jacobsen, 2016).  
As for sensory test, it is the primary method for assessing oxidative stability. A well-trained 
panel is required to assess the smell, taste, appearance and mouthfeel of the samples, scores 
and describes the attributes of the samples. Sensory test is closely correlated to customer 
acceptance. However, it is expensive and gives low level of precision (Frankel, 1998). 
 
2.3 Antioxidants 
Antioxidants are the compounds which inhibit the oxidative processes of a substrate, such 
as lipid, carbohydrate, protein or DNA. They can be applied in skin care emulsion to limit 
lipid oxidation and maintain the quality of the products. 
 
2.3.1 Mechanism 
In general, antioxidants can be classified as primary or secondary antioxidants based on 
their mechanisms. 
Primary antioxidants (Type I) 
Primary antioxidants, also referred to as chain-breaking antioxidants or free radical 
scavengers, can react directly with radicals to convert them into more stable products by 
donating a hydrogen atom or an electron to a radical. Primary antioxidants play an 
important role in limiting initiation and propagation as they can react with lipid alkyl (L⦁), 
alkoxyl (LO⦁) and peroxyl (LOO⦁) radicals. The antioxidant (AH) donates a hydrogen 
atom to the lipid radicals and forming a stable antioxidant radical (A⦁): 
 
L⦁+ AH →LH + A⦁ 
LO⦁+ AH →LOH + A⦁ 
LOO⦁+ AH →LOOH + A⦁ 
==PAGE:24==

Phenolic compounds are effective free radical scavengers as they contain a phenol ring 
with one or more than one hydroxyl group (-OH). They can donate hydrogen atom to free 
radicals efficiently and the subsequent phenoxyl radical is stabilized by resonance 
delocalization throughout the benzene ring structure. Figure 4 demonstrate the resonance 
delocalization of a phenolic compound. Common synthetic phenolic compounds includes 
butylated hydroxyanisole (BHA) and butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT).  They are widely 
applied as food preservatives and very effective. However, their use is declining in food 
industry due to the consumer demand for natural products (Decker et al., 2010). Tocopherol, 
ascorbic acid or caffeic acid are examples of natural phenolic antioxidants.

Secondary antioxidants (Type II) 
Secondary antioxidants are preventive antioxidants that slow the rate of oxidation without 
interacting with free radicals. They can act by chelating metal ions, quenching singlet 
oxygen (in photooxidation), decomposing hydroperoxides to non-radical species and 
absorbing UV radiation (Decker et al., 2010; Frankel, 1998) Secondary antioxidant can 
deactivate metal ions, which promote the initiation of autoxidation and decomposition of 
hydeoperoxides, and therefore limit the formation of both primary and secondary oxidation 
products. Examples of metal chelators include citric acid and ethylenediaminetetraacetic 
acid (EDTA) (Frankel, 1998). 
Moreover, a synergistic effect is generally observed when primary antioxidants are used 
together with secondary antioxidants. For example, a strong synergistic effect is observed 
when natural tocopherols is used in the presence of ascorbic acid (Frankel, 1998). One of 
the reason is that secondary antioxidant may be able to regenerate primary antioxidant 
depended on redox potential and concentration. Another reason is that multi antioxidant 
compounds can inhibit oxidation in different phases such as suppressing initiation and 
propagation of free radicals (Decker et al., 2010).  Metal chelators can decrease the metal-
promoted hydroperoxide decomposition and thus decrease the generation of free radicals. 
Less free radicals formation requires less free radical scavengers to inactivate the lipid 
oxidation, leading to higher concentration of free radical scavengers available through the 
reactions. Therefore, the most effective antioxidants systems would contain antioxidants 
with different mechanisms in limiting oxidation, i.e. combination of free radical 
scavenging antioxidants and metal chelating antioxidants (Decker et al., 2010). Some 
antioxidants can be multi-functional when they possess both primary and secondary 
antioxidant properties. Polyphenolic compounds are often multi-functional due to their 
structure and location of –OH groups (Hermund, 2016).

antioxidant to donate electrons and thereby the ability to reduce others. Examples of 
reducing agents are ascorbic acid and ascorbyl palmitate. Measuring the reducing power is

2.3.2 Natural antioxidants from brown algae 
Seaweeds contains a wide range of bioactive substances, such as polysaccharides, proteins, 
lipids, tocopherols, vitamins, pigments, metals, and polyphenolic compounds (Holdt 
&Kraan, 2011). 
Tocopherols 
Tocopherols act as strong primary antioxidants by radical scavenging and directly 
inactivate free radicals. It belongs to part of the carotenoid family. It exists in four isomer 
forms: α-, β-, γ- and δ- tocopherols. α-tocopherol is the most reactive form and followed 
by β-, γ- and δ- tocopherols (Decker et al., 2010). Mainly α-tocopherol is found in Fucus 
sp. and it is 38-73mg/kg dw depending on the season. The higher concentrations was found 
in autumn and winter, and low concentration in spring (Jensen, 1969). 
Pigments 
Pigments such as carotenoids and chlorophyll are found in brown algae and show 
antioxidant activity. They can work synergistically with tocopherol (Bernard Le Tutour, 
1990). Carotenoids are linear polyenes that can act as antioxidants to inactivate reactive 
oxygen species formed by exposure to light and air (Holdt &Kraan, 2011). β-Carotene, 
violaxanthin and fucoxanthin are found in all brown seaweed species (Haugan &Liaaen-
Jensen, 1994). Fucoxanthin is the most abundant carotenoid. It is a xanthophyll with an 
intense brown color. Nygård and Ekelund (2006) found that there are 0.2 μg/mg dw β-
carotene and 1 μg/mg dw fucoxanthin in F. vesiculosus. Xanthophylls can act as 
antioxidants in photooxidation as they are efficient to quench singlet oxygen and they can 
also scavenge peroxyl radicals (Nomura et al., 1997; LeTutour et al., 1998). However, 
under high oxygen pressure, carotenoids may act as prooxidants (Burton &Ingold, 1984). 
Another major pigment present in brown algae is chlorophyll. Nygård and Ekelund (2006) 
found that there are 5 μg/mg dw chlorophyll in F. vesiculosus. Chlorophyll tend to promote 
photooxidation under exposure to light and thus possess prooxidant activity (Usuki et al., 
1984). The prooxidant activity of chlorophyll requires the presence of light. However, 
==PAGE:27==
17 
 
Endo et al. (1985) suggested that chlorophyll possess antioxidant activity in the dark. 
Chlorophyll can donate hydrogen to break the radical chain reaction, limiting the 
autoxidation of vegetable edible oil stored in the dark. 
Phenolic compounds 
Phenols are the compounds consisting of a hydroxyl group (–OH) bonded directly to an 
aromatic hydrocarbon group. Phenolic compounds give high antioxidant activity because 
they can donate hydrogen atom readily from the hydroxyl group and the subsequent 
radicals is stabilized by resonance delocalization throughout the benzene ring. (Decker et 
al., 2010). Fucus vesiculosus contains high amounts of polyphenolic compounds called 
phlorotannins, which are the main contributors to the overall antioxidant activity of extracts 
from brown algae (Farvin &Jacobsen, 2013; Jiménez-Escrig et al., 2001; Wang et al., 2012, 
2009), can inhibit lipid oxidation by radical scavenging and metal chelating (Farvin 
&Jacobsen, 2013; Wang et al., 2012, 2009). In addition to antioxidant properties, 
phlorotannins have been found to possess a wide range of bioactive properties which are 
beneficial in skin care products. These functions includes antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, 
anti-bacterial, anti-senescence and UV protection (Dutot et al., 2012; Schoenwaelder, 2008; 
Singh, Sidana, Bharate, &Foley, 2006). 
Phlorotannins are made up of basic units called phloroglucinol unit (PGU) and it may 
contribute up to 25% dry weight in brown algae (Ragan &Glombitza, 1986). Based on the 
linkage of phloroglucinol units, phlorotannins can be classified into three main groups: 
fucols, phloroethols and fucophloroethols. Fucols are the polymers in which PGU are 
linked by only phenyl linkages (C-C); phloroethols are polymers in which PGU are linked 
by arylether bonds (C-O-C); fucophlorethols are polymers in which the PGU are linked by 
a mixture arylether and phenyl linkages. A subgroup of phlorethols is eckol, which 
structurally include a 1,4-dibenzodioxin linkage (Singh et al., 2006). Each of the primary 
groups can be grouped into linear or branched phlorotannins. When extension units have 
only two inter phloroglucinol connections, it is linear; if the extension bind to three or more, 
it is branched (Martínez &Castañeda, 2013). Figure 5 demonstrates the structures of 
phloroglucinol and main classes of phlorotannins derived from brown algae.

Metals 
Seaweed contains both heavy metal and minerals. Heavy metals such as lead (Pb), 
cadmium (Cd), mercury (Hg) copper (Cu), manganese (Mn) and zinc (Zn) are present in 
the environment and may accumulate in the seaweed (Holdt &Kraan, 2011). They are 
undesirable to human health. Besides, inorganic arsenic (As) accumulation in seaweed is 
an example of another problem because it has an effect on the human nervous system 
(Holdt &Kraan, 2011). Therefore, presence of the heavy metals should be taken into 
account and tested if seaweed are to be used as ingredients for food or cosmetic products 
as these metals might be concentrated during extraction of antioxidants from seaweed. 
Furthermore,  Van Netten (2000) found that iron (Fe) and copper (Cu) are present in Fucus 
vesiculosus and they are 520 μg Fe/g dw and 1.4 μg Cu/g dw respectively. Cu and Fe are 
transition metals. Their active forms, i.e. as metal ions, can act as an initiator to promote 
==PAGE:29==

initiation and are usually unwanted in skin care products due to their prooxidant activity as 
discussed in in section 2.2.1. 
 
2.3.3 Extraction  
An effective extraction method is important to yield high amount of phlorotannins. The 
choice of solvent medium should be made with consideration of intended use of the 
products. Application with pretreatment may increase the extraction efficiency. In this 
project, antioxidative extracts from Fucus vesiculosus would be produced by using 
ultrasound assisted solid phase extraction (SPE) method.  
Solid phase extraction and types of solvent 
Solid phase extraction method is a frequently applied extraction method for extraction of 
seaweeds (Farvin &Jacobsen, 2013; Hermund et al., 2015; Honold et al., 2016; Wang et 
al., 2012, 2009). As for the choice of solvent, Wang et al. (2012) and Honold et al. (2016) 
found that less polar solvent can extract higher yield of phenolic compounds. Wang et al. 
(2012) concluded that due to the considerably extraction ability of 80% ethanol to extract 
phlorotannins from F. vesiculosus, the use of ethanol would be preferred over acetone and 
methanol for the extraction of food-grade natural antioxidants, although 70% acetone and 
80% methanol has higher extraction efficiency than 80% ethanol. Besides, the composition 
of co-extracted compounds varied much depending on the type solvent. Hermund (2016) 
found that water can extract a higher degree tocopherols and iron, and acetone and ethanol 
can extract a larger extent pigments. As water is a polar compound, some other highly polar 
compounds with metal chelating properties could be co-extracted as well (Wang et al., 
2009). Therefore, ethanol and water, which are green solvents, would be used as extraction 
solvents. 
Pretreatment 
Traditional methods employed for extracting bioactive compounds are time consuming and 
have low extraction efficiencies. In order to improve the extraction efficiencies, 
pretreatment with ultrasound was applied. Ultrasound has been reported for the extraction 
of bioactive compounds from natural sources including lycopene from tomatoes, 
==PAGE:30==
20 
 
anthocyanins from raspberries, phenolic compounds from citrus peel and brown algae 
(Chen et al., 2007; Kadam et al., 2015; Lianfu &Zelong, 2008; Ma, Chen, Liu, &Ye, 2009). 
Kadam et al. (2015) applied the ultrasonic energy in extraction of brown seaweed 
Ascophyllum nodosum by soaking the sonication probe to the sample solutions directly and 
they found that 10 minutes of ultrasound pretreatment increased the total phenolic content 
(TPC) from 79.28 ± 0.42 to 82.70 ± 0.96 mg GAE/g using water as solvent and from 83.05 
± 0.27 to 84.54 ± 0.46 mg GAE/g using 0.03 M hydrochloride acid as solvent. 
 
3. Experimental Design 
Highly antioxidative extracts from F. vesiculosus were produced by using solid phase 
extraction (SPE) method with and without ultrasound pretreatment. Water and ethanol (80% 
v/v) were used as extraction solvents. The pretreated extracts would be compared with 
extracts where ultrasound had not been applied. The extracts would be characterized and 
their antioxidant activity would be determined by in vitro assays. A 6-week storage trial 
was conducted to study the application of the extracts in cream. The oxidative stability of 
the cream emulsions was evaluated out of 7 sampling points over storage. Figure 6 gives

tocopherol/g oil, 362158 μg γ-tocopherol/g oil and 103890 μg δ-tocopherol/g oil. All 
solvents used were of high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) grade and

Extraction was performed according to Wang et al. (2012) with modification. Water and 
80% ethanol (v/v) were used as extraction solvents. Five gram of powered seaweed was 
added to 100 mL of water or 80% ethanol (v/v) and they were shaken vigorously for 30 
seconds to ensure the powered seaweed was dissolved and mixed well with the solvents.

4.3 Characterization of extracts 
The extracts were analyzed in form of solution in antioxidant assays for screening and they 
were in form of freeze-dried power in total phenolic content, phenolic compounds, and 
pigments analysis for full characterization and application to cream emulsions. The extracts 
solution were freeze-dried for three days to convert to powder form. 
 
4.3.1 Antioxidant Assays  
The extract solutions were diluted in 13 concentrations from 0.00047 to 0.75 mL extract 
/mL water or ethanol. The antioxidant properties were determined by three different 
colorimetric assays described below. The effective concentration (EC) value was 
determined and it was calculated as the concentration of sample required to reach 50% 
inhibition or activity in the reaction mixture. The calculation was described in appendix A. 
DPPH radical scavenging effect  
The radical scavenging ability of the extracts was determined using 2,2-diphenyl-1-
picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) radical. DPPH is a stable radical which can be used to evaluate 
antioxidant efficacy to scavenge free radicals or donate H-atoms. The free DPPH radical is 
purple and the absorbance can be measured by spectrophotometry. The higher the 
scavenging activity of the antioxidant, the lower the absorbance since the purple color turns 
yellow during incubation. 
The DPPH radical scavenging activity was determined according to Yang, Guo, &Yuan 
(2008) modified using multiplate reader. 100 μL extract solution and 100 μL 0.1 mM 
DPPH (in 96 % ethanol) were added to microtiterplate. They are mixed on the 
microplateshaker and incubated for 30 min at room temperature in the dark. The 
absorbance (As) was measured at 517 nm after the incubation. Triplicate measurements 
were performed, and BHT (10 mg in 50 mL ethanol (96%)) with approximately 70% 
inhibition was included as a positive control. A sample blank was made with DPPH but 
without extract solution (Ab), and a sample control was made without DPPH but with 
extract solution (A0). The results were given as percent inhibition and were calculated as 
follows:

4.3.2 Chemical composition analysis 
Total Phenolic content (TPC) 
The total phenolic compounds of the extracts were determined according to the method 
used by Farvin and Jacobsen (2013) with modification. 100 μL of extract solution was 
mixed with 0.75 mL of Folin-Ciocalteu reagent (10% w/w in distilled water). After 5 min, 
0.75 mL of sodium carbonate (7.5% in distilled water) was added. The samples were 
incubated for 1.5 hour at room temperature in the dark. The absorbance was measured at 
725 nm using a UV−vis spectrophotometer (Shimadzu UV mini 1240, Duisburg, Germany). 
A standard curve with serial gallic acid solutions (0–500 μg/mL) was used for calibration. 
The analysis were conducted in duplicates and the results were expressed as grams gallic 
acid equivalents per 100 g extract dried weight, g GAE/100g dw. TPC were performed on 
the extract solution and also freeze-dried extract power. In the analysis of powder, the 
samples solution is prepared by dissolving 2.0 -2.2 mg seaweed extract powder in methanol. 
The calibration curve of gallic acid are included in appendix B.

PV measures lipid oxidation by measuring lipid hydroperoxides, the primary oxidation 
product from initial stage of lipid oxidation. The method determines the substances that 
oxidize ferrous ion (Fe2+) to ferric ion (Fe3+). These substances are assumed to be 
hydroperoxides or other oxidizing compounds derived from lipid oxidation. The analysis 
was performed according to Shantha and Decker (1994), based on the formation of an iron–
thiocyanate complex. Oil extract was prepared by evaporating 0.5 g chloroform extract 
from lipid extraction under nitrogen flow for approximately 1 hour. Ammonium 
thiocyanate and ferrous chloride were added to the oil extract. Peroxides that was present 
in the oil would oxidize the ferrous ions to ferric ions. The ferric ions would then react with 
thiocyanate to form a red colored ferric-thiocyanate complex. The colored complex was 
measured spectrophotometrically at 500nm (Shimadzu UV1800, Shimadzu Scientific 
Instruments, Columbia, MD, USA). The analyses were done in duplicate and the results 
were expressed in milli equivalents peroxides per kg oil (meq O2/kg oil). In addition, the 
oxidation rates were calculated as follows:

4.5.5 Volatile compounds (VC) 
As lipid oxidation propagates, lipid hydroperoxides is decomposed into secondary volatile 
oxidation products. These volatile oxidation products can be collected by dynamic 
headspace (DHS) and analyzed by gas chromatography (GC) based mass spectrometry 
(MS).  
Tenax GRTM packed tubes were used to collect volatile compounds by dynamic headspace. 
4 g of emulsion [including 30mg internal standard (30mg/g of 4-methyl-1- pentanol in oil)] 
and 20mL of distilled water were weighed. The volatile secondary oxidation products were 
collected at 45°C under flushing with nitrogen (flow of 150 mL/min) for 30 minutes, 
followed by flushing with the nitrogen (flow of 50 mL/min) for 15 minutes to remove water 
from Tenax GRTM packed tube. The trapped volatiles were desorbed using an automatic 
thermal desorber (ATD-400, Perkin- Elmer, Norwalk, CT) connected to an Agilent 5890 
IIA model gas chromatograph equipped with a HP 5972 mass selective detector. The 
settings for the MS were: electron ionization mode, 70 eV, mass to charge ratio (m/z) scan 
between 30 and 250mAU. Chromatographic separation of volatile compounds was 
conducted on a DB1701 column (30m x ID 0.25mm x 0.5 μm film thickness, J&W 
Scientific, Folsom, CA, USA) using helium gas flow (1.3 mL/min). The temperature 
programme was as follows: 3 min at 35°C, 3°C/min from 35 to 120°C, 7°C/min to 120°-
160°C, 15°C/min 160–200°C and hold for 4 min at 200°C.  
The auto sampler collector setting details were: 9.2 psi, outlet split: 5.0 mL/min, desorption 
flow: 60 mL/min. The analysis was performed in triplicate in all sampling points and the 
results were given in ng/g of emulsion.

4.5.6 Droplet size distribution 
The size of fat globules in the o/w emulsion systems was measured by laser diffraction

5.1.1 Effect of pretreatment 
All extracts possess antioxidant activities of DPPH radical scavenging, metal chelating and 
reducing power. Extracts with pretreatment did not perform better than extracts without 
pretreatment. There was no significance (p > 0.05) difference between EWP and ENP; 
WWP and WNP in DPPH radical scavenging activity, the metal chelating activity, 
reducing power and total phenolic content except that ENP had significantly higher TPC 
than EWP. Hence, pretreatment did not increase the extraction efficacy. These results were 
contradicting to Kadam et al (2015) since they found that ultrasound pretreatment increased 
==PAGE:47==
37 
 
the TPC. The difference was likely because the ultrasound equipment setups were different. 
In the present study, the pretreatment was performed by placing the extract solution in 
sonication bath while Kadam et al (2015) used an sonication probe inside the extract 
solution. The difference might cause the inefficient pretreatment in the present study. 
Therefore, extracts without pretreatment, ENP and WNP were selected for application in 
cream emulsions. Furthermore, the extraction was performed in duplicates and no 
significance between the antioxidant actives and TPC of the duplicates in ENP and WNP 
were found, showing high reproducibility of the extraction.  
 
5.1.2 Effect of solvent type on extraction 
Total phenolic content (TPC) 
An aqueous solution with 80% ethanol extracted significantly higher (p < 0.05) amount of 
TPC than water as shown in table 4. The result was in agreement with previous studies on 
extraction of phenolic compounds from F. vesiculosus (Hermund et al., 2015; Honold et 
al., 2016; Wang et al., 2012, 2009). Wang et al. (2012) and Honold et al. (2016) found that 
less polar solvent can extract higher yield of phenolic compounds because phenolic 
compounds are more soluble in polar organic solvents than in water.  
TPC of ENP and WNP were 16.67 
± 0.60 and 13.75 ± 0.19 a g GAE/100 g dw, 
respectively (table 4). Wang et al. (2012); Honold et al. (2016) found a higher TPC in 
extracts both ethanol (35.4 ± 0.2 g PGEs/100 g dw and 20 ± 2.4 g GAE/100 g dw, 
respectively) and water (26.3 ± 0.2 PGEs/100 g dw and 12.6 ± 2.1 g GAE/100 g dw, 
respectively) compared to this study. On the other hand Hermund et al. (2015) found a 
lower TPC in water extract (18.4 ± 0.1 mg GAE/100 g dw) compared to this study. 
The reason could be due to the seasonal variation, different origin and growing 
environments of seaweed. The F. vesiculosus used in the previous studies were from 
Iceland while the seaweed used in present study were collected in Denmark and the 
collection time varied. Iceland has a longer day length in summer compared to Denmark. 
This may contribute to the higher phenolic content accumulation. Phenolic content, such 
as phlorotannins, can vary according to local environmental conditions including salinity 
==PAGE:48==
38 
 
(Ragan and Glombitza, 1986) light intensity and nutrient availability (Pavia &Toth, 2000). 
Genetic different between individuals also exhibit differences in phlorotannin contents 
(Stengel et al., 2011).  
 
Antioxidant properties 
The ethanol extracts had significantly higher scavenging abilities on DPPH radicals 
compared to water extracts, according to the lower EC50 value of the ethanol extracts. 
Hence, ethanol extracts had both higher TPC and higher DPPH radicals scavenging 
activities. This agrees with the previous studies that there is high correlations between TPC 
and DPPH scavenging activity (Farvin &Jacobsen, 2013; Hermund et al., 2015; Wang et 
al., 2012, 2009).  
Water extracts and ethanol extracts both possess metal chelating activity and they do not 
have significant difference between their metal chelating activities. This was similar to 
previous study in which no significance difference in metal chelating activities were 
noticed between water and ethanol F. vesiculosus extract (Hermund, 2016; Honold et al., 
2016). However, Farvin &Jacobsen (2013) and Wang et al. (2009) found out water extracts 
showed significantly higher metal chelating activity than ethanol extracts. Farvin 
&Jacobsen (2013) conclude that some highly polar compounds, other than phenolic 
compounds, were extracted by water also influence the metal chelating activity. Hence, the 
difference between the results among studies could be due to seasonal variations and 
different growing environments which affects the accumulation of other metal chelating 
polar compound in F. vesiculosus. However, further studies need to be done to investigate 
the reason behind. 
Ethanol extracts had a significantly higher reducing power than water extracts. Previous 
studies from Honold et al. (2016) and Wang et al. (2012) also found similar results. Wang 
et al. (2012) found out there is strong correlations between TPC and reducing capacities. 
The high reducing power of F. vesiculosus has been reported to be due to the presence of 
fucoidan (Ruperez et al., 2002). 
==PAGE:49==

In summary, both extracts possess antioxidant properties including radical scavenging 
activities, metal chelating activity and reducing power. Ethanol extracts had a higher TPC, 
DPPH radical scavenging activity and reducing power than water extracts, indicating that 
phlorotannins is related to the antioxidant properties, which has also been previously 
reported (Hermund, 2016; Wang et al., 2012). 
As the pretreatment with ultrasound did not improve the TPC and antioxidant properties of 
extracts, the ethanol extracts without pretreatment and water extracts without pretreatment 
were used to work continuously for full characterization and application trial.

5.3.1 Physical changes during storage 
Droplet size distribution 
The distribution of oil droplets in the skin care emulsions was determined at the beginning 
and the end of storage as shown in figure 8. At day 0, the D3,2 value of RF was significantly 
higher than all the creams with extracts. At the end of storage, all the cream emulsions

suggested to be a reason of destabilisation of the emulsion system (Poyato et al., 2017).  As 
lipid oxidation is regarded as an interfacial phenomenon as discussed in section 2.2.3, the 
smaller the droplet size, the larger the interfacial area. However, Berton-Carabin et al. 
(2014) found that there are different contracting results of the droplet size on lipid oxidation 
in different dispersed system underline the fact that droplet size and interfacial area 
probably influence lipid oxidation in o/w emulsions through several mechanisms, which 
are not fully understood. In the present study, both RF (with initial bigger droplet size) and 
cream emulsions with extracts (with initial smaller droplet size) reached the similar droplet 
size at the end of storage (except WE1). Therefore, no clear effect of the extracts on the 
cream emulsion systems could be drawn. Further studies in better understanding of the 
mechanism behind droplet size on oxidative stability in skin care products is required.

5.3.2 Oxidative changes during storage 
Fatty acids composition  
The fatty acids composition of the samples was determined at the beginning, middle and 
at the end of the storage in every sample. It was observed that it remained unchanged during 
the storage period. Therefore these results were not included. The major fatty acid in cream 
emulsion was oleic acid, followed by linoleic acid, and the saturated FA, palmitic and 
stearic acid. On average, the fatty acid composition was as follows (only the content 
≥0.5%): 16:0, 11.21%; 16:1 (n-7), 0.53%; 18:0, 10.72%; 18:1 (n-9), 53.29%; 18:1 (n-7), 
0.26%; 18:2 (n-6), 18.11%; 18:2 (n-4), 0.69% and 20:0, 0.61%. Similar results was found 
by Poyato et al. (2017) in which no significant changes in fatty acid composition was 
noticed in cream emulsions with and without F. vesiculosus extract. 
Almond oil was the main source of fatty acid contribution in cream emulsions. Comparing 
the fatty acid composition to almond oil, cream emulsions contained 5.72% higher of 16:0 
(palmitic acid) and 8.8 % higher of 18:0 (stearic acid) which comes from other raw 
materials, such as VE far and MF fat. On the other hand, there were 8.7 % lower in 18:1 
(n-9) (oleic acid); 1.4 % lower in 18:1 (n-7); 2.37 % lower in 18:2 (n-6) (linoleic acid) and 
2.52 % lower in 20:1 (n-7) in cream emulsions compared to almond oil. These are 
unsaturated fatty acids, which might be oxidized and decomposed during the high 
temperature production process.

suggested that the tocopherols can influence the oxidative stability and there might be a 
synergistic effect between tocopherol and phenolic compounds or pigments, contributing 
to the regeneration of tocopherol in skin care emulsions containing extracts (Hermund, 
2016; Honold et al., 2016; Poyato et al., 2017). This indicated the higher stability of cream 
emulsions of EE0.5 and EE1, which possess positive TTC.

also found a lower formation of hydroperoxides in acetone extract compared to water F. 
vesiculosus extract in mayonnaise at the end of storage. 
At day 42, creams with the highest concentration, EE1 and WE1, had a significantly lower 
PV compared to day 28. These results were similar to previous study that a decrease in 
concentration of PV in fish oil enriched milk were found after 6 days (Hermund, 2016) and 
9 days (Sørensen et al., 2015) of storage. The decrease in PV might be due to formation of 
secondary oxidation product from primary oxidation products. This can be caused by the 
presence of reductants or prooxidative activity of the compounds in EE or WE. Reductants 
can cause the reduction of the Fe3+ to the more reactive Fe2+ and will promote degradation 
of hydroperoxides.

6. Conclusion and Perspectives 
6.1 Conclusion 
This project extracted and characterized the antioxidants from Danish F. vesiculosus and 
evaluated their antioxidative efficacy in application to skin care emulsions. 
Regarding to the pretreatment, the application of pretreatment with ultrasound did not 
improve the extraction efficacy in terms of DPPH radical scavenging activity, metal 
chelating activity, reducing power and total phenolic content in the present study. The 
different way of setting up ultrasound equipment is suggested to be the reason for the 
ineffective outcome. 
Use of different types of extraction solvents produced extracts with different antioxidant 
activities and co-extracted compounds. Ethanol extract had a higher TPC, DPPH radical 
scavenging activity and reducing power than water extracts, indicating that TPC is related 
to radical scavenging activity and reducing power. Ethanol also co-extracted more 
pigments and phenolic compounds compared to water.  
Application of F. vesiculosus ethanol and water extracts in facial cream improved the 
oxidative stability of the facial cream over the storage period. Ethanol extract in a 
concentration of 1 mg/g was the most efficient as antioxidant in skin care emulsions. The 
higher efficacy of EE over WE was related to high TPC, high radical scavenging activity, 
high reducing power, and moderate or high metal chelating ability. In conclusion, the 
Danish F. vesiculosus extracts were effective in protecting unsaturated skin care emulsions 
in dark and room temperature storage.

6.2 Future Perspective 
This study gives evidence that F. vesiculosus extracts can improve the oxidative stability 
of skin care products due to its antioxidant properties. In addition to the antioxidant 
properties, it has been reported that there are extra functions of phlorotannins, which are 
anti-inflammatory, anti-inflammatory, anti-bacterial, anti-senescence and can give UV 
protection. These functions are beneficial in skin care products. Therefore, the effects of 
their efficacy can be further investigated and determined when applying the extracts in skin 
care products. Besides, the anti-elastase, anti-collagenase and anti-hyaluronidase activities 
are important to provide anti-aging effect. It has been reported that some plant extracts 
possess these effects. Therefore, testing the anti-elastase, anti-collagenase and anti-
hyaluronidase activities of the F. vesiculosus extract is of high interest and more research 
is needed to investigate them. 
Furthermore, as the extraction also co-extracts a wide range of bioactives in addition to 
phlorotannins, the extracts may possibly contain hazardous compounds such as cadmium, 
inorganic arsenic and other heavy metals. Therefore, a screening and filtering of the extract 
should be considered in order to develop a safe natural antioxidant extract from F. 
vesiculosus. 
Marine algae are an attractive natural source of antioxidants, which has not been widely 
utilized as antioxidant ingredients for the food and cosmetic industry. In order to apply 
seaweed based antioxidants to different cosmetic products and food for human 
consumption, the selection of extraction solvent is of extreme importance. Solvent 
extraction should not only be effective in extracting phlorotannins from F. vesiculosus but 
also comply with the requirements set by the EU regulations so that the antioxidant extracts 
can be legally applied in consumer products. Moreover, in order to meet the demands for 
natural antioxidants for the food and cosmetic industry, more research is needed on 
sustainable cultivation and up-scaling of the extraction process.

A. In vitro antioxidant properties of SPE extracts  
The study was performed to determine the effective concentration (EC) of the SPE extracts 
(ENP, EWP, WNP and WWP) to reach a specific inhibition, i.e. 50% or absorbance 0.7, 
according to table A.1. 
1. Material methods 
1.1. Antioxidant assays 
Three assays were applied to determine the EC of the SLE extracts: DPPH (1,1-diphenyl-
2-picrylhydrazyl) radical scavenging, metal chelating and reducing power assay. 
Each extract was dissolved in water or ethanol in a stock solution of 4 mg/mL. Dilution 
rows were prepared for the assays. The concentrations from 0.00047 to 0.75 mL extract 
/mL water or ethanol were analyzed for the three antioxidant assays. Triplicates (within the 
mentioned ranges) were used to conduct the dose/response curves of the extracts in each 
assay means including standard deviations). 
1.2. Data treatment 
For each triplicate a dose/response curve was prepared and linear regression performed 
on the linear area of the exponential phase. A dose-response curve is made (figure A.1) 
and the linear area of the exponential phase of the curve is taken to make linear 
regression in figure A.2. 
The effective concentration (EC) value was determined and it was calculated as the 
concentration of sample required to reach 50% inhibition or activity in the reaction 
mixture. EC50 were determined for DPPH radical scavenging capacity, metal chelating 
ability and EC (0.7) for reducing power, respectively. These values and standard 
deviations (±SD) were calculated based on these triplicates. An example of calculation is 
shown in figure A.1 and A.2. 
The response in the reducing power assay was an absorbance at 700nm (OD700). Therefore 
the EC0.7 values were determined in this assay equal to 0.7 in absorbance.
