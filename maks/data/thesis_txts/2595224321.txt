Abstract
This project is concerned with the investigation of near real-time monitoring of the
ionosphere above Greenland. There is right now no continuous real-time monitoring
of the ionosphere above Greenland even though the ionosphere above Scandinavia
and Canada is being monitored in near real-time [27] [38]. Near real-time-monitoring
of the ionosphere and its scintillation can be an advantage for Global Navigation
Satellite System (GNSS) users in Greenland, since it might indicate where the largest
position errors will be spatially. GNSS Ionospheric Scintillation and TEC Monitor
(GISTM) receivers, which would be the optimal tool for monitoring scintillation in
the ionosphere, can also provide valuable data to the scientiﬁc community.
The conclusions drawn from this project can help indicate what the next step should
be on the path towards monitoring the ionosphere above Greenland. The project
provides suggestions that include areas such as data coverage, the elevation cut-oﬀ
angle, and interpolation methods.
The GNSS data that is analyzed in this project is from seven selected GNSS re-
ceivers that are a part of the Greenland GPS Network (GNET). The data is used
for computing the phase scintillation Rate of TEC index (ROTI), that was used for
mapping the scintillation. To investigate the possibility of visualizing the ROTI val-
ues spatially, ﬁrst the spatial data coverage was examined. Furthermore, two spatial
interpolation methods, chosen based on the study [6] (pp. 54-57), were tested for
interpolating ROTI spatially above Greenland. Last, the eﬀects of scintillation on
GNSS receivers was examined.
It was found that there are some large spatial data gaps, which can probably only be
eliminated by another spatial distribution of the receiver stations, however adding
data from the Global Navigation Satellite System (GLONASS) did improve the cov-
erage in areas that were thinly covered by the Global Positioning System (GPS).
Another result is that there is a relation between high ROTI values and low elevation
angles, and that this relation is more prominent at geomagnetically quiet times. The
test of the interpolation methods lead to a conclusion that kriging provided slightly
better maps than natural neighbor at geomagnetically active times, while natural
neighbor might be preferable at geomagnetically quiet time if a map is desired. Last,
it was found that receivers at all the tested latitudes were aﬀected by ionospheric
phase scintillation, this was seen as an increase in cycle slips.

1
Introduction
The purpose of this project is to investigate the possibilities of near real-time mon-
itoring of the ionosphere above Greenland. This part of the ionosphere is at the
moment not being continuously monitored, even though it is the case for the iono-
sphere above Canada and Norway [27] [38]. Monitoring of the ionosphere provides
knowledge of the navigation errors caused by the ionosphere. This is an advantage
in rescue operations, and general navigation in and around Greenland. It can also
be an advantage for other infrastructure that is dependent on precise navigation,
and scientists who study the ionosphere.
The main data used in this project is from the Greenland GPS Network (GNET).
These GNET receivers are meant for geodetic purposes, but in this project they
will be used for monitoring scintillations in the ionosphere above Greenland. The
GNET stations were chosen because they have been active during a solar maximum
and are spatially distributed along the coast of Greenland.
The measure of scintillation will in this project be the rate of total electron content
index (ROTI). This is chosen based on the sampling rate of the GNET stations.
The ROTI was ﬁrst computed in 1997 in a study with the purpose of monitoring
the ionosphere globally with GPS data [9], and has also later been used in studies
of the polar ionosphere [6]. It is therefore a well documented index which will be
used to map the ionospheric scintillations above Greenland.
A map of the scintillations in the ionosphere is an essential part of monitoring the
ionosphere, since it provides a good illustration of the spatial distribution of the

2
Theory
This section will provide theory about GNSS, the ionosphere, scintillation indices,
and spatial interpolation methods.
2.1
GNSS systems
The Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) includes multiple navigation sys-
tems, the American GPS, the Russian GLONASS, the European Galileo and the
Chinese system Beidou. It is only GPS and GLONASS that will be used in this
project, since these are the only GNSS systems that are compatible with the re-
ceivers on Greenland that are used.
GPS was the ﬁrst GNSS system that was developed. It designed to have 24 satel-
lites in six orbit planes, which have an inclination of 55 degrees to equator. There
are four satellites in each plane, and the constellation is designed so four to eight
satellites are always visible in the sky with an elevation angle above 15 degrees [4]
(pp. 222-223).
The GLONASS satellites are placed in three orbit planes, with eight satellites in
each plane. The inclination of the planes is 64.8 degrees [4] (p. 348). The inclina-
tion of the GLONASS satellites is thereby larger than the inclination of the GPS
satellites. The GLONASS satellites will therefore have larger elevation angles in the
sky seen from receivers at high latitudes on the Earth.
GLONASS generally works in the same way as GPS, there are however a few diﬀer-
ences. GLONASS uses slightly diﬀerent frequencies, and the orbits are as mentioned
above diﬀerent from the orbits of the GPS satellites. Another diﬀerence is that all
the GLONASS satellites use individual frequencies [1], while the GPS satellites all
use carrier waves with the same frequencies which are modulated with individual
pseudo random noise (PRN) codes [4] (p. 329). A receiver which is compatible with
GPS is therefore not necessarily compatible with GLONASS.

2.2
GNSS Signals
The GNSS satellites transmit three sinusoidal carrier waves at diﬀerent frequencies.
These frequencies can vary depending on which GNSS system is used, but they
generally lie in the L-band which is in the range 1-2 GHz. GPS utilises the frequen-
cies L1, L2, and L5. The L1 signal transmits a navigation message, and a coarse
acquisition (C/A) code, which is a PRN code that is individual for each satellite.
Furthermore, L1 also transmits a precision code (P-code) which is encrypted, and
is only available for military use [1]. The L2 signal also transmits the P-code, but
the newer satellites additionally use this signal to transmit the L2C code which is
similar to the C/A code.
2.3
Navigation Messages
The GPS navigation message is divided into 5 subframes [5]. The ﬁrst subframe con-
tains the satellite health information, parameters for the clock correction, and the
GPS week. The second and third subframes contain the ephemeris, which describes
the position of the satellite relative to the Earth. The fourth subframe contains
parameters for modelling the ionosphere, however this model can only account for
around 50% of the signal delay that occurs in the ionosphere [2]. The other parts of
the fourth subframe are Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) information, and half
of the almanac, which predicts the location of all satellites in the constellation. The
receiver can use the almanac to ﬁnd other satellites once it has connected to one.
The remaining part of the almanac is in the ﬁfth subframe.
2.4
Code Pseudorange
The code pseudorange is basically found by multiplying the travel time of the signal
with the speed of light. There are however some errors in the travel time which is
why it is a pseudo range. The travel time is found by correlation in the receiver.
When a GNSS signal is received the signal will be correlated with a replica of the
signal produced in the receiver. An early, late and prompt replica will be moved
back and forth, until the prompt replica has a maximum correlation with the signal.
The correlations of the signal with the early and late replica should at this point be
equal to each other. The replica are synchronised with the receiver clock. Therefore
the time shift (∆t) between the signal and the prompt replica is the same as the
diﬀerence between the satellite clock reading (ts) and the receiver clock reading (tr)
in a common time system.
The satellite signal emission clock reading (tsat) which is transmitted in the naviga-

2.6
Errors and biases
According to [4] (p. 109) there are three types of errors: satellite related errors,
errors related to the propagation-medium, and receiver errors. The satellite related
errors are satellite clock and orbital errors. Propagation-medium errors are atmo-
spheric errors caused by the ionosphere or troposphere, and receiver errors can be
related to the receiver clock or multipath.

2.6.1
Clock errors
There is both a clock error in the satellite and in the receiver. The GPS satellites
have two rubidium clocks and two caesium clock. The rubidium clocks are most
precise over short time intervals, while the caesium clocks are more precise than the
rubidium clocks over longer time intervals. The satellite clock errors are estimated
every time they connect to a control station on the Earth. The control stations,
which have known locations, are spread across the Earth so that each satellite will
connect to a station several times each day [1].
The clock used in the receivers is often a quartz clock which is less precise. The
receiver clock error is estimated in the position calculations, which is why a mini-
mum of four satellites are needed for positioning. The equation that we get for each
satellite has to be solved for the three coordinates and the receiver clock error [1].

2.6.3
Multipath
Multipath occurs when the signal is reﬂected from one or more surfaces on its way
from the satellite to the receiver. This results in a longer signal path and it creates
noise on the correlation of both the C/A code and the P-code in the receiver [1] (p.
43). Multipath is mostly a problem in regions with tall buildings or mountains [1],
and since the receivers used in this project are stationary, then it would be fair to
expect that they are in locations where multipath is not a problem. Furthermore,
the geodetic receivers that are used in this project can mitigate multipath. The
mitigation can be done instrumentally by utilizing an elevation-mask, or by sorting
the data based on the signal strength, since reﬂected signals will have a lower signal
strenght [2].

2.6.4
Dilution of Precision
Dilution of Precision (DOP) concerns the geometry of the satellite constellation.
When the satellites are further apart from each other in the sky the position DOP
(PDOP), which is the DOP in 3D, will be smaller. The horizontal DOP (HDOP) is
generally smaller than the vertical DOP (VDOP). This means that there is a larger
error on the vertical measurements than on the horizontal measurements [1]. The
reason for this is that it is not possible to measure from satellites under the horizon,
and often an elevation cut-oﬀangle is also used. This means that a better geometry
is possible in the horizontal plane than is the vertical plane.
2.7
The ionosphere
The ionosphere is the ionized top part of the atmosphere. The lower bound of the
ionosphere is generally considered to be around 60 km, the upper bound is however
not as well deﬁned but is often chosen to be around 1000 km. The limits are de-
termined by observations of the ionization. There is still measurable ionization at
1000 km, but it decreases exponentially with altitude [6] (p. 5).
2.7.1

2.8
Scintillations and indices
Scintillations are ﬂuctuations in the phase and amplitude of radio signals. They are
caused by small scale variations in the electron content of the ionosphere. These
variations in the electron density cause ﬂuctuations in the refractive index of the
ionosphere, which results in a diﬀraction of the signal. This can cause phase ﬂuctua-
tions, and the interference between scattered signals results in amplitude scintillation
[8] [16]. In the high latitudes scintillation is primarily caused by increased particle
precipitation in the auroral region or high speed convection of plasma patches, both
processes are increased during geomagnetic storms [9].
Phase scintillation can aﬀect GNSS receiver by introducing cycle slips, where the
continuous phase lock is discontinued momentarily. Cycle slips can be harmful to
carrier phase measurements since the integer ambiguity becomes unkown. The am-
plitude scintillations can result in a decrease of signal to noise ratio. If the signal to
noise ratio fades to below a certain threshold is can result in a loss of lock [18] (p.
374).
While the earlier mentioned ionospheric delays could be mitigated by utilizing dual
frequency GNSS this is not the case for diﬀractive scintillation [18] (p. 373). Fortu-
nately there are indices that can be used to characterise scintillation.

2.9
Current systems
This section both presents ionospheric currents at high latitudes, and a magneto-
spheric current around the equator. Both currents systems are related to solar wind
magnetosphere interactions. However geomagnetic substorm aﬀect the high latitude
ionospheric current system most, while an increase in the strength of the ring cur-
rent is a characteristic of geomagnetic storms.
2.9.1
Current systems in the polar ionosphere
The ﬁeld lines of the Interplanetary Magnetic Field (IMF) and the ﬁeld lines of the
Earth magnetic ﬁeld can under special conditions reconnect with each other. The
reconnection occurs when the z-component of the IMF (Bz) is negative, meaning
that the ﬁeld lines of the IMF and the Earths magnetic ﬁeld are anti-parallel. This
creates a strong gradient in the magnetic ﬁeld which allows the two ﬁeld lines to
reconnect at the sub solar point. This is the point of the magnetosphere that is
closest to the Sun. The previously closed magnetic ﬁeld line of the Earths magnetic
ﬁeld is thereby turned into an open ﬁeld line connected to the IMF. The open ﬁeld
line is dragged across the polar cap into the magnetosphere tail. As more ﬁeld lines

are dragged to the magnetosphere tail, the pressure will cause the open inner ﬁeld
lines in the magnetotail to reconnect. The now closed and elongated ﬁeld line will
bounce back towards the Earth, transporting part of the stored energy towards the

[18](p. 60). The ﬁeld line will afterwards ﬂow around the Earth back to the front
of the magnetosphere [13] (pp. 13-14).
The convection cycle in the magnetosphere described above is known as the Dungey
cycle. The cycle results in a convection of plasma across the polar cap towards the
night side, and back towards the day side. Opposites of the convection system is a
corresponding ionospheric current system. The two cells are the Hall currents, where
the equatorward part of each hall current cell is known as the auroral electrojets.

2.9.2
Ring current
The ring current is an electric current in the inner magnetosphere. It is a result
of particle drift, where particles of positive charge drift westward and particles of
negative charge drift eastward which results in a westward current. The current
induces a magnetic ﬁeld which is opposite the Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld [18] (p. 150).
An increase in the intensity of the ring current can be caused by an injection of
plasma related to reconnection in the magnetotail [18] (p. 132). It can also be
related to pressure from passing plasma [18] (p. 60). This increase of current inten-
sity can be measured on ground as a decrease of the horizontal component of the
magnetic ﬁeld on the Earth.
2.10
Geomagnetic substorms
A substorm is the sudden relaxation of the stretched arrangement of magnetic ﬁeld
lines in the magnetotail [18] (p. 60). This stretching and build up of excess magnetic
ﬂux in the magnetotail is a result of a continuously negative Bz for about an hour
or less [18] (p. 59). As mentioned earlier, the negative Bz will result in reconnection
on the day-side and the ﬁeld lines will move to the magnetotail. This process will
enlarges the polar cap, which is the area between the two outermost closed ﬁeld lines
of the Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld. Since less ﬁeld line are closed, the polar cap expands
southward. This expansion is known as the substorm growth phase.
The release of energy from the reconnection in the magnetotail is often seen as the
onset of the substorm. Part of the energy released in the reconnection is transported
towards the Earth and injected into the ionosphere where bright aurora will occur.
The reconnection in the magnetotail causes a current wedge on the night-side which
results in the ionospheric substorm electrojet. The substorm electrojet extends the
aurora westward, while the shrinking of the polar cap moves it northward. This
is called the expansion phase [13] (p. 22) [18] (p. 60). During the substorm both
the magnetospheric and ionospheric convection will increase [18] (p. 59), and the
disturbances in the magnetic ﬁeld will be measurable on the Earth at high latitudes.
Substorms on average occur a few times per day, but it depends on the solar wind
[18] (pp. 60- 61).

2.11
Geomagnetic storms
Geomagnetic storms are generally related to an interplanetary coronal mass ejection
(ICME) or a co-rotating interaction region (CIR).
An ICME originates on the solar surface where a reconnection of magnetic ﬁeld lines
eject plasma with an embedded magnetic ﬁeld. When a fast ICME passes through
the slower ambient solar wind it will create an interplanetary shock wave in front.
The region between the ICME and the shock front will be compressed, resulting in
a higher density and stronger magnetic ﬁeld than the ambient wind. If the ICME
is Earth-directed then it might cause a geomagnetic storm. Whether a geomagnetic
storm occurs depends on the direction of the magnetic ﬁeld of the compressed region
and the ICME. This type of geomagnetic storm lasts approximately a day [18](p.
61).
A CIR is caused by a stream of high speed solar wind behind a slow stream of solar
wind. A compression region will form between the two streams, where the density
and magnetic ﬁeld strength are increased. A geomagnetic storm can occur when the
CIR passes the Earth provided that Bz is negative. The storm will generally last
several days [18](p. 61).
Both types of geomagnetic storms can be detected globally by magnetic ground
stations. ICMEs are most common around solar maximum, while the high speed
streams originate from corona holes which are most common during solar minimum.
A corona hole is a region of open ﬁeld lines where the plasma escapes more easily. A
CIR might therefore return after 27 days, which is approximately the Sun’s rotation
period.

2.12.1
Dst
The disturbance storm time (Dst) index is measured in nano tesla (nT). It is a
measure of the ring current [17]. An increase in the westward drift will generally
cause a decrease of the magnetic ﬁeld measured on the Earth. So the strength of
the ring current is measured by how much it changes the horizontal component of
the magnetic ﬁeld measured at the ground. This disturbance is not the same in all
magnetic local time (MLT) zones, since it is generally more negative on the night
side. The Dst is therefore measured as an average from four near equatorial mag-
netometer stations at diﬀerent longitudes [18] (pp. 149-151).
The magnetometer stations may also measure contributions from other current sys-
tems, but the largest contributions comes from the ring current [18] (p. 150).
2.12.2
AE and PCN
Both the Auroral Electrojet (AE) index and the Polar Cap North (PCN) index are
geomagnetic indices. AE is in units of nT, and is based on data from a network of
groundbased magnetometer stations in the high latitudes. The index is a measure of
the strength of the auroral electrojets, however it has the limitation that the auroral
electrojets might move away from the stations during geomagnetically active times,
and it will therefore seem like they are weaker than they actually are. The AE index
is well correlated to the PCN index, especially during equinox and winter [20].
The PCN index is measured in mV
m , and is based on the data from one station placed
in Thule, which is generally inside the polar cap. The polar cap is smallest when
Bz is positive. The PCN index is a measure of the current that ﬂows above the
pole from midnight to noon MLT. The transpolar current can turn the PCN index
negative, but these values are not related to the auroral electrojets. Negative PCN
values mostly occur during local summer, since the pole is faced towards the Sun,
which makes the ionosphere more ionized [20]. The ionization means that the trans-
polar current occurs more easily. This current has not been discussed previously
since it only occurs when Bz is positive.

2.13.2
Natural Neighbor
The natural neighbor interpolation is a linear combination of the values of the neigh-
boring data points and their weight at the chosen point. The primary diﬀerence
between kriging and natural neighbor is how the weights are assigned. In ﬁgure 3
the method for computing the weights in the natural neighbor method is illustrated.
Figure 3: Illustration of how the voronoi diagram is used to determine the weights of the
natural neighbor interpolation. The illustration is borrowed from [31]
A voronoi diagram is computed with the data points. The diagram outlines which
points that are closest to each data point. The point of interpolation is then added
and a voronoi shape is made around it. All the data points that have a voronoi
shape which overlaps with the new voronoi shape will have a weight above zero.
The weights are computed as the fraction of the overlapping area to the full area of
the new voronoi shape. This makes natural neighbor a local method and an exact
interpolator, since the interpolation will only be based on the nearest points in all
directions. An eﬀect of this method is that the value of the interpolated point will
always be within the limits of the data, meaning that the interpolation will not
predict any troughs or ridges which are not represented in the data [32].

Method and Results

3.3
Map projection
The Earth is not a sphere or an ellipsoid, but has its own complex shape. It can
however be estimated as an ellipsoid or spheroid. Such an ellipsoid is called a refer-
ence ellipsoid. A reference ellipsoid and its placement relative to the Earths gravity
centre are together called a datum [1] (p. 13). A datum gives a common way of
describing locations on the Earth. However, previously the most common way to
measure the gravity centre of the Earth was to measure the plumb lines. The datum
could therefore be very good locally, but have big errors globally [1] (p. 13). Dif-
ferent datums were therefore used at diﬀerent locations. After launching satellites
into space it became necessary to have a global datum [1] (p. 14), and since there is
a vast amount of satellites in space, it became relatively simple to know the centre
of gravity of the Earth. The WGS-84 is a global datum that is used by the GPS
satellites, and is also called the EPSG-4326 coordinate system, which has the unit
degrees. This is a 3-dimensional coordinate system, and it can therefore not directly
be shown on a 2-dimensional map.
When mapping a 3-dimensional surface onto a 2-dimensional surface, like a paper
or a screen, projections are needed. A projection transforms the coordinates of the
3-dimensional surface onto a 2-dimensional one. There are multiple diﬀerent ways
to translate the coordinates, and they all have their strengths and weaknesses. It is
not possible to translate the coordinates without a certain degree of distortion.
Since EPSG-4326 is a geographic coordinate system and not a projected coordinate
system, the data will often be plotted under the assumption that the decimal degrees
are linear. This is almost the same as using the Plate Carr´ee projection, where the
longitudes are straight lines that do not converge towards the same point [25]. In
reality the distance between longitudes becomes smaller further away from Equator.
The Plate Carr´ee projection will therefore cause data points closer to the poles to
appear further apart than they are. This should however not be a large problem
in this project since a map is shown under the data points, and the map will be
distorted in the same way as the positions of the data points. furthermore, a grid
of longitude and latitude is overlaid on all the computed maps of Greenland in this
project.

3.6.1
ROTI timelines
A ROTI timeline is a plot with ROTI on the y-axis and time on the x-axis. The
ROTI is calculated from data between one speciﬁc station and one speciﬁc satellite.
It is calculated over a period of time where the satellite is visible from the station,
which means that there is both a spatial and temporal change, since the satellite
moves across the sky.
It is therefore important to be cautious when concluding
something from a ROTI timeline, but this does not mean that nothing can be con-
cluded from it. The timeline can be used as a general measure of the activity, and
it can also give indications of daily variations on geomagnetically quiet days (see
appendix A).

3.7
Spatial interpolation
This project aims at monitoring the scintillations in the ionosphere above Green-
land. This can be done by computing visual maps of the scintillation, but there are
not enough measuring stations to have data points everywhere above Greenland.
Spatial interpolation is therefore used to estimate the scintillation between the data
points. There are diﬀerent methods of spatial interpolation, but a common limita-
tion is that the interpolation becomes more uncertain with larger distances between
the data points. This is why the coverage was earlier investigated.
Five diﬀerent interpolation methods were tested in [6] (pp. 52-57). The methods
were inverse distance weighing, kriging, natural neighbor, spline, and trend inter-
polation. It was found that the methods which provided most details of small scale
variations were kriging and natural neighbor [6] (pp. 54-57). Kriging was identiﬁed
as the optimal method because the root mean square of the interpolation residuals
41

DTU
June 19, 2020
were a bit lower than for natural neighbor interpolation [6] (p. 55). It should be
mentioned that the study was made based on TEC data from the Iberian peninsula.
This introduces two primary diﬀerences bewteen the study in [6] and this project.
First, it means that the study was done in the mid latitudes, and the mid latitude
ionosphere does not contain the same phenomenons as the high latitude ionosphere.
Second it was done in a region where the GNSS stations are much more regularly
spaced than in Greenland. Nonetheless, it is assumed that these diﬀerences do not

3.8
Precise Point Positioning
Now that the scintillations have been investigated, it should also be examined how
they aﬀect the receivers. In this section Precise Point Positioning (PPP) results
are examined at the stations THU2, SISI, and QAQ2 for day 30 and 50. PPP is
a positioning method which is primarily based on the carrier phase measurements
[12] (page 13), it resembles the pseudorange method, but the errors are estimated or
modeled [12] (page 13). Therefore the satellite clock and orbit errors are provided
from external sources eg. the International GNSS Service (IGS), and the ionospheric
delay is removed with dual frequency signals. The troposphere error and the am-

biguity are modeled. It can take up to 30 minutes for the method to converge to
a decimeter precision, and it is therefore not likely that this method will be used
for navigation in Greenland. It is also far from all commonly used receivers that
are dual frequency receivers, and the ionospheric delay would for those receivers
have to be estimated by the model in the navigation message. PPP is therefore not
used to reﬂect the navigation errors, that regular GNSS users in Greenland would
encounter due to ionospheric scintillation, it is simply used to examine the eﬀects
on the receiver.

4
Discussion and Conclusion
This section discusses and perspectives the results and methods that have been pre-
sented in the project. In the end of the section the discussion will be wrapped up
by the conclusion.
4.1
ROTI timeline
The calculation of ROTI timelines was done in the beginning of the project in ﬁg-
ure 14 to provide validation of the ROTI computation method used in this project.
This means that the ROTI calculations were validated based on one satellite and
one receiver station, which is not even one of the stations that are used later for the
ROTI maps. This can be done because the method is the same for all satellites and
receiver stations. Furthermore, this validation method provides a very clear picture
of whether the ROTI values of this project are in accordance with the ROTI com-
putation method of a previously published study [6]. This is a more direct form of
validation than the maps from the Norwegian Mapping Authority that are used later.
Even though ROTI timelines have to be analyzed with care it seems that they can be
used to investigate a daily variation in the arctic scintillations. This can be seen on
24-hour ROTI timelines in appendix A. At geomagnetically quiet time the strongest
scintillations occur in the night time ionosphere. This diurnal variation is more clear
during geomagnetically quiet time since the day time ionosphere presents very low
ROTI values. The increase in scintillations during night time is probably caused by
plasma from the mid-latitude and equatorial regions that is transported northward
on the dayside, and further across the polar cap towards the night side. During the
transport across the polar cap the plasma is separated into smaller patches which
causes stronger scintillations [26]. This is relevant knowledge for GNSS users in
Greenland, since it indicates that scintillations will be worse at night during geo-
magnetically quiet times.
4.2
IPPs and Elevation
In the calculation of IPPs the ionosphere was modeled as a spherical shell at a spe-
ciﬁc height. In reality the ionosphere consists of multiple layers and has an extent
of around 1000 km. TEC calculations, which ROTI is based on, are therefore a
projection of the signal path through the ionosphere onto one surface at a speciﬁc
62
==PAGE:76==
Master’s thesis
DTU
June 19, 2020
altitude. In the high latitudes there are several ionospheric structures which can
cause GNSS scintillation, and these structures are not all at the same altitudes.
In this project the height of the ionospheric shell was set to 350 km in the IPP
calculations. This is a realistic altitude in the polar cap and cusp, but in the auroral
region scintillation generally occurs at lower altitudes [6] (p. 129). The consequence
of choosing an altitude which is not the true altitude, is that the data points might
be a bit displaced, which aﬀects the interpolation. It might be relevant to investigate
this error source in the future if a more precise map of the ionospheric scintillations
is needed. At this time it will not be considered a problem.
The elevation cut-oﬀangle is a trade oﬀbetween avoiding unnaturally high ROTI
values and having the highest possible number of data points. The correlation be-
tween ROTI and elevation in table 3 indicates that there is a connection between
low elevation angles and high ROTI values. However, this does not mean that all
high ROTI values are related to low elevation angles, and this is also why the corre-
lation on day 50 is very low, since most of the high values on this day are caused by
geomagnetic activity. Nevertheless, this does not preclude that some ROTI values
on day 50 can have been increased by low elevation angles.
It is noted that the correlations in table 3 are not high correlations, which is also not
expected since there can be high ROTI values which are not related to elevation,
even on geomagnetically quiet day. Nonetheless, table 3 does show a very stable
correlation on both day 30 and 50 with a notably higher correlation on day 30 than
on day 50. This would not have been the case if there was no connection between
elevation and ROTI.
A method for mitigating the eﬀect of the elevation angle on the ROTI values would
be to increase the elevation cut-oﬀangle. It was chosen not to do it in this project,
since it would decrease the number of data points which was already low in some
regions. However, if more data points were available then an increased elevation
cut-oﬀangle could be a solution.

All the GNET station receivers that were used in the ROTI maps are compatible
with both GPS and GLONASS, and the IPP coverage was therefore improved when

the GLONASS data was added. However, the coverage was mostly improved in the
areas where there were already some GPS IPPs. This indicates that a more equidis-

After the GLONASS data was added there were still areas on the eastern coast and

connection, and since the eastern part of Greenland is thinner populated, there are
also fewer stations with internet than on the west coast. This would of course also
be a challenge when deciding the locations of GISTM stations
4.4
Spatial interpolation methods
Theoretically kriging would be expected to be the best interpolation method, espe-
cially in areas with sub-optimal data coverage such as Greenland. This is because the
kriging method creates a statistical estimation that takes the data noise into consid-
eration, and also provides a variance grid. Other methods such as natural neighbor
are deterministic and do not consider data noise. Even though the kriging method
is a theoretically better method for this purpose, the natural neighbor method did
produce the same overall structures and reasonable smoothing in scintillation-maps
of day 50, even though there were some chimneys-like structures based on single
64
==PAGE:78==
Master’s thesis
DTU
June 19, 2020
data points. Natural neighbor interpolation could be chosen because the computa-
tion time is generally shorter than for kriging, and no parameters need to be chosen.
However, the kriging method did produce smoother interpolations, while still re-
ﬂecting the overall variations in the data on day 50, and it is a clear advantage to
know the variance of the estimates.
Nonetheless kriging was not optimal for day 30 where the ROTI values were low.
It might therefore be best to use natural neighbor interpolation at geomagnetically
quiet times. It could also be argued that GNSS users will mostly be interested in
scintillation maps at geomagnetically active times, and that the maps should there-
fore only be produced at these times. No matter which one of the two options are
chosen it would require an activity parameter and a threshold. The parameter would
need to be available in near real-time, and it would therefore make sense to use the
GISTMs as they can provide scintillation indices in near real-time. The problem is
that there are only two GISTM stations in Greenland right now, but they are fortu-
nately placed in diﬀerent parts of Greenland, which means that they cover diﬀerent
parts of the ionosphere. A combination of multiple parameters is also a possibility.
The scintillation data could be complimented by geomagnetic data such as the PCN
index which is available in near real-time [41]. Another possibility is to include solar
wind data which could also provide an opportunity of forecasting the geomagnetic
activity.
4.5
PPP
Table 4 showed that the diﬀerence between the mean position residuals on day 30
and 50 are on millimeter scale or even smaller at all three stations. Part of the
reason for this is probably that the GNSS receivers are for geodetic use. Geode-
tic GNSS receivers needs to be of high quality since the geodynamic movements
that they are measuring are on the mm level. These high quality dual-frequency
receivers are therefore not comparable to the receivers that will generally be used for
navigation in Greenland. Because of this, the GNET receivers are not optimal for
determining the impact of geomagnetic activity and scintillation on positioning with
regular GNSS receivers. The results may however be useful to researchers who are
using the GNET stations for geodetic purposes, since it provides knowledge about
the magnitude of change in position residuals that should be expected during geo-
magnetically active times.
It is likely that the PPP data processing algorithms has corrected for position errors,
65

June 19, 2020
since part of the PPP method is to estimate and model the errors. The clock errors,
pseudorange residuals, and cycle slips were therefore examined. The clock errors in
the receivers at THU2 and QAQ2 are clearly diﬀerent on day 30 and day 50. The
diﬀerence between the clock errors at THU2 is likely related to a drift in the receiver
clock during the 20 day period from day 30 to day 50, and it can therefore not be
concluded that this rather large diﬀerence is related to ionospheric scintillations. At
QAQ2 the clock seems to reset multiple times during day 50, and this might indicate
that the clock error estimate has been aﬀected by the increased ionospheric activ-
ity. The receiver at SISI is probably newer than at the two other stations, which
explains why the clock errors at SISI are much lower. Even though the estimated
clock errors for THU2 and SISI did not reveal much about the ionospheric scintilla-
tions eﬀect on the receiver, it is still an important error to estimate precisely since
it can introduce large errors. The receiver clocks that were investigated seems to be
of varying quality, and it could be argued that the data from the stations will be
more comparable if receivers of the same quality are used. This could be obtained
by installing a network of GISTM stations.
The pseudorange residual is the diﬀerence between the measured pseudorange and
the estimated geometric range. In this case a dual frequency receiver is used and
therefore the part of the pseudorange residuals that is due to the ionosphere will
under normal conditions be centimeter - decimeter level after the ionosphere has
been modeled [1] (p. 48). The diﬀerence in pseudorange residuals from day 30 to
day 50 at THU2 was approximately ± one meter. The other parameters which can
aﬀect the pseudorange residuals are satellite clock errors, ephemeride errors, the
troposphere, multipath and correlation errors in the receiver [1]. When using PPP
the satellite clock error and ephemeride errors are modeled and provided by IGS so
these are assumed very precise. There is also no reason to assume that the multipath
error should have increased, since the stations are stationary. The tropospheric er-
rors might be larger due to the geomagnetic storm, and the correlation error might
also be aﬀected by scintillations. This concludes that the increased pseudorange
residuals at THU2 on day 50 are probably caused by the increased geomagnetic
activity. The reason why this is only observed at THU2 might be that THU2 is
situated in the polar cap. It is therefore subject to other ionospheric processes than
the other two stations.
It is however clear that all three receivers experience a signiﬁcant increase in cycle
slips on day 50 relative to day 30 2014. This is probably a direct result of phase scin-
tillation on the signals in the ionosphere. There is a larger number of cycle slips on

GLONASS signals than on GPS signal at THU2 and SISI. This might be because

can make them more vulnerable to scintillation.
It can therefore be concluded that even though the PPP processed data from the
GNET receivers does not exhibit large position errors, the geomagnetic activity does
still aﬀect the receivers.
4.6
Future Work
For a real time monitoring of the ionosphere it would be best to have GISTMs
in Greenland. This is because GISTMs calculate scintillation indices locally. This
makes the data ﬁles smaller which is an advantage in near real-time monitoring, es-
pecially in Greenland where the internet connection can be very limited, particularly
on the east coast. There are as mentioned above two PolaRx5S receivers in Green-
land. When there are only few receivers it is best if they can generate the ROTI
index, since it can then be supplemented with GNET data. The PolaRx5S receivers
provide both σφ and S4 indices in ionospheric scintillation monitoring (ismr) data
ﬁles, but they do not calculate ROTI [39] (pp. 32-33). ROTI is well correlated to
σφ, but not linearly [6] (p. 76) and it can therefore be challenging to compare the
two indices. The TEC values in the ismr data ﬁles can not be used for calculating
ROTI, since there is only one TEC value every 15 seconds.
One possible solution is to develop a program which can compute the ROTI values
locally and then transmit the ROTI data either separately or in the ismr ﬁle [23].
Another solution is to install more GISTMs along the coast of Greenland, since the
GNET data would then not be needed as a supplement. Hence the σΦ index could
be used exclusively.
The PolaRXS receivers are used in both Canada and Greenland. Installing the new
PolaRx5S receiver might therefore make the comparison of data easier, and permit
better cooperation for monitoring the high latitude ionosphere in the future.

It was mentioned earlier in the discussion that solar wind parameters might be use-

interesting ways of using solar wind parameters in future work. It is described in [6]

there will be strongest scintillations. This could potentially improve predictions of
the spatial distribution of scintillations in the future.
4.7
Conclusion
It is possible to monitor the ionosphere above Greenland with data from the GNET
stations. However, if this monitoring is going to be in near real-time, then it would be
better to install GISTMs in Greenland. If enough GISTMs are placed in Greenland,
then the system can be independent of the GNET stations, and other scintillation
indices than ROTI can be used. If new stations are placed in Greenland, then it
would be advised to increase the number of stations on the east coast with internet
connection, since this will insure a better IPP coverage, and thereby a lower stan-
dard deviation of the kriging estimate. The GISTMs will also increase the coverage
by being compatible with Galileo. Furthermore, it will make the data from diﬀerent
stations more comparable if the receivers are of the same age and quality.
The correlation between ROTI and elevation was investigated. It was found that
the correlation was stable during both day 30 and 50, but it was notably higher
during day 30. This indicates that some higher ROTI values are caused by a low
elevation angle of the satellite. However, a better coverage might enable an increase
in the elevation cut-oﬀangle, which will hopefully counter this eﬀect.
The spatial interpolation with ordinary kriging and natural neighbor both gave
good result for geomagnetically active times. However, the kriging interpolation
was smoother than the natural neighbor, while still reﬂecting the variability of the
data. During geomagnetically quiet times the natural neighbor interpolations was
preferred since it honored the values of the data points more than the ordinary
kriging interpolation. It is therefore assumed that there is a geomagnetic threshold
where the signal to noise ratio becomes so small that kriging is no longer the best so-
lution. In a future study it can be investigated which parameter a threshold should
be based on, and what the optimal threshold is. It is possible that a combination of
parameters is best, but the key criteria is that the parameters are available in near
real-time.
