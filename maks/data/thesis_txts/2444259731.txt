Abstract
Low- and middle-income countries lack of appropriate medical devices to operate as
an effective health-care system, which means these countries rely heavily on dona-
tions from high-income countries. This presents another shortcoming, since 40% of
the donated devices are nonfunctional due to the inadequate technologies and the
shortage of skilled health care workers to operate and maintain the received products
[1], [2].
Biosensors are characterized as portable, rapid, and relatively simple to operate and
presents itself as a suitable option to address this issue. Nonetheless, inkjet-printing
can be seen as an applicable technology to fabricate a precise and low-cost biosensor
appropriate in resource-poor settings [2]–[5].
This study aims to examine the feasibility of utilizing reduced Graphene Oxide (rGO)
and PEDOT:PSS in an electrochemical enzymatic glucose sensor based on the enzyme,
glucose oxidase (GOx), for diagnosis of Diabetes Mellitus. The materials are aimed
to be applicable for commercially available inkjet-printed technology.
The conductivity and redox properties of rGO and PEDOT:PSS were confirmed
by cyclic voltammetry, however rGO demonstrated a significantly larger conduc-
tivity. Three biosensor prototypes were developed on a gold electrode, where the
gold/rGO/GOx reflected the largest current response followed closely by the
gold/rGO/[PEDOT:PSS(x10)-GOx] sensor. Additionally, the gold/[PEDOT:PSS(x10)-
GOx] sensor reflected the lowest current response.
The reported findings indicate that rGO and PEDOT:PSS are suitable for immobiliz-
ing GOx, and rGO contributes to enhance the sensor performance. Furthermore, the
high solubility of PEDOT:PSS demonstrated to be inkjet printable, whereas a stable
dispersion of rGO still needs to be further investigated.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Medical devices are essential for effective diagnosis, monitoring, treatment, and pre-
vention of diseases.
A broad spectrum of laboratory instruments and measuring
techniques are well established and widely used in modern medical diagnostics, but
the functionality usually requires specific infrastructure and qualified personnel to
operate correctly [6].
Many Low- and middle-income countries (LMIC) face issues of accessibility of such
medical devices and rely strongly on donations from High-income countries (HIC).
Despite LMIC receiving generous medical device donations still presents another
shortcoming. Specifically, 40% of the donated devices are nonfunctional due to the
inadequate technologies that are not suitable in resource-poor settings [1], [2]. Ad-
ditionally, LMIC suffer from a shortage of skilled health care workers and lack the
necessary personnel to operate and maintain the donated products. The high depen-
dence on HIC due to the lack of consumables and the limited access to spare parts
lead to an unsustainable development in LMIC [1], [2].
Point-Of-Care (POC) devices are suitable candidates to partially address this issue
by providing relatively low cost, and simple operation, and performance comparable
with the traditional diagnostics methods. POC devices usually employ biosensors,
which have become an evolving diagnostic tool due to the portable properties and the
rapid, accurate and straightforward detection suitable in low-resource settings [2]–[4].
However, the standard fabrication methods of biosensors are particularly high in cost
due to the operating facilities such as cleanrooms and the large floor space required
for the equipment [7]. As an alternative, printing technology has become extremely
popular due to its ease of use and low-cost equipment.
Over the past years inkjet printed technology has extended its application into the
fabrication of biosensors [5]. The non-contact dispensing technology offers a low-cost,
simple and precise technique to deposit picoliter droplets (10-20 pL) onto a substrate
[5], [7]–[9]. The droplets are generated through one of two mechanisms. In piezoelec-
tric printing, a crystalline material undergoes mechanical stress, creating a pressure
that ejects the droplet through the nozzle [5], [9].
In thermal printing a heating
element (∼300◦C) creates a pressure of vapor bubbles, resulting in droplet ejection
[9]–[11].

These low-cost and simple properties are comparable with the screen printed technol-
ogy, which employs a paste forced through the pores of a mesh screen. Currently,
different pastes for biosensor fabrication are commercial available as electronic and
biological pastes, with a resolution as high as 390 dots per inch (dpi), and a dot
diameter around 40-70 µA [9]. However, the high viscosity of the paste results in
limitations in the resolution when small precise layouts are printed [9], [11], [12].
In inkjet printing the low viscosity of the ink composition results in a resolution higher
than 1200 dpi, with a mean dot diameter in micrometer range (15-40 µm) [9]. The
digital technique of inkjet printing allows to design and change the printed pattern
from a software, which is in contrast to screen printing that requires stencils mask
to facilitate the printing patterning [12]. Furthermore, a minimum amount of ink is
required to perform the printing process resulting in minimal material waste, com-
pared to screen printing where a lager consumption of material are needed to design
the printed pattern [5], [7], [10], [12], [13].
However, the development of ink materials are considered to be a challenge due to
the specific rheological requirements in terms of viscosity and surface tension. The
ink must be well-dispersed to avoid clogging and particle aggregation in the nozzle
of the printer [12]. Stabilizing agents such as surfactants are often used to fulfill the
rheological requirements of the ink composition, but too much surfactant can dena-
ture the biological material [5].
The relatively new application of inkjet printed technology in biosensor fabrication,
results in a limited amount of commercial available inks and these are often expensive
to purchase [5], [7].
Consequently, inkjet printing is often used in combination with other printing meth-
ods, such as screen printing, in order to utilize the advantages of both technologies
[5], [12].
Today, glucose biosensors are the most studied biosensors, as they are widely used
for blood glucose monitoring for diagnosis of Diabetes Mellitus [11], [14]. Diabetes
Mellitus is a chronic disease, which corresponds to 8.5% of the world’s population
and continues to be a progressing global disease, particularly in LMIC [15], [16].
As a result, printed technology can be employed to fabricate an accessible and af-
fordable glucose sensor that are suitable in low-resource settings and can easily be
manufactured in the LMIC, thus contributing to a sustainable development [16].
Accordingly, enzymatic inkjet printed glucose biosensors are reviewed to obtain knowl-
edge about the current employed materials in inkjet printing.

1.1
Literature review
The first enzymatic inkjet printed glucose sensor was proposed in 2005 [9].
The
thermal inkjet printed technology was utilized to print the conductive polymer, PE-
DOT:PSS, on an Indium-tin-oxide (ITO) coated glass substrate. Consequently, the
biological material, Glucose oxidase (GOx), was inkjet printed [9]. The study demon-
strated an enhanced sensitivity of the biosensor when the conductive polymer, PE-
DOT:PSS, was employed, compared to when GOx was deposited directly onto the
ITO-coated glass surface [9]. The sensitivity was 6.43 µAmM−1cm−2, which is lower
compared to the literature for GOx biosensors, where the sensitivity is often com-
prised between 2 and 5 mAmM−1cm−2 [17]. According to Piro et al. the optimum
film thickness of the polymer was found to be 10-20 nm [17]. However, the thickness
of the inkjet printed PEDOT:PSS/GOx was 230 nm, which might explain the lower
electrical response since an increase in the film thickness limits the diffusion of both
the mediator and the substrate [9]. The setup of the fabricated biosensor is illustrated
in Fig. 1.1(a).
In 2008, Wang et al. presented a study where GOx was inkjet printed using a piezoelec-
tric printer, onto a screen-printed carbon electrodes. Surfactant was added to ensure a
more uniform deposit of the GOx solution [10]. However, the biosensor demonstrated
a quite lower electrical response than the previous reported PEDOT:PSS/GOx biosen-
sor [9]. The poor performance was due to the poor electrical conductivity of the so-
lution, which can be improved by adding nanoparticles or immobilizing components
such as conductive polymers [10].
In 2011, a glucose sensor was proposed by Yun et al., which is comparable with
the biosensor reported in [9]. However, GOx was inkjet printed with the enzyme,
Horseradish Peroxidase (HRP) by piezoelectric printing on a ITO coated Polyethy-
lene Terephthalate (PET) film. The ink consisted of a mixture of PEDOT:PSS, GOx
and HRP [11].
The conductive polymer, PEDOT:PSS demonstrated excellent re-
dox properties and was able to immobilize the enzymes by entrapment [11]. The
sensitivity increased by 1.5 times when PEDOT:PSS was present and the electrical
conductivity was three times larger compared to the proposed biosensor in [9]. As
a result, the study suggested that the conductive polymer facilitates the electrode
transfer between the enzymes and the electrode surface. The sensitivity was obtained
to be 20.5 µAmM−1cm−2 and the Limit of Detection (LOD) was calculated to be 270
µM with a Signal-to-Noise ratio (SNR) ratio of ∼3.
The thickness of the printed PEDOT:PSS/GOx/HRP was around 2.0 µm, which is
thicker than the optimal thickness determined in [17]. However, the biosensor still
offers a larger electrical response. This might be due to the choice of the piezeoelec-
tric printer, since the thermal printer employed in [9] can cause denaturation of the
biological materials due to heating element [5].
On the other hand, one study showed that the shear stress occurring in the piezoelec-

can play a larger role in the sensor performance rather than the choice of the printing

Inkjet printing has been employed to print a gold and silver electrodes on a recy-

Gold electrodes were chosen due to its high conductivity, high catalytic activity, ex-

lectivity to glucose where the LOD was calculated to be 0.1 mM. The linear range
was small, but still covering the range of human blood glucose concentration [13].
Another interesting study presented by Wu et al., utilized PDMS as the substrate
due to its remarkable biocompatibility and ease of fabrication [19]. The study demon-
strated that the surface wettability of PDMS can be changed from hydrophobic to
hydrophilic by modifying the PDMS. As a result, adhesion to metals increased, where
silver electrodes were inkjet printed followed by a deposition of GOx via electrochem-
ical polymerization [19]. The glucose biosensor is illustrated in Fig. 1.1(c).
In 2016, the same previous group proposed a glucose biosensor, where a commercial
modified graphene, consisting of pieces of Reduced Graphene Oxide (rGO), was inkjet
printed onto a gold electrode [20]. Graphene showed to improve the electroactivity
and contributed to a more uniform distribution of the electrochemical active sites,
thereby enabling the detection of glucose at low levels. Consequently, Gold Nanopar-
ticles (AuNP)s were electrodeposited into the graphene layer to improve the electron
transfer rate between the active site of the enzyme and the electrode [19]. GOx was
immobilized via electropolymerization onto the surface of the graphene layer. The
electrodes were fabricated on a flexible Polyimide (PI) substrate via a flexible printed
circuit board process [19]. The study demonstrates that the presence of graphene
improves the detection of weak glucose signals significantly. The LOD was calculated
to be 0.02 mM with a SNR ∼3. The sensitivity of the biosensor increased by four
times when AuNPs were deposited onto the graphene layer. This is due to the cat-
alytic effect of AuNP. The sensitivity of the biosensor was 667 nAmM−1cm−2 [20].
A schematic of the fabricated biosensor is presented in Fig. 1.1(d).
Song et al. and Li et al. proposed a glucose sensor where PET was used as the
substrate [21], [22].
In the latter study, the conductive layer consisted of inkjet printed Carbon Nanotube
(CNT)s, whereas GOx and Platinum Nanoparticles (PtNP) were deposited by drop-

1.2
Project goals
Based on the literature review the conductive materials, rGO and PEDOT:PSS, were
chosen to be further investigated.
The objective of this study was to examine the feasibility of utilizing rGO and PE-
DOT:PSS as materials in the fabrication of an enzymatic electrochemical biosensor
for glucose detection.
The materials are aimed to be applicable for commercially
available inkjet-printed technology.
The aims of this study were to:
• Evaluate and test the rGO material
• Evaluate and test the PEDOT:PSS material
• Gain understanding for the surface functionalization techniques to modify the
electrode surface with GOx
• Fabricate different electrochemical glucose biosensors
• Perform measurements and evaluate the electrochemical glucose biosensors

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter a brief introduction of electrochemical biosensors are given followed
by a description of the employed enzyme, GOx.
Consequently, the electrochemi-
cal processes and techniques employed to obtain a measurable signal are described.
Lastly, the employed materials, rGO and PEDOT:PSS are described in terms of their
properties and structure.
2.1
Electrochemical enzymatic biosensor
The development of biosensors consist of a multidisciplinary research spanning chem-
istry, physics, biology, electronics and software engineering [4]. As a result, based on
the transducing element, the biosensors are divided into five classes: electrochemical,
optical, thermometric, piezoelectric and magnetic [14].
In 1962 Clark and Lyons invented the first biosensor, which was an electrochemical
biosensor based on the detection of glucose in human blood by utilizing the enzyme,
GOx [3], [25]. Since then, the development of electrochemical biosensors have evolved
rapidly where the majority of the glucose sensors belongs to the electrochemical class,
due to the low cost, simplicity, improved sensitivity and reproducibility [3], [14].
A biosensor is comprised of: a) bioreceptors, such as enzyme, nucleic acids and an-
tibodies, that specifically binds to the analyte; b) the transducing element which
converts the bioreceptor-analyte interaction to an electrical measurable signal; c) a
signal processor that converts the signal into a readable form [3], [14].
A typical
design of a biosensor is depicted in Fig. 2.1.
The signal generation from the interaction between the bioreceptor and the analyte

2.3.2
The electrical double layer
The non-faradic current is due to the electrical double layer, which exists as an uni-
form layer of charged particles, or oriented dipoles, in each material interface when
an electrode is immersed into an electrolyte solution.
The electrical double layer consist of an inner layer, namely Inner Helmholtz Plane
(IHP) and an outer layer, namely Outer Helmholtz Plane (OHP), which together is
called the compact layer [32]. A schematic representation of the electrical double
layer is shown in Fig. 2.6.
The IHP layer is closest to the electrode surface and is composed of solvents and ions
oppositely charged from the electrode, which are not fully solvated, but specifically
adsorbed to the electrode surface [34].
The OHP layer consist of solvated ions, which are non-specifically adsorbed and at-
tracted to the surface by long-range coulombic forces [32]. The size of the OHP is
defined from the electrode surface to the center of the oppositely solvated charged
ion.
Beyond the OHP, scattered ions extending from the OHP to the bulk solution exist,
called the diffuse layer [32].
The compact layer together with the diffuse layer is
called the electrical double layer and occurs over a distance of approximately 1 nm
[35]. Usually the magnitude of the diffusion layer is several orders of magnitude larger

For an ideal capacitor, the charge is directly proportional to the potential difference

distribution of the ions, which is influenced by random thermal motion. [32]

2.3.3
Electron transfer controlled reactions
The current of an electrochemical experiment is also controlled by the rate of electron
transfer. The structure of an electrode consist a conduction band, where electrons
move freely [35]. The energy level in the band can be filled up to an energy maxi-
mum, called the Fermi level. The Fermi level is achieved by applying a potential that
provokes the transfer of an electron from the conductive band of the electrode to the
molecular orbital of the reactant (for a reduction, from the conduction band into an
unoccupied orbital in O) [32].
When the Fermi level of the electrode is higher than the Lowest Unoccupied Molecular
Orbital (LUMO) of the reactant, the electrode is able to transfer an electron to the
reactant (reduction), and the reaction becomes thermodynamically favourable [35].
This process is also illustrated in Fig. 2.7, where the reaction is thermodynamically
favourable on the right, but thermodynamically unfavourable on the left.

unfavourable reaction; (right) a thermodynamically favourable reaction [35].
However, in many circumstances the rate of mass transport to the electrode dominates
the overall reaction rather than the kinetics of the electron transfer [35].

Graphene was discovered in 2004 by Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov, using

The structure of graphene is characterized as a two-dimensional material arranged in

2.6.2
PEDOT:PSS
In 1977 the conductive polyacetylene was discovered and since then studies of con-
ductive polymers have evolved into multiple fields [48]. Conductive polymers have
attracted considerable attention in electrochemical biosensor applications due to the
shared properties of both plastics and metals or semiconductors [49], [50]. Further-
more, conductive polymers are known to be biocompatible and suitable for function-
alization of bioreceptors. Electrochemical entrapment and physical adsorption into
the conductive polymer and attachment to the conductive polymer by affinity inter-
actions or covalent bounds are the most common immobilization methods [50].
Among the various conductive polymers, PEDOT:PSS has received a lot of attention
mainly due to its high water solubility, but also the low cost scalability, ease of prepa-
ration, flexibility, high charge mobility and high thermal stability. [24], [49], [51], [52].
The structure of PEDOT:PSS consist of a Three-Dimensional (3D) network composed
of positively charged PEDOT located every three to five monomeric units along a
polymeric backbone, and negatively charged Poly(4-styrenesulfonate) (PSS) chains.
The interaction between PEDOT and PSS occurs through electrostatic ionic forces
[53]. The structure of PEDOT:PSS is depicted in Fig. 2.12.
Figure 2.12: The structure of PEDOT:PSS. Modified from [54].
PEDOT:PSS has a high electron affinity and redox activity, resulting in the ability to
reversibly switch between the positively charged conductive state and the insulating
state when immersed into an electrolyte solution [32], [50].
The positively charged PEDOT work as a charge carrier for conduction whereas the
PSS serves to maintain the electrical neutrality of the polymer backbone and provides
the high solubility. Furthermore, the redox reaction occurs over a number of polymer
groups rather than at a specific center.

The cyclic voltammogram of the rGO film corresponds to the shape described in Sec.

In the reverse scan the [Fe(CN)6]3−molecules, accumulated near the surface, are re-

The ferri/ferro redox couple contributes to one electron-transfer between the media-
tor solution and the electrode surface.

controlled reaction with no adsorbed molecules on the electrode surface, which is in
accordance to Randless-Secvick described in Sec. 2.4.1 [32].

Randless-Sevick 2.12. A slow SR allows the diffusion layer to cover a larger area.
As a result, the diffusion to the electrode surface becomes thicker and the reactants
becomes insufficient to support the Nernst Eq. 2.7, resulting in a lower current re-
sponse. Conversely, a faster SR results in a larger current response. This behavior
is in accordance to Fick’s law described in Eq 2.10. [33]. The linear relationship was
also obtained from the cathodic peak currents.
The linear relationship between the peak current and the square root of the SR in Fig.
4.7b implies that Fc-COOH redox reaction are controlled by a freely linear diffusion
rather than adsorption [38], [64], [65].

Discussion
The common characteristics of the obtained cyclic voltammograms of the three sen-
sors in Fig. 4.10, 4.11 and 4.12 demonstrates the expected electrochemical behaviour
in the absence and presence of glucose.
In the absence of glucose the redox couple undergoes a reaction, where the oxida-
tion peak corresponds to the accumulation of Fc-COOH+ near the electrode surface,
whereas the reduction peak resulted in the depletion of Fc-COOH+ molecules.
When glucose is introduced into the system it reacts with FAD in the active site of
GOx by oxidizing glucose into glucolactone and simultaneously reducing FAD into
FADH2. Consequently, FADH2 is reoxidized to FAD by Fc-COOH+, which in return
is converted to the reduced form of Fc-COOH.
The absence of the cathodic peak is caused by the reoxidization of the reduced form
of Fc-COOH to Fc-COOH+ occurring at the electrode surface. As a result, the anodic
peak decays or disappears when glucose was present [68]–[70]. These reactions are
in agreement with Eq. 2.3-2.5 in Sec. 2.2, where Mox and Mred are replaced with
Fc-COOH+ and Fc-COOH, respectively. The measured current is proportional to the
amount of glucose oxidized by the GOx, which explains the increase in the anodic
==PAGE:68==
56

peaks upon an increasing addition of glucose.
Furthermore, two characteristics are observed in the cyclic voltammogram for sensor
2 in Fig. 4.11. Firstly, for high glucose concentrations (7.5-15 mM) the steady state
current after the oxidation peak is suggested to be due to the reaction rate, which is
only limited by the rate of the catalytic reaction at the electrode surface [3]. In other
words, steady state at a high potential is sufficient to keep the active site of GOx to
remain fully oxidized. The obtained results are in accordance with [3], [68], [70], [71].
Secondly, the decreasing current after the oxidation peak at low glucose concentra-
tions (2.5 mM and 5 mM) is proposed to be due to the enzyme consuming all the
glucose molecules. In other words, the redox reactions in Eq. 2.3-2.5 occur until all
glucose molecules are consumed by the enzyme and afterwards the current decreases.
The following behaviour is in consistent with [66].
Furthermore, the decrease in the hysteresis of the catalytic wave upon addition of
glucose for sensor 2 and sensor 3 demonstrates a firmly entrapped GOx in the PE-
DOT:PSS network, which is in well agreement with [64], [69], [70].
For sensor 1 and sensor 3 the anodic current increases until the reverse scan occurs in
Fig. 4.10 and 4.12. This is believed to be caused by a heterogeneous electron transfer
caused by unknown molecules being oxidized on the surface due to the presence of
rGO. As explained in Sec. 4.3.1 the quasi-reversible reaction reaction of the gold/rGO
electrode in Fig. ?? was suggested to be due to the residual oxygen-containing groups,
defects and possibly adsorbed impurities on the rGO film [72]. Perhaps, the residual
redox active species on the rGO surface contributes to the increasing anodic current.

According to Wisitsoraat et. al., the high conductivity of rGO can be used to reduce
the pathway of the electrons, since it plays a significant role in facilitating the electron
exchange. The generated electrons will then travel via the shortest resistance path
through the Two-Dimensional (2D) rGO structure. Without rGO electrons will have
to travel through the PEDOT:PSS medium [24].
For sensor 1 the immobilization of GOx into the rGO film was achieved in a single
step without any cross linking agents. This is proposed to be attributed by physical
adsorption through non-covalent binding. According to [42], the remaining functional
groups in the rGO structure can be utilized as a scaffold to immobilize GOx [42]. Thus,
the non-covalent binding make use of strong hydrogen bonding between the amine
group in the GOx and the oxygen-containing groups in rGO, such as carboxylic acid
[42], [65].
The structure of PEDOT:PSS indicated to be suitable for enzyme immobilization due
to its network structure depicted in Fig. 2.12 [25], [73], [74]. The immobilization of
GOx for sensor 2 and sensor 3 was encapsulated within the PEDOT:PSS structure by
entrapment. However, the sensors containing PEDOT:PSS demonstrated the lowest
current response according to the bar chart in Fig. 4.19.
This was in contradiction with the studies presented in Sec. 1.1, which reported an
increase in conductivity when PEDOT:PSS was employed in the surface modification
[11], [69], [75].
The proposed reason could be due to the large amount of PSS in the commercial PE-
DOT:PSS (1:6) AI4083 solution. The PSS is an insulating polymer but contributes
to the high solubility, as explained in Sec. 2.6.2.
Park et al. reported an electrochemical study on the commercial PEDOT:PSS (1:6)
AI4083 film, which was compared to other commercial available PEDOT:PSS (1:2.5)
films. The study reported the lowest electrical conductivity for PEDOT:PSS AI4083
[51].
Another paper by Worfolk et al., pre-treated PEDOT:PSS (1:6) AI4083 with methanol
by spin casting. As a result, the segregation between PEDOT and PSS domains in-
creased and the PSS domains were filtered out.
The reduction of PSS proved to
increase the conductivity of the PEDOT:PSS films and the ratio 1:1 was obtained
[76].
This implies that the commercial PEDOT:PSS has a considerable high resistance (in
mega ohms range) due to the large amount of PSS. As a result, the electron transfer
will be slower, resulting in a lower current response.

Discussion
The highest enzymatic affinity to glucose is obtained by sensor 2, due to the lowest
Km=3.81 mM. The Km value demonstrates the substrate concentration at 1/2 Vmax.
As a result, the low Km value results in a higher affinity of glucose to GOx at a faster
reaction velocity [24].
Beyond a particular glucose concentration all active sites of the enzyme becomes sat-
urated. Thus, Vmax has been reached and is reflected by the asymptotic behavior for
sensor 2. This saturation state is indicated by a decrease in the current response at
higher glucose concentrations in CV and CA. The amperometric response for sensor 2
in Fig. 4.15 demonstrates the narrowest linear range, since the current was decreasing
at a glucose concentration larger than 7.5 mM. As a result, the Vmax is lowest for
sensor 2 in comparison with sensor 1 and sensor 3.
A possible explanation on the narrow linear range of sensor 2 can be due to the change
in the local pH value at the electrode surface. The optimum pH of GOx is 5.5, but
has a while broad activity range from pH 4 to 7. The pH of PEDOT:PSS is 5 but
during the catalytic reaction the pH decreases further due to the two protons that are
produced as a byproduct during the glucose oxidation [70], [77]. Since the enzyme
kinetics are known to be pH dependent the larger concentrations of glucose (< 7.5
mM) decreases the pH at the electrode surface, resulting in a narrow linear range and
slower reaction velocity.
However, the wider linear range of sensor 3 is caused by the rGO, which contributes
to an enhanced conductivity.
On the other hand, sensor 1 and sensor 3 reflects a linear range from 0 mM to 15 mM.
However, according to Fig. 4.20 the reaction velocities are still increasing and the
Vmax have not yet been established when the concentration of glucose was 15 mM.
This indicates a possibility for a larger linear range [24]. A larger linear range was not
examined, since the obtained linear range is sufficient with the glucose concentration
in the human blood (2-10 mM), hence adequate for diabetes applications [74].

sensitivity and LOD compared to the three obtained prototypes. Additionally, the
reproducibility of each sensor was calculated to be very large according to the RSD
values. This indicates that further modifications and improvements of the prototypes

Conclusion
During this project three electrochemical biosensor prototypes were developed, where
rGO, PEDOT:PSS and GOx was employed to detect glucose.
The rGO films were successfully obtained by chemical reduction, which was confirmed
by the removal of the oxygen-containing groups and the increase in conductivity by
Raman spectroscopy and CV, respectively. However, the composition of rGO remains
not fully understood due to the different C/O ratio, which depends on the reduction
method and the degree of reduction. Furthermore, rGO is known to resemble pristine
graphene, however, residual oxygen-containing groups and defective sheets of sp2 car-
bon network still remains to some extent after the reduction. As a result, the defects
and disorder was utilized to immobilize the GOx by non-covalent binding.
According to cyclic voltammetry, PEDOT:PSS(x10) demonstrated a lower conductiv-
ity compared to rGO. This was suggested to be due to the 6 times larger insulating
PSS compared to the conductive PEDOT. On the other hand, PSS contributes to
the high solubility which are favorable for ink printed purposes. Furthermore, the
network structure of PEDOT:PSS was employed to successfully functionalize GOx
by entrapment.
Altogether, the electrochemical measurements confirmed the redox activity of both
materials and can thus be employed as electroactive conductors in biosensor applica-
tions.
Three different electrochemical biosensor prototypes were developed, which all demon-
strated a stable and fast response to glucose.
The largest current response was obtained by the gold/rGO/GOx sensor followed
closely by the gold/rGO/[PEDOT:PSS(x10)-GOx] sensor with a linear range span-
ning from 0 to 15 mM. On the other hand, the lowest current response was reflected by
the gold/[PEDOT:PSS(x10)-GOx] sensor, which also had the narrowest linear range
spanning from 0 to 7.5 mM. Since the glucose concentration in human blood spans
from 0 mM to 10 mM the latter sensor are not sufficient for diabetes applications.
Based on the three sensors rGO contributes to enhance the sensor performance. On
the other hand, PEDOT:PSS needs to be pre-treated to reduce the content of the
insulating PSS in order to increase the conductivity.

technology. The highly soluble PEDOT:PSS was printed with a commercial printer.
However, a stable dispersion of rGO still needs to be further investigated. As an
alternative, the commercial available carbon ink was printed on a paper substrate.
Several challenges were encountered during inkjet printing.
The aqueous solution
had to meet specific rheological requirements to avoid leaking of the PEDOT:PSS
material and nozzle clogging of the carbon ink.
Altogether, the reported findings indicates that inkjet printing could be a feasible
technology for an easy and appropriate way to develop glucose biosensors for LMIC,
but further modifications are still needed.

CHAPTER 6
Future Perspectives
The future goal is to utilize regular office printers that can be modified to be used
with customized ink suitable for sensor fabrication in low resource settings. A deeper
examination of the employed materials and new proposals to try different methods
are necessary to improve the performance of the sensor.
Following ideas could be tested:
• To obtain a stable dispersion of rGO ink, the amino acid, L-tryptophan, can be
added to the L-AA solution as a stabilizer to avoid agglomeration and precipi-
tation of rGO. L-tryptophan acts as an electron donor, which can be absorbed
onto the rGO sheets based on π−π interactions. The negative charge of the ter-
minal carboxylic acid contributes to electrostatic repulsion, resulting in a stable
dispersion of rGO sheets [60] [42]. However, the surface of the rGO will degrade
the electronic properties to some extent, since L-tryptophan is absorbed to the
surface [60].
Furthermore, the literature has suggested to employ capping reagents, such as
polymers and surfactants to obtain a stable rGO dispersion [42].
• Incorporating PEDOT:PSS into the GO dispersion followed by a reduction in
a L-AA solution. As a result, the dispersion stability and the conductivity of
the rGO will be examined.
• Incorporating GO into the PEDOT:PSS(x10)-GOx, since the many oxygen-
containing reactive sites in GO can bind covalently to the free amino groupso
of GOx molecule, rather than non-covalently [42].
• Increase the conductivity of PEDOT:PSS by adding organic compounds, such as
glycerol [54]. This material is also favorable as a co-solvent in ink development
to prevent evaporation during printing.
Furthermore, the desirable ratio of
PEDOT:PSS is 1:1.
• Improve the immobilization of GOx by the addition of a cross linker such as
glutaraldehyde or Poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG). The cross linker ensures stable
a immobilization of the enzyme by cross linking the amine groups in GOx to
the carboxylic acid groups of PEG [17].
==PAGE:82==
70
6 Future Perspectives
Furthermore, in terms of long-time stability a linker molecule between the sur-
face of the rGO and the GOx can be necessary to maintain the functional
property. The direct contact on the rGO surface may produce denatured on
the long term [42].
• Increase the efficiency of the electron transfer and to allow the sensor to be used
in vivo by incorporating the mediator Fc-COOH into the surface modification of
the electrode. As a result, the enzyme and the mediator are confined to a small
volume adjacent to the electrode surface, which restricts the electron transfer
distance between the mediator and the enzyme [3].
The above ideas are suggested to modify the performance of the sensor. Nonetheless,
the ultimate goal is to develop an aqueous stable dispersion of rGO that can be
inkjet printed as the conductive material. Furthermore, the proper ink composition
of PEDOT:PSS needs further investigation, but is intended to function as a scaffold
to immobilize the enzyme as well as enhancing the conductivity of the biosensor.
