Abstract
This thesis contains a computational study of the characteristics of the ow around wind
turbine rotors with constant circulation distribution along the blade. The computations
were carried out using the simulator for wind farm application SOWFA, which combines
the actuator line technique with the computational uid dynamics software OpenFOAM.
The application of the SOWFA code is validated through simulations of the Tjæreborg
rotor, where grid and solver parameters are determined. The results obtained from the
simulation of the Tjæreborg rotor operating with the tip speed ratios λ = 7.07, 5.05 and
3.21 have been found to be in good agreement with results from a blade element momentum
code, measurements and simulations from similar studies.
The rst simulation of a rotor with constant circulation along the blade span is imple-
mented through optimum aerodynamic blade design proposed in the textbook of Burton
et al. (2011). The simulation performed with a tip speed ratio of λ = 7.07 showed that
the circulation is close to be constant along the blade span. The computed eciency of
the rotor is similar to the ones from analytical results and similar studies.
The second approach to simulate the aerodynamic optimum rotor was implemented
through modications to the source code of the applied solver. Here, optimum circulation
was enforced using the Kutta-Joukowsky theorem, calculating the lift force from a manu-
ally set constant circulation. The rotor eciency and wake characteristic was found to be
in good agreement with the results from analytical calculations and similar computational
studies.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Denmark has ambitions to reduce the greenhouse gas immersion by 20% and increase the
share of renewable energy up to 30% by 2020 (Energistyrelsen, 2017). Until 2050, Denmark
wants to be independent of fossil fuels (Energistyrelsen, 2017). With a total share of 71.8%
in 2016 (Energinet, 2017), wind energy is a signicant factor for the renewable energy
sector. To accomplish these goals, it is necessary to optimize every single component of
the wind turbines in such a way, that they produce as much energy as possible within the
given site conditions.
In the present work, the characteristics of an aerodynamic optimum horizontal axis
wind turbine rotor are investigated and analyzed through the wind farm simulator SOWFA
using the actuator line technique.
1.1
Literature review
In the following section, a brief overview of prior works and related topics is presented.
Searching for the keywords Actuator line, Wind turbine wakes and Optimum rotor
for the period 2002-2017 on DTU ndit (Findit.dtu.dk, 2017), shows that there is a growing
interest regarding the actuator line and wind turbine wake topic, especially within the last
four to ve years. Figure 1.1 illustrates the growth of interest in number of published
articles and shows that the topics currently are at high interest and up-to-date.
1

Figure 1.1: Number of articles when searching (1) Actuator line, (2) Wind turbine
wakes and (3) Optimum rotor limited to the years 2002-2017 on DTU ndit.
Furthermore, it shows that there is a large amount of information regarding these
topics and usable literature needs to be selected carefully. The majority of the considered
literature is selected by the following criteria:
1. Provide fundamental understanding of the topic, which in particular applies for the
optimum rotor and general momentum theory for horizontal axis wind turbines.
2. Give insight into the theoretical background of the applied methods such as the
actuator line technique and the SOWFA code.
3. Be related to the present work and give an overview of what already has been
investigated and what the research status is today.

1.1.1
Optimum rotor and blade geometry
The concept behind the optimum energy extracting rotor is primarily described by two
dierent approaches. A century ago (1912-1918), Joukowsky dened the optimum rotor as
one having a constant circulation along the blade (Joukowsky rotor) (Okulov & Sørensen,
2010), resulting in tip vorticies forming vortex tubes in the wake of a wind turbine. The
emerging vortex system is often referred to as a rotating horseshoe vortex, visible in gure
2
==PAGE:18==
2.2b. Nearly at the same time (Betz, 1919), Prandtl and Betz described the most ecient
blade as one having varying circulation along the wingspan (Betz rotor), generating a
helicoidal vortex structure (gure 2.2a).
Okulov & Sørensen (2010) compared the theoretical maximum eciency of Betz (Okulov
& Sørensen, 2008a,b) and Joukowsky (Okulov & Sørensen, 2010) rotors for a nite number
of blades, based on a analytical model provided by Okulov (2004). As outcome, it could
be concluded that the optimum power coecient of the Joukowsky rotor for all number
of blades is higher than that for the Betz rotor. However, the eciency of the Betz rotor
is found to be larger if it is compared for the same deceleration of the wind speed.
A proposition of how to create the aerodynamic optimum blade geometry is presented
in the textbook of Burton et al. (2011). The method is based on a constant circulation
along the blade and presents blade designs for a turbine rotor with variable rotational
speed, which should ensure a maximum possible power coecient regardless of the wind
speed.
1.1.2
Actuator line technique and wind turbine wakes
Caused by the overall growth of wind turbine farms, the interest in investigating the wake
behind a wind turbine increased in the recent years (see gure 1.1 graph (2)).
Wind
turbines placed in wind farms are in addition to the atmospheric turbulence also aected
by the wake of the upstream wind turbine. Understanding the generated wake of a wind
turbine prevents fatigue damage and thereby increases the overall lifetime and ensures
maximum eciency.
Sørensen & Shen (2002) presented an aerodynamical model for investigation of the
ow around a wind turbine rotor.
The routine combines a three-dimensional Navier-
Stokes solver with the actuator line technique. The turbine blades are substituted by
actuator lines, on which the blade forces are distributed using a three-dimensional Gaussian
function. The computations, carried out on a 500 kW Nordtank wind turbine, delivered
detailed characteristics about the turbine wake and the power prediction was in good
agreement with the measurements.
Troldborg (2008) carried out comprehensive simulations of wind turbines operating
in dierent conditions such as interacting wakes of wind turbines placed in rows. The
computations where performed in the three-dimensional Navier-Stokes solver EllipSys3D
combined with the actuator line technique presented by Sørensen & Shen (2002). Beside
estimation of the wind turbine wake in form of spectral coherence, length scales and reynold
stresses, dierent parameters aecting the computation where investigated. These include
for instance mesh size, time step, actuator line elements and the smearing parameter ϵ.
Initial computations for verication of the model are carried out on the Tjæreborg wind
3
==PAGE:19==
turbine.
Jha et al. (2014) presented a guideline for the blade force distribution within the
actuator line technique. While the blade forces typically are distributed using a single
smearing parameter, the smearing parameter in this work varies along the blade following
an elliptic distribution.
The results show that the varying projection radius leads to
improved prediction of the loads at the blade tip.
A comparison between a two dimensional and three dimensional force distribution has
been performed by Mikkelsen (2003). It is discussed that using a three dimensional force
distribution, the tangential loading increases all the way up to the tip of the blade, where
a continuous decreasing trend is expected.
The behavior is explained as the eect of
smeared force which extends beyond the tip and thereby interferes the original loading.
A two dimensional force distribution on the other hand results in a better trend near the
tip.
Sarmast et al. (2015) presented a comparison of the near wake between actuator line
simulations and a simplied vortex model using two dierent turbines, one turbine with a
Joukowsky rotor and one with varying circulation distribution (Tjæreborg wind turbine)
along the blade span. Here, the actuator line simulations where used to verify the compu-
tations carried out with the vortex model. The results show that the vortex code manages
to estimate the ow around a wind turbine similar to the actuator line technique with low
computational eort.
Using the actuator line technique, Kleusberg et al. (2016) performed simulations of the
ow around a Joukowsky rotor and the Tjæreborg turbine using nite volume methods in
EllipSys3D and spectral elements in the code Nek5000. It is concluded that for comparable
resolution, the spectral element code Nek5000 returns more accurate results. Additionally,
it is observed that the breakdown process of the individual vortex tubes generated by the
tip vortices is faster using nite volume methods, whereas the merging of vortices using
Nek5000 occurs after a period of vortex pairing.
(Martinez-Tossas et al., 2012) provides a comparison between actuator disc and ac-
tuator line models. The results of the simulations are compared with a blade element
momentum code. It is concluded that the lowest percentage dierence occurs when using
the actuator line method. The developed wake behind the wind turbines are comparable,
however the actuator disc model is not able to capture ow structures near the blade such
as root and tip vortices. It has also been investigated that the use of a tip- and root-loss
correction decreases the predicted power.
In their overview of the simulator for wind farm application, Churcheld et al. (2012)
presents the fundamentals of the SOWFA code. General experience with model param-
eters connected to the actuator line method are described, such as grid resolution and

determination of the smearing parameter ϵ. Their recommendations mainly refer to the
work of Troldborg (2008).
An evaluation of the actuator line model with coarse resolutions is presented by Draper
& Usera (2015). In their work, it has been possible to show that the actuator line technique
is able to capture the main characteristics of the wake for coarser spatial resolutions and
larger time steps than recommended in the previous presented papers.
1.1.3
Summary and status
From the literature it can be stated, that there is no clear proposition of how to set up
the correct actuator line parameters in order to obtain correct results. Furthermore, it is
discussed whether a three dimensional or a two dimensional Gaussian distribution should
be applied and in combination with that, whether Prandtl's tip loss correction should be
applied or not. Because of the overall discussions regarding the application of the model,
the presented restrictions and recommendations are used as a guideline to determine the
grid and solver parameters. The resulting settings however are based on own parameter
analysis in order to determine the grid and solver parameters which are suitable for the
present work.
1.2
Aim of the project
This thesis presents three dimensional Navier-Stokes simulations of the ow around hor-
izontal axis wind turbines operating in uniform inow conditions. The objective of the
investigation is to resolve the ow around Joukowsky type rotors with constant circula-
tion along the blade span designed according to the method described in the textbook of
Burton et al. (2011) and by modication of the source code through the Kutta-Joukowsky
theorem. The simulations are performed using the open source software SOWFA and the
here in implemented solver featuring the actuator line technique. The main focus is on
the ow structures near the blade tip and the overall blade aerodynamics, where loadings
and the emerging tip vortices in the vicinity of the rotor are analyzed.
Foremost, the applied model is validated through initial simulations of the Tjæreborg
wind turbine. The simulations are compared with the calculations of a self-made blade
element momentum code, the results of similar studies and experimental measurements.
Constant circulation along the blade span is introduced by designing a Joukowsky rotor
based on the method proposed in the textbook of Burton et al. (2011).
Finally, the source code of the solver is modied to ensure actual constant circulation
along the blade. The results are compared with the analytical results obtained by Okulov

2.1.1
Blade element momentum code
Based on the approach described above, a steady blade element momentum (BEM) code
is developed in order to calculate the forces acting on a wind turbine blade. Lift- and drag
forces are calculated through tabulated two dimensional airfoil data which stores lift- and
drag coecients for dierent angles of attack. The BEM-code is developed following the
instructions presented in the textbook of Hansen (2008) and can be seen in appendix A.1
In general, the blade element momentum method is based on the assumption of a rotor
having an innite number of blades, according to a rotor disc. The emerging vortex system
of a rotor with a nite number of blades is dierent from a rotor with an innite number
of blades. Since the rotors investigated in this project are three-bladed rotors, Prandtl's
tip loss factor is implemented into the BEM-code. Furthermore, in the case of high values
of the axial induction factor a, a Glauert correction is implemented (Hansen, 2008).
The routine of the algorithm works as follows and is repeated from step 2 for the
desired wind speeds, number of necessary iterations for convergence, number of blades
and amount of blade elements:
1. Initialization of the variables.
2. Calculation of the relative velocity Vrel based on equation 2.5.
3. Computation of the angle of attack α.
4. Look up of lift- and drag coecients in the set of airfoil data and interpolation to
the corresponding α.
5. Calculation of lift- and drag forces.

6. Calculation of the induction factors a and a′.
7. Include Prandtl's tip loss factor and the Glauert correction.
8. Computation of normal- and tangential forces.

Software
The simulations performed throughout this project are executed using the open source
CFD software OpenFOAM. The software is developed by OpenCFD Ltd since 2004 (Open-

The National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) has developed a Simulator For
Wind Farm Application (SOWFA, Churcheld et al. (2012)) based on OpenFOAM. The
code is under ongoing development with the overall vision to combine numerical weather
prediction with uid simulations and wind turbine aerodynamics. For simulating the ow
around a wind turbine, the code uses the actuator disc method and the actuator line
technique described in section 2.4.
The simulations of the present work are executed using the horizontalAxisWindTur-

the actuator line technique. This extension was created at the National Renewable Energy
Laboratory (NREL) in January 2012.
3.4
Numerical setup and discretization
The focus of this project is to investigate whether it is possible to resolve the tip vorticies
emerging from a wind turbine blade with constant circulation along the blade span. For
this purpose, the actuator line technique is chosen instead of the actuator disc method
because it is able to capture the ow structures near the blade.
The simulations executed in this project are laminar with an uniform inow, which
means wind shear and a atmospheric boundary layer is not implemented into the calcu-
lation. The ow is solved using the PISO algorithm implemented in the pisoFoam solver
for transient incompressible ow (Openfoamwiki.net, 2017).
For discretization in time and space, a second order implicit backward Euler scheme
and a linear Gaussian nite volume integration is used respectively (User Guide v5 v5
User Guide OpenFOAM, 2017).

3.5
Actuator line parameters
Using the actuator line technique introduces a number of additional solver parameters
which have a signicant inuence on the computed results and the overall stability of
the calculations. In the following sections, these parameters are described and their rec-
ommended limitations are presented. Considering the smearing parameter ϵ, the applied
version of SOWFA features a three dimensional Gaussian distribution which is constant
along the blade span as described in section 2.4.
3.5.1
Smearing parameter
As described in section 2.4, the calculated blade force which is substituted into the ow
is distributed along and away the blade span. This distribution is regularized using the
smearing parameter ϵ. Since the forces are spread to the surrounding grid points, the
smearing factor is dependent on the local cell size of the grid and the number of points
on the actuator line. Troldborg (2008) has investigated the inuence of this parameter on
the axial induction factor a and concluded that the smearing factor is restricted by the
condition
ϵ ≥2∆r,
(3.4)
22
==PAGE:38==
where ∆r is the grid spacing between the points on the actuator line. For values that
are smaller than 2∆r, the distribution of the axial induction factor along the blade shows
instabilities. A higher value of ϵ shows a smoother distribution of the axial induction.
Kleusberg et al. (2016) has performed a similar study of the smearing parameter, with
the result that a higher value leads to a higher power coecient Cp and vice versa.
A method for determination of the smearing factor concerning the SOWFA code is
presented by Churcheld et al. (2012). This method is based on a trial and error approach,
where ϵ is determined through simulations of wind turbines with a known power output.
At a too high calculated power output, the smearing factor should be decreased and vice
versa. The determined smearing factor should then be suitable for every tip speed ratio.
Jha et al. (2014) as well as Martinez-Tossas & Leonardi (2013) and Martinez-Tossas
et al. (2016) have proposed the implementation of a force distribution method, where ϵ
varies along the blade span and is dependent on the chord length of the local blade section.
This gives a more controllable distribution which for instance restrict the smearing of the
blade forces not to exceed the blade tip. For the present simulations however, the smearing
parameter is set to be constant along the actuator lines.
From the literature it can be stated that the smearing factor has a huge impact on
the results of the simulations and that there is no clear proposition of the ideal smearing
factor. Consequently, the impact of the smearing factor is analyzed in section 4.1.4 to
ensure reasonable results.

Chapter 5
Constant circulation distribution
This chapter describes the implementation and simulation of wind turbine rotors with

5.1
Constant circulation through aerodynamic optimum
blade geometry

the Tjæreborg turbine. This includes the blade radius R = 30.56 m and the rotational

Furthermore, a constant lift coecient Cl = 1 is assumed.
The
implementation of the generated airfoil geometry is similar the one for the Tjæreborg

Chapter 7
Discussion
The investigations conducted in the previous chapters show, that it is possible to visualize
and resolve the tip vortices emerging from a rotor with constant circulation distribution
along the blade span. Furthermore, the tip vortices in the vicinity of the blade are de-
termined to have approximately the same circulation as the blades have. However, it is
observed that the simulations tend to become unstable after a certain amount of simu-
lation time as described in chapter 6. Additionally, the distribution of the loads along
the blade shows a spike at the tip, which is continuous throughout every simulation and
observed for dierent blade geometries. In the following, the determined grid and solver
parameters, which are found to have signicant inuence on the addressed irregularities
are discussed considering the balance between accuracy and instability.
Model parameter
Considering the grid resolution, it is desired to decrease the cell size until the tip vortices
are adequately resolved. The grid size in the region of mesh renement is decreased until
the inuence of the wake on the blade loading is visible, which occurs at a resolution of
∆x = 0.25 m. The overall mesh size is held at ∆x = 2 m in order to reduce the amount
of cells in the computational domain. Visible on gure 3.2, the region of mesh renement
does not go all the way up until the outlet of the domain. At the point where the region of
renement stops, there will be an increase in grid size from ∆x = 0.25 m to ∆x = 2 m. As
a consequence, the resolved information about the wake will get lost in the coarse mesh of
the overall grid. It is expected that this will cause a numerical interference and advance
the observed instability in the wake. A solution to this problem could be to extend the
region of mesh renement up until the outlet of the domain. Another possibility could be
to use dierent grid resolutions at the end of the region of renement in order to smooth
out the transitions between grid sizes. Both solutions however would increase the total
61
==PAGE:77==
number of grid cells. But considering the benets of the actuator line technique, namely
bypass the mesh restrictions that comes with boundary layers, the mesh should not be
rened any further. Increasing the mesh any further would obviate the cause for applying
the actuator line technique and a full wind turbine could be modeled instead.
Regarding the occurring spike at the tip of the blade as for instance visible in gure
5.10, it is observed that it is due to a too coarse mesh at the tip of the blade. With
increasing grid resolution it is expected that the spike will decrease and eventually not be
visible if the grid is rened suciently. Additionally, the smearing parameter ϵ has to be
selected carefully in close agreement with the mesh size and the actuator line resolution.
A too large smearing parameter which is constant along the blade span will distort the
induced velocities especially at the tip of the blade. This is in close relation to the point
distribution on the actuator line, from which the forces are spread out. An actuator line
element to close to the tip of the blade will project the forces even further beyond the
blade tip. A look into the source code of the basic SOWFA package shows the option to
implement another type of distribution for the actuator line points. This could be used in
advance to distribute the points near the tip of the blade so that the forces are not spread
out beyond the tip. Another possibility would be to implement a smearing parameter
which is based on the local chord length at the actuator points, which would enhance
control ability of the force distribution.
Implementation of constant circulation along the blade span
Through optimum blade design according to Burton et al. (2011), constant circulation
along the blade span is implemented through the rotor geometry. From gure 5.3 it is
visible that the actual circulation distribution along the blade is not entirely constant. An
explanation for the decit at the root of the blade could be the inuence of the emerging
root vortices together with the implementation method as described in the start of chapter
5.
Regarding the actual blade geometry it is questionable whether it could be possible to
actually build a blade featuring the chord length and twist angle as shown in gures 5.1
and 5.2.
Considering the modication in the source code, the computational time is signicantly
increased.
This is most probably due to the implementation of the circulation to be
changeable without compiling the source code. The issue of increased computational time
could be solved by either dene the value of circulation in the source code or optimize the
implementation method.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Throughout this project, it has been achieved to implement a constant circulation distri-
bution along a horizontal axis wind turbine blade and resolve the emerging tip vortices
using the SOWFA code featuring the actuator line technique.
Initial simulations of the Tjæreborg turbine have been performed in order to deter-
mine the grid and solver parameters which come with the actuator line technique. These
parameters where determined in close relation to the work in similar studies and adapted
to this work. Furthermore, the applied code with the determined parameters was val-
idated through simulations of the rotor operating in tip speed ratios of λ = 7.07, 5.05
and 3.21. The results where compared with a blade element momentum code, measure-
ments and computational results from similar studies. In general an good agreement was
achieved, with a simulated rotor eciency of Cp = 0.485 compared to the measurements
Cp,exp = 0.492 and the BEM-code Cp,BEM = 0.475 for λ = 7.07. The behavior of the
generated vortex system behind the rotor however was observed to break up early in
comparison to similar studies.
An optimum circulation distribution along the blade span was introduced by the design
of a Joukowsky rotor with the same rotor radius as the Tjæreborg rotor. This enabled the
possibility to apply the same grid and solver parameters as used for the initial simulations.
The obtained rotor eciency of Cp = 0.577 is in good agreement with analytical results
calculating an eciency of Cp = 0.575 and similar studies computing power coecients
between Cp = 0.550 and Cp = 0.575. The circulation around the blade lies about the
design value of Γ = 40.24 m2/s, but is observed not to be completely constant along the
entire blade span.
Finally, constant circulation along the blade was implemented through modications
to the source code of the applied solver. Using the Kutta-Joukowsky theorem, lift forces
where calculated through a manually set circulation of Γ = 40.24 m2/s. The rotor eciency
63
==PAGE:79==
was computed to Cp = 0.578 which is both in good agreement with the eciency from
similar studies with power coecients between Cp = 0.550 and Cp = 0.575 and the one
obtained through the optimum aerodynamic blade geometry. The resulting circulation
along the blade has been observed to be close to constant around the design value of
Γ = 40.24 m2/s. Eventually, the four tip vortices closest to the rotor have been determined
to have aproximately the same circulation as the blades of the rotor, which is in agreement
with the theory.
