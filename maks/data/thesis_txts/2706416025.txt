Abstract
District heating(DH) system plays an important role in the implementation of the future en-
ergy system. Low-temperature district heating(LTDH) networks can be realised through the
low-temperature operation of heating systems in buildings. This project did some studies for
low-operation operation of radiator system in 6 buildings by calculating the minimum supply
temperature, evaluating the the operation of the heating systems on risers and flats level through
using data from electronic heat cost allocators and return temperature measurements and
simulating an improved weather compensation control. This project provided the minimum
supply temperature curves for 6 buildings. The evaluation to the operation of the heating
systems using the measurements from heat cost allocators pinpointed that using part of the
radiators to heat the whole flat led to higher return temperatures in the risers and pinpointed
the critical flats with high heat load in the 6 buildings which were the obstructions of lowering
the supply temperature. An improved weather compensation control using 12-hour outdoor
temperature as input smoothed out the supply curve and lowered the supply temperatures by
2.3-3.0°C and heat load by 2.3-4.0% during high heat demand. This improved control is helpful
to secure low supply temperature and peak shaving.
Keywords: Low-temperature district heating; Digitalization of heating system; Heating system
operation evaluation; Weather compensation control

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Background
To overcome the challenges of climate change and environmental degradation, the European
green deal [1] has an ambition of zero net emissions of green gases by 2050. The smart integration
of renewables, energy efficiency and other sustainable solutions benefits to achieve this goal [2,3].
Heat Roadmap Europe [4] indicates that geothermal heat, solar thermal heat, and large-scale
heat pumps must substitute the fossil fuel based energy supply means like excess heat from
electricity generation, industrial excess heat and heat from boilers to be the main strategic heat
sources for District Heating(DH) by 2050. This transition requires large-scale integration of an
increasing level of intermittent renewable energy.
Hence, one of the challenges will be to integrate the DH networks with the electricity sectors
and the transportation sectors [5]. A number of studies including Heat Roadmap Europe [4,6]
emphasise that the DH system plays an important role in the implementation of the future
energy system. This requires the present DH network going through a thorough change to a
low-temperature DH network being an integrated part of the smart energy system. These future
low-temperature district heating (LTDH) networks are expected to operate with supply and
return temperature of approximately 50 °C and 20 °C [7].
The primary energy consumption [7] and the heat supply costs [8] can be reduced by the
reduction of DH operating temperatures and the distribution heat loss in pipes due to the
decrease in the temperature level. It is estimated that 2.2-2.6 billion Danish Kroner can be
saved in Denmark by upgrading and operating the DH systems in a low temperature [9].
For the exist buildings, LTDH can be realized by lowering the operating temperature of the
heating system. The supply temperature is controlled by the network operator based on the
heat demand. However, the return temperature which is highly influenced by the usage habit of
the residents is a limit of lowering the supply temperature. As a result, the network operator
has a strong interest in lowering the return temperature with the residents.
Digital technologies are believed to make the whole energy system smarter, more efficient, and
reliable and to boost the efficiency and the integration of more renewables into the system.
Some common approaches available now are the energy meters installed on the DH substations,
electronic heat cost allocators installed on every single radiator and temperature sensors installed
on main pipes and risers. Heat cost allocators are devices attached to individual radiators

the EU directive [11] in 2018, it is mandatory for multi-family building with central heating

1.2
Intention of work
The thesis aims to investigate the strategy to lower the operating temperature in the heating
system. This is applied by using the measurements from energy meters and heat cost allocators
to calculate the potential minimum supply temperature curve. The measurement can also be
used to pinpoint the problematic part of the heating system that may limit the reduction of the
operating temperature. The thesis also aims to investigate an improved weather compensation
control.

STATE OF THE ART
2
State of the art
Low temperature operation of DH system of buildings is very important in the energy system
in the future. Lund et al. [5] defined the concept of 4th Generation District Heating (4GDH).
4GDH systems provide the heat supply of low-energy buildings with low grid losses in a way in
which the use of low-temperature heat sources is integrated with the operation of smart energy
systems. Nord et al. [7] founded that by lowering the DH operating temperatures, the heat loss
in the DH networks can be reduced.
There is a potential for the existing heating systems operating with low temperatures without
leading to thermal discomfort for the end-users. Brand et al. [14] stated that due to the DH
system in Denmark is designed for the condition that an operative indoor temperature of 20 °C,
a steady-state outdoor temperature of -12 °C, and no internal and solar heat gains, the operating
temperature can be significantly reduced. The results showed that for a single-family house
built in 70s can be heated by LTDH with 50°C to an operative temperature of 22°C roughly for
59% (3600 h) of year. The reduction of the DH supply temperature to below 60°C also requires
the upgrade of the DH and DHW infrastructures including DHW storage tanks and the DH
substations. Jangsten et al. [15] studied the data from 109 radiators in Gothenburg’s district
heating system. The results showed that there is a potential to lower the operating temperature
of the radiators due to the design outdoor temperature is 16°C. Generally, there is a potential
to lower the operating temperature of the radiators because they were designed for extreme
conditions and not considering the heat gains.
Several studies have investigated the low-temperature operation in the district heating systems.
Lauenburg et al. [16] investigated an adaptive control method to obtain the lowest supply
temperature by employing an optimal combination of radiator circuit supply temperature and
circulation flow rate. The results of the test showed that the district heating return temperature
was reduced by 2°C. Yuan et al. [17] investigated a feedback predictive model of the secondary
supply temperature based on the indoor temperature. Then, the solar radiation, the uncertainty
of outdoor temperature and the heat consumer behavior on the heating parameters were consid-
ered to correct the supply temperature. The results of the test showed the energy saving rate is
approximately 5.8%. However, the users’ behaviour will influence the accuracy of the model
seriously. Ashfaq et al. [18] presented a novel hydraulic model using real weather and hourly
monitored operational heating data from an existing boiler based building to investigate the
hydraulic imbalance in the LTDH network. The study showes that it is technically possible to
lower the supply temeprature below 60°C.

==PAGE:12==

STATE OF THE ART
Heat cost allocators originally were installed for billing. Now there are several studies using
the measurement from the heat cost allocators to improve the operation of the heating system.
Tomasz et al. [19] investigated a long term experimental evaluation of the influence of heat
cost allocators on energy consumption in a multifamily building. The result shows that the
reduction of the heat consumption by using heat cost allocators is possible by influencing the
users’ behaviour when the heating system is equipped with regulatory devices. Benakopoulos et
al. [20] investigated a strategy using the measurements from the heat cost allocators to simulate
all the available radiators and minimize the supply temperature. The results of the model shows
that the heat demand and the heat comfort can be satisfied only when all the radiators are in
operation under the minimum supply temperature. The energy-weighted supply and return
temperatures are 44°C and 30°C, respectively.
2.1
Novelty
The novelty of this study is using the measurement from energy meters and heat cost allocators
to calculate the minimum operating temperatures and pinpoint the errors or problematic part
of the heating system in operation. This will help the activities of the building service personnel
to secure low temperature operation.

3.1
Potential lower operating temperatures
A method was used to estimate the minimized supply/return temperature of a certain building.
The model used heating degree days approach, the heat capacity of the building and the data
from energy meters. The model was developed in the research group in the department. This
model is used here as a foundation of analysing the heating system in the building. The following
is a brief introduction to the principle of this model.

3.2
Correction of the total heating capacity of the building
Total heating capacity is a necessary input for the method that was presented in chapter 3.1.
The heating system emits heat to the apartments through radiators and pipes in the building.
As a result, the total heating capacity in a building should include both radiators and pipes.
The capacity of the radiators in the 6 buildings had already been documented and was easy to
obtain and use, while there was no information about the heat loss from the pipes that could
be used directly. So one aim of this section is to calculate the heat loss capacity of the pipes
that emit heat to the apartments. In addition, it was found in the previous studies to the
measurements of the heat cost allocators that Danish users love to keep the radiators off or
running at low power to maintain a low temperature in the bedrooms. Hence, in this study, an
assumption was made that all the radiators in the master bedrooms were not in use and as a
result they were removed from the total heating capacity.
3.2.1
The calculation of the heat loss from pipes
The pipes that emit heat to apartments include 2 parts: the vertical risers and the pipes from
manifold to radiators. The heat loss from both 2 parts can be regarded as the heat gains to the
apartments. The steps are as followed:
1) Measure the lengths of the distribution pipes, risers and pipes in the flats from the drawings.
The floor height was assumed in the range of 2.8m to 3.0m and the riser length on the top
floor was assumed to be 0.6m because it might only reach the height of the radiator.

3.2.2
The calculation of the heating capacity of radiators
The total capacity of all the radiators and the capacity of the radiators in the master bedrooms
need to be found. The steps are as followed:
1) Calculate the total heating capacity by summing up the heat capacity of each radiator.
2) Locate the radiators in the master bedrooms using the drawings and the radiator list.
Calculate the total heating capacity in the master bedrooms.
3.2.3
Corrected heating capacity
The corrected heating capacity can be determined having calculated the heat loss from pipes,
total radiators’ capacity, and heat capacity of the radiators in the main bedrooms as described
by Equation 9.
CC = CT + CP −CB
(9)
where:
CC is the corrected heating capacity
CT is the total capacity of all the radiators
CP is the capacity of the heat loss from pipes
CB is the total heating capacity of the radiators in master bedrooms
3.3
Correction of the space heating energy consumption
• The total annual energy in 2019 and other energy information were obtained from the DH
energy meters installed in each apartment.
• The daily average DHW energy consumption that subtracted from the total annual energy
was estimated from the measurements during summer. As the space heating system was
on sometimes in summer, it was assumed that 50% of the total energy consumption was
for DHW. Due to the DHW consumption is fairly constant through the year, this daily
average DHW energy consumption obtained from summer data was assumed for the whole
year.
• The internal heat gains considered the heat delivered in the rooms by the human activities
and equipment. These were assumed as 5W/m2 for the heating season period from
September to May. The heated area was obtained from the energy certificate of the
buildings. This heat gain correction was also used in the heat load.

CASE STUDY
4
Case study
This thesis is based on 6 building cases located in Viborg, Denmark. All buildings are residential
buildings connected to the local district heating network. The focus of the investigation was
based on the space heating system only. For all the 6 buildings, the 2D floor plans are available
while the 3D layouts of the heating system are only available for some of them.
4.1
Building 1
This building is located in Viborg and built in 1970. There are 3 floors, 42 apartments, 235
radiators and 11 risers in the building. The heating system was renovated in 1990s. The floor
plan of the basement with the heating installations are represented on figure 2. The placements
of the risers are specified with red dots.
Figure 2: Floor plan of the basement with the risers’ positions in Building 1

7
Data analysis to evaluate the operation of the heating
systems on risers level
The goal of this chapter is to explore the possibility to locate problems in the heating system
by looking at the measurements from the temperature sensors installed on the risers. The
assessment was done by looking at the measurements from both previous and current heating
season. For the previous heating season, a number of very cold days were selected to investigate
if some anomalies could be identified in the space heating systems during critical conditions.
Similar approach was done during this heating season although the data referred to milder
outdoor temperature. The supply/return temperature and the return temperature of the risers
were used for the evaluation together with the measurements from the heat cost allocators. This
part is also regarded as a pre-assessment before doing the real test in the heating systems.
7.1
Identification of critical operation of the heating system
This part of the study mainly focuses on the risers with high return temperature. Those risers
with return temperatures higher than the average were the potential candidates of the critical
operation. As a result, there were two steps in the approach of the research. First, using the
temperature measurements, compare the return temperatures from all the risers against the
average return temperature for the building. Highlight the risers with higher return temperature
than the average in the given time period. Second, using the measurements from heat cost
allocators, check the working mode and output of the radiators that connected to the risers with
high return temperature and investigate the relation between the performance of the radiators
and the return temperature of the riser.

In this chapter, the measurements from the heat cost allocators installed on the radiators

temperature in the low-temperature operation.
8.1
Energy indicators calculation using the measurements from heat
cost allocators
This section introduces the method to calculate the energy indicators using the measurements
from heat cost allocators. There are 3 steps in total:
1) Data acquisition and preprocessing: The raw data obtained from the database needed to
do preprocessing to be adapted to the calculation method afterwards.
2) Using the heat cost allocators’ readings for the calculation of energy and power indicators:
The main indicators such as the total energy consumption and part load was calculated in
this section.
3) Correction of results by comparison with centralized energy data: Due to the inaccuracy
and uncertainty of the measurements from the heat cost allocators, there was a difference
between the calculated total energy using the measurements from heat cost allocators and
the measured total energy. The difference was considered as a correction factor to the
original measurements of the heat cost allocators.
8.1.1
Data acquisition and preprocessing
The heat cost allocators record the heat output of radiators in a cumulative form. Hence, the
radiator heat output of a certain period can be obtained by the difference of the readings from
the beginning and the end of the time period. Due to the original data was discrete and in order
to simply the calculation process and to ensure the time consistency of data and calculation
method, the original data was normalized as 12-hour interval.

IMPROVED WEATHER COMPENSATION CONTROL
9
Improved weather compensation control
This chapter investigated an improved weather compensation control method that can make
the supply temperature curves more smooth and reduce the supply temperatures during the
period of high heat demand. The simulations were performed using IDA ICE [21] software.
9.1
Purpose and Method
In the current weather compensation control, the variation of the supply temperature is almost
instantaneous according to the variation of the outdoor temperature. However, due to the time
continuity and thermal inertia in buildings [22], there is not an instantaneous variation of the
indoor comfort. Therefore, using the 12-hour average outdoor temperature as the input of
the weather compensation control is a possible way to smooth the supply temperature curves
avoiding unnecessary high temperatures and securing lowering the heat loss in the distribution
network. This may reduce the supply temperature as well securing the peak shavings during
high heat demand.
Two scenarios were investigated. The difference of the two scenarios was that the reference one
named ‘Normal’ used the ambient temperature as input for the weather compensation control;
whereas in the second scenario the input ambient temperature for the weather compensation
control was the average outdoor temperature of the previous 12 hours. The two scenarios were
modeled in IDA ICE which is a simulation software for the study of thermal indoor climate as
well as the energy consumption of the building. The detailed setup and inputs of the models
will be presented in next section.
The pattern of the supply temperatures, the supply temperature and the power in the period of
high heat demand could be important indicators to compare the performance of the two scenarios.
9.2

A section including 3 identical apartments of Building 1 was modeled in IDA ICE, along with 2
sections of neighboring apartment to simulate the heat transfer between apartments in both
vertical and horizontal directions. In figure 39, the grey section represents the target apartments
and the red parts represents the neighbor sections.

DISCUSSIONS
10
Discussions
10.1
Methodology to estimate the potential for low temperature
operation in buildings
This section is the discussions concerning about Chapter 3 and 6.
In the methodology presented in chapter 3, an assumption was made that Danish users prefer
to keep the radiators in the master bedroom off. This was supported by the results in chapter
7.2 like Figure 25. The measurements from the radiators in the bedrooms were small values in
most cases. Another assumption that a heat gain of 5W/m2 was used in the calculation. The
effect of the solar gain was not considered in the process. In addition, the effect of the wind
velocity on the natural ventilation in the apartments was only reflected as an average value in
the heat loss coefficient. For the days with high wind velocity, the heat loss of the apartment
could be higher and a higher supply temperature may be required.
For the case of Building 1, by increasing the mass flow rate from the measured value of 0.85kg/s
to the max value 2.5kg/s, Table Figure 13 and 14 highlighted the fact that the potential of
lowering the supply temperature is greatly dependent on the maximum value of the circulation
flow. In real operations like the case shown in Figure 23, it was documented that lower supply
temperature and high mass flow rate ensure lower return temperature which was because the
radiators were poorly controlled. Also, high mass flow rate should be preferred to mitigate the
negative effects of uncontrolled radiators.
10.2
Measurements and diagnosis of the operation of the heating
systems
This section is the discussions concerning about Chapter 7 and 8.
The return temperatures of the risers can be the indicators to describe the usage of the radiators
connected to them as shown in results like Figure 25 and 26. The results from two buildings
show that if the riser has a high return temperature, that is because the users in the flats
connected to the riser prefer to only use part of the radiators to heat the whole flat. This can
be explained by: When only a few radiators were in operation to offer the heat demand of the
whole flat, the thermostats increased the flow through the radiators and as a consequence the
return temperature got higher. These flats are the key to lower the return temperature. If the
67
==PAGE:76==
10
DISCUSSIONS
users in these flats turn on all the radiators for heating, the return temperature in the risers can
be lowered and as a result, the average return temperature can be lowered.
The condition in Building 3 was very special. Figure 28 and Table 14 showed that the operating
temperatures in Building 3 were very low and the supply temperature was just 1.7°C higher
than the calculated minimum supply temperature in Table 14. By studying the historical data,
there were even some periods when the measured supply temperature lower than the calculated
minimum temperature. It was heard that there was no complains about the thermal discomfort
from users. Figure 30 illustrates that the supply temperature control of Building 3 may be
determined by solar radiation rather than outdoor temperature. This may provide a new method
for supply temperature control in low-temperature operation avoiding thermal discomfort.
Data analysis to evaluate the operation of the heating systems on flat level pointed out the
critical flats with high heat load. When implementing the low-temperature operation to the
buildings based on the calculated minimum supply curve presented in section 6.3, these flats
will suffer different level of thermal discomfort. Hence, before implementing the calculated
minimum curve, the thermal comfort of these flats have to be satisfied first, that is using the
supply temperature associated to these flats to ensure zero complains.
The results of the flat-level part load calculation such as Table 9-10 showed that in many cases,
one critical flat had a high part load was because of the low part load of its neighbors. A low
indoor temperature setting in adjacent flats increased the heat loss from flat to flat and in order
to maintain the thermal comfort, the user in the critical flat had to increase the valve of the
radiators. On the other hand, the neighbor apartment enjoyed more heat gains not only from
the critical apartment but other apartments. This may cause unfairness in using the heat cost
allocators for billing. One possible way to avoid this is the payment should also consider a
reference indoor temperature.
The digitalization of the heating system especially for the demand side is a key element for
securing the thermal comfort in the low temperature operation. These measurements can be
used to pinpoint the potential problems, analyze the behaviours of the residents and ensure the
key indicators within the normal range.

10.3
Improved weather compensation control
This section is the discussions concerning about Chapter 9.
Figure 43-48 show that using 12-hour average outdoor temperature as the input of the weather
compensation control smoothed out the supply temperature and during the high heat load
period, the supply temperature was reduced. Table 29 and 30 show that the supply temperatures
can be lowered by 2.3-3.0°C and heat load can be lowered by 2.3-4.0% for high heat demand
period. This results can be helpful for the DH operators to avoid high supply temperature and
using the highly polluting fossil fuel peak boilers especially for the morning peaks. Running the
operation more smoothly also benefits the integration of renewables to the energy system.
10.4
Common problems
A common issue for the whole study was sometimes the lack of data. In those cases some
assumptions were made to estimate for instance the pipe length, the capacity of the radiators
and the risers’ positions.
As the use of digital devices and relative measurements is and will be one of the key elements
in building services, it was found that more attention is required on the maintenance of the
database in the aspects like database update, convenience of data acquisition and automatic
analysis scripts of commonly used indicators.

CONCLUSIONS
11
Conclusions
11.1
Methodology to estimate the potential for low temperature
operation in buildings
This thesis presented a methodology of calculating the minimum supply/return temperature of
the heating system. This methodology can help benchmarking different buildings and highlight
the potential for low temperature operations of the heating system if the systems are well
controlled and operated. The minimum supply/return temperature curves were presented for all
6 buildings. By considering the heat loss from the pipes and the removal of the radiator capacity
in the master bedroom and using the measurements from energy meters, the calculations were
closer to reality. By comparing different circulation flow in the same heating system, it was
found that high mass flow led to the reduction of the operating temperatures.
11.2
Measurements and diagnosis of the operation of the heating
systems
The data analysis to evaluate the operation of the heating systems on riser level is helpful for
lowering the return temperature of the heating system. 12 risers with higher return temperatures
than the average were located by the analysis to the 6 buildings. High return temperature of
the risers indicates that not all the connected radiators are in operation. As a result, the users
are expected to be encouraged to use all the radiators to heat the rooms rather than only use
some of them to achieve lowest return temperatures, optimal operation of the system and better
thermal comfort.
The data analysis to evaluate the operation of the heating systems on flat level is helpful for
lowering the supply temperature of the heating system. This analysis pointed out the critical
flats with higher part load than the calculated minimum relative heat load for all the 6 buildings.
These critical flats are the limitations of lowering the supply temperatures. One reason for the
higher heat demand of these critical flats is the lower indoor temperature setpoints of their
neighbor flats.

11.3
Improved weather compensation control
Using 12-hour average outdoor temperature as the input of the weather compensation control
smoothed out the supply curve. During the high heat demand periods, compared to the normal
control, the improved control lowered the supply temperatures by 2.3-3.0°C and heat load by
2.3-4.0%. This finding is very important in transition towards LDTH and securing low supply
temperature and peak shaving.
