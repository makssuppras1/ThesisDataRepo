Abstract 
Many valuable products can be produced by oxidizing hydrocarbons, like terminally 
hydroxylated fatty acids which are important building blocks for biopolymers, 
pharmaceuticals and other fine chemicals. However, the oxidation reactions required to 
make these products are difficult to perform chemically because they require harsh 
operating conditions, expensive reagents and are poorly enantioselective. Cytochrome 
P450 monooxygenases are capable of oxidizing unactivated carbons in a hydrocarbon 
chain, which is particularly challenging to do synthetically, and they can do so at mild 
operating conditions with exceptional regio- and stereoselectivities. CYP153A from 
Marinobacter aquaeolei is able to catalyze the terminal hydroxylation of lauric acid to 12-
hydroxydodecanoic acid with 95% regioselectivity. Unfortunately, very few industrial 
processes use P450s because they suffer from many limitations, like the need for expensive 
cofactors and redox partners, which have been studied extensively. However, relatively little 
research has been done on how dissolved oxygen (DO) concentrations affect P450-
catalyzed oxidations. 
As such, the purpose of this investigation was to determine whether DO concentration has 
a significant impact on resting whole-cell P450-based biotransformations. To do this, a 
model system was selected comprising of the fusion protein CYP153A-CPR expressed in 
E. coli BW25113 ∆fadD, to catalyze the conversion of lauric acid to 12-hydroxydodecanoic 
acid. Biotransformations were performed in bioreactors at DO concentrations of 10, 50 and 
100%, based on air saturation, and it was found that product formation did not differ 
significantly within this range of concentrations. However, when biotransformations were 
performed in shake flasks, which were demonstrated to be oxygen limited by the occurrence 
of mixed acid fermentation, product formation was reduced. This shows that there is a DO 
concentration, less than 10%, below which the product formation is affected. Therefore, 
biotransformations must be operated with at least 10% DO to improve performance. The 
production of acetic acid due to overflow metabolism was found to be a problem in the 
bioreactors, likely due to excessive concentrations of glycerol as a carbon source, allowing 
cellular metabolism to become saturated. As such, fed-batch operation should be used to 
prevent over-feeding of carbon sources. 
==PAGE:4==

The performance of biotransformations was also found to be greatly influenced by the P450 
expression level and the co-expression of a fatty acid transporter, FadL, to facilitate 
substrate intake by the cells. P450 expression was found to be improved in shake flasks 
due to lower growth rates which allow for more efficient protein folding. Therefore, cell 
growth and protein expression should be operated in fed-batch mode so that the feed can 
be used to control growth rate.

1. Introduction 
The selective oxidation of carbon-hydrogen bonds in large organic molecules is highly 
challenging but would allow the simplification of complex reaction routes that are currently 
necessary in several industrial applications (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Bell et al., 2012). 
Using enzymes as biocatalysts to perform such oxidation reactions is very attractive 
because they are energy efficient, require mild operating conditions and do not produce 
waste containing toxic compounds or heavy metals (Bordeaux et al., 2011; Jung, Lauchli 
and Arnold, 2011; Bell et al., 2012; Notonier et al., 2016). The number of characterized 
enzymes continues to grow, due to new genomic sequencing programs, which is greatly 
expanding the possibilities for biocatalysis (Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; Bornscheuer et al., 
2012; Turner and Truppo, 2013; Duan et al., 2016). Rising energy costs and stricter 
environmental regulations have contributed towards the development of industrial 
biocatalytic processes for sustainable and safe production, since enzymes are produced 
from inexpensive renewable sources, are biodegradable and non-toxic (Bordeaux et al., 
2011; Bell et al., 2012; Bornscheuer et al., 2012; Turner and Truppo, 2013; Truppo, 2017). 
However, more importantly, the large structure of an enzyme causes it to have multiple 
points of contact with the substrate, allowing much better selectivities than chemical 
catalysts, and the structure can be modified through protein engineering to change its 
properties (Truppo, 2017). Enzymes are also highly chemo- and regiospecific (Bühler and 
Schmid, 2004; Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; Bühler et al., 2008; Scheps et al., 2011). As 
such, biocatalysts tend to generate fewer by-products than chemical catalysts (Bornscheuer 
et al., 2012; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). 
Cytochrome P450 monooxygenases (EC 1.14.X.Y), commonly referred to simply as P450s 
or CYPs, are hemeproteins that catalyze many synthetically challenging oxidation reactions 
(Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; O’Reilly et al., 2011; Lundemo et al., 2015). Of particular 
interest is the fact that they can catalyze the regio- and stereoselective oxidation of 
unactivated carbon-hydrogen bonds, as shown in the reaction scheme below, which 
continues to be one of the most challenging reactions in chemical synthesis (Bell et al., 
2012; Cornelissen et al., 2013; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014; Guengerich, Waterman and 
Egli, 2016; Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016).

This unique ability to activate molecular oxygen and selectively insert one atom into a 
substrate, even at an unactivated carbon, while the other is reduced to water, gives P450s 
great potential for industrial application, especially since they can do this at normal 
pressures and temperatures (Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015; 
Biggs et al., 2016; Notonier et al., 2016; Truppo, 2017). There are a wide variety of P450s, 
more than 21000 spread across all biological kingdoms, that utilize many substrates 
including alkanes, aromatic compounds, terpenes, amino acids and antibiotics to synthesize 
products like steroids, fatty acids and vitamins to name just a few (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; 
Bordeaux et al., 2011; O’Reilly et al., 2011; Bell et al., 2012; Bleif et al., 2012; Braun et al., 
2012; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014; Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016; Abdulmughni et 
al., 2017). To do this, they catalyze many different types of reactions, some of which are 
shown in Figure 1, including hydroxylations, dealkylations, sulfoxidations, dehalogenations, 
N-oxide reductions as well as the formation and cleavage of carbon-carbon bonds, which 
allow P450s to be used for a broad range of reaction pathways (Bell et al., 2012; Bleif et al., 
2012; Turner and Truppo, 2013; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). In particular, P450s are 
uniquely capable of catalyzing very stereospecific hydroxylations of complex molecules like 
steroids and antibiotics (Podust and Sherman, 2012). Moreover, they have been found to 
catalyze unusual reactions like the nitration of tryptophan and the transfer of carbene for 
cyclopropanation (Barry et al., 2012; Coelho et al., 2013; Key et al., 2017). 
To date, oxidation reactions have had to be performed chemically which often requires harsh 
conditions as well as expensive, unstable or toxic oxidants and is typically not very enantio- 
or regioselective, especially towards terminal positions in a hydrocarbon chain (Scheps et 
al., 2013; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015; Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016; Hoffmann, 
Weissenborn, et al., 2016; Abdulmughni et al., 2017). This gives biocatalytic processes the 
potential to be more economically viable than traditional synthetic processes (Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015). In some cases, P450s have even been found to catalyze reactions that 
have not been achieved using synthetic chemistry, like the 15-α-hydroxylation of 11-keto-β-
boswellic acid (Bleif et al., 2012).

However, despite the advantages of P450s, very few have been implemented in industrial 
processes due to low productivities (Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015). This stems from a number of limitations, the first of which is the need for, 
and regeneration of, nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide cofactors, NADH or its 
phosphorylated form NADPH, which supply the P450 with electrons for the catalytic cycle 
(Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; Bühler et al., 2008; Bordeaux et al., 2011; O’Reilly et al., 2011; 
Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). This prevents them from being used in vitro without feeding 
these expensive cofactors or being coupled to a cofactor regeneration system (Cirino and 
Arnold, 2003; Law et al., 2006). The next limitation is that the electrons from the cofactor 
cannot be transferred directly to the heme domain of the P450 and so, one or two redox 
partners are normally required to facilitate the transfer of electrons which can further limit 
the reaction (Bühler et al., 2008; Bordeaux et al., 2011; Braun et al., 2012; Hoffmann,

These factors severely limit the potential of in vitro processes using P450s and so, whole-
cell biotransformations are often preferred because they avoid the costs involved in isolating 
the enzymes and feeding the cofactors (Zambianchi et al., 2004; O’Reilly et al., 2011). 
Whole cells also improve the stability of the P450 and can house the many components 
required for its effective operation (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; 
Bühler et al., 2008). However, there are also a number of limitations when using whole cells, 
such as substrate and product inhibition, solvent intolerance, temperature or pH intolerance 
and the stability of the cell (Doig et al., 2003; Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011). As a result, 
whole-cell P450-based biotransformations generally also have low productivities and yields, 
which limits their feasibility in industry (Law et al., 2006; Blank et al., 2008; Julsing et al., 
2008; Cornelissen et al., 2013). However, the overall productivities of whole-cell 
bioprocesses can be dramatically improved using metabolic or protein engineering and by 
developing high cell density fermentation operations with effective downstream processing 
(Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). 
New advances in the fields of metabolic engineering and synthetic biology are continually 
providing better opportunities for the production of complex natural products in versatile 
microbial hosts (Ajikumar et al., 2010). For instance, the biosynthetic pathway of the highly 
effective anticancer drug, Taxol, has been reconstructed in Escherichia coli and 
==PAGE:13==
5 
 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Huang et al., 2001; Engels, Dahm and Jennewein, 2008; Jung, 
Lauchli and Arnold, 2011). Approximately half of the steps in the biosynthesis of Taxol are 
catalyzed by P450s and so the expression of these enzymes in vivo is vital for Taxol 
production, especially since the P450 activity has often been found to be the limiting factor 
(Ajikumar et al., 2010). One of the most successful instances where P450s have already 
been applied in industry is the production of artemisinic acid, a precursor of the anti-malarial 
drug artemisinin, in S. cerevisiae at concentrations of up to 25 g/L (Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 
2011; Paddon et al., 2013). 
Alkane hydroxylases from the CYP153 family of P450s have been found to convert medium 
chain length alkanes to their corresponding alcohols, as part of the first step in alkane 
metabolism in their native organisms (Bordeaux et al., 2011; Scheps et al., 2011). This 
family of P450s has also been used for numerous other bioconversions like the production 
of perillyl alcohol from limonene using CYP153A6 expressed in Pseudomonas putida 
(Bordeaux et al., 2011). To investigate whole-cell P450-based biotransformations 
experimentally, a model system was selected that uses an artificial fusion protein expressed 
in E. coli. Fusion proteins, which combine the heme domain of a P450 with its redox partner 
in a single protein, are typically easier to express and allow more efficient electron transfer, 
while E. coli is often used in research due to the ease with which it can be genetically 
modified (Scheps et al., 2013). The selected fusion protein, CYP153A-CPR, consists of the 
heme domain of CYP153A from Marinobacter aquaeolei and the reductase domain of 
P450BM3 (CYP102A1) from Bacillus megaterium as shown in Figure 3 (Scheps et al., 2013; 
Lundemo et al., 2015).

Terminal fatty acid hydroxylation is one of the most difficult reactions to be catalyzed by 
P450s since the terminal carbon is much less reactive than its neighbors and so it must be 
isolated within the enzyme so that it alone interacts with the heme domain (Jung et al., 2016; 
Hsu et al., 2017). For this reason, enzymes lacking the specialized structures of P450s are 
typically only able to oxidize the sub-terminal carbons of fatty acids. CYP153A catalyzes the 
highly regioselective terminal hydroxylation of medium to long chain length fatty acids (C9:0 
– C20:0) to produce terminally hydroxylated fatty acids (ω-OHFAs) that are of commercial 
interest as building blocks in the production of biopolymers, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics and 
fragrances (Scheps et al., 2013; Lundemo et al., 2015; Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 
2016; Kirtz et al., 2016; Notonier et al., 2016). In particular, CYP153A-CPR has been found 
to exhibit 95% ω-regioselectivity when using lauric acid (C12:0) as substrate and has resulted 
in conversions of up to 63% (Scheps et al., 2013; Notonier et al., 2016). This is 
advantageous since the chemical routes used to synthesize these compounds are often 
very unselective, especially if the fatty acid is unsaturated,  and they require many steps as 
well as harsh chemical reagents (Labinger, 2004; Scheps et al., 2013; Lundemo et al., 2015; 
Duan et al., 2016; Jung et al., 2016). These chemical routes include hydrocracking ω-
hydroxyl-γ-butyrolactones and ω-acyloxyalkyl-γ-butyrolactones in the presence of hydrogen 
or reducing 2-tetrahydrofuran-undecanoic acid and opening its ring in the presence of both 
a hydrogenation and a solid acid catalyst (Liu et al., 2011).  
Interestingly, despite the significant amount of research that has already been done on 
cytochrome P450 monooxygenases, there is still very little data on the effect that dissolved 
oxygen concentration has on the reaction. Therefore, the aim of this investigation was to 
determine whether the concentration of dissolved oxygen affects the performance of a 
resting whole-cell P450-based biotransformation. The target reaction was chosen to be the 
conversion of lauric acid into 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid by CYP153A-CPR G307A 
expressed in E. coli BW25113 ∆fadD.

2. Properties of P450s 
Although they have much potential for industrial application, cytochrome P450 
monooxygenases have many limitations that have historically prevented their use in industry 
(Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). For instance, their specific activities and expression levels 
are usually quite low, they are frequently insoluble and membrane bound, they are 
dependent on electron transfer partners which may also be membrane bound, and they 
require stoichiometric amounts of expensive NAD(P)H (Wichmann and Vasic-Racki, 2005; 
Urlacher and Girhard, 2006; Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; O’Reilly et al., 2011; Bleif et 
al., 2012). Furthermore, oxygenases are typically less stable than other types of enzymes 
like hydrolases, isomerases and lyases (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). These limitations can 
lead to low catalytic rates, uncoupling of the enzyme reaction and electron transfer, and the 
occurrence of multiple undesired oxidations (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Cornelissen et al., 
2011). Unfortunately, these limitations cannot be targeted independently. For instance, 
improving the specific activity without reducing uncoupling would result in the formation of 
large amounts of reactive oxygen species that would compromise stability in a whole-cell 
system (Hoffmann, Weissenborn, et al., 2016). 
2.1 Classification 
Individual P450s can often catalyze multiple reactions and so the traditional method of 
naming enzymes is not very useful in this instance. Instead, P450s are named according to 
their structural homology as follows: the name begins with the abbreviation CYP, followed 
by a number representing the protein family, a letter representing the subfamily and a 
number representing the individual protein within that subfamily (Hannemann et al., 2007). 
Due to the large number of known P450s, they have been grouped further into 10 classes 
based on the arrangement of their electron transport chains, as shown in Figure 4 with an 
example of a P450 that belongs to each class (O’Reilly et al., 2011; Lundemo and Woodley, 
2015). Each class is described briefly in the following sections based on a review by 
Hannemann et al (2007).

2.1.1 Class I 
P450 systems in this class are comprised of three separate proteins, namely a reductase 
containing flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) which transfers electrons from NAD(P)H to a 
an iron-sulfur ferredoxin (Fdx) which subsequently reduces the P450 itself (Werck-Reichhart 
and Feyereisen, 2000). This class contains most bacterial P450 systems, including the 
CYP153 family, and the mitochondrial P450 systems of eukaryotes. CYP153A from 
Marinobacter aquaeolei depends on a ferredoxin reductase/ferredoxin pair (Fdr/Fdx) to 
transfer electrons from NADPH in its native host (Scheps et al., 2013). In bacteria, all three 
protein components are soluble, however, in eukaryotes only the ferredoxin is soluble and 
the other components are associated with, or bound to, the inner membranes of the 
mitochondria (Hannemann et al., 2007). One of the most widely studied P450s in this class 
is P450cam (CYP101) which catalyzes the first step in the breakdown of camphor in 
Pseudomonas putida. 
2.1.2 Class II 
This class contains most eukaryotic P450 systems, which are located in the endoplasmic 
reticulum, and are comprised of two membrane bound proteins (Hannemann et al., 2007). 
In these systems, cytochrome P450 reductase (CPR), a diflavin reductase partner 
containing FAD and flavin adenine mononucleotide (FMN), transfers electrons from NADPH 
to the P450. CYP3A4, found in human livers, is a significant enzyme in this class because 
it is one of five P450s that collectively metabolize 90% of the pharmaceuticals in use today 
(Lynch and Price, 2007; Guengerich, Waterman and Egli, 2016). 
2.1.3 Class III 
Class III P450 systems are very similar to those in Class I, however, instead of a ferredoxin 
to transfer electrons from the FAD-containing reductase to the P450, Class III systems use 
a flavodoxin which contains FMN. The only P450 found to naturally use this system so far 
is P450cin (CYP176A1) from Citrobacter braakii (Hawkes et al., 2002; Hannemann et al., 
2007; Stok et al., 2016). 
2.1.4 Class IV 
Class IV is reserved for thermophilic P450s, which are extremely rare. CYP119 was the first 
of these enzymes discovered in the extreme acidothermophilic archaeon, Sulfolobus 
solfataricus (Wright et al., 1996). It is smaller in size than other P450s and highly stable at 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
temperatures of up to 91 °C as well as pressures of up to 200 MPa. It is possible that these 
properties are due to the higher density of salt bridges in the protein, which may aid towards 
engineering other P450s to be more thermostable (Hannemann et al., 2007; Dydio et al., 
2017). The redox chain is comprised of a ferredoxin and a 2-oxo-acid:ferredoxin reductase 
(OFOR) making CYP119 the first P450 to not receive electrons from an NAD(P)H-
dependent flavoprotein. 
2.1.5 Class V 
Class V systems are comprised of two separate proteins: an NAD(P)H-dependent reductase 
and a fusion of the P450 and a ferredoxin in a single protein (Hannemann et al., 2007). The 
only P450 to use this system is CYP51, which is also the only P450 found in Methylococcus 
capsulatus (Jackson et al., 2002). 
2.1.6 Class VI 
Class VI P450 systems are composed of an NAD(P)H-dependent flavoprotein reductase 
and a fusion of the P450 and a flavodoxin containing FMN (Hannemann et al., 2007). The 
first known enzyme to use this system was encoded by the gene xplA in Rhodococcus 
rhodochrous 11Y (Seth-smith et al., 2002; Zhang et al., 2017). 
2.1.7 Class VII 
P450s in this class are C-terminally linked to a phthalate dioxygenase reductase (PFOR) 
domain to form a single fusion protein that is catalytically self-sufficient, which means that 
they only require NADPH and no other electron transfer proteins (Ciaramella, Minerdi and 
Gilardi, 2016). PFOR is comprised of an FMN-binding domain, an NADPH-binding domain 
and a ferredoxin domain (Hannemann et al., 2007). P450RhF (CYP116B2) from 
Rhodococcus sp. and other members of the CYP116 family have this structure (Roberts et 
al., 2002). 
2.1.8 Class VIII 
P450s in this class, most notably P450BM3 (CYP102A1), are fused to CPR, which allows 
them to be catalytically self-sufficient (Hannemann et al., 2007). In these systems, NADPH 
reduces the FAD cofactor in CPR which then internally transfers electrons to its FMN 
cofactor so that it can reduce the P450. Such systems have been found in prokaryotes as 
==PAGE:19==
11 
 
well as some eukaryotes. P450BM3 was the first P450 discovered to be naturally fused to its 
reductase domain (Miura and Fulco, 1974). 
2.1.9 Class IX 
P450nor (CYP55) from Fusarium oxysporum is unique because it is the only known soluble 
eukaryotic P450 and it has no monooxygenase activity (Kizawa et al., 1991; Higgins et al., 
2016). It is catalytically self-sufficient, requiring only NADH to catalyse the conversion of two 
molecules of nitric oxide to nitrous oxide (Hannemann et al., 2007). 
2.1.10 Class X 
Class X contains P450s that catalyze substrate conversion using an intramolecular electron 
transfer system instead of via redox partners, which means they do not require oxygen, CPR 
or even NAD(P)H to function (Hannemann et al., 2007). Instead, they use acyl 
hydroperoxides as both oxygen donors and substrates, exemplified by the conversion of 
prostaglandin (PGH2) to prostacyclin (PGI2) (Ciaramella, Minerdi and Gilardi, 2016). All of 
the P450s in this class belong to the CYP74 family which are located in the membranes of 
chloroplasts and are part of the plant lipoxygenase pathway (Toporkova et al., 2017). 
2.2 Structure 
Denisov et al (2005) wrote an extensive review on the structure of P450s which found that, 
despite the substantial number of P450s that catalyze a wide variety of reactions using a 
wide variety of substrates, the enzymes often share a similar folding structure (Hasemann 
et al., 1995; Guengerich, Waterman and Egli, 2016). For instance, the fifth ligand to the 
heme iron is cysteine and this sulfur ligand is responsible for the characteristic absorbance 
of the ferrous-CO complex at 450 nm, from which the term P450 was derived (Guengerich, 
Waterman and Egli, 2016). However, although they share such similar folding patterns, there 
is a significant amount of structural diversity across different P450s, which allows them to 
bind to substrates of different sizes. Additionally, while some individual P450s are highly 
regio- and stereospecific, others can bind to multiple substrates, like human liver CYP3A4 
which is able to metabolize many of the pharmaceuticals that are currently in use. P450s 
that can bind to multiple substrates are able to do so because their active sites are flexible, 
allowing them to undergo conformational changes (Denisov et al., 2005; Guengerich, 
Waterman and Egli, 2016). Therefore, P450s do not appear to follow the typical “lock and 
key” model when it comes to substrate binding and instead follow an “induced fit” model 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
(Denisov et al., 2005). However, despite differences in substrate shape, size and specificity, 
P450s seem to utilize a common substrate binding mechanism. 
Although P450s are not very stable enzymes, their active sites have been found to maintain 
their folding structure and catalytic activity even after they have been mutated (Jung, Lauchli 
and Arnold, 2011). This tolerance to mutation of the active sites is likely due to their greater 
conformational variability compared to the active sites of other enzymes (Jung, Lauchli and 
Arnold, 2011). CYP153A catalyzes the hydroxylation of fatty acids with chain lengths from 
C9 to C20 and is over 95% regioselective towards the terminal position (Hoffmann, Danesh-
Azari, et al., 2016). The structure of the enzyme is thought to be responsible for the high 
degree of selectivity it exhibits and certain protein sites in the chain have been found to have 
a large effect on its activity, like G307 which is located in the binding pocket close to the 
heme iron (Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016). The side chain from this amino acid helps 
form the lower part of a hydrophobic tunnel within the protein in which the long alkyl chain 
of the substrate is situated when the enzyme binds to it. Another protein site of interest is 
Q129 whose amide chain has been found to make contact with the carboxyl group of the 
bound product through observation of the crystal structure of CYP153A complexed with 12-
hydroxydodecanoic acid (Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016). This positioning suggests 
that the carboxylate is anchored in the active site after the substrate has been recognized. 
For these reasons, sites G307 and Q129 have been targeted using site-directed 
mutagenesis, an iterative process whereby random changes are made to the amino acid 
sequence of the enzyme followed by screening for variants with improved traits. A single 
amino acid substitution mutant, G307A, has been found to be more active, and have a better 
coupling efficiency, than wild-type CYP153A, resulting in a better catalytic efficiency towards 
medium chain length fatty acids (Malca et al., 2012; Scheps et al., 2013; Notonier et al., 
2016). When Q129 was substituted with glutamic acid it resulted in a 5-fold decrease in 
activity, possibly due to the fact that the negatively charged side chain of glutamic acid repels 
the carboxyl group of the fatty acid substrate (Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016). In 
contrast, amino acids with positively charged side chains, like arginine, should attract the 
carboxyl group. Consequently, the substitution of Q129 with arginine (Q129R) resulted in a 
2-fold increase in the conversion of lauric acid to 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid compared to 
the wild-type enzyme (Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016). Although this mutation did not 
significantly increase the catalytic rate, kcat, (6.38 min-1) compared to that of the wild-type 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
enzyme (6.32 min-1), it resulted in a 1.2-fold reduction in the substrate saturation constant, 
KM, which caused a proportional increase in catalytic efficiency (kcat/KM) and suggests that 
the substrate binding was improved (Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016). Further protein 
engineering could be used to increase the substrate scope of the enzyme, improve regio- 
and enantioselectivity towards new substrates and make the enzyme more thermostable 
(Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; Turner and Truppo, 2013). 
2.3 Catalytic cycle 
Figure 5 shows the common catalytic cycle, steps 1 to 7, of all P450s (Denisov et al., 2005; 
Groves, 2005). Firstly, the substrate, RH, binds to the active site of the enzyme (1). Next, 
the ferric cytochrome P450 (2) is reduced to a ferrous state (3) by a reductase that derives 
an electron from NAD(P)H. Molecular oxygen is then able to bind to the ferrous heme group 
to form a ferrous cytochrome P450-dioxygen complex (4), the last stable intermediate in the 
cycle (Denisov et al., 2005). This complex is further reduced to form a peroxo-ferric 
intermediate (5a) which is subsequently protonated to a hydroperoxo-ferric intermediate 
(5b). A second protonation then occurs at the distal oxygen atom, resulting in the cleavage 
of the O-O bond to form water and a reactive oxo-iron intermediate (6). The oxygen from 
this intermediate is then transferred to the bound substrate molecule (7) which is then 
released as the product to complete the cycle. 
 
Figure 5: Catalytic cycle of cytochrome P450 monooxygenases (Denisov et al., 2005) 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
The catalytic cycle is known to contain at least three branching points that allow multiple 
side reactions under physiological conditions (Denisov et al., 2005). The first branch is the 
autoxidation of the ferrous cytochrome P450-dioxygen complex (4) which returns the 
enzyme to its resting state (2) and produces a superoxide anion. This process is known as 
chemical redox cycling (Jan et al., 2016). The second branch is known as the peroxide 
shunt, whereby the hydroperoxide anion dissociates from the heme iron (5b) to form 
hydrogen peroxide, the reverse of which is also possible. The third branch occurs when the 
oxo-iron intermediate (6) is oxidized to form water instead of carrying out the oxygenation of 
the substrate. The occurrence of these side reactions is referred to as uncoupling (Denisov 
et al., 2005). 
2.4 Uncoupling and by-product formation 
The oxidation of NAD(P)H and the formation of product can sometimes be uncoupled which 
can lead to the formation of reactive oxygen species that can destabilize the enzyme or 
degrade the heme domain, and also consumes valuable cofactors and oxygen, all of which 
reduce the amount of substrate that can be converted to the product (Bordeaux et al., 2011; 
Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; O’Reilly et al., 2011; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014; Jan et 
al., 2016). In some cases, the reactions that take place during uncoupling, like chemical 
redox cycling, outcompete substrate conversion because they have lower activation 
energies allowing them to preferentially use electrons (Jan et al., 2016). The ratio of the 
amount of substrate reacted to the amount of NAD(P)H consumed is commonly referred to 
as the coupling efficiency and should ideally be close to 100%. However, when P450s are 
presented with non-native substrates, as is often the case when attempting to develop new 
processes of industrial interest, the coupling efficiency is frequently less than 10% (Jung, 
Lauchli and Arnold, 2011). Therefore, it is critical that coupling efficiencies be improved. 
P450s activate molecular oxygen in their active sites using the iron in the heme domain as 
a cofactor, while the rest of the protein structure controls the specificity of the substrate 
oxygenation (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Activated oxygen is highly reactive which can result 
in undesirable side reactions like those related to uncoupling and even multiple unwanted 
oxidations of the substrate (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Cornelissen et al., 2011). During 
uncoupling, the reduced oxygen is not used to oxidize the substrate but instead to form 
water, toxic hydrogen peroxide or superoxide anions. This is very problematic for whole-cell 
==PAGE:23==
15 
 
biotransformations since it consumes cofactors and oxygen that could go towards substrate 
conversion, thereby increasing the need for cofactor regeneration as well as the oxygen 
demand for production (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Superoxide anions produced by 
chemical redox cycling can also be converted to hydrogen peroxide, by superoxide 
dismutase, which, in the presence of transition metals, can form hydroxyl radicals that cause 
cell damage (Jan et al., 2016). E. coli is able to repair the damages of hydrogen peroxide 
when its concentration is less than 50 µM, however, Scheps et al (2013) measured 
concentrations of up to 274 µM after a 20 h biotransformation of cells expressing CYP153A-
CPR. These concentrations can cause the P450 to lose its heme domain, which is critical to 
the catalytic cycle, and resulted in a 20% loss of biocatalyst activity over the course of the 
reaction (Scheps et al., 2013). Furthermore, superoxide anions have also been found to 
react with nitric oxide to form peroxynitrite which can induce nitrostative stress in a host cell 
(Jan et al., 2016). 
Uncoupling is frequently caused when compounds that have similar structures to the 
substrate, including the product in many cases, enter the active site but do not fit as well as 
the substrate (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). In cases where uncoupling is induced by the 
product of the biotransformation, in situ product removal can be used to reduce its likelihood. 
Uncoupling can also result from mutations of the enzyme, possibly as a side effect of 
directed evolution (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). However, some mutations have also been 
found to reduce uncoupling in P450cam and directed evolution of P450BM3 has improved the 
coupling efficiency during the hydroxylation of propane (Fasan et al., 2007, 2008). 
Additionally, Scheps et al (2013) found that the coupling efficiency of CYP153A-CPR G307A 
was higher than that of the wild-type enzyme. This shows that mutagenesis of the active 
sites can help stabilize the substrate or improve binding efficiency (Scheps et al., 2013). For 
instance, inserting bulkier amino acids close to the active site can help to displace water 
molecules which usually allows for better coupling (Malca et al., 2012). Alternatively, 
adjusting the length of the peptide linkers in fusion proteins could also help to reduce 
uncoupling, by allowing greater flexibility so that the protein can achieve the conformation 
required to catalyze the desired reaction (Scheps et al., 2013).  
Apart from the side reactions that occur during uncoupling, P450s can sometimes catalyze 
multiple oxidations of their hydrocarbon substrates to form unwanted byproducts, like 
==PAGE:24==
16 
 
dihydroxy-, keto- or ketohydroxy acids, typically under excess oxygen (Schneider et al., 
1999; Bühler and Schmid, 2004). These byproducts are produced even when the enzymes 
are purified, indicating that they are not formed by other oxidoreductases in whole cells or 
cell lysates (Scheps et al., 2011). The side reactions can either be distributive, oxidizing the 
substrate at multiple locations along the carbon chain, or sequential, where the desired 
product is oxidized further in a step-wise manner (Le-huu et al., 2016; Khatri, Schifrin and 
Bernhardt, 2017). P450BM3 has been found to perform multiple oxidations resulting in varied 
product profiles that depend on the dissolved oxygen concentration during the 
biotransformation (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). CYP153A has been found to produce 
undesired aldehydes and fatty acids when the chain length of the substrate increases 
(Scheps et al., 2011). It has also been found that CYP153A-CPR can further oxidize ω-
OHFAs to form α,ω-dicarboxylic acids (Lundemo et al., 2015). Likewise, fatty acid 
hydroxylases from the CYP4 family have been found to do the same but can also further 
transform the α,ω-dicarboxylic acids into glucuronides or esterify them to form glycerolipids 
(Hsu et al., 2017). Performing the biotransformation under oxygen limited conditions usually 
allows the regioselectivity to be controlled to prevent the production of such unwanted 
byproducts (Schneider et al., 1999). 
2.5 Cofactor dependence and electron transfer 
Most P450s are external monooxygenases which require the transfer of two electrons from 
a cofactor, for oxygen activation and substrate conversion, via proteins that act as redox 
partners (Bell et al., 2012; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). Therefore, the protein-protein 
interactions and electron transfer between the P450 and its redox partners, which is often 
rate-limiting, are vital steps for efficient substrate conversion (O’Reilly et al., 2011; Bernhardt 
and Urlacher, 2014; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). The expression level of the redox 
partners will also contribute to the observed activity of the P450, since, in most cases, an 
equimolar ratio is typically required (Braun et al., 2012; Scheps et al., 2013). However, when 
expressing plant P450s in bacteria it has been found that lower CPR to P450 ratios are 
better (Biggs et al., 2016). The proximity of the P450 to its redox partners may also have a 
large effect on the rate at which the P450 is reduced. For instance, steroidal P450s have 
been found to bind tightly to their redox partners, resulting in increased product formation 
rates (Denisov et al., 2005). 
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
In most bacterial P450 systems, a reductase that is dependent on FAD accepts two 
electrons from the cofactor and transfers them to a redoxin, usually a ferredoxin or an FMN 
flavodoxin which then goes on to reduce the P450 itself (Bell et al., 2012). Identifying these 
redox partners is often a major challenge in developing P450-based processes because the 
genes for the electron transfer proteins are often not found close to the gene for the P450  
(Hannemann et al., 2007; Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; Bell et al., 2012; Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015; Khatri, Schifrin and Bernhardt, 2017). Furthermore, even if the correct 
partners have been identified, they are often unstable and it can be difficult to express them 
heterologously (Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; Bell et al., 2012). This is usually due to a 
failure to incorporate, or retain, key prosthetic groups, like FAD, FMN and Fdx, which are 
critical to their function (Scheps et al., 2013). In eukaryotes, it is often easier to identify the 
correct redox partners since most of them interact with a ferredoxin and a ferredoxin 
reductase or with CPR (Hannemann et al., 2007; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). However, 
microbial P450s are typically preferred in industry because they are easier to handle and 
they do not need to be bound to a membrane (Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016; Lee et 
al., 2016). 
For cases where the native redox partners cannot be identified, studies have been done 
using surrogate electron transfer partners, the most common of which are the putidaredoxin 
reductase/putidaredoxin pair (Pdr/Pdx), the adrenodoxin reductase/adrenodoxin pair 
(Adr/Adx) and the FMN-ferrodoxin domain of P450Rhf (Lawson et al., 2004; Jung, Lauchli 
and Arnold, 2011; Bell et al., 2012; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015; Khatri, Schifrin and 
Bernhardt, 2017). However, the use of these surrogates often results in far lower P450 
activities compared to cases where the native electron transfer partners are used since the 
interactions during protein binding and electron transfer are very specific (Bell et al., 2012; 
Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). Nevertheless, progress is being made toward overcoming 
these recognition barriers (Bell et al., 2012). In recombinant whole-cell systems the source 
of the redox partners can have a significant effect on the activity of the P450, as was found 
to be the case when co-expressing bovine adrenodoxin with CYP11B1 in 
Schizosaccharomyces pombe, which resulted in a roughly 3-fold increase in activity despite 
S. pombe having its own native redox system (Hakki et al., 2008). However, it has also been 
found that co-expression of different redox partners may affect the substrate conversion, 
selectivity and product profile of the P450 (Neunzig et al., 2013; Khatri, Schifrin and 
==PAGE:26==
18 
 
Bernhardt, 2017). Also, when using the same redox partner, different product profiles were 
observed during in vitro and in vivo experiments, likely due to the significantly lower 
expression of the redox partner in whole cells (Khatri, Schifrin and Bernhardt, 2017).  
Some P450s are effective peroxygenases, which allow them to use hydrogen peroxide, or 
other hydroperoxides, to directly oxidize the substrate via the peroxide shunt pathway 
without requiring any electrons from NAD(P)H, as was observed when using CYP107A1 
from Streptomyces peucetius to catalyze the dealkylation of 7-ethoxycoumarin (Cirino and 
Arnold, 2003; Niraula et al., 2011; O’Reilly et al., 2011; Bleif et al., 2012). This means that 
the reductase domain, NAD(P)H and oxygen are not required for these enzymes to function, 
which is advantageous, however, they are often less stable (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). For 
instance, it has been shown that P450BM3 and P450cam can be modified to utilize hydrogen 
peroxide as the oxidizing agent, however, they quickly became inactive due to 
destabilization of the heme domain induced by the hydrogen peroxide (Bühler and Schmid, 
2004; Bleif et al., 2012; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). As such, the concentration of 
hydrogen peroxide must be kept very low or it must be generated in situ from molecular 
oxygen, for which a number of techniques are being investigated (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; 
Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). In the meantime, the use of natural P450 peroxygenases, 
like those of the CYP152 family, are more practical for industrial application as they are 
better adapted to the presence of hydrogen peroxide (Matsunaga et al., 1997; Imai et al., 
2000; Girhard et al., 2007; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). 
A common method of overcoming the need to feed expensive NAD(P)H to a P450-based 
process is to regenerate it in vitro or in vivo from NAD(P)+ using additional enzymes, like 
glucose 6-phosphate dehydrogenase or formate dehydrogenase, after the cofactor has 
been consumed by the P450 system for substrate oxidation (Ahmed et al., 1999; Falck et 
al., 2001; Bleif et al., 2012; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). Alcohol dehydrogenases are 
especially useful since the alcohols they require as co-substrates may act as a solvent for 
the substrates of the P450, which are often hydrophobic (Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). 
The disadvantage with this technique is that additional substrates are required (Bleif et al., 
2012).  
Metabolic engineering of E. coli has resulted in up to 6-fold improvements in NADPH 
regeneration rates (Lee et al., 2013).  However, it may be preferable to engineer NADPH-
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
dependent P450s to utilize NADH instead because it is cheaper and more stable (O’Reilly 
et al., 2011). This has already been accomplished for P450BM3 during the hydroxylation of 
myristic acid (Maurer et al., 2005). Furthermore, it has been found that E. coli BW25113 
contains about twice as much NADH as NADPH, making it less likely to be depleted (Jung 
et al., 2016).  
An alternative method for regenerating the required NAD(P)H would be to do so directly 
using an electrochemical method (Shumyantseva, Bulko and Archakov, 2005; Schneider 
and Clark, 2013; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). For instance, the terminal hydroxylation of 
lauric acid has been achieved electrochemically using cobalt (II) sepulchrate trichloride as 
a mediator because of its reducing ability (Faulkner et al., 1995; Estabrook et al., 1996). 
However, due to the limited stability of the enzymes at the surface of an electrode, such 
methods are not yet applicable in industry (Bleif et al., 2012; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). 
It is thought that the low stability results from the formation of hydrogen peroxide on the 
electrode (Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). Additionally, the regeneration of NAD(P)H at the 
electrode is usually accompanied by a number of side reactions that can only be avoided in 
the presence of certain organometallic complexes (Bleif et al., 2012). Research is being 
done to modify the surfaces of the electrodes or immobilize the enzymes in such a way that 
they remain stable for longer (Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). 
Finally, it may be possible to design synthetic cofactors, which have higher stabilities and 
similar activities to natural nicotinamide cofactors, as a cheaper source of electrons (O’Reilly 
et al., 2011). 
2.6 Fusion proteins 
Some P450s have been found to be fused to their native redox partners, such as P450BM3 
which has one of the highest turnover rates of all known P450s and is currently the most 
active P450 for the hydroxylation of sp3-hybridised carbon atoms (Sevrioukova and 
Peterson, 1995; Bordeaux et al., 2011; Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; Braun et al., 2012; 
Hoffmann, Danesh-Azari, et al., 2016). Artificial fusions of P450s and their native redox 
partners have been developed for several P450s and have shown improvements in 
expression, activity, stability and coupling efficiency, due to the fact that they are only 
encoded by a single gene ensuring adequate expression of both the P450 and its redox 
partners (Scheps et al., 2013; Hoffmann, Weissenborn, et al., 2016). It has also been 
==PAGE:28==
20 
 
successfully shown that P450s from one organism can be fused with reductases from 
another (Bordeaux et al., 2011; Braun et al., 2012). This opens up a new avenue for 
optimizing electron flow through the use of artificial fusion proteins, which have already been 
designed for mammalian, plant and bacterial redox systems (Hotze, Schröder and Schröder, 
1995; Venkateswarlu, Kelly and Kelly, 1997; Hirakawa et al., 2007; Kitahama et al., 2009; 
Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). These fusion proteins are self-sufficient, since the P450 and 
reductase remain together, often allowing for improved coupling efficiencies and reaction 
rates (Bordeaux et al., 2011).  
However, it must be noted that fusion proteins do not always result in better efficiency, as is 
the case when expressing plant P450s in E. coli (Sibbesen, De Voss and Ortiz de 
Montellano, 1996; Cao et al., 2000; Biggs et al., 2016). For instance, oxidoreductases can 
be toxic to cells and so higher activities, like those achieved with fusion proteins, may 
increase the toxicity which could reduce the efficiency of a whole-cell biotransformation 
(Hoffmann, Weissenborn, et al., 2016) . Additionally, it has been found that although P450s 
that are naturally fused to their electron transfer partners typically have higher activities than 
multicomponent P450 systems, they are often more likely to catalyze multiple undesired 
oxidations at the same site on the substrate (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). The same can be 
true of artificial fusion proteins. For example, a fusion of synthetic taxadiene 5α-hydroxylase 
and a P450-reductase from a Taxus species was found to be highly efficient, achieving 
conversions of over 98%, however, it caused the production of a byproduct in equal parts to 
that of the desired product (Ajikumar et al., 2010). 
Artificial fusion proteins are often used in bacteria when the specific redox partner for the 
P450 is not known, in which case the surrogate redox partner is frequently the CPR domain 
of P450BM3 or the FMN-ferrodoxin domain from P450Rhf (Bordeaux et al., 2011; Bernhardt 
and Urlacher, 2014; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). The selection or design of the best 
possible reductase domain can have a significant effect on the activity of recombinant P450 
fusion proteins and their resulting product formation, making them more feasible for 
industrial application (Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). Ideally, it may even be possible to 
design a universal redox partner that can work with most P450s. 
Fusion proteins are covalently linked to their reductase domains via peptide linkers, the 
length of which can affect activity (O’Reilly et al., 2011; Scheps et al., 2013; Lundemo and 
==PAGE:29==
21 
 
Woodley, 2015; Hoffmann, Weissenborn, et al., 2016). It has been found that shortening the 
peptide linker by only a few amino acids has resulted in significant losses in enzyme activity 
despite increased initial rates (Hoffmann, Weissenborn, et al., 2016). In contrast, increasing 
the linker length in the artificial fusion protein P450cam-RhF resulted in a 42% improvement 
in conversion (Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011). Hoffmann, Weissenborn et al (2016) found 
that the optimum linker length for CYP153A-PFOR fusion constructs is between 11 and 18 
amino acids. One variant, with a linker length of 18, was found to be 2-fold more stable than 
other variants with no loss in active heme domain after 2 hours. This suggests that fewer 
reactive oxygen species were formed to destabilize the heme domain, which was 
substantiated by a measured coupling efficiency of 94%. 
It has also been found that substituting different amino acids in the linker can affect activity, 
although to a lesser extent than changes to the linker length (Hoffmann, Weissenborn, et 
al., 2016). For instance, certain amino acids, like glycine and proline, may affect the flexibility 
of the linker and its ability to accommodate movements of the different domains. Additionally, 
glutamic acid could allow the linker to interact with positively charged amino acids on the 
P450 or reductase domains. 
2.7 Specific activity 
Specific activity is typically expressed in U/gcdw, where a unit of activity (U) is defined as 
1 μmol of substrate converted per minute (μmol/min), and significantly influences the 
productivity and biocatalytic yield of a reaction (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). The specific 
activity of a whole-cell P450-based biocatalyst is influenced by the P450 expression 
(µmolP450/gcdw) and the activity of the enzyme (U). Generally, the in vivo activity of the 
enzyme is usually lower than the in vitro activity because of limitations in cofactor supply 
and electron transport in whole cells. Overexpressing a P450 too much can result in heme 
depletion which can stress cells (Michener, Nielsen and Smolke, 2012). Typically, an 
overexpressed protein can only account for up to 12.5% of the dry weight of the cell (Pflug, 
Richter and Urlacher, 2007; Tufvesson et al., 2011). This means that if any additional 
proteins, like the electron transfer partners or a substrate transporter, also need to be 
overexpressed, the P450 expression will be reduced (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). 
However, the expression levels of these additional proteins also greatly influence 
==PAGE:30==
22 
 
production, potentially allowing similar product concentrations to be achieved despite lower 
P450 expression. 
The specific catalytic rate, kcat, of an oxygenase, which is typically lower than that of most 
hydrolytic enzymes, is a crucial factor that determines the maximum productivity of a 
biocatalytic process, while other factors like inhibition or cofactor regeneration determine 
how close the actual productivity is to this maximum (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). The 
dependence of P450s on NAD(P)H for electrons puts a limit on how much of the enzyme 
can be expressed in a whole-cell system before cofactor regeneration limits the apparent 
kcat (Baldwin and Woodley, 2006). Oxygenases that are not dependent on NAD(P)H, like 2-
nitropropane dioxygenase and protocatechuate-3,4-dioxygenase, have been found to have 
substantially larger turnover numbers than NAD(P)H-dependent oxygenases; with kcat 
values on the order of 10 to 1000-fold greater due to the absence of a slow electron transfer 
step (Duetz, van Beilen and Witholt, 2001; Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Baldwin and Woodley, 
2006). Most P450s have kcat values ranging from 1 to 300 min-1; however, there are 
exceptions, like P450BM3 and P450cam, which have kcat values ranging from 1000 to over 
3000 min-1 (Fulco, 1991; Peterson et al., 1997; Cirino and Arnold, 2003). This makes 
P450BM3 and P450cam very attractive for use in industry, however, they cannot possibly cover 
the entire range of substrates that make P450s such a versatile class of enzymes and so 
other P450s will have to be developed for industrial use (Jung, Lauchli and Arnold, 2011; 
Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). For instance, CYP153A family has a relatively low activity 
compared to engineered P450BM3 but its high regioselectivity towards terminal hydroxylation 
of fatty acids makes it desirable for industry (Scheps et al., 2011).  
One reason that P450s typically have low specific activities is that when the substrate is 
bound to the enzyme, it is positioned away from the active site which is not ideal for 
hydroxylation (Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). The specific activity of P450BM3 has been 
improved by site-directed mutagenesis and directed evolution resulting in a 9000-fold 
increase in its catalytic efficiency (kcat/ Km) during the hydroxylation of propane (Bühler and 
Schmid, 2004; Fasan et al., 2008; O’Reilly et al., 2011; Bornscheuer et al., 2012). P450BM3 
naturally hydroxylates long chain fatty acids but has also been engineered to hydroxylate a 
number of other substrates that the wild-type cannot, such as shorter chain fatty acids, 
alkanes, aromatics and even alicyclic compounds like cyclododecane (Bühler and Schmid, 
2004; Weber et al., 2011). For instance, one variant of P450BM3 exhibited a 2-fold increase 
==PAGE:31==
23 
 
in its activity towards lauric and palmitic acid after 11 amino acid substitutions (Glieder, 
Farinas and Arnold, 2002). Protein engineering has also been used to improve other P450 
catalyzed processes, like the 25-hydroxylation of vitamin D3 which was made 400-fold more 
efficient (Hayashi et al., 2008, 2010). Additionally, site-directed mutagenesis was used to 
achieve a 15-fold increase in the activity of CYP119 during the hydroxylation of lauric acid 
(Koo et al., 2002). This shows that when substrate conversion is limiting, it can be overcome 
by rational design or directed evolution of the P450, two processes which are illustrated in

3. Whole-cell P450-based biotransformations 
Growing or resting whole-cell systems are currently the most favorable and economical 
options for processes that utilize P450s as biocatalysts primarily due to the fact that, in a 
whole cell, cofactors can be regenerated in situ by metabolism, usually via the pentose 
phosphate pathway, tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle or transhydrogenase system (Schneider 
et al., 1999; Scheps et al., 2013; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015; Lundemo et al., 2015). They 
also provide a protected environment, which helps stabilize the P450s, making whole-cell 
biotransformations a simple means of overcoming some of the biggest limitations of these 
enzymes (Bleif et al., 2012; Bornscheuer et al., 2012; Braun et al., 2012; Scheps et al., 2013; 
Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). Additionally, reactive oxygen species formed as a result of 
uncoupling, which can reduce the stability of the P450, can be decomposed by enzymes 
commonly present in whole cells, like catalase, glutathione peroxidase and peroxiredoxins 
(Cornelissen et al., 2013; Scheps et al., 2013; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014; Jan et al., 
2016). Furthermore, the use of whole cells avoids the need to purify the enzymes which 
would add a number of steps to, and increase the cost of, the overall process, especially if 
the purified enzymes cannot be recycled due to low stability (Schneider et al., 1999; 
Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). Moreover, it is easier to co-express the electron transfer 
partners of the P450 in a whole cell than to implement a multicomponent system in vitro 
(Cornelissen et al., 2011, 2013). However, by using whole cells the physiology of the cell 
becomes another constraint on the efficiency of the overall biocatalyst (Cornelissen et al., 
2011).  
Whole-cell biocatalysts can be used in fermentative processes, whereby cells convert a 
substrate like glucose into biomass and a valuable product, or in biotransformation 
processes in which one substrate is utilized by the cells for growth and maintenance while 
another is converted to a valuable product (Blank et al., 2008). If the host can utilize different 
substrates, the choice of substrate can have a significant effect on the process because it 
may allow the growth of the cell to be decoupled from either enzyme synthesis or production 
so that growth does not compete for the same substrate (Cornelissen et al., 2011). In the 
current investigation, biotransformations were performed using glucose and glycerol as the 
substrates for cell maintenance and lauric acid as the substrate for the P450.

3.1 Growing cells vs resting cells 
Growing cells are typically preferred for expressing highly unstable enzymes, since they can 
continue to express the enzyme during the biotransformation and typically have higher 
biocatalyst activities than non-growing cells (Braun et al., 2012). Additionally, growing cell 
processes are often simpler since the growth of the cells and biocatalysis take place 
simultaneously, as shown in Figure 7A (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). However, the 
accumulation of biomass also increases the cost of aeration and downstream processing 
(Li, Jendresen and Nielsen, 2016). Furthermore, growing cells consume large amounts of 
cofactors for metabolic processes like oxidative phosphorylation. Therefore, the expression 
of an NAD(P)H-dependent P450 will impact the metabolism of the cell, and vice versa, which 
could lead to reduced biomass growth rate and yield, as well as stress the cells and reduce 
their viability (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Bühler et al., 2006, 2008). In some cases it has 
even been found that the production and presence of active NADPH reduces growth yields 
and metabolic activity (Bühler et al., 2006). Lastly, growing cells are much more likely to be 
limited by the availability of substrate (Cornelissen et al., 2011).

If growth could be decoupled from product formation, as shown in Figure 7B, it would allow 
each process to be optimized independently of one another, however, this requires a 
==PAGE:34==

mechanism for inhibiting growth during the biotransformation (Braun et al., 2012). The 
growth of cells requires the regulation and careful coordination of many cellular activities (Li, 
Jendresen and Nielsen, 2016). The interruption of any of these processes can inhibit growth, 
by exposing the cells to antibiotics or toxins for instance. However, this tends to reduce the 
viability of the cells which is undesirable for whole-cell biocatalysis. Starving the cells of 
amino acids also inhibits growth but reduces metabolic activity. Therefore, what is required 
is a way of inhibiting growth whilst maintaining metabolic activity. In natural environments, 
cells can become nutrient limited and so they have evolved regulatory mechanisms to adjust 
to changes in nutrient availability. However, cells will eventually stop growing and enter a 
stationary phase, which protects the cell, after they have been deprived of key elements for 
a prolonged period of time. Cells in this state are referred to as resting cells. Traditionally, 
growth and protein expression are inhibited by starving cells of nitrogen, which prevents 
them from producing proteins. However, Li, Jendresen and Nielsen (2016) found that when 
nitrogen was limited to inhibit the growth of E. coli, the mass yields of tyrosine and 
mevalonate were reduced, which showed that central metabolism was not sufficiently active, 
while the reverse was found when phosphate, sulfate or magnesium was limited. Nitrogen 
limitation has also been found to result in reduced concentrations of many intermediates in 
glycolysis, the pentose phosphate pathway and the TCA cycle, as well as reduced glucose 
uptake rates (Brauer et al., 2006; Chubukov and Sauer, 2014). Therefore, limitation of 
phosphate, sulfate or magnesium seem to be the least harmful ways of inhibiting growth so 
that production is not severely compromised. 
Biotransformations that use resting cells can be operated at much higher cell concentrations 
than growing cell biotransformations, which improves productivity (Braun et al., 2012). In 
particular, resting cells are often preferable for the research purposes because they allow 
the biotransformation to be studied in the absence of growth which allows easier 
identification of the limitations specific to the reaction (Braun et al., 2012). Resting cells also 
have lower carbon and energy demands than growing cells which allows cofactors formed 
by cell metabolism to be used primarily for the biotransformation instead of for cell growth 
(Scheps et al., 2013). However, resting microbial cells have been found to lose their bio-
oxidation activity over time, possibly due to inactivation of the oxygenase or the formation of 
reactive oxygen species by uncoupling (Bühler et al., 2008). Alternatively, this may be due 
to the cells adapting their metabolic activity to suit their current growth stage, resulting in 
==PAGE:35==
27 
 
slower cofactor regeneration (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). In contrast, growing cells have 
higher metabolic activities, which allows them to maintain high cofactor regeneration rates 
(Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Some short chain fatty acids, like octanoic acid, can cause 
irreversible damage and cell death when cells are in a resting state, however, medium and 
long chain fatty acids do not adversely affect the metabolism of resting E. coli cells 
(Marounek, Skrivanová and Rada, 2003; Scheps et al., 2013).  
There is one final consideration that must be made on a case by case basis and that is 
whether recovery of the product will require the cells to be disrupted. If so, the use of growing 
cells would be preferable, since resting cell processes already require the additional cost of 
harvesting the cells and this, together with the added cost of cell disruption, would likely 
make the process uneconomical (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). In the current investigation 
the cells do not need to be disrupted to recover the product. 
It should be note that, as the technology for protein engineering continues to improve, it may 
eventually become more practical and economical to use engineered enzymes in vitro since 
isolated enzymes typically have higher specific activities, which simplifies the purification of 
the product downstream because lower biocatalyst concentrations can be used (Schneider 
et al., 1999; Bornscheuer et al., 2012). They also have a wider range of favorable 
environmental conditions than cells, avoid any diffusion limitations through cell membranes 
and are easier to distribute around the world (Bornscheuer et al., 2012). As a result of this, 
reactions catalyzed by isolated enzymes are generally one to two orders of magnitude faster 
than whole-cell catalyzed reactions (Chen, 2007). However, in some cases cell-free extracts 
can have lower activities than living cells because the process of extracting the proteins can 
denature the electron transfer partners required by the P450 (Cornelissen et al., 2011). Also, 
in the absence of the cell, the multicomponent system is less structurally organized and the 
concentrations of the various components may be reduced, resulting in lower activities. 
3.2 Expression 
Many organisms with native P450 systems have the potential to be used for industrial 
processes, like B. megaterium which contains CYP106A2 that can catalyze the 
hydroxylation of steroids (Bleif et al., 2012). However, when using native hosts the P450 is 
typically only produced at low expression levels during the stationary growth phase and its 
expression cannot be induced. For this reason, expressing the P450 and its redox partners 
==PAGE:36==
28 
 
in a recombinant host is attractive (Bleif et al., 2012). When selecting a heterologous host, 
many factors may need to be considered, like genetic stability, membrane interactions, 
tolerance towards the substrate and product, ability to express stable recombinant proteins, 
NAD(P)H regeneration rate, by-product formation and whether it can be reused multiple 
times (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Scheps et al., 2013). E. coli is a common choice for a 
heterologous host because it grows rapidly on a range of inexpensive carbon sources, it is 
easily genetically modified and it can be used at high cell concentrations (Bleif et al., 2012; 
Scheps et al., 2013; Jung et al., 2016). The choice of host can have a great impact on the 
productivity and product profile of a reaction. For instance, the productivity of limonene 
hydroxylation to perillyl alcohol was improved when the host was changed from P. putida 
GPo12 to P. putida KT2440 and the production of by-products was significantly reduced by 
changing the host to E. coli W3110 (Cornelissen et al., 2011, 2013). 
There are, however, limitations to using E. coli. For instance, it can produce endotoxins and 
the expression of eukaryotic P450s, especially those native to plants, can be very 
challenging due to some inherent limitations of bacterial hosts (Schuler and Werck-
Reichhart, 2003). This is to be expected since the new molecular environment may differ 
greatly from that of the native host, which can affect how stable and functional the enzyme 
will be (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). For instance, bacterial hosts often have limited electron 
transfer capabilities and native P450-reductases, both of which are required by P450s, and 
because they lack an endoplasmic reticulum, there is often some translational incompatibility 
of the P450 signal modules (Ajikumar et al., 2010). Transmembrane engineering and the 
use of fusion proteins have allowed some plant P450s to be expressed in E. coli, however, 
each P450 has a unique signal sequence and electron transfer characteristics which can 
slow the development of new processes (Nelson, 1999; Chang and Keasling, 2006; Leonard 
and Koffas, 2007). Traditionally, overexpression and the use of strong promoters improve 
the performance of P450-based systems, however, when expressing plant P450s in E. coli 
it was found that lower expression levels and weaker promoters resulted in better 
functionality of the P450 and CPR (Biggs et al., 2016). 
Bacterial P450s are particularly desirable for use in industrial whole-cell biotransformations 
because they are not membrane bound and are often more stable and active than P450s 
from plants or animals (Bleif et al., 2012; Khatri et al., 2016; Abdulmughni et al., 2017). 
However, they are also typically more substrate specific which makes them less flexible 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
without further engineering while, in contrast, eukaryotic P450s often have broader substrate 
scopes but comparatively poor selectivities and activities (O’Reilly et al., 2011). For instance, 
there are eukaryotic P450s that can catalyse terminal fatty acid hydroxylation, like the CYP4 
family in mammals or CYP86, CYP94 and CYP96 in plants, but their turnovers are typically 
less than bacterial P450s, like CYP153A, and they are difficult to express in E. coli (Jung et 
al., 2016). 
It is possible to express eukaryotic P450s in the cytosol instead of in the membranes of the 
recombinant host by removing hydrophobic residues from the N-terminus, which typically 
results in higher expression and puts less stress on the system (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; 
Biggs et al., 2016). However, eukaryotic P450s typically have higher turnovers when they 
are membrane associated (Biggs et al., 2016). Genetic instability can often be overcome by 
inserting the oxygenase genes into the chromosome of the recombinant host. However, if 
the new enzymes require special functional groups, the cells may need other external 
supplements, like iron and δ-aminolevulinic acid, which are precursors of the heme domain 
in P450s (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). This is especially true when E. coli is used as a host 
since it does not contain any native P450 genes or heme proteins (Lundemo and Woodley, 
2015). Additionally, when using E. coli, a high proportion of the expressed enzyme may not 
fold correctly and will remain insoluble (Scheps et al., 2013). Aggregation of these insoluble 
proteins can trigger stress cell responses that can reduce the efficiency of a whole-cell 
biotransformation (Gasser et al., 2008; Scheps et al., 2013). However, despite these 
limitations, P450s have successfully been expressed in E. coli many times (Bühler and 
Schmid, 2004). 
If the oxygenase is expressed heterologously, the specific oxygenation activity of the cells 
can often be increased by overexpression of the oxygenase genes, however, the improved 
activity rarely exceeds that of the native host (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). In some cases, 
overexpression has improved production when using the native host, as demonstrated by a 
roughly 6-fold increase in the specific activity of P. putida for toluene degradation when 
toluene dioxygenase and cis-dihydrodiol dehydrogenase were overexpressed (Bühler and 
Schmid, 2004). However, rational metabolic engineering approaches that have historically 
been used often cannot account for factors like unknown regulatory effects, the possible 
toxicity of metabolic intermediates or pathways that may compete with, or inhibit, the 
==PAGE:38==
30 
 
production of the desired product (Ajikumar et al., 2010). However, new multivariate 
approaches can overcome these limitations and have allowed Taxol concentrations of 
roughly 1 g/L to be achieved in fed-batch fermentations of modified E. coli, where before 
they were typically less than 10 mg/L (Ajikumar et al., 2010). 
3.3 Limitations 
Generally, whole-cell biotransformations are limited by oxygen supply, product inhibition and 
the stability of the biocatalyst (Doig et al., 2003; Law et al., 2006; Scheps et al., 2013). These 
limitations are illustrated in Figure 8 below, where region A represents the area where the 
biocatalytic reaction is the rate-determining step. The reaction rate during a whole-cell 
biotransformation can be increased in a number of ways, such as by using a higher 
biocatalyst concentration. However, whole cells often require oxygen for their metabolism 
and so the reaction rate during a whole-cell biotransformation is also dependent on the 
supply of oxygen to the reactor. This is especially true when the reaction of interest is an 
oxidation, as is the case in P450-based systems, because oxygen is required not only by 
the cells but also for consumption during the reaction. Therefore, if the reaction rate is 
increased it will eventually become limited by the rate at which oxygen can be supplied to 
the reactor and enter the rate-limited region B (Law et al., 2006). Moreover, higher reaction 
rates result in higher product concentrations which can cause the rate to become limited 
due to product inhibition in region C. As such, it is crucial that the product concentration is 
maintained below inhibitory levels. This should preferably be done using some form of in 
situ product removal because diluting the biotransformation would increase the cost of 
downstream processing. Unfortunately, even if these limitations can be overcome, the 
biocatalyst loses its activity over time and so, the longer the biotransformation progresses 
the more likely it is to enter region D, where the stability of the biocatalyst limits production 
(Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; Law et al., 2006).  If the biocatalyst is very stable at the 
operating conditions, the rate at which it loses its activity will be relatively low, which means 
that the other two limitations will have a greater impact on reaction performance. However, 
P450-based whole-cell biocatalysts are typically quite unstable, often only lasting a few 
hours before they are completely inactive, which greatly limits the final product concentration 
and reaction yield (Law et al., 2006; Lundemo et al., 2015). Therefore, improving the overall 
stability of the whole-cell biocatalyst should allow higher product concentrations to be 
achieved, provided product inhibition is not limiting, which would reduce the cost of

downstream processing. These limitations will be explored in more detail in the following 
sections.

3.3.1 Oxygen supply 
In a P450-based whole-cell biotransformation, molecular oxygen is required as a substrate 
for both the enzyme and for the oxidation of a carbon source so that the host can regenerate 
NAD(P)H (Li et al., 2002; Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Additionally, the cells typically consume 
oxygen and cofactors for oxidative phosphorylation to generate adenosine triphosphate 
(ATP) for cell maintenance (Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; Bühler et al., 2006). It has been 
suggested that the cells prioritize the use of oxygen for cell maintenance and once this 
demand has been satisfied, any remaining oxygen can be utilized by the enzyme as 
illustrated in Figure 9 (Baldwin and Woodley, 2006). Microbial respiration is often unaffected

once the dissolved oxygen concentration is above a critical value, typically between 0.01 
and 0.038 mg/L for bacteria and yeasts (Shaler and Klecka, 1986).

As such, there are three possible outcomes depending on the rate of oxygen supply. Firstly, 
if the oxygen supply rate is very low, there may be insufficient oxygen for cell metabolism, 
represented by region A. In this case, the cell prioritizes the use of all available oxygen for 
maintenance, outcompeting the P450 and preventing the biocatalytic reaction from 
occurring. If the oxygen supply is increased to meet the metabolic oxygen demand, then any 
excess oxygen is now available to the P450, however, the biocatalytic reaction may still be 
limited by insufficient oxygen as shown by region B. Only once the oxygen supply rate 
exceeds the total oxygen demand, represented by region C, will the biocatalytic reaction 
become the rate-determining step.  
Furthermore, the metabolic and total oxygen demands are both proportional to the 
concentration of the whole-cell biocatalyst (Law et al., 2006). It follows that, when the cell 
concentration is low and oxygen supply is not the limiting factor, the cells are able to operate 
at their maximum specific rate as indicated by point 1 in Figure 9. However, if the cell 
concentration is increased but the oxygen supply remains constant, the biocatalytic reaction

rate will eventually begin to decrease because oxygen becomes the limiting factor as 
indicated by point 2. The oxygen supply into a reactor can be increased by raising the inlet 
mass flowrate of air, increasing the oxygen concentration of the inlet air or by raising the 
stirrer speed to improve mass transfer of oxygen into the liquid phase (Baldwin and 
Woodley, 2006; Law et al., 2006; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). These techniques can be 
used to raise the oxygen supply such that the cells can operate at their maximum specific 
rate despite a higher cell concentration, shown as point 3, however, some of these 
techniques have limitations. For instance, increasing the inlet mass flowrate of air can cause 
volatile compounds to be stripped out of the bioreactor, however, the substrates and 
products of P450s generally have large molecular weights and so are not highly volatile, 
making them less susceptible to air stripping (Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015). Nevertheless, there is also a limitation to how much air can be transferred 
to a large bioreactor before it becomes too costly. Similarly, the stirrer speed can be limited 
by the energy cost of agitating large scale reactors or simply by the design of the equipment 
(Baldwin and Woodley, 2006). Therefore, using oxygen enriched air appears to be the best 
way of increasing oxygen supply (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Baldwin and Woodley, 2006). 
The use of alternative reactor types, like bubble columns, could also help to improve oxygen 
transfer (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). 
In the event that oxygen is limiting but the supply rate cannot be improved any further, a 
different approach is needed. For instance, increasing the specific activity of the whole-cell 
biocatalyst would allow a lower cell concentration to be used, which would reduce the 
metabolic oxygen demand, thereby increasing the oxygen availability for production (Bühler 
and Schmid, 2004). However, as discussed earlier, increasing the specific activity is not a 
simple solution. Alternatively, it has long been known that, in the absence of a nitrogen 
source, cells consume less oxygen (approximately 55% less in E. coli) than cells that are 
growing in the presence of a nitrogen source (Armstrong and Fisher, 1946). For this reason, 
it may be beneficial to carry out P450-based biotransformations using resting cells, which 
have lower metabolic oxygen demands, so that more oxygen is available for product 
formation. Metabolic engineering of the host could also be done to remove any non-essential 
enzymes that consume oxygen, as long as cofactor regeneration is not compromised in the 
process.

Schneider et al (1999) performed in vitro and in vivo experiments with P450BM3 and found 
that the product profiles for both were identical under conditions of oxygen limitation and 
excess, however, oxygen excess resulted in higher productivities. Unfortunately, it also 
resulted in the formation of more by-products that had undergone multiple oxidations. They 
also found that at low dissolved oxygen concentrations (5%) the P450 expression remained 
stable, however, at 100% oxygen saturation the P450 expression had decreased by a factor 
of 10 after only 8 hours which effectively canceled out the increase in productivity. Therefore, 
operating at oxygen limited conditions appears to be preferable to avoid the formation of 
unwanted byproducts. 
In an industrial scale bioreactor, oxygen transfer is usually limited to approximately 
100 mmol/L/h. This would be sufficient for an activity of roughly 1670 U/L which for the 
production of 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid, with a molecular weight of 216.32 g/mol, 
corresponds to a productivity of 21.7 g/L/h if all of the supplied oxygen is used only for the 
catalytic reaction and the yield is 100% (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). A productivity of 
2 g/L/h is typically required for a process to be economically feasible in industry. As such, to 
reach this target, only 10% of the supplied oxygen would have to be used by the catalytic 
reaction while the remaining 90% would be available for metabolism and growth of the cells. 
In reality it is estimated that the maximum oxygenase activity of living cells in a large-scale 
reactor is limited to 500 U/L which still corresponds to a  satisfactory productivity of 6.5 g/L.h 
if the product is 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). 
The oxygen saturation constants (Km,Oxygen) of oxygenases typically range from 10 to 60 μM 
while a typical Km,Oxygen for an electron transport protein is only around 1 μM (Shaler and 
Klecka, 1986; Baldwin and Woodley, 2006; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). Therefore, when 
oxygen becomes limiting, an NAD(P)H-dependent oxygenase is often unable to compete 
for available oxygen which severely impacts productivity (Duetz, van Beilen and Witholt, 
2001). In fact, when oxygen is limiting, the reaction rate has been found to decrease 
proportionally with an increase in cell concentration which shows that the share of the 
available oxygen used for cell maintenance does not change (Baldwin and Woodley, 2006). 
Atmospheric oxygen has been found to be sufficient for saturating P450cam which has a 
Km,Oxygen of 77 μM (Purdy et al., 2004).

3.3.2 Inhibition 
In P450 catalyzed reactions, the substrate and product may be toxic to the cells, depending 
on the host, or inhibitory to the enzymes, which can impact the efficiency of a 
biotransformation (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014; Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015). For instance, β-nootkatol and nootkatone were found to be toxic to 
S. cerevisiae at concentrations of only 100 mg/L whereas the viability of E. coli is unaffected 
by these compounds at concentrations of up to 120 g/L (Girhard et al., 2009; Gavira et al., 
2013). In vitro experiments indicate that CYP153A-CPR is inhibited by lauric acid 
concentrations of over 1 mM, which is much lower than what is industrially feasible 
(Lundemo et al., 2015). Similar experiments have shown that the fusion protein is inhibited 
by 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid concentrations of 1 and 1.5 mM for enzyme concentrations 
of 0.5 and 1 µM respectively (Lundemo et al., 2015). The fact that the inhibitory concentration 
is dependent on enzyme concentration suggests that the mechanism of inhibition is the 
product irreversibly binding to the active site. 
Substrate inhibition can typically be overcome by implementing a continuous feeding 
strategy that prevents over-feeding (Bird, Woodley and Sharp, 2002; Bühler and Schmid, 
2004; Bühler et al., 2008; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). Alternatively, adding the substrate 
in its solid form is a strategy that has been used to avoid substrate inhibition, because the 
low solubility of most P450 substrates prevents large dissolved concentrations, while also 
avoiding the need for a co-solvent that could reduce the stability of the P450 (Lundemo et 
al., 2015). This strategy may be particularly useful for whole-cell biotransformations where 
the substrate is a fatty acid, like those using CYP153A, because high concentrations of fatty 
acids, 20 mM in the case of decanoic acid, have been found to damage the cell membranes 
of E. coli, which compromises their viability (Royce et al., 2013). Furthermore, the products 
of these biotransformations, ω-OHFAs and α,ω-dicarboxylic acids, are typically even more 
toxic to the cells because they are more hydrophilic and reactive (Scheps et al., 2013). 
Therefore, it is equally important to prevent large product concentrations. 
Product inhibition can normally be prevented by in situ product removal for which there are 
a number of techniques, the choice of which depends on the physiochemical properties of 
the product like volatility, molecular weight and solubility to name a few (Bühler and Schmid, 
2004; Chen, 2007; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). The most common techniques are 
==PAGE:44==
36 
 
adsorption to, or generation of, a solid phase and extraction into a second liquid phase, 
either directly or through a membrane (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Dafoe and Daugulis, 
2014). Adsorption to a solid ion-exchange or hydrophobic resin has been effectively used in 
the production of high value products like antibiotics or plant metabolites, however, its 
feasibility is often limited by the cost of the required resin and equipment as well as the 
availability of a resin with the necessary selectivity (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Adsorbent 
resins may also be used to continuously supply substrate while simultaneously removing 
product to keep both concentrations below their respective inhibitory levels (Baldwin and 
Woodley, 2006). 
Two liquid-phase systems, comprised of aqueous and organic phases, can allow higher 
concentrations of the toxic substrate to be used, since it is kept separate from the cells in 
the aqueous phase, and reduces substrate inhibition (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Braun et 
al., 2012). The use of an organic phase can also prevent air stripping of secreted compounds 
from the biotransformation, as was found to be the case with taxadiene during Taxol 
biosynthesis (Ajikumar et al., 2010). Furthermore, two liquid-phase systems can reduce 
product inhibition by extracting the product into the organic phase as it is produced (Scheps 
et al., 2013). Doing so can increase the productivity and yield of the biocatalytic reaction by 
preventing uncoupling induced by the product as well as reducing the number of steps 
required downstream and the likelihood of product degradation (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; 
Bühler et al., 2008; Braun et al., 2012; Dafoe and Daugulis, 2014). Two-phase systems can 
also be used to drive an equilibrium reaction in the desired direction, and so improve 
stereoselectivity, as long as the organic phase is carefully chosen based on the toxicity of 
the solvent towards the cells and the solubilities of the substrate and product (Bühler and 
Schmid, 2004; Braun et al., 2012; Dafoe and Daugulis, 2014).  
It has been found that adding a surfactant to a two-phase emulsion increases initial reaction 
rates which suggests that there exist some transport limitations between the phases (Ryan 
and Clark, 2008). If the two phases are in direct contact and form a stable emulsion, it makes 
it difficult to separate them. In these cases the phases should be divided by a membrane 
which also prevents the cells from coming into contact with a potentially toxic organic solvent 
and allows the two phases to have independent dilution rates in continuous or semi-
continuous processes (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Dafoe and Daugulis, 2014). However, 
membranes may limit mass transfer between the phases and the formation of a biofilm on 
==PAGE:45==
37 
 
the aqueous side of the membrane would further reduce the efficiency of the process, 
necessitating large membrane areas in industrial operations (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). 
If product inhibition can be overcome, the stability of the biocatalyst becomes a much greater 
limit to an industrial process. If the biocatalyst is very unstable, the reaction rate must be 
optimized to both improve productivity and reduce the cost of the process, since the 
biocatalyst cannot be reused, which is very challenging (Baldwin and Woodley, 2006). For 
instance, as mentioned previously, utilizing a low cell concentration often allows the cells to 
operate at their maximum specific rate which can significantly improve productivity, however, 
this may require the cells to be diluted which adds to the cost of downstream processing. 
Alternatively, to avoid diluting the cells, a higher oxygen supply rate can be used but this 
can also increase costs. Nevertheless, by employing some form of product removal and 
optimizing the cell concentration and oxygen supply it is estimated that the productivity of a 
biocatalyst could be improved 100-fold which would greatly reduce the cost of the process 
(Baldwin and Woodley, 2006). 
3.3.3 Biocatalyst stability 
In multicomponent systems, the stability of the entire system hinges on the component which 
is least stable at the desired operating conditions (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). Whole-
cell P450-based systems are dependent on many unstable components, namely the P450, 
the redox partners, the host cell itself and even the cofactors (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). 
The conditions during biotransformations, as well as during the isolation and storage of 
P450s, can often destabilize them which greatly limits their applicability in industry (O’Reilly 
et al., 2011). Lundemo et al (2015) found that 50% of correctly folded CYP153A-CPR was 
lost after just 2 h of biotransformation in a stirred bioreactor. High temperature is one of the 
factors that often compromises the stability of P450s. However, it has been found that a 
P450 from Sulfolobus solfataricus has a melting temperature of roughly 90°C due to 
clustering of its aromatic side chains (O’Reilly et al., 2011). This can help focus the directed 
evolution of other P450s for improved thermal stability. Random mutagenesis has also been 
found to produce mutants that are more stable at elevated temperatures (O’Reilly et al., 
2011). However, it is a time-consuming process to generate and screen large libraries of 
variants, most of which will have reduced activity or none at all (Bornscheuer et al., 2012).  
==PAGE:46==
38 
 
Historically, immobilization has been used as a means of improving the stability of enzymes 
but the improvement is typically too low to affect the feasibility of the process because of the 
additional cost (O’Reilly et al., 2011; Bornscheuer et al., 2012). Furthermore, immobilization 
is typically implemented with purified enzymes which means cofactor regeneration remains 
an issue (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). 
Inactivation of the P450 or its redox partners during a biotransformation may also be caused 
by reactive oxygen species that are produced by uncoupling. Therefore, improving coupling 
efficiencies should have a positive effect on the overall stability of a whole-cell biocatalyst. 
3.3.4 Cofactor regeneration 
Whole cells are typically favored for the use of P450s because they can regenerate the 
necessary cofactors, thus avoiding the extra cost associated with feeding them. However, 
the reaction rate can still be limited by cofactor depletion if the cofactors are consumed faster 
than they can be regenerated, as was found to be the case when producing 12-
hydroxydodecanoic acid with CYP153A-CPR in resting cells of E. coli (Bernhardt and 
Urlacher, 2014; Lundemo et al., 2015). This is because the native cofactor regeneration 
system is not adapted to overexpressed systems (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). To 
overcome this, the enzymes responsible for cofactor regeneration can be overexpressed 
(Mouri et al., 2006; Schewe, Kaup and Schrader, 2008; Schewe, Holtmann and Schrader, 
2009; Zehentgruber et al., 2010). Alternatively, enzymes like dehydrogenases can be co-
expressed to aid in cofactor regeneration, but this would likely reduce the amount of P450 
that could be expressed (Lundemo et al., 2015). It should be noted that initial rates in whole-
cell biotransformations have been improved by external supplement of NADPH which shows 
that the cofactor regeneration in the cell was limiting, however, this is not an economical 
solution due to the cost of pure cofactors (Lundemo et al., 2015). The best approach to 
remove this bottleneck would be to improve coupling efficiency so that the cofactor is used 
more effectively to produce the desired product (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015; Lundemo et 
al., 2015). However, these improvements cannot simply be made by process optimization 
and so further engineering of the proteins will be required (Lundemo et al., 2015). 
Theoretically, cofactor regeneration rates in metabolically active cells have been estimated 
to be between 500 and 1000 U/gcdw, which should allow similar oxygenase activities (Duetz, 
van Beilen and Witholt, 2001). However, in reality, the maximum specific activities for 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
oxygenases in whole cells are typically closer to 200 U/gcdw which suggests that cofactor 
regeneration may be limiting (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Alternatively, processes like 
uncoupling, by-product formation or increased cell maintenance at biocatalytic conditions, 
which all consume cofactors, might explain why the theoretical oxygenase activities have 
not yet been reached (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). If the substrate or product of the 
biotransformation is toxic to the cells, they may require more ATP and consequently 
consume more cofactors for cell maintenance, which reduces the amount available for the 
biotransformation (Blank et al., 2008). The rate at which cofactors can be regenerated by 
the cells can vary with the growth rate and the rate at which the substrate for growth, typically 
glucose, is taken up by the cell (Blank et al., 2008). The expression of oxygenases may 
cause an increase in the glucose uptake rate to accommodate a higher demand for cofactors 
and this would also allow faster cofactor regeneration (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Growing 
cells typically prioritize the use of cofactors for growth and so resting cells are preferable, 
because they only require cofactors for cell maintenance (Blank et al., 2008; Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015; Lundemo et al., 2015). However, a continuous supply of carbon source is 
still required by resting cells for cofactor regeneration. 
3.3.5 Substrate solubility and solvent tolerance 
P450s have been found to utilize a wide variety of substrates, including alcohols, phenols 
and detergents which are typically quite soluble in water, however, more often the substrates 
used by P450s are hydrophobic, such as lauric acid, which can be problematic for processes 
using cells adapted to aqueous environments (Denisov et al., 2005; Scheps et al., 2013; 
Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). This is due to limited uptake rates of the substrate into the 
cells, which reduces the productivity and product concentration that can be achieved in a 
biotransformation (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Braun et al., 2012; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 
2014). Some yeasts, like Yarrowia lipolytica, and some bacteria can secrete surfactants 
which allow them to directly interact with droplets of hydrophobic organic substrates like 
alkanes or long-chain fatty acids (Braun et al., 2012; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). 
However, in general, if the substrate cannot be dissolved in the aqueous phase, then it must 
be solubilized in an apolar solvent (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). Moreover, for many 
biotransformations, the substrate is required at concentrations above the solubility limit in 
water, which can be as low as 7 mg/L for medium chain length fatty acids (Braun et al., 
2012; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). As such, the substrate often has to be dissolved at a 
==PAGE:48==
40 
 
high concentration in other solvents, like DMSO, which are usually toxic to the cells, unless 
solvent resistant strains like Pseudomonas putida S12 or Bacillus subtilis 3C5N are used 
(Ruijssenaars et al., 2007; Braun et al., 2012; Siriphongphaew et al., 2012; Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015; Lundemo et al., 2015). The choice of solvent is affected by many factors 
such as its cost, whether it is toxic towards the host cell or enzymes and its solubility in water 
to name a few (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). 
Solvent tolerant strains either have the ability to adapt the composition of their cell 
membranes or they contain energy-dependent efflux pumps to remove toxic compounds 
from the cells (Ramos et al., 2002). Occasionally they even form vesicles where the toxic 
compounds are isolated from the rest of the cell (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). Random 
mutagenesis and screening have been used to produce mutants of P450BM3 that can tolerate 
DMSO and tetrahydrofuran (Wong, Arnold and Schwaneberg, 2004; O’Reilly et al., 2011). 
Alternatively, two-phase systems can be used to isolate the cells from toxic solvents while 
still allowing the substrate to be solubilized (Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). In some cases, 
cyclodextrins have been used to enhance the solubility of severely hydrophobic substrates 
so that they could be taken up by the cells, however, these have also been found to be toxic 
towards some yeast cells (Donova et al., 2007; Braun et al., 2012). 
Another problem with the substrates and products of P450s is that they can sometimes be 
volatile and flammable. Since P450s require a high oxygen input rate to function, this 
presents an explosion hazard for large scale processes which could be overcome by using 
inert solvents to transport the substrate and product (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Lundemo 
and Woodley, 2015). 
3.3.6 Substrate and product transport across the cell membrane 
Due to the hydrophobicity of the substrate and product, it can often be challenging to 
transport them across the cell membrane, depending on the host, which is why in vivo 
biocatalysis is often significantly slower than reactions with isolated enzymes (Chen, 2007; 
Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014; Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). For instance, 11-keto-β-
boswellic acid and other hydrophobic acids can cross the cell membranes of B. megaterium 
but not those of E. coli (Bleif et al., 2012). However, antibiotics like polymyxin B have been 
used to permeabilize E. coli cells, allowing diterpenes to cross the cell membrane (Janocha 
and Bernhardt, 2013). 
==PAGE:49==
41 
 
Some small nonpolar substrates can diffuse through cell membranes, in which case no 
substrate uptake system is required, and thus substrate uptake does not limit the 
biotransformation (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). However, these small molecules are often 
toxic to cells, primarily because they can cause the cell membrane to disintegrate (Bühler 
and Schmid, 2004). 
It is equally important that the product can be transported across the cell membrane to avoid 
its accumulation to levels that are toxic or inhibitory within the cell. This also allows it to be 
recovered without disrupting the cells, so that they can be used multiple times or in 
continuous processes, as only viable cells can regenerate cofactors, express proteins and 
degrade reactive oxygen species (Chen, 2007; Cornelissen et al., 2013; Lundemo and 
Woodley, 2015). Furthermore, if the cells have to be disrupted to access the product it makes 
the purification of the product downstream more complex and costly, and it means that every 
new batch must start with the growth of new cells. This makes hosts that are slow-growing 
too impractical for industrial use which could limit the range of potential products that can be 
produced using P450s (Chen, 2007). 
As enzymes are continuously improved by protein engineering it becomes more likely that 
mass transport of substrate into the cells will become the limiting factor for a whole-cell 
biotransformation (Chen, 2007). There are a number of techniques for permeabilizing cells 
to allow for better substrate and product transfer through the cell membrane, beginning with 
the use of solvents and detergents, which can be effective but typically disrupt the cells 
irreversibly. Repeated freezing and thawing has also been used to increase the permeability 
of cells but is very time and energy consuming (Chen, 2007). All of these permeabilization 
methods add additional steps to the process which is undesirable at industrial scale. 
Therefore, a more attractive way of permeabilizing the cells is to genetically modify them to 
allow transport through the membranes or to co-express membrane proteins in the host that 
facilitate the uptake of substrate and efflux of product (Chen, 2007; Cornelissen et al., 2013; 
Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). In particular, the gene AlkL from P. putida Gpo1 encodes an 
outer membrane protein that creates a channel for the potential uptake of a number of 
hydrophobic substrates, as demonstrated by a 28-fold increase in the activity of lauric acid 
methyl ester oxygenation in E. coli when AlkL was expressed (Julsing et al., 2012; 
Cornelissen et al., 2013; Scheps et al., 2013; Bernhardt and Urlacher, 2014). However, it 
also reduces the stability of the cell (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). Another outer 
==PAGE:50==
42 
 
membrane protein, FadL, facilitates the uptake of fatty acids with chain lengths of 10 and 
above, like lauric acid (Tan et al., 2017). Conversion of palmitic acid by CYP153A in whole 
cells was improved 5.5-fold by overexpressing FadL and inhibiting the expression of FadD, 
a long chain fatty acid acyl-CoA synthetase (Bae et al., 2014; Pan et al., 2017). This enzyme 
catalyzes the first step in the β-oxidation pathway, shown in Figure 10, which allows the cell 
to utilize fatty acids as a carbon source to produce acetyl-CoA (Kirtz et al., 2016; Gonzalez, 
Long and Antoniewicz, 2017). Since the reaction of interest in this investigation uses a fatty 
acid substrate, a knockout strain, E. coli BW25113 ∆fadD, was used to prevent substrate 
consumption for cell metabolism.

Lundemo et al (2015) tested sonication, acetone treatment and freezing and thawing to 
permeabilize E. coli cells and found that none of these methods increased the performance 
of 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid production by CYP153A-CPR. This suggests that the 
reaction is not limited by transport of lauric acid across the cell membrane (Lundemo et al.,

3.3.7 Product degradation 
In whole-cell biotransformations the desired product can sometimes be degraded by 
enzymes in the cell or by other chemical means if they are unstable (Bühler and Schmid, 
2004). P450s often naturally occur as part of catabolic or anabolic pathways in cells. 
Therefore, if the desired product is an intermediate in the pathway it may be degraded by 
==PAGE:51==
43 
 
downstream enzymes which is why native hosts should typically only be used if the desired 
product is at the end of its biosynthetic pathway (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Cornelissen et 
al., 2013). In some cases, the native host can be mutated or downstream enzymes can be 
inhibited to prevent product degradation, however, this can be very costly and time-
consuming (Bühler and Schmid, 2004; Cornelissen et al., 2011). Instead, expression of the 
desired P450 in a heterologous host which grows quickly on inexpensive media, and is not 
pathogenic, is often preferred to limit product degradation. However, if it still occurs using a 
heterologous host, then in situ product removal may be necessary to recover the product, 
and stabilize it in some cases, before it can be degraded (Bühler and Schmid, 2004). 
Alternatively, the heterologous host can also be genetically modified to prevent product 
degradation or, in extreme cases, the isolated enzyme could be used, but this would require 
feeding of the cofactors (Bühler and Schmid, 2004).

4. Economics 
A number of metrics can be used to indicate the likelihood that a biocatalytic process will be 
feasible at industrial scale. Reaction yield (gproduct/gsubstrate) is an important metric for all 
processes. Similarly, final product concentration (gproduct/L) is important because it has a 
large impact on the cost of downstream processing. Of course, the cost of equipment also 
influences the feasibility of a process and this is usually reflected by greater demands on 
productivity (gproduct/L/h), which is often used to scale unit operations. Productivity is a useful 
metric for both growing and resting cell processes, however, resting cell operations require 
further biocatalyst processing and so the biocatalytic yield (gproduct/gcatalyst) is typically the 
preferred metric for those systems because it represents the total amount of product that 
can be synthesized by the biocatalyst in its lifetime. As such, it is influenced by the cell 
concentration, the protein expression and the number of times the cells can be recycled. 
The target values for each of these metrics are dependent on the value of the product, 
however, Lundemo and Woodley (2015) have estimated a set of minimum values that a 
process should meet for it to be industrially feasible, summarized in Table 1. The minimum 
value for reaction yield is given as a percentage because the stoichiometry and molecular 
weights differ for all reactions. It must also be noted that the minimum value for biocatalytic 
yield of 10 g/g was estimated for high value chemicals like pharmaceuticals. Resting cell 
processes that produce lower value chemicals would require significantly larger biocatalytic 
yields to be feasible at commercial scale (Tufvesson et al., 2011).

5. Materials and methods 
5.1 Plasmids and bacterial strain 
The experimental procedures used in the current investigation are based on those used by 
Lundemo et al (2015) in experiments to produce 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid with E. coli 
expressing CYP153A-CPR with a single amino acid substitution, G307A, which improves 
activity and coupling efficiency. Since then, another amino acid substitution, Q129R, has 
been found to improve catalytic efficiency. However, experiments have not previously been 
performed with CYP153A-CPR that has both substitutions to assess whether overall 
performance is improved. Furthermore, it is of interest to assess the effect of co-expressing 
fatty acid transporters to improve substrate uptake. 
As such, three arabinose inducible plasmids were provided by the University of Stuttgart, 
the first of which, pbad33, contains the gene for CYP153A-CPR G307A as well as genes 
that confer resistance to chloramphenicol. The second plasmid is a variation of the first 
containing the gene for CYP153A-CPR G307A Q129R instead. The third plasmid, pbad 118, 
contains the gene for FadL, a fatty acid transporter, as well as genes that confer resistance 
to ampicillin. Using these plasmids, three enzyme combinations were investigated, shown 
in Table 2 below, to observe any differences in performance between the single and double 
amino acid substitutions of CYP153A-CPR, G307A and G307A Q129R respectively, as well 
as to ascertain the effect of co-expressing FadL.

5.2 Expression 
Seed cultures were grown in lysogeny broth (LB) containing 100 mg/L ampicillin and/or 
34 mg/L chloramphenicol, depending on the plasmids that were transformed into the cells, 
by inoculation with glycerol stocks stored at -80 °C. The seed culture was grown overnight 
at 37 °C and 180 rpm, so that the cells could reach the late exponential stage of growth, and 
used to inoculate either a shake flask with 400 mL working volume or a stirred bioreactor 
with 1 L working volume (Infors AG, Bottmingen, Switzerland) containing terrific broth (TB) 
with the same concentrations of antibiotics as well as 0.1 mM FeCl3. The use of a bioreactor 
instead of shake flasks is advantageous because it allows monitoring of the dissolved 
oxygen (DO) concentration and control of the temperature and pH (Baldwin & Woodley, 
2006). The cells were grown in batch mode at 37 °C until the optical density at 600 nm 
(OD600) was within the range of 0.8 to 1. The expression of the protein was then induced by 
adding 0.02% (w,v) arabinose. At the same time, the heme precursor, 5-aminolevulinic acid, 
was added to a concentration of 0.1 mM and the temperature was lowered to 25 °C 
Expression was then allowed to continue for 16 hours before the cells were harvested. 
5.3 Whole-cell biotransformation 
The cells were harvested by centrifugation at 4000 x g for 20 min at 4 °C. The resulting 
pellets were then washed with 100 mM phosphate buffer and supplemented with glycerol 
before undergoing centrifugation a second time. Finally, the pellet was resuspended in 
phosphate buffer to a cell concentration of 100 gcww/L and supplemented with antibiotics and 
FeCl3. 
The cell suspension was then mixed with phosphate buffer (pH 7.4), 20 mM glucose and 
1% glycerol such that the final cell concentration was 50 gcww /L. This mixture comprised the 
reaction medium and caused the cells to enter a resting state due to the absence of nitrogen 
and sulfates. Resting cell biotransformations were carried out in shake flasks and Applikon 
my-Control bioreactors (Applikon, Delft, Netherlands) with working volumes of 20 and 150 
mL, respectively. The biotransformations were operated at 30 °C and stirring speeds of 180 
rpm in the shake flasks and 1000 rpm in the bioreactors. The pH during the 
biotransformations was maintained at 7.4 by phosphate buffer, as well as the addition of 
5 M NaOH in the bioreactors. Furthermore, the bioreactors were constantly sparged with air 
at a rate of 0.5 vvm and the DO concentration was controlled by supplementing the feed of 
==PAGE:56==
48 
 
air with pure oxygen. The substrate, lauric acid, was pre-dissolved in DMSO, at a 
concentration of 50 mM, and added so that the initial substrate concentrations in the shake 
flasks and bioreactors were 1 mM, so as not to exceed 2% DMSO in the reaction vessels. 
The biotransformations were run for 5 hours with duplicate samples taken every hour for 
analysis by gas chromatography (GC) and single samples for analysis by high performance 
liquid chromatography (HPLC). The samples for GC analysis were mixed with 30 µL of HCl 
(37%) to stop the reaction and samples for HPLC analysis were centrifuged at 14000 rpm 
for 5 min to isolate the supernatant. 
5.4 Analytical methods 
5.4.1 P450 expression 
The concentration of correctly folded P450 can be measured by a carbon monoxide (CO) 
differential spectral assay (Scheps et al., 2013; Lundemo et al., 2015; Hoffmann, 
Weissenborn, et al., 2016). A portion of the 100 gcww/L cell suspension was lysed by 
sonication for 5 min with a cycle time of 0.3 s and an amplitude of 40%. The cell debris was 
then removed by centrifugation at 4700 x g for 45 min at 4 °C and the lysate was stored on 
ice. 
To carry out the assay, 0.35 g of sodium dithionite was dissolved in 10 mL of 50 mM 
phosphate buffer, after which the solution was split into two. One half of the solution was 
then bubbled with CO for 10 min. The cell lysate was diluted individually with each of these 
solutions in a 1:1 ratio in cuvettes and the differential spectra was measured from 400 to 
500 nm. This was done 6 consecutive times to allow adequate time for formation of the 
P450-CO bound complex that exhibits a characteristic absorption peak at 450 nm. 
Appendix 2 shows the calculations required to convert the absorbances to the corresponding 
concentration of correctly folded P450. 
5.4.2 GC analysis 
Samples were prepared by a two-stage extraction with 200 µL of tert-methyl butyl ether 
(MTBE) containing 1 mM tridecanoic acid as an internal standard. The standard curves for 
lauric acid and 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid are shown in Appendix 3, as well as the 
calculation to convert the peak areas to their corresponding concentrations. The organic 
phases were pooled and centrifuged before being left to evaporate overnight. The residue 
==PAGE:57==
49 
 
was resuspended in 60 µL of MTBE, without any internal standard, and 60 µL of N,O-
bis(trimethylsilyl) trifluoroacetamide (BSTFA) containing 1% trimethylchlorosilane (TMCS). 
The samples then underwent derivatization at 70 °C for 30 min before being analyzed by 
GC using an Elite-5 column from PerkinElmer® with helium as the carrier gas. A linear 
temperature gradient from 180 to 300 °C was used, with an injection volume of 1 µL and a 
split ratio of 20. 
5.4.3 HPLC analysis 
Samples were acidified with 5 M HCl before being analyzed by HPLC using an Aminex® 
HPX-87H Ion Exclusion Column from Bio-Rad. The mobile phase was 5 mM sulfuric acid 
flowing at 0.6 mL/min. A column temperature of 50 °C and an injection volume of 5 µL were 
used.

The highest final product concentration and initial product formation rate were achieved with 
enzyme combination A in bioreactors, both of which were significantly lower in shake flasks

typically higher due to better mixing (Lundemo et al., 2015). In contrast, the

biotransformations with combination C resulted in significantly better reaction performances.

show that the mass transfer rate of substrate into the cells does limit the reaction rate in the 
absence of a fatty acid importer. Nevertheless, even with the co-expression of FadL, the

enzyme combination A, which shows that the increase in catalytic efficiency is outweighed

the bioreactors, likely due to alkaline hydrolysis after addition of NaOH to the reaction 
mixture for pH control (Scheps et al., 2013). This can be problematic because the resting 
cells can become entrained in the foam which can reduce the cell concentration in the 
reaction medium. Therefore, pH control by base addition may need to be avoided. However, 
this could affect the rate of the terminal fatty acid hydroxylation, since it has previously been 
found that sub-terminal fatty acid hydroxylation by P450s can be pH dependent (Su, Horner 
and Newcomb, 2012).

6.3 Phase 3: Effect of dissolved oxygen concentration 
Three DO concentrations, 10, 50 and 100% based on air saturation, were investigated. 
Figure 16 shows product formation and substrate consumption over the course of 
biotransformations at these concentrations. The dotted lines in Figure 16 represent the 
product and substrate profiles that resulted from performing biotransformations with cells 
that had been stored for 24 h, where storage consisted of refrigerating and gently agitating 
cell suspensions that had been supplemented with 20 mM glucose. The storage containers 
were not air-tight and so no additional air was fed. Braun et al (2012) have shown that resting 
cells can be stable for up to 90 h. It was also found that the fusion proteins remained 
relatively stable at over the course of a week in cell-free extracts, as shown in Appendix 5. 
Table 4 shows the average final product concentrations achieved during the 
biotransformations at  different operating conditions, from which it can be seen that product 
formation appears to be largely unaffected by the DO concentration. This indicates that the 
P450 is already saturated with oxygen at DO concentrations above 10%. Therefore, it would 
seem that CYP153A-CPR G307A has a high affinity for oxygen in accordance with most 
oxygenases, which generally have oxygen saturation constants (Km,Oxygen) of less than 
100 µM. For comparison, recent studies have achieved 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid 
concentrations of 0.51 mM in batch operation and 7.86 mM in fed-batch operation (Lundemo 
et al., 2015; Bowen et al., 2016). As would be expected, fed-batch operation results in much 
better reaction performance and should be used in all future work.

Figure 15A showed that the performance of biotransformations was better in bioreactors 
compared to shake flasks. Both the shake flasks and bioreactors were operated in batch 
mode. Therefore, the only changes between the two setups that could account for the 
differences in initial product formation rate and final product concentrations are pH control, 
agitation and aeration. The majority of the medium in which all biotransformations took place 
was phosphate buffer which helps to regulate the pH, therefore it is more likely that the 
reaction rate in the shake flasks was limited by mass transfer of the substrate or oxygen. 
Figure 15B suggests that mass transfer of the substrate across the cell membrane is a 
greater limiting factor than mass transfer of the substrate from the bulk medium to the cells, 
and so it is unlikely that the lower degree of agitation in the shake flasks would be the cause 
of the reduced production. However, the lack of aeration in the shake flasks likely limits the 
DO concentration in the bulk medium drastically because oxygen can only enter the medium 
by diffusion over a small surface area. Therefore, the DO concentrations in the shake flasks 
were probably far lower than 10%. This implies that there is a threshold concentration below 
which DO does affect the reaction rate. The pentose phosphate pathway, which is primarily 
responsible for the production of NADPH, does not require molecular oxygen and so the 
reduced reaction rate in shake flasks was likely due to insufficient oxygen for the biocatalytic 
reaction and cell maintenance (Wamelink, Struys and Jakobs, 2008). Therefore, the DO 
concentration should always be maintained at a minimum of 10% to ensure sufficient oxygen 
supply, which could be very challenging at industrial scales due to equipment limitations or 
the cost of aeration. 
On average, final product concentrations of 0.64 mM and 0.61 mM were achieved with cells 
before and after storage, respectively. Although this difference is not significant, the use of 
stored cells did result in lower initial production rates, which may indicate a loss of activity 
due to instability of the cell or enzyme. At 10% DO, the substrate consumption was similar 
for cells before and after storage. However, at 50% and 100% DO the substrate was almost 
fully consumed when using cells that had not been stored. The corresponding amounts of 
product formed do not account for this degree of substrate consumption. Moreover, the cells 
cannot utilize the substrate as a carbon source via the β-oxidation pathway because a ∆fadD 
knockout strain was used. It could be that the presence of excess oxygen stimulated the 
P450 to over-oxidize the substrate, forming by-products that were not measured by the GC 
analysis. However, after storage of the cells, the slight decrease in product formation 
==PAGE:66==
58 
 
suggests that the activity of the P450 has only decreased marginally, while the larger 
decrease in substrate consumption would indicate otherwise. Therefore, it seems that the 
P450 was not responsible. Another possibility is that excess oxygen may cause the 
spontaneous oxidation of the substrate to form sub-terminally hydroxylated fatty acids, 
however, if this were the case, the same effect would have been measured for cells before 
and after storage. Therefore, it seems more likely that the substrate was consumed by native 
oxygenases within the cell that became inactivated during storage as a result of cellular 
stress. It is of critical importance that the cells remain metabolically active and do not 
become stressed since cell recycle will likely be required in industrial scale resting cell 
biotransformations to achieve economically feasible biocatalytic yields. Therefore, the 
conditions during storage will have to be optimized. 
The product formation rates drop substantially after the first three hours of the 
biotransformations, however, since the substrate and product concentrations are so low it is 
unlikely that this is caused by inhibition of the P450. Therefore, it could be that the whole-
cell biocatalyst is very unstable at the operating conditions and is almost entirely inactive 
after only three hours. 
Figure 17 shows a portion of the metabolic network of E. coli BW25113 that includes the 
metabolism of carbon sources and the oxidative branch of the pentose phosphate pathway. 
Figure 18 shows the consumption of the carbon sources, glucose and glycerol, over the 
course of the biotransformations. Table 5 shows the corresponding consumption rates. 
Glycerol is an attractive carbon source for industrial whole-cell biotransformations because 
it is very inexpensive (Yao et al., 2016). In E. coli, the first, and rate-limiting, step in the 
metabolism of glycerol is the formation of sn-glycerol 3-phosphate, catalyzed by glycerol 
kinase. Unfortunately, this enzyme is inhibited by fructose-1,6-biphosphate under aerobic 
conditions. As an alternative, glycerol can also be metabolized by glycerol dehydrogenase 
to form glycerone, which E. coli can use as its sole carbon source (Jin and Lin, 1984). 
However, the native expression levels of glycerol dehydrogenase are usually very low which 
limits carbon uptake (St Martin, Freedberg and Lin, 1977). Additionally, when glycerol is 
used as the only carbon source, the formation of NADPH is greatly reduced because the 
carbon flux from glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate to glucose 6-phosphate, the start of the 
pentose phosphate pathway, is very low (Yao et al., 2016). Because of these limitations, the 
use of glycerol as the sole carbon source normally results in low consumption rates and 
==PAGE:67==
59 
 
productivities, and so glucose is often added as a second carbon source. However, E. coli 
grows faster on glucose than glycerol and so, when glucose is available, the synthesis of 
enzymes that metabolize glycerol is inhibited so that the cells preferentially consume the 
glucose first and only metabolize other, less preferred, carbon sources after it has been 
depleted. This is known as carbon catabolite repression. But, from the results it can be seen 
that this does not appear to be the case, since both carbon sources were consumed from 
the start of the biotransformations. This shows that cells grown in TB, which contains 
tryptone, yeast extract and glycerol as carbon sources, express the enzymes for both 
glucose and glycerol metabolism simultaneously. Thereafter, the resting cells cannot 
synthesize any new enzymes because they are nitrogen limited.

In itself, the consumption of carbon sources is not problematic. However, since resting cells 
cannot grow, the carbon source is only required to produce energy for maintenance of the 
cell and for regeneration of cofactors, neither of which would account for the large amounts 
of glycerol consumed. This points towards the formation of by-products which would 
complicate downstream processing and possibly inhibit production. For instance, when the 
supply of oxygen is limiting, E. coli switches to mixed acid fermentation to derive energy 
from available carbon sources (Smith and Hippler, 2017). Alternatively, if the oxygen supply 
is adequate but the concentration of the carbon source is very high, the rate of carbon uptake 
can surpass a critical value which causes the TCA cycle and electron transport 
phosphorylation within the cell to become saturated (Sales et al., 2017). This is known as 
overflow metabolism and results in the production of acetic acid. 
Figure 19 shows the concentration profiles of formic, acetic and lactic acid during 
biotransformations in shake flasks and bioreactors at different DO concentrations. The 
production of formic and lactic acid take place during mixed acid fermentation under oxygen 
limited conditions (Knappe et al., 1974; Jung et al., 2010). A large amount of both formic 
and lactic acid were produced in shake flasks while very little of the two were produced in 
bioreactors. Furthermore, both acids appear to have been produced during storage, as seen 
from the substantially higher initial concentrations in biotransformations using stored cells. 
This confirms that oxygen supply was limiting in the shake flasks as well as during storage. 
The concentrations of formic acid can be seen to decrease after production, either due to 
evaporation or by conversion to carbon dioxide. 
Acetic acid can either be produced by mixed-acid fermentation under anaerobic conditions 
or by overflow metabolism. Since the production of both formic and lactic acid in the shake 
flasks has shown them to be oxygen limited, it is likely that the corresponding production of 
acetic acid was due to mixed acid fermentation. However, in the bioreactors, with DO 
concentrations above 10%, conditions appear to have been aerobic, since no formic or lactic 
acid was formed, and so the acetic acid was more likely produced by overflow metabolism. 
In fact, a significantly larger amount of acetic acid was produced compared to mixed acid 
fermentation.

6.4 Economics 
In order to best assess the effect of DO concentration on the biotransformations, the initial 
substrate concentration was selected to be only 1 mM so as to avoid the effects of substrate 
and product inhibition. However, this means that many of the economic metrics are 
extremely far from the targets required for economic feasibility at industrial scale, as shown 
in Table 6. For this reason, Table 6 also shows the results of 8 hour fed-batch resting cell 
biotransformations in 1 L bioreactors, to produce 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid with 
CYP153A-CPR G307A, obtained by Lundemo et al. (2015). Although these results are 
closer to each of the target values for the economic metrics, improvements of roughly 10-
fold are still required for the final product concentration and productivity. Furthermore, the 
biocatalyst yield will have to be increased almost 100-fold to meet the required target. 
However, it has been shown that the mass transfer rates of oxygen and substrate into the 
cells do not appear to be limiting, as long as DO concentration is kept above 10% and a 
fatty acid transporter is co-expressed, which suggests that the productivity was likely limited 
by the reaction rate. Therefore, since resting cells are being used, the target productivity of 
2 g/L/h could be achieved relatively simply by increasing the cell concentration during the 
biotransformation and so this metric is less of a concern for this type of operation.

Of all the metrics, the reaction yield is the closest to the required target, due to the high 
regioselectivity of CYP153A towards lauric acid, and could be further improved simply by 
optimizing the concentrations of substrate and cells (Lundemo et al., 2015). However, 
increasing the coupling efficiency through further protein engineering, to ensure optimal use 
of the available cofactor for substrate conversion, would likely have a much larger effect on 
reaction yield (Fasan et al., 2007; O’Reilly et al., 2011). This is particularly important for 
P450-based biotransformations because the substrate, product and by-products all have 
very similar structures which can make their separation downstream very complex and 
costly. Whereas, a high selectivity coupled with a high reaction yield will prevent large 
concentrations of impurities that need to be removed to isolate the product. Uncoupling can 
also cause the depletion of NADPH and oxygen, which impacts cell metabolism, and 
produces reactive oxygen species that damage the biocatalyst (Jan et al., 2016). Therefore, 
improving the coupling efficiency could also increase the stability of the biocatalyst which 
would allow longer reaction times.  
In general, the product concentrations began to level off after the first 3 hours of the 
biotransformations which suggests that production was being inhibited. Since the substrate 
and product concentrations were so low, it is unlikely that they were causing the inhibition. 
Instead, the overall stability of the whole-cell biocatalyst or reduced cofactor production due 
to glycerol metabolism may have been responsible (Lundemo and Woodley, 2015). 
Although the cells and enzymes were found to remain stable after 24 h of storage, it is 
possible that at the operating conditions of the biotransformation the stability was reduced 
enough to impact the final product concentration. For instance, the large amounts of acetic 
acid produced during the biotransformations may have destabilized the P450. The final 
product concentration could be improved by increasing the cell concentration or by 
improving the specific activity of the whole-cell biocatalyst, but increasing the stability of the 
P450 and reductase domain through further protein engineering would likely have a greater 
long-term impact (Lundemo et al., 2015). Improving the final product concentration is vital 
to reducing the cost required to recover the product during downstream processing. 
The extremely low biocatalyst yield indicates that the cells would have to recycled many 
times to meet the required target, however, due to the instability of the P450 during the 
biotransformation this would not be possible. Therefore, using a growing cell process may 
be necessary to allow continuous expression of the P450 (Lundemo et al., 2015). However, 
==PAGE:75==
67 
 
as discussed previously this would also have many disadvantages. Therefore, a better 
option for improving the biocatalyst yield is to engineer the host and P450 to be more stable, 
allowing them to be recycled multiple times. Additionally, improving the specific activity of 
the whole-cell biocatalyst would improve the biocatalyst yield. For instance, higher P450 
expression levels would result in better specific activities, and could be achieved simply by 
optimizing the growth and protein expression before the cells are harvested for the 
biotransformation, as was done in the first phase of experiments (Pflug, Richter and 
Urlacher, 2007; Zhang, Li and Xu, 2010; Tufvesson et al., 2011). Alternatively, improving 
the activity of the P450 and the rate of electron transfer would also result in better specific 
activities and this can be done by further engineering of the fusion protein (Bell et al., 2012). 
It is also possible that cofactor depletion is still a limiting factor, in which case metabolic 
engineering of the host will be required to increase the rate at which cofactors are 
regenerated or remove non-essential pathways that consume cofactors (Fasan et al., 2011; 
Lee et al., 2013). 
In general, it seems that although some improvement might be made towards reaching the 
target values for economic feasibility through optimization of the process, the majority of the 
improvements will have to be made by protein and metabolic engineering of the P450 or 
redox partners and the host, respectively.

7. Conclusions 
In conclusion, it has been found that varying the dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration 
between 10 and 100% during a resting whole-cell biotransformation has no significant effect 
on the production of 12-hydroxydodecanoic acid by CYP153A-CPR G307A expressed in 
E. coli.  However, since better reaction performance was achieved in aerated bioreactors 
compared to shake flasks, a threshold DO concentration must exist (<10%), below which 
the supply of oxygen does limit the reaction. Therefore, it is important that industrial 
biotransformations are operated with a minimum DO concentration of 10% to ensure aerobic 
conditions that not only result in better production, but also prevent mixed acid fermentation 
which produces many undesired by-products that would otherwise complicate downstream 
processing. Likewise, if the whole-cell biocatalyst is to be recycled, it must also be stored 
under aerobic conditions to avoid by-product formation that can reduce cell viability. 
Furthermore, biotransformations should be operated in fed-batch mode to avoid over-
feeding of carbon sources which should prevent overflow metabolism that produces large 
amounts of inhibitory acetic acid. 
Higher P450 expression levels and the co-expression a fatty acid importer, to facilitate 
substrate transport through the cell membrane, significantly improved the performance of 
biotransformations. P450 expression was found to be substantially higher when cells were 
grown in shake flasks compared to bioreactors, as slower growth rates allow more efficient 
protein folding. Therefore, cell growth and protein expression should be carried out in fed-
batch mode, where the feed of nutrients is used to limit the growth rate. 
Regarding the limitations of P450s, uncoupling is the most detrimental since it consumes 
NADPH to form reactive oxygen species instead of the desired product. Furthermore, these 
reactive oxygen species can then go on to form undesired by-products which will increase 
the cost of downstream processing or toxic products that can destabilize the whole-cell 
biocatalyst, reducing possible reaction times and preventing its reuse. This greatly impacts 
the economic feasibility of whole-cell P450-based biotransformations and so it is vital that 
coupling efficiencies be improved by further protein engineering of the P450 and its redox 
partners, to ensure optimal use of the cofactor for substrate conversion.

8. Future Work 
The way forward will involve work on both the process and the P450 fusion protein. Firstly, 
to optimize the process, it is recommended that further experiments be done to confirm 
whether fed-batch operation can increase P450 expression levels by limiting growth rate. 
Following that, fed-batch biotransformations, with at least 10% DO, should be carried out to 
identify a feed rate that reduces carbon uptake by the cells and prevents overflow 
metabolism. Additionally, instead of using nitrogen limitation to force the cells into a resting 
state, limitation of phosphates, sulfates or magnesium should be tested to determine 
whether this results in better performance. Aerobic storage of cells should also be tested to 
assess whether performance losses can be avoided. The stability of the P450 could also be 
determined at the operating conditions of the biotransformation, by measuring P450 
expression throughout, to ascertain whether P450 inactivation is limiting production. Finally, 
experiments with higher substrate concentrations should be carried out to determine if the 
improved process comes closer to the economic targets for industrial feasibility. However, it 
should be noted that substrate and product inhibition may become a new bottleneck in such 
experiments, in which case methods of removing the product in situ will have to be 
investigated as well.  
Regarding the fusion protein, the coupling efficiency of CYP153A-CPR G307A should be 
determined by comparing the rate of cofactor consumption to the rate of product formation 
during in vitro experiments. If the coupling efficiency is found to be low, further protein 
engineering will become a priority. Alternatively, CYP153A-PFOR fusion constructs, some 
of which have been found to have coupling efficiencies of greater than 90%, should be used 
to assess the impact of higher coupling efficiency on the performance of biotransformations.

Appendix 5: P450 stability at 25°C 
 
Figure 26 shows how the concentration of correctly folded P450 in a cell-free extract, as 
measured by the CO differential spectral assay, changed over the course of a week at 25°C 
for the single and double amino acid substituted fusion proteins. Although there is some loss 
of expression, it is not as significant as would be expected. Nonetheless, the overall stability 
of the whole-cell biocatalyst was thought to be a limiting factor during the biotransformations. 
The biotransformations are operated with a slightly higher temperature (30°C) at which the 
P450s may be less stable. However, it is more likely that the P450 is destabilized during the 
biotransformation by hydrogen peroxide resulting from uncoupling or acetic acid formed by 
mixed acid fermentation or overflow metabolism. Acetic acid was also produced during 
storage because of anaerobic conditions and so the P450 expression in Figure 26 may have
