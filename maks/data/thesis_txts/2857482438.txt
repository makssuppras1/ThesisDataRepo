Abstract
This thesis attempts to jointly utilize a foundation model and event detection for classification of
OCD within children and adolescents, in the hopes of assisting in treatment of the disease. This
study utilizes a Foundational model (TimesFM) in order to help mitigate the data scarcity problem
that exist when working with in-the-wild data in a clinical context. Additionally we propose our
own evaluation method for model evaluation when detecting events in time series.
This thesis extends previous work done within the Wrist Angel study. It is a study conducted
at Børne- og Ungdomspsykiatrisk (Child and Adolescent Psychiatry) Center, Gentofte Hospital,
where physiological data from children and adolescents are collected over 8 weeks. The previous
work shows that it is feasible to classify OCD events using in-the-wild data, while transfer learning
and simulated data improve this classification.
We investigate the possibility of zero-shot forecasting on the original events and using a classifi-
cation threshold of δ = 0.51, TimesFM achieves a precision of 94%, a recall of 50% and FDR of
11.11%. Analysis of the threshold used shows that TimesFM’s actual values for events are around
δ = 0.71. Analysis of the numerical distribution of events shows that by implementing two event
adjustment methods, LA and ELA, TimesFM achieves better dispersion and approximates values
similar to the actual values. These event adjustment methods are also used for zero-shot forecast-
ing, achieving a precision of 98%, a recall of 97% and an FDR of 2% for ELA. By using ELA data
in TimesFM, we therefore achieve very favourable results.
Lastly we propose our own model evaluation method, as previous metrics shows to either over or
underestimate event detection in time series. The evaluation is a window-wise evaluation, which
results in us decreasing the score for FDR and increasing precision, as we classify an event as true
if it is predicted within a 25-second window of the actual event. The newly proposed evaluation
method results in us achieving an FDR of 0 in some cases and very close to 0 in others. This means
by introducing the new event detection methods, we lower the FDR compared to the baseline
evaluation. We emphasize the use of FDR, precision and recall to evaluate event detection in time
series, whereas the F1 score can be difficult to use for comparison in very temporal frames and
large unbalanced datasets.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a chronic psychiatric condition characterized by the pres-
ence of obsessions, which manifest as uncontrollable and recurrent thoughts, compulsions which
are expressed through repetitive behaviors, or both. These obsessions and compulsions are com-
monly associated with a range of negative emotional states, including fear, aggression, a sense of
incompleteness, embarrassment, and sadness, as well as cognitive processes such as monitoring,
suppression, and uncertainty [Mental Health 2023]. OCD is relatively prevalent among children
and adolescents, affecting approximately 2% to 4% of this population. The disorder significantly
impairs social, academic, and familial functioning in affected individuals. Children and adoles-
cents with OCD are at increased risk of developing comorbid conditions such as depression and
social phobia, and the presence of obsessions and compulsions further exacerbates the risk of dis-
ruptions in their normative social and educational development [Storch et al. 2004].
OCD can be treated with both therapy and medication, depending on the severity of OCD symp-
toms. Therapy can include learning methods to resist the urge to perform various compulsions
[Mind 2024]. It can therefore be considered beneficial to have a tool that can give individuals
with OCD an early warning of upcoming obsessions, so the individual can prepare themselves
and thereby reduce the symptoms and obsessions’ impact on their daily lives. Several researches
highlight that replacing paper diaries with electronic versions can improve the quality of subjec-
tively reported data [Izmailova, Wagner, and Perakslis 2018]. Furthermore, and a foundation for
this study, it can be especially valuable in the case of children. Given that children often lack a
well-developed sense of time, these tools can provide crucial temporal context for their experiences
and behaviors. By providing psychologists with a comprehensive timeline of events, these tools
can enhance the effectiveness of therapy, offering a clearer picture of the frequency and patterns
of obsessive thoughts or compulsive behaviors. As the use of wearable sensors becomes more
widespread, there is an incentive to use wearable-based affect recognition systems, as it could help
to monitor the state of the patients with mental disorders and provide valuable insights for therapists
[Schmidt et al. 2019].
This study is part of a larger research initiative focusing on utilizing biosensor data to enhance the
treatment of OCD among children and adolescents. The aim is to improve the precision and effi-
cacy of mental health treatments, specifically, develop a model capable of functioning effectively
in clinical settings on previously unseen in-the-wild data. The data in the Wrist Angel study is
collected in-the-wild, using wearable sensors. Wearable sensor data typically takes the form of
multivariate time series, in which an event is therefore only considered to be a short time interval.
The scope of this thesis is therefore, to develop and apply different event label methods, which
are tested on different Times Series foundation Models (TSFM), in order to determine the effect
of the different event label methods impact on the TSFM’s ability to zero-shot forecast on in-the-
wild data. The testing is done by utilizing an already existing TSFM, more precisely TimesFM
by [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024]. Zero-shot forecasting on the original events will be used as a
baseline, as the literature shows great abilities within the use of TimesFM, compared to regular
Time series forecasting methods [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024]. As we assume it being difficult for
a model to predict the exact time-point an event happens and we are more interested in predicting
when an event happens, rather than the exact time-point, we need an evaluation metric in order to
evaluate the performance of the models. We are therefore further developing the well-known F1
score to take into account the different event label methods. These methods will be compared and

The research questions this thesis intends to answer are as follows:

2. How does variable input size and the influence of data context have an impact on pattern

forecast on unseen data and its ability to distinguish between events and nonevents?
4. How does one correctly evaluate events in time series forecasting, taking into account the
clinical setting?
1.2
Literature review
The following review will investigate different sources of literature, creating the foundation for
this study. The first section covers the fundamentals of OCD and the correlation between OCD,
stress events, and physiological signals. This is followed by an introduction to the feasibility study
of the Wrist Angel Project and other research done within this project. Next, we look into the
development of using time series foundation models for zero-shot forecasting and how events
in time series previously have been evaluated. Finally, the above reviews are summarized in a
research gap.
1.2.1
Background
Stress can contribute to the appearance and severity of obsessions experienced by someone with
OCD. Empirical evidence suggests that unmitigated chronic stress significantly elevates the risk of
psychiatric disorders. In light of this, recent studies have underscored the potential of leveraging
multimodal biosensor data for precise stress detection. This approach integrates various physiolog-
ical parameters, to enhance the accuracy and reliability of stress assessment [Schmidt et al. 2019].
The physiological manifestations of obsessions and compulsions related to OCD can be observed
in the activity of the autonomic nervous system, including heart rate, electrodermal activity, and
skin temperature, all three measured by the E4 Empatica wristband used in the Wrist Angel Study
[Lønfeldt et al. 2023]. Recent research has increasingly used wearable biosensors to collect data
on these physiological indicators, particularly in studies of stress and depression in adults. Among
these approaches, the use of electrocardiogram (ECG) data has become particularly widespread in
the prediction of events related to mental disorders [Lønfeldt et al. 2023].
1.2.2
The Wrist Angel Project
In a feasibility study [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] presents significant findings regarding the use of wear-
able biosensors for the detection of OCD events in children and adolescents. The analysis re-
vealed that generalized models demonstrated greater performance compared to personalized mod-
els. Specifically, the Random Forest (RF) and Mixed-Effect Random Forest (MERF) models
achieved the highest accuracy rates, reaching 70% accuracy in both random and participant cross-
validation scenarios. The classification was done by dividing the dataset into events, buffer win-
dows and nonevents. The buffer window was included to account for the precision of the event,
as it is assumed that an event does not only occur and happen in one timestamp. The study high-
lights the potential of utilizing wearable technology combined with machine learning techniques
to objectively monitor OCD symptoms in this population. However, the size of the WA data does

With the lack of data being a problem in the feasibility study, [Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024] have inves-

data to a greater extent. The results are presented using average F1-scores and even as they show
great results, the methods used on similar domain datasets are still not sufficient enough to use
as a zero-shot predictor for in-the-wild applications. They suggest that more research is done on
strategies and methods for models more robust to domain shifts and in-the-wild applications [Skat-
Rørdam et al. 2024]. Common to the conclusions of the studies conducted is that the training data
appears to be too small and thus lacks diversity in order to build a general model. Therefore, it is
now investigated in more detail how FM’s can be used to predict OCD events.
1.2.3
State-Of-The-Art Foundation Models
FMs have emerged as transformative tools in both computer vision and natural language process-
ing, significantly enhancing the capabilities of artificial intelligence systems. In computer vision,
FMs have shifted the paradigm from traditional pattern recognition to sophisticated models ca-
pable of complex visual analysis. For instance, models like the Vision Transformer (ViT) have
demonstrated remarkable performance by adapting transformer architectures, originally designed
for language tasks, to image recognition. This approach allows for the effective segmentation of
images into patches, facilitating tasks such as image classification and object detection with im-
proved accuracy and generalization capabilities, compared to conventional convolutional neural
networks (CNNs) [Yun 2024] [Labelbox 2023].
Similarly, in natural language processing, foundation models such as BERT and GPT have revolu-
tionized the field by enabling machines to understand and generate human-like text. These models
are pre-trained on extensive datasets, which allows them to quickly adapt to various downstream
applications, thereby streamlining the development process for AI practitioners [Yun 2024]. The
reason for FM success is their ability to leverage heterogeneous data to cultivate general purpose
representations, fine-tune them, or even deploy them directly in a zero-shot manner [Liang et al.
2024].
1.2.4
Time Series Foundation Model
Time series (TS) data are characterized by their sequential order and temporal dependencies, having
valuable information about the dynamics of diverse systems and processes. TS forecasting has for
a long time been an essential component of the decision-making process, across various domains
such as retail, finance, energy, healthcare, among others. Traditionally statistical models such
as ARIMA and ETS held state-of-the-art (SoA) status. But a recent shift towards deep learning
techniques, as described in Section 1.2.3 and the availability of large and diverse time series data
sources, have changed this [Ansari et al. 2024]. The use of deep learning and transformers in TS
models is adequate as it automatically learns comprehensive representations and captures complex
non-linearity within the TS data [Liang et al. 2024]. The evolvement of Large Language Models
(LLM) and their zero-shot capabilities have started the study on enabling foundation models within
time series. Within this domain, two research lines can be found: pre-training foundation models

Recently a decoder-only FM for TS forecasting, pre-trained on over 100B time points, has been
released, namely TimesFM. When TimesFM is applied to a variety of previously-unseen forecast-
ing dataset across different domains, it obtains a close to state-of-the-art zero-shot accuracy. [Das,
Kong, Sen, et al. 2024] states that the FM trained from scratch, exclusively on time series data, ob-
tains much better zero-shot performance than LLM, such as GPT-3 [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024].
The TimesFM architecture incorporates several innovative methods that enhance its forecasting
capabilities. One key feature is its patched-decoder architecture, which segments time series data
into patches, similar to tokenization in natural language processing. This approach does not only
improve processing efficiency but also allows the model to learn from diverse temporal patterns
effectively. Additionally, the model’s ability to generate longer output patches directly, rather than
relying on auto-regressive methods, significantly enhances accuracy, particularly in long-horizon
forecasting scenarios. Moreover, TimesFM is pre-trained on a large corpus of both real-world and
synthetic time series data, enabling it to generalize well across various forecasting tasks. This ex-
tensive pre-training allows the model to adapt to variable context and prediction lengths, making
it versatile for different applications. TimesFM demonstrates impressive zero-shot forecasting ca-
pabilities across various public datasets, achieving accuracy levels comparable to state-of-the-art
supervised models. In addition to showing impressive zero-shot forecasting, a comparison made
with more classic time series models, such as ARIMA, also shows better predictive capabilities on
some datasets [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024].
1.2.5
Event detection in time series
Using FMs for classification in multivariate time series is not a widely used technique. However,
there has been an increase in using machine learning techniques for detecting anomalies in mul-
tivariate time series, in the last couple of years. FMs show great capabilities of detecting and

1.2.6
Research gap
Despite the advances in FMs, several critical gaps remain in the application to real-world clini-
cal scenarios, particularly for predicting binary events like OCD episodes in in-the-wild settings.
Firstly, the utilization of foundation models such as TimesFM for binary event predictions in clin-
ical applications remains largely unexplored. Although it has demonstrated impressive zero-shot
forecasting capabilities across various domains, their effectiveness in predicting discrete clinical
events, especially in uncontrolled environments, are yet to be thoroughly investigated. This gap is
particularly crucial as it hinders our understanding of how these models can be optimized to pre-
dict OCD events in naturalistic settings, potentially limiting their integration into clinical practice.
Furthermore, the challenge of label adjustment in in-the-wild applications remains somewhat un-
explored. In real-world scenarios, the accuracy and reliability of event labels can be compromised
due to various factors such as patient recall bias or inconsistent reporting. The limited volume of
data, specifically events, and severe data imbalance of labeled events, results in large amounts of
unlabeled data being left unused. The lack of research on methods to adjust or validate these labels
in the context of TSFM’s represents a significant limitation in current literature. As an extension
of this, an evaluation matrix that can be used in the context of event prediction in time series, with-
out either over or underestimation, is missing. Addressing these gaps is essential for advancing
the field of predictive analytics in mental health care. By exploring the application of foundation
models to binary event prediction, developing methods for label adjustment for in-the-wild use,
and evaluation these predictions in a fair way, we can significantly enhance the accuracy and re-
liability of OCD event predictions. This, in turn, could lead to improved clinical practices, more
personalized interventions, and ultimately better patient outcomes in real-world settings.

Fundamental principles for Time-series Foundation models
2.1.1
Foundation Models
Foundation models represent a significant advancement in artificial intelligence, characterized by
their ability to learn from vast, diverse datasets and adapt to a wide range of tasks. FM’s are built
on pre-training and fine-tuning and are adaptable to various downstream tasks through fine-tuning.
FM’s are trained on a broad dataset, and usually contains several billions of parameters. In Table

2.1.2
Time series
Time series can be categorized into uni- and multivariate time series. Given a multivariate time
series sample with N(N > 1) channels, can be expressed as: X = {xt}t∈{1,...,T} ∈RN×T ,
and contains N-dimensional vectors. Each record xt is the temporal signal collected at a given
timestamp t. For univariate times series, xt ∈RT . As we work with several different physiological
features, we continue to work with multivariate time series. Typically, the primary tasks of time
series analysis include time series classification, forecasting, imputation, and anomaly detection.
However, it should be made clear that what we want to investigate in this thesis cannot be compared
to the classic time series classification, as ts classification aims to distinguish the time series data
into C different categories. In this thesis, we aim to classify a specific time step and/or events that
stretches over a shorter period of time. Therefore, we utilize time series anomaly detection and
time series forecasting. Our primary focus is forecasting and from forecasting to determine events
where there has been an OCD case. We therefore use anomaly detection methods to account for
events in our evaluation [Ye et al. 2024].

In contrast, few-shot learning facilitates adaptation through fine-tuning on minimal amounts of
domain-specific data.

2.1.3
Transformers
The transformer is proposed by [Vaswani et al. 2017] in their paper: ”Attention is all you need”.
The transformer relies only on the self-attention mechanism, allowing the model to perform more
parallelization, thereby increasing the training speed. All the existing FMs mentioned in Table 1.1
use transformers as its backbone models. Traditionally, a transformer is a sequence-to-sequence
model consisting of both an encoder and a decoder stack. However it is possible to use the two
stacks separately. The encoder contains a multi-head self-attention and a fully connected Feed-
Forward Network (FFN) sub-layer. We will come back to the multi-head self-attention in Section
2.1.4. The FFN is a position-wise feed-forward layer, meaning each embedding is processed inde-
pendently instead of the whole embedding is processed as a single vector. While the decoder has
the same capabilities, it also contains a encoder-decoder attention, that helps focusing on relevant
parts of the input sequence. Within LLMs, we see that encoder-only has been very popular in the
early stages, but after the introduction of GPT-3, decoder-only has increased in popularity. The
classic encoder-decoder architecture handles the entire input sequence at once and predicts based
on the whole input sequence, hence this type of transformer is ideally for tasks requiring transfor-
mation between two sequences, e.g. translation between two languages. The encoder-only ensures
the normalization of predicted values within the entire context window, which the decoder-only
architecture does not, due to its causal attention mechanism. The decoder-only architectures as
seen in TimesFM and Lag-llama, generates tokens auto regressively, based on all previous tokens.
Experiments shows that the decoder-only mode has better performance in zero-shot and few-shot
learning than other architectures [Ye et al. 2024].
Looking at a decoder-only architecture, the input consists of previously generated tokens. For
instance when predicting heart rate, the model uses all previously known heart rate values, as
context. Each token is mapped, through learned linear transformations, into three vectors: query
(Q), key (K) and value (V). For each attention head, attention scores are calculated by computing
the dot product of the query vector with all keys. The scores are scaled by the square root of the key
dimensions, passed through a softmax function to obtain attention weights, which then are used to
compute a weighted sum of the value vectors [Vaswani et al. 2017].
2.1.4

The core idea behind multi-head self-attention (MHSA) is to apply the attention mechanism multi-
ple times in parallel, allowing the model to capture various aspects of the input data simultaneously.
Each instance of this parallel processing is called an attention head. In MHSA, the input embed-
dings are projected into three linearly-weighted vectors: query Q, key K and value V. Each head
is built using a separate set of query, key and value weight matrices and with the use of the input
sequence, self-attention is computed in parallell with other heads. Drawing parallels to the use of
MHSA in LLMs, we can consider a query to be a question consisting of one or more words. The
relationship between query, key and value can therefore be exemplified by the query being a search
term ’network’ on the internet, while its keys include ’social, neural, deep learning, computer and
communication’. Values are the websites that contain information about the query being searched
for. Each attention head operates independently, applying its own learned linear transformations

3.2.2
Butterworth filter
Another issue following the use of in-the-wild data is the presence of noise. Noise is an inher-
ent challenge as it significantly impairs the performance of the predictive models. This issue has
been mitigated by implementing a Butterworth filter. This filtering process allows sinusoids with
frequencies below a specified cutoff frequency to pass through unchanged while mitigating those
with frequencies higher than the cutoff. This type of filtering is called low-pass, while high-pass
filtering accounts for the opposite. The approach to implementing the Butterworth filter is heavily
inspired by [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] and [Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024], who both did a deeper investiga-
tion into the best use of the filter. [Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024] also emphasizes how the use of the
Butterworth filter is consistent across similar studies, making it a robust method for handling noise.
The above studies leads to an implementation of a 2. order band-pass Butterworth filter, featuring
a low-pass cutoff frequency of 2 Hz and a high-pass cutoff frequency of 12 Hz, for BVP. This
means that it allows frequencies between 2 and 12Hz to pass through, while frequencies above 12
or below 2Hz gets attenuated. For both EDA and TEMP a 6. order low-pass Butterworth filter
with a cutoff frequency of 1 Hz is implemented.

3.2.5
Outlier Removal
When analyzing data collected from E4 in uncontrolled environments, the presence of outliers is
an inevitable challenge, particularly in projects lacking supervision and controlled environments.
Such circumstances can lead to the emergence of scenarios that contribute to data contamination,
resulting in noisy and unreliable datasets. To improve data quality, it is essential to systematically
identify and eliminate instances that exhibit clear deviations from expected norms. Outliers may
be recognized by comparing physiological signals with established average values for each param-
eter. Given that E4 is worn on the wrist, specific thresholds can be applied to various physiological
signals. For the participating children and adolescents, a typical resting heart rate ranges from 60
to 70 beats per minute (bpm). Consequently, any recorded HR below 50 bpm is deemed abnor-
mally low and warrants exclusion from the data set [UCSF Benioff Children’s Hospital 2024]. The
removal of temperature signals is similar to what has been done previously, and it can be seen that
both [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] and [Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024] have used a threshold of 23 °C for the
temperature measurement. This is to our assumption that when the temperature is 23 degrees or
below, while the acceleration being equal to 0 in all 3 directions (x,y,z), over an extended period
of time, the E4 device is taken of and the indicated temperature is therefore room temperature.
Since the event button is placed on the side of the E4 device, it is unavoidable that it is touched
by mistake when it is being put on and taken off. Therefore, tags that occurred at the beginning or
end of a session are removed. Those tags are removed before the other tags are defined as binary
events in the final pre-processed data. LA for the events described in Section 3.2.4 are performed
before removing outliers. Table 3.3 shows the number of initial events, the events after LA and
after outlier removal.
It is important to note that it is only when a combination of multiple signals is outside the normal
limits that it is assumed that the E4 device has been taken off or is not being worn correctly. Only
in such cases are they considered to be outliers and the individual data points are removed. This is
because the nature of missing data when using wearable sensors often correlates with real-world
events. This means that data is missing not at random, and we therefore should not interpolate or
impute, but delete the instances, that fall outside our requirements. This approach ensures consis-
tency across the dataset and prevents misinterpretation of concurrent readings.
3.2.6

3.2.7
General versus Patient specific model
Creating patient-specific models (PSMs) for children and adolescents with OCD presents signifi-
cant challenges due to the heterogeneous nature of this population. The variability in age, cognitive
development, and disease awareness among young patients contributes to the complexity of model
development. Children and adolescents may experience and report OCD symptoms differently,
and some may attribute certain behaviors to normal developmental phases or other childhood ex-
periences. Another significant challenge in developing PSMs for OCD is the highly unbalanced
nature of our patient-specific datasets. In Figure 3.3 we see that some patients have very few
recorded events, while only a few show more events, especially when considering the two label
adjustment methods. This imbalance can lead to difficulties in model training and validation, po-

addition to the above arguments, [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] also highlight that the general model per-

3.3
Utilization of Multiple Input Signals in TimesFM
The following is an explanation of how multiple signals are used in Google Research’s TimesFM.
The knowledge is gained by analyzing the TimesFM code [Google Research 2024], while using it
for forecasting. The TimesFM architecture is specifically designed to leverage multiple input sig-
nals, enhancing its capability to model complex temporal relationships inherent in time series data.
In a system where the physiological values come from the same measurement device, it is assumed
that the variables are endogenous. In this system, the endogenous variables are considered to be
all physiological measurements other than the tag of events. Endogenous variables are particularly
advantageous in time series analysis for several reasons, as they are influenced by other variables
within the system, making them crucial for understanding the internal dynamics of a time series.
The architecture of TimesFM allows for the incorporation of diverse input signals, including nu-
merical covariance, categorical features, and primary time series data, more specifically the time
series we want to predict. By accommodating these different types of input, TimesFM can capture
a richer set of relationships and dependencies, which is crucial for accurate forecasting.
The architecture utilizes a tokenization process where time series data is divided into equal-length
patches and transformed into a token through linear projections and residual blocks, which can be
seen in more detail in Appendix B. The use of a binary padding mask ensures that inputs of varying
lengths are handled effectively, allowing the model to ignore padded values during computations.
The tokens generated from multiple inputs are processed in parallel, enabling the model to learn
both the historical context and additional features simultaneously. Currently, the event tags, is
referred to as the input signals and the column we want to forecast, while the four columns EDA,
TEMP, HR and BVP are referred to as the numerical covariance columns [Google Research 2024].
3.3.1
Processing the multiple signals - the input encoder
During an initial input load of the data we have to define, which input signal the model should fore-
cast. While doing this, it is also possible to define numerical and categorical covariance columns.
The multiple input signals from the WA data can be interpreted as both Times series input signals
or the numerical covariance columns. Most of the handling of the numeric covariance columns is

done in the input encoder. After the initial load of the input signal in the model, a numerical feature
matrix is constructed on the basis of the defined numerical columns and converted into an array.
The matrix is transposed, in the same manner as the input signal, giving the model easier access
during training and prediction. As normalization is enabled during the process of initializing the
data_loader in the TimesFM, the numerical feature matrix is standardized in the same way as
the input signals, using the StandardScaler. The numerical features are incorporated into the
tokenization process, described in Appendix B.1. Each tokenized sequence generated from these
features is combined with time-based tokens. Each patch goes through the residual block and po-

positional encoding are fed into the stacked transformer layers, MHSA, as described in Section
2.1.4, comes into play. Each transformer layer utilizes MHSA to allow the model to focus on dif-
ferent parts of these input sequences simultaneously. This means that for a given output token, the
model can consider multiple aspects of preceding tokens, enhancing its ability to capture complex
patterns and dependencies in the data.

we apply a window-based approach, the granularity of both the ground truth (y) and predicted
(ˆy) values are changed. This methodology involves the creation of two new arrays, which are
constructed by applying a binary transformation across windows of size w. Specifically, for each
window, a value of 1 is assigned if at least one event occurs within that window, and 0 otherwise. In
addition to recw, the adjusted arrays are also used to calculate the FDR. Even though FDR is based
on the windows, we cannot expect prect = 1, even if FDRw = 0.0, as precision is calculated per
time-point t and FDR is calculated per window w. All this can be seen illustrated in Figure 3.5.

Discussion
This chapter discusses, among other things, the methodological choices made during the project.
In addition, it discusses the limitations that such choices have entailed, as well as what other lim-
itations there have been throughout the thesis. Finally, it concludes with the future work within
TSFM and event detection. The exploration of future work serves to identify promising directions
for extending and refining the current research, thereby contributing to the ongoing advancement
of knowledge in predicting OCD events.
5.1
Data
In the context of this discussion, it is necessary first to address the characteristics, limitations, and
challenges associated with the data set used throughout this thesis. This initial discussion of the
data is warranted, given its fundamental role in all subsequent discussions. The study utilizes in-
the-wild data, while presenting inherent challenges, it did not pose significant obstacles beyond the
necessary removal of outliers. However, the primary limitation encountered is from ambiguous
instructions regarding the interaction with the E4 device, when tagging events. This lack of clarity
has introduced a degree of uncertainty concerning the accuracy of event tags within the dataset.
Although attempts were made to mitigate this issue through the implementation of the two label
adjustment methodologies, a residual level of uncertainty persists regarding the precise occurrence
of events. The uncertainty arises, among other things, as the predicted values for TimesFM have
nonevents which behave very much like the actual events. For the very basic zero-shot forecasting,
performed only on the original events, it is clear to see nonevents that numerically lie around the
classified events. This ambiguity in event labeling presents a substantial challenge for machine
learning models, potentially impeding their ability to generalize and discern patterns effectively.
The heterogeneity of the study population, which comprises eight distinct individuals, further com-
plicates the development of a universally applicable approach to address this uncertainty.
The persistence of these misclassifications underscores the complexity of event prediction in in-
the-wild settings and highlights the need for more robust methodologies and further investigation,
in handling ambiguous or imprecise event labeling. These observations not only inform the inter-
pretation of the current results but also emphasize the critical importance of precise data collection
protocols in future studies of this nature.
5.2
Limitations of working in a clinical setting
A key limitation encountered during this study arises from the constraints associated with work-
ing in a clinical setting and handling patient-sensitive data. Specifically, the use of open-source
packages and similar tools is restricted due to stringent regulations governing computers that store
or process sensitive patient information at Region H. This posed challenges when working with
TimesFM, as the package was not available in the Region Hovedstadens approved program cat-
alog, thereby heavily complicating its installation and use. Furthermore, the computational re-
sources provided by Region H. were insufficient for the requirements of TimesFM. The initial
allocated computer was equipped with inadequate RAM, failing to meet the minimum memory
specifications necessary to initialize model checkpoints. Additionally, it is important to highlight
that cloud-based tools such as Google Colab, which offer access to GPU/TPU resources, could not
be utilized due to data security concerns. Uploading patient-sensitive data to a cloud environment
is strictly prohibited in this context, further limiting access to computational resources. These con-
straints collectively underscore the challenges of conducting advanced machine learning research
in a clinical environment with strict data security protocols.

5.3.1
TimesFM’s ability to zero-shot forecast
In RQ1 we investigate the potential of TimesFM, a TSFM, for binary classification in zero-shot
forecasting of OCD events using in-the-wild data. This application of TimesFM for binary clas-
sification is novel and has to our knowledge, at the end of this thesis, not yet been explored in
prior research. The results demonstrate that TimesFM exhibits promising capabilities in zero-shot
forecasting within binary events, achieving performance metrics that are comparable to those re-
ported in previous studies. However, several important considerations must be addressed when
interpreting and comparing these findings. Compared to the preliminary study by [Lønfeldt et al.
2023] they report an average F1 score just below 60%, according to them, this is due to a low recall
score, at 50%, meaning only half of the tagged events are found on average. However, they report
a better precision score of 66%, indicating that 1/3 of the positive predictions were false positive
[Lønfeldt et al. 2023]. Considering that the model from [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] is trained on the WA
dataset, we have to recognize that TimesFM does a good job in its zero-shot forecasting in compar-
ison. Especially when compared to precision, where TimesFM does a better job with its highest
precision of 94%. We see that both [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] and TimesFM only finds about half of
the tagged events, however, TimesFM has a higher precision, meaning that it is more confident in
its positive predictions, something we value highly in clinical contexts.
Since we only calculate FDR for the event labels, we cannot make a direct link between preci-
sion and FDR and thus cannot interpret anything based on their precision, as one usually can with
FDR = 1 −precision. However, we know from our own evaluation that following this ap-
proach yields a lower FDR than simply looking at only events that are falsely discovered - the one
we report. Therefore, we can assume that TimesFM reports a much lower FDR than the results
from [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] shows, as their FDR then would yield a higher FDR than we have
obtained. In continuation of this, by using models employing transfer learning, without data sim-
ulation, [Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024] achieves an average F1 score of approximately 60%. Notably,
[Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024] reported an increased F1 score of 73%, only after incorporating exten-
sive data simulation strategies, underscoring the challenges posed by limited labeled data. While
TimesFM does reach this benchmark, only the F1 score is reported, making it difficult to assess
and compare the performance of the two methods, as we are working with such an imbalanced
dataset, in a very sensitive domain. It can therefore be considered whether the F1 macro is a good
evaluation metric for event detection in time series. Furthermore, it is important to emphasize that
both [Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024] and [Lønfeldt et al. 2023] evaluated their models on event-adjusted
and simulated datasets, whereas this study utilized the original dataset without such adjustments.
This distinction highlights the potential of TimesFM and TSFMs more broadly for addressing data
scarcity challenges and predicting events in time series. Although TimesFM and TSFMs gener-
ally do not solve the data scarcity problem due to low recall, we still see valuable high precision
and low FDR. We cannot use TimesFM to predict OCD events in children in real time, due to
the low recall, but there is a potential to use TimesFM to support psychologists in terms of event
occurrences within the individual child, and thus help improve the treatment.
At the time of this thesis, fine-tuning TimesFM was not feasible due to technical limitations associ-
ated with ARM-architecture computers and the unavailability of HuggingFace checkpoints for the
very same. This limitation restricted the ability to optimize TimesFM for the specific task at hand,
potentially limiting its performance. [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024] shows a 9% reduction in the
reported MAE from fine tuning an ETT dataset. As TimesFM is a relatively recent development,
ongoing advancements in its implementation and support infrastructure may further enhance its
applicability in future studies.

provide a complete picture of the model’s efficacy.

and FDR, both of which provide crucial perspectives on the efficacy of our approach. The F1
score, widely used in machine learning evaluations, has been a standard metric for assessing model
performance. However, our analysis reveals potential limitations of the F1 score in the specific
context of event detection, particularly in time series and clinical settings. We observed instances

debatable whether F1 is ambiguous about the way the models perform, as it can be seen how much
influence the amount of events and quality of data has on the F1 score. On the other hand, FDR is
more comparable as it only looks at events.

tion issues associated with the traditional F1 score. However, contrary to our initial expectations,
this desired outcome was not consistently achieved, particularly when applied to the ELA dataset.
Notably, in numerous scenarios involving ELA data, the window-wise F1 score (F1w) surpassed
both the baseline F1 and (F1PA). We posit that this phenomenon does not indicate over fitting;
rather, it reflects an enhanced accuracy of the window-wise approach. This improvement is pri-
marily attributed to the method’s capacity to minimize false positives, thereby augmenting both
precision and the overall F1 score. The superior performance of the window-wise evaluation can
be explained by its methodological distinctions from both baseline and PA approaches. Unlike the
baseline method, which focuses on specific time points, and the PA method, which is confined to
events within a predefined event boundary, the window-wise approach employs a more compre-
hensive temporal window. This window encompasses not only the events within its boundaries
but also considers predicted events that may fall outside the immediate event time frame. Conse-
quently, we argue that the window-wise evaluation offers a more nuanced and clinically relevant
assessment, particularly in the context of OCD event detection. Its utility in clinical settings is
predetermined on its ability to more accurately quantify the frequency and distribution of OCD
events. However, it is crucial to emphasize that the optimal application of this method in clinical

5.4
Future work
Future research in the application of FMs for time series event detection should focus on several
key areas to enhance both performance and efficiency. Firstly, addressing the computational chal-
lenges associated with transformer-based models like TimesFM is crucial. Despite their impressive
zero-shot capabilities, especially when using ELA and to some extent LA, the high runtime poses
significant limitations for clinical applications. Recent research by [Dorszewski et al. 2024] has
shown that it is possible to prune 5-45% of transformer layers without retraining, while maintaining
near-original performance levels. Furthermore, the study demonstrated that the entire transformer
stack could be replaced with a more compact and efficient network, retaining over 95% of the orig-
inal accuracy. It would therefore be interesting to see if the extensive run-time could be reduced by
pruning some of the transformer layers. This investigation would make TimesFM, LA and ELA
data more viable for real-time clinical use.
Secondly, the lack of comprehensive data and uncertainty surrounding the tagged events remain
a significant challenge. To address this, future research could explore the inclusion of control
groups in the study design. There exists a dataset containing physiological signals from nurses
tagging and evaluating stressful situations during the COVID-19 crisis. The data collection is
done using the same device as in the WA study and could be interesting to use as a control group
[S. Hosseini, Gottumukkala, and Katragadda 2021]. This should be done in order to prevents
the amount of false positives, that behave like the actual events and get an even better pattern
recognition and numerical split between events and nonevents. Additionally, as demonstrated in
their findings by [Skat-Rørdam et al. 2024], incorporating simulated data can significantly enhance
model performance. Investigating the integration of simulated time series data that accurately
represents event occurrences could provide a valuable avenue for improving the robustness and
generalization of event detection models. This could also help focusing on the nonevents that have
temporal behavior like events. Although TimesFM achieves excellent results on the ELA data,
FMs trained on domain data are shown to perform better. Therefore, it would be interesting to
create a FM trained only on physiological data. A small amount of these exist, but nothing that is
currently used in the literature. It would therefore be interesting to investigate the possibility of
creating an FM built solely on physiological signals. First of all, the different event adjustment
methods can be investigated further. Our results suggest that there may be more precision and
higher recall to be gained if the window in ELA is increased from 1-minute.
Lastly, further refinement of our novel evaluation methodology is warranted. The current window
size for evaluation was chosen based on a very small preliminary analysis. Future work should
conduct a more comprehensive investigation into the impact of varying window sizes on evalua-
tion metrics, particularly the FDR, as that is important within event detection in a clinical setting.
Understanding how different window sizes affect the balance between precision and recall could
lead to more nuanced and accurate evaluations of model performance in time series event detec-
tion. These directions for future research aim to address the current limitations in computational
efficiency, data availability, and evaluation methodology, potentially leading to more accurate,
efficient, and clinically applicable FMs for time series event detection.

Conclusion
This chapter summarizes the main results achieved during the work with this thesis. The main
results include the feasibility of utilizing TSFM for zero-shot forecasting within event detection,
creating and evaluating different methods to account for events in time series prediction and lastly
the creation of the evaluation method for label adjustment.
RQ1: How well does the time series foundational model, TimesFM, zero-shot forecast OCD
events using in-the-wild data and does it address the data scarcity problem?
TimesFM, a time series foundational model, was evaluated for zero-shot forecasting of OCD events
using in-the-wild data. The model was tested with various input sizes and prediction lengths, using
MAE, F1 macro score, precision and FDR as the primary evaluation metrics. The best performance
was achieved with a classification threshold δ = 0.51 and an input size and prediction length of
24, resulting in an F1 score of 52.79%, while the highest precision of 94.44% was obtained with an
input size of 24 and prediction length of 96, and a corresponding FDR of 11.11%. Even though we
obtain great values for zero-shot predicting, TimesFM has a high average run-time, which means
it is not applicable in all clinical contexts.
TimesFM demonstrated promising capabilities in addressing the data scarcity problem through
its high precision value and low FDR, suggesting that only a small portion of predicted events
were false positives. Despite being used in a zero-shot forecasting scenario, TimesFM’s perfor-
mance was comparable to models specifically trained on the WA data. These characteristics make
TimesFM particularly valuable for clinical contexts where minimizing false positives is crucial.
However, some challenges remain in the application of TimesFM. The recall was moderate, cap-
turing only about half of the tagged events with a 51% recall rate. Additionally, the numerical
predicted values before conversion to events or nonevents are far from the actual values for events.
Performance variability was observed, with the model’s effectiveness decreasing when longer pre-
diction horizons were combined with shorter input sizes.
RQ2: How does variable input size and the influence of data context have an impact on pat-
tern recognition and TimesFM’s ability to distinguish between events and nonevents?
Although TimesFM’s predictions on the original events initially shows high precision, it demon-
strates a narrow span of predicted values, particularly for events classified as positive. This com-
pressed range suggests that TimesFM struggles to effectively differentiate between event and non-
event instances, potentially leading to an increased risk of false positives.
TimesFM’s prediction on both LA and ELA shows promising potential, offering greater stability
in threshold values and aligns more closely with the actual label value range. This alignment in-
dicates a more robust foundation for event detection, potentially mitigating challenges associated
with the original event labels. The analysis revealed the importance of careful threshold selection.
The original low threshold (δ = 0.51) may have contributed to the apparent higher performance of
the original events. Adjusting the threshold to δ = 0.71 for LA and ELA methods aims to optimize
the balance between precision and recall, prioritizing higher precision and lower FDR in clinical
settings. Both LA and ELA methods demonstrated improved pattern recognition, with some non-
events exhibiting temporal patterns similar to events. This observation suggests the possibility of
untagged or unregistered events in the dataset, potentially indicating that TimesFM’s actual perfor-
mance may be better than initially stated. The combination of a low input size and a long prediction
length, aggravates TimesFM’s ability to distinguish between events and nonevents, as we don’t see
as much divergence between the two events as with shorter prediction lengths. This is true for all
three types of event alignment methods.

The analysis highlighted that while TimesFM demonstrates excellent performance with ELA data,

RQ4: How does one correctly evaluate events in time series forecasting, taking into account
the clinical setting?
Traditional metrics such as the F1 score often fail to accurately reflect model performance, particu-
larly in event detection within time series. We propose a new window-based evaluation method: w.
This new method typically positions itself between the baseline and PA evaluation metrics. The
findings reveal that this window-based approach not only improves precision (precw), but also
significantly reduces FDR, as we see some cases where FDR are close or equal to 0. Compared to

This is especially positive in clinical applications where false positives can lead to unnecessary
interventions. The introduction of window-wise precision (precisionw) further enhances the eval-

includes, among others, if an event is predicted within 25 seconds of when it actually occurs. The
results demonstrate that this method yields exceptionally high precision scores, indicating that the
model achieves near-perfect event detection within defined temporal windows. This improvement
emphasizes the importance of considering temporal context in event detection tasks, a factor often
neglected by traditional metrics like the F1 score. We find that the F1 score is insufficient to eval-
uate clinical event detection in time series, as we do not get a nuanced view on the performance.
These improvements above emphasizes the importance of considering temporal context in event
detection tasks. The window evaluation method’s ability to consistently lower FDR and increase
precision, across various scenarios suggests its potential as a robust tool for anomaly detection, pro-
viding a more meaningful assessment than the bland F1 score in event detection contexts. FDRw
and precidionw offer a more comprehensive and accurate evaluation of model performance, com-
pared to F1 in time series event detection, particularly in clinical settings where precision and
context are paramount.

B.1
Input layer
The input layer maps the time-series data into tokens, which will be the representation units for
the model. This is done by splitting the time-series into equal-length patches p, which are non-
overlapping. In figure B.1 the input patch length is given as p = 32, which is also the one we will
be utilizing. The input layer applies a linear projection to each patch in order to obtain a token.
The individual patch represents a tokenized sequence. Each patch is transformed into a vector by
a residual block. As not all inputs have the same length, a binary padding mask m1:L is added, in

a binary vector that corresponds to the input sequence y1:L where L represents the total number
of elements in the sequence. Each element of the mask indicates whether its corresponding input
should be processed or skipped. Here, m1:L = 1 means that the corresponding input in y1:L is
padding and should be ignored during the model computations. The opposite applies if m1:L = 0
[Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024].
The residual block is a crucial component that is used in the input layer to transform input patches
into vectors suitable for processing by transformer layers. It consists of a Multi-layer Perceptron
(MLP) with a hidden layer and ReLU activation, which introduces non-linearity and helps mitigate
issues like the vanishing gradient problem. The use of fully linear skip connections in the residual
block indicates that the model allows outputs from earlier layers to bypass subsequent layers and
be added to the output of a later layer. This feature is essential in deep learning architectures to
maintain information flow and improve training efficiency. Dropout is used in the linear layer
to regularize the model and prevent overfitting, while layer normalization is applied to stabilize
training and ensure consistent activation in varying input lengths [Das, Kong, Leach, et al. 2024].
The input encoder within the residual block is responsible for transforming each input patch into
a format compatible with the dimensions required by the transformer layers. Specifically, the j-th
patch is represented as ˜yj = yp(j−1)+1:pj. Currently, the corresponding binary padding mask is
defined as ˜mj = mp(j−1)+1:pj Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024. This enables us to express the j-th
token for subsequent processing in the transformer layers as follows:
tj = InputResidualBlock(˜yj ⊙(1 −˜mj)) + PEj
(B.2)
Here PEj is the j-th added positional encoding, denoted PE in the architecture in figure B.1. The
use of positional encoding is as described in the original transformer paper, Attention is all you
need, more precisely sinusoidal encoding. Sinusoidal encoding is made up of sine functions with
different frequencies: PE(pos,2i) = sin(pos/100002i/dmodel). The PE has the same dimensionality
as the embedding of the residual block, as this enables the two to be summed [Vaswani et al. 2017].
The sinusoidal functions make the model learn to attend tokens based on their relative position at
the same time as the encoding assures that the attention weights gradually decrease with distance.
This means that tokens closer together in the sequence will have a stronger influence on each other
compared to those further apart, which is crucial when dealing with time series. The total of input
tokens will be N = ⌊L/p⌋[Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024].

for all j ∈[N] , where mj serves as the masking indicator for the j-th token, defined as minmp(j−1)+1:pj.
The use of the min function implies that if any previous time point (up to j −1 is non-masked (i.e.,
has a value indicating it should be attended to), then the corresponding token j is marked as not
being masked. mj ensures that tokens are only attended to if they correspond to non-masked time
points, allowing the model to ignore irrelevant or padded information [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024].
B.3
The output decoder
The model operates as an auto-regressive framework, predicting the next set of tokens in the time
series. Each output token is designed to forecast a segment of the time series that follows the
last input patch associated with it. This task is fundamentally a regression problem rather than a
probabilistic one, and the model utilizes a point forecasting loss function, such as Mean Squared
Error (MSE), during training. This approach allows the model to effectively minimize the error
between predicted and actual values over the entire sequence [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024]. Lastly,
the output tokens must be mapped into the final forecast. As the model is trained in decoder-only
mode, each output token should be capable of predicting the segment of the time series that comes
after the last input patch associated with it. However, a key difference in TimesFM is its ability to
predict a larger amount of time-series data compared to what is encoded from the input sequences.
When the output tokens have variable lengths, prediction-length (PL) tokens are used. These PL
tokens are appended to the input token sequence. The prediction length equals the number of
output tokens and is represented using a one-hot encoded vector. For example, PE = [0, 0, 1, 0, 0]
indicates that three output tokens will be generated. Each output token undergoes a linear projection
to obtain a scalar value, which represents the predicted value for the corresponding time point. This
process can be described mathematically as:
ˆypj+1:pj+h = OutputResidualBlock(oj)
(B.4)
All the time series data from the beginning up to the pj-th time point, denoted as y1:pj, is encoded
into oj. This encoded representation in oj is then utilized to predict the subsequent h time points,
represented as ypj+1:pj+h. The length of the output is specified as output_patch_len(h) [Das,
Kong, Sen, et al. 2024].
B.4
Pre-training
In the TimesFM architecture, pre-training plays a critical role in equipping the model with the
ability to understand and forecast time series data effectively. The model is trained on a diverse
set of datasets, including real-world and synthetic time series, to capture a wide range of temporal
patterns. The data are sampled with 80% being real data and 20% are represented via synthetic
data. The real data provides equal weights to the groups: hourly + sub-hourly, daily, weekly, and
monthly datasets. When possible, the model is trained using a context length of the maximum of
512. For all time series categories except for weekly and data with a granularity ≥monthly, a
context length of 256. The context lenght defines the length the model allows for each decode call.
Lastly, they use the standard normalization part of reversible instance normalization of reversible
instance normalization. This means that the model takes in a patched time series, calculates the
mean and standard deviation of the first patch, and uses these statistics to normalize the entire input
sequence. The objective of the pre-training is to maximize the likelihood of the output tokens,
given the input tokens. This is done using Masked Language Modeling (MLM). MLM works by
randomly masking some of the input tokens and asks the model to predict the masked tokens based
on the input token. MLM is bidirectional, compared to other Language models as GPT 2 and 3,
which only predicts the next token [Das, Kong, Sen, et al. 2024].
