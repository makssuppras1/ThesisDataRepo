Abstract
Optimization of truss structures based on plastic equilibrium has been carried out as a
linear programming problem since 1964. Since then, topology optimization which is a
continuum formulation based on elastic equilibrium, has evolved and is presently used in
multiple industries such as the aerospace industry.
The scope of this report is an extension of the plastic framework for bar elements from
1964. However, the framework is extended in order to include the global stability phe-
nomenon as well as the local stability phenomenon. The framework allows for optimiza-
tion of truss structures in two and three dimensions subjected to multiple load cases.
Optimization of truss structures considering stability constraint is difﬁcult to perform by
hand. However, in this report a number of classical solution are presented and the frame-
work is tested against the classical solutions in order to built conﬁdence in the presented
framework. The framework has proved to be able to reproduce the classical solution and
provides results similar to ﬁndings from previously conducted compliance based opti-
mizations.
However, it has been found that the framework will generally utilize the plastic distribu-
tion to reduce the amount of compression in the structure in order to reduce the required
cross-sectional area. Additionally, it has been found that the optimal solutions may utilize
post buckling behaviour of the structure. In some cases, the framework can lead to large
tensile members supported by a lightweight substructure, known as tensile chains.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Formulating the optimization of structures as a mathematical problem leads back to 1964,
where the plastic design of trusses were formulated as a linear programming problem [1,
pp. 221]. Later, an elastic formulation was introduced in which the elastic problem was
considered for continuum in which the objective is to minimize the compliance of the
structure. This approach made progress during the 1980’s and has since lead to topology
optimization. Topology optimization is relatively mature and is applied within manu-
facturing, automotive and the aerospace industry [2]. Structural optimization may be of
interest for different reasons. One reason may be that by reducing the amount of material
used, the price of a product may be decreased. However, it may also yield beneﬁcial
secondary effects, such as leading to less fuel consumption in the case of the aerospace
industry.
The general objective in structural optimization is to ﬁnd an optimized material distribu-
tion within a speciﬁed region. The considerations made within this report are limited to
the sizing problem, in which the goal is to ﬁnd the optimal member areas within a truss
structure. Thereby, the topology of the structure is assumed to remain constant through-
out each iteration as opposed to the more generalized topology optimization.
The topology of truss structures has previously been shown to converge towards slender
lightweight structures that are prone to instability [3, pp. 1]. The goal of this thesis
is to explore the implementation of sizing optimization of truss structures as a convex
programming problem. The key challenge is to consider the global and local stability of
the structures, due to the tendency of slender lightweight structures being optimal. The
problem is formulated as a mathematical programming problem, for which optimization
solvers such as interior point methods can be utilized in order to optimize the material
usage.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Plastic optimization
Optimization of structures in plastic design may be performed by determining the mini-
mal amount of material used in order to withstand a safe and statically admissible stress
ﬁeld. For truss structures the objective can be said to minimize the total volume of the
bars while satisfying the yield constraints, which is the previously mentioned linear pro-
gramming problem.
One of the major limitations of this kind of optimization is the disregard of the global
and local stability. Since the optimization only accounts for yielding, plastic optimization
may lead to slender compressive bars which may experience instability both locally and
in the global structure. This is of particular concern, since the plastic stress distribution
assumes that the structure has sufﬁcient ductility.
Material optimization considering stability
Previous optimization algorithms have been employed in order to solve the stability prob-
lem in order to ﬁnd the maximum load [4] and to large-scale truss structures [3]. In the
previous formulation of the optimization problem, the problem was formulated by using
the state variables, displacements, and stress within each bar member. Additionally, the
positive semi-deﬁniteness of the global stability problem was insured by introducing a
lower-bound on the cross-sectional area of each bar [3, pp. 4]. The approach within this
report is to utilize the commercial MOSEK solver to ensure the positive semi-deﬁniteness
rather than imposing an explicit lower bound on the cross-sectional areas.
Objective of the thesis
The approach in this report is to ensure equilibrium through a lower-bound plastic rigid-
plastic analysis. The stability constraint is introduced by assuming a linear relation be-
tween the displacements and loads and by assuming that the axial forces in each bar mem-
ber remains constant during the deformation up to the instability load. Following these
assumptions, the Lagrangian strain tensor is utilized to derive the geometric stiffness ma-
trix for a bar element. The global stability is ensured by formulating the optimization
problem as a mathematical programming problem and solving it by means of a commer-
cial semideﬁnite optimizer.
The objective of the thesis can be summarized to the following points
1. Development of a model accounting for yield constraints and global stability for
multiple load cases.

2. Extension of the two dimensional model to a three dimensional model.
3. Investigation of the possibility for implementation of local stability constraints
within the framework.
Additionally, linear and non-linear Finite Element programs are developed in order to
check the validity of the optimized solutions. The solutions are compared to previously
performed investigations and simpliﬁed analytical models.

2.1
Rigid-plastic material
A rigid/plastic material, is a material that does not deform at stress levels below its yield
point [5]. From experiments it is known that rigid-plastic modelling may be applicable
to some perfect plastic metals, such as mild steel. Mild steel behaves as a linear elastic
material at low stresses, but above a certain stress level, the yield stress, mild steel can
obtain large deformations resulting in minor changes in the stress level. The linear elastic
behaviour is typically observed until 80 % of the yield stress [6]. The strain hardening
of steel is disregarded. Typically, steel is modelled with a linear-elastic perfect plastic
stress strain relationship as shown in Figure 2.1b. Additionally, the elastic deformations
are disregarded since the elastic deformations of steel are small compared to the plas-
tic deformations. This leads to the assumption, that the material remains undeformed,
i.e. rigid, until it is yielding. A rigid perfect plastic stress-strain relationship is shown
in Figure 2.1c. The perfect plastic assumption requires that the material can sustain a
large strain and still maintain the stress level. The assumption of a rigid plastic mate-
rial therefore ﬁts with the material behaviour of mild steel, but may also be applicable to
appropriately reinforced concrete structures as demonstrated in [5].

Finite Element Formulation
The limit load of a structure can be determined through either the lower-bound or upper-
bound method. In the presented Finite Element analyzes, a lower-bound bar element
is considered. The lower-bound formulation ensures that the equilibrium conditions are
satisﬁed and that the yield condition is not violated. The lower-bound bar element formu-
lation and linearization of yield conditions have been treated by multiple authors, such as
[11].

The fundamental problem of the optimization shown in Figure 4.1 is treated by the
Maxwell scalar counting rule. The rule states the following condition for determinacy
of three dimensional trusses [15]
m −s = 3j −b −6
(4.1)
where b is the number of bars, j is the number of joints, m is the number of inﬁnitesimal
internal mechanisms and s is the number of states of self-stress. Assuming that the system
does not have states of self-stress, it can be seen that the system is stable if every node is
supported in three linearly independent directions as also noted in [16]. Additionally, for
plane trusses the criteria becomes two linearly independent supports in each node, which
can be either supports or bars.
Since the approach in this report is to model global stability as a linear elastic problem,
disregarding effects of initial deformations and geometric non-linearity, the global stabil-
ity constraint becomes a semideﬁnite programming problem, as described in [3]. This
approach has been described as the best approximation in relation to civil engineering
application for topology optimization [3, pp. 2] with its application primarily in the pre-
liminary design phase. However, in the present case the framework differs slightly from
classical topology optimization, as the objective function in topology optimization typi-
cally is chosen as the compliance of the structure, while in this framework the objective
function is the total material usage and the equilibrium is ensured through the plastic
lower-bound framework presented in Section 3. By choosing this approach, the volume
is left as a free variable, which is thereby minimized and will not lead to any redundancy.
The effect of local buckling has been treated by Leonhard Euler, back in the 18th century.
The most important takeaway is that when a beam is loaded by a normal force of sufﬁcient
magnitude, the effective stiffness is reduced, which may lead to instability of the structure
[7]. The concept is that at a certain load level, two equilibrium states exist. In the ﬁrst
state, the beam will remain straight. However, the other state is a buckled form in which
the displacement of the beam is unbounded. The inclusion of local buckling constraints
within the presented framework is discussed in the following section.

4.1
Local Buckling
For the local stability, the scope of this report is limited to circular hollow sections (CHS).
However, the presented framework should be extendable to any type of cross-section us-
ing reasonable assumptions. The local buckling is only considered as Eulerian buckling,
however, the Eurocode approach is also presented as reference and its geometrical limits
for thin-walled cylinders are included within the framework.
Local buckling of cylinders
Previous investigations have introduced conditions regarding the local stability by con-
sidering Eulerian buckling and formulating a relation between the cross-sectional param-
eters, such as diameter and thickness, and the moment of inertia [3, pp. 10]. In order
to consider Eulerian buckling, it is convenient to express the moment of inertia of the
truss member as a function of the cross-sectional area in order to ﬁt into the presented
framework. In this report a conceptual implementation is performed for hollow circular
sections.
However, in addition to the Eulerian buckling thin-walled members may also experience
the local stability phenomenon in the shape of shell buckling. In this report, the effect
of shell buckling is neglected by introducing a bound on D
t as recommended by the Eu-
rocode in order to further simplify the problem.
Thin-walled circular cylinders are usually given one of four classiﬁcations [17, 18]
1. Short Cylinders
2. Intermediate Cylinders
3. Long Cylinders
4. Very Long Cylinders
These four categories are characterized by their respective buckling failure as:
Short Cylinders tend to behave as ﬂat plate columns. Short cylinders buckle in sinusoidal
wave form similar to ﬂat plates and the supporting conditions have a considerable inﬂu-
ence.
==PAGE:42==
30

Intermediate Cylinders buckle in mixed patterns are combined sinusoidal and diamond
shapes. Supporting conditions of Intermediate Cylinders are of minor importance.
Long Cylinders buckle in diamond shaped patterns, and the length and supporting condi-
tions are of limited importance.

the constraint functions are linear [8, pp. 6]. Objective function is the term used for the

Linear programming is the simplest subclass of convex optimization. A fundamental

Every linear programming problem, called the primal problem, have an associated prob-

function. An optimal feasible solution as well as an optimal lower bound solution exist
for linear programming problem. The two solutions attain the same objective value. For
rigid-plastic analysis of bars, this corresponds to the upper bound method being the dual
function to the lower bound optimization as demonstrated in [9].
In general, the dual problem may be formed by adding the constraints to the objective
function using Lagrange multipliers as weighting parameters. This function is then min-
imized and gives an expression of the primal variables as function of the Lagrange mul-
tipliers. This function is denoted as the dual function. The dual problem is then to maxi-
mize the dual function, which yields the dual solution [22]. The rearranging of the linear
programming problem, i.e. the primal problem, to the dual problem through the La-
grangian function has been presented by multiple authors such as [10].

Parallel Computing
The motivation behind considering parallel computing is twofold. For a large number of
elements and/or load cases, the increase in the number of processors should result in a
faster solution time. This is known as scalability [25]. One of the measures that can be
used to consider the scalability of a program is Amdahl’s law.
The speedup of the task, S, may be formulated in terms of the number of CPUs, N, and
the percentage of the program that is parallelized, P, as
S =
1
(1 −P) + P
N
(6.1)
However, it is important to note that Amdahl’s law assumes that the serial part of the pro-
gram does not speedup from the additional processors. Additionally, Amdahl’s law does
not consider any potential communication overhead.
In addition to the speedup from utilizing multiple processors, nodes in a computing clus-
ter have a memory signiﬁcantly larger than typical desktops, which may be required for
large problems. This is especially important within the scope of this report, since the
high degree of coupling between constraints and the positive semideﬁnite optimization
problem may result in large matrices having to be allocated.

Non-Linear Finite Element Analysis
In the previous section, the post processing of the optimizations showed that the Eulerian
buckling load would be exceeded in an elastic model. However, since the buckling of a
single member may not cause the entire structure to collapse due to potential redundancy,
a non-linear program was implemented. The non-linear program is based on the Finite
Element formulation of the co-rotating beam element as presented in [9]. The co-rotating
beam element is a standard two node beam element, where the state of deformation is
described by the local coordinate system, which rotates with the element. The non-linear
effects of normal forces are not taken into account from the element itself. However, it is
included through the global deformations and as a result multiple elements are needed in
order to capture the buckling load of a structure.
The non-linear analysis has only been performed for the square domain, in a case where it
has been subjected to multiple loads. However, based on the results presented in this sec-
tion, it could be interesting to investigate the other problems found in Section 7 using the
co-rotating beam model. This has not been done in this report, due to time limitations. In
the non-linear analysis the vertically loaded square domain is considered to be subjected
to three independent load cases, as shown in Figure 8.1.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
A bar formulation involving rigid-plastic equilibrium and a linear elastic stiffness has
been established in order to consider the global stability. Two local optimization methods
were introduced in order to consider the local buckling constraint: the iterative approach
and linearization approach. The linearization approach has proved to converge faster and
has obtained results closer to the analytic results.
The framework has proved to be in accordance with simple analytic models. Optimization
of more complex two dimensional structures have shown to correspond well with previ-
ous compliance-based optimizations. Often the inclusion of the global stability constraint
has proven to result in lightweight substructures supporting large tensile or compression
members. Additionally, it has been found that optimization considering global stability
generally was comparable to the following linear elastic calculation. However, inclusion
of the global stability constraint has shown to result in structures with large tensile chains
when many elements are considered. Tensile chains may become optimal, due to the
pre-stressing of the bars which reduces the area requirement of the supporting bar. This
has shown the importance of considering multiple load cases, as a tensile chain may be
avoidable by considering multiple relevant load cases.
Three dimensional structures as well as structures subjected to local stability constraints
did not correspond well with the elastic calculation. In these cases, the stress ﬁeld in the
optimization was chosen to minimize the amount of compression that had to be carried.
Therefore, the elastic calculation predicted signiﬁcantly larger compressive stresses in
some members. However, when considering a co-rotating beam model for a two dimen-
sional optimization, the bearing capacity of the structure was found to be slightly larger
than the load optimized for. This was found to be due to the redundancy of the struc-
ture, which means that the local buckling of a single member may not cause the entire
83

structure to collapse. The non-linear calculation with co-rotating beam elements was only
performed for a single of the investigated examples. Therefore, it can not be concluded
that the optimized solutions are generally viable, but the results do give some conﬁdence
to the optimized material distributions.
9.1
Further work
The ideas for future work can be divided into two groups: Extension of the bar framework
and development of new models. This section will give a brief overview of parameters
not taken into account in the presented framework and which aspects future development
might improve.
Expansion of the bar framework
The presented framework has treated yielding, global stability and local buckling of
bar members. However, it did not take the self-weight of the members into account.
The framework may be extended by introducing loads linearly proportional to the cross-
sectional areas, assuming the cross-sections are kept constant along the bar.
Another topic is the type of loading. In this report, solely static loads were considered.
However, dynamic loading of a structure may occur for multiple reasons. Dynamic load-
ing may be of a concern in relation to fatigue. However, in recent years, this has also been
discussed in relation to comfort. For instance, the Millennium Bridge experienced large
lateral vibrations due to synchronous pedestrian loading when it initially opened and thus
it had to be retroﬁtted. In order to limit the vibrations, it may therefore be desirable to im-
pose restrictions related to the natural frequency of the optimized structure, which could
possibly be formulated as a semideﬁnite constraint.
Formulation of a new model
The presented framework ran into a problem, in which the considerations for local sta-
bility could not be formulated as a convex problem. However, using a sufﬁciently ﬁne
mesh, the phenomenon of local buckling can be modelled using either beam elements or
shell elements. In this case, satisfying the global stability constraint may be sufﬁcient in
order to also consider local buckling. The advantage of this approach would be that the
problem is convex and that the optimized solution is not a local optimum.

essential for the framework.

- Square domain
- MBB beam
- Laced Column
- L-shaped truss

linear elastic calcuations and the functions required in order
to setup the optimization problems.  The folder contains
scritps in order to establish the local stiffness and geometric
stiffness matrix, the transformation matrix and the scripts

calculation. The three dimensional functions are appended
