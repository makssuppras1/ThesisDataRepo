Abstract
The design and development of large cable-stayed bridges leads to longer and more slender
cables. The impact from wind, rain and snow can lead to large cable vibrations and in worst
case destructive behaviour. In order to cope with the more slender designs an advancing
development of external damping of cables is present. This project concerns the
implementation of the inerter in a inertia-based resonant vibration absorber to be installed on
a taut cable. The project discusses the invention and advantages of the inerter and three
dierent constellations of inertia-based absorbers are implemented in the theoretical model
and analysed in order to identify the main dierences and advantages.
The basis of this project is the development of the necessary theory for the implementation of
the inerter in the resonant vibration absorber. The theoretical foundation is based on the
dierential equation for the taut cable along with a Finite Element-model which are to be
compared for the natural frequencies of the cable with dierent congurations of the absorber
exerting a force on the cable. The FEM-model will furthermore provide a calibration of the
absorber parameters and the results are analyzed through the concept of equal modal
damping, frequency responses and a root locus analysis. The results obtained for the
inertia-based resonant vibration absorber will nally be compared to that of the viscous
damper which is one of the most used dampers on cables today.
An experimental setup of a 12 meter taut cable is used in the calibration of the absorber. The
properties used in the theoretical model is based on this cable. The goal is to design and
develop a prototype of an inerter to install on the experimental setup. This prototype will be
based on the design of a ball screw inerter. Final comments and improvements are given on
the design process of the prototype along with suggestions for the further work and
development of the inerter.
The project shows that the obtained damping ratio for the three dierent constellations of
inertia-based absorbers is as much as three times larger than that of the viscous damper that
is used today. The physical parameters of the absorbers varies depending on the location on
the cable and it is seen that the parameters decreases as the absorbers are moved further
away from the support when the optimum damping ratio is sought. The design of the ball
screw inerter prototype shows its limitations due to veil between the screw and the rotating
nut and suggestions for the further improvement of the inerter is given based on the
experimental work. This project clearly presents the potential of a inertia-based absorber on
a taut cable for implementation on a cable-stayed bridge.

Resumé
Designet og udviklingen af store skråstagsbroer fører til længere og mere slanke kabler.
Påvirkningen fra vind, regn og sne kan lede til store vibrationer af kablet og i værste fald føre
til ødelæ ggende bevægelse. For at modstå de mere slanke designs er en fremskridende
udvikling af ekstern dæmpning af kabler i gang. Dette projekt omhandler implementeringen
af inerteren i en inerti-baseret resonant vibrations absorber, der skal installeres på et kabel.
Projektet diskuterer opndelsen og fordelene ved inerteren og tre forskellige konstellationer af
den inerti-baserede absorber er implementeret i en teoretisk model og analyseret for at
klargøre de vigtige forskelle og fordele.
Fundamentet for dette projekt er udviklingen af den nødvendige teori for at implementere
interteren i den resonante vibrations absorber. Det teoretiske fundament are baseret på
dierentialligningen for det opspændte kabel sammen med en Finite Element-model, hvilke
skal sammenlignes for de naturlige egenfrekvenser af kablet med forskellige kongurationer af
absorberen, der påfører en kræ ft på kablet. FEM-modellen vil yderligere levere en kalibrering
af absorber parametrene og resultatet er analyseret igennem konceptet om ens modal
dæmpning, frekvens respons og en root locus analyse. De opnåede resultater for den
inerti-baserede resonante vibrations absorber vil endelig blive sammenlignet med dem fra den
viskose dæmper, som er en af de mest anvendte dæmpere på kabler i dag.
En eksperimentel opstilling af et 12 meter opspændt kabel er brugt i kalibreringen af
absorberen. Egenskaberne brugt i den teoretiske model er baseret på dette kabel. Målet er at
designe og udvikle en prototype af en inerter, der skal installeres på den eksperimentelle
opstilling. Denne prototype er baseret på designet af en kugle spindler inerter. Afsluttende
kommentarer og forbedringer er givet angående design processen af prototypen sammen med
forslag til det videre arbejde og udvikling af inerteren.
Dette projekt viser at den opnåede dæmpning for de tre forskellige konstellationer af
inerti-baserede absorbere er så meget som tre gange større end dem fra den viskose dæmper,
der er brugt i dag. De fysiske parametre af absorberen varierer afhæ ngig af placeringen på
kablet og det er observeret at paramtrene aftager når absorberen er yttet væk fra
understøtningen når den optimale dæmpning er søgt fundet. Designet af kugle spindler
inerter prototypen viser sine begrænsninger grundet slør mellem skruen og den roterende del
og forslag til den videre forbedring af inerteren er given baseret på det eksperimentelle
arbejde. Dette projekt præ senterer tydeligt potentialet af en inerti-baseret absorber på et
opspændt kabel med hensyn til implementering på en skråstagsbro.

Introduction
Ever since mankind started constructing the inuence of natural phenomenons like earthquake
and volcanic eruptions have been of great importance. As time went by we learned to design
taller and more slender structures and suddenly natural eects like wind, rain and snow
became a larger issue in the design process. Today a lot of structures like bridges and
high-rise buildings advantages from dierent kinds of dampers to extract mechanical energy
from the structure. In this chapter a general introduction of cables and dampers is presented.
1.1
Cables and slender bridges
"Machinae Novae" [1] or "New Machines" was written in 1595 by the Venetian author Fausto
Veranzio. Amongst many other things, e.g. the parachute, the book described the concept of
a cable-stayed bridge. Now more than 400 years later the application of cable-stayed bridges
is used worldwide.
Figure 1.1.1: Fausto Veranzio's design of a cable-stayed bridge from "Machinae Novae" [2].
Since the rst cable-stayed bridge was build the designs has become longer and more slender
[3]. Most cables have a high exiblity because of the long span distances and low weight. Due
to this they get easily excited to large displacement by minimal forces, e.g. from wind and
rain [4]. Because of this the dynamic response of the structure has become an important
aspect in the design of new bridges. Several examples have shown that the dynamic response
can be damaging or even destructive.

Eects of cable vibrations
An example of destructive behaviour due to high wind speeds is the Franjo Tudjman Bridge
in Dubrovnik which in March 2005 and 2006 were exposed to heavy wind with windspeeds
around 110 kilometers pr. hour combined with snow load. The longest cables with a length of
220 meters was excited to an amplitude of around 2 meters at the middle. The damages was
seen on the protective sheath on the cable and at the anchorage.
A)
Damages on the cables
B) Damages at the anchorage
Figure 1.1.2: Illustrations of the damages on the Franjo Tudjman bridge [5].
Cables and the anchorage of cables are of great importance when designing for dynamic
response. When wind and rain-induced vibrations are present in cables, two problems occure.
First of all the vibrations can lead to failure of the cable or breakdown of the corrosion
protection systems, which will reduce the life span of the cable. The biggest structural
problem is fatigue at anchorage. Additionally large vibrations of cables will weaken the public
condence in the safety of the bridge [6]. Both circumstances are of great importance since a
long lifespan of the cables and and thus the bridge is wanted. Furthermore people must feel
safe crossing the bridge.
Longer and more slender cables
The Russky Bridge crossing the Eastern Bosphorus is the cable-stayed bridge which is in use
today with the longest span in the world of 1,104 meters. The longest cables on the bridge are
almost 580 meters (579.83m) long. This is already to be beaten by the Third Bosporus
Bridge, which started construction in May 2013, with the longest cables of almost 600 meters
(597.0m).

1.2
Stay cables
Cables are used in many aspects of construction. From small cables that are used for power
and signal transmission to large wire ropes used in structural design as anchors for buildings
and bridges [8]. The design of a stay cable consists of a bundle of individual strands of steel in
a protective sheath. Several categories of cables exist, e.g. as mechanical wire cables for guy
wired and structural use or as electrical cables as optical ber cables or for signal
transmission. Just as electrical cables transmit a signal, a mechanical wire is transmitting
forces between two locations. [4]. Transmission of forces between a bridge deck and a pylon is
exactly what stay cables are used for in construction of cable-stayed bridges. Cables can only
transfer normal forces as tension and with a high tensile strength of steel most cables in
constructions have a high tension-to-weight ratio which only gives a small sag in the order of
1% sag-to-length ratio [9].
Structural damping
Structural damping is present in all kinds of structures from high-rise buildings to cables. The
structural damping for a free cable vibration is generally low and will typically lie below 1%.
The structural damping in a cable is due to the internal solid friction and friction between the
wires. Around 5 % of the structural damping is due to the internal solid friction, leaving the
majority of energy dissipation to the friction between the wires [4]. Because of this more
energy dissipation will be seen in a cable than in a single wire. A structural damping of less
than 1% will in many cases be insucient and will require external damping.

1.3
Damping of stay cables
The implementation of external damping oers many opportunities regarding the cost,
installation, level of damping wanted, types and length of cables.
Three main categories of dampers are used today; passive, semi-active and active dampers. A
passive damper is a mechanical device which can only be optimised for a single frequency and
thereby one mode of vibration. The passive damper is the most common one and the most
simple one to design and install. An example of the passive damper is the viscous uid
damper which is a piston that can be tuned through the viscosity of the oil in the piston. The
disadvantage of the passive damper is that if the viscous coecient changes from its desired
and optimum value, a rapid decrease in the modal damping ratio is seen [9]. Passive dampers
are seen to have the highest eect on shorter cables. In order to provide sucient damping on
long cables some signicant changes will have to be made to the aestetics, e.g. moving the
damper further away from the support or making larger and more visible dampers.
A semi-active damper is made like the passive damper, but can change properties in real time
and "attack" the given situation of vibration. This change can be made by sending an electric
current through a uid containing metal and thereby changing the viscosity. If this electric
current is shut down, the semi-active damper becomes a passive damper. The active damper
is the most eective and complicated category of dampers, since it provides energy into a

system and can "attack" multiple modes of vibrations at the same time [6]. In the following
section the two most common types of passive dampers are presented.
Viscous and friction damper
The viscous and the friction damper are the most used dampers in the construction of bridges
today [9]. For long cables the friction damper may be insucient and thus an external damper
such as the viscous damper is used. The architectural advantage of the friction damper is that
it is included in the anchorage of the cable and thus does not require an external damper to
be attached somewhere on the cable. The friction damper is very easily tuned on site by
adjusting the friction force by tightening or loosening the bolts. This is an important
dierence from the viscous damper which is tuned by changing the viscosity of the uid.

1.4
The inerter
The inerter is a mechanical device with two terminals which key property is that the applied
force at the two terminals is proportional to the relative acceleration between the terminals.
The proportionality constant is called inertance and has the unit of kilogram [19]. This device
can replace the vibrating mass in the tuned mass damper and with two terminals it leads to
dierent possible constellations of the spring, damper and inerter.
The invention of the inerter
In the years before 2005 Professor Smith from Cambridge worked on the inerter with the
application of a potential performance advantage on vehicle suspension. In the 2005 Spanish
Grand Prix the McLaren-team used the inerter and it led Kimi Raikkonen to the victory. In
order to keep the inerter a secret for as long as possible it was given the name "J-damper". If
a technician from McLaren would switch teams, he would not know what the technolgy
actually was. The "J-damper" is not a damper and the "J" could only refer to the ywheels
moment of inertia even though this was not the case. It was rst mentioned in the 2007
Formula One "spy scandal", although this was more speculations than actual facts. In May
2008 two articles in the magazine Autosport revealed what the J-damper actually was and

Advantages of the inerter
The invention of the inerter has brought many new aspects to the development of structural
damping. Some advantages or dierences are evident. First of all the inerter is a two terminal
device. This key feature enables applications of the inerter between two structural parts, e.g.
the cable and the bridge deck. This means that an inertia-based absorber could be installed
close to the anchorage of a stay cable. Compared to the tuned mass damper the inertance, bd,
compared to the vibrating mass, md, could be signicantly smaller. This is due to rotation of
the ywheel which creates the inertance. This means that the actual mass of the inerter will
be far smaller than the inertance it is providing. Finally the inertance can possibly be tuned
on site with the right design. Changing the inertance is simply a question of changing the
ywheel's moment of inertia also called the rotational inertia or by changing the number of
rotations.

Background exibility - quasi-static correction
The concept of background exibility is introduced due to excitation of higher modes from
the force provided by the resonant vibration absorber on exible structures like the taut
cable. The resonant vibration absorber is designed to target one specic mode i.e. the
resonant mode ur. In cases where the resonant mode corresponds to the lowest natural
frequency, ur = us, a quasi-static exibility correction is sucient. This includes the eect of
higher modes where the additional displacement is considered as the introduction of an
additional exibility denoted k0. For resonant absorbers targeting higher modes a
quasi-dynamic exibility should be performed in order to avoid large errors in the calibration
of the parameters. The theory is based on the work by Krenk and Høgsberg [14] [24].
The theory is applied on a tuned mass absorber, which is the equivalent of the Series I-model.

Magnetic damping
In order to variate the damping parameter the concept of magnetic damping is utilized. This
is done by installing an aluminum disk on the rotating nut. Note that the disk now serves two
purposes. Aluminum is chosen since it is a conductive but not magnetic material. In this way
the magnet is not attracted to the disk but will still apply a force that slows down the
motion. This happens because the magnetic eld will create an opposite force on a moving
conductive material.

B)

The aluminum disk will act as an electrical conductor. When the disk is rotating each little
piece of the disk will see a change in the magnetic eld and tries to oppose that change. A
little current is set up in the disk which creates its own little magnetic eld which pulls back
towards the magnet thus slowing it down. The mechanical energy that was in the wheel is
turned into heat inside the disk [30].
The magnetic eld from the magnet and thus the opposing force in the disk will change with
the distance from the magnet to the aluminum disk. Due to the problems of getting the nut
to rotate, no investigations were made on the provided damping from the magnet depending
on the distance.

Conclusion and discussion
This project has presented and derived the necessary theory for the implementation of the
inerter in three dierent models of an inertia-based absorber. The theory included the
establishment of the structural matrices, the concept of the complex eigenvalue problem used
to calculate the damping ratio, and the calibration of the parameters in the absorber based on
a desired damping ratio. The calibration of the absorber parameters was based on an initial
desired damping ratio, ζ∗, from which the stiness, damping parameter and inertance can be
found. The results claried that an optimal desired damping ratio is found for all three
absorbers resulting in the maximum damping ratio, ζ. The optimal desired damping ratio
showed to depend on the location of the absorber on the cable. The spring-based absorber,
i.e. the Series II-model, and the Parallel-model showed the highest damping ratio of 5.3% and
with a conguration of the calibration as much as 6.05%.
It was found that a viscous damper at the same location as the inertia-based absorbers had a
damping ratio of only one-third compared to the inertia-based absorber. This means that the
introduction of the inertia-based absorber has shown a signicantly higher eciency in
damping. This project has discussed three dierent constellations of the inertia-based
absorber with some interesting similarities. The Series II- and the Parallel-model gave a lot of
similar results regarding damping. For the optimum damping, ζ, it was clear that the physical
parameters of all three inertia-based absorbers decreased when the location of the absorber
was changed which is in clear contrast to the viscous damper. This is an important aspect
from an architectural and structural point of view. High damping can be obtained for
dierent constellations of spring, damper and inerter and the physical properties of the
absorber depends on where the absorber is installed. Often it is convenient to install the
damper close to the support which means that a damper with high damping and small
parameters would be preferable. It was also discussed that the Parallel-model is the most
simple one to design and construct for an experimental setup. The design of the prototype of
the ball screw inerter has shown its limitations. The veil between the screw and nut turned
out to be too large and at the same time the stability of the inerter caused problems. These
malfunctions lead to a inerter that did not rotate as it was supposed to. This means that the
further work on the experimental setup could not continue. Dierent suggestions for the
further development of the inerter was proposed based on the experience obtained by the
experimental work and the design process of the prototype.
Based on this project the future of inertia-based absorbers has a great potential. Based on
the theoretical results, damping ratios three times larger can be obtained compared to the
viscous damper. The design process of the inerter in a inertia-based absorbers still have some
way to go, but with the right congurations an experimental setup should be able to be
carried out in order to see the eciency of an inertia-based absorber from a practical view.

Further work
Suggestions for the further work is based on the experience obtained by working with this
project. Some new things should be done and some alterations should be made to the existing
project. New aspects of the application of the inerter should also be investigated. Most parts
of the suggestions regards the experimental part of the project and the physical application of
the theory derived in this project.
• An investigation of the design of other kinds of inerters should be carried out. The
experimental part of this project has been based on the ball screw inerter. Now some
weaknesses and malfunctions have been claried which sets the stage for other kinds of
inerters where these malfunctions could be neglected.
• The calibration of the physical inerter should be based on the optimal damping ratio
from the theoretical model. This could be done by introducing a tuneable inertance.
One suggestion for this extension is moveable masses in the inerter that can change the
moment of inertia easily. If possible a small inertance should be preferable.
• The application of magnetic damping should be thoroughly investigated. It was shown
that the damping parameter in the resonant absorber is substantial lower than that in
the viscous damper. Whether or not the magnetic can actually provide this damping is
important. Other kinds of conductive materials should be studied along with the
number of magnets and their distance to the rotating disk.
• It turned out that the Parallel-model has the highest damping ratio along with the Series
II-model. Whether or not the two series-models can be made for an experimental setup
should also be investigated. The introduction of viscous damper on the Parallel-model
should be considered since it is a simpler device than the concept of magnetic damping.
• The construction of a new or modied inerter should include a force test to clarify the
force needed to excite the inerter. This force has to be provided by the cable. The force
from the cable can be altered by the modal mass of the cable. The 7x19 strand cable
could be replaced by a heavier cable instead of applying concentrated masses on the
cable.
• After the design of a prototype the process of developing an actual inerter for
implementation of cable-stayed bridge should be carried out. This regards; The physical
dimensions of the inerter, casing to protect the inerter from the weather, cost estimate,
maintenance of the inerter and so on.

3.7 
Stay geometry verification 
The theoretical angles, cable length and cable quantities have been calculated and 
compared to the drawings. 
Angles 𝜶 
The angles of the stays with horizontal (denoted 𝛼) were found to be in accordance 
with the drawings. A slight discrepancy of 0.04 degrees were found for some of the 
cables, which is insignificant. 
This is possibly, because the tangent point at the pylon saddle were assumed at 
the pylon edge by the IDC (for simplicity as no information of the work point were 
given in the drawings), whereas the designer may have used a more correct 
tangent point. 
Cable length 𝑳 
The length of each cable from work point to work point, including the length of the 
cable passing through the saddle in the pylon, was calculated and rounded off 
(regular rounding to nearest digit) to match the numbers given in the drawings. 
Similar cable lengths were found, but for the shortest cable in each pylons a length 
of 64 m was found by the IDC where 65 m was stated by the designer. 
Also, cable 109/159 in pylon S5 was found by the designer to be 250 m, whereas 
the designer found the similar cable 609/659 in pylon S10 to be 249 m (similar to 
all the other pylons). As the cables have a symmetrical layout around the crown of 
the bridge, cable 109/159 and cable 609/659 should be similar. 
Cable quantity 
The complete quantity of the cable were found by multiplying each cable length by 
1.172 kg/m, in accordance with EN 10138-3, Table 4. The results of the quantity 
summation and a comparison between the designer's and IDC's values have been 
compared in Table 3. 
It shall be noted, that this quantity refers only to the theoretical cable length 
between work points, excluding sag, over length of cables for jacking, etc.  
Only a small deviation of 0.7 % has been found, which is regarded to be 
acceptable. However, the designer states a different quantity for pylon S5 and S10, 
which should be similar due to symmetry.

Critical Speed
It is necessary to check if the ball screw rotation speed is resonant with the natural frequency of the screw shaft. 
We recommend 80% or less of this critical speed as an allowable rotation speed. The figur shows a diagram 
which summarizes the allowable rotation speed for shaft nominal diameters up to outside diameter of the screw 
shaft exceeds 125 mm. Select the graduation of allowable rotation speed according to the method of supporting 
the ball screw. Where  the working rotation speed presents a problem in terms of critical speed, it would be best 
to provide an intermediate support to increase the natural frequency of the screw shaft.

Functional Principle, Technical Data
3.
Functional Principle, Technical Data
3.1
Short Description
The sensor uses the principle of optical triangulation,
i.e. a visible, modulated point of light is projected onto
the target surface.
Depending on the distance the diffuse fraction of the
reflection of this point of light is then focussed onto a
position sensitive element (CCD-array) by the receiving
lens, which is arranged at a certain angle with respect
to the optical axis of the laser beam.
The controller calculates the measured value from the
CCD-array. A internal closed-loop control enables the
sensor to measure against different surfaces.
A LED on the sensor indicates:
- In range
- Out of Range (upper and lower range values),
poor target (unfit or no object)
- Mid range

measuring range

Instructions for Operation
7.
Instructions for Operation
7.1
Reflection Factor of the Target Surface
In principle the sensor works with the diffuse part of the reflected laser light. A statement concerning a
minimum reflectivity is difficult to make, because even a small diffuse fraction can be evaluated from highly
reflecting surfaces. This is done by determining the intensity of the diffuse reflection from the CCD array signal
and subsequent compensation for intensity fluctuations.
7.2
Error Influences
Colour differences
Because of intensity compensation, colour difference of targets affect the measuring result of the ILD1300 only
slightly.
Temperature influences
When the sensor is commissioned a warm-up time of at least 15 minutes is required to achieve uniform
temperature distribution in the sensor.
If measurement is performed in the micron accuracy range, the effect of temperature fluctuations on the sensor
holder must be considered.
Mechanical vibration
If the sensor is to be used for high resolution measurements, special care must be taken to ensure stable and
vibration-free mounting of sensor and target.
Surface roughness
In case of traversing measurements surface roughnesses of 5 µm and more lead to an apparent distance
change (also-called surface noise).

Angle influence
Tilt angles of the target both around the X and the Y axes of less than 5 ° only have a disturbing effect with
surfaces which are highly reflecting. Tilt angles between 5 ° and 15 ° lead to an apparent distance change of
approx. 0.5 % of the measuring range (fig. 7.1). Tilt angles between 15 ° and 30 ° lead to an apparent
distance change of approx. 1 % of the measuring range.
