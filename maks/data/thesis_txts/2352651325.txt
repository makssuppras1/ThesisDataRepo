Rotavirus type A associated diarrhoea in neonatal piglets  
 
Abstract  
Rotavirus A (RVA) is a well-known cause of diarrhoea in neonatal piglets, but the infection dynamics 
and clinical impact are not fully elucidated. The aim of this study was to investigate the significance 
of infection with RVA in relation to neonatal diarrhoea from the 2nd to the 5th day of the piglets’ life.  
Two commercial swine herds with a problem of neonatal diarrhoea and a positive RVA diagnosis in 
the farrowing unit were included. Five litters from each of the two herds and a total of 132 piglets 
were analysed on a day defined as the case day of the litter. The case day was the day where more 
than 25% of the piglets in the litter showed clinical diarrhoea, and in all 10 litters the case day was 
either the 4th or 5th day of life. On the case day, fecal samples from all piglets in the litter were 
analysed for RVA. Additional analyses were made on samples collected two days prior to the case 
day from 54 of the 132 piglets. The analyses were made by an in-house RVA specific RT-qPCR. 
Virulent Escherichia coli (E. coli) was ruled out by PCR as differential diagnosis in all litters except 
one by testing a pooled sample on the case day. On a daily basis all the piglets were subjected to a 
clinical evaluation and feces was collected and scored as diarrheic or non-diarrheic. The piglets were 
weighed at the beginning and at the end of study so that the weight gain during the four-day study 
period could be calculated.  
In total, 43% (57/132) of the piglets had clinical diarrhoea and 66% (87/132) were positive for RVA 
on the case day. For comparison, 89% (51/57) of the diarrheic piglets were positive for RVA whereas 
only 48% (36/75) of the non-diarrheic piglets were positive for RVA which was significantly different 
(p<0.001). However, no association between the RVA load in feces and the fecal consistency in RVA 
positive piglets on the case day was found. Piglets that tested negative for RVA on the case day had 
a higher weight gain over the four-day period (mean 363g vs. 278g, p<0.05) despite that the RVA 
positive piglets had a significantly higher start weight (mean 1.45kg vs. 1.27kg, p<0.05). 
Furthermore, 49 out of the 54 piglets that were tested for three consecutive days were RVA positive 
in at least one of the three samples. Within this group, 69% (34/49) of the piglets developed diarrhoea 
within 24 hours after a positive RVA diagnosis. 
The results confirmed that RVA has a significant impact on incidence of diarrhoea and body weight 
gain in piglets also in litters negative for virulent E. coli. Furthermore, the results of this study have 
shown that it is the presence- and not the load of RVA that is essential for the association between 
RVA and diarrhoea in neonatal piglets. 
 
Keywords: Rotavirus A, RVA, piglets, neonatal diarrhoea, virus dynamics, E. coli, RT-qPCR

Resumé 
Rotavirus A (RVA) er en velkendt årsag til diarré blandt nyfødte grise, men dynamikken og den 
kliniske påvirkning ved en infektion er ikke fuldt belyst. Formålet med dette studie var at undersøge 
betydningen af en infektion med RVA i relation til spædgrisediarré fra grisens 2. til 5. levedøgn. 
To svinebesætninger med konventionel produktionsform blev inkluderet i studiet og begge 
besætninger havde en positiv RVA diagnose i farestalden samt et problem med spædgrisediarré. Fem 
kuld fra hver besætning med i alt 132 pattegrise blev analyseret på kuldets case dag. Kuldets case dag 
var defineret, som den dag hvor mere end 25% af grisene i kuldet havde klinisk diarré, og for alle de 
10 analyserede kuld var case dagen enten grisenes 4. eller 5. levedøgn. På case dagen blev alle grise 
i kuldet analyseret for RVA. Prøverne som var indsamlet to dage op til case dagen blev analyseret fra 
54 af de 132 grise. Analyserne blev udført på en in-house RVA specifik RT-qPCR. Virulent 
Escherichia coli (E. coli) blev udelukket som differentialdiagnose i alle kuld på nær et via PCR 
analyser, som blev udført på poolede prøver fra case dagen for hvert enkelt kuld. Alle grise fik dagligt 
foretaget en almen klinisk vurdering samtidigt med at en fæcesprøve blev udtaget og dennes 
konsistens blev scoret som diarré eller ikke-diarré. Grisene blev vejet på forsøgets første og sidste 
dag, således at en tilvækst kunne udregnes for den 4-dages studieperiode.  
På case dagen havde i alt 43% (57/132) af grisene klinisk diarré og 66% (87/132) af grisene var 
positive for RVA. I gruppen af diarrégrise var 89% (51/57) positive for RVA mens kun 48% (36/75) 
af grisene uden diarré var positive for RVA, hvilket var en signifikant forskel (p<0,001). Der blev 
dog ikke fundet nogen sammenhæng mellem mængden af RVA i fæces og konsistensen af fæces 
blandt de RVA positive grise på case dagen. Grise som var negative for RVA på case dagen havde 
en signifikant højere gennemsnitlig tilvækst over de fire dage (gennemsnit 363g vs. 278g, p<0,05), 
og det til trods for at de RVA positive grise havde en signifikant højere vægt ved forsøgets start 
(gennemsnit 1,45kg vs. 1,27 kg, p<0,05). Ud af de 54 grise som blev testet tre dage i træk, var 49 
grise positive for RVA på minimum én af de tre dage. Ud af disse 49 grise udviklede 69% (34/49) 
diarré indenfor 24 timer efter at RVA blev påvist i fæces. 
Resultaterne fra dette studie har bekræftet at RVA har signifikant betydning for udviklingen af diarré 
samt tilvæksten blandt nyfødte grise, også i kuld som er negative for virulent E. coli. Endvidere har 
resultaterne i studiet vist, at det alene er tilstedeværelsen af RVA i fæces og ikke den kvantitativ 
mængde, som er afgørende for sammenhængen mellem RVA og diarré i spædgrise.

1. Introduction 
Neonatal diarrhoea in piglets is a well-known problem in commercial swineherds, causing both 
increased mortality and lower productivity which causes a negative economic impact (Ruiz et al. 
2016). Diarrhoea is often a multifactorial symptom which can be set off by infectious agents, 
management errors or in some cases the aetiology remains unclear to our knowledge despite efforts 
to set a diagnose (Kongsted et al. 2013).  
Rotavirus (RV) infects the mature enterocytes in the jejunum, and causes diarrhoea due to 
malabsorption and the enterotoxic effect of non-structural protein 4 (NSP4) (Lundgren & Svensson 
2001). The pathogenesis however is still being discussed, and other theories will be mentioned in the 
following literature review. RV as the causative agent for diarrhoea in neonatal piglets has been 
described in several studies (Morin et al. 1990)(Barreiros et al. 2003), but in most studies the 
subclinical infections are not analysed or taken into account.  
This Master’s thesis contains a study review of neonatal diarrhoea in piglets, a description of RVA 
as the causative agent of diarrhoea, the diagnostic possibilities and measurements to prevent neonatal 
diarrhoea caused by RVA. Afterwards follows a research field study and finally the results will be 
analysed and discussed. 
2. Literature review  
2.1 Characteristics of rotavirus 
RV is a genus under the family of Reoviridae. The virus genome is enclosed in a triple-layered 
icosahedral capsid and consists of 11 segments of double-stranded RNA (ds-RNA). These segments 
encode six viral structural proteins (VP1 to VP4, VP6 and VP7) and six non-structural proteins (NSP1 
to NSP6) (Matthijnssens et al. 2008). The virus measures approximately 75 nm in diameter and the 
total genome size is approximately 18.522 base pairs. The eleven segments range in size from 667 
base pairs up to 3.302 base pairs (Estes & Cohen 1989). 
There are five official RV species (A-E) and three tentative species (F-H) which have been established 
based on nucleotide similarities of the genes encoding the inner capsid protein of the virus, VP6. The 
RV species are often referred to as RV groups. RVA, RVB and RVC infect both animal and human, 
whereas RVD, RVE, RVF, RVG and RVH only infect animals (Matthijnssens et al. 2012). RVA, 
RVB and RVC can infect pigs, but RVA is, due to its high prevalence and pathogenicity, considered

the most important (Chandler-Bostock et al. 2015). To a lesser extend also RVE and RVH can be 
found in pig feces (Theuns et al. 2014).

2.2 Epidemiology 
The main route for transmission of RV is fecal-oral. However, RV has been found in nasal secretion 
in both human and pigs (Azevedo et al. 2005) and in 46 out of 61 air samples (75 %) collected from 
hospital rooms with infants infected with RV (Dennehy et al. 1998). In a study by Fu and Hampson 
from 1989, the period of time from where the first pig in a litter was infected with RVA, until the 
entire litter was infected was four to 10 days (Fu & Hampson 1989). After infection pigs generally 
have diarrhoea over a period of 2-5 days, but in immunocompromised animals, infection and virus 
excretion may persist for several months (Estes et al. 2001). It is also evident that there is an age-
related resistance to developing RV associated diarrhoea. This is well documented in humans and has 
also been shown in pigs in a Danish veterinary Master´s thesis conducted on weaned pigs 0-
3 weeks post weaning (Estes et al. 2001) (Pedersen 2016). The prevalence of RVA and RVC found 
in different studies of suckling piglets vary quite a lot, which is demonstrated in table 1.

2.3 Rotavirus and diarrhoea 
2.3.1 Pathogenesis of rotavirus diarrhoea  
An infection with RV is characterized by viral replication in enterocytes in the small intestine, with 
subsequent cell lysis and attendant villus blunting, watery diarrhoea and dehydration (Estes et al. 
2001). 
RVs have been associated with outbreaks of diarrhoea in neonatal piglets (Barreiros et al. 2003) 
(Morin et al. 1990). The infection route of RV is mainly fecal-oral and by passing through the 
alimentary tract the virus reaches the small intestine (Azevedo et al. 2005). In the small intestine RV 
infects the mature enterocytes in the mid and upper part of the villi by a multistep process. There are 
two hypotheses on how RV enters the enterocyte; through direct entry/fusion or through Ca2+ 
dependent endocytosis (Lundgren & Svensson 2001). All ds-RNA viruses have capsid-associated 
transcriptase activity, but this is not activated until the virus has entered the cell. The transcriptase 
activation of RV is associated with conformational changes of the membrane, and this is driven by a 
decrease in Ca2+ concentrations (Ruiz et al. 2000).  
The transcription of the virus is undescribed, but after transcription the double layered virus enters 
the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). In the ER the virus matures due to the high Ca2+ concentration and 
the outer capsid proteins VP4 and VP7 are assembled, making it the complete triple layered virus. 
The invaded cell undergoes necrosis due to a rise in cytosolic Ca2+, possibly involving activation of 
Ca2+ dependent enzymes (Ruiz et al. 2000).  
 
2.3.2 Pathophysiology  
There are different theories on the mechanisms by which RV induces diarrhoea. Malabsorption in 
relation to RV diarrhoea have been demonstrated in many studies (Lundgren & Svensson 2001). This 
is believed to be correlated to the destruction of enterocytes and the histopathological changes that 
happen in the intestines. The changes are most pronounced in the proximal small intestine and occur 
24 hours post infection (Lundgren & Svensson 2001). In a study by Shaw et al., orally inoculated 3 
week-old piglets started showing symptoms (anorexia, vomiting and diarrhoea) 30 hours post 
inoculation. The pigs were inoculated with 1 x 106,5 TCID50 (tissue culture infectious dose, red.) of 
RVA, OSU strain (Shaw et al. 1989).

It is also believed that an activation of the enteric nervous system (ENS) plays an important role in 
fluid and electrolyte secretion in RV diarrhoea. This was demonstrated in vitro by measuring the 
transmural potential differences (PD) in mice intestine. A twofold increase in PD of RV infected mice 
relative to uninfected mice was shown. In the same study, the role of the ENS was also evident in 
vivo when RV infected mice were treated with four different drugs that inhibited the ENS, which 
significantly attenuated the secretory intestinal response to RV (Lundgren et al. 2000). This is a 
possible explanation why RV diarrhoea in some cases occurs without any visible tissue damage 
(Lorrot & Vasseur 2007). 
Another factor of influence is the enterotoxic effect of NSP4. NSP4 is absent in the infective virion 
and therefore needs to be synthesized in the infected enterocyte. NSP4 inhibits the Na+-D-glucose 
symporter SGLT1, which supports water reabsorption (Lorrot & Vasseur 2007). The effect of NSP4 
as an enterotoxin was also shown in an experiment where mice were injected with a synthetic peptide 
of NSP4 (and no RV), which led to diarrhoea in the mice (Horie et al. 1999). NSP4 also stimulates a 
Ca2+-dependent cell permeability and alters epithelial cell integrity (Estes et al. 2001). NSP4 is 
released from the infected cell either by secretion or in relation to lysis of the cell (Ruiz et al. 2000).  
In humans many extraintestinal manifestations of RV have been reported. The most common 
extraintestinal symptom is benign seizures following gastroenteritis, but also splenic lesions, acute 
arthritis and pneumonia are among the reported clinical spectrum of a RV infection (Rivero-Calle et 
al. 2016).  
2.4 Co-infections and differential diagnosis in neonatal pigs 
2.4.1 Escherichia Coli 
In a Belgian study from 2014, the bacterial pathogens found in suckling piglets together with RVA 
were Escherichia coli (E. coli), Clostridium perfringens (Cl. perfringens), Salmonella and/or 
Brachyspira sp. (Theuns et al. 2014). A simultaneous infection with enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC) 
and RV was shown to cause a more severe diarrhoea in neonatal gnobiotic piglets compared to piglets 
infected with only one of the two pathogens. The dual infection did not increase the severity of 
villus atrophy (Benfield et al. 1988). 
In a Danish study on neonatal diarrhoea in four commercial swine herds, non-haemolytic E. coli was 
the most predominant finding after aerobe culturing in both diarrheic and non-diarrheic piglets 
(Kongsted et al. 2013). This was also the case in a similar Swedish study on neonatal diarrhoea, where 
non-haemolytic E. coli was found in all piglets (Larsson et al. 2015). In both these studies the

prevalence of classic porcine ETEC with both fimbrial- and toxin genes was very low in diarrheic 
piglets, with 1/35 and 2/50, respectively. This is a bit peculiar as ETEC has commonly been 
incriminated as main etiological agent of neonatal diarrhoea (Ngeleka et al. 2003). 
There are many different virulence factors of E. coli thought to be associated with neonatal diarrhoea. 
It is generally accepted that the fimbrial adhesion factors F4, F5, F6, F18, F41 and the enterotoxins 
LT, STa and STb are of influence (Francis 1999). Some of the more discussed factors lately regarding 
neonatal diarrhoea is the enteroaggregative heat-stabile (EAST-1) enterotoxin   and the adhesin 
involved in diffuse adherence (AIDA-1). Some studies have found that EAST-1 alone cannot 
cause diarrhoea in neonatal piglets (Ruan et al. 2012) (Ngeleka et al. 2003). 
However, other studies have found, that EAST-1 could be of importance in the development 
of neonatal diarrhoea (Kongsted et al. 2016) (Larsson et al. 2015).  
2.4.2 New neonatal porcine diarrhoea syndrome (NNPDS) 
Another recent phenomenon in neonatal piglets, is a type of diarrhoea unresponsive to antibiotic 
treatment and without detection of any well-known infectious pathogens. The syndrome has been 
reported in different countries since 2008, and has later become known as new neonatal porcine 
diarrheic syndrome (NNPDS) (Kongsted et al. 2013)(Larsson et al. 2015). NNPDS can be present 
within the first week of life and the period of time in which the piglet is diarrheic was found to vary 
from 1-5 days in a study by Kongsted et al from 2014. In the same study, the onset of the diarrhoea 
varied from herd to herd, and within herds 25-50% of piglets did not start showing symptoms until 
the 4th or 5th day of life. Figure 2 on the following page illustrates the clinical pictures observed in the 
study by Kongsted et al. (Kongsted et al. 2014).

2.5 Piglet immunity  
Piglets rely on maternal immunity through colostrum intake, until their own immune system has 
matured sufficiently to respond to foreign antigens. This is due to the epitheliochorial nature of the 
porcine placenta, which prevents transfer of immunoglobulins (Ig) across the placenta (Rooke & 
Bland 2002). Figure 3 illustrates how the concentration of IgG in the colostrum changes rapidly 
within the first 24 hours post-farrowing (Le Dividich et al. 2006).

In addition, the period of time in which the piglet is able to absorb Ig from the gut is limited to around 
24 hours post-farrowing, due to gut closure (Rooke & Bland 2002). Gut closure happens as a 
consequence to ingestion of milk (Werhahn et al. 1981).   
The intake of colostrum is the main determinant of piglet survival, through provision of energy for 
thermal regulation and immune protection (Devillers et al. 2011). The colostrum intake of the piglet 
is influenced by several things such as litter size, position in the birth order, birth weight and vitality, 
all as a means of competing for teats at the sow utter while the level of antibodies in the colostrum is 
high (Farmer et al. 2006) (Devillers et al. 2011).  
The colostrum contains many immunoactive components and antimicrobial substances that 
contribute to neonatal immunity besides from Ig. This includes phagocytes, lymphocytes, epithelial 
cells, lactoferrin, lysozyme and cytokines that all contribute to the protection of the neonate 
(Wagstrom et al. 2000). Ig make up around 80 % of the protein in sow colostrum. The vast majority 
of Ig in sow colostrum is IgG with an average concentration of 95,6 mg/ml whereas the average 
concentration of IgA is only 21,2 mg/ml (Farmer et al. 2006). Another study however found the 
average concentration of IgA in colostrum to be 12,26 mg/ml and also concluded, that the IgA 
concentration was greatly influenced by breed and parity number (Inoue 1981). The ratio between 
the different Ig excreted through the mammary gland also changes during the lactation. This means 
that IgA are the main Ig in milk, whereas IgG are the main Ig in colostrum (Wagstrom et al. 2000). 
In general, first parity sows have lower colostrum IgG concentrations than multiparous sows, which 
may lead to a weaker immunity in the piglets of gilts (Dividich et al. 2005). It is generally thought 
that the protection from RV infection is mediated by local IgA (Lundgren & Svensson 2001). 
2.6 Cow colostrum as protection from rotavirus diarrhoea  
Cow colostrum is by some farmers being used as a supplement to ensure sufficient colostrum uptake 
for small piglets or in big litters. The protection of piglets from enteric pathogens through supplement 
of cow colostrum has been studied. Protection against clinical signs of RV diarrhoea was shown in 
piglets inoculated nasally with RV on their 6th day of life. The piglets were fed cow colostrum for 10 
days in a row, starting two days before inoculation (Bridger & Brown 1981). A similar study was 
made in 1- to 2-days old piglets on Porcine Epidemic Diarrhoea (PED) virus, another viral enteric 
pathogen. Here it was concluded that feeding piglets colostrum from PED immunized cows, was 
effective in preventing PED virus infection and reducing mortality in piglets (Shibata et al. 2001). 
These studies indicate that it to some extend is possible to control some enteric pathogens by feeding 
piglets colostrum from immunized cows.

In general, porcine serum Ig are superior to bovine serum Ig in providing immunity to colostrum 
deprived neonatal piglets, when given in a controlled concentration together with a sow milk replacer. 
But the sow milk replacer with bovine serum Ig do however provide better immunity than sow milk 
replacer alone (Drew & Owen 1988). A Danish study from 2001 investigated the use of bovine 
colostrum as a substitute for porcine colostrum and milk replacer in neonatal piglets and found similar 
results when measuring plasma IgG levels in the piglets. Here it was concluded, that bovine colostrum 
or preferably bovine colostrum mixed with porcine plasma proteins was a useful substitute to porcine 
colostrum (Jensen et al. 2001). 
2.7 Vaccination against rotavirus  
There are different types of vaccines available, including killed vaccines and modified live vaccines 
(MLV) (Welter & Welter 1990) (Saif & Fernandez 1996).  In Denmark there is only one registered 
vaccine available against RV, Rokovac Neo. Rokovac Neo is an inactivated RVA vaccine containing 
RV OSU 6 strain (G5[P7]), and four inactivated E. coli strains. Detection of RVA is a prerequisite 
for using the vaccine in a herd (see appendix A). Rokovac Neo is administered intra muscularly (IM) 
to sows and gilts 2-4 weeks before farrowing to increase the amount of antibodies in the colostrum 
and milk (see appendix A). 
As mentioned above the Rokovac Neo is based on the OSU 6 strain G5[P7], which as previously 
mentioned is the most common G/P combination globally seen (Papp et al. 2013). However, in the 
also previously mentioned study by Midgley et al. from 2012 where 16 Danish RVA isolates were 
genotyped, only one out of the 16 genotypes contained G5 and no genotypes contained P[7] (Midgley 
et al. 2012).  
Little or no cross-protection between RVs with distinct G and P genotypes was found in a study by 
Bohl et al., but viruses that shared at least one common G or P genotypes induced some partial cross-
protection in gnotobiotic pigs (Bohl et al. 1984). A questionable cross immunity between the different 
genotypes has also been shown for human RVs (Jalilvand et al. 2015). Due to this, vaccination against 
RV is complex because of the diversity of the genotypes present in pigs (Midgley et al. 2012) 
(Miyazaki et al. 2012). 
In other countries live vaccines against RV are registered and possible to use. In a study by Welter 
and Welter from 1990, a MLV RV vaccine was administered both IM and orally and hereby 
prevented morbidity and virus shedding in feces post challenge. The degree of seroconversion was 
significantly higher in pigs that received MLV compared to pigs that received killed vaccine (Welter 
& Welter 1990). Orally administered RV vaccines for sows have also been described. By inducing

mild replication in the intestinal tract an active local immunization occurs and stimulates significantly 
greater numbers of intestinal IgA antibody secreting cells (Saif & Fernandez 1996). This is important 
for a protective lactogenic immunity as it has been shown that a precursor for the mammary gland 
IgA producing plasma cell, originate from the intestinal tract. This ensures stimulation of the local 
intestinal immunity in the sow leading to a higher production of IgA in the colostrum and milk 
(Chattha et al. 2014). Studies have shown that a parenteral booster vaccine is effective in boosting 
lactogenic immunity, when administered after natural infection or oral vaccination in sows (Yuan et 
al. 2001). This can be explained by the IgA memory-B cells that reside in both the gut and the spleen 
of pigs. Parenteral vaccination stimulates the IgA memory-B cells residing in the spleen tissue, and 
thereby more IgA-secreting plasma cells will migrate to the mammary tissue (Chattha et al. 2014) 
(Yuan et al. 2001).   
2.8 Diagnostic possibilities  
When collecting samples, there are some important factors that should be taken into account, such 
as timing of sampling, the type and quality of the specimen, and the time from sampling to 
analysis. When testing for viral pathogens, samples from affected animals should be collected as early 
as possible, when the viral load is at its highest and before secondary infections occur (Quinn et al. 
2011).   
There are different methods to detect RV. This includes detection of the viral particle, detection of 
viral antigens and detection of the viral genome. Detecting the whole viral particle by electron 
microscopy (EM) is one of the earliest described methods used for detection of RV (Bishop et al. 
2009). This method requires a relatively large number of virus particles for a reliable confirmation. 
To differentiate between the serogroups, immune electron microscopy can be performed. In 
many laboratories EM has been replaced by newer and more sensitive techniques such as enzyme 
linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) or reverse transcription real time polymerase chain reaction 
(RT-qPCR) (Quinn et al. 2011).  
Because of the time from infection to seroconversion and the influence of maternal antibodies, 
serology is often of little diagnostic value when diagnosing diarrhoea in piglets. Maternal antibodies 
persist for 2-3 weeks, and up to 80% of the adult animals may have antibodies against RV (Taylor D. 
J. 2013). 
The best way to confirm an active infection with RV is to test for shedding of virus in feces, and this 
is done either by antigen ELISA or by RT-qPCR (Quinn et al. 2011).

2.8.1 Detection of viral antigens  
There are several techniques to detect the viral antigens of RV, including immunochromatic test 
(lateral flow test) and antigen ELISA which is the most commonly used (Taylor D. J. 2013). There 
are different kinds of ELISA formats, including direct-, indirect-, sandwich- and competitive ELISA. 
The overall purpose of an ELISA test is to capture the antigen. It can be done by absorption of the 
antigen to the assay plate or via a capture antibody attached to the plate. Detection of the antigen is 
accomplished by adding an enzyme labeled antibody which makes a fluorescent signal when it comes 
in contact with an added substrate. If the ELISA has been run properly the signal produced will be 
directly proportional to the amount of antigen captured on the plate (Thermo Fischer Scientific 2011) 
(Quinn et al. 2011). The Danish Laboratory for Swine Diseases uses a RVA sandwich ELISA for 
which the protocol can be found in appendix B. The diagnostic section at The National Veterinary 
Institute (DTU Vet) uses a RVA sandwich ELISA as described in the article by Grauballe et al. 
(Grauballe et al. 1981).  
2.8.2 The Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)  
Detection of the virus genome is one the most sensitive technologies in modern molecular 
biology. When using PCR, a specific single sequence of DNA or complementary DNA (c-DNA) can 
be amplified more than a million times using specific primers, heat-stable DNA-polymerase and 
thermal cycling. Real-time PCR (qPCR) makes it possible to quantify the PCR product at the end of 
each cycle. The advantage is that the exponential amplification in each PCR cycle is monitored, and 
can be used to determine the quantity of the PCR-product (Thermo Fischer Scientific 2014).   
2.8.3 Conventional PCR 
Before initiating the PCR, the sample DNA must be extracted reducing the risk of inhibition. After 
the extraction, two oligonucleotide primers are added, to bind to the target sequence and be amplified. 
Nucleotides and a heat-stable polymerase is also added, and the PCR-cycle is ready to start (Kubista 
et al. 2006).   
One PCR-cycle includes three temperature cycles as illustrated in figure 4 and is repeated a specific 
number of times according to the assay protocol. In theory, after each cycle the number of amplicons 
is doubled. This results in an exponential increase in amplicons. If the original sample was positive 
for the target DNA sequence, the PCR-reaction will after some cycles reach a plateau, indicating that 
the sample is positive for the target DNA. The result can be visualized by running a gel 
electrophoresis of the amplified product (Kubista et al. 2006)(Arnheim & Erlich 1992).

2.8.4 Reverse transcription PCR (RT-PCR) 
When running PCR on RNA (e.g. RV), the RNA must first be transcribed to c-DNA by reverse 
transcriptions (RT). This step is critically for an accurate and sensitive quantification, since the 
amount of c-DNA products must be similar to the amount of RNA in the sample. The primers can be 
based on oligo(dT) primer, random sequence primers or gene specific primers. In one-step RT-real 
time PCR a gene specific primer is needed because random primers will generate nonspecific 
products and will reduce the amount of target sequence (Kubista et al. 2006) (Thermo Fischer 
Scientific 2014). 
2.8.5 Real-time PCR  
In qPCR the amount of target sequence is measured after each PCR-cycle. Real time simply refers to 
amplification of DNA that is monitored while the amplification is occurring (Valasek & Repa 2005). 
The qPCR needs a fluorescent that binds to the target DNA produced and “light up” to reflect the 
amount of DNA present at each cycle. There are several types of dyes, including the SYBR green I. 
The SYBR green I binds to the minor groove of the double stranded DNA (ds-DNA), thereby emitting 
a greater fluorescence than when in free solution. The SYBR Green-I is nonspecific, and binds to all 
ds-DNA. Sequence specific probes such as hydrolysis probes, are likely the most used probes. An 
example of that is the TaqMan probe. It consists of a quencher and a reporter fluorophore attached to 
the same short DNA molecule (the probe). When close to each other, the quencher absorbs the 
signal/fluorescence from the reporter. The probe attaches to the single stranded DNA (ss-

DNA), and when the elongation occurs, the bound probe is broken down by the DNA polymerase.

The amount of fluorescence produced is correlated to the amount of DNA produced after each cycle. 
The PCR-instrument must detect the fluorescent signal and record the progress of the PCR-reaction.

3. Research study  
3.1 Background  
RVA is known to cause neonatal diarrhoea and it is also the most commonly found type of RV 
(Barreiros et al. 2003). In Denmark there is an increasing focus on RVA as the etiological cause of 
neonatal diarrhoea, as a recent Danish study found it to be one of the only pathogens of statistical 
influence in the development of neonatal diarrhoea (Kongsted et al. 2016). Due to the increased focus 
and the acceptance of its relevance, the RVA sow vaccine Rokovac Neo was introduced to the Danish 
market. The vaccine has given the veterinarians a new possibility to react if RVA is found in relation 
to an outbreak of diarrhoea, but a correct diagnosis is very important before initiating vaccination. At 
the moment the routine diagnostic tool in Denmark for RVA in neonatal piglets is antigen ELISA on 
stool or gut samples, but this test only has a dichotomized outcome, positive or negative. When using 
a RT-qPCR test it is possible to get a more nuanced picture of the RVA status of the piglets. This 
study will provide important information on how to interpret RVA RT-qPCR test results from 
neonatal piglets. Also, this study will provide important information on the dynamics of RVA in 
relation to diarrheic outbreaks in neonatal piglets. 
3.2 Objective  
The main objective of this study was to investigate the association between neonatal diarrhoea and 
the presence of RVA in feces on a day defined as the case day. On the case day, the prevalence of 
clinical diarrhoea within the litter should be at least 25%. Another objective was to investigate the 
dynamics of a RVA infection through multiple analyses of a cohort of piglets and comparing these 
results to the appurtenant clinical registrations. A third objective was to assess the use of pen floor 
stool samples in the farrowing crates to detect litters with RVA positive piglets and whether the level 
of RVA in the pen floor sample resembled the level found in the piglets of the litter. A final 
objective was to investigate whether shedding of RVA in feces affected the weight gain of the piglet 
from the 2nd to 5th day of life.

3.3 Hypotheses  
H1:  There is a positive association between the detection of RVA in feces and the occurrence of   
         diarrhoea in a neonatal piglet on the case day.  
H2:  There is a significant difference between the average fecal load of RVA in diarrheic RVA      
        positive piglets and non-diarrheic RVA positive piglets on the case day.  
H3:  The load of RVA found in the pen floor stool sample of a farrowing crate on the case day is   
        representative to the average load of RVA found in the litter of the farrowing crate.  
H4: The shedding of RVA in feces on the 4th or 5th day of life has a negative impact on the weight  
        gain of a piglet.

4.3.3 Sample size  
A one-sided formula was used to calculate the required sample size to test our main hypothesis H1,

within the first week varied from 10% to 50%. The RVA prevalences found in non-diarrheic piglets 
of the same age varied from 5% to 15%.  
In order to demonstrate an expected difference of RVA prevalence between the diarrheic and non-

diarrhoea, 24 litters (8 x 3) should be sampled to get 8 diarrheic litters. 
It was decided that two herds should be included in the study, as it would give a more nuanced picture 
compared to sampling from only one herd, so 12 (24 / 2) litters were sampled in each herd.

4.4 Laboratory analysis   
The samples were processed in the laboratory within 24 - 48 hours after collection. At the laboratory 
the 10 ml plastic tubes containing the fecal swabs were vortexed to solute the fecal material from the 
cotton swab into the saline buffer. The fecal solution was then poured into a 2 ml Eppendorf 
tube and frozen at -80℃ until further processing.  All samples were processed and analysed at DTU 
Vet. The fecal samples from the piglets were tested for RVA by RT-qPCR. The pen flor samples 
were tested for both RVA by RT-qPCR and E. Coli by culturing and PCR analyses for factors of 
virulence.  
4.4.1 Pre-treatment of the fecal samples 
Before pre-treatment of the samples, 50 µL of the fecal solution was transferred from each pen floor 
sample to a 2 ml Eppendorf tube (Sigma-Aldrich, Ependorf Safe-Lock microcentrifuge tubes, 2 ml). 
Before the RNA extraction each fecal sample was processed in a TissueLyser II (Qiagen, TisueLysser 
II). A stainless steel bead (QIAGEN, stainless steel beads, 5mm) was added to each sample and the 
sample was processes in the tissuelyser for 30 sec. at 15 hertz. The suspension was then centrifuged 
for 180 sec. at 12.000 rpm (Ekspresso Personal Centrifuge, ThermoFischer). 400 µL supernatant was 
collected with a pipette and transferred to a 2 ml Saarstedt tube (Saarstedt 2.0 ml SC Micro Tube 
PCR-PT) and was subsequently frozen at -80℃ until purification.   
4.4.2 Purification of RNA from the fecal samples  
The sample RNA and DNA was extracted from the 400 µL supernatant mentioned above. 
The purification was done by an automated purification robot QIAsymphony and the QIAsymphony 
DSP Virus/Pathogen Mini Kit (QIAGEN, Copenhagen, Denmark), extracting both RNA and DNA 
from viruses and bacteria. The protocol used was Complex 200_V6_DSP.   
The extraction method used, is based on magnetic particle technology, which enables purification of 
high-quality nucleic acids that are free from proteins, nucleases and other impurities. The elution 
volume was 110 µL and the processed sample volume was 200 µL. All racks for analyses contained 
22 samples plus a negative- (water) and a positive control for RVA (RVA-isolate). 
The purified product was kept at -80℃ until further analysis.

4.4.4 Bacteriology of the pen floor samples  
As previously described, 50 µL of the fecal solutions from each of the case day pen floor samples 
were submitted to the diagnostic section of DTU Vet for E. coli bacteriological testing. The fecal 
solution was streaked onto a blood- and Drigalski agar using a 1 µL inoculation loop and then 
incubated for 24 hours at 37℃. Morphological characteristics of colonies and semi-quantification of 
bacterial growth was recorded.  
If E. coli was suspected, two colonies were selected for subculturing. A single colony from each 
subculture was then tested by Maldi Tof analysis (personal communication, Gitte Larsen, DTU Vet). 
Verified E. coli isolates were tested by qPCR for adhesion factors and enterotoxins by the commercial 
available test at the diagnostic section at DTU Vet (F4, F5, F6, F41, F18, STa, STb, 
VT2E, LT). Furthermore, two additional virulence factors; AIDA-1 and EAST-1 were tested by 
conventional PCR at the diagnostic section at DTU Vet.

4.5 Statistical analyses  
Statistical analyses were performed with Microsoft Excel, GraphPad Prism 7.02 and the website 
www.niwa.co.nz/services/statistical.  
To evaluate potential associations between two categorical variables, Fischer’s exact tests were 
performed. The odds ratio (OR) was reported with significance level of 5 %. OR and not relative risk 
(RR) was reported because the study was a case control study, and the sample size was not 
representative to the entire herds.  
Continuous data was tested for normal distribution and was reported with mean and standard 
deviation (SD). When performing a student t-test the variances of the two groups were first tested 
with an F-test to see if the variances of the two groups were significantly different. If the variances 
were equal to each other a parametric analysis was carried out and if the variances were significantly 
different a non-parametric test was carried out.  
To evaluate and visualize the quantitative agreement between the pen floor sample and the theoretical 
pool (average RVA load of the litter) a Lin Concordance correlation test and a Bland-Altmann plot 
was made. Both tests were performed as done by Pedersen et al. (Pedersen et al. 2014). The agreement 
between the individual pairwise differences were reported with a 95% confidence interval (CI). A 
student t-test was carried out to see if the means of the two tests were significantly different.  
4.5.1 Transformation of Ct-values  
To test hypotheses H2 and H3, an arbitrary standard curve was made on the RVA concentrations, 
since no validated standard curve for the assay existed. This meant that the concentrations should not 
be interpreted as the absolute number of RVA copies. Before calculating the mean load of RVA 
within certain groups, the Ct-values were transformed using the arbitrary standard curve and then log 
transformed into log10 values.

6. Discussion 
6.2 Method 
It was chosen to include two herds in the study to get a more nuanced clinical picture. It turned out 
that the clinical pictures in the two herds were quite different, with a much stronger correlation 
between RVA and diarrhoea in herd 1. To see if one of these two clinical pictures is more common, 
more RVA positive herds should have been included in the study. 
When designing the study, the main focus was to find diarrheic litters. This was necessary to get 
enough diarrheic piglets, to answer the hypotheses and to describe the dynamics of RVA in 
association to diarrhoea. However, by only analysing diarrheic litters the prevalence and dynamics of 
RVA in non-diarrheic litters was unknown.  
To increase the chances of finding diarrheic litters, mainly gilt litters and 2nd and 3rd parity litters were 
sampled. All case litters were either gilt litters or 2nd parity litters, which should be kept in mind when 
interpreting the results, as the amount and composition of colostrum is influenced by the parity of the 
sow (Farmer et al. 2006)(Cabrera et al. 2012). 
In four of the 10 case litters, the case day was not the day with the highest prevalence of diarrhoea 
within the litter. In these litters the prevalence of diarrhoea was highest on the 1st or 2nd day of 
sampling, but due to the risk of these symptoms being NNPDS, as described by Kongsted et al. 
(Kongsted et al. 2014), a later sampling day with the required prevalence of diarrhoea was chosen to 
be the case day.  
The amount of fecal material on the swabs varied according to the consistency and amount of feces 
in the rectum. As a result of this, the amount of fecal material in the samples sent for RNA extraction 
prior to PCR analysis varied. The test results were not corrected for this factor, but while processing 
the samples, the investigators noted a number of samples that visually seemed to contain very little 
or no fecal material. These samples were not overly represented in the RVA negative group, and nor 
did they only come out with high Ct-values.  
When testing hypothesis H2 and H3, the loads of RVA used for the calculations were made from an 
arbitrary standard curve. However, the conclusions drawn in hypotheses H2 and H3, would not have 
been different if a validated standard curve had been used (personal communication, Lars Erik Larsen, 
DTU vet).

6.3 Differential diagnoses 
As previously mentioned, only E. coli was investigated as a possible differential diagnosis due to 
financial constraints. Other pathogens such as Cl. perfringens, Enterococcus hirae and RVC could 
have been of relevance in this study. In both herds the sows were vaccinated to provide maternal 
immunity against Cl. perfringens type C and E. coli (ETEC), and therefore the piglets were 
theoretically protected against both these pathogens. However, Enterococcus hirae can be relevant in 
relation to an infection with E. coli in cases of neonatal diarrhoea (Larsson et al. 2014) (Hermann-
Bank et al. 2015). RVC has also been reported as the cause of diarrhoea in neonatal piglets (Morin et 
al. 1990) (Theuns et al. 2016), and could therefore have been of relevance in this study. 
E. coli only positive for STb was found in one pen floor sample in this study. A study by Casey et al. 
found that STb toxin is not important to the development of neonatal diarrhoea, even together with 
fimbria (F41) (Casey et al. 1998). However, an older study found that STb together with fimbria K99 
(F5) was enteropathogenic (Moon et al. 1980). The study by Moon et al. also found that STb toxin 
without adhesion factors was non-pathogenic, in concordance with other studies (Dubreuil 1997). 
E. coli PCR positive only for EAST-1 was found in five pen floor samples in this study, but without 
additional virulence factors, E. coli positive for EAST-1 is not associated to neonatal diarrhoea 
(Kongsted et al. 2016). 
Only pen 9 had E. coli as a possible differential diagnosis to the diarrhoea. In this pen floor sample 
both of the pure cultured E. coli were positive for AIDA-1, EAST-1 and STb (see table 8). This made 
the E. coli pathogenic, since it had both adherence- and toxin genes. This litter was treated with 
antibiotics on the 4th day of sampling (the case day of the litter), but since no further clinical 
registrations were made, the effect of treatment could not be evaluated. Had an improvement in the 
diarrheic piglets been observed, it could have indicated that a bacterial agent was involved in the 
diarrhoea. However, all of the diarrheic piglets in this litter were also RVA positive. 
6.4 The outlier in herd 2 
In this study an outlier was observed in herd 2 as a diarrheic gilt litter totally negative for RVA. Non-
haemolytic E. coli with EAST-1 genes was found in the case day pen floor sample, but with no 
additional virulence factors. Due to this, the differential diagnoses mentioned in the previous section 
6.3 should have been taken into account to find the etiology of the diarrhoea. However, no assay was 
available to test for RVC, and the diarrheic samples from the litter had all been exposed to oxygen 
multiple times, which made it impossible to culture for Cl. perfringens (personal communication, 
Svend Erik Jorsal, DTU Vet). It was not considered to test for Enterococcus hirae, though it might 
==PAGE:48==

have been of relevance. When considering the onset of the diarrhoea observed, NNPDS could be a 
possible explanation, as illustrated in figure 2 from the study by Kongsted et al. (Kongsted et al. 
2014). 
As not all differential diagnoses were ruled out, only assumptions could be made as to why this litter 
was diarrheic and how the litter remained RVA negative, despite being right next to RVA positive 
litters. Possibly, this gilt had a different composition of colostrum, which provided a better immunity 
in the piglets, or maybe the litter was simply not exposed to RVA though it seems unlikely.  
6.5 Describing the virus dynamics 
To the authors’ best knowledge, no studies have previously been made on RVA in neonatal piglets, 
where the same piglets were analysed and subjected to clinical evaluation each day for a number of 
consecutive days.  
Only one of the 12 piglets that were analysed on the first day of sampling was RVA positive, and this 
piglet was also the only diarrheic piglet of the 12 (figure 13). In this context, it would have been 
interesting if clinical registrations and RVA analyses had been made on the piglets’ first day of life 
as well, to see how early post farrowing the piglets start shedding RVA in feces. This was impeded 
by the cross-fostering routines on the piglets’ first day of life. The result could also indicate that there 
is a stronger association between RVA and diarrhoea in the first two days of life.  
An error was made in the study design regarding the virus dynamics. Clinical registrations on the 
piglets should have been made one day beyond the sampling period, to see whether non-diarrheic 
RVA positive piglets on the fourth day of sampling, developed diarrhoea on the following day. Three 
piglets were in this category, which could potentially have changed the ratio of piglets becoming 
diarrheic within 24 hours after a positive RVA diagnosis from 34/49 (69%) (table 10) to 37/49 (75%) 
in this study.  
When looking at figures 9, 10 and 11, that displays the development in Ct-values for piglets that were 
analysed for three consecutive days and developed diarrhoea on the first-, second- or third day (the 
case day), 34/38 piglets had a Ct-value <28 on the day where clinical diarrhoea was observed. Only 
two out of these 34 piglets also had a Ct-value <28 the day before clinical diarrhoea was observed. 
These results strongly suggest, that a piglet develops diarrhoea when the load of RVA in feces 
exceeded the equivalent to a Ct-value of 28. This association could possibly be affected by the strain 
of RVA and the immunity of the piglets, and should be further investigated. 
In the perfect scenario, all samples from the case litters should have been analysed for differential 
diagnoses. In that case, some more definite statements regarding the dynamics of RVA in neonatal 
==PAGE:49==
49 
Rotavirus type A associated diarrhoea in neonatal piglets  
 
piglets could have been made. However, some very strong indications were observed as for the 
association between a positive RVA diagnosis and the development of diarrhoea, especially on the 
piglets’ second day of life. In relation to this, it had also been of interest to screen some non-diarrheic 
litters (non-case litters), to investigate the RVA prevalence and virus dynamics in these litters. This 
was not possible due to financial constraints. 
As the piglets were not monitored beyond the fourth day of sampling, it was unknown for how long 
the piglets kept shedding RVA in feces. A Danish Veterinary Master’s Thesis found RVA in 106 out 
of 170 (62%) pooled fecal samples from pigs 0-3 weeks post weaning, but with no association 
between RVA and diarrhoea at that age. The samples came from both case- and control pens 
(Pedersen 2016). In relation to this result, it would have been interesting if the piglets had been 
monitored throughout the suckling period, to see when or if they stop shedding RVA, and when the 
association to diarrhoea disappears.  
A Japanese study from 2012 monitored 10 piglets in a commercial herd for RVA with 3- to 14-day 
intervals from 7 days of age until slaughter. During this period all the pigs shed at least 3-6 different 
combinations of G and P genotypes, with the highest detection rate in the weaning period. The pigs 
were kept in continuously driven stables that were not cleaned between farrowing groups, and they 
found that all pigs had multiple periods with no shedding of RVA in feces (Miyazaki et al. 2012). A 
similar study under Danish conditions looking at the Danish genotypes and excretion patterns could 
be of interest.  
6.6 Hypothesis H1 
One of the main objectives in this study was to investigate the association between RVA and 
diarrhoea in neonatal piglets within their first five days of life. The prevalences of RVA found in both 
diarrheic and non-diarrheic piglets in this study, were much higher than the prevalences found in the 
studies mentioned in table 1. A possible explanation for the high prevalences found in this study, 
could be that the sampling was only done in RVA positive herds. Also, only litters from first and 
second parity sows were analysed, and as previously mentioned, these litters have a different maternal 
immunity compared to litters of more mature sows (Farmer et al. 2006)(Cabrera et al. 2012).  
The association between shedding of RVA in feces and diarrhoea was only found to be significant in 
herd 1, but in herd 2 a strong tendency was observed (table 11). When the sample size was initially 
calculated, it was estimated that the prevalence of RVA in diarrheic piglets was 40% and the 
prevalence in non-diarrheic piglets was 10%. The RVA prevalences observed were much higher, 
especially in herd 2 (diarrheic=85 %, non-diarrheic=66%, red.). The required sample size to 
==PAGE:50==
50 
Rotavirus type A associated diarrhoea in neonatal piglets  
 
demonstrate an association between RVA and diarrhoea, based on the prevalences found in herd 2, 
should have been 63 piglets in both the diarrheic and the non-diarrheic group. The actual number of 
piglets in the diarrheic and the non-diarrheic group in herd 2 were 27 and 38, respectively.  
One of the reasons for the different clinical pictures observed in the two herds, could be due to 
different strains of RVA. As previously mentioned, a study by Midgley et al. found nine different 
strains of RVA in Denmark, based on the G and P genotypes (Midgley et al. 2012). Therefore, it is 
possible that the RVA strain present in herd 1 was different and more virulent than the RVA strain 
present in herd 2, as different virulence for different genotypes has been shown (Bridger & Brown 
1981). Another reason could be a better maternal immunity in herd 2. This could have been 
investigated by analyzing the composition of the colostrum or by measuring the level of antibodies 
in the neonatal piglet.  
6.7 Hypothesis H2 
No significant difference was found between the average RVA load in the diarrheic group and the 
non-diarrheic group of RVA positive piglets.  
This might seem a bit contradictory to the result of hypothesis H1, but the two results should be 
interpreted together as follows; in this study, piglets that shed RVA in feces on the case day had a 
higher risk of being diarrheic (table 11), but there is no association between the load of RVA and the 
consistency of the feces. A possible interpretation of this result is, that a quantitative RVA test result 
has no value compared to a dichotomized test result on the case day, given that the tests perform 
equally in sensitivity and specificity.  
The difference between the RVA loads of the two groups was also tested based on the average Ct-
values instead of the average log10 concentration (results not presented), and in this case a significant 
difference was seen. In a study by Anh et al. from 2014, the difference between RVA loads of 
diarrheic and non-diarrheic pigs (non comparable results as pigs were post weaning) was tested based 
on Ct-values (Hong Anh et al. 2014), but in this study it was decided to test the difference based on 
log10 concentrations. 
The test result of hypothesis H2 would also have been different if both the RVA positive and the 
RVA negative piglets on the case day from both herds had been included. As seen in the bottom line 
of table 13 a significant difference was observed given these circumstances. This difference should 
be seen as a consequence of a vast number of piglets with no viral load being added to the non-
diarrheic group. As seen in table 12, the number of piglets in the non-diarrheic group increased from

36 to 75 piglets when including RVA negative piglets from both herds, whereas the diarrheic group 
only increased from 51 to 57 piglets.  
6.8 Hypothesis H3 
A poor correlation between the load of RVA in the pen floor sample and the theoretical pool on the 
case day was found. The Lin concordance correlation coefficient was found to be 0.66 when including 
all the piglets from the litter in the theoretical pool and 0.84 when only including RVA positive piglets 
in the theoretical pool. This was in both cases a poor correlation but the load in the pen floor sample 
however had a closer resemblance to the theoretical pool only including the RVA positive piglets in 
the litter.  
As demonstrated in figure 17, the pen floor sample in most cases detected a higher load of RVA than 
the corresponding theoretical pool. In three RVA positive pens (pens 2, 4 and 5) less than half of the 
piglets were RVA positive on the case day, which for mathematical reasons will lower the RVA load 
of the theoretical pool including all the piglets. The difference between the RVA load in the two 
theoretical pools were in these three pens; 1.86, 1.44 and 1.94 log10, respectively.  
The representativeness of the pen floor sample to the litter was also very discussable. On the case day 
in some litter there was only three droppings on the pen floor, but regardless of the number, it was 
impossible to estimate from how many piglets the droppings originated. It could be assumed, that in 
diarrheic litters the diarrheic piglets would be overly represented in the pen floor sample at any given 
time due to a higher defecation frequency.  
However, when evaluating the ability of the pen floor sample to detect litters with at least one RVA 
positive piglet on the case day, 100 % agreement was found between the positive pen floor samples 
and the RVA status of the piglets.  
6.9 Hypothesis H4 
The weight gain during the study period, of the piglets negative for RVA on the case day, was 88 g 
higher than the weight gain of the piglets positive for RVA on the case day (p=0,02). This may be 
due to the well described pathological consequences of a RVA infection, such as destruction of 
enterocytes and villous atrophy leading to malabsorption and diarrhoea (Lundgren & Svensson 2001). 
The decreased weight gain of the RVA positive piglets observed in this study could also be due to 
the association between RVA and diarrhoea that was found. The average weight gain of the RVA 
positive non-diarrheic piglets was not significantly different to the average weight gain of the RVA 
negative non-diarrheic piglets (364 g vs. 400 g (p=0,85)), which could support this theory.

It was observed that the piglets negative for RVA on the case day had a lower average start weight 
than the piglets positive for RVA on the case day. This was a peculiar result, as the amount of 
colostrum ingested increases with live weight at birth (Quesnel et al. 2012) (Devillers et al. 2007) and 
as the level of Ig found in the piglet is positively related to the amount of colostrum ingested 
(Devillers et al. 2011), it could be assumed that piglets with a higher birthweight would both grow 
faster and have a better maternal immunity against RVA. This result might be a coincidence and 
should be investigated further.  
In relation to the association found between start weight and RVA status on the case day, it could be 
interesting to investigate how this difference in weight gain so early in the piglets’ life affects the 
weight gain throughout the suckling period. Possibly, the destruction of enterocytes and villous in the 
beginning of the suckling period could have a greater impact on the weight gain compared to an 
infection later in the suckling period. The economic impact of an infection in the neonate piglet cannot 
be estimated from this study, but according to SEGES, the value of +1kg in average weight at weaning 
has a value of 353 DKK per year sow (Christiansen 2015). To evaluate the impact throughout the 
suckling period, it would be necessary to monitor the continuous shedding of RVA and the incidence 
of diarrhoea.  
7. Conclusion 
Based on the results from the present study, there is a significant association between the detection 
of RVA in feces and the occurrence of diarrhoea in a neonatal piglet on the case day (OR = 9.02). 
Furthermore, the results of this study showed that it was the presence- and not the load of RVA that 
was essential for the association between RVA and diarrhoea in neonatal piglets on the case day, as 
no association was found between the fecal consistency and the RVA load in feces.  
Piglets that were RVA negative on the case day had a significantly higher weight gain from their 2nd 
to 5th day of life (mean 363g vs. 278g, p<0.05) despite that piglets positive for RVA had a 
significantly higher start weight (mean 1.45kg vs. 1.27kg, p<0.05). 
When investigating the virus dynamics, 34 out of the 49 piglets (69%) that were analysed for three 
consecutive days and were RVA positive in minimum one of the samples, developed diarrhoea within 
24 hours after a positive RVA diagnosis. Also, the results strongly suggested that a piglet developed 
diarrhoea when the RVA load in feces exceeded the equivalent to a Ct-value of 28. 
There was a poor correlation between the RVA load found in the pen floor samples and the 
corresponding theoretical pools, based on the average RVA load in the litter. The correlation was 
poor, regardless if the theoretical pool was based on the entire litter or only based on the RVA positive 
==PAGE:53==
53

piglets in the litter. However, the pen floor sample had a sensitivity of 100% in detecting litters with 
at least one RVA positive piglet on the case day. 
In nine of the 10 litters E. coli was ruled out as a differential diagnosis to the diarrhoea on the case 
day based on PCR analyses of pure cultured E. coli from the pen floor samples. 
8. Perspectivation 
In future studies, it would be interesting to monitor the piglets for a longer period, to investigate for 
how long piglets keep shedding RVA in feces and how the weight of the piglet at the time of weaning 
is affected by RVA. In addition to this, it could be interesting to see at what age the association 
between the shedding of RVA in feces and diarrhoea disappears. 
More knowledge is needed on the different RVA genotypes are circulating in the Danish pig 
population and if the different genotypes are equally virulent. In this study it was suspected that 
different genotypes were present in the two herds, as different associations between the shedding of 
RVA and diarrhoea was found. In the future, when diagnosing RVA as a means of initiating 
vaccination, genotyping could be relevant, as the cross immunity between different RVA genotypes 
is questionable. This should be further investigated. 
A peculiar observation was made when analysing the weight gain of the piglets in this study. The 
start weight of the piglets (piglets’ 2nd day of life) that were RVA positive on the case day, was 
significantly higher than the start weight of those negative for RVA on the case day. This might be a 
coincidence, but it should be investigated further to see if piglets with a higher birthweight truly are 
more prone to get infected with RVA. 
Initially the plan was to include RVC analyses in this study, as RVC is of relevance in relation to 
neonatal diarrhoea in piglets, but is poorly investigated. RVC has to the authors best knowledge never 
been investigated in Danish herds. As this aim was not fulfilled in the present study, future studies 
should preferably include RVC to elucidate the importance and the dynamics of RVC.
