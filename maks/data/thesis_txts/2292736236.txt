Abstract
The present thesis investigates total cost of ownership in the construction industry based
on Building Information Modeling (BIM). At ﬁrst there has been investigated in how to
approach total cost of ownership in the industry. Analyses of the costs within the life cycle
of a project have been made to do so. Calculation methods to compare the life cycle costs
have been introduced and the Net Present Value (NPV), which is a discount calculation
method, has been used as the preferred in the construction industry to quantify the total
cost of ownership. Life cycle analyses are further investigated to determine and evaluate
the parameters, which have to be included in the calculations of NPV.
There has been investigated in the potential of BIM and how the reuse of digital infor-
mation and collaboration through object based models can add value to the construction
industry. The prerequisites for a successful implementation and the absence of knowledge
have been analysed in order to link BIM and total cost of ownership. The digital infor-
mation is delivered by the diﬀerent disciplines in construction projects and the reuse of
information ensure mutual assumptions.
Two diﬀerent cases have formed the basis in the development of a tool to standardize
the calculations of NPV for alternative solutions, so total cost of ownership can be con-
sidered in decision-making processes. The tool is based on quantity takeoﬀs from object
based building models. It has been used to quantify the savings within a life cycle of
additionally construction costs and to identify critical design parameters for the life cycle.
The total cost of ownership must be considered in the design phases, where changes and
optimizations can be made without considerable costs.

Motivation
The construction industry is often criticized for being conservative. It is an industry with
risks attached to every project and dependent on the state of the market. Global, national
and local economy dictates the demand and construction is one of the industries to suﬀer
in economical depressions. The development of employment and started projects in the
Danish construction industry can be seen in ﬁgure 1 and ﬁgure 2.
There has been a
ﬁnancial depsression in 2007, and it can be seen that the graph goes down after this.
Figure 1: Employment in the Danish con-
struction industry 2000-2016 (Byggeri 2015).
Figure 2:
Started construction projects in
Denmark 1985-2016 (Byggeri 2015).
This is probably one of the reasons why the industry is very careful when it comes to
innovative solutions. An innovative solution must add a value, which can be quantiﬁed in
time or money. In order to optimize the construction industry it is important to implement
solutions that can be economic quantiﬁed.
Building Information Modeling (BIM) has been a part of the construction industry for
some years. It has been an innovative tool in the industry since the ﬁrst object based
software were developed. The intention of BIM is to optimize the interaction in a project,
but the value has been diﬃcult to assess. An executive order on ICT and a massive interest
from the industry have initiated and evolved the use of BIM in Denmark. Several Danish
contractors state that it is costly to implement and the use of BIM is conﬁned to major
projects. The costs and the added value depend on how BIM is used in projects. If BIM
is only used to a limited degree, then the implementation will be costly. This relation is
illustrated in ﬁgure 3 where a green box indicates the use of data.

INTRODUCTION
1.2
Problem Statement
Building Information Modeling (BIM) has made virtual planning and communication
through 3D models possible in the construction industry. The focus is often on the con-
struction costs. Modern technology has made it possible to estimate the price of a project
based on building models. However, the total cost of ownership is not considered in this
relation. A project’s life cycle can be simulated, and the costs can be calculated based
on building models. Since information can be extracted from existing models, it must be
possible to link an estimate of the total cost of ownership to the models. The concretiza-
tion of models determines the accuracy of the total cost of ownership. The total cost of
ownership can be a decision parameter to base a design upon or to optimize the solutions
for individual components. It will potentially increase the quality of the construction in-
dustry. Since private and public owners have a great interest in BIM, and the total cost
of ownership will determine the whole life proﬁt of an investment, there must be a market
and an interest in this link. Before a solid tool can be established to make the extracts
and calculations the following must be studied:
• What is required in order to make a model based estimate of the total cost of
ownership?
• How will it add value to the industry and what is the potential?
• Why is it not already an integrated part of the construction industry and how can
it be implemented?

1.3
Method
There are primarily used litterature review and case studies to clarify and solve the prob-
lem statement. Furhtermore discussions on the industry appraoch with people from the
industry has formed the basis of practical knowledge.
The literature review is a method to collect empirical knowledge. It is a study of the
existing work from secondary sources. Books, papers, articles, standards etc. are a part
of a literature review and used to evaluate and understand the topic and the challenges.
An analysis of the literature and empirical knowledge will form a basis for the conclusion
and answer to the problem statement together with case studies.
Case studies are used to form a general approach based on analyses of individual cases. The
method will result in an inductive and deductive approach to solve problem statements,
where a universally accepted conclusion or theory based on observations and hypothesis is
the objective. The theories are then again tested on a speciﬁc cases in order to conﬁrm the
theories. The strategy is in general used when the focus is on a contemporary phenomenon
within some real-life (Yin 2013).
Figure 6: The inductive and deductive cycle (Blaikie 2009).
Figure 6 shows the cycle of an inductive and deductive process. A case study is used to
generate some propositions and to collect data. This is analyzed and principal calculations
can be induced by the analysis.
The general calculations are then tested on speciﬁc
instances in another case study in order to conﬁrm the principle. This process will create
a logic in the structure of life cycle calculations and an understanding of the speciﬁc
problems.

Whole Life Cost (WLC) is covered by a combination of three general parts. The three
parts are Non-construction Costs, Income and Life Cycle Costs (LCC) (ISO 2008). They
are all further covered by a combination of several items.
Figure 7: Whole Life Costs in the construction industry (ISO 2008).
Evaluation of the Whole Life Cost requires some strategic planning. A construction project
is an investment and before the investment is made there are several decisions to make.
The location and size of the property will determine some of the future ﬁxed expenses as
tax. Additionally, the future needs must be evaluated, since expansion can be expensive
(Jensen 2008). Location can be important if the property must be sold in the future. An
alternative must be evaluated as well, in order to make sure that the investment will be
proﬁtable. The alternative will often be to lease instead of own if it is a company, which
needs the space. A capital fond will invest in a property and then lease it. The alternative
to a new investment is to invest in an existing building. It will be possible to lease from
day one and get a positive income, but there will also be a potential higher maintenance
cost. Proﬁt on the investment can potentially be achieved within a shorter time period
than investing in a new property, but the whole life proﬁt can be greater if an investment
in a new property is made. The investment must be evaluated by the cash ﬂow with all
the expenses and earnings. An example of typical investment considerations is given. This
is a strategic decision.

2.2
Life Cycle Cost (LCC)
2.2.1
Introduction
General terms and deﬁnitions in this section about Life Cycle Cost (LCC) are based on
(ISO 2008). This is an ISO standard with a standard approach to perform Life Cycle
Cost analyses of buildings and construction assets and their parts.
An ISO standard
is chosen as the foundation for the analyses in order to arrange the costs consistently.
The purpose of Life Cycle Costing is to quantify the LCC for input in decision-making
processes, which will usually also include inputs from other evaluations (e.g. environmen-
tal assessment, design assessment, safety assessment, functionality assessment, regulatory
compliance assessment) (ISO 2008).
The LCC is composed of four general categories.
These are Construction, Operation, Maintenance and End of Life

2.2.2
Construction
In a life cycle perspective, the construction costs are all the expenses attached to the
erection of a building or project. Design is also a part of the construction costs in this
relation. The temporary works and management are a part of the construction costs as
well. Estimates of the construction costs are based on previous projects, similar projects
or databases with information about materials, components, labor and equipment. Gen-
eral life cycle costing, where an estimate is based on the average of similar projects, is a
benchmark LCC analysis. This is typically an initial budget where the square meter price
is estimated or the functional unit cost (cost per bed) (ISO 2008). A benchmark analysis

Operation
A building is in operation from the day it is delivered to the client to the day it is de-
molished. The primary operation expenses are heating, cooling, power, water and sewage
costs. The rent is a part of the operation as well and must produce a positive cash ﬂow
to make the project proﬁtable. Operation costs can be a basis for decision when pumps,
radiators and lighting are purchased or replaced.

2.2.5
End of Life
Demolishing of a build can be expensive but can also generate an income. If the materials
are recyclable it is possible to get an income from the sale. Materials can be expensive
to dispose. Equipment is one of the expenses and factors, which determines the price for
demolishing. End of life costs must be considered in the design phase if the intention is
to generate an income. The choice of materials can be based on the end of life costs.
A refurbishment of the building is also an opportunity to consider the end of life costs
and will indicate the costs of disposal for the materials and components which are to be
replaced.
2.3
Evaluation of Life Cycle Costs
Construction projects are often complex and there are many variations from one project
to another. All projects are customized based on the owners’ needs, the architects’ design
and the engineers’ technical solutions. Each profession should ideally be represented in
all phases to ensure that the necessary knowledge is provided. This minimizes the risk of
unforeseen complications in future deliveries. The economy in a project must be considered
in the early phases in order to keep the costs inside the budget.
This applies for the
construction costs as well as for the maintenance costs and the life cycle costs in general.
Usually the operation and maintenance aspects will be considered when the project is
delivered. By then changes of solutions in order to optimize the operation and maintenance
costs will be expensive. (Norton & McElligott 1995) indicates the potential of reducing

“Life cycle costing is a mathematical method used to form or support a decision and is
usually employed when deliberating on a selection of options. It is an auditable ﬁnan-
cial ranking system for mutually exclusive alternatives which can be used to promote the
desirable and eliminate the undesirable in a ﬁnancial environment.” (Bull 2003).
Life cycle costing is a way to evaluate several solutions, only by the initial costs and the
future expenses. The output is a sum of the costs within the evaluated period. This makes
it possible to choose the most economical solution throughout the life time or the evaluated
period of time. In addition to the life cycle estimate, any other desired evaluations must
be considered. Before the life cycle estimate can be made the solutions must observe all
general requirements. This can be the energy consumption stated by public requirements
or the static requirements for a construction.
Aesthetic requirements, environmental requirements, social requirements etc. are evalu-
ated as well, and everything must generate one score in order to choose the optimal project
or solution. There must be a set up to diﬀerentiate all aspects set out in the evaluation
in order to emphasize the economical aspect. An approach is to distinguish between all
aspects and asign a relative weighting to each aspect. An example is to consider technical

2.3.1
Simple Payback
Simple payback is a calculation of the time taken for the return on an investment to repay
the investment. It is a simple method to create an overview of the investment and can be
expressed as:
P = I/R
(1)
Where P is the payback period in years, I is the capital sum invested and R is the annually
money returned or saved as a result of the investment (Bull 2003).
Since the output is in years the evaluation is then to determine if the period is acceptable
or not. It can be a strategic decision and will be individual for each case. This is one
of the limitations of the method and a practical evaluation of the acceptable payback is
necessary. Another limitation is that the method makes no allowance for:
• Inﬂation
• Interest (payable or received)
• Cash ﬂow
• Taxation
Furthermore the simple payback does not relate to the life time of the investment and
assumes that the payback time is less than the life time.
Simple payback should not be the only ﬁnancial basis for a decision but can be used as a
coarse ﬁlter of solutions at an early stage. It can be used to eliminate unrealistic options.

2.3.3
Internal Rate of Return
The internal rate of return (IRR) is another discounting method that is based on the NPV.
In this method it is the discount rate that is evaluated. IRR is the necessary discount rate
before the NPV is zero and it is then the discount rate that needs to be evaluated. If the
internal rate of return is low then the project will be more proﬁtable than if the internal
rate is high. An assessment must consider whether the internal rate is acceptable or if a
lower discount rate can be obtained in other projects. IRR is used to evaluate the expected
target for return of an investment. The IRR assumes that an investment will generate a
proﬁt and is therefore mostly used in other industries than in construction. The advantage
of the IRR method is that alternative solutions are not essential for the analyses but only
the “do nothing” alternative since the IRR will determine if the investment will generate

2.4
Life Cycle Analyses
The set up for the LCC analyses and the parameters included are based on ISO 15686-5:
Buildings and constructed assets- Service-life planning - Part 5: Life-cycle costing (ISO
2008). This is chosen since it is essential to establish a clear terminology and a common
methodology, which can be used as a standard in the construction industry. It is not the
intention to create a new approach, neither to develop the approach in (ISO 2008) but
to give an overview of the important factors to be considered in LCC analyses and the
consequences.
It is intended primarily for (ISO 2008):
• Procurers of constructed assets, with an interest in long term ownership
• Designers
• Constructors and their specialist suppliers of materials and components
• Facility operators
• Cost consultants and other specialists
The scope of the analyses is to compare diﬀerent solutions economically. All the solutions
must fulﬁll the design or system requirements before they can be assessed. A solution,
which does not meet the requirements, must be rejected in order to remove the possibility
of choosing the cheapest solution, but with insuﬃcient performance. The Client set up
the requirements themselves or in collaboration with consultants.
The requirements can state the service life or maintenance frequency. LCC analyses can
be used as input in a decision-making process or evaluation process. This can be used
to enlighten any decision and will reduce the uncertainty. It is commonly used in the
evaluation of new assets or major refurbishments. It can also be used to evaluate the
existing assets and their future performance.
A LCC analysis for a complete project
should be the sum of independent parts.
The analyses can be made at diﬀerent stages in a project and at diﬀerent levels. Typical
decisions where LCC analyses are a part of the decision-making processes can be (ISO
2008)
• Evaluation of diﬀerent investments
• Choices between alternative designs
• Choices between alternative components
• Comparison and/or benchmarking analysis of previous decisions
• Estimation of future costs for budgetary purposes, or for the evaluation of the ac-
ceptability of and option on the basis of cost of ownership
The evaluation of diﬀerent investments is at a strategic level. It is the overall strategy
where it has to be considered if purchasing an old building or investing in a new building
is the most beneﬁcial for the general company strategy. Example 1 indicates some of the

Setting Parameters
The LCC analyses are used to understand the implications of investments and to compare
several alternatives. Doing nothing should always be considered as an alternative to an
investment. In practice this must especially be considered in case of refurbishment.
A LCC analysis will include diﬀerent parameters for the comparison of alternative so-
lutions. The parameters will be assumptions of the ﬁnal design and performance. The

the assumptions are considered in the requirements, otherwise it will not be possible to
predict the outcome of the constructed asset. If the initial results are unacceptable the
assumptions may be revised together with the requirements.
Analyses cover a deﬁned list of costs over the physical, technical, economic and functional
life of a constructed asset. The following input must be considered and form the basis of
the analyses:
• Construction costs
• Operation, maintenance and replacement costs
• End of life costs
• Discount rate
• Inﬂation
• Taxes and subsidies
• Utility costs including energy costs
The LCC analysis can be separated in the headers above (construction costs, operation

The construction costs are explained in section 2.2.2. It includes the costs of materials,

Operation and maintenance costs are explained in section 2.2.3. The operation costs are
primarily related to energy consumption and maintenance to the activities carried out to
maintain the performance and obtain the predicted service life of objects.
As described in section 2.2.2 the estimate of construction costs are related to previous
projects, indicative prices from suppliers and price databases (based on experience from
the industry). The same is valid for the operation, maintenance and replacement costs.
It is possible to estimate the service life, the maintenance activities and the energy con-
sumption based on experience and information from suppliers.
The discount rate represents the opportunity costs of investing in capital in the private
sector. Within the public sector discount rates may be determined by central government.
Where the discount rate is not a ﬁxed requirement by the owner, sensitivity analyses can
be made on a range of rates to test the validity of conclusions if the input changes.

2.4.3
Period of Analysis
The period of analysis is the time period over which the investments or alternative solutions
are analysed. The period of the LCC analysis is a critical variable. Costs outside the period
may have a huge impact on the total cost of ownership. This can be refurbishment of main
construction parts as facades or roofs. Some of the approaches to determine the period of
analysis can be (ISO 2008):
• The period of foreseeable need or occupation (the entire life cycle)
• A period determined by contractual liability (Maintenance contracts or for a mort-
gage ﬁnancing the investment)
• A standard period within an organization
Sensitivity analyses on the period of analysis can be made as well to reveal how a diﬀerent
input will aﬀect the calculations.
2.4.4
Uncertainty and Risk
Risk considers probability and impact. Uncertainty diﬀers from risk and is used when
the probabilities cannot be estimated. The level of risk and uncertainty depends on the
available information. It will also depend on the assumptions and the robustness of the
estimates. There are other issues due to the fact that LCC analyses can be based on
diﬀerent evaluation methods. Before uncertainty and risk can be addressed the following
key issues and barriers in LCC must be understood (ISO 2008):
• Costs to be included/excluded (distinction between LCC and WLC elements)
• Variation in evaluation methods (e. g. NPV, IRR, Simple Payback)
• Transparency of the underlying assumptions and methods
• Lack of information about detailed design at the beginning of a project
• The introduction of new technology and products

• Contractual incentives to do life cycle assessment on build an construct only contracts
• Shortage of expert LCC practitioners and professional programmes
Some assumptions and judgements will potentially increase the uncertainty with the use
of optimistic estimates, unattainable service lives and unfavourable maintenance and re-
placement schedules. A reason can be to justify the project (ISO 2008).
2.4.4.1
Monte Carlo Simulation
Monte Carlo simulations can be used to model the uncertainty of a possible cost range
or function of costs. It generates a random outcome within the range or function. The
number of simulations will determine the accuracy of the results. The simulation will only
indicate the probabilities and not certainty (Faber 2012). Therefore the simulations will
only be as good as the estimates they are based upon (RiskAMP n.d.). The costs can be
estimated at diﬀerent conﬁdence levels and thereby decrease the uncertainty. Cumulative
frequency curves can be used to plot the results of the analyses and the conﬁdence level
can be read oﬀthe curves. An analysis can indicate the probability as a function of the
costs. This can be done for the maintenance activities associated with a certain design to
determine the sum of yearly maintenance costs or to estimate the NPV. The variance of
single activities can be considered and Monte Carlo simulations on the sum can be made
to estimate the probability and expected costs.
Figure 18: Visualization of a Monte Carlo simulation.
Figure 18 illustrates the outcome of 100 simulations on the sum of ten random activities
that all varies in cost between a minimum and maximum cost. The cumulative frequency
curve indicates that the sum of the costs will be 18,000 $ or less with a 95 % probability
in this simulation for the ten activities.

2.5
Reﬂection
The total cost of ownership must be considered in the design phase to get the full potential
of LCC analyses. This will make changes possible and reduce the costs of changes when
optimizations are discovered. It must be possible to compare alternative solutions. There
must be a standard approach to compare solutions with diﬀerent construction, operation
and maintenance costs over time. NPV is an eﬃcient and transparent approach to consider
the yearly expenses and cash ﬂows. NPV considers the costs over time. Furthermore it
is possible to include discount rates, interest rates and inﬂation. NPV can be used to
compare and identify the most cost eﬀective solution within a period of analysis. One of
the assumptions is the fact that all requirements must be observed before a comparison
in NPV can be made.
Non-construction costs and income must be considered at a strategically level.
These
costs should not be included in the calculations of diﬀerent design alternatives but in the
evaluation of acquisition. They will induce speculative numbers and therefore increase the
uncertainty and complexity.
An interval based maintenance strategy is assumed in NPV calculations to estimate the
yearly expenses and the cash ﬂows. The typical maintenance activities must be consid-
ered in the calculations of the maintenance costs. Preventive and corrective maintenance
must be considered and carried out to obtain the service life of components. The replace-
ment costs are interval based and determined by the service life of a component in the
calculations.
The uncertainty and the risk of the calculations can be considered and quantiﬁed. The
calculations can be supported by sensitivity analyses, in order to identify the critical
parameters, and by Monte Carlo simulations to identify the probability of expected costs.

Building Information Modeling (BIM)
BIM and the structuring of data can be presented within three concepts (Chudley &
Greeno 2014):
• Building Information Modeling.
• Building Information Model.
• Building Information Management.
Building Information Model is a digital representation of the processes that provides struc-
tured data management including reports and schedules for the diﬀerent phases in the
project (Chudley & Greeno 2014).
Building Information Management is the organization, strategy and control of a project
using the data from a building information model. A communication process that applies
from the design to the demolishing (Chudley & Greeno 2014).
Building Information Modeling will be referred to as BIM in the following. It is a method
to digitize the construction industry and improve the design, construction and operations
of a project (DTU 2013) and (Computer Integrated Construction Research Group and
others 2010). There has been a progression in model software and applications which have
made progression in the use of BIM possible. Especially as CAD systems became more
intelligent and the possibility of generating data rather than drawings and 3D images
more users wanted to share data associated with a given design (Eastman et al. 2011).
A building model is a digital object based representation of a project and follows the life
cycle from design to demolishing. Information is attached to the objects. This is geometry,
location, characteristic properties etc. The information is exchanged across the diﬀerent
disciplines in a project and a model based working relationship can be implemented. BIM
is the model containing information combined with a working method (DTU 2013). A
common and consistent use of the information in the life cycle must be handled with
common standards and rules (BIPS 2006b).

3.1
Model Development
3.1.1
Discipline Models
To gather information from all disciplines (BIPS 2006b) proposes to use discipline models.
A discipline model is submitted for each discipline. All discipline modela will jointly form
an aggregate model to assemble information. The aggregate model is a useful tool for the
coordination in the project.
The discipline model ensures a well-deﬁned scope for each party and clarify the interfaces.
It will also facilitate the development and exchange of data through all phases based on
the same information. It will hold each party responsible for the delivery of information
throughout the project or to a point where the responsibility is handed over to a new
party. This method will initiate a digital workﬂow similar to a workﬂow without BIM,
and procedures can be adopted to a digital approach (BIPS 2006b). Information about
updates of models will be essential for this approach in order to avoid mistakes and secure
a mutual foundation. (BIPS 2006b) proposes the following setup for the responsibility of
discipline models:
• The party who prepares a discipline model is responsible for both information content
and structure.
• The party responsible for the discipline model is also responsible for extraction of
information (drawings, quantities etc.) from the discipline model.
• The party that uses another party’s discipline model for reference is not responsible
for the content, but is responsible for correct interfacing.
• A party to whom another party’s discipline model is transferred takes over at that
point the responsibility for the content and structure of this discipline model.
3.1.2
Aggregate Models
The aggregate model is used for model coordination and data sharing. It is assembled from
the discipline models and used to detect clashes and collisions, common data extraction
and visualize the whole project.

3.2.1
Industry Foundation Classes (IFC)
The Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) is an open neutral data format. The format is
developed by the International Alliance for Interoperability (IAI), now buildingSMART.
It represents an open speciﬁcation for BIM data which is exchanged between involved
participants in a construction project. The intention is to systematically describe all the
components in the main families of the construction industry. Any compliant application
or software must be able to generate all the information about a speciﬁc component in
this neutral format and import it as well. This shall be generated automatically with no
interference of users.
IFC is the international openBIM standard (Liebich 2013). openBIM is an initiative by
buildingSMART together with some of the software vendors in the industry (buildinS-
MART 2014b). It supports an open workﬂow with no limitations of software tools but
with a common exchange format.
The intention of IFC is to address all building in-
formation over the life cycle of a project (Karlshøj et al. 2007). An IFC model contains
information about geometry but also information about relations of objects and properties
(Eastman et al. 2011).
All the vendors have not desired an open neutral data format since it will increase the
competition. IFC is still under development and that is one of the reasons why a neutral
format has not been fully implemented in the industry.
There are limitations in the information that can be exchanged between modelling systems.

3.3
Implementation
When properly implemented, BIM can add value to a project. In the design phases the
added value of BIM is an increased design quality through eﬀective analysis cycles, greater
prefabrication due to predictable ﬁeld conditions, and improved ﬁeld eﬃciency by visu-
alizing the planned construction schedule (Computer Integrated Construction Research
Group and others 2010). But if a proper and successful implementation is not achieved
it can be very expensive due to modelling services and delays due to missing information
and lack of coordination.
The scope of BIM and the motivation to introduce it is partly based on the eﬃciency
improvement, which non-farm industries, as car production, has gained due to automa-
tion and improved collaboration tools (Eastman et al. 2011).There is a great interest in
standardizing the construction industry in order to achieve the same beneﬁts as the car
industry. buildingSMART is an organization with the purpose to implement and develop
standards in diﬀerent processes, workﬂows and procedures in the industry (buildinSMART
2014a). Integration between involved parties and systems require some mutual values,
terms and common deﬁnitions for the industry (BIPS 2006b). Furthermore competences
need to be represented in the processes before an implementation is possible (BIPS 2006b)
and (Falck 2015).
Replacing a work method from 2D or 3D CAD is more than investing in new software,
hardware and training. An understanding of the technology and the related processes is
necessary in such a conversion (Eastman et al. 2011). A vision and a common goal must
be deﬁned and broadly accepted to gain the beneﬁts (See et al. 2012).
To implement BIM and eﬀectively gain the beneﬁts of the working method, it is necessary
to develop an execution plan and to identify the strategy.
It must outline the vision,
purpose and intention for the individuals in the project.
A general description of the
BIM tasks, the information exchanges between the involved team members and a digital
infrastructure must be deﬁned (See et al. 2012). The beneﬁts of a clear execution plan
and an early involvement are:
• All parties will understand the purpose and the goals for the implementation.
• Roles will be simple to identify.
• An execution process can be developed and be customized to ﬁt the individuals in
the project.
• Additional resources and eventually further training can be outlined to successfully
implementation.
• There will be a clear description for any new project members.
• It will be possible to detect if deliveries and obligations are fulﬁlled.

3.3.1
Goals and Use
A deﬁnition of the BIM goals and use, requires a good understanding of the project and
a general understanding of the client’s needs and desire. It is important to understand
and deﬁne the potential value of BIM in the project. The team members must deﬁne the
overall goals by implementing BIM (Eastman et al. 2011). They can be incorporated as a
part of the overall project goals. These are often stated based on some of the performance
parameters in the project as increasing the schedule duration, achieving higher ﬁeld pro-
ductivity, increase the use of prefabrication, reducing the costs of change orders or be able
to incorporate important data for the operation.
When the project goal and the company strategy are deﬁned, the use of BIM can be
identiﬁed and related to speciﬁc processes. It must be identiﬁed where the BIM use is
beneﬁcial in order to achieve the project goals.
3.3.2
Execution proces
When the BIM use is identiﬁed a process map can be established. The overall activities
in the project must be mapped and the associated BIM use. This is done to visualize the
interaction of diﬀerent team members and to identify how the information they deliver
is going to be used in the project (See et al. 2012). Afterwards the detailed information
exchanges can be established.
3.3.3
Information Exchange
To be able to receive information it is important to address how the information is going
to be used, and how the information must be delivered in order to do so. The approach
of how information is exchanged can be project speciﬁc but generally there need to be a
standard way for receiving and deliver information. The information exchanges can be
set up in a worksheet as in (See et al. 2012). After the process map has been developed
and the information exchange between parties have been established a procedure for infor-
mation exchange requirements must be created. It is important that each team member
understand the information content especially the author and receiver (See et al. 2012).

3.3.3.1
Information Delivery Manual (IDM)
buildingSMART have introduced a process for information delivery called Information De-
livery Manual (IDM). The IDM is a buildingSMART process standard for identifying the
diﬀerent processes and information required to execute the processes in the Architecture,
Engineering and Construction (AEC) and Facilities Management (FM) industry (Mon-
drup, Karlshøj & Vestergaard 2014). It is recognized by the International Organization
for Standardization (ISO) and is speciﬁed in ISO 29481 (Standard 2010). The method-
ology can be used to describe new processes or to structure existing processes (Karlshøj
2011). It identiﬁes a set of exchanges between two project participants in a process. Fur-
thermore it speciﬁes the content of the exchanges in order to use them properly. There
are often a number of diﬀerent software applications involved in a process in the AEC in-
dustry and that is one of the reasons why IDMs are developed. It is necessary to state the
information to be exchanged from the user’s perspective before it can be used eﬃciently.
The core purpose of the methodology is to standardize AEC/FM processes. There are four
deliverables in the IDM (Mondrup, Treldal, Karlshøj & Vestergaard 2014) and (Mondrup,
Karlshøj & Vestergaard 2014):
• IDM use case
• IDM Process Map
• IDM Exchange Requirements
• IDM Exchange Requirements Models
The use case deﬁnes project participants, the information exchange and the activities for
a speciﬁc process in the AEC/FM industry.
The Process Map (PM) links the participants, the information and the activities.
The IDM Exchange Requirements (ERs) deﬁne the format and units of information for
each speciﬁc information exchange in the process.
The IDM Exchange Requirements Models (ERMs) organize the ERs into general exchange
concepts so the information can be reused.
IDMs can evolve to a comprehensive and complex information exchange process when
they are made too general. Therefore IDMs are sub-divided into smaller packages to be
more manageable where an IDM package represents a speciﬁc use case and the limited
information exchanges at a speciﬁed stage of the project’s life cycle (Mondrup, Karlshøj
& Vestergaard 2014).
The process modeling notation used in the IDM is the Business Process Modelling Notation
(BPMN). The BPMN is developed by the Object Management Group (OMG) and has
rapidly gained acceptance amongst the modeling and business communities (Wix 2007).
When an already developed notation method is chosen it is not necessary to develop
a speciﬁc one for this methodology. This will simplify the procedure where the PM is
created.
BPMN uses some standard shapes and icons for visualization. There are some common
rules for the link between the icons in the BPMN speciﬁcation and these must be applied.
Within the BPMN notation, there are four types of elements.
These are actors, pro-
cesses, connections and artefacts (Wix 2007). Processes are the key concept. Actors and
sub-actors perform the processes. A sub-actor can be a division within an organization.
Connections are linking the diﬀerent processes or passing messages between processes.
Artefacts are the elaborating data within a process or between processes. They simply
provide further information.

All processes are owned by actors. They are represented in Pools. Lanes are used to
organize the processes within a Pool. A process is mapped in a lane for the responsible
actor. Connections are used to link a process to another within the lane or between actors.
A connection is either a sequence ﬂow if it is within the same lane or a message ﬂow if it

3.4
Information Levels
The diﬀerent models in a project undergo a concretization through the project life. Up-
dating of solutions and materials is a concretization process in a project. The concept of
Information Levels can be used to describe and deﬁne what information can be expected
at a given time in the project life cycle.
LOD is sometimes interpreted with Level of Detail instead of Level of Development (BIM-
Forum 2015). The Level of Detail is the indication of how detailed the object is represented
in the model. The Level of Development considers the geometry and the attached infor-
mation.
In the following (BIPS 2006b) will be referred to as (DDB 3DA) and (Cuneco 2014) as
(CCS).
(DDB 3DA) and (CCS) proposes 7 diﬀerent Information Levels. The two solutions resem-
ble each other. A conceptual graphical model is shown in every level to visualize what
to expect at the given level in (DDB 3DA) but only until the ﬁfth level in (CCS). The
Information Levels can be used in the traditional phases in construction projects but are
not limited to this. Procurement forms are not important and the Information Levels
can be used in all types of procurement since they only describes the type of construction
objects, their occurrences, their characteristics and properties (DDB 3DA). There is not a
standard approach to deﬁne the information contained at the diﬀerent stages of a project.
This is what Information Levels try to standardize, which potentially eases the collabo-
ration and the initial agreements. The objective is to develop a mutual understanding
in the progression of information levels in correlation to speciﬁcations of services, infor-
mation that can be exchanged, object representation etc. Information Levels are used to
group object information at diﬀerent stages as they occur from design idea to realizable
physical objects (CCS). Information Levels are attached to the information for a speciﬁc
object as building structures, rooms, building components and materials. When an object
is composed of various objects the Information Level attached, is valid for all the objects
unless otherwise stated (CCS). Furthermore when an Information Level is attached to an
object all the information included in the object is at the given Information Level.
New phases in projects will rely on existing information included in the models, which
ensures reuse of data through the life cycle (DDB 3DA). The Information Level can be
used to deﬁne the information needed and what to deliver at a certain time or phase in
the project.

3.5
Project Organization
BIM will aﬀect all involved parties in a project when it is implemented early. The beneﬁts
are not the same for everyone since it is used for diﬀerent purposes. The workﬂow will
therefore also diﬀer through the phases in the project since design and construction will
be executed diﬀerently.
3.5.1
The Design Team
The design team will use BIM for visualization, coordination and simulation of the building
performance. The architect will develop a discipline model for visualization of the thoughts
and ideas based on the owner’s requirements. This model will undergo an iterative process
of development. The updated models will be analyzed by diﬀerent engineers in the design
phases.
Simulations of the statics will be performed by the structural engineer, who
will decide on an overall structural system with load bearing walls, columns, beams or
structural cores. The architect will use this input to proceed with the interior design.
An energy simulation and simulations of the indoor climate and acoustics will be made
and must be considered in the development of the interior and exterior design. An overall
responsible for coordination and validation, will make sure that there are no clashes across
disciplines and must address the aﬀected if eventual clashes or coordination problems
occur.
The design is an iterative process, and any update will undergo a validation cycle to analyze
the eﬀects on the diﬀerent disciplines. Modeling in the design phases will optimize the
project in each iteration and will help the project team to make proper decisions.

The Contractor

The contractor will have to base estimates and schedules on manually

project collaboration.

When the models in the design have been made

The objects and the information included in the models can be used to simulate activi-

The contractor can beneﬁt from BIM since it potentially add value in the form of:

• Better coordination, collaboration and planning
• More accurate cost estimates
• More accurate time schedules
3.5.3
The Owner and the Facility Manager
The owner is interested in the best quality and performance for the money when the
project is delivered. This should be supported by information about the components, so
the quality and the performance can be maintained through the life cycle. The owner is
eventually the only one to be aﬀected by the life cycle performance and the costs related
to operation and maintenance.
In order to get a project within the time schedule and budget, it is important to manage
it.
The owner is in the best position to lead and manage the project and set up the
collaboration in the project team to exploit BIM to its fullest beneﬁt (Eastman et al.
2011). Furthermore the owner can establish requirements for digital delivery at the end
of construction, which can be used in the facilities management of the project. Previously
information about the facilities has been delivered in paper form and was then entered
manually into a software (Teicholz et al. 2013). The requirements must reﬂect upon how
the information will be used in order to specify the correct amount of data and information.
By receiving all the required information attached to objects it is possible to maintain and
operate the project at delivery.

3.6
Reﬂection
BIM provides the industry with a new method of working. The BIM concept initiates
a potential reuse of information and collaboration through all the phases in the project.
This is the essential beneﬁt of the concept. The method of working can potentially create
optimized and proﬁtable projects if proper implemented. It will require a change in the
traditional working method since the beneﬁts will not be achieved if information models
are only used in the ﬁnal delivery and not during the process. The opportunity of 3D
visualization is a great beneﬁt in the collaboration to support the information. The im-
plementation process and the use of BIM in projects, or within an organization, must be
considered and evaluated before an implementation. The purpose of the implementation,
the use of BIM and the beneﬁts must be communicated to all project members or within
the organization in order to clarify it.
A general set up for the exchange of informa-
tion must be considered as well, in order to ensure mutual use of information. Building
Model Repositories are described in section 3.1.3. It will introduce some problems with
respect to responsibility. If the approach is applied there must be an overall responsible
for the control of the disciplines. Furthermore there must be a project participant with
the responsibility for clash detection between disciplines, and there must be a structured
procedure for how clashes are communicated to the aﬀected parties. This resembles the
description in (BIPS 2006b) but a sever structure will be more complex since every single
discipline will have every single discipline model available. This will require control and a
system to determine which version of other disciplines the information included is based
upon.
A common ﬁle exchange format must be considered and established to exchange informa-
tion between diﬀerent models. It is essential to exchange data and the IFC format is a
specially developed format to the construction industry which structures the information.
A problem with an open format is the fact that it requires a strict data structure in the
models in order to exchange a proper model without errors. It is required in the industry
due to the number of diﬀerent applications and software. In the process of exchanging
from one ﬁle format to IFC and then convert from IFC to another ﬁle format there can
be lost information and it can be diﬃcult to track. An understanding of the IFC format
and the exchange of this is required by all the users before it can be fully implemented. It
is essential to know, how the objects in a speciﬁc software will be imported and exported,
in order to be able to structure the information in the correct way for optimal exchange.
In projects where all participants are using software from the same vendor it is obvious to
use the software’s preferred format. If all project participants are using the same software
it will reduce the risk of losing information in the exchange. The openBIM approach must
not be bureaucratic but must be incorporated whenever there is the need for an open
format. This is diﬀerent in public projects. (Ministeriet for By, Bolig og Landdistrikter
2013) and (Klima-, Energi- og Bygningsministeriet 2013a) require that building models
must be delivered in the IFC format and that the owner makes the models available in the
IFC format. It demands a knowledge and competences from each discipline and initiates
the development of them. This will be beneﬁcial in future projects when openBIM is fully
developed and understood but will also induce the present problems as described.
The concretization of models and the information can be diﬃcult to determine. (BIPS
2006b) and (Cuneco 2014) propose how this can be done in several Information Levels.
One of the diﬃculties in the industry is what to include in the model at diﬀerent stages.
A speciﬁc description has not been made in any of the proposals.
Therefore it is up
to the individual project participant to decide what “Detailed speciﬁcation of a physical
realizable solution” (Cuneco 2014) is or “All necessary information for tendering” (BIPS
2006b). (BIPS 2006b) proposes how the model at a certain level may be used and this

is an assistance in determining the requirements. It can be diﬃcult to specify the details
of information due to the customization in the construction industry and may not be
necessary. Furthermore each discipline will require diﬀerent information. The speciﬁcation
of data in the Information Levels can be diﬃcult due to the amount of objects and the
related data. It is a complex task and may diﬀer between projects.
The Information Level can be used to establish what the information can be used for,
what is deﬁnite and what conﬁdence level is appropriate.
“The assigned information level says something about the trustworthiness of the data in a
model, not about the amount of information in it.” (van Berlo & Bomhof 2014)
This citation indicates the concretization of models and information instead of the detail-
ing. It means that it is possible to assign a lot of information in an early phase but the
Information Level will indicate what data and information to use and extract.
It is up to the project participants to specify what they need. The IDM from buildingS-
MART can be used to specify an output based on a given input. The process of how this
is proceed is further described in the IDM and creates a transparency in the delivery. It
will be possible to identify the expectations to the delivered information in the process
and what it must be used for.
MT Højgaard’s approach to the Information Level is to consider the speciﬁc information
that must be attached to objects before the contractor can use them. This will make it
possible to require a LOD of a certain level where the receiver knows what to expect and
the sender knows what to deliver. It is related to the Information Levels in (CCS) but
level 6 and 7 are not used. This could indicate an ambiguity in these levels or simply that
they are not relevant for a contractor like MT Højgaard.
The approach must deﬁne LODs for all objects, which potentially can be used, and every
time a new object is introduced the information at diﬀerent LODs must be considered
and updated. This will be a complex and time-consuming task if the approach should be
considered in a general standard for the industry.
Information Levels are important for the further development of BIM. It is important for
the use of information and must be considered to optimize the collaboration and under-
standing in a model based working environment. It may not be possible to make a deﬁnite
standard, which can be used for everyone in the industry. There is a customization and a
diﬀerence in how each participant prefers to further specify or adopt industry standards,
but an increased understanding of what Information Levels actually is and may be used
for beneﬁts everyone.

Requirements to ICT and BIM in Procurement

rebuilding and extensions to existing buildings, renovation, maintenance of buildings and

Executive Order on ICT
The executive order on ICT requires that the owner must ensure a coordination of all ICT

perspective.
In project competitions there must be delivered digital object based building models in
addition to project proposals. The models must visualize and document the proposed
aesthetics, functional and technical solutions in a certain Information Level and must be
delivered in the IFC format. It is the owner’s responsibility to ensure that agreements
about discipline and aggregate models are made in the project design phase.
When construction work is put out to tender, it must be digital and the bids must only be
received digital as well. If the quantities are included in the tender, there must be digital
object based building models where quantities can be taken oﬀ. These models must as
well be in the IFC format, and the rules for measurement of the quantities must be stated.
The owner must state the requirements to the digital delivery of information relevant for:
• Documentation of the project

Reﬂection
All the legal requirements demand expertise and competences from the owner, within the
area of digital collaboration, object based building models and the IFC format. Some
participants may not possess the required competences and knowledge yet, but the fact
that it is a legal requirement will evolve this.
The executive order on ICT will potentially entail that public projects are delivered on time
to the estimated costs and of a better quality. It will also entail an eﬃcient collaboration
and communication within future public projects. It may be considered as bureaucratic,
but the purpose is to make sure that everything included is considered and managed
in the project to obtain a good result and gain the full potential of BIM and a digital
collaboration. An open format is necessary due to the fact that the owner cannot require
a certain software according to (Parliament 2004) and (Erhvervs-, og Vækstministeriet
2011).
Therefore the openBIM philosophy must be incorporated also by the software
vendors.
The executive order on quality and total cost of ownership does not describe the period of
analysis or the rates to be used in the calculations. Furthermore it does not state which
method to use in the calculations. It is not mentioned if it is the owner, the consultant
or the contractor who should make the calculations or take the responsibility for the
assessment. The extent of the calculations and the assessment is not mentioned either.
The Danish Ministry of Finance dictates a socio-economic discount rate of 4 % in the ﬁrst
35 year, 3 % between the 35-70 years and 2 % after 70 years (Finansministeriet 2013). This
can be referred to in the executive order to indicate the rates. Furthermore there should
be a requirement about the minimum of components to be included in the assessment.
This could be facades, roofs and windows for buildings with the opportunity to assess
other relevant or critical elements. It must be required that the owner states a period of
analysis in order to compare diﬀerent solutions. It should also be stated that the owner
has the responsibility as stated in (Klima-, Energi- og Bygningsministeriet 2013a).
(Ministeriet for By, Bolig og Landdistrikter 2011) states that private owners can receive
public funding in the construction of social housing. To receive the funding the owner
has to obey requirements about total cost of ownership. There are requirements to how
the calculations must be made, which program to use and which building elements to
consider as a minimum. Furthermore it is stated that there has to be investigated in
minimum one alternative. The results must then be approved to receive public funding.
If the requirements can be made for private owners it should be possible to include the
requirements in (Klima-, Energi- og Bygningsministeriet 2013b) as well to increase the

Purpose
The purpose of the case is to investigate in four diﬀerent alternatives for a tunnel cladding
system, which all obey the design criteria. The intention is to ﬁnd the most cost eﬀective

are included and compared for the diﬀerent alternatives to identify the most cost eﬀective

7
Discussion
7.1
What is required in order to make a model based estimate of the
total cost of ownership?
Before a model based estimate can be considered, there must be a collaboration and
information exchange through models. BIM as a working method must be implemented
and generally accepted. The productivity in the construction industry has been compared
with non-farm industries.
There can be a standard set up for BIM and the working
methods, but it will never be possible to achieve the same productivity as the car industry
due to the fact that the construction industry will never be as standardized as the car
industry and other non-farm industries. It will always be possible to customize projects
because this is how the design team win competitions. Within a single project or portfolio
where there are repetitions it will be possible to sub optimize some of the processes but
this cannot be done for the industry as a unit.
This is probably not the intention with the comparison. The intention is to investigate in
how non-farm industries evolved. This was done by the use of information systems and
improved collaboration tools, which are possible to implement and develop further in the
construction industry. To adopt BIM as a working method, where one of the essential
beneﬁts is the reuse of information throughout the life cycle of projects, will potentially
increase the productivity in the industry. This will add value in the form of increased
quality in the industry. There is a continuous development. Especially in the area of what
to expect from models and how requirements and expectations are communicated. It can
be diﬃcult to create detailed standards for the whole industry, which covers all phases due
to the variation in how the design, construction and facilities management are carried out.
A map of the process where input and output are identiﬁed will make the requirements
more transparent and it eases the understanding of diﬀerent processes. IDM’s can be used
to state the requirements for the input and map the processes to get a certain output.
Information Levels can be used to describe what the model can be used for and what
has been determined. A model at a certain Information Level is included in the process
where other speciﬁc requirements to data are included as well. With this approach it is
not necessary to deﬁne all the speciﬁc data parameters for each single object and how it
ﬁts the diﬀerent Information Levels. This can be separated and the individual project
participant can state what is needed to get a speciﬁc output.
The reuse of information is essential when changes are considered. It must then be possible
to generate a new output, based on the updated material to investigate in the consequences
for each individual project participant. This operation must be as simple as possible in
order to report implications and additionally optimization early in the process. Reuse will
entail that project participants base their work on common assumptions and generally
increase the quality of projects.
The exchange of information is not a frictionless process.
Information can be lost in
the exchange from one application to another.
A standard format for exchange, and
knowledge about how information is exported and imported in this format, is essential
for the building models and building components. The continuous development of the
standard IFC format is important for the industry to get an openBIM collaboration and
to be independent of a speciﬁc software. Since it is a required format in public projects,
there will be a natural development and the limitations will be identiﬁed. When a general
knowledge of the limitations is gained and the use is fully understood, it will be possible to
increase the productivity and fully implement BIM. The owner and the project participants
must be able to receive and use an IFC model, and the information included, without
comprehensive adjustments before it unleashes the full potential of BIM in public projects.

DISCUSSION
There must be a standard approach before a comparison of life cycle analyses and total
cost of ownership estimates can be made. A cash ﬂow calculation as NPV is one of the best
for evaluating construction related options where it is possible to consider inﬂation and
interest. The cases show that it is possible to calculate a NPV within a period of analysis.
This can be the service life of components or the design life of a building. The inputs
for the calculations are a period of analysis, the construction costs, the operation costs,
the maintenance costs, the intervals for maintenance activities. Furthermore there have
to be indicated an inﬂation rate and interest or a discount rate. Estimates of construction
costs are commonly executed in early phases, but the maintenance costs are not usually
considered in the design phases. The maintenance costs can be based on experience ﬁgures
or maintenance data bases as (V&S PrisBog 2007).
The estimates can be made in the design phase and will progress as the design concritizes.
It is possible to make sensitivity analyses and Monte Carlo simulation in order to determine
the uncertainty and risk and, the results will be more accurate as the project concretizes.
A tool that calculates NPV based on model quantity takeoﬀs has been made to standardize
the calculations. The calculations are standardized, but it will still require manual inter-
action and entry of unit prices for construction, operation and maintenance costs. The
model extracts must be converted and entered in a template to ensure that the results are
entered correctly. This will be done manually. Furthermore the sensitivity analyses and
Monte Carlo simulations must be made manually, by changing parameters and store the
results, before they can be plotted and analyzed.
7.2
How will it add value to the industry and what is the potential?
Life cycle analyses and estimates of the total cost of ownership must be included in the
design phases to utilize the full potential. It is possible to aﬀect the maintenance costs
early in the design phases without any considerable costs. The costs will be considerable
for changes in the phases after the design is speciﬁed.
NPV calculations for diﬀerent
alternatives must be considered in decision-making. The results must contribute to en-
lightenment of the diﬀerent opportunities so the owner or the design team can make their
decisions on a solid basis. This will make the owner aware of the consequences of the dif-
ferent alternatives. It will then be the owner’s responsibility to choose, which alternative
is the most attractive in the speciﬁc situation.
To consider the costs in the early design, will make value engineering possible as an
integrated part of the design. It will not be a task that is carried out to save money
right before construction, because the project is too expensive, but will contribute to a
continuous optimization of the project in the design.
An estimate of the NPV will indicate the potential savings within the period of analysis
for diﬀerent alternatives. Furthermore it can be analyzed if additional construction costs
will initiate savings in the design life.
The Femern case investigates in four diﬀerent
alternatives, which all meet the design criteria. There is a diﬀerence in the NPV for the
cheapest and the most expensive alternative of about 30 % within the period of 60 years.
This is a diﬀerence of about 200,000,000 kr. in the two speciﬁc alternatives due to the
extent of the project. This is a considerable amount of money to save. A diﬀerence in
the two alternatives might have been predicted, but would not have been supported or
quantiﬁed without the analyses. It was also found that the alternative with the lowest
construction costs, was not the alternative with the lowest NPV. Therefore additional
construction costs initiate a saving within the period of analysis. The results of the DTU
case indicated savings within the period of analysis with a higher initial expense as well.
This indicates that if long term costs are considered there will be potential savings within
the design life of an asset. The estimates can be made in the design phase and sensitivity

analyses and Monte Carlo simulations can be used to support the results and decrease

Furthermore it can be used to determine the critical aspects of a design and optimize this.
The owner will be the one to beneﬁt from the analyses. The design team can optimize the

project and the owner can potentially get lower maintenance costs.
The construction
industry is generally project minded and this constellation necessitates a radical change
in the common practice.
A further development of the tool to calculate the NPV for diﬀerent alternatives will ease
the process. A direct link between the calculations and the model will reduce the risk
of errors in the conversion. Furthermore it will be simple to implement changes and get
the result immediately. A development of automatically generated risk and uncertainty
analyses will be needed to get the results immediately and get the full beneﬁt of a direct
link. RIB iTWO is a software that tries to include all the tasks in every phase of the
construction industry digitally. It is possible to make model based cost estimates, cost
control, time schedules, logistic planning, clash control etc. A combination of the program
to calculate NPV and a software like iTWO will ensure a link between the model and
the estimates.
It is already possible to make Monte Carlo simulations and sensitivity
analyses on time schedules in iTWO, so this could potentially be incorporated in the NPV
calculations as well to generate them automatically. A platform to analyse everything in
the diﬀerent processes ensures a consistency and a reuse of data.
7.3
Why is it not already an integrated part of the construction industry
and how can it be implemented?
Total cost of ownership is a part of public construction projects. The requirements are
stated in (Klima-, Energi- og Bygningsministeriet 2013b). The set up for the calculations
and the analyses is not speciﬁed and the use is therefore limited or not standardized.
NPV is mathematics. It can be used regardless of the speciﬁc project to assess the cost
eﬀectiveness of alternatives. The inputs are an initial cost, running costs, and intervals
for the running costs, which have to be discounted at a given discount rate or NDR to a
present value. Therefore it can form the basis of a standard approach. It should be the
owner’s responsibility to include requirements about total cost of ownership. The consult-

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
The total cost of ownership must be considered in the design phase, because it is in the
design phase changes can be made without considerable costs. The decisions in the design
phase determine the life cycle costs of solutions. NPV calculations and life cycle analyses
must be used in the estimate of the total cost of ownership to compare diﬀerent alternatives
on the same basis. This gives the owner an enlightenment to base decisions upon supported
by the economic consequences for the decision. NPV calculations, supported by sensitivity
analyses and Monte Carlo simulations, can potentially increase the quality of projects. The
calculations can be used to identify critical design parameters and quantify the design life
proﬁt of greater construction costs. It will make value engineering an integrated part of
all design phases and can be used in decision-making processes.
A calculation of the NPV requires estimates or assumptions about the construction costs,
the maintenance costs, the intervals for maintenance activities and a discount rate or
interest and inﬂation rate.
The full potential of total cost of ownership in decision-making processes is achieved when
reuse of all information in the project is possible. To base the calculations on extracts
from object based building models it is required that the collaboration in projects is based
on the working method, which BIM provides. The calculations can be based on models
developed early in the design phases, which only contains geometry and construction
objects. Thereby total cost of ownership will be considered in the further progression and
concretization of the design. There must be a continuous concretization of the digital
information and the object based models. Models and digital information shall not be
delivered, only by contractual reasons, but because it adds value and must be reused in
the whole lifecycle. The requirements for input and output in order to progress further
must be stated by project participants and be supported by a digital object based model
at a given Information Level.
The Information Level must represent what have been
concretized and determined in the project, so each project participant knows what to
expect and what the models can be used for. IDM’s can be used in the mapping of input
and output data to structure processes.
Information can be lost in exchanges. A better understanding of how models and infor-
mation are imported and exported between ﬁle formats and applications will optimize the
data structure and unleash more of the potential in BIM.
The tool that has been developed in the two cases is an example of how NPV calculations
can be made based on quantity takeoﬀs from object based models. The critical parameters
have been investigated and supported by sensitivity analyses and Monte Carlo simulations.
It has been possible to give the respective owners a basis for decision and to quantify the
economic consequences. The potential savings for additional construction costs have been
indicated as well. It is important to consider the life cycle costs in order to choose the
most cost eﬀective project hence the project that generates the lowest NPV.
The owner must specify the requirements to the total cost of ownership before it is con-
sidered in projects and before the potential savings can be obtained. This can either be
included in contracts between the owner and consultants or by updating (Klima-, Energi-
og Bygningsministeriet 2013b) with a similar approach as (Ministeriet for By, Bolig og
Landdistrikter 2011) to ensure a standard use in all public projects. Furthermore the con-
sultants can provide it as a service, which makes the owner aware of the potential savings
within the life cycle of projects.

FURTHER WORKS
9
Further Works
To include total cost of ownership in decision-making processes related to design requires a
change in the traditionally interpretation of a cheap project. The costs within the life cycle
must be analyzed to generate a cost eﬀective project within the whole project life cycle
and not only the construction costs. A further study of the implementation of this radical
change will be relevant in order to utilize the tools and the knowledge from this thesis.
The tool can be further developed and standardized to generate risk reports automatically
based on the input requirements. A directly link between the calculations and quantities
to a model software decreases the manual interaction and minimizes the risks of errors. A
further investigation of how this can be done, or how the tool can be implemented in an
already existing application will optimize the correlation between object based building
models and NPV calculations.

2.4 Komfoft
Der er samtidig med blowerdooftesten foretaget målinger af lufthastigheder ved
utætheder.
Ved en lokal lufthastighed på over 1,2 m/s målt ved en utæthed i facaden under de
anvendte konditioner (trykforskel på 50 Pa) kan det forventes, at lufthastigheden i
opholdszonen vil overskride 0,15 m/s under normale trykforskelle, hvilket vil føles
som træk iht. BR10 (kap. 6.3.1.1 , stk 3), den danske standard for termisk indeklima
(DS 474) samt anbefaling fra Arbejdstilsynet.
I forhold til grænseværdien på 1,2 mls er der målt nogle ganske høje luftstrømme
på op til 7 m/s i 8.204 og op lil2,7 m/s i 8118, hvor vinduerne er blevet renoveret.
Det må derfor forventes, at der vil komme utilsigtede luftstrømme fra de renovere-
de vinduer, som kan give anledning til følelsen af træk. Dette vil ikke forekomme i
samme omfang med nye vinduer.
Derudover er der de øvrige forhold omkring indeklimaet hhv. vinter og sommer. De
eksisterende vinduesrammer er dårligere isolerede end nye rammer, hvorfor de om
vinteren vilvære kolde, hvilket generelt vilgive forøget kuldestråling fra vinduerne.
Om sommeren vilder i praksis ikke være nogen forskel på nye og gamle vinduer,
så længe glassene er de samme samt at muligheden for og funktion af vinduesåb-
ning og solafskaermning er den samme.
2.5 Sammenfatning - pilotprojekt
Det forudsættes, at pilotprojektet er udført på repræsentative rum. Under den for-
udsætning kan det konkluderes, at man ved renovering af vinduer kan forvente at
opnå den fornødne tæthed, så indeklimaet/ventilationen kan styres og så varme
ikke utilsigtet forsvinder ud af sprækker o.l. Dog er der målt utætheder, hvilket i
opholdszonen giver anledning til trækpåvirkninger.

Eksisterende trævinduer og fyldn ingspartier
Levetiden for trævinduer regnes i almindelighed til omkring 40 år, hvilket svarer til,
at levetiden for de eksisterende vinduer er opbrugt. Der kan foretages en levetids-
forlængelse ved at udløre de arbejder, der er beskrevet i beslutningsnotat af
21.08.2014 ikap. 5.2 (baseline), 5.3 (minibilen) og Bilag B.
Herefter skal vinduerne og fyldningspartierne vedligeholdes med reparation af træ-
værk, malebehandling, udskiftning/justering af hængsler og beslag og udskiftning
af tætningslister ca. hvert 6. år. Til arbejdet skal anvendes facadestillads eller lift.
Det vurderes at vinduernes levetid herved kan forlænges med omkring 1O-20 àr.
Udgiften til renovering af én bygning anslås til 5,0 mio. kr. ekskl. moms inkl. udgif-
ter til miljøsanering men ekskl. stillads.
Udgiften for hver bygning til vedligehold ca. hvert 6. år anslås til kr. 0,7 mio.kr.
ekskl. moms. Hefiil kommer udgift til stillads. Det er forudsat, at det ikke vil være
nødvendigt at udføre miljøsanering i forbindelse med vedligeholdsarbejdet.
De ovenfor beskrevne arbejder til forøgelse af levetid af vinduer er arbejder, der i
et eller andet omfang allerede foretages i dag, idet der ifølge DTU CAS sker et
kontinueft vedligehold af eksisterende traevinduer. COWI kender ikke omfanget og
frekvensen for disse ar,bejder.
