Abstract 
This thesis aims to examine the quality of planning processes used to establish car free areas in cities that 
are currently dependent. To investigate the research question the use of both qualitative semi-structured 
interviews and quantitative survey methods has been used, in relation to the empiricism and theory. 
Literature study and case studies have also been conducted. Existing theory and theory with parallels to 
other research areas has been used to describe theory used here and discussion.  
Oslo Municipality wants to develop a car free area within Ring Road 1, of about 2 km², within 2019. The 
plans have been approved by the City Council and the next step is how to implement the plans to the area. 
The research has led to the following problem statement: 
“Which overarching planning process can Oslo use and develop as its own process towards the city 
becoming car free?” 
Four European cities and their planning process and implementation of a car free area have been 
researched. The cities and some main elements are as follows: 
• 
Odense, Denmark – Removal of a four-lane highway to develop a car free city center with new 
housing, underground parking and a light rail through the area. 
• 
Groningen, the Netherlands – Closing of the city center to prioritize pedestrians and cyclists, 
solving the problems of traffic jams and pollution. 
• 
Gent, Belgium – Closing of various streets during summer time to bring people to the streets for 
celebrations and interactions. 
• 
Münster, Germany – A neighborhood in the center allowing only citizens without cars to move 
into the settlement. 
Their characteristics vary, but the commonality in all cities is to keep private motorized vehicles outside 
the downtown area. 
Findings of the survey showed that the majority of Oslo’s residents do not believe that a car free city 
center is manageable in such a short time span, and that there is a lack of communication between 
inhabitants and the municipality. Assuming people are creatures of habit, if given the right information 
and time to change daily routines this would be key to making the process streamlined and effective. With 
various participation processes as workshops, charrettes, and scenario planning tools the public can 
contribute and show an interest, giving the municipality a successful planning process.

1 Introduction 
“The content of the thesis is to be agreed with the [program] supervisor. The thesis may contain a 
combination of experimental work, fieldwork, theoretical studies, synthesis, modelling and analysis. All 
theses must include elements of literature studies and criticism. In addition, the thesis contains [various] 
overarching learning objectives” (Program Specification at DTU 2015/2016, M.Sc. Engineering 
Management). 
The thesis looks at various European cities that have taken car free cities to the next level – to version 2.0. 
The world evolves and cities develop new suburbs accordingly. In the twentieth century the automobile 
was introduced and influenced the “once-attractive small-scale suburbs into an endless, congested sprawl” 
(LeGates & Stout, 2011). This stage was dependent on the existence of a vital central city as a center for 
production and employment, and cultural goods. This pattern has changed over time and the suburbs and 
city centers have a broader function of houses, jobs and activities, all connected to a way of commuting. 
Everything is connected to the quality of life one wants to achieve, and to live in the city center. Even 
though no one knows for sure what the future holds for urban civilization, when city centers and suburbs 
developed from one thing (version 1.0) to another, and making it better (version 2.0) this leads to a break 
with the past. This new urbanization is characterized by a combination of sustainability and comfort. 
Cities that have figured out what attract people to the centers and what makes them spend time there are 
one step closer to having the life and city quality that everyone strives for. Often this does not include the 
stress and loud noise of the automobile. How a city reaches this goal depends on how they define an 
attractive city and the planning process that lies behind it. The focus of this report lies on how Oslo can 
make use of other cities’ planning processes to acquire the best possible version, version 2.0, of a car free 
center.  
1.1 Background 
For many years now, there has been talk of improving public transportation in Oslo, and giving the city 
back to its inhabitants. In October 2015, Oslo municipality decided that the city is to be car free within 
Ring 1 (appendix A.1 and A.2) by 2019. The road network within Ring 1 will be affected, but how is not 
finally concluded. The councils are working on alternate impact assessments at this moment. The Institute 
of Transport Economy (TØI) in Oslo published an article in 2003 to gather information and experiences 
from different cities around Europe about their subway and tram usage. The study was done with cities 
comparable to Oslo, to see whether equal solutions could be implemented. In April 2016 TØI again 
published an article about European cities. This time about car free city centers and their competence 
value for Oslo. As this thesis is being written, the municipality and the City Councils involved in the 
project are working alongside. Seeing as the results will not be visible until 2019, the results of this report 
are tainted by the fact. 
1.2 Problem Statement 
This report was written with the aim of bringing forth new knowledge, recommendations, and good 
examples that are based on the facts and long term decisions to make Oslo car free. As well as to figure 
out a solution to strengthen the city center as an attractive area of establishment for commerce and 
services, where everyone can contribute actively to a positive, environmental friendly development 
(Tennøy, Midtskog, Øksenholdt, & Nore, 2014). The overall objective for Oslo is to reduce the 
greenhouse gas1 (GHG) emission by 60% within 2020 and up to 100% by 2030. For this to be done, the 
City Council wants to remove 1/5 of the cars that are on Oslo’s roads today (Eggesvik & Sørgjerd, 2016).

The aim of this study is to explore ideas, concepts and solutions for how Oslo can implement a car free

“Which overarching planning process can Oslo use and develop as its own process towards the city

building the city from scratch. Everything has to come together in a new way, with different procedures 
and tools. This, along with the above mentioned problem statement became the foundation of the 
following report.

2 Methodology 
Designing an academic study can be divided into three phases: choice of field of study/ research question, 
theoretical approach, and choice of methodology and research design. The latter depends on the first two 
phases and determines what kind of information the researcher collects and in what way it is gathered. 
How the information is used in the analysis depends on what type of method is used.  
In this report there were four methods used to collect data about the topic. Firstly, a literature study was 
done to gain knowledge about different cities and their approach and processes towards car free city 
centers. The literature study was done with the intention of finding out how to conduct a process of 
implementing a car free or car reduced area. In relation to this, a research on trade and commerce was also 
conducted in regards to what it would do to remove a large portion of shoppers. Secondly, a case study of 
four cities across Europe was executed. Research determined which cities would be relevant to include 
and what those cities had done to make them interesting as a case study here. Lastly, semi-structured 
qualitative interviews and a quantitative survey were created in order to establish starting point from 
which to move further and to complement the case study. For this thesis both qualitative and quantitative 
tools were used, in order to develop an overall understanding and a representative overview. 
The surveys were answered by stakeholders; they are the ones who will be using the city when the project 
is finished. A different set of questions concerning the interview was answered by five persons connected 
to or interested in the project. These five people have more experience concerning planning of areas and 
are more aware of how the stakeholders think and what their preferences are.  
The results from the literature study, together with the case studies, interviews and surveys were molded 
together to create some guidelines and recommendations that were used to answer the research question in 
full. To understand how the methods worked together to produce this report a model of the process was 
made. The process is outlined below in figure 1. The main challenge of developing a car free area within 
Ring 1 is that it is large and the time span is short. The process of gathering information within each 
method consisted of analyzing and identifying how a car free development comes to life and what it 
means to live in a sustainable society. The interpretation of the data collected is reflected in the discussion 
and conclusion.

2.1 Literature Study

literature that relates to a particular topic. The reason for the literature study might be to indicate that a 
new research study is needed.  
Throughout this process, the literature study has been the base for many issues and views. This type of 
study was initially done to find out about car free cities in general, and a car free city center in Oslo. 
Another reason for the literature study was to find out whether commerce would be affected, and if so, 
would it be positive or negative. Research was obtained first through rough search on the topic in general

• 
Relevant for the topics discussed.

methods and findings should be presented in such a way that the quality of the studies can be 
considered, that the quality should be satisfactory and that it should be possible to understand 
what the results imply.

2.2 Case Study 
Case studies are reports about something or someone that has been studied over time and are normally 
used to describe a behavior or to provide insight into a phenomenon. Case studies have strengths and 
weaknesses. One advantage might be that a study of this sort allows the researcher to do extensive 
research into a case and conclude with a detailed description of said case. On the other hand, it can often 
be hard to identify a causal relationship only by the means of a case study since there can be many 
phenomena that may influence a given outcome (Wæhle & Sterri, 2015). Nevertheless, case studies are 
useful. They can be used as an independent study, or as in this thesis, to supplement other used methods 
(interviews and survey).  
Selection 
The four cities that were chosen each has specific background information that the author found 
interesting. Each city has various elements that could be transferred to Oslo. At the beginning there were 
ten different cities that seemed appropriate for a case study (see appendix B for which cities considered). 
As more information was gathered about each case, the stack thinned and the remaining deemed the best 
in this situation. The cities chosen are very different from Oslo when it comes to size, population, 
geographical location and structure. The cities are not only chosen for their relevance, but on how the 
implementation is conducted, and how their structure of the area is or will be. The aim of this report is to 
show that although many cities have introduced a car free area there may be aspects of various cities 
combined that can be transferred to Oslo. Another important reason these cities were chosen and not more 
known ones was due to the amount of research done previously. As car free cities are becoming more and 
more popular and cities are aiming for  (Carbon Dioxide) neutral areas2, many reports and articles 
have been published about the more common places and their efforts towards prioritizing pedestrians. In 
view of this, the cities that were chosen give another perspective than has been looked at before and show 
new information about a popular topic. 
When the cities were being reviewed, the climatic differences were also researched, along with terrain and 
geographical location. Even though all of the cities chosen lie further south than Oslo, which affects

2.5.2 Possible Sources of Error 
In every survey conducted there are possible sources of errors. There are possibilities that the results have 
been affected by the methods used for selection, and gathering and analyzing of the information (Larsen, 
2012). Possible sources of errors that might occur here are study effects, where the respondent is affected 
by the researcher or research. This is also mentioned by one of the respondents:  
“Little silly with such a cumbersome link / QR code. I used the QR code but had to install an App. It raises 
the threshold somewhat and gives you a skew, where only the most interested in the topic has responded.” 
Another source of error could be bias in the sample, as mentioned earlier, that the sample is not 
representative due to the fact that friends and family are included in the survey. 
There might also be errors connected to the interviews. The interview effect, or control effect, is the 
biggest downside of an interview (Larsen, 2012). The interviewee can have been affected by the presence 
of the interviewer’s behavior. By reacting to information given by the interviewee, he or she can later 
change the answer or modify it. Female researchers can also get different answers than male researchers 
within certain topics. Another possible source of error is in the way the questions are formulated. Having 
leading questions will most likely raise the chance of getting a certain answer. Thirdly, the context effect 
is the error that might occur under this designation is that the answers have been affected by earlier 
questions asked in the interview. Therefore it is important to think about the order of the questions, 
although this tends to be difficult when the interview is a conversation.

3.1 City Life and City Quality 
“Standard of living refers to the level of wealth, comfort, material goods and necessities available to a 
certain socioeconomic class in a certain geographic area” (Investopedia, 2016). 
A city is characterized by three main points (Thorsnæs & Solerød, 2015): 
• 
Population over a certain number. This is not a set number for every city in the world, however it 
varies due to density, settlement, and construction patterns.   
• 
Settlement/buildings are concentrated. This is a requirement saying that people need to stay 
relatively close to a limited total area, although difficult to set a limit to how tight the settlement 
should be. 
• 
Industry and activity is urban, i.e. not belonging to agriculture and forestry. The city has to have a 
certain minimum of urban function. It should be a meeting place, a center of commerce, schools, 
cultural activities and health care. 
With the aforementioned points set as background for what a city is, it should also be an attractive and 
lively place in an environmental friendly structure. As more and more people are moving to the larger 
cities there is a continuous need to create larger areas for settlement and better living qualities. To develop 
the spaces needed for larger communities, first and foremost there has to be an agreed upon term of what 
city life and city quality means. These terms change from city to city and can mean the opposite in two 
neighboring cities. It is estimated that over half of the world’s population live in urban areas and cities 
(cia.gov, 2016) and people continue to move there because of job opportunities and better standard of 
living (ITU-T, 2014). The above definition is about the standard of living which refers to how people want 
to live and what they need in order to have a comfortable quality of life. This is not the same as city 
quality, but it can be applied in almost the same way. The standard of living refers to having the 
necessities available; this is the same for a city. By examining what a city needs so that it may be an 
investment for its inhabitants will secure people’s wellbeing and safety.

People have different needs and different regards to what it includes to be safe. The challenge that many 
European cities face is the unique balance between demand for increasing personal mobility and economic 
growth, and the need to respect the environment and provide acceptable quality of life for all inhabitants 
(European Commission, 2004). A city center is the most complex part of a town and development is an 
important part of creating a functional and sustainable society. Functions of social, economic, cultural and

walking than cities with through traffic and larger traveling distances. The areal structure of a city has 
large impact on how dependent one is of the car and how much traffic is generated.  
3.2 Car Free or Car Reducing Cities – Version 1.0 
The English Dictionary (Collins, 2016) does not actually have a definition of car free, but uses the term 
pedestrian precinct, which is defined thusly: “pedestrian precinct (British) or pedestrian zone (US) is an 
area that has been concerted for the use of pedestrians only, by excluding all motor vehicles”. 
The Institute of Transport Economics (TØI) recently posted a paper about 15 car free cities in Europe. 
They state the following: “What is included in a car free area, and how the traffic in these areas is 
regulated varies among European cities […]. This goes for both exemptions from ban of travel by car and 
about how it is organized for bus, tram, taxi, bike, and walking” (Tønnesen, Meyer, Skartland, & Sundfør, 
2016). 
With that said, a car free city is not the same as a car reducing city. There are degrees of car ban in 
various cities and when it comes to what degree of car ban there is in which city, the size and geographical 
location plays a part. The larger the area is, the harder it is to get people comfortable with the idea. While 
streets and neighborhoods are discussed as completely car free, none of the cities researched is 100 % car 
free. What they all have in common is that they have areas where the use of cars is highly limited and 
where pedestrians, bicycles, and public transportation are prioritized. There are different rules for delivery, 
where to park, and low speed limits in the city centers, which mean that some places people are allowed to 
drive through some streets with a special permit or a pre-approval, but they are not allowed to park. When 
analyzing and discussing car free cities here, this is what the term means. No city will ever be able to 
remove all motor vehicles from its city due to transportation of goods, emergency vehicles and taxies, to 
mention a few. When talking about car free cities, these vehicles are often neglected to be mentioned, 
making the term more car reducing.  
While car free is not the same as car reducing it is often perceived as the same. Car free zones or car free 
city centers is a measure that allows areas to be closed for private cars all or parts of the day (Haraldsen, 
2016). Transport of goods amongst other necessary entry is maintained although regulated. Though it 
seems like the only way to acquire a car free city is by prohibiting motor vehicles, there are other options 
to consider, some of which have been used in other cities. The planning policies in the UK refer to the 
term car free as “not allowed to park” (Melia, 2010). As far as car free development goes it implies either

new buildings or change to existing ones and two different aspects that come up are i) residential areas 
where motor vehicles are excluded and/or ii) housing where people live without a car. Both will be 
discussed further down. As car free3 is not the obvious term for this report, car reducing areas or low car 
developments have the following definition according to Melia (2010): 
Low car developments are residential or mixed use developments which: 
• 
Offer limit parking, and: 
•

Even though the objective is to be car free, a prohibition does not remove a market. There seems to be an 
agreement that there should be less GHG emission, but planners keep developing cities for the use of cars. 
There are, however, many factors that affect urban development of traffic volumes. These are economic 
development, labor force participation, population growth, and work places. Nevertheless, how urban 
development is planned and how it affects the above-mentioned factors is well documented (see e.g. 
Tennøy, 2010). In January 2015 the Government in Madrid, Spain launched a new rule fining everyone 
that drives into the capital € 90. This measure only applies to the drivers that do not live in Madrid and do 
not have a guaranteed space in one of the 13 official parking lots (O’Sullivan, 2014). The initiative is 
Spain’s way of making its core free of cars. In Strasbourg, France the plan was to create two new 
tramlines as a way to reduce the dominance of private cars. To create space enough for more public 
transport, cycling and walking, the city had to relocate the highway from private car traffic (European 
Commission, 2004). Through traffic was removed, ‘loops’ were made to be able to access parking 
facilities in the city center, but a passage from one district to another is no longer possible. It is believed 
that without this reduction traffic would have increased with 60 000 vehicles per day between 1990 and 
2000 (Langberg, 2015). Another option could be measures based on license plates prohibiting cars to drive 
20 % of days. TDM Encyclopedia (Wright, 2005) documents that this alternative only reduces the total 
traveling time by 10 %. This is because, as stated previously, industrial and commercial vehicles are 
excused from these restrictions, but also because wealthy families will often purchase a second car to 
bypass the prohibition (Haraldsen, 2016). 
Many residents and local retail owners argue that by removing cars and on-street parking, stores and trade 
will lose business investments and eventually face bankruptcy. However, throughout the last decade there 
have been many different studies executed on whether removing cars from the city center will have an 
effect on shops and retail. Some of these studies (from Ireland, Canada and Australia) were put together in 
an article for CityLab. They are all studies on possible economic impacts which all show the same thing; 
“replacing on-street parking with a bike lane has little to no impact on local business, and in some cases 
might even increase business” (Jaffe, 2015).  
The Norwegian Public Roads Administration (SVV) did a similar study getting the same result. Retail 
owners often assume that people coming in cars are the best customers. They assume that people often

the people of the city and creates a better living and working environment, as well as enhances the 
potential of an attractive place for tourists and other visitors. 
3.3 Planning Process 
The Planning and Building Services (PLB) of Oslo, together with the municipality published a report  
stating that the municipality has a responsibility to ensure the active participation by stakeholders when it 
comes to drawing up development plans (Plan- og Bygningsetaten, 2013). The planning of Oslo’s new 
area inside Ring Road 1 is meeting tough criticism along the way and it is highly important that all parties 
involved keep themselves informed and have a say in the matter. As will be discussed further on, the 
planned implementation date may be too ambitious and the general planning could be lacking certain 
elements to make the process as effective and efficient as possible. As of this time, Oslo is working on 
developing the process of which will outline the project. The planning process of the project is at a state 
prior to the implementation of the project. The focus here is on the former.  
A project is temporary work to create something unique. Temporary here is referred to the fact that a 
project must be finished in a pre-determined period of time (Bejestani, 2010). The idea of planning may 
appear vague and evasive to define. To construct or build a project, there has to be a plan, but not 
necessarily ending up in a physical representation or design. The main idea is to capture, perform and 
evaluate beneficial suggestions in projects as soon as possible. To do this, there has to be a system with 
well-defined processes (Bejestani, 2010). In most cases there is need for something to be written down, 
but these plans may never involve the production of a single physical representation of the entity that is 
being produced (Hall, 2011). A plan refers to the work that is being done, whether it may be different 
human activities or designing objects such as cars or buildings. The Oxford English Dictionary defines 
planning the following way: “The action or process of forming a plan; the action or work of a planner, 
(later) esp. the designing or controlling of urban or economic development; an instance of this. Also 
occas.: the making or delineation of a plan or diagram.”(OED, 2016) 
The process of forming a plan, or planning processes are different depending on what is to be planned. 
The urban and territorial planning process uses evaluation as a core activity. Murgante, Borruso, & 
Lapucci, (2011) explains this in their article by saying “it enables to face environmental sustainability 
complexity and permits to find out, for each specific territory, those solutions capable to best integrate 
environmental, social, cultural and economic objectives”. The strategic planning process, on the other 
hand is used when the objective is to carry out a “strong” sustainability, meaning that it aims to analyze 
areal transformations and to distinguish which resources to be protected and which to be exploited when it 
comes to cultural, historical and environmental values in a certain area (Murgante et al., 2011). This can 
also be said for urban planning, which means something more limited or precise. A planning process

should not be confused with spatial planning, which is a tool in the work of environmental authorities to 
ensure that nature values, landscape and outdoor recreation is sufficiently accounted for 
(Miljødirektoratet, 2009). Urban planning refers to planning with a dimensional structure of activities 
(Hall, 2011). It has been said that the whole planning process is a great improvement in practice, meaning 
that it is more clearly stated and more logical and explicit. A plan needs to start with a discussion about 
what should be achieved and this discussion have to be a continuous part of the whole operation 
throughout the planning process. The planning process should ensure the greatest possible participation of 
all parties involved (landowners, stakeholders, NGOs, neighbors, public authorities etc.). A planning 
process in relation to the Planning and Building Act (Norway) is initiated by the announcement of a plan 
startup and ends upon posting of final planning decisions. The Planning and Building Act goes on to state 
that “[p]lanning must promote entirety by sectors, tasks and interests in an area seen in context through 
coordination and collaboration on exercises between sector authorities and intergovernmental, regional 
and municipal agencies, private organizations and institutions, and the general public. 
Planning shall be based on financial and other resource requirements for implementation and be no more 
extensive than necessary. 
Plans should contribute to implementing international conventions and agreements within the scope of the 
Act” (Lovdata, 2008). 
The law must ensure that the recapitulative planning unites the interests of society in land use and 
contributes to protecting the country's nature and environment, so that the development of society can 
happen on a sustainable basis respecting human conditions of life and the preservation of animal and 
plant life”(Danske Love, 2015). 
Norwegian law requires all property developers to set up a planning description which includes a planning 
process (Lovdata, 2008). This planning process will essentially contain a participation process, 
notification of commencement, and if necessary, planning programs (figure 4). The first step of the 
planning process is for the plan to be approved by a city council. When this is done, the project starts with 
an initial meeting and announcement of the initiation. Step 3 consists of a participation process, were the 
municipality must ensure that the process is adapted for every party interested. The municipality also has a 
responsibility to provide active participation of groups that require special arrangements. Everyone must 
be ensured ample opportunities for participation, even for those who cannot participate directly in the 
process. Landowners, neighbors, public consultative bodies, as well as other affected parties must be 
notified by letter at this stage. When all is in order, if no objectives to the initiation are received, 
implementation/construction may start. During the construction period regular meetings must be held 
during the process to ensure that everything is as planned. This continues until the end of the project. The 
planning process is a requirement for all regional plans and municipal plans which may have significant 
effects on the environment and society (Kommunal- og Moderniseringsdepartementet, 2009).

For the development to be on point and well integrated with what a city needs the most important step the 
municipality and other professionals involved can contribute with is communication. Figure 4 shows 
communication with the public in steps 2, 3, and 5. Communication and public participation are the most 
significant methods used in a planning process. Legacy (2012) presents in her article Achieving 
Legitimacy through Deliberative Plan-Making Processes—Lessons for Metropolitan Strategic Planning 
that planning processes need to provide stakeholders with a choice on when and how they engage. Public 
hearings and written statements are still often used as official methods, but many cities are choosing an 
extensive range of methods like workshops, charrettes, open houses and public meetings in addition, as a 
way to attract wider groups of participants (Kahila-Tani, Broberg, Kyttä, & Tyger, 2015). During plan 
preparation the quantity of planners and time that is originally dedicated to the process might increase 
sustainability related issues (Conroy & Jun, 2016). Due to this it is highly important to continue with 
communication and regular meetings throughout the process. Public participation, as indicated before, is 
extremely beneficial to the planning process. The general public whose lives are impacted by the planning 
process can contribute by reducing conflict, improving compliance, and improving content of the plan for 
a sustainable development. Participation efforts can be characterized by two dimensions: participation 
breadth and participation depth (Conroy & Jun, 2016). The former refers to “the extent to which an 
opportunity is offered to every community member to participate at every stage of the policymaking 
process”. The latter is “the extent to which the citizens who choose to participate have the opportunity to 
determine the final policy outcome by means of the participation process” (Berry, Portney, & Thomson, 
1993). 
While these methods are valuable, the place and time can limit the number of participants if it is not 
executed during a certain phase of the planning process. Providing the opportunity for public involvement 
allows stakeholders and other interested parties adequate time to consider the intricacies of contemporary 
challenges facing urban regions (Legacy 2012). Due to the many opportunities at particular stages of the 
plan-making process, stakeholders can easily decide their nature of involvement.

4 Background and Future Development of Oslo  
The greater Oslo has about 625 000 inhabitants that work in and around the city. The capital is Norway’s 
largest city, 121 km², and center for economy, culture and education. By 2030 the population is expected 
to increase by nearly 200 000 (Plan- og Bygningsetaten, 2013). Oslo city center is the main public 
transportation pivot and the most important meeting place for its population. Trade and services have 
historically been among the most important driving forces for the development of cities and towns and are 
still essential elements in an attractive and vibrant center (Tennøy et al., 2014).  
4.1 Grounds for Political Decisions 
In October 2015 the three largest parties in the Norwegian government signed the council declaration, 
which stated that a reduction of car traffic in Oslo is important to make better room for pedestrians, 
cyclists and public transportation. These means of transportations will be prioritized on the cost of private 
motorized vehicles. One of the main problems seems to be that people are traveling by car from various 
municipalities on the outskirts of the city. The political cooperation declaration wants to show that the city 
center will be more attractive and accessible without traffic (Byrådserklæring, 2015). 
It is known that cities play a large part when it comes to the production of GHG. In 2012 the transport 
sector contributed to 26 % of the GHG emission in Norway. Personal transport accounts for about 60 % of 
these emissions (Fridstrøm & Alfsen, 2014).  Climate measures that make a difference are measures that 
directly or indirectly, drastically reduce GHG emissions from one or more of the following emission 
sources: 
• 
Personal vehicles 
• 
Airplane trips 
• 
Transport of goods on roads and water 
The focus on this report will be on how to plan for an area without the former source, motor vehicles. 
Continuing and intensifying the climate profile in the registration tax for cars can reduce the fuel 
consumption and emissions in personal automotive. This in turn can halve the vehicles'  emissions 
over the next 25-30 years (Fridstrøm & Alfsen, 2014). Even stronger cuts are possible in the longer term. 
As stated by Tennøy (2010) among others (see e.g. Fridstrøm & Alfsen, 2014; Solomon et al., 2007)  to 
avoid dramatic and irreversible changes GHG emission need to be reduced by 50-80 % by 2050. Most 
planning and policy documents at political and administrative levels in Norway are in agreement when it 
comes to this objective. Although the impetus to plan for climate challenges is there the process and the 
means might be lacking.

4.3 City Plan and City Life 
A center is the most complex area of a city; this is true for Oslo as well. A variety of different functions 
and people gather in the center. People travel to, from and in the center with various purposes, transport 
and speeds. Property structure is complex, and many public and private actors are responsible for 
planning, constructing, operating and maintenance of buildings, streets and other areas. Different 
stakeholders in and users of the center have various desires, aspirations and needs (Tennøy et al., 2015).  
Oslo is a multi-cultured city with immigrants from 187 different nations. There are differences in living 
conditions and different challenges in various parts of the city. Public health is good and the life 
expectancy is increasing yearly (Plan- og Bygningsetaten, 2013). Culture and sports are key elements for 
the quality of life for the people and strengthens the city's foundation for creativity and wealth creation. 
The City Council’s goal is for Oslo to become the environmental capital of Europe by limiting the climate 
changes and develop a sustainable society, and by 2030 be a fossil free city (Byrådserklæring, 2015). In 
close cooperation with the residents, businesses, organizations, the state and the municipality, the City 
Council will base all new building initiatives in a climate and energy strategy.

4.5.2 Public Transportation Habits 
In 2010 a report by Prosam (see Ruud, Ellis, & Norheim, 2010) was published about how vehicle users 
value different characteristics of public transportation offers. The goal was to figure out what residents in 
and around Oslo feel about the offer, to test whether there is consistency between passengers' valuations 
and their emphasis on the different aspects of public transport, and to identify passengers' attitude towards 
a larger part of the public transport system running under ground. This may be a possible strategy to cope 
with expected traffic growth (Ruud et al., 2010). People living in Oslo and Akershus, from 18 years and 
up were asked to participate in a survey. The survey, which was on online, was answered by around 2000. 
It consisted of the vehicle users’ valuation of time (total travel time, walking to and from their stop, and 
delays), money, change and means of transport. The results show that amongst people traveling with 
public transport at least once a month, close to 70% of the asked are satisfied with the overall offer. This 
will be more closely analyzed with the results of the survey conducted here and Ruter’s annual report.

focus was to measure the market share of cycling, and to build a framework for the bicycle strategy that 
Oslo was working on during the fall of 2013 and spring of 2014. The transportation survey can also be 
used as a basis for traffic planning in general in the municipality.

5.2 Groningen, Netherlands  
5.2.1 The Size of the City and Regional Role 
The city of Groningen is the capital of the province Groningen in the Netherlands. The municipality, about 
81 km², populates about 200 000 inhabitants, which makes it the largest city in the north of the country 
(Centraal Bureau voor de Statistiek & Den Haag/Heerlen, 2016; Tønnesen et al., 2016). 78 % of all 
residents and 90 % of all jobs can be found within a 3 km radius of the town (Ligtermoet, 2009). The area 
which is car free is about 0,3 km² (Tønnesen et al., 2016). 
5.2.2 Grounds for Political Decisions 
Due to extreme growth in motor vehicles going in and out of the city every day, local authority presented 
a traffic plan with a radical vision. Car ownership was just above the national average of 100 cars per 
1000 inhabitants (Groningen’s being 110 cars per 1000 inhabitants) in 1965 and car traffic tripled within 
13 years. The number of passengers on public transportation was reduced with 45 % during the same time 
(Ligtermoet, 2009). Considering these numbers and an increase in traffic jams and queues in the 60’s, 
Groningen chose to develop the city for pedestrians and cyclists with the help of the Circulation Plan of 
1977. 
5.2.3 Main Structure in the Transport System 
The city is most famous for having the highest percentage of bicycle usage in the world (Ligtermoet, 
2009). By providing proper infrastructure and amenities the city has maximized the usage of bicycles and 
made 420 km bike paths divided by 46 routes of bicycle networks that are used daily. Groningen is 
prioritizing bikes over car traffic and over 150 000 trips are today made by bike (Karlenzig, 2007). The 
percentage of trips taken by Groningen residents on bikes has been just above 30 % for the last 20 years. 
Residents and authorities are able to keep this percentage and even increase it due to the competitiveness 
of bicycles compared to cars, which is indicated by a relation with integral transport and traffic policies 
and urban planning (Ligtermoet, 2009). Groningen has the highest percentage of bicyclists in the

5.3 Gent, Belgium 
5.3.1 The Size of the City and Regional Role 
In a Belgian province, in the Flemish region, lies Gent, the capital and largest city of East Flanders (third 
largest city of Belgium). With its 251 000 residents (2014) Gent is Belgium’s second largest municipality 
by number. The city is 158 km², with a car free area of 0,35 km² (Intelligent Energy Europe Programme of 
the EU, n.d.; Step up Smart Cities, 2015).  
5.3.2 Grounds for Political Decisions 
During the 1980’s there were a lot of traffic congestions and noise- and air pollution. This resulted in the 
mobility plan, giving pedestrians and bikers top priority.  
5.3.3 Main Structure in Transport System 
To tackle several problems with one solution, an area of 35 ha was made car free in 1997. The problems in 
the city included (Step up Smart Cities, 2015): 
• 
Persistent traffic congestion 
• 
Poor pedestrian and cyclist safety 
• 
Poor air quality and high energy use 
For the last decade there has been made large changes when it comes to the road infrastructure and car 
driving in Gent. With a wish to decrease both unnecessary driving and driving through the city center, the 
city has directed traffic around the center, making the public transportation offer larger and better. There is 
free parking around the center and a parking regime inside to reduce the amount of cars in the city center 
(Tønnesen et al., 2016). Figure 19 shows changes from 2009 to 2014 in an area where cars are now 
banned.

In 1993, the accessibility by bikes was prioritized and in 1997 an ambitious mobility plan was put in place

5.4 Münster, Germany 
5.4.1 The Size of the City and Regional Role 
In Münster, Germany there is a colony located 2,5 km south of the city center. The Garden City is a 
neighborhood with a total of 196 accommodations that were constructed on former military grounds, an 
area no more than 3,2 ha. (Bäumer & Wilker, 2008), (Meister, n.d.). The apartments are between 1 and 5 
bedroom units. Assuming the Garden City follows the German household standards of approx. 2,0 persons 
per unit, the area houses no more than 400 persons10.   
5.4.2 Grounds for Political Decisions 
Due to a competition called “living without a car”, which is more closely described further down, the 
government of Münster dedicated a whole neighborhood to the idea. It was to be called the Garden City 
Weißenburg. In 1996 the model was ready and the settlement of car independence began to take form. 
5.4.3 Main Structure in Transport System 
There are many ways of getting around, including bus lanes and the main train station, all within 15 
minutes walking. Seeing how Münster is called the German capital of cycling, there are also very good 
connections to cycle paths that connect to the Old Town (Köllinger, 2007). There is a car-sharing facility 
located close by the development. This facility offers all tenants in the neighborhood a reduced 
membership fee. The car-sharing station offers 6 vehicles at the time (2008) and the main target group are 
the residents of the Garden City Weissenburg (Meister, n.d.).  
5.4.4 City Plan and City Life 
In the German federal state of North Rhine-Westphalia (NRW) there was a competition called “living 
without a car”. The city of Münster was influenced by this and started the car-free housing project 
“Garden City Weissenburg”. This is the first car-free colony in NRW where mobility measures were

6.4 Findings of Planning Processes 
Going back to chapter 3 where planning process is defined and where different definitions interpret what 
is meant with the word, the process of a plan is initiated by the announcement of the plan startup. A plan is 
the start of everything, a building, a new area, roads etc. But a plan does not need to end up as a physical 
thing. When planning something the process needs to be laid out in a way that it refers to the work that 
will be done. When designing an urban area the planning process is the main activity from the beginning 
to the end. The process needs to be analyzed, processed and carried out in such a way that the 
transformations in a certain area are precise. The discussions that start the planning process should include 
all parties involved and last throughout the construction time set out. Oslo is already behind schedule. The 
project had a starting date in January 2016 but did not commence until April, as told by the project leader, 
Kari Eliassen. Hence, there is not much information on the actual planning process. Due to this fact, the 
planning process of Thomas B. Thriges Street in Odense, Denmark will be more closely analyzed after 
some information about Oslo’s coming plans. As brought up in chapter 3, Oslo is currently working with 
the planning process of the project, and has not commenced the project itself.   
6.4.1 The New Oslo 
The city council wishes to create a greener and fairer city with room for everyone. As the city grows 
larger and larger, and more and more are travelling through the city center, the council wants to create an 
inclusive and living city. The city life should be livable, the air should be cleaner and the facilitation for 
pedestrians and cyclists should be better. A city center without cars will be more pleasant, more available, 
and give room for more life in the streets and nicer meeting spots (Oslo Kommune & Byrådsavdeling for 
Miljø og Samferdsel, 2016). The objective of the project is therefore to create a better city environment, 
increased city life, and a car free city center within Ring Road 1.

7.2 Review of Survey Results 
The survey contributed to the results of both a qualitative and quantitative nature. People on the streets 
were asked to participate in a survey about a car free city center in Oslo. As stated before, the answers that 
were received were most likely answered by a group of people that were more interested in the topic than 
others. Thus, the answers contributed to the overall understanding being that communication between the 
municipality and the community is very low. The public is not aware that “car free” in reality means “car 
reducing”. As shown in figure 25 in chapter 6.2 the majority does not think a car free city is possible due 
to the short time frame and poorly developed infrastructure, to mention a few comments.  
As previously mentioned the time aspect is an argument brought up time and time again. While Odense is 
spending 12 years removing cars and constructing an area of 51 000 m², Oslo will attempt close to the 
same in 4 years with an area of 1,9 km². Although Odense is developing a whole new district and this 
itself is a larger project than solely banning cars from an area, Odense’s planning process is closer to the 
general plan that is described in chapter 3.3. This is not to say that their plan is better, and that the general 
plan is to be followed very precise, but it shows that with proper communication and a visionary plan 
everyone can get behind the implementation of said plan. With the help of the Red Box located in the 
middle of the city center in Odense (figure 10) the process and procedures of the development helped the 
citizens connect and have time to readjust. Due to the openness people feel they have a say, hence, part of 
the process. 
The main issue that connects the obstacles that are defined by the citizens of Oslo seems to be lack of 
communication. As mentioned under chapter 6 close to 50 % of the people asked do not believe a car free 
city is manageable by 2020. This is not only because they do not have faith in the municipality, but 
because the process of how everything will unfold has not been sufficiently disclosed. But again the issue 
about Norwegians having to be comfortable arises. There is a steadfast belief that public transportation is a 
degradation of comfort and convenience, and although people think that it will take time before the 
subway, busses and trams can fully takes over the car, people’s beliefs are that it is expensive and it takes 
too long to travel with public transport. Ruter, however, has the actual numbers to oppose this belief.

7.2.1 Urban Spaces and Living Quality 
Every person has a different understand of what a city is and what it means to them. What is attractive to 
one person is not necessarily attractive to the next. By including various characteristics in a city it invites 
everyone to be a part of that specific society. Culture, activities, shops and cafés are what most people 
think about when asked what they would want in a city. Each city explored here has mixed opinions of 
what is attractive to their citizens and their community. What is equal to all cities, however, is that there 
most likely will be an increase in population over the next decade or two, which, in turn, puts pressure on 
the cities to expand. Communities that are presented with high developmental pressures, while increasing 
their attention to sustainability related goals, often do not recognize that residents of that community have 
the ability to realize common goals (Conroy & Jun, 2016). These common goals have to be agreed upon 
and the attractiveness of a city will change as the standard of living changes from city to city. 
How and where people decide to live and what they feel is needed is dependent on what is available 
within a short distance from their home. The needs people have will range from having an activity center 
close by to being able to shop whenever they want. In Oslo it was found that people like to be outside 
doing things, whether it is having coffee with a friend or walking around exploring new things in the city 
they know so well. An enhanced walking and urban environment can consist of walking paths, pathways 
for bicycles and higher parking fares close to the center (Haraldsen, 2016). By making pedestrians priority 
number one the city will gain increased activity. Many Norwegian cities, Oslo included, have expressed 
their interest in being an attractive city for its residents and for the city to be a meeting place in the region. 
Some even feel that developing an urban center contributes to attracting competent work force (Tennøy et. 
al. 2014).  For the city center to create a positive living environment and a lively city, services and retail 
has to be extensive. The offer of work places, housing and different culture attractions as well as other 
activities has to be available for everyone.  
As can be found in the city life survey of Oslo under chapter 4, many places that could be available for 
residing are inactive and not attractive. In the analysis done by the municipality it is concluded that only 
12 % of ground floors were welcoming and friendly. There are many places to sit if you want to go to a 
café or coffee shop, but not as many public benches. The city life survey concluded that twice as many 
people spend time at cafés, benches etc. on a Saturday than on a Tuesday. Even after this has been 
concluded Oslo still manages to place benches at inappropriate places contributing to urban spaces not 
being exploited to its full potential (Gehl Architects & Oslo Kommune, 2014). Not only are benches not 
optimally placed, the quality is not ideal, which makes the benches uncomfortable to use. For the area 
within Ring 1 to be utilized as best as possible the outskirts should be considered to a greater extent. The

7.3 Review of Interview Results 
The semi-structured qualitative interviews contributed to answer the problem statement and lead to how 
Oslo’s planning process should look in the end. The first part of the questions was about the people in the 
city and their habits. Here, everyone seemed to agree. Various cultural offers, more urban space and 
outdoor sitting places are what people come to the city for and make them spend time there, along with 
studies and work places. How people shop and where they spend time might vary at first after the closing, 
but society has a way of adapting to the changes that are being made around it. Car restrictions affect the 
composition of businesses in the city, but this does not mean there has to be any social problems. As for 
people not wanting to do their shopping because of fewer parking spaces Gustav Nielsen answered that 
what matters is what replaces the parking spaces. One car space can for instance accommodate a 
newspaper stand, a bush or a bench. Even though this does not answer the question at hand, it does 
provide alternatives for how car parking can be used and in turn provide easier access to stores for people 
traveling without a car. David Brasfield mentioned research that has been done when it comes to removing 
street parking that in most cases there is increasing resurgence of trade if there are fewer cars and that it is 
more accessible for pedestrians and cyclists. This goes back to Sustrans, (2006) and how shoppers travel. 
Again, retail owners are not aware of how many of their customers actually travel by public transport or 
bike. This also makes them overestimate the drivers, which is not as high as one would think. “The 
removal of cars, and street parking”, says Brasfield, “will not affect stores in the long run, as long as we 
keep thinking innovatively”.  
The latter part of the interview was about the planning process, how a car free city might work, and other 
measures that might be necessary or appropriate to put in place. Here, the answers differed from each 
other. Kari Eliassen, while understanding that there might be challenges sees no difficulties in the short 
time span. She is sure that with a broad involvement of businesses, organizations and private individuals, 
this will work out as planned. There is nothing to worry about when it comes to the public transport 
system, and there is little reason to believe that the increase in traffic will be so dramatic that the offer will 
be perceived as much worse for those who use it. On the other hand, Brasfield is skeptical. In spite of Oslo 
having some good examples of participatory processes and that the result of this project depends on who is 
responsible for it, he believes that the process will be very “Norwegian”, and does not expect any 
significant improvements over the use of participation, because, as he states, the planning profession has a 
basic mentality and an outdated approach. Gustav Nielsen and Hans Kristian Lingsom again agree that 
there should be an open process with all documentation public. Nielsen goes on to say that there are too 
many random choices of technical solutions for the city's traffic system and street spaces, and even though 
there are a lot of good source materials, it is an academically demanding task to put the project together in 
a good and educational manner as a basis for dialogue with the many stakeholders. 
Troels Andersen had many interesting comments about each issue, despite the fact that he was talking

7.3.1 Car Free Cities 2.0 
The main objective in three out of the five cities is not to be completely car free, but car reducing. While 
the last two places (the Garden City in Münster and Thomas B. Thriges Street in Odense) consists of 
neighborhoods and larger streets, Groningen, Gent and Oslo have been and will be reducing the amount of 
cars entering the city center. As has been clarified in chapter 3 there is a significant difference when it 
comes to car free versus car reducing cities. While car reducing cities are designed to reduce car use by for 
instance offering limited parking, a car free city offers no parking and provides a traffic free immediate 
environment (Melia, 2010). Each city prioritizes pedestrians, bicycles and public transport, some more 
than others, before motorized vehicles, and to which degree they ban cars is determined to some extent by 
size and location. 
When parking spaces are removed, it contributes to a better walking environment by attracting fewer cars 
to the center. Fewer parking spaces will encourage a higher use of public transport as well as biking. A 
better walking environment creates more activity and accessibility in the center. As mentioned earlier, 
fewer cars allows for better pedestrian and bicycle paths, more living spaces and outdoor areas for 
recreation, which in turn contributes to better air quality, enhanced prosperity, and better traffic safety.  
In chapter 4 it is indicated that people arriving by car shop more. While it is correct that car owners leave 
a larger sum of money when they first do shopping, they do not return as often as the customers that travel 
by public transport or bicycles. This result in lower spending throughout the year compared to walkers, 
cyclists and public transport travelers. In addition, when store owners in general are asked how many 
customers arrive by car, the numbers are exaggerated and often doubled in most studies. The normal is to 
overrate the customers arriving by car and underrate how many people arrive by something else (bike, 
walk, public transport etc. (Olimstad & Gjellebæk, 2015)). Given multiple studies on this, it is fair to say 
that stores are not chosen due to accessibility of parking, but rather the selection of goods and atmosphere 
of the area. Because of this it is safe to say that removing parking spaces on street level and prohibiting 
privately owned car of entering the city center will not have as much effect on shopping as presumed.  
7.4 Planning Processes 
A large increase in population is to be expected in the future. The main challenge when planning an area is 
to find a good balance between urban planning, traffic need, and effective traffic management (Norheim, 
Solli, & Haugsbø, 2014). Today’s situation is in need of an understanding between various measures used 
and the importance of where to place housing, businesses and shops. The planning of Oslo’s new area 
==PAGE:71==
Discussion 
59 
 
inside Ring Road 1 is meeting unfavorable criticism along the way. As has been mentioned previously, the 
timing of the process is described by many (ref. survey) as too ambitious and the general planning could 
be lacking certain elements to make the process as effective and efficient as possible. 
The concluding results with Oslo’s and Odense’s planning process show that the main difference was the 
time frame between the two areas opposing one another. While Thomas B. Thriges Street is to be 
transformed from a four lane highway to a multipurpose area in 12 years, Oslo is attempting to remove 
fewer cars (e.g. table 9 for a comparison of cars in the area) from a much larger area in 1/3 of that time. 
Oslo is at the planning stage of their development, meaning that the process consists mostly of paperwork 
and how to realize such a plan. It includes economic consequences, communication strategy, and plans for 
the streets, to mention a few. Hamid Shafaei Bejestani (2010) states in his article about planning processes 
that there has to be a well-defined process in a project. He refers to the time frame, limitations connected 
to time, and that providing a special fast-performing process for managing suggestions would be 
beneficial (Bejestani, 2010). Due to the fact that Oslo’s project is only in the starting phase and no one 
knows how the communication strategy will turn out or what the plans for the streets are, an idea could be 
to include traits from other processes, such as what is suggested by Bejestani. 
As is stated by Conroy & Jun (2016) a community can tackle the needed analyses and projections for an 
efficient plan in the manner the resources are driven. These resources include time, personnel, funding, 
and technology, and skills. The citizens of a certain community have to be given the right information and 
time to change their daily routines in order for a plan to be as effective as necessary. Some communities, 
Conroy & Jun continue, often face limited resources, which can lead to the process outsourcing planning 
efforts due to a lack of dedicated planning efforts. These communities are typically at a comparative 
disadvantage (Conroy & Jun, 2016). The mandate states that there will be a communication strategy put in 
place (see figure 33), and hopefully this will include a participatory process inviting not only affected 
parties, but everyone interested.  
To facilitate public participation, the method explained by Mansourian, Taleai & Fasihi (2011) increases 
the capabilities to solve urban problems that planners come across by including the general public in the 
planning process. The framework, WPUP (Web-based participatory urban planning), shows how the 
system can help residents share ideas related to the plans and help planners to implement applicable 
bottom-up and public participation decision-making theory in the urban planning process (Mansourian et 
al., 2011). Local knowledge and traditional approaches complement each other to facilitate the complex 
and multidimensional design of a planning process. This could be used as a way to enhance the Oslo 
model. By bringing together knowledge from the residents and a system for them to contribute with ideas 
the planning process could be upgraded. The method could also be combined with a non-comprehensive 
planning approach discussed by Salah Ouf (2007) in his article. Due to rapid development recent 
theoretical developments and past planning models have not been discovered or not approved, forcing 
planners to invent new approaches for specific context (Salah Ouf, 2007). 
A last idea is the scenario planning tool, as described by Chakraborty & McMillan (2015). By identifying 
new insights participants can analyze outcomes of present-day decisions, which can be especially useful 
when working with uncertainties. The outcome of any plan is determined by how the future might unfold.

The scenario planning tool allows users to plan according to past, present and future, and given past 
experiences, planning in the most efficient way possible (Chakraborty & McMillan, 2015). This, along

planning process.  
8 Conclusion and Recommendation 
There is no question that the world is expanding and we need to take care of the planet. More and more 
cities are banning cars, and developing sustainable, climate friendly city centers and lowering GHG 
emission with it. Oslo is no different and intends to ban private cars in the city center within four years. 
This is a relatively short timeframe and it is important that the implementation of this ban happens as 
effective and efficient as possible, all the while, being flexible when needed. Throughout this report the 
importance of having a successful planning process has been analyzed and discussed. Alongside looking 
at four very different cities, scientific articles and previously conducted surveys have contributed to an 
extensive analysis of possible changes to the planning process that can be beneficial to Oslo’s area within 
Ring Road 1 and its residents.  
It is evident from the conducted survey and case studies that emphasis on communication between the 
municipality and the inhabitants is key to a successful execution of such a project. The citizens of Oslo are 
not fully aware of what will happen and with the planned short time-frame it does not seem as if the 
municipality has sufficient time for implementation of a car free city center. Without the residents on their 
side, the realization of a downtown without private cars might be extremely challenging. Although the 
mandate represents a thorough process, if the communication aspect is not put in place very early on there 
will be many delays along the way. A strategy is expected to see light by the end of June 2016. This 
should include some drastic measures if the City Council would like considerable participation, as less 
than half asked here would like to participate if meetings were available. Again, 148 respondents are not 
statistically representative for the 600 000 residents of Oslo and hopefully more are interested in the topic, 
but the immediate tendency seems to be negative.  
The research question, stating “which overarching planning process can Oslo use and develop as its own 
process towards the city becoming car free?” has been investigated, along with whether Oslo could 
include parts of other cities’ planning process in their own, and what alternatives these could be. While 
Oslo does have a plan and a process of how to become car free, the city is very early in the development. 
This gives the municipality the opportunity to look at other cities and their implementations. There is not 
one planning process Oslo can incorporate as its own by itself, but rather parts of many. Looking at 
various planning processes can help the City Council achieve the overall goal they have in mind. By 
researching cities that are not merely interested in reducing cars, but also wants to reconnect the city to the 
people, the City Council will have a better grasp at how to implement certain elements when doing the 
same. Using information about earlier implementation and past planning processes are essential. By 
looking at planning processes from the past and up to today one can identify threats and risks that could be 
imperative for the future. The scenario planning tool, together with knowledge and traditional approaches 
prevail a multidimensional planning process designed to reach the goal of Oslo – version 2.0. 
As stated in their mandate and planning process there will be upcoming plans for some assorted streets. It 
should be noted that Gent has yearly closing of blocks, attracting neighbors down for a celebration of the 
streets without cars. This measure could be welded together with the measure in Münster of how residents 
are not allowed to move into neighborhoods with cars. As described by one of the interviewees, this will 
be a long process, but when reached the end there will be no cars in the area in question. The measure 
resembles Odense’s construction plans in a sense that no cars are allowed in the 51 000 m² area. The

initiative of having underground parking could also relate to Oslo’s plans of having parking outside the 
periphery, which is valuable, but without connecting transportation feeding to the main network, people 
will complain and resist the changes.  
Due to lack of flexibility that has been shown in the planning process and the fact that one deadline has 
passed, consequences will likely occur. Unpredictable shifting of deadlines may result in the project not 
finishing in the predetermined period of time and unnecessary resources used. Certain flexibility, such as 
transition between resources or changes in plans, allows the use of planning processes to adapt to 
unforeseen changes and risks, while still satisfying the end target. 
As Oslo is moving towards a new urbanization, the planning process must involve communication, 
flexibility and measures aiming for a common goal; an attractive, multidimensional and inclusive 
community.
