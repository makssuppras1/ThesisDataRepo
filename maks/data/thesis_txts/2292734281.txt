Summary (English) 
___________________________________________________________________ 
 
 
The research objective of this thesis is two-fold. The first is to ascertain 
whether naturalistic and spontaneous facial expressions differ depending on 
whether an experiment takes place in a real-world environment or a 
laboratory environment. The facial expressions in question are surprise, 
smile, dislike/disgust and valence. The concentration and attention metrics 
were omitted for the hypothesis, since presumably a controlled environment, 
will influence the participants to be more attentive and concentrated than in 
a real-world environment. 
The second research objective is to examine if eye movement patterns differ 
depending on the participants emotional arousal. The selected eye tracking 
metrics under scrutiny are all affirmed to be affected by emotional arousal 
and cognitive load in existing literature.  
In order to examine these two research objectives an experiment is carried 
out which combines facial gesture recognition and eye tracking. The 
experiment has 15 participants watch 5 specific commercials in a controlled 
environment. The facial gesture recognition data is compared to a similar 
study done by Affective under real-world conditions. The facial gesture 
recognition data is further used to segment the commercials in intervals 
based on emotional arousal defined by the smile metric and then the eye 
movement patterns are analysed to ascertain whether there is a difference 
between the neutral and aroused state. 
The experiment led to the conclusion that surprise and dislike/disgust 
complied with the stated hypothesis and smile and valence did not. Bearing 
in mind the humorous content of the commercials, the smile is the most 
significant metric in this thesis. A possible explanation is the participants 
were told that the experiment was strictly to monitor eye movement 
patterns, and thus the social pressure and expectation associated with a 
==PAGE:5==
ii___________________________________________________ 
laboratory environment and the basis for the first hypothesis became 
negligible in this context.  
Additionally the experiment led to the conclusion that pupil size showed to 
be more dilated when the participants were emotionally aroused thus 
affirming the second hypothesis. Inconsistently however the amount of 
fixations, fixation duration and gaze path length did not comply with the 
hypothesis.

1.1 Emotions in computing 
 
During the early years of cognitive psychology thinking and feeling were 
misconceived as antithetical. Thinking being the product of logic and reason 
and feelings being the product of biological impulses. A remnant of our 
ancestorРђЎs reptilian brains as first proposed by Charles Darwin  
Cognitive psychology has progressed since then, and today we know 
thoughts and feeling are mutually present in mental abilities and processes 
such as perception, judgement and reasoning. But although there is a 
relative consensus regarding what constitutes cognition the same does not 
apply to emotions. Some psychologists favor the interpretation that 
emotions are involved in the conscious and unconscious assessment of event 
(Young & Arnold, 1963) while others say emotions are inherently linked to 
drive and motivation and elicited based on rewards and punishment (Rolls, 
2009). Fully understanding how emotions and cognition coexist will be a 
big leap in fields such as neurology and psychology. 
In either case emotions are intrinsically coupled with the mind as well as 
bodily functions. According to Damasio the body and emotions play an 
essential role in thinking and decision making (Damasio, 1994).          
Emotional response to visual and auditory stimuli can be measured in 
physiological functions such as monitoring electroencephalography (EEG) 
(Machleidt, Gutjahr, & Hinrichs, 1994) respiration (Masaoka, Kanamaru, & 
Homma, 2001), perspiration (Kamei et al., 1998) and heart rate (Whitehorn, 
Kaufman, & Thomas, 1935) as well as observable non-intrusive methods 
like monitoring facial expressions, body language and vocal pitch. 
Furthermore studies have shown emotional response can be measured by 
analyzing eye properties (Lemos, Sadeghnia, ├Њlafsd├│ttir, & Jensen, 2014). 
But tracking and accurately inferring human emotions does entail 
difficulties in terms of physical appearance and arguably to a higher extent 
the cognitive processes, which help us interpret, understand and react to our 
environment. These processes are linked to our social milieu (Muza, 1994) 
as well as cultural milieu (D. McDuff, El Kaliouby, Kassam, & Picard, 
2010), which varies immensely from person to person. For instance people 
victims of a socially neglected youth can suffer from emotional problems 
(apathy, trust issues and envy etc.). Another example is Japanese people can 
==PAGE:16==
P a g e  | 3 
 
 
 
be culturally inclined to disguise overt displays of emotions in favor of a 
more socially acceptable smile. Consequently the inner cognitive processes, 
when experiencing emotions differ and thus might lead to discrepancies 
while tracking and analyzing emotional biomarkers in affective computing  
(Mandal & Ambady, 2004). For these reason, it is inherent to incorporate 
the collective individual differences across the human spectrum in affective 
computing. The advantageous would be enormous. Consider the potential in 
having a user adaptive system able to recognize and react to userРђЎs affective 
state. This could be a breakthrough in providing truly personalized user 
assistance and could also improve decision making in computing (Picard, 
2002). 
A preconceived notion is that facial gestures are solely used communicative 
when interacting with other people. Reeves & Naas conducted a series of 
studies from sociology in human-computer interaction and led to the 
conclusion people are predisposed to behave towards computers as if it were 
a person. Despite the computers not having a face or body and referring to 
itself as Рђўthis computerРђЎ instead of РђюIРђЮ. The inherently natural and social 
behavior in human-computer interaction further led to the conclusion that 
elements of normal social interaction that are enjoyable and contribute to 
productivity and positive experiences also can occur in human-computer 
interaction. Hence emotional intelligence is likely useful and important in 
human-computer interaction (Reeves & Nass, 1996) 
 
1.2 Facial gestures 
 
The face can express a wealth of information, and is one of the most affluent 
sources in communicating affective and cognitive information (Paul Ekman, 
Freisen, & Ancoli, 1980). The feat of communicating subtle emotions by 
contracting certain facial muscles like lowering an eyebrow or curling the 
nose is an innate human feature (Darwin, 1956; P. Ekman et al., 1987) and 
thus universal across all races, gender and cultures. 
The monitoring of facial expressions have been shown to be an effective 
tool in understanding non-verbal communication (Messinger, Mahoor, 
Chow, & Cohn, 2009). Facial expressions can have many different 
==PAGE:17==
P a g e  | 4 
 
 
 
applications in affective computing. For instance facial expressions have 
been shown to be effective in medical purposes such as detecting pain and 
depression or helping people with autism (Ashraf et al., 2007; Cohn et al., 
2009). Commercially the prevalent use of facial expressions are in 
advertisement, marketing and media testing in order to provide insight into 
the consumerРђЎs emotional engagement (D. McDuff, el Kaliouby, & Picard, 
2011).  
 
1.3 Eye tracking 
 
Eye tracking can reveal a lot of information about the visual system, user 
engagement and attention and has found extensive applications in research 
and commercially in fields such as advertisement, marketing and product 
design. Like when a company wants to design a product, which draws the 
attention of consumers among a series of competing products.

Chapter 2 
 
 
 
Literature Review 
___________________________________________________________________ 
 
 
This chapter will review scientific work related to the thesis objectives and 
serve as a starting point for the theoretical underpinnings of this thesis. 
 
2.1 Facial gestures in different settings 
 
Hoque & Picard conducted a study in which they created two experimental 
situations to evoke frustration and delight respectively. In the first 
experiment the participants were asked to recollect situations while 
artificially expressing either frustration or delight. In the second experiment 
the participants were provoked to elicit these affective states naturally with 
either a frustrating experience or a delightful video. The study showed 
significant differences between the naturalistic and spontaneous affective 
state and the artificially expressed affective state. For 90 percent of the 
==PAGE:19==
P a g e  | 6 
 
 
 
artificial cases the participants did not smile when frustrated and 
correspondingly for 90 percent of the naturalistic occurrences of affective 
state the participants smiled when frustrated (Hoque & Picard, 2011) 
This study and numerous others (Schmidt, Ambadar, Cohn, & Reed, 2006) 
lay claim to the notion that naturalistic and spontaneous facial behavior 
exhibited in a real-world context differs from the facial behavior exhibited 
in a laboratory setting. The unnatural setting may influence how the 
participants experience and engage with the content (D. McDuff et al., 
2011).  
Further studies have shown that external factors like an unfamiliar room, 
device or people can all influence facial behavior and that facial behavior 
response in social situations is heightened compared to nonsocial situation 
(Fridlund, 1991). This is due to the social display rules, which induce the 
participants in a laboratory setting to subconsciously control their emotional 
expressions to avoid negative consequences for the self. (Wallbott & 
Scherer, 1989)  (Friesen, 1977). Which means participants in a laboratory 
setting might feel inappropriate expressing emotions precipitated by the 
social pressure and expectations of being monitored and observed.  
 
2.2 Pupillary changes 
 
Pupillary changes are affirmed to respond to emotional arousal in visual 
stimuli. In a study by Bradley et al. the correlation between pupillary 
response and emotional arousal was examined by having 27 participants 
view pictorial stimuli of either pleasant, unpleasant or neutral content. The 
results showed that both the pleasant and unpleasant content correlated with 
pupillary changes (Bradley, Miccoli, Escrig, & Lang, 2008).  
But using the pupil diameter as a metric for emotional arousal can be 
challenging. The drawback is pupillary changes do not respond exclusively 
to emotional arousal. Dilated pupils can also be influenced by higher mental 
workload (Just & Carpenter, 1993) and constricted pupils can even be 
influenced by drowsiness and fatigue (Ukai & Howarth, 2008). Additionally 
pupillary changes are known to be responsive to luminance. A higher light 
intensity causes the pupils to constrict and in turn a lower light intensity 
causes the pupil to dilate. 
==PAGE:20==
P a g e  | 7 
 
 
 
Another complication when studying pupillary changes as a measure for 
emotional arousal is the time it takes from stimuli exposure to pupil dilation 
or constriction. It can take somewhere between 0.2 and 0.8 seconds for the 
pupillary changes to fully react and it is very individual from person to 
person (Bojko, 2013). 
 
 
 
2.3 gaze path and fixations 
 
In normal viewing many saccades can occur each second and the direction 
is subconsciously determined by cognitive processes related to internal 
reward (Platt & Glimcher, 1999). Saccade frequency is known to increase 
with the complexity of a given task (Hayhoe & Ballard, 2005). Saccades are 
interrupted by fixations, which are visual gaze on a specific location. 
Fixations are task specific and occur when information is obtained.  
Eye movement patterns and pupil dilation can reveal a great deal of 
information about the emotional and cognitive processes taking place in 
response to visual or auditory stimuli (Just & Carpenter, 1993). In a study 
conducted by Bradley et al. eye movement response was related to 
emotional and neutral pictorial stimuli with varying complexity. The study 
showed that the emotional and complex stimuli resulted in more fixations, 
shorter fixation duration and longer gaze path lengths compared to the 
neutral stimuli (Bradley, Houbova, Miccoli, Costa, & Lang, 2011). Hence 
fixations and gaze path are influenced by both cognitive workload and 
emotional arousal.

Hypotheses

Bearing in mind the preceding considerations stipulated in chapter 2, this 
thesis has two main research objectives. The first objective is to examine the 
differences in naturalistic and spontaneous facial expressions, depending on 
the whether it takes place in a laboratory environment or in a real-world 
environment. 
The second objective is to examine if there is a correlation between 
emotional arousal and the eye movement patterns affirmed to be affected 
according to the reviewed literature in section 2.2 and 2.3.  
Bearing in mind the related work and the experiment specifics two 
hypothesis can be formulated: 
 
1. РђўThe controlled environment and context in which the experiment 
takes place will influence the participants to show emotional 
restrainРђЎ 
 
2. РђўWhen the participants are in a state of emotional arousal the pupil 
size, amount of fixations, fixation duration and gaze path length are 
increasedРђЎ

Chapter 4 
 
 
 
Tools 
___________________________________________________________________ 
 
 
The various hardware and software tools used to conduct the experiments 
are chosen based on their compatibility, accessibility, functionality as well 
as affordability.  
In order to record the participantРђЎs facial expressions and eye tracking 
patterns, these tools were required to work accurately and synchronically in 
real-time. 
 
4.1 The Affectiva Affdex Software Development Kit 
 
The participants are recorded using a Logitech HD webcam model C525, 
with a resolution of 1280 x 720 pixel. The facial expressions and emotions 
are processed and analyzed in real-time using emotion recognition 
algorithms (classifiers) provided by the Affectiva Affdex Software 
Development Kit (SDK) v2.0.  
==PAGE:23==
P a g e  | 10 
 
 
 
According to Affectiva, the classifiers have been trained and tested on more 
than 3.2 million faces across 75 countries, which amount to over 12 billion 
emotion data points. 
As illustrated in figure 4.1.1 the Affdex SDK can identify and measure 15 
facial expressions and 7 emotions excluding valence and engagement, 
which are categorized under emotions for readability. 
 
 
Figure 4.1.1. Range of measureable facial expressions and emotions by the Affdex SDK 
2.0 
 
The facial data has been captured with a frame rate of 30 Frames per second 
(FPS) opposed to 60 FPS, since ironically initial testing revealed that 
lowering the framerate would yield more processed frames and thus provide 
more facial data to analyze. 
The reason is the Affdex SDK while working concurrently with the eye 
tracker consumes more processing power the higher the frame rate. Hence 
lowering the frame rate will free up processing power used in analyzing, 
managing and storing the frames. 
  
 
==PAGE:24==

4.2 The Eye Tribe Tracker and SDK 
 
The Eye Tribe tracker is a non-intrusive and easy to use eye tracking device. 
The Eye Tribe tracker uses infrared illumination to determine the pupil 
corneal reflection, which is used to calculate the position relative to the 
screen and the eye gaze characteristics. 
The Eye Tribe SDK v0.9.56 is used to obtain the eye tracking patterns in 
real-time. The eye tracking patterns are captured with a frame rate of 30 
frames per second (FPS) opposed to 60, for the reasons stated in the 
previous section, with the additional benefit of offering more reliably in 
capturing the data.  
 
4.3 Miscellaneous software and technology specifics 
 
The Affdex SDK and the Eye Tribe SDK are integrated to work 
synchronically in a Windows Forms application using Visual Studio 2013. 
The application is implemented using C# and .NET Framework 4.5 
Data management is implemented using a Python v2.7 plugin in Visual 
Studio 2013 and the data analysis and visualizations are produced using 
JMP and Microsoft Excel.

Chapter 5 
 
 
 
The Experiment 
___________________________________________________________________ 
 
 
This chapter contains the experiment specifics, which defines the frame in 
which the hypotheses are evaluated. Overall this thesis has a single 
experiment in which participants passively view 5 commercials, while being 
monitored by an eye tracker and facial gesture recognition system. The 
commercials used in this experiment are identical to the commercials used 
in the Affdex website demo. The facial behaviour recognition data the 
Affdex demo is based on, is collected by crowdsourcing data through a 
website, which means the participants were likely monitored when alone 
and in a familiar and natural setting and thus the participants were not 
influenced by social pressure and expectations. 
To compare the real-world setting with the laboratory setting, this 
experiment sets out to replicate the facial gesture recognition results from 
the Affdex related to the 6 emotional parameters included in the Affdex 
website demo, namely surprise, smile, concentration, dislike, valence and 
attention. Affectiva has a seventh expression labelled expressiveness present 
==PAGE:26==
P a g e  | 13 
 
 
 
on their demo site. But the expressiveness feature was removed in the 
Affdex SDK v2.0, and thus this feature was omitted from this thesis. 
Additionally concentration has been relabeled brow furrow and dislike has 
been relabeled disgust. 
 
5.1 Methods and setup 
 
This section clarifies the experiment specifics and frame within how the 
experiment was conducted. 
5.1.1 Theoretical Consideration 
 
To limit the interference of emotional restrain on the facial expressions, the 
participants are informed that the purpose of the experiment is to monitor 
eye movement patterns, leaving out the facial gesture part. This way the 
participants are not subconsciously aware that their facial behavior is being 
monitored and thus are more likely to act naturally. 
Furthermore in the interest of keeping the facial behavior as naturalistic as 
possible, it is inherent that the participants are left alone while the 
experiment is underway. 
A problem which might occur in within-subject design is the interference of 
carryover effects like emotional bias and associations. To minimize these 
effects the commercials used in the study are shown in a randomized order. 
Prior to playing each commercial, there is approximately a 30 second 
latency while the webcam and eye tracker are initialized followed by a 5 
second count down in the centre of the screen. This adds a natural break in-
between the commercials and helps to alleviate fatigue (Bojko, 2013).

The stimulus is comprised of 5 specific super bowl commercials, which are

5.1.3 Participants 
 
15 participants were included in this study, out of which 12 were male and 3 
were female. The participants were in the ages between 26 and 30, with an 
average age of 28.5. 
==PAGE:28==
P a g e  | 15 
 
 
 
The presented stimuli was unknown to the participants, since the self-report 
questionnaire revealed that none of the participants had watched any of the 
commercials before. 
 
5.1.4 Preparation 
 
Before conducting the experiments the participants were instructed to 
undergo a calibration procedure on the maximum of 16 screen locations, 
which ensured an accurate gaze estimation.  
All the participants had to receive a calibration rating that exceeded 
moderate to be included in this study. 5 test subjects scored lower 
calibration results and were subsequently not included.  
The reason these participants scored low calibration results was not 
examined in depth, but since all the experiments were conducted under 
uniform conditions, the poor results were presumably caused by exterior 
factors such as unsuitable eye characteristics or droopy eye lids. For 
instance two of the five test subjects wore contact lenses during the 
experiment and one has poor eye sight and couldnРђЎt achieve a satisfying 
calibration result with or without the glasses. 
 
5.1.5 The environment 
 
The experiments were conducted in a controlled environment under uniform 
conditions. A darkened room with a single light source at a specific location 
and set at a specific luminosity. 
The experiments were conducted on a 24┬┤ inch monitor, which is the largest 
possible screen size compatible with the Eye Tribe tracker. The eye tracker 
and camera position were adjusted to the participantРђЎs height, so the position 
was centered while keeping the participants at an approximate distance of

Chapter 7 
 
 
 
Conclusion 
___________________________________________________________________ 
 
 
In order to clarify the Influence the environment has on facial expressions 
the Affdex experiment results were compared to this experiment. According 
to the hypothesis the emotional metrics should yield less emotional 
probability compared to the Affdex experiment. An exception to the 
hypothesis was the attention and concentration, which were presumed to 
yield a higher emotional response. This is because in a controlled setting 
participants would presumably feel compelled to be attentive and focus on 
the visual stimuli whereas in the real-world environment the participants did 
not feel the same social pressure. The presumption proved correct and the 
results showed that the emotional probability of concentration and attention 
was undoubtedly higher for all the commercials. 
The visual stimuli was humorous in nature and the emotional metrics 
surprise and dislike/disgust did not contain any significant negative or 
positive fluctuations in emotional probability. This can arguably be caused 
by the stimuliРђЎs lack of content able to trigger surprise and dislike/disgust. 
However in the case of surprise however a possible explanation is the 
surprise expression is more masked and thus harder to detect in facial 
behavior recognition. In any case both surprise and disgust/dislike had a 
==PAGE:47==
P a g e  | 34 
 
 
 
generally lower emotional probability throughout all the commercials 
compared to the Affdex demo and were thus in concordance with the 
hypothesis. 
For the smile metric 3 out of 5 commercials failed to comply with the 
hypothesis. A possible explanation could be the participants were told that 
the purpose of the experiment was to strictly track eye movement patterns. 
Which inadvertently causes the participants to be relieved of the social 
pressure and expectations associated with a laboratory environment. The 
valence metric was uninteresting for the scope of this thesis, since given the 
lack of negative content in the commercials, the valence metric was 
proportionate with the smile metric. 
The participants were instructed to fill out a self-report questionnaire after 
each commercial. The purpose was to examine if the participants smile 
probability was higher based on if they liked the commercial or would be 
willing to watch it again. Both answers yielded the same result. The 
participants that liked the commercials and the participants that were willing 
to watch the commercial again were generally more likely to smile than the 
other segments. However the results also showed that the participants that 
did not like the commercial and the participants that wouldnРђЎt like to watch 
it again were generally more inclined to smile than the participants that 
liked the commercial somewhat and the participants that would maybe 
watch the commercial again. 
The second hypothesis relied on reviewed literature which affirmed pupil 
size, amount of fixations, fixations duration and length of gaze path were 
increased in pictorial stimuli when participants are in a state of emotional 
arousal compared to a neutral state. The objective was to apply these eye 
movement features to video. The commercials were segmented into 
intervals of equal duration of aroused content and neutral content.  
In accordance with the hypothesis the pupil size was presumed more dilated 
when the participants were aroused. The results showed that 4 out of 5 
commercials complied with the hypothesis. Coincidentally the commercial 
that didnРђЎt, had a comparably small smile probability mean difference in the 
aroused intervals and neutral intervals. This leads to the conclusion that 
pupil size can in fact be used as an indicator of emotional arousal in video. 
However using pupil size as such has drawbacks, the pupil is reactive to 
several other factors including scene complexity, luminosity and fatigue to 
name a few. 
The amount of fixations, fixation duration and gaze length were presumed 
to be increased when the participants were emotionally aroused. The results 
==PAGE:48==
P a g e  | 35 
 
 
 
showed that this was not the case. On the contrary for these eye movement 
characteristics the results showed to have the opposite effect. In 4 out of 5 
commercials the fixation duration and amount of fixations were smaller 
when the participants were in an emotional state. In 3 out of 5 commercials 
the gaze lengths were smaller when the participants were in an emotional 
state. The reason is the nature of the stimuli being based on video, in which 
the scenes being presented undoubtedly has a larger effect on these eye 
characteristics than the emotional arousal. 
Based on the preceding results the first hypothesis is questionable and 
inconclusive within the frame in which it was conducted. The second 
hypothesis revealed some interesting results regarding pupil dilation, but 
unfortunately given all the uncertainties regarding the stimuli such as scene 
complexity and context that emotional arousal was negligible and thus did 
not reveal any interesting results regarding the remaining eye movement 
characteristics.
