Abstract 
Phage display is a powerful tool that allows in vitro selection of antibody fragments targeting 
a desired antigen. This study employed the use of the Predator phage display library to select 
for human nanobodies targeting surface components of the probiotic gram-positive 
Lactobacillus reuteri DSM17938. Clones were selected by panning the library against isolated 
cell surface-exposed antigen, a collagen-binding protein (CnBP), previously identified by 
Goletz group as a good target through whole-genome sequencing and proteomics studies. 
Another suitable antigen was identified in the present thesis, a homologue to a mucus binding 
protein (MUB), through analysis of the proteomic and whole-cell NGS data sets. MUB was 
chosen due to its accessibility, high cell surface abundance and its unlikeliness to compromise 
the viability of L. reuteri DSM17938 when bound by nanobodies. The exposed part of MUC 
not anchored in the cell wall was cloned, expressed and partially purified. Previously selected 
nanobody-phage clones against CnBP were further characterized, and seven nanobodies were 
subcloned and expressed. Further work is needed to sufficiently purify and characterize the 
subcloned candidates for more detailed characterization, and selection against bacterial cells 
needs to be optimized.

1. Introduction 
Antibodies (Abs) are proteins that play a crucial role in the immune system. They are engaged 
in the opsonization of pathogens and malignant cells and neutralization of toxins, by binding 
to their respective targets, known as antigens. The exact part of the antigen recognized for 
binding by Abs is called the epitope. Most Abs are made of heavy and light chains which 
include variable and constant regions; the antigen is recognized/bound by the variable regions.  
 
Abs are increasingly being used for clinical purposes such as in cancer therapy and against 
infectious diseases like HIV (1). They are also used in research and have vast applications 
outside of therapy; however, they are difficult to express in non-mammalian cells (2), making 
their production costly (For therapeutic application mammalian expression systems are 
required). One strategy has been to trim the Abs by removing most of the constant regions, 
reducing the need for mammalian post-translational modifications. This limitation has also 
been overcome by the discovery of the camelid Abs. The heavy chain variable domain of 
camelid Abs (VHH), also termed nanobody, is a highly soluble and stable protein and is easily 
expressed in E. coli. Its smaller size compared to the full-length human Abs makes it an 
interesting therapeutic candidate, also, due to its ability to access less exposed epitopes (2). 
Despite its smaller size, the camelid VHH is capable of high affinity towards antigens and is 
considered to be safe for human administration due to its close homology to the human variable 
heavy chain domain (VH) (2). Despite the vast therapeutic potential of camelid VHHs, 
nanobodies sourced from humans are desired for intravenous therapy and human 
administration, to reduce the risk of immunogenicity. A suitable nanobody scaffold substitute 
has been found by Jespers et al. 2004. They discovered a single domain ab, which they named 
HEL4. It is a human-derived VH domain with properties similar to camelid VHH (3).   
 
Despite its suitability, the HEL4 scaffold has been modified by Mandrup et al. 2013. They 
performed an I29D substitution in order to improve its solubility. HEL4  is composed of three 
complementary determining regions (CDR) which determine antigen-binding specificity. They 
also changed the length of CDR3 from 11 to 7 residues. And, the end of CDR3 was substituted 
with the sequence FDY as this is a reoccurring motif in humans. This modified HEL4 based 
nanobody was used to construct a phage display library, called Predator, by the same. Phage 
display is a technology that allows the isolation of a nanobody and its gene content in parallel; 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
A phage has a nanobody associated with one of its coat proteins while carrying the gene of this 
fusion protein in its chromosome. Based partly on the amino acid frequencies they applied 
within each position of the CDRs that were randomized, in construction of the library; the 
diversity has been estimated with confidence to be of 6.2 x 10^7 unique nanobody-phage 
clones, each displaying different CDR sequences (4). Furthermore, the library was designed to 
be highly modular with the introduction of unique restriction sites framing all the CDR’s (4), 
increasing the plasticity of this nanobody. 
 
The aim of this thesis is to isolate and characterize HEL4 based nanobodies specifically binding 
to antigens on the surface of the probiotic Lactobacillus reuteri DSM17938 using the Predator 
phage display library. Based on proteomics data (generated by Goletz group), a candidate 
surface-exposed antigen named F8DLY8, was identified and cloning, expression and 
purification were attempted for future phage display analysis. Furthermore, another L. reuteri 
surface antigen known as CnBP— which was found and reported previously by Goletz group 
— was used for further phage display analysis and ELISA screenings of previously identified 
VH-phage clones. Candidate VH genes, selected against CnBP and L. reuteri were subcloned 
and seven were expressed and screened on whole-cell ELISA.

2. Background 
 
2.1 Overview of Lactobacillus reuteri 
L. reuteri is a gram-positive and rod-shaped bacteria that belongs to a genus of beneficial 
bacteria. It plays a major role in human health by producing antimicrobial compounds, such as 
organic acid, reuterin, reutericyclin, and ethanol, which can inhibit pathogen colonization and 
affect host microbiota composition (5). Furthermore, it has been shown that L. reuteri can 
reduce pro-inflammatory cytokine production and promote regulatory T-cell maturation, 
thereby alleviating autoimmune diseases, in porcine and mice, respectively (6). It has also been 
shown that L. reuteri decreases the microbial movement from the intestinal lumen to the lining 
tissue by strengthening the intestinal barrier (6). As such, several strains of L. reuteri can 
withstand the low pH conditions of the stomach and small intestine, caused by gastric acids 
and bile salts, respectively (7,8). It is well tolerated in humans as prior research has shown that 
a dose of 2.9 x 109 CFU can be safely administered (9). Overall, this microbe lives up to all the 
requirements of a probiotic such as being able to establish in environments that have low pH 
and are enzyme abundant, to establish an interaction with the host by epithelial adherence, to 
compete with pathogens, and of course, being safe towards the host (6).  
 
L reuteri has many health benefits for the hosts they inhabit. However, its abundance has seen 
a reduction during recent decades, within humans. Lifestyle changes experienced during these 
decades have been suggested as the cause. Such as the emergence of antibiotics, changes in 
diet, and better hygiene. The reduction in L. reuteri correlates with the higher occurrences of 
inflammatory diseases. Overall, Humans are benefitted by administrating L. reuteri as a 
probiotic supplement (6).  
 
2.1.1 L. reuteri substance production 
L. reuteri produces several health-promoting metabolites such as reuterin, also known as 3-
hydroxypropionaldehyde (3-HPA). 3-HPA is produced from glycerol under anaerobic 
conditions (10) and mediated by a vitamin B12 dependent reaction (6). 3-HPA is found as a 
mixture including its hydrate, dimer, and acrolein (11); This mixture is found to inhibit both 
gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria (12), and L. reuteri is distinctive in its ability to 
produce and secrete large amounts of reuterin, among lactic acids bacteria (13). This likely 
gives L. reuteri, a significant effect on the microbiota it inhabits.  
==PAGE:11==
4 
 
Several strains of L. reuteri can produce vitamin B12, including CRL1098 (14) and JCM1112 
(15). Thus, L. reuteri can act as a dietary supplement because it has been shown that the oral 
administration of L. reuteri CRL1098 could improve the side effects connected with B12 
deficiency in pregnant mice (and their offspring) exposed to a diet lacking B12. Such as 
improve weight loss for the females and growth delay in their offspring (16).  
 
Besides, some L. reuteri can produce histamine from L-histidine. This compound has been 
shown to suppress the production of the pro-inflammatory tumour necrosis factor (TNF), in 
humans, as such L. reuteri is involved in intestinal immunomodulation (17). L. reuteri also 
produces tryptophan catabolites derivatives that bind the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR). 
Binding the Ahr of innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) induces IL-22 production, in mice (18). In 
return, IL-22 secretion can promote the host’s defence system against extracellular pathogens 
(19). These derivatives are also believed to promote the formation of CD4+ CD8aa+ 
intraepithelial lymphocytes (DP-IELs) by binding to the AhRs of CD4+ T-cells, in mice. DP-
IELs are believed to be involved in oral tolerance (20). It has been reported that AhRs are 
expressed by various immune cells (21), thus, further emphasizing L. reuteri’s 
immunomodulatory effects. 
 
2.1.1.1 L. reuteri exopolysaccharides in health 
Exopolysaccharide (EPS) production by L. reuteri is crucial for its biofilm formation and 
adherence to epithelial surfaces (22). Furthermore, it confers several benefits to the colonized 
host.  L. reuteri produced EPS can block subsequent adherence of enterotoxigenic E. coli 
(ETEC), which causes diarrhea, towards Intestinal Porcine Epithelial Cell line-1, in vitro (23). 
This blocking also suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine gene expression, which includes IL-
1b and IL-6, that would otherwise be expressed during ETEC infection (23). The same has 
been observed in piglets, where L. reuteri EPS prevented ETEC induced diarrhea by preventing 
ETEC adhesion (24). It has also been shown that EPS from wild type L. reuteri 100-23 in a 
lactobacillus-free mouse model can induce splenic Foxp3+ regulatory T-cells (Treg). Tregs 
maintain tolerance to self-antigens, thereby preventing autoimmune disease. A mutation in the 
fructosyl transferase (frf) gene of L. reuteri 100-23, halted its EPS production, and no Treg 
cells were induced in the model (25). This highlights the potential of using L. reuteri 100-23 
in the treatment of Treg deficiency (6). Lastly, EPS from L. reuteri mh-001 has shown to reduce

secretion of TNF-alpha from murine macrophages emphasizing the immunomodulatory effects 
of EPS (26). 
 
2.1.2 Effects of L. reuteri on the microbiota 
L. reuteri influences the different microbiotas it inhabits, in terms of their diversity, 
composition, and metabolic function (6). Microbiota perturbations have been correlated to 
many diseases (27) such as cystic fibrosis, bacterial vaginosis and peptic ulcer disease (caused 
by Helicobacter pylori infection), and L. reuteri has proven to be essential for promoting a 
healthy flora and combating these diseases. The dysbiosis within the gut microbiota, 
experienced in those diagnosed with cystic fibrosis, can be partially rescued by L. reuteri DSM 
17938 administration; which reduces the density of the γ-Proteobacteria phylum and increases 
firmicute representation, consistent with a healthy flora (28). Within the oral/subgingival 
microbiota, L. reuteri treatment decreased the number of periodontal pathogens (29). At the 
vaginal microbiota, lactobacilli constitute most of the flora in healthy women (30), and it has 
been shown that oral administration of L. reuteri RC-14 and L. rhamnosus GR-1, for 14 days, 
can partially restore the vaginal flora in postmenopausal women who have Nugent scores 
between 5-6 (indicates bacterial vaginosis) (31). L. reuteri is also efficient against GI bacterial 
infections caused by H. pylori. It has been shown to increase the eradication rate of intestinal 
H. pylori (32), and suggested that L. reuteri blocks H. pylori colonization by inhibiting its 
binding to a glycolipid receptor (33). 
Previously it has been concluded, that oral administration of L. reuteri induces the gut 
microbiota to produce inosine, in scurfy mice (who suffer from a lethal autoimmune disease) 
(34). Inosine is known to reduce the number of effector T cells, known as type 1 helper T cells 
(Th1) and type 2 helper T cells (Th2) (34), involved in fighting viral/bacterial and parasitic 
infection respectively. Consequently, the decrease in effector T cells led to a decrease in 
inflammatory cytokines such as IFN-gamma (34). Oral administration of human L. reuteri 
DSM17938 strain to scurfy mice resulted in increased lifespan for the mouse, caused gut 
microbiota remodelling and lessened its multiorgan inflammation (34), as such L. reuteri has 
anti-inflammatory properties when it is present in the gut microbiota. Also, Lactobacillus has 
been found to enhance anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 and inhibit pro-inflammatory IL-6. 
These benefits were observed for female mice and castrated males only (35). Indicating an 
interaction between gut microbiota and sex hormones in the development of autoimmune 
disease (35). 
==PAGE:13==

Lastly, it has been stated that stressor exposure leads to a change in the composition of the gut 
microbiota (36). For example, Mackos et al. 2016, showed that social stress in mice leads to an 
increase in Citrobacter rodentium colonization, resulting in gut inflammation. Interestingly, L. 
reuteri ATCC 23272 administration could reverse colitis (37). Overall, these studies highlight 
that, microbiota perturbations/dysbiosis seen within many diseases can be rescued by L. reuteri. 
 
2.1.3 L. reuteri effect on the intestinal barrier 
L. reuteri has been found to strengthen the intestinal barrier. When the permeability of the 
intestinal barrier increases due to changes in mucins, antimicrobial compounds, IgA Abs, 
cytokines, and/or tight junction (tj) proteins (found as linkers between the lining cells), it is 
referred to as a “leaky gut” (38). Lupus prone mice suffer from an autoimmune disease that is 
promoted by leaky gut; mice with a genetic predisposition to autoimmune diseases are triggered 
when leaky gut allows environmental factors to enter the body, provoking immune responses. 
When these mice had a mixture of lactobacillus species including L. reuteri, administered 
orally, it resulted in increased expression of tj protein ZO-1 and possibly a mucin protein Muc2, 
thereby strengthening the intestinal mucosal barrier, reducing permeability and thus 
inflammation (35). A significant reduction in the amount of Lactobacillaceae (the family L. 
reuteri belong to) in the gut was observed in lupus-prone female mice compared to healthy 
controls (39). And they experienced a prolonged life, reduced serum autoantibodies and 
improved renal function when their Lactobacillales (the order L. reuteri belong to) gut content 
was increased. An uncultured Lactobacillus sp. and L. reuteri was responsible for most of the 
effects (35). L. reuteri has also been shown to increases the amount of secreted IgA (typically 
found at the mucosa), in rats. This is done in a vitamin A-dependent manner (40), thereby 
contributing to the intestinal immunological barrier.  
 
Several L. reuteri strains can maintain barrier integrity of the pig intestine, by affecting tj 
protein expression. One study showed that L. reuteri can increase the abundance of the tj 
protein ZO-1 (41) enhancing the interaction between the lining cells of the gut. Another study 
showed that L. reuteri was able to prevent the disruption of ZO-1 by enterotoxigenic E. Coli 
(ETEC) (42), maintaining the interaction between cells in the face of pathogens.  
 
L. reuteri’s ability to reduce intestinal permeability has been observed in humans as well. The 
intestinal mucosal barrier of children with atopic dermatitis (pathogenesis involves leaky gut) 
==PAGE:14==
7 
 
was stabilized with treatment using a consortium of L. reuteri DSM12246 and L. rhamnosus 
19070-2. The children experienced fewer symptoms, such as vomiting, diarrhea, and/or 
abdominal pain post-treatment (43). Thus L. reuteri colonization of the intestine is crucial for 
intestinal barrier integrity.  
 
2.1.4 Role of L. reuteri in obesity 
L. reuteri can have roles in energy uptake as well. Some L. reuteri strains have been associated 
with obesity, and some with weight loss (44,45). A previous study has confirmed that, in 
humans, the microbiota between lean and obese individuals differ, with regards to the relative 
abundance of certain genera. They collected fecal samples from obese and lean children and 
subjected them to qPCR in order to determine the concentrations of species from the different 
genera. Their study showed high concentrations of lactobacilli in the microbiota of obese 
children (46). L. reuteri GMNL-263 is recognized as a healthy food in Taiwan and has been 
shown to reduce insulin resistance (insulin resistance cause weight gain). It was shown that 
once it is orally administrated to obese rats, it reduces their body weight. Also, a reduction in 
their adipose tissue and liver weight was observed (47). Similar results were achieved with 
heat-killed L. reuteri GMNL-263 (47). This indicates that some L. reuteri produce molecules 
that play a significant role in the body’s energy uptake, in humans and rats. 
 
2.1.5 Role of L. reuteri in behavior 
L. reuteri can influence behavior in humans and mice. Children who were exposed to maternal 
obesity during pregnancy, are reported to have an increased likelihood of developing 
neurodevelopmental disorders, such as autism spectrum disorder (48). The same observation 
was monitored in mice as it has been shown that a maternal high-fat diet (MHFD) can induce 
offspring with social deficits (49). It was shown that more than a nine-fold reduction of L. 
reuteri occurs in the gut microbiome of offspring whose mothers were subjected to MHFD 
compared to that of maternal regular diet (MRD). The social deficits were rescued by the 
administration of L. reuteri (49). Social deficits were concluded based on interaction time 
between pairs of unfamiliar mice. Interaction time increased with L. reuteri administration. 
 
L. reuteri’s ability to enhance oxytocin levels —a peptide hormone and a neuropeptide— is 
linked to neurodevelopment (49). Thus, in the absence of L. reuteri; synaptic maturation in the 
==PAGE:15==
8 
 
ventral tegmental area dependent on L. reuteri enhanced oxytocin is retarded (49), which 
affects behavior.  
 
2.1.6 Effects of L. reuteri on the nervous system 
The enteric nervous system (ENS) is affected by the intestinal microbiota and L. reuteri 
(50,51). L. reuteri can reduce ENS activity and prevent visceral pain response associated with 
colorectal distension pressure in mice (50) and heat-killed or gamma-irradiated L. reuteri had 
a similar effect (50). Strains of this probiotic can also produce the important inhibitory 
neurotransmitter of the central nervous system, gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) (51,52). In 
rats, L. reuteri can increase neuron excitability by reducing the activation threshold, increase 
the number of action potential per depolarizing pulse, and decrease the opening of calcium-
dependent potassium channels which leads to a decrease in the afterhyperpolarization of 
sensory neurons and thereby decreasing the refractory period (53). Thus, L. reuteri has a 
significant influence on neuronal activity, in mice and rats.

2.2 Overview of Antibodies  
Conventional human Abs are Y shaped protein complexes (Figure 1) and can be found further 
complexed together into larger structures. These Y shaped protein complexes are composed of 
two heavy chains and two light chains. Each light chain is linked to a heavy chain through a 
disulfide bond, and the two heavy chains are linked together through two disulfide bonds. Each 
heavy chain has three constant domains termed CH1, CH2, and CH3 and one variable domain 
(VH), while each light chain has one constant domain (CL) and one variable domain (VL). The 
region of an ab, which is engaged in the antigen-binding, is referred to as a paratope; it contains 
complementary determinant regions (CDR) that determine antigen specificity and is located on 
the Abs variable domains. Each one of the four variable domains has three CDRs. Therefore, 
each arm of the assembled ab has one paratope composed of six CDRs, three from VL, and 
three from VH (54,55). VL and VH domains share a hydrophobic interface and therefore, 
cannot be expressed as monomers (3). Thus, each paratope has contributions from both a heavy 
and a light chain. 
 
Heavy-chain only Abs (hcAbs) occur naturally in camelid ungulates and cartilaginous fishes. 
Each arm from these Abs carries a single variable (heavy) domain with a paratope composed 
of only three CDRs. It has been proposed that these hcAbs are products of converging 
evolution, that have occurred at least three times (55,56). The heavy chain variable region in 
camelid IgG are referred to as VHH, and that of shark immunoglobulin is referred to as VNAR 
(New antigen receptor) (55), (Figure 1). Single domain Abs/nanobodies are engineered from 
these hcAbs and constitute the VHH or VNAR regions. VHH is highly soluble compared to 
the VH fragment of conventional Abs. Hydrophilic substitutions at L11S, V37Y, G44E, L45R, 
W47G/I, and W103R/G have partly explained the highly soluble nature of VHH (3). In 
addition, the CDR3 loop of VHHs contains approximately 17 residues —longer than the human 
CDR3 loop of 12 residues— and folds back onto the framework region 2 (FR2). FR2 
corresponds to the VH:VL interphase in conventional Abs, within which it is hydrophobic 
(3,55). VHH can undergo reversible denaturation, even at high concentrations such as 100 µM, 
and after extended heat incubation between 80 to 92° C (3). Thus, it is not aggregation-prone 
and can be expressed as a monomer.

2.2.1 Nanobodies; epitopes, paratopes, and cross-strain neutralization. 
Nanobodies target different epitopes compared to that of conventional Abs. This is due to their 
differences in the paratope surface area and paratope diameter, which are significantly smaller 
for nanobodies (55). The paratope surface area of nanobodies is convex —can be flat as well— 
while for conventional Abs,  it is usually flat or concave (55,57). Studies on α-amylase (58), 
lysozyme (59) and urokinase (60), have shown that VHH target the recessed active sites of 
these enzymes, by penetrating the active site pocket with one of their CDR loops. It has been 
hypothesized that VHHs can access a cryptic epitope conserved on the variant surface 
glycoproteins of Trypanosoma brucei, more efficiently due to their smaller size. Conventional 
Abs were unable to bind to this epitope (61). Overall, it seems that the small size of nanobodies 
makes them preferable to access areas of pores, channels, grooves, and cavities, unlike 
conventional Abs (55). 
 
 
Cross-strain neutralization efficacy differs between nanobodies and conventional Abs. For 
example, when the larger conventional Abs associate with the conserved residues of the histo-
blood group antigen (HBGA) (HBGA is a glycan located on host cells) binding pockets of the 
norovirus, the surrounding variable residues of this binding pocket make these Abs strain-
==PAGE:18==
11 
 
specific because these residues need to align up as well during the association (62). Therefore, 
it has been suggested that a smaller nanobody paratope surface area may lead to greater cross-
reactivity by occluding the need to associate with the variable strain-specific regions (62). For 
instance, the smaller VHHs have been able to neutralize many different genotypes of norovirus 
VP1 by binding to the conserved capsid residues (63). This has been observed with an HIV-1 
glycoprotein trimer as well, which is part of the HIV-1 envelope. HIV-1 cross-strain 
neutralization is time-consuming and difficult to achieve with conventional Abs, whereas it can 
be easily achieved with camelid heavy chain only Abs (55). As a result, nanobodies are more 
suitable for cross-strain neutralization. 
 
Gly, Tyr, and Ser are frequent in the paratopes of VHH, VNAR, and conventional Abs. These 
paratopes engage in non-covalent interactions with their antigens through the formation of salt 
bridges and H-bonds. Though conventional Abs have larger paratope surface area, nanobodies 
are more enriched/concentrated for these interactions per loop (55). Despite being enriched for 
the same amino acids, these respective paratopes bear no sequence homology (55). 
 
The efficacy of binding small molecules differs between conventional Abs and nanobodies. 
Conventional Abs form groves\binding pockets between the VL and VH chains to 
accommodate the small molecules (64), whereas nanobodies cannot. However, despite the 
convex nature of the nanobody paratope; some studies have shown nanobodies to be able to 
bind small molecules as well, though so far, no hapten specific VNAR has been reported (55). 
Overall, conventional Abs are better suited to bind small molecules. 
 
Nanobodies are found to target many different types of macromolecules. One VHH has been 
found, to target a Neisseira meningitidis lipopolysaccharide (endotoxin) (65), and a minimum 
of two VHH’s have been found to bind glycoproteins (61,66). Though so far, no nanobodies 
have been found to bind lipids or nucleic acids (55). 
 
2.2.2 Human derived nanobodies  
When nanobodies are to be administered to humans, it is desired to have them from a human 
source. Camelization, the process of endowing human VH with properties of VHH, has been 
attempted by changing the amino acid positions of 44, 45, 47 in FR2 of VH to those found in 
VHH. This resulted in a more soluble VH; however, expression was reduced, thermodynamic 
==PAGE:19==
12 
 
stability was compromised, and some aggregation was still observed (3). The solubility of 
human VH has been improved by extending the CDR3 loop, also (3). That said, a human VH 
domain, referred to as HEL4, has been discovered with similar properties to VHH, occluding 
the need for camelization. It is very soluble at concentrations above 3mM, rendering it 
monomeric. It is aggregation resistant; It can be exposed to thermo-denaturation at 
concentrations up to 56 µM and remain functional upon refolding (3). However, many features 
involved in the reversible folding of HEL4 were not fully known until the crystal structure of 
HEL4 was solved (3). By revealing the molecular details of HEL4 is was found that there are 
some dissimilarities between HEL4 and VHH. In contrast to VHH; the FR2 of HEL4 is 
composed of the hydrophobic residues found in conventional Abs. Unlike other nanobodies, 
the conformation of Trp47 in HEL4 is such that its sidechain is buried in a cavity formed by 
surrounding residues, Gly35, Val37, and Ala93, which renders the HEL4 surface more 
hydrophilic. This feature has been suggested to play an important role in the aggregation 
resistance of HEL4 (3). Furthermore, HEL4 contains 11 residues in its CDR3 loop; it is 
suggested that they contribute to solubility as well (3).  
 
HEL4 and VHHs share similar characteristics besides solubility. HEL4’s melting temperature 
is around 62.1°C (3), which is comparable to that of VHHs, ranging from 60.1 to 66.7°C (67). 
The conformational stability of HEL4 (27.0–28.4 kJ/mol) (3) is in the lower half of VHHs 
range (21.1–35.0 kJ/mol) and thus, requires approximately similar energy to unfold (67). 
 
To conclude, nanobodies are better able to bind conserved recessed regions of pathogens while 
avoiding the surrounding more variable residues, that more easily undergo mutations. In that 
way, they are better suited for cross-strain reactivity. They have better access to cryptic 
epitopes due to their smaller size and make up for their smaller paratope surface area by having 
more H-bonds and salt bridges per loop. Also, as mentioned in the introduction, nanobodies 
are safe and easily expressed in E. coli. Lastly; the human-sourced HEL4 VH domain scaffold 
is a good substitution candidate for the VHH scaffold however the sequences of the CDRs will 
affect overall refolding and aggregation characteristics (4), these should be estimated on the 
basis of each clone.

2.3 Phage display and Phage rescue 
A phage display library contains a high number of unique clones, and the library diversity is 
based on the number of CDRs carried by the expressed ab fragment (AF) as well as the number 
of randomized positions within the CDRs. The phage library can be panned against any specific 
antigen/target to capture the phages expressing the AF with the desired specificity; this is 
referred to as positive selection (PS) where non-binders are washed away. The binding phages 
are eluted from the antigen with trypsin since there is a trypsin sensitive site between the 
binding AF and the phage coat protein. When amplification of selected AF carrying phages  
(AF-phages) is desired for characterization; the eluted phages can be used to infect F-pilus 
expressing E. coli. However, a library phage carries a chromosome lacking genes necessary for 
phage amplification. As such, coinfection with a helper phage (HP) whose chromosome does 
carry the needed amplification genes, is required; this is referred to as phage rescue. The 
amplified/rescued AF-phages can be subjected to ELISA. Following this, the desired AF gene 
content can be subcloned into an expression vector and expressed in isolation for further 
characterization. The following subsections will provide an in-depth explanation of phage 
rescue from infection with the AF-phage chromosome to phage production based on the library 
utilized during this thesis, known as Predator library.  
 
2.3.1 The Predator library 
The Predator phage display library is built on the HEL4 nanobody and the M13 filamentous 
phage. An M13 displays a HEL4 scaffold-based VH domain in succession with one of its G3P 
coat proteins while carrying the gene for that fusion protein in its chromosome (4). The 
diversity of the Predator nanobodies was made by randomizing CDR2 and CDR3 with four 
and seven residues randomized, respectively, in construction of the library (Figure 2) (4). The 
bacteriophage carries a single-strand DNA (ssDNA) Predator construct with a total size of 4788 
bp (4), (Figure 3). This construct is covered by a general term, phagemid as it has both 
bacteriophage and plasmid properties. The Predator phagemid contains four key elements: 1) 
An antibiotic resistance gene which can be used for selection of the infected cell and to pressure 
the infected cell to keep the plasmid through antibiotic treatment, 2) the gene encoding the 
fusion protein VH-G3P, 3) The f1 bacteriophage origin of replication (ori), 4) and an M13 
bacteriophage packaging signal. The last two elements are required for rolling circle 
amplification and phage packaging, respectively (4,68). The f1 phage is closely related to the 
M13 phage —they are both filamentous phages, Ff phages— thus, replication from this ori can 
==PAGE:21==

be initiated by M13 proteins, as the nucleotide sequence of the two phages only differs by a 
few bases (69).

2.3.2 Infection 
A predator phage carries one copy of the VH-G3P fusion protein and four copies of modified 
G3P (68). VH-G3P contains a Myc-tag that cleaves off the VH domain from the phage 
following the addition of trypsin. The remaining G3P are modified with a trypsin sensitive site 
between their domains D3 and D2. Their cleavage results in phages not expressing the fusion 
protein being rendered non-infective (68,70,71), due to the lack of a functional G3P (lack of 
domains D1 and D2), (Figure 4). Therefore, only phages that express the HEL4 based VH can 
infect E. coli for further amplification, reducing background noise or carryover of sticky phages 
not expressing nanobodies. Following PS, the trypsin eluted M13 phage only infects E. coli 
carrying an F-plasmid and expressing the F pilus. M13 binds to the F pilus tip through the D2 
domain of G3P. When the F pilus disassembles, the phage will be brought closer to the surface 
of E. coli, where the G3P D1 domain binds TolA (68) (Figure 5). TolA, TolR, and TolQ are 
crucial for phage infection as they mediate phage coat disassembly and uptake of the phage 
ssDNA by E. coli (68,72).  An E. coli can be infected by more than one phage at a time, thus 
to maintain monoclonal behaviour, the ratio of E. coli to phage should be 20:1.

2.3.3 Phage rescue 
Once the predator phagemid is in E. coli, it is unable to replicate and produce more VH-M13 
as it lacks the necessary genes. Thus, phage rescue is initiated by coinfecting the E.coli with a 
phage chromosome that carries the missing genes. Phage rescue is performed using the KM13 
HP (4,68); this HP contains all genes necessary for M13 chromosome replication, capsid 
production, phage assembly, budding and release (4,68), (Table. 1). G3P expressed from the 
KM13 chromosome will compete with VH-G3P expressed from the Predator phagemid for 
phage particle incorporation. It has been stated that 99% of the phages will not display VH-
G3P (70), and the majority of those do, most likely will only display a single copy of the fusion 
protein (73). 
 
2.3.3.1 Construct amplification and preparation for packaging 
Following the infection of an E.coli with both HP and Predator phage, both coding ssDNA 
phage chromosomes will have a complementary template strand synthesized. This will lead to 
the generation of supercoiled dsDNA chromosomes; each referred to as the replicative form 
(RF) (68). Chromosome replication will start when G2P encoded on the HP chromosome 
reaches high enough numbers to nick the coding strands at their ori. G2P covalently binds the 
generated 5’, while the 3’ will be elongated by DNA polymerase using the template. During 
elongation the coding strand will be displaced by Rep helicase and following one round of 
elongation the resulting displaced coding strand is cut by another G2P at the ori, generating a 
new 5’ for the new G2P to associate with. The displaced coding strand is then re-circularized 
and either synthesized into a new RF or packaged into M13 phages (68). RF formation is 
stopped, when G5P, encoded on the HP chromosome has accumulated to a certain point. G5P 
associates back to back while binding the ssDNA giving it a rod-shaped appearance and 
preventing RF (74). An exposed hairpin loop function as a packaging signal, and the ssDNA is 
thus ready for phage packaging (68,75) (Figure 6).  
 
2.3.3.2 Packaging of Predator phagemid 
The HP chromosome carries an inefficient origin of replication. This means that in the presence 
of Predator which contains the f1 origin of replication —the HP plasmid— will be outcompeted 
for packaging into phage particles, due to the difference in copy number. The majority of 
phages will contain the Predator phagemid vector (68). Reducing the probability that the HP 
==PAGE:25==
18 
 
plasmid is packaged can be achieved by removing its packaging signal or by mutating its 
sequence adversely, preventing the correct hairpin loop formation.  
 
2.3.3.3 Phage assembly 
Thioredoxin is an absolute requirement for phage assembly (76). Thioredoxin  1 binds to the 
viral DNA polymerase encoded on the viral chromosome, changing its conformation to 
increase its DNA association (77). It thereby enhances the transcription of the viral genes 
needed for assembly (Table 1). M13 phage production is carried out during five steps, 
including preinitiation, initiation, elongation, pre-termination, and termination (68). During 
preinitiation, a transmembrane channel is formed by G1P, G11P, and G4P at the infected cell 
(68). Once the G5P:ssDNA complex is formed, and the M13 coat proteins G7P and G9P are 
assembled at the budding end of the channel, initiation occurs (68,78). During initiation, the 
packaging signal of the G5P:ssDNA complex interacts with G7P and G9P (68). Elongation 
follows, and constitute translocation of the ssDNA across the transmembrane channel, while 
bound G5P is replaced by G8P, until the whole of the predator phagemid is completely coated 
with G8P (68). Elongation is followed by pre-termination and involves the association of the 
complexed coat proteins (VH)G3P and G6P opposite of the budding end. Association of the 
(VH)G3P:G6P complex with the remaining phage induces a conformational change in the 
(VH)G3P:G6P complex and lead to the termination step. Termination involves the release of 
phages from the bacterium (68,78). The M13 phage does not apply a lytic or temperate cycle. 
It establishes a chronic infection in the E.coli, where it is constantly produced and continuously 
released (68).

3.1 Antigens 
This thesis aims to characterize nanobodies targeting the L. reuteri DSM17938 CnBP surface 
protein. Another goal is to find an alternative suitable L. reuteri DSM17938 cell surface antigen 
for phage display analysis. To accomplish this, the surface proteome of L. reuteri DSM17938 
and its constituent’s abundances —which were determined previously by Andreas Visbech 
Madsen (AVM)— were further analyzed for antigen identification. For further information on 
how this proteomics data was generated, I refer to his thesis. 
 
Klara Baath et al. 2005 identified 126 extracellular proteins from L. reuteri ATCC 55730 (79). 
For further information on the screening process, I refer to their study. 
It should be noted that the AVM proteomics data yielded abundance scores for a lot more 
proteins than that identified by Klara Baath et al. 2005, indicating either incomplete 
characterization by the mentioned study, heavy background noise possibly due to cell lysis 
during the generation of the proteomics data, or a combination of both. Thus, any conclusions 
based on these data should be done carefully.  
In this study, the cell-surface proteins identified by Klara Baath et al. 2005 were sequence 
aligned against the identified L. reuteri DSM17938 proteome, using BLAST+, to attach each 
surface protein with a respective arbitrary abundance score, generated for each constituent of 
the identified L. reuteri DSM17938 proteome. F8DLY8 was picked as antigen, see “4.5 
Antigen determination” for applied selection criteria. The antigen carries a GW cell wall 
anchor domain, which was removed. The entire gene block of F8DLY8, with the exception of 
the GW anchor domain, was sequenced optimized for E. coli expression systems and purchased 
from Twist Bioscience to be used as an antigen for future selections using the Predator library.

4.1 VH-Phage selections on CnBP 
Strong VH binders targeting CnBP were sought to be identified, as such the CnBP protein was

selections would select for stronger binders. However, prior to these selections, the CnBP 
protein needed to be expressed and purified.

a positive selection with this protein mixture, it may be that binders towards this second 
unknown protein are isolated as well. 
It was concluded that the plates from which the CnBP transformants were picked and grown 
from were too old. A new plate was streaked, and the picking, expression, and purification 
repeated. This produced a single band at approximately 27.2 kDa as expected, on an SDS-

4.4 Titered ELISA with the VH 
The seven VH were included in a titered ELISA against three different bacteria (Figure 25). 
The results show very low OD450-OD620 values against all three types of bacteria. It seems 
that all VH have an affinity towards BL which contradicts the predictions based on 
bioinformatics tools such as BLAST, which showed BL not to harbour CnBP on its surface, 
and only carried protein with low identity compared to CnBP. This needs to be confirmed 
through whole-genome sequencing studies and should be included in future studies. It also 
seems that BL has better adherence than LR and DH5-alpha towards maxisorp plates, and the 
lower OD450-OD620 measurement for LR may be due to washout of these cells. These results 
were obtained previously by the Goletz group for VH-phages, washout was confirmed, and it 
was hypothesized that the OD450-OD620 measurement for BL is due to unspecific binding by 
the VH-phages. These results show this to be the case for VH as the binding profile of the 
negative control HEL4 resembles that of the remaining VH. Thus unspecific binding also 
happens at the VH level, and the HEL4 based scaffold has some intrinsic sticky quality. Also, 
the plates were only coated for two hours which could partly explain the poor adhesion of LR. 
Future studies should be focused on creating a more robust whole-cell ELISA.

4.5 Antigen determination 
The selected proteins from Klara Baath et al. 2005 were considered a match with the generated 
L. reuteri DSM17938 proteome when they had an identity score of 99% or above. Also, all the 
expected values (E value; hits expected by chance when searching a database of a particular 
size) of the best matches were zero or very low, which means that it is improbable to get a 
similar matching score by chance, making the hits significant. 
Among the obtained hits, the goal was to search for an antigen candidate that would be 
accessible for nanobodies and when coated with nanobodies would not have a high probability 
of compromising the viability of L. reuteri, such as an adhesion protein. Sengupta et al. 2013 
identified two L. reuteri cell surface factors implicated in microbe-host interaction, the mucus 
binding protein (MUB), and the collagen-binding protein (CnBP) (81). MUB (Uniprot: 
Q9RGN5) was blasted against the matches from the L. reuteri DSM17938 generated proteome, 
and the closest hit showed the annotation F8DLY8 (Figure 26), which was scored within the 
12 most abundant surface proteins at both culturing conditions, (Table 3).

5 Discussion 
Besides characterizing VH binder towards CnBP, this thesis attempted to identify another 
suitable antigen on the surface of LR based on proteomics data previously generated by the 
Goletz group. It was desired to have an antigen that did not compromise cell viability or had a 
low probability of doing so when VH associated. Therefore another adhesion protein was 
desired. Its targeting may prevent LR adhesion to the gastrointestinal tract (GIT) but does not 
compromise cell functions, e.g. as the targeting of a transporter would. Also, it was desired for 
the antigen to have a high abundance. The protein F8DLY8 was selected. F8DLY8 is here 
annotated as having three overall domains; a fibronectin-binding domain, a mucus-binding 
domain and a GW anchor domain that anchors the protein to the L. reuteri cell wall (86) 
(Figure 26). According to uniport (https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/F8DLY8), the central part 
of F8DLY8, which is annotated as the mucus binding domain in this thesis is composed of 
three mub-B2 like domains and two mubBP domains. The part annotated as the fibronectin-
binding domain was characterized by BLAST sequence alignment as a leucine-rich repeat 
surface protein. Davis et al. 2006 state that leucine-rich repeat motifs are linked to fibronectin-
binding (87). F8DLY8 is cell-wall anchored to LR through a GW domain. To facilitate easy 
expression and subsequent usage in future phage display selections, the GW domain was 
removed when the gene was ordered. F8DLY8 is a relatively large protein and was here 
expressed with multiple tags, to improve solubility, giving it a total size of 150 kDa. A suitable 
expression profile was adopted that prevented aggregation/pelleting and allowed antigen 
purification from the lysate supernatant. Due to lack of time, the protein was cloned, expressed 
and partially purified. Further purification is needed for future use of this antigen in phage 
display selection, in order to reduce PS of impurities. This can be done using one of its 
previously mentioned tags, but the tags should be cleaved off using enterokinase prior to phage 
display, in order to not select for tag binders. Enterokinase was ordered but did not arrive in 
time. As alternatives, a negative selection using an irrelevant protein with the same tags could 
have been applied, or the Predator phages could have been panned first against F8DLY8 and 
then LR. However, time did not permit any of these strategies. For future analysis, the F8DLY8 
may be divided into smaller parts, to more precisely conduct epitope mapping.  
 
This thesis employed the use of CnBP for phage display selection. It was desired to conduct 
panning against the antigen instead of LR, as previously conducted by the Goletz group, as LR 
panning would be unspecific for CnBP. Also, whole-cell panning may be complicated by the 
==PAGE:59==
52 
 
cells changing their surface expression patterns during handling, in a way that deviates from 
that found in vivo at GIT. This is also true when cells are applied in ELISA screenings. 
However, using a purified antigen also has some drawbacks. The exposure of CnBP, as found 
on the surface of LR, may differ when the antigen is expressed in isolation. Thus nanobodies 
may recognize parts not available for binding in vivo. Though phage display selections were 
carried out on CnBP the ELISA screenings included LR. This would overcome some of the 
mentioned drawbacks, and increase the probability of isolating nanobodies that can target the 
antigen in vivo. For future recommendation, it is recommended to conduct cross-panning, by 
first panning against the antigen followed by panning against LR. 
 
Also, the CnBP expression construct inherited by the Goletz group expresses the antigen with 
a His-tag, which should be selected against prior to PS, or CnBP should be re-cloned with a 
cleavage site in between CnBP and its tags. Furthermore, the His-tag prevented the usage of 
CnBP in VH ELISA screenings, as the VH was here expressed with a his-tag as well. Thus 2nd 
anti-his-tag ab, as was available at the time, would bind to both the His-tag of CnBP and VH. 
A protein A based detection protein could be applied instead. The process of using purified 
antigen in phage display selections and ELISA screenings was complicated further by not 
having liquid nitrogen available to flash freeze antigen aliquots. Thus the stock needed to be 
replaced every week, by repeating expression, purification and SDS-PAGE validation, in a 
time-consuming process.  
 
The Goletz group previously showed that when running selections on LR twice, the second 
selection had an increased OD450-OD620 measurement. This work shows that increasing the 
number of selection on CnBP does not produce stronger binders. Due to the HP having a trypsin 
sensitive site in G3P, following one round of selection no carryover of non-VH expressing 
phages occurs. Thus more rounds of selections are not needed to reduce background noise. All 
present VH binders are selected for in the first selection round.  
As previously stated by Goletz group, the VH-phage CnBP association verified on ELISA 
screenings may differ for the VH in isolation. Thus seven VH clones were successfully cloned, 
expressed, partially purified and used in ELISA to screen against three different bacteria. It 
became evident that bacteria differ in their ability to adhere to maxisorp plates. Thus techniques 
need to be adopted that standardize cell concentrations when conducting screening towards cell 
==PAGE:60==
53 
 
association. Such method could be fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) which can 
be used to measure the distance between proteins, such as a VH and a surface protein both in 
vivo and in isolation, and flow cytometry.  
Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) can be applied to characterize VH affinity towards isolated 
antigen, using chips carrying the antigen. And folding and unfolding properties can be 
characterized by CD spectroscopy. However, in order to proceed with further biophysical 
characterization of the VH, they need to be purified better, to reduce/remove background noise. 
This can be done with ammonium sulphate precipitation, size-exclusion chromatography and 
Protein A affinity chromatography. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:61==

6 Conclusion 
This project aimed to discover and characterize nanobodies targeting LR, by conducting phage 
display selections using the Predator library, on the CnBP antigen, previously identified as a 
suitable surface-exposed antigen on LR by the Goletz group. This project also identified 
another suitable antigen, termed F8DLY8. Nanobodies selected against these antigens are of 
great value when studying the biology of LR. This study shows that increasing the rounds of 
phage display selections against CnBP does not select for stronger binders; thus, one round of 
selection is sufficient. Further VH-phage candidates were identified through monoclonal 
ELISA. And those previously identified by the Goletz group were subjected to titered ELISA, 
their VH genes subcloned into new expression vectors, and seven vectors were successfully 
transformed into suitable expression hosts. Effective expression conditions of VH were 
identified that resulted in close to no aggregation. Due to a lack of a robust whole-cell ELISA, 
further VHs characterization towards LR was not possible within the time frame of the project. 
Also, the VHs need further purification prior to any biophysical characterization. F8DLY8 was 
subcloned into an expression vector, expressed and partially purified. Optimum expression 
conditions were identified. However, further purification steps need to be applied to F8DLY8 
prior to its usage in phage display selection experiments, to prevent selection on undesired 
proteins. Both VH and F8DLY8 should be sufficiently purified through their tags, ammonium 
sulphate precipitation and gel filtration. Furthermore, the VH can be purified using protein A 
affinity chromatography. Future work should be directed towards phage display analysis on 
F8DLY8 and affinity characterization of all candidate VH domains, by conducting SPR with 
the VH towards the isolated antigens, and FRET and/or flow cytometry can be applied towards 
whole-cell binding studies.

D) Phage display selection on antigen 
Preparation 
1) In order to prepare for the selection process, a PS immunotube was coated with 3ml 
100µg/ml antigen in 1xPBS. This PS tube is left with the antigen solution O/N at 4°C.  
 
2) A TG1 culture is also prepared for amplification of the phages isolated during the 
selection process. One colony of TG1 grown on a minimal medium plate is transferred 
to an autoclaved Erlenmeyer flask with 50ml 2xTY. It is important to keep sterile 
during inoculation since no antibiotic is used. The culture is incubated for 14-15 h, 
at 37°C, at 200rpm. 
 
3) Six plates of 2xTYGA and one 2xTY agar plate with 2% glucose and 50μg/ml 
kanamycin (2xTYGK) are prepared, per round of selection. 
 
Blocking 
4) Following PS tube coating, the antigen can be collected for ELISA and stored at 4°C. 
The PS tube is washed 3x with 1xPBS (It is filled to the brim and emptied, without 
squirting on the antigen absorbed surface).  
 
5) A NS tube along with the PS tube are blocked with 2% MPBS (The blocking solution 
is made by adding 0.2g MP to 10 ml PBS).  Filled to the brim; the NS and PS tube are 
left at RT for 1h and 2h’s respectively. 
 
Negative selection 
6) After blocking, the NS tube is washed 3x in 1xPBS. This is done by squirting at the top 
of the tube.  
 
7) A Predator library (10^14 phages/ml) solution is prepared, by mixing 3 ml 2% MPBS 
with 100 µl Predator library (reaching 3.3*10^12 phages/ml).  
 
8) The Predator solution is transferred to the NS tube and left at RT for 1h. 
 
Positive selection 
9) After blocking, the PS tube is washed 3x in 1xPBS. This is done by squirting at the top 
of the tube, like before.  
 
10) The predator solution is transferred from the NS tube to the PS tube, without touching 
the sides of the tubes so as not to scratch off the blocking agent or antigen. The PS tube 
is left at RT for 1h. 
 
TG1 dilution

which corresponds to an OD600 between 0.4 - 0.5. The O/N TG1 culture is diluted 
(1:100) in fresh pre-warmed 2xTY; no antibiotics are added, so it is crucial to keep 
the culture sterile. The diluted culture is incubated at 37°C at 280 rpm until 
OD600=0.4-0.5.  
 
12) A water bath is started at 37°C.

G) ELISA 
 
Standard protocol 
1) All well content of the plate was decanted into a waste beaker, by turning the plate 
upside down.  
 
2) The plate was blocked by adding 200 μl 3% MPBS (3g skim milk powder in 100 ml 
1xPBS) in each well and leaving it for 2h at RT.  
 
3) The wells were then washed 3x in 1xPBS.  
 
4) Phage solutions (diluted in 3% MPBS) were added according to the plate layout. 
 
5) The plate was then left at RT for 1h.  
 
6) Due to phage content, the plate was decanted into a waste beaker; then each well was 
washed 3 times in 1xPBS w\ 0.1% tween 20 and 2 times in 1xPBS.  
 
7) 100 μl anti-M13-HRP/anti-Histag-HRP, diluted 1:5000 in 3% MPBS, was then added 
per well and the Maxisorp plate was left for 1h at RT.  
 
8) Again, content was poured into a waste beaker, and each well was washed 3 times in 
1xPBS w\ 0.1% tween 20 and 2 times in 1xPBS.  
 
9) 100 μL TMB (3,3´,5,5´-tetramentylbenzidine) was then added per well, and the plate 
was left covered in aluminium foil at RT until sufficient colour generation.  
 
TMB is a substrate for HRP; its enzymatic treatment will generate a colour.  
 
10) The reaction was stopped by adding 50 μl 1M HCl, to each well.  
 
11) The plates were then read using an ELISA plate reader, at A450 and A620. 
 
Absorbance at 450 nm is found for the colorogenic compound made from the reaction between 
TMB and HRP.  Absorbance at 620 nm represents the background from the Maxisorp plate.
