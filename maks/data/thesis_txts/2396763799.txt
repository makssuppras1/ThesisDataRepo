Abstract
The topic of this thesis was to develop ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight concrete which can
ﬂoat on water and investigate its compressive strength, ﬂexural strength and elastic mod-
ulus. The aim was to decrease the density as much as possible and still have the concrete
retain some strength.
It was possible to get the density below 800kg/m3 which was well below the density
of water, with the compressive strength being between 5.4 and 13MPa and the ﬂexural
strength being below 1MPa. It was determined that there was a good correlation between
the density and compressive strength of the concrete and that the main factors affecting the
compressive strength were the relative volume of coarser aggregates and the water-binder
ratio. However, the water-binder ratio did not affect the compressive strength as much as
it would have for normal-weight concrete. The smaller aggregates and the cement content
did not have any signiﬁcant effect on the compressive strength.
It was also determined that the elastic modulus had an excellent correlation with the
compressive strength and also has a strong correlation with the density. The addition of
ﬁbres did not affect the mechanical properties. The compressive and ﬂexural strength did
not change when ﬁbres were added. Fibres did inﬂuence the post-cracking performance,
giving the concrete much more ductility with post-cracking strength almost become as
high as the ﬁrst-cracking strength which was 1MPa.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Lightweight concrete has found applications in high-rise buildings, long-span bridge and
marine structures, because of its many advantages such as reduced weight, better thermal
insulation and enhanced durability [ACI Committee 213, 2013]. Because sustainability
has become a more important factor in recent year, lightweight concrete has seen a rise
in interest. It has been a way to reduce cost and materials consumption, because of the
reduced weight, and a method of reduction the heat loss in buildings. Research has also
been focused on using recycled materials as aggregates, an example of such materials is
waste glass, which is being used as lightweight aggregates.
The search for more sustainable solutions has also led companies and researcher to
investigate the possibility of storing excess heat energy in large reservoirs to be used at a
later time. For these reservoirs to have any real beneﬁts, they must be insulated from the
environment to reduce the heat loss. One method could be to build a lightweight concrete
slab over it. Lightweight concrete already has an excellent thermal insulating property
which can reduce the heat loss. Additionally, it is possible to develop lightweight concrete
that can ﬂoat on water. Thereby, a lot can be saved with regards to structural supports
since the concrete will ﬂoat on the water.
The main challenge when it comes to lightweight concrete is that a lowering of a
concrete’s density is usually followed by a decrease in its strength. Therefore, lightweight
concrete will in general performance worse than normal-weight concrete when it comes
to strength. This is especially true when it comes to lightweight concrete in the lower end
of the density spectrum, with concrete having a density below 1000 kg/m3 often being
very weak. This inferiority in strength has limited the structural application of lightweight
concrete, with them often being used as an insulating material instead.
Several methods have been used to address it inferiority. A higher cement content,
which has been known to get as high as 70%, has been used to increase its strength
[Neville and Brooks, 2010]. However, this will make the concrete heavier and lead it

Problem Formulation
The mechanical behaviour of normal-weight concrete has been extensively investigated
by researchers and is well understood. However, lightweight concrete has not been inves-
tigated as much, and especially lightweight concrete that has a lower density than water.
The knowledge of how it behaves mechanically at very low density can be hugely
beneﬁcial. This knowledge can propel its application even further and give rise to new
possible usages, for example, usages as an insulating platform. Floating lightweight con-
crete has not been utilised very much, probability because of the lack of knowledge.
This thesis will focus on developing ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight aggregate concrete
which has a very low density and can ﬂoat on water. The thesis will characterise its me-
chanical properties, which will be the compressive strength, ﬂexural strength and elastic
modulus.
Hypotheses
From existing knowledge of concrete, the following hypotheses are made:
1. It is expected that the compressive strength of the lightweight concrete decrease
with the density
2. Adding ﬁbres to the lightweight concrete will not affect the compressive strength,
only the tensile and ﬂexural strength.
3. As with normal-weight concrete, the elastic modulus of the lightweight concrete
will have a strong correlation with the compressive strength and density.
Aim and Objectives
The goal of this thesis is to develop a ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight concrete which can
ﬂoat on water. The aim is to decrease the density as much as possible, while retaining
some strength. The following objects are deﬁned:

CHAPTER 2
State of the Art
This chapter summarises the existing literature about lightweight aggregate concrete.
The chapter starts by presenting some general aspects of lightweight aggregate concrete
and lightweight aggregates. After that, the chapter will go into the design methodology
used by researchers to develop it, and things to consider. The mechanical properties of
lightweight aggregate concrete are discussed, and existing ﬁndings are stated. The me-

normal-weight concrete. Lastly, the chapter goes into ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight aggre-
gate concrete, which will focus on how ﬁbres affect the compressive and ﬂexural strength.
2.1
Lightweight Aggregate Concrete
The principal method of developing lightweight concrete is to introduce air voids into the
concrete. There are three ways of doing this, which are:
1. Introducing air voids with the aggregate (Lightweight aggregate concrete)
2. Adding air voids in the paste (Aerated, cellular, foamed or gassed concrete)
3. Omitting the ﬁner aggregates (No-ﬁnes concrete)
Only lightweight aggregate concrete is discussed in this chapter since this thesis
concerns lightweight aggregate concrete. Lightweight aggregate concrete is similar to
normal-weight concrete, except that the aggregates are lightweight instead of normal-
weight. The deﬁnition of lightweight aggregate concrete varies between national codes,
but in general concrete having an oven-dry density of less than 2000 kg/m3 can be con-
sidered lightweight. [ACI Committee 213, 2013] deﬁnes structural lightweight concrete
as having a 28-days compressive strength of 17 MPa and an equilibrium density between
1120 and 1920 kg/m3.

According to [Newman and Owens, 2003] RILEM categorises lightweight concrete
into three types, which are structural, structural/insulating and insulating. The required
properties are listed in table 2.1. Lastly, EN 206-1:2002 deﬁnes lightweight aggregate
concrete as having a density of 800 to 2000 kg/m3, with the aggregates being partly or
entirely comprising of lightweight aggregates. Concrete which is lighter that 800 kg/m3
is usually called ultra-lightweight aggregate concrete.
The main beneﬁt of lightweight aggregate concrete is its low density compared to
normal-weight concrete. The density of lightweight concrete varies between 300 kg/m3
to 2000 kg/m3, while normal-weight concrete has a density of 2100 kg/m3 to 2500 kg/m3
[Newman and Owens, 2003]. This reduction can be hugely beneﬁcial since the dead
weight of the concrete usually is a signiﬁcant part of the total load. However, lightweight
aggregate concrete is not as strong as normal-weight concrete, and especially if it has a
very low density it can be especially weak. It can be expected to have a compressive
strength between 1 and 40 MPa. In some instances, with a high cement content, it can
get up to 60 MPa [Neville and Brooks, 2010]. This strength is much lower than normal-
weight concrete which can be expected to have a compressive strength between 15 MPa
to 100 MPa. Though since lightweight concrete is much lighter, it may in many cases
have a higher strength to weight ratio than normal-weight concrete [Newman and Owens,
2003].
Another main advantage of lightweight aggregate concrete is its low thermal con-
ductivity, which usually lies between 0.1 and 1.2 W/m/K [Neville and Brooks, 2010].
Normal-weight concrete has a thermal conductivity that is ranges from 1.6 to 1.9 W/m/K
[Newman and Owens, 2003]. The substantial improvement in the thermal conductivity is
due to the air voids in the aggregates, which signiﬁcantly reduces the heat transfer within
the concrete. Having such low thermal conductivity makes lightweight aggregate con-
crete a good option as a building material where insulation is necessary, e.g. in buildings.

2.2
Lightweight Aggregates
The component that makes lightweight aggregate concrete different from normal-weight
concrete is the lightweight aggregates. They are a special kind of aggregates which have a
lower density than normal ones. As with lightweight aggregate concrete, the deﬁnition of
lightweight aggregates varies between codes. EN 13055-1:2002 deﬁnes them as having a
particle density of less than 2000 kg/m3 and a loose bulk density of less than 1200 kg/m3.
ASTM C 330 deﬁnes them as having a bulk density of 1120 kg/m3 for ﬁne aggregates
and 880 kg/m3 for coarse aggregates.
Lightweight aggregates have a porous interior with air trapped within them. It is
this trapped air that makes them so light. The density of lightweight aggregates can
vary greatly depending on the material and manufacturing. Perlite, for example, is a
lightweight aggregate with a very low density, having a bulk density as low as 100 kg/m3
[Neville and Brooks, 2010]. However, with such low density, the aggregates are too
porous and weak to be used for any structural purpose
Lightweight aggregates can be divided into natural and synthetic lightweight aggre-
gates. Natural lightweight aggregates are usually crushed igneous volcanic rocks like
pumice and perlite. Synthetic aggregates, also called artiﬁcial aggregates, are manufac-
tured lightweight aggregates. The manufacturing usually consists of thermally expanding
various materials. These materials are often clay, slate or waste glass. Of the two types,
synthetic aggregates are the most widely used since they are more readily available and
are manufactured in controlled conditions whereby having less variability than natural
aggregates (Neville, 2010).
Because of the porous nature of lightweight aggregates, they tend to have a much
higher water absorption than normal aggregates. They can be expected to have a water
absorption between 5 to 25 % of dry mass. This is considerably higher than normal aggre-
gates which usually have a water absorption of less than 2 % of dry mass [ACI Committee
213, 2013].

Particle Packing
Strength is one of the most important factors when considering concrete performance.
Many factors can inﬂuence the strength of concrete; however, porosity is known to be one
of the most signiﬁcant factors . The porosity, or void fraction, can be reduced by optimis-
ing the packing of the dry composite materials. An optimal packing of the dry composite
materials will improve not only the hardened properties but also the fresh properties. By
optimising the packing, the solid materials will be tighter packed which will make more
water available as a lubricant between the particles [Brouwers H.J. and Radix, 2005], and
thereby increase the workability of the fresh concrete for the same amount of water.

Workability and Segregation
Because the lightweight aggregates have a much lower density than the binder materi-
als, lightweight aggregate concrete is prone to segregate. If the fresh concrete has too
high slump or is over-vibrated, the lightweight aggregates will ﬂoat to the top. This
phenomenon is called ﬂoating of coarser aggregates, which is a particular case that can
==PAGE:23==
2.4. MECHANICAL PROPERTIES OF LIGHTWEIGHT AGGREGATE CONCRETE11
happen for lightweight aggregate concrete [mehta2013concretee]. However, lightweight
aggregate concrete does not need as high of a slump as normal-weight concrete. Slump
tests tend to underestimate the workability of lightweight aggregate concrete because the
gravitational work done on it is lower due to its lower density compared to normal-weight
concrete.
The primary way of counteracting segregation is to lower the amount of water in the
fresh concrete. However, reducing the mixing water will also reduce the workability. By
using superplasticiser, it is possible to reduce the mixing water without losing workability.
Superplasticiser has been used in many papers. [Chung et al., 2017] used superplasticiser
corresponding to 1.8% of binder mass, [Suraneni et al., 2016] used 1 to 2% superplasti-
ciser, [Spiesz and Hunger, 2017] used 0.2 to 0.7% superplasticiser, [Yu et al., 2016] used
1%, [Glas et al., 2015] used 0.77%, [Yu et al., 2015] used 0.5% and [Yu et al., 2013]
used 0.8 to 1%. Another method of reducing the risk of segregation or bleeding is to use
a viscosity modifying agent. A viscosity agent will increase the viscosity of the paste,
which make it harder for the aggregates to ﬂoat to the top. A viscosity agent was used
by [Chung et al., 2017], along with superplasticiser, corresponding to 0.3% of the binder
mass.

Strength is the most valued property of concrete. Concrete’s strength is, compared to
other properties, easy to measure. Also, other properties are known to correlate with the
strength of concrete. Examples of such properties are the elastic modulus, permeabil-
ity and resistance to weathering. For concrete, the most signiﬁcant factor affecting the
strength is the porosity. According to [Mehta and Monteiro, 2013], there exists a funda-
mental inverse relationship between the porosity and the strength, which can be expressed
as followed:

but the porosity can be difﬁcult to determine in practice. However, other factors are also
known to inﬂuence the strength. These factors are for normal-weight concrete secondary
factors to porosity. Such factors are the water-binder ratio, air content, the aggregates,
age and density.

Air Content
The air content is also known to inﬂuence the strength of concrete. As can be seen in
equation (2.6) and (2.7), the air content is incorporated in these formulations. The air
content will inﬂuence the porosity just like the water-binder ratio. Therefore, it should
also inﬂuence the strength. According to [Mehta and Monteiro, 2013], the effect air
content has on the strength of concrete seems to depend on the water-binder ratio and the
cement content. High strength concrete, containing high amounts of cement, tend to get
the most signiﬁcant losses in strength with high air content, while low cement containing
concrete can get an increase in strength.
The affect air content has on the compressive strength was investigated by [Wang et
al., 2013], where they used shrinkage admixtures to reduce the air content in the concrete.
The concrete mixes without shrinkage admixtures had an air content of 15 to 26 % and
a compressive strength ranging from 36 to 47 MPa. With the shrinkage admixtures, the
air content dropped to 4 to 7 %, and the compressive strength increased to 63 to 68 MPa.
[Yu et al., 2015] developed lightweight aggregate concrete with a dry density of 650 to
700 kg/m3 and investigated how air-entraining agent affected the compressive strength.
They reported a reduction in compressive strength from 15 MPa to 10 MPa when adding
a dosage of 2.25 kg/m3 air entraining agent to the mix.
It is evident that air content inﬂuences the compressive strength of concrete, and this
is also the case for lightweight aggregate concrete. Lightweight aggregate concrete tends
to have a high air content compared to normal-weight concrete. Several papers observed
a high air content. [Suraneni et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2013; Yu et al., 2013, 2015; Yu et
al., 2016], all observed large air voids in their cut concrete specimens, which most likely
would have affected the strength of the concrete.
The recommended air content for lightweight concrete is according to [ACI Commit-
tee 213, 2013] between 4.5% and 7.5% if the maximum aggregate size is 19 mm and 6%
to 9% if it maximum size is 10 mm

Lightweight Aggregates
Normal aggregates usually have little effect on the strength of concrete and are therefore
often overlooked as a factor. Normal aggregates will most of the time outperform the con-
crete when it comes to strength; however, this is not the case for lightweight aggregates.
Lightweight aggregates are much more likely to utilise their strength entirely, and thereby
having a more prominent effect on the strength.
The effect of lightweight aggregates on the concrete strength was investigated by
[Suraneni et al., 2016]. They increased the volume fraction of lightweight aggregates
from 30 %to 50 % and saw a decreased in compressive strength of 30 % from 23 MPa to
16 MPa. They also investigated how the maximum lightweight aggregate size impacted
the compressive strength. By reducing the maximum aggregate size from 16 mm to 1 mm,
the compressive strength increased 30 % from 23 MPa to 30 MPa.
The decrease in compressive strength with increasing amount of aggregates is rea-
sonable to assume. They used lightweight agregates from LIAVER, which only have a
crushing strength of around 2 to 4 MPa. The increase in compressive strength by reducing
the maximum aggregate size can be explained several factors. The strength of lightweight
aggregates often decreases with increasing size, so by taken away the weaker aggregates
the concrete will get stronger. Another factor is that larger aggregates tend to form weaker
inter transition zones with more microcracks [Mehta and Monteiro, 2013].
Concrete Age
The strength of concrete increases as it hardens. Most of the gain in strength will happen
within the ﬁrst month, but the process can go on for years. For lightweight aggregate
concrete, this does not seem to be the case. Since lightweight aggregate concrete has a
lower strength ceiling, the development of strength will stop after a short time.
[Suraneni et al., 2016] compared the 7-day and 28-day compressive strength of light-
weight aggregate concrete (1200 to 1600 kg/m3), made with expanded waste glass aggre-
gates, they saw little difference between the compressive strength at those ages. [Yu et
al., 2015] compared the 7-day and 28-day compressive strength for four different cement
types, CEM I 52.5N, CEM II/B-V 42.5N, CEM III/A 52.5N and CEM V/A(S-V) 42.5N.
All the cement types had reached over 85 % of their 28-day compressive strength after 7-
day maturity. CEM I 52.5N and CEM II/B-V 42.5N had almost no difference, while CEM
V/A(S-V) 42.5N and CEM III/A 52.5N had a more signiﬁcant difference between their
7-day and 28-day. The difference was explained by the slower reaction of these cement
types. [Yu et al., 2013] found that the lightweight aggregate concrete they had developed
had reached their maximum compressive strength at the age of 7-day maturity. After the
7-day maturity, no increase in compressive strength was seen. For lightweight aggregate

7-day maturity little seems to be gained in strength. The reason for this is most likely that

Density
The strength of concrete is not directly affected by the density; however, there is a cor-
relation between them. The density of well compacted lightweight aggregate concrete is
mainly affected by the volume fraction of lightweight aggregates and their particle den-

affects their strength. Therefore, lightweight aggregate concrete with a low density will
likely have a higher amount of low-density aggregates, which leads to a lower strength.
This relation was seen in [Chung et al., 2017; Glas et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2010; Spiesz
and Hunger, 2017; Suraneni et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2013; Yu et al., 2013, 2015], where

Elastic Modulus
For homogeneous materials, there is a direct relationship between the elastic modulus and
the density. However, for inhomogeneous materials, like concrete, the elastic modulus
depends on the volume fraction and elastic modulus of the individual composite materials.
The elastic modulus of concrete has been found to correlate with its strength and density.
This correlation arises because these properties are all affected by the porosity of the
concrete. Due to this correlation, many empirical expressions have been formulated to
express the elastic modulus as a function of compressive strength and density. According
to [ACI Committee 213, 2013], the elastic modulus for concrete with a density between
1500 kg/m3 and 2500 kg/m3 can be expressed as:

The relationship, expressed by equation (2.8) from ACI, is an empirical relationship
obtained from experimental data for normal-weight concrete. Therefore, the predicted
value may differ considerably from the measured value, especially when using special
types of concrete like lightweight aggregate concrete [Neville and Brooks, 2010]. Mois-
ture content is known to affect the elastic modulus of concrete. Usually, concrete which
has a high moisture content shows a higher elastic modulus, which is opposite to the
strength that decreases with increasing moisture content.
According to [Neville and
Brooks, 2010], a wet concrete specimen can have an elastic modulus 3 to 4 GPa higher
than a dry specimen.
Concrete is a two-phase material consisting of aggregates and paste, so its elastic
modulus will depend on the volume fraction and elastic modulus of the phase materials.
The elastic modulus of the paste is determined by its porosity, which is mainly affected
by the water-binder ratio, the air content and the degree of hydration of the cement. Ce-
ment usually has an elastic modulus ranging from 7 to 28 GPa [Mehta and Monteiro,
2013]. Porosity also affects the elastic modulus of aggregates, especially for coarser
ones. Aggregates that are porous have a lower elastic modulus since the porosity lowers
their stiffness. Normal aggregates can have an elastic modulus of 70 GPa to 14 GPa,
while lightweight aggregate can have as low as 7 GPa [Mehta and Monteiro, 2013]. For
normal-weight concrete, increasing the volume fraction of denser aggregates will usually

2.5
Fibre-Reinforced Lightweight Aggregate Concrete
Lightweight aggregate concrete is a brittle material with little to no tensile strength, which
loses all its bearing capacity after it cracks. These shortcomings can be overcome by
adding ﬁbres to the concrete. The addition of ﬁbres aims to increase the tensile strength
of the concrete, by delaying crack formation and crack growth, while also increase its
toughness [Neville and Brooks, 2010]. Furthermore, ﬁbres improve the concretes post-
cracking performance by giving it some post-cracking bearing capacity by bridging the
cracks and thereby allowing tensile force travel across the them.
The most widely used ﬁbres today are made of steel, glass, carbon or polymers. The
amount of ﬁbres added to concrete depends on the application but usually ranges between
0 to 2 percent by volume fraction. The failure in ﬁbres can happen in two ways: ﬁbre
rupture or ﬁbre pull-out. The preferable failure is ﬁbre pull-out, which gives the concrete
a more ductile failure mode instead of a brittle one. The ductility of ﬁbre pull-out comes
from the friction force between the ﬁbres and paste when they are pulled out.
With low ﬁbre content, which usually is below 2% vol., plain and ﬁbre-reinforced con-
crete behave very similar before ﬁrst-cracking [Mehta and Monteiro, 2013]. After this, the
load-carrying capacity of plain concrete vanishes rapidly. However, ﬁbre-reinforced con-
crete will show some post-cracking load-carrying capacity. Figure 2.2 illustrates the post-
cracking performance of concrete in tension. Plain concrete has one peak load, which is
the ﬁrst-cracking strength σcc. After this load the concrete has no more bearing capacity.
However, if it is ﬁbre-reinforced it will peak twice, once at ﬁrst-cracking strength σcc and
the second time at the post-cracking strength σpc
Because of the brittleness and low tensile strength of lightweight aggregate, some
researchers have investigate how adding ﬁbres will affect the mechanical properties of
lightweight aggregate. The effects ﬁbres have on the compressive strength is debated in
the literature. The effects seem to depend on the size, shape, material and amount of
ﬁbres used. [Yu et al., 2016] investigated how adding ﬁbres would affect lightweight ag-
gregate concrete’s mechanical properties. They used two types of polypropylene ﬁbres,
macroﬁbres being 45mm long and 0.5mm wide and microﬁbres being 18mm long and
22µm wide. They casted concrete specimens with 0, 0.1, 0.2, 0,3, 0.6, 0.9 and 1.2%
volume of macroﬁbres. The compressive strength with no ﬁbres was around 10.6 MPa,
and at a volume content of 0.6%, the compressive strength increased to around 14.5 MPa.
However, after 0.6% the compressive strength decreased. The ﬁbres disruptiveness on

the ﬁrst-cracking strength, if enough ﬁbres are added.

CHAPTER 3
Experimental Work
This chapter aims to explain the methodology used for the experimental work, which
consisted of two phases. The ﬁrst phase concerned plain lightweight aggregate concrete,
while the second one concerned ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight aggregate concrete.
The experimental work of the ﬁrst phase consists of four parts, which were mix design,
mix casting and curing, testing of fresh concrete and testing of hardened concrete. These
parts took place alongside each other. One or more mix recipes were designed and cast.
The information obtained from the casting and testing of the fresh concrete was then
used to adjust or design new mix recipes. The aim was to lower the density as much as
possible, and still have a concrete that did not show any sign of segregation or bleeding.
Additionally, the aim was to have air content below 9% and a table ﬂow of 150 mm.
The second phase consisted of adding ﬁbres to the concrete. A mix recipe from the
ﬁrst phase which had shown promising results was chosen to be further tested by adding
ﬁbres. This phase consisted of adding different amount of ﬁbres to the concrete to see
how the mechanical properties changed.

3.3
Casting and Curing
Two different casting procedures were used in this thesis. One was used for the plain
lightweight aggregate concrete, and another was used for the ﬁbre-reinforced concrete.
The curing procedure was the same for both plain and ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight aggre-
gate concrete.
Casting of Plain Lightweight Aggregate Concrete
For the plain concrete six 60x120mm cylinders were cast for each mix recipe. The moulds
surfaces were oiled before use, to ensure that the concrete did not stick to them. The mix-
ing procedure started with adding the powdery superplasticiser to the water, and mixing
them so that it dissolved in the water. After that, the aggregates and binder were mixed
in the mixer for one minute. Then they were stirred with the hand to see if they were
well mixed. The water with the superplasticiser was added, and the water, binder and
aggregates were mixed for an additional one minute. After one minute, the mix was again
stirred with the hands to ensure that everything had mixed together and that no dry binder
was stuck at the bottom. The mix was then mixed for additional 5 minutes before the
mixing was complete. The total mixing procedure took around 7 minutes

Casting of Fibre-Reinforced Lightweight Aggregate Concrete
For the ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight aggregate concrete six 60x120mm cylinders and four

The mixing procedure was the same as for the concrete without ﬁbres until the water

ﬁbres were evenly distributed in the concrete. The fresh concrete was then mixed for an
additional 3 minutes; whereafter the mixing was complete. The total mixing procedure
took 10 minutes.

Flow Table Test
The workability of the fresh concrete was estimated using the ﬂow table test described in
DS/EN 1015-3:1999.
Apparatus
The test was carried out using the following apparatus:
• A ﬂow table with a clean 400x400mm metal surface and a drop height of 12mm
• A truncated conical mould of brass with inner diameter of 65 and 75mm and height
of 40mm.
• A tamper with a head diameter of 40mm and thickness of 12mm
• A calliper with a measuring length of 300mm and accuracy of 0.05mm
• A ﬂat metal bar of brass with dimension 300x30x6mm
Procedure
The fresh concrete was stirred for 15 seconds, to counteract any false settings before
testing. After that, the mould was ﬁlled halfway with concrete, which was then compacted
by stroking it ten times with the tamper. The remaining half was then ﬁlled up with
concrete, which again was compacted by stroking it ten times with the tamper. Any

performed by gently moving the bar over the mould top while moving the bar sideways.

for any excess concrete. The mould was then slowly lifted off without any concrete
sticking to it. The concrete was then given 15 jolts over 15 seconds. Two measurements
perpendicular to each other are then made of the diameter of the concrete. The average
value of the two measurements was calculated and noted.

The dry density of the concrete specimens was calculated according to DS/EN 12390-

Apparatus
The test was carried out using the following apparatus:
• A scale with an accuracy of 0.001kg
• A calliper with a measuring length of 300mm and an accuracy of 0.05mm
Procedure
The mass of each specimen was measured to an accuracy of 0.001kg before testing. Three
measurements of the diameter were taken at each end of the cylinder with an angle be-

of 120o. The average diameter and height were calculated to the nearest millimetre. Once
the mass and volume are known the density was calculated to an accuracy of 10 kg/m3.

Results and Discussion
The previous chapter presented the experimental methodology and presented the mix
designed developed during the experimental phase. This chapter presents the ﬁndings
from the experiments and will analyse and discuss the results. The results from testing
the mixes are presented and compared with each other. The variation of results between
the mixes is analysed to ﬁnd correlations and possible factors that are inﬂuencing the
results. The analysis concludes with a discussion about ﬁndings from the analysis.
For the compressive strength, the analysis involves determining whether there is a cor-
relation between the compressive strength and density. The composition of the aggregates
and binder is also analysed to determine how they affect the compressive strength. The
relationship the elastic modulus has to the compressive strength and density is analysed,
to see how they correlate with each other. Lastly, the ﬁbre content is analysed to ﬁnd how
it is affecting the mechanical properties of the concrete. The chapter concludes with com-
paring the results with other authors to determine how they compare with their ﬁndings.

Density
The air-dry density of the concrete at testing can be seen in ﬁgure 4.2. The average density
of the mixes developed was between 563 kg/m3 and 888 kg/m3. These densities are well
below the density of water, whereby all the mixes developed will ﬂoat. Many of the
densities were also below 800 kg/m3, which is lower than the deﬁnition of lightweight
aggregate concrete made by EN 206-1:2002. Thereby, these mixes can be categorised as
ultra-lightweight aggregate concrete as mentioned in the chapter state of the art.
Many of the air-dry densities were around 700 kg/m3, which gives them a ﬂoating
capacity of around 300 kg/m3 concrete. If the ﬂoating concrete slab has a thickness of
1 m, it will have a carrying load of almost 3 kN/m2. However, this is the air-dry density,
and the concrete will absorb some water over time which will reduce the carrying load.
Also, the structural bearing capacity is not taken into account in this example.

Compressive Strength and Density
The strength of concrete is known to decrease with its density. This relation was also
found in the literature review in the chapter state of the art. There the denser concrete
generally has a higher compressive strength. The compressive strength is plotted against
the air-dry density of the mix at testing, which can be seen in ﬁgure 4.6. It shows a
positive correlation between the density and compressive strength, with a R2 of 0.47.
This conforms with the hypotheses and the that the compressive strength would increase
with increasing density. The linear regression conﬁrmed that the relationship between the
compressive strength and density was statically signiﬁcant.
[Suraneni et al., 2016] found that the compressive strength of their lightweight con-
crete increased at a rate of 1MPa per 100kg/m3 density with a maximum aggregate size
of 16mm and 6MPa per 100kg/m3 density with a maximum aggregate size of 1mm. The
rate of increase of the compressive strength for mixes in ﬁgure 4.6 is around 1.6MPa per
100kg/m3 density. The maximum aggregate size these mixes was 4mm. Thereby, it lies
between the ﬁndings made by [Suraneni et al., 2016], so the rate of increase seems to
depend on the maximum aggregate size.
Even though there is a strong correlation between the compressive strength and den-
sity, there is no causality between them. Instead, these properties are both affected by the
same underlying factors, such as the volumetric amount of aggregates and water-binder
ratio. However, the density could be used as a predictor of the strength, since it much
easy to determine.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis dealt with the development of ﬁbre-reinforced lightweight aggregate concrete,
with the goal of being able to ﬂoat on water.
This thesis was able to develop lightweight aggregate concrete with air-dry densities
ranging from 563 to 888kg/m3 and compressive strength between 5.4 and 13MPa. As
hypothesised there was a good correlate between the density and compressive strength,
giving on average a 1MPa increase in compressive strength per 100kg/m3 increase in
density. The main factors affecting the compressive strength were found to be the vol-
ume of coarser aggregates and the water-binder ratio. The volumetric amount of coarser
aggregates was found to have a signiﬁcantly higher impact on the compressive strength
compared to the total volume of aggregates. Increasing the volume of coarser aggregates
by 1% gave on average a 0.2MPa decrease in compressive strength. The water-binder
ratio had a strong negative correlation with the compressive strength when keeping the
volume of aggregates ﬁxed. An increase in of 0.1 in water-binder ratio would decrease
the compressive strength by 1MPa on average. The addition of ﬁbre did not impact the
compressive strength as expected in the hypotheses. However, this conﬂicted with some
papers which reported an increase in compressive strength by adding ﬁbres. The discrep-
ancy was determined to come from the use of different type of ﬁbres. When testing the
cylinders and prisms, it was noticed that the concrete had large air voids within them,
which was also reported in other paper. These voids are postulated to have come from the
lightweight aggregates or are air bubbles that did not escape during the vibration of the
mould. The large voids are expected to have inﬂuenced the compressive strength of the
concrete, due to the size.
The ﬂexural strength of the mixes ranged from 0.7MPa to 1MPa and did not increase
by adding ﬁbres, which contradicted the hypotheses. However, the reason for this was
due to the low amount of ﬁbres used, which were 0.2%, 0.4% and 0.6% vol. Other
paper reported increased ﬂexural strength by adding ﬁbres, but they had a higher ﬁbre

content and used ﬁbres with rough surfaces. However, the post-cracking performance of
the concrete improved signiﬁcantly from it having no post-cracking strength to having
0.9MPa. This post-cracking strength is close to the ﬁrst-cracking strength which was
1MPa.
5.1
Further Work
Further work must be done to determine the long-term durability of the concrete. The
long-term water absorption of the concrete should be investigated to determine how much
and how long it will absorb water and if it will sink over time.
More work should also be done to increase the compressive strength while trying
to keep the density low. This work includes investigating how much the compressive
strength will increase by removing the coarser aggregates and how it will affect the den-
sity.
Lastly, the large air voids that were present in the concrete should be studied more. It
should be investigated how to reduce the air voids in number and size.
