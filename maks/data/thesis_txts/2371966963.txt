                 Abstract 
The main purpose of this study was to investigate the effect of feedback on the 
energy consumption and the indoor environment in 45 dwellings in Copenhagen. 
Feedback on the indoor environment was provided to the occupants throughout a 
5-week period of the experiment.  
 
The effect of feedback on energy conservation behaviour has been investigated in 
recent years, and direct energy feedback can reduce the energy consumption with 
5-15%. Several studies have showed that feedback should be interactive, 
personalized and frequent to have the greatest effect on the energy consumption, 
and the feedback in the current study was developed on this basis. 
 
Through continuous measurements, the indoor environment and occupancy was 
assessed through 10 weeks between February and May 2017 in the 45 dwellings in 
the current study. Half of the dwellings were provided with a weekly feedback 
letter, for the last 5 weeks, with the results compared to pre-defined thresholds in 
order to improve the indoor environment. A sensor measured relative humidity, 
temperature and CO2 concentration in the living room and bedroom of each 
dwelling, and Passive Infrared sensors detected presence in the dwelling together 
with door monitoring. Due to unexpected challenges with the equipment and 
administrative problems, neither the window opening behaviour nor energy 
consumption was evaluated in the current study.  
 
The results showed a large difference in improvement of relative humidity in the 
dwellings due to feedback. A smaller, but still remarkable, difference of 
improvement was seen for the CO2 concentration. The indoor temperature 
decreased in all dwellings, which indicated a strong relation to the outdoor 
temperature, but a difference between the feedback and reference group was 
detected for indoor temperature as well, which restates the effect of the weekly 
feedback letter. 
 
An effect of the feedback on the indoor environment was clear, but it was not 
possible to evaluate the effect on energy consumption. Furthermore, it was not 
possible to assess the precise size of the improvement.  
 
Generally, the provided feedback had a positive effect on the indoor environment 
during the feedback period.

Hovedformålet med dette studie var at undersøge effekten af feedback på 
energiforbruget og indeklimaet i 45 boliger i København. Igennem en 5-ugers 
periode blev feedback på indeklimaet stillet til rådighed for beboerne.  
 
Effekten af feedback på energibesparelsesadfærd er blevet undersøgt i de senere 
år, og direkte energi feedback kan reducere energiforbruget med 5-15%. Flere 
undersøgelser har vist, at feedback skal være interaktiv, personlig og hyppig for at 
få størst effekt på energiforbruget, og tilbagemeldingen i den aktuelle 
undersøgelse blev udviklet på dette grundlag. 
 
Gennem løbende målinger blev indeklimaet og tilstedeværelsen vurderet gennem

1 Introduction 
With global heating and climate changes, the demand for energy efficiency in both 
existing and new buildings, rises. Energy regulations increase the importance of 
both predicting and reducing energy use of buildings. The housing industry is 
responsible for 40% of the general energy consumption, and domestic energy use 
covers 26% [1]. Influencing occupant behaviour can be an important factor when 
lowering the energy consumption in residential housing in the future [2], [3]. 
 
The understanding and influencing of occupant behaviour has been investigated 
in recent years, and the solution of lowering the energy consumption even further 
might lie in this direction [3]–[6]. The effect of feedback on energy conservation 
behaviour has been investigated, and technical solutions such as smart metering 
and bills are only part of the solution [7]. Energy consumption is often invisible to 
the consumer, but awareness and affordance are key terms when influencing 
consumers energy behaviour [7]–[9]. The user needs to actively take part in the 
process of lowering the energy consumption and improving the indoor 
environment, and the challenge of overall energy savings might be solved in the 
domestic sector [10]. Direct energy feedback can reduce the energy consumption 
with 5-15%, and with this potential the subject is relevant to explore [2].  
 
Indoor environment with the physical parameters such as relative humidity, air 
pollution and indoor temperature are affected by occupant behaviour in dwellings 
[1], [11], [12]. The perceived indoor environment is highly affected by the 
adjustment of building controls e.g. windows and thermostats. Adjustment of 
building controls is connected with both the outdoor temperature, individual 
preferences and sensitivity of the occupant [13].  
 
The term energy feedback covers methods to reduce the energy consumption 
through influence of the occupant behaviour. Several studies have showed that 
feedback should be interactive, personalized and frequent to have the greatest 
effect on the energy consumption [2], [8], [14]–[16]. The good practise for useful 
energy feedback will, in this study, be applied for indoor environment as well as 
energy consumption.  
 
The main purpose of this study was to investigate the effect of weekly feedback on 
the energy consumption and the indoor environment in 45 dwellings in 
Copenhagen.

2 Background 
The current study is based on experimental field work, and the results obtained in 
the project period. A literature review has been made to consolidate the approach 
and method. The results are also based on chosen metrics founded in the literature 
study and current European standards.  
2.1 
The UserTEC Project 
UserTEC is a five years’ multidisciplinary research project started in 2013, which 
the current study is a part of. The project’s main themes are user practices, 
technologies and residential energy consumption. The project is divided into four 
work packages. The current study is part of Work Package 3, which develops and 
tests new user adapted energy efficient building technologies.  
 
The UserTEC project aim to get a better and well-documented insight into the 
sector with the largest potential for energy savings – residential housing. Both 
new developments and research of existing buildings and practices, are part of the 
project [17].  
 
The experiments described in this thesis are used for both assessing the effect of 
feedback, and researching occupant behaviour with the aim of creating a realistic, 
dynamic and validated model of both occupancy in general and how and why 
occupants adjust building controls. The focus of the current study has been the 
feedback process only.  
2.2 
Indoor Environment in Dwellings 
Indoor environment has long been known to be very important for the indoor 
comfort level [18], [19]. The indoor environment in dwellings are not as well 
studied as for example in offices, where the connection between indoor 
environment and productivity level have been assessed [20], [21]. The individual 
adjustment of building controls is higher in residential housing, and the occupants 
can easier regulate the environment to match their personal comfort zone, which 
might be the reason why the area is less studied.

A study conducted in 2013 investigated the economic effects of a poor indoor 
environment in dwellings, and did a review on former studies of the health effects 
of an insufficient indoor climate. Both poor indoor air quality and humid buildings 
is known to increase symptoms of various health issues as asthma, respiratory 
infections and other infectious diseases [12].  
2.2.1 
Thermal Comfort 
Thermal comfort is essential when discussing indoor environment, and the 
thermal sensation can be measured and evaluated using various methods [22]. It 
is not, however, important to the current study, since the thermal comfort will not 
be evaluated, and only the air temperature will be measured.  
 
Literature is scarce on the subject on preferred indoor temperature in dwellings, 
but overall an optimal operative temperature can be evaluated using either the 
Predicted Percentage of Dissatisfied (PPD) or the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV), 
where PMV=0 is the optimal condition. In the European Standard 15251 [23], the 
design temperature for ventilated space is described in both Table A.1 and Table 
A.2.  
 
Table A.1 concerns the PPD and PMV, and Table A.2 the design temperature for 
the different building categories for a met value of 1.2 and clo value of 1.0 for 
winter and 0.5 for summer. A summary of the two tables from the standard are

The recommended temperatures from EN 15251 concerns thermal comfort. 
Thermal comfort varies for different occupants, and comfort is difficult to design 
for individuals [22], [24]. There is no physical reason why the temperature range 
cannot exceed the recommendations.  
 
Several factors influence the thermal comfort, and both environmental and 
personal parameters are important. But also social and psychological factors can 
influence the thermal sensation, and must therefore be accounted for. In a 
dwelling, a social factor can be a family living together, and taking each other into 
consideration, when adjusting the building controls, e.g. opening windows or 
adjusting the thermostats.

2.2.2 
Indoor Air Quality 
The indoor air quality is affected by pollutants emitted by furniture and 
equipment, building parts and the occupants. Ventilation of indoor air with clean 
outdoor air can reduce the pollutants, and contribute to a healthy indoor 
environment. CO2 is emitted by humans, and is a well-known measure of air 
quality [25], [26], since it is easy to measure and gives an indication of the air 
pollution in a room caused by occupants.  
 
The European Standard 15215 recommends design CO2 concentrations for energy 
calculations, and they are seen in Table 2. The outdoor CO2 concentration can be 
assumed to be approximately 400ppm.

The Danish Building Regulation 2015 demands a supply rate of minimum 0.3 l/s 
per m2 heated space in dwellings. For non-residential buildings, it is 
recommended that the CO2 level does not exceed 1000ppm for longer periods of 
time [27], [28]. Both phrases from The Danish Building Regulation are vague, and 
the recommendations from category II is often used as a guideline for sufficient 
air change in a room.  
 
Studies show that insufficient indoor air quality affects productivity and 
health[12], [20]. In addition to personal gains, the economic and social gains of 
efficient productivity and good health has a substantial size [19].  
 
Most dwellings in Denmark does not have a mechanical demand-controlled 
ventilation system, and relies completely on natural ventilation controlled by the 
occupants [29]. Studies suggest that many dwellings in Denmark have too high 
CO2 concentrations [12], [30], and among others a solution can be influencing the 
occupants window opening behaviour to increase the air change in the houses. It 
appears that even though the air quality is poor, the perceived air quality does not 
affect the window opening behaviour compared to how much the thermal 
sensation does [16]. External impact such as energy feedback used for indoor air 
quality might be useful, which is what the current study will investigate.

2.2.3 
Moisture in Residential Housing 
A correlation between the air change in dwellings and the appearance of 
condensation on windows was found in the study from 2013 [12], which indicates 
that problems with too much moisture in indoor air is closely connected to 
ventilation and indoor air quality.  
 
Mould can cause huge problems in building parts, which can lead to continuous 
and expensive renovations [12], [31], [32]. A connection between the relative 
humidity and visible mould or moisture in households in Denmark has been made, 
and very humid air should therefore be avoided [12].    
 
Mould and visible moisture are also related to several health issues, especially 
with children, and should not only be avoided to maintain the building parts, but 
also to obtain a healthy indoor environment [33].  
2.3 
Domestic Energy Consumption 
Domestic energy consumption constituted 8% of the households’ overall 
consumption in 2015 [34]. There is a huge potential in this sector when it comes 
to reducing energy.  
  
Energy use is invisible for most occupants in a building. How to influence an active 
change in consumption can be difficult for the occupant to understand [2],[8]. 
Energy consumption in identical dwellings tend to differ with a ratio of up to 3, 
which suggest that the energy consumption is strongly attached to the occupant, 
not the dwelling [6]. Communicating energy issues can be difficult, but visual 
information and individual tailored feedback are key tools [8]. 
2.3.1 
Occupant Behaviour 
In recent years, several studies have concerned stochastic modelling of occupant 
behaviour to create realistic simulations of energy consumption of new buildings. 
The so-called performance gap between the simulated and measured energy use, 
is often due to mistakenly predicted occupant behaviour, and the area should be 
further explored in order to accurately predict energy consumption [35]–[37]. 
 
Window opening behaviour is very important to obtain comfortable, natural 
ventilated buildings, and several studies have documented that both the in- and 
outdoor temperature are large triggers for window opening [1], [13], [16].

2.3.2 
Household Characteristics 
Several factors affect the energy consumption in a household, and a study 
conducted in the Netherlands in 2009 did a review of the household characteristics 
that influences the energy use. The study suggests that in general, the age of the 
occupants play a large role in consumption, together with number of occupants 
and rooms in the household. A correlation is also suggested to exist between the 
household income and the requested energy use. The ownership of the dwelling 
also proved to influence the energy use, and rented houses with heating included 
in the rent consumed the most energy. The study also suggested that presence of 
occupants is a large influence on energy consumption [5].  
2.3.3 
Smart Metering and Energy Reduction  
A mean to reduce energy consumption in households in which several technical 
solutions are included is “smart metering”. Smart metering is a term for advanced 
meters that gives the consumer more information than a regular energy meter. It 
also gives the possibility for communicating with the utility and to bill more 
accurate and individually [7].  
 
Smart meters can be an affordance to the energy consumers. For many 
households, energy consumption is still an unfamiliar area, and a smart meter 
might give them important information to change behaviour and procedures in 
the household.  
2.4 
Feedback 
Providing feedback on energy consumption to the consumer has, through many 
studies, proven itself an important technique when talking about residential 
energy savings [2], [3], [6], [15], [38].  
 
If the consumer is interested in saving energy prior to feedback, the feedback is 
more useful. The challenge is to give useful feedback, that changes behaviour and 
habits, and have an impact on energy consumption, and in the current study, also 
the indoor environment. Several studies show that the feedback needs to be 
continuously, direct and frequent to influence energy consumption. Affordances 
in household energy needs to be visible to the users, not to cause direct change, 
but to create interest prior to the change and withhold it [7], [9]. 
 
The main goal of providing feedback is to make energy reduction tangible to the 
consumer, and provide them with useful tools and aid their decision-making 
process in the direction of energy saving, with specific information [8]. It is 
important to make the feedback as simple and understandable as possible, to

Direct feedback is known to influence the energy consumption with 5-15%. Direct 
feedback could be a meter, or some other direct display of kind of the energy

example of indirect feedback could be a weekly feedback letter with processed 
data, which is the type of feedback provided in the current study together with 
real-time measurements.   
 
Direct feedback requires that the user displays an interest in the results. If the 
measurements are shown on a meter or website, it requires a direct action from 
the user to gain knowledge of the energy consumption. Studies have shown that 
direct feedback is more effective if followed up on, and the user develops

ownership of their own energy consumption [2]. Access to relevant comparison 
with either historic results or similar households have also proven to be an

To ensure that the feedback has a long-term effect, it should develop new habits

• At least monthly feedback 
• Detailed, appliance-specific breakdown of usage 
• Comparisons with previous periods 
2.4.2 
Visual Feedback 
A study in the UK tested how thermal images worked as visual feedback on energy 
consumption. The study suggests that using visuals is a strong remedy when 
urging people to save energy.  
Previous research suggests that visual, personalized, feedback is more efficient 
when trying to impact behavioural changes with the goal of saving energy. The 
visual feedback has proven itself easier to understand than text, and it is suggested 
that using visuals will make technical feedback more comprehensible to the 
occupants [8]. 
2.4.3 
Online Feedback 
A recent study with online displays in 10 households in Japan investigated online 
feedback. The results from this study showed a 9% saving on electricity, when 
installing information terminals, compared with a reference group. The study 
investigated, among other things, how interactive the users where with the screen, 
and the results showed that the interaction decreased gradually after the 
installation [4]. 
 
Online feedback does have some limitations, due to the fact that the user needs to 
be highly interactive and interested in the provided results to actively access the 
feedback [7]. Online feedback is a reasonably cheap and easy way of providing the

Chapter 2 ½ Background 
 
 
 
 
Page 10 of 104 
consumer with feedback, and make them realise the affordances in energy saving 
regulations in their homes.  
 
A study from Denmark investigated the use and effect of online feedback through 
a system called eButler [39]. The conclusion was that although the system created 
interest and intentions, it did not lead to an overall behavioural change and energy 
reduction. The system did not offer any tailored solution other than a visual 
feedback on the consumption, and the study suggests that with domestic 
households, a more interactive process is needed, together with an online 
solution.  
2.4.4 
Written Feedback 
The feedback presented in the current study consist of a personalized (on some 
level) feedback letter combined with an online solution from the commercial 
product Netatmo. Tailored, personalized information is said to have the greatest 
effect and motivate action, and the provided feedback in the current study was 
designed based on this [8], [15].  
 
A study conducted at DTU in 2015 included a survey on different feedback letters 
and their content. The study showed that the users preferred the results presented 
in a simple way - in either pie charts or histograms. They also liked an overall 
assessment of the dwelling, but got confused if the feedback contained too many 
numbers [40].  
 
The final design of the feedback letter, used in the current study, was made based 
on a PhD study that investigated how to design a useful letter, and how the 
feedback procedure should be. The PhD study concluded that a feedback letter 
should be easy to understand, with few numbers, with social comparisons and with 
an overall assessment of the measurements in the previous period. The feedback 
process in the PhD study started with an introduction letter, that explained the 
letters content thoroughly. The study found significant improvements in the 
indoor environment for the partitioning dwellings that received the letter 
compared to a reference group [11].    
2.5 
Occupancy 
Occupancy models have been discussed and reviewed in former studies, because 
presence and understanding occupant behaviour is an important parameter in 
interactive energy reduction [35]. Detecting occupancy is difficult, and several 
sensor-based methods are used in the literature [41], [42]. 
 
==PAGE:23==
 
 2.5 Occupancy 
 
Page 11 of 104 
A study tried combining different measurements to detect presence, both in a test 
setup and in a real-world setup. The combined parameters were CO2, noise and 
volatile organic compounds (VOC) concentration, together with a Passive Infrared 
sensor (PIR sensor). The study got good results, with a combined accuracy up to 
78% for the real household [42].  
 
Another method is image-based, and relies on camera technology to detect 
presence, which can be done with up to 99% certainty. This solution is not suitable 
in dwellings, were the occupants could perceive it as a violation of privacy if 
cameras were installed [42].   
 
The most used method with PIR sensors is not without flaws. A PIR sensor does 
not detect occupants sitting still, or how many occupants that are present. Other 
studies have used CO2 measurements to detect presence, but this solution 
presents even more uncertainties and are hard to implement without regulating it 
for the specific room or building volume [16], [42]. In the current study, PIR 
sensors will be combined with door openings to detect occupancy in the dwellings.

3 Method 
To investigate the effect of feedback on energy consumption and indoor 
environment, a field study was set up were CO2, relative humidity and indoor 
temperature was measured together with window opening and occupancy in 
dwellings. During 5 weeks, half of the participants received written feedback on 
the measurements, to reduce the energy consumption and improve the indoor 
environment. The experiment in the current study was set up and conducted in 
the period from January to May 2017. To conduct the investigation in a suitable 
quantity, an area with terraced houses was chosen near Copenhagen. A total of 45 
dwellings signed up for the experiment as a response to a sign-up letter. An 
example of the sign-up letter is seen in Appendix B.  
 
Two different types of equipment were used to measure indoor environment 
parameters, presence of occupants, window openings and adjustment of 
thermostats. The Netatmo Weather Station was used to measure indoor climate 
parameters, and sensors from Wireless Tag and Kumo were used to monitor the 
occupants’ window opening and thermostat behaviour, and presence in the house.  
The equipment was installed in the dwellings, as they gradually signed up, and the 
actual measuring period started when the desired number of participants was 
achieved. A total number of 50 participants was the goal, but to keep up with the 
time schedule, the enrolment of participants ended with number 46.

3.1.2 
Installation of Equipment  
The installation of the equipment had to take place when at least one of the 
residents was at home, to ensure that the equipment was placed in the most 
occupied rooms. The most used room in waken hours were often the living 
room/kitchen (in one room).  
 
The installation took approximately 1 hour per house, which included an 
introduction to the occupants about the equipment. The project also included 
additional communication with the participants through emails. It was important 
to get an indication of which type of people were living in the area, to later use in 
the feedback design. 
 
There were some delays in the original schedule. The delivery of the equipment 
was delayed several times which delayed the installation process. Furthermore, 
the internet operator changed during the measuring period which resulted in more 
delays, since all equipment needed to be re-connected to the wireless internet.

3.2.1 
Thresholds 
To visualize the weekly measurements to the participants, the results were 
compared with pre-defined thresholds, and parted into a green, yellow and red 
zone.  
 
For temperature, no threshold was chosen, since the results would only be 
presented and compared with an average indoor temperature for the area, and not 
to limits. From Table B.2 in EN 15251, a lower comfort temperature of 20°C is 
recommended during the heating season, but it was decided not to give feedback 
on either a low or high threshold in this study [23]. If an occupant chose to have a 
lower indoor temperature than the recommended comfort temperature, but did 
not have problems with humidity, there was no reason to recommend otherwise, 
when the objective was to influence the energy consumption. Optimal operative 
temperature in a dwelling is also very individual, since it is very different how 
much clothes is worn indoors.  
 
For the CO2 measurements, the values from Table B.4 in EN 15251 were used as a 
basis. The thresholds used in this study is seen in Table 6 together with the 
corresponding values from the standard and the recommendations from Netatmo. 
(The Netatmo Station has very little information about recommended values, but 
can be set to give warnings when the CO2 concentration exceeds the thresholds.)

The tags were associated with the manager through the app, and were fastened in

sensitivity of 80%.

4 Feedback 
To evaluate the effect of feedback on indoor environment and energy 
consumption in the 45 dwellings, 22 of the households were chosen to receive 
weekly feedback. Several former studies showed that frequent, individual feedback 
was most beneficial, and the feedback process was conducted on a weekly basis 
[2], [47]. In this research, the feedback was provided as a feedback letter on paper, 
to ensure that the participants saw it, and to give them a possibility to discuss it if 
they were more people in the household. The design of the letter will be discussed 
and presented in this chapter. 
 
The feedback included measurements of the CO2 concentration, the relative 
humidity and the indoor temperature in the two most occupied rooms. The CO2 
concentration and relative humidity was evaluated and compared with thresholds, 
which will be further described later in the chapter. The temperature 
measurements were only displayed in a graph in the letter, and not evaluated 
because the participants might find it intrusive to receive feedback on their 
comfort temperature. A comparison with the average indoor temperature for the 
area was made every week, and this method is well founded in the literature as a 
mean of giving useful feedback [3], [14], [15]. 
 
The Netatmo Whether Station also provided an online solution, where the 
occupants could access the measurements in real-time either on a website or a 
mobile application.   
4.1 
Feedback Groups 
The participants were divided into a reference and a feedback group of equal sizes. 
The two groups had to be as similar as possible concerning indoor environment, 
to make sure the results were comparable afterwards. Comparing the results with 
a reference group would help determine whether the differences was a result of a 
change in occupant behaviour, or change in physical metrics such as the outdoor 
temperature.  
 
The groups were divided based merely on indoor environment data from a two-
week period (Week 8 and 9) prior to the reference period. Several other metrics of 
division was considered. Among them was age or number of occupants in the 
==PAGE:40==
Chapter 4 ½ Feedback 
 
 
 
 
Page 28 of 104 
household, size or type of house, behavioural pattern of the occupants or a 
combination of one of these and the measurements. It was chosen to base the 
grouping merely on the measurements.  
 
The calculations and data used is enclosed in Appendix A.1. 
4.1.1 
Creating the Groups 
When creating the groups, two different approaches were conducted. The main 
aim was to make the measurements from the two-week period comparable 
(temperature, CO2 concentration and relative humidity) despite the different 
ranges. In both cases, the mean values for the three parameters from the period 
were used to sort the groups.  
 
The first approach was, to sum the mean values of the measurements from each 
dwelling as a percentage of the median of all mean values (temperature, CO2 
concentration and relative humidity). The sum was then sorted from smallest to 
largest, and every second dwelling was in the feedback group. This approach would 
give preferential treatment to the CO2 measurements, as the range of CO2 
concentration is much higher than for the two other parameters. 
 
The other approach was to sum the mean values as a percentage of the range of 
the mean values for each parameter. This would give all three parameters equal 
weight.  
 
Both approaches would part the dwellings in two almost similar groups, and the 
results were examined and discussed thoroughly before a decision was made.  
Since both indoor environment and energy consumption was interesting to this 
study, and not only indoor air quality and thus CO2, the second method was 
chosen. This was because of the equalisation of parameters, which led to the most 
equal grouping with the aim at thought.  
 
The results from the approach with the summed percentage of median is displayed 
in Appendix D.

the weekly feedback letter. The results from both the PIR and Reed Kumo sensors

5.3 
CO2 Concentration  
The CO2 concentration describes the indoor air quality in the dwelling, and is 
measured in the two main rooms. The measurements of CO2 were processed for 
the occupied period. This meant that the measurements from the Netatmo were 
combined with the joined PIR/Reed data.  
 
The measurements of CO2 were used for the pie chart in the feedback letter. In the 
cases where there were no results from the PIR or Reed sensor, the CO2 diagram 
would be for the “simple” case – which was all time without consideration of 
occupancy. Due to this, the results for CO2 concentration presented to the 
occupants was not as accurate as planned in all cases. This solution was chosen 
rather than leaving the results out from the current week’s feedback letter. It was 
decided that an approximation with all results were better than no results, since 
the main goal of the feedback was to change the occupants’ behaviour.  
 
In the cases where the PIR or Reed Sensor was partly offline, the CO2 
concentration would, if it was possible, still be used in the final data processing, 
and Table 10 gives an overview of which weeks were insufficient. If the sensors 
were offline for a whole week, the occupancy will not be determined, and the CO2 
measurements was disregarded.  
5.4 
Relative Humidity 
The relative humidity was measured by both the Netatmo station and the Wireless 
Tag and sensors in the houses. The humidity measurements were used for the 
entire time, since a problem with too much moisture in a house will cause not only 
the occupants harm, but also influence the building materials, why all 
measurements are important. To evaluate the humidity in the house, the 
measurements from the Netatmo was used, and compared with thresholds 
obtained by combining the outdoor and indoor temperature with experimental 
thresholds for mould and moisture in the building. The thresholds were calculated 
as described in section 3.2.1.  
5.5 
Temperature 
The temperature was not processed during the feedback period, but only 
visualized in the letter for comparison. The outdoor temperature was used for 
creating the thresholds for the relative humidity, and to give an overview of the 
exterior changes through the project. This will be presented with the results.

7 Discussion 
The discussion contains an overall assessment of the results and the study, with a 
more detailed evaluation of the various parts in the sections below.  
 
The problematic measurements of window opening and adjustment of building 
controls affected the study in a large scale, since the occupant behaviour prior and 
during feedback could not be assessed and analysed. This, together with the lack 
of information on heat consumption, limited the analysis in various matters, since 
the comparison with energy consumption was one of the main objectives in the 
study. 
 
The measurements were carried out from February to May 2017, and thus were an 
overlap of the heating and cooling season. The outdoor temperature varied greatly 
during the period, from below 0°C to above 25°C, which affected the results. 12 
consecutive weeks in the heating season would have been preferable. Both 
environmental and personal parameters might differ greatly through the seasons, 
why a whole year study would be very interesting.  
7.1 
Energy Consumption 
A recent study from 2017 suggest that energy saving advice and methods might 
increase the consumption instead of decreasing it. The study investigates 
thermostat settings, and it would have been very interesting to compare the study 
with the adjustment of thermostats in the current study, since Revell (2017) [49] 
suggests that occupants use the thermostats set point completely wrong in many 
cases, due to differences in mentality of the occupants.  
 
The increase in temperature in all dwellings might be a result of more open 
windows, with no adjustment of the thermostats. In that case, the heat 
consumption through the feedback periods might have increased. This is 
unfortunately not possible to investigate further in this study.

7.2 
Thermal Environment 
Studies have shown that the indoor temperature is highly related to the outdoor 
temperature. Occupants tend to adjust the thermostat especially when the 
outdoor temperature decreases [13], which was the case several time through the 
current study. It would have been very interesting to have the results from the 
thermostat adjustment and the heat consumption to compare with the indoor 
temperature. The outdoor temperature varied the most during the reference 
period, which can have affected the indoor measurements compared with the 
more stable outdoor temperature during the feedback period. 
 
The results in both bedroom and living room from the current study confirms the 
trend, with decreasing indoor temperature through the two periods as the outdoor 
temperature increased. This can explain the decrease in indoor temperature for 
the reference group, whereas the gap between the feedback and reference group 
indicates an effect of the feedback on the indoor temperature.  
 
The feedback could affect the temperature in different ways – either the 
temperature decreased due to a lower set point on the thermostats as a response 
to the weekly tip on the matter, or, more likely, the decrease was a result of more 
frequently window openings. The correlation can be further explored through 
statistical methods e.g. linear regression.  
 
It is difficult to evaluate the overall thermal comfort in dwellings, since it is 
difficult to assume a standard met and clo value. (Met value describes the activity 
level and metabolism, and clo value the insulation of clothing.) Occupants both 
dress and act in complete different matters in their homes, and the large 
temperature range is probably a result of this.  
7.3 
Indoor Air Quality 
The indoor air quality was assessed based on the CO2 measurements. The 
concentration of CO2 in the houses was generally high, compared to the limits set 
by the Danish Building Regulation and suggested values in EN 15251 [23], [27].  
 
The boxplots showed that just under 40% of the dwellings had an initial CO2 
concentration below the recommended 1000 ppm, for 75% of the time. Some 
dwellings had alarming high CO2 levels, which should be attended to [19].  
 
When comparing the CO2 levels from the two groups with the thresholds, a clear 
difference was not seen. A difference between the initial distributions in the 
reference period might have influenced the measurements, and the lack of 
==PAGE:95==
 
 7.4 Relative Humidity 
 
Page 83 of 104 
significant improved results for the feedback group can be caused by unequal 
groups. When comparing the overall distribution in the zones for more than 10% 
of the time, it was very clear that the CO2 level was generally better in the 
reference group, from the beginning. The CO2 concentration above 1500 ppm was 
largely reduced in the living room for the feedback and not the reference group, 
which was the only notable difference between the two groups concerning CO2 
levels. 
 
The feedback provided in this study did improve the indoor air quality during the 
5 weeks, just not a lot. The mean CO2 levels decreased with 100 ppm more for 
bedrooms and 60 ppm more for living rooms in the dwellings that received 
feedback than in the reference dwellings. Even though the difference is relatively 
small, it still indicates an influence of the weekly feedback on the occupant 
behaviour. The CO2 levels also decreased in the reference dwellings during the 
feedback period, which is probably caused by higher outdoor temperatures and 
thus more natural ventilation in all dwellings, together with the possibility of an 
unequal initial grouping. 
 
The CO2 level often increased above 1500 ppm in the bedroom. This probably 
happened during the night, where the air change rate was small it the window was 
closed. Most dwellings have two occupants, and relatively small bedrooms. During 
most of both periods, the outdoor temperature was lower than 10°C at night, 
which might have refrained the occupants from leaving the window open while 
they slept. An improvement in the CO2 concentration might be difficult to obtain 
with closed windows.   
7.4 
Relative Humidity 
The relative humidity was generally higher than the calculated thresholds during 
the reference period. The results were best evaluated based on the percentage-
wise distribution of measurements in the green, yellow and red zone. Prior to the 
interference, the dwellings had much too high relative humidity, compared to the 
calculated thresholds. Just under 20% of the dwellings had mean values in the 
green zone for 75% of the time, which means that more than 80% of the houses 
have a risk of mould and condensation that can damage the building materials on 
the long-term, and affect the service life of the building [31], [32].  
 
During the feedback period, the relative humidity generally decreased for both 
groups. The decrease was more notable for the feedback group, when assessing 
the boxplots, but also when evaluating the measurements compared to the 
thresholds, the feedback clearly influenced the relative humidity. Very few 
dwellings in the feedback group had any measurements above the top threshold 
==PAGE:96==
Chapter 7 ½ Discussion 
 
 
 
 
Page 84 of 104 
(calculated based on the 75% relative humidity that damages cause mould) during 
the feedback period, and the difference between the two groups are large.  
 
The difference in percentage-wise improvement of mean values also show a clear 
difference between the values from the two groups, with largest improvements for 
the feedback group. The relative humidity was generally higher in the reference 
group than the feedback group in the reference period, which made a larger 
potential to decreasing values of humidity, but the results did not indicate this 
trend.  
 
The general difference in relative humidity is immediate greater than for CO2 for 
most households, when assessing the more detailed results presented in the 
boxplots. This indicates that the decrease in relative humidity does not only derive 
from increased ventilation in general. This might have one or more of the 
following explanations. The air will often get humid due to an action in the 
household – cooking, laundry or bathing. If the tenants have been attentive to the 
problem with moisture in the air, they could easily air out immediately after an 
action that humidifies the air, and lower the overall relative humidity in the house. 
To ventilate enough to lower the general CO2 level is another thing, and requires 
more frequent and longer window openings.  
 
The Tip of the Week often recommended the user to ventilate the room after one 
of the actions mentioned above, where the tips for fresh air were more general and 
advised them to air out “at least three times a day” and thus had a more passive 
wording.  
7.5 
Feedback Process 
The effect of the feedback was the main topic in the current study, and the results 
of both CO2 and relative humidity measurements indicated a difference in 
improvement for the feedback group. 
 
The trends found in earlier studies of energy saving feedback was the ground for 
the feedback letter. The design of the letter was discussed with several third 
parties, but a survey among the participants were not made. This was partly to 
avoid an increased Hawthorne Effect [50], where behaviour changes just because 
of the observation. Some effect is inevitable when doing research with human 
samples that are aware of the observation, but to keep the effect at its minimum, 
a survey was not conducted. The participants in the study were not initially told 
that the research concerned the effect of feedback, but just that the measurements 
were made to evaluate the state of the indoor environment.  
==PAGE:97==
 
 7.5 Feedback Process 
 
Page 85 of 104 
Another reason why a survey was not conducted was the timeframe of the project. 
The results from the survey made in the PhD project concerning a similar study 
was also used as reference [11]. 
 
Many things concerning the feedback process included a great range of 
uncertainty. The community in the area where the study was conducted is very 
involved with each other. The feedback group were asked not to tell their 
neighbours about the weekly tips and the general state of their own indoor 
environment, to avoid second-handily giving the reference group feedback as well. 
Even so, the offhand impression was that the tenants discussed the current study 
amongst them through both periods, and this might have had some effect on the 
reference group.  
 
A study from the United States suggests that more vivid feedback have a greater 
effect. They investigated the effect on hot water use for virtual handwashing and 
showering with Virtual Reality equipment that simulated water use. They 
discovered a difference between the ones that got vivid, individual feedback and 
the ones that did not [51]. This method might be a little controversial and 
unrealistic to implement, especially in a larger scale, but the idea is interesting. It 
would be interesting to investigate how more vivid images of the effect of for 
example poor indoor air quality would affect the occupant behaviour.   
 
The two groups were parted based on data from a two-week period prior to the 
reference period, and the process included only 32 out of the total 45 participating 
dwellings. An ideal scenario would have included all dwellings in the grouping 
process, to ensure that the groups were highly comparable. The groups could have 
been investigated further, and maybe it would have given more accurate results to 
change the groups after the control (with data from week 10 and 11), or include a 
larger time span in the grouping. Overall, the grouping could have been more 
accurate with the chosen method, or another approach could have been used.  
7.5.1 
Online Feedback 
Only 21% of the feedback group created a user account at Netatmo, which gave 
them access to the measurements in their home. The comparison between the 
households that downloaded the application and the ones that did not showed no 
correlation with larger improvement for the online feedback, which was a bit 
surprising. 
 
The literature and former studies suggested that frequent, individual feedback had 
the greatest effect on energy consumption[2], [3], [11], [52]. Since the number of 
occupants that used the online solution was so low, it is difficult to evaluate on 
the results in the current study. The lack of significant improvement as a result of 
==PAGE:98==
Chapter 7 ½ Discussion 
 
 
 
 
Page 86 of 104 
online feedback might indicate that the process concerning the online solution 
was insufficient. It could be due to one or more of the following reasons.  
 
1. The online feedback solution from Netatmo was not presented and 
explained enough from the beginning, because of the precise introduction 
at the webpage or mobile application itself. This could present a barrier for 
some of the occupants, since the introduction was in English only.  
 
2. The online solution from Netatmo had a high demand of active 
involvement from the participants, since they had to create an account to 
access the results, and this could also trigger a barrier.  
 
3. The feedback letters where distributed on a weekly basis, which might be 
an appropriate interval for receiving feedback. If the need and interest for 
more feedback had been present, perhaps more households had used the 
opportunity.  
 
Following the feedback period, the reference group got access to their 
measurements through Netatmo. After just one week, 39% of the households had 
made an account to access the website. This indicates that the curiosity of those 
in the reference group, who have been “left out” in the feedback process, were 
greater than for the feedback group who received the letters on a weekly basis. It 
might be that the barrier presented by the online solution for the feedback group 
were lower for the reference group due to this curiosity.  
 
To see if this curiosity transferred to the feedback group after the feedback period 
stopped, it was investigated if more households had accessed the online solution 
after two weeks without feedback, but no change was discovered. It seems that the 
reference group were more interested in the measurements because they did not 
receive any feedback, and perhaps this trend of initial curiosity can be explored 
further in another study.  
7.6 
Method and Equipment 
The method of the experiment was chosen based on the earlier studies and 
experiences with the equipment. The Netatmo stations were very easy to set up, 
but some of them had an unstable connection to Wi-Fi, which resulted in loss of 
data. The largest problem with the Netatmo stations were due to the change of 
internet operator in the area, why all stations were manually re-connected to the 
router. The process was time consuming and resulted in periods with no data for 
some dwellings, which was very unfortunate for the overall results. 
 
==PAGE:99==
 
 7.6 Method and Equipment 
 
Page 87 of 104 
Another concern with the use of Netatmo is that the station recalibrates the CO2 
sensor automatically every week. It assumes that the CO2 concentration lowers to 
400 ppm at least once a week, and calibrates accordingly. Furthermore, it is not 
possible to turn the automatic re-calibration off [45]. This is a large uncertainty, 
since the calibration was not controlled during the study. It was assumed that the 
initial calibration was correct, since the equipment was new, but the data could 
have been examined for minimum concentrations when the house was unoccupied 
for longer periods of time, in order to control the re-calibration of the stations.   
 
The Tag Manager, which was connected to the internet through a cable, had a very 
unstable connection to the internet, and often needed re-installation. Most of the 
time, the users could do it without assistance, but it still caused delays in the 
project.   
 
The feedback letter included the CO2 levels for occupied time – unless the Wireless 
equipment had been offline, in which case the results would be “simple”, with CO2 
results for all time in the previous week. The simple case would probably be better 
than for occupied hours only, and the results could give the occupants a wrong 
impression of the air quality in the dwelling.  
7.6.1 
Feedback Groups 
The grouping process was important, and can have influenced the results. It was 
clear when comparing the results that the two approaches gave similar results 
with only little difference, but that the one with summed percentage of median 
favoured the CO2 measurements too much, compared with the other approach 
with summed percentage of range where all parameters were equally adjusted. 
 
As mentioned in section 4.1, several factors could have been used to part the 
participants into the two groups. The number of occupants in the dwellings 
affected the indoor environment, since humans produce both heat, moisture and 
CO2. To improve the experiment, this factor could have been included in the 
grouping process, to ensure that the sizes of household were similar in the two 
groups, and did not distort the assessment of the measurements.  
7.6.2 
Occupancy 
The method of detecting occupancy was chosen based on the available equipment, 
and the combination of PIR and Reed sensors seemed reasonable. Further 
combinations with sound and CO2 measurements were considered.  
 
The combination with sound was bypassed because of the risk of mistakes. The 
sound level in the dwellings was observed and evaluated during the initial weeks 
of the project, and it varied greatly among the households. A general algorithm 
==PAGE:100==
Chapter 7 ½ Discussion 
 
 
 
 
Page 88 of 104 
for occupancy based on sound level was considered difficult to create without 
large uncertainties.   
 
The chosen method with a combination of two PIR sensors with a Reed sensor was 
not perfect. The sensors were placed in strategically locations in the dwelling, to 
obtain the most accurate impression of presence in the house. This solution did 
not show if an occupant remained sitting still without going to the bathroom for 
hours, until the front door opened and another occupant entered, but it gave an 
acceptable idea of presence in the house.    
 
The accuracy of the used method with PIR and Reed sensors was not evaluated in 
this study, which would have been interesting to do to assess the results more 
accurately. With the current measurements of occupancy, the number of errors is 
unknown, which can have affected the CO2 results, since they were linked with the 
occupied hours. 
7.7 
Further Work 
The study and especially all the collected data could be the root to several further 
investigations. The Wireless Compass Tags ended up working, with manual 
calibration, and the results from the window opening will be highly interesting 
and relevant to combine with the findings in this study. The effect of feedback on 
window opening have been investigated in earlier studies, but the Wireless 
equipment can, when calibrated, give precise measurements of how much the 
windows are opened, which is a new factor to evaluate and combine with indoor 
environment and energy consumption. The improvement of measurements 
indicated a difference in occupant behaviour, but it was difficult to conclude how 
and when the occupants behaved differently. It was clear that the houses were 
ventilated more through the feedback period, since both CO2 levels and relative 
humidity improved, but a more thorough investigation that could clarify whether 
the windows have been open for a longer period, or have just been opened more 
frequently, would be very interesting. The number of used windows might also 
have increased, which would have been revealed if the Wireless Tags for windows 
had worked as initial thought. 
 
The data on heat consumption would add another element to the findings. The 
provided feedback did not include feedback on energy, but it would be interesting 
to evaluate if and how large an effect the feedback had on the heat consumption 
during the project period.  
 
The observed differences between the groups could be interpreted through a 
variety of other statistical methods, including linear regression. A linear 
regression analysis could indicate which outer parameters that had affected the

8 Conclusion 
The findings in the current study suggests that the indoor environment in the 45 
dwellings were poor, prior to the feedback period. All results indicate that the 
energy feedback used in the study improved the indoor environment through the 
feedback period. 
 
The indoor temperature decreased in almost all dwellings through the period, 
which literature suggests is due to warmer outdoor temperatures. A larger 
temperature decrease was found in the dwellings that received feedback, and some 
effect of the feedback were seen. Because of problems with the equipment it was 
not possible to evaluate on the occupant interaction with the building controls, 
and it was not clear if the temperature decrease was due to window opening or 
thermostat adjustment.  
 
The CO2 levels in most houses were high prior to the intervention, compared to 
suggested values of maximum 1000 ppm. The feedback had some effect on the CO2 
levels, but in most cases the indoor air quality remained poor after the feedback 
period. A difference between improvements in the feedback and reference group 
was detected, even though it was small.      
 
Relative humidity was also influenced by the feedback, and the improvement was 
large. Before the intervention, most houses had too high humidity and a risk of 
mould. The feedback reduced this risk greatly. 
 
In addition to the written, weekly feedback letter, an online mobile application 
was made available for the feedback group. No significant effect of the online 
feedback compared with the letter was found.  
 
An effect of feedback on the indoor environment was clear, but it was not possible 
to evaluate the effect on energy consumption, since the needed data was not 
available. Furthermore, it was not possible to assess the precise effect on the 
values. 
 
Generally, the provided feedback had a positive effect on the indoor environment 
during the feedback period.

Appendix H 
List of “Tip of the Week” 
 
Tips for ”Fresh air” 
• Luft ud i min 5 minutter, gerne med gennemtræk, mindst 3 gange dagligt. 
• Luft ud mindst 3 gange om dagen, selvom det er koldt. 
• Sørg for at åbne vinduerne i soveværelset/stuen oftere og i længere tid. 
• Hvis muligt, åben døren til soveværelset mens du sover. 
• Ved at trykke på den grå Netatmo station i stuen kan du se status på 
luftkvaliteten – luft ud når den er orange. 
 
Tips for ”Humidity” 
• Ved at hæve temperatuen vil du sænke luftfugtigheden i rummet. 
• Husk at lufte ud efter madlavning og bad, og at bruge emhætte ved 
madlavning 
• For høj luftfugtighed kan medføre skimmelsvamp – husk at lufte ud flere 
gange dagligt. 
• Husk at lufte ud i forbindelse med tøjvask, og især hvis du tørrer tøj 
indendørs. 
• Det kan hjælpe på for høj luftfugtighed at tørre badet af efter brug.
