Abstract 
CO-RO Foods experience stability problems with some of their Juice products. The problem CO-RO Foods is 
facing is that some Juice batches loses viscosity over time, while others do not, it seems that the food 
additive carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) is degraded over time. CMC is added to provide viscosity and 
stabilization to the Juices.  
This project showed that CMC-ase like activity could be measured, after dialysis and ultracentrifugation (at 
least 3 times of concentration) of Juice concentrate delivered from CO-RO Foods, by DNS assay after 24 h of 
incubation at 37 ⁰C. The reducing sugars increased in the Juice, with viscosity degrading capability, if it was 
incubated with either phosphoric acid swollen cellulose (PASC) or CMC. Incubation with Proteinase K made 
the activity disappear, which indicate that the CMC degrading component is a protein. These data suggests 
the activity measured is caused by an endoglucanase. It was shown that the Juice could be further 
concentrated by ammonium sulphate precipitation at saturation percentages between 30 and 70 with a 
following dialysis. It is concluded that further concentration and sample handling is necessary to determine 
what is causing the viscosity degradation of the Juice. The results in the report suggest that there is 
interference by other substances in the Juice, as sufficient dialysis seems to be necessary to detect activity.

CO-RO Foods oplever stabilitets problemer med nogle af deres Juice produkter. Problemet for CO-RO Foods 
er, at nogle Juice batches mister viskositet over tid, mens andre ikke gør. Tilsætningsstoffet CMC bliver

kunne måles, efter dialyse og ultracentrifugering (mindst 3 gange koncentration) af Juice koncentrat leveret 
fra CO-RO Foods, ved hjælp af DNS-assay efter 24 timers inkubation ved 37 ⁰C. Reducerende sukre steg i 
den Juice med stabilitetsproblemer, hvis det blev inkuberet med enten PASC eller CMC. Inkubation med

bestemme ,hvad der er årsag til viskositets nedbrydningen i Juicen. Resultaterne i rapporten viser, at det er 
muligt at andre stoffer i Juicen påvirker aktiviten, derfor anses det for at være vigtigt med tilstrækkelig 
dialyse af Juicen.

1. Introduction 
In this section the general processing of fruit Juice will be shortly described and the material which is the 
subject of study in this project. The CO-RO Foods juice problem will be described. I will also shortly go 
through the former research and results on this project. 
The the substrates, which have been included in this work, will get a short description and what they are 
used for in this project. The enzyme group cellulases will be shortly described. Then a brief description of 
some compounds found in pineapple juice. 
1.1 
Processing of fruit Juice 
CO-RO Foods is a company which produces fruit based soft drinks. The company is based in Frederikssund 
and sells its product all over the world. The raw material for the product is fruit Juice. In the countries of 
origin the fruits are squeezed and evaporated to one sixth of the original volume with a brix value 
(Frederick B. 1942) of 65°. The Juice concentrates are then pasteurized and exported either aseptically in 
drums or frozen in tanks. Once CO-RO Foods receives the fruit concentrates samples are taken out for 
quality control.  
CO-RO Foods main product is made from syrup which is stabilized by use of carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC). 
The syrups exported contain 20 % sugar, 16 % fruit Juice, citric acid, water and 1% CMC (as well as sulphite 
and sorbic acid). The typically brix value of the syrup is 40° and the pH 3.2. Once arrived at the bottler, 
sugar, citric acid and water is added to reach a brix value of 62° at pH 3.5 in the final product (“Sunquick”). 
All the syrups exported are stable at ambient temperature for approximately one year even in the tropics. 
(information obtained from Christian Sylvest Vestergaard)

the separation problem and the decrease in viscosity found in the 
quality control. As stated earlier CMC is added to stabilize the

Juice and it is suspected that CMC degradation is responsible for the decrease in viscosity. 
CO-RO uses many kinds of fruits Juices but mostly orange. Juices with orange, peach, pineapple, 
passionfruit and mango are the Juices which seems to have the separation problem, while neither apple 
nor grape concentrates appear to have separation problems. For those which do react, there are great

All the suppliers claim that they don’t use enzymes for extraction. CO-RO Foods test for CMC degrading 
activity by incubating the Juice with a 3% CMC solution at 40°C for 72 h. Sodium benzoate (1700 ppm) assures 
that changes are not microbial. The drop in viscosity before and after incubation is measured. The values found 
are converted to cellulolytic activity by use of a standard curve (recorded as viscosity drop as a function of 
purified Aspergillus niger cellulase (of known activity) in the same system. From this curve the cellulolytic 
activities of the fruit Juices, are quantified to be in the order of 1 mU/g. It should be mentioned, that this test is 
quite imprecise (coefficient of variance ~15%) and unacceptably time-consuming.   
CO-RO Foods hypothesizes that the problem originates from cellulase being present in some, but not all, fruit 
Juice batches. Most likely the “cellulase” originates from molded fruits in the primary production.  
Ideally the enzymatic activity would be inactivated in the two pasteurization processes, i.e. after evaporation (at  
the Juice concentrate manufacturer) and before bottling (at CO-RO), but certain types of cellulases are known to 
be quite heat resistant (Asha et al. 2012; Li et al. 2011). Also, the high dry matter of the concentrate could 
protect cellulase against heat denaturation. If this is the case, it would make sense, that low cellulolytic activity 
degrades the CMC network over time, causing the separation observed. (Information obtained from Christian 
Sylvest Vestergaard) 
 This project is going to investigate what is causing the CMC degradation.

Substrates 
1.4.1 Carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) 
Cellulose Gum or Carboxymethyl Cellulose (CMC) is a chemical derivative of cellulose where some of the 
hydroxyl groups (-OH) are substituted with carboxymethyl groups (-CH2COOH). In figure 2 the repeating 
unit of sodium CMC is shown. CMC is produced by reaction between alkali cellulose and sodium 
monochloroacetate under controlled conditions. The properties of cellulose gum depend on the degree of 
substitution and the length of the cellulose chains. The degree of substitution (DS) is the number of 
carboxymethyl groups per glucose unit. There can be up to three substitutents on each glucose unit thus 
the maximal degree of substitution (DS) is 3. CMC is soluble when DS is above 0.4 commercially CMC is 
usually less than 1.5. (Karlsson et al. 2002)

Cellulose gum is non-toxic and becomes very viscous when combined with water. It is used as a thickener 
for and as an emulsion stabilizer in products like ice cream. Cellulose gum is also used in personal 
lubricants, diet pills, water-based paints, detergents and paper coatings. 
CMC is known for being non-reproducible (with respect to degree of hydrolysis), this is only linear within a 
limited degree of hydrolysis of CMC to glucose due to interference by substitutents. In this case, 
substituted glucose units available in different CMC’s are also accessible to cellulase that caused non-
reproducibility (Liu et al. 2009; Eveleigh et al. 2009). In addition the quantity of reducing sugars produced 
will be highly affected by the particular type of CMC (Mandels et al. 1976; Eveleigh et al. 2009). These 
difficulties arise from two important variables: The degree of substitution (DS) that will affect its solubility 
and the degree of polymerization that will affect its viscosity. 
To prevent CMC from degradation in Foods products the product can be heat treated to kill 
microorganisms while having little effect of CMC properties. A preservative is needed to prevent viscosity 
degradation because even trace amounts of cellulases can affect viscosity if introduced. Under certain 
conditions CMCs are susceptible to chemical degradation. Heat and UV light can affect viscosity and 
oxidative degradation can happen at alkaline conditions and is accelerated by the presence of metal ions 
like Fe2+. Oxidants like chlorine and hydrogen peroxide cause degradation of the cellulose chain. CMC is 
precipitated at 50% or saturated solution of ammonium sulfate. (Aqualon carboxy methyl cellulose 
customer manual; CP Kelco world’s leading hydrocolloid solutions manual)

For the experimental work in this project some other substrates have been representing polysaccharides

Barley β glucan consists of linear unbranched polysaccharides of β-D-glucose like found in cellulose, but

Mannan is an insoluble linear polysaccharide consisting of 1,4 β linked mannose units and the chain 
terminates either in a mannopyranose or a galactopyranose unit and contains decorations of 1,6 β linked

Laminarin is a linear polysaccharide primarily consisting of 1,3 β glucan chains with 1,6 β glucan branch 
points with a ratio of 3:1. (NISIZAWA et al. 1963)

1.5 
Cellulases 
The complete degradation of cellulose to glucose requires the action of at least three types of enzymes: 
endo-1,4-β-glucanase, exo-1,4-β-exoglucanase (cellobiohydrolase) and β-glucosidase (Väljamäe 2002). In 
addition, systems producing hydroxyl radicals based on cellobiose dehydrogenase, quinone redox cyclin or 
glycopeptide-based Fenton reaction are involved in degradation of several plant wall components, 
including cellulose. (Baldrian & Valásková 2008) 
1.5.1 Cellobiohydrolase 
Cellobiohydrolases (CBH) are producing predominantly cellobiose from insoluble cellulose and having very 
low activity on CMC  (Schülein 1997). The enzymes are monomeric with molecular masses typically 
between 50 and 65 kDa. Catalytic optima of cellobiohydrolases between 4 and 5 pH and a temperature 
optima between 37 and 60 ⁰C. (Baldrian & Valásková 2008) 
CBH I is regarded as a strict exoenzyme. CBH1 is able to hydrolyze crystalline cellulose extensively, although 
the rate of degradation is slow. The main product of hydrolysis is cellobiose (Fägerstam & Pettersson 1980). 
The hydrolysis proceeds via a double displacement mechanism resulting in retention of configuration at the 
anomeric C1 carbon (Knowles et al. 1988; Claeyssens et al. 1990). It has been shown that the hydrolysis of 
cellooligosaccharides and cellulosic substrates proceeds from the reducing end (Barr et al. 1996; Nutt et al.

1998). Hydrolysis of para-nitrophenol cellobioside by CBH I is strongly inhibited by cellobioside with a 
competitive inhibition constant around 20µM (Claeyssens et al. 1989; Vonhoff et al. 1999). 
CBHII prefers to act on cellulose from the nonreducing (Chanzy & Henrissat 1985; Nutt et al. 1998; Boisset 
et al. 1998). The hydrolysis product is mainly cellobiose and hydrolysis proceeds via single displacement 
mechanism resulting in inversion of configuration at the anomeric C1 carbon atom (Knowles et al. 1988; 
Claeyssens et al. 1990). It has been shown that hydrolysis by CBH II has some endo-character, placing the 
enzyme between the strict exo-glucanases and endoglucanases according to its mode of action (Teeri 1997; 
Boisset et al. 1998). The kcat values for CBH II acting on cellooligosaccharides increases with increasing DP of 
the substrate and the maximum kcat is observed with cellohexaose (Harjunpaa et al. 1996). 
1.5.2 Endoglucanases 
Endoglucanases can lower viscosity of a CMC solution (Schülein 1997) by cutting randomly in cellulose 
chains at 1,4 β bonds. Endoglucanases are monomeric with molecular masses between 22 and 45 kDA and 
show catalytic optima at pH between 4.0 and 5.0 temperature optima is between 50 and 70 ⁰C (Baldrian & 
Valásková 2008). The endoglucanases of thermophilic fungi are thermostable and their molecular masses 
range from 30 to 100 kDa they show optimal activity between 55 and 80 ⁰C at pH 5 (Pérez et al. 2002). CMC 
and amorphous cellulose (e.g. PASC) are good substrates of endoglucanases and indicate that the enzyme 
activity is mainly directed towards amorphous regions in the cellulose molecule. EG I (Cel7B) from T. reesei 
has significant xylanolytic activity and acting on cellulose produces nearly equal amounts of glucose and 
cellobiose together with some cellotriose (Väljamäe 2002). EG II (Cel5A) from T. reesei belongs to the 
glycoside hydrolase family 5 and hydrolyses of glycosidic bonds via a double displacement mechanism to 
give products comprised of equal amounts of glucose and cellobiose together with traces of cellotriose 
(Väljamäe 2002). The mechanism of activity is not known but it is proposed that it can initiate cellulolytic 
attack through disintegration of cellulose fibers (Väljamäe 2002).

1.5.4 Oxidative decomposition of cellulose 
The detection of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) production by several fungi led to the proposal of a degradation 
pathway of cellulose based on the Fenton reaction: 
𝐻2𝑂2 + 𝐹𝑒2+ + 𝐻+ →𝐻2𝑂+ 𝐹𝑒3+ + 𝑂𝐻• 
This reaction is a well-recognized route of OH• production in biological systems. Hydrogen peroxide is 
produced by white and brown (Ritschkoff & Viikari 1991) rot fungi by the action of enzymes such as glyoxal 
oxidase (Kersten & Kirk 1987), glucose oxidase (Kelley & Reddy 1986) and aryl alcohol oxidase (Guillén et al. 
1990; Muheim et al. 1990). Cellobiose dehydrogenase (CDH) is an extracellular enzyme produced by 
basidiomycetes and ascomycetes. It efficiently oxidizes cellobiose but also soluble cellodextrins, 
mannodextrins and lactose to their corresponding lactones using a wide spectrum of electron acceptors 
including quinones, phenoxyradicals, Fe3+, Cu2+, cytochrome c or triiodide ion (Henriksson et al. 2000; 
Zamocky et al. 2006). CDH is dependent on cellobiose to be functional (Baldrian & Valásková 2008). 
CDH is a monomeric protein of 90-110 kDa with glycosylation in the range of 10-20% and the pH optimum 
is between 4 and 5 (Schmidhalter & Canevascini 1993; Fang et al. 1998; Baminger et al. 2001). The 
temperature optimum is generally between 30 and 55 ⁰C (Henriksson et al. 1997; Baminger et al. 2001). 
CDH binds specifically to cellulose i.e. it does not bind to insoluble xylan, mannan, starch and chitin 
(Henriksson et al. 1997; Temp & Eggert 1999). Depolymerization with CDH, cellobiose, Fe3+ and H2O2 was 
demonstrated for CMC, water soluble xylan, radioactively labelled synthetic lignin (Henriksson et al. 1995), 
some depolymerisation occurred even without addition of H2O2 due to its formation by the enzyme itself 
(Nutt et al. 1997). CDH moderately enhances the activity of a crude mixture of cellulases and also of 
isolated cellobiohydrolases (Bao & Renganathan 1992; Igarashi et al. 1998).

(Ioannou, I.; Mohamed 2013). Exogenous ascorbic acid reduced PPO activity by about 90% in lettuce. POD 
did not appear to be closely related to browning in lettuce (Landi et al. 2013). 
A high total sugar content is observed in the Juices. A kinetic study on the 1,4-β-endoglucanase of 
Aspergillus niger ANL301 showed that Mn2+ and Fe2+ can induce endoglucanases while Mg2+, Ca2+, Cu2+, 
Hg2+, Zn2+ and EDTA inhibited the activity of the enzyme (Nwodo et al. 2011). An endoglucanase produced 
by Chalara paradoxa CH32 showed inhibition against Hg2+, Mg2+, Fe2+, Ag2+ and EDTA. Activity of the 
purified enzyme was also inhibited by the detergents Triton X-100, Tween80 and SDS. The enzyme was 
induced by Mn2+. (Lucas et al. 2001)

A few common proteins in pineapple Juice 
Bromelain is a protein which is found in pineapple Juice there is two kind of bromelain: stem bromelain and 
fruit bromelain. Bromelain has been widely used in textile, leather, brewing, food and medical care. The 
inactivation rate of bromelain increases significantly when temperature exceeds 60°C (pH 3.9). Bromelain is 
active at a wide range of pH and is most active at a pH of 3.9. The most suitable temperature for bromelain 
was found to be 50-60 °C (pH 3.9). Mg2+, Fe2+ and Ca2+ could promote activity while Cu2+, and Fe3+ had great 
inhibitory action. (Liang et al. 2011). A transcriptome study revealed that the pineapple had a small 
transcriptome dominated by metallothionein (Moyle et al. 2005).

Two pineapple concentrates was chosen for the project. The first Juice concentrate which I decide to call

2.1 
Storage of Juice concentrate 
Five L of Juice concentrate was received from CO-RO Foods. The Juice concentrates were stored at -20⁰C 
when received. An aliquot of 1 L was taken out and sodium azide was added to a final concentration of 
0.005% to avoid microbial contamination of the Juice concentrate. This aliquot was stored at 4⁰C in 
refrigerator.  
2.2 
Production of supernatant 
This experiment was done to make an efficient procedure to work with the Juice concentrate. The Juice 
concentrate was centrifuged at different dilutions by addition of milliQ water. Five different samples were 
made, nondiluted sample, 50%, 25%, 12.5% and 6.1% diluted sample for both Juice1 and Juice2. The 
samples were centrifuged at 21⁰C 14000 RPM in an Eppendorf centrifuge 5417R and the supernatant was 
transferred to new sample tube. When larger volumes needed to be centrifuged 40 mL centrifugal tubes 
and Sorvall RC5C centrifuge with SS34 fixed angle rotor at 23428g 30 min was used. After this the 
supernatant was filtered by a Sartorius stedim 0.45µM filter (serie nr. 25006—47). 
2.3 
Ultrafiltration with centrifugal tubes 
Juice solution was transferred to an Amicon Ultra-0.5 mL or an Amicon Ultra-15mL Centrifugal Filter (mostly 
10kDa filters was used). Eppendorf centrifuge 5417R was used with 14000g for 30 min for the small 
centrifugal filters. For larger volumes Amicon Ultra-15mL Centrifugal Filter and Eppendorf centrifuge 5810R 
at 3220g for 30 min were used. After centrifugation the permeate was collected and Juice solution was 
added to the filtrate and mixed by pipette. The centrifugation procedure was repeated until a minimum of 
10 times concentrated and followed by dialysis using Spectra/Por dialysis bags either with 6-8 or 12-14 kDa 
pore-size membrane. The dialysis was done in a 2L or 5L bucket where the buffer was changed each day 
and the process was in the first times done overnight but later monitored by DNS assay until the 
background of reducing sugars was almost zero. MilliQ water or 0.01 mM acetat buffer with 0.005% 
tween20 was used as dialysis buffer. 
2.4 
Ultrafiltration with SARTOFLOW® Slice 200 Benchtop System 
Ultrafiltration by using the crossflow system SARTOFLOW® Slice 200 Benchtop System was used with 
constant backpressure mode. The backpressure was adjusted to 0.3 bar as the back pressure must not

2.5 
Reducing sugar assay  
To measure CMC-ase activity in this project the reducing sugar assay (DNS assay) is chosen. The activity in 
the samples in this project is expected to be low. This is why the sample volume for this project is high. One 
hundred µL of sample and 100 µL substrate solution is added and mixed by pipette and incubation is done 
for 24 h. Then 300µL of 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid (DNS) solution is added to the sample and put on a 
preheated 95⁰C (double checked with thermometer) heat block Techne Dri-block DB3D for 15 min. 
Hereafter the sample is put on ice for 15 min. Some precipitate may form and therefore all samples is 
centrifuged with Heraeus Biofuge Pico centrifuge for 10 min at 10.000 RPM. The solution is now ready to be 
measured. Sample (150 µL) is transferred to a microtiterplate and read at 540nm by a Powerwave XS Biotek 
spectrophotometer. For translating the absorbance signal to reducing sugars a glucose standard curve is 
made from 020 mM and treated as other samples but no incubation needed.

standard in the linear range of this assay which is 8 µg/mL to approximately 80 µg/mL. The samples were

3.2 
Choice of strategy to investigate CMC-ase like activity  
In this section different methods will be presented and a short discussion of why this method was chosen 
and mention why it was chosen over other methods. 
To shortly summarize the information before the start of the project we knew orange, peach, pineapple, 
passion fruit and mango are amongst the most CMC reactive, while neither apple nor grape concentrates 
appear to be reacting with CMC. Another project could not detect activity in supernatant from concentrate 
delivered from CO-RO Foods which was concentrated 2 times. It also appeared to be difficult to perform 
ultrafiltration with the Juice concentrate. 
In this project we chose the assay method of activity by doing a reducing sugar assay. This assay is also 
called DNS assay and yields a yellow color (540 nm) after reaction of the sample at 95⁰C. This assay was 
chosen for the project as we don’t know the product(s) of the reaction happening in the Juice concentrate.

A drawback of using this assay is the Juice concentrate has very high content of reducing sugars as it 
contains a high amount of total sugars (see chapter 5.6) which result in a high background in the assay.  
Even though it is expected that the CMC-ase component is in the supernatant, the first thing needed to be 
done was to exclude the existence of the CMC-ase component in the insoluble material, as it has not been 
tested before.  
The cellulolytic activity in the Juices is regarded to be very low as it so far only had been detected by 
viscosity changes of the Juices, which is why concentration of the Juices is an essential step to try to detect 
activity. The chosen concentration strategy was to use ultrafiltration membrane with a molecular cutoff of 
10 kDa. Even though a former project had problems with ultrafiltration, this method was chosen because of 
the ease of the method and materials were available. The molecular cutoff of 10 kDa membrane was 
chosen because cellulases can vary much in mass. A dialysis of the supernatant from the Juices is regarded 
as an essential step as the DNS assay is sensitive to high background of reducing sugars in the Juices. To 
measure protein concentration the Bradford assay was chosen as method. Ammonium sulphate and 
acetone precipitation was chosen as methods for further concentration after ultrafiltration. Proteomics of 
the two Juices would be tried to be done by doing SDS-PAGE and following electrophoresis. This could 
reveal if any susceptible proteins in the Juices could be responsible for the CMC-ase activity.

4. Further work 
The reason for the viscosity drop in the Juices is not yet explained. Based on the conclusion of this report it 
is likely it is of enzymatic origin. It is recommended to add an extra purification step of ammonium sulphate 
precipitation, based on the ammonium sulphate experiment to further concentrate the Juice. 
It is necessary to get more time points on the activity to test if the reaction is finished or it still continues. 
A silver staining of the concentrated product will on a SDS-page can show proteins which could not be 
shown on a coomassie staining which was done in this project. The temperature experiment done in this 
project may need a repetition with material with a lower background of reducing sugars. 
A TLC of the Juice supernatant and retentate, before and after incubation, with and without CMC would be 
interesting to see if any sugar components increase and possibly determine which products. A zymogram 
could be used to further prove that CMC is degraded. 
There are indications based on the experiments that the reducing sugar background of the samples may 
affect the activity of the CMC-ase like component therefore it would be of interest to investigate if there is 
a correlation between dialysis of background and CMC-ase like activity. 
In the literature it is mentioned that cellobiose, glucose, gluconolactone, cellobiolactone, Hg2+, Mg2+, Fe2+, 
Ag2+, Mn2+, Zn2+, Ca2+, Cu2, EDTA, Triton X-100, Tween80 and SDS can affect cellulase activities. This could 
possibly give an indirect hint of the origin of the activity.

5. Conclusion 
In this project pineapple Juices from CO-RO Foods, which difference was one of them had viscosity 
degrading capabilities (Juice1), while the other didn’t had this capability (Juice2). A strategy was developed 
to investigate if the difference were enzymatic. It is expected based on knowledge from CO-RO Foods that 
the activity is low and it is their stabilization material CMC which is degraded. The Juices were concentrated 
by ultrafiltration and a following dialysis revealed that the reducing sugars increased with CMC as substrate 
(measured by DNS assay) in Juice1, however no clear correlation could be made by concentration and 
activity. The activity measured was low and 24 h of incubation was needed, the specific activity of Juice1 
was determined to be 2.22 · 10-11 U/mg. Juice1 also seemed to degrade PASC, this is an indication that the 
CMC-ase component in the Juice could be an endoglucanase. Measurement of the protein concentrations 
in the retentate after ultrafiltration revealed that the Juice1 had three times the protein content of Juice2, 
however the supernatant of Juice2 revealed a slightly higher protein concentration. There is an indication 
that sufficient dialysis may be important to display activity. It was shown that further purification could be 
achieved by making an ammonium sulphate precipitation at 3070 % of saturation with a following dialysis. 
It was shown that proteinase-K could deactivate the CMC-ase component in Juice1 which supports the 
hypothesis that the CMC-ase like component may be of protein origin.
