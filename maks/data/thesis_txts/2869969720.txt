1.1
Electroless Nickel Plating Theory
1.1.1
Ni-P
Summary
Electroless nickel plating is an autocatalytic process facilitated in solution between a
source of metal ions and a reducing agent via the presence of a catalytic metallic sur-
face.
The basics
Electroless Nickel Plating (ENP) is the process of chemically coating a surface in a ho-
mogeneous layer of metallic Nickel. This is done in an electrolyte solution, where metal
ions are reduced by an oxidation reaction on the metal surface by a chosen chemical re-
ductant. In this study, we are focused on nickel plating, but it is also possible to plate a
range of other metals and metalloids with a bit of care. The most commonly plated ones
being Ni-P, Ni-B, Cu and Ag.[Book page 2]
Electroless deposition is an electrochemical process that follows the principles of thermo-
dynamics and kinetics of electrochemical reactions. This is understood to be happening
by the potential difference between at least two half-cell reactions. An anodic, supplying
the electrons for the reduction of the metal ions, and a cathodic one that supplies the
metal ions being reduced into the metallic coating. Because of the reliance on the an-
odic half-cell for supplying the electrons to the reaction, a very limited number of suitable
reducing agents are actually available for electroless plating. The most common are re-
ferred to in the book by D.Fabienne, V. Veronique and B. Luiza [Reference book page
5] and are each suited for different metals and bath compositions. In this work the focus
will be on sodium hypophosphite as the main reducing agent but a few word will be given
on Sodium borohydride due to it‚Äôs use during the electrochemical activation of the sub-
strate. The combined reaction occurs under mixed potential control and the potential for
the reaction can be derived from the Nernst equation for each of the electrode potentials:

1.2
Substrates
1.2.1
Summary
In this project a range of substrates were used for plating attempts. The main was nickel
foam from the company Alantum. This porous substrate was chosen for its ease of plating
since it is conductive. Secondly, polyacrylonitrile (PAN) was used, as this project is a spin-
out of a previous project to nickel-coat on locally spun polymer nanofibers. The same
principles and methods were attempted with varied success. Finally, attempts were made
to coat polysulfone (PSU) Udel as the previously mentioned project found this polymer
better suited for the rigours an electrode is exposed to when running for extended time
periods.
1.2.2
Nickel Foam
For most of the project Alantum nickel foam was used for coating as this substrate consists
of a robust 3d structure of pure nickel that is easy to coat due to its catalytic properties.
It was therefore deemed a suitable trial medium for designing and testing different bath
compositions before applying these to polymer fibers which are harder to coat due to
the lower amount of nucleation sites. Since the nickel was already in a metallic state
the most important parameters for this substrate were cleaning and deoxidation. As any
surface pollution or oxide layers can block the catalytic surface hindering the adsorption
of reducing agent and nickel ions thereby preventing the coating process from occurring.
The depassivization would normally, in literature, be done by a chemical attack activation-
based treatment using either hydrochloric or sulfuric acid to remove the pollutants from the
surface layer. But in the interest of keeping the activation step for metallic and polymeric
substrates as close to each other as possible it was decided to use the same procedure for
both substrates. The applied procedure for both cleaning and activation were performed
as a combined one step electrochemical activation reaction by spraying the surface with
a mixture of water, ethanol and sodium borohydride. The ethanol and water acting as
cleaning agents against any grease, fat or oils on the surface, while the borohydride acts
as an activation agent reducing any oxides or non-metallic nickel. Any leftover reducing
agent on the surface also worked to enhance the initial plating of the substrate as sodium
borohydride is a strong reducing agent that can also be used in alkaline nickel plating.
1.2.3
Polymer
For polymeric substrates PAN and PSU Udel were both attempted. Both polymers were
used in the form of polymer fiber mats electrospun at DTU energy by supervisor Russo. To
allow for the plating of polymeric substrates a series of preparation steps would normally
be needed to make a surface catalytically active. These steps consists of (i) etching the
surface to increase roughness and surface area with a strong acid (ii) sensitization with a
tin chloride to promote chemisorption of a catalytic agent. (iii) chemical activation of the
surface with metal ion for use as catalyzing agent, commonly palladium and finally (iv)
acceleration stage reducing the ions into metallic catalytic sites for formation of the nickel
coating [[1], chapter 3].
Many of these steps are quite expensive and not very environmentally friendly, hence
why a selection of different variations of this procedure exists. For this project though
all steps but the final acceleration step were circumvented by incorporating the nickel
ions directly into the polymeric solution pre-spinning. Leading to the spun nanofibers
having the nickel ions integrated into their structure awaiting activation without further
modifications. Therefore the same one-step cleaning and activation procedure used for
the nickel foam could also be used on the polymers. Leading to a similar procedure being

1.3
Plating parameters
In the plating optimization process there are some key parameters that need to be un-
derstood and tested. The most important are the bath composition, pH, temperature, and
the agitation or stirring of the solution. Each one of these parameters contributes to the
speed, efficiency and outcome of the coating, and has therefore to be optimized alongside
the others.
1.3.1
Composition of plating baths
The composition of the electrolyte bath is an important step in plating design as each
component have a major impact on the content and morphology of the finalized coat-
ing. Therefore careful consideration should be taken when choosing reagents and their
concentrations.
Nickel salt
The nickel salt is one of the two main components of the electrolyte bath. Supplying the
metal ions used for the cathodic half-cell reaction, this component is vital for determining
the deposition rate of the reaction while the choice of counter ion can also have a large
impact on the finalized product. The type of counter-ion of nickel can, in fact, improve the
efficiency of the reaction by pairing with the other ions present in the solution. In industry,
sulfate salts are normally used to avoid the risk of localized corrosion when plating on
steel and aluminum.
However,sulphur is known for having overlapping peaks with Mo in EDX which could com-
plicate analysis at later stages. Therefore, this project opted for using the alternative of
nickel chloride as the substrate used is not in danger of localized corrosion, the salt is
slightly cheaper and there is no interference in the EDX analysis.
Reducing agent
The reducing agent is considered the second of the two main components of the elec-
trolyte bath. It functions as the main components of the anionic half-cell, supplying the
electrons for the plating process, and therefore its concentration is critical for the reduction
rate of the nickel ions, greatly affecting deposition rate. The deposition rate is important
as higher rate tends to lead to more defects and higher P content in the plating. P content
is what governs the plating structure and is categorized in three categories:
‚ÄĘ 1-5 % of phosphorus amount leads to crystalline structure, with high conductivity
and poor corrosion resistance
‚ÄĘ 5-8 % of phosphorus determines a mixed crystalline and amorphous structure, which
presents intermediate characteristics of the two structures. Specifically, a generally
good conductivity with a significant corrosion resistance, which make this composi-
tion the ideal target for the purposes of this project.
‚ÄĘ 8-12 % of phosphorus results in a completely amorphous structure, which is char-
acterized by poor to absent conductivity and marked corrosion resistance
If the concentration of the reductant becomes to high it can also lead to reduction occurring
in the bulk solution which leads to solution decomposition and bath death.
Complexing agent
Complexing agents are normally organic salts that form metastable chelates complexes
with nickel. Their function is to limit or avoid the uncontrolled reaction of the metal with
reducing agent in the plating bath, which would result in the decomposition of the bath
itself. In addition, at high pH, above 8, complexing agents determine the formation of
metal complexes that allow for multi-step reduction of high-oxidation states metals, such
as Mo [2]. Commonly used chelating agents are citrates and ammonia, whose mechanism

Bath loading
Bath loading is a term that defines the ratio between the surface of the working piece to
coat and the volume of solution in the tank. As a general rule, the the deposition rate
decreases with increasing bath load, which therefore needs to be optimised depending
on the bathing solution. The bath load for commercial applications is usually in a range
of 0.1‚Äď1.0 dm2/L.
1.3.2
pH
One of the most relevant factors for the efficiency and speed of the plating is the pH of the
plating bath. In fact, the kinetics of reactions involved in the electroless plating of Ni has
been observed to change with pH. In particular, the nickel reduction reaction is accelerated
at a higher pH, and the self-reduction of phosphorus from hypophosphite is slower in the
same condition. Since the nickel reduction reaction controls the deposition rate, the use
of high pH baths enhances the deposition rate, with an observed linear increase with the
pH increase. The combination of the above mentioned phenomena have as an effect,
the decrease of phosphorus content in deposits at higher pH. Despite these desirable
characteristics, the increase in the pH of the solution decreases the solubility of nickel
phosphate, which can lead to decomposition and rough deposits.
In order to maintain the chosen pH for electroless deposition, a buffer is often added to
the plating solution. It counteracts the decrease in the pH by supplying OH- to balance
the excess H+ produced during the reduction of nickel ions to its metallic form. Organic
acids, such as acetic acid, and propionic acid, are commonly used as buffers.
1.3.3
Temperature
The temperature of the bath is another factor that can influence the coating. Its importance
is due to the endothermic character of the reactions that are involved in the deposition pro-
cess, for which the heat provides the necessary energy. In particular, for these reactions,
an increase in temperature leads to an increase in the deposition rate.
An exponential relationship between the temperature and the deposition rate has been ob-
served. The temperature range between 60¬įC and 95¬įC is the operative range for plating
using hypophosphite baths. Normally, acidic baths are operated at 80¬įC-90¬įC, whereas
alkaline baths can be used at lower temperature, as low as 40¬įC, and are therefore more
suitable for plastic substrates. In addition to the deposition rate, the temperature of the
bath influences the crystallinity, the phosphorus content, as well as the visual appearance
of the coating.
1.3.4
Stirring
Stirring or agitation of the coating solution influences both the speed and the structure of
the coating. It allows for higher diffusion rates, and better interaction between reacting ions
and the surface, alongside with the faster removal of reaction side-products. In addition,
stirring avoids solution stratification, which can cause gas pitting, patterns and/or streaking
of the deposit. There are two main ways to provide agitation of the solution, which are
conventional magnetic stirring, and ultrasound ‚ÄĚstirring‚ÄĚ. For electroless plating, a few
alternatives are aeration (bubbling of clean air), continuous pumping, or, in the case of
small substrates, agitation of the substrate itself. The use of ultrasound for agitation differs
from all the other approaches and has, theoretically, several advantages in improving the
coating performance. In particular, as reported in Chapter 5 of ‚ÄĚElectroless Nickel Plating‚ÄĚ:

Characterizations
1.4.1
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and energy-dispersive X-ray
spectroscopy (EDX)
The Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) is an advanced tool for non-destructive sam-
ple analysis. It works by directing a focused electron beam onto the sample‚Äôs surface,
generating an interaction volume where electrons interact with the material. These inter-
actions release different types of electrons, which are detected to produce high-resolution
images.
SEM primarily captures two types of emitted electrons. Backscattered electrons provide
information about atomic mass, with heavier elements appearing brighter in the image.
Secondary electrons come from the sample‚Äôs surface, especially edges and fine details,
helping to reveal surface morphology and texture.
SEM is often paired with Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS) to analyze the ele-
mental composition. When the electron beam hits the sample, it generates characteristic
X-rays, which EDS detects to identify the elements present. This combination enables
both detailed imaging and precise chemical analysis.
In this study it was used a Zeiss Merlin with a Bruker detector for EDX analysis at DTU
Energy.
1.4.2
Electrochemical characterization
Linear Sweep Voltammetry (LSV) is a widely used electrochemical technique for studying
oxidation and reduction reactions. It works by sweeping the potential in one direction and
measuring the resulting current, providing insights into redox processes.
Chronopotentiometry, another electrochemical method, applies a constant current over
time to evaluate electrode stability. The resulting potential-time curve helps assess the
electrode‚Äôs performance under continuous current. Stability is indicated by minimal fluc-
tuations in potential, while a significant increase suggests instability. At high current den-
sities, potential fluctuations often occur due to gas bubble formation and detachment from
the electrode surface.
The ohmic drop (iR compensation) refers to voltage loss caused by the solution‚Äôs resis-
tance or electrical contacts. Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) helps mea-
sure this by analyzing impedance at different frequencies. The ohmic drop is determined
from the high-frequency intercept of impedance on the real axis in the complex plane.

Purpose: activate the electrode.
4. Potentiostatic EIS: 100 kHz to 1 Hz, 5 mV amplitude.
Purpose: check resistance after activation.
5. Linear sweep voltammetry: 0.1 V to -0.6 V vs Ref, 5 mV/s.
Purpose: evaluate HER performance on the activated electrode.

Experimental
2.1
Plating procedure
2.1.1
Equipment and chemicals
Sodium borohydride, Ethanol, Water, 50 mL glass container with lid, airbrush gun, 250
mL glass beaker, 100 mL plating solution, VWR Ultrasonic cleaner USC 600 - TH, 1*2cm
Alantum Nickel foam, 3d printed sample holder, basket or other holder for beaker during
plating, lid for the ultrasonic cleaning unit (optional).
2.1.2
Procedure
The ultrasonic cleaning unit is filled with water to a bit above the indicator for safe usage
and the heating is turned on at 70¬įC. It should take between 30-240 min depending on
model and lid for the water to hit desired temperature. 100 mL of demineralized water is
added to the 250mL beaker, which is put into the ultrasonicator for heating. While waiting
114mg Sodium borohydride is weighted and mixed with 3mL of Ethanol and 7mL of water.
A piece of substrate is cut, in this case a piece of 1*2cm Alantum Nickel foam.
When the unit is heated, empty the warm 250mL beaker, add the 100mL plating solution
and leave it to heat in the sonicator. The Airbrush is filled with the sodium borohydride
solution and the piece of substrate is reduced by spraying both sides 3-4 times with a
10-15 second interval allowing for the solution to seep into the substrate. Before the last
spraying make sure the plating solution have reached 70 ¬įC, after the last spraying add
the reduced substrate to the solution while turning on the sonic stirring.
Leave the sample in for the desired amount of time, making sure its orientated correctly
for optimal gas release and retrieve when time is up. After plating wash the sample with
water to remove any leftover solution and leave to dry in fume hood or cupboard until
use.

Results
The knowledge on polyalloyed electroless plating theory and principles is still developing.
Despite the availability of references for polyalloyed electroplating, it is not immediate to
derive an explanation for the phenomena observed in this study. Therefore, the obser-
vations on the obtained results are based on inferences derived from adjacent fields of
study [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 2, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18].
3.1
Initial test platings - Mo
Initial plating was attempted using a supplied method for Ni-P with the changes of being
conducted with ultrasonic stirring and the addition of 6wt% molybdenum trioxide as a
molybdenum precursor. Plating was successful as confirmed by both a visible darkening
