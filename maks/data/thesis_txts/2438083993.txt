Abstract
Background: Antibiotic resistance is an increasing problem, leading to bacterial infec-
tions, that can no longer be treated with antibiotics. Therefore it is imperative to search
for new ways to combat these pathogens. One strategy includes using commensal bacteria
to inhibit the growth and/or virulence of a given pathogen.
Purpose: In this study two porcine commensals; Lactobacillus johnsonii and Lactobacil-
lus reuteri, are investigated for their inhibitory eﬀect on a range of pathogens; Staphy-
lococcus aureus, Staphylococcus hyicus and Enterococcus faecalis. These pathogens have
been chosen as they pose a serious threat to both humans and pigs. In this study the
possibility of using these lactobacilli as biocontrol in order to modulate pathogen colo-
nization and/or virulence is evaluated. The evaluation is based on two types of interaction
assays; an insert-well plate assay and a pigskin assay. Furthermore the susceptibility of
the pathogens to lactic acid and H2O2 is determined. Lastly the eﬀect of the lactobacilli
on the S. aureus gene expression of selected virulence-genes RNAIII, spa and hla is in-
vestigated.
Results: L. reuteri inhibited the pathogens, more eﬃciently compared to L. johnsonii
in the two interaction assays. In the insert-well plate assays, L. reuteri signiﬁcantly in-
hibited the growth of S. aureus and S. hyicus. In the pig skin-explant assay it was found
that L. reuteri signiﬁcantly inhibited S. hyicus, despite S. hyicus being the strain growing
best on the pig skin. The inhibition seen is likely due to lactic acid production by the
lactobacilli. The MIC of H2O2 and low pH was 0.004% and pH 5.5 for the staphylococci,
and 0.016% and pH 5 for E. faecalis.
No eﬀect of the lactobacilli was found on the expression of RNAIII, hla and spa virulence
genes.
Conclusion: The insert-well plate assay and the pig skin assay show promising results,
however, the precise correlation between the possible chemical interactions and the growth
conditions is still to be determined, and the methods used should be further developed
and reﬁned.

1
Introduction
Today we are facing a threat.
A threat caused by the rapid emergence of antibiotic
resistant bacteria. This has led to the spread of pathogens, causing infections that can
no longer be treated with antibiotics. Therefore we are facing a future, where bacteria
we once could control escape our means to kill them. This has become a major problem
in many countries, including Denmark.
Therefore scientists across the globe are looking for alternative strategies to combat these
pathogens. One of these strategies include using commensal bacteria to out-compete or
in other ways inhibit the growth and/or virulence of a given pathogen [1–7].
1.1
Context of the Project
A preceding individual course was performed at DTU from September-December 2017
by Pultz and this author. In that course, the interactions between four Lactobacillus
spp. (Lactobacillus amylovorus, L. reuteri, L. johnsonii and L. ruminis) and a range
of pathogens (Staphylococcus aureus, S. hominis, S. hyicus, Escherichia coli nr6, E. coli
mcr-1 and Enterococcus faecalis) were investigated. The lactobacilli and S. hominis were
isolated from weaners (young pig recently separated from the sow), while E. faecalis was
isolated from a piglet (pig still suckling), all isolates were sampled from the outer part of
the vaginal tract. S. aureus, S. hyicus, S. ruminis, and both E. coli are reference strains.
The interactions were investigated in order to evaluate the possibility of using the lacto-
bacilli to modulate the abundance of the opportunistic pathogens.
It was found, that L. reuteri and L. johnsonii showed increased growth when grown in
proximity to a range of pathogens, while an inhibition of growth of the pathogen was
often found. The results, however, varied greatly depending on growth media and type
of assay [8]. In the preceding individual course three assays were performed; paired spot-
ting, overlay, and insert-well plate assay. The paired spotting assay was performed twice,
while the overlay assay and insert-well plate assay was performed once.
1.2
Purpose and Aims
The purpose of this current study is to identify and evaluate interactions between two
lactobacilli; L. reuteri and L. johnsonii, and three pathogens; S. aureus, S. hyicus and
E. faecalis.
The evaluation is based on two types of interaction assays; an insert-well plate assay
and a pig skin-explant assay, assessing the eﬀect of the lactobacilli on the growth of the
pathogens. The inhibition will be evaluated by calculating the cell concentration by the
end of the interaction experiment, measured by counting colony forming units (CFU) on
plates, compared to the cell concentration achieved when the cells were grown in mono-
cultures. Furthermore, using a susceptibility assay it will be evaluated whether possible
growth inhibition may be due to a potential production of H2O2 or acidiﬁcation of the
media. Lastly, it is important to note, that the interaction may not only result in a
change in growth, but may also bring about a change in expression of virulence genes.
Thus this is also investigated for S. aureus.
Speciﬁc Aims:
• Examine microbial interactions in MRS broth, when L. reuteri or L. johnsonii are
1
==PAGE:8==
grown near to the pathogens S. aureus, S. hyicus or E. faecalis without direct
cell-cell contact.
• Examine microbial interactions of L. reuteri and L. johnsonii when co-cultured
with S. aureus, S. hyicus or E. faecalis directly on pig skin-explants.
• Examine the eﬀect of L. reuteri and L. johnsonii on expression of agr-quorum
sensing system regulated virulence-associated genes in S. aureus.
2
Background
Denmark is one of the biggest exporters of pig meat in the world. Annually approximately
28 million pigs are produced in Denmark, on the around 5000 pig farms [9]. Thus the
life cycle of danish pigs must be pushed to a high rate, which often involves weaning the
piglets very early. This in turn leaves the weaners with an underdeveloped microbiota.
This, along with a high rotation of piglets within the stable, may lead to the piglets be-
coming sick. The limited digestive capacity, due to the underdeveloped microbiota of the
gastrointestinal tract, may damage the intestinal microbiota, thereby leading to diarrhea
[10](and personal communication,Vibe DalhoﬀAndersen). Indeed, weaning stress is the
main cause pig of farmers’ economic losses [10]. Treatment is often based on antibiotics,
administered perorally through drinking water or feed. This way of administration may
cause the antibiotics to spread to unintended areas. Thus, the antibiotics might for ex-
ample aﬀect the skin-microbiota (personal communication, Vibe DalhoﬀAndersen).
Such spread of the antibiotics, may lead to development of resistance among bacterial
strains [11].
Antibiotics resistance can occur spontaneously via mutations, or be transferred among
diﬀerent species by horizontal gene transfer [3]. Importantly, the use, and often overuse,
of antibiotics kills oﬀantibiotic sensitive stains, leaving behind antibiotic resistant strains
to reproduce [3].
The emergence of antibiotic resistant strains in farms, pose a threat to the livestock, as
an infection with these strains may be more diﬃcult to treat. However, there is also an
increased risk for humans. In case of zoonotic bacteria, these and their resistance genes
may spread to bacteria which are pathogenic to humans [11].
In the following sections, the lactobacilli and the pathogens of interest are presented.
Here it will be explained why these strains have been chosen, and how the particular
strains relate to pigs.
2.1
Lactobacillus spp.
The Lactobacillus genus was proposed by Beiierinck in 1901, and are a part of the normal
ﬂora in the gastrointestinal tract, mouth and vaginal ﬂora of humans and many animals,
including pigs [12–14]. The Lactobacillus genus includes high number of GRAS species
(Generally Recognized As Safe), and are widely used in fermented food production, for ex-
ample in fermented meats, vegetables and dairy products [13]. Furthermore some strains
are now used as probiotics [13, 15].
2
==PAGE:9==
Lactobacilli are members of the broadly deﬁned group; lactic acid bacteria (LAB). This
group is characterized by the formation of lactic acid as the main end-product of carbo-
hydrate metabolism. They are generally characterized as being catalase negative, gram-
positive non-sporeforming coccobacilli or rods, often seen forming chains. Furthermore,
they have complex nutritional requirements, and need fermentable carbohydrates as en-
ergy source. Thus they grow in nutritionally rich media, including MRS [13, 15, 16].
They prefer growth under under microaerophilic conditions, and under slightly acidic
conditions (optimal growth between pH 5.5–6.2), but can grow in a pH range from 3 up
to 8. The optimal growth temperature is between 30–40◦C [12, 13, 15, 17].
Another mention-worthy aspect of lactobacilli, is their ability to produce strain-speciﬁc
bacteriocins and bacteriocin-like products that may inhibit growth of other organisms.
An example of this is the bacteriocin reuterin, which is produced by some L. reuteri
strains [18]. Indeed, a screening study found that up to 80% of vaginal lactobacilli, could
produce bacteriocins or bacteriocin-like substances.
However, this eﬀect seems to be
strain dependent.
Lactobacilli may also inhibit growth of neighbouring cells by lowering pH, and by pro-
duction of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) [17, 19]. These abilities will be discussed further
in section 2.5.
In this study, two species of lactobacilli have been chosen; L. reuteri and L. johnsonii.
These were chosen as they were found to to be most eﬀective at inhibiting the oppor-
tunistic pathogens [8]. Furthermore both strains have been intensely studied for their
probiotic abilities, and because they are prevalent in pig gastrointestinal tract and vagina
[6, 20, 21].
2.2
Enterococcus faecalis
Enterococcus is a genus of LAB, and has existed as a unique taxonomic entity since ex-
periments in the mid 1980s suggested them to be separated from the streptococcus genus
[12, 22, 23]. Enterococcal species, often constitute an important part of the intestinal
microbiota of both animals and humans [23, 24]. A highly selective media for detection of
enterococci has been devised by Slanetz and Bartley. On this media, the colonies appear
as pink to dark maroon color [25, 26]. Furthermore, enterococci are able to grow at high
salt concentration, and they can grow in a wide range of pH (4.5-10) [23, 27].
In the food industry, Enterococcus species have been widely used as starter-culture or
as probiotics. However, some enterococci are pathogenic to humans, and are one of the
most common causes of nosocomial infections, with high mortality rates [27].
One of the most epidemiologically important enterococci, is E. faecalis [24].
E. faecalis are coccus shaped gram-positive bacteria which often occurs in short chains
or pairs (diplococci) [28].
E. faecalis can cause a variety of infections in humans, including meningitis, endocardi-
tis (infections in heart), bacteremia (infections in blood) and urinary tract infections
[28], and is recognized as major nosocomial pathogen [23]. It is especially the capability
to easily acquire resistance to many important antimicrobials that makes this pathogen
dangerous from a medical perspective. These antimicrobials include ampicillin and gly-
copeptides [23, 24]. Even gentamicin and vancomycin resistant species of E. faecalis have
3
==PAGE:10==
been reported [23, 24, 29–31]. Spreading resistance between Enterococcus species of both
human and animal origin is thought to be mostly due to transferable elements or conju-
gation of genetic material carrying resistance genes. This is believed to happen during
enterococcal passage through the intestinal tract [24]. Recent studies have shown, that
this antibiotic resistance trend is increasing worldwide, thus limiting treatments to last
resort antibiotics [23, 30].
E. faecalis from pigs may constitute a human hazard, as the same types have been de-
tected in meat, and patients with bloodstream infections and human faecal samples [32].
Genetic relatedness between E. faecalis isolated from infective endocarditis cases in Dan-
ish patients and pigs has been conﬁrmed [33]. Furthermore, pigs are now considered
reservoirs for resistance genes that may spread to other pathogens, and reservoirs for E.
faecalis with clinical importance to humans [24, 32, 33].
E. faecalis was chosen for this study, due to the serious situation of emerging resistance,
and the presence of clinically important E. faecalis in the Danish pig population [33].
Furthermore, the bacteria found in the gut of a pig, may be transferred to other areas,
including the skin of the pigs, thus making it a relevant skin-associated strain [10, 31,
34].
2.3
Staphylococcus hyicus
Staphylococcus hyicus has been known under other names. In 1950 Sompolinsky isolated
and described strain NCTC 10350, a gram-positive coccus which had been isolated from
pigs with exudative epidermitis. It was named Micrococcus hyicus [35]. After redeﬁnition
of the genera Micrococcus and Staphylococcus, it was suggested that M. hyicus should
be transferred to the genus Staphylococcus [36]. Later, in the 1974th edition of Bergeys
manual, M. hyicus was listed as belonging to S. epidermidis [35]. It was in 1978 in a
study by Devriese et al., one hundred and thirty-two strains similar to strain NCTC 10350
were investigated, and ﬁnally proposed to be named S. hyicus subsp. hyicus.
A general description of this strain is also made by Devriese et al., here they are described
as being gram positive, 0.6 to 1.3 μm in diameter, nonmotile, and occurring in pairs of
two or three, and in irregular groups.
Furthermore they were found to be resistant to lysozymes, but susceptible to a range
of antibiotics including penicillins, streptomycin, erythromycin, lincomycin, tetracycline
and chloramphenicol [35].
S. hyicus is also known to be pathogenic to pigs. This was concluded by Sompolinsky in
1950 as he was able to reproduce the exudative epidermitis in apparently healthy pigs by
inoculating them with the NCTC 10350 strain. [35].
Exudative epidermitis, also known as greasy pig disease, is an acute skin disease of suck-
ling and weaned pigs. It is recognized by exfoliation of the skin, and sebaceous exudation
as well as a formation of a brownish crust [37, 38].
Especially damage from biting or scratches from the stable bedding may expose the skin
to infection by S. hyicus [38].
An outbreak of exudative epidermitis may cause morbidity of up to 90%, as well as high
mortality of the pigs. This may have ﬁnancial consequences for the farmer, as even mild
cases of the disease may inﬂuence the price of the pigs, as the readily visible lesions on the
skin make the pigs harder to sell [37, 38]. The infections are treated with antimicrobial
4
==PAGE:11==
agents, however, the occurrence of S. hyicus with resistance to antimicrobials has made
treatment more diﬃcult [37, 39].
The resistance proﬁle for S. hyicus, has been determined in a study by Park et al.. In that
study, 143 S. hyicus isolates, from pigs with clinical signs of exudative epidermitis were
tested for their antibiotic susceptibility. It was found that many S. hyicus isolates were
resistant to β-lactam antibiotics, including penicillin, ampicillin and ceftiofur. Resistance
to tetracycline was also common. Another disturbing discovery by Park et al. was that
resistance to one or more antimicrobials was found in 99.3% of the S. hyicus isolates
tested, and resistance to 5 or more antimicrobials was found in 40.6% of the cases [38].
Furthermore, a Danish surveillance system was established in 1995, where both healthy
and clinically ill animals as well as randomly selected bacteria were included. There,
the resistance proﬁle of 90 S. hyicus isolates from pigs was also determined. Resistance
towards penicillin G, Streptomycin, Tetracycline or Trimethoprim was found among rel-
atively many S.hyicus tested [11].
Thus it is clear that resistance against antimicrobials is an emerging problem in the ﬁght
against S. hyicus infections.
Due to this increasing occurrence of antibiotic resistance found in S. hyicus strains, and
due to the disease causing properties of S. hyicus in pigs, this strain was included in
this study. Furthermore, the individual course, explained in section 1.1, showed that S.
hyicus was the pathogen most inhibited by L. reuteri and L. johnsonii.
2.4
Staphylococcus aureus
Staphylococcus aureus is a cocci shaped gram positive bacteria, the cells are approxi-
mately 0.8 to 1.0 μm in diameter, and tend to grow in clusters resembling grapes [40,
41]. They can grow anaerobically and aerobically in temperatures ranging from 18-40◦C.
Furthermore, they are able to grow on media containing up to 10% salt, and they are
often found on skin and mucus membranes [41].
S. aureus is a pathogen to humans, whom in the same time are the major reservoir for
these organisms. In many cases S. aureus is part of a persons normal microbiota, making
them asymptomatic carriers. This asymptomatic infection of healthy individuals is one
of the fundamental properties of S. aureus. Indeed it is estimated that up to half the
adult population carry S. aureus at some point, and approximately 15% carry S. aureus
persistently in their anterior nares. However, these rates are even higher when looking in
speciﬁc populations. Among health care workers, hospitalized patients, and immunocom-
promised individuals, up to 80% are colonized with S. aureus. These S. aureus carriers
are believed to be both at a higher risk of disease, and an important source of the S. au-
reus strains that spread among individuals. The mode of transmission is via direct, often
skin-skin, contact with an infected or colonized person. Another mode of transmission is
contact with contaminated surfaces and objects [41, 42].
S. aureus is well known as a human pathogen, indeed it is the leading cause of osteoartic-
ular (bone and joints) infections, pleuropulmonary (lung) infections, endocarditis, bac-
teremia, and infections in skin and soft tissue [43].
Cases of S. aureus infections are reported across the globe and have reached endemic
5
==PAGE:12==
proportions. They can be either community - or hospital acquired.
S. aureus is naturally susceptible to essentially all antibiotics developed, and it was due
to this high susceptibility that Alexander Fleming discovered the eﬀect of penicillin in
the 1940s [42]. However, despite this natural susceptibility, S. aureus is notorious for its
ability to become resistant to antibiotics. This resistance is often acquired by horizontal
gene transfer from other bacteria, or from mutations and antibiotic selection arising from
antibiotic usage [41, 42].
S. aureus has attracted a lot of attention lately, as antibiotic strains have begun to emerge,
making infections with this pathogen increasingly diﬃcult to treat. Here methicillin resis-
tant S. aureus (MRSA) is of importance, and the burden of infections is increasing in both
community and healthcare setting in many counties [42–44]. Also live-stock associated
MRSA has emerged in North America and throughout Europe during the past decade.
Thus animals used for food production are now recognized as a reservoir for a methicillin
resistant strain, and the clonal spread and transmission of MRSA between pigs and hu-
mans has been reported [45, 46]. It has also been indicated that this pig-MRSA is able to
persist within pig herds, and transmit via direct contact between pigs, resulting in an en-
demic situation within pig herds. Furthermore a higher transmission rate has been found
between pigs treated with β-lactams and tetracycline compared to untreated pigs. This
indicates a selective advantage towards MRSA, when using these classes of antibiotics [5].
Infections with S. aureus are believed to remain both serious and common [43]. Therefore
it is now imperative to search for other ways to ﬁght S. aureus infections. Especially the
increased spread of pig-MRSA is of concern [3, 47], and this is why this pathogen has
been included in this study.
2.4.1
Virulence Gene Regulation of S. aureus
Due to treatment failures associated with antibiotic resistance it has been proposed to
search for new treatment strategies. These include anti-virulence therapies, where the
activity or production of the disease causing factors are inhibited. Thereby anti-virulence
agents can be used to disarm a pathogen of its virulence traits [44, 48].
The pathogenesis of S. aureus is attributed to a repertoire of virulence factors, which help
the pathogen to adhere to host tissue and evade host defenses. Many of these virulence
factors in S. aureus are controlled by a quorum sensing system. [48–51]. This quorum
sensing, enables S. aureus to detect and respond to the density of its own population and
to diﬀerentiate between species in consortia. Thereby allowing S. aureus to adjust gene-
regulation, to achieve the best phenotype in a given situation [52]. Many virulence traits
in S. aureus, are controlled by the agr-quorum sensing system, which uses the regulatory
RNA molecule RNAIII as an eﬀector to regulate density-dependent pathogenicity [48–
52]. The agr quorum sensing system is presented in ﬁgure 1.

2.5
Interactions between Species
Microbes are rarely found as single species populations in the environment, as even small
samples taken from various habitats show large species richness [53]. Until recently, it
was commonly believed that bacteria live somewhat reclusive lives. However, research
has found, that bacteria have elaborate chemical signaling systems, enabling them to
communicate within and between species close by. Indeed, bacterial species that are able
to detect and respond to competitors in their environment may have the advantage of
being able to alter their gene expression appropriately [54]. Thus it is suggested that
interspeciﬁc microbe-microbe interaction is essential for development and persistence of
any kind of complex community [53, 55].
Microbial cell-cell communications, especially in bioﬁlms, have been intensively stud-
ied, largely due to their omnipresence in natural environments. However, despite of the
bacterial importance to host health and disease, the molecular mechanisms underlying
microbe-microbe interactions is not yet fully understood [55, 56].
The many years of co-evolution of species has lead to specialization and adaptation and
thus has resulted in a great variety of ecological relationships. As presented in ﬁgure 2,
these include mutualistic, amensalistic or competitive relationships [53]. These relation-
ships are a contributing factor in shaping microbial abundances [57].
Figure 2: Types of interactions between species Three types of cell-cell interactions are
presented: Top: mutualistic relationship, both species coexist in mutually beneﬁcial manner.
Middle: amensalistic, one species is inhibited by the interaction. Bottom: competition, species
battle for something they both need. This ﬁgure was made by this author.
8
==PAGE:15==
A mutualistic relationship, also known as a cooperative or symbiotic relationship, is one
that is mutually beneﬁcial to both species involved. These may be due to for example
formation of bioﬁlms, co-colonization or cross-feeding. An example of a negative relation-
ship is amensalism, where the association between diﬀerent species harms or inhibits one,
but leaves the other unaﬀected. This is for example seen when metabolic byproducts of
one species result in inhibition of other microorganisms [53, 57].
Another negative relationship is competition. There are many diﬀerent forms of compe-
tition between species, these may be nutritional competition, or competition for space
[19].
Some bacterial strains have the ability to produce toxic compounds to inhibit rivaling
species. As mentioned, lactobacilli may inhibit neighbouring cells, by lowering the pH or
by the production of H2O2 or bacteriocins. [14, 15, 18, 19, 58–60].
Bacteriocins:
Bacteriocins are peptides or proteins, secreted by bacteria to inhibit growth of other bac-
terial strains or species. Lactobacilli are known to produce various types of bacteriocins.
One example is reuterin, produced by some strains of L. reuteri.
Reuterin has been
identiﬁed as the compound 3-hydroxypropionaldehyde (3-HPA). When grown in media
containing glycerol, L. reuteri is able to secrete high amounts of reuterin into its sur-
roundings. Reuterin is highly reactive, and can induce oxidative stress. This is believed
to be due to interaction with thiol groups, which are critical for the function of many
enzymes and metabolites within the cell [14, 59].
LAB are known to produce bacteriocins with relatively broad spectrum against foodborne
pathogens. Some of the desirable traits of bacteriocins produced by lactobacilli include
that they are not active or toxic against eukaryotic cells. Furthermore, they become
degraded in the human gut, hereby they have little inﬂuence on gut microbiota [18, 19,
61].
Hydrogen Peroxide
Certain microorganisms, including lactobacilli, are capable of producing H2O2 when
grown aerobically [60]. The production of H2O2 by lactobacilli has been found to be
antagonistic to S. aureus, and is a widely accepted hypothesis to explain the inhibitory
activity of vaginal LAB [19]. Indeed, it has been demonstrated that H2O2 can inhibit
growth of S. aureus even at low concentrations [19, 45]. The inhibiting eﬀect exerted by
H2O2 is due to the oxidative stress applied by the molecule itself or by its metabolites
(O–
2 and OH). These metabolites may act on proteins, nucleic acids or other biomolecules,
thereby killing the cell
pH
As mentioned lactobacilli are LAB, characterized by the formation of lactic acid as main
end-product of carbohydrate metabolism [15]. Secreting this product, may in turn acid-
ify the immediate environment, and inhibit growth of other species [19]. For example,
it has been demonstrated that acidiﬁcation of laboratory medium using lactic acid to a
pH as low as 4.5–4.4 completely inhibits growth of S. aureus. However, it is important
to mention, that the amount of growth inhibition depends not only on the pH, but also
on the type of acid used. For example, Minor et al. showed a 99% reduction of biomass
9
==PAGE:16==
after lowering the pH of milk to 4.6 using lactic acid. A similar reduction eﬀect was
observed when using citric acid, phosphoric acid and hydrochloric acid, at pH 4.5, 4.1,
and 4 respectively [62].
A cell is inhibited when lactic acid enters and acidiﬁes the cytosol. This acidiﬁcation
permeabilizes bacterial membranes, and interferes with intracellular function [63].
2.6
Pig Production in Denmark
As mentioned, one of the assays performed is a pig skin-explant assay. Therefore it is
interesting to understand the production of pigs in Denmark, in order to appreciate what
the skin has undergone before it was used in this study.
In Denmark there are a number of regulations on how to manage the pigs [64].
When the pigs are ready for slaughter, they are transported to the slaughterhouse by
trucks. Upon arrival the pigs are sedated by CO2. This is done by letting the pigs onto a
lift that lowers them into a pit containing CO2. After sedation, the pigs are hung by the
back legs, and the carotid artery is cut, leading the pig to bleed out. Hereafter they are
transported into the so-called ’unclean’ or ’black’ slaughtering area where the exterior
parts of the pig are processed. Firstly the pigs are scalded. This is either done by water
steam or by dragging the carcass through 62◦C water. This step loosens the bristles and
disinfects the exterior of the pig.
Secondly, the carcass enters the dehairing machine, where hoofs and bristles are removed.
The last hairs and impurities are removed from the skin by singeing and scraping, or by
ﬂaming and whipping. After this the meat processing is begun in the clean slaughtering
area. Here the pigs are eviscerated, decapitated and divided into two halves which are
branded.
After the carcass has been divided, it is quickly cooled. It should have a core temperature
of 7◦C 24 hours after slaughter. Cooling is done by transporting the carcasses through
a cooling tunnel blowing air between -20 and -30 ◦C. The carcasses are stored in a large
refrigerator at 5◦C until next day where the meat is cut up [65, 66].
Due to this extensive processing, it is expected that the skin surface and thereby the
microbiota of the skin in no longer the same as when the pigs were alive.
Ex vivo models and assays studying various aspects of colonization and treatment strate-
gies have been performed on pigskin. Ex vivo porcine models have especially been valuable
for studying wound healing and pathogen colonization [34, 67–69]. This is mostly due to
the skin of pigs being similar to that of humans, and because of ethical reasons. Further-
more, it is easier to produce replicates in ex vivo studies, compared to in vivo studies,
due to the more controlled conditions [69, 70]. Despite this, the pig skin microbiome is
largely uncharacterized [34]. Additionally no studies, that investigate microbe-microbe
interactions on pig skin-explants were found in published literature. Therefore the study
performed here is, to the best of my knowledge, the ﬁrst of its kind.

3.6
pH Susceptibility Assay
This method was developed with inspiration from Valero et al. [73] and Medved’ová et
al [74].
The pH susceptibility was tested on all three pathogens; twice with S. aureus, S. hyicus
and once where E. faecalis was included. MRS broth was prepared in 9 ·100ml ﬂasks,
after which the pH of each ﬂask was adjusted to 3.0, 3.5, 4.0, 4.5, 5.0, 5.5, 6.0, 6.5 and
7.0 . This was done by addition of NaOH (5M) or lactic acid, while measuring the pH
using a pH meter. As autoclavation may alter the pH of the medium, the media was re-
measured and adjusted after autoclaving. After incubation on plates, according to strain
maintenance, the strains were suspended in MRS broth with each pH, in a concentration
of approximately 5 · 105 CFU/ml. After this, 100μl from each pH and cell concentration
adjusted suspensions was transferred to a 96 microtitre plate, this was done in triplicates.

==PAGE:20==
The plates were incubated under microaerophilic conditions at 37◦C for 24 hours, after
which the plates were inspected visually. After inspection, the contents of the wells were
mixed by pipetting, and the OD585 was measured using Victor, Perkin Elmer 2030 Multi-
ple Workstation X3, and the Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC ) was determined.
The MIC value is deﬁned as the lowest concentration of a compound giving rise to no
detectable growth.

3.10
Statistical Analysis
A statistical analysis was made on the insert-well plate assay 1, and pig skin-explant
assays 1 and 2.
In insert-well plate assay 1, the CFU/ml was calculated for each strain (mono or co-
cultured) on each type of plate. The calculation was based on the volume of the suspension
plated, and the average CFU count on a speciﬁc plate (there were three spots per dilution
per plate). Subsequently, the average CFU/ml across all types of media with growth was
calculated.
The statistical analysis in insert-well plate assay 1 was based on a two sample, two tail
t-test, on the average CFU/ml for mono-culture results from two replicates, and the
average CFU/ml results for co-cultured strains from two replicates.
In the pig skin-explant assay, the statistics was also based on a two sample, two tail t-
test. Here the average CFU/ml was calculated for each of the three technical replicates on
MSA media (which each had three spots of from each dilution). The CFU/ml calculated
from the uninoculated skin pieces, regarded here as background noise, on MSA plates

average CFU/ml for mono-culture – background noise for three replicates, to the average
CFU/ml results for co-cultured strains from three replicates.

thus it inhibits this agr quorum sensing system. This control is expected to reveal a clear
circle on the RNAIII and hla reporter strains (these are now down regulated), and a blue
circle for the spa reporter strain (now upregulated).
RNAIII↓→hla ↓and Spa↑( →S. aureus is not virulent)
The MRS control was included in order to exclude this as a reason for any up -or down

Discussion
5.1
Growth Assay
In this assay the selectivity of media towards the ﬁve strains used (L. reuteri, L. johnsonii,
S. aureus, S.hyicus and E. faecalis was determined. Two non selective media were used
in this study; BHI and MRS.
BHI is a general and nutritionally rich media, used for cultivation of many types of
bacteria [77, 78].
MRS was originally designed to be selective towards lactobacilli [79, 80]. However, all
strains in this study were able to grow on MRS. Indeed MRS has recently been proposed
to be non-selective, and is used to support growth of LAB in general [81]. Staphylococci
have also been found to grow on this media [81].
Slanetz media is supposedly highly selective towards Enterococcus sp. [25, 26]. However,
besides from E. faecalis, L. reuteri and L. johnsonii were here found to grow well on this
media. In a study by Weiss et al., 2 (including L. reuteri) out of 11 lactobacilli, along
with 5 of 8 strains of Pediococcus sp. were also found to grow on slanetz. This may be
due to incubation temperature, as incubation at 35◦C allows growth of species other than
enterococci as stated by Oxoid (the manufacturer of Slanetz used in this assay) [82].
MSA is a media selective towards pathogenic staphylococci. Most other bacteria are
inhibited by the high salt concentration found in MSA. In this study both staphylococci
grew well on this media as expected. Additionally, E. faecalis grew well on MSA. This
may be due the capability of E. faecalis to grow in high salt concentrations [23, 27]. The
ability of E. faecalis to grow on MSA was also presented by Quiloan et al., who proposes
to use this media to distinguish E. faecalis from other enterococci.
Rogosa agar is a selective medium for lactobacilli. Its low pH suppresses growth of most
strains, even most other lactic acid bacteria [83, 84]. Indeed the results from this assay
show that only L. reuteri and L. johnsonii were able to grew on rogosa. However, it has
been demonstrated that rogosa media can also select for species, including Clostridium
or Biﬁdobacterium spp. [85].
The results in the growth assay, indicate that the diﬀerence in CFU/ml across the various
types of plates was minimal. Therefore all media with growth were deemed suitable for
CFU counts for the species studied here.
5.2
Insert-Well Plate Assay
In this assay the interaction between the lactobacilli and the pathogens was evaluated
based on the amount of growth, after the tested species were co-cultured separated by a
ﬁlter. Thus they would solely be able to interact by secreted metabolites. This type of
assay was preferred over a paired spotting assay, as it is easier to control and replicate.
An overlay assay could also have been performed, but an inhibition zone may be diﬃcult
to measure exactly. Furthermore in an overlay assay the initial colonies will be well es-
tablished before overlay agar is applied, and have a much higher cell-density compared
to the bacterial suspension in the overlay agar. Lastly, the insert-well plate assay enables
generation of quantitative data on inhibition.
In insert-well plate assay 1 it was found that L. reuteri inhibited growth of S. hyicus
and S. aureus, while L. johnsonii inhibited growth of S. hyicus. There was no signiﬁcant
34
==PAGE:41==
reduction in lactobacilli growth. This indicates an amensalistic relationship, where the
lactobacilli produce something that inhibits the staphylococci, while leaving the lacto-
bacilli unaﬀected.
In insert-well plate assay 2, the initial ratio of lactobacilli to pathogens was increased.
This was decided based on the results in the growth assay, where a 2 log diﬀerence in CFU
of the pathogens and the lactobacilli was found at OD600 0.05. Having a 100 fold higher
OD for the lactobacilli would therefore result in an approximate 1:1 ration of pathogens
to lactobacilli. Thus this should equal the playing ﬁeld for the two co-cultured strains.
When comparing the results from insert-well plate assay 1 and 2, a few diﬀerences are
found: The CFU/ml for S. aureus mono - and co-culture in insert-well plate assay 1 and
insert-well plate assay 2 are very similar. Thus it seems that the lower P:L ratio did not
have much eﬀect on S. aureus inhibition.
S. hyicus, on the other hand, is much more aﬀected. The lower P:L ratio, allowed complete
inhibition of S. hyicus by both L. johnsonii and L. reuteri.
E. faecalis was also found to be aﬀected by the lower P:L ratio, as this is now slightly
inhibited by L. reuteri.
In most cases across the 2 well plate assays the highest inhibition is seen for L. reuteri.
This suggests that L. reuteri could produce an additional inhibiting molecule or that lac-
tic acid or H2O2 is produced faster compared to L. johnsonii, thereby reducing growth
of the pathogens sooner, and to a greater extent. However, it is also interesting that the
general growth of L. reuteri in the insert well plate assay is 1.5 log higher than that of
L. johnsonii, see ﬁgure 8. In the growth assay the CFU/ml for OD600 0.05 was found to
be quite similar between these two species. This indicates that L. reuteri grows better in
MRS broth than does L. johnsonii. Thus the higher inhibition seen for L. reuteri may
simply be due to a better growth by this strain.
Considering that the pH of the media with pathogens co-cultured with lactobacilli was
found to be lower than the pH of the staphylococci mono-cultures, inhibition may be due
to production of lactic acid, and the concomitant drop in pH.
As mentioned in the introduction, Enterococcus sp are, like Lactobacillus sp, a member
of the lactic acid bacteria. Indeed, the media pH of the E. faecalis mono-culture was
similar to that of the lactobacilli mono-cultures. Due to this strain’s ability to produce
lactic acid [22], it is unlikely that the lactic acid is the cause of the inhibition seen in
insert-well plate assay 2.
The inhibition may also be due to production of H2O2 by the lactobacilli. The role of
these two inhibition factors is described in more detail in the next sections.
5.3
pH Susceptibility Assay
As described in the introduction, lactobacilli may inhibit pathogens by a variety of means,
including lowering the pH. Indeed, the pH susceptibility assay and H2O2 susceptibility
assay reveal that the pathogens in this study were susceptible to both change in pH and
to low concentrations of H2O2.
The staphylococci and E. faecalis were inhibited from pH 5.5 and pH 5 and below, re-
spectively. Tolerance to low pH was expected for E. faecalis, as this is a LAB, known to
35
==PAGE:42==
produce lactic acid [22]. Additionally, it has been demonstrated in previous studies that
E. faecalis grows until pH 4.6 [23, 27]. Furthermore, the pH of the media where the E.
faecalis mono-culture had grown in the insert-well plate assay was 4.25, as seen in ﬁgure
10, thereby suggesting that E. faecalis can survive in this pH.
It was expected that the staphylococci could grow, albeit slightly, in lower pH, as growth
was detected at pH 4.5 in the well-plate assay. However, in the insert-well plate assay
the pH of the media changes gradually to become more acidic, whereas in this assay they
are subjected to an initial acidic environment.
After 48h the pH of the media with lactobacilli dropped from pH 6 (initial pH of the
MRS media) to pH 4. However the actual rate of lactic acid production in the insert-
well plate assay is not known. In a study by Tomás et al., the production of lactic acid
by Lactobacillus acidophilus was analyzed by using a lactic acid dehydrogenase test kit.
Here it was discovered that the production of lactic acid was dependent on both media,
temperature and initial pH. In that study the pH dropped from 6.5 to pH 4 within 8
hours [16].
Similar results have been found for Lactobacillus salivarius when grown in pH adjusted
MRS. In that case the pH dropped from 6.5 to pH 4.5 within 8 hours. Furthermore, it was
found that that the optimal conditions for growth coincided with the optimal conditions
for decrease in pH. Thus it was concluded that these factors were directly related [86].
The results from these studies, demonstrate that lactobacilli are able to acidify their
environment, and that the acidifying eﬀect correlates with the growth conditions of the
lactobacilli. If the eﬀectiveness of lactic acid production by L. johnsonii and L. reuteri
are similar to that of L. acidophilus and L. salivarius, it may be expected that the MRS
media in the well plates are lowered to pH 5, which in the current assay was found to
inhibit both staphylococci and E. faecalis, within the ﬁrst 8 hours of incubation. Fur-
thermore, as mentioned, it seemed as L. reuteri grew better in MRS broth, than did L.
johnsonii. If growth is directly correlated to amount of lactic acid produced, this may
imply a faster production of lactic acid by L. reuteri compared to L. johnsonii.
It is important to note that if the acidiﬁcation is slow, and therefore does not inhibit
the pathogens suﬃciently initially, the pathogens may have reached suﬃcient density to
become virulent [19].
Thus, results obtained in the pH susceptibility assay and insert-well plate assay suggests
that media acidiﬁcation may cause the growth inhibition for S. aureus and S. hyicus.
However, this may not be the cause of growth inhibition found for E. faecalis.
5.4
H2O2 Susceptibility Assay
The prevailing view was, until recently, that H2O2 was the major antimicrobial factor
produced by lactobacilli [63]. Indeed, the H2O2 susceptibility assay revealed that even
small amounts of H2O2 were suﬃcient to inhibit growth of both staphylococci.
S. aureus and S. hyicus were in this assay found to be more susceptible to this antimi-
crobial agent than was E. faecalis.
This may be due to the ability of E. faecalis to produce O–
2, which under acidic environ-
36
==PAGE:43==
ments may spontaneously dissociates into H2O2 [87]. Thus E. faecalis has developed a
defence for this agent in order to avoid the oxidative stress conferred by the H2O2. The
main available defense lactic acid bacteria posses against H2O2 is NADH peroxidase, also
known as NPX, which converts H2O2 into water (NADH+H++H2O2 →NAD++2H2O)
[88–90], leaving E. faecalis less sensitive towards H2O2.
The MIC values of 0.004% H2O2 for the staphylococci and 0.016% H2O2 for E. faecalis
obtained in this study are comparable to those previously observed in other studies. In
a study by Seier-Petersen et al. the MIC of H2O2 for 79 porcine S. aureus isolates was
found to be between 0.001–0.002% H2O2, thus even lower than was found here. The
reason for this diﬀerence in MIC may be due to the diﬀerence in cultivation conditions,
as Seier-Petersen et al. used Mueller–Hinton II broth.
Results similar to results from this study and the study by Seier-Petersen, were found
by Aarestrup et al.. Here 43 isolates of S. aureus from cattle, 38 isolates of S. hyicus
from pigs with exudative epidermitis and 52 isolates of E. faecalis from faeces (pigs and
broilers) were tested for their susceptibility to a range of antimicrobials, including H2O2.
In that study, the MIC of H2O2 was determined on Mueller-Hinton-II agar plates con-
taining H2O2. The MIC of H2O2 distributions were S. aurues: 0.002-0.004%, S. hyicus:
0.002% and E. faecalis: 0.008-0.016% [75]. Thereby these results are very similar to those
obtained in this study.
Thus it is clear that all the tested pathogens are susceptible, of various degrees, to H2O2.
However, it has been demonstrated that lactobacilli only produce H2O2 under aerobic
conditions [60, 63, 91, 92].
In the insert-well plate assay, the incubation was microaerophilic, thus it only contains
3-5% oxygen. It has been demonstrated that L. johnsonii produces approximately 107.5
+– 17.5 nmol H2O2/OD under microaerophilic conditions (3% O2). However, in this case
L. johnsonii was grown in LAPTg media pre-saturated with 3% O2, for 24 hours at i
37◦C, in a growth chamber maintaining 3% O2. Thus the conditions of growth were not
the same as in the present study, where the MRS media had not been pre-saturated with
oxygen. In the same study, it was found that L. johnsonii does not produce H2O2 when
under anaerobic conditions [92].
In a study by Jørgensen et al. two strains of L. reuteri were tested for their ability to
produce H2O2. In that study, it was veriﬁed that both strains tested (DSM 17938 and
ATCC PTA 5289) produce H2O2 after being incubated anaerobically on MRS plates for
48 h at 37◦C and subsequently exposed to ambient air. However, the method used in
that study was qualitative, and thus the amount of H2O2 produced was not determined
[93].
Nevertheless, these results indicate that the lactobacilli are to likely produce H2O2 in
the insert-well plate assay however, due to the low oxygen availability the amount may
be low -possibly too low to have an eﬀect on the pathogens.
To sum up, the inhibition of the staphylococci seen in the insert-well plate assay, is likely
due to the acidiﬁcation of the media, and not the production of H2O2 by lactobacilli, due
to the low oxygen availability. Furthermore, E. faecalis was found to be less sensitive to
H2O2 and lactic acid compared to the sensitivity of the staphylococci. This may be due
37
==PAGE:44==
to the fact that E. faecalis is a LAB, and therefore is able to produce both lactic acid
and O–
2 which spontaneously may form H2O2.
A third possibility is that the pathogens, including E. faecalis, was inhibited by reuterin,
as most inhibition in the well plate assay was found when the pathogens were co-cultured
with L. reuteri.
However, as reuterin is produced as an intermediate step in the conversion of glycerol
to 1,3- propanediol [59], and to my knowledge MRS broth does not contain glycerol
[94], it is unknown whether L. reuteri is capable of producing this bacteriocin under the
circumstances provided in this study. This was not investigated in this study.
5.5
Pig Skin-Explant Assay
A substantial part of this project, was to establish an assay for evaluating the interspecies
interaction when the strains were in a habitat similar to the natural habitat of the tested
species.
In order to do this, it was decided to design an ex vivo assay, using pig skin-explants. This
type of assay has the beneﬁt of being cheap, easy to handle, easy to make replicates and
more ethical, compared to an in vivo assay [69, 70]. A number of in vivo and ex vivo skin
models have been established. However, these are mostly used to study human wound
healing [34, 67, 68]. As no other studies using skin explants to study microbe-microbe
interactions were found, a preliminary study was conducted, to determine how to handle
the pig skin-explants, and determine appropriate bacterial concentration to inoculate the
species of interest.
It was found that all species tested were able to grow on the explants. Especially S. hyi-
cus, which is a known pathogen to pigs, grew well and caused severe damage to the skin
explant. Unfortunately, in pig skin-explant assays 1 and 2, a high background noise was
found. This was especially an issue in assay 2, where adjusting for this high background
noise revealed no growth of S. hyicus and E. faecalis mono-cultures. However, there is
also the possibility that the added strains inhibited the growth of the bacteria present
on the skin after disinfection, but only grew to a cell concentration similar to that of
the background noise. Thus when adjusting for the background noise, it seemed as the
mono-cultures did not grow.
In the preliminary assay, the background noise control revealed a 1.5 log lower noise
compared to the noise found in pig skin-explant assays 1 and 2. This may be due to
these pieces of skin being bought from diﬀerent places; Føtex and Slagtergården Lyngby,
respectively. The reason for switching supplier, was because Slagtergården Lyngby was
more ﬂexible.
To the best of my knowledge, the skin from both butchers had undergone similar process-
ing, including scalding during the slaughter. However, it is conceivable that the number
of microbes will increase over time, resulting in higher microbial background in older skin
samples. Time since scalding was not controlled, so this eﬀect may contribute to the
variation observed in the pig skin explant assays.
While the pig skin model seem closer to real life, there is as problem with the background
bacteria. Thus one aspect in improving the protocol for this assay, is to achieve bet-
ter disinfection of the skin. Other studies have been made on both porcine and human
skin. These skin explants had been disinfected with fungizone, betadine or a range of an-
38
==PAGE:45==
tibiotics, including penicillin, streptomycin, kanamycin, primocin [69, 95, 96]. However,
when using antibiotics or other antimicrobials to disinfect the pig skin, there is a risk of
leaving trace amounts on the skin. This could result in false-positive, as the inhibition
of the pathogens may not be due to the lactobacilli, but to leftover antimicrobials. With
ethanol, the disinfecting properties is believed to vanish as it evaporates.
Another possibility of disinfecting the skin is by UV. UVC (280–315nm) is widely used to
disinfect inanimate objects, but recently it has also been suggested as use for disinfecting
wounds, especially when these are infected with antibiotic resistant bacteria [97].
Thus, if this assay is to be improved, the above mentioned disinfection methods should
be investigated for their eﬀectiveness, maybe in combinations, in order to ﬁnd the best
disinfection method, with minimal damage to the skin.
In this assay ﬁve types of media were used, including both selective and non-selective
media.
Rogosa media was included as this is selective towards lactobacilli, however,
growth on this media, was very limited, and due to extremely small colonies, exact
counting was not possible. Therefore it was decided not to include results from growth
on rogosa in the result section. An example of large colonies and almost imperceptibly
small colonies on rogosa is seen in ﬁgure 20.

As the processing of the pigs is extensive, as mentioned in section 2.6, it is unlikely that
it and its microbiome is comparable to skin on a live pig. Therefore, it would have been
preferred to sacriﬁce the pigs just before the skin was needed as it was recommended by
Rubinchik et al., and as it was done in the study by Tulinskiet al. [69, 70]. Unfortunately,

the pigs before, during and after slaughter.
In this assay, it was chosen to add lactobacilli and pathogens to the pig skin-explants si-
multaneously. It could also be interesting to let the pathogens establish an infection, and
then add the lactobacilli. This would give insight into the eﬀectiveness of the lactobacilli
to eradicate already established colonies, and thus insight into the possibility of admin-
istering the lactobacilli to pigs, who might already be colonized with the pathogens. A
third possibility is to do the opposite, i.e. inoculate the pig skin-explants with lactobacilli
ﬁrst, and then expose them to the pathogens. This would give an insight into how well
the lactobacilli are at helping the skin withstand colonization of the pathogens.
Unfortunately due to the inconclusive results in the pig skin-explant assays these aspects
were not investigated further.
5.6
Virulence Reporter Assay
In the result section it is explained that due to the working AIPI and AIPIII controls,
it should be safe to assume that the assay works as intended. However, there is no clear
inhibition of the virulence associated genes RNAIII and hla, and upregulation of spa
when these reporter strains of S. aureus was subjected to the supernatant from L. reuteri
and L. johnsonii grown in MRS.
This was somewhat surprising, especially for L. reuteri, as Laughton et al. found L.
reuteri RC-14 to inhibit expression of RNAIII in S. aureus. Furthermore, they found an
inhibition of SSL11, which is commonly believed to be a staphylococcal exotoxin [98].
However, it must be taken into account, that the strains used in that study, and the
strains used here are not the same.
Furthermore, the S. aureus strain used in the virulence reporter assay (8325-4), is not
the same as the strain used in the well plate assay (SO385). Therefore it is also diﬃcult
to say whether the lactobacilli may inhibit the virulence genes in S. aureus SO385.
Lastly, it is important to emphasize, that not all virulence genes are under control of the
agr system. In the study by Laughton et al. it was hypothesized that the discovered
repression of SSL11, occurred independently of the staphylococcal agr system. Other
toxins have also been shown to be expressed independently of agr [99, 100]. Thus, al-
though L. reuteri and L. johnsonii were not found to inhibit the three virulence genes
tested in this assay (RNAIII, hla and spa), it may still reduce S. aureus virulence in other
ways.
To the best of my knowledge, there has been no published study on the eﬀect of L. john-
sonii on the virulence gene expression of S. aureus.

Conclusion
Antibiotic resistance is an emerging and increasing problem, leading to infections, that
can no longer be treated with antibiotics. Three pathogens, commonly found on or within
pigs and known to be excellent at developing antibiotic resistance are S. aureus, S. hyicus
and E. faecalis. S. aureus and E. faecalis are notorious for their role in causing severe
nosocomial infections in humans, while S. hyicus is a pig pathogen, known to cause ex-
udative dermatitis, with a very high morbidity for the pigs and leading to great economic
loss for farmers.
Therefore researchers worldwide are looking for alternative strategies for ﬁghting these
infections.
Within the past decade, it has been established that microbes can communicate with
each other via elaborate chemical signaling systems. Furthermore microbes are capable
of producing a variety of substances to inhibit rivaling species. In this thesis, it was
investigated whether two lactobacilli; L. reuteri and L. johnsonii, possess the ability to
inhibit the growth of any of the before mentioned pathogens, or if they are able to alter
the virulence gene expression of S. aureus. These two strains have been chosen, as they
are prevalent in pigs vaginal and gastrointestinal tract and thus are able to survive in
this niche. Furthermore, lactobacilli, as members of the lactic acid bacteria, are known
to produce a variety of antimicrobial compounds including lactic acid, H2O2 and bacte-
riocins.
There is an immense need for methods to study bacterial interactions. In this study
diﬀerent methods are tested, under various conditions thereby contributing with, not
only valuable data, but also valuable insight into the strengths and weaknesses of each
method. Thus a large part of this study has been focused on method development and
reﬁnement.
In this thesis, two interaction assays, evaluating eﬀect on growth, were performed: an
insert-well plate assay only allowing communication between species to happen with no
direct cell-cell contact, and a pig skin-explant assay where both pathogen and lactobacilli
were co-cultured directly on pig skin.
Results from the insert-well plate assay suggests that the inhibition of the pathogens is
due to a secreted extracellular metabolite. Whether this is production of lactic acid,
H2O2, bacteriocins or other unknown factors is yet to be determined. The clear advan-
tage of the insert-well plate assay, is the controlled circumstances of growth. However,
it has been found in an earlier study that the media plays a great role in an inhibition
pattern, and this assay is not representative of the natural environment of the strains.
The pig skin-explant assay was more representative of the natural environment, although
still not exactly the same. Furthermore, when co-cultured on the pig skin, the lactobacilli
and the pathogens had direct cell-cell contact. This form of growth is more representative
of the natural growth pattern of bacteria.
The pig skin-explant assay, suﬀered from inconclusive results, likely due to the diﬀerence
in the original bacterial load on the skin when brought from the butcher. This growth
found on uninoculated skin, resulted in a background noise, which disturbed the results.
Out of the three allegedly selective media, one was found to be selective enough to dis-
41
==PAGE:48==
tinguish between all tested pathogens and the lactobacilli. This was a valuable result, as
it could be used in to determine the amount of growth of the pathogenic strains, when
these were mixed with lactobacilli.
It is important to mention, that this type of in vitro studies are over simpliﬁed compared
to the natural situation. This study focuses on 1:1 interplay between species. In vivo,
the bacterial interaction is vastly more complex, with several species at play at the same
time. Thus, although it is relevant to develop simple methods to study interspecies in-
teraction, it is important to understand that the interaction in vivo may be diﬀerent.
The pH and H2O2 inﬂuence on growth was examined on the strains used in this study.
Here all tested pathogens, were found to be susceptible to H2O2. However, it is unclear
whether this is the cause of the growth inhibition seen in the insert-well plate assay. This
is due to the low amount of oxygen (3-5%) available under microaerophilic conditions, as
O2 is likely needed in the production of H2O2 by the lactobacilli.
Production of H2O2 by the lactobacilli was not studied, and the eﬀect remains specula-
tive.
It is more feasible, that the inhibition is due to lactic acid production, as the pH of the
media in the well plates of pathogens co-cultured with the lactobacilli had dropped to
approximately 4, which is below the MIC of all tested pathogens. therefore, pH drop is
a plausible explanation for the inhibition. Whether the lactobacilli produce lactic acid,
and whether this is the cause of the cause of the pH drop should be further investigated.
Lactobacilli are known to produce strain-speciﬁc bacteriocins and bacteriocin-like prod-
ucts. Thus the cause of inhibition seen in the interaction assays may be due to production
of these. One known bacteriocin is reuterin, which is produced by L. reuteri. Produc-
tion of reuterin may be the reason a higher inhibition is seen when the pathogens are
co-cultured with L. reuteri, however, it is unknown whether reuterin is produced under
the growth conditions in these assays.
An interaction assay evaluating the eﬀect of lactobacilli on the expression of selected vir-
ulence genes within the agr-system control in S. aureus was also performed. Considering
the controls working as expected, but no sign of altered expression of the virulence genes
it is concluded that this assay works as intended, but that the lactobacilli do not have an
obvious eﬀect on the agr-quorum sensing system in S. aureus. However, there are other
virulence genes in S. aureus that are not under the control of the agr-quorum sensing
system. As this study did not investigate this, it cannot be ruled out that the lactobacilli
may have an eﬀect on the expression of these.
In is clear that additional studies are needed in order to determine whether L. reuteri
and L. johnsonii can be used in designing a microbiota-targeted strategy to modulate
pathogen abundance.

Future Perspectives
This work represents a stepping stone towards designing an alternative strategy to combat
antibiotic resistant pathogens, by using lactobacilli to inhibit the growth and/or virulence
of pathogens.
However, it is evident that there are still knowledge gaps in the understandings of lacto-
bacilli and pathogen interactions. Firstly, the amount of H2O2 and bacteriocins produced
by the lactobacilli under the growth conditions provided should be investigated. The de-
tection of H2O2 could be done using AmplexTMUltraRed reagent, as was done by Singh
et al. [92]. This coupled with horseradish peroxidase may be used to detect H2O2 re-
leased from cells [101].
The amount of lactic acid may be detected using a lactic acid dehydrogenase (LDH)
commercial test kit, as done by Tomás et al. [16]. LDH converts NAD+ to NADH, and
this can be measured on a spectophotometer. The amount of NADH is proportional to
the amount on lactic acid [102].
Secondly, the pig-skin assay should be optimized in order to understand interactions oc-
curring in an environment mimicking the natural habitat of the bacteria on pigs. This
includes ﬁnding better ways to disinfect the pig skin explants. Various disinfection meth-
ods could be investigated for their eﬀectiveness, and perhaps a combination of several
techniques would be favorable in order to aﬄict minimal damage to the skin. Further-
more, it is imperative to choose a media that is more selective in order to distinguish
growth of lactobacilli versus growth of the pathogens. When these aspects are improved,
the mechanisms behind pathogen inhibition on the pig skin-explants could be further
investigated.
In order to investigate the impact of the environment on the metabolic products of the
lactobacilli or pathogens, a metabolic proﬁle may be assessed. This proﬁle represents
the cellular state when under speciﬁc conditions, and is a complex function of several
regulatory processes within the cell. This type of investigation could elucidate how spe-
ciﬁc bacteria alter their metabolic proﬁle when near other species, while still being in a
controlled environment [103, 104].
When the methods explained in this study are further reﬁned, it is also interesting to
shift focus onto more complex interspecies interactions, which are more representative of
an in vivo context.
Exploring these future perspectives, could bring researchers one step closer in evaluat-
ing the possibility of using L. reuteri and L. johnsonii as biocontrol to modulate the
colonization of E. faecalis, S. aureus and S. hyicus in and on pigs.
