Summary
The vertical structure of turbulent wind fields becomes of more interest for wind en-
ergy science since the size, swept area and hub height of the turbines has increased
substantially in the last years. This necessitates characterization of wind field and
its variation in the vertical direction and assessment of the loads encountered by the
turbine, including effects on the different structural components.
The present work investigates the characteristics of turbulence during events of ex-
treme turbulence intensity—according to the definition of the IEC standard. From
the analysis of 10-minute and 30-minute wind speed signals that present this con-
dition, we observe two marked contributions to the wind speed standard deviation:
large fluctuations due to changes in the flow occurring at large scales, and fluctua-
tions at short time scales associated to turbulence. A methodology to decompose the
observed signal in these two contributions is implemented, and the characteristics of
each one examined.
The turbulent component is studied in terms of turbulence energy, length scale and
turbulence anisotropy, via the analysis of the spectra of wind speed signals observed
in a met-mast located in Høvsøre, Denmark. Mann-spectral tensor model is fitted
to the observed spectra, and its parameters used to characterize the turbulence at
several heights during these events. Additionally, we studied turbulence statistics via
velocity increments and anisotropy aspects using Reynolds stress invariants, in order
to complement the results.
We found interesting and not expected turbulence characteristics, during the tran-
sition between two wind speed levels (or large scale fluctuations):
• Higher turbulence energy.
• Smaller length scales.
• Increased anisotropy.
Finally, possibilities to extend this work and apply its results to wind field simulations
and load calculations on wind turbines are discussed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The vertical structure of turbulent wind fields becomes of more interest for wind en-
ergy science since the size, swept area and hub height of the turbines has increased
substantially in the last years. This necessitates characterization of wind field and
its variation in the vertical direction and assessment of the loads encountered by the
turbine, including effects on the different structural components.
Of particular interest is to characterize turbulent wind field during events of ex-
treme turbulence intensity (according to the definition of the IEC standard), where,
as will be seen later, we observe a superposition of high turbulence and wind speed
fluctuations occurring at time scales longer than turbulence integral time scale.
The IEC 61400 standard deals with the turbulence component recommending wind
field simulations to assess the effects of the turbulence on the loads originated in
the structural components of the turbine. One of the approaches used is the wind
filed generation using Fourier simulations that are based in the spectral tensor model
developed by [Man94]. In order to use this approach in cases which present a super-
position of long-time scale and turbulent wind speed fluctuations, each fluctuation
type shall be well determined. The motivation of the present work is the attempt to
separate them and study how they change with height, as follows:
• Turbulence fluctuations: Investigation of the parameters associated with the
spectral tensor model at heights relevant for the wind industry. In parallel, a
study of Reynolds stress tensor anisotropy characteristics and turbulence statis-
tics and intermittecy effects are carried out.
• Large scale fluctuations: Velocity fluctuations due to front passage, for instance,
will be studied via wind speed rise time and wind speed rise magnitude.
It is important to point out that the IEC standard defines gusts as a wind speed
rise within the order of 10 seconds (for details see section 2.1), a time scale that lies
in between the time scales of the wind speed fluctuations mentioned above. This
definition is related mainly to extreme loads and treated by the standard with a
deterministic approach. In this work two types of gust-like fluctuations are studied,
one associated to time scales in the order of minutes, or ”frontal” gusts, corresponding
to long scale fluctuations, and the second one with gusts occurring over time scales
like those defined by the standard.

1.2
Background
The contribution to the turbulence intensity from front passage, gustiness and turbu-
lence was explored by [CQH11], as well as their vertical variation. However in that
work it is not clear how the limits between each contribution to velocity fluctuation
were determined. Furthermore, only the turbulence kinetic energy and its variation
with height is presented, but no other important characteristics, such as anisotropy
or stream wise turbulence length scale.
The latter is of importance when it comes to simulate loads on turbines. As stated
in [DNM16], simulations characterized via the dissipation, length scale and anisotropy
parameters in Mann´s spectral tensor model, have influence in the determination of
fatigue and extreme loads on turbines. The IEC standard recommends values that
fit with Kaimal spectra, even though they can vary substantially in different terrains,
stability conditions and height.
The work from [Cho+14] examine this issue and
estimates vertical profiles of these parameters under different stability and terrain
conditions. Nevertheless, [Cho+14] considers only wind fields over a limited range
of wind speeds, which don’t present extreme turbulence characteristics or gust-like
events due to front passage.
The turbulence characterization through gust-like events, with application to load
simulations on turbines, is not yet well-documented in the literature; neither is the
characterization of the turbulence anisotropy aspect for such events. Both topics are
important in order to determine the characteristics of wind field during the front pas-
sage and how severe the conditions which a wind turbine experiments during these
events are.
Regarding gusts, the IEC 61400 standard currently follows a deterministic approach
for estimating Extreme Operational Gusts (EOG), treating wind gusts and turbulence
as separated phenomena. Extreme operational gusts are specified with an amplitude
that will depend on the hub height wind speed and the reference turbulence intensity,
with a fixed duration of 10.5 seconds. In this sense, both phenomena are connected in
an indirect way by the standard, but the summation of fatigue loads from turbulence
and maximum loads from gusts is not explained.
The vertical characterization of gust is treated by [Suo+14] in terms of the peak
factor, gust factor and gust duration. Again, this analysis is made using data that
present turbulence intensities that are below the limit imposed by the extreme tur-
bulence model (ETM) from the IEC standard.

2.2
Gust definition
According to World Meteorological Organization (WMO), a Peak Gust corresponds
to the maximum wind speed observed within a time interval T of the t0 running av-
erage wind speed measurements (3 seconds for modelling and forecasting purposes).
The t0 running average will define the Gust Duration, tG, which will depend on
the response of the measuring system. Slowly responding systems will measure long
and smooth gusts after smear out the extreme values. Quickly responding systems in
the other hand, will be capable of measuring sharp increases in wind speeds of short
duration.
As discussed more in detail in section 2.4, the definition of gust duration uses an
ideal measuring chain, consisting of a single running average filter of duration t0, to
define the extreme winds that are detected behind this filter. This defines a peak
wind gust with duration t0. A particular measuring chain with different filters will
measure peak gusts with duration t0 if the peak gust obtained by this system is have
same if just the ideal running average of duration t0 filter is applied to the incoming
signal.

Due to the highly stochastic nature of wind, the characterization of the high fre-

continuous evolution of wind measuring systems make the specification of sampling

2.7
Chapter summary and remarks
2.7.1
Turbulence models from IEC
The turbulence models defined by the IEC standard, in particular, the Extreme Tur-
bulence Model (ETM), is used here to define the study cases. Sampling periods of
10 minutes that present turbulence standard deviations in the stream wise direction,
σu, larger than the specified by the model, was the main criteria used to select the
study cases.
2.7.2
Gust models definition
According to the IEC 64100 standard, the timescale of gusts corresponds to 10.5
seconds. Wind speed increments within longer time scales, namely, within periods of
minutes, are not considered gusts but fronts. In any case, the definition of a front
implies more characteristics than only large wind speed fluctuations, and changes in
scalars, like temperature, should also be present. In this work, wind speed fluctuations
occurring at larger time scales than the IEC gust definition will be treated as gust-like
fluctuations.
2.7.3
Spectral models
The spectral models presented here are used in two different ways. The Kaimal spec-
tra, presented in section 2.3.1, is used here to characterize a gust duration at time
scales within a range from seconds to 1 minute. The cases studied here are assumed
to happen under neutral conditions and expressions (2.15) to (2.18) applies.
The spectral tensor model in section 2.3.2 is the model used to fit the observed spectra
for the different cases of study. This model is the selected one because its application
in the estimation of loads exerted on turbines, and because it is characterized through
the the parameters αϵ2/3, L and Γ the main characteristics of turbulence. Unlike the
Kaimal spectra, this model allows us to characterize atmospheric turbulence at high
points (Kaimal spectra model is based on measurements up to 32 meters) that are
relevant for wind turbines.
2.7.4
Gust sampling and signal filtering
The methodology developed by [Bel87] uses Rice’s theory to estimate a gust mag-
nitude and its dependency on the filters imposed for a measuring chain, using this
approach to define a gust duration, tG, which characterizes the width of a wind speed
spike from an arbitrary measuring chain in terms of a running average filter. This
definition is used here in order to estimate the time scale at which the gusts occurs.
One result obtained by Beljaars is that the gust magnitude is better predicted at
==PAGE:33==
2.7 Chapter summary and remarks
23
low heights using the Kaimal spectral model, which, even though it is the result of
measurements at heights up to 32 meters, will be used here to have an estimation of
the time scale of sudden wind speed rises within a time range of seconds. In this work
expression (2.57) is used, since the sampling frequency of the sonic anemometers, as
shown in section 3.1, is high, 20 Hz, and the non-discrete form to estimate an average
peak factor can be used. Finally, the assumption of bi-variate Gaussian between wind
speeds and their derivative is a simplification that may not apply to atmospheric flow
as stated by [BBP07] because the intermittent nature of turbulence spread along a
wide range of length scales of wind speed fluctuations.
2.7.5
Invariant analysis
The invariant analysis is useful to have a qualitative characterization of the turbulence
nature and turbulence anisotropy before, during and after long scale wind speed
fluctuations. The analysis in this work is made on the sonic signal transformed to a
stream wise coordinate system. The location of the Reynolds stress tensor invariants
in the Lumley triangle will give information about the distortion present in eddies
and its evolution over time during long scale wind speed fluctuations.
2.7.6
Turbulence statistics and structure functions
A statistical analysis of turbulent wind speed fluctuations is interesting in order to
grasp some particular characteristics of this type of events. As mentioned earlier,
the assumption of fluctuations of Gaussian nature can be assessed with the proba-
bility density functions of speed increments, and intermittent characteristics can be
observed. Additionally, the turbulence energy dissipation, and in consequence, tur-
bulence energy, is well described by third order structure function. The results from
this analysis can be used to compare to the results from spectral analysis.

3.2

According to the IEC 61400-13 standard, cases in which the wind speed signal are
affected by a trend should be identified and highlighted as not reproducible by stan-

wind speed standard deviation), σU from the wind speed gust—understood in this
case as a trend—and the turbulence itself. In order to do so, it is necessary to define
a filtering methodology which can separate both contributions.

ity of the filter at the cut-off frequencies, the so called Gibbs phenomenon of Fourier
series arises, which causes that the approximation will be bad regardless of the value
of M and unwanted ripples will appear in the filter near the cut-off frequencies.
To cope with this problem, a non-rectangular window can be specified to give con-
tinuity in the neighbouring of the truncating bounds. The Hamming and a Kaiser
windows implement this approach, the first one using a cosine function to define the
truncating window, and the latter a modified Bessel function of first kind and order
0. Figure 3.6 shows the different windows. The setback of the Hamming and Kaiser
filters is that, even though the ripples in the filter shape are minimized considerably,
there is no a sharp transition anymore at the cut-off frequency, which is replaced by
a band transition. In this regard, the Kaiser window method comes to be the most
flexible, since it allows to control the width of transition band, as well as the level of
the overshoot due to the ripples. This method was selected to be used for de-trending
purposes, implemented in the form of a high-pass filter.

For more details see [Orf10].

by the event (front passage) that increased the turbulence intensity above the limit
defined by the extreme turbulence model of the IEC. Since the trend corresponded to
fluctuations in the low frequency region, in other words, where the energy containing
region of the spectrum lies, the selection of the appropriate low cut-off frequency is
critical for a later spectral fitting and analysis.
The frequency associated with the trend fluctuations, ftrend, may lie in the same

as the time of the wind speed increment within 10 minutes, as shown in Figure 3.7.

• The raw signal is smoothed using a moving average window of one minute.
• The differential of the smoothed signal is estimated and then smoothed again
using a short window of N = 101 elements.
• The zero-crossing points in the differentiated and smoothed signal are detected.
• The difference in wind speed between consecutive zero-crossing points is calcu-
lated.

3.5
Turbulence statistics via wind speed increments
Turbulence statistics and intermittecy effects were investigated using the wind speed
increments at different time scales. Since the cases studied present different average
wind speed, making increment statistics out of time differences [BBP07] is not longer
possible. The methodology used is the following:
• In each individual signal, a set of time increments, τ, going from the sampling
time step, dts = 0.05 s to a time scale slightly longer than the end of the inertial
range, 25 seconds, is defined. The time increments were logarithmically spaced
as τ = dts2m, with m going from 0 to 9.
• Spatial increments, r, were then calculated as r = τU, with U as the mean wind
speed of the individual signal.
• Wind speed increments are calculated using expression (2.78) for each signal,
spatial increment, r and height.
• Spatial increment bins with centers ranging from 0.3 m to 500 m were created.
The range of the bins is wide as the time increments τ and changes at each
height.
• Each bin at each height is then filled with the corresponding wind speed incre-
ments from all cases.
• Wind speed increments pdf are then calculated for each bin. Structure functions
are also estimated using the expression (2.80). From the third order structure
function, and following Kolmogorov’s 4/5 law, the dissipation rate is investi-
gated.
• This procedure is applied to the signal corresponding to 10 minutes before,
during and after the wind speed rise event.

3.7.1
Data selection processing
The necessity of having three component wind velocity observations restrict the anal-
ysis to 26 out of 69 cases. This is a consequence selection criteria, which requires
valid measurements at all heights, in each selected case.
The fact that the cases selected correspond to wind coming from the sea, prevents
their categorization by stability conditions, despite the heat flux data available at the
site. The transition between the sea surface, with no data available, and land, makes
this categorization not recommendable. Nevertheless, the cases selected in general
presents high wind speeds and the assumption of neutral conditions is reasonable.
3.7.2
Signal decomposition
The methodology used to estimate the contributions from long time scale and tur-
bulence fluctuations to the total wind speed signal is based on a FIR filter using a
cut-off frequency with a upper limit defined by the rise time, trise. This methodology
takes advantage in the fact that the variance associated to long time scale fluctuations
is much larger than the associated to turbulence. A sensitivity analysis around the
==PAGE:49==
3.7 Chapter summary and remarks
39
reference cut-off frequency detects quickly changes of importance in the variances of
the decomposed signal, due to the rapid decay in each component’s variance when
the reference cut-off frequency approaches.
The use of a FIR filter presents three advantages:
• It allows the extraction of a smoother and simpler low frequency signal (this
is not easy to accomplish using a moving average window or a Kalman filter),
that suits better for its characterization in terms of rise time, wind speed rise
and Gust/Front-like factor.
• The estimation of a cut-off frequency is directly associated to the time scale of
the ”trend”. This information, in terms of frequency is useful when it comes to
assess if the information extracted from turbulent fluctuations is reasonable.
• The Kaiser window is convenient because it allows flexibility in the filtering
around the cut-off frequency. This is important for the spectral model fitting
since the Hamming window for instance, restrict the buffer zone width around
the cut-off frequency and cannot be reduced to have a sharp transition with
minimal ripples.
The main disadvantage of the FIR filter is the computational cost that becomes
important when the sensitivity analysis made to detect a reasonable cut-off frequency
implies many filtering operations on numerous original signals.
3.7.3
Observed spectra and fitting
Prior spectral fitting, the observed spectra is smoothed using log-spaced bins. In
order to reduce noise, the number of bins chosen was 10 per decade. This operation
is done for 30-minute signals and also to signals split in 10-minute intervals before,
during and after the wind speed rise.
The Mann-spectral model is fitted to the observed spectra using a Least-Squares
approach. This fitting considers weights that will prioritize the frequencies in the
inertial range and the energy containing region using the expression (3.13).
This
approach will avoid fitting problems originated by the spectra overestimation at high
frequencies, which may be consequence of wind speed fluctuations happening at scales
near the response length of the sonic anemometers. Additionally, the minimization
problem associated to the LSQ fitting is constrained via the standard deviation of
the stream wise wind component. The Mann-spectral tensor is fitted on:
• Each observed spectra.
• A direct average of all observed spectra.
• An average of the spectra normalized by the friction velocity, u∗.

3.7.4
Turbulence statistics
The methodology used to investigate turbulence statistics via wind speed increments
define a range of log-spaced time increments which, in order to make possible the
stacking of wind speed increments of all the cases selected, are transformed to spatial
increments using the mean wind speed of each individual signal. After the transfor-

after the wind speed jump are constructed for spatial increments falling in the inertial

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
5.1
Data analysis and processing
From all the cases showing extreme turbulence and detected in the meteorological
mast 1—69 in total— just 26 were considered for analysis. Since the characterization
of turbulence at different levels required measurements from three dimensional sonic
anemometers, this study used the observations made during the same events in the
meteorological mast 2, which counts with 3D sonic anemometer installed between
10 m and 100 m height. Unfortunately, the measurements from these anemometers
presented invalid data spread over all heights. The restriction of using only records
with valid observations at all heights, reduced the cases of study significantly. Even
though the data recovery was on average higher than 80%, the measurements at 60
and 100 meters showed recovery rates of only 50% and 62%, respectively.
The cases studied showed in general high wind speeds, and the assumption of neutral
conditions is reasonable. Nevertheless, 6 cases showed low wind speeds, low friction
velocities and low values of u′w′, during the 10-minute period before the wind speed
jump event. Measurements of the sea surface temperature would allow us to separate
cases in terms of stability conditions, which would improve the analysis.
The averaging window considered when transforming the original signal from sonic
to stream wise coordinate system was 1 minute. This window length may be too
short, but it acts already as a filter that minimize long time scale fluctuations in the
transverse component of the wind speed, simplifying the methodology. This early de-
cision minimize the values of the Svv (Transverse component spectrum) in the lower
frequency range, which makes easier to identify the cut-off frequency for low pass
filtering. Furthermore, the response time of a turbine’s yaw control system is shorter
than the length of the averaging window, and the stream wise and transverse wind
speed fluctuations experimented by the turbine will be similar.
5.2
Signal decomposition
The methodology developed to decompose the original signal allowed the identifica-
tion of cut-off frequencies that spread in a range from 0.001 to 0.01 Hz, at 10 meters
and 100 m height respectively. A fixed value, as the one used by [CQH11] of 0.0017
Hz, appears to be not applicable for all the cases investigated, since the spectral
fitting and the resulting Mann-model parameters were very sensitive to the cut-off
==PAGE:84==
74
5 Discussion
frequency. The methodology developed—rise time estimation and sensitivity analy-
sis of the variance of low and band-pass filtered signals— allowed to identify proper
cut-off frequency values but with a high computational cost, when it comes to filter
hundreds of wind speed signals. Additionally, a sensitivity analysis could be neces-
sary, this time to determine the effect of the Kaiser window transition band width.
This study used a fixed value of ±50% of the low-pass cut-off frequency.
From signal decomposition, we yielded an expression that relates the ratio of tur-
bulence standard deviation and the total signal standard deviation, σturb/σtot. This
expression should be improved and validated using more data, since the number of
cases used in its estimation is reduced.
5.3
Mann-model fitting
The spectral fitting showed in general low residuals. The parameters estimated fell
in general within the limits of the look-up table method, but around 15% out of all
fittings showed values for the anisotropy parameter too close to the table limits. This
could be improved by selecting a better methodology to find the cut-off frequency or
extending the limits of the table used. The latter is beyond the scope of these work,
since spectral tensor model calculations are very costly in terms of computational
time.
The fitting show some problems when it tried to adjust parameters during the wind
speed jump. It was possible to identify the spectral peaks in the three velocity com-
ponents, but it showed to be off when it comes to describe the dissipation rate in the
inertial rate, as well as the anisotropy parameter. This can be solved again extending
the limits of the tables used during the look-up table method.
The estimation of the parameters using an average of normalized spectra instead
of the averaging of a pool of parameters obtained from individual spectral fittings
seems to show more realistic results. Nevertheless, the spectra are normalized using
the friction velocity, which is a reasonable assumption within the surface layer, but
could lead to deviations at heights that may not correspond to this limit, then these
results should be taken carefully.
In general, the spectral averaging can be done without normalization, using cases
that lies within a narrow range of mean wind speeds (between 8 and 10 m/s for in-
stance). The data available ranges from 5 to 25 m/s and this type of averaging is not
possible due to the reduced data. Anyway, the normalized spectra seems to collapse
well at high levels, as Figure 4.18 shows.
==PAGE:85==
5.4 Turbulence statistics
75
5.4
Turbulence statistics
The results from the turbulence statistics showed discrepancies in the dissipation
parameters obtained from the 4/5 Kolmogorov’s law and the spectral fitting. This
could be originated from the several averaging steps followed in both methods, but in
general they are not comparable. In any case, this approach was useful to observe the
relative turbulence energy differences before, during and after the wind speed jump
event.
5.5
Invariant analysis
The invariant analysis made before, during, and after the wind speed jump showed in
general results that agree with the ones obtained using spectral fitting. The anisotropy
level during the wind speed jump is much higher than before and after. Nevertheless,
it was hard to see a consistent behavior with height for some cases, since we expected
to see more anisotropic turbulence as we approach to the ground. In general we can
see this behavior, but there are some cases which show heights, specifically at 40
and 60 m, with higher anisotropy levels than the levels closer to the ground. It is
important to notice that in this analysis the wind speed signal was not filtered for
frequencies with noise due to the sonic anemometer tolerance, same situation for the
rest of the stages.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The aim of this work was to study the turbulence characteristics along the vertical
direction, for wind speed signal showing turbulence intensities above the values speci-
fied by the extreme turbulence model in the IEC standard. It was found that in many
cases, the origin of such a large turbulence intensities comes from the superposition
of long time scale speed fluctuations—which are not turbulence and do not present a
linear behavior–and turbulence itself. A methodology was developed to identify the
contribution of each type of fluctuation to the total wind speed standard deviation.
The signal decomposition used signal filtering methods that allowed to characterize
the frequency limit between the two components and investigate how the standard
deviation of each type of fluctuation varies with height. Finally, a relation for the
ratio between the turbulence standard deviation, σturb and the observed standard
deviation, σtot is proposed.
After the identification of the turbulent component, a characterization was carried
out in terms of turbulence length scale, turbulence energy and turbulence anisotropy
at different heights above ground. These characteristics were studied using the pa-
rameters L, αϵ2/3 and Γ in Mann-spectral model by fitting it to the observed spectra.
The fitting on the 30-minute signals show results in line with findings in [Sat+13]
and [Cho+14], regarding their vertical variation.
The long time scale fluctuations showed an evolution from low to high mean wind
speeds that occurs in the range of minutes. When turbulence is characterized in these
three stages, before, during and after the wind speed jump, the resulting Mann-model
parameters show interesting features:
• The turbulence length scale during the transition between the two wind speed
levels shows values that are much smaller than the recommendations from the
IEC standard. Before and after the transition, the length scale parameter shows
values in line with these recommendations.
• The turbulence energy dissipation rate during the transition increase to levels
that are even higher than after the higher wind speed is reached.
• The turbulence anisotropy increase noticeably during the transition, showing
values that are much larger than the ones observed before and after the wind
speed jump.
Although the wind speed is higher after the transition zone and one would expect
to see higher turbulence energy than in the other two stages, the results showed
==PAGE:88==
78
6 Conclusion
that during the wind speed jump there exist mechanisms of self-generated turbulence.
Furthermore, during the transition we observe highly distorted eddies of small length
scale. This phenomenon is more evident when we move further up from the ground
level to heights comparable to the hub height of a modern wind turbine. As stated
by [DNM16], these would lead to increased turbine loads.
In order to compare the results obtained with this methodology, the turbulence en-
ergy dissipation rate was estimated via the third order structure function of velocity
increments, δr, and the 4/5 Kolmogorov’s law, within the inertial range. The results
from these analysis showed larger values at all stages when compared to the spectral
analysis, but in relative terms, we observed again higher dissipation rates during the
transition stage, at heights above 10 meters from the ground. From both methodolo-
gies, we observe that the difference in the dissipation rate between stages increases
as we depart from the surface.
The probability density function of the normalized wind speed increments shows also
interesting features. During the transition stage, we observe more intermittecy effects
than the other stages, and a higher probability of large wind accelerations than the
one predicted by a Gaussian distribution. This is observed in different length/time
scales and at all heights.
Regarding the turbulence anisotropy, we investigate its characteristics via the location
of Reynolds stress tensor invariants in the Lumley triangle. This analysis confirmed
that during the transition stage, the eddies observed present high levels of distortion,
approaching to the limit of 1-component turbulence anisotropy in the Lumley triangle.
From these results, we speculate that, in the cases studied, the transition stage would
correspond to a thin shear layer between two bigger structures.
The characteris-
tics of this type of structures, according to [Hun+10], include highly distorted eddies,
self-generated turbulence, smaller turbulence length scales and increased intermittecy
effects, in line with the results of this study.
Regarding the gust characteristics—in reference to the definition in the IEC standard—
we also observed increased peak and gust factors during the transition zone. Further-
more, the values of the peak factor are larger than the ones predicted by the model
in [Bel87], which can not be applied to this flow condition.
6.1
Future work
The methodology developed here should be applied in the future to data sets from
other locations. One of the main issues with this study is the lack of cases to study,
since this type of events are not usual. A characterization of turbulence characteristics
depending on stability conditions would be also interesting.
==PAGE:89==
6.1 Future work
79
Additionally, the turbulence characteristics depicted in this work, are observed at
wind speeds that lay within the operational range of commercial wind turbines and
they will face harsh conditions in terms of loads. It would be interesting to develop
a model that considers this type of non-stationary events, as well as their particular
turbulence characteristics, with applications to wind field simulations.
