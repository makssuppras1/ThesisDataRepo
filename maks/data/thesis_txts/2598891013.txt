Abstract
Current production of methyl ketones is purely based on chemical synthesis and relies on
the usage of fossil resources. A more sustainable approach for production can potentially be
achieved by utilizing cell factories. In this thesis, Pseudomonas putida was engineered to pro-
duce 2-pentanone through the CoA dependent chain elongation pathway by using acetate and
butyrate as a carbon source. Several genes from various bacteria were assembled into the path-
way required for 2-pentanone production and introduced into strain P. putida EM42, a genome
reduced derivative of P. putida KT2440; growth and production of constructed strains were
compared and analyzed. Moreover, we investigated the toxicity of 2-pentanone to determine
the production limits for P. putida strains. Furthermore, we established the extraction proto-
col for detection of methyl ketones using GC-FID, and optimized fermentation conditions for
2-pentanone. The highest production of 9.94 mg/L was achieved from 3 g/L of butyrate as
the sole carbon source in the P. putida EM42 strain, harboring a plasmid encoding thl (thio-
lase from Clostridium acetobutilucum), atoDA (CoA-transferase from Escherichia coli) and adc
(decarboxylase from C. acetobutilucum).
Additionally, the prospect of utilizing engineered cells for production of bioﬂuorinated com-
pounds was demonstrated. Fluorine have been shown to improve pharmacokinetic and physio-
chemical properties and is an important drug component which can enhance bioavailability and
eﬃcacy. We cloned the acsA gene from Bacillus Subtilis into the best performing plasmid for
2-pentanone production, transformed it into the strain P. putida EM42 and observed improved
consumption of ﬂuoroacetate. Our ﬁndings suggest that using of ﬂuorinated substrates for the
constructed strains will allow production of ﬂuorinated derivatives in the further study.

Aims & Objectives
The main aim of this thesis was to design, construct and assemble a biosynthetic pathway for the
production of 2-pentanone in a suitable production host. The secondary aim was to investigate
the possibility of producing bioﬂuorinated compounds utilizing the constructed pathway. An
overview of the main scope of the project is visualised in Figure 1. The project was separated
into several objectives described below as a means of reaching the ﬁnal goal of engineering a
strain capable of producing 2-pentanone and potentially produce ﬂuorinated compounds:
• Determine the toxicity towards 2-pentanone and tolerance of potential production hosts
to ensure viability of production titers and to determine the most suitable host.
• Design, construct and assemble diﬀerent expression systems harboring the biosynthetic
pathway for 2-pentanone production.
• Integrate the pathway into a suitable production host.
• Analyze and compare production rates of the diﬀerent expression systems.
• Investigate the possibility of incorporating ﬂuorine into the biosynthetic pathway of 2-
pentanone

Background
1.1
Cell factories
In the last century, the chemical industry has primarily been relying on fossil fuels. The ex-
tensive use of fossil fuels has resulted in increasing concerns of both eventual depletion of
non-renewable resources and also the impact it has on global warming. Even though climate
change has led to an unreliable supply of resources, it has, fortunately, also spawned increased
interest and motivation in developing more sustainable methods of production. This has given
rise to a branch in biotechnology, known as ’white biotechnology’, being fully devoted to solving
these complications in a much more sustainable fashion [1].
The central concept of white biotechnology is the utilization of living cells (e.g. derivatives of
plants and animals, yeast, bacteria and fungi) with the goal of exploiting enzymes to synthesize
energy favorable, bio-compatible products while also reducing waste signiﬁcantly. Over the
past decades, the primary focus has been on the production of biodegradable plastics such as
polyesters of polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs). It has since then expanded into other ﬁelds such
as the medicinal-, agricultrural-, and biofuel industry. However, even though applying white
biotechnology in the industry is promising, the main burden of most products are still to meet
the economic requirements for industrial scale production [2]. Through evolution, cells have
developed extensive regulation and intricate interactions in their metabolic pathways to ensure
homeostasis under diverse conditions. To establish suﬃcient carbon ﬂux in the desired pathway
requires precise modulation of endogenous pathways and their regulation [3].
Cell engineering for improved biosynthesis of chemicals is the center of industrial biotechnology
and is marketed to be worth more than hundreds of billions of USD/year. In 2015, the petro-
chemical industry was valued to be higher than 3 trillion USD/year, warranting a considerably
larger market for metabolic engineering to be able to expand into [4].
The term ’cell factory’ is used to describe organisms utilized in bioprocesses to convert sub-
strate into certain products of interest. Cell factories are often manipulated through rational
approaches with the intent of improving performance in a production environment and in-
creasing production yield. This can be done indirectly by improving production conditions or
directly through metabolic engineering [3]. Cell factory engineering can be utilized for numerous
applications in which some of the central ones are [4]:
• Fabrication of industrially relevant products in a more sustainable manner.
2
==PAGE:12==
• Study of biological functions of genes and proteins by genome engineering. Removing,
regulating or introducing new functions can be performed to analyze cellular functions
such as biochemical reactions, phenotype changes, and regulatory networks.
• Generation of analytical components such as antibodies for immunoassays and proteins
for structural and functional analysis etc.
Davy et. al. (2017) recapitulated that nearly all cell factory engineering eﬀorts can be classiﬁed
with one or a combination of the following purposes: (1) optimizing the production of a native
metabolite; (2) expression of a heterologous biosynthetic pathway to produce a non-native
metabolite; (3) expression of a heterologous protein [4]. Strategies towards mitigating these
challenges can be found for each of the three classiﬁcations. Since the primary objective of
this study is to enable production 2-pentanone, a non-native metabolite, by expression of a
heterologous biosynthetic pathway, the challenges and strategies surrounding this type of cell
factory engineering will be reviewed in the following section.
1.1.1
Host selection
According to Davy et al. (2017), challenges of introducing a new heterologous pathway into
a novel host can be associated with either (i) the compatibility of the pathway with the novel
host, or (ii) the expression of the pathway itself. Compatibility issues include compartmental-
ization (rarely for prokaryotes) and availability of enzymes, co-factors, precursors. Furthermore,
proper functionality of enzymes has to be considered. These challenges can be overcome to cer-
tain degrees by selecting a proper host for production before beginning metabolic engineering.
Even though a desired biosynthetic pathway is preexisting in a native host, metabolic and/or
enzyme engineering strategies are often necessary to increase production yields. In a recent
study by Jos´e Manuel Otero et al. (2013), the yield of naturally produced succinate in S.
cerevisiae could be improved by 30-fold through metabolic engineering. Succinate is consumed
by the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle. Consequently, deleting genes encoding succinate- and
3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase isoenzymes improved the production of succinate and gly-
oxylate to prevent glycine and serine depletion [5].
The selection of an appropriate cell factory host is generally based on following criteria: (1) the
level of metabolic engineering required and the current state of the art in relation to genome
editing tools, (2) the integrity between the desired product and the toxicity towards the host,
and (3) metabolic requirements in terms of available pathways, co-factors and precursors [4].
The most dominant choices for cell factories are still the simpler and more established organisms
such as E. coli and S. cerevisiae. However, as superior genomic tools and methods becomes
available, the scope will most likely be shifting towards organisms with improved innate capa-
bilities. The ﬁnal aspiration would be to achieve a minimalistic strain fully optimized for the
3
==PAGE:13==
desired biosynthetic pathway [6].
Abundance of co-factors and precursors such as adenosine triphosphate (ATP) and nicotinamide
adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NAD(P)H) in the host are necessary if high levels of expression
are desired. Strategies for increasing reductive power of cell factories were recently reviewed by
Wang et al. (2013) [7]. Based on the requirements of the chosen biosynthetic pathway, co-factor
engineering should be considered in order to improve the metabolic ﬂux through the pathway
and ﬁnal production yields. For example, the primary reducing co-factor NADPH is facilitated
by the pentose phosphate pathway, isocitrate dehydrogenase in the TCA cycle and malic en-
zyme amongst others [7]. Overexpression of a NAD+ kinase was found to result in accelerated
supply of NADPH in E. coli, which in turn improved poly(3-hydroxobutyrate) production [8].
Other strategies for improving cofactor utilization includes pathway rerouting [9] and replac-
ing enzyme dependencies e.g. from NAD+ dependent glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydroge-
nase (GAPDH) from C. glutamicum to non-phosphorylating NADP+ dependent GAPDH from
Streptococcus mutans which resulted in increased growth and lysine production [10].
Based on the desired product, the host’s secretion capabilities are important to consider when
selecting an appropriate host. It is commonly seen that Bacillus spp. are superior in protein
secretion compared to E. coli, while C. glutamicum appears to be superior at secretion of amino
acids, biofuels and chemicals [6].
Some organisms are disputed to possess innate characteristics beneﬁcial for industrial produc-
tion. P. putida has been recognized as a strain with superior redox capacity and high tolerance
towards solvent [11, 12, 13]. The intricate characteristics of the P. putida species have been
developed through evolution by survival of harsh conditions as a rhizosphere bacterium and in
soil [14]. P. putida KT2440, the highly characterized strain, has become a representative model
as a highly versatile production chassis for complex bioprocesses [11, 15, 16].
1.1.2
Metabolic pathway (re)construction
The presence of metabolites in nature is vastly abundant. The plant kingdom alone has been
estimated to contain between 200.000 and 1.000.000 metabolites, while humans is believed to
contain over 40.000. Identiﬁcation and characterization of these metabolites and their metabolic
pathway can broaden the extent of synthetic biology and help counteract environmental issues
by opening the door towards more sustainable production methods [17]. As of 2020, the Kyoto
Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database contained 537 pathway maps with
∼19.000 metabolites. This discrepancy in numbers signiﬁes the extensive number of metabolic
pathways yet to be mapped and potentially utilized in the scientiﬁc community and industry.
In the ﬁeld of synthetic biology, various biotechnological and computational methods and tools
have been developed to engineer host genomes for biosynthesis of biofuels, commodity chemi-
4
==PAGE:14==
cals and pharmaceuticals [18]. Many chemical compounds are rarely found in nature or only
inherent to very speciﬁc organisms. Instead, novel metabolic pathways can be constructed to
produce desired compounds from microorganisms. Synthetic metabolic pathway construction
often involves assembling multiple genetic parts from diﬀerent species to construct a metabolic
pathway for a speciﬁed purpose. The enzymes involved are naturally not ﬂux-optimized through
evolution compared to their natural counterparts [18].
Metabolic pathways can be constructed using a variety of methods and tools. Typical strategies
involve reconstructing heterologous metabolic pathways by multiple known metabolic reactions
stored in databases such as KEGG, MetaCyc, and BRENDA combined with reaction rules,
which is a generic description of chemical bonding required to catalyze the product from the
substrate. Furthermore, exploiting the promiscuity of enzymes towards multiple substrates can
help in designing a novel metabolic pathway [19]. Computational tools are also available to
assist in the design of novel metabolic pathways. Recently, RetroPath 2.0, an automated open
source tool was provided by Del´epine et al. (2018) to the scientiﬁc community [20]. It allows for
the user to input a desired product, the substrates involved and a set of reaction rules. The tools
will subsequently produce a network of potential pathways based on Enzyme Commission (EC)
numbers (classiﬁcation scheme for enzymes based the chemical reactions) as seen in Figure 2.
In silico pathway design methods generates pathways based on predictions. However, due to
the promiscuity of enzymes, hypothetical pathways which might not work in reality are also
generated. Additionally, in silico generated pathways may include reactions not occurring in
nature, excessively long pathways or form cyclic structures where the target metabolite is being
used as an intermediate instead of an end-product. These are all considerations which have to
be taken into account during the construction of metabolic pathways [18].

1.1.4
Integration of the heterologous pathway into the engineered
host
Production of a compound of interest can be performed in the native host followed by optimiza-
tion or alternatively be transferred to an engineered host. The selection of host severely impli-
cates how both upstream- and downstream processes should be managed [27]. Unestablished
native hosts can occasionally produce toxic by-products which require intricate downstream
processing for removal, or the host might not be properly adaptable for industrial fermenta-
tion. Furthermore, native hosts can have inhibitory metabolites, which are often avoided by
using non-native hosts. On the contrary, industrially established organisms might have dif-
ﬁculties adjusting to the transferred pathway [4].
Some organisms have been shown to be
more suitable for large scale productions and the stress conditions which follows. In a study by
Ankenbauer et al. (2020) the metabolic and transcriptional response of P. putida under glucose
deﬁciency was studied [11]. P. putida displayed superior adaption towards glucolytic depletion
by swiftly increasing intracellular ampliﬁcation of guanosine pentaphosphate (ppGpp), which
is the suggested to regulate rpoS, the stress response gene [28]. Furthermore, P. putida was
able to recover and maintain a healthy Adenylate Energy Charge (AEC) (a measure of cell’s
energy state) under continuous starvation [11]. Large-scale fermentations can impose stressful
conditions on the cells, and the organism’s ability to adapt to the circumstances is crucial
Some of the general challenges seen in expressing a heterologous biosynthetic pathway in an
alternative host are:
• Co-factor availability: Overexpressed pathways naturally depletes available cofactors.
Deleting genes from other pathways utilizing the same cofactors as the pathway of interest,
can signiﬁcantly increase ﬂux through the desired pathway [29].
• Substrate and co-substrate availability: Suﬃcient production of substrates and precursors
are necessary for high production titers. In some cases, hosts produce similar competing
enzymes which can reduce substrate availability [30].
• Accumulation of product due to missing speciﬁc eﬄux pumps which can result in de-
creased ﬂux through the biosynthetic pathway [31, 32]. Furthermore, accumulation of
certain compounds increases the risk of toxicity towards the host.
• Inactivity of a single/multiple genes resulting in limited product formation. [4].
8
==PAGE:18==
• Low production yields due to improper folding of foreign enzymes caused by variance in
di-sulﬁde bond formation between expression [33]
Some of these challenges can be avoided to certain degrees by selecting an appropriate host
such as ensuring co-factor availability and compatible protein folding of the host. Additionally,
following strategies elucidated by Davy et. al. [4] could be considered to improve heterologous
pathway expression:
• Removal of competing pathways to ensure suﬃcient co-factor, substrate and co-substrate
availability.
• Transporter engineering to provide the necessary metabolic ﬂux by addition of passive or
active transporters.
• Transcriptional engineering e.g. by overexpressing regulatory genes to provide increased
expression of pathway genes.
• Removal of by-products can in some situations improve production yield [34].
Conclusively, the performance of a heterologous pathway introduced into a host relies heavily
on the considerations and strategies addressed throughout the whole engineering process.

Current production of organic compounds relies heavily on fossil resources such as coal, petroleum,
and natural gas. The practice of using nonrenewable resources places a heavy toll on the envi-

Methyl ketones are deﬁned as a group of organic compounds in which one of the hydrocarbons
next to a ketone group (C=O) is a methyl group as seen in Figure 4. Methyl ketones are useful

1.3
P. putida - a promising production platform
1.3.1
The metabolic versatility of P. putida
Pseudomonas putida, a soil bacterium, have long been known for its prowess in tolerance to-
wards oxidative stress, robustness and capacity of degradation of aromatic compounds. P.
putida has shown high tolerance towards extreme environmental conditions such as high tem-
perature, extreme pH and in presence of toxins or solvents. Furthermore, the growth rate is
fast and nutritional requirements are relatively low. Combined with the thorough research
performed on the organism, this bacterial specie has great potential to become well-established
hosts for industrial applications [43, 44]. Thus, P. putida is a model host to be utilized as pro-
duction platform for solvents such as methyl ketones, which can be toxic for other organisms.
P. putida shows great metabolic potential by having inherently encoded pathways for metabo-
lizing an extensive range of compounds [45]. Recently, Mart´ınez-Garc´ıa et. al. (2014) created
a novel P. putida strain derived from the well-known P. putida KT2440, from which limiting
genes, such as the ﬂagellum machinery, and other non-essential genes from a production per-
spective, were removed by knock-out [12] to improve net accumulation of ATP and NADPH.
Furthermore, genes encoding deoxyribonucleases, which can interfere with gene insertion were
removed to improve physical integrity of inserted DNA. This resulted in a strain with im-
proved growth potential, higher tolerance towards endogenous oxidative stress and enhanced
the strain’s ability to retain recombinant constructs [12].

1.4
Biocatalytic ﬂuorination of compounds
The chemical group known as amide (nitrogen linked to carbonyl) is one of the most prevalent
groups in medicinal chemistry. All but four of the most sold drugs in 2018 contained amide
groups [50]. The reason for this is the stability of amides and polarity which allows for these
drugs to bind with biological receptors and enzymes. However, for drugs to perform eﬀectively,
it must be able to sustain metabolic degradation. A well-established method to diminish drug
metabolism is the incorporation of ﬂuorine atoms. Generally unbeknownst to biology, ﬂuorine
both introduces polarity to drugs and reduces enzymatic catalysis. Furthermore, ﬂuorine can
improve pharmacokinetic and physiochemical properties by e.g. enhancing potency, metabolic
stability, and membrane permeability [51]. The application of ﬂuorine in drug development
takes advantage of the small size of the ﬂuorine atom (van der Waals radius of 1.4˚A) and the
binding strength between carbon-ﬂuorine at 98-115 kcal/mol, the strongest of common carbon-
hetero-atom bonds [51, 52]. Current production of ﬂuorinated compounds relies heavily on
the usage of chemical products. This often results in high amounts of by-products which can
be considered as chemical waste [53]. Besides the environmental pollution resulting from pro-
duction of ﬂuorinated compounds, exposure to ﬂuorinated compounds is also toxic for humans
and animals which can result in various negative health eﬀects [54]. Consequently, it would be
beneﬁcial to attain more sustainable processes for manufacturing ﬂuorinated compounds. An
ideal method would be to develop cell factories capable of synthesizing ﬂuorinating compounds
using safer substrates such as Na-F and sugars as a main carbon source. Biological ﬂuorination
is a very rare occurrence, however, in the last decades multiple organisms naturally producing
ﬂuoro-containing metabolites have been characterized. Streptomyces cattleya has the unique
capacity to biosynthesize ﬂuoroacetate and 4-ﬂuorothreonine from inorganic ﬂuoride catalyzed
by the enzyme 5’-ﬂuoro-5’-deoxyadenosine synthase (EC 2.5.1.63) [55]. Dichapetalum cymo-
sum, a plant found in South Africa, was found to be able to naturally metabolize ﬂuoroacetate,
via ﬂuoroacetyl-CoA, to the toxin (2R,3R)-ﬂuorocitrate [56]. Identiﬁcation of these ﬂuorinase
enzymes has led to interesting prospect of utilizing cell factories for procuring ﬂuorinated com-
pounds in a more sustainable way.
In this project, we attempted to utilize P. putida as a means to bioﬂurinate compounds. In an
earlier performed study (conﬁdential), some enzymes have been exhibited capacity to catalyze
ﬂuoroacetate into ﬂuoroacetyl-CoA. Utilizing these enzymes, we will further attempt to produce
ﬂuorinated compounds with the objective of producing ﬂuorinated 2-pentanone.

1.5
Fermentation and downstream processing
The term ’fermentation’ stems from the latin verb fervere, to boil, and was ﬁrst used to describe
the anaerobic process of catalyzing sugars to carbon dioxide and ethanol. Since, then the use of
the term has been extended to generally describe any process for production of products by mi-
croorganisms [57]. Fermentation technology is deﬁned as the ﬁeld in which enzymes are utilized
for production of compounds which are industrially applicable [58]. Fermentation of commer-
cial relevance can generally be categorized into ﬁve diﬀerent groups where the desired target
products are: (1) cells or biomass as product, (2) enzymes, (3) metabolites, (4) recombinant
products (e.g. antibodies), (5) modiﬁed compounds [57]. This central focus of this section will
be dedicated to metabolites as the goal of this project is the production of 2-pentanone, which
can be considered a secondary metabolite. The phase of production of secondary metabolites
(idiophase) generally begins at the end of the exponential phase when cell growth is limited due
to exhaustion of the primary source of essential nutrients (carbon, nitrogen or phosphate). It
can be considered a property of slow-growing and non-growing cells [57]. Secondary metabolites
are products of cells reaching a maintenance state and are suspected to either have functional
beneﬁts for survival in a natural environment or merely be a by-product of other necessary
processes [59].
Cells in stationary phase are drastically diﬀerent than growing cells, both physically and
metabolically. Stationary phase promoters drive metabolic changes in the cells towards a more
viable state in nutritionally limited and/or stressful environments [60]. Genes linked to e.g.
DNA repair, glycogen production, thermotolerance and osmotolerance have been shown to be
expressed at higher levels in stationary phase E. coli [61]. One of the central regulators behind
stationary phase metabolism in E. coli has been identiﬁed as RpoS. The rpoS gene encodes a
sigma factor involved in RNA regulation for proteins responsible for the transition from rod
shaped cells to coccus shape and many stress response proteins [60]. A homologous rpoS gene
is also found in Pseudomonas ssp. including P. putida, P. aeruginosa, and P. ﬂuorescens and
is shown to be involved in stress adaptation, regulation of virulence factors and secondary
metabolites [62, 63]. Knowledge of the metabolic ﬂux in stationary phase can be used improve
production by eliminating unnecessary by-product formation and thereby improve utilization
of raw material [4].
Industrial fermentation is performed in bioreactors and can be classiﬁed according to various
characteristics of the fermentation process such as: feeding system (batch, fed-batch or contin-
uous), immobilization of cells (free or immobilized), state of matter in system (submerged or
solid state), single/mixed cultures, mixing system (e.g. mechanical, pneumatic), and oxygen
14
==PAGE:24==
availability (aerobic, micro-aerobic, and anaerobic processes) [64]. For any fermentation process
the advantages and disadvantages for each system should be considered to ensure an eﬃcient
operation for an optimal return of investments, however, this will not be further elucidated.
Downstream processing
Downstream processing (DPS), the chain of operations involved in separation and puriﬁcation,
has become the bottleneck of bioprocessing in terms of costs [65, 66]. The contribution of
DSP to manufacturing costs can in unique cases be higher than 80% [65]. However, the lofty
DSP costs are mainly in the biopharmaceutical ﬁeld, as additional puriﬁcation steps might
be necessary to ensure purity and safe use. DSP in general consists of following steps: Cell
disruption, removal of insoluble compounds, isolation, puriﬁcation, and a ﬁnishing step (e.g.
crystallization, drying, sterilization). For extracellular products, the cell disruption step is dis-
regarded [67].
Cell disruption
Biosynthetic products are produced using various living cells such as plants, bacteria and an-
imal cells. The products are either secreted into the media or retained inside the cells. Cell
disruption is a process in which the cell membrane is disrupted to release intracellular compo-
nents. This is usually done with through mechanical measures: bead mills or homogenizers, or
non-mechanical: solvents, ultrasonication or lysosomes [68, 69].
Removal of insoluble compounds
Removal of insolubles is the process in which cell debris or whole cells is separated for in-
tracellular and extracellular products, respectively.
The most commonly used methods are
centrifugation, sedimentation or ﬁltration, which will leave behind the crude fermentation me-
dia [67].
Isolation of product
The next step of the process comprises of isolation the product. The isolation normally based on
physical properties of components in the media. Common procedures include chromatography
and ﬁltration [70]. For proteins, the product can be further puriﬁed through ultraﬁltration by
plastic-based polymers followed by high resolution chromatography high degrees of purity [67].
Selection of cultivation method and growth conditions can aﬀect the required downstream pro-
cessing as formation of complex streams can occur depending on the conditions of the upstream
processing. For instance, selecting a sub-optimal production strain allows for a development in
terms of production titers and yields, but might result in production of co-products which are
harder to separate downstream [71]. The selection of fermentation media also inﬂuences the
15
==PAGE:25==
requirements of downstream processing and puriﬁcation of the product. Complex medias can
signiﬁcantly increase the cost, challenges and requirements of DSP by introducing production
of undesirable by-products. Simpliﬁed and deﬁned medias are therefore often desired to reduce
the required downstream processing [72].

Shake ﬂask fermentation

Elx808 absorbance readers.

Results & Discussion
3.1
Determining the tolerance limit towards 2-pentanone
The tolerance limit of the constructed strains towards 2-pentanone was determined to ensure
viability as a potential production chassis, and as a foundation for selecting the best candidate
to utilize as production host. The strains, P. putida EM42, P. putida DOT-T1E, and E. coli
BW25113 all showed suﬃcient tolerance towards 2-pentanone from a production perspective
and was growing at the concentration between 2.5-25 g/L as shown in Figure 8, 9 and 10.
At concentrations above 2.5 g/L, the growth of all strains started to be aﬀected in terms of
increased lag phase as seen in Figure 11. Concentrations above 20 g/L was shown to severely
aﬀect the lag phase of both P. putida strains. The lag phase of E. coli BW25113 was also
prolonged, but surprisingly not as gravely as the P. putida strains. The lag phase of EM42
almost doubled comparing the lag phase at 20 g/L with 25 g/L, while P. putida DOT-T1E
had a lag phase more than 5 times longer under these conditions (Figure 11). This was highly
surprising as P. putida DOT-T1E earlier has been characterized to be highly tolerant towards
solvents such as toluene, benzene, styrene and xylenes due to the presence of the eﬄux pumps:
TtgABC, TtgDEF and TtgGHI [85, 86, 87]. It was furthermore observed that E. coli BW25113
reached a stationary phase after approximately 10 hours of growth, while both P. putida strains
seems to be continuously growing even after 70 hours.

3.2
Engineering of P. putida for 2-pentanone production
One of the primary tools utilized for DNA recombination is plasmid vectors. Plasmids are
extrachromosomal, self-replicating, double stranded DNA molecules, which occur naturally in
bacteria, yeast and some eukaryotic cells. In nature, plasmids often carry beneﬁcial genes such
as antibiotic resistance, which can be transferred between cells through conjugation. Plasmids
32
==PAGE:42==
Figure 14: Growth of P. putida EM42 over 2 weeks in M9 medium + 1 g/L of 2-pentanone.
can be designed and utilized to insert and express desired genes into cells through transformation
methods such as heat-shock or electroporation. In this study, plasmids encoding the pathway
for 2-pentanone was designed according to the SEVA standard by assembling plasmids with
only necessary components such as an origin of replication, selection marker, and the cargo
segment in which the biosynthetic pathway is placed under a chosen promotor.
3.2.1
Design, construction and assembly of pathway using the SEVA
framework
To enable production of 2-pentanone in a production host, a biosynthetic pathway utilizing
acetate or butyrate as a carbon source to produce 2-pentanone as the ﬁnal product was designed.
The pathway designed took inspiration from the Lan et al. (2013) in which they successfully
engineered an E. coli strain to produce 2-pentanone using glucose as substrate [41]. Contrary
to the pathway designed by Lan et al. (2013), the pathway in this project employed the innate
acs gene in P. putida encoding acetyl-CoA synthetase, which has been shown to catalyze the
formation of acetyl-CoA from acetate [89, 49]. This allows for a direct route for the formation of
acetyl-CoA, compared to glucose which has to be metabolized in the TCA cycle for acetyl-CoA
formation. The designed pathway is shown in Figure 15, and comprises of the codon optimized
synthetic genes, homologous to the genes used by Lan et al.
(2013) from the organisms:
Cupriavidus necator (phaAB), Helicobacter pylori (thl), Clostridium acetobutylicum (crt, fabV,
ctfAB, adc) and Escherichia coli (atoDA). The homologous genes thl/phaA and atoDA/ctfAB
for pathway steps 2/6, 3 and 7 were assembled on diﬀerent plasmids to have multiple options
33
==PAGE:43==
if the initial constructs did not yield results. The pathway was separated into two sections for
improved control and troubleshooting by utilizing the inducible expression systems XylS/Pm
and rhaSR/Prham for the ﬁrst part of the pathway, and a constitutive promotor, Pem7, for
the second part. The genes phaB, crt and fabV was placed under a Pem7 promotor on a
plasmid encoding for gentamycin resistance.
The ﬁrst part of the pathway was placed in
various plasmids with mixtures of XylS-Pm, Pem7, and rhaSR promotors. Additionally, the
plasmids were constructed with varying antibiotic resistance selection markers: streptomycin,
kanamycin and gentamycin. All the constructed plasmids have been shown in the Table 4 and 5
in the Materials & Methods (section 2.3.2).

3.3
Production of 2-pentanone
3.3.1
Fermentation - Phase I
The initial fermentations were performed in GC-MS ﬂasks in the mediums deﬁned as FM1
and FM3 (see Table 8). However, due to technical diﬃculties with the GC-MS columns, these
samples were not analyzed until months later. Furthermore, these did not yield any detectable
production of 2-pentanone, and thus will not be elucidated further upon in this project. As a
result of the complications concerning the GC-MS machine, alternative methods of detection
were explored. This resulted in extraction of methyl ketones utilizing n-hexane and detection
by GC-FID. The relative polarity of 2-pentanone in comparison to water is 0.321 [92] and was
thus assumed to be extractable to a non-polar solvent as n-hexane.
3.3.2
Optimizing n-hexane extraction protocol
Fermentations were performed to determine the viability of the extraction method by examining
fundamental factors such as the detection limit, extraction volumes and to perform an initial
screening of 2-pentanone production. From the three preliminary fermentations, the detection
limit was determined to be ≤1 mg/L of 2-pentanone using known concentrations of spiked
samples of P. putida EM42 culture. Based on a peak from the resulting chromatogram of
40
==PAGE:50==
the GC-FID from one of the production strains with a calculated amount of 0.02 mg/L was
detectable. This indicated that very low amounts of 2-pentanone were detectable using this
extraction and detection method. Furthermore, it was established that extraction with 1 mL of
n-hexane resulted increased extracted concentration of 2-pentanone by up to 213% as compared
to 5mL of n-hexane (Figure 24a). The standard calibration curve was made based on 5 mL
n-hexane extractions of EM42 samples spiked with 25 mg/L, which is why the 1 mL extraction
shows such a high concentration. Additionally, O/N extractions could increase the extraction
concentration by up to 157% (Figure 24b).

3.3.5
Fermentation - Phase II
Final fermentations were performed after assessing the growth and yield on diﬀerent media
compositions. The medium used consisted of 0.3% acetate, 0.4% butyrate to achieve a fast
growth on acetate in the initial growth phase, and a slower sustained growth on butyrate for

plasmid Strain 4 carrying the plasmid: pS43Prham::thl-atoDA-adc (Figure 33). No products
were detected in Strain 1 which was expected as it only carries the ﬁrst part of the pathway,
which was established earlier to supply additional butyryl-CoA.
The proportional increase of 2-pentanone for most strains was signiﬁcantly higher in the last
fermentation as compared to the initial. More than 10 times the amount was extracted from
Strain 4 and 10, as compared to the initial fermentations. However, in general, the extracted
concentrations were below the 2-pentanone concentrations extracted from the fermentations
performed in conical tubes while testing the inﬂuence of yeast extract. One crucial diﬀerence in
between the two fermentations was a late induction of Strain 10 in the yeast extract experiment,
as opposed to the ﬁnal fermentation, which was induced immediately. This could be the reason
for the increased yield in Strain 10 in the yeast extract experiment. Furthermore, the increased

head space in the shaking ﬂasks could aﬀect the extraction, as a lower gas pressure would result
in higher concentrations of volatile compounds such as 2-pentanone in the headspace.

was added to the calibration standard. Based on the integrated area of the calibration stan-

to mediate this, would be to utilize a medium with same molar concentrations of acetate and
butyrate, or utilize an acetyl-CoA acetyltransferase with higher speciﬁcity towards acetyl-CoA.
Furthermore, constructing strains with an acetyl-CoA- and butyryl-CoA synthetase with simi-
lar reaction rates to ensure a similar pool of acetyl-CoA and butyryl-CoA.

In the attempt to enable P. putida to bioﬂuorinate compounds, an acsA homolog from Bacillus
subtilis, was cloned into the plasmids: pS43Prham::thl-atoDA-adc and pS43Prham::thl-ctfAB-
adc resulting in pS43Prham::thl-atoDA-adc-bsAcs and pS43Prham::thl-ctfAB-adc-bsAcs, re-

These plasmids were transformed into P. putida EM42 and Strains 4 and 5 to
obtain the strains designated Strain 4A, 5A, 10A and 11A (see Table 7 in Materials & Methods
Section 2.3.2). The acsA gene has earlier been proven to be able to be able utilize ﬂuoroacetate
as a substrate by another member of the research group.
Based on the earlier fermentations utilizing acetate and butyrate the carbon sources, ﬂuorine
would only be able to enter the metabolic system either as ﬂuoroacetate or ﬂuorobutyrate. How-
ever, as ﬂuorobutyrate was unavailable at that point in time, ﬂuoroacetate was utilized as the
ﬂuorine source in combination with butyrate as a carbon source. The pathway that is proposed
for production of ﬂuoropentanone is shown in Figure 34. The B. subtilis acetyl-CoA synthetase
(bsAcs) synthesizes ﬂuoroacetyl-CoA and butyryl-CoA, followed by a Claisen condensation by

CoA to form 2-ﬂuoro-3-ketohexanoate, which can then be decarboxylated by the adc encoded
acetoacetate decarboxylase to produce 3-ﬂuoro-2-pentanone.

Conclusion
In this project, we engineered P. putida strains to produce 2-pentanone using acetate or bu-
tyrate as a carbon source. By optimizing our extraction protocol and fermentation conditions
we were able to establish 2-pentanone production in P. putida EM42 and improve the yield
from 1.03 mg/L to 9.94 mg/L. The highest amount of 2-pentanone was detected in the Strain
10, using 3 g/L of butyrate. The second highest measured concentration was obtained from
the Strain 4 in which we were able to detect 7.40 mg/L of 2-pentanone using 3 g/L of acetate
and 4 g/L of butyrate. Both of these strains harbor the pS43Prham::thl-atoDA-adc plasmid,
which in our study proved to be the most eﬀective expression system for the pathway of the
evaluated strains. The yields could potentially be signiﬁcantly improved by controlled fermen-
tation conditions in bioreactors by managing pH, temperature and oxidation.
Compared to the 2-pentanone synthesis performance in E. coli by Lan et al. (2013) in which
they utilized 50 g/L of glucose for production of 240 mg/L 2-pentanone, the (YPS) parameter
is determined to be 4.8 (mg/g) which is 44% higher than the 3.31 (mg/g) achieved in this
study. However, based on the number of carbon atom in glucose (n=6) and butyrate (n=4),
the carbon utilization eﬃciency for 2-pentanone is the same. Strain 4 could potentially produce
higher amounts of 2-pentanone using butyrate as the sole carbon source.
We also observed the consumption of ﬂuoroacetate by Strain 4A and 5A which were engineered
for the further study.
4.1
Future perspectives
Further work on the project would be focused increasing the yields of 2-pentanone by strain
engineering (potential knock-outs to remove competing pathways) in order to increase the ﬂux
through the established pathway. Furthermore, we could determine the limiting steps of 2-
pentanone synthesis and clone homologous genes from other organisms in order to increase
productivity. Alternative enzymes with higher speciﬁcity towards condensation of acetyl-CoA
and butyryl-CoA could be utilized in order to reduce the formation of the side products. Ad-
ditionally, overexpression of the native CoA transferase PcaIJ from P. putida, might increase
yields as this was determined to be in limiting step by Lan et al.(2013).
Given the ﬂuctuations we observed in 2-pentanone yields, we could upscale fermentations to
smallscale bioreactors for more controlled fermentations in order to precisely monitor growth
54
==PAGE:64==
and production to determine optimal conditions for production.
In the bioﬂuorination part, mass spectrometry could be employed in order to determine which
ﬂuorinated compounds the strains accumulate and how current strain design can be improved.
Screening of homologous enzymes could be performed to ﬁnd better candidates capable of uti-
lizing ﬂuorinated substrates. Protein engineering could be employed as a means of generating
mutants with potentially optimized enzyme properties to continue the process of bioﬂuorina-
tion.
