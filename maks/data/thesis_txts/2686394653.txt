Abstract
The rising environmental awareness regarding the CO2 emission and global warming is
continuously increasing the importance of seeking alternatives to fossil fuels. Renew-
able energy is an excellent alternative source of clean energy. The inherent variability of
renewable resources influences Hybrid Power Plant’s research advancement to ensure
renewable energy sustainability. The Hybrid Power Plant combines multiple renewable
technologies to avoid intermittent and attenuate their resources’ unpredictability with in-
tensifying their benefits. The storage system with a Hybrid Power Plant enhances further
efficiency and controllability.
The Hybrid Power Plant’s optimal operation performance is determined by optimizing its
design, size, and operation strategies, usually affected by the instruments and machines’
downtime due to failure occurrences. Therefore, it is essential to identify the reliability
impacts due to these failure occurrences over the optimization. This thesis study focuses
on a Hybrid Power Plant combining wind, solar, and battery storage to determine and
analyze the reliability’s impacts over its optimal sizing.
The size optimization model is a mathematical model that maximizes the Net Present
Value (NPV) of a Hybrid Power Plant to determine its technologies’ capacities by ensur-
ing the maximum revenue stream with a viable investment. The reliability of different
instruments, machines, and elements of the Hybrid Power Plant are estimated using the
probability distribution function of the failure rate and the repair rate. A random sample
generator is utilized to address failure and downtime events by sampling from defined
probability distribution functions to evaluate the reliability parameter, i.e., availability time
series. The availability time series is used with electricity price profiles, power time se-
ries, cost parameters, and rated power capacities as inputs to the size optimization model.
The impacts and shifts on the optimal sizing due to reliability are identified. Economic and
technical metrics are analyzed to determine the interactions and effects of the reliability
over them. A list of cost profiles is introduced to explore all output metrics’ shifts with vary-
ing cost profiles, which is essential to realize the synergies between reliability parameters
with other parameters. At the final step, the uncertainty analysis is performed to estimate
the uncertainty involves with the models.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Renewable energy sources are acknowledged as the most significant solution for the
global challenge of providing reliable, clean, and cost-effective energy [6]. Among all dif-
ferent renewable energy resources, wind and solar power are becoming economical due
to their significant development in recent years. The environmental awareness and the
reduced cost of renewable energy sources, i.e., wind and solar, lead to a global transition
from conventional energy systems to renewable energy systems. This transition is also
favored by the environmental policies and acts introduced to reduce the CO2 emissions.
Renewable energy sources have their challenges to manage with the utility-scale grid
system due to their variability caused because of the weather fluctuations like the wind
speed, temperate, solar irradiance, clouds, season [4]. The integration of the energy
storage system with wind and solar assures reduced power curtailment, energy shifting
to smoothen the generated energy variability, and additional controllability in dispatching
energy to the grid. Furthermore, the hybrid technology of combining multiple renewable
energy resources can offer better controllability and advantages over a single renewable
energy system. The opportunity to support wind and solar resources’ overplanting allows
the electrical infrastructure to be utilized more efficiently than a single renewable energy
source. A vital feature of HPP is that it connects to the grid through a point of common
connection (PCC) and operates as a single asset [14]. Its potential efficiency, reliabil-
ity, and controllability are comparable to that of a conventional plant and suitable for the
current extensive grid system [20].
Wind and solar resources’ complementary nature at many geographical locations leads
to the potential reduction of the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) through overplanting
to increase the profitability [14]. The investigations and advancements in HPP combining
wind and solar with a storage system are thrusting to improve renewable plants’ ability
to operate with large-scale grid systems. Therefore, grid utility-scale HPP plants have
become the most concerned research field these days in the energy sector. Different
approaches of sizing and operation optimization of HPP plants have been researched
and implemented focusing different objectives to obtain optimal performance [36], [21],
[37], [39], [40], [32], [8]. However, this optimal performance prediction may be affected
by unexpected failures and extended downtime periods due to those failures in reality.
Numerous researches are also accomplished to identify the reliability due to these fail-
ure occurrences in relation to the renewable energy-based plants and their decreased
performance due to this reliability measures [10], [30], [28], [24], [31], [1], [3], [18].
However, the optimization of HPPs considering the reliability measures is a new concept,
and relevant research is hard to find. The knowledge of impacts of the reliability over the
optimization reveals HPP’s performance shift due to any instrument and machine failure.
Optimizing the HPP design concerning reliability measures may improve it for a realistic
operation and better performance, which is the primary factor that motivates this project
undertaken in this study. This study is performed to identify the reliabilities impacts on
size optimization to address the scope of enhancing the utility-scale grid-connected HPP
plant’s operation.

1.2
Problem definition
The design of HPP plants mainly focuses on sizing and operational optimization. A lot of
research is accomplished on the size optimization of HPP. However, most all of these size
optimization methodologies concentrate on the ideal conditions without considering HPP
components’ failures. A component’s failure may interrupt the generation or the transmis-
sion of energy from the plant. Therefore, an HPP plant’s performance with optimal sizing
under ideal conditions deflects from the expected outcome. An appropriate HPP sizing
model should consider the components’ reliability in its optimization problem.
Secondly, the component’s failure reduces the overall generation capacity of the HPP
plant, which may cause a failure to meet the transmission capacity declared by the siz-
ing model. Therefore, the impacts of the components’ failure need to be addressed to
estimate the HPP plant’s appropriate size adequately.
Finally, the failure rate varies from component to component, changing the technologies’
generation cost separately. The optimal solution from the sizing model may shift from
its original value considering these component’s failure. An investigation is required to
evaluate these impacts over the optimal sizing of the HPP.
1.3
Assumptions
The major assumptions and limitations in this study are listed below.
• The HPP size optimization model is developed based on deterministic information.
• The sizing model input parameters and data are collected from the project [8], having
the root source of an HPP located in India. However, the acquired parameters and
data are assumed to be valid for the general case study.
• The market price, wind, and solar resources are used as one-year hourly time series
in the HPP sizing model and assumed valid for the project’s entire lifetime. There-
fore, the result is extrapolated for the lifetime to estimate the economic metrics.
• The HPP generators only charge the battery of the storage system, and the degra-
dation of the battery is disregarded.
• Overplanting is used to increase the overall capacity factor of the HPP plant.
• The sizing model is developed to use with a co-located HPP configuration only.
• The connection configuration and the dependencies of the HPP components are
assumed to be known.
• The power capacity or power rating of HPP components are known.
• The primary assumption concerning the developed reliability model is that the time-
between-failures and time-to-repair follow an exponential distribution, where the fail-
ure process and the repair process are the Homogeneous Poisson Process with a
constant parameter, i.e., the failure rate, λ and the downtime per failure rate.
• All the collected MTTF and MTTR from various researches are valid for the general
case study.
• During the integration of the reliability model and the sizing model, the maximum
number of HPP components is assumed to be known so that the corresponding
reliability parameters, i.e., availability time-series, can be evaluated separately.

2
State of art
2.1
Hybrid Power Plant
Hybrid power plants (HPP) are formed by combining different power generation tech-
nologies behind a single grid connection point and controlled together. The generation
technologies can either be renewable or conventional generation technologies as a part of
HPP. The essential feature of HPP is that all the generation technologies transmit power
through a Point of Common Coupling (PCC). HPP also can consist of a power storage
system with single or combined storage technologies. A grid utility-scale HPP should
have a size in the order of hundreds of megawatts of capacity [14]. Due to their com-
plementary nature, a popular combination of HPP is wind and solar technologies with a
Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) [14]. This study will concentrate on this combina-
tion of HPP having a shared substation and PCC connection-configuration with the grid.
Other possible configurations of HPP with the same variety are identified by the associ-
ation Wind Europe, which promotes wind technology in Europe [14]. In the concerning
configuration, both technologies utilize the same geographic place resources. However,
both technologies generate power independently [14].
2.1.1
Generation technologies
A standard generation technology has many significant attributes in supporting large-scale
grid systems like low-cost, stable, secure, and reliable operation capability over the long
and short term. Conventional fossil fuel-based technology excels at performing these at-
tributes with predictable and controllable energy supply [12]. However, the demand for
green energy over the years leads to significant advancements in various renewable en-
ergy technologies. The production cost for renewable technologies like wind and solar
reduced to an acceptable range compared to conventional technology. Resource and
generation predictability for these technologies are gaining better performance with im-
proved forecasting methodology. Integration of energy storage technology with them can
provide high-level controllability with valuable ancillary services. The HPP of combined
wind and solar technologies can improve resource utilization by operating at the same site.
In addition to that, the wind and solar profiles can emerge as complementary resources
for many prospective sites. Their resource statistics demonstrated that wind and solar
profiles’ shapes combine into more predictable energy for majority sites both in terms of
a daily and seasonal basis [14] [35].
2.1.2
Market opportunities
The day-ahead market and the balancing market dominates the revenue stream of the
renewable energy market. Besides, in the real-time market, revenue can be improved
by providing additional controllability to TSO and DSO through ancillary services. HPP is
potentially suitable for all types of energy markets, but considering the improved reliability
for green energy, the market opportunities of HPP at the renewable energy market rises
to a tremendous level. The spot price in a market fluctuates daily, and seasonal basis
due to power demand and purchase agreement such as standard price, feed-in-tariffs,
and incentives [35]. Coupling wind with solar energy in an HPP can provide energy with
less variability over time, and implementing an energy storage system may lead to extra
advantages to increase revenue and substantial control over the market.

2.1.3
Advantages
The significant advantages of wind and solar technology-based HPP are briefly listed
here. The advantages of integrated BESS are also listed.
Increased Annual Energy Production and capacity factor: The HPP plant allows the
over-planting possibility to utilize both wind and solar energy resources from the same
geographical location. It increases the installed capacity of the plant per unit area. Though
the grid connection capacity is not affected by this, the grid utilization will be higher than a
single technology-based plant. The ultimate result is the transmission of more energy to
the grid. Hence the total energy output is higher than the production of a single technology-
based plant. The integration of the energy storage system reduces the curtailment. It
stores the extra generated energy from the plant and discharges it back when generation
is lower than market demand. Therefore, the Annual Energy Production (AEP) increases
with the apparent Capacity Factor (CF) of the HPP plant.
Reduced variability: Unpredictability is one crucial concern to renewable energy such as
wind and solar. Solar energy directly depends on the solar irradiance, environment, and
weather conditions. Because of the sun’s angular position and distance, solar irradiance
at a site changes slowly. Aside from that, the weather and environmental factors are
entirely uncertain. The advanced weather forecasting technology cannot predict an exact
weather condition for a specific period. On the other side, wind energy directly depends on
wind speed, which is unpredictable due to the environment, weather, temperature, solar
radiation, geographical position, physical obstacles, and other things. HPP lessens the
uncertainties on supplying combined energy from wind and solar by utilizing the storage
systems. It ensures the low bidding risk at the day-ahead market and compensates for
the surplus forecasting errors at the balancing market.
Reduction in investment and operation cost: The operation of HPP is quite similar
to a conventional renewable energy plant. Sharing Balance of System (BOS) like hard-
ware, interconnection, engineering, labor, land resources, and others are standard HPP
criteria. It leads to a possible reduction in both Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) and Opera-
tion Expenditure (OPEX) [35]. This cost-sharing depends on the configuration of the HPP
and its Point of Common Coupling (PCC). Moreover, BESS’s optimized scheduling allows
short-term plant shutdown for maintenance without affecting the energy delivery [27].
Increased Net Present value: From the owner’s point of view, the most important ob-
jectives are to increase profitability [12]. By utilizing resources from the same geographic
location, HPP exploits resources effectively. Simultaneously by engaging shared BOS,
HPP controls assets more effectively. It reduces the cost of production and increases the
Net present value of the plant. The increased NPV value leads to higher profitability.
Prospective for investors: Improved capacity factors and efficient resource exploitation
provide more energy production at an economical cost with enhanced NPV. Green energy
awareness leads many beneficiary societies to grant subsidies like incentives, investment
tax credit, and other investment facilities. Following these advantages, HPP projects are
becoming more attractive to investors [17].
Operation flexibility and reliability: With reduced variability, HPP ensures sustainable
energy delivery from the plant with integration of the BESS system. The BESS system
provides several other benefits to the operation [8] [14] [13] [29].
• Energy arbitrage: With optimized scheduling, the BESS can store energy during
a lower spot price and discharge it back during a higher spot price to increase prof-
itability.

• System inertia and frequency control: System inertia and frequency are vital
parameters for electrical power systems’ stability. In the conventional grid, the sys-
tem inertia appears due to the continuous energy exchange from the massive rotat-
ing prime-mover of Synchronous Generators (SG). In a quick response to a large
load disturbance, this heavy inertia from the rotating mass of SG autonomously ex-
changes extra kinetic energy. This inertia response directly supports the primary
and secondary frequency control due to its natural characteristics. BESS integrated
HPP is suitable for high speed response to assist the frequency regulation and fast
in response to the power supply in regulating the load imbalance.
• Ramp control and transmission capacity: The TSO often assigns a limit over

energy fluctuations, low inertia, and inadequate frequency response. BESS storage
system can assure ramp rate by smoothing down energy fluctuations and enhance
the transmission quota by providing fast inertia and frequency control ancillary ser-
vices.
2.1.4
Challenges
HPP often operates through a complex shared BOS system, challenging to establish.
The coupling of multiple technologies with a single PCC to the grid is also challenging
to develop with stability and optimal operation. The lack of clear policies, well-defined
standards, and complex processes of granting permission for multiple-technologies are
significant drawbacks for building HPP plants in different lengths at different countries
[14]. The utility-scale HPP is in the early stage of development, and its technical and
commercial information is limited as there are not many publications are available. A vi-
tal challenge is to develop an optimal operational strategy for HPP, which requires lots
of analysis of physical configuration, site resources, and market structures. Due to the
lack of data on resource availability and the local market, it is challenging to develop
a suitable HPP operational strategy for new locations. Developing market engagement
strategies require continuous data analysis on previous market data, challenging to pre-
pare for new renewable markets. The market participation models of HPP are not well
developed. BESS’s combined constraints with technologies and their physical coupling
for energy dispatching strategies are complicated to formulate than standard conventional
dispatching technology [8] [17].
2.1.5
HPP Topology
Topology is an essential topic to understand the connection-configuration of a plant. There-
fore it is essential to define the plant’s topology before designing and developing any op-
timization methodology. In general, all the components of different technologies share
the same sub-station inside an HPP. There are three generalized topologies proposed in
[27] i.e., co-located HPP, WTG-coupled HPP, and DC-coupled HPP. This study focuses
on the co-located HPP topology. Wind, solar, and battery storage technology reside in a
co-located HPP and have individual Points of Connection (PoCs) to the plant. Still, the
final interconnection point PCC to the grid is common. The connections-configuration of
the selected component is vital to evaluate the reliability parameters and define the sizing
constraints.

energy over the electrical grid to keep the grid operation stable. However, in HPP sizing,

Figure 2.1: Bathtub curve [33]
In systems engineering, the term reliability states the quantitative measure of a system’s
ability to function adequately under designated conditions for a specified period. In gen-
eral, reliability measures the probability of a system’s successful operation without failure
occurrence for a specific period [1]. Reliability specialists often use the Bathtub curve to
represent the failures of a component visually. Figure 2.1 shows the bathtub curve, which
describes three different periods- infant mortality, normal life cycle, and end of life wear-
out. The bathtub curve represents the failure rate of the entire population of products over
a lifetime. Some of the products fail relatively early, and some others last until wear-out
[33]. The early failure of infant mortality mostly causes due to manufacturing defects and
always has an undesirable high value. This high value decreases exponentially. The end
side of the curve also gives a high value of failure rate, representing the failure occurs at
the end of the product’s lifetime due to fatigue or depletion of materials. The value rises
exponentially from the failure rate of the normal life. A bathtub curve’s crucial period is
the normal life period. It describes the product’s operation during regular services. During
that period, the failure rate is low and can be considered a constant [33].

There are plenty of research-articles available on sizing the storage system for the HPP.
When it comes to the optimal sizing of utility-scale HPP of wind, solar, and battery combi-

authors of the conference paper [39] presented an analytical sizing model for BESS in an

Numerous research-articles are available concerning the reliability of renewable energy

measure for different years of operation to illustrate how the component may perform af-

the reliability, availability, and maintainability analysis of several practical grid-connected
solar plants. It carried a novel approach to estimate the best probability density function
(PDF) for the failure rate of different sub-assemblies and concluded that the exponential
PDF is the best fit for many sub-assemblies like PV module, converter, charge controller.

[1] to estimate a large scale solar plant’s reliability considering various component failure.

authors of the article [18] introduced a logic model to illustrate a grid-connected solar

Most of the reviewed research-articles are investigating the reliability of wind technology
or solar technology. However, few of them are investigating the HPP, and fewer are
investigating the wind, solar, and storage technology-based HPP. Besides, most of these
investigations are concerning reliability analysis in terms of system uptimes, capacity,
generation, and economic yields. The investigation of the impact of reliability over optimal

Reliability model
The primary objective of the reliability model is to measure the reliability of a system. In
this study, the purpose of the model is to measure the reliability of the main components of
HPP separately, as these reliability parameters are subject to be integrated with the sizing
model. The reliability model assumes that failure and repair occurrences are distributed in
a well-defined space like the Homogeneous Poisson Process model. The model indicates
that the time-intervals between the failure events identically and independently distributed
according to an exponential distribution, distinguished with a constant λ. The distribution
of the failure and the repair events are represented as the Time to Failures (TTF) and
Time to Repair (TTR), respectively.
3.1
Parameters
Identifying suitable parameters to address the model’s functionality is essential in design-
ing a reliability model. As a measure of the component’s reliability, availability is ideal
for integration with the HPP sizing model. The sizing model optimizes the size of an
HPP by maximizing the plant’s NPV. It uses the time series record of a full year to solve
this maximization problem. Therefore, for integration, the availability parameters must be
evaluated separately for each component as a time series of that specific period. Equa-
tion 2.6 provides the theoretical formula for calculating availability in term of percentage.
However, to evaluate the availability as a time series, the model needs to identify all down-
time moments throughout the period. This way model can define whether a component
is available or unavailable to engage for power generation at a specific time-unit.
The failure occurrence of a component is entirely uncertain. From section 2.2 and Equa-
tion 2.2, the failure rate, λ and MTTF (Mean time to failure) can be considered as a con-
stant during the normal life operation. The reliability model uses the Homogeneous Pois-
son Process to identify the distribution of the failure events using the exponential prob-

matically inverse of MTTF. This probability distribution function (PDF) of TTF presents the
entire population’s failure occurrence. A set of failure occurrences is produced using a
random sample generator in the simulation’s virtual realization, which is then transformed
into the TTF time series. Similarly, the time series of TTR (time to repair) of a component

of the solar PV unit’s reliability illustrates the power reduced by its PV modules’ failure.

their series connection. This way reliability parameter of the inverter impacts the PV units’

reliability parameters requires a lot of computational energy and large variable-memory

PV modules, may require computational power and large memory allocation capacity, like
powerful servers. Therefore, for evaluating the reliability parameters, each PV unit is con-

execute in a standard computer.

the availability time series. It is done by stacking the uptime and downtime events from
the samples related to the PDF of the failure events and PDF of the repair events.
3.4
Reproducibility
There are distinct functions to compute different distribution functions, but all these mod-
ule functions depend on the pseudo-random number generator function. Python has a
built-in pseudo-random number generator function like all other programming languages
[15]. The reliability model uses it to evaluate the random but distinct samples of event
occurrences for all components. In each execution, it generates a random set of samples
in a random sequence. Consequently, the random sampling from the joint PDF is also
different at each simulation for a specific component. Therefore, the model can not repro-
duce the same result in every simulation, even running under identical methodology and
procedure. Two essential parameters, i.e., master seed number and sampling constant,
are introduced to avoid this irreproducible nature.
3.4.1
Master seed number
The pseudo-random number generator generates a random value programmatically based
on the previous value generated by itself. However, there is no previous value for the first
time, and the generator chooses it randomly. This first chosen value determines the gen-
erated random values and the sequence of their generation. Seeding the pseudo-random
16
Impact of reliability on optimal sizing of hybrid power plant
==PAGE:26==
number generator can provide a unique set of random values and identical sequence or-
der by setting its initial value [15]. Setting a seeding value before sampling from the joint
PDF (section 3.3) can support the reliability model achieve reproducibility. However, the
seed value needs to be different for each component type. By introducing a master seed
to the model, the model can decide distinct seed values for each component type. This
way, the model can ensure a distinct sampling with reproducibility while running with the
same master seed value.
3.4.2
Sampling constant
The sampling constant is another crucial parameter for generating sample records from
joint PDF (section 3.3). It is also vital to gain the reliability model’s reproducibility. The
number of the samples from the joint PDF needs to complete the desired length of the
avail. Therefore, a sampling constant is introduced to estimate the approximate number
of samples required. This constant is empirical to the model. It is tuned to ensure events
cover 20 years of hourly simulations. Again, the number of samples needs to be large
enough to ensure full converge of the time series. Therefore for a small sampling con-
stant, the availability time-series will represent a poorly-converged result. With a higher
sampling value, the sampling results will be well-converged at the cost of lots of additional
computational power to generate the sampling.

4.1
Research design
Depending on the perspective, an HPP sizing model’s primary objective may differ from
research to research. Therefore, establishing a sizing model is subject to identify the
primary objective and its functions.
• Identification of primary objective: The HPP sizing model’s general purpose is
to estimate the rated capacity of wind, solar, battery energy, and battery power. A
plant’s sizing model should ensure the highest profitability by maximizing the rev-
enue stream with a viable investment. This aspect is achievable through the max-
imization of Net present value (NPV). Therefore, the HPP sizing model’s primary
objective is to estimate the optimal size upon guaranteeing maximum NPV.
• Problem identification: The objective function NPV is identified as the linear math-
ematical problem, and the optimization tasks, limits, conditions, aspects, and factors
are formulated into constraints.
• Input parameters: The input parameters are identified by defining different param-
eters’ relations in the optimization problem. The model’s primary input parameters
are the market price, available wind and solar resources, and the reliability model’s
availability time-series. Other parameters are the cost parameters, the power rating
of each component.
• Metrics: The output metrics are identified along with economic and performance
metrics. The identified the metrics for the sizing model are presented in Table 4.1.

4.1.1
Technology characteristics
The following technology characteristics have been considered during the problem for-
mulation to identify the limits and constraint conditions for the sizing model
Lifetime
The reliability model deals with the component’s lifetime, where the sizing model deals
with the whole technology’s lifetime. The technology lifetime is the number of years for
its operation with standard performance under specific criteria. The expectation of the
lifetime of a recently constructed wind turbine is around 25 to 27 years, which is expected
to rise to 30 years by 2030 [34]. The expected lifetime for a utility-scale solar plant is
around 30 to 35 years, and it may rise to 40 by 2030 [34]. Lifetime a crucial parameter for
the sizing model for calculating the LCOE and lifetime energy yields.
Overplanting
The term overplanting refers to the over-dimension of an energy plant’s installed capacity
than its grid connection. In general, the capacity factor (CF) of the renewable energy
resources are relatively low. The CF of the wind technology is around 30-40%, while it is
20
Impact of reliability on optimal sizing of hybrid power plant
==PAGE:30==
around 20% for the solar technology [8]. Combining wind and solar will only reduce the
capacity factor from the best technology to somewhere in between. The apparent capacity
factor of an HPP plant is increased by overplanting capacity concerning the combined
technologies are controlled as a single asset at the grid PCC connection. Therefore, an
HPP of wind, solar, and battery storage requires a higher quantity of assets with a large
area to install a utility-scale plant. The anti-correlation profile of wind and solar resources
with many assets reduces the resources’ intermittency [11].
Storage system
The storage system is not a power generation unit, but an energy shifting component.
Preceded by hydro storage, Lithium-ion batteries are the dominant storage technology
in the renewable energy sector [9]. The development and advancement of this technol-
ogy reduce the cost of installing a utility-scale battery storage system. Besides that, they
satisfy a wide range of power and energy capacities. Lithium-ion batteries are mainly
electrochemical energy storage technology. The structure and operational principle, con-
trol strategies, industrial sizing, degradation over the lifetime, and dispatching strategies
altogether define the values of coupling a storage system with an HPP plant. Its primary
storage value is related to its cost and type of application. Therefore, the characteristics
that can affect the sizing of HPP are subject to formulate with the sizing problem.
• Power and energy rating: A battery storage system’s power rating indicates the
rated power that a storage system can charge or discharge spontaneously. It en-
sures the energy shifting rate of the storage system. The energy rating of a storage
system identifies its energy storage capacity. The energy rating is expressed in the
ampere-hour (Ah) unit, and the power rating is defined in the watt-hour (Wh) unit.
• Duration of discharge: The duration of discharge time is a crucial characteristic
for the storage system. Typical lithium-ion batteries can provide a duration ranges
from few seconds to 8 hours [9], which is suitable for integration with HPP for reg-
ular energy shifting. The energy to power ratio of a battery provides the maximum
time duration of the battery. It indicates the duration that the battery storage can
continuously dispatch the energy back [8].
• Depth of discharge: The depth of discharge (DoD) is the maximum percentage of
discharge released from a battery. It is the ratio of discharged energy to the capacity
of the battery energy. For lithium-ion batteries, the DoD is between 80-100
• Efficiency: The charging and discharging efficiency refers to the ratio of discharging
energy to the charging energy while its state of charge (SOC) comes back to the
same state after discharge. There is a small energy loss due to the dissipating
heat while charging and discharging in every cycle. It also affects the SOC and
instantaneous power flow capacity of the battery itself. Lithium-ion batteries have
high efficiency, like around 99%.
• Life cycles and lifetime: The life cycle of a battery system implies the maximum
number of full cycles of charging and discharging from it throughout its lifetime. An
equivalent full-cycle is a cycle defined by using a battery from its full-charged state
to the full-discharged state and then recharged back to its full-charged state. De-
pending on the type of electrochemical technology, a battery’s life cycles can vary
from 500 to 20000 cycles [19]. However, the lifetime of a battery is the maximum
period that it can operate with standard quality. The lifetime of a battery depends
on the degradation of its chemical substances over time. An expected lifetime for a
battery can be 5 to 25 years [19].

Electricity price
The market price profile is a primary input parameter for the sizing model. The day-ahead
market decides the primary share of the market price at any zonal market. It consists of
short term auctions at the electricity pool, performed in an electrical platform. This platform
allows the energy producers to bid on a specific quantity of electricity at their expected
price and the consumers to express the price they are willing to pay for the electricity
they needed. Using the market-clearing tool, the market operator clears the market by
scheduling the production and consumption level with their price. This scheduling en-
sures the optimum price for the market demand. However, it is possible to have different
purchase agreements, and incentive offers in the renewable energy sector to guarantee
the revenues for the long term. The balancing market, which compensates the forecasted
scheduling surplus to balance the market demand and generation, also affects the rev-
enue. The electricity price profile of the HPP sizing model represents the ultimate price
that they are getting for supplying the generated energy to the market.

The power time series represents the local resource availability for the sizing model, which

generated by the Conversion of Renewable Energy Sources (CoRES) tool. It derives the
weather time series using the meteorological inputs produced by dynamic downscaling.

measure. The wind power time-series conversion is performed based on the power curve

Annual energy production
Annual energy production (AEP) is a term mostly used for wind and solar plants. It mea-

power plant is always lower than the total capacity of the plant. The total capacity repre-

other hand, AEP represents the maximum expected electricity generation from the whole
plant considering resource availability.
Curtailed energy
The curtailment is the reduction or restriction of the energy delivery from a generation unit
to the grid. There may be many possible reasons for curtailment. However, the most crit-
ical reason for the sizing model is to avoid congestion in the transmission line. The sizing
model keeps it as low as possible without causing any congestion to the transmission line.

by overplanting the capacity under the constraint of transmission limit. Therefore, the

• Equation 4.16 sets a relational limit to the power dispatch from the plant. It can not

4.7
Algorithm for implementation
The HPP sizing model is implemented in python using the CPLEX solver. In a typical
linear optimization problem, the solver controls the decision variables and solves the op-
timization problem by manipulating their value. A decision variable is a variable that the
optimizer intends to obtain for the assigned job, i.e., maximization or, minimization of a
linear problem. A typical solver takes all the parameters and expends them into the de-
fined problem and its constraints. Then it starts to change the decision variables in the
given limit to resolve the optimization.
In this study, to analyze the reliability’s impact over optimal sizing, HPP components’
availability time-series are used as input parameters. However, to estimate the overall
28
Impact of reliability on optimal sizing of hybrid power plant
==PAGE:38==
availability, the quantity of HPP components should be known. The solver can not ma-
nipulate the control variables, i.e., the number of different HPP components, while using
the availability parameters. Therefore, an iterative algorithm is deployed with the CPLEX
solver to solve the optimization problem, presented in the Figure 4.2.
Figure 4.2: Iterative algorithm for implementation of sizing Model
At the first iteration, the model considers 100% availability for all the component-types.
The solver solves the maximization problem and estimates the sizing metrics, i.e., quantity
of each component-type for 100% availability. In the next iteration, the model computes
each component type’s availability for the last estimated quantity using reliability model
generated availability time-series. Then the solver solves the optimization problem again
and investigates whether the solver evaluates the same quantity. If the solver estimates
the same quantity, it indicates that the solver has used the right availability value and got
the optimal solution. Otherwise, the model needs to continue through more iterations until
it gets the optimal solution.
4.7.1
Model behavior
This iterative process may cause complications in solving the maximization problem due
to uncertainty in availability value. The sizing model calculates all the component-types’
availability based on the reliability model’s availability time-series. From section 3.5, this
availability fluctuates rapidly. The lower the component quantity higher the fluctuation.
Due to this fluctuation and uncertainty, the sizing model may fail to produce an optimal
solution directly. It may appear frequently in the simulation when the estimated compo-
nents’ quantity is low.
The primary aim of the iterative algorithm is to converge the solver towards the optimal
solution. In every iteration, the solver estimates new sizing metrics, i.e., components’
Impact of reliability on optimal sizing of hybrid power plant
29
==PAGE:39==
quantity, which leads to a correction in the availability parameters. Based on this correc-
tion, the solver tries to shift the results towards the optimal solution. Sometimes, due to
the uncertainty and rapid fluctuation of the availability value, the sizing model may not
converge the solver as expected. In that situation, the model may start jumping around
the optimal solution like an infinite loop. In some rare cases, the model may diverge out
from the solution area. Therefore, a sub-algorithm is developed to identify and resolve
these complexities at the end of every iteration.
4.7.2
Handling model behavior
A sub-algorithm is developed at the end of every iteration to identify and handle model be-
haviors. It investigates the newly estimated result, compares it with the last iteration, and
identifies if the solver is not converging by calculating the components’ quantity changes.
If the solver diverges from the solution area or is jumping around the optimal solution, the
model can not directly get the solution. In that situation, the model needs to check all the
generated results in different iterations and estimate the optimal solution by comparing
the economic metrics such as highest NPV with lowest components’ quantity changes.

The observed optimal sizing and other metrics from the base study case are analyzed

listed below.
• Reduced energy generation: The boundary of all members of a component types’
availability is 0 to 1, and usually, it remains below 1. Its application in the sizing
model reduces the technologies’ generation by acting as a factor with the overall
generation capacity without impacting the production cost (Equation 4.5). Therefore,
the cost per unit generation increases and the NPV decreases with it.
• Cheaper components’ quantity: As the NPV decreases, the model tries to re-
cover the value of NPV by decreasing costly components and adding cheaper com-
ponents to the optimal sizing depending on the cost differences between them. As
the generation decreases, the model increases the generation capacity back to the
limit defined in Equation 4.14, and Equation 4.15 by adding more generation units.
The model adds more quantity of the cheaper components than costly components
considering the production cost per unit and investment cost. From Table 4.3, solar
technology is cheaper in investment, operation, and maintenance than wind tech-
nology. Therefore, a significant increase in solar technology’s capacity is observed
as an impact of the availability parameter.
• Costly components’ quantity: The expensive component’s quantity may increase
or decrease moderately based on the technologies’ cost differences. The same rea-
sons for cheaper components influence this moderate shift. The model first removes
the costly components to recover the NPV value; then, it again adds few expensive
components to improve the NPV value further. Therefore, the overall change to the
expensive component’s quantity is less than the cheaper component type.
• AEP and curtailed energy: The model increases the quantity of the cheaper com-
ponent, i.e., solar PV unit. It cannot produce energy continuously throughout a
whole day. It also decreases the wind turbines from the optimal solution, which
can generate power with less intermittency. Therefore, the Annual energy produc-
tion (AEP) is reduced due to the impact of the availability. However, there are no
significant changes in the curtailed energy.
• Battery energy and power rating: The decreased quantity of wind turbines and the
increased quantity of solar PV units influence the change in AEP. Wind turbines can
generate energy with less intermittency than solar PV units. Therefore, a noticeable
reduction in the battery system’s energy rating and power rating is observed. Fur-
thermore, The battery’s availability will disconnect the HPP battery system during
a failure, reducing the battery energy and power rating. If any failure event occurs,
the battery system will not be able to store energy.
• NPV: It is explained that the NPV decreases due to the increase of the generation
cost per unit. Then the model adds additional generation units and changes the ratio
between the quantity of different component-type considering their cost to improve
the NPV value again. However, the decrease of the NPV due to reliability is more
than the increase of the NPV by adding additional generation units. Therefore the

6
Uncertainty analysis
The uncertainty analysis assesses the uncertainty in a statistic model’s result by quan-
tifying the output variability due to the input’s uncertain variability. The elemental quan-
tification for the uncertainty analysis are mean, median, and population quantiles, relying
on uncertainty propagation techniques. The steps to the uncertainty propagation are as
follows [16].
• Identification of the model’s input parameters subject to uncertainty
• Exploration of the knowledge regarding the variable using the probability density
functions (PDF)
• Generation of samples from the defined PDF functions
• Execution of the model using the generated sampled value sets as input to evaluate
the result sets
• Application statistical methods to compute the quantification of interest
The reliability model is developed based on the pseudo-random number generator to
generate the random failure event samples. The model generates the random samples
from the joint PDF (section 3.3). A master seed is deployed to achieve reproducibility
in sampling from the joint PDF, which defines the required seed number for all different
components. Changing the master seed number produces an entirely different realization
of samples set from the same joint PDF. Therefore, an uncertainty analysis is essential
to assess the results’ robustness standing on the assumptions involving the uncertain
reliability due to the failure of various components.

Summary
The study leads to a more realistic analysis than 100% reliability. However, significant
uncertainty is involved with the assessment. This chapter presents the uncertainty con-
cerning the results for different cost profiles. It explains how the same optimal sizing may
shift from one solution to another in various failure realization by changing the master
seed in the reliability model. It presents the estimation of the possible value ranges of
different metrics by defining the uncertainty in terms of relative standard deviation and
standard deviation.

7
Conclusion
The common PCC point of the HPP plant results in a favorable condition for overplanting
the capacity to exploit the resources more efficiently from the same geographical location.
The battery storage system increases this capacity in optimal sizing by reducing power
curtailment and energy shifting. The reliability parameter reduces the overall capacity of
the HPP plant.
The power dispatch from an optimal HPP depends on the technologies’ size, battery siz-
ing, and grid constraints for all different scenarios. The cheaper technology is always ac-
quiring more capacity in optimal sizing. Moreover, the components’ reliability increases
this priority by increasing the cheaper technology’s capacity for high-cost profiles. The
primary key to make the decision is generation cost per unit production for all different
technologies. The reliability parameter affects this per unit cost production, which leads
to the observed impacts on the sizing metrics. Uncertainty is another crucial factor to any
reliability model. A sizing model’s result needs to be assessed for robustness through un-
certainty analysis before it comes to a reliable solution when it involves the components’
reliability.
The interesting fact is that the reliability’s impact is insignificant on the internal rate of
return (IRR), which is an essential parameter for deciding whether a business project is
profitable. A business project is profitable when the NPV value is observed positive, which
is only achievable by setting a discount rate less than the IRR value. An HPP project is
promising for future investment when a modest discount rate is set with a green energy
incentive.
The developed models serve for an initial assessment to characterize whether an HPP
plant is a viable project concerning reliability impacts. They can be applied to other study
cases involving different parameters in a general perspective. However, this study is
performed under consideration of a typical physical connection configuration of various
components. The function to estimate the reliability parameters might need to modify
concerning project-specific components’ connection configuration.
Future work
In this study, the uncertainty analysis is performed on a small quantity of the result sets
that a regular computer simulates. It can provide insight regarding the uncertainty involved
with the sizing consequence, but the robustness assessment requires to execute the sim-
ulation of the models numerous times, i.e., a few hundred times to millions of times. It may
require a tremendous amount of computational power with a large size variable alloca-
tion capacity. It would be great to estimate the models’ robustness to evaluate a precise
optimal size considering the sizing model’s reliability.
In the [8], the author presented a sensitivity analysis regarding the sizing model’s input
parameters and showed that the optimal size from the sizing model is most affected by
the discount factor. The next step in the sensitivity analysis field should be estimating
how optimal size of the HPP is affected by the change in the mean-time to failure (MTTF)
and mean-time to repair (MTTR) input parameters. It also would be fascinating to have a
complete sensitivity analysis on both models combinedly.
The selection of the approximate MTTF and MTTR is performed based on the different

studies, and research works done in the field of reliability estimation on wind, solar or hy-
brid plants. However, these parameters’ estimated value is different from site to site, con-
sidering the components’ model, size, manufacturer, site weather, environment, and other
uncertain conditions. For a specific HPP project, numerous assessments need to perform
on the selected site’s components. It would be great to develop a proper procedure to
conduct these assessments to estimate the appropriate value for these parameters.
This study is performed concerning the topology of co-located HPP plant. It would be
fascinating to develop the models considering the other topologies, i.e., wind turbine gen-
erator coupled HPP, DC-coupled HPP. It will help to study how components’ different
configuration and their reliability combinedly impact the optimal sizing.
In this study, a loop-based algorithm is developed to implement the models to simulate
them on a regular computer with average computational power. The used computer spec-

an appropriate solution when the direct result is not achievable. An exciting future ex-
tension to this project can be developing a new algorithm to avoid the looping technique.
However, building a complete solver-based algorithm may require large data allocation
with substantial computational power concerning the availability time-series of numerous
components that may need to expand inside the model to solve it.
