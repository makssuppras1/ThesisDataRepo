Abstract
This study explores the integration of Bitcoin mining as a form of Power-to-X (PtX) within renewable energy
systems (RES) to mitigate electricity curtailment. The proposed hybrid blockchain energy system (HBES)
model incorporates Bitcoin mining in both Day-Ahead (DA) and reserve markets, with the reserve market
modeled stochastically.
A case study of the 300 MW Kassø Solar Park in DK1, paired with a 11.5 MW Bitcoin mining operation
participating in DA, FCR, aFRR, and mFRR, is analyzed from May 2023 to September 2024. The results
show a significant increase in profits, from 24.29 million euros to 30.69 million euros, while curtailment is
reduced from 85.9 GWh to 78.8 GWh.
This study demonstrates that Bitcoin mining can enhance profitability, decrease curtailment, and support
grid stability through reserve markets, offering a promising pathway for the adoption of Bitcoin mining and
the deployment of sustainable energy systems.

Introduction
The European Union aims to achieve carbon neu-
trality by 2050, with an interim target of reducing
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by at least 55%
by 2030 compared to 1990 levels [1]. Denmark has
set even more ambitious goals to reduce GHG emis-
sions by 70% by 2030 and achieve carbon neutrality
by 2050 under its Climate Act [2]. Renewable en-
ergy systems (RES) such as photovoltaic (PV) and
wind power are critical for achieving these targets [3].
Renewable energy is typically prioritized in electricity
grid scheduling and delivery to the grid due to its zero
marginal cost characteristics. As grid capacity be-
comes increasingly dominated by RES, curtailments
of unused power will become more common [4].
Power-to-X (PtX) technologies play a crucial role in
mitigating curtailments of renewable energy by con-
verting excess electricity into storable fuels or chem-
icals, thereby enhancing grid flexibility and efficiency
[5]. Moreover, large-scale electrolysis facilitates the
de-carbonization of hard-to-abate sectors such as
transport and agriculture by providing sustainable al-
ternatives to fossil-based fuels [6]. Despite signifi-
cant advancements, green hydrogen and its deriva-
tives remain substantially more expensive than con-
ventional fossil-based alternatives, primarily due to
high production, storage, and infrastructure costs [7].
Similarly, energy storage systems require substan-
tial upfront investment, often resulting in negative net
present value calculations without state sponsored
subsidies [8]. Consequently, both technologies cur-
rently face economic barriers that hinder their large-
scale deployment as viable solutions to integrate and
make RES more flexible.
Data centers engaged in cryptocurrency mining are
increasingly recognized as an emerging source of
flexibility in modern power systems [9]. The Bitcoin
mining power consumption has more than doubled
between 2023 and 2024, and currently, its annual
worldwide electricity consumption stands at around
180 TWh [10]. Due to their significant demand flex-
ibility, these systems can enhance grid stability by
enabling more effective load balancing through ca-
pacity optimization and load shifting, while also uti-
lizing otherwise curtailed electricity from RES [11].
This investigation focuses on integrating Bitcoin min-
ing in renewable energy systems, specifically it fo-
cuses on optimizing profits by analyzing the interplay
between the RES, the Bitcoin miners, electricity mar-
kets, and the electrical grid. Mathematical model-
ing is employed to optimize decision making, factor-
ing in the day-ahead (DA) market, the Bitcoin mar-
ket, and the frequency reserve markets. The anal-
ysis includes energy production from RES, energy
consumption by the Bitcoin miner, power exchanges
with the grid, and participation in the reserve capacity
market.
The simulation uses real-world data to assess the
feasibility of implementing a Bitcoin mining system
in various use cases. The model calculates the rev-
enues and costs associated with each market, and
by factoring in the initial investment, it provides an es-
timate of the potential profits the system could have
generated if it had been integrated in the past.
The structure of this paper is as follows: Section 2
presents the foundational knowledge underpinning
this study, addressing the challenges of RES curtail-
ment and exploring the limited but emerging body of
research on Bitcoin mining as a flexible load. Fur-
thermore, it provides a comprehensive overview of
the technical and economic aspects of Bitcoin min-
ing and its interactions with electricity markets.
Section 3 develops a general optimization model
for integrating Bitcoin mining with RES into hybrid
blockchain energy systems (HBES). The model is
designed to maximize profitability by strategically
participating in electricity, Bitcoin and reserve mar-
kets, leveraging the flexibility of the HBES.
Following this, Section 4 introduces the solar park
under consideration and details the mining compo-
nents used in a real-world use case analysis. Sec-
tion 5 presents the operational and economic results
of the analysis, accompanied by a sensitivity analy-
sis of key parameters.
Lastly, Section 6 provides a discussion of the case,
while Section 7 concludes the remarks of the study.
Appendix A contains a complementary analysis
about the data, methods and additional results.

Background
The intermittent and variable nature of RES presents
challenges in terms of flexibility and supply-demand
balance. One of the main issues is the management
of curtailments, which refers to the forced reduction
of renewable electricity production when supply ex-
ceeds demand [4]. In the existing literature, these
concepts have been considered with varying degree.
As the penetration of wind and solar energy gener-
ation increases, curtailment levels on electric grids

have also risen in recent years. A study from 2016,
[12], found that curtailment may become more preva-
lent as renewable energy sources grow in share.
The paper highlights that in some highly developed
electricity markets, curtailment levels have exceeded
10% of renewable generation in certain years. How-
ever, investments in transmission capacity and grid
infrastructure have substantially helped lower cur-
tailment levels. Notably, solar generation was not
curtailed in any of the regions examined in this re-
view, although this has become an issue nowadays
as higher solar penetration was reached [13].
Study [14] addresses the challenges posed by the in-
creasing penetration of renewable energy sources,
which can introduce variability and uncertainty, po-
tentially affecting the reliability of power systems due
to low supply while simultaneously leading to curtail-
ments during periods of high supply. To mitigate this,
the authors propose a robust optimization approach
to ensure sufficient flexibility in power systems un-
der high levels of renewable energy integration. The
method involves energy storage systems (ESS) to
prevent such deficits.
The study compares deter-
ministic and robust optimization approaches, with the
proposed method demonstrated on the power sys-
tem in the Republic of Korea. The study shows en-
hances in flexibility and promotes renewable energy
consumption by leveraging thermal inertia of build-
ings and ESS equipment.
A 2017 study, [15], investigated the use of ESS to
mitigate renewable energy curtailment in northern
Vermont, where transmission constraints frequently
limit wind and solar output. By storing excess re-
newable energy during curtailment and deploying it
when constraints ease, ESS can increase renewable
energy utilization. However, the study found that the
financial payback is not viable under current condi-
tions, with an estimated payback period of approx-
imately 400 years, due to Vermont’s unique power
system characteristics and relatively limited cost sav-
ings compared to other regions.
A more recent study, [16], proposes a scheduling
model for a fleet of truck-mounted Mobile Battery
Energy Storage Systems (MBES) in a distribution
network with wind and PV resources, where re-
newable energy curtailment occurs due to techni-
cal constraints.
The model minimizes curtailment
by optimizing the spatio-temporal deployment of
MBES units to absorb and release excess energy as
needed. Numerical tests demonstrate the model’s
effectiveness, achieving a 63.79% recovery of cur-
tailed renewable energy and reducing total daily op-
erational costs by 12.77% with just two MBES units.
However, no information was provided regarding the
investment costs of the solution.
Another approach to reducing the curtailing of RES,
is by adding flexible loads, such as electric vehi-
cle charging stations, while it could help, these re-
quire expensive infrastructure, making them finan-
cially less attractive. In study [17], Bitcoin mining is
proposed as a flexible load that can make effective
use of surplus renewable energy and also prevent
high excess power penetration into the grid. Simu-
lations conducted on a 33-node distribution system
under scenarios where the grid is congested due to
overgeneration from RES show that Bitcoin mining
can completely alleviate feed-in grid congestion, of-
fering a viable alternative to curtailment and serving
as a flexible load.
A master thesis from the University of Uppsala in
Sweden [18] demonstrated the feasibility of Bitcoin
mining powered by RES. This study evaluated the
potential of establishing a Bitcoin mining farm in-
tegrating photovoltaic systems, wind turbines, and
storage mediums to maximize the use of clean en-
ergy.
The economic analysis suggested that the
project can be profitable based on the installed ca-
pacities and the mined Bitcoin. However, the suc-
cess of such a investment heavily relies on the in-
clusion of a storage medium and a supplementary
fossil-fuel based power production source.
A 2024 study, [19], assessed the impact of utilizing
renewable energy from a PV system for Bitcoin min-
ing, simulating a 50.9 MW solar power plant and a
corresponding Bitcoin mining operation of 9.3 MW in
the United Arab Emirates. The system was designed
ensuring continuous electricity supply through en-
ergy swapping with the grid instead of using a sepa-
rate ESS. The results showed that an investment of
$42 million at the start of 2020 could recover costs
in about 3.5 years, whereas selling electricity to the
grid would extend the return on investment period to
8.1 years.
Lastly, study [20] examines the impact of large Bit-
coin mining loads on carbon footprint, grid reliability,
and electricity market prices in the Texas grid. The
study finds that the carbon footprint per megawatt-
hour of Bitcoin mining can vary by up to 50% across
different locations compared to the system average.
It also shows that the flexibility of mining loads can

2.1
Bitcoin mining and energy usage
Bitcoin is a digital currency, introduced in 2009 by
an individual or group under the pseudonym Satoshi
Nakamoto.
It operates on the Bitcoin Network,
a peer-to-peer decentralized system that enables
transactions without the need for a central author-
ity.
The network relies on blockchain technology,
a distributed and immutable ledger that records all
transactions. Transactions are verified by network
nodes using cryptography, ensuring security and re-
sistance to manipulation or counterfeiting. Bitcoin’s
open-source and permission-less design allows any-
one to participate in the network, making it a revolu-
tionary form of decentralized currency [21].
Bitcoin units are generated through a process known
as ”mining”, wherein computational resources are
employed to solve cryptographic problems referenc-
ing a collection of recent Bitcoin transactions. Any
participant in the Bitcoin Network can act as a miner,
utilizing their computing systems to contribute to
transaction verification and network security. Every
10 minutes on average, one miner is able to validate
and secure previous transactions in a block, receiv-
ing newly created Bitcoin and transaction fees as re-
wards [22].
The Bitcoin protocol incorporates algorithms de-
signed to regulate the mining process across the net-
work. The difficulty of the computational tasks re-
quired for mining adjusts dynamically, ensuring that
a block is mined approximately every 10 minutes, re-
gardless of the number of miners or the total compu-
tational power in the network. Additionally, the proto-
col includes a mechanism to periodically reduce the
issuance rate of new Bitcoin units, capping the total
supply at just under 21 million coins [22].
Miners aggregate and broadcast new transactions
into blocks, which are subsequently verified by the
network. For a block to be accepted, it must include
a valid proof of work (PoW), a computational chal-
lenge that involves finding a hash value numerically
smaller than the network’s current difficulty target.
This PoW is simple to verify but hard to generate,
requiring many hashes (attempts). PoW forms the
basis of Bitcoin’s consensus mechanism [22].
The probability of mining a block is proportional to the
computational work performed by the miner in veri-
fying hashes. The total number of computers com-
peting to add transactions into blocks and claim re-
wards can be estimated from the difficulty target of
the Bitcoin blockchain, measured in hashes per sec-
ond. The current hashrate is at 800 EH/s (8 × 1020
H/s), as can be seen in Figure 1.

2.2
Electricity markets and frequency
reserves
In Denmark, the wholesale electricity market is orga-
nized into several sub-markets based on the time be-
tween trade and the delivery of electricity. The day-
ahead (DA) market serves as the primary scheduling
mechanism for electricity generation and consump-
tion. To accommodate deviations from the initial DA
schedule, intra-day and balancing markets provide a
secondary, real-time adjustment mechanism [23]. In
this work, only the DA market is considered.
As the transmission system operator (TSO), En-
erginet is responsible for ensuring the reliability and
stability of Denmark’s electricity grid. Energinet em-
ploys various ancillary services, including frequency

reserves, to maintain grid frequency within permis-
sible limits.
Frequency reserves are critical for
handling sudden imbalances, such as unanticipated
changes in renewable energy production or unex-
pected demand surges, and are procured through
dedicated capacity reserve markets, which operate
independently of the DA market trading mechanism
[24].
Denmark’s electricity system is divided into two price
zones: DK1 (western Denmark) and DK2 (eastern
Denmark), each with distinct ancillary market dynam-
ics influenced by differences in grid infrastructure
and synchronicity with neighboring systems.
This
study focuses on DK1, which utilizes three types
of frequency reserves: Frequency Containment Re-
serves (FCR), automatic Frequency Restoration Re-
serves (aFRR), and manual Frequency Restoration
Reserves (mFRR) [25].
2.2.1
Day-ahead market
The DA market is the primary platform for electric-
ity trading in Denmark, where producers and con-
sumers commit to their production and consumption
schedules one day before the delivery. The market
operates on a uniform price scheme and closes at
12:00, at which point all hours of the following day
are cleared simultaneously. Any deviations between
the cleared DA schedules and real-time operations
are resolved through intra-day or balancing markets
[24]. However, since this study adopts a determinis-
tic framework for the DA market, real-time deviations
and their associated markets are not considered.
2.2.2
Frequency Containment Reserve
The FCR is the fastest-responding reserve designed
to counteract grid frequency deviations of up to ±200
mHz. This symmetric product ensures that reserved
capacity can be activated either upward or down-
ward, depending on the grid’s needs. Activation is
automatic and proportional to the frequency devia-
tion, achieving full response within 30 seconds, with
at least 50% response in the first 15 seconds. FCR
is procured in 4-hour blocks under a uniform price
scheme, with a minimum bid size of 1 MW and no
upper limit, requiring integer resolution. This reserve
is available exclusively in DK1 [26].
2.2.3
Automatic Frequency Restoration
Reserve
The aFRR is activated following significant events
that exceed FCR’s corrective capacity, providing ad-
ditional stabilization within a full activation time of
5 minutes. Activation is triggered by direct signals
from the TSO, and the reserve operates asymmetri-
cally, with separate auctions for upward and down-
ward regulation. As of 2024, aFRR procurement in
DK1 is integrated into the PICASSO platform, with
a capacity of ±100 MW. The minimum bid size is 1
MW, and the maximum is 50 MW. Daily auctions de-
termine aFRR capacity at the marginal price [27].
2.2.4
Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve
The mFRR is the third and final reserve type in
DK1, activated manually upon a direct request from
the TSO. It is employed after FCR and aFRR have
been fully utilized to address new imbalances and/or
to relieve these previously activated reserves. The
mFRR requires a full response within 15 minutes and
is procured asymmetrically, with only upward regula-
tion being purchased. Energinet currently maintains
+300 MW of mFRR capacity in DK1, with a minimum
bid size of 5 MW and a maximum of 50 MW. Like
aFRR, mFRR capacity is procured through daily auc-
tions at the marginal price [28].

3.2
Reserve provision capability
For the HBES to participate in reserve markets, an
assessment of the system’s technical attributes is
necessary to ensure compliance with the Transmis-
sion System Operator’s (TSO) pre-qualification crite-
ria [29]. Each reserve type has specific requirements
for bid sizes and resolution:
• FCR: Minimum bid size of 1 MW, no upper limit,
symmetrical, and integer bid resolution.
• aFRR: Minimum bid size of 1 MW, maximum of
50 MW, asymmetrical, and a bid resolution of
0.1 MW.
• mFRR: Minimum bid size of 5 MW, maximum
of 50 MW, asymmetrical, and a bid resolution
of 0.1 MW.
The ramp rate for ASIC miners is instantaneous, as
they are designed to operate at a constant power
consumption rate during mining. Unlike traditional
power plants or electrolyzers, ASIC miners do not
gradually ramp up; they operate at full power as soon
as mining begins. This makes them well-suited for
fast-acting reserves like FCR or aFRR, as well as
slower reserves such as mFRR.
3.3
Mathematical model
The model is designed to maximize profits by in-
creasing revenues while minimizing expenditures.
It is categorized into four distinct configurations for
evaluating the profitability of integrating the HRES
with Bitcoin mining operations across different mar-
ket considerations:
1. RES-DA-Det: Renewable energy system par-
ticipating in the deterministic DA market.
2. HBES-DA-Det:
RES combined with Bitcoin
mining, participating in the deterministic DA
and Bitcoin markets without importing energy
from the grid.
3. HBES-DA+Res-Det: HBES participating in the
deterministic DA, Bitcoin and reserve markets.
4. HBES-DA+Res-Sto:
HBES participating in
the deterministic DA and Bitcoin market, and
stochastic reserve markets.
Participating deterministically means that the model
has perfect foresight of the prices and behaves
as price-taker. The stochastic modeling was done
through two-stage optimization where first the model
bids for prices in the different reserve markets, and
once all the clearing outcomes are done, it partici-
pates deterministically in the DA market.
The uncertainty of the reserve market is represented
by the use of 1000 scenarios created through a block
bootstrapping method and later reduced to 4 scenar-
ios with a manhattan distance k-medoids. More in-
formation can be found in Appendix A.

be shown because it is the most complex, integrat-

the decisions made are the power set-points for the

5.3
Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis was carried out to evaluate how
different variables affect the results of the HBES-
DA&Res-Sto model. The examined parameters in-
clude the amount of containers of Bitcoin miners in-
stalled and the price of Bitcoin.
Figure 5a demonstrates the impact of increasing the
number of Bitcoin mining containers on the energy
flows and revenues of the HBES. As the number
of containers increases, electricity sold in the DA
market decreases, while grid imports rise. Bitcoin
mining revenues grow with the additional containers,
whereas DA market revenues decline. However, re-
serve market revenues remain similar to the original
case because of the regulatory limits on security of
supply prevent the HBES from providing more than
20% of the market volume.
Profits plateau after 200 containers as the system
reaches the grid capacity limit of 238 MW, forcing
the model to simultaneously consume electricity from
both the PV system and the grid. This also explains
the leveling off in grid-related costs.
The sensitivity analysis in Figure 5b further reveals
that even if Bitcoin prices dropped to zero, the HBES
would still generate profits from the reserve market.
A rise of Bitcoin prices increases the inelasticity of
the system’s response to DA market prices, leading
to a decrease in electricity sold in the DA market and
a corresponding increase in grid imports.

Discussion
The results from the optimization models highlight
the potential of integrating Bitcoin mining into Kassø
Solar Park operations. While the reduction in cur-
tailment levels is modest, the inclusion of Bitcoin
mining significantly increases revenue during curtail-
ment hours. This finding is particularly relevant for a
future energy landscape dominated by RES, where
revenue streams may need to diversify beyond the
DA market alone.
The additional revenue generated from Bitcoin min-
ing in the first two models—where no grid electric-
ity is consumed—is comparatively small relative to
DA market earnings. However, the models incorpo-
rate only one-fifth of the Bitcoin mining capacity rec-
ommended by the sensitivity analysis, 50 containers
would have reduced the curtailments by an additional
25% and incremented the profits by 92%. In the third
and fourth models, where the HBES is allowed to im-
port electricity from the grid, the revenue from min-
ing nearly doubles. This demonstrates that grid flex-
ibility, combined with Bitcoin mining, can substan-
tially enhance profitability, with additional revenues
increasing by approximately 50%.
The deterministic treatment of the DA market aligns
with real-world market operations. Balance Respon-
sible Parties (BRPs), who submit bids on behalf of
power producers, typically forecast DA prices with
reasonable accuracy. Nevertheless, this study as-
sumes perfect foresight in DA prices.
A key en-
hancement would be to incorporate stochastic vari-
ability in the DA market, enabling a more realistic
representation of price uncertainty and its impact on
decision making.
Another limitation concerns the modeling of reserve
markets. While the models account for the obliga-
tion to respect sold reserve capacity, such as turning
off miners when providing down-regulation, reserve
activation itself is not explicitly modeled. Incorporat-
ing reserve activation into the model as a third-stage
decision would better represent reality.
A deeper analysis of the correlation between Bit-
coin prices and global hash rate dynamics would pro-
vide insights into the sensitivity analysis. In practice,
higher Bitcoin prices incentivize greater global min-
ing activity, increasing the total network hash rate.
As a result, the share of Bitcoin mining rewards cap-
tured by the HBES decreases, which counteracts the
revenue gains from rising Bitcoin prices.
7
Conclusion
This study introduces a novel optimization model
for integrating Bitcoin mining with renewable energy
systems (RES) in both day-ahead (DA) and reserve
markets. The proposed model combines determin-
istic DA market participation with a stochastic formu-
lation for reserve market operations, allowing for a
realistic representation of market uncertainties.
Using Kassø Solar Park, a 300 MW photovoltaic
plant located in DK1, as a case study, the results
demonstrate the significant economic benefits of in-
corporating Bitcoin mining as a flexible load.
The
addition of 11.5 MW of mining capacity led to sub-
stantial profit increases, particularly in models that al-
lowed grid electricity imports alongside reserve mar-
ket participation. These considerations achieved the
highest profitability, highlighting the value of grid flex-
ibility in maximizing system revenues.
While the reduction in curtailed energy was modest,
the model efficiently allocated mining capacity to op-
timize participation across energy and reserve mar-
kets. This outcome underscores the economic vi-
ability of hybrid blockchain energy systems (HBES)
and their potential to mitigate RES curtailment while
creating alternative revenue streams.
Overall, this work illustrates that Bitcoin mining can
serve as an effective flexible load, supporting RES
integration and enhancing profitability in energy sys-
tems. Future research should focus on further refin-
ing the model, including stochastic DA market partici-
pation and reserve activation dynamics, to provide a
more comprehensive assessment of hybrid energy
systems under real-world operational conditions.

A.2
Stochastic modeling
To incorporate uncertainty into the simulation, a two-stage stochastic optimization framework was selected
for the final model, HBES-DA&BTC-Sto. This approach captures the inherent variability of market condi-
tions. The first stage involves making bids in the reserve markets, as the gate closure times for FCR, aFRR,
and mFRR are at 08:00, 07:30, and 09:30, respectively. Once these reserve market bids are cleared, the
second stage focuses on the DA market, where prices are determined.
Since the DA market is modeled deterministically, the HBES has perfect foresight and acts as a price-taker.
However, to account for the uncertainties in the reserve markets, a scenario-based approach was adopted.
Specifically, the uncertainty is modeled using a set of scenarios, denoted as ω. Prior to generating the
scenarios, statistical properties of the market data, including correlations and seasonality, were analyzed.
Figure 6 illustrates that the prices of different markets exhibit weak correlations. For instance, the correlation
between DA and FCR prices is -0.43, while aFRR Up and aFRR Down prices exhibit a moderate correlation
of 0.52. These values suggest that the markets operate relatively independently. Additionally, Figure 7
demonstrates that each variable exhibits significant autocorrelation at 24 lags (corresponding to 24 hours),
indicating a daily seasonality pattern. This finding aligns with the expected behavior of energy and reserve
markets, which are influenced by recurring daily demand and supply cycles.
Figure 6: Linear correlation between markets.
Given these observations, the block bootstrap method was chosen for scenario generation. Block bootstrap
is a resampling technique that groups the data into blocks of a specified length and then samples these
blocks randomly to construct scenarios. A block size of four weeks was selected to preserve the identified
seasonality, while the total number of generated scenarios was set to 1000 to capture the full variability of
the data.

Although 1000 samples provide a comprehensive representation of the data’s variability, this large number

metric calculates the sum of the absolute differences between the coordinates of two points, making it well-
suited for scenarios with distinct patterns. The probabilities assigned to the resulting scenarios are [2.7%,
3.8%, 57.5%, and 36%], reflecting their relative likelihood.

HBES-DA&Res-Det: Solar park with Bitcoin mining in DA and reserve markets

flows, including electricity sold to the DA market, consumed by Bitcoin miners, or curtailed, as well as the

Figure 24 demonstrates the behavior of the HBES during a day with negative DA prices. In this model, the
system maximizes Bitcoin mining by utilizing electricity from both the PV system and the grid. The model
also provides aFRR up-regulation reserves, which contribute additional revenue. Interestingly, during hours
13:00 and 14:00, the system curtails 100% of the PV-generated electricity and instead uses imported grid
electricity for mining. This occurs because the exceptionally low DA prices make grid-imported electricity
more profitable for mining.
Figure 25 highlights the system’s operation during a day with high DA prices. Here, the HBES opts to sell all
PV-generated electricity to the DA market. However, at 16:00, the system switches to mining Bitcoin at full
capacity while also providing aFRR up-regulation reserves. This decision is driven by the higher combined
revenue from Bitcoin mining and reserves compared to selling in the DA market.
Figure 26 illustrates the system’s winter operation, where the HBES prioritizes Bitcoin mining during hours
of low electricity prices and high reserve prices. This figure shows the additional revenue of the HBES
compared to other models, such as the one from Figure 20.
Figure 27 depicts a summer day characterized by high electricity prices during periods of low PV production.
Under these conditions, the HBES prioritizes selling down-regulation reserves instead of mining Bitcoin.
However, when high FCR prices occur, the system provides symmetrical reserve services by activating the
miners. This day exemplifies the model’s effective calibration to both price signals and PV production levels
even during times of curtailment.
