Abstract
In this project the seismic vulnerability of a structure subjected to mainshock-afershock ground
motions and mainshock ground motions was investigated with the use of collapse fragility curves.
Based of an in-depth literature review the framework for deriving the seismic collapse fragility
curves for mainshock events and mainshock-afershock events is described and provided. An ex-
isting building in Van Nuys, California, USA was used herein as the numerical analysis testbed.
Te structure is a seven storey RC building which was modelled using the OpenSees ﬁnite element
sofware. Te ﬁnite element model was validated in terms of modal proprieties and time-history
response based on real recorded data. Based on a well deﬁned criteria a suite of 20 mainshock
ground motions and 20 afershock ground motions were selected from the PEER database. Re-
sulting in 20 real recorded earthquake sequences. Incremental nonlinear time history analyses
were performed, were the ground motions were scaled in terms of spectral acceleration result-
ing in 800 analyses, and the structure’s response in terms of Roof Drif (RD), Interstorey Drif
(IDR) and Maximum Displacement at the roof level (Umax) is presented. Based on the structure’s
response the fragility analysis was conducted and the collapse fragility curves were derived by us-
ing the collapse limit state according to the codes. Te results indicate that the structure subjected
to mainshock-afershock ground motions presents an small increase in the collapse vulnerability
when compared to only mainshock ground motions excitations.

Chapter 1| Introduction
Buildings represent a general, basic and continuous necessity, whether that these serve as
shelter or they are used for several diﬀerent purposes there’s a continuous demand for bigger,
more economical, more environmental friendly and the most important more reliable structures.
When designing diﬀerent types of structures and infrastructures the main focus is on strength,
durability and safety and how to achieve all of these in the most economical way. And in order to
achieve these the structural design of a building represents a critical and essential part. Structures
are designed to safely operate under typical loads throughout their lifetime, such as the vertical
loads (i.e. dead loads, live loads, snow loads) and the horizontal loads (i.e. wind loads, seismic
loads).
In seismic prone regions of medium to high seismicity a special perception is required as to
account for these natural hazards. An important factor in the structural design is the extreme load
that the earthquake’s ground motions can exert on the building’s structure. Terefor the eﬀect of
the earthquake’s ground motions on the structure’s response is to be investigated in this study.
Te typical design guidelines and national normatives propose a design approach based on
a single and main seismic event with high intensity, therefor accounting only for the presence
of mainshock ground motions. Te presence of the foreshock ground motions and/or afershock
ground motion is neglected and their impact on the structures behavior. In the recent years more
and more researchers are investigating the eﬀect of multiple earthquakes (earthquake sequences)
on diﬀerent aspects of nonlinear behavior of structures ([24], [23], [21], [60], [4]). It was found
that structural damage was present due to the earthquake’s sequence ground motions eﬀect on
the structure.
Terefore, it is necessary to consider the eﬀect the mainshock event presents to the structures
damage and its impacts on the nonlinear behavior when subjected to subsequent seismic events.
Since intensive mainshocks events can trigger intensive afershocks events within a rather short
time interval between the two events. Due to the possible short time span between mainshock
and afershock event, it can be diﬃcult to retroﬁt a damaged structure due to preceding intensive
shaking to withstand a following possible intensive ground motion. Another case is the presence
of the foreshock event which can present a certain level of stiﬀness and strength degradation,
therefor the structure subjected to the larger seismic event (mainshock) behaves diﬀerent than
expected and the potential of collapse may increase.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Along these lines, the objective of the following study is to quantify the performance of an
existing building and to investigate the stiﬀness and strength degradation as a result of accumu-
lated damage due to real recorded seismic sequences. Te investigated building is a seven storey
reinforced concrete frame structure located in Van Nuys, California, USA. Using nonlinear mate-
rial proprieties and the OpenSees ﬁnite element sofware the numerical model was created as a
detailed 2D frame nonlinear ﬁnite element model. Te numerical model was subjected to rigor-
ous validation, in terms of modal analysis and nonlinear response history analysis, to ensure the
reliability of the obtained response. Knowing that the structure’s response is greatly inﬂuenced
by the characteristics of the ground motions used in the numerical analysis special atention was
dedicated to the selection of real recorded ground motion suite, used to excite the structure. Te
mainshock and afershock events were selected based on a rigorous and well deﬁned criteria, aim-
ing to obtain relevant response from the structure. Incremental dynamic analysis was performed
in order to cover a wider range of responses. For this the mainshock ground motions were scaled,
in terms of spectral acceleration at the fundamental period of the structure (Sa.MS), from 0.1g to
1.0g at a step of 0.1g while the afershock ground motions were scaled three times such that the
relative spectral intensity (∆Sa) to the mainshock’s spectral intensity would be: 0.5, 0.8 and 1.
Te time history response, induced by the mainshock and mainshock-afershock ground motions,
was quantiﬁed in terms of RD, IDR and Umax. Using interstorey drif response as the engineering
demand parameter and the spectral acceleration as intensity measure the fragility analysis was
performed in order to derive the collapse fragility curves accounting damage demand induced by
the mainshock events and mainshock-afershock events. Collapse fragility curves are a statisti-
cal tool which represent the probability of exceeding the collapse limit state as a function of an
engineering demand parameter that represents the ground motion. In order to investigate the
building’s performance when subjected to real recorded seismic sequences, these fragility curves
were used.

Chapter 2| Literature review
Te present chapter introduces a thorough literature review in order to provide a detailed
presentation of previous researches related to the topic of the current project. Te chapter’s ﬁrst
section Section 2.1 presents to the reader the eﬀect of seismic events on diﬀerent types of struc-
tures. Te following section Section 2.2 presents the eﬀect of the seismic sequence excitation on
the structure’s demand based on diﬀerent studies related to this topic. While the last section of
this chapter Section 2.3 presents the seismic risk assessment in terms of fragility analysis used by
multiple researchers to present the seismic vulnerability of diﬀerent structures.
2.1
Structures subjected to earthquakes
Earthquake induced ground motions excite the structures in three ways, two horizontal and
one vertical. Out of the components of these strong ground motions the horizontal actions are the
ones that excite the structure the most and create high structural stresses and deformations. In
terms of the way the structure is aﬀected by the vertical translational motions it is ofen safe to
neglect them and this is due to their lower eﬀect on the structure’s response in comparison with
the horizontal ones. Another aspect taken into account is the fact that these vertical translational
motions are counteracted by the self weight of the structure resulting in reduced eﬀects in the
structural elements.
Te overall structure of a building is designed, according to the codes, so that its structural
components can withstand the maximum seismic loads and deformations generated during an
earthquake event, ensuring the protection of its occupants and the building’s integrity throughout
the seismic event. Te building’s structure, in terms of stiﬀness and strength, is designed in such
way that it is able to resist during an main earthquakes event, as stated before, but at the same
time compromising with the appearance, function and cost. Tere are two essential factors to take
into consideration when addressing this request while designing the structure to withstand such
occurrences as earthquakes, and these are: Stiﬀness and Damping. Tese factors were taken into
account in diﬀerent researches involving the eﬀect earthquakes present on structures.
Te eﬀect of earthquake ground motions on steel structures in terms of cumulative damages
is presented in [36], experimental data is presented from low cycle fatigue tests of structural steel
components. In these tests the failure modes of local buckling in beam ﬂanges and fracture at
weldments were studied in detail. Cumulative damage models are proposed which permit a life
prediction for arbitrary cyclic loading histories. Adequate predictions of life were obtained from
the analytic models.

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW
In [19] another aspect is taken into consideration when addressing the eﬀect of these motions,
and that is the reliability of an reinforced earth retaining structure subjected to earthquakes. In this
paper earth retaining walls containing geotextiles are investigated under horizontal earthquake
forces in order to asses the safety of the structure. Based on the probabilistic safety concept, which
allowed for the reliability analysis to be performed in three diﬀerent locations in Japan. It was con-
cluded that in two of the locations the construction seems feasible while in the other place a special
caution has to be accorded. Te following paper [49], describes an application of discrete wavelet
analysis for damage detection of a framed structure subjected to strong earthquake excitation. Te
numerical results show the eﬀectiveness of the discrete wavelet approach to damage detection of
framed structures. Te response simulation data on each ﬂoor were obtained by the use of non-
linear dynamic analysis. Due to the dynamic behaviour of an inelastic structure discrete wavelet
analysis was performed as to analyze the simulation response data at each ﬂoor. It concluded that
the damage can be detected by observing the spikes from the wavelet details.
Another research [16] addresses the eﬀect of the soil-structure interaction. In this paper the
authors evaluate the soil-structure interaction for structures subjected to earthquakes taking into
consideration diﬀerent types of foundations. Tey considered that the structural response is inﬂu-
enced by the type of soil and structure, starting from the fact that the motion of the ground changes
due to the interaction between the foundation and soil during an earthquake. Multi storey build-
ings were subjected to seismic forces and analyzed under diﬀerent soil conditions such as hard,
medium and sof. Te buildings were analyzed using response spectrum method. Te response
of building frames in terms of lateral deﬂection, storey drif, base shear, axial force and column
moment values for all of the buildings were presented and compared.
Due to intensive damage and collapse of parking structures observed during the 1994 Northridge
earthquake, diﬀerent analyses made showed that ﬂexible diaphragms can experience larger hor-
izontal accelerations and interstorey drifs than prescribed by the codes of practice [37]. In this
study, the ampliﬁcation of forces and displacements in ﬂexible diaphragms was investigated for
low-rise structures of diﬀerent heights with stiﬀperimeter shear walls, during inelastic dynamic
analyses. Te paper concludes that current code provisions can underestimate diaphragm forces
at diﬀerent ﬂoors under common conditions.
An important response and aspect of a structure’s behavior during seismic excitation is the
seismic induced storey drif. By minimizing the residual interstorey drifs, roof drifs and inter-
storey drifs the damage suﬀered by the structure can be reduced. Diﬀerent researches have been
conducted in order to evaluate this topic, among them [5] and [61]. [5] presents a drif design
structural model for the design of high-rise buildings in seismic zones. Te focus was on mini-
mization of the structure’s weight, roof drif and the uniform distribution of the interstorey drifs
over the building’s height in order to minimize earthquake damage. Te paper concludes that the
drif design structural model performed well and that it can be of great importance to the struc-
tural design of buildings. Where in [61] a storey drif estimation procedure found in the National
Building Code of Canada was evaluated for ductile frame structures in diﬀerent seismic prone ar-
eas. Tis was performed by comparing the results from inelastic dynamic analyses with the one
obtain from estimations based on the code. It concluded that the code’s procedure to estimate the
storey drif underestimates the seismic response for low-rise frame structures.

2.2
Structures subjected to earthquake sequences
Numerous research papers have been published in the recent years where the importance
of earthquake sequences is addressed and the way these inﬂuence the structural response of a
structure. Designing the structure to withhold one extreme earthquake doesn’t take into account
the accumulated damage under earthquakes of smaller intensities than the mainshock’s. But these
smaller ground motions can be many in number and successive, meaning that the structures are ex-
cited on a certain scale. Tese translate to the fact that the structural design doesn’t take into con-
sideration the possible cumulative degradation in stiﬀness and strength under seismic sequences,
especially when there’s a small time frame in between mainshock and afershock event.
Afer diﬀerent earthquake events that took place around the world, it has been observed that
in some cases during these sequences the damage induced by repeated ground motions was accu-
mulating in the structural stiﬀness. Weakening the stiﬀness and strength of the overall structure.
Terefor studies regarding the structure’s degrading behavior, under multiple earthquakes, be-

Seismic related fragility curves
Fragility analysis, having as a result the fragility or vulnerability curves, is a probabilistic
method of presenting the performance and reliability of a structure, or structural elements, when
exposed to earthquake events. Fragility or vulnerability curves present a way of communicating
the vulnerability of the structure and its components to the natural hazard, presenting it in terms
of probability of exceeding or reaching a certain level of damage. Te probability of failure related
to a certain criteria (e.g. maximum inter-storey drif ) is constituted as a function of the intensity
that the earthquake’s ground motion has. Tis tool is helpful when desiring to present, in a clear
and simple manner, the implications diﬀerent decisions on the structure have. In the last two
decades the number of probabilistic seismic fragility analyses performed has increased.
Te steps, that the procedure for performing fragility analysis present, can be applied as well
for residential buildings, dams, industrial structures or other any other structure or component. In
this section diﬀerent scientiﬁc papers on seismic fragility curves are presented related to diﬀerent
types of structures and approaches.
A type of structure that beneﬁts from extensive literature on fragility curves are bridges. In
[11] a set of fragility curves for four typical bridges commonly found in United States is presented.
Using a suite of artiﬁcial seismic ground motions and nonlinear analytic models, the fragility
curves were ﬁrst generated for components of the bridges and then transformed into fragility
curves that depict the state of the whole bridge system, for each of the four cases. It was found
that multi-span simply supported and multi-span continuous steel-girder bridges are the most
vulnerable between the four common types. Another interesting case where fragility curves were
developed in order to asses the vulnerability for several limit states of an existing bridge in Mexico,
which is of high importance in the region, is presented in [31]. As it can be observed, in many
of the literature related to fragility curves for bridges, the vulnerability of the whole structure
is based on the vulnerability of certain important structural elements that constitute the bridge
system. Compared to other structures (e.g. buildings and dams) where the whole structure can
be directly subjected to fragility analysis, in the case of the bridges the problem is a bit more
complicated. A research paper [46] presented an expanded methodology for the fragility analysis,
taking into consideration the contribution of the major components of the bridge, such as the
columns, bearings and abutments, to its overall fragility. Te study concludes that the bridge as
a system is more vulnerable than any of its components. As in [46] in a diﬀerent paper [39] an
oﬀshore bridge is subjected to fragility analysis. In this study the individual structural elements
are examined in terms of vulnerability. Te conclusion reached was that the vulnerability of a
bridge system cannot be ignored in the coastal environment and fragility analyses are required
for the whole life period. Other similar studies [9] and [10].
When speaking about dams the fragility analysis represents a tool for evaluating the safety
of an existing dam and decision-making for modeling diﬀerent components that may impact the
dam’s performance. In [54] and [15] concrete gravity dams are subjected to fragility analyses and
a methodology for developing these fragilities to assess their performance against seismic hazards
is presented. Another paper [32] presents an IDA approach to analyze a thin high arch dam and
develop the fragility curves. Tis paper concluded that the dam’s body seems to be weak and
needs retroﬁting works.

Fragility Curves
Seismic fragility curves are deﬁned as the probability of reaching or exceeding a speciﬁc given
damage state (limit state) as a function of a measure of excitation during an earthquake event.
Tese curves represent an important and usefully tool in order to assess and communicate the
seismic vulnerability of a structure. In a scientiﬁc paper reviewed [50] the author presents the

component, reaches or exceeds some clearly deﬁned limit state) as a function of some measure of en-

hurricane, or other extreme loading condition)”.

Diﬀerent structures have have diﬀerent fragility curves, these are tailored based on the char-
acteristic of the structure and its location. Te most covered types of structures, in literature, are
steel structures and reinforced concrete structures. Numerous papers, some of them reviewed in

developed based on diﬀerent methods developed along the time. Based on the literature review
three main methods were identiﬁed, for example in [51] three methods are identiﬁed and stan-
dard procedures for developing fragility curves, for each method, are presented. Another addi-
tional method is found in [43], where the author presents four methods: expert-based, empirical,
analytic and hybrid. A short explanation, with the main advantages and disadvantages, of the
methods is presented below:
Expert-Based Method
Expert-based method is the oldest and simplest method to develop fragility curves. For this
method, the damaged suﬀered by a structure subjected to earthquake ground motions is evaluated
by various qualiﬁed experts. In [30] the authors present two types of expert elicitation approaches,
mathematical and behavioral, together with the diﬃculties these two present when using this
particular method. Fragility estimates are then obtained from the probability distribution of the
damage state at each intensity level.
Even though this method is presented as simple and therefor atractive, being dependent on the
expert’s opinion, which is inﬂuenced by their experience and how many they are, transforms this
method into a slightly inaccurate method.
Empirical Method
Empirical fragility curves developed by this method are based on previous recorded earth-
quake events, employing damage data collecting, suﬀered by the structures subjected to historical
earthquakes. Te observed damage at diﬀerent locations can then be correlated to a certain type of
measure of intensity selected. Terefor construction of empirical fragility curves requires obser-
vations of two variables: the damage sustained and the corresponding intensity-measure levels.
In [29] the authors used the empirical method in order to determine the seismic fragility curves of
a reinforced concrete frame. Tey concluded with two main points, that the impact of variability
in ground-motion levels is signiﬁcant, and leads to considerably ﬂater fragility curves and wider
conﬁdence intervals, and that there is a need for a very dense network of ground motion recording
stations in order for the recorded data to reduce uncertainty in empirical fragility functions. Us-
ing the damage data observed at the location of the structure represents the most realistic way to
develop fragility, and that is due to the ability to take into account the variability of the structural
capacity of the exposed structure and also the variability in the soil-structure interaction. Haniclar
et al.[22] described that empirical fragility functions can also serve as validation and calibration
of analytically derived fragility curves.

Analytic Method
Te following described method is the most popular method used to develop fragility curves. It
has been widely used in literature and in order to develop the fragility curves simulation methods
need to be applied for real or artiﬁcial seismic ground motions, mostly nonlinear static analysis
and nonlinear time history analysis. One of the reasons why this method is ofen selected is that
the fragility curves can be developed even if the damage data is insuﬃcient, therefor the approach
has less bias. Te only main two disadvantages that can result, from selecting the use of this
method, are that this method can be extremely time consuming and costly as well in terms of
computation.
As presented in Chapter 2 diﬀerent researchers opted for this method ([26],[52],[2],[58],etc.) and
this analytic method to develop fragility curves has been used in this project. A reason for which a
more detailed description of the method will be presented in Section 3.2 Analytic Fragility Curves.
Hybrid Method
Kammula et al. [33] presents the conﬁguration of the hybrid simulation in order to present the
seismic performance of a structure equipped with T-SCED braces. Te study supports the selec-
tion of hybrid (analytically-experimental) simulation based on the idea that the fragility curve’s
accuracy can be compromised when using the analytic method to derive fragility curves, and that
is due to the fact that an analytically model of every component of the structure is required and
these can be modelled unrealistically or inaccurately. Terefor the hybrid fragility curves are de-
rived by combining both the experimental method and analytic method.
[33] describes the hybrid method as ”a performed based on a step-by-step numerical integration
method to solve the governing equations of motion for a numerical structural model that is formu-
lated considering both the analytically and physical components of a structural system”. Te hybrid
simulation method combines physical testing of a substructure with computer models of the re-
mainder of the structure. It provides the structural vulnerability of diﬀerent structures, subjected
to seismic ground motions, without having to physically test the entire structure therefor present-
ing a huge advantage that the computational eﬀort can be reduced.
Te main disadvantage that this method presents is that it requires a vast amount of data. Having
more information available can reduced the uncertainties involved in this method, in the a study
[42] the authors reported that the hybrid approach involves large aleatory uncertainties such as
material properties, wind loads, lack of knowledge and incorrect modeling.

4.2
Finite element model
Te design information, presented in Section 4.1 and Appendix (A), are used to create a numer-
ical model in order to run the nonlinear time-history analyses. Te ﬁnite element model contains
the structure of the building’s south frame, which was selected to be analyzed in terms of struc-
tural response when subjected to strong ground motions and was created using the OpenSees
ﬁnite element code [41].
A summary of the steps followed to create the ﬁnite element model is presented in the follow-
ing. Deﬁning the numerical model using the OpenSees basic model command includes OpenSees
speciﬁc commands. Tese additional commands are used for the construction of Nodes, Masses,
Materials, Sections, Elements, Load Paterns, Time Series, Transformations, Blocks and Constraints.
Te model, basic builder, deﬁnes the number of dimensions and degrees of freedom per node. And
for the structural frame model created, the number of spacial dimensions is two and three degrees
of freedom at each node (two translations in the plane and the rotation about the plane’s normal)
are considered. Te material nonlinearities are considered and presented in the following Subsec-
tion 4.2.1,while the geometric nonlinearities ignored.
Te structural elements of the frame (beams and columns) were modeled as nonlinear elements.
Te nonlinear Beam\Columns elements are based on the iterative force formulation, and con-
siders the spread of plasticity along the element. Te integration along the element is based on
Gauss-Lobato quadrature rule which states for two integration points at the element ends.
For each nonlinear element a section was assigned, section which deﬁnes the stress resultant
force-deformation response at a cross section of the structural element. In this model the sec-
tions were modeled using the ﬁber section. As presented in [41] these sections are discretized
into smaller regions for which the material stress-strain response is integrated to give resultant
behavior. Te cross sections are discretized into ﬁbers of conﬁned concrete, unconﬁned concrete
and reinforcing steel.
Te soil-structure interaction and its eﬀect are neglected during this study, therefor the frame
was modeled as ﬁxed at the base of the ﬁrst storey columns.
For every case of ground motion excitation, the analysis was performed in two steps:
• First, the static loading that is due to the self weight is applied (Gravity Analysis).
• Secondly, the dynamic loading is applied (Nonlinear Dynamic Analysis).
For the dynamic analysis Newton-Raphson algorithm was used to solve the nonlinear residual
equation, introduced by line search. According to [41] line search increases the eﬀectiveness of
the Newton method when convergence is slow due to roughness of the residual. Te 5 % rayleigh
damping was assigned to all of the elements and nodes.

5.1
Criteria used for the seismic sequences selection
An important part of the present study was the selection of earthquake sequences ground
motion, in terms of mainshock and afershock, which would later be used in the nonlinear time-
history analyses. As presented in Chapter 2 diﬀerent studies were conducted as to asses and ob-
serve the structural behavior of individual structures subjected to either only mainshock seismic
events or seismic sequence events. In the aforementioned chapter it was found that the selection
of seismic ground motions used in the numerical simulations can be based on past seismic events
which were selected on a arbitrary deﬁned criteria, or due to the lack of real recorded seismic
sequences the artiﬁcial seismic sequences were created and for some of the researches a mix of
artiﬁcial and real recorded events was used. Based on the literature reviewed a general conclu-
sion reached was that it’s unrealistic and not recommended to use artiﬁcial simulated seismic
sequences in order to analyze the structural performance of a structure subjected to earthquake
sequences, based on the comparison made with the use of real as-recorded seismic sequences ([6],
[21] and [27]).
Due to this mentioned reasoning, a selection of real-recorded seismic sequences (mainshock-
afershock) was done in this project by using the Paciﬁc Earthquake Engineering Research Center
(PEER) ground motion database which provides tools for searching, selecting and downloading
ground motion data [47]. Te recordings available at PEER database are baseline corrected and
ﬁltered, and the recordings consists of three separate ﬁles for each directional component (two
horizontal and one vertical).
Te mentioned PEER database was scrutinized in terms of well based criteria in order to se-
lect relevant earthquake ground motions. Te relevant criteria for the collection of earthquake
sequences was established taking into consideration the criteria used in earlier studies along with
the purpose that the ground motions should signiﬁcantly reﬂect the structural performance of the
frame and accurately represent the structural response.

5.3
Response spectra of selected seismic sequences
Te frequency contents of the selected seismic ground motions depends on the source mecha-
nism, the moment magnitude, epicentral distance and the soil conditions. Te frequency contents
that a suite of seismic strong ground motions has can be used for describing and understanding
the structural response experienced during these seismic events. Based on the selected ground
motion, in order to determine the frequency contents of these, the response spectra was used.
Response spectra is practical tool used for analyzing the structural performance of the 2D
frame subjected to earthquake ground motions, and this is due to the fact that the structure’s
frame behaves like a simple oscillator with known frequency. Te response spectrum is a function
of frequency or period of the ground motion and of its damping, therefor evaluating the eﬀect
that the ground motions have on the structure can be done by using the structure’s fundamental
frequency along with the response spectrum.
Based on the modal analysis performed on the studied reinforced-concrete frame in this project
it was concluded that the natural period at the ﬁrst mode equals to T = 1.5s. A linear response
spectrum is a plot of the peak response (displacement, velocity or acceleration) of the ground
motion of varying frequency with diﬀerent degrees of damping. For the herein study 5% damping
was considered.
Te most commonly used forms of linear response spectra are: Acceleration spectra Sa, Dis-
placement spectra Sd and Velocity spectra Sv. Te Pseudo-Spectral Acceleration (PSA) was used in
order to investigate the inﬂuence that the frequency content, of the selected ground motions, has
on the structure’s frame. Te pseudo-acceleration response spectrum of a ground motion presents
a relationship between the natural period of vibration of the structure’s frame and the maximum
absolute acceleration that it experiences under the respective ground motion. Te period at which
maximum absolute pseudo-acceleration is read is called the predominant or peak period (Tg).
Comparing the Structure’s fundamental period (T1) and Predominant period (Tg), of a certain se-
lected seismic ground motion, a good suggestion can be made on how much the structure will be
inﬂuenced or excited by the respective motion.
A presentation of the pseudo-acceleration response spectrums for the ground motion suit se-
lected is presented, in Figure 5.4 for the selected mainshock seismic events and in Figure 5.5 for the
afershock ground motions.
Based on the ﬁgures presented it can be observed that the peak period, where the maximum ab-
solute pseudo-acceleration occurs, in both cases (mainshock and afershock ground motion suite),
is found between 0.1 s and 1 s. Covering a wider range of peak periods would improve the study
since the structure’s frame would be excited to diﬀerent levels. However due to the structure’s
frame high fundamental period (T1 = 1.5s) and limitation imposed by the PEER database, which
is relatively insubstantial when applying the mentioned criteria, this was not possible.
As presented in the section above, the seismic ground motions were scaled in terms of spectral ac-
celeration at the fundamental period, measure that allowed for this inconvenience to be somehow
solved and the structure to be subjected to diﬀerent seismic spectral intensities.

Te performance and seismic time-history response of the structure’s frame, subjected to
mainshock events and mainshock-afershock events, is to be analyzed and presented in the follow-
ing chapter, based on the 800 nonlinear time-history analyses that were performed. Te non-linear
dynamic analyses were carried out with the intention to present the eﬀect seismic sequences have
on the structure’s response when compared to the building’s response when considering only the
mainshock events. Scaling the seismic ground motions on three levels of relative spectral intensity,
between the afershock and the mainshock records, the investigation that the afershock seismic
events present on the seismic response is made for wide range of seismic intensities.
In ﬁrst part of the the present Chapter (Section 6.1) the structure’s response when excited by
two aleatory selected seismic ground motions is presented while in the following sections (Sections
6.2, 6.3 and 6.4) the general structural mean response is presented in terms of drif-related demand
parameters such as RD and IDR, and displacement demand parameters such as Umax.

Chapter 7| Fragility Analysis
In the present chapter, the analytic fragility curves are derived and presented to the reader.
Te fragility of a structure or a component is the probability that a given damage state or a limit
state is exceeded as function of an engineering demand given an intensity measure parameter that
describes the ground motion which excites the structure.
In order to evaluate the vulnerability of the structure’s frame, described in this study, under
seismic mainshock ground motions and seismic sequences ground motions used to instigate the
structure’s seismic response, the methodology is presented in Chapter 3.
Te following presents a summary of the basic steps performed in order to develop mainshock
fragility curves and mainshock-afershock sequence fragility curves, as presented in Chapter 3
based on the framework developed by [58].
• Selection of the mainshock-afershock ground motion database.
• Modelling of the investigated building’s structure.
• Selection of the engineering demand parameter (EDP) which characterizes the cumulative
damage experienced by the structure and determining the limit states.
• Selection of appropriate intensity measure (IM).
• Conduct the incremental dynamic analysis (IDA) for mainshock ground motions and mainshock-
afershock ground motions excitation.
• Based on the results of nonlinear time-history analyses, determine the mainshock fragility
curves and the mainshock-afershock sequence fragility curves.
Te ﬁrst step, selection of the mainshock-afershock ground motion database was com-
prehensively described and presented in Chapter 5 while the resulting database with the resulting
seismic ground motions selected is presented in Table 5.2. Te modelling of the investigated
building’s structure as numerical model which was used to run the analyses is presented in
Chapter 4 together with a description of the investigated building.

Engineering Demand Parameters and Limit States
Engineering demand parameters (EDP), such as inter-storey drif, roof drif, maximum dis-
placement or ﬂoor acceleration, can be correlated to structural damage within a structure. In this
study, the structural response when the building is excited by mainshock ground motions and
mainshock-afershock sequence ground motions is presented in terms of three EDPs: peak roof
displacement (Umax), peak roof drif (RDmax) and peak interstorey drif (IDRmax). All of these
three parameters are measured during the nonlinear analyses performed and are presented in
Chapter 6.
Limit states for structures or components can be deﬁned using diﬀerent approaches which diﬀer-
entiate based on the structure investigated. However in the present study, as described in Chapter
6, the limit states were governed by the peak inter-storey drif ratio (IDRmax) for which clear
limitations are imposed in both the European code EN 1998 and American FEMA P-356[12], limi-
tations that are presented in Table 6.2 and Chapter 6. Terefor the EDPLS was chosen as 4% which
represents the limit state as the collapse state FEMA P-356 [12].
Selection of appropriate intensity measure (IM)
Te incremental dynamic analysis preformed in this study using mainshock ground motions
and mainshock-afershock sequence ground motions, implicated scaling, to diﬀerent speciﬁc in-
tensity levels, of the mainshock and afershock ground motions in order to observe the structural
response. For a good correlation between the seismic ground motion excitation and the structure’s
response it is important how the intensity of the selected motions is described. Among the most
frequent used intensity measures are the Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA), Spectral Displacement
(Sd) and Spectral Acceleration (Sa).
A study [35], which assessed the seismic vulnerability of reinforced concrete silos using PGA
and Sa as intensity measures. One of the conclusions presented at the end of the study is that
the spectral acceleration is the one that ﬁts the response results beter and creates an accurate
representation of the structure’s demand. When using the PGA as a intensity measure it was
found that the response results had a bad ﬁting. Another paper [58], where part of the study was
accorded to comparing the PGA with Sa by the correlation with the response of structure. Te
authors conclude that the correlation of Sa is larger than that of PGA, and therefor continuing
with the derivation of fragility curves using the Sa as a intensity measure.
Te intensity measure in terms spectral acceleration at the structure’s fundamental period
presents additional information regarding the investigated structure’s speciﬁc case while the use
of PGA as an intensity measure implicates a more general presentation which is related to the
intensity of the seismic motions.
Based on this ﬁndings the Spectral Acceleration (Sa) was used as IM in this present study as
well, and the scaling performed as a base for the incremental nonlinear analysis was in terms of
spectral intensity. Scaling which is presented in Chapter 5.

Chapter 8| Conclusion and Further Work
Te study conducted in this project determined the inﬂuence of the afhershock’s ground mo-
tion on the structure’s response when subjected to mainshock-afershock earthquake sequences.
Te analyzed building is a seven storey reinforced concrete structure located in Van Nuys, Cal-
ifornia, USA. In order to asses the structural response a reﬁned ﬁnite element model was devel-
oped using OpenSees sofware, and the validation was done in terms of modal validation and
real recorded response data, during the Northridge 1994 earthquake, by the use of accelerometers
mounted in the building. Based on a thoroughly selected criteria a suite of 20 real recorded seismic
sequences was created. In order to perform the numerical analysis the mainshock ground motions
were scaled in regards to the spectral acceleration at the fundamental period, while the afershock
was scaled based on three diﬀerent mainshock-afershock relative spectral intensities. Based on
the results, from the nonlinear time-history analyses, the main ﬁndings of the present study are
enumerated as follows:
• Due to the limited database, regarding real recorded seismic sequences, and the structures
high fundamental period the selection of real recorded mainshock and afershock ground
motions, based on a well deﬁned criteria, which would adequately excite the structure at
diﬀerent intensities so that the response can be observed for a wider range is limited.
• Te use of the incremental nonlinear time-history analysis enables the structure to be ad-
equately excited, resulting in a beter investigation on the structure’s seismic performance
under diﬀerent intensities.
• Te numerical analysis results, presented in terms of Umax,RDmax and IDRmax, showed
that the structure’s demand tended to increase when the ground motions intensity increases.
While for the inﬂuence that the afershock presents in the structure’s demand, when sub-
jected to MSAS ground motions, an upward trend when the spectral relative intensity in-
creases can be observed but the eﬀect is slightly insigniﬁcant.
• Afershock ground motions can slightly increase both the median seismic response and
dispersion of the structure’s response, depending strictly on the intensity of mainshock
and afershock ground motions respectively. While in the most unfavorable case where
the relative intensity is the highest ∆Sa = 1.0 an average of 6.5% can be observed in the
mean demand ratio between seismic sequence excitation and mainshock ground motion
excitation.
• Scaling the ground motions in terms of spectral acceleration at the fundamental period of
the structure oﬀered a good IM, which was used in developing a reliable probabilistic seismic

• Te structure’s vulnerability of collapse when excited by mainshock ground motions and

Further work
Diﬀerent limitations occurred along the course of the current study. Tese limitations present
several opportunities to more comprehensive studies and to further investigate the structure’s
behavior under seismic sequences. Some of these opportunities are presented in the following:
• In the present study the peak-deformation based damage measures were observed during
the nonlinear analyses preformed. Te energy-based damage measure can be used to present
the additional damage induced by afershock’s ground motions.
• Another important aspect, part of the seismic response, which can present a good depiction
on how much the structure is inﬂuenced by the seismic ground motions is the residual
deformation. Due to the high number of analyses performed and the duration of one analysis
this was not possible during this project.
• Structure’s response under foreshock-mainshock-afershock and foreshock-mainshock se-
quences.
• In the present study a single frame of the building studied was investigated, further work
can be done where a more complex structural model is used.
• Te soil-structure interaction should be accounted and it’s inﬂuence in the seismic response
can be determined.
• Te present study investigated the eﬀect of the seismic sequence recorded on ﬁrm soils,
while further investigations can be made on the eﬀect that seismic sequences recorded on
sof soils present in terms of the structure’s response.
