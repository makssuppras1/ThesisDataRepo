Abstract
Amidst escalating environmental concerns such as global warming and the depletion
of fossil fuel reserves, electric vehicles have surged to the forefront of today’s industrial
landscape.
One of the pivotal challenges confronting electric vehicles is the attainment
of state-of-charge (SOC) balancing for parallel battery packs, a matter that has garnered
significant research focus owing to its paramount importance for safety and performance
optimization.
In the prevalent parallel structure adopted for battery packs in electric vehicles, the
expectation is for rapid charging at very high power. However, the charging voltage for bat-
teries remains relatively low, typically around 25V, necessitating exceptionally high charging
currents. Conversely, charging stations operate at significantly higher output voltages, often
around 800V. Consequently, an isolated DC-DC converter becomes imperative to reduce the
voltage to the desired level. The primary challenge in this design endeavor lies in maximiz-
ing the converter’s efficiency while addressing the substantial voltage disparity between the
charging station and the battery packs.
In this thesis, the initial phase involves a thorough analysis of various DC-DC converter
topologies through theoretical calculations.
Selecting an appropriate topology is crucial
not only for enhancing performance but also for optimizing cost in converter design. For
instance, considerations include choosing between full-bridge or half-bridge configurations
for the primary side, and between full-bridge or center-tap setups for the secondary side.
Subsequently, the circuit structure undergoes scrutiny to achieve heightened efficiency.
Factors such as whether to employ diodes or MOSFETs on the secondary side, and deter-
mining the optimal number of switches to be paralleled on both sides, are carefully analyzed
to mitigate losses arising from switch operations.
Furthermore, attention is directed towards magnetic design considerations. Strategies
such as implementing interleaving turns for the primary and secondary sides of the trans-
former are explored to minimize losses. This approach aims to enhance the efficiency and
overall performance of the transformer, contributing to the overall efficacy of the DC-DC
converter design.
In the final phase, two PCB layouts will be devised–one with an interleaving structure
and the other without. The experimental results will be compared with the simulation and
theoretical calculations. This comparative analysis will facilitate the determination of the
most suitable topology for the given application.
Keywords: battery charging; state-of-charge (SOC); full-bridge DC-DC converter; in-
terleaving; center-tap; MOSFETs parallel

Introduction
1.1
Background
In response to the depletion of fossil fuels and mounting environmental concerns, electric
vehicles (EVs) have emerged as a pivotal component in the expanding market of green energy
technologies [7]. Notably, research has highlighted a negative correlation between EV sales
and carbon emissions within certain contexts, particularly in the United States market [8].
Case studies focusing on Portugal reveal a compelling trend, with a substantial increase in
EV adoption resulting in a notable 39% reduction in EV-specific CO2 emissions between
2020 and 2030 [9].
Batteries serve as the primary power source for the entire EV system, underpinning
its reliability and performance. Among the various battery configurations, parallel battery
packs remain a cornerstone in EV design. Their enduring prevalence is attributed to several
advantages, including high energy density, rapid charging capabilities, and enhanced safety
features. These attributes not only contribute to the extended range of EVs but also ensure
their efficiency, positioning them favorably against traditional fuel-powered vehicles.
Despite advancements in battery technology, EVs faced initial market reluctance due
to various practical challenges, including high costs and inadequate charging infrastructure.
A prominent limitation of EVs is their extended charging duration, which contrasts starkly
with the rapid refueling of traditional fuel-powered vehicles. Fast charging technology has
emerged as a crucial solution to mitigate this weakness, offering the potential to significantly
reduce charging times and enhance the practicality and convenience of EV ownership.

1.2
Previous Research
Recent research has predominantly emphasized thermal management [10, 11, 12], cur-
rent equalization (balancing)[13, 14, 15], and meeting the demands of grid control[16, 17],
among other areas. Multiple studies have addressed these challenges, contributing valuable
insights into potential solutions. For instance, [18] delves into the advantages of powertrain
control in EVs to enhance battery thermal management. Imbalance issues during fast charg-
ing are investigated in [19], proposing strategies for rapid equalization. Additionally, [20]
introduces a comprehensive AI framework for managing distributed energy sources in EVs,
encompassing demand profiling and forecasting, among other functionalities.
However, further optimization of the converter design remains crucial, with particular
attention required for magnetic design and switch selection [21, 22, 23, 24]. Reducing power
loss can mitigate converter heating issues while simultaneously enhancing efficiency and
power density. This thesis concentrates on in-depth research and analysis in this area to
address these critical aspects.
1.2.2
Converter & Battery Packs
Battery packs have diverse applications across numerous domains, ranging from portable
electronics to electric vehicles, providing portable and reliable power sources. Often, battery
packs need to generate high currents, prompting the configuration of batteries in parallel
to address this requirement. However, ensuring balanced current distribution among paral-
lel batteries poses challenges. The configuration intricacies of paralleled battery packs are
elucidated in detail in Figure 2.

From Figure 2, it is evident that the output of the DC-DC converter topology must
directly connect to the ’DC-link(+)’ and ’DC-link(-)’. As mentioned previously, the primary
function of the DC-DC converter is to convert high voltage to low voltage while ensuring
voltage stability.
Additionally, the output voltage of the converter can be controlled by
adjusting the duty cycle.
Moreover, to prevent individual batteries from exceeding a maximum voltage during
charging and allow weaker batteries to fully charge, thereby restoring balance to the entire
pack [25], the output voltage of the converter must also be controlled. This control can be
achieved by modifying the duty cycle of the converter.

topologies can be utilized for the DC-DC converter: the half-bridge structure and the full-

The power grid encompasses a range of power stations, each potentially delivering vary-
ing output voltages, typically at high levels. Consequently, the implementation of a DC-DC
converter becomes imperative to regulate and adapt the incoming voltage to suit the require-
ments of the system.

In essence, this thesis aims to design an optimal DC-DC converter by meticulously
addressing various aspects such as transformer design, inductor design, and MOSFET se-
lection to minimize total losses. To achieve this goal, each topology will undergo detailed
individual analysis. Additionally, the effectiveness of employing an interleaving structure for
the transformer will be examined to compare its efficiency with that of a non-interleaving
structure. This necessitates the design of PCB layouts for both structures, followed by per-
formance evaluation. Subsequently, the most suitable structure will be determined based on
the research findings. If time permits, different control methods will also be explored and
analyzed.

3.1.2
Design of the Winding and Copper Loss
In light of the decisions made in Section 2.3.4 regarding the turns ratio (16) for the full-
bridge buck DC-DC converter and the selection of the N87 ELP 43/10/28 core as discussed
in Section 3.1, considerations for the PCB design are crucial. Typically, PCB boards come
in configurations with 2, 4, or 6 layers. Hence, the number of turns on each layer should
ideally align with these layer counts: 16 turns for a single layer, 8 turns for two layers, or 4
turns for four layers.
However, cramming 16 or even 8 turns into a single layer would necessitate narrow
turns, potentially leading to higher current density within the copper traces. This height-
ened current density could elevate the temperature along the conduction path, potentially
compromising performance. Furthermore, packing multiple turns into a single layer would
increase the magnetomotive force (MMF) ratio horizontally, consequently raising the AC
resistance of the winding and exacerbating winding losses, which, in turn, could diminish
efficiency.
After careful deliberation, it has been determined that each layer will accommodate 4
turns, totaling 16 turns overall. Consequently, to accommodate these turns, the primary side
will require 4 layers. In traditional magnetic design, with a turns ratio of 16, the secondary
side necessitates only one turn. A visual representation of the core and winding design is

6
Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
Compared to the half-bridge buck DC-DC converter, the full-bridge buck DC-DC con-
verter is better suited for handling higher power requirements. By reducing the current
passing through the primary side, it mitigates stress on the MOSFET. Optimal selection of
the turns ratio ensures a more balanced duty cycle between the primary and secondary sides,
thereby enhancing efficiency, especially under higher secondary side currents. Additionally,
selecting a core material compatible with the frequency reduces core losses; for instance, at
a frequency of 100 kHz, a flux density of approximately 150 mT yields improved efficiency.
In contrast to the non-interleaving structure, the interleaving structure offers lower
AC resistance, thereby reducing copper losses and enhancing efficiency.
For high-power
applications, careful consideration of MOSFET losses is essential, as these constitute the
primary source of power dissipation. Designing the PCB as a single integrated board, rather
than employing external boards, is preferable, particularly for comparative experiments, as
external boards introduce additional resistance and noise.
6.2
Future Work
6.2.1
Simulation
While ’PLECS’ provides ideal simulations, real-world scenarios involve parasitic param-
eters inherent to all components, significantly influencing the board’s operational behavior.
In future simulations, we intend to employ software such as ’LTspice’ and ’Pspice,’ which fa-
cilitate the inclusion of parasitic parameters for various components. This approach ensures
more accurate modeling, reflecting the true performance of the circuit in practical conditions.
6.2.2
PCB Layout
The initial PCB prototype encountered several issues, including lengthy conduction
paths between components, the need for additional external boards to accommodate com-
plex winding layouts, and suboptimal component placement. Additionally, the absence of
measuring points to safely access signals in the MOSFETs posed a safety concern, espe-
cially given the high input voltage. Therefore, incorporating measuring points to enhance
experimental safety is imperative. Consequently, a revised version of the PCB layout will be
developed to effectively address these challenges.
6.2.3
Control
The project currently lacks ZVS or zero current switching (ZCS) for both primary
and secondary sides, resulting in switching losses in the MOSFETs.
Consequently, the
frequency range for the project is constrained. Future iterations will explore alternative
control methods to enable ZVS and ZCS, thereby allowing for higher operating frequencies.
Page 54 of 60
==PAGE:65==
6.2
Future Work
This advancement not only facilitates size reduction in the core but also enhances power
density.
6.2.4
Experiment
The project’s output current requirement is 40 A, yet the current shunt utilized in the
experiment is limited to handling only 20 A. In subsequent iterations, a new current shunt
capable of withstanding 200 A will be employed to meet the project’s demands adequately.
Moreover, to enhance safety measures, an isolated cover will be implemented to prevent
direct exposure to high voltage.
