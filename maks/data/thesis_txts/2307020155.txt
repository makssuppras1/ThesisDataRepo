Abstract
The increased complexity and wide-spreading of electric power systems challenges the
monitoring and control operation of conventional energy management systems.
Thus,
accurate and fast system state estimation is of growing importance, and have led to an
increased focus on real-time state estimation based on synchrophasor measurements from
phasor measurement units (PMUs).
However, the high measurement sampling rate of
PMUs may complicate the process of correctly detecting and distinguishing power system
dynamics from bad data. In case of fast network dynamics initiated from sudden topolog-
ical changes, this process is further complicated. Modernized energy management systems
therefore call for novel methods that treats this area adequately, in order to enhance the
robustness of PMU-based real-time state estimators.
This thesis introduces a novel method that distinguishes topological errors from bad
data in PMU-based linear power system state estimators. The method is implemented
in a novel pre-estimation bad data and fast dynamic algorithm that ﬁlter erroneous mea-
surements and handles unpredictable topological changes. Furthermore, a method based
on predicted normalized branch current residuals for topological error detection during
system dynamic conditions is investigated.
The pre-estimation algorithm has been tested against various everyday present bad
data events and, as a ﬁrst, against sudden dynamic changes in power system operating
point. It is concluded that the pre-estimation bad data and fast dynamic algorithm ef-
ﬁciently reduces system impact during bad data and high-system-inﬂuencing branch trip
events. However, further research has to be conducted to resolve ﬁlter tuning issues dur-
ing light branch load tripping events. Furthermore, it is concluded that this method is
integratable with topology error identiﬁcation based on the largest normalized branch cur-
rent residuals. It is to the author’s believe, that the developed methods will improve the
robustness of phasor measurement unit based state estimators.

Resumé
Kompleksiteten og udbredelsen af moderne elsystemer besværliggør driften af konven-
tionelle energistyringssystemer. Af denne årsag er der øget fokus på vigtigheden af hurtig
og præcis tilstandsestimering i realtids systemer baseret på synchrophasor målinger fra
phasor measurement units (PMUs). Dog kan den hurtige PMU målingsfrekvens give an-
ledning til komplikationer når dynamiske systempåvirkninger og fejlfyldt data skal iden-
tiﬁseres og skelnes fra hinanden. I tilfældet af at dynamisk systempåvirkninger fremkom-
mer på bagrund af en topologiændring intensiveres denne komplikation yderligere. For
at kunne modernisere energistyringssystemer, er man derfor nødsaget til at udvikle nye
metoder der behandler dette område og forbedrer pålideligheden af PMU-baserede realtids
tilstandsestimatore.
Denne afhandling introducerer en ny metode til at skelne topologifejl fra fejlfyldt
data in PMU-baserede linære tilstandsestimatore. Metoden er implementeret i en pre-
estimerings algoritme der frasorterer fejlagtige målinger og håndterer uforudsete topolo-
giske ændringer. Endvidere undersøges en metode der, ved brug af forudsagte normalis-
erede linjestrøms residualer, kan identiﬁcere topologifejl i forbindelse med dynamiske sys-
tempåvirkninger.
Den designede algoritme er blevet testet i forskellige realiske fejlfyldt data scenarier,
og som noget helt nyt, er algoritmen blevet testet mod pludselige ændringer i elsystemets
arbejdspunkt.
Det kan konkluderes, at den implementerede pre-estimerings algoritme
eﬀektivt reducerer systemindvirkning i forbindelse med scenarier af fejlfyldt måledata
og højbelastnings linjenedbrud. Yderligere forskning skal dog gennemføres for a løse de
ﬁlter tuning problemer der medfører ukorrekt tilstandsestimering idet systemet påvirkes
af et lavbelastnings linjenedbrud. Desuden kan det konkluderes, at den implementerede
algoritme kan integreres med en topologiprocessor, der lokaliserer positionen af topologifejl
ved brug af normaliserede linjestrøms residualer.
Det er af forfatterens opfattelse, at
de udviklede metoder kan forbedre robustheden af phasor measurement unit baserede
tilstandsestimatorer.

Chapter 1
Background
Conventional state estimation (SE) is formulated as a weighted least squares (WLS) prob-
lem and includes data from remote terminal units (RTUs) and supervisory control and
data acquisition (SCADA) systems. RTUs provide information on conventional AC sys-
tem measurements, such as voltage-, and current magnitudes and active-, and reactive
powers. The lack of phase angle data yields a non-linear, and thus computationally re-
quiring, relation to the system state. Applying the WLS method on acquired data gives a
ﬁrst-approximation of the state. The actual state is estimated through an iterative post-
analysis check, where potentially corrupted measurements and system disturbances are
ﬁltered away [1]. In conventional energy management systems (EMS), system parameters
are measured every every four seconds, and states estimated every two or three minutes
[2].
The increased complexity and wide-spreading of electric power systems are challenging
conventional energy management systems. Ensuring a stable and secure operation of the
future electric power system has motivated for research in improving the accuracy and rate
of state estimators. As a result, the development of phasor measurement unit (PMU) tech-
nology, has opened the possibility for real-time state estimation (RTSE). PMUs measure
synchrophasors of voltage and current amplitudes and phase angles with a much higher
sampling rate than conventional RTUs (i.e.
30 samples/second [3]), and can thereby
provide continuous streams of valuable node-to-node information. Moreover, PMU mea-
surements are synchronized with the global positioning system (GPS), which enables for
wide-area measurement system (WAMS) application. This could potentionally solve the
wide-spreading challenges embedded in present electric power system state estimators.
However, implementing the phasor measurement unit technology is not straightforward,
and many precautions have to be taken. As an example, state estimates may appear er-
roneous when the sampling rate of phasor measurements increases to levels so high that
present bad data and power system dynamics pass unﬁltered through the estimator.
The main purpose of this thesis is to design and implement a novel ﬁltering process
that distinguishes topological errors from bad data in PMU-based linear power system
state estimators. As an expansion, a method based on predicted normalized branch cur-
rent residuals are implemented for topological error detection during dynamic conditions.

The created pre-estimation bad data and fast dynamic algorithm is implemented in
MATLAB and tested on a novel wide-area test platform created by members of the Secure
Operation of Sustainable Power Systems (SOSPO) R&D project. Assumptions of ideal
PMU operation conditions and so forth have been deﬁned by the SOSPO group. The
algorithm is tested on the Cigré Nordic32 power system, where everyday present bad data
is simulated to replicate results from [4].
The thesis contributes to further research by simulating and analyzing performance of the
created pre-estimation ﬁlter under dynamic system conditions, which, to the best of the
authors’ knowledge, have not been done before.
The thesis is categorized into three chapters;
• Chapter 2 describes the theoretical principles of the designed real-time state estima-
tor and illustrates with ﬂowcharts how the algorithm is implemented in MATLAB.
The performance of the pre-estimation ﬁlter is analyzed from bad data and fast
dynamic simulations in the last part of the chapter.
• Chapter 3 introduces the importance of topology processing and tests the imple-
mented topology error identiﬁcation method during two branch trip events.
• Chapter 4 provides conclusions and perspectives on future research.
Notice, that theory described in section 2.2.1, 2.2.7 and 2.2.8, extends a previous
project, Implementation of methods for wide-area monitoring in real-time of power systems
[5]. The project was prepared as part of the SOSPO R&D project and completed in the
fall 2015.

Chapter 2
Part 1
.
The Pre-Estimation Bad Data &
Fast Dynamic Algorithm
This chapter ﬁrst addresses the theoretically principles of the designed pre-estimation
bad data (BD) and fast dynamic (FD) algorithm. Then two diﬀerent PMU measurement
approaches are described and implemented, case scenarios established and state estimation
results displayed. Next, two methods for topology error identiﬁcation are tested. Finally,
interesting ﬁndings are discussed and the chapter is concluded.
2.1
Introduction
The objective of the ﬁrst part of this project is to design and implement a linear dynamic
pre-estimation algorithm that ﬁlter bad data (BD) and handles fast dynamics (FD) in
phasor measurement unit (PMU)-based power systems. The pre-estimation BD and FD
algorithm is designed around a predictor-corrector process introduced in discrete kalman
ﬁlter (DKF) theory and analyzes innovations of predicted and actual PMU measurements
to identify anomalies and take proper actions before estimating the next state.
The implemented algorithm is simulated on the Cigré Nordic32 grid, and has PMU devices
installed at all nodes and branches. Real-time PMU data streams are broadcasted from
playbacks of time-domain simulations using the SOSPO wide-area test platform.
The performance and robustness of the implemented real-time state estimation (RTSE)

2.2.3
The Bad Data & Fast Dynamic Process
In order for real-time state estimators to provide reliable and accurate outputs, the input
synchrophasors measured by the PMU devices need to be correct. In real life power sys-
tems PMU devices can be aﬀected by electromagnetic interferences, while communication
systems may encounter telecommunication failures. Hence, the state estimator can receive
misleading and erroneous data, zk. In case zk contains synchrophasors with values that
lie outside acceptable boundary levels of metering and communication system accuracies,
measurement ﬁltering and process tuning is conducted. In this report, a state is consid-
ered reliable when pre-estimation ﬁltering of erroneous input measurements and tuning of
noise covariances during fast dynamics lead to improvements in the estimated state.
The bad data (BD) and fast dynamic (FD) process explained in this section is inspired by
[4], but also includes research development expansions created in this thesis. It is imple-
mented between the prediction and estimation part of the DKF algorithm and is used to
enhance robustness of the linear real-time state estimator.
The BD and FD process consists of four parts and is described in the following sections.
Subsequently, a ﬂowchart is created to illustrate and summarize the entire pre-estimation
BD and FD algorithm. For a quick overview, the process parts are listed below.

measurements from adjacent buses record anomalies at the same time-step, these are
considered as due to a fast dynamic somewhere in the grid. Otherwise, if an anomaly is
solely detected from one PMU device, the anomaly is identiﬁed as bad data.

2.2.7
Data Scrambler
In order to manipulate measurements from a metering device a data scrambler is used. A
data scrambler adds small normal distributed deviations to the real and imaginary parts
of each measurement. The manipulated voltage and current phasors are obtained in (2.49)
and (2.50).
Vscrambled = Re(Vmeas) + εa + jIm(Vmeas) + εb
(2.49)
Iscramble = Re(Imeas) + εc + jIm(Imeas) + εd
(2.50)
Where εa,b,c,d represents random normal distributed noise generated by the data scrambler.
It is important to emphasize, that the data scrambler generates a new number on every
run. The data scrambler is created with a ﬁxed standard deviation, σ, of,
σ = 0.01/3
This value is chosen to simulate real life PMU measurement noise, [16], and infers a 99.7 %
probability that the real and imaginary parts of synchrophasors are found within ±0.01pu
of their original value. With a ﬁxed σ, measurements from all metering devices are treated
equally.

to the precision of the voltmeter. While phase angle, δ, errors are correlated to time mea-
surements. The phasors are measured in polar coordinates and converted to rectangular
form before being processed by the state estimation algorithm. The SOSPO simulation
platform transmits per-unit normalized snapshot measurements represented in rectangu-
lar coordinates. Thus, the data scrambler is implemented on these measures, with the
assumption that,
ε(|V |) = ε(δ)

2.3
Implementation
The designed algorithm is implemented in MATLAB R2015b. The created code is part of
a larger program and is only able to run when connected to the SOSPO snapshot server.
Description of the SOSPO software platform is not within the scope of this thesis. Though,
a former project [5], describes its operation in broad terms.
The overall implementation methodology is displayed in Figure 2.6.
Figure 2.6: Implementation Methodology
Figure 2.6 deﬁnes the four steps that are handled during a simulation. In the conﬁgu-
ration step, connection to the SOSPO software platform is created and oﬄine data from
Power Transmission System Planning Software (PSS/E) simulations accessed. Network
data is extracted and transmitted to the snapshot server, from where system parameters
and real-time data streams of snapshots are broadcasted to the initialization step. Initial
nodal and branch admittance matrices are deﬁned, and parameters for ﬁrst cycle compu-
tations are deﬁned. Snapshots of bus voltage phasors and branch current phasors then
enter the real-time state estimation (RTSE) step, where admittance matrices are validated
and Gaussian noise added. The RTSE step introduces the pre-estimation BD and FD al-
gorithm, that in ongoing cycles are called until the last snapshot is broadcasted from the
20
==PAGE:37==
snapshot server. Finally, the performance of the state estimator can be analyzed from
various plots and output ﬁles.
In this project, two diﬀerent algorithm implementation approaches are investigated.
Implementation A solely considers synchrophasor measurements from PMUs installed at
buses, while implementation B treats measurements from PMUs placed at buses and
branches. In implementation A, the nodal admittance matrix is deﬁned, while implemen-
tation B operates with the branch admittance matrices.
The objective of implementation A is to replicate bad data events described in [4], and to
test the performance of the pre-estimation algorithm under dynamic system conditions.
Implementation B is simulated in the fast dynamic scenarios, and compared to implemen-
tation A to determine the eﬀect of adding more measurement points into the network. The
primary objective of implementation B, though, is to identify topology errors as described
in chapter 3.

□bad_data_assessment.m

□bad_data_assessment_db_init.m

• admittance_matrix_from_database()
The nodal admittance matrix, and branch from- and to admittance matrices
are deﬁned from created incidence matrices and initial network database pa-
rameters. The resulting nodal admittance matrix is further used to determine
the bus adjacency matrix.
□bad_data_assessment_assess.m
Function that takes PMU snapshots, data from the init function and the number
of received snapshots as input. The function handles numerous function calls which
collectively deﬁne the pre-estimation BD and FD algorithm. The combination of
function calls depends on which of the implementation approaches that are inves-
tigated. For oﬄine assessment of the results, the length of the simulation and the
examined network buses are deﬁned. Furthermore, this function delivers an output
text ﬁle that describes simulation events and identiﬁed anomalies.
• check_admittance_matrix()
This function is called every cycle and takes as input original PMU data. It
calculates the expected current ﬂow injections from the admittance matrix and
bus voltage relation and compares this to the actual bus or branch current ﬂows
retrieved from the snapshot server. The function is able to handle admittance
matrix checks for both implementation approaches, and will output a ’false’ if
any of the input admittance matrices is detected as non-valid. Moreover, it
outputs the index of the conﬂicting bus or branch.
• admittance_matrix_from_snapshot()
Only if an admittance matrix is deemed incorrect, this function is called. It
calculates new admittance matrices from the network model parameters deﬁned
in the init function and the received non-manipulated PMU data. As output,

the function submits updated nodal-, branch from-, and branch to admittance
matrices.
• data_scrambler_A()
The data scrambler created for algorithm implementation A holds PMU data
inputs of bus voltages and line to-, line from-, transformer to-, and transformer
from currents. The function is called every snapshot (when included in the
simulation). In order to ﬁt the nodal environment of implementation A, branch
currents are represented as bus current injections, using line- and transformer
current incidence matrices. Real and imaginary parts of bus voltages and cur-
rents are scrambled with a ﬁxed standard deviation deﬁned in the init function.
The output of data scrambler A, is an array of scrambled bus voltage phasors
and scrambled bus current injection phasors.
• data_scrambler_B()
The data scrambler B function manipulates PMU input data of bus voltages
and line to-, line from-, transformer to-, and transformer from currents. The

scrambled branch to and branch from currents.

In order to reproduce test scenario simulations from [4], this function takes the
manipulated data deﬁned in data scrambler A as input. Various disturbance
events are deﬁned by manually injecting bad data into investigated measure-

phasors are formed as output.
• topology_error_approaches()
This function examines two diﬀerent approaches for topology error identiﬁca-
tion. The function takes scrambled data as input and attempts to ﬁnd a relation
between present measurements, a-priori predicted measurements, the incorrect
bus admittance matrix and the measurement sensitivity of PMU devices. The
function is created to run with implementation A and without the check and
update admittance matrix functions. The performance of the topology error
approaches are continuously analyzed from the output command window, hence
the calculations in this function does not aﬀect any simulation parameters.
• kalman_new_state_A()
Applying implementation method A, yields state estimation based on scram-
bled bus measurements. In this function, network parameters, scrambled data,
the nodal admittance matrix, ﬁrst cycle initializations and the number of re-

==PAGE:42==
ceived snapshots are taken as input. Through the predictor-corrector nature of
the dicrete kalman ﬁlter, a variety of parameters are predicted, model parame-
ters tuned and improved state estimates determined, every cycle. The function
includes a bad data and fast dynamic processor that enables handling of mea-
surement anomalies. The main focus of kalman new state A is to correctly
detect bad data and fast dynamics, apply countermeasures and tune covariance

• kalman_new_state_B()
Implementation B assumes that PMU devices are installed in buses and in both
ends of the branches. This yields a state estimator based on more phasor mea-

the branch admittance matrices, ﬁrst cycle initializations, network parameters
and the number of received snapshots are taken as input. The process of the

This function is used to create plots of real and imaginary parts of original,

This function calculates the TVE of every scrambled and estimated bus voltage
phasor with respect to original measures. The ﬁrst TVEs are calculated when
the estimator has tuned its parameters, and is then stored in data matrices
every cycle. At the end of simulation, the nodal mean and standard deviation
of all snapshot TVEs are calculated and plotted in an errorbar plot.
The
resulting errorbar plot contains nodal mean and ± standard deviations for both
scrambled and estimated bus voltages in order to determine the performance
of the pre-estimation bad data and fast dynamic algorithm.

2.5
Topology Error Identiﬁcation
As shown in the simulated scenarios in section 2.4, the implemented pre-estimation bad
data and fast dynamic algorithm does well in estimating the system state. However, the
algorithm relies heavily on perfect knowledge of the true nodal admittance matrix, Ybus.
As described in section 2.2.5, Ybus is checked for validity every time-step before scrambling
data and entering the state estimation (SE) part of the algorithm. The check, and possi-
ble update of Ybus, is formed around from- and to branch current measurements extracted
from the incoming snapshots. From the state estimator point of view this data is com-
pletely unknown, since it solely operates with bus current injections. If a branch trips, its
current ﬂow turns zero, and is distributed along adjacent branches. If the tripped branch
carries only a small current it might be diﬃcult to identify the location of such topology
error when using a node-based SE algorithm.
Still, two possible approaches for topology error identiﬁcation based on non-normalized
residuals are designed and implemented in function: topology_error_approaches().
The ﬁrst approach utilizes the a-posteriori state estimate, ˆxk, and a probabilistic ±4σ, to
create upper and lower limits of |Y | · |Vest| = |Iest|. The limits are then compared to the
scrambled bus current injections, |Iscramble|. If a measurement, |Iscramble|, lays outside the
deﬁned intervals, the system is assumed to be aﬀected by a topology error.
The second approach is based on the assumption that the SE algorithm estimates the
next state with very high accuracy. Therefore, in normal operation, (2.59) is expected.
Iscrambled −Ybus · Vestimated ≊0 ± εmax
(2.59)
The maximal expected error,
εmax = 4σ + 1i · 4σ
is deﬁned from knowledge on normal distribution probabilities, yielding a 0.006334% prob-
ability, that εmax is exceeded during normal operation.
Hence, if exceeded, (2.60), a
topology error must be inﬂicting the system.
Iscrambled −Ybus · Vestimated > εmax
(2.60)
Both approaches are simulated with algorithm implementation A on line trip scenarios
B and C. Unfortunately the non-normalized residuals approaches are unable to uniquely
identify the actual tripped line in either cases. Simply due to the eﬀect of current ﬂow
changes in multiple branches around the aﬀected branch.
Ideally the pre-estimation BD and FD algorithm is able to identify the location of sud-
den topology errors in real-time, and make corrections to the system admittance matrices.
In the second part of the report, this topic is investigated and research on topology error
detection using normalized residuals is conducted.

It is further determined that tuning of c within an interval of c = [0.001 : 0.050], does not
change the state estimation results.
2.7
Part 1 Conclusion
In this chapter, a novel PMU-based real-time state estimation algorithm is designed and
tested against various everyday present bad data and branch tripping events. The al-
gorithm is built around a discrete kalman ﬁlter (DKF) and detects potential anomalies
by analyzing innovations of predicted and actual PMU measurements.
Anomalies are
then distinguished as erroneous measurements or as due to system dynamics. Bad data
is replaced by pseudo-measurements, and hence model conﬁdence in data from aﬀected
metering devices decreases. Anomalies identiﬁed as due to fast dynamics yields lowered
process model trust, whilst data proceed uninterrupted to the DKF estimation part.
Simulation results show that the algorithm estimates the system state with an average
oﬀset of 0.02% during steady state operation. Through manually injected non-temporary
bad data events, the algorithm generally provides robust state estimation results. Though,
it is concluded that phase drifting events can reduce state estimation accuracy of buses
where measurement anomalies are only partially detected. This can further infer negative
impact on state estimates of neighboring buses.
The algorithm is also tested against various branch trip events, and it is concluded that
the algorithm appears robust when applied high load branch trips. In this particular case,
an average state estimate oﬀset of approximately 0.2% is found. It is further concluded
that this oﬀset can be reduced by developing a sophisticated covariance tuning process,
that is less vulnerable to temporary and unexpected measurement innovations. Moreover,
it is concluded that the algorithm is unable to estimate reliable states during low system
inﬂuencing branch trips. The reason for this, is that the proposed measurement and pro-
cess covariance error tuning method is vulnerable to ﬁlter conﬁdence resets.
Finally, two diﬀerent algorithm implementations have enabled the analysis of state estima-
tor performance with phasor measurement unit devices deployed at various grid locations.

.
Topology Error Identiﬁcation
This chapter introduces a method for topology error identiﬁcation. First theory on the
importance of reliable topology processing in power system state estimation is described.
Next, general principles of the normalized residuals approach is introduced and it is shown
how this method can be implemented in the pre-estimation bad data and fast dynamics
algorithm created in project Part 1. In the last section of this chapter, the performance
of the topology error identiﬁcation method is evaluated from test case simulations and
conclusions are made.
3.1
Introduction
In present power systems, sudden unexpected topology changes are usually detected and
corrected in control centers by interpreting circuit breaker (CB) status information of
electrically connected nodes. If one or more breaker statuses are unknown or CBs are out
of operation, chances are, that topology errors are not detected and resolved properly. This
will inevitably cause erroneous state estimations and thus increase the risk for equipment
failure and power system blackouts. Furthermore, present topology processors operate
at slow sample rates and need to be improved to ﬁt the real-time state estimation (SE)
demands of future monitoring and control systems [16].
In project Part 2, a method for topology error detection based on the comparison of state
normalized branch ﬂow error residuals is introduced. The method is inspired by [1] and
[17] and is implemented in the PMU based real-time pre-estimation bad data and fast
dynamic algorithm described in Part 1. To evaluate the performance of the method, two
diﬀerent branch trip cases are simulated and results analyzed.

Robust topology processing is important for precise execution of power system monitoring,
control and protection actions.
In this chapter a real-time method for topology error
identiﬁcation is implemented in the pre-estimation bad data and fast dynamic algorithm.

Chapter 4
Conclusions and Perspectives
This chapter summarizes the partial conclusions from chapter two and three, and states
the major ﬁndings of this thesis. Furthermore, perspectives to real-life power systems and
interesting areas for further research are described.
This study has investigated the performance of a novel pre-estimation algorithm that
completely relies on measurements from phasor measurement unit devices. Furthermore,
a method based on predicted normalized branch current ﬂow residuals has been imple-
mented in the algorithm in order to detect and identify sudden topological changes in
real-time.
It is concluded that the novel state estimator is compatible with the real-time nature pro-
vided by data from phasor measurement units, and that state estimation results of various
bad data and fast dynamic scenarios appear robust. Although, it is concluded, that fur-
ther research has to be conducted on measurement and process noise covariance tuning,
to ensure state estimation robustness at any given power system scenario. Furthermore,
it is concluded that branch current ﬂows predicted by the pre-estimation algorithm can
be used to identify branch status errors.
The research provided in this thesis contributes to the following conclusion; Real-time
pre-estimation ﬁltering of bad data and system dynamics in phasor measurement unit
based linear state estimators brings great potential for upgrading the operation, super-
vision, protection and control of modern energy management systems.
Moreover, the
pre-estimation process has shown integratable with a method for real-time topology error
processing, which is expected to enhance the robustness of state estimators.
By considering the contributions of this thesis, several research topics with potential
to continue the work carried out in this project is identiﬁed.
Primarily, non-ﬁxed phasor measurement unit noise can be implemented to give a more
realistic view of the disturbances that interfere voltage and current phasor measuring PMU
devices in diﬀerent voltage levels of the simulated power system. Literature presented in
[16], states realistic PMU measurement error standard deviations.
Secondarily, the investigated topology error identiﬁcation method can be expanded to
60
==PAGE:77==
operate as a topology error processor, which updates the system admittance matrices as
illustrated in Figure 3.3.
Although, research in process latency has to be included to
ensure real-time operation compatibility. For improved state estimator robustness, the
re-availability of formerly tripped network branches can also be considered.
A major research project that may continue this study, is found when considering network
observability and investigating the actual implementation of phasor measurement units
in real-life power systems. Whereas this research studies ideal conditions with PMU de-
vices deployed at all buses and/or branches, this situation is unrealistic in real-life power
systems. First of all, the inclusion of PMUs will happen gradually, and thus PMUs have
to operate under conventional energy management system conditions with metering mea-
surements being transmitted every four seconds and states estimated once every two or
tree minutes [2]. As an addition, PMU devices are expensive and installation costs very
high. From an economical point of view, cost/beneﬁt ratios as well as the implementation
of PMUs to maximize observability by minimizing costs have to be considered.
As for any novel research project it is possible to draw perspectives to countless im-
portant areas of theoretical and practical development. The above mentioned perspectives
only cover a minor part of the areas that hold the potential to secure robust operation of
future energy management systems.

function [ bus_admittance_matrix, branch_from_admittance_matrix, ...

% admittance_matrix_from_database()
% Description: form sparse 'bus admittance matrix', 'branch to admittance
% matrix', and 'branch from admittance matrix', from initial database
% parameters using the incidence matrix for the complete system. Buses,
% lines and transformers hold structs of data from db_reader_raw_jdbc.
%
% Y = B^T * Yp * B + Y_shunt
%
% Y is the bus/branch_to/branch_from admittance matrix,
% B is bus/branch_to/branch_from incidence matrix
% Yp is a primitive admittance matrix with series admittances in diagonal
% Y_shunt is a diagonal matrix of accumulated shunt admittances

m_trafo = length(assessment_db.transformers_branch_index);

from_bus_entries = −1*ones(size(assessment_db.lines_bus_number_from));
% t−line incidence matrix, B_line

assessment_db.lines_branch_index]),...
[ double(assessment_db.lines_bus_to_index);

[ to_bus_entries; from_bus_entries], m_lines + m_trafo, n);

double(assessment_db.lines_branch_index),...

(assessment_db.lines_resistance +...
1i* assessment_db.lines_reactance).^−1, m_lines+m_trafo,...

m_lines+m_trafo);

line_shunts(assessment_db.lines_bus_from_index(j)) = ...
line_shunts(assessment_db.lines_bus_from_index(j)) + ...
0.5*1i*assessment_db.lines_susceptance(j);

(line_incidence_matrix)) + (transformers_primitive_admittance_matrix * ...
transformers_incidence_matrix) + branch_to_shunts;

% current from branch shunts, Y_shunt_branch_from
branch_from_shunts = sparse(double(assessment_db.lines_branch_index),...
double(assessment_db.lines_bus_from_index), ...
0.5i*assessment_db.lines_susceptance, m_lines + m_trafo, n);

to_bus_entries = −1./trafo_w2_over_w1_ratio;

transformers_incidence_matrix = ...

function [ assessment_db ] = topology_error_approaches ( assessment_db )
% Description: two topology error identification approaches.
% run using data_scrambler_1 and kalman_new_state_1 on trip cases.
no_of_buses = length( assessment_db.bus_number );
% collect scrambled data
v_scrambled = assessment_db.scrambled_data(1:no_of_buses);
i_scrambled = assessment_db.scrambled_data(no_of_buses+1:end);
%%% APPROACH #1: 'Identify faulty Y_bus from I=Y*V boundaries relation'
% A voltage−scrambling−interval is calculated from predicted state values.
% Working with absolute values; if scrambled voltage input gives I=Y*V output
% which is outside upper or lower defined boundary, flag as topology error.
==PAGE:102==
% Issue; unable to determine the exact location of line trip.
% Issue; voltage effect to small to spot errorr on 1042−1045 trip case.
v_predict = assessment_db.x_a_priori(1:no_of_buses)...
+1i*assessment_db.x_a_priori(no_of_buses+1:end);
gamma_interval = 4;
% find minimal and maximal absolute value of predicted bus voltages
V_orig_a = abs(v_predict+(−gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma...
−1i*gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma));
V_orig_b = abs(v_predict+(+gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma...
+1i*gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma));
V_orig_c = abs(v_predict+(−gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma...
+1i*gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma));
V_orig_d = abs(v_predict+(+gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma...
−1i*gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma));
V_orig_matrix = [V_orig_a V_orig_b V_orig_c V_orig_d];
min_V_orig = min(V_orig_matrix,[],2);
max_V_orig = max(V_orig_matrix,[],2);
% compute min and max predicted I_inj_from_Y, using I=Y*V
I_inj_from_Y_min = abs(assessment_db.bus_admittance_matrix)*min_V_orig;
I_inj_from_Y_max = abs(assessment_db.bus_admittance_matrix)*max_V_orig;
% define I_inj_from_Y from the actual scrambled bus voltage inputs
I_inj_from_Y = abs(assessment_db.bus_admittance_matrix)*abs(v_scrambled);
% if the actual value exceeds the min−and max boundaries, flag the
% corresponding bus as topology error (analyze data)
top_err_bus_approach1(:,1) = (((I_inj_from_Y) < (I_inj_from_Y_min)) | ...
((I_inj_from_Y) > (I_inj_from_Y_max)));
[top_err_bus_approach1 assessment_db.bus_number];
%%% Approach #2: 'Identify faulty Y_bus from I−Y*V relation'
% At normal operation conditions, the algorithm is able to estimate the
% next state with a very high accuracy. Therefore, if I−Y*V does not equal
% zero +−scrambler probability limits, the system is flagged as being
% affected by a topology error, and the admittance matrix can be updated.
v_predict = assessment_db.x_a_priori(1:no_of_buses)...
+1i*assessment_db.x_a_priori(no_of_buses+1:end);
I_sum_from_Y = i_scrambled −assessment_db.bus_admittance_matrix*v_predict;
gamma_interval = 4;
I_sum_max_error = (gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma)...
+(1i*gamma_interval*assessment_db.sigma);
% if value exceeds the maximal error boundary flag as possible top.error.
top_err_bus_approach2(:,1) = abs(I_sum_from_Y) > abs(I_sum_max_error);
[top_err_bus_approach2 assessment_db.bus_number];
end

K_e_new = meas_Jacobian_new*inv(meas_Jacobian_new'*inv(R_new)...
*meas_Jacobian_new)*meas_Jacobian_new'*inv(R_new);
% covariance of the residual
cov_r_new = (speye(length_of_meas−(2*no_of_buses)...
,length_of_meas−(2*no_of_buses))−K_e_new)*R_new;
omega_new = diag(cov_r_new);
% create identity matrices based on indexed flow direction [184x92]
M_branch_to = −1*speye(no_of_branches);
M_branch_from = +1*speye(no_of_branches);
M_branch = [ M_branch_to ; M_branch_to ; M_branch_from ; M_branch_from ];
% calculate the sensitivity matrix [274x92]
sensitivity_matrix = (diag(omega_new.^(−1/2)))...
*(speye(length_of_meas−(2*no_of_buses)...
,length_of_meas−(2*no_of_buses))−K_e_new)*M_branch;
% compute current flow errors, z_I−H_I*x_k^−
flow_errors = z(2*no_of_buses+1:end)−(H_i*assessment_db.x_a_priori);
% determine normalized residuals in terms of branch flow errors
E_norm_residuals = sensitivity_matrix'*flow_errors;
% determine the largest absolute value of normalized residuals
[E_res_max_abs,index_max]=max(abs(E_norm_residuals));
% define threshold for maximal allowable value of normalized residual
if E_res_max_abs > 10*mean(abs(E_norm_residuals));
fprintf...
('topology error detected at branch #%d (between bus %d−%d)\n',...
index_max,assessment_db.lines_bus_number_from(index_max),...
assessment_db.lines_bus_number_to(index_max))
end
% update a−priori estimate error covariance matrix
est_error_cov_matrix_a_priori
= ...
assessment_db.est_error_cov_matrix_a_posteriori + ...
assessment_db.proc_noise_cov_matrix;
end
==PAGE:114==
% create output file with list of detected FD buses
if nnz(bus_fast_dynamic)
for i = 1:length(bus_fast_dynamic)
fprintf(assessment_db.output_file , 'Fast dynamic at bus %d (%d) ...
time: %d.%d (# %d)\n' ...
, bus_fast_dynamic(i) , ...
assessment_db.bus_number(bus_fast_dynamic(i)) , ...
snapshot.seconds_of_century , snapshot.milliseconds_of_second , ...
no_of_received_snapshots);
end
end
%%% Estimation Equations %%%
% compute kalman gain, K_k [92x388], that minimizes a−posteriori estimate ...
error covariance
kalman_gain = est_error_cov_matrix_a_priori*meas_Jacobian'...
*inv(meas_Jacobian*est_error_cov_matrix_a_priori*meas_Jacobian'...
+assessment_db.meas_error_cov_matrix_3);
% calculate improved a−posteriori estimate, x_a_posteriori, of true state,
% using x_a_priori predicted at last snapshot iteration. [92x1 bus voltages]
x_a_posteriori = assessment_db.x_a_priori + ...
kalman_gain*(z−meas_Jacobian*assessment_db.x_a_priori);
% determine a−posteriori estimate error covariance matrix, P_k
assessment_db.est_error_cov_matrix_a_posteriori = ...
(speye(2*no_of_buses,2*no_of_buses)−kalman_gain*meas_Jacobian)...
*est_error_cov_matrix_a_priori;
% improved estimated state is stored as a−priori estimated state for next
% cycle, and is used to plot estimated bus voltage states
assessment_db.x_a_priori = x_a_posteriori;
% in moving_window replace diagonal of P_k−3 by P_k−1
assessment_db.moving_window(:,mod(no_of_received_snapshots−1,2)+1) = ...
diag(assessment_db.est_error_cov_matrix_a_posteriori);
end

function [ assessment_db ] = total_vector_error ( assessment_db , ...
no_of_received_snapshots , snapshot , last_snapshot )
% Description: calculate Total Vector Error (TVE) with and without state
% estimator implemented. errorbar plot of mean and std of nodal sum of TVEs
% The system has to tune its parameters, and the first TVE is therefore
% not calculated from the time that simulation start.
TVE_snap_start = 10;
%#snapshots, Scenario A, B, C.
% TVE_snap_start = 60; %#snapshots, Scenario D.
==PAGE:118==
if no_of_received_snapshots > TVE_snap_start
no_of_buses = length( assessment_db.bus_number );
% seperate scrambled bus voltages from scrambled sum of branch currents.
v_scramb = assessment_db.scrambled_data(1:no_of_buses);
% collect original bus voltages from snapshot server
v_orig = (snapshot.pmu_bus_voltages_re(assessment_db.pb_bus_index) + 1i ...
* snapshot.pmu_bus_voltages_im(assessment_db.pb_bus_index)).';
% collect estimated bus voltages from state estimator
v_est = assessment_db.x_a_priori(1:no_of_buses)...
+1i*assessment_db.x_a_priori(no_of_buses+1:end);
% calculate TVE of each snapshot with and without state estimator algorithm
% 'old' = without, 'new' = with (state estimator)
TVE_scr = sqrt(((real(v_scramb)−real(v_orig)).^2 + ...
(imag(v_scramb)−imag(v_orig)).^2)./ ...
((real(v_orig).^2)+(imag(v_orig).^2)))*100;
TVE_est = sqrt(((real(v_est)−real(v_orig)).^2 + ...
(imag(v_est)−imag(v_orig)).^2)./ ...
((real(v_orig).^2)+(imag(v_orig).^2)))*100;
% store all TVEs
assessment_db.TVE_snapshot_scr(:,no_of_received_snapshots−TVE_snap_start) ...
= TVE_scr;
assessment_db.TVE_snapshot_est(:,no_of_received_snapshots−TVE_snap_start) ...
= TVE_est;
% when last_snapshot is obtained, errorbar plot TVE (mean and +−std)
if no_of_received_snapshots == last_snapshot
% calculate old and new mean and standard deviation of each bus TVE
TVE_est_mean = mean(assessment_db.TVE_snapshot_est,2);
TVE_scr_mean = mean(assessment_db.TVE_snapshot_scr,2);
TVE_new_std
= std(assessment_db.TVE_snapshot_est,1,2);
TVE_old_std
= std(assessment_db.TVE_snapshot_scr,1,2);
figure()
errorbar(TVE_est_mean,TVE_new_std,'rx')
set(gca,'fontsize',22)
hold on
errorbar(TVE_scr_mean,TVE_old_std,'bo')
set(gca,'XLim',[1 length(TVE_est_mean)]);
set(gca,'XTick',[1:5:length(TVE_est_mean)] );
set(gca,'XTickLabel',...
[assessment_db.bus_number(1:5:length(TVE_est_mean))']);
legend('TVE_{estimated}','TVE_{scrambled}');set(gca,'fontsize',26);
xlabel('Network Bus (every 5th)');set(gca,'fontsize',22);
ylabel('TVE [%]');set(gca,'fontsize',22);
end
end
