Project definition and learning objectives 
Up-Scaling of Organic Solar Cells to a Full Roll-To-Roll Processing 
With a world in need of a green energy solution the distribution of such technology and known-how is 
important to implement a change in the where the energy comes form. 
The field of organic solar cells has the main focus on the efficiency, PCE, in the lab-scale solar cells 
fabricated through non-scalable methods as spin-coating. 
Most of these champion systems ultimately fail when are fabricated using scalable deposition techniques in 
a roll-to-roll platform. In order to achieve a competitive commercialization of the technology, it is a 
paramount task to reduce the scalability lag between lab scale and large scale. 
The starting point is one of the last non-fullerene organic solar cells developed and optimized by our group 
in DTU Energy in a roll-coater platform.  
This Master thesis project has two main objectives: 
1) The optimization of a non-fullerene OPV based system using green solvents by slot-die coating in a 
roll platform and  
2) Transfer the optimized parameters to a to a full roll-to-roll fabrication of larger area devices. 
During the project, the following will be taken into account: 
 
What is an Organic Solar Cell and how is its basic operation? 
 
Previously fabrication conditions and parameters as coating temperature, ink flow, donor –
acceptor ratio, hole transport layer. 
 
Testing of the modules in simulated outdoor conditions. 
The upscaling project are often learning by doing, so on-going considerations of testing methods, 
parameters and materials will be conducted.  
Learning objectives 
1. Describe the constituent parts of organic photovoltaic solar cells (OPVs) and their working 
principles. 
2. Explain the difference between inverted and normal configuration OPVs and their importance for 
stability and operational lifetime. 
3. Explain the electrical, optical and structural properties of the different parts of an OPV and how to 
measure their properties. 
4. Relate performance measurements of an OPV device to the underlying material properties. 
5. Understand, explain and try to solve the challenges related to the upscaling of OPVs. 
6. Analyse the optoelectronic characteristics of several OPVs when exposed to simulated climatic 
conditions. 
7. Formulate criteria for choosing materials for particular applications or locations. 
8. Learn to describe the findings in a scientific report.

Abstract 
 
The new generation is inheriting a world with a high-energy consumption and fossil fuel are phased out. So 
more green energy solutions have to be implemented. Solar cells has been a part of the green solution for 
many years, but the third generation, the organic solar cell, is showing great potential with a cheaper and 
easier production. Many developments of organic solar cell with high PCE, Jsc, Voc and FF have been 
fabricated in the laboratory but are not suitable for upscaling.  
This report focus on finding a recipe which is green and can be used to upscale from production on a mini 
roll coater to a roll-to-roll (R2R) process. The experiment was carried out with the slot-die coating and 
inverted geometry. Experiment with HTL layers flow rate “thickness” showed that 1.25 mL/min was both 
thin and in contact with the electrode. Besides the flow rate the temperature was also fixed at 60 C. The 
experiment also showed potential for encapsulation in the PET substrate instead of Thermo glass. With a 
fixed point on the HTL the green solvent became the focus with o-xylene as the main interest, but 1,3,5-
trimethylbenzene was also tried with no useful results as the material was not properly dissolved. The o-
xylene solvent was tested at different temperatures. The 80 C showed great potential in both the JV curve 
and EQE, however the distribution of all the 80 C was wide with different values. The stability test showed 
the 70 C had the most promising potential. Nevertheless, more data was needed before a more 
representative decision could be made. The ratio experiment also showed great potential as the curve 
changed providing a good starting point for future work. The reports results has potential for upscaling as 
all the experiments is produces R2R friendly in open air and has a PCE at around 3 %.

beslutning kunne blive taget. Forholds eksperimentet viste også stor potentiale da begge kurver ændrede 
sig, hvilket gav et godt start punkt for fremtidig arbejde. Rapportens resultater har potentiale for 
opskalering da alle eksperimenter kan produceres R2R venligt i åben luft og har en PCE på omkring 3 %.

1. Introduction 
A new generation is growing up with an awareness of the planet they are going to inherit. The global 
warming debate is somewhat caused by the use of fossil fuels as the main energy consumption. Richard E. 
Smalley addressed the problem in 2004 with his “The terawatt challenge”. He states that the problem at 
hand is for the future generation to solve and that they must be inspired. The last generation build up the 
society with and increasing need of energy, but what when the storage of fossil fuels are gone? (1) 
In the 1970’s there was energy crises due to a shortage of oil and many countries had restrictions like in 
Denmark with no cars on Sundays. Today the demand of energy is still increasing and the amount of 
available fossil fuels decreasing; it places the urgent need for a sustainable solution. In 2019 Denmark had 
an energy consumption of 1241 PJ (PetaJoule), whereas only 261 PJ was from sustainable energy 
production such as wind, however this was the highest amount ever. (2) 
With a green mind-set on energy use, there are several options of renewable energy such as solar, 
geothermal, wind, biomass and hydropower.(3) The sun is a huge energy source lasting millions of years to 
come. A way to extract the energy from the sun is using photovoltaic solar cells.  
Photovoltaic solar cells has been around for a long time and developed over time from first to third 
generation. A.E.Bacquerel discovered in 1893, the photovoltaic effect witch is how voltage and electric 
current is generated in a material when exposed to light. Solar energy was not considered before the 1950’ 
where the first generation of solar cells was made. It was used during the space age with satellites and 
spacecraft as solar panels to harvest energy during their travels.(4) The first solar cell developed by Daryl 
Chapin, Calvin Fuller, and Gerald Pearson at Bell laboratories had an efficiency on 6 % and it was very 
expensive to make. (4)  
The first generation of photovoltaic solar cells have an efficiency on 15-20 %, (4) are based on silicon, have 
a high stability and are mainly known as panels seen on rooftops. However, they are rigid and expensive to 
produce.  
The second generation have an efficiency of 10-15 %, are based on amorphous silicon, CIGS and CdTe and 
therefore is mostly inorganic. The cells are flexible to some extent and less expensive than the first 
generation. The production still include vacuum processing and high temperature steps, which makes it 
difficult to reduce their price. 
The third generation photovoltaic solar cells have organic material like polymers, which makes them 
flexible, easy to fabricate from solution and cheaper in production costs (5)(4). This generation opens the

2.1 The organic solar cells structure and operation 
An organic solar cell is a photovoltaic semiconductor made from organic materials, such as polymers or 
small molecules and consist of several layers. Using the photovoltaic effect, the photos from the sun is 
absorbed and converted into a current and a voltage, which exists as electric energy.(5)(4)  
Thru the use of a semiconductor, an advantage of a less expensive process is obtained. The semiconductor 
is a blend of conductors such as metal and non-conductors like ceramics. The organic solar cell consists of 
several layers as followed: negative electrode, electrode transport layer (ETL), active layer, hole transport 
layer (HTL), positive electrode and substrate. The layer stack for an organic solar cell is shown in Figure 1 
below:

Geometry  
The design of an organic solar cell can have two different types of geometry, which is depending on the 
order of the layer stack, as shown in Figure 1, and thereby the current flow.  
The normal geometry is the one shown in Figure 2A. By flipping the geometry upside down, the inverted 
geometry is made, making the negative electrode the transparent one. The inverted geometry is shown in 
Figure 2B. The HTL and ETL has different pro and cons according to transparency, easy coating, protection 
ect. The inverted geometry has an cheaper and easier production method, better efficient stability and PCE 
(6).   
 
Figure 2. Geometry of an organic solar cell. A) normal geometry B) inverted geometry 
 
Electrodes 
The organic solar cell has two electrodes, negative and positive (front and back). The electrodes conduct 
the cell to an external circuit and needs low resistance and good conductivity. Noble metals like Ag, Au and 
Cu are often used but transition metal oxides like MoO3 along with doped HTL and ETL layers is possible. By 
choosing a high work function metal, the stability of the cell is increased (6).  A common used but scarce 
electrode is indium tin oxide (ITO), its transparency, low resistance and high optical transmission makes it 
ideal as electrode (5). Nevertheless, despite the worthy abilities indium is a scarce metal, which makes the 
search for a substitute more important.   
Transport layers, electrons and holes 
The two transport layers of the cell is an intermedia which carries either electrons (electrons transport 
layer, ETL) or holes (hole transport layer, HTL) to the right electrode. The definition of the layer depends on 
its energy position level. The holes will wander to the positive electrode and the electrodes to the negative 
one. Examples of ETLs could be ZnO and LiF, whereas HTL could be PEDOT:PSS or MoOx. (5)

Active layer 
The middle layer of the organic solar cell layer stack is the active layer, which is considered the most 
important layer in the cell. It consist of an intermedia of a donor and acceptor. The donor absorbs the light 
and allow the holes to travel to the positive electrode. Whereas the acceptor extract the electron and let it 
travel through the phase to the negative electrode. The organized network of the donor/acceptor 
streamline the transport of the charge carriers. This network or bulk heterojunction (BHJ) is widely used to 
optimized the effectivity of the cell. By introduction different ratios of the D/A and materials the absorption 
of light and thereby the exchange of excitons is effected. The D/A material is polymers or small molecules 
dissolved in a solvent.    
Substrate 
The layers of the solar cell is coated on a substrate base. Commonly used substrates are PET, or “plastics” 
and glass. Depending on the type of cell the substrates creates possibilities. A flexible substrate, like PET, is 
suitable for large scale production such as roll-to-roll (R2R). Whereas glass is more fit for laboratory scale 
production or specific application.   
The working principle 
When light in the form of photons are applied on the organic solar cell and the active layer absorbs it an 
exciton is created, which is a bound electron and electron hole. By diffusion and splitting the exciton, the 
electron can excite to an unoccupied or partially occupied orbital, in most cases from the highest occupied 
molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied orbital (LUMO). This transition creates energy. The last 
step is to collect the electrons and hole by the respectively electrode through the HTL and ETL. The 
absorbing donor material must have a range of 0.1-1.4 eV to generate the electron-hole pair. The life of the 
exciton is short and the distance of the D/A interface should be in 5-20 nm. Furthermore, loss in the form of 
trapping of charges and recombining is expected. (7) 
Often the used polymers in organic solar cells are a conjugated polymer. This is a polymer with a backbone 
there has double and single bonds there are alternating (8). The polymers has overlapping p-orbitals. 
According to the molecular orbital theory (MO) will the overlapping make two phases in the orbital: The 
low and stabilized, and high destabilized energy also called the -orbital. All this and the morphology of the 
active layer is small pieces of puzzle to find the most efficient combination of the D/A. 
The energy from the HOMO-LUMO transition corresponds to the wavelength between 100-800 nm, which 
is the UV-VIS (ultraviolet and visible) region of the electromagnetic spectrum.(9) This information can be 
used to characterize the D/A association and efficiency of the photovoltaic cell.

2.2 Characterization techniques 
To compare the performance and efficiency of the different types of solar cell it is necessary to characterize 
them. In this section some of the commonly used methods are described.  
JV measurements 
The solar cells performance are measured according to the absorption of the photons in the sun. However, 
it is not possible to control the sun. Instead a simulation of the natural illumination of a sun, defined as 
AM1.5 Global spectrum that has an has an integrated power of 1000 W/m2 (100 mW/cm2) is used for the 
purpose.   
By placing the cell under the solar simulator and applying a voltage data points for the current and voltage 
is plotted and thereby measured. The type of graph is called an IV curve and illustrated the work of the cell 
by its photovoltaic parameters such as current, voltage, fill factor and power conversion efficiency. By 
dividing the current with the area, the JV curve for current density and voltage is plotted. The JV curve is a 
safer choice for comparison of solar cells as the data are based on the area, making the differences more 
visible.

EQE and transmittance measurements  
The incident to photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) or Quantum efficiency (QE) is the efficiency of the 
devices ability to convert the incident light to electrical energy at a given wavelength.  
There are two types of QE the internal (IQE), which is the ratio between the collected carriers and all the 
absorbed photos, and external (EQE), the ratio of collected carriers and all the incident photons. (5)  
The EQE and transmittance is measured using a monochrometer, QEX10 system (PV Measurements Inc.) 
from 300 nm to 900 nm (5 nm step size) calibrated with a Si photodiode.  
A monochrometer disperses light through a selected narrow band to pass on to the solar cell. By only 
selecting one wavelength at the time to pass through the entrance slit the transmittance or EQE can be 
measured at the exit slit. Widening the slit, leads to a more precis measuring of absorbance. In the EQE this 
means that the number of generated carriers are recorded. 
As an organic solar cell operates in the visible light area, the wavelength of the measurement is started at 
300 nm and goes to 900 nm. This is from the UV to the IR light with the visible light as the primary source. 
(10) For an optimal good solar cell the curve should have as high an EQE as possible covering a wide range 
of wavelength, making the top as wide as possible.  
  
Stability measurement 
Stability is a parameter of great interest as the life time of the solar cell depend on its stability.  
When comparing OPVS, it is often the PCS there is of interest, nevertheless stability is a critical holdup in 
the implementation on OPV in the society. The stability of the cell is depending of the degradation.  
The degradation of the solar cell can be both internal and external. Many of them happen simultaneously, 
but not all are yet identified. The layer stack is a delicate system and even small degradations can have an 
impact on the lifetime.  
The most well known degradation factors are water, oxygen, heat and mechanical force (stress). The 
interference inside the cell can cause oxidation, electro-migration, rusting and swelling of water-soluble 
layers. (7) The outside factors are the same, but also include photo-degradation in the form of irradiation. 
By continuous illumination the temperature within the cell will increase leading to degradation, at some 
wavelengths the cells should be extra sensitive.

The mechanical degradation, or stress, is in the form of bending, scratching etc. there could affect the 
surface. The mechanical degradation is not always enough by itself but cause greater harm if the 
degradation leads to penetration of water and/or oxygen.  
Due to the complexity of the organic solar cell most of the stability terms are uncontrollable.  
Strategies to prevent degradation and improve stability: encapsulation, geometry and morphology 
optimization. The inverted geometry is more stable and has a better PCE. The encapsulation creates a 
physical barrier around the cell protecting it from external degradation. The morphology and optimization 
is more of a puzzle as there are many different combinations of each layer and the combination of layers as 
well. However, with more studies it might be narrowed down. (7)(11) 
 
Process techniques for application  
There are several types of application techniques for production of organic solar cell as well as other 
devices. Two of the techniques are coating and printing in different forms. A selection are descripted 
below. (12),(13) 
Coating methods 
Spin-coating, is addition of solvent to the substrate on a horizontal plate, which rotates at high speed 
dispensation the solvent as a uniform thin film. The result is very homogenous, however the method is not 
fitting for upscaling or larger production as it is expensive and a lot of material is lost.  
Slot-die coating, has a controlled pattern by pumping the solvent through a small head and on to the blade, 
there applies it on the moving substrate. The material waste is very limited, however the head also limit the 
flow rate and ink viscosity.    
Knife coating (or blade coating), is a technique where solvent is added on the substrate and a knife or 
blade is controlling the distribution. It is suitable for flexible as well as rigid substrates, but has a lack of 
control of the pattern. 
Spray coating, is an industrial well known method, where solvent is added to a head with gas and droplets 
are sprayed through a nozzle. The quality is highly depending on the several properties of the substrate and 
solvent such as wettability, surface and speed. In practice not optimal for solar cell coating as the thickness 
can be difficult to control.

Printing methods 
Screen printing, the oldest method where the ink is press through a pattern onto the substrate. The 
method is used today in act work. With the flatbed technique, the viscosity of the ink has to be high and the 
solvent a low volatility to prevent evaporation. However, as a rotating technique the more suitable for 
larger scale production.  
Flexographic, is a system of several rolls. The anilox roll transfer the ink from the bath to the ink-collecting 
micro-cavities there has the pattern imprinted. Then the pattern is printed by contact on the substrate, 
while passing by. The method is used in the industry for foils and the limitations of its use is found in the 
composition and morphology of the ink. 
Gravure, a method where two rolls interact. One gets applied ink form a bath while rotating, a blade scrape 
off excess ink and thereby printing the cells in the pattern on the other roll. The method is known from 
magazines printing and is very efficient. Limitation of the method is also found in the composition and 
morphology of the ink.    
Thermal evaporation is a category by itself. The method is placing the cell under vacuum and heating the 
metal until it melt and evaporate. The metal then deposition on the cell as a homogenous thin film. The 
pattern is controlled by shadow masks, there cover the rest of the cell. The method is expensive and 
somewhat difficult in a larger scale.  
All of the diverse techniques each have a niche, which shows the diversity of the industry and complexity in 
upscaling as one does not fit all. The morphology and composition of the solvent (ink for the active layer) is 
key to find and use the right procedure.

2.3 Green solar cells 
The organic solar cell is a green energy source for harvesting energy; however, the production of the cell 
along with the disposal, the so called life circle is influenced by all elements of the production.  
Life cycle assessment (LCA) is a further development of the Kyoto Protocol made by the United Nations. 
(14) The protocol was adopted in 1997 and encourage the countries in the United Nations Framework 
Convention on Climate Charge to introduce laws and targets to limit their greenhouse gases emissions. By 
keeping track of every step of the process energy emission and material consumption the products LCA can 
be evaluated. It is a tool to optimize the green production and identifying possible improvements to benefit 
human health, the environment and use of resources. LCA is based on ISO standards defining impact 
assessment, goal and scope and inventory analysis.(4) It works with a cradle-to-grave description or cradle-
to-cradle describing how the process can be green by recycling things like glass and metals. It also 
determent the different impact each part of a process can have on the environment and on what level it is 
considered a problem. (15)    
It is difficult to put a definition on a term such as green solvent. The could easily be a question of greener 
than other solutions or simply a common comparison factor like emission of ton CO2. A widely used method 
is the Median lethal dose (LD50). The method tests the material as oral ingestion in rats as g/kg) (16)(17).  
A lower value than the LD50 point toward increasing toxicity. Everything can be poison to humans in the 
right amount including air and water. However, some materials may be toxic to people but does not have 
the same effect on the environment and vice versa. It is therefore important to pay attention towards the 
impact that the solvent has on the surroundings. In many cases at home and in the laboratory this 
awareness is help by pictograms, indication if the solvent is bad for the environment, humans or both.   
Halogens are an example of materials, found in the 17 group of the periodic table, there are very reactive 
and forms acid when reacting with hydrogen. They can be lethal or damaging for biological organisms 
depending on the dose. Which is why a typical non-toxic, green solvent is often halogen free. The best 
choice would be water or alcohol, however this encounter another problem: the solubility of polymers or 
small molecules. The solubility of a polymer depends on its morphology, side chains and molecular weight. 
The ideal coating is homogenous and without a dissolved polymer it is not possible to coat a working active 
layer. (16)(17)  
New methods to predict and use green solvent has been discovered recent years, however mostly used in 
laboratory scale and not suitable for larger scale production. If the water or alcohol should be used as 
solvent is must be in the form of nanoparticles within a mini-emulsion or precipitation. However, even with

production is profitable.  
 
2.4 The purpose of this work 
The purpose of this work is to find a way to upscale organic solar cells. As stated in the introduction and 
under coating technics, there are many different options to produce and fabricate organic solar cells. 
However, several of the technics are not fitting for a larger scale production, as the method is too 
expensive and time consuming. Furthermore, has the organic solar cells the disadvantage of a low power 
conversion efficiency, which means the cells, has to have another advantage to be competitive with other 
techniques or a greener cell. By finding and producing a green cell an acceptable trade-off between the 
PCE, payback time and recycling could be obtained.  
The project will be working with an inverted geometry cell with the layer stack: PET/ITO/ZnO/active 
layer/PEDOT:PSS/Ag and coating with a slot-die coating technique.  
Through the report an optimization of an organic solar cell produced by mini roll is made with the upscaling 
to a R2R production in mind.

3.2 Experimental procedure 
The described method and procedure below is the experimental procedure. Unless something specific is 
mentioned the production is done with the listed parameters. 
Mini roll coater 
1. A piece of substrate with an ITO electrode was aligned on the drum in the mini roll coater.  
2. A slot-die head with a small hole was applied on the machine and the parameters set before 
coating the ZnO layer. 
Drum temperature: 75 C, pump rate: 0.06 mL/min, speed rate: 36 % 
3. After coating the ZnO layer, the module was taken of the drum and placed in the oven for 10 min. 
4. After drying the module was aligned on the drum.  
For coating of the active layer a new slot-die head, small hole and blade was applied. The ink, in a 
syringe, was placed on the pump. The machines parameters was set before coating. The alignment 
of the coating can be seen in Figure 4. 
Drum and head temperature: 50 C , 60 C, 70 C, 80 C, pump rate: 0.08 mL/min, speed rate: 30 % 
5. The next layer is coated butanol covering all the coated layers and then as fast as possible coating 
the PEDOT:PSS layer. The parameters was as followed: 
Butanol: drum temperature: 70 C, pump rate: 0.25 mL/min, speed rate: 60 % 
PEDOT:PSS: drum temperature 70 C, pump rate: 1.25 mL/min, speed rate: 40 % 
6. The PEDOT:PSS layer was left to dry for 45 min. on the drum. 
7. Silver paste was applied on the rolling pad, aligned and slightly press down during printing.  
The machine parameters: Drum temperature: 75 C, speed rate: 30 %.  
8. After short drying time and cleaning of the equipment the module was taken of the drum and the 
cells could be cut out and measured. (see characterisation) 
9. After first measurement the best cells was encapsulated using epoxy and Thermo Menzel-Gläser.  
For the evaporated silver the first 6 steps are the same. 
7.     The module was removed from the drum and 12 cells were cut from it and placed in the form for 
evaporation.  
8.     The form was placed in the evaporator and the program for evaporating started. 
9.     After the silver was applied the cells was removed and measured.  
10.  After first measurement the best cells was encapsulated using epoxy and Thermo Menzel-Gläser.

4.1 P3HT:OIDTBR solar cells in dichlorobenzene 
The purpose with the first presented experiment is to study the influence of the PEDTO:PSS thickness on 
the photovoltaic performance of the solar cells. As the coating of the PEDTO:PSS, HTL is controlled by flow 
rate, the variations in the PETOT:PSS thickness are stated as flow rate. The other parameters of the organic 
solar cell, such as coating temperature, solvent and ratio are keep fixed in this section.  
Before coating a full solar cell with the HTL layer, different flow rates and curing temperatures with two 
different types of PEDOT:PSS was tried as shown in Figure 5. The transparency is comprehended without 
difficulty as the PEDOT:PSS PH1000 is more clear and transparent than the PEDOT:PSS FET on the left 
(Figure 5). However, the PEDOT:PSS PH1000 is not as smooth and homogeny in the coating as the 
PEDOT:PSS FET at any given flow rate. The transmittance of the different PEDOT:PSS FET layers is shown in 
Figure 6. The maximum of the transmittance centred on 400 nm and decreases as the deposition flow rate 
increases. The deposition of PEDOT:PSS PH1000 by slot-die coating was not visual homogeneous and its 
sheet resistance very high (>50 kΩ/□). Based on these observations the PEDOT:PSS FET, on the left, was 
chosen for production of solar cells.

4.4 P3HT:OIDTBR solar cells in 1,3,5-trimethylbenzene 
To get a wider view of the possibilities of the green solvent, before deciding which one to carry on with, 
1,3,5-trimethylbenzene was used under the same conditions as the dichlorobenzene and o-xylene. The 
molecular structure of 1,3,5-trimethylbenze is more or less the same as the other two but with one more 
methyl group and with systematic placed around the ring. The structure is shown in Figure 25 below. 
 
Even though the ink was prepared the same way and was expected to behave like the other two, the 
experiment was not a success. The P3HT:O-IDTBR was not properly dissolved in the solvent, which made 
the ink lumpy and grainy. The solvent was successfully transferred to the syringe, however not all material 
followed. As the ink was removed from the magnet stirrer the ink seemed dissolved and homogenous, but 
it only took a few minutes before the material separated from the solvent. Despite this the ink was used for 
coating.   
The cells did not work. The JV- measurement of the cell showed only resistance from the cells and no 
current. As the polymers was not properly dissolved this could be the reason behind the failure. Still various 
thing could have an influence on the result. The printed silver was applied with a new roll, the drying time 
for the PEDOT just to mention a few.  
Due to the time limit and maximal output of the project, it was decided to go back to the o-xylene as 
solvent to make sure that the material was properly dissolved. However, with more time further study with 
the solution of 1,3,5-trimethylbenzene would be interesting.

4.5 Roll to roll 
The main problem with upscaling is to find a recipe and process there not only work in the laboratory but 
also in production scale as mass production. What might work one time as a small sample, made by hand, 
does not necessary work as every time when mass produced. The different coating type is important for 
the process to be scalable or not. Spin coating is an example of a coating type not fitted for upscaling, 
whereas slot-die coating and printing is very fitting, because this process is continuously.  
This project has mainly been focusing on the recipe and variable regarding temperature and composition of 
the ink in the active layer. For the upscaling several meters can be produced at the time with same abilities. 
The advantage of upscaling is besides the mass production the reduction of the human errors caused by 
controlling the coating of each layer by hand. The pilot scale is automatized by a computer and substrate as 
well as coating material is hold steady at all times. The two flowsheets in Figure 26 show how the process 
of mini roll coater and the R2R coater is constructed. The details of the process for the mini roll coater is 
listed in section 3.1. Unlike the mini roll coater the R2R has a continual process, which include the drying 
and change of solvent. However, it depends on the number and type of module of the machine. By 
construction a process where each part fulfil the process step while moving the production will run more 
smoothly than by hand changing.  
The flowsheet in A is the mini roll coating process. Each arrow is bending which indicate that an action has 
to be taken by the operator. Furthermore is the drying time place outside of the line as the process is pause 
during the wait.  
The flowsheet in B is a draft for a R2R process. Each arrow is leading to the next box and the two drying 
times are still in the flow. The overlapping of the drying time arrows reflect the drying is continuously while 
moving on to the next step.   
Comparing the two flow sheets is difficult, especially because the underlying operation is not visible. The 
flowsheet in A is hand-handled and the flowsheet in B mechanical. The R2R should be operated by a 
computer to minimize human error and optimizing production.

Many of the reviewed coating and printing techniques are fitting for upscaling and should be considered 
according to recipe of the ink. Considering the many different possibilities of the morphology and 
combination of the ink, the solution could be a combination of one or several methods.  
There is another mayor issue when working with upscaling. Unlike theory and laboratory work, upscaling is 
learning by doing and a lot of trade-offs. If the a change in temperature reduces a solar cells PCE but 
prolong its stability it should be considered to keep the change. However, if a reduction of drying time 
reduces the all other factor regarding the solvent, then the drying time or method should be change to a 
better setup. As with the hunt for the recipe, upscaling and optimization of the process takes time and can 
be adjusted to fit specific request according to altering setups.

5 Conclusion and future work 
The report includes many different experiments with the purpose of finding a fitting green solvent as the 
first part of the upscaling of the process.  
The PEDOT:PSS experiment showed that the flow rate (thickness) of the HTL layer should be as thin as 
possible but still have back contact to the electrode. The flow rate for the PEDOT:PSS became 1.25 mL/min 
as it best refiled this purpose, based on the best current density and highest EQE. 
Furthermore the PEDOT:PSS experiment showed two different types of encapsulation. The substrate PET 
and the Thermo glass. Of the two it would seem that the flexible encapsulation show great potential for 
further studies. However, the experiment did not give any implication of big influence.  
After changing the solvent to the green o-xylene testing of the different temperatures for the drum on the 
roll coater and slot-die heat showed the optimal temperature was at 60 C. However, the conclusion was 
based on a small amount of data, which might look different with a wider distribution.  
The experiments with the green solvent o-xylene was considered a success, whereas the one with 1,3,5-
trimethylbenzene was considered a failure and needed more work if time had allowed it.  
The stability study of the solar cells showed that the temperature should be around 60-70 C, with 70 C as 
the best performing cell. But the cells was stopped after 275 hours and not at burn out. The T80 had an 
approximately 10 hours mark for most of the cells under the solar simulator and an approximately 4 hours 
under the LED light. The calibration of the solar simulator and LED light should be the same as the 
concentration of light should be equal for the cells, which would make the comparison of the data more 
reliable.   
For the optimal advantage of the EQE the flatten out of the curve will be the changing of the ratio of the 
acceptor and donor. The 1:1.2 ratio of P3HT:O-IDTBR showed a slightly difference according to the 1:1 
ratio. 
5.2 Future work 
For future work it would be beneficial with a larger study on each subject as well as looking more deeply 
into each of them.  
As the literature states there are many types of green solvent with the same abilities as the one used in this 
report. This report covered o-xylene and 1,3,5-trimethylbenzene. The work with o-xylene could be further 
studied regarding temperatures, ratio and stability along with repeating the experiment and procedure to

collect more data on the solvent. The stability test should furthermore be tried carried out until burnout of 
the cells. 
It would be interesting to change the donor/acceptor ratio in more than one way, to see how the EQE is 
behaving according to the change. Maybe even with a pure donor and acceptor as a reference. 
The 1,3,5-trimethylbenzene had a lot of possibilities starting with solving the problem of the dissolving of 
the polymers. This could include a completely different procedure for using the green solvent as mini-
emulsion of nanoparticle or alike.  
Further studies could also include the encapsulation as stated before. As the flexible encapsulation showed 
potential compared to the glass encapsulation, a study based on this fixing the other variables.  
With all the experiments carried out, the next step would be upscaling from the mini roll coater to the R2R. 
Besides optimizing the recipe, each piece of the process had to be added to the R2R machine, changing the 
different parameters one by one. Among the parameters are the drying of the ETH in the oven and the HTL 
in open air.
