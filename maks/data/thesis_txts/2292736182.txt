1. ABSTRACT  
A comprehensive study is presented of Chaetomium spp., Stachybotrys spp., Aspergillus spp., 
Penicillium spp., Trichoderma spp., Ulocladium spp. and Cladosporium spp. all obtained from indoor 
niches, except for the reference strains.  
Fungi are abundant in nature and their abundance is associated with their capability of secreting 
variety of enzymes. The different isolates had different morphology, e.g. Cladosporium spp., and they 
had different growth rate at 25℃ on V8. Trichoderma spp. had almost the highest growth rate 
compared to a low growth rate such as Aspergillus versicolor.  
One focus of this study was to screen the used isolates for hydrolases mainly cellulases and 
hemicellulases. These enzymes are of great importance to the biodegradation of cellulose, which is 
the most abundant polymer in nature. Their ability of cellulose conversion into monomers have given 
them several biotechnological significant roles such as in the bioethanol production. 
The used isolates were cultured on two SSF systems and screened for enzyme and ergosterol 
production. A novel media recipe was used in order to mimic the indoor isolates natural habitat. 
Though, this media was not able to induce the enzyme productivity compared to the traditional WB 
media.  Among the indoor isolates, Penicillium chrysogenum isolates had an overall high enzymes 
expression compared to the Stachybotrys spp., which generally had a low expression of cellulases 
and hemicellulases.  
It was investigated whether the usage of ergosterol as a measure for fungal biomass could be used  to 
correlate the growth to enzyme production. To determine that, growth experiments were carried out 
using four reference strains (Aspergillus carbonarius, Aspergillus saccharolyticus, Taloromyces 
pinophilus and Trichoderma reesei). The information obtained from these experiments have showed 
that the fungi with high production of ergosterol were not able to produce as diverse neither high 
enzyme activities. It was observed that Trichoderma reesei production of ergosterol in agar-WB was 
time dependent and it correlated with the fungal dry weight. Losses of ergosterol were realized to be 
due to homogenization, extraction and the instrumental (UHPLC) variation. 
Finally, the mycometer value was used to quantify both Trichoderma reesei and Aspergillus 
carbonarius and the possibility of correlation between the measured mycometer value and their 
production of ergosterol. Quantification using the mycometer does not require advanced equipment 
such as UHPLC, therefore having a correlation between the mycometer value and the production of 
ergosterol, can lead to a potential alternative index for fungal biomass. A correlation was found 
between ergosterol and the mycometer value, being R2 72 for Trichoderma reesei and for Aspergillus 
carbonarius, it was 91.

3. GENERAL INTRODUCTION 
Fungi are very abundant in nature as commensals, contaminants or pathogens for both animals and 
plants. Generally, they have a semi-rigid cell wall containing chitin, cross linking glucans, ergosterol 
and proteins that are permeable to water and solutes, figure (1). As 
heterotrophs, fungi obtain nutrition via enzyme secretion, thus they are 
entitled as natural recyclers (Webster and Weber 2007). Fungi participate in 
many biotechnological applications, such as alcoholic beverages 
fermentation, production of industrial enzymes, agricultural bio-control 
agents and pharmaceutically effective compounds. Conversely, fungi can 
cause undesirable impacts, such as the mycotic infections leading to food and 
feed spoilage (Kavanagh 2011).  
Fungi propagate asexually or sexually depending on the environmental 
conditions.  The classification of fungi is based on the reproduction nature as 
Basidiomycetes and Ascomycetes (Kavanagh 2011). Basidiomycetes are 
haploid produced by two different parental types, forming a mycelium with 
two nuclei, one from each parent. The two nuclei fuse in the basidium, 
followed by meiosis to form four haploid daughter nuclei. These nuclei 
migrate into the externally formed basidospores.  
The Ascomycetes are the largest phylum with over 40,000 species identified. They reproduce both 
sexually and asexually. In the asexual reproduction, spores are formed by mitosis and called conidia. 
Whereas, the sexual propagation occurs by forming an ascus by the fusion of two hyphae of different 
mating types. Two nuclei are fused together to form diploid nuclei. The nuclei is divided by meiosis 
to produce four spores, then mitosis to eight haploid ascospores. The ascospores have thick cell walls 
and are able to persist in harsh environments for a longer time compared to the conidia. The different 
indoor isolates belong to the Ascomycetes. 
Fungi produce several two categories of metabolites, primary and secondary metabolites (Kavanagh 
2011). The primary metabolites are perquisites for growth such as enzymes, carbohydrates, lipids, 
nucleic acids, alcohol and organic acids. The primary metabolites are produced in both the lag and 
exponential phase of the growth. Whereas, secondary metabolites are produced in the stationary phase 
and often associated with differentiation and sporulation (Kavanagh 2011).  In the current study, the 
expression of cellulases as primary metabolite will be studied and ergosterol as secondary metabolite.

Cellulases 
Multiple types of cellulases have been discovered including at least two exo-β-glucanases or 
cellobiohydrolases (CBHs, CBH1 and CBHΙΙ), four endoglucanases (EGΙ, EGΙΙ, EGIII, EGΙV, 
EGV), and one β-glucosidase (BG)(Lynd et al. 2002). The general accepted mechanism of cellulose 
hydrolysis involves the synergistic action by endoglucanases, exoglucanases or cellobiohydrolases 
and β-glucosidases, figure 5. Exoglucanase (CBHΙΙ) hydrolyze the non reducing end and the 
exoglucanse (CBHΙ) hydrolyze the reducing end. Endoglucanases hydrolyze internally in the 
cellulose chain and attack the amorphous regions in cellulose, providing new chain ends for CBHs. 
β-glucosidases hydrolyze O-glycosyl of the non-reducing end in cellobiose, with release of β-D-
glucose (Quiroz-Castañeda and Folch-Mallol 2013). 
There is a wide specificity for β-D-glucosidases, as they hydrolyze α-L-arabinosides, β-D -xylosides, 
or β-D-fucosides. Based on substrate specificity, they are divided into three categories: first, 
cellobiases that have high specificity towards cellobiose, second aryl- β-glucosidases with high 
specificity for substrate such p-nitrophenyl-beta-D-glucopyranoside (pNPG) and thirdly broad 
specificity β-glucosidases. The third group is the most common  (Sørensen et al., 2013). Many 
microbial species have been reported as cellulose degraders including molds (Trichoderma viride, T. 
reesei, Aspergillus niger, Aspergillus terreus, Penicillium chrysosporium), yeast (Aureobasidium 
pullulans) and bacteria (Pseudomonas fluorescens, Bacillus subtilis and Serratia marscens) (Sethi et 
al. 2013); (Kricka, et al., 2014)and (Juhász et al., 2005).

5. Hemicellulose 
Hemicelluloses are not chemically homogeneous, unlike cellulose. They are diverse polymers of 
pentoses (xylose, arabinose), hexoses (mannose, glucose, and galactose). Pentoses are reluctant to 
fermentation by microorganisms. Therefore, xylan represents a large fraction of inefficient utilized 
substrate in fermentation of lignocellulose (Rennie and Scheller 2014). Hemicellulose composition 
is variable based on the plant of origin. However, the major component of hemicellulose leads to two 
distinguished types of wood as softwood or hardwood.  Xylan is the major component in hardwood 
while glucomannan is the major component of softwood, figure 7.

6. Ergosterol  
Ergosterol is vital component of fungi cell membranes, partially bound as different esters (Kavanagh 
2011). Ergosterol is stable under harsh physical treatment and has therefore been suggested as a global 
marker for food and feed mycological quality (Porep et al. 2015)and (Lau et al. 2006). It has been 
used by several studies to quantify fungal biomass (Reeslev, et al., 2003) and (Chiocchio 2011). 
However, ergosterol concentrations vary between the same species depending on the growth state of 
the fungus (Kavanagh 2011) and can be present at considerable concentrations for a period of time in 
the absence of living fungi (Mille-lindblom, et al., 2004).

The extraction method define which form of ergosterol is being determined. The free ergosterol is 
determined without saponification; total free ergosterol with saponification after the extraction and 
total ergosterol by saponification of the sample during the extraction (Nielsen 2000). Some factors 
have been reported to affect the ergosterol stability and therefore in some studies internal standard 
have been used to account for the ergosterol losses, e.g.  corticosterone with HPLC analysis 
(Chiocchio 2011) or an [4-2H2] ergosterol isotope with gas chromatography analysis (Nielsen 2000).  
The effect of using different solvents were studied by (Chiocchio 2011) to determine the extraction 
performance. The best extraction was accomplished by using n-hexane compared to n-heptane and 
ether-ethyl. However, the re-extraction resulted in additional amounts of recovery. The lowest amount 
was produced by using n-heptane, followed by ether-ethyl. Although, these two solvents generally 
produced less recovery in the first time as well. In this study, only the total ergosterol is quantified 
and there is no usage of an internal standard, as most methods using HPLC based only external 
standard (Nielsen 2000).  
7. INDOOR FUNGI 
Indoor fungi are abundant in humid locations and they spread fast and efficient with airborne spores 
or mycelium fragments. They can release several enzymes and mycotoxins (Rand, et al., 2010). Some 
strategies have been developed in order to rapidly quantify the fungal growth, e.g. Mycometer test. 
The test encompasses several advantageous such as simplicity and onsite performance with no need 
for complicated equipments as well as low procedure time compared to other fungal indexes such as 
ergosterol or β-glucan (Reeslev, et al., 2003); (Rylander et al., 2010) and (Krause and Hammad 2002). 
Mycometer test is based on the determination of a fungal cell wall agent, which is the enzyme β-n-
acetylhexose aminidase (NAHA) commonly found in both mycelium and spores (Rylander and 
Hulander 2010).  
The different indoor fungi studied in this project were cultured as solid state fermentation (SSF), 
which mimics their natural environment. SSF as well presents several biotechnological advantages 
on the laboratory scale: higher fermentation productivity, higher end-concentrations and higher 
product stability (Raimbault 1998). Diaz-Godinez et al. reported less production of undesired 
proteases on SSF compared to submerged fermentation and hence preserving the product of interest. 
Two different media were used to provide SSF, wheat bran media and chip wood media. Wheat bran 
has been reported by many studies as cellulase inducer and is a rich media of mono and 
polysaccharides (Annette et al. 2014) and (Meijer et al. 2011). The wheat bran chemical composition 
was studied by (Maes and Delcour 2001) see table (1). While the chip wood media was studied by 
(Andersen et al., 2014) showing a chemical composition varying between glucose, ribose and inositol.

8. MAIN OBJECTIVES: 
The current investigation is based on the ability of some fungi to thrive in a nutrient poor environment 
such as the indoor environment, which contains dust and wood. Several factors have been reported 
providing them to flourish such as water, temperature and their ability to secrete a variety of degrading 
enzymes. It is hypothesized that the enzyme production could be stimulated in a laboratory setting 
using a rich WB media, which has been reported among several studies as an efficient enzyme 
inducer. In addition to a novel media, the chip wood media, to mimic the natural habitat of the studied 
indoor isolates.  
The different used reference strains are reported as efficient producers of both cellulases and 
hemicellulases. Hypothetically, the fungi efficiency of enzyme production should be associated with 
the increase of secondary metabolites; especially the ones are required for fungal growth such as 
ergosterol. Therefore, the growth characteristic of the reference strains was analyzed to correlate the 
fungal growth (biomass) to the production/secretion of enzymes in SSF systems.

the mycometer value could be found.

Media preparation 
Wheat bran is the hard outer layer of wheat cereals, which consists of 10-15% cellulose; 25-30% 
hemicellulose and 4-8% lignin, for detailed chemical composition see table 1 (Maes and Delcour 
2001). A homogenized master mix was prepared in order to reduce the experimental error between 
samples. The master mix was autoclaved at 120℃  for one hour. Thereafter media was then distributed 
as 40g/ 250 ml shake flask and re-autoclaved for one hour and stored at 4℃ until use.  
Chip wood media 1 was used to mimic the natural environment of the indoor isolates. A master mix 
was prepared and autoclaved at 120℃  for 15 min. The medium was distributed as 40 g/250 ml shake 
flask, re-autoclaved for 15 min and stored at 4℃ until use.  Media 2 was an optimization of media 1 
by increasing the water content in order to match the water content of WB media. A second 
optimization was applied for media 2 by extending the fermentation period to 14 days.  
Inoculation   
The different isolates were inoculated on V8 antibiotics media for acquiring enough colony diameter 
and density (Andersen and Nissen 2000).  T. pinophilus was inoculated on MEA media to allow the 
inoculation of the flasks in the same time basis by having sufficient growth. The incubation for one 
week at 25℃. Three cavities (10 mm diameter) were excised by cork-borer for inoculation of one 
shake flask. The cork-borer was sterilized between different isolates with care for appropriate 
temperature before reusing for maintaining cells viability. The flasks were shaken thoroughly before 
located at 25℃ for one week. Hereafter, the flasks were shaken twice a day with 8 hours interval, see 
figure 9 for the experiment set up. 
Fermentation and enzyme harvest 
The fermentation was carried out for the isolates in WB media for one week at 25℃. In media 1 and 
2, only Chaetomium and Stachybotrys strains were cultured. The fermentation period was one week 
in media 1 and 14 days in media 2. The enzyme harvest occurred after adding 50 ml MilliQ and 
shaking at 4℃, 175 rpm overnight. The slush was then squeezed through Miracloth in 50 ml Falcon 
tubes and centrifuged at 5℃, 10,000g for 10 min. The supernatants were transferred to clean 15 mL 
Falcon tubes and stored at 5℃ until used for enzyme assays.

Ergosterol preparation 
The ergosterol preparations were done only for the reference strains in wheat bran medium. After one 
week fermentation, the biomass of one shake flask was divided between two 50 mL falcon tubes. The 
samples were centrifuged at 10,000g for 15 minutes at 5℃. Afterwards, 15 mL of the supernatant was

washed with distilled water to transfer the entire biomass into the two 50 mL falcon tubes. Surplus of 
supernatant was discarded as there was no further need for it. After transferring the whole biomass, 
the samples had to be frozen at -4℃ prior to freeze-drying. Freeze-drying or lyophilisation is a 
downstream process used to remove water from biological commodities without impairing effects. 
The freeze drying occurs through vacuum of previously frozen samples. The samples were weighed 
after 3 days of freeze-drying then stored at -20 ℃ until further analysis.  
Sample homogenization  
The biomass of each biological sample was divided in two 50 ml Falcon tubes, as mentioned 
previously. The content of each falcon tube was homogenized individually. The homogenization was 
carried out by grinding with a spatula and repeated vortex. The content of one intact biological sample 
was then transferred into one of the two 50 ml Falcon tube, in order to reduce the number of samples 
and subsequently have a full homogenized sample in one tube. When transferring the biomass, a 
challenge occurred with regard to the second “empty” Falcon tube having spores/dust attached to the 
interior wall. This could not be transferred easily and therefore extraction had to be done for this tube 
as well, in order to quantify the losses during homogenization. The weight of both Falcon tubes, the 
one including almost the entire biomass and the one including dust in addition to spore traces (from 
here the content of this tubes will be named as “traces”) were noted down for further quantification.

10. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
A progression of screening process is presented in this section. The enzyme production was 
qualitatively and quantitatively determined in order to enhance the current library of cellulases and 
hemicellulases, as well as for the potentiality of novel enzymes. Ergosterol and mycometer were used 
in order to quantify the fungal growth and correlate it to enzyme production as well as to some extent 
investigating the correlation between both.    
10.1 WB-AZCL  
The enzyme production is different from one species to another and is dependent on many factors, 
e.g. the carbon source, water activity, pH, temperature etc. a study reported by (Krogh, et al., 2004) 
showed a huge difference between both T. reesei and T. pinophilus in enzyme production using 
cellulose and xylan as carbon source. Different AZCL chromogenic substrates were used for enzyme 
activity determination. The occurrence of specific enzymes in the crude filtrate causes the release of 
Azurine as a blue halo zone around the application site (Zantinge et al., 2002).  
As can be seen in figure 14, not all the used isolates showed β-mannanase activity, neither did the 
different isolates of the same species have analogy to each other. The highest activity observed among 
the indoor isolates was for two isolates of P. chrysogenum, which produced activity as 9 mm radius. 
However, the third isolate, P. chrysogenum 30128 did not show β-mannanase activity at all, this 
isolate was obtained from vaccine producing factory, see table 3. The highest activities observed for 
the reference strains were for A. carbonarius and A. saccharolyticus (9 mm). T. reesei was observed 
for a low activity of β-mannanase as (5.5 mm). A significant high activity of β-mannanase was 
observed for T. harzianum 40876 with an activity as (8.2 mm) and U. alternariae 41862 with an 
activity as (7.5 mm). A moderate enzyme production was observed for C. sphaerospermum 41982 
(6.7 mm) and for C. globosum 7029 (6.8 mm). The lowest activity was observed for T. pinophilus, 
which had an activity as the lowest activities produced by some of the indoor isolates. Stachybotrys 
spp. did not produce any activity neither did the two isolates of C. elatum, see table 9 for the assay 
results. For detailed figures including the standard deviation, see appendix section 14.1. 
Not all the indoor isolates were able to produce endo-cellulase activity, figure 14. However, the 
indoor isolates producing endo-cellulase were observed to produce higher activities of endo-cellulase 
compared to the activities produced by the reference strains. The highest activities were observed for

10.3 β-glucosidase  
Different concentrations were used in technical duplicates to construct the calibration curve (figure 
26) from 0.025 to 1.2 mM pNP in sodium citrate at pH 4.8, according to table (6). The linear 
regression of the calibration curve was used to calculate the activity of β-glucosidase in different 
biological samples as U/ml. One unit (U) of enzyme activity was defined as the amount of enzyme 
needed to hydrolyze 1 µmol of pNPG in 1 min at pH 4.8 at 50℃. The assay is based on the cleavage 
of pNPG by β-glucosidase leading to the release of chromogenic pNP, which was measured at 400 
nm.

10.4 Ergosterol  
In this section, the study was accounted only for the four reference fungi. To narrow down the samples 
number in the limitation of the given time, as the intention was to study day to day variation. Using 
technical triplicates was repeated in two different days in order to study the variation in the extraction 
between days 
Dry weight determinations 
Water is one of the important factors for fungal growth. It mediates several biological reactions, 
especially the ones resulting by the act of hydrolysis enzymes, allowing fungi to attain their nutrients. 
There are two definitions of the water existence in the growth media. Water activity (aw), defines all 
the accessible water for biological reactions and water content, which defines the total amount of 
water. Han and Nout reported the necessity of high water activity as well as controlling it through 
SSF in order to enhance the fungal growth. The water activity was not determined, as the optimal aw 
varies between different microorganisms and is not stable all the time ( Mugnier and Jung 1985). The 
water content was determined by weighing the media before and after drying overnight at 100℃. The 
water content for the chip wood media was 55.72 %∓0.79 %, whereas for the WB media was 71.27 
%∓0.72 %.  
WB-Chip wood dry weight 
Water loss and media weight for WB and chip wood media were determined by weighing after one 
week of fermentation. There was no significant change in the media wet weight due to fungal growth, 
see appendix (figures 50&52). Yet, T. pinophilus was as high in the weight as the background and 
the three others were slightly less. This could be due to a lower consumption of water by T. pinophilus 
compared to the others. Hence, there was no significant increase in the dry weight after freeze drying 
compared to the background, appendix (figure 51).  The media contained fungi had generally a lower 
weight of 1 to 2 g after freeze drying compared to the background, see appendix (figures 51& 53).  
Agar-WB 
The dry weight of the four reference strains was determined in agar-WB (section 9.5 for agar-WB). 
Among the four fungi, A. saccharolyticus had the highest water content 17.3∓2.7 g, followed by A 
.carbonarius as 13.05 ∓0.43 g. T. pinophilus had almost the same water content as T. reesei, table 
10. The latter was incubated only for four days while the others were incubated for one week. The 
short incubation period for T. reesei was a procedure consideration to minimize the colony diameter. 
Incubating T. reesei for one week resulted in it overgrowing the surface area in the petri dish, leading 
to complications in dividing and comparing with the others on the same basis.

Variation sources 
Homogenization inefficiency could be one of the causes for variation between the three technical 
replicate samples, see section 9.5 with regards to homogenization. Nevertheless, avoiding other 
homogenization methods such as using coffee grinder was necessary. This was in order to minimize 
spores loss, which were electrically charged and would have stuck the sides of the grinder.  
Another source for variation was mentioned in method optimization on the biomass weight through 
using the different technical triplicates of different biological samples of the same isolate.  
A third source was the variation through the extraction procedure, as it included several experimental 
steps, which could have led to experimental errors meanwhile the extraction procedure.  
An additional source for variation could be from the instrument (UHPLC). This concern based on the 
fact that, using the instrument on different days led to different results.  
Finally, for the external standard preparation, it was observed that ergosterol was not completely iso-
proponyl soluble. For that, the iso-proponyl volume was increased for facilitating complete solubility. 
That was proceeded in a significant consumed time. Therefore, an optimization discussed in section 
12, should be considered.

Ergosterol-cellulases and hemicellulases 
Studying the correlation between ergosterol and enzyme production is a significant objective for the 
project. For many commodities, ergosterol had been used to qualify and quantify the fungal growth. 
Whether ergosterol can be correlated to their ability of enzymes production, can be observed in figures 
(34, 35). T. reesei showed the highest biosynthesis of ergosterol as 28538∓3581 µg/biomass; 
however, it did not show the same efficiency of expressing different cellulases and hemicellulases. 
Among the low enzyme production for T. reesei were β-glucosidase as 0.494∓0.011 U/ml, β-
mannanase as radius 5.5 mm and no activity of endo-galactnase neither endo-arabinanase.  
The second high ergosterol producer was T. pinophilus as overall average was 20563∓1939 
µg/biomass. T. pinophilus neither showed high efficiency of enzyme activities production. Among 
the low enzyme production for T. pinophilus were β-glucosidase as 0.392∓0.018 U/ml, β-mannanase 
as radius 4 mm and no activity of endo-arabinanase.  
The third ergosterol producer was A. carbonarius as 12716∓397 µg/biomass. A. carbonarius had 
high efficiency in producing different enzymes activities except for lacking the expression of endo-
arabinanase activity. The enzyme activities for A. carbonarius were β-glucosidase as 7.267∓0.31 
U/ml, β-mannanase measured as radius 9 mm, endo-cellulase was 6.8 mm, endo-xylanase as13 mm 
and endo-galactanase as 6.8 mm.  
The lowest ergosterol producer was A. saccharolyticus as 11170∓2530 µg/biomass. Nevertheless, A. 
saccharolyticus showed the efficacy in producing the different studied enzymes in the highest 
activities among the used isolates. The enzymes activities for A. saccharolyticus were β-glucosidase 
as 7.8∓0.11 U/ml, β-mannanase measured as radius 9.3 mm, endo-cellulase was 6.8 mm, endo-
xylanase as 13.7 mm, endo-galactanase as 8 mm and endo-arabinanase 7.5 mm. This indicates that 
there was not a correlation between a high ergosterol amount and a good enzyme production.

11. CONCLUSION  
During the project progression, it was found out that the indoor isolates such P. chrysogenum isolates 
and C. sphaerospermum were able to produce cellulases and hemicellulases in higher activities 
compared to the regarded industrial T. reesei, based on the AZCL results of β-mannanase, endo-
cellulase, endo-galactanase, endo-arabinanase and β-glucosidase. Disagreement concerning that was 
reported by (Wyman 1994) and (Rahman et al. 2009). Trichoderma spp. were found generally as 
inefficient endo-galactnase and endo-arabinanase producers.  
The different isolates of the same species did not have similarity to each other with different enzyme 
activities. However, generally there was no difference between the two isolates of the same species 
with regard to endo-xylanase activities. Which were the highest enzyme activities expressed among 
the hemicellulases by the used isolates.   
The highest activities among reference strains were observed for A. saccharolyticus, followed by A. 
carbonarius. T. pinophilus had generally the lowest expressed activities. However, it showed high 
activity of endo-galactanase activity compared to null for T. reesei. A. saccharolyticus and A. 
carbonarius were found as more efficient producers of enzyme diversification and in magnitude 
compared to T. reesei. Moreover, WB media was an efficient inducer of enzyme activities, in 
agreement with what has been reported for Aspergilli (Meijer et al. 2011). Whereas chip wood media 
needs optimization.  
There was no correlation found between ergosterol and enzyme production. T. reesei, which produced 
the most ergosterol showed lower enzyme activity compared to A. saccharolyticus. Therefore, 
ergosterol can not be used as an index for attaining high enzyme activity. Eventually, studying the 
mycometer value for the used isolates have showed correlation with the ergosterol production, for T. 
reesei the correlation was with R2 72 and for A. carbonarius was 91.  
12. FUTURE PERSPECTIVE  
The enzyme activity in the Chip wood media was almost non-existing, showing that an optimization 
of the media is needed especially for the indoor fungi. The poor growth of these fungi in the chip 
wood media points out that there were unconsidered factors when mimicking their natural habitat as 
a media for the lab. One of these factors could be aw provided during the fermentation. Another factor 
could be by using the species, which happened to produce the majority of high levels of cellulases 
and hemicellulases, mentioned previously such as the isolates of P. chrysogenum. It could also be 
interesting to analyze the correlation between the enzyme production and ergosterol production for 
different cultivation times such as 5, 7, 10 and 14 days, as has been mentioned in the current study 
with regards to the analysis of agar-WB and the ergosterol dependence of time for T. reesei.   
Other methodological issues should be considered with regard to dissolving ergosterol in other 
solvents except isopropanol such as ethyl acetate, pentane, hexane or dichloromethyl in order to avoid 
the complications associated with the ergosterol insolubility. As well as, the recovery standard should 
have been repeated for three different days for method validation. The losses during the extraction 
should be evaluated through random choice of different samples for another re-extraction.
