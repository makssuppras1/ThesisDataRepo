Abstract:
Motivated by the increased need for optimization in transportation, this project studies
logistic collaboration. Logistic collaboration is experiencing a higher interest as the
demand for transportation of physical goods increases. Collaboration in logistics
introduces new problems for the involved companies and logistic service providers (LSP).
These problems occur when managing the relationships between the companies and their
joint activities. This implies that logistic collaborations often are prone to complexities,
which increase with the cardinality of the coalitions. Cost allocation is found to be an
area of interest as all participating parties must ﬁnd the allocation method fair. This
paper investigates how a Mixed Integer Linear Program can model a multi-commodity
ﬂow problem with collaborations and how complexities can be made measurable.
Furthermore, it investigates whether the Shapley allocation method is applicable for the
model which is proposed within the thesis. A collaboration variable that keeps track of
which agents collaborate was introduced. With this variable, a limitation on coalition
sizes could be implemented. Analyzing a scenario with 6 agents, showed that increasing
the allowed coalition sizes resulted in diminishing returns. Increasing the coalition size to
more than 4 agents resulted in minor additional savings. An inconsistency variable,
linked to the collaboration variable, was introduced to register every time pairwise
collaborations changed. These changes were deﬁned as inconsistencies. Limiting the
inconsistency of the coalitions showed to increase the cost. For a scenario with 6 agents
over 3 time periods, limiting coalition sizes to 3, and enforcing full consistency in
collaborations an increase of 7.2% in total cost was found. Limiting coalition sizes to 4,
and enforcing full consistency in collaborations an increase of 9.2% in total cost was
found. The Shapley Allocation Method showed to be suitable for small coalitions, but
also showed to be time-consuming, and is thus not recommended for large coalitions.

Introduction 1
As the demand for physical goods from food to furniture to clothing and more, is increasing
worldwide, the demand for transportation of the goods follows. Currently, transportation
accounts for one-ﬁfth of the world’s combined CO2 emissions.
Of the transportation
emissions, 29.7% originates from on-road freight transportation either by trucks or lorries
[Ritchie, 2020]. As detailed by Eurostat [2020] one in ﬁve kilometers driven by freight
transport was done by an empty truck. This information creates an incentive for optimizing
the current transportation logistics.
Solutions such as electrical-powered passenger and freight vehicles hold potential but also
require an immense production of new cars and trucks and would take years to implement.
A lesser extensive solution to solve the problem at hand could be to adopt a more eﬃcient
logistic strategies. Reducing the distance driven by empty freight vehicles would not only
reduce CO2 emissions but in turn, also reduce congestion and potentially save money for
companies.
Companies currently have several options for handling their transportation needs. The
transportation can be executed by the company itself or be outsourced.
Outsourcing
transportation can entail diﬀerent scopes of tasks depending on whether it is solely the
physical transportation that is outsourced or the entire logistic handling. Typically logistics
are outsourced and handled by a Third Party Logistics provider (3PL) or a Fourth Party
Logistics provider (4PL). A 3PL is a service provider which allows companies to outsource
operational logistics.
A 4PL manages resources, technology, infrastructure and even
includes management of external 3PLs to construct solutions for businesses [quickbooks,
2020]. It is the LSP’s task to come up with a transportation solution beneﬁcial for the
companies and the LSP itself.
Collaborative logistics have been shown to address the problem of non-full trucks and
decrease transportation costs for the participating parties. Collaborative logistics focus
on reducing the cost of executing logistic activities, improving service, gain market
share, enhancing capacities, and reducing the environmental footprint [D’Amours and
Ronnqvist, 2010]. Through a literature review, it was found that several real-life cases
where collaboration was introduced have been studied. 14% of the cost could be saved
by introducing collaboration for eight Swedish forest companies [Frisk et al., 2010].
A study on three product goods manufacturing companies in Belgium showed that a
potential 25% decrease in savings was found to be possible [Vanovermeire and Sörensen,
2014].
But collaboration can also be the source of new problems such as increased
complexity.
Complexities can occur from areas such as information sharing, trust,
managing relationships and negotiations, etc Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015]. Facilitators

1.1
Problem Statement
The problem statement and objectives for the thesis is detailed below.
Problem Statement
Investigate the complexities which arise from collaboration, to gain insight into how
complexities can be kept at a minimum while generating cost-eﬀective solutions.
Objectives
1. Study the eﬀect of limiting coalition sizes on cost.
2. Study the eﬀect of enforcing consistent collaborations.
3. Attain insight into the Shapley Allocation Method
The mentioned problem statement and objectives were created on the background of a
literature study targeting cost allocation, third player logistics, and collaborative logistics
(see chapter 2).
The goal was to create a Mixed Integer Program model to solve a multi-commodity ﬂow
problem. The model should enable a 3PL or 4PL with insight into several agents’ logistic
demands to oﬀer cheaper and simultaneously customizable transportation solutions.
Through the literature review, it was found that increasing the sizes of coalitions could
impose complexity costs which could overcome the potential gain from the increase in
size.
Therefore a collaboration variable and constraints which can limit coalition size
were implemented.
Furthermore, the project builds on a hypothesis that changes in
the coalitions over time causes complexities and thus hardens the task of managing
the coalitions.
These changes are referred to as inconsistencies, and are seen if two
agents collaborate in one period, but not in the next.
To limit the inconsistencies an
inconsistency variable and constraints were implemented within the model. The reasoning
of the hypothesis can be seen in section 4.2. The project investigates how constraining
these complexities aﬀect the savings realized from the coalitions.
The popular Shapley Allocation Method used to allocate cost and savings between
collaborating agents, is utilized to get insight into whether or not this method is ﬁtting in
combination with the proposed model.

This chapter consists of a literature review of contributions related to the topic of

Notable topics which are covered in the review are; cost
allocation, coalition forming, horizontal collaboration, and third-party logistics.
The
chapter serves as the cornerstone of the project and is used to deﬁne the scope of the

Firstly a research question is formulated somewhat broadly to understand the foundation

2.1
Logistics and collaboration
In the year 2018, the European logistic market was worth over 1.1 trillion EURO [Statista,
2020] while one in ﬁve kilometers driven was done so by an empty truck [Eurostat, 2020].
If eﬃcient utilization of resources and reducing emissions of greenhouse gasses are to be
addressed, this number of empty trucks has to be decreased.
3PLs originated in the 1980s and has become a reliant way to improve eﬀectiveness and
eﬃciency within supply chains. As identiﬁed by Maloni and Carter [2006], there are three
reasons why a ﬁrm would engage with a 3PL: (1) Expertise and economies of scale of LSPs
leading to cost reduction. (2) LSPs focus and eﬃciency leading to service improvements
and (3) Engaging of LSPs enabling a ﬁrm to focus on core competencies.
Premkumar et al. [2020] conclude that as a result of increasing competition within
the logistics industry LSPs are forced to diﬀerentiate themselves to survive and/or
excel. By providing sustained value, innovative solutions, and information to facilitate
data-driven decisions,
one can ensure a diﬀerentiation.
Innovation within route
optimization incentivized scheduling and real-time electronic tracking, and communication
is recommended as a conclusion of their study of trends in 3PL.
Ferrell et al. [2020] concludes through their article that they believe that collaboration
will play a key role in addressing the logistics needs of the future.
In logistics, two
types of collaboration exist; Vertical collaboration (VC) and horizontal collaboration (HC).
Figure 2.1 illustrates what VC and HC involve. Vertical collaboration can be done through
collaboration with customers, internally and with suppliers, while horizontal collaboration
includes collaboration with competitors, internally and with non-competitors [Barratt,
2004].

Figure 2.1. Illustration of vertical and horizontal collaboration [Barratt, 2004].
Mason et al. [2007] discusses combining VC and HC for transport optimization and

Collaborative logistics, through resource and/or information sharing, focus on reducing
the cost of executing logistic activities, improving service, gain market share, enhancing
capacities, and reducing the environmental footprint [D’Amours and Ronnqvist, 2010]. The
growing trend of focusing on collaborative logistics is pushed by the increasing awareness
of the environment and companies seeking to get a competitive edge over competitors.
Reducing the cost of logistic activities can be achieved by e.g. sharing trucks and pooling
commodities within trucks to reduce the number of trucks on the road.
This reduces
congestion on the roads and the total distance driven by the truck ﬂeet.
Emissions
produced by the truck ﬂeet are directly related to the distance driven therefore reducing
driving distance reduces emissions. Other examples of collaborative logistics are sharing
infrastructure such as pipelines, terminals, and warehouses among others.
Sharing supply and demand with others is an alternative way to achieve gains. A case
study by Frisk et al. [2010] covering 8 Swedish forest companies concluded that up to 14%
of the cost could be saved through the collaboration and that cooperation results in a 20%
reduction of emission from the trucks. The transportation problem was formulated as a
Linear Programming problem and of the 14% savings, 6% originated from better planning
within each company and the remaining 8% from the cooperation in between. It should
be noted that in this case-study commodities were interchangeable. Thus the 8 Swedish
forest companies could cover each other’s demands.
The article by Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] can be seen as an extension of the article
by Frisk et al. [2010]. Here, Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] uses the collaborative forest
transportation problem as a case study to use mixed-integer linear programming (MILP)
formulations to solve the collaborative transport problem.

2.2
Coalition Forming
Theory regarding coalition formation revolves around the analysis of one or multiple groups
of agents called coalitions, which jointly decide their collective actions. Coalition formation
can help with identifying relevant players to enter a coalition beneﬁcial for all players.
Several complexities arise when forming coalitions consisting of two or more entities. This
section will describe and reﬂect on these complexities. A prerequisite is that every entity
should want to improve their logistics [D’Amours and Ronnqvist, 2010].
This project will focus on coalitions where an LSP leads the coalition.
This has the
advantage that companies can focus on their core businesses [Cruijssen et al., 2010].
The fact that the LSP is in charge of the coalition eases the issue of sharing sensitive
information, as the companies involved will not have access to each other’s information.
This can potentially also facilitate the negotiation phase of the coalition forming, as one-
one negotiation rounds are preferred over a single negotiation with all companies, as it
reduces the risk of strategic behavior and prevents envy [Cruijssen et al., 2010].
An extension of this approach, where the LSP both leads and initiates the coalition
formation is what Cruijssen et al. [2010] call supplier-initiated outsourcing or insinking.
Cruijssen et al. [2010] describe insinking as a pull approach in contrast to the traditional
push approach, where it is the entities that drive the coalition.
Insinking provides an advantage compared to outsourcing, as it allows the LSP to exploit
synergetic eﬀects by merging several companies’ logistic needs.
Cruijssen et al. [2010]
provides an example of a coalition of companies in the Dutch Randstad in the fashion
industry. They touch on the topic of collaborating with possible competitors. They argue
that visibility for the customer is a vital characteristic for determining whether cooperation
with competitors should take place.
As transportation does not aﬀect core business
values such as product prices and product assortment collaborating on transportation is
sustainable. Collaborating with competitors is denoted co-opetition. Cruijssen et al. [2010]
make the point that companies that are active in the same sector, will have products of the
same characteristics and ordering dynamics and thus presents an opportunity for synergy.

2.2. Coalition Forming
The ﬁrst step of this insinking procedure is the target group selection i.e. the coalition
formation. Here Cruijssen et al. [2010] suggest sectoral specialization, where they identify
four necessary aspects of a successful grouping: information, the LSP’s capabilities, and
capacity, synergy, and a sustainable path towards the end solution.
Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] investigate the premise of cardinality. They focus on the
complexity of creating and maintaining a coalition with high cardinality. Therefore, they
implement a maximum cardinality constraint and investigate the upper bound on the
cardinality and its implications. They voice a concern that the total savings from a grand
coalition might not overwhelm the managerial complexities of forming and managing large
coalitions.
They ﬁnd that when increasing the number of parties in the coalition, the
relative cost savings decrease. Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] suggest adding a cost on
the coalition cardinality to encourage smaller coalitions. This cardinality cost can have an
important eﬀect on the resulting coalition formation. They found that increasing the upper
bound from 7 to 8 companies provided only 0.08% of relative savings to the participating
agents.
Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] argue that an agent’s goal is to minimize its own cost,
whilst maintaining stability for the entire coalition. Here the companies are called agents
mimicking the game-theoretical notation. To enforce this stability they implement stability
constraints. These stability constraints are based on game-theoretic principles, and can
result in a higher cost for individual agents but assuming unstable environments are
unattractive, higher costs are preferable. The stability constraints depend highly on one
player being the motivator of the coalition.
To solve the individual cost minimization
problem, this player would need the information of all other players, which is not preferable.
Instead of individual cost minimization, Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] also investigate
the maximum diﬀerence between savings.
They implement the Equal Proﬁt Method
(EPM) as mentioned in Frisk et al. [2010], which addresses the maximum diﬀerence in
savings.
However, they keep the objective of minimizing the total allocated cost and
instead introduce a parameter for the upper bound on the diﬀerence in cost savings. The
value of this parameter is investigated, and its relation to the results of the core stability
model is denoted price of fairness.
Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] has shown how to solve problems with up to 17 agents and
131,000 coalitions in a short time. They propose a further investigation into the uniqueness
of the solutions and uncertainty on the used parameters. They also propose an extension
where one adopts non-cooperative or partly cooperative strategies. Lastly, they identify
that the major barrier for collaboration is getting all players to agree on the cost and
beneﬁt allocation.
Entities in the same coalition might not have the same incentives or gains. Some might
gain little but provide a lot or vice versa [D’Amours and Ronnqvist, 2010]. Several factors
make coalition forming complex: the make up of the companies i.e. their synergy potential,
information sharing, and trust. Daudi et al. [2017] states that trust is an essential factor for
successful resource sharing. They state that lack of trust results in hesitations in sharing
resources due to the uncertainties of outcomes of a given collaboration. Daudi et al. [2017]
outline several diﬀerent aspects of what trust entails and how it can be measured. They
touch on cloud-enabled collaborative environments where "online trust" lacks face-to-face

establishing trust, which underlines Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] point of managerial

Daudi et al. [2017] state that the

Across many of the articles on coalition formation Shapley values are used in some capacity.

Cruijssen et al. [2010] describe their problem as a cooperative game. They use Shapley
values to determine their sequential oﬀers, arguing that even though all players will beneﬁt
from the grand coalition, the sequence in which they are approached matters. Players must
be approached in a sequence in which there is a saving regardless if more players are added
to the coalition. Cruijssen et al. [2010] introduce the notion of Rational Shapley Strictly
Monotonic Paths (RSMPs), which dictates the most promising sequence of oﬀers.
An
RSMP ensures that at any point in the selection process no further step will decrease the
compensation of the already committed players.

Theory 3
This chapter contains a short description of the theoretical concepts used within the
project.
This project seeks to investigate aspects in a ﬂow network i.e. sending objects from one
place to another. A ﬂow network is modeled as a directed graph G = (V, E). V is a set of
nodes and E is a set of edges. In a ﬂow network, every edge has a capacity and can have a
ﬂow of objects. In operations research, the objects which ﬂow in the network are typically
units of a product. The node or nodes in which the units enter the network are called
source nodes and the nodes in which they exit are called sink or destination nodes. The
source nodes have a speciﬁc supply of units and the destination nodes have a corresponding
demand of units. Such a ﬂow problem can be investigated as a game, which is then called a
ﬂow game, where the games also incorporate players and their collaboration [Reyes, 2006].
3.1
Multi Commodity Flows
In the book, Network Flows [Magnanti et al., 1993] an overview of multi-commodity
ﬂow problems is presented. In typical applications of a multi-commodity ﬂow problem,
commodities are distinguished as they represent diﬀerent physical goods and/or because
their points of origin and destination are diﬀerent.
Magnanti et al. [1993] splits the application of the multi-commodity ﬂow problem into
two, (1) "several physical distinct commodities sharing a common network" and (2) "a
single physical good ﬂowing on a network with multiple points of origin and destination
deﬁned by diﬀerent pairs of nodes in the network that need to send the good to each
other". This project will be focusing on the ﬁrst type. Examples of areas where this is
used are communication networks, computer networks, railroad transportation networks,
distribution networks, and food grain export-import networks [Magnanti et al., 1993].

3.2
Game Theory
Game theory is a mathematical discipline that examines aﬀairs of competition and
cooperation between multiple parties[Peters, 2008]. The deﬁnition is broad but is stated
by Peters [2008] to be consistent with the many applications. Game theory is used to
answer questions within areas of warfare, understanding economic competition, economic
or social problems of fair distribution to the behavior of animals in competitive situations
among others.
With cooperative games such as the one which this project studies, focus lies upon
the payoﬀs and coalitions. In cooperative games, players can form coalitions and enter
agreements on how to allocate the gains realized through a coalition. In a cooperative
game with transferable utility (TU-game), it is assumed that a number can express the
gain of a coalition. This number can then be distributed to the players in any way.
As deﬁned by Peters [2008]: A TU-game is deﬁned as a pair (N,v), where N = 1, 2, ..., n
with n ∈N is the set of players, and v is a function assigning to each coalition S, i.e.,
to each subset S ⊆N a real number v(S), such that v(Ø) = 0. The function v is called
the characteristic function and v(S) is called the worth of S. The coalition N is called the
grand coalition. A payoﬀdistribution for coalition S is a vector of real numbers (xi)i∈S.
Two questions are to be answered when studying a TU-game: which coalitions are formed;
and how is the gain of each coalition allocated among the members.
The Shapley Value
The Shapley value is a solution that assigns a unique payoﬀdistribution to the grand
coalition in a TU-game [Peters, 2008]. It estimates the average marginal contribution of
each player. Determining the Shapley value is ﬁrst done by calculating the marginal vector
for each sequence in which players enter the coalition. For a game with a set of players
N = {1, ..., n}, there will be n! diﬀerent possible sequences. The Shapley values assign
each player the average of all their marginal contributions. This is manageable for smaller
games but much too comprehensive for larger-scale games.
For reducing the number of computations, it is noted that the marginal contribution for
player i ∈N occurs in more than one marginal vector. Suppose there is an arbitrary
coalition S, which does not contain player i. Player i then joins coalition S, and receives

4.2
Complexity Variables
In an eﬀort to address complexity in the model, two variables are introduced:
The
collaboration variable cplx and the inconsistency variable rplxs.
These variables were
implemented to make complexity measurable and constrainable.
The collaboration variable cplx, measures whether two agents collaborate. This entails,
that for each possible pairing of agents, there is a collaboration value. The collaboration
variable is binary. It takes on a value of 1 if the agent p and l share a truck at some point in
the network in period x. If one wishes to limit the group sizes of the collaborations, one can
use the collaboration variable to constrain how many agents agent p can collaborate within
each period x. Thus, one way of constraining complexity is introduced. How this variable
is implemented and constricted can be seen in Equation 4.7, Equation 4.8, Equation 4.9,
Equation 4.10 and Equation 4.11. The eﬀect of Equation 4.9 is illustrated in Figure 4.2.
Figure 4.2. Coalition forming with and without Equation 4.9
As seen in Figure 4.2, Equation 4.9 enforces that if agent 1 wishes to collaborate with
agents 2 and 3, a coalition of all three agents are formed rather than two coalitions of
agents 1 & 2, and agents 1 & 3. This reduces the number of coalitions an agent can enter
to one, which means that fewer coalitions need to be managed. As complexities within
each coalition needs to be managed, the overall complexity is reduced by utilization of this
constraint. The eﬀect of Equation 4.9 is studied and is addressed in the analysis.
The inconsistency variable rplxs registers when the collaboration variables for agent p and
l changes values between period x and s. Thus, it registers all changes in collaborations
throughout the network. If one sees it preferable for agents to collaborate with the same
agents across periods, the inconsistency variable can be constrained. The model will then
ﬁnd solutions, where agents are more consistent in their choice of groups. This is the
reasoning behind the naming of rplxs as inconsistencies. Thus, another way to constrict
complexity is introduced. How this variable is deﬁned and constricted by the collaboration
variable can be seen in Equation 4.12, Equation 4.13 and Equation 4.14.
Introducing these variables creates the possibility to control the size of coalitions that the
agents can form and how inconsistent these coalitions are. This is done on the background
that with increasing coalition sizes comes increased complexity and complexity cost. A
hypothesis stating that changes in the agents who form a coalition from period to period
result in more complexity is the reasoning behind adding the inconsistency variable. As

managing relationships, and negotiations, etc. The reasoning behind the hypothesis on
inconsistencies is that with changing coalitions one has to manage these aforementioned

Maximum Inconsistency
A mathematical understanding of inconsistencies is required, to estimate the order of
magnitude of an inconsistency.
The inconsistency variable has the index of p and l,
which denotes the pairwise combination of agents. Suppose we have two agents, the two
combinations of p and l are (1,2) and (2,1). But for this model, those two combinations
imply the same. Therefore the inconsistency variable is limited by l > p, to cut down
computational power, and thus only combination (1,2) is identiﬁed. This entails that the
amount of possible collaborations follows the triangular number sequence n(n+1)/2, where
n is (N −1), i.e. how many agents one agent has to collaborate with. For the scenario
with two agents, N would be 2 and n would the 1. To ﬁnd out the maximum number
of inconsistencies, one has to envision a scenario that generates the most inconsistencies.
Such a scenario occurs when all agents alternate between collaborating with everyone and
collaborating with no one.

6.1
Collaborations
The coalition constraint seen in Equation 4.9 was used to ensure that agents could only
collaborate with agents within their coalition. This was determined on the background
that increased complexity cost would follow from freely collaborating agents. The eﬀect
of removing the constraint can be seen in Figure 5.13. The reduced cost from removing
the constraint was minor. Having only a slight eﬀect on the cost this strategy not only
reduces complexity but does so in a very cost-eﬀective manner.
From the analysis of coalition sizes’ eﬀect on cost savings (section 5.1), it can be stated
that a larger coalition size results in lower costs for participants. As coalition sizes increase
the savings diminishes. It was seen that the majority of the savings were realized with
coalitions of sizes 3 to 4. As a coalition increase in size, the complexity increases as well,
which in turn needs to be managed by the facilitator.
As mentioned in the literature
review, Guajardo and Rönnqvist [2015] states that the trade-oﬀby increasing coalition
size must overwhelm the cost of managing large coalitions for it to be an economically
sustainable coalition.
An expert in managing coalitions will have to determine if the
increased managerial complexity of the larger coalition makes the savings insigniﬁcant.
Due to this, it can be more beneﬁcial for all partners in a real scenario to choose a limit
for the coalition size.
Increasing the size of the agent pool to 10 showed the same trend of diminishing returns
from increasing the allowed coalition size (see Figure 5.3). It was also seen that a larger
agent pool resulted in larger savings. This is reasonable as when an agent pool increases
in size there is a higher chance for agents to form coalitions with high synergy. Synergy
for this case is based on the location of supply and demand.
The analysis was done on the tier network (see Figure 4.1). The tier network enforces a
ﬂow in a similar direction for the agents and their commodities. In a realistic scenario
the number of nodes creating a transportation network would be substantially larger and
the geographical location of the supply and demand more widespread. This would likely
incentivize agents to act alone.
The distances between the nodes in the tier network were kept constant throughout the
analysis. In subsection 5.1.2 an analysis was done, where the distances were randomly

6.2.2
Non-intuitive Groupings
Level 5 in Table 5.6 shows a grouping where one agent chooses to stay alone. We can
assume that the 3 agent group has good synergy, and the two agents in the 2 agent
group have poor synergy with the 6th agent. Keep in mind that the grouping and their
distribution in one time period aﬀect the groupings and distributions of the surrounding
period(s). Thus, when the 6th agent in period 3 chooses to stay alone, it could be to not
alter the groupings or distributions in period 2. The objective value is for all three periods
collectively. A grouping of one 3 agent group, one 2 agent group, and one single agent,
might not be the constellation that generates the lowest cost for the speciﬁc period, but
within the inconsistency, limits generate the lowest objectively value for all three periods.
6.2.3
Levels of Inconsistency
Without a concept of how the model works, and how the groupings and distributions of the
agents work, the number of inconsistencies carries no actual value. To give inconsistencies
a context of meaning, levels were introduced. These levels attempt to create a concept
where one level accounts for a common re-grouping or re-distribution of agents.
The
inconsistencies which were deemed important were diﬀerent for a maximum of 3 agents
per coalition and 4 agents per coalition. This makes comparing results diﬃcult as the
levels are named the same, but hold diﬀerent information in regards to the actual number
of inconsistencies.
However, groupings and distributions of agents in a constellation
with a maximum of 4 agents per coalition, do not necessarily match the groupings and
distributions of agents in a constellation with a maximum of 3 agent groups.
This is
evident by comparing Table 5.6 and Table 5.8. Therefore the level will be informative
within its constellation, but not necessarily across constellation. Perhaps a better means
of comparison is by how much the given inconsistency levels aﬀect the overall price. This
is why Figure 5.12 was made. As mentioned in the analysis, it is the trend between the
selected inconsistencies, which are interesting. It is seen that at an inconsistency of 13 and
5 the accumulated cost percentage for coalitions of size 4 is smaller than that of coalition
size 3.. This entails that an allowed inconsistency of 13 and 5, is more important for a
constellation where an allowed coalition size is 3 as it results in a higher increase in cost.

6. Discussion
6.2.4
Main Inconsistency Takeaways
This subsection highlights the main takeaways from the analysis in section 6.2. Limiting
inconsistency to 0, the costs increased by 6.8% and 9.4% for allowed coalitions of 3 and 4
agents, respectively. Leveling up from level 1 to level 2 the cost is increased insigniﬁcantly
for both allowed coalition sizes.
If the hypothesis of inconsistencies in the collaborations adds a complexity cost is true, a
facilitator of coalitions and collaborations would be able to ﬁnd a break-even point between
imposed complexity cost from the changing coalitions and the cost savings from allowed a
certain amount of inconsistencies.
6.3
Running Time
As seen from the analysis subsection 5.3.3, the increase of agents results in an almost
exponential time increase. As covered in the literature review, the synergy between the
agents is an important part of forming coalitions. By ensuring high synergy before adding
an agent to the agent pool for which a facilitator will create coalitions by utilizing the
model, the running time can potentially be held at a reasonable level. A synergy assessment
between agents should be made before adding an agent to the model and solving for
coalitions. A synergy assessment can be based upon a screening of the location of supply
and demand and the type of product that needs transport.
Utilization of the Shapley Allocation method proved to be ineﬀective for larger coalitions
as the number of iterations and the run time for the model mimics an exponential increase
(seen Table 5.12). However, if a manager enforces ﬁxed coalitions over all periods of smaller
sizes, say 3, for an agent pool of, say 9, three coalitions are formed as the model is solved
for a minimum cost solution. The problem can then be separated into three sub-problems
where the Shapley allocation method can be used eﬀectively for each coalition. To acquire
the results the model is then solved 3 times where each run only includes the agents of a
coalition.
For a constellation where coalitions sizes are small and inconsistencies are zero, the Shapley
Allocation Method can be used eﬀectively.
The model is solved for all agents with a
constraint on the coalition size and the ﬁxed coalitions are determined.
The Shapley
Values can then be determined for each of the coalitions.
For a scenario of 9 agents,
which forms 3 coalitions this would result in a total number of 3·7+1=28 iterations of
the problem. Only one iteration would have to solve the problem with 9 agents and the
remaining with 3 agents. In comparison, by allowing coalition sizes to be 9 the model had
to run 511 iterations to be able to determine the Shapley values.
In combination with the ﬁndings that the majority of the savings are realized within
coalitions of sizes 3-4 and the idea that complexity costs increase as coalition sizes increase,
it can be stated that utilizing the Shapley Method is an appropriate tool for allocating
the savings. If in a real case scenario signiﬁcant larger coalitions are found to be the best
solution diﬀerent allocation methods should be looked into.

Conclusion 7
It can be concluded that the model proposed was able to tackle complexities that arise
from collaboration in logistics.
The deﬁnition of coalitions proposed in Equation 4.9, enforces consistency in itself, as
described in section 4.2. It entails that if agent 1 collaborates with both agents 2 and 3,
agents 2 and 3 also collaborate by default. This deﬁnition proved to have a minor eﬀect
on the cost for the participating agents (see Figure 5.13). From this, it is concluded that
the deﬁnition of coalitions reduces complexity while doing so in a cost-eﬀective manner.
Increasing the allowed coalition size for 6 agents showed a trend of diminishing returns.
Allowing coalition sizes of 6 agents compared to coalition sizes of 4 agents resulted in a
small increase in savings for the collaborating agents (see Figure 5.1). When the agent
pool was increased to 10 agents, it showed the same trend of diminishing returns when
increasing the allowed coalition size (see Figure 5.3).
Furthermore, it was seen that a
larger agent pool results in bigger savings (see Table 5.3).
Since more agents entail a
higher chance of having better synergies between the participating agents these ﬁndings
are reasonable. Inclusion of aspects such as time windows for pick-ups and deliveries and
type of commodity would result in fewer synergies, but could, in turn, be countered by
increasing the agent pool.
By attaining an estimate for the cost of the complexity imposed by the diﬀerent coalition
sizes the model could be updated to include these costs in the objective function. This
would enable the model to pick the most optimal solution to a problem while considering
the complexity.
Due to the hypothesis that changes in the distribution of agents in coalitions impose
complexity, an inconsistency variable was introduced. The inconsistency variable proved to
be usable in constraining the changes between periods. Reducing inconsistency throughout
the periods for the coalitions raised the total cost of the agents (see Figure 5.11). When
complete consistency was enforced for a problem with 6 agents an increase of cost by 7.2%
and 9.2% were found for an allowed coalition size of 3 and 4, respectively.
Similar to
the complexity imposed by certain coalition sizes, knowing the complexity cost added by
inconsistencies could be introduced in the objective function. This would enable the model
to consider inconsistency complexities when ﬁnding solutions.
The Shapley Allocation Method proved to be a ﬁtting method for allocating the savings
realized. Due to the trend of the coalition sizes and the trade-oﬀof these, it was concluded
that the coalition needed not to be large to achieve a large amount of savings.
As
stated in the literature review; The Shapley allocation method is manageable for smaller

7.1
Future Studies
This section contains a brief overview of future studies which can be built upon the thesis.
Optimizing Running Time
A big challenge of the project was the solving time of the model. Simple models took no
time, but the running time of the model for 100 sets of 6 agents demands for 3 periods,
while solving for 30 or just 7 inconsistencies takes 10 days in total running time. However,
with the HPC, such a problem could be split into 10, and thus takes 1 day. To reduce
the running times of the model, the problem could be reformulated into a master program
and n subprograms using the Dantzig Wolfe decomposition method.
This enables the
possibility of solving the problem using parallel processors, to simultaneously solve each
sub-problem individually Magnanti et al. [1993].
An Agent Screening Method
A high synergy between agents ensures cost-eﬀective solutions. To ensure high synergy
between agents a method for screening potential agents can be created. Such a screening
method could consider areas such as the geographical location of the supply and demand
of the commodities, the type of commodity, time windows of pick-up and delivery, etc.
Determining Complexity Cost
The complexity cost imposed by coalition sizes and inconsistencies in the coalitions should
be determined in cooperation with a facilitator or manager of coalitions. If the complexity
cost can be modeled, this should be included in the model.
Network
The analysis within the thesis was run on a distinct network with locked distances. The
proposed model can be applied to several real-life cases to study if the ﬁndings within the
thesis are similar for a large variety of networks.

m=Model(with_optimizer(Gurobi.Optimizer, MIPGap = 0.00))
set_optimizer_attribute(m, "Threads", 4) #Necessary for HPC
@variable(m, t[i=1:N, j=1:N, z=1:K, x=1:X] >=0, Bin) #trucks on (ij)
@variable(m, q[i=1:N, j=1:N, o=1:O, z=1:K, x=1:X] >=0) #units on (ij)
@variable(m, a[i=1:N, j=1:N, p in comb[ii], z=1:K, x=1:X], Bin)
@variable(m, c[p in comb[ii], l in comb[ii], x=1:X], Bin)
@variable(m, r[p in comb[ii], l in comb[ii], x=1:X, s=1:X] >=0)
#--------OBJECTIVE: Minimize the total distance
@objective(m, Min, sum(sum(t[i,j,z,x] for x=1:X, z=1:K)*d[i,j] for i=1:N,
j=1:N))
#--------CONSTRAINTS
#Flow conservation
@constraint(m,[i=1:N,p in comb[ii], o=1:O, x=1:X], sum(q[i,j,o,z,x] for
j=1:N, z=1:K)-sum(q[j,i,o,z,x] for j=1:N,z=1:K)>=D[i,o,x])
#Goods in trucks limited by the capacity
@constraint(m,[i=1:N,j=1:N,z=1:K,x=1:X], sum(q[i,j,o,z,x] for o=1:O)/tcap<=
t[i,j,z,x])
#Capacity constraints on the arcs
@constraint(m,[i=1:N,j=1:N,x=1:X], sum(t[i,j,z,x] for z=1:K)<=u[i,j]*
arc[i,j])
#Matching commodities to agents + enforcing a to be one if subarc is used
@constraint(m,[i=1:N,j=1:N,p in comb[ii],z=1:K,x=1:X,o=1:O], q[i,j,o,z,x]
*V[p,o] <= a[i,j,p,z,x]*M)
#Constraining t to be at least one, if a is 1.
@constraint(m, [i=1:N,j=1:N,p in comb[ii],z=1:K,x=1:X], t[i,j,z,x] >=
a[i,j,p,z,x])
#Constraining c=1 if two player collaborate at any point in the network.
@constraint(m,[i=1:N,j=1:N,p in comb[ii],l=comb[ii], z=1:K,x=1:X],
a[i,j,p,z,x]+a[i,j,l,z,x]<=c[p,l,x]+1)
