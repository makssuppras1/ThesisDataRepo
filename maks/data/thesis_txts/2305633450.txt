Abstract
In nature, glycosylation is a very important post-translational modiﬁcation where a carbohydrate (glycosyl
donor) is attached to a functional group of another molecule (glycosyl acceptor). This is often the hydroxyl
group or other amino acid side-chain group of a protein. One class of glycosylation which has been attempted
mimicked in recent research produces N -linked glycans, and many of the recent methods use highly speciﬁc
chemoselective ligation reactions where neither the glycosyl donor nor acceptor has any protecting groups at-
tached, avoiding cumbersome protection and deprotection steps often necessary in synthesis of carbohydrates.
Many of these methods use rather large functional groups for this chemoselective ligation, which do not mimic
the natural amide bond of N -linked glycan very well. The goal of this project is to develop a novel synthetic
method for creating a stable and fully biologically active glycosylamide with an amide bond between three
components: A carbohydrate, a primary amine, and a carboxylic acid.
This thesis presents said novel method for the synthesis of a glycosylamide. Several carbohydrates, includ-
ing monosaccharides, oligosaccharides, and derivatives hereof, were used. The amines used were various short
(two to ﬁve amino acids) peptides, both in solution phase and on solid phase. The ﬁnal component, the car-
boxylic acid, were diﬀerent fatty acids or variations hereof. Reaction conditions were varied, testing the eﬀect of
reaction time, temperature, reagents, and acylation reactant, to develop the most eﬃcient synthetic method.

1
Introduction
Glycoconjugates are some of the most important compounds in modern organic chemistry[1]. As the name
suggests, they consist of a carbohydrate of any size, usually termed the glycan[2], covalently bonded to a non-
carbohydrate moiety. The non-carbohydrate moiety can be any organic molecule, but is usually a biomolecule,
such as a protein, a peptide or a lipid[1]. These compounds often play an integral part of biological processes,
such as viral entry, signal transduction, inﬂammations, fertility, and development[3]. They are often found on
cell-surfaces attached to surface proteins or lipids integrated in the cell surface, where they serve as points of
attachment for antibodies, other proteins/enzymes, or foreign bodies such as bacteria or vira. This makes them
essential to cellular communication, especially for the immune system[4].
1.1
Synthesis of Glycoconjugates
As in all organic chemistry, understanding the relationship between structural features of a compound and its
eﬀect on organisms is key. Both the analysis and the synthesis of these compounds is complicated by their often
very complex structures, so developing new synthetic methods for constructing well-deﬁned scaﬀolds is of great
interest. Carbohydrate chemistry has been studied for decades and many diﬀerent synthetic techniques have
been invented to create glycoconjugates with diﬀerent and more potent eﬀects than those found in nature[5].
Glycoconjugates as well as carbohydrates themselves can have very complex structures.
Carbohydates can
have both linear and branched polymeric structures due to their multiple functional groups, and the units in
these polymers are linked to each other through O-glycosidic bonds, and as this is the most common type of
bonding involving carbohydrates in nature it is also the most studied. The ﬁrst studies in this area were done
by Michael, Fischer, Koenigs, and Knorr, whose work have earned them all to have reactions named after them.
In addition to the reactions created by these four, many others exist, and in the Scheme 1, a simple outline of
the methods for formation of O-glycosides is shown. As can be seen in this scheme, the common technique in
all of these strategies is to functionalise the anomeric hydroxy group of the substrate to create a glycosyl donor
with a good leaving group, which will then allow a nucleophilic group on the glycosyl acceptor to attack. The
four routes shown can be divided into acid/base activation with (A/C) or without (B/D) direct attack of the
glycosyl acceptor nucleophile, depending on the strength of the nucleophile and whether or not the anomeric
oxygen is exchanged. These reactions can of course also be used to attach carbohydrate units to aglycans which
have a hydroxy group.
As can be seen in Scheme 1, one disadvantage of most of these reactions is that they require the carbohydrate
unit to be converted into a fully protected glycosyl donor with a leaving group at its anomeric center before
the reaction[6]. This is of course not uncommon in organic chemistry, where protecting groups are a stable.
However, as convergent synthesis is widely used in the synthesis of biomolecules, using unprotected reactants
with highly speciﬁc reactivities in so-called chemoselective ligation reactions is desired. Since the compounds
produced by these reactions are most likely to be used in a biological setting, it is very useful if the reaction
can be performed in aqueous media, where further modiﬁcations might be diﬃcult. One example of this from
another area of the synthesis of biomolecules is the native chemical ligation (NCL) where two unprotected
peptide segments are attached to form a larger polypeptide. The ﬁrst practical method for this was created by
Kent et al. in 1994[7].

As seen in Scheme 2, the principle of NCL is to take two peptide segments, the ﬁrst (Peptide-1) containing a
thioester at the α-carboxyl group and the second (Peptide-2) a cysteine residue at the N-terminal. This reaction
can be done completely without any protecting groups and as any amino acid can theoretically be converted to
a thioester, the only requirement is the cysteine residue in Peptide 2.
This can be done because the reaction between the thioester and the cysteine residue is highly chemoselective,
and NCL is thus a good example of the ideal type of reaction for the convergent synthesis of biomolecules. In
the last two decades, this method has been expanded to other amino acids as well. For example, Yan et al.
developed a method to follow the ligation by desulfurisation thus producing a peptide with an alanine residue,
instead of cysteine[8]. Alanine is far more abundant in natural peptides so this greatly broadens the application
of this NCL for synthesis of natural products. Similarly, Chen et al. developed a method where, by using β-

or γ-thiol valine instead of cysteine, the ligation product can be turned into valine via desulfurisation[9]. By
introducing non-natural, sulfurylated versions of aliphatic amino acids into Peptide 2, many diﬀerent peptides
can be synthesised. Okamoto et al. expanded the possibilities further, by developing a method to convert
the cysteine residue into a serine (see Scheme 3)[10]. The cysteine is S-methylated after which CNBr is added
to promote a rearrangement into an O-ester linked peptide by removal of the -SMe group and subsequent
nucleophilic attack by water. The now introduced water molecule delivers the oxygen atom for the serine side
chain after an O to N acyl shift yielding the product peptide.
Scheme 3: Kent NCL followed by conversion of cysteine into serine as developed by Okamoto et al.[10].
Thus, the product peptide is functionalised and from here, almost any peptide can be produced with NCL.
This is a good example of the development of a very useful technique for the convergent synthesis of biomolecules,
and illustrates perfectly the type of reaction that is sought in all areas of bioconjugation: A highly speciﬁc,
chemoselective ligation of two fragment compounds with no protecting groups and as little as possible post-
ligation modiﬁcation required. This type of convergent synthesis is also very useful in the synthesis of gly-
coconjugates. The general strategy is, as in all convergent synthesis, to synthesise both the glycan and the
aglycan individually and then create the link with the unprotected forms of these two fragments. For glycocon-
jugates this is especially useful because of the instability of the glycosidic bond[1] which can easily be broken
in deprotection steps that often involve relatively strong acids or bases.
1.1.1
Glycopeptides
In nature, one of the most common forms of glycoconjugation exists in glycopeptides/glycoproteins, where
the carbohydrate is linked to a polypeptide chain either via O- or N-glycosylation. O-glycosylation, which
was mentioned before, is the most dominant of these two in polypeptides.
Here, the carbohydrate can be
attached to serine, threonine or tyrosine, while in N-glycosylation it is almost exclusively asparagine which is
the glycosylated type of residue (see Figure 1).
3
==PAGE:11==
Figure 1: Examples of glycosylation in peptides[1].
Naturally, these amino acid residues are glycosylated by enzymes, but in chemical synthesis various methods
can be used. The traditional approach is a so-called cassette method, which is a type of stepwise synthesis where
the carbohydrate is already attached to the amino acid when it is incorporated into the peptide using standard
Fmoc solid phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) methods (see 1.5)[11]. Boc SPPS, where the Boc-protecting group
is removed with triﬂuoroacetic acid (TFA) before every step, is not used due to the general acid lability of the
glycosidic linkage. For N -linked glycopeptides, the glycosylated asparagine is created by reducing glycosylazide
to glycosylamine and then coupling it with an activated ester of aspartic acid. This strategy can be disadvan-
tageous because the incorporation of the carbohydrate into the peptide can pose diﬀerent problems during the
remainder of the synthesis. Sometimes, an unprotected carbohydrate can be used, but to avoid accidental reac-
tions on the hydroxy groups they are usually protected. Benzyl protecting groups are preferred as they ensure
high reactivity during the glycosylation, and with acetyl protecting groups acetyl migration can occur[12]. On
the other hand, the common technique for removal of benzyl groups is hydrogenation with palladium on carbon,
but this is not compatible with sulfur-containing amino acids, due to sulfur’s strong aﬃnity for metals.
Another technique is using HF, but this is even more incompatible with the synthesis in general, especially when
using oligosaccharides. More complex or advanced synthetic methods, like chemoenzymatic synthesis or native
chemical ligation, can be used to circumvent these problems, but it is clear that the synthesis of glycopeptides
is not a trivial endeavour.
Due to the biological importance of these molecules, frequent attempts at new methods have been made, and
some covergent synthetic methods exist. An obvious route would be to draw inspiration from the previously
mentioned method where a glycosylamine is reacted with an activated ester of aspartic acid, and build the
peptide with a protected aspartic acid where the N -linked glycosyl on asparagine is wanted[13]. The aspartic
acid can then be deprotected, activated, and reacted with glycosylamine after completion of the peptide syn-
thesis. A big problem with this tactic is that the back bone amide located in the C-terminal direction will
attack this activated ester. This leads to cyclisation into an aspartimide and possible ring-opening into the
aspartic acid again, in either an α or β-peptide form, as seen in Scheme 4. The ratio between the three possible
products depends on the chemical structure of the amino acids near the aspartic acid. If the aspartic acid is
N-terminal to a small amino acid, like glycine or alanine, the cyclic product is favoured. This side-reaction is

As mentioned, other techniques for convergent synthesis exist. These fall into two categories: 1) Addition of
a sulfur nucleophile to an electrophile, and 2) reaction of a carbonyl group (ketone or aldehyde) with a strong
nucleophile[14]. The ﬁrst category includes the Koenigs-Knorr reaction shown in Scheme 1 (and Scheme 5D).
The second category was inspired by the fact that no natural amino acids have a ketone or aldehyde on their
side-chain, while reducing sugars do in their open-chain form. A non-natural nucleophile can be introduced
to a side-chain of the peptide and will then attack the aldehyde or ketone of the sugar, thus giving a simple
chemoselective reaction. There are several nucleophiles well suited for this type of reaction, as described Hang
et al. [15]: Hydrazides can react with the aldehyde or ketone to give an N -acyl hydrazone (Scheme 5A), while an
aminooxy group gives an oxime (Scheme 5B), and a thiosemicarbazide gives a thiosemicarbazone (Scheme 5C).

1.2
Carbohydrates
When synthesising glycoconjugates, many diﬀerent carbohydrates can be used. Most interesting are the carbo-
hydrates with interesting properties, e.g. exhibiting immunostimulatory eﬀects. Such a carbohydrate is mannose
(see Figure 2), the C-2 epimer of glucose, which exists in glycoproteins in the human body. It is recognised
by a protein called mannose-binding lectin (MBL), which is an important part of the innate immune system of
humans[21]. This protein was discovered in 1946 by Burnet et al. as one of three inhibitors in serum capable
of inactivating inﬂuenza virus[22], and has since been studied extensively. MBL has three calcium dependent
carbohydrate-recognition domains (CRD) which contain the active sites of the protein. Each CRD binds a cal-
cium ion which enables it to form co-ordination bonds with the C3 and C4 hydroxyl groups of carbohydrates
(see Figure 3)[23]. One lone pair of the oxygen in the hydroxyl group coordinates to the calcium ion, while the
other lone pair accepts a hydrogen bond from the amide proton of an asparagine (Asn185 for C3, Asn205 for
C4) side-chain of MBL (see Figure 3). Furthermore, the proton of the hydroxyl group forms a hydrogen bond
to a glutamic acid (Glu185 for C3, Glu193 for C4). Finally, the calcium ion coordinates to an aspartic acid
side-chain (Asp206), which holds it in place.

The carbohydrates that can be recognised by MBL are: Mannose, N-acetyl mannosamine, glucose, N-acetyl
glucosamine, and fucose (6-deoxy-L-galactose), which can be seen in Figure 2.

1.3.1

The ﬁrst step in creating the amide bond is the formation of a hemiaminal, i.e. substituting the hydroxyl group
of the anomeric carbon of the carbohydrate for an amine. This is achieved by nucleophilic attack of a primary
amine onto the aldehyde of a carbohydrate in its open-chain form. Such an attack yields an imine, which then
exists in an equilibrium with the hemiaminal form, as can be seen in Scheme 8.

reasons: Firstly, the glycosidic bond formed in this reaction is acid labile, so the back-reaction will be more
favourable (see Scheme 9) until the glycosidic bond is stabilised via the acylation. The glycosylamide is stable
both at high and low pH, as well as at high temperature[25].

This hap-

of the reaction is further increased by adding the conjugate base of the catalytic acid, such that the reaction is
reality is acid-base catalysed. Thus, as long as the reaction is done in basic conditions the glycosylamine is the
favoured product.
1.3.2
Acylation of Glycosylamine
After the imine formation and subsequent ring-closure which yields the glycosylamine, the next step is the
acylation to give the glycosylamide.
Forming an N -substituted amide bond can be diﬃcult due to steric
eﬀects, especially with relatively large substituents, such as carbohydrates[28]. To overcome this problem, one
would naturally choose as strong an acylating reagent as possible. However, problems can arise when using
unprotected carbohydrates (as discussed in Section 1.1), as the carbohydrate can be acylated on the hydroxyl
groups. Depending on the pH of the reaction mixture and the pKa of the hydroxyl group and the secondary amine
in the glycosylamine, either reaction will be favoured. In a previous study done by Boas et al., a glycosylamine
was acetylated with acetic acid anhydride, and deacetylation of the carbohydrate was necessary[29]. Conditions
for this were treatment with 0.1 M NaOH for 2 hours at room temperature, which are relatively mild assuming
the product is stable in aqueous solution. Acylation of non-carbohydrate substituted secondary amines have
been shown to work, even with larger acid anhydrides and satisfactory yields (>80%)[30][31]. Acid anhydrides
can be formed with carbodiimides in mild conditions[32], and then reacted with the glycosylamine to yield the
desired glycosylamide product (see Scheme 11).
Scheme 11: Formation of symmetric anhydride from carboxylic acid and N,N ’-diisopropylcarbodiimide
(DIC), and subsequent formation of glycosylamide from glycosylamine via acylation with acid anhydride.

1.5
Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis
Solid phase peptide synthesis is a range of techniques and procedures for the synthesis of small peptides to large
proteins[35][36]. Peptide synthesis involves numerous repetitive steps, so when the technique was invented it
revolutionised the ﬁeld completely. The basics of the techniques revolve around attaching a functional unit,
the linker, to porous polymer beads (the resin), followed by covalent bonding of an amino acid and subsequent
iterative building of a peptide before cleaving the product compound with an acid or base depending on the
linker. The synthetic principles are that the N -protected C-terminal amino acid residues is attached via its
carboxyl group to the linker which usually contains a hydroxyl, chloride, or amino group which will yield a
ester or amide linkage, resulting in either a C-termianl acid or C-terminal amide respectively. After this ﬁrst
loading of the resin, the peptide sequence is assembled until reaching the desired N-terminal. This can be
seen in Scheme 12. The functional groups of the amino acid side-chains are usually protected with permanent
protecting groups, while the α-amino group is protected by a temporary protecting group, usually Boc or Fmoc.
SPPS procedures are divided into two groups depending on whether a Boc or an Fmoc group is used.
11
==PAGE:19==
Scheme 12: The principle of SPPS. Adapted from Amblard et al.[35]
Solid phase synthesis in general is very useful because excess reagents and side products are separated from
the product bound to the resin easily by ﬁltration and washing. This also means that the entire synthesis can
be performed in the same vessel, and no transferring of the resin is needed.
As mentioned, a wide range of procedures for SPPS exists, and the version used in this project is described in
detail in Section 5.2.

1.6
Aim of the Study
The aim of this study is to develop a novel synthetic method wherein glycoconjugates are synthesised from
three components, some of which have immunostimulatory eﬀects. The three components are: A carbohydrate,
an amine, and a carboxylic acid. The synthetic strategy to be used is based on previous work by Blomberg
et al. [25] and Boas et al. [29], but here the acylation step was done with Ac2O to stabilise the glycosidic bond.
The goal of this project is to use this acylation step to introduce further functionalities to the molecule via
diﬀerent acids. This strategy was described in Section 1.3 and is summarised in Scheme 13. Some preliminary
experiments were done by the author[37], and the initial work in this thesis is based on previous ﬁndings. The
purpose of this to create a new synthetic method for creating glycoconjugates which will be both stable and
fully biologically active due to their central amide bond. Other methods using larger and more complex bonds
as described in Section 1.1 exist, but using a simple amide bond in this fashion is a novel technique.
Scheme 13: Synthetic strategies for this project.
Various compounds from the three groups are to be tested for the method to attempt to determine the
optimal components for the general method. The goal is to use an amine attached to a solid phase support
for convenient and fast synthesis. Various reaction conditions such as solvent, temperature, reaction time, and

eventual reagents and catalysts will be investigated in search of the optimal procedure for this synthesis. Fatty

Results and Discussion
As stated earlier, the work in this project begins on the basis of earlier work done by the author[37]. Earlier
experiments were done with simple amines such as decylamine, but also with short peptides where the N-
terminal amine was glycosylated. The amines were glycosylated with mannose under basic conditions. From
the general acid lability of the glycosidic linkage, it was expected that acidic conditions were detrimental, but
that basic conditions were beneﬁcial was not a given. However, a base could help stabilise the imine intermediate
during glycosylation, thus favouring the formation of the glycosylamine. Subsequent to the formation of the
glycosylamine it was acylated with acids such as Boc-6-Ahx-OH and palmitic acid. Several diﬀerent acids were
used to show the compatibility of the method with diﬀerently sized reactants. DMSO was determined to be the
best solvent when wishing to avoid bisglycosylation of the amine. It was determined that an excess of base (up
to ﬁve equivalents) was beneﬁcial to the reaction.
Due to time and instrument limitations, only HPLC/MS analysis was used in this project. Almost all reactions
were not worked up, and only the reaction mixture was analysed to monitor the reaction.
All compound
detection was based on MS, and as such, could be erroneous.

Three-Component Reaction with Hydroxylamines
As mentioned in Section 1.4, investigation into performing the three-component reaction with a hydroxylamine
instead of an amine could be very interesting. For this reason a small series of experiments was conducted
trying take the three-component reaction method developed previously combined with a procedure developed
by Peri et al. [33] and apply it to hydroxylamines.
For the initial experiment, a simple hydroxylamine with a chromophore for UV-detection was chosen: N -
Benzylhydroxylamine. N -Benzylhydroxylamine was glycosylated with glucose at room temperature followed by
acylation with hexanoic acid. The desired product (94) was observed by HPLC/MS as well as some bisacylated
hydroxylamine (95) (see appendix A.50). In this initial experiment, there was no noticeable diﬀerence between
the amount of product (94) formed after four hours and after over-night reaction.
Therefore, the time of
acylation was investigated, comparing one hour, two hours, and over-night (see appendix A.51 and Figure 20).
This experiment showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the ratio of desired product (94) to bisacylated hydroxylamine
(95) even between one and two hours of reaction time. After 1 hour, the ratio of 94 to 95 was 1:6.2, after 2
hours 1:3.3, and ﬁnally after over night reaction 1:2.5. Of course, the reaction was performed with excess of
hydroxylamine and acylating reactant, which could be the reason. Additionally, the next step was to perform
this reaction in solid phase, where the optimal reaction time might be diﬀerent.

3
Conclusion
The goal of this project was to develop a novel synthetic method for the synthesis of stable and biologically
active glycoconjugates from three components: A carbohydrate, an amine, and an acid.
Several carbohydrates, including monosaccharides (glucose, galactose, and mannose) and oligosaccharides (mal-
tose and maltotriose) were used to glycosylate the amines. Initially, mannose were used, but several experiments
of lesser success proved that mannose was a very cumbersome carbohydrate to work with, and the project was
carried on using other sugars.
The amines used were small peptides of two to ﬁve amino acids, such that the method could be developed
for solid phase synthesis. Solid phase synthesis is an easy and fast synthetic technique ideal for synthesising
a large library of compounds, and several diﬀerent peptides were synthesised on various resin types.
Both
simple peptides such as H-Ala-Phe-Phe-OH, but also immunologically important ones such as H-Ala-isoGlu-OH
were used. The investigation revealed that some more bulky peptides like H-Phe-Phe-OH were diﬃcult to fully
glycosylate, while adding a hexane spacer creating peptides such as H-Ahx-Phe-Phe-OH greatly increased the
yield of three-component product in most cases.
Finally, various fatty acids were used to acylate this glycosylated amine, primarily hexanoic and palmitic acid.
These were used both to stabilise the glycosidic bond, but most importantly to introduce a third functionality,
in this case increasing the hydrophobicity of the three-component compound.
In addtion to this, a variation of the three-component reaction using a hydroxylamine instead of the amine
was also investigated based on work by Peri et al., who showed that glycosylation of a hydroxylamine is highly
chemoselective, even in presence of amines. This variation of the three-component reaction was employed to
moderate success.
The reaction conditions for the three-component reaction were investigated and optimal conditions were found,
both in terms of reaction temperature, time, and reagents. The reaction was performed both in solution phase
and solid phase with procedures for both amines and hydroxylamines as seen in Section 5. Some examples of
the compounds synthesised using the three-component reaction are shown in Figure 22.

4
Outlook
As mentioned in Section 2.2, the three-component reaction was developed to a satisfactory version, but due to
time limitations much work still remains. Work-up and puriﬁcation procedures were not investigated, but it
was assumed that preparative HPLC would be a suitable puriﬁcation process. The standard work-up procedure
used in most organic syntheses is as follows:
1. Quenching with H2O
2. Extraction with an organic solvent such as EtOAc or DCM
3. Washing with H2O/brine
4. Drying with Na2SO4
5. Filtration
6. Concentration in vacuo
7. Freeze-drying
Since the hydrophilicities of the reactants are suﬃciently varied such that it would be possible to separate
them in diﬀerent solvent phases, a similar work-up procedure could possibly be found without much investi-
gation. Following the full development of the three-component reaction procedure, it should be tested with
other components, initially some similar to the ones already used successfully in this project. For the car-
bohydrate, larger oligosaccharides (similar to maltotriose) or more complex and immunologically important
monosaccharides (similar to mannose and N -acetyl glucosamine) could be used. For the amine/hydroxylamine,
only peptides were used in this project. The main reason for this was that peptides are easy to synthesise with
SPPS, but many other amines could be interesting, even ones that can be attached to solid phase support.
It would be very interesting to further investigate the eﬀect of the size of the amine, as that was one of the
main problems in this project. Lastly, for the acid, the possibilities are endless. The only type of acid used
in this project was fatty acids because of their relevance to biological systems, but many other types could be
interesting. For example more bulky acids could be used to investigate the limits of the method in this regard.
Even other carbohydrates such as sialic acid could be very interesting to employ.
Subsequent to the completion of the full three-component reaction method many diﬀerent compounds should
be synthesised and tested for biological activity using various assays. One type of assay which would be very
interesting to use is a lectin assay. The mannose-binding lectin was previously described in Section 1.2, and
could be ideal for this purpose, but many other proteins could be used as long as they can bind one of the three
components.
The last thing of these initial future parts of the project is testing the stability of the compounds produced by
this method. It is assume that the amide bond created is stable, but it would be interesting to investigate the
rate of degradation in diﬀerent conditions such as acidic, basic, or high temperature.
Since this project deals with basic methodology, the possibilities for further work, future projects, and side
projects are virtually inﬁnite and the above described are just a fraction of the interesting work that lies
ahead.
