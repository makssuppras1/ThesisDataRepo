Abstract
Functional near­infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) is a non­invasive imaging technique in­
creasingly used to study auditory processing due to its portability, silence, and tolerance
for movement. However, extracting reliable and reproducible signals, especially at the
individual level, remains a challenge. One important factor influencing fNIRS outcomes is
the choice of region­of­interest (ROI), which is commonly based on predefined anatomical
locations. These predefined ROIs may fail to account for individual anatomical variability
and may not align with the actual functional activation patterns of each participant.
This thesis investigates whether a data­driven approach to ROI selection can improve the
test­retest reliability of auditory fNIRS responses compared to traditional, anatomically
predefined ROIs. A passive listening paradigm using speech, noise, and silence stimuli
was employed in a test–retest design across 24 participants. After preprocessing and ar­
tifact rejection, both waveform averaging analysis (WAA) and generalized linear modeling
(GLM) were used to identify activation patterns and derive data­driven ROIs. Reliability
was assessed at the group and individual levels using Pearson correlation coefficients (for
ROI­level waveform similarity) and reproducibility metrics (RQUANTITY and ROVERLAP, for
channel­level activation overlap). Additionally, Intraclass Correlation Coefficients (ICC)
were computed at the group level to quantify test–retest consistency across sessions,
both at the channel and ROI levels.
The results show that at the group level, data­driven ROIs, particularly those obtained
through GLM, led to more consistent and robust activation compared to predefined ROIs.
Speech and noise conditions elicited largely overlapping functional ROIs in the right au­
ditory cortex, supporting the hypothesis of shared auditory processing pathways. At the
individual level, Pearson correlation analysis showed that data­driven ROIs led to higher
waveform similarity across sessions compared to predefined ROIs, indicating improved
test–retest reliability at the ROI level. However, reliability remained lower than that ob­
served at the group level.
Overall, the findings support the use of data­driven ROI selection, particularly with GLM,
as a means of improving the reliability of auditory fNIRS studies. However, the limited
reproducibility at the individual level underscores the need for improved data quality and
preprocessing techniques to enable reliable single­subject analysis.

Introduction
Functional near­infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) is a non­invasive neuroimaging technique
that measures cortical brain activity by detecting changes in oxygenated (HbO) and de­
oxygenated hemoglobin (HbR) concentrations. It operates by emitting near­infrared light
through the scalp and skull and measuring the reflected light, providing indirect markers of
neural activity associated with cerebral hemodynamics. Compared to traditional imaging
modalities such as fMRI, fNIRS is portable, silent, and cost­effective, making it particularly
suitable for studies in auditory and developmental neuroscience [1].
Despite these advantages, extracting reliable information from fNIRS signals remains
challenging due to motion artifacts, physiological noise such as heart rate and breath­
ing, and variations in optode placement. While many sources of noise can be reduced
through preprocessing, issues related to optode placement, such as inconsistent posi­
tioning or poor contact with the scalp, depend on the setup and calibration of the cap and
cannot be corrected afterwards. To improve signal quality, extensive preprocessing is of­
ten needed to address other noise sources. Common techniques include short­separation
channel regression to remove superficial signals, motion correction using principal com­
ponent analysis (PCA) or wavelength decomposition, and statistical modeling with gen­
eral linear models (GLMs) combined with spatial averaging at the region level [2, 3, 4].
These methods have been shown to improve the quality and interpretability of fNIRS data,
especially in studies involving passive listening and other low­effort cognitive tasks.
Even with such preprocessing pipelines in place, a key concern in fNIRS research is
the reliability and reproducibility of results. Numerous studies have evaluated test­retest
reliability in auditory and cognitive paradigms and consistently report a disparity between
group­level and individual­level outcomes. While group­level averages often yield robust
and reproducible activation patterns, individual­level responses tend to be more variable
and less reliable [5, 6, 7, 8]. For example, in event­related designs and verbal fluency
tasks, group averages demonstrate high reproducibility, yet significant intra­ and inter­
subject variability is observed across sessions [5, 7].
Similar patterns have emerged in passive auditory paradigms, where fNIRS responses to
stimuli such as speech and noise produce consistent activation at the group level but suffer
from lower reliability in individual participants [9, 8]. This variability may, in part, result from
the use of anatomically predefined regions of interest (ROIs), which are typically based
on standard cortical atlases or sensor layouts [10]. Such fixed ROIs do not account for
inter­individual differences in cortical folding, head shape, or optode placement, and may
therefore misalign with true functional regions [8, 2].
A central question of this work is whether defining ROIs in a data­driven, functional man­
ner can improve the reliability of fNIRS results, especially at the individual level, com­
pared to conventional anatomical ROIs. While other studies have applied data­driven
approaches [11], few studies have directly compared activation­based ROIs with prede­
fined anatomical ones in the context of test­retest reliability. This lack of comparative
analysis is particularly important given the growing need for individualized neuroimaging
methods, especially in clinical and translational research.
Evidence from prior studies supports the idea that different auditory stimuli elicit distinct
patterns of cortical activation. Increased activity in superior temporal regions has been
linked to speech intelligibility [12, 13], and decoding analyses have shown that speech

and noise can be distinguished based on cortical responses [14, 15]. However, these
findings are predominantly derived from group­level analyses. Whether such distinctions
persist consistently at the individual level across sessions has not been systematically
examined.
A relevant study by Luke et al. [3] compared waveform averaging and finite impulse re­
sponse (FIR) GLM modeling for fNIRS data in a passive listening task using speech and
noise stimuli. Despite methodological differences, both approaches yielded similar group­
level activation patterns in the superior temporal gyri (STG), a key region for auditory
processing. The addition of short­separation channels improved individual­level signal
detection but had minimal impact on group­level outcomes. However, the study relied
exclusively on anatomically predefined ROIs.
Building on this methodology, the present work investigates whether functionally defined,
data­driven ROIs can enhance the test­retest reliability of fNIRS measurements at both
the group and individual levels. Specifically, we examine whether the ROI definition pro­
cess itself can be optimized to improve the reliability and interpretability of fNIRS results.
By leveraging functional activation patterns to define ROIs in a data­driven manner, we
aim to determine whether this approach yields more consistent and robust responses
to auditory stimuli than traditional anatomical definitions. This hypothesis is based on
the premise that functional ROIs better reflect individual cortical anatomy and activation
profiles. Additionally, we expect that speech and noise conditions will elicit largely over­
lapping ROIs, reflecting shared auditory processing pathways.
To address these questions, the present study is structured around the following objec­
tives:
1. Expand the dataset from previous master’s theses by conducting the test–retest
auditory fNIRS experiment developed in [16] with additional participants.
2. Develop a robust preprocessing pipeline to address low­quality fNIRS signals, in­
cluding artifact correction, signal filtering, and quality control procedures.
3. Define data­driven regions of interest (ROIs) for each auditory condition (Speech
and Noise), using activation patterns identified through two different methods: Wave­
form Averaging Analysis (WAA) and Generalized Linear Modeling (GLM).
4. Extract both group­level and individual­level activation responses across conditions
and sessions.
5. Investigate the influence of low­quality channels and phenotypic factors over the
individual­level activation.
6. Compare the test–retest reliability of fNIRS responses using predefined anatomical
ROIs versus the proposed data­driven ROIs.
7. Evaluate whether the data­driven ROI approach enhances reliability at both the
group and individual levels.

Functional Near­Infrared Spectroscopy
Functional Near­Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS) is a non­invasive neuroimaging technique
that estimates changes in cortical hemoglobin concentrations by exploiting the absorption
characteristics of biological tissues in the near­infrared spectrum. Human tissue exhibits
a spectral window between approximately 700 nm and 950 nm in which near­infrared light
can penetrate relatively deep, as absorption from water is minimal and the scattering from
other biological components is reduced [17]. This range is often referred to as the “optical
window”, with the upper bound limited by water absorption above 900 nm, and the lower
bound limited by strong hemoglobin absorption below 700 nm.
Within this window, oxyhemoglobin (HbO) and deoxyhemoglobin (HbR) display distinct
absorption profiles: HbO absorbs more strongly at wavelengths above 800 nm, whereas
HbR absorbs more at shorter wavelengths. These spectral differences enable the differ­
entiation of changes in oxygenation levels in cortical tissue by using multiple wavelengths
of light.
fNIRS systems typically consist of light sources and detectors (optodes) positioned on
the scalp. Photons emitted from the source travel through the scalp, skull, and superficial
cortical layers, where they undergo both absorption and scattering. A portion of these
photons are scattered back toward the detector along a characteristic “banana­shaped”
trajectory, allowing the system to capture localized changes in chromophore concentra­
tions near the cortical surface [17]. Importantly, the source­detector (SD) separation plays
a crucial role in determining the penetration depth of the near­infrared light and, conse­
quently, the anatomical origin of the measured signal. As illustrated in Figure 2.1, short
SD separations primarily sample superficial tissues, such as the scalp and skull, whereas
longer SD separations enable deeper penetration into cortical brain tissue.
Figure 2.1: Schematic of photon propagation paths at different source­detector separa­
tions. Short­separation channels primarily capture signals from superficial tissues, while
longer­separation channels allow light to penetrate deeper, reaching cortical brain tissue.

Neural activity itself does not directly alter light absorption; instead, fNIRS relies on the
principle of neurovascular coupling. When a specific brain region becomes more ac­
tive, its metabolic demand increases, which triggers a compensatory increase in regional
cerebral blood flow. This results in a transient increase in HbO and a relative decrease in
HbR, known as the hemodynamic response. Although the underlying molecular mecha­
nisms remain incompletely understood, evidence suggests a coordinated interaction be­
tween neurons, astrocytes, and vascular components via a complex cascade of signaling
molecules [19].
To quantify changes in hemoglobin concentrations from measured light intensities, fNIRS
uses the Modified Beer–Lambert Law (MBLL) [20]. This law accounts for both absorption
and scattering in tissue:
∆A = ε(λ) · ∆c · d · DPF(λ)
(2.1)
Here, ∆A is the change in optical attenuation measured by the detector; ε(λ) is the ex­
tinction coefficient of hemoglobin at wavelength λ; ∆c is the change in chromophore con­
centration; d is the source–detector separation; and DPF(λ) is the differential pathlength
factor, which corrects for the increased path length due to photon scattering. The DPF
depends on several factors including age, wavelength, and tissue type, and is a crucial
parameter in accurate quantification [20].
Figure 2.2 illustrates the various outcomes of photon interaction with biological tissue:
photons may be transmitted without interaction with the surrounding tissues, be refracted,
be absorbed, be scattered out of the detection path, or be scattered and redirected toward
the detector. Only the last class of photons contributes meaningfully to the fNIRS signal.
Taken together, these principles form the foundation of fNIRS as a tool for studying func­
tional brain activity. By measuring light attenuation at multiple wavelengths and correcting
for pathlength and scattering effects, fNIRS enables the estimation of dynamic changes in
HbO and HbR concentrations in the outer cortex during cognitive or sensory tasks. How­
ever, due to the limited penetration depth of near­infrared light, fNIRS primarily captures
signals from superficial cortical layers. As such, the technique is better suited for assess­
ing activation in higher­level auditory regions, such as the superior temporal gyrus (STG)
[21].

Methodology
This chapter outlines the methodological framework adopted to investigate auditory re­
gions activation using functional near­infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) and assess its test­
retest reliability. The structure follows a logical progression, beginning with the exper­
imental design, including the stimulus protocol, participant setup, and data acquisition
parameters. This is followed by a detailed account of the preprocessing pipeline applied
to the raw fNIRS signals to ensure signal quality and minimize artifacts. Subsequently, two
analytical approaches are presented: waveform averaging analysis (WAA), and the Gen­
eralized Linear Model (GLM). For each analytical approach, the process of data­driven
ROI generation is described, along with analyses conducted at both the group and indi­
vidual levels. The chapter then explores variability in individual­level activation patterns
across sessions, followed by a dedicated section on test­retest reliability analysis. To­
gether, these sections form a comprehensive methodological basis for evaluating the ro­
bustness and reproducibility of auditory fNIRS measurements.
3.1
Experimental Design
This study builds upon the experimental protocol developed by Luke et al. [3], which
focused on analyzing auditory­evoked responses using fNIRS but did not include a test–
retest component. In a previous master’s thesis, Andreas Hafstrøm adapted this paradigm
to our laboratory setting, incorporated a test–retest design, and collected initial data to in­
vestigate individual­level reliability of auditory­evoked fNIRS responses [16]. The primary
objective was to assess auditory cortical responses under a passive listening paradigm us­
ing functional near­infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) with a test­retest setup. For this study,
a total of 24 participants were measured, with each undergoing two sessions separated
by at least three hours, including cap removal from session to session. Among the par­
ticipant measurements, this thesis extended the original dataset from 12 to 24 participant
measurements.
3.1.1
Participants
Twenty­four participants (11 male, 13 female; mean age = 28.3 years, SD = 3.9) were re­
cruited for this study. Inclusion criteria required normal hearing and proficiency in Danish,
due to the use of Danish­language auditory stimuli. Hearing was assessed using the Affin­
ity Compact audiometer and insert earphones (ER­3A, Interacoustics). All participants
had pure­tone air conduction thresholds of 20 dB HL or better at all octave frequencies
between 0.125 and 6 kHz, and 30 dB HL or better at 8 kHz in both ears.
To support subsequent analyses related to signal quality and optical attenuation in the
fNIRS recordings, participants also self­reported their skin color, hair color, and hair thick­
ness. Skin tone was classified using the Fitzpatrick scale, a widely accepted dermatolog­
ical classification system [23]. This information was collected given prior findings that
phenotypic characteristics, particularly skin pigmentation, can influence light absorption
and, consequently, the accuracy and reliability of fNIRS measurements [24]. Table 3.1
summarizes the phenotypic characteristics of all measured participants.
All participants provided written informed consent prior to their participation. The study
protocol was approved by the Science­Ethics Committee for the Capital Region of Den­
mark (reference H­16036391).

3.1.2
Equipment and Calibration
Data were collected using a NIRx NIRScoutX system (wavelengths: 785 and 830 nm) with
avalanche photodiode detectors. Signals were recorded via NIRStar software. Audio was
delivered using an RME Fireface UCX soundcard and played through a Yamaha model
HS5I speaker positioned at head level.
Sound levels were calibrated with a Brüel & Kjær 2250 meter and 4189 microphone. To
ensure SPL matching, speech and noise stimuli were RMS­equalized. Noise was further
amplified to ensure a 5 dB increase over the speech condition, similar to the protocol
followed in [3].
3.1.3
Auditory Stimuli and Presentation
Three types of auditory stimuli were used: speech, noise, and silence (control), each
presented 20 times per session (60 total stimulus trials). The inter­stimulus interval was
varied uniformly between 10 and 20 seconds.
• Noise: Band­passed white noise (300–700 Hz) at 75 dB SPL, 5 s duration.
• Speech: Three concatenated Danish sentences (5.25 s) at 70 dB SPL, sourced
from [25].
• Silence: 5­second silent periods.
Each session lasted approximately 20 minutes. Participants were seated in a soundproof,

The optode placement on the scalp was distributed as follows:
• Sources: AF7, F3, F7, FC5, T7, CP5, O1, POz, O2, Iz, CP6, T8
• Detectors: F5, C5, TP7, CP3, P5, PO3, PO4, Oz, P6, CP4, TP8, C6
• Short­channel detectors: AF7, F7, T7, CP5, O1, O2, CP6, T8

3.2
Preprocessing of fNIRS Data
This section describes the preprocessing steps applied to the fNIRS data, which were
adapted from the pipeline proposed by Luke et al. [3] to better suit our dataset and exper­
imental design. We first outline the preprocessing steps, followed by criteria for participant
exclusion, and conclude with a custom epoch rejection strategy developed to enhance
signal quality. An overview of the entire workflow is shown in Figure 3.3.
3.2.1
Signal Preprocessing Pipeline
The fNIRS data were preprocessed using a custom pipeline implemented in Python, pri­
marily utilizing the MNE­NIRS toolbox. The preprocessing steps were largely based on
the procedure described by Luke et al. [3], with some modifications introduced to better
accommodate the characteristics of our dataset and experimental design. The primary
objective of the pipeline was to obtain clean and reliable hemodynamic signals from long­
distance source­detector pairs for subsequent analysis.
The following steps were applied in sequence:
1. Bad Channel Identification: Channels were automatically excluded if their scalp
coupling index (SCI) [27] fell below 0.8 in the 0.7–1.45 Hz frequency band, indicating
8
The effects of region selection methods on the reliability of auditory fNIRS responses
==PAGE:16==
poor optode­skin contact or unstable signals [3].
2. Motion Artifact Correction and Superficial Signal Regression: Temporal Deriva­
tive Distribution Repair (TDDR) [28] was applied to reduce the influence of motion
artifacts. Subsequently, short­channel regression [29] was used to remove superfi­
cial physiological signals by regressing out the average short­channel contribution
from each long channel.
3. Optical Density and Hemoglobin Conversion: Cleaned intensity signals from
long­distance channels (source­detector distance >20 mm) were converted to changes
in hemoglobin concentration using the modified Beer–Lambert law with a partial
pathlength factor of 0.1 [3].
4. Negative Correlation Enhancement: A negative correlation enhacement [30] was
applied over the raw hemoglobing concentration signals to improve the signal qual­
ity.
5. Bandpass Filtering: A zero­phase fifth­order Butterworth bandpass filter between
0.05 Hz and 0.2 Hz was applied to isolate the low­frequency hemodynamic re­
sponse. The cut­off frequencies were adjusted from the ones reported in [3] as
it was visually observed that non­relevant physiological signals, such us heart rate
and respiration, were not removed using the bandpass filter with cuttoffs 0.01 and
0.7 Hz.
6. Epoching and Baseline Correction: The data were segmented into epochs from
–3 to +14 seconds relative to stimulus onset. A linear detrending step was applied

3.3
Waveform Averaging Analysis (WAA)
Waveform Averaging Analysis (WAA) is a widely used technique to summarize the hemo­
dynamic response by averaging time­locked segments of the fNIRS signal across re­
peated stimulus presentations. This method enhances the signal­to­noise ratio by atten­
uating random fluctuations while amplifying consistent, stimulus­evoked responses.
Previous studies have shown that the timing of peak oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO) re­
sponses varies across cortical regions [32]. For instance, in response to speech stimuli,
HbO peaks typically occur around 5.2 seconds post­stimulus in the bilateral superior tem­
poral gyri (STG), and around 6.7 seconds in the left inferior frontal gyrus (L­IFG) [32].
The effects of region selection methods on the reliability of auditory fNIRS responses
13
==PAGE:21==
These peak timings are based on stimuli with a duration of approximately 5 seconds,
highlighting that the hemodynamic response timing is also influenced by stimulus length.
As a result, many studies adopt a fixed averaging window between 5 and 7 seconds to
capture the bulk of the task­evoked response [3].
However, to better tailor this window to the characteristics of our data, we conducted
an empirical analysis across all participants and conditions to identify the time interval
that captures the highest number of peak responses. This analysis revealed that, in our
dataset, a window from 4.25 to 6.75 seconds post­stimulus captures a larger quantity of
HbO peaks across ROIs and conditions. This interval was considered the best compro­
mise between maximizing the number of included peak responses and minimizing the
influence of surrounding noise or unrelated signal fluctuations, thereby improving the ro­
bustness of the mean response estimation.
In the present study, WAA was applied to the HbO signal, which is considered more sen­
sitive to task­related cortical activity in auditory processing paradigms [5].
3.3.1
WAA Data­Driven ROIs Generation Process
Data­driven ROIs were defined based on a statistical analysis of the WAA signals at the
group level. Specifically, WAA was first performed independently for each long­distance
optode, condition (Control, Noise, Speech), and session. This produced one condition­
specific hemodynamic waveform per optode, per subject, per session.
To identify optodes that consistently responded to auditory stimuli at the group level, a lin­
ear mixed­effects (LME) model was applied to the WAA­derived time courses of each op­
tode across all participants. For a general overview of LME models, refer to Appendix A.2.
The specific model used in this analysis was defined as:
Value ∼Condition + (1|Subject/Session)
(3.2)
Here, Value denotes the HbO amplitude at each time point, Condition is a fixed effect
with three levels (control, noise, speech), and Subject/Session is included as a random
intercept to account for between­participant variability.
Statistical significance was assessed for the Control vs. Noise and Control vs. Speech
contrasts. A 5% false discovery rate (FDR) correction was applied across optodes, and
only those optodes that survived this correction in both sessions were considered sig­

• Speech–WAA ROI: all optodes significantly activated in response to the speech

3.3.3
WAA Individual­Level Analysis
While the group­level analyses provided insights into generalizable patterns, we also con­
ducted an individual­level assessment to evaluate the reproducibility of activation at the
single­subject level. Two complementary strategies were applied: a channel­wise ap­

In the channel­wise analysis, we compared Control vs. Noise and Control vs. Speech

was classified as activated for the condition comparison (Control vs Noise or Control vs

3.5
Effects of Variation in Individual­Level Activation
To investigate factors that may influence individual­level activation detection in fNIRS
data, we conducted two complementary analyses. The first focused on the impact of
poor­quality channels on ROI activation outcomes, while the second assessed the role of
differences in subject’s phenotypic characteristics, specifically skin type and hair style.
3.5.1
Bad Channel Analysis
Channel quality was assessed using the Scalp Coupling Index (SCI) [27], with a threshold
of 0.8 used to identify bad channels. For each session, source­detector (SD) pairs with
SCI values below this threshold were marked as bad and categorized according to their
predefined ROI (frontal, left auditory, right auditory, or visual). This allowed us to compute
the frequency of bad channels across the 32 sessions and assess their distribution by
region.
To evaluate the potential impact of channel quality on activation detection, we merged the
bad channel information with individual­level ROI activation results obtained from both the
WAA and GLM pipelines. Activation status was treated as a binary variable, defined as 1
if activation was detected in a given ROI for a session, and 0 otherwise. Each ROI was
then classified into two groups based on its channel quality: ”Low” (no bad channels) and
”High” (one or more bad channels). Associations between activation status and channel
quality were tested using Fisher’s exact test, a non­parametric method well­suited for
small sample sizes and categorical data.
In addition to the predefined ROIs, we constructed a composite region named All_Opt,
which included all long­distance SD pairs. This allowed us to assess whether overall data
quality, across the full cap, was associated with activation detection.
Importantly, the Fisher’s exact test analysis was performed independently for each ROI,
each activation detection method (GLM and WAA), and each condition (Noise and Speech),
in order to examine whether the relationship between activation detection and channel
quality varied across these factors.

3.6
Test­Retest Reliability Analysis
Following the completion of analyses using both the WAA and GLM approaches at the
group and individual levels across all ROIs, test–retest reliability between the first and
second sessions was assessed using three complementary metrics. The Intraclass Cor­
relation Coefficient (ICC) was used at the group level to evaluate consistency of activation
strength across sessions. The RQUANTITY and ROVERLAP metrics were applied at both the
group and individual levels to assess reproducibility of channel­level activation patterns.
Finally, the Pearson correlation coefficient was used to evaluate waveform similarity at
the ROI level. These metrics were chosen to capture different aspects of reliability across
spatial scales and analysis levels.
3.6.1
Intraclass Correlation Coefficient
The Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) is a statistical metric used to evaluate the reli­
ability of repeated measurements, particularly in assessing test­retest consistency of neu­
roimaging modalities such as functional near­infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS). ICC quanti­
fies the proportion of total variance in observed measurements that is attributable to dif­
ferences between subjects, as opposed to measurement error or variability across testing
occasions. This makes it a valuable tool for determining the stability of brain activation
patterns over time.
To select the appropriate ICC model, we followed the decision­making flowchart proposed
by Li et al. [34] (See Figure 3.7), which guides model selection based on study design
characteristics. The first consideration is whether the test effect (in our case, Session) is
negligible. This is not the case as we cannot assume that session­related variability is ab­
sent, due to potential factors such as fatigue, habituation, or minor procedural differences.
The next decision point is whether the test effect is treated as a fixed or random effect.
In our study, the sessions are assumed to be representative samples of a broader set of
possible test­retest scenarios. Therefore, we treat Session as a random effect. Finally,
we are interested in the reliability of single measurements (not averaged values), so we
use the single­measurement form of ICC.

To assess the similarity in the temporal dynamics of the hemodynamic response across

4.4.1
Intraclass Correlation Coefficient
To evaluate the test–retest reliability of fNIRS activation measures across sessions, the In­
traclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) was computed following the ICC(2,1) model, a two­
way random­effects model for single measurements assessing absolute agreement. ICC
values were calculated for both region­of­interest (ROI) clusters and individual Source­
Detector (SD) pairs, separately for Waveform Averaging Analysis (WAA) and General
Linear Model (GLM) approaches.
Table 4.7 presents Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) values at the ROI level for
both the Waveform Averaging Analysis (WAA) and the General Linear Model (GLM) ap­
proaches. Significance is determined at α = 0.05. As described in section 3.6.1, ICC
values are interpreted using common thresholds: ICC < 0.40 indicates poor reliability;
0.40–0.59 is fair; 0.60–0.74 is good; and 0.75–1.00 is excellent.
Under WAA, the Left and Right Auditory ROIs yielded fair reliability (ICC = 0.436 and
0.426, respectively), both statistically significant. The Frontal ROI showed good reliability
(ICC = 0.601, significant), whereas the Visual ROI had poor reliability (ICC = 0.0017, non­
significant), indicating almost no consistency across sessions. For the data­driven ROIs,
ICCs were also in the fair to good range. Noise WAA (ICC = 0.499), Speech WAA (ICC =
0.422) and Noise GLM (ICC = 0.559) showed fair reliability, both statistically significant.
Notably, Speech GLM had a lower ICC (0.377) and did not reach significance, falling into
the poor reliability category.

4.4.3
Pearson Correlation Coefficient
As described in Section 3.6.3, the Pearson correlation coefficient was computed over the
timecourse of waveforms for the test and retest sessions across all conditions, at both
the individual and group levels. This metric assesses the similarity in the shape of the
waveforms between session 01 and session 02, based on the average signal across
optodes within each ROI. In addition to predefined and data­driven ROIs, the analysis
included a composite ROI labeled All Optodes, which encompasses all 25 SD pairs.
Starting with the individual­level analysis, Table 4.11 presents the Pearson correlation
coefficients for each ROI across conditions and participants. For the Control condition,
correlations were consistently low across all ROIs and participants, with several coeffi­
cients even being negative. This indicates a lack of consistent waveform shape across
sessions under baseline stimulation. The mean r value across participants and ROIs was
close to zero, suggesting minimal to no test­retest reliability in this condition.
In contrast, correlation values increased for the Noise condition. Although some partici­
pants still exhibited low correlations for certain ROIs (e.g., participant 5 had r = 0.06 for the
Right Auditory ROI under the Noise WAA analysis), others demonstrated remarkably high
correlations. For example, participant 19 showed values as high as r = 0.99 for multiple
ROIs, including Right Auditory, Speech WAA, and Noise GLM, indicating near­perfect re­
producibility. Overall, when averaging r values across participants, most ROIs remained
in the low range (<0.4), with the exception of the Noise GLM and Speech GLM ROIs, which
reached moderate reliability levels with mean r values of 0.42 and 0.41, respectively.
For the Speech condition, correlation coefficients were even higher. Five out of nine ROIs
showed average r values exceeding 0.4, including one predefined ROI (Right Auditory)
and all four data­driven ROIs. Notably, the Noise GLM ROI reached an average r = 0.61,
the highest correlation observed across all ROIs and conditions, suggesting a moderate
to strong level of test­retest reliability in this specific configuration.

5
Discussion
This chapter provides a comprehensive interpretation of the key findings from our fNIRS
analysis. We begin by analyzing the data­driven ROIs identified through our preprocess­
ing and analytical pipeline. Subsequently, we interpret the activation patterns observed
at the group level and then focus on the individual­level results to evaluate consistency
and inter­subject variability. In addition to exploring the functional insights provided by
the activation maps, we assess the test–retest reliability of the measurements to deter­
mine the robustness of our findings across sessions. Finally, we discuss the limitations
of the current study and propose directions for future work, followed by a set of practical
recommendations for working with fNIRS data in similar research contexts.
5.1
Data­Driven ROI Interpretation
This section discusses the spatial distribution and functional interpretation of the data­
driven ROIs identified using WAA and GLM methods for both the Noise and Speech con­
ditions. The data­driven ROIs are compared both within and across methods and condi­
tions, in the context of predefined anatomical regions of interest: Left Auditory (L­STG),
Right Auditory (R­STG), Visual (OCC), and Frontal (L­IFG), visually displayed in Figure
3.2.
5.1.1
Comparison of Noise and Speech WAA­Derived ROIs
The data­driven ROIs derived using the WAA method for the Noise and Speech conditions
exhibit substantial spatial overlap, as illustrated in Figure 5.1a. Both WAA ROIs consist
of seven SD pairs, five of which are shared between the two conditions, with two unique
pairs per contrast. This shared core includes SD pairs S10_D11, S10_D12, S11_D11,
S11_D12,and S10_D9, all of which are located within the Right Auditory (R­STG) region.
The presence of these channels in both ROIs suggests that auditory stimulation, for both
speech and noise, consistently engages the right superior temporal gyrus, a region well­
known for its role in auditory processing.
Condition­specific differences also emerge. The Speech WAA ROI also includes S10_D10,
which lies within the R­STG region, and S5_D4 that belongs to the left auditory region (L­
STG), suggesting that for speech there is an expanded recruitment of auditory channels
in both right and left hemispheres . In contrast, the Noise WAA ROI includes SD pairs
S6_D8 and S4_D2, corresponding to the OCC (Visual) and L­STG (Left Auditory) regions,
respectively.
Overall, the WAA­derived ROIs exhibit consistent engagement of auditory regions across
both conditions, with the R­STG emerging as a central locus of activation. This suggests
a robust and shared neural response to auditory stimulation, irrespective of stimulus type.
However, this results does not match our initial hypothesis from which we were expecting
that the Speech ROI would encompass all SD pairs found in the Noise ROI, along with
additional ones reflecting increased processing demands. Although there is notable over­
lap in the right auditory region, the Noise WAA ROI includes SD pairs (S6_D8, S4_D2)
that are not present in the Speech WAA ROI. This indicates that, under the WAA ap­
proach, speech does not consistently recruit all regions engaged by noise, contrary to our
expectations.

5.1.2
Comparison of Noise and Speech GLM­Derived ROIs
The data­driven ROIs identified using the GLM method show a nested relationship be­

ROI, along with additional SD pairs. This pattern is visualized in Figure 5.1b. Specifically,
both ROIs share two SD pairs, S11_D11 and S11_D12, located in the Right Auditory
(R­STG) region, highlighting consistent right­lateralized auditory activation across both
contrasts.
In addition to the common SD pairs, the Speech GLM ROI includes four additional SD
pairs: S4_D2 and S4_D3 from the Left Auditory (L­STG) region, S9_D8 and S7_D7, from
the Visual (OCC) region. The inclusion of these extra channels suggests that speech,
compared to noise, engages a broader and bilateral network comprising both auditory
and visual processing areas.
This expansion supports our hypothesis that speech stimuli, due to their greater com­
plexity and linguistic content, would activate the same regions as noise and additionally
engage higher­order sensory areas. The GLM method appears particularly sensitive to
this graded difference, capturing both core and extended activation patterns across the
two conditions.
5.1.3

To better understand the methodological differences in data­driven ROI identification, we
compared the spatial patterns derived from WAA and GLM separately for the Noise and
Speech conditions. As shown in Figure 5.2, both methods identify overlapping auditory
regions but diverge in the extent and distribution of additional recruited areas.
For the Noise condition, both WAA and GLM ROIs share two SD pairs: S11_D11 and
S11_D12, located in the Right Auditory (R­STG) region. This overlap suggests that both
methods reliably detect the core auditory activation expected in response to noise stimuli.

The divergence between methods becomes even more apparent in the Speech condi­

hypothesis.
It is also important to highlight that among all data­driven ROIs, regardless of method
(WAA or GLM) or stimulus type (Noise or Speech), a consistent activation of two SD pairs,
S11_D11 (T8–TP8) and S11_D12 (T8–C6) , was observed. Their repeated presence
across all ROIs highlights their likely functional importance in auditory processing. This
consistency reinforces the reliability of these channels as core components of the auditory

5.2
Interpretation of Group­Level Activation
This section compares the group­level activation results obtained from two distinct anal­
ysis pipelines, WAA and GLM applied to both predefined and data­driven regions of in­
terest. By contrasting the spatial and temporal profiles of activation across methods and
stimulus types (Noise and Speech), we aim to characterize the consistency, specificity,
and sensitivity of each approach.
5.2.1
Comparison of Predefined and Data­Driven ROIs Using the WAA
Method
The WAA­based analysis revealed meaningful differences between predefined anatomi­
cal ROIs and the data­driven regions derived from activation clustering. Among the prede­
fined ROIs, the right auditory region exhibited significant activation for both the Noise and
Speech conditions, suggesting its central role in processing auditory stimuli regardless of
type. In contrast, the left auditory ROI was only significantly activated in the Noise con­
dition, potentially reflecting hemispheric asymmetries involved when processing different
stimuli types. Neither the visual nor frontal ROIs reached significance for any condition,
indicating limited involvement of these regions during passive auditory stimulation under
the WAA approach. When compared to the findings of Luke et al. [3], the results are
largely consistent, with the notable exception of the left auditory ROI: while our analysis
found it significant only for the Noise condition, Luke et al. reported significant activation
for both conditions at the group level.
In contrast, the WAA data­driven ROIs, Noise WAA and Speech WAA, demonstrated
consistent and robust sensitivity to both auditory conditions. Its important to note that this
ROIs not only captured activation for the condition they were derived from, but also gener­
alized to the alternate condition suggesting some degree of overlap or shared processing
resources in these functionally defined networks.
Interestingly, WAA also identified significant activation within GLM­defined ROIs (Noise
GLM and Speech GLM), highlighting the robustness of waveform averaging to detect
task­related effects even in ROIs derived from a different analytic framework.
5.2.2

Under the GLM framework, the results were generally more conservative, with fewer ROIs

ROI did not reach significance for either condition, which may reflect reduced sensitivity
of the GLM approach in this region, increased inter­subject variability, or the presence of
time­locked noise and false positives affecting the detection of true activation.
A notable finding was the significant activation of the visual ROI during the Speech condi­
tion, a result not observed in the WAA analysis. Interestingly, this visual activation may be

5.2.3
Cross­Method Comparison: WAA versus GLM Group­Level
Activation
Comparing the WAA and GLM pipelines reveals clear methodological contrasts in both
sensitivity and temporal resolution. WAA identified a broader set of significant activations
across both predefined and data­driven ROIs, including regions defined by GLM contrasts.
This suggests high sensitivity, but may partly reflect how time­locking in WAA enforces
peak alignment across trials. As seen in Figure 4.3, WAA­defined ROIs (Noise_WAA,

In contrast, GLM results were more selective in identifying activated regions. Because
the GLM approach fits a predefined hemodynamic response model (with a fixed shape
and peak timing), it is less influenced by temporal variability or noise in the signal. This
may lead to more robust detection of true activations, particularly in low signal­to­noise
conditions.
Despite these differences, both methods converged on the right auditory cortex as a ro­
bust site of activation for both conditions. Additionally, each method detected activation in
regions defined by the other, indicating cross­method generalizability. This pattern is con­
sistent with findings by Schulte et al.[11], who also reported strong agreement between
WAA and GLM at the group level, but notable discrepancies in individual­level results.
In sum, WAA offers greater sensitivity and consistency across trials, but may be more
prone to false positives due to event­locked noise and may obscure temporal differences
due to enforced alignment. In contrast, GLM provides better control over false positives

5.3
Interpretation of Individual­Level Activation
This section compares the performance of the WAA and GLM pipelines in detecting individual­
level activation at both the optode and ROI levels. In addition to evaluating methodological
differences, it examines key factors that may influence activation detection, including sig­

5.3.1
Channel­Level Comparisons Across Methods
At the channel level, both pipelines identified task­responsive optodes, but with notable
differences in sensitivity and robustness. The WAA analysis revealed that optode S11_D12
was the most consistently activated in the Control vs. Speech contrast, reaching signifi­
cance in only 5 out of 32 sessions for Speech and just 2 sessions for Noise. In contrast,
the GLM analysis identified optode S11_D11 as the most consistent, with significant acti­
vation in 14 sessions for Speech and 11 for Noise.
The low number of significant activations in the WAA results is primarily attributable to
the impact of false discovery rate (FDR) correction. Before correction, a greater num­
ber of optodes showed potential task­related activation; however, many did not survive
statistical thresholding. In contrast, GLM results retained a higher number of significant
activations after FDR correction, suggesting that the detected effects were stronger and
more consistently observed across participants. This indicates that GLM may offer greater
sensitivity and robustness in detecting reliable task­evoked responses.
This discrepancy likely stems from fundamental methodological differences between the
pipelines. In the GLM pipeline, activation estimates and associated p­values were given
directly from the model, resulting in smaller and more stable p­values. In contrast, the
WAA pipeline performed within­subject paired Welch t­tests on a smaller number of epochs,
which likely produced larger p­values due to reduced statistical power. Consequently, the
FDR procedure had a more severe filtering effect on WAA results.
These findings highlight a key limitation of the WAA approach: its increased susceptibility
to false positives due to less sophisticated noise handling, as it does not separate the true
neural response from noise with a dedicated noise term as the GLM does. This results
in reduced power after statistical correction on the individual level. In contrast, the GLM
pipeline demonstrates greater resilience to statistical correction procedures by explicitly
modelling noise components, providing more reliable identification of consistent, localized
activation at the channel level. Therefore, while both pipelines offer valuable insights,
GLM emerges as the more robust method for detecting individual­level activation with
high spatial specificity.

5.4
Test­Retest Reliability
The evaluation of test–retest reliability is essential for assessing the stability and robust­
ness of fNIRS­derived activation patterns across sessions. In this study, reliability was
assessed at both the group and individual levels using complementary metrics. At the
group level, the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) was employed to quantify the con­
sistency of activation amplitudes across sessions. In addition, reliability was evaluated
using reproducibility indices, RQUANTITY and ROVERLAP, as well as Pearson correlation co­
efficients, both at the group and individual levels. These metrics provide a multifaceted
view of reliability, capturing consistency in both activation strength and spatial­temporal
patterns of the hemodynamic response.

the Speech condition, reaching moderate to high levels of correlation. This suggests that
data­driven ROI selection can enhance the sensitivity to consistent task­related signals
at the individual level.
Consistently, both group and individual analyses revealed a consistent advantage of data­
driven ROIs over predefined anatomical regions. This fact suggests that this ROIs may
better capture consistent task­evoked responses, especially under auditory stimulation.
In sum, while group­level analyses benefit from noise cancellation through averaging and
exhibit strong test–retest reliability, individual­level reliability remains more variable and
susceptible to sources of noise and anatomical differences, matching to previous literature
findings [9]. Nonetheless, the use of data­driven ROI selection enhances the reliability of
both individual and group­level analysis.

Limitations and Future Work
While the present study provides valuable insights into how region selection methods in­
fluence the reliability of auditory fNIRS responses, several limitations should be acknowl­
edged, and directions for future research can be proposed accordingly.
First, the final sample consisted of only 16 participants after applying quality control exclu­
sion criteria. This limited sample size may have reduced the statistical power of the anal­
yses and restricts the generalizability of the findings. Furthermore, all participants were
required to be fluent Danish speakers, introducing a demographic bias. Most participants
shared similar phenotypic characteristics, particularly in terms of skin tone and hair type,
which are known to affect fNIRS signal quality. This homogeneity may have masked the
variability in signal quality that could emerge in more diverse populations. Future studies
should aim to include larger and more heterogeneous cohorts to better understand how
physiological traits influence both signal quality and neural activation patterns.
Second, the test­retest interval between sessions was relatively short, with a minimum
separation of three hours. Although this design ensured complete cap removal and repo­
sitioning, it does not provide insight into the medium­ or long­term stability of fNIRS re­
sponses. Future work should extend the interval between sessions to days or weeks in
order to evaluate the robustness and reproducibility of activation patterns over clinically
and experimentally relevant timescales.
Third, although the data­driven ROIs yielded higher activation rates, they were derived
from the same dataset used for the group­level activation analysis. This may inflate the
observed reliability and activation rates. Future studies should validate the functional
ROIs identified here using larger, independent datasets to confirm their generalizability
and robustness across samples.
Fourth, a key limitation in interpreting activation results lies in the absence of a definitive
ground truth. Since fNIRS measures relative changes in hemoglobin concentrations, we
cannot conclusively determine whether a given region is truly activated or not based on
fNIRS data alone. This uncertainty makes it challenging to evaluate the true accuracy of
different ROI definitions or analysis pipelines. Ideally, future work could incorporate com­
plementary neuroimaging modalities to establish ground­truth benchmarks for activation,
allowing for more objective validation of fNIRS­based activation detection.
Moreover, this thesis employed a GLM with a fixed hemodynamic response shape. Future
work could improve upon this by implementing a Finite Impulse Response (FIR) GLM ap­
proach, as proposed by [3], which may help address the timing sensitivity issues observed
in the frontal region.
Finally, several challenges during the acquisition process may have affected data quality.
Issues such as improper calibration, suboptimal cap placement, and limited operator ex­
perience led to the exclusion of some data and may have introduced noise into the results.
While the preprocessing pipeline was designed to mitigate these effects, residual artifacts
cannot be entirely ruled out. Future studies would benefit from improved training proto­
cols, rigorous calibration procedures, and real­time quality monitoring tools to ensure high
data quality and reproducibility across sessions.

7
Some Recommendations When
Working with fNIRS Signals
Based on the experience gained throughout this study, the following recommendations
are proposed to help researchers obtain reliable and high­quality fNIRS data, particularly
in auditory­related experiments:
• Cap fitting and calibration are critical. Allocate sufficient time to ensure optimal
cap placement and calibration. Aim for all source­detector (SD) pairs to achieve
good calibration, with special attention to the right auditory cortex. In our study, this
region showed the highest number of activations and is therefore crucial for accurate
data capture.
• Record optode positions. Save the exact positions of the optodes during the first
session. If the cap needs to be removed and reapplied in subsequent sessions, hav­
ing precise coordinates or photographic references can help maintain consistency
in placement across sessions.
• Use the strongest available spring tension that the participant can comfortably
tolerate for the whole session. Opt for the maximum spring strength in optode
holders to ensure firm contact with the scalp. Good contact reduces motion artifacts
and improves signal quality, especially in regions with hair.
• Visually inspect preprocessing results. Always verify that the filtering process
effectively removes irrelevant physiological noise, such as respiration or slow drifts.
In our case, the default filter parameters did not completely eliminate breathing­
related fluctuations, so the filter needed to be adjusted. Visual inspection of the
filtered signals for each participant is essential to identify channels or sessions with
unusable data.
• Use GLM for detection, WAA for visualization. We recommend using the Gen­
eral Linear Model (GLM) for detecting statistically significant activations, and the
Weighted Average Approach (WAA) for visualization. While WAA can be prone to
false positives, it provides a clear and intuitive way to visually confirm GLM findings.
This approach is especially valuable if fNIRS is to be adopted in clinical settings,
where clinicians need to interpret and validate activation maps visually in addition
to trusting statistical outputs.

8
Conclusion
This thesis investigated how different regions of interest (ROIs) selection strategies affect
the test–retest reliability of fNIRS responses to auditory stimuli. The results demonstrate
that data­driven ROIs improved test–retest reliability at both the group and individual lev­
els, compared to predefined anatomical regions.
At the group level, both the waveform averaging analysis (WAA) and general linear mod­
eling (GLM) pipelines showed that functionally defined ROIs yielded more robust and
spatially consistent activation patterns. This was particularly evident in the right superior
temporal cortex. Data­driven ROIs derived from the GLM pipeline for speech and noise
stimuli also exhibited substantial spatial overlap, supporting the idea that both conditions
engage shared auditory processing networks. The Control condition consistently showed
the lowest group­level reliability, reflecting its higher variability and greater susceptibility
to noise.
At the individual level, reliability was assessed using both channel­level reproducibility
metrics (RQUANTITY, ROVERLAP) and Pearson correlation of ROI­averaged timecourses.
Again, data­driven ROIs outperformed anatomical ROIs, with higher reliability values and
more consistent detection of task­evoked responses. Among analysis pipelines, GLM
outperformed WAA, as WAA failed to detect significant activation at the individual level
after FDR correction, while GLM yielded non­zero reproducibility in several participants.
While these findings support the utility of data­driven approaches, individual­level reliabil­
ity remained lower overall than group­level results. This discrepancy can be attributed to
multiple factors, including inter­subject anatomical variability, motion artifacts, poor optode
coupling, and lower signal­to­noise ratios. Importantly, this study highlights that channel­
level analyses are more vulnerable to these noise sources, while ROI­level analyses pro­
vide more robust and interpretable results by averaging across channels and reducing
the impact of local artifacts.
Taken together, these findings lead to the following key conclusions:
• Data­driven ROI selection improves test–retest reliability at both the group and
individual levels.
• GLM­based analyses outperform WAA at the individual level as WAA failed to
detect significant activation after FDR correction.
• The Control condition showed the lowest group­level reliability, likely due to
its higher variability and limited evoked activation.
• Group­level reliability remains higher than individual­level reliability, under­
scoring the ongoing challenge of capturing consistent single­subject activation.
• Signal quality is a key limiting factor, especially at the individual level. Future
studies should emphasize improved optode placement, real­time quality control, and
artifact mitigation.
• Test–retest reliability analyses should be performed at the ROI level, not the
channel level, to reduce the impact of local noise, poor optode coupling, and anatom­
ical variability.

A.2
Linear Mixed Effects Modeling
Linear mixed effects (LME) models are an extension of linear regression models designed
for analyzing dependent or grouped data, such as repeated measurements taken from
the same subjects. These models are particularly well­suited for fNIRS and other neu­
roimaging studies where multiple observations are nested within participants or other
units. LME models incorporate both fixed effects, which represent population­level re­
lationships, and random effects, which account for subject­ or group­level variability. For
example, participant­specific differences in baseline activation can be modeled as random
intercepts.
A simple LME model with a random intercept can be written as:
yij = β0 + β1xij + u0j + ϵij
(A.1)
where yij is the response for observation i in group j, β0 and β1 are fixed effect coefficients,
u0j ∼N(0, σ2
u) is the random intercept for group j, and ϵij ∼N(0, σ2) is the residual error.
In cases where we expect the effect of a predictor to vary between participants, random
slopes can be added. The inclusion of random effects allows the model to generalize
beyond the specific sample of participants or stimuli used.
Choosing whether a variable should be treated as fixed or random depends on its role in
the study design. Fixed effects are used for predictors of theoretical interest, while random
effects represent grouping structures sampled from a larger population. As explained in
[38], careful specification of the random­effects structure is critical for valid inference,
especially in repeated­measures designs.
In this thesis, LME models were implemented using the statsmodels library in Python.
This framework supports both random intercepts and slopes and allows specification of
grouped designs through the MixedLM class. The models used here follow the structure:
Value ∼Condition + Session + (1|Subject)
(A.2)
which estimates fixed effects of auditory condition, and session while accounting for re­
peated measurements by including a random intercept for each participant. This modeling
approach is flexible and statistically robust, making it particularly useful for the analysis of
fNIRS data in test­retest designs.

A.3
False Discovery Rate Correction
When performing multiple statistical tests, the likelihood of obtaining false positives in­
creases. The False Discovery Rate (FDR) is a correction method designed to control the
expected proportion of false positives among all results declared significant [33].
In this study, FDR correction was applied using the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure [33].
This method involves ranking all obtained p­values in ascending order and comparing
each to a corresponding threshold defined as:
FDRthreshold = i
mq
(A.3)
where i is the rank of the p­value in the sorted list, m is the total number of comparisons,
and q is the desired false discovery rate, set to 0.05 (5%). The largest p­value that satisfies
the condition
pi ≤i
mq
(A.4)
is identified, and all p­values smaller than or equal to this value are considered statistically
significant. This procedure allows for more sensitive detection of true effects while con­
trolling the false discovery rate at 5%, accepting that approximately 5% of the significant
findings may be false positives.
