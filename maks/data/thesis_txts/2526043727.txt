Abstract 
Flow chemistry has become a promising technology for research, development and manufacturing of 
pharmaceuticals, fine chemicals, catalysts, and even polymers in recent years. Due to the inherent 
continuous nature of flow chemistry and scaling relations such as high surface-area-to-volume ratio, 
a higher degree of process control and straight forward automation is achievable which translates into 
higher yields, lower reaction times, increased reproducibility and safety. Photochemistry benefits 
from a high surface area, due to the required absorption of photons, which allows for accessing high 
energy intermediate pathways not possible thermally. These reaction pathways are possible at mild 
conditions and with a limited number of reagents. By downscaling flow processes to the micron-level 
portable and easy-to-setup reactors relevant for research and development laboratories can be 
fabricated with low required residence times achieving near-instantaneous steady-state conditions, 
making it a powerful tool for rapid reaction optimization and kinetic studies. 
In this project a microfluidic system for photochemical cycloadditions was fabricated using silicon 
micro processing technologies. The photochemical reactions were facilitated using high power LEDs 
and triplet photosensitizers. The system was optimized to yield residence times of a few minutes. A 
range of [2+2]-cycloaddition adducts using different excitable olefins with different linear-, 
carbocylic- and heterocyclic coupling partners were synthesized to examine the feasibility of using 
photochemistry in microflow in an academic research environment.

1 Introduction 
For the most part, organic synthesis is still carried out the way it has been since the field first emerged 
over a century ago. Despite advances in analytical instrumentation and purification tools, reactions 
are still carried out in round-bottomed flasks in conditions that are hard to reproduce exactly. New 
technologies for simplifying and automating these processes are thus in high demand. One of these 
technologies is flow chemistry. 
 
1.1 Organic chemistry in continuous flow 
In flow chemistry one or more streams of reactants are continuously driven through a tube or chamber 
where the reaction takes place (Fig. 1). The resulting product is then collected at the end. The fluids 
are most often driven by applying a pressure gradient between the in- and outlet of the reactor by use 
of pumps. 
 
 
Figure 1 Example of a simple flow setup with two pumps introducing reagent A and B into a reactor. Product C is then collected at 
the end of the channel. 
 
 
When comparing flow chemistry with batch chemistry, a few definitions of parameters change. In 
flow chemistry the reaction stoichiometry is decided by the reagent concentration and flow rate ratio 
of inlet streams, whereas in batch it is decided by the reagent concentration and volumetric ratio. By 
==PAGE:13==
Page | 13  
 
having a defined reactor volume, the reaction time is described as the residence time of the solution 
within the confines of the reactor. Hence by controlling the applied overall flowrate the reaction time 
is controlled with great precision whereas in batch it is decided by the time period the reaction is held 
at a given temperature. The continuous nature and rel. smaller confined space of flow chemistry offers 
a range of advantages and disadvantages compared to batch chemistry. Advantages arise from the 
increased surface-area-to-volume ratio allowing for more efficient heat transfer and faster and more 
homogenous mixing due to the smaller confined space relative to batch chemistry. The enclosed 
nature of the reactor allows for safe work of reactive and toxic intermediates and easy pressurization 
of the system, hence conducting reactions at temperatures above the solvents boiling point at ambient 
pressure is possible. Coupled with better control of reaction time, flow chemistry allows for a higher 
degree of overall process control and automation of processes with real-time in- or on-line analysis 
to provide information about reaction performance with feedback loops [1,6,9], hence reproducibility 
of results [1,3], minimal required human interaction [1,2,3,6,9], increased safety [1,4,5], 
stereoselectivity [7,8] and often higher yields, by minimizing byproduct formation due to increased 
process control, and lower reaction times compared to batch chemistry [10,11,12]. By conducting 
chemistry in flow, the reaction time is translated to a function of position allowing for precise control 
of the introduction of reagents. Scale-up of the reaction can be achieved by increasing the reactor 
volume or by parallelization of the system with no or minor process development [13]. Disadvantages 
regarding flow chemistry lies in expensive or dedicated equipment such as pumps, connectors and 
potentially the flow-reactors themselves. For scale-up, economy of scale may not apply especially if 
parallelization is required in order to achieve a desired fluid behavior not feasible on the macroscopic 
scale (see section 1.2.1). Finally, reactions involving insoluble reagents or precipitation of products 
may be inadequate for flow due to fouling and clogging-related issues. 
By downscaling flow chemistry to so-called microfluidics, the advantages and disadvantages of flow 
compared to batch chemistry are further enhanced. Steady-state conditions are very rapidly 
established as the small volume allows for near instantaneous heat transfer and mixing, hence very 
steep changes in reaction conditions are possible. The small characteristic length scales allow for 
handling of smaller volumes, thus lower reagent consumption, faster analysis due to shorter reaction- 
and separation times and increased measurement resolution. At this length scale the fluid flow 
exhibits laminar flow behavior which allows for manipulation of fluids not feasible on the 
macroscopic scale such as multiphase flow (droplet-based microfluidics) and transport phenomena 
relevant in biological systems. The dimensions allow for fabrication of transportable reactors 
==PAGE:14==
Page | 14  
 
relevant, modular and are easy to use in a laboratory with drawbacks related to throughput and higher 
susceptibility to fouling. 
 
1.2 Microfluidics 
Microfluidics is the manipulation of fluids and suspensions at the micron-level influenced by external 
forces. The advance of the field is mainly driven by technological application were downscaling 
allows for development of entire biological and chemical laboratories in hand-held systems, a so-
called lab-on-a-chip, often developed in either polymers or of silicon [14]. Technologies developed 
for silicon-based microelectronics are well-suited for the fabrication of lab-on-a-chip systems and 
have been used in this project for reactor fabrication. 
 
1.2.1 Lab-on-a-chip systems and their scaling relations 
By downscaling standard laboratory and conventional flow setups from the macroscopic scale to the 
~100 µm scale several advantages arise. A linear reduction of 1000 results in a volume reduction of 
109, making handling of 1 nL or 1 pL volumes feasible. Hence an obvious advantage is the reduction 
in the required sample as well as fast and efficient analysis and detection schemes, limited by the 
sensitivity of the method of analysis [14]. From a chemical synthesis point of view scaling relations 
advantageously yields controlled mixing, enhanced heat and mass transfer, expanded reaction 
conditions (high temperature and pressure), reproducibility, controlled reaction time, narrow size 
distributions of nanoparticles (in two-phase flow) and safety allowing for working with chemistry 
involving highly reactive and toxic intermediates with future opportunities in synthesis planning and 
prediction using machine learning [15,16]. 
It is helpful to introduce the concept of scaling laws to appreciate the physical properties of 
microfluidic systems. As an example, consider the variation in magnitude of volume forces such as 
gravity and inertia compared to surface forces such as surface tension and shear stress as the length 
scale, l, is decreased.

1.3 Chemical synthesis processes that are advantageous in flow 
The interest in continuous flow has grown in recent years particularly in pharmaceuticals, catalysis, 
polymer- and green chemistry [7, 8, 18, 19, 20, 21, 22]. These include potentially hazardous reactions 
involving toxic gases, nitrations, halogenations, hydrogenation reactions and reactions where a large 
surface-area-to-volume ratio is advantageous such as electrochemistry and photochemistry. 
In this project photochemistry in microfluidic systems will be examined. 
 
1.4 Photochemistry 
In photochemistry photons interact with matter resulting in electronically excited molecules with 
fundamentally different chemical reactivity than the ground-state molecule. The first step of a 
photochemical process is photoexcitation of the substrate where an electron is excited to a higher 
lying energy state. The first law of photochemistry (Grotthuss-Draper) states that only absorbed 
==PAGE:18==
Page | 18  
 
photons can induce photochemical transformation. The second law of photochemistry (Stark-
Einstein) states that absorption of a photon is a quantum process. No more than one molecule per 
absorbed photon is activated for a potential photochemical transformation (this does however not 
hold true for photocatalytic processes). The probability of any photo-physical or -chemical event can 
be described as a quantum yield, which is the number events per incident absorbed photon. Hence 
most events cannot exceed unity, some exceptions being photo redox catalysis or photoinitiated 
polymerizations [24]. 
 
1.4.1 Photophysical processes 
The energy state diagram of a molecule during absorption and emission of a photon is expressed as a 
Jablonski diagram and strictly speaking only describes photophysical processes but allows for 
extraction of information relevant for favoring photochemical transformations. In figure 3 a singlet 
ground state system is depicted, hence it will be used as an example. The thick horizontal lines 
correspond to electronic states while the thin horizontals lines are vibrational states. The straight 
arrows correspond to radiative processes (absorption or emission of a photon) while the curly arrows 
are nonradiative processes. The numbering is represented in table 1 with the corresponding time 
scales.

When an incident photon of adequate energy is absorbed by a molecule, a ground state electron (S0) 
is excited to a higher energy electronic state (S1, S2, S3…) of the same multiplicity as the ground state 
due to conservation of angular momentum [24]. The transition may be from the ground state to any 
vibrational state (Vn) at a given electronic state of higher energy that coincides with the energy of the 
photon. Absorption is a very rapid process on the femtosecond scale. Following excitation, several 
radiationless photophysical processes can occur depending on the electronic excitation state. 
Vibrational relaxation (5) occurs through dissipation of excess vibrational energy (relevant after any 
vibronic transition) to the surrounding medium through collisions. Internal conversion (IC) (2) is an 
isoenergetic transition between different electronic states with the same multiplicity. The same 
processes occur in the case of a triplet excited molecule. Radiationless decay through vibrational 
relaxation and internal conversion occurs predominantly until the vibrational ground state in the first 
excited electronic state (either S1 or T1) is achieved after which fluorescence may occur. The observed 
behavior (Kasha’s rule) can be explained through the energy gap law which states that rate constants 
of internal conversion and intersystem crossing (ISC) (3) decrease exponentially as the energy gap 
increases between electronic states [24]. For increasing principal quantum number of a given state 
the energy gap decreases. Hence favoring internal conversion relative to the rate of fluorescence until 
the energy gap between electronic states becomes large enough for the rate of fluorescence to be able 
to compete. Rather than undergoing IC, vibrational relaxation or fluorescent relaxation mechanisms 
from the first excited singlet state back to the ground state, the excited electron may undergo 
intersystem crossing to the first excited triplet state given that the energy gap and orbital overlap is 
adequate [25]. ISC requires a change in parity of the excited electron making it a spin forbidden 
transition, hence for many functional groups the ISC quantum yield is low. However, in the case of 
==PAGE:20==
Page | 20  
 
ISC the excited electron is only able to relax back to the ground state through reverse intersystem 
crossing (4) or phosphorescence (7). Both photophysical processes are forbidden, because they entail 
a spin flip and vibrational relaxation is relatively so rapid that they essentially are irreversible. It 
makes the triplet excited species very long lived relative to the time scales of other photophysical 
processes (see table 1) [24].

1.4.2 Photochemical processes 
If the electronically excited state leads to structural change, these processes are photochemical in 
nature. The pathway of a photochemical reaction can be visualized as potential energy surfaces for 
the excited- and ground state. Following vertical excitation due to absorption of a photon and

triplet species.  As the molecule travels down the potential energy curve, the energy is irreversibly 
dissipated to the surroundings until it reaches a local minimum. If the energy barrier is sufficiently 
low, the excited species can continue its travel through thermal activation.

adiabatic pathway is also possible for singlet excited state species, they are however limited to low 
energy barriers such as e.g. geometric isomerization and proton transfer [24].

Case b) and c) proceed through reactive intermediates such as a biradical, zwitterion or carbene in 
the ground- and excited state, respectively. Many photochemical transformations proceed through 
reactive intermediates [24]. The bond energies in organic molecules are similar to near-UV photons, 
hence the cleavage of a covalent bond leads to the formation of a biradical or zwitterion [25]. The 
radicals can be localized, connected through covalent bonds, or delocalized over a conjugated π-
system. Cleavage of a π-bond leaves the molecule with two nonbonding molecular orbitals with a 
total of two electrons. If the electrons are paired, three configurations are possible. A biradical and 
two zwitterions. Whereas in the case of unpaired electrons only one configuration is possible. Thus, 
three singlet and one triplet state are possible (Fig. 8). Considering electronic interactions, the triplet 
==PAGE:24==
Page | 24  
 
state is often the lowest in energy due to less electron repulsion relative to the zwitterion 
configurations and a stabilizing effect from the exchange of parallel spins in degenerate states [24].

1.4.3 Intermolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition 
Organic compounds containing cyclobutane ring(s) are a basic structural unit found in a wide variety 
of complex natural products and drugs (Fig. 9). Cyclobutane containing  natural products exhibit 
versatile biological activity such cytotoxic [50], antifungal [48, 50], anti-HIV [49, 50], antimicrobial 
[50, 51], antiviral [50] and immunosuppressive effects [50], and may serve as potential drug leads. 
The strained system of the cyclobutane ring may lead to interesting properties and bioactivity due to 
its inherent 3-dimensionality. 
 
Photocycloaddition reactions enable a versatile and straightforward approach in order to access 
carbocylic four-membered rings. It involves two unsaturated compounds, where one is required to be 
excited by ultraviolet or visible light, in which two new bonds is formed. The strained four-membered 
rings formed may lead to interesting properties and bioactivity due to its inherent 3-dimensionality

and the synthetic route using photochemistry is able to provide pathways at mild conditions. The 
cyclobutane motif can be achieved both through intra- and intermolecular [2+2]-cycloadditions. In 
this project the focus has been in intermolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition, hence a sufficiently long 
lifetime of the excited unsaturated compound, is required for translational movement to be relatively 
rapid for efficient coupling to be possible. 
 
Triplet states are biradical in nature [25], and as described above, have a relatively long lifetime 
compared to the singlet state. The biradical molecule can undergo typical radical reactions such as 
hydrogen abstraction or it react with another unsaturated molecule forming four-membered rings. 
Since bimolecular reactions requires the encounter of two molecules, triplet state excited species are 
favored as the probability of an encounter with a coupling partner is higher. Due to the reduced π-
bond character of the excited species rotation is favored usually resulting in non-stereoselective [2+2] 
cycloadditions [25]. The bimolecular reaction may involve the photo-induced transfer of an electron 
between the excited and ground state species. The excited species can act as both the acceptor and 
donor, depending on the coupling partner and their relative electronic energy levels. The 
cycloaddition, however, usually occurs between an electron rich and –poor coupling partner [24]. The 
energy transfer process is isoenergetic, hence if the electronic excitation energy of the acceptor is 
below the electronic energy of the donor electron, an excited vibrational level of the acceptor and the 
excess energy will be very rapidly dissipated to the surrounds through vibrational relaxation to the 
surrounding medium, hence energy transfer is irreversible.  
 
To describe the regioselectivity in [2+2]-cycloadditions the reaction of cyclic enones with alkenes 
will be used as a case study. The cyclization of cyclohex-2-enone with alkenes with electron 
withdrawing groups usually yields head-to-head (HH) regioselectivity, 6, due to formation of the 
most stable 1,4-biradical 4 (Fig. 10a). This can further be rationalized from a dipole/dipole interaction 
point of view between the triplet excited enone 5, with the most nucleophilic radical on the β-carbon, 
and electron-poor alkene, which acts as a Michael acceptor (Fig. 10b). For the [2+2]-
photocycloaddition of enones and alkenes with electron donating groups, head-to-tail (HT), 11, is 
generally favored (Fig. 10c). Both the HH (7) and HT (8) biradical is formed, even though the HT is 
a less stable biradical. The cyclization from the HT is however faster, while the probability of 
fragmentation into starting materials from the HH biradical is greater, yielding HT cyclization as the 
major product. The behavior can again be rationalized through dipole/dipole interactions, 9. Where

1.4.4 Photosensitization 
To circumvent the issues related to poor ISC quantum yield, low molar extinction coefficient and 
UVB/UVC irradiance required for direct excitation (section 1.4.1), many photochemical processes 
[25] are dependent on photosentization. The sensitizer exhibits the relevant properties which 
otherwise makes direct excitation problematic. Hence, a good sensitizer absorbs rel. low energy 
photons, has a higher molar extinction coefficient and efficient ISC to the first excited triplet state. If 
the triplet energy of the sensitizer is higher than the triplet energy of an acceptor molecule, energy 
transfer is possible (Fig. 13). The energy transfer effiency depends on the triplet state lifetime of the 
sensitizer, the concentration of the acceptor, velocity of brownian motion of the sensitizer (hence 
==PAGE:29==
Page | 29  
 
viscosity of the medium) and on competitive processes capable of wasting the excitation energy or 
results in degradation of the sensitizer or acceptor [25].

mechanism, which occurs through collision and requires direct interaction of the wavefunctions of 
the sensitizer and acceptor [25].

2 Aims and objectives  
The aim of this project is to develop a continuous-flow chemistry setup for photo-induced 
cycloadditions. The setup will consist of in-house developed microreactors using state-of-the-art 
cleanroom facilities, connection between reactor and pumping system and UV light source using high 
power LEDs.  
The cycloadditions will be facilitated by various photosensitizers of adequate triplet energy as it 
allows for the use of lower energy photons relative to the wavelengths required for direct excitation, 
thus reducing safety concerns. 
Due to the small internal volume of microreactors, the system will be optimized regarding minimal 
residence time required for complete conversion of starting materials, thus making lab-scale 
production of chemicals feasible in a microfluidic reactor. A range of [2+2]-cycloaddition adducts 
using different excitable olefins with different linear-, carbocylic- and heterocyclic coupling partners 
will be attempted synthesized to examine the feasibility of using photochemistry in microflow in an 
academic research environment.

3 Results and discussion 
The focus of this section will be design, development and fabrication of the microreactor, and UV 
light source followed by examination of photocycloadditions conducted in the system. 
 
3.1 Microreactor fabrication 
Material choice, based on versatility and availability, development techniques and channel 
characteristics, with optimal resolution and laboratory-scale production in mind, and connection 
between microreactor and macroscopic equipment will be discussed in this section. 
 
3.1.1 Reactor materials 
It was desired to fabricate an enclosed reactor with high transmittance of UVA irradiation, limited 
compliance effects for rapid steady state conditions, high thermal and chemical resistance for reactor 
versatility and with high resolution within the microfluidic domain for examination of advantageous 
scaling relations described in section 1.2.  
It was decided to fabricate the reactor at the DanChip cleanroom facility in order to meet the above-
mentioned criteria. Silicon is the second most abundant element in the earth’s crust, cheap and 
extensively used in the semiconductor industry. For these reasons many silicon processing techniques 
has been developed making highly tailored structures with high resolution possible at sub-micron 
level [33]. Along with high thermal and chemical resistance, silicon was deemed an adequate reactor 
material. Since silicon does not transmit UV irradiation a lid with the desired properties was required. 
For this purpose, Borofloat® 33 was selected. Besides its optical, chemical and thermal properties the 
composition includes an adequate concentration of alkali ions. The ions allow for sealing between 
Borofloat® 33 and silicon through anodic bonding techniques forming irreversible siloxane bonds 
between the two materials. Using Borofloat® 33 limits transmittance of UV irradiation to the UVA 
region (>90 % transmittance, Fig. 16).

Structuring of the silicon wafer was initially attempted using the Bosch process, a deep reactive ion 
etching (DRIE) method, which is a standard technique for silicon micro- and nanofabrication. The 
Bosch process is a multiphase process consisting of an isotropic etch using SF6 followed by a 
passivation step that covers the entire wafer, using C4F8. Through ion bombardment of the sample, 
the bottom layer is sputtered away, and the two phases are cycled until the desired etching depth is 
achieved. Hence the combination of phases makes it a completely anisotropic process capable of 
producing high aspect ratio structures with vertical sidewalls at high etching rates (>10 µm/min) [36]. 
Coupled with a simple, automated batch handling and relatively rapid process flow (Fig. 19), 
fabrication from design to finished reactor can be completed in a few hours per wafer.

3.3 Intermolecular [2+2]-cycloadditions in a microfluidic system 
Triplet sensitized intermolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition provides a reaction pathway for making four-
membered motifs where only coupling partners of adequate triplet energy is excitable. It allows for 
tailoring of which compounds are excited, thus limiting byproduct formation, but at the cost of a 
scope limited to include at least one compound with lower triplet energy than the photosensitizer 
used. The photo-induced cycloadditions are examined in the developed microreactors.

The triplet state excited maleimide has a long enough lifetime for it to traverse through the medium 
and collide with a potential coupling partner (either through homo- or heteromolecular collision)

or form a new triplet biradical intermediate. The biradical is usually [24] formed between an electron-
deficient and an electron-rich coupling partner, which acts as an acceptor and donor, respectively. In 
the case of biradical formation, the intermediate may undergo inter system crossing forming a singlet 
excited biradical intermediate that may cyclize into the desired [2+2]-cycloaddition product or 
fragment back to the starting materials. Four-membered rings are highly strained, especially when 
unsaturated as in the case of the model system, hence establishing a covalent bond between the two 
radical species may be highly improbable favoring fragmentation, such that photosensitized 
excitation may have to occur many times prior to a successful cyclization increasing the probability 
of byproduct formation through e.g. hydrogen abstraction [24]. Fragmentation from the triplet excited 
biradical is in parenthesis, since it is unknown whether it is a feasible pathway. The formation of the 
first covalent bond between the coupling partners of the triplet excited biradical species may occur at

A UV-vis measurement of thioxanthone in acetonitrile was conducted to explain the large difference 
observed in rate of reaction dependent on the UV LED used. According to the above description of 
the reaction mechanism, changing the source of irradiance should only affect the probability of 
excitation of the photosensitizer, while the remaining parts of the mechanism remains unaffected. A 
λmax of 378 nm was observed (Fig. 36). According to the Lambert-Beer law the amount of absorbed 
light is proportional to the concentration and molar extinction coefficient of absorbent and that the 
fraction of incident photons of adequate wavelength absorbed is independent on light intensity [44]. 
Hence a linear correlation between molar extinction coefficient and excitation probability is expected 
at constant absorbent concentration and irradiance pathway. Thus, a linear correlation in rate of 
reaction is expected if excitation probability of the photosensitizer is the rate limiting step. According

4 Conclusion 
A microfluidic photochemical reactor with characteristic dimensions of 200 x 800 µm and an internal 
volume of 489 µL was designed and developed using silicon wet etching techniques. A UV LED light 
source was developed for triplet sensitized photochemical cycloadditions. The system was optimized 
using the model [2+2] cycloaddition of maleimide with propargyl alcohol and the required residence 
time for full conversion could be reduced from 73 to 3 minutes. Fouling related issues due to 
precipitation of the maleimide dimer 30 were solved by using N-methyl maleimide 31 instead of 26, 
which was found to behave similarly.  
A small N-methyl maleimide-based compound set of 5 cycloaddition adducts with carbocyclic, 
heterocyclic and linear unsaturated coupling partners was synthesized in moderate yields to examine 
the scope of the [2+2]-cycloadditions. The possibility of excitation of citraconic anhydride 44 for the 
synthesis of electrophilic 3-dimensional fragments was examined resulting in the synthesis of 3 
compounds with carbocyclic, heterocyclic and linear unsaturated coupling partners, albeit in poor 
yields. The two major limitations to the scope of possible photocycloadditions were solvent 
incompatibility of the epoxy resin and organic sensitizer triplet energies. 
Having examined a range of reactions at various conditions and with varying success, a few 
observations regarding (photo)chemistry in microflow should be highlighted. Microfluidics offers a 
broad range of advantageous characteristics arising from scaling relations, most notably in this 
project, from decreased reaction times due to a large surface area and small irradiance pathlength. 
However, due to the small dimensions of the reactor, fouling posed a serious challenge limiting 
possible reaction pathways as well as throughput. Following optimization of the system, throughput 
for lab-scale production was deemed adequate. Numbering-up strategies by running hundreds (or 
even thousands) of microreactors in parallel is possible, although difficult requiring exceptional 
control of the hydraulic resistance of the individual reactors to ensure a homogenous flow distribution 
across the array of reactors, allowing for industrial-scale production. However due to unit price 
considerations this strategy is probably limited to reactions in which microfluidic scaling relations 
are detrimental for the success of a given reaction. 
For reactions where the behavior of the system was unknown or difficult to predict, initial 
examination was advantageously performed in GC vials for easy parallelization and circumventing 
precipitation issues. For high-throughput experimentation a microwell-based setup is believed to be 
==PAGE:68==
Page | 68  
 
much more efficient from a “hit-or-miss” screening perspective. However, the properties of 
microfluidics and the possibility of conducting fluid operations along with implementation of in-line 
analysis makes automated reaction optimization, through e.g. design of experiments and machine 
learning, as well as kinetic studies of a given system, possible using microfluidic-based technology. 
Microfluidics is a precision tool and needs to be used as such for it to be a valuable addition to both 
research and industrial application.

5 Future work 
For future work Lewis acid promoted [2+2]-cycloadditions appears attractive although not 
successful in the few attempted experiments using this strategy – especially since xanthone is one of 
the known organic triplet sensitizers with highest triplet energy [46]. Since it is unknown whether 
the Lewis acids decreases the triplet energy of carbonyl-based sensitizers used due to complexation, 
transition metal-based triplet sensitizers could be interesting to examine in the case of [47].

6.3 Reactor process flow 
Silicon wafers were placed in a LPCVD furnace along with a dummy wafer and a 150 nm Si3N4 
masking layer was deposited (810-845 ºC (temperature varies of over furnace tube), processed for 30 
minutes). The masking layer thickness was controlled by examining the dummy wafer using 
ellipsometry (157.6 nm (σ = 3.3 nm) average thickness). The silicon wafers were then cleaned using 
a piranha solution, rinsed with water and spin dried prior to being spin coated with 1.5 µm positive 
photoresist using hexamethyldisilazane (HMDS) as a primer for increased adhesion between 
photoresist and wafer (5 minutes process time). The desired reactor design was then introduced using 
a maskless aligner by exposing the photoresist to UV irradiation (365 nm, ~1.5 hours per wafer) 
followed by puddle development of the exposed regions using 2.38 % (v/v) tetramethyl-
ammoniumhydroxid (TMAH) in water. The wafers were then subjected to an inductively charged 
plasma (ICP) using CF4 gas, which were directed towards the substrate selectively reacting with the 
exposed Si3N4 masking layer forming volatile species (5.3e-3 mbar, 0 oC, 174 sccm He, 40 sccm CF4, 
1300 W coil power, 350 W platen power, 30 seconds process time). The residual photoresist layer 
was removed using a plasma asher (1000 W, 400 mL/h O2, 70 mL/h N2, 30 minutes process time) 
revealing a silicon wafer with a structured Si3N4 masking layer. The wafers were then cleaned using 
a piranha solution, rinsed with water and spin dried. The exposed silicon area was etched using 28 % 
(w/v) KOH (80 oC, etching time: ~2.75 hours, etching rate: ~1.2 µm/min). The desired etching depth 
was 200 µm, which was achieved by underdevelopment of the wafers, rinsing with water, spin drying 
followed by the channel depth being analyzed using profilometry to estimate etch rate dependent on 
position in the KOH etching bath. The wafers were then reintroduced to the KOH bath and the etching 
continued based on the etching rates found. Channels were then analysis by profilometry to validate 
desired etching depth. The Si3N4 masking layer was then removed by submersion in 85 wt. % 
phosphoric acid (160 oC, 4 hours process time) yielding a fully structured silicon wafer. 
Borofloat® 33 wafers were brought out of the cleanroom to a laser micromachining tool where 0.5 
mm inlet and outlet holes were introduced (TBW Picosecond laser, 1064 nm, 10 min process time pr. 
wafer). The Borofloat® 33 wafers were then brought back into the cleanroom and subjected to the 
following cleaning procedures: ultrasonication with Triton X-100, water rinse, piranha solution, water 
rinse and -spray, spin drying.

mbar tool force, 5e-3 mbar chamber pressure, 1.5 hours process time per set of wafers).  
For metal sputtering a 100 µm stainless steel shadow mask with 1 cm inlet and outlet holes was 
fabricated using the laser micromachining tool (TBW Picosecond laser, 1064 nm, 5 min process 
time). The shadow mask was brought into the cleanroom and cleaned by ultrasonication with Triton 
X-100 and a water rinse. The shadow mask was placed on top of the Borofloat® 33 side of the bonded 
wafer stack, aligning the holes with the inlet and outlet holes of the wafer stack, and fastened with 
Kapton tape. The wafer stack was then inserted in a sputtering-system where it was subjected to a 
surface cleaning using an argon plasma (50 W, 4e-3 mbar chamber pressure, 3 minutes process time), 
followed by deposition of 100 nm titanium (150 W, 4e-3 mbar chamber pressure, 33 minutes process 
time) and deposition of 500 nm copper (220 W, 4e-3 mbar chamber pressure, 50 minutes process 
time). The thicknesses of the metals deposited was not measured but based on other users’ process 
parameters. This was due to layer thickness is difficult to estimate by ellipsometry when multiple 
metal layers are on top of each other. Preparation and analysis of individual layers would have added 
~2 hours of extra process time per reactor. Since soldering of connections failed by microcracking of 
the Borofloat® 33 wafer due to thermal shock, and not due to layer thickness, a point where 
optimization of the bonding strength based on deposited metal thickness was never reached. Hence 
verification was omitted for the sake of improved process time.

6.4 General procedures 
General procedure 1: [2+2] cycloadditions with N-Methyl maleimide 
A standard solution of thioxanthone (0.1 equiv.) and N-Me maleimide (1 equiv.) dissolved in MeCN 
at 0.1 M was prepared. To 20 mL of the standard solution an alkene (1.5 equiv.) or alkyne (1.5 equiv) 
were added and degassed for 30 min under nitrogen. The solution was transferred to a syringe and 
pumped through the chip at 6 mL/h under 375 nm irradiance at 35 ºC using a Model 11 syringe pump 
from Harvard Apparatus. 2 mL of the solution was pumped through the chip to ensure steady state 
conditions. A 1 mL aliquot was sampled for analysis of the crude reaction mixture by NMR and TLC. 
The reaction was sampled for 2.75 h followed by concentration under reduced pressure to yield a 
residue that was further purified by flash chromatography. 
 
General procedure 2: [2+2] cycloadditions with citraconic anhydride 
Thioxanthone (0.1 equiv.) was dissolved in MeCN. Citraconic anhydride (1 equiv., 0.1 M) and alkene 
(1.5 equiv.) were added and degassed for 30 mins under nitrogen. The solution was transferred to a 
syringe and pumped through the chip at 6 mL/h under 375 nm irradiance at 35 ºC using a Model 11 
syringe pump from Harvard Apparatus. 2 mL of the solution was pumped through the chip to ensure 
steady state conditions. A 1 mL aliquot was sampled for analysis of the crude reaction mixture by 
NMR and TLC. The reaction was sampled for 2.75 h followed by concentration under reduced 
pressure to yield a residue that was further purified by flash chromatography.
