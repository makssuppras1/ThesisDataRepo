Abstract
This thesis covers the design process, and results of a project focused on
prototyping an asset tracking and temperature monitoring system, using
existing internet connected temperature sensors — the project aimed at dis-
covering how Eupry could engage in the domain of temperature monitoring
for transports.
Three practical use cases, for such a system, were analysed and evaluated
based on contextual interviews and ﬁeldwork.
A concept and a working
prototype were developed based on the results.
The resulted prototype is a system, which interacts with the sensors,
servers and the user with a smartphone app, deployed with modern prototyp-
ing platforms. It allows further investigation of the potential of combining
temperature monitoring with asset tracking, e.g. in medical transportation
domains.

Motivation
Eupry is a young Danish company that provides temperature monitoring as
a service to their customers. It is crucial to monitor storage temperatures for
certain types of medicine, to ensure its potency and security. Their primary
customers are pharmacies, laboratories and practitioners, but Eupry are
interested in exploring new domains. They are interested in exploring how
their existing solutions could be modiﬁed and expanded to allow them to
engage the transportation domain.
All tobacco products have to be traceable in the EU where each pack
has a unique ID and can be traced back to its original production, as part
of the EU Tobacco Products Directive [1]. EU also aims to strengthen the
security of the supply chain of medicines as well and has in 2019 required
most prescription medicines and some over-the-counter medicines to include
a unique ID as part of their Falsiﬁed Medicines Directive [2].
This is an opportunity for Eupry to expand their temperature monitoring
system to include the distribution of medicine. This thesis aims at looking
at the technical part for Eupry to engage this new domain and not the
commercial part.

1.1
Introduction
Food and medicine, i.e. temperature-sensitive goods, degrade relatively to
their ambient temperatures. It is therefore important to monitor ambient
temperatures to ensure quality, potentiality, and safety. There is a growing
market for IoT solutions for refrigerator monitoring, which uploads temper-
atures and alarms users when temperatures exceed deﬁned limits. These
solutions are based on the fact, that refrigerators are stationary and tem-
peratures are supposed to never deviate from the deﬁned limits.
After production temperature-sensitive goods have to be transported to
where the products are to be used, i.g. laboratories, restaurants and practi-
tioners. Monitoring the temperatures during the transportation is necessary
to ensure quality during the goods complete lifecycle. Today, this is mostly
monitored by logging the temperatures and reviewing the observations after
transport. This is insuﬃcient since deviations are identiﬁed too late. [3]
This project aims to explore the possibilities of internet-accessible tem-
perature monitoring when transporting temperature-sensitive goods and de-
velop a system for such a solution. This is done by using design methods,
to explore use cases, conduct potential users, conceptualise, prototype and

1.4
Technology research
The ﬁeldwork phase concluded that a solution is required to be cellular
connected. The technology research phase was conducted in order to explore
ways to connect Eupry’s sensors the cellular network.
Eupry’s sensors (in their standard conﬁguration) stores measurements
every third minute and uploads data every 12th hour. The sensor uploads
instantly if it measures values that exceed user-deﬁned thresholds.
This
means that Eupry requires a modem that provides:
• Low power consumption
• Good coverage (to be able to penetrate fridge walls)
• Connection that supports deep sleep mode
The regular cell networks (EDGE, 3G, LTE) fall short on power con-
sumption and deep sleep mode [11].
Multiple Low Power Wide Area Networks (LP-WAN) were superﬁcially
analysis, including LoraWAN, Sigfox, Narrowband-IoT (NB-IoT) and LTE-

1.4.1
Narrowband-IoT
NB-IoT is a newly deﬁned cellular network standard which aims at providing
a secure and viable network for the growing market for machine to machine
(M2M) communication [12]. NB-IoT is utilising the already widely imple-
mented LTE infrastructure and is considered to be the best suited network
for scenarios where coverage, energy eﬃciency and the cost is prioritised [13]
[14].
A relevant downside of this network is that roaming is not natively sup-
ported, and it is up to the network carrier to make agreements across borders
[11]. NB-IoT does not support cell tower handover, which means that a de-
vice would have to disconnect before being able to reconnect to a new tower.
This issue is mostly related to constant data steams [15].
Telia and TDC have launched complete coverage in Denmark, and both
claim to launch Release 14 in 2020, which among other will provide geo-
location to a device when it connecting [11, 16].
1.4.2
Experiment
NB-IoT were analysed in an academic paper-styled text in chapter 3. It
includes a literature study on the network and a practical experiment. The
goal of the experiment was to test whether or not the network was feasible
to use to transfer data reliable when in movement. The setup consisted of
Eupry’s NB-IoT prototype in a car which was driving from Copenhagen to
Sønderborg.
The conclusion was, that coverage along that route is good and average
delay was aligned with the hypothesis, but the device lost connection for 6
hours at one point of the experiment. Unfortunately it was not possible to

Conceptualisation & Prototyping
This section describes the development of a working prototype, based on the
deﬁned problem deﬁnition. It includes three iterations, which each includes

1.5.1
First concept iteration
To identify and brainstorm on which functions to introduce to Eupry´s
exsisting was a function/mean tree analysis [6] conducted. This revealed four
key functions: Enclosure, state change, matching, and start/stop of delivery.
Diﬀerent means were explored for each function, which was sketched and put
into a morphology chart [17], where more sketches and storyboards where
created on the basis of diﬀerent combinations of the means.
A ﬁnal concept was created based on thought and ideas from the other
concepts. This is further described in section 4.2.

Server
The server was chosen to be implemented on a Raspberry Pi 3 1,
which allowed the server to be online and accessible at all times anywhere.
The software was deployed with Docker2, which allows the application to
be shared and run independently of the users own system.
The application was chosen to be developed with Node-Red3. This plat-
form was chosen over e.g. PHP or Node.js since it is to develop asynchronous
functions and gaining an overall overview of the complete system. The down-
side is, that Node-RED is lacking scalability, but since this is a prototype
development, this was not considered an issue.
Scanner
The scanner was developed using Apple iOS Shortcuts. Most
of the desired methods were already build into the platform, which made
it ideal for the prototype. Alternatives here were either programming an
actual app or make a mock-up, which were concluded to be too ambitious
and too unambitious.
Dashboard
To get an initial idea of how the dashboard should look, was
sketches of the dashboard drawn on paper (appendix I. The sketch was
realised using HTML, CSS and JavaScript, using libraries for styling and
charts. This approach was chosen over digital mock-ups to allow the dash-
board to responsive and displaying actual data from the server. The down-
side of this method is that pure HTML and JavaScript quickly becomes
chaotic since functions cannot be spread into multiple ﬁles.
This would
have been avoided by developing in more ambitious JavaScript frameworks,
e.g. React, Angular or Vue.

1.6. Conclusion
1.6
Conclusion
This project ends at this concept and prototype for Eupry to server cus-
tomers in the transportation domain. It is a way for Eupry to investigate
whether or not they should expand into this area. The developed prototype
serves as an example on how Eupry’s system can integrate with asset track-
ing systems and how their system can be managed a more dynamic way.
The prototype requires further development before being ready for actual
use cases. The prototype can be used to verify the concept or showcased to
potential customers with interest. Perseqour could be a potential partner
with Eupry, as their combined assets will provide features unmatched by the
existing solutions.
The system could be used by Coop Food Trucks, by considering each
styrofoam box as an area and placing the NB-IoT sensors in the daily used
boxes.
They would still need integration into their management system,
to avoid supporting two separate systems. Buur & Søgaard could use the
system by considering each van and their warehouse as areas and use their
delivery IDs as deliveries.
Further development in integration into their
system would be required.
Rigshospitalet’s Blood Bank would be more
diﬃcult because of the way they transport their blood bags. They would
either have to use insulated cabinets instead of boxes or use one sensor for
each box and develop a system where sensors would return.
NB-IoT was found troublesome for usage in transportation, but reliable
for stationary instalments which would reduce installation time and price
signiﬁcantly.
The developed system is considered a success for investigating how Eupry
can use their existing platform in the transportation domain.

Fieldwork
This chapter analyses potential users of an internet-accessible monitoring
system for transportation. Three cases will be investigated, including Coop
Food Trucks, Buur & Søgaard, and Rigshospitalet’s Blood Bank.
These
cases are identiﬁed by Eupry as potential users and represent diﬀerent uses

The chapter results in a problem deﬁnition based on conclusions derived
from the analysis and similar existing solutions will be identiﬁed.
This exploration is following the Emphasise phase from Design Thinking
[4] and the Discover phase from Double Diamond [5], and not all described

I. INTRODUCTION
The recent development of Low Power Wide Area Network
(LP-WAN) technologies has allowed Internet of Things (IoT)
applications to be used in various new scenarios where long
range is needed, and power is scarce. Implementations of LP-
WAN is diverse with multiple network technologies, such as
Sigfox, LoRaWan, LTE-M and NB-IoT, which each has its
advantages. NB-IoT is considered to be the most suited for
scenarios where coverage, energy efﬁciency and the cost is
prioritised since NB-IoT is utilising the already widely imple-
mented LTE infrastructure [1] [2]. NB-IoT was standardised
in 3GPP LTE Release 13, providing a cellular radio access
technology for IoT in licensed spectrum. NB-IoT has three
deployment options; inside an LTE carriers band (in-band),
in the guard band (guard band) or a designated spectrum
(standalone). In release 14, several enhancements and new
functionalities are introduced to NB-IoT, including positioning
of the devices. [3]
NB-IoT is designed and optimised for static use and does
not support cell tower handover, which means that a device has
to disconnect before being able to connect to a new cell tower
[4]. This feature is primarily used to preserve a continuous
connection while streaming of data, e.g. voice or video, when
travelling.
One use case of NB-IoT is temperature monitoring of stored
foods. Food degrades relatively to their ambient temperatures,
and it is, therefore, vital to monitor ambient temperatures to
ensure quality and safety. While this can be done using a wiﬁ-
connected IoT-thermometer, the task is more complicated in
transportation, since LTE modems are not power optimised.
Monitoring the temperatures during transportation is necessary
to ensure quality and safety during the goods complete life-
cycle. Today, this is mostly monitored using an unconnected
logger and reviewed after the transport. This is insufﬁcient
since deviations are identiﬁed too late.
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows; Section
II describes other works related to this. Section III describes
the experimental setup, followed by the results in Section IV.
Discussion and conclusion will be covered in section V and
VI.
II. RELATED WORKS
Cisco predicts 28.5 billion connected devices by 2022,
where 51% will be machine to machine (M2M) devices. This
is a growth from 18 billion in 2017 [5]. Is it, therefore,
necessary for LTE carriers to investigate required sites to cover
areas and amount of supported connected devices.
[6] simulates coverage in a large area in Northern Denmark
and concludes that NB-IoT can provide coverage to 99.9%
of connected outdoor and indoor devices (up to 20 dB signal
loss), and 96% to deep indoor devices (30 dB signal loss).
Similar simulations have been conducted in [7] [1].
[8] have tested NB-IoT coverage at Tallinn University of
Technology using a physical experiment, and validates the
simulated results from [7], [6], and [1].
This paper presents a practical implementation of an NB-
IoT connected device in motion. This will test the connection
when a device has to shift connection to new cell towers as
the device is transported through Denmark.
III. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
Eupry provides the NB-IoT connected device. It is utilising
a SimCom NB-IoT modem (SIM7020G) on Telia’s network
to connect via NB-IoT. The device is programmed to store
ambient temperature every third minute, connect and upload
data every 10th minute. If the transfer is successfully are the
modem set into sleep until next transfer, it will otherwise
attempt to retry the transfer. The device will retry after 13
minutes, and subsequent fails will double the previous delay.
The device is placed in the windshield of a car, travelling
from Copenhagen to Sønderborg, Denmark, and is paired with
an iPhone 7 which logs GPS data, which is merged into the
data set.
1) Assumptions & Uncertainty:
The network is newly
deployed in Denmark. It is unknown how well the route is
covered.
Eupry’s device is in a prototype state and not a ﬁnished
product. It is expected that the performance can be optimised
by further development of the ﬁrmware. The same goes for
the antenna in the device.
Effects from the weather conditions are considered as noise.
In actual transport, are the device not placed in the wind-
shield. It will most likely be placed inside a van (metal cage),
in a box. This will bring additional dampening effect on the
connection.

A. Hypothesis
The experiment aims to explore whether or not NB-IoT is
viable to use for uploading frequent small packages when in
movement. This is assumed to be true if a device can connect
and upload within 30 minutes when it is in movement. The
device is conﬁgured to upload every 10 minutes, and this
will effectively give the device 20 minutes to upload the data,
which is time for one retry attempt plus 7 minutes to connect
and transfer.
IV. RESULTS
The collected data is separated into two subsets, and one set
which includes all data, including during the night between 11
Dec 2019 and 12 Dec 2019, where the device was stationary.
This is done to easier explain an anomaly.
The ﬁrst subset, Out, includes all data from the outgoing
part of the drive, going from Copenhagen to Sønderborg and
the driving around on Als, from 11 Dec 2019 06:00 to 19:00.
In includes data from the next day, including driving around
on Als and driving back from Sønderborg, from 12 Dec 2019
09:00 to 18:00.
The data includes the time between each successful uploads

V. DISCUSSION
As apparent in table I and ﬁgure 1, are the average time
between each upload very close to 10 minutes. It is even
lower than the expected 10 minutes for the 2nd part of the
experiment, at 8.9 minutes, which probably is due to ﬁrmware
on the device. During the experiment was there two delays
which exceeded the success criteria of 30 minutes. First of
52 minutes occurred between Copenhagen and Køge and
second of 364 minutes, between Kolding and Sønderborg.
Both occurred in the ﬁrst part of the experiment.
It is unknown why this happens, but as it appears in ﬁgure
2c is this not due to coverage issues since the device was able
to connect on the 2nd half of the experiment. Unfortunately is
there too many uncertain variables in the experiment to fully
ﬁnd the cause, yet some reasons are listed in the following.
a) Prototype ﬁrmware: The device is in an early devel-
opment stage, which means that the ﬁrmware might include
some mistakes. The device can have been unable to connect
due to software being stuck.
b) No tower hand-over: NB-IoT does not support tower
hand-over, which might be problematic when the device
changes towers. This is usually only a problem when having a
streaming connection and not when uploading small packages
as in this experiment. The problem might be due to the need
to renew its IP address, and the device is slow to recognise
this problem. If this is the case, then the problem might be
solvable by updating the ﬁrmware.
The LTE-M, which includes tower hand-over but compro-
mises at power efﬁciency and range, will be another solution
to this problem, but since this network is not deployed in
Denmark, is this not viable yet.
c) Network failure: The network may have been ofﬂine
during this time, but Telia has not conﬁrmed this.
VI. CONCLUSION
The device reliably transferred 210 out of 212 connections,
which indicates that the network is suitable for uses cases
where data is not required on demand, but this does not
conclude that it is reliable.
The experiment disproved the reliability hypothesis since
the device was ofﬂine in two time periods of 52 and 364
minutes. This does not necessarily mean that NB-IoT cannot
be used in this use case, as there are too many uncontrolled
variables in the experiment. It would require further work to
conclude this. To further investigate the reason should the
modem’s debugging text be logged and investigated. This
could reveal if the device is unable to connect because of
coverage issues or handover issues.

4.4
Second Concept Iteration
The prototype was shown to Buur & Søgaard and Perseqour (section 2.6.2),
as described in section 1.5.3. This feedback was used to revise the concept.
The new concept drops the idea of a transport and replaces it with a
new term, area. Items, e.g. medicine, can be in areas, e.g. warehouses, vans
or outside of the user’s responsibility. When items change area, an event
containing the speciﬁc item and an area happen. Events can be initiated
with a scanner (similar to the ﬁrst concept). Sensors can also be in areas
and provides data, e.g. temperature or humidity, to each item which is in the
same area at the same time. This means that ambient data of a speciﬁc item

cycle. Both areas and items can include thresholds for alarms. Area alarms
can, e.g. be used for areas for managers to ensure that temperatures are
within certain levels, to avoid items inside to spoil. Item alarms can, e.g. be
used for receivers of the item to control whether or not it has been stored
correctly during its complete life cycle.
The system is not registering if an item or area is in motion, but when
items and sensors enter new areas, controlled by events. This allows the
concept to comply with GS1 EPCIS 2.6.1 potentially.

Further development
Though this prototype is working, are multiple features from the User Story
Map not yet implemented. These, including additional works, are listed in
the following.
Test alternatives to QR-Codes
QR-codes could be replaced with RFID-
tags, which would make a diﬀerent user experience. The limitation of RFID
is that it requires a newer iPhone to function with iOS Shortcuts than what
was available.
The barcode on Eupry’s sensors could also be used to avoid cluttering
the sensors facade.
Random ID
The QR-code (or RFID-tag) should just include a random
code. A user can then add this code to the system with a user interface, and

A.1
Terminology
The following sections will use some speciﬁc terms:
Device is the connected thermometer, where Eupry’s NB-IoT prototype
will be used as an example.
Vessel is the medium in which goods can be transported in. This can i.e.
be the trunk of a cooled van or a styrofoam box. The devices main purpose
is to monitor and upload the ambient temperatures inside the vessel.
A device can either be in an alert-state or an idle-state. Recorded tem-
perature, which are outside user deﬁned thresholds are uploaded immedi-
ately when a device is alert, and ignored when idle.
It is important to note, that the device should always measure the temper-
ature, in case users forget to change mode on the device. The users will
therefore be able to inspect data even when the device is in an idle state.
The server, where the data is uploaded to, can choose to react to these de-
viations, either by ignoring them or sending an alarm to the user. Eupry’s
system is currently doing this either by SMS and/or e-mail. The same terms
are used to describe the state of the server.
A delivery is when a batch of goods is transported from a to b in a vessel.
It is ongoing when it is not yet complete.
Alarms happens when temperatures outside user-deﬁned thresholds are
registered. The alarm deﬁnition is taken from Eupry’s system, to allow a
prototype to utilise their existing system. The deﬁnition contains 5 values:
critical high, critical low, high, low and duration. When Critical high or low
is exceeded, will an alarm be initiated instantly, while readings are allowed
to exceed high or low for the time of duration. E.g., if high is set to be 5°,
and duration to 30 minutes, are the temperatures allowed to be above 5°for
30 minutes.

The server is built with Node-Red1 on a Raspberry Pi 3 2,

have inputs and or outputs, which connected becomes trigger-based pro-
grams. More technical speaking is Node-red simplifying asynchronous server
programming into a graphical and beginner-friendly interface, with great de-
bugging features.

component.
