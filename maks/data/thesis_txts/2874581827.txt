Abstract 
The first world’s first wind farm was installed in the Northeast of the United States in December 1980. Five 
years prior to this event, the Energy Policy and Conservation Act of 1975 was created as a response to the 
1973 oil crisis. Almost 50 years later and, with almost 140 GW of installed wind power capacity, the U.S. 
announced new actions to tackle a very well-known and alarming crisis, the climate crisis. An ambitious 
goal of 30 GW of offshore wind by 2030 is one of these initiatives.  
 
The Gulf of Mexico has been identified as a potential location for offshore wind energy generation. In 2021 
the National Renewable Energy Laboratory determined that the Gulf of Mexico has the potential to 
generate almost 500 GW of offshore wind energy per year.   
 
The first part of this thesis contains a brief analysis of the offshore wind energy potential in the Gulf of 
Mexico, aiming to understand the magnitude of the contribution that it could make to the overall 2030 
U.S. goals. The second part of this thesis focuses on the identification and analysis of the different barriers 
that could prevent or slow down the completion of these projects on a timely manner. 
 
The current lack of manpower, the trans-gulf bird migration, grid connectivity, the incorporation of equity 
and justice in decision making with local indigenous tribes and low-developed coastal communities, the 
lack of critical infrastructure and manufacturing capabilities, investment uncertainty, and hurricanes, are 
all challenges analyzed in this thesis. 
 
There is a lot of work ahead of the United States to analyze and address all these challenges in a timely 
and successful manner. Implementing its first offshore wind energy projects in the Gulf of Mexico is not 
going to be easy but the United States seems to be on the right track. The analysis included in this thesis 
leads me to believe that the 2030–2035-time frame for implementing offshore wind energy projects in the 
Gulf of Mexico is a realistic estimate.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Introduction 
Installed wind energy capacity has greatly expanded in the U.S. with a huge increase from approximately 
9 GW in 2005 to 143 GW as of January 2023, making it the fastest-growing energy source in the country.   
 
Figure I. Annual and Cumulative Growth in U.S. Wind Power Capacity. Data from the Office of Energy Efficiency 
and Renewable Energy. 
This growth is expected to maintain its momentum with offshore wind energy potentially being the major 
source of growth. A series of announcements made by the U.S. government and other federal authorities 
are proof of this. The first announcement was made by the White House in March 2021, with the goal of 
achieving 30 GW of offshore wind energy by 2030. Additional announcements from the White House were 
made throughout 2022 and 2023, including measures to expand the offshore wind industry in the U.S. In 
late 2021, the Department of Energy established the Energy Earthshots, “series of actions to accelerate 
breakthroughs of more abundant, affordable, and reliable clean energy within the decade”. Furthermore, 
the Inflation Reduction Act, in late 2022, provided additional incentives and legislation to foster renewable 
energy growth in the U.S. 
The U.S. Department of Energy included 40 GW from the U.S. Offshore Pipeline in the 2022 Offshore Wind 
Market Report. The majority of those are projects located on the northeast coast of the U.S., where wind 
speeds make these projects attractive. As floating technology develops, offshore wind energy projects in 
the Pacific Ocean are also garnering more interest. It was not until recently that the Gulf of Mexico started 
attracting some attention as well. In October 2021, the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM) 
included the Gulf of Mexico as one of the seven sites for new offshore wind lease auctions as part of their 
“Offshore Wind Leasing Path Forward 2021-2025” (Office of Energy Efficiency & Renewable Energy 2022). 
==PAGE:13==
 
2 
Despite lower wind speeds compared to the East and West coasts of the United States, approximately one 
third of U.S. shallow waters are located in the Gulf of Mexico (NREL 2022). A local oil and gas workforce 
and infrastructure, with experience in offshore projects, has started transitioning into the renewable 
industry and will most likely continue doing so as the offshore wind energy industry gains momentum. 
Furthermore, specific state goals such as the one in Louisiana where, through their first ever Climate Action 
Plan, with the goal of five gigawatts of offshore wind power generation by 2035 (State of Louisiana 2023), 
could make offshore wind energy projects in the Gulf of Mexico become a reality. 
On the other hand, some important challenges need to be overcome before the world sees energy from 
offshore wind turbines being produced in this region. A robust and well-established supply chain is needed 
if the U.S. is to reach its offshore 2030 goals. Obtaining all the manpower, manufacturing, and 
infrastructure resources required to complete all the offshore projects’ pipeline will be a major challenge. 
Specific challenges inherent to the Gulf of Mexico such as the trans-Gulf bird migration, the presence of 
hurricanes, or the incorporation of equity and justice practices for indigenous tribes and coastal low-
income communities as the supply chain gets established, could complicate the development of these 
projects. 
The two goals of this thesis are: 
 
To explain the growth opportunity for the offshore wind energy industry that the Gulf of Mexico 
offers from an energy generation and supply chain perspective.  
 
To describe the major challenges that threaten the prompt development of these projects. 
Finally, based on the analysis of those opportunities and challenges, a personal assessment of the potential 
time frame for deploying these projects in the Gulf of Mexico is provided. 
 
1.2 Project Scope 
The first part of this thesis includes a general overview of the current status of wind energy in the United 
States, the major milestones and incentives that have led the country to where it is today, and the most 
recent announcements made by the White House and other federal authorities that will shape the future 
of wind energy in the United States. 
The second part of this thesis delves into the specifics of wind energy in the U.S. states bordering on the 
Gulf of Mexico. An analysis of the status of wind energy in these coastal states describes the role that each 
one of these states could potentially play in the development of offshore wind projects in this region. This 
analysis contains information on the approaches that these states have taken, if any, to develop the wind 
energy industry; the amount of installed wind energy capacity; and the policies and incentives that have 
shaped the past and the future of wind energy in their states. The resource potential of the Gulf of Mexico 
and the progress achieved in this development process currently underway in the western area of the Gulf 
of Mexico are also discussed. 
The nucleus of this thesis, contained in the third part, explores the major challenges which could hinder 
the development of offshore wind projects in the Gulf of Mexico. The challenges covered in this research 
are, in my opinion, the most critical from the standpoint of their relevance to the timely and ethical 
development of these wind energy projects. These challenges are as follows:

2. Project Methodology 
2.1 Selection of Topic of Research – Offshore Wind Energy in the U.S. 
The number of land-based wind turbine projects in the U.S. has been significantly increasing since the 
early 2000’s, but offshore wind generation is relatively new. Only a couple of projects, totaling 42 
megawatts of offshore wind power, have been commissioned so far on the northeast coast of the United 
States. However, as of 2022, the U.S. has more than 40 GW in its offshore wind pipeline (Musial et al. 
2022). Recent announcements made by the White House and other federal agencies indicate that the U.S. 
has initiated a historical transformation to renewable energy.  
This transformation won’t be an easy process due to the relatively little experience that the U.S. has had 
in the offshore wind energy industry. In addition, many economic, social, logistical, environmental, and 
technical obstacles need to be overcome first. To cite just a few examples from an economic perspective, 
there is uncertainty about how laws, incentives and initiatives supporting the transition to renewables will 
be legally interpreted and implemented (Lantz et al. 2021): specifically, the Inflation Reduction Act (White 
House, 2022) and the Earth Shot Initiatives (Office of Policy, 2021). From a logistics point of view, the 40 
GW offshore wind pipeline provides the U.S. with the opportunity to develop a robust supply chain.  
However, the requirements and time constraints for developing this supply chain in a timely manner 
renders this process very challenging (Shields et al. 2022).  
For all the aforementioned reasons, the U.S. is going through an exciting but complex stage in its history. 
It is very difficult to predict how the U.S. will exploit these opportunities and address these challenges. 
However, regardless of the manner in which it is done, the U.S. is taking some important steps towards a 
promising future in renewable energy.  
 
2.2 Selection of Location – Gulf of Mexico  
Wind energy projects in the northeast United States have been a priority for the country due to its high 
wind energy resources and shallow waters. The two existing offshore projects, plus the approximately 35 
GW in the offshore wind pipeline (Musial et al. 2022, 10), makes this region the focus of the growth 
required for achieving 30 GW of installed offshore wind energy generation by 2030 (White House, 2021). 
Considerable wind resources off the U.S. Pacific coast, combined with the current progress in developing 
offshore floating technology, are the reasons why an additional 5 GW of offshore wind can be found in the 
U.S. offshore pipeline (Musial et al. 2022, 10). For these reasons, many news reports and articles have 
been published covering offshore wind energy generation in these two regions.  
However, it was not until 2021 when the area of the U.S. bordering the Gulf of Mexico attracted sufficient 
investor interest to be included in the seven new offshore wind lease auctions announced by the U.S. as 
part of its “Offshore Wind Leasing Path Forward 2021-2025” pipeline (Musial et al. 2022, 9). 
==PAGE:16==
 
5 
Additionally, the 2022 Offshore Wind Market Report from the U.S. Department of Energy listed the Gulf 
of Mexico, the Great Lakes, and Hawaii as areas of interest for potential offshore wind energy generation 
projects. Due to my proximity to the Great Lakes, my initial response was to choose this area as my 
research topic, but sufficient data on this topic are unavailable. After some preliminary research on the 
Gulf of Mexico, the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management was found to have taken a series of measures 
for developing projects in these waters (BOEM 2023).  
Offshore wind energy projects on the Gulf of Mexico pose some additional challenges compared to those 
on the U.S. northeast and Pacific coasts: massive trans-Gulf bird migrations; frequent highly destructive 
hurricanes; and the presence of tribes and coastal low-income communities that have been historically 
excluded from decision-making processes (Shields et al. 2022). All have been negatively impacted by the 
consequences of climate change, such as flooding due to rising water levels (PBS News 2023). 
Incorporation of equity and justice issues into offshore wind development for tribes and low-income 
communities is an ethical consideration. Furthermore, it can also result in project slowdowns, relocation, 
or even cancellations due to community push back if these tribes and low-income communities are not 
adequately engaged at an early stage in offshore wind development (Shields et al. 2022). Engagement with 
these communities is a fundamental component of an ethically-based and successful planning process.  
For the reasons described in this section, the Gulf of Mexico has some unique characteristics compared to 
the rest of the U.S. The core of this thesis is to analyze why this region has attracted so much interest in 
the last several three years despite the additional challenges, and what the U.S. is doing to overcome all 
those challenges that could pose a threat to the developments of offshore wind energy projects in this 
region.  
 
2.3 Data Acquisition  
A great deal of data has been generated regarding the potential of offshore wind energy in the Gulf of 
Mexico since the BOEM included this region as part of their new “Offshore Wind Leasing Path Forward 
2021-2025” pipeline in 2021 (Musial et al. 2022, 9).  
Specifically, in-depth reports have been prepared covering critical areas, such as the supply chain, which 
could impact the timely implementation of these projects. For instance, the NREL, which is a national 
laboratory of the U.S. Department of Energy, drew up the report entitled “Supply Chain Implications of 30 
GW Offshore Wind Power by 2023” by Lantz et al. in 2021. An additional report called “A Supply Chain 
Road Map for Offshore Wind Energy in the United States” was produced by Shields et al. in 2023. The U.S. 
Department of Energy included specific information about offshore wind energy projects in the Gulf of 
Mexico in their 2022 Edition of the Offshore Wind Market Report. A great deal of useful and detailed 
information was provided by these reports, especially for the chapters of this thesis on “Infrastructure 
Needs” and “Manpower Needs”.  However, many additional points of view were needed to validate the 
accuracy of the data. This methodology, called Triangulation in Qualitative Research, “focuses on the 
combination of data drawn from different sources and at different times, in different places or from 
different people” (Flick, U. 2004, page 178). The triangulation methodology has been the object of criticism 
due to the fact that “every different method constitutes the issue it seeks to investigate in a specific way” 
and that “if this aspect is neglected, triangulation is faced with the accusation of ‘extreme eclecticism’” 
==PAGE:17==
 
6 
(Flick, U. 2004, page 179). Therefore, with this in mind, I used the triangulation methodology as a valuable 
instrument for obtaining additional information, while exercising caution as to how to use this information 
in a timely manner for validation purposes.  
 
Major additional sources of information used as part of the Triangulation Methodology are as follows: 
 
 
Interviews: All the interviews were conducted after research had provided a good insight into the 
topic. Specific points needed to be clarified that were deemed relevant to this thesis.  An open 
and friendly atmosphere had to be fostered to encourage the people interviewed to offer me 
additional information. This approach resulted in newly generated data for subsequent interviews 
or follow up conversations with people who had already been interviewed. These interviews could 
be categorized as semi-structured Interviews. “In semi-structured interviews, researchers use 
information they have acquired to construct questions that are more focused. A semi-structured 
interview is commonly used later in a research study, when the researcher is seeking further 
clarification about some area that was discussed in earlier interviews and is relevant to the 
research question” (Olson 2011, 40).  
 
Phone interviews were conducted with two former oil and gas workers who are now working for 
renewable energy companies. As former students in the DTU master’s degree program in Wind 
Energy, they openly shared a great deal of useful information as to how the skills acquired from 
the oil and gas industry had proven beneficial for their current roles in renewable energy, and how 
this had motivated them to switch to the wind energy industry.  This information was used mainly 
in the chapters on “Infrastructure Needs” and “Manpower Needs”.  
 
Another semi-structured interview was conducted via email with a member of the U.S. Fish and 
Wildlife Service. This provided a better insight into the activities of this agency and other federal 
and state groups who are addressing the current challenges posed by offshore wind turbines and 
bird migration through the Gulf of Mexico. Through this interview, not only the questions were 
answered, but also additional information and references were given. Several follow-up emails 
with additional questions were subsequently sent. 
 
Attempts were made to interview members of local tribes (Tunica-Biloxi) and parishes from low-
income communities in the State of Louisiana (Vermilion, St. Bernard, and Terrebonne) to 
understand the outcome of their engagement with federal or state agencies as offshore projects 
are being developed in the State of Louisiana. However, these e-mails went unanswered and two 
people who were supposed to be interviewed terminated phone calls after details on the nature 
of the interview were provided.

3. Status of Renewable Energy in the U.S.  
3.1 General Information about Renewable Energy in the United States 
The U.S. is in the midst of a rapid energy industry transformation. Electricity generated by non-renewable 
sources of energy has historically been predominant; however, in the most recent years (2020’s), a series 
of political decisions and the development of more reliable technologies for generating energy from 
renewable sources, started reshaping the energy industry in the United States. Renewable energy is the 
fastest-growing energy source in the United States, increasing 42 percent from 2010 to 2020 (up 90 
percent from 2000 to 2020). On the other hand, coal generated about 19.5% of the electricity at utility-
scale facilities in the United States in 2022; it is down from 42% in 2014. Renewables account for nearly 
20 percent of utility-scale U.S. electricity generation in 2020, with the bulk coming from hydropower (7.3 
percent) and wind power (8.4 percent). The image below shows the trends for the different sources of 
electricity generation in the U.S. since 1950. The year 2020 marked an important milestone in the history 
of the U.S. when the use of renewable energy surpassed the total amount of electricity generated by coal 
for the first time.

3.2 General Information about Wind Energy in the United States 
As of January 2023, the U.S. has approximately 143 GW (~70,000 wind turbines) of installed wind energy 
capacity which accounts for 12% of total US energy generation.  
The first projects were a consequence of the 1970´s energy crisis when the Western world faced 
substantial petroleum shortages. The Energy Policy and Conservation Act of 1975 encouraged programs, 
research and projects involving alternative fuels. The Altamont Pass wind farm, commissioned in 1981, 
was the first major wind energy project in the history of the U.S. Additional policies and incentives such as 
the Public Utility Regulatory Policies Act (PURPA) or the National Energy Conservation Act (NECPA), both 
from 1978, were implemented to facilitate interconnection with renewable power production facilities 
and interstate commerce. It was, however, thanks to the Investment Tax Credits (1980-1986) and then 
with Production Tax Credits (first one in 1992), that wind energy in the U.S. experienced faster growth. The 
graph below shows the annual and cumulative growth in U.S. wind power capacity from 1999 to 2021. As 
can be seen in the graph, wind power capacity started growing in the early 2000’s, and then at a faster 
pace after 2005. 
 
 
Figure 3. U.S Installed Wind Capacity (MW). Image from energy.gov 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
11 
The Production Tax Credit (PTC) has been the primary incentive for wind energy in the U.S. It was first 
established in 1992, and then renewed on several occasions. The Production Tax Credit (PTC) allows 
owners and developers of wind energy facilities (land-based and offshore) to claim a federal income tax 
credit on every kilowatt-hour of electricity sold to an unrelated party for a period of 10 years after a facility 
is put into service. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) extends the renewable energy PTC through 2024; it 
previously expired for wind at the end of 2021. 
Wind energy policy mostly originates at the state level due to limited access to relevant federal incentives. 
State policies offer incentives and tax credits for both producers and consumers to make wind energy more 
affordable. Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) and state grant programs are also used to increase wind 
energy usage in the United States.

3.3.1 Wind Energy in the State of Florida 
Approximately 75% of Florida’s electricity is provided by natural gas. Of the ~5.5% electricity from 
renewable energy sources, most comes from solar energy, and none from wind energy. 
Due to relatively low wind speeds in the state of Florida, there is not much potential for wind energy 
generation. In 2013, Wind Capital Group received local and state permits to build Florida’s first wind farm, 
the Sugarland Wind project, but it was canceled before construction had begun due to “market forces” 
(Pittman 2021).  
 
3.3.2 Wind Energy in the State of Alabama 
Electricity in the state of Alabama is supplied mainly by natural gas (38%), nuclear power (33%), coal (19%), 
and hydro (8%). There is only a small percentage of energy generation from solar energy, and none from 
wind.  
Alabama’s mean wind speeds are also low. Only certain areas in the north of the state have relatively high 
wind speeds. However, there are no current plans for offshore wind energy farms in Alabama in the near 
future. According to the Corporate Clean Energy Procurement Index, “Alabama makes it harder than any 
other state for companies to use clean energy” (Pillion 2021). 
 
3.3.3 Wind Energy in the State of Mississippi 
Natural gas is also the main source of electricity in the state of Mississippi (73%). Nuclear energy accounts 
for 18% and coal for 8%. Less than 1% of the electricity comes from renewable energy (mainly solar), and 
there is no wind energy generation.  
Overall, mean wind speeds are low in the state of Mississippi, but they are higher along the Mississippi 
River. As a consequence of this, this state recently announced their first wind farm in the Mississippi Delta. 
The 200-MW Delta Wind Farm project will consist of 41 turbines, and it is expected to be operational in 
2024 (AES. Delta Wind Project). 
 
3.3.4 Wind Energy in the State of Louisiana 
Louisiana’s electric generation comprises 56% of natural gas, 24% of nuclear power, 11% coal, 5% oil, and 
approximately 2% of renewable energy. No wind energy is generated in this state.  
Mean wind speeds are low in general, except along the Mississippi River and on the western coastal areas. 
Although no wind energy is generated in Louisiana, this state recently announced their first ever Climate 
Action Plan (Office of the Governor 2022). The major goal of this action plan is to “limit the severity of 
climate change while positioning the state to maintain its economic competitiveness in a low-carbon 
future”. This plan includes a goal of 5 gigawatts of offshore wind power generation by 2035, citing the

as a strong offshore energy-producing state. 
3.3.5 Wind Energy in the State of Texas 
In contrast with the remaining states bordering the Gulf of Mexico, Texas has a lot of experience with wind 
energy generation. Texas is the leading state in wind energy installed capacity in the United States. There 
are approximately 19,000 land-based wind turbines for a total of ~40 GW of installed power. The graphs 
below show the annual and accumulative growth of installed wind energy power and number of turbines 
from 1999 through 2021. 
 
 
Figure 5. Annual and accumulative growth of installed wind energy power and number of turbines. USGS – The

In the early 2000’s a combination of high wind speeds and pro-wind energy policies and incentives, created 
the ideal scenario for the development of hundreds of land-based wind energy farms. The Clean Energy 
Act of 1999 created a hungry market where utilities were required to buy renewable power. At about the 
same time, production tax credits were offered that encouraged investors to put money into wind power. 
Furthermore, dry-land cotton farming regions in central Texas discovered that installing wind turbines on 
their farms would provide them with a more reliable source of income. The number of installed wind 
turbines increased dramatically after 2006.  The Roscoe wind farm, located in central Texas, is one of the 
largest onshore wind farms in the world. It has 627 turbines and a total installed capacity of ~780 MW. 
Wind energy accounts for 24% of the electricity generation in Texas. Natural gas is still the highest source 
of energy generation in the state with 44%, followed by coal with 22%, and 10% from nuclear power. 4% 
of the electricity comes from solar energy.

6.1 Challenge 1 – Manpower Needs 
6.1.1 General Information on Manpower Needs for Offshore Projects in the USA 
and the Gulf of Mexico 
A large workforce would be required in the U.S. to undertake all the projects needed for attaining 30 GW 
of offshore wind power by 2030. Manufacturing and supply chain jobs for producing components at 
different stages of the manufacturing process (from engineering and component design to production) 
would account for approximately 81% of the total workforce. About 7% of the workforce would be 
responsible for undertaking these projects (site assessment, plant design, obtaining permits, financing, 
project management, and other preconstruction activities), and the remaining 12% would be involved in  
port and staging activities (terminal crews and port logistics and management roles), maritime 
construction (projects at sea, including a maritime crew, engineers, and installation crews), and operation 
& maintenance (project operation and maintenance for the duration of the project, including wind 
technicians and plant managers) (Shields et al. 2023). 
The National Renewable Energy Laboratory presents two different scenarios in their document “A Supply 
Chain Road Map for Offshore Wind Energy in the United States.” In the first scenario, the U.S. would cover 
25% of the manufacturing and supply chain requirements to reach the goal of 30 GW of installed power 
by 2030, with the remaining requirements to be supplied by other countries. The U.S. would cover 100% 
of these requirements in the second scenario. For the first scenario, an average of 12,300 jobs would be 
needed between 2024 to 2030 and, for the second scenario, 49,000 jobs. The following graph shows the 
estimated number of jobs in these two scenarios. 
 
Figure 19. Potential job estimates across industry segments to support a project pipeline of 30 gigawatts of 
offshore wind energy by 2030. From the NREL “A Supply Chain Road Map for Offshore Wind Energy in the U.S.” 
==PAGE:38==
 
27 
As shown in the graph, total workforce requirements are expected to begin their upward trend at the 
bottom of the range and grow over time as the offshore wind energy industry develops. 
Approximately 9,000 workers would be required during construction for each one of the two proposed 
leasing areas in the Gulf of Mexico with ~1.2 GW each (Gerdes, J., 2020). 
 
6.1.2 Finding Enough workers to Cover Manpower Needs 
The first challenge for the U.S. to undertake all the projects required for generating 30 GW by the year 
2030 is to find the large manpower levels that are required. As described in the previous section, between 
12,300 and 49,000 workers must be hired, depending on the total number of the domestic workforce. At 
first glance, this domestic workforce might not appear to be very significant when compared to the total 
U.S population (approximately 330 million). However, workforce shortages already exist for some trades 
such as welders, and iron and steel workers, which are critical for the manufacturing and supply chains of 
offshore wind projects. This shortage results in a fierce competition for skilled labor among these 
industries.  
The offshore wind industry should hire workers in other industries, such as the oil and gas, land-based 
wind energy, aerospace, or maritime industries, who possess skills that are applicable to the offshore wind 
business (NREL). 
As the U.S. delves deeper into the transition to renewable energies with the support of different national 
goals and incentives, there will be a similar impact on jobs in the fossil-fuel industry. That transition has 
already started. The oil and gas industry shed some 700,000 workers between 2016 and 2022, while the 
renewable energy industry gained 113,000 during that same period (The New York Times 2022). From an 
interview sustained with a former oil and gas worker who is now employed at a renewable energy 
company, much of the decline in the number of oil and gas jobs can be attributed to low gas prices during 
COVID. Although not as steep, the decline in oil and gas employment will continue due to retirement and 
the unwillingness of younger people to work in the oil industry because of the social stigma it carries. 
Several other information sources agree that many oil and gas workers left their jobs due to the uncertainty 
that resulted from extreme variations in oil prices and the grave consequences of climate change (Texas 
Climate News 2021). On the other hand, this transition won’t be as appealing for many oil and gas workers 
because the renewable energy industry does not currently have the potential to pay the same salaries and 
benefits that the oil and gas industry offers (information from the former oil and gas worker interview). 
Also, the oil and gas industry is more unionized than solar and wind companies. According to the chief 
executive of the solar energy company Sunnova, wages have been increasing over the last two years, so 
the wage gap between these two industries has narrowed.  
This transition of U.S. oil and gas company workers to renewable energy firms would be facilitated by 
having the former diversify their portfolio into renewal energies. Many of the traditional fossil-fuel 
companies in Europe such as Equinor, Orsted, and Shell have already begun to transition to renewable 
energy, so this trend could be repeated in the United States. Several information sources have shown that 
some commitment from U.S. oil and gas industries to invest in clean energy research has already started 
but, as of today, clean energy only accounts for a very small portion of their overall revenue (Murray, J. 
2020). From another interview sustained with a former oil and gas worker who transitioned to a renewable 
==PAGE:39==
 
28 
energy company, he acknowledged that U.S. oil and gas companies are starting to diversify their portfolio 
by becoming more involved in renewable energy projects but, he feels as if they just do it to clean up their 
image and because of the incentives currently being given by the U.S. government.     
Some examples of the skills that the oil and gas industry could provide to the offshore wind energy industry 
are geotechnical engineering, surveying, marine engineering, foundation package managers, offshore 
construction specialists, installation managers, cable installation engineers, subsea engineers, and 
environmental surveyors (Shield et al. 2022).  
The transition from manufacturing companies that supply components to the offshore wind energy 
industry has already started as well. One example is the Louisiana-based company Gulf Island Fabrication, 
which has built steel foundations for offshore drilling platforms for nearly 40 years and has played a key 
role in the early development of U.S. offshore wind energy in the Atlantic Ocean (Larino, J., 2019). This 
type of transitions will help with fulfilling the manpower needs related to the supply chain. 
 
6.1.3 Training the Workforce  
As described in the previous section, oil and gas workers could move to the offshore wind energy industry. 
Many of them possess transferable work skills that could be applied directly to the offshore wind industry. 
For instance, a former oil and gas worker who is now working for a renewable energy company, explained 
that his experience in oil and gas was primarily as a deepwater/subsea project engineer/manager. He was 
involved in all phases of projects from conceptual evaluation through engineering/design, 
construction/installation, and handover to operations. Even though he recognizes that some training was 
still required, many of his skills were transferable and allowed him to adapt quicky to his new job in the 
offshore wind energy industry. He acknowledges that the oil and gas industry and infrastructure are 
already well established, and he will need to adapt to a new industry that is basically starting from scratch. 
From a second conversation with another former oil and gas worker, he explained that his mechanical 
engineering skills and experience analyzing subsea corrosion protection of steel components such as ducts 
and structures, are transferable to the offshore wind energy industry.  
Even though the offshore wind industry will hire many workers with transferable skills, that won’t be 
enough to cover all the workforce needs (Shields et al. 2022), so attracting and training additional workers 
will be needed. According to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory, a wide variety of education and 
training programs to prepare the future offshore wind energy workforce are being implemented. These 
programs include entry-level courses, Global Wind Organization safety training, offshore wind technician 
courses, general introductions to the offshore wind industry, and graduate-level classes on the technical 
aspects of offshore wind energy system design. Even though these education and training programs are 
critical, it will still take some time before the workforce is fully trained.  
Training the necessary workers for the manufacturing and supply chain sectors is critical as well. 
Community colleges and union-led training apprenticeship programs are some of the best positioned to 
support training and education for these roles, since they already provide it for similar roles in other 
industries. However, these programs will need to be expanded to ensure a sufficient number of workers, 
especially in a job market that is competitive for skilled trades. 
==PAGE:40==
 
29 
As far as engineering, management, and professional roles, maritime academies and universities are 
already implementing new programs. Community colleges and unions are also supporting training 
programs for the development of operation and maintenance roles, such as wind technicians.  
Given the pressure to reach offshore wind energy goals by 2030, collaboration between the above-
mentioned education and training institutions, industry, and community organizations will be critical for 
the development of the large and qualified workforce required to supply the offshore wind energy pipeline 
(Shields et al. 2022). Certain partnerships between organizations such as the National Offshore Wind 
Institute and the Offshore Wind Training Institute have already been set up.  
The Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM) is an example of other initiatives being undertaken by 
federal agencies to support workforce training programs. The BOEM is using a multi-factor auction format 
for the proposed leasing areas in the Gulf of Mexico which are involved in the bidding process. Under this 
system, the BOEM would consider a combination of factors, which would include a monetary factor (cash 
bid) and up to two non-monetary factors in the form of bidding credits, to determine the outcome of the 
auction. The BOEM is proposing the granting of bidding credits to bidders who support workforce training 
programs for the offshore wind industry, who develop a domestic supply chain for the offshore wind 
industry, or a combination of both (BOEM 2022). 
 
6.1.4 Location of the Workforce 
An additional challenge involves the location of the necessary manpower. Workers will need to be 
distributed across the U.S. depending on the projects’ needs. The types, numbers, and geographic 
locations of jobs will vary greatly during the operation of an offshore wind power plant. The projects are 
spread all over the country and this can result in greater variability in workforce demand. Therefore, 
workers should be trained and hired strategically to alleviate potential peaks of workforce demand. For 
example, jobs related to installation activities are temporary, but a large deployment pipeline allows 
workers to move to other projects if those projects are properly timed (NREL 2022).

procurement targets, or non-coastal states.

6.1.5 Discussion on Manpower Needs 
A large workforce will be required for the U.S. to successfully implement all the necessary projects to 
achieve the offshore wind energy goal of 30 GW by the year 2030.  
The main challenge for the offshore wind energy industry is to acquire a large workforce despite the 
workforce shortages for some critical trades such as welders, and iron and steel workers. The offshore 
wind energy industry can and should take advantage of the potential workforce from the oil and gas 
industry who are willing to transition to renewable energy. However, the number of oil and gas workers 
who may consider transitioning to renewable energy in the coming decade is less than the estimated 
workforce needs of the offshore wind industry. For this reason, collaboration between the education and 
training institutions, industry and community organizations will be critical for the development of a large 
and qualified workforce to supply manpower to the offshore wind energy pipeline.

The second challenge is related to the efforts that will be required to coordinate the distribution of the 
manpower potential to the manufacturing and supply chain sectors across the different states in the U.S. 
in accordance with the specific needs of each project. Most of the workers and supply chains within the 
oil and gas industry are located in the Gulf of Mexico, which does not align geographically with the offshore 
wind project pipeline. Strategies to have a portion of this workforce support projects in other regions of 
the U.S. without the need for relocation are being implemented, but this strategy does not work for all the 
different roles. This is, however, an advantage for the development of potential future offshore wind

6.2.1 General Information on Supply Chain Needs to Achieve Offshore Wind 
Energy Development Goals by 2030  
After the White House announced the target of 30 GW of offshore wind energy by 2030 (White House 
2021), several additional announcements were made afterwards with initiatives to boost and advance the 
national offshore wind supply chain road map.  
According to Shields et al. (2022), more than 2,100 offshore wind turbines would need to be installed to 
reach the goal of 30 GW by 2030. Historically, the U.S. has been capable of bringing together many 
components supplied by other countries to build wind turbines; however, currently the U.S. is not the only 
country that has announced ambitious offshore wind energy deployment goals. The limited global 
manufacturing capacity for these components creates a risk for U.S. projects that choose to import their 
components from Europe. Therefore, developing a domestic supply chain would reduce reliance on global 
manufacturers. This will require great efforts for the U.S. to create the necessary infrastructure and supply 
chain to meet those goals. Achieving this sustainable supply chain could help the United States create 
significant job opportunities and economic benefits throughout the entire country, not just in coastal 
states. However, developing such a supply chain requires strategic decision-making from many different 
stakeholders including federal and state governments, equipment manufacturers, suppliers, community 
representatives, project developers, port and vessel operators, and organized labor.

6.2.2 Developing Essential Supply Chains in a Timely Manner 
The previous section contained an estimate from the National Renewable Energy Lab (NREL) indicating the 
number or range of the infrastructure required to meet the goals. NREL estimates 6-9 years as the time 
frame for development; therefore, according to these estimates, the U.S. would need to move promptly 
to ensure this supply chain reaches the 2030 goal.   
There are still many uncertainties and constraints that prevent the U.S. from developing the supply chain 
in a timely manner. As an example of this, major offshore wind manufacturing facilities can only be built 
in suitable ports, but construction of these facilities is constrained by limited available port space and 
unpredictable timelines for obtaining permits and for construction. Furthermore, wind turbine technology 
continues evolving and increasing in size as higher capacities are reached, so certain infrastructure and 
ports could become obsolete quickly or require additional investment. To reach the 2030 goal, prompt 
decision-making and investment are required due to the long-time frame for obtaining permits and 
completing construction (Shields et al. 2022).  
The U.S. has been sourcing many sub-components for wind turbines from Europe; however, as European 
countries develop their own supply chains to meet their needs, their potential to supply parts to the U.S. 
will decrease. It is unlikely that sufficient production capacity exists to meet the demands of the national

offshore wind target. The U.S. could increase its capacity to supply these components by having existing 
domestic manufacturers, who are currently active in other sectors, make some of these parts. Some of 
these sectors could be land-based wind energy, oil and gas, or shipbuilding. However, this transition would 
not be easy because these domestic manufacturers would have to invest heavily in new certifications, 
training, and equipment. An example of this transition is the Louisiana-based company Gulf Island 
Fabrication, which supplied components to the oil & gas industry for nearly 40 years; they are now focused 
on supporting the development of offshore wind energy projects.  
Another challenge is the number of vessels required for installing wind turbines. There is a global shortage 
of wind turbine installation and heavy lifting vessels, and they have very long construction timelines: at 
least 3 to 4 years. Having the shipyards required for constructing such vessels is an additional challenge. 
There are currently not enough shipyards with the capacity and availability to build all the required vessels 
in a timely manner to meet the 2030 goals.  The Jones Act, a U.S. protectionist law that requires goods 
shipped between U.S. ports to be transported on ships that are built, owned, and operated by United 
States citizens or permanent residents, makes it even more difficult for the U.S. to obtain vessels 
elsewhere.

6.2.4 Discussion on Infrastructure and Supply Chain Needs 
A big investment of at least $22.4 billion needs to be made this decade to develop the necessary supply 
chain to meet the 2030 goal (NREL). This investment would be used to build thirty-four manufacturing 
facilities and ten marshalling or integration ports, among other construction projects.  Investments must 
be forthcoming in order to meet NREL’s estimated 6-to-9-year timeframe for developing this supply chain. 
However, there are still many uncertainties that could lead to doubts on the part of the investors, thus 
resulting in project delays.   
Supply chain manufacturers may not want to assume risks for making new investments without having a 
reliable 10-year customer pipeline (NREL). The U.S. Government and the BOEM have been making 
announcements and providing incentives to encourage offshore wind energy investments. Investor 
confidence should grow as new offshore projects move forward in the development and construction 
pipeline phases. 
Some of the economic incentives that are being provided or proposed to support project investments are 
the manufacturing tax credits announced as part of the Inflation Reduction Act; federal loans and grants; 
public-private partnerships; and bidding credits.  
Establishing collaborative industry supply chain groups to combine resources and strategies would ease 
the development of the required supply chain. Resources such as manufacturing facilities, ports, and 
vessels will be used by multiple project developers; creating a robust domestic supply of these assets will 
reduce the risk of delayed deployment due to bottlenecks in the global supply chain.

6.3 Challenge 3 - Investment Uncertainty 
As seen in the previous chapters, in order for the USA to reach the goal of 30 GW offshore wind energy by 
2030 and to be able to develop the offshore wind industry in the Gulf of Mexico, many challenges need to 
be overcome. Time to plan and collaboration efforts between many stakeholders are required for these 
challenges to be resolved but, in addition to that, big financial investments are needed. For instance, as 
seen in chapter 6.2.3, Entergy estimates that $1 billion are required for their energy grid resilience plan in 
the states of Louisiana to be completed. Investments will also be required for the proper authorities to 
study the impact that turbines in the Gulf of Mexico could have on bird migration. More investments 
required for wind turbine developers to come up with a technology that will be able to handle tornados. 
A huge investment for the development of the potential projects. The list could go on and on. These 
investments can’t wait given the time limitations to reach the established goals, however, there are still 
too many uncertainties surrounding these projects that can make investors hesitate. In this chapter I cover 
some of the main investment uncertainties identified. Most of which are a direct consequence of the 
challenges being covered in this report. 
 
6.3.1 Uncertainty Generated by the Implementation Timelines for the U.S. 
Offshore Wind Pipeline 
As seen in chapter 6.2, in order for the USA to be ready to deploy 30 GW of offshore wind power by 2030, 
a large manufacturing workforce is required. In addition to workforce shortages for some trades and the 
necessary time to train workers, there is also uncertainty about the necessary skills needed to manufacture 
next generation offshore wind turbine components (Shields et al. 2022). For these reasons, there is 
uncertainty regarding the availability of a specialized manufacturing workforce. This could delay the entire 
pipeline and therefore, it creates investment uncertainty.    
It is not only the manufacturing workforce that generates uncertainty around the timely delivery of turbine 
components, also the fact that permitting requirements for building offshore wind energy manufacturing 
facilities varies from state to state, creates additional complexity which increases the uncertainty regarding 
the viability of manufacturing components within the timeline required. The rapid growth in wind turbine 
size creates additional uncertainties since it is hard to know how it will continue evolving and, therefore, 
it is hard to design the appropriate manufacturing facilities for those unknown future needs. 
Many times, it is not only the size of the investment itself, but the uncertainty of when the offshore wind 
energy projects will start to be implemented. Without being able to project revenue streams, it is hard to 
justify the upfront investment. For example, Wind Turbine Installation Vessels may cost at least $500 
million and can take years of manufacturing. Such vessels require 500-800 MW of installation per year 
for 5 years to be economical (NREL). Vessel operators need to have multiple confirmed contracts to justify 
the investment in a large installation vessel, and it is a challenge to sign a contract to install an offshore 
wind project before the vessel is built. 
==PAGE:49==
 
38 
Additionally, since the Offshore Wind Energy is such a young industry in the USA, there is uncertainty 
surrounding the potential impacts of construction delays, cost overruns, legal complications, or changes 
in government support, thus creating an investment risk that makes it difficult to secure financing for new 
supply chain facilities. 
Different initiatives are being taken to mitigate the investment risk associated with uncertainty in the 
implementation timelines for the USA offshore wind pipeline. The White House has announced the 
national offshore wind target and BOEM has already provided leasing schedules through 2025 that 
expands the project pipeline into the 2030s. Furthermore, several projects are in different stages and some 
states have started investing in supply chain facilities. These activities convey a stronger demand signal 
than has been present in the United States in recent years and are important to increasing investor 
confidence in new supply chain facilities. 
 
6.3.2 Uncertainty Generated by IRA Policies 
Even though the Inflation Reduction Act will provide a significant benefit to domestically manufactured 
wind turbines, the full impacts of the IRA cannot be fully estimated at this stage due to some remaining 
uncertainty about how the law will be interpreted and how its implementing guidance will affect domestic 
manufacturing (Lantz et al. 2021). Additionally, as of now, these incentives are set to expire in 2032. Given 
the relatively long permitting and construction time it takes to bring new facilities online, the expiration of 
these incentives limits the ability of manufacturers to take advantage of tax credits during the planned 
useful life of the factory. 
 
6.3.3 Uncertainty Generated by the Merchant Marine Act of 1920 – Jones Act 
The Jones Act is a protectionist law that regulates maritime shipping in the United States and it requires 
goods shipped between U.S. ports to be transported on ships that are built, owned, and operated by 
United States citizens or permanent residents.  
Investment in new U.S.-flagged vessels has been slowed because of uncertainty surrounding future 
revisions or reinterpretations of the Jones Act that could limit access to the global installation vessel fleet 
and adversely impact project development timelines (Lantz et al. 2021).

6.4.1 General Information on Hurricanes in the Gulf of Mexico  
A tropical cyclone is a rotating low-pressure weather system with thunderstorms but no fronts. When a 
storm's maximum sustained winds reach 119 km/hr, it is called a hurricane. The Saffir-Simpson Hurricane 
Wind Scale is based on a 1 to 5 rating, or category, according to a hurricane's maximum sustained winds. 
The higher the category, the greater the hurricane's potential for property damage (information from the 
National Weather Service).  
Hurricane season in the Gulf of Mexico officially runs from June 1 to November 30. During this time, 
meteorologic and oceanographic conditions are conducive for hurricanes to develop anywhere in the Gulf. 
On average, hurricanes occur about 24 times a year in this region. Most hurricanes do not offer a major 
risk due to their lower relative speeds or due to the trajectories that they follow (information from the 
National Weather Service). However, hurricane movements and speeds can be very unpredictable and, 
therefore, it can be hard for the population to be ready for such events. An example of that is 1985’s 
hurricane Elena. Its unpredictable shifts in direction created what was considered the largest peacetime 
evacuation in the nation's history. It kept shifting directions and intensity and remained stationary in 
specific areas for days. The map below shows the trajectory that it followed. 
 
Figure 24. Map plotting Hurricane Elena track and intensity. Figure from Wikipedia  
Hurricane Wilma (2005) is another example of hurricane unpredictability. It had a slow, strengthening start 
but on its third day, it went through an explosive strengthening episode with speeds up to 278 km/hr. By 
the time it hit the Yucatan Peninsula it was category 4 but remained stationary over a specific area for two 
days, causing heavy damage (information from the National Hurricane Center).   
Hurricane Katrina (2005) was the costliest and one of the five deadliest hurricanes to ever strike the United 
States. Even though it was category 3 when it reached the Louisiana and Mississippi coasts, it had 
devastating effects, destroying more than 800,000 houses, and leaving more than 1 million people without

disasters in United States history (information from the National Hurricane Center).

6.4.2 Development of Hurricane-proof Wind Turbines 
As we have seen in the previous section, the Gulf of Mexico is a region with high hurricane activity, and 
this needs to be taken into consideration when developing offshore wind energy projects in this region.  
This is an area of concern not only for projects in the Gulf of Mexico but also for other regions of the world 
where hurricanes or typhoons make it difficult to install offshore wind energy turbines. Typhoon loads on 
wind turbines is of critical importance in emerging offshore wind markets such as Taiwan, Japan, or Korea. 
For this reason, wind energy turbine manufacturers have been developing stronger turbines that can 
handle higher wind speeds.  
The Chinese company Mingyang Smart Energy recently announced the rollout of their MySE 12MW hybrid 
drive, typhoon-resistant wind turbine. Several different on-line media state that this wind turbine can 
withstand speeds up to 283 km/h (BBC News 2022). They claim that this turbine is useful for over 98% of 
China’s sea areas, and that the operating wind speed limit went from less than 10 m/s to 15 m/s, improving 
construction efficiency by more than 30% (4C Offshore 2022). 
GE Renewable Energy also recently announced the launch of their Haliade-X Typhoon Certified wind 
turbine (GE Renewable Energy Website). Not much information can be found regarding the maximum 
wind speed rating it has been designed for. 
 Vestas, a Danish wind turbine manufacturer, has also designed a typhoon-resistant turbine. Its website 
does not provide specific information regarding maximum wind speeds these turbines can withstand. 
According to some on-line information sources, they have been designed to withstand speeds of up to 205 
km/h (Forbes 2021).  
 
6.4.3 Discussion on Hurricanes in the Gulf of Mexico  
The U.S. will need to address the need for wind turbines capable of resisting the high wind speeds 
generated by frequent hurricanes in the Gulf of Mexico before the first offshore wind energy projects in 
this region can be undertaken. 
Since hurricanes and typhoons are an area of concern not only for the Gulf of Mexico but for other 
countries such as Taiwan, Japan, or Korea, design work for developing wind turbines capable of resisting 
high wind speeds is being undertaken. Several wind turbine manufacturers such as Mingyang Energy, GE 
Renewable Energy, and Vestas have recently announced the launch of their own typhoon-resistant wind 
turbines. The specifics of the maximum wind speeds that they can withstand varies according to the 
information source.  Details regarding how long turbines can withstand those speeds are not provided. 
Therefore, it is difficult to determine what these numbers are with any degree of accuracy. The range of 
wind speeds can vary considerably. If Mingyang’s MySE 12 MW turbine can actually withstand a maximum 
wind speed of 283 km/h., that speed alone would exceed the maximum hurricane wind speed recorded 
in the Gulf of Mexico over the last 20 years. Turbines supplied by other manufacturers capable of 
withstanding lower maximum speeds of 205 km/h. could resist ~75% of the major hurricanes that have 
been detected in the Gulf of Mexico over the last 20 years.

43 
The results of new wind turbine research and development will be available by the time the U.S. is starting 
up its first offshore wind energy projects in the Gulf of Mexico. No definitive conclusions can be reached 
regarding the maximum wind speeds that wind turbines will be capable of withstanding in the future. 
However, wind turbine manufacturers seem to be overcoming the current limitations of their products, 
and hopefully their wind turbines will be capable of resisting most, if not all, of the hurricanes that occur 
in the Gulf of Mexico.

6.5 Challenge 5 - Bird Migration in the Gulf of

6.5.1 General Information on Bird Migration Across the Gulf of Mexico  
Every spring and fall, north American skies are filled with billions of birds in one of nature’s most 
spectacular shows, bird migration. Despite the enormous number of birds involved, this massive migration 
is done in an orderly manner. Birds follow roughly the same routes every year when migrating. In North 
America, these routes are generally grouped into the Pacific, Central, Mississippi and Atlantic Flyways. 
Habitats along the U.S. coast of the Gulf of Mexico (GoM) provide critical resources for North America's 
migratory birds. The majority of the birds that breed in North America travel across the Gulf of Mexico 
every spring and fall, as they migrate between temperate breeding grounds in North America and 
wintering grounds in the southern U.S., Caribbean, and Central and South America. 
More than 395 species of birds (18 orders and 53 families) take part in this bi-annual migration across the 
Gulf of Mexico, mostly between August and October (southbound) and between March and May 
(northbound) (U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service). Approximately 2.1 billion nocturnal migrants cross the Gulf 
of Mexico yearly (this only covers the northward trajectory and does not account for any diurnal migration 
or resident birds) (Horton et al. 2019). 
Within the Gulf of Mexico, birds follow two major migration paths, one being the trans-gulf flight, basically 
“straight across”, and the other being the circum-gulf flight, which “hugs” the coastline (see figure X.X) 
(John H. Rappole and Mario A. Ramos. 1994). Prior to a study carried out by the National Renewable 
Energy Laboratory with the Department of Wildlife and Fisheries Sciences between 2000 and 2005, the 
general wisdom had been that the spring trans-Gulf migration involved a roughly straight-line, which is the 
shortest distance between the Yucatan Peninsula (Mexico) and the upper Gulf Coast.  These studies 
showed that it is far more complex than that. Radar and direct observational evidence indicated that most 
of the trans-Gulf migration takes place over the western Gulf and the route is curvilinear and divergent, 
veering from a probable mean heading of northwest from the points of origin, to north off the southern 
Texas coast, to northeast off the Upper Texas Coast and Louisiana.

As the Gulf of Mexico offshore wind energy planning and leasing process progresses and more is known

6.5.3 Bird migration and wind turbines’ interactions in the Gulf of Mexico 
Although, as seen in the previous section, some work and actions have been implemented to start 
minimizing the potential impact of future offshore wind farms on bird migration in the Gulf of Mexico, a 
more in-depth analysis needs to be undertaken, especially for understanding and determining the 
appropriate mitigation strategies for birds that migrate across the Gulf of Mexico.  
The first draft Environmental Assessment was published by BOEM in July 2022 and it’s major purpose is to 
“analyze the effects of site characterization and site assessment activities that take place following of 
commercial and research wind energy leases.” These activities would mainly include the temporary 
placement of meteorological buoys and oceanographic devices, and the preparation of geographical, 
geotechnical, biological, and archaeological surveys. It was determined that this type of activities would 
have a negligible impact to birds. This draft Environmental Assessment does not contain any information 
regarding the interaction of the construction and operation of wind turbines with bird migration and, 
therefore, it does not respond to how any challenges related to bird migration would be handled.   
During my interview with the USFWS member, he stated that “the deconflicted areas likely pose little 
threat/impact to circum-gulf migration, as this type of migration is largely land-based, i.e. birds migrating 
from the United States southward through Mexico then down through Central and South America and 
vice-versa.”. The 20 NM of shore excluded from the areas of interest is a protection layer for this migration 
path.  
In addition, on the basis of the information gathered during the interview, the trans-gulf migration path 
does not seem to have such a straight-forward mitigation strategy solution. “As for trans-gulf migration, 
yes, there are still concerns that development of offshore wind infrastructure (turbines) will have an impact 
on birds using this strategy. Unfortunately, there is not a defined travel corridor, in that birds typically 
depart the United States from a variety of locations around the GoM, with most (a lot, but not all) making 
landfall near the Yucatan Peninsula in Mexico”.  
The Rappole and Ramos 1994 publication entitled Factors affecting migratory bird routes over the Gulf of 
Mexico indicates that some of the major selective factors affecting migratory bird routes are the prevailing 
wind direction and the probability of meeting turbulence over the Gulf. Other important factors are related 
to the specific ecology and physiology of individual birds within a particular species. This is due to the fact 
that these factors will impact their decision on which specific path offers the least resistance. The 
document concludes that “these factors are extremely difficult to document, requiring both long-term 
study of migrant ecology at stopover points and accurate distributional data from broad geographical 
areas.”. The following maps give a couple of examples of the routes taken by two different types of birds, 
the Prothonotary Warbler and the Purple Martin, on their migration through the Gulf of Mexico.

6.5.4 Discussion on Bird Migration in the Gulf of Mexico  
The BOEM has recognized the need to consider birds and migratory pathways within and across the Gulf 
of Mexico from the very early stages of the planning process, which started in 2021. The Gulf of Mexico 
Intergovernmental Renewable Energy Task Force, which was set up as a forum for stakeholders to discuss 
issues, exchange data and information about ocean use and resources, and provide opportunities for 
collaboration, is an extremely valuable tool for ensuring that stakeholders are being informed and engaged 
in the appropriate manner. This enables bird migration experts to offer their input and have their opinions 
be taken into consideration when discussing potential mitigation strategies.  
Bird migration through the Gulf of Mexico is a complex phenomenon requiring additional research. Studies 
and observations have been performed on bird migration across the Gulf of Mexico for more than a 
century; however, there are still a lot of unknowns regarding this complex topic (Rappole et al. 1994). It 
will take a lot of time, planning and investment to come up with adequate mitigation strategies. This topic 
was recognized by BOEM as a critical challenge from the very early stages of the planning process and the 
proper authorities are already working to better understand it and come up with mitigation strategies. It 
is hoped that sufficient information will be made available in a timely manner and specific mitigation plans 
will be in place by the time the site assessment is over to avoid potential bottlenecks that could delay the 
construction and operations phase.

6.6.1 General Information on Grid Transmission in the State of Louisiana 
Situated at the mouth of the Mississippi River, Louisiana has abundant crude oil and natural gas reserves 
both onshore and offshore, buried beneath the thick sediments of the Mississippi Delta. Louisiana's total 
energy consumption ranks fourth among the 50 states and its per capita energy consumption is the second 
highest, largely because of the energy-intensive chemical, petroleum, and natural gas industries in the 
state's industrial sector. Louisiana's demand for air conditioning is high during the hot, humid summer 
months. 
In 2021, natural gas was the primary fuel used to generate electricity in Louisiana, accounting for 65% of 
the state's electricity net generation. Coal was Louisiana's second-leading source for electricity generation 
for decades, but in 2021 coal fueled about 8% of the state's electricity generation. In 2021, the industrial 
sector consumed the most electricity in the state, accounting for about 42% of the state total, followed by 
the residential sector at 33% and the commercial sector at 25%. Louisiana is ranked in the top five cheapest 
states for electricity in America. As of 2023, the average residential electricity rate in Louisiana is 12 ¢/kWh, 
which is 31% lower than the national average rate of 17 ¢/kWh (U.S. Energy Information Administration 
2023) 
Louisiana does not generate enough electricity to meet in-state demand and receives almost one-eighth 
of its power supplies from other states by way of the regional interstate grid (U.S. Department of Energy 
2021). 
Louisiana is part of the Midcontinent Independent System Operator (MISO), an independent system 
operator that manages the flow of high-voltage electricity across 15 U.S. states and the Canadian province 
of Manitoba. See figure 30.

6.6.2 Low Resilient Power Grid 
According to the U.S. Energy Information Administration, Louisiana has one of the least resilient 
distribution grids in the nation. When Louisiana was impacted by heavy tropical weather in 2020, including 
hurricane Ida, the state ranked number one for the number of minutes power was out for each customer 
on average (Energy Information Administration 2021) 
While Entergy has been upgrading its transmission network to bear wind speeds in excess of 140 miles per 
hour, a lot of its transmission equipment in and around New Orleans was built to withstand wind gusts of 
around 110 miles per hour, or a category 2 storm (The New York Times, 2021). According to Entergy, Ida 
damaged or destroyed as many as 31,000 poles that carry lower-voltage distribution lines in 
neighborhoods, nearly twice as many as Hurricane Katrina. It left more than one million without power of 
which more than 400,000 still had no power nine days after the hurricane. This was a huge deal considering 
the high temperatures and humidity that coastal communities of Louisiana have in the summer. According 
to the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers, in coastal areas of Louisiana, large transmission 
equipment should be designed to withstand winds of up to 150 m.p.h. 
As seen in Figure 31, the number of outages in the state of Louisiana between 1992 and 2009 was 38, the 
majority caused by hurricanes, tropical storms, or high winds.

6.6.3 Power Injection to Grid  
How the power generated from the potential 30 GW offshore wind farms would be connected and 
distributed in the U.S. electric grid is a topic of discussion not only in the state of Louisiana but also 
nationwide. Building new transmission lines in the U.S. is a complex subject. There are seven primary 
regional transmission operators in the U.S. (see figure X.X) and, furthermore, legislation is different on 
each state, making the approval process of new multi-state transmission lines very lengthy. According to 
an example provided by The Economist, it takes more than a decade to obtain the necessary permits to 
connect a renewable project in Wyoming to California’s grid. In this specific case, environmentalism is the 
cause of the lengthy approval process. Also, according to different sources of information, delays in the 
transmission planning and construction process are currently a roadblock for deploying clean energy 
(National Governors Association 2023). 
Given the challenges related to injecting and distributing new power from renewable energy projects, 
experts agree that this subject must be addressed as a whole and not individually project by project. The 
proposed path forward would be a “meshed” network, which connects multiple turbines, projects, and 
systems into a backbone. This approach would make the grid connectivity process much more efficient. 
The network of state, federal and independent U.S. grid managers has begun to address broader and 
longer-range transmission planning, but it will take some time before the appropriate decisions are made 
(Liu, C., 2021).

In late 2022, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) announced a series of actions to speed-up

new announcement regarding the U.S. electrical grid, through which they listed a series of actions to

Within the state of Louisiana, there is also a high level of complexity when responding to the question of 
how the power from offshore wind farms should be injected into the grid. One of the first steps would be

Center for Climate and Energy Solutions gathered in March 2023, sixty local leaders across Louisiana for a

to the grid. They mentioned that uncertainty about the regulator’s approach to offshore wind could 
become a roadblock for project development in the Gulf of Mexico.  
The state of Louisiana is aware that, in order to attain the offshore wind power generation goal of 5 GW 
by 2035, strategic collaboration across Louisiana’s state agencies and the federal government, energy 
regulators, transmission planning agencies, utilities and the private sector is required. For this reason, 
Louisiana’s Climate Initiatives Task Force Plan was created. “Louisiana should continue collaboration across 
sectors and develop plans for the accelerated implementation of offshore wind power generation”, the 
Plan states (State of Louisiana 2023) 
Given the time, investment, and collaboration efforts required for a successful injection and distribution 
of the power generated by offshore wind projects, a series of alternatives are being discussed by different 
stakeholders.

Some proposals involve injecting power into oil refining, steel, fertilizer manufacturers, and other 
industrial processes located in the coastal areas of Louisiana. This would minimize the need for new 
transmission lines and substations. During the Center for Climate and Energy Solutions roundtable of 
March 2023, this topic was also discussed, and many of the attendees expressed concerns with it because 
they want offshore wind benefits to reach a wider customer base. The industrial sector already consumes 
approximately 42% of the electricity produced in the state. 
Multiple companies interested in leasing offshore parcels in the Gulf of Mexico wish to use the energy to 
make renewable hydrogen to power industrial processes and reduce their carbon footprint. However, this

The Gulf of Mexico offers a specific advantage over offshore projects in the Atlantic Ocean in that the oil

complicated and very expensive.  As of April 2019, there were approximately 1,900 oil platforms, 515 of 
which had been converted to permanent artificial reefs. As oil platforms continue being decommissioned, 
some people suggest repurposing them for renewable energy and other productive purposes connected 
to the mainland through pipeline conduits. Some of these offshore platforms could also be used to locate 
the substations required for potential offshore wind projects (Hall, K. 2020). 
6.6.4 Discussion on Grid Transmission in the State of Louisiana 
Louisiana’s climate action plan includes 5 GW of offshore wind capacity by 2035 (White House 2021). Two 
main challenges related to how the power could be injected and transmitted through the state have been 
identified while drawing up this project: the existence of a low resilient power grid, and the challenge of 
injecting and transmitting power through the existing power grid.   
As explained in section 6.6.2., the state of Louisiana has suffered from a high number of electric 
transmission outages since the 1990’s, some of them long-lasting. This was caused mainly by high winds 
during tropical storms and hurricanes, combined with the fact that many transmission poles and overhead 
power lines had not been designed to withstand those wind speeds. Due to climate change, severe 
weather events seem to be becoming more frequent. Building a more resilient energy grid is the main 
priority for Louisiana, but this requires very large investments and a long process that could last up to 10 
years before the core of the upgrade is completed. Therefore, a lot of this work would need to be done 
before a large amount of additional renewable power is injected into the grid. 
Also, as explained in section 6.6.2, there is no easy way to connect the potential offshore power to the 
grid. One of the easiest ways to inject and utilize the power without major power lines or substations’ 
additions would be to use the power earmarked for heavy industry which is located near the coasts of the 
Gulf of Mexico. However, it seems likely that this move would incur the opposition from many residents 
who wish to benefit directly from the energy of these potential offshore projects.  
Given these existing two challenges, large investments and planning would be required before the state 
of Louisiana is ready to have future offshore wind energy connected to its grid.

6.7.1 Tribes in the State of Louisiana 
The United States recognizes four Native American tribes in Louisiana: the Chitimacha Tribe of Louisiana, 
the Coushatta Tribe of Louisiana, the Jena Band of Choctaw Indians, and the Tunica-Biloxi Indian Tribe of 
Louisiana. They account for ~1.3% of the state´s population (Louisiana State University).  
   
   
   
 
Figure 33. Logos of the four different tribes recognized by the U.S. in the state of Louisiana. 
Since the mid-1800s, the official policy of the United States government towards the American Indians 
was to confine each tribe to a specific parcel of land called a reservation. Agencies were established on or 
near each reservation. A government representative, usually called an agent, was assigned to each agency. 
Their duties included maintaining the peace, making payments to the Native Americans based on the 
stipulations of the treaties with each tribe, and providing a means of communication between the native 
population and the federal government. 
These tribes have been struggling with different scenarios that have taken place in the regions where they 
live. The health of the waters due to the various industries involved in oil extraction and processing has 
been significant. As an example, the BP oil spill of April 2010 contaminated the waters that tribal groups 
use for fishing activities (Darensbourg, J., 2019). Also, many of these tribes live by coastlines and rivers 
where the waters are rising due to climate change (PBS News 2023). 
 
6.7.2 Disadvantaged port communities in the State of Louisiana 
According to the U.S Census Bureau, Louisiana ranked as the second poorest state in the U.S. in 2023, with 
17.8% of its population living under the poverty threshold. The national average is 11.7%. The state’s high 
poverty rate is attributed to various factors, including a lack of well-paying jobs, inadequate access to 
education and healthcare, and a low minimum wage. Additionally, the state’s history of racial 
discrimination has contributed to systemic inequalities that perpetuate poverty.  
In addition to the high poverty rates in many counties in the coastal communities in the state of Louisiana, 
the majority of those suffer from additional threats. One of them is the chemical corridor between Baton

6.7.3 Engagement with Tribes and Low-Income Communities in the State of

As explained in the previous sections (6.7.2 and 6.7.3), Louisiana’s tribal and underdeveloped communities 
are suffering from the consequences of climate change and from the activities of local heavy industry.  
Due to the location of potential future offshore projects near Louisiana’s coasts, critical infrastructure and 
manufacturing sites would most likely be located in these coastal areas. Meaningful and equitable 
community engagement during the development process is critical for developing a successful offshore

avoid project slowdowns, relocations, or cancellations that can stem from community pushback. However, 
due to the negative experience of non-inclusive environmental decision-making involving certain 
communities, including port communities, there is often a higher baseline of community distrust of the 
decision-making and development authorities. (Lantz et al. 2022). Additionally, according to Shields et al.

residents may be less likely to engage in decision-making processes when given the opportunity, due to

The U.S. has already experienced the cancelation of an offshore wind energy project in part due to 
indigenous opposition related to historical sites. It was the Cape Wind Project on the coast of 
Massachusetts. Project development started in 2001 but, “at the last minute, Native American tribes said 
turbines would disturb their cultural and historical heritage” (Environmental Justice Atlas 2019). 
One of the major challenges is the fact that each community that could host supply chain facilities is

why the Gulf of Mexico Intergovernmental Renewable Energy Task Force, established by the BOEM in 
2021, in an effort to keep open lines of communication, included ten members from different tribal 
governments, Chitimatcha (one member), Coushatta (one member), Jena Band of Choctaw Indians (one 
member) and the Tunica-Biloxi (seven members), and fifteen from low-developed communities in the 
state of Louisiana (mostly coastal communities). Some of these communities included in the Task Force 
are the St. Mary Parish, the St. Bernard Parish, the Terrebone Parish, the Vermilion Parish, Port of New 
Orleans, and Port of Lake Charles. Three of the four Task Force meetings that have taken place so far

support them during the development of offshore wind projects in the Gulf of Mexico. Two of these 
meetings even included a tribal invocation done in their tribal language.  
 
According to Bacchiocchi et al. 2022, the concerns and perspectives of indigenous communities are mostly 
brought forth by nongroup members and were found to be leveraged or diminished by non-indigenous 
individuals pushing anti- or pro-offshore wind sentiment. This reveals the finding that indigenous concerns 
are being coopted or sidelined through formal and legal decision-making processes in the U.S. The Gulf of 
Mexico Intergovernmental Renewable Energy Task Force could be a great tool to allow indigenous 
communities to express their concerns and perspectives directly without being coopted. 
 
There are several mechanisms to protect the right to involvement and consultation of indigenous 
communities in the U.S. One example is Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act, which 
requires federal agencies to consider the effects of all projects and developments on historic properties 
and to give interested parties the opportunity to express support, opposition, and suggestions throughout 
the development process. However, there is widespread recognition that consultation under these 
frameworks has been inadequate and has historically failed to meet standards of energy justice

More initiatives other than acknowledgments are necessary to help tribes be become involved and help

announced. The purpose of this guidebook is to help tribes understand how they can benefit from the

The U.S. government and other agencies such as BOEM seem to be aware of this and, through different 
initiatives and incentives, they are trying to support and make the development process of potential future 
projects in the areas where they live, more equitable and just.  
As a first step, twenty-five members from local tribes and low-income communities were included in the 
Gulf of Mexico Intergovernmental Renewable Energy Task Force in an effort to facilitate engagement with 
these groups. However, even though through Task Force meetings BOEM acknowledges the importance 
of engaging with these groups, not much information has been given during these meetings as far as how 
they plan on supporting them. Also, no questions regarding this topic have been asked during these 
meetings which makes me wonder if this is the type of engagement that these groups want and/or need. 
From a list of meetings with different stakeholders that BOEM published, it doesn’t seem like much 
engagement has taken place between BOEM and tribes or low-income communities through meetings. I 
did try to corroborate this information by contacting two tribes and four parishes from low-income 
communities via email and phone calls, but none of them replied to my emails and two of them hung up 
on me. This could be an indication that engaging with these groups is not that easy and, therefore, it might 
not be that easy for BOEM either.    
From an incentives point of view, federal and state agencies do seem to be doing an effort to make the 
supply chain process more equitable and just. Multitude of initiatives are being taken to support tribes, 
low-income communities, and minority groups throughout the U.S. Time will tell us if these initiatives and 
incentives are indeed making the supply chain development process for equitable and fair. If the proper 
engagement with these groups is not taking place, even with all these incentives being given, the process 
could end up not being that equitable or just if there is not a good understanding of the real needs that 
these groups possess.

7. Conclusions  
7.1 General Conclusions 
The Gulf Coast of the United States is a region that had not attracted much attention from an offshore 
wind energy generation perspective until the year 2020. Recent wind resource studies, in addition to 
federal and state initiatives, have made these waters an area of interest for many different stakeholders. 
On the west side of the Gulf of Mexico, along the coasts of Texas and Louisiana, the Bureau of Ocean 
Energy Management recently identified approximately 8 million hectares as areas of company interest 
inside the Call Area. Of those 8 million hectares, approximately 1 million hectares, with a potential of more 
than 10 GW of installed wind power generation, have been identified as de-conflicted areas, following 
years of analysis and public engagement. The State of Louisiana, with its offshore wind generation goal of 
5 GW by 2035, has been energetically promoting the development of projects on their coasts. As a result, 
approximately 120,000 hectares have been included in an auction process as of June 2023. This area has 
the potential for 3.5 GW of wind power generation.   
However, there are several challenges that could delay the implementation of these projects.   
The supply chain required for the U.S. to successfully undertake all the projects necessary for achieving 
the offshore wind energy goal of 30 GW by the year 2030 is not yet available.  A large amount of strategic 
planning, collaboration between stakeholders, manufacturing companies, and educational and training 
institutions, as well as large investments, are critical for the development of a robust supply chain that 
will enable all the projects in the U.S. offshore wind energy pipeline to be completed in a timely manner. 
Although the existing infrastructure supply chain and workforce from the oil and gas industry have already 
begun to transition to the renewable energy industry, it will not be enough to cover all the supply chain 
requirements. The location of the oil & gas infrastructure supply chain and workforce, mainly in the states 
of Texas and Louisiana, could ultimately benefit the development of offshore wind energy projects in the 
Gulf of Mexico.  
The uncertainty generated by the timing for the development of a supply chain of this nature has resulted 
in a new challenge: investment uncertainty. Large financial investments are required, but investors may 
be reluctant to invest due to their inability to project revenue streams as a result of all the unknown 
components of the supply chain. Incentives from both state and federal levels to reduce investment 
uncertainty have been announced. Those will help reduce investment uncertainty but, ultimately, 
investors might want to see the successful completion of the first projects before accelerating their 
financial investments.  
According to the U.S. Energy Information Administration, Louisiana has one of the least resilient 
distribution grids in the nation and, therefore, building a more resilient energy grid should be prioritized. 
This upgrade process could take as long as 10 years to complete and large financial investments are 
required. Supplying offshore wind energy to the heavy industrial corridor on the coast of Louisiana could 
facilitate and speed up the process; however, the local population may object as they want this energy to 
be supplied to the residential sector.   
==PAGE:71==
 
60 
The presence of hurricanes in the Gulf of Mexico creates another challenge for the development of these 
projects. However, international wind energy turbine manufacturers are already working on the design of 
typhoon-resistant wind turbines. They may be close to finding a solution which would satisfy turbine 
requirements in the Pacific Ocean and the Gulf of Mexico, where typhoons and hurricanes are frequent. 
Another challenge are the billions of birds following the trans-Gulf migration path on a yearly basis. 
Although this event has been studied in the Gulf of Mexico for more than a century, it is a very complex 
and dynamic phenomenon, and little is known regarding the impact that offshore wind energy farms could 
have on this trans-Gulf migration. Additional studies are currently being conducted but they could take 
years and their outcome remains unknown.  
The last challenge is the incorporation of equitable and just practices in the supply chain as it is being 
developed in the communities of Louisiana where indigenous tribes and historically marginalized low-
income communities are present. These communities should be adequately integrated into the decision-
making process, so that they can benefit from the development of the supply chain. 
Although bird migration and the incorporation of equitable and justice practices into the supply chain 
would not have a major impact on the timing of the projects, it would be unethical to develop projects 
that are supposedly sustainable and environmentally oriented, without addressing the aforementioned 
challenges.   
Therefore, of all the challenges just mentioned, we can conclude that developing a reliable and robust 
supply chain is one of the most critical and time-sensitive challenges. For that to happen, a successful 
development of the current offshore wind energy projects in the northeast of the U.S., in addition to 
federal and state incentives, are needed to generate confidence on investors. On the next level of 
criticality, we have the energy grid transmission needs in the state of Louisiana. The easiest way to 
overcome this challenge would be to inject the power from offshore wind energy projects to their 
industrial corridor. However, this does not seem to be the ideal solution. Making their energy grid more 
robust could be done simultaneously as the needed supply chain develops. Developing hurricane-proof 
turbines is also very important but, this challenge is already being addressed in other parts of the world 
and, therefore, this type of turbines will probably be ready by the time the Gulf of Mexico is ready to 
implement the first offshore wind energy projects. Finally, although bird migration and the incorporation 
of equitable and justice practices into the supply chain would not have a major impact on the timing of 
the projects, it would be unethical to develop projects that are supposedly sustainable and 
environmentally oriented, without addressing the aforementioned challenges.   
 
7.2 Personal Conclusions 
When the opportunities posed by the development of offshore wind energy projects in the Gulf of Mexico 
are analyzed, the potential for wind energy generation would appear to be attractive enough for investors. 
The existence of shallow waters in this region makes these projects more attractive, at least until the 
floating technology for implementing wind energy projects in deeper waters is more developed. 
Louisiana’s specific goal of achieving 5 GW of offshore installed power by 2035 will most likely result in 
additional incentives to accelerate the implementation of these projects. However, from the standpoint of 
the Levelized Cost of Energy, the economics of developing offshore wind energy farms in this region remain 
==PAGE:72==
 
61 
relatively unattractive. It is very unlikely that these wind energy projects will be implemented until 
sometime between 2030 and 2035. This assumption is based on the time frame for achieving Louisiana’s 
wind energy goals, and the National Renewable Energy Laboratory’s (NREL) estimated Levelized Cost of 
Energy trend for offshore wind energy projects off the coast of Louisiana, as explained in Chapter 2.  
Although the U.S. is expected to address each challenge in a timely manner, a great deal of research and 
uncertainties remain. The following key points lead one to believe that the 2030–2035-time frame for 
implementing offshore wind energy projects in the Gulf of Mexico is a realistic estimate: 
 
Supply chain-related challenges involving projects in the northeast of the U.S. will most likely be 
given the highest priority. These projects, which are in a more advanced stage with 20 GW already 
in the permitting phase, are more attractive from an economic standpoint. The NREL has provided 
an estimate of 6-9 years as the time frame for the development of the required supply chain. By 
the early 2030’s the supply chain should be robust enough to support the projects in the Gulf of 
Mexico. 
 
The state of Louisiana will most likely prioritize investments for upgrading their electric grid. The 
first phase of this upgrading process will take approximately five years to complete. Therefore, by 
the early 2030’s, Louisiana’s electric grid might be ready to absorb the energy generated by the 
offshore wind energy projects on their coast.  
 
Investment uncertainty should decrease as the projects in the northeast of the country are 
implemented. Several wind energy projects are expected to be commissioned in this region by the 
end of this decade according to the “Offshore Wind Market Report: 2022 Edition” published by 
the U.S. Department of Energy.  
 
Five to ten years should be enough time for the proper agencies to do an in-depth analysis and 
mitigation plan regarding bird migration in the Gulf of Mexico, and to engage and come up with 
ideas to incorporate equity and justice into supply chain development within the tribes and low-
income communities of the state of Louisiana. 
• 
Lastly, it would appear that turbine manufacturers are close to producing typhoon-resistant 
turbines capable of handling the highest wind speeds generated by hurricanes in the Gulf of 
Mexico. Even more progress will have been made by the end of this decade.

7.3 Personal Insights 
I had little knowledge of the offshore wind energy industry in the U.S. prior to embarking on this thesis. I 
knew about the two existing offshore projects in the northeast region of the United States, the existing 
plans to expand the wind energy installed capacity in that region, and also the future potential for offshore 
wind energy generation on the Pacific coast with the development of floating technology. However, the 
potential for offshore wind energy growth and its possible impact on the U.S. energy sector are much 
greater than I had anticipated. Bearing witness to such an important phase in the history of the U.S. energy 
industry is a very exciting experience.   
Due to its size and diversity, the United States could almost be categorized as a continent in itself, 
containing many different countries. The research undertaken for this thesis has revealed that this size and 
diversity adds a level of complexity to the development of the offshore wind energy industry in the U.S. 
Each U.S. state has unique characteristics that should be taken into account when undertaking new 
offshore wind energy projects, thus adding an additional level of complexity. For example, energy 
transmission across the U.S. is a very complex issue with a great diversity of laws and regulations in each 
state. In addition, the diverse needs and demands of the tribes and communities living throughout the 
U.S. make it impossible to apply on a large scale the lessons learned from successful offshore wind 
projects, thus adding to the overall complexity of undertaking new projects.  
Most of my educational and professional background is of a very technical nature. Therefore, undertaking 
research and acquiring knowledge on unfamiliar topics were a gratifying endeavor. Examples of this new 
knowledge involve the ways in which the local authorities tackle the challenges of bird migration and the 
engagement with tribes and low-income communities in the Gulf of Mexico. Contributing to the ongoing 
efforts to ensure a more promising future for local communities by means of wind energy projects while 
protecting the environment, is very appealing to me. For this reason, this thesis has strengthened my 
commitment to gain a better understanding of how the wind energy industry addresses these societal and 
environmental complex issues that arise in this industry.
