Abstract
A major focus on sustainable energy lead an exponential increasing of renewable resources. In the
other hand there is a focus on energy storage in overproduction scenarios, in order to use that stored
energy in underproduction scenarios. For the transport sector the focus is on sustainable vehicles and
here is electric vehicles an obvious option. These can be operated using electricity produced by re-
newable resources. The purpose of this storage energy is to increase the amount of ’¼éexibility between
production and consumption and thereby help the grid.
The topic for this project is the necessary converter that converts the energy between the vehicles
battery and the grid. This has been performed based on the theory behind the converter technology
and an implementation of the converter controller.
This project focuses on:
The possibilities to combine the converter between grid and battery with the converter between bat-
tery and motor. The possibilities to separate the battery into two parts and operate the motor using
a combination of both batteries or only one of the batteries. The possibilities to skip the galvanic
isolating transformer between battery and grid in order to increase the e’¼āciency of the converter.
The project includes:
Calculations of currents, voltages and three phase transformations in the grid, in order to obtain
positive, negative, active and reactive power ’¼éow. Simulations on a converter that produce/absorb
active and reactive power to the grid by charging/discharging the battery. Simulations of the same
converter that drives an electric motor with a combination of both batteries and only one of the
batteries. Practical work in the laboratory with an establish converter and implementing the software
in the embedded system of the converter control. Implementing the software includes measuring of
currents and voltages, three phase transformation, generating of the converter modulation and set-
ting the converter gate signals in order to operate the establish converter. Design of an alternative
converter controller, by investigating modern components and embedded systems.
The project obtains:
It is possible with a battery voltage of 2x400V to connect the converter direct to the electricity grid
without galvanic transformer and obtain positive, negative, active and reactive power ’¼éow into the
grid. By using a 120Vrms (208Vrms line to line) electric motor, it is possible to drive the motor with
one single battery and also switching between batteries under operation. To implement theoretical
calculations required for regulating power ’¼éow, in the embedded system. To modulate an input volt-
age generating gate signals for a converter leg and operate the establish converter with a inductive
load. To demonstrate converter voltages and currents generated by the converter.
To describe usable modern modules and embedded systems that can be combined into a complete
alternative converter controller.

Introduction
There is a high focus on sustainable energy around the world using renewable energy sources. In
Denmark the politician are establishing goals for the future: By 2050 the energy sector in Denmark
should be powered by 100% renewable energy sources. There are some sub-goals like that the elec-
tricity and heat in Denmark should be supplied from 100% renewable energy in 2035, power plants
and oil boilers should be phased out before 2030 and half of the electricity demand should come from
windpower in 2020. [1]
With the energy sector changing toward more renewable energy sources. Mostly wind power because
this is the most a’¼Ćordable technologies at the moment, but also some solar power and a small imple-
mentation of other alternative renewable sources.
The change for at better environment does not comes without problems. The earlier used fossil fuel
power plants are full controllable, within a range from a minimum generation to maximal capability
and it can also be turned o’¼Ć.
The renewable sources is not controllable like a power plant, their momentarily maximum power
capabilities are depending of the natural forces, wind blowing, sun shining etc. Renewable sources
have the controllability to be less e’¼ācient, but that can not balance the network at times without
natural forces. Here comes energy storage into the picture by instead of turning down renewable
power, then storage if for later use.

Ancillary services
The Transmission System Operator (TSO) are responsible for supplying and delivering of electricity
through out the country to the local electricity Distribution System Operators (DSO). The TSOŌĆÖs
responsibility includes building and maintaining electricity infrastructure and ensure power balance
in the network. (The electricity infrastructure is also called the electricity grid). The TSO pay energy
producers to supply power into the electricity grid. If the demand and production becomes of out
balance, the frequency will start deviating from nominal. Frequency deviating can in worst case force
the TSO or DSO to cut of parts of the networks, which results that customers experience a blackout.
Of cause this can be avoided by compensate the power balance within time. To help balance within
the electric grid the TSO introduces ancillary services, where they pay energy producers for helping
balance the electricity grid.

ŌĆó Inertia is the energy stored in a moving object. For the power system this is realised using
generators, motors and synchronous compensators. The inertia reduces the frequency change
under a fault or variation in demand.
ŌĆó Primary reserve: The fastest response service, this reacts almost immediately, but have limited

and helping restoring the frequency.
ŌĆó Secondary reserve: A service slower that primary reserve, it contains a higher energy capacity
to helping restoring the frequency, and maintain the extra demand in power.
ŌĆó Tertiary reserve: A manual service of starting up extra power generation.
With less fossil fuel power plants causing a lack of controllability in the power system. The grid gets
more vulnerable for deviations and are therefore more reliable of fast responding power producers as
energy storages to compensate the power balance.
Large energy storage with batteries can be a part of ancillary services. An example of this is the
European largest battery energy storage project in Feldheim, Germany [3]. This is a 10MW/10MWh
battery storage power plant, that potential can deliver or consume 10MW for an hour. The best prac-
tical operation point will be to have the battery half full, only then it has the potential to delivering
5MWh or consume 5MWh.
Battery energy storage is hugely expensive, and another way to solve this can be to use batteries
within existing electric vehicles. These have maybe another purposes and is owned by public people,
but with compensation of money people may be willing to lean out some of the battery capacity for
ancillary services.

Electric vehicles
The concept of an electric vehicle is a vehicle driven by an electric motor. This motor is then driven
from batteries within the vehicle. Electric vehicles is not a new concept. In facts have been around
since the late 19th century, just as the ICE (Internal Combustion Engine) vehicles.
Today at the 21th century the technologies have made electric vehicles interesting again. This is
partly due to development in batteries, to store more energy within the same size batteries. This
development in batteries makes it possible for electric vehicles to compete against ICE vehicles.
By compared electric vehicles with ICE vehicles are some of the advantages for choosing electric
vehicles:
1. The electric motor have an e’¼ācient above 90% versus the ICE are below 30%. Where the ICE
e’¼āciency is like a signi’¼ücant curve with only high e’¼ācient within a small variation of rpm.
2. The acceleration of an electric vehicles is way faster than ICE vehicles, even as electric vehicle
are heavier due to carrying the onboard battery.
3. Possibility of emitting zero CO2 under driving, if the energy stored comes from renewable energy
sources. The production of the vehicles and specially the batteries are constitutes to CO2 and
should be a part of the total CO2 calculations for the electric vehicle.
4. The electric vehicle have the potential of been charged at home.
Some of the disadvantages of using electric vehicles:
1. Vehicles are all about transporting stu’¼Ć, things and people. Distance is a high priority and ICE
vehicles can achieve longer travelling distances before re’¼ülling. This is due to the energy density
of fuel, Gasoline 46 MJ/kg VS. batteries Ōēł0.9MJ/kg (TESLA batteries Ōēł250Wh/kg). This
results in an approximately ’¼üfty times di’¼Ćerence.
2. The charging time of electric vehicles takes between half an hour and to up to normally 10
hours. (Under special conditions: With a Tesla 100kWh battery charging at a home power
outlet, limited to 2 kW, a complete charging will take 2 days)
3. Electric vehicles are still above the common paid prices for vehicles.
Politicians around the world votes for a greener future. A part of this is to reduce CO2 emitting
technologies. This have caused some countries to reduce the taxes for the public to buying an electric
vehicle.
Electric vehicles batteries
Electric vehicles is a bit heavier than similar vehicles, this is due to carrying a heavy battery. The
most interesting electric vehicles today is those with the largest battery, a battery with higher capacity
can transfers higher power to the motor. Those Electric vehicles with the highest capacity are also
some of the fastest vehicles around and have the longest driving distance of electric vehicles.
The battery within electric vehicles are today economically divided in two classes, 15-30 KWh for
middle class prices and 60-100 KWh for high class prices. The battery pack voltage of electric vehicles
today are increased to around 300-400V, this reduce the current for achieving same power output
versus with lower voltages. The current have a high impact to power loss in electric components.

electric grid.

10kW, that can be delivered back to the grid from the vehicles.

Problem statement
With more renewable energy sources, comes a need for more energy storage. Energy storage is not
free, actually it is quite expensive compared to paid energy prices. All battery based energy storage
uses a converter, which results in a reduced e’¼āciency. This comply also when stored energy is used
to deliver energy back to the grid. To minimise the converting loss, the energy storage voltage could
be raised to match the grid voltage.
Batteries of electric vehicles have a certain size, that if a number of electric vehicle were combined,
they could replace a relative scale energy storage system. This require that they include V2G capa-
bilities, for generating energy back to the grid.
Could it be possible to raise the electric vehicle battery voltage to match the Grid voltages, thereby
avoid using the step up/down isolation transformer for raising the e’¼ācient if the converter with V2G ?
Could it be possible to drive the electric motor with the higher battery voltages and with the same
converter as for V2G ?

Semiconductors and converter topolo-
gies
A semiconductor is a component that can lead current in one direction, the most common semicon-
ductor is the diode. Controlled semiconductors are components that still only can lead current one
direction, but the current is controlled from an input. The most used controlled semiconductor would
be the transistor, endless of these are putted into computers. In power applications other controlled
semiconductors are used that acts as one direction switch, build for high voltages and currents.
Here is a list of semiconductors used for power applications:
ŌĆó Transistor
ŌĆó Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-E’¼Ćect Transistor (MOSFET)
ŌĆó Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistor (IGBT)
ŌĆó Silicon carbide MOSFET (SiC MOSFET)
Transistor
Transistors were some of the ’¼ürst controlled semiconductors. It is a current ampli’¼üer, driven by an
input current at the base, and with a current gain hF E controlling the maximum collector-emitter
current. The transistors have a low on-state voltage.
MOSFET
It were ’¼ürst seen in the 1970ŌĆÖs, where it replaced the bipolar transistors in power applications. MOS-
FET is a voltage driven semiconductor, mostly used for switching on/o’¼Ć. It has high switching capa-
bilities, above 200kHz. Today MOSFET is used in lower voltage applications and in high frequency
applications, an example is for IGBT gate drivers.
IGBT
The IGBT is a combination of a bipolar transistor and a MOSFET. The bipolar transistor have a
low on-state voltage, and the MOSFET have a high impedance gate that lead to simple switching the
IGBT. These are the most used semiconductors in power applications today.
SiC MOSFET
Silicon carbide is the newest development with semicunductors. It is seen in diodes "Schottky diode",
which have a lower forward voltage down to around 150mV-450mV, where silicon diodes have a
voltage drop above 600mV. New technologies use Silicon carbide in MOSFETs, therefore the name
Silicon carbide MOSFET. These are beginning to replace IGBTs in modern power applications.

2.1
Semiconductor losses and diode reverse recovery current
The loss in semiconductors is separated into two parts, conducting loss and switching loss. Most of
the time a semiconductor is o’¼Ćor conducting. When it is conducting it is the on resistance and a
forward voltage that constitutes to a loss. The loss equals to the current trough the on resistance
and the current times forward voltage. When the semiconductor switching it controls the current and
within the switching time it has the full voltage across it. This is leading to a switching loss equal to
the total voltages times the current. The switching loss is extremely high but in a very short period.
The switching time depends on discharging of the IGBT parasitic capacitors and the freewheeling
diode.
A freewheeling diode is placed in opposite direction of the IGBT, to protect the IGBT. A disadvantage
of this freewheeling diode is that it introduce a reverse recovery current (Ir) from charged internal
parasitic capacitor in the diode. For the semiconductor to switch, the parasitic capacitor is discharged
through the IGBT, that also contributes to the switching loss.

Switching frequency
The switching frequency relates to the accuracy of the modulation, this can be seen by applying an
amplitude spectrum for the modulation, showing harmonics in the modulation. A lower switching
frequency leads to higher current peaks, to compensate for this the inductance can be increased in
the converter reactor. Thereby forms the relation between switching frequency and reactor size.
The purpose of using smaller converter reactors, is due to material cost, weight and physical dimen-
sions. IGBT as semiconductors allow for higher switching frequencies and are a preferable choice for
high power converters.

A 3 level 3 phase converter uses a total of 12 controlled semiconductors. IGBTs is the mostly used
semiconductor for higher power converter applications, due to it has higher switching capabilities.
Every single IGBT will have an individual isolated IGBT controller, which controls the IGBT within
its boundaries. The IGBT controller include a gate driver, over voltage and over current protection.
Due to the construction of the IGBT some capacitances are unavoidably, which need charging and

deliver a high current for a short moment to charge/discharge the internal capacitors. These high
current gate drivers are mostly MOSFETs. The IGBT protection works by measuring the voltage drop
across the IGBT, the current through it will results in a voltage due to on resistance. The protection
will turn the IGBT o’¼Ćin case the voltage increase beyond the the set limit. The gate driver deliver
+15V to turn the IGBT on and -5V for turning it o’¼Ć.

2.6
Dead time
Dead time is introduced to avoid short circuits in the converter with the IGBTs. It have been mention
earlier that the IGBT have some switching time, which apply both of switching on and o’¼Ć. Due to
this time it takes for switching, will occur that two IGBT that are suppose to switching opposite to
each other actually within a small time both are conducting. This is a problem if it causes a short
circuit of the DC source.
The switching times is as low as 1-2 ┬Ąs for IGBTs. To avoid these problems dead time is used, it works
by waiting the a small time (dead time) before turning a desired IGBT on. Switching the IGBT o’¼Ć
will still happens immediately.
Dead time can be applied by the control software, of within the gate driver. Using dead time within
software ensures equally dead times of the IGBTs, it require more resources keeping track of a dead
times. By using dead times within gate drivers is the easiest solution, due to some IGBT gate drivers
include the dead time capability.

4.5
Construction of converter control modulation
The converter takes the IGBTs gate signal, which are binary 1/0 or on/o’¼Ć. The converter control
generates those gate signals for the IGBTs by modulating reference voltages. A PWM algorithm is
used to generate the converter phase modulation. The PWM algorithm for a three level converter uses
two triangular waves with the desired modulation frequency. The two triangular waves are mirrored
around zero amplitude. The triangular amplitude is equal to the DC voltages. By comparing the
reference voltages with both triangular waves, two pulse pattern are created. Subtracting the lower
comparison pattern from the upper, creates a three states pattern: positive, neutral and negative.
The converter modulation frequency is chosen to 10kHz, to compare it with an establish converter in
chapter practical work.

4.7

The regulating is performed in the DQ domain. ID and IQ are the input in the regulation, representing
respectively the real and the imaginary part of the current. The measured Id and Iq compared with

into a desired current in DQ domain and written in the constants ID and IQ
These calculation can be seen in with Park transformation, section 2.4.2.
The regulating output in the DQ domain is converted to voltages by the inverse Park transformation.
This is the desired converter output voltage that will have to be modulated. The desired voltage
equals to the grid voltage plus the reactor voltage.
The inverse Park transformation uses the angle reference calculated from grid voltage to obtain the
same angle reference. See section 2.4.4 page 29 for calculation of the converter voltage in DQ domain
and the inverse park transformation.
The results of the inverse park transformation are the three phases, which is used for the converter
voltage modulation.

4.9.1
Simulation 4: Driving the induction motor using the upper battery
In this ’¼ürst simulation with the induction motor, a implemented controller algorithm drives the motor
from only the upper battery.
For the three level converter to operate with only the upper battery a modi’¼ücation to the PWM
Generator is required. The principle is like a 2 level PWM algorithm. A voltage input is compared
with a triangle wave, which creates the binary PWM pattern 1/0 (on/o’¼Ć). The input voltage and the
triangle have same average potential. The triangular wave amplitude peak to peak is the DC voltage
of 400V. The triangle wave is still at 10kHz.
The PWM state pattern uses the "state to pulse decoder" and generates the gate signals for the IGBTs.
Because the PWM state pattern only vary between 1 and 0, only the upper batter is used to modulate
the converter output voltage.

Conclution
This project addresses converting power within di’¼Ćerent application as charging batteries, driving
electric motors and mostly controlling active and reactive power ’¼éow into the electricity grid.
A key element for implementing this project has been the exponential interest in a cleaner environ-
ment. Within electricity this interest is with focus on renewable energy sources and CO2 neutral
transportation as electric vehicles.
This project is based on using electric vehicles for energy storage besides of being a vehicle.
The core of this project is a three level three phase converter and is exploring its possibilities for
performing serial applications without changing converter structure.
The project presents how electric vehicles can obtain the most e’¼ācient method of converting power
’¼éow in grid applications without multiple converters installed in the vehicles, that only will increase
the production costs of the vehicle.
This project describes in depth the converter topologies and operation procedure with theories, sim-
ulations and practical tests.
In the theoretical chapters it were proven that a power ’¼éow can be controlled by controlling the
voltage across an impedance. This were derived using mathematical expressions and demonstrated
using practical examples. This formed the basis for the simulations and practical work.
Investigation of the converter topologies were performed in order to establish simulations structure.
This includes the 3 level converter structure and implementing controlling algorithm.
Executing the simulations proves operation of the 3 level converter with and without regulating. With
design and implementation of the regulating algorithm, a controlled power ’¼éow is obtained, both for
active and reactive power. Furthermore it demonstrates that the active power ’¼éow is proportional
to charging/discharging of the electric vehicle battery. The converter structure with applied 2x400V
proves obtainable power ’¼éow without a galvanic isolated step up/down transformer. The simulation
demonstrates the modulations in’¼éuence of generating current ripples, which also results of ripples
within the power ’¼éow. The harmonic currents was analysed with FFT spectrum analyse, for this part
it can be concluded that that the switching frequency leads to harmonics. The dominating harmonics
were the 5. and 7. harmonic, the 3. harmonic is eliminated due to a three phase system without
neutral connected to ground. For implementing the motor the simulation model were modi’¼üed.
A special designed algorithm were implanted to operate the motor using ether the upper or lower
part of the 3 level converter. The prove of concept were proven that it is possible to operate the mo-
tor using only one single battery, furthermore also to switch between the two batteries under operation.
It is obtained to design a complete converter and converter control. Choosing semiconductors and re-
actors for the three level converter based on the rated power of 10kVA. Designing the converter control

GENERAL DESCRIPTION 
The AD7656/AD7657/AD76581 contain six 16-/14-/12-bit,  
fast, low power, successive approximation ADCs all in the one 
package that is designed on the iCMOSŌäó process (industrial 
CMOS). iCMOS is a process combining high voltage silicon 
with submicron CMOS and complementary bipolar technol-
ogies. It enables the development of a wide range of high 
performance analog ICs, capable of 33 V operation in a 
footprint that no previous generation of high voltage parts 
could achieve. Unlike analog ICs using conventional CMOS 
processes, iCMOS components can accept bipolar input signals 
while providing increased performance, which dramatically 
reduces power consumption and package size.  
The AD7656/AD7657/AD7658 feature throughput rates up  
to 250 kSPS. The parts contain low noise, wide bandwidth, 
track-and-hold amplifiers that can handle input frequencies  
up to 12 MHz.  
The conversion process and data acquisition are controlled 
using CONVST signals and an internal oscillator. Three 
CONVST pins allow independent, simultaneous sampling of 
the three ADC pairs. The AD7656/AD7657/AD7658 all have  
a high speed parallel and serial interface, allowing the devices  
to interface with microprocessors or DSPs. In serial interface 
mode, the parts have a daisy-chain feature that allows multiple 
ADCs to connect to a single serial interface. The AD7656/ 
AD7657/AD7658 can accommodate true bipolar input signals 
in the ┬▒4 ├Ś VREF range and ┬▒2 ├Ś VREF range. The AD7656/ 
AD7657/AD7658 also contain an on-chip 2.5 V reference.
