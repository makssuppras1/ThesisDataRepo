Summary
Planning based on dynamic epistemic logic is a uniﬁed way to plan under non-
determinism and uncertainty of agents. DEL framework also allows for designing
communication actions in the same manner as other actions which agents can
perform, and thus makes it possible to include agent coordination and commu-
nication in planning, so they become implicit. The key moment of such planning
is taking perspective shifts - switching from the objective perspective of an om-
niscient observer to a subjective perspective of an acting agent.
Using the materials mainly from papers [EBMN15] and [BEMN16], we develop
a technique for computing implicitly coordinated strong conditional plans in a
form of policies. These are solutions to planning tasks where agents are acting to
achieve a joint goal. This study is quite novel, as algorithms in the current liter-
ature cover single-agent planning and centralized multi-agent planning (without
subjective perspectives of agents). The theoretical background is recapped and
organized with relation to planning. Certain attention is also given to studying
equivalence of states which occur during plan search.

Chapter 1
Introduction
“Theories are nets: only he who casts will catch” - Novalis (a poet, mystic,
philosopher).
In the latest decades artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) is becoming more and more inte-
grated into the everyday day life. One can distinguish between two approaches
to AI - symbolic reasoning and machine learning - with the latter usually being
implied in media. This thesis focuses on the symbolic reasoning approach and
here are the problematic aspects of it.
Symbolic reasoning approach requires an AI-engineer to create some discretized
model of a real life task which is attempted to be solved by an artiﬁcial intelli-
gent system. Discretized modelling is usually achieved by formalizing the task.
The process of formalizing can be described as follows. Every real life task has
its goal (what has to be achieved) and a domain in which is has to be achieved.
The number of factors (conditions, agents, action eﬀects, etc.) present in the
domain is probably inﬁnite, since we are dealing with a real life scenario (prov-
ing its ﬁniteness would be equivalent to proving the ﬁniteness of the Universe,
which is more of a physical or a philosophical question rather than the question
of computer science). Formalization of a real life planning task is taking the set
of factors which a modeller considers relevant to the task together with its goal
and expressing it in a symbolic language with mathematical properties.
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction
The beneﬁt of this is that now there are distinct parts of the planning task,
lack of ambiguity and a possibility to use already developed mathematical tech-
niques. The disadvantage is tightly related to the beneﬁt because the formalized
task is an idealization of a real life scenario and may ignore some of its common
phenomena, which for instance can be agent’s uncertainty about initial state of
the task, agent’s uncertainty about action outcomes, non-deterministic action
outcomes. As it was shown in various papers, it is possible to formalize these
properties too. Paper [EBMN15] shows how, using dynamic epistemic logic (re-
ferred to as DEL and discussed later in the report), it is possible to introduce
uncertainty and non-determinism into the framework without changing the un-
derlying formalism. This is clearly a major advantage of DEL, because it is
a step towards general artiﬁcial intelligence which would ultimately be able to
solve any type of planning task in a uniﬁed way with no domain-speciﬁc external
components involved.
Nevertheless, there is another complication in multi-agent planning which is
not solved by DEL solely. As it is discussed in [WMW05], when multiple agents
are planning for a joint goal, the planning process normally involves some form
of coordination and communication, which can be done before, during or after
planning. This can be needed in a scenario when, for instance, every agent is
planning for himself, but the resulting plans of diﬀerent agents are incompatible,
i.e. have conﬂicts.
An example of conﬂict can be when an agent, acting according to his plan,
is attempting to move to a place which is already taken by some other agent,
which was acting according to her own plan, and only one agent can be in this
place at a time. In this case agents need to replan and/or coordinate on how
they are going to solve this conﬂict.
Another example of communication is when one agent cannot reach the goal
on its own and thus requires other agents to act. An act of communication in
this case would be a message from this agent requiring some other agent to carry
out a necessary action.
Even though these techniques work and are quite widespread, there is a clear
distinction between planning and communication. In simple words, it is com-
mon to see planning and communication as two diﬀerent things and design the
latter speciﬁcally for the planning domain. In this way, it would be a major
improvement to treat communication and planning in a uniﬁed way. Papers
[EBMN15], [BEMN16] demonstrate how modifying epistemic models (underly-
ing structures in epistemic logic) and using so called perspective shifts allows
for treating communication abilities as planning. This leads to the notion of
implicitly coordinated plans, but before it can be studied, dynamic epistemic
logic as well as other components of planning have to be described.

epistemic models (states), event models (actions) are using globally given ﬁnite

2.1
Epistemic logic
Epistemic (modal) logic is a variation of modal logic which has to do with
representing knowledge passing and uncertainty of agents and is often attributed
to Finnish philosophers and logicians Georg Henrik von Wright and Jaakko
Hintikka [HS15], while the beginnings of modal logic date back to Aristotle and
medieval thinkers. Key components of epistemic logic will be given now.
2.1.1
Epistemic language and epistemic states
Definition 2.2 The epistemic language LKC is encoded in the Backus-Naur
form in the following way: ϕ ::= ⊤| ⊥| p | ¬ϕ | ϕ ∧ϕ | Kiϕ | Cϕ, where p ∈P
and i ∈A.
Several clariﬁcations can be given regarding the language. Term ϕ is a formula
scheme. Elements ⊤and ⊥are trivial truth and fallacy correspondingly. Since
epistemic logic is, in fact, a superset of propositional logic, inference of the
latter can be used, so formulae like, for instance, ϕ →ψ and ϕ∨ψ are also well-
formed. Ki and C are modal operators, which deﬁne the name of this language.
In natural language their usage can be read as "agent i knows that ϕ is true"
for Kiϕ and "it is common knowledge that ϕ is true" for Cϕ. Note also, that a
modally weaker operator Bi (agent i believes) is not a part of the language.
Definition 2.3 An epistemic model M = ⟨W, (Ri)i∈A, V ⟩is a triple con-
sisting of:
1. The ﬁnite non-empty set of worlds W (also called the domain, not to be
confused with an informal planning task domain mentioned in Chapter 1).
2. The collection of indistinguishability (or accessibility) relations for all
agents, whose element for agent i is Ri ⊆W × W.
3. The valuation function V . Paper [EBMN15] deﬁnes it as V : P →P(W),
i.e. mapping a proposition p to the set of worlds in which p is true. Paper
[Bol17], on the other hand, uses labelling function L instead of V to map a
world w to the set of propositions true in this world, that is L : W →2P .
The expressive power of these two appears the same so both might be used
throughout this report.

A = {1, 2} and the set of atomic propositions P = {h, p}, where h stands for

A pointed epistemic model gives form an epistemic state (M, w) consisting of
an epistemic model M and a single actual world w. In order to make it possible
for one agent to plan on behalf of another agent, the notion of pointed epistemic
model is extended to the notion of multi-pointed epistemic model [EBMN15],
which ﬁnalizes the deﬁnition of a state used when building a state-space for a
planning task.
Definition 2.4 (M, Wd) is an epistemic state (also can be referred to simply
as state), where Wd ⊆W is a set of designated worlds.
If Wd is a singleton set, then the state is called global. If Wd is closed under
accessibility relation for some agent i, then this state is called local for agent
i. The closure means that from any world w ∈Wd it is possible to access any
other world v ∈Wd. Given some state s = (M, Wd) it is possible to obtain
an associated local state of agent i by taking a perspective shift of agent i :
si = (M, {v | wRiv and w ∈Wd}). Taking perspective shifts is the central mo-
ment of epistemic planning (especially when computing implicitly coordinated
policies), because it shows internal perspectives of agents on states, that is, how
agents see the states they are planning for.
Additionally, papers [EBMN15]
and [BEMN16] introduce a function Globals(s) such that for any s = (M, Wd),
Globals(s) = {(M, w) | w ∈Wd}. In other words, the function returns the set
of global states contained in some arbitrary state s.
It is now possible to semantically deﬁne truth in epistemic states.

3. If s is local for agent i then s |= ϕ iﬀs |= Kiϕ

There are certain remarks about these properties which are worth making.
Property 1 is true because after taking a perspective shift on s, its set of
designated worlds changes from Wd to W ′
d, and the latter contains exactly those
worlds in which ϕ must be true in order for Kiϕ to be semantically entailed in
s. This is a good example of relationship between syntax and semantics. By
making perspective shifts it is possible to remove the knowledge operator from
the query formula and perform model checking on on the epistemic model with
extended set of designated worlds. As it will be shown further in the report, it
plays a role in planning, because when performing an action an agent must know
that the action is applicable. Applicability is usually ensured by checking that
an action’s precondition formula is semantically entailed in a state the agent
is planning for. By using perspective shifts, it is possible to keep precondition
formulae purely propositional and handle the associated local states separately.
Property 2. When planning, it can happen that taking an agent’s perspective
shift does not extend the set of designated worlds Wd of some state s. The
reason for this can be that the original state s is already local for that agent.
This demonstrates how, with transitive accessibility relations, it makes sense
to take a perspective shift only once. That is, for a global state s we ﬁnd an
i-associated local state si. Taking the perspective shift again will have no eﬀect
si = (si)i. It is necessary to mention that equality symbol = can be perceived
as "states are the same". There is no need to speak about underlying structure
isomorphism, because taking a perspective shift only aﬀects the Wd part of a
state (M, Wd), leaving the M part untouched. Later in the report, another
kind of identity will be introduced to ﬁt the plan searching needs.
Property 3. As stated in the original paper, this property follows directly
from the ﬁrst two properties.

2.1.3
Adding dynamics - epistemic actions and product
update
In order to achieve a goal under uncertainty represented in an epistemic state,
an agent must be able to perform actions and hence change the epistemic state
he is currently in. Epistemic actions dictate how a state will change when they
are applied to it. Similarly to epistemic states, epistemic actions consist of two
parts: an event model and a set of designated events.
==PAGE:20==
10
Theoretical background
Definition 2.9 An event model E = ⟨E, (→i)i∈A, pre, post⟩is a quadruple
consisting of:
1. The ﬁnite non-empty set of events E (the domain).
2. The collection of indistinguishability (or accessibility) relations for all
agents, whose element for agent i is →i
⊆E × E.
3. Precondition labelling function pre : E →Lp. It assigns a precondition
formula in the language of propositional logic Lp to every event.
4. Postcondition labelling function post : E →Lp. It assigns a postcondition
formula in Lp to every event. Usually the postcondition formula consists
of conjunctions of atomic propositions, negations of atomic propositions,
⊥and ⊤.
The diﬀerence from paper [EBMN15] and [BEMN16] is that preconditions and
postconditions formulae cannot contain epistemic logic operators Ki and C
(however, events with such formulae have not been observed in the mentioned
papers). By this restriction it is ensured that all the knowledge and uncertainty
is handled semantically with the help of indistinguishability relations, which
makes other stages of the project development (such as state-space deﬁnition)
more distinct.
In practice, it appears to be more convenient to represent post component of an
event model as postadd and postdel; postadd : E →P(P) maps every event in an
event model to a set of propositions which it makes true, postdel : E →P(P)
maps every event in an event model to a set of propositions which it makes
false. A special case when for all e ∈E post(e) = ⊤, which is the same as
postadd(e) = ∅, postdel(e) = ∅, is equivalent to reducing the event model to
E = ⟨E, (→i)i∈A, pre⟩. This tells the modeller that this event model is purely
epistemic, so is changing only agents’ knowledge of the facts but not the facts
themselves. In contrast, if an epistemic action is also changing facts, then it is
called an ontic action (has to do with ontology) [BA11].
Definition 2.10 (E, Ed) is an epistemic action (or simply an action), where
E is an event model and Ed is a set of designated events.
Epistemic actions too can be global and local for agents. Arbitrary epistemic
actions which are not local for any of the agents in A are not allowed in the
current planning framework. Agent locality of actions implies that | Ed | (car-
dinality of a set) can be greater than 1. In case of epistemic states a set Wd

Theoretical background
In classical planning [Bol17], a propositional action is applicable in a proposi-
tional state if the propositions in the state satisfy the precondition formula in
the action. By removing agent uncertainty, non-deterministic action outcomes
and making all states and actions fully observable by all agents, it is possible
to represent classical planning tasks in DEL framework. In this way, all states
will have only one world which is the actual world and all actions will have
only one event which is the actual event. This makes the notion of applicability
equivalent to that in classical planning. However, conditions for an action to be
applicable in a state are diﬀerent on arbitrary states and actions.

3.3
Epistemic planning state-spaces
The traditional way of planning under non-determinism is by using AND-OR
planning trees/graphs (depending on whether it is allowed to have loops and
to reach a node in a graph by several diﬀerent paths). AND-OR graphs are
used in [CPRT03], [RN09]. AND-OR graph approach for epistemic planning is
suggested in [EBMN15] and used in thesis [Jen13].
In short, an AND-OR graph is diﬀerent from an ordinary graph (with OR-nodes
only) by having AND-nodes, every of which is solved only if all its children are
solved. Furthermore, unlike in classic planning with ordinary (planning) graphs,
ﬁnding some goal state does not signify the termination of planning. The plan-
ning, that is expanding the AND-OR graph, is terminated when the root of the
graph is solved. This in turn, might require ﬁnding several goal nodes (e.g. in
various branches of the graph).
For strong and i-strong policies without implicit coordination (centralized in
the language of [EBMN15]) computation it might be suﬃcient to use the for-
mat presented in [Jen13], which has two types of nodes: AND-nodes for non-
deterministic action outcomes and OR-nodes for opting between several appli-
cable actions. Although, there would be a need for a slight modiﬁcation for
extending the set of initial states from s0 to Globals(si
0) for an i-strong policy,
but principle of building the graph would not change.

3.3.1

To begin with, state-space for epistemic planning is going to be represented as

Definition 3.14 An enhanced AND-OR graph is a directed graph G =
(V, E), where V is the disjoint union of nodes V = VP ShOR ∪VAgActOR ∪
VActResT oGlsAND ∪VP ShT oGlsAND and E is the disjoint union of edges E =
Eagt ∪Eact ∪Eblank.
There exist 4 types of nodes (names are given based on outcoming edges):
1. VP ShOR: OR-nodes branching on perspective shifts of diﬀerent agents.
2. VAgActOR: OR-nodes branching on diﬀerent actions of the same agent.
3. VActResT oGlsAND: AND-node branching on all global states contained in
some action application resulting state.
4. VP ShT oGlsAND: AND-node branching on all global states contained in
some perspective shift taking resulting state.
Every node n ∈V is represented by an epistemic state S(n). It must be men-
tioned that epistemic states of nodes in VP ShT oGlsAND and VAgActOR are always
global.
There exist 3 types of edges:
1. Eagt: given nodes n and m such that n ∈VP ShOR and m ∈VP ShT oGlsAND,
the edge (n, m) ∈Eagt is labelled by an agent i, i.e. A(n, m) = i, such
that S(m) is an epistemic state which is a result of taking i-th perspective
shift in the state S(n).
2. Eact: given nodes n and m such that n ∈VAgActOR and m ∈VActResT oGlsAND,
the edge (n, m) ∈Eact is labelled by an action A(n, m) = a, such that
S(m) is an epistemic state which is a result of S(n) ⊗a.

3.4.5.3
Arguments for only ancestral comparisons
The algorithm only checks for equivalence to the states on the path to some
current node and not everywhere in the planning tree, which was inspired by the
approach in the textbook [RN09](ﬁgure 4.11). It is not expected that this aﬀects
termination negatively, i.e. for some nodes m, n ∈VP ShOR, S(m) ↔S(n), n and
m are not related by ancestral relationship, the branch of n terminates iﬀthe
branch of m terminates (considering decidable fragments of DEL planning, see
the next section). Additionally, VP ShOR ⊂V . More precisely, let us study the
simplest case of node q expansion. That is, Globals(S(qi)) = {t}, Globals(t ⊗
a) = {t′}. So, for one node in VP ShOR we have three nodes of other types.
Which means that in case we were checking for equivalence in the whole tree,
we would have to traverse the nodes of these other types as well. Nevertheless,
we can agree that this can be done with constant complexity because we know
precisely the structure of the planning tree, in other words, for all k ∈V we
know the type of parentOf(k) and the type of childOf(k), where childOf(k)
is an inverse of parentOf(k). As a consequence of this decision, we need to
check for equivalence between states of nodes in VAgActOR when extracting the
policy. It is possible to say, that the gain of checking for equivalence at policy
extraction stage and not at planning stage is that in the former case we are sure
that the queried states are not contained in descendants of dead end ancestors
(unlike the latter case, where this can happen), so equivalence checks are never
redundant.

3.4.6
Termination and correctness
Per-branch termination is ensured by spotting equivalent states on the path and
eliminating the loops. However this is not enough to ensure termination for the
general case of planning based on dynamic epistemic logic, because in papers
[BA11] and [AB13] it is proven that epistemic planning is undecidable for 2 or
more agents for S5 system.
As for correctness, we claim that if the algorithm returns a global policy then
this policy is of speciﬁed type. Due to the fact that any implicitly coordinated
policy "includes" a centralized policy [EBMN15](see corresponding successor
function comparison), the correctness will be argued for for implicitly coordi-
nated policies. Recall the deﬁnition of global policy with (APP) requirement
(3.5). Knowledge of preconditions ((APP) and (UNIF) requirements together)
is ensured by making the nodes which contain perspective shift results to be of
AND-type. (UNIF) requirement is satisﬁed by tree expansion rules (see algo-
rithm 1) and the ways in which goal nodes and dead end nodes are propagated.
(DET) requirement is satisﬁed by making the nodes which contain the states of
Dom(π) to be OR-type nodes as well as putting constraints on when parents
of these nodes are considered solved (see action counters in subsection 3.4.2).
Strength of the policy, i.e. the success of all its executions, is ensured by two
parts. The ﬁrst one is making the nodes which contain action application re-
sulting states to be AND-type nodes, all children of which must eventually lead
to goal nodes. The second part resides in how a policy is extracted by recon-
structing paths from all alive (not having a dead end ancestor) goal nodes to
the root of the planning tree.

4.1.1
Motivation
The question about what does it mean for two epistemic states to be equivalent
was raised multiple times in this report before. We shall now study this in detail.
Two pointed epistemic models are modally equivalent when they satisfy exactly
the same formulae [LPW+07]. For image-ﬁnite models, modal equivalence im-
plies bisimilarity [ABvJ13], [vE06]. The notation bisimilarity between states is
s ↔t, which can be read as s is bisimilar to t. As we shall see below, bisimilar
states may not be isomorphic. To make a distinction, isomorphic states will be
denoted as s ≃t.
The simplest case is depicted in the ﬁgure below (note that state t is multi-
pointed, this will be discussed further).
==PAGE:70==

Figure 4.1: Simple bisimilar but not isomorphic states.
Treating s and t as graphs, it is clear that they are not isomorphic, i.e. s ̸≃t.
Nevertheless, it is the case that s and t are bisimilar, i.e. s ↔t. Solely from
the symbolic point of view, the number of formulae which can be constructed in
the language of epistemic logic is inﬁnite, so checking for modal equivalence ex-
haustively is not an option. However, if we leave only one world in state t, then
s ≃t, so the process of model checking in s becomes the same as in t. Adding
the removed world back simply makes model checking for t twice as bigger as
for s, because worlds 0′ and 1′ have the same valuation and are both isolated.
Another capability of this example is that it shows bisimulation between two
worlds of one model. Bisimulation between worlds is going to be used later in
the algorithm for epistemic state equivalence checking.
Unfortunately, modal equivalence between states is not always obvious. In his
master thesis, Mads Krogsgaard Pico Jensen gives the following example (ﬁgure

Applying a to s′ leads to state s′′ which again is modally equivalent to s′ and

termination completely because with a certain searching strategy (not depth-

branch of an OR-node. Although even in this situation the state space size
would be larger than needed. Apart from that, internal structures of epistemic
states contained in state space nodes are also growing in size in terms of worlds.
This issues is mentioned in [Jen13] and tackled in [LPW+07]. Such growth in
turn has a negative impact on model checking, which, for example, is done for
action applicability checks and global states set membership checks. The need
to check if a bisimilar state has been visited before becomes clear.
There exist, of course, various equivalence treating optimizations. For instance,

so can be removed or simply ignored. Another example is when state s has
a designated world w where proposition r is true, but in state t no world has
such valuation. It is clear, that s and t cannot be modally equivalent. However,
these examples are rather situational, so a proper notion of bisimulation, given

6.1
Further steps
During this project work the following ﬁelds of future work were discovered.
• Using best-ﬁrst search instead of breadth-ﬁrst search by introducing a
heuristic function. Studied planning tasks so far had diﬀerent domains,
so using a domain-speciﬁc heuristic function (such as Manhattan distance
for path ﬁnding problems as an example) would not be an option. For
planning under uncertainty and non-deterministic action outcomes, the
natural beginnings of heuristic function development could be based on
the number of outcomes an agent considers possible, i.e. the cardinalty of
Globals(s⊗a). The idea is to give higher priorities to expanding the nodes
in VP ShOR whose parents have lower numbers of children. Another idea
is to commit to actions by giving a higher priority to such nodes which
are reached by action a such that the counter for action a in the grand-
grandparent node in VP ShT oGlsAND has the higher value than counters for
other actions.
• A deeper study is needed to understand how switching from planning tree
to planning graph aﬀects planning. This would imply that a node can have

• As it was state before, the planning tree contains all types of nodes, while

state-space can be achieved by keeping nodes of certain types outside of

• A better technique for splitting a state into global states can be developed.
For now, splitting into globals only works with the set of designated worlds
of the original state without modifying the underlying model. Imagine
that for some s′ ∈Globlas(s), w is the designated world, and there exists
v ∈W of s′ such that v is not A-reachable from w. In this case, v will
not play a role when evaluating formulae in s′, so can be removed from
W. This can lead to smaller epistemic states and can simplify the process
of identifying bisimilar states.
• From the practical point of view, more tests on planning tasks with dead
end nodes are required.
• General code improvements can be done. An example of such improve-
ment is creating a hierarchy of nodes of diﬀerent types with the help of
inheritance of struct’s supported in C++. This would improve readabil-
ity of the code.
• Propagation of goal nodes and dead end nodes relies on mutual recursion of
associated methods. For planning tasks leading to large planning trees and
long policy executions, recursive approach has a risk of causing function
call stack overﬂow. In this way, changing to an iterative approach would
be a good decision.

6.2
Conclusion
In earlier phases of the thesis work, a deep understanding of the theoretical basis
was required. The knowledge of epistemic logic and its dynamics was obtained
within "Logical theories for uncertainty and learning" course taught at DTU.
==PAGE:87==
6.2 Conclusion
77
However, moving to multi-pointed epistemic states and associated manipula-
tions (taking perspective shifts, extracting contained global states, etc.) was a
new subject. The topic of computing implicitly coordinated plans with DEL
framework is quite new, so many deﬁnitions, propositions and, generally speak-
ing, structural entities are not yet stable, meaning that they can be improved
in later literature and diﬀer in various sources. That is why it was extremely
important not to take things for granted and determine the diﬀerences and
similarities between approaches to DEL-based planning. This clariﬁes why a
relatively large part of the report (section 3.2) is dedicated to understanding
diﬀerent types of conditional plans. The skills obtained within "Data logic" and
"Knowledge-based systems" courses helped with comprehending the building
blocks of DEL framework as well as sometimes overwhelming formal notations
used in the studied scientiﬁc papers. "Data logic" course was especially useful
because it presented the big picture of what logic is. Predicates, quantiﬁers,
rules of inference, the distinction between object-language and meta-language,
syntax and semantics was clearly communicated to the students during this
course, and without this it would be much harder to see the place of DEL in
the world of logic.
Once the solid theoretical background was obtained, it was possible to develop
the planning algorithm with respect to formal requirements.
The algorithm
was developed and tested on a wide range of smaller planning tasks each of
which challenged some property or requirement of the algorithm. Determining
the scalability of the algorithm (how well it performs on large planning tasks)
can be considered an open question. The same goes for applying it to practi-
cal cases. "Artiﬁcial Intelligence and Multi-Agent Systems" course provided a
solid knowledge of how planning is usually done. The key components of classic
AI (state-spaces, search algorithms, etc.) were communicated in a clear and
comprehensive way, and the programming project developed within this course
was employed to develop the skeleton for the planning algorithm presented in
the current thesis. Finally, the planning algorithm was implemented in C++
programming language, some sides of which were covered in "Programming in
C++" course. There are still many sub-topics of DEL-based planning to be
elaborated on and as always there is room for improvements.
