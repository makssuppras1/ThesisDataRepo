The usage of Independent Component Analysis (ICA) in decomposing fMRI data
has been very popular throughout the years. Although in recent years the Inde-
pendent Vector Analysis (IVA) has increased its popularity because of its ability
to deal with subject speciﬁc comparisons which most ICA methods can not do.
IVA has been proposed with many extension before, one of them being the IVA-
Normal-Inverse-Gamma (IVA-NIG) distribution which is based in independent
Normal distribution with a mean also drawn from the Normal distribution and
scaled down from the inverse Gamma distribution. The aim of this project was to
implement the IVA-NIG model in python that would be successful in spatial map
estimations of fMRI data. Along with looking into the relationship and aﬀects of
the hyper-parameter V0 to the model. In this project a special case of the IVA-NIG
model was implemented that optimized the value of V0 so it would better ﬁt to the
data. The model is noted as IVA-NIG-V0. The results from the project indicated
that the IVA-NIG model did successfully estimate true spatial maps of the data
being analyzed but better yet the results also showed that the IVA-NIG-V0 model
outperformed the IVA-NIG model in estimating the true spatial map and task
speciﬁc components.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Neuroscience is an important research ﬁeld that helps us understand how our
minds work and how it gets aﬀected by certain deceases and why. Functional
magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) is one of the most important methods in said
research ﬁeld available today, since the procedure is noninvasive and can be very
informative (Lee et al., 2008). The procedure uses MRI technology that measures
brain activity by detecting changes in the blood ﬂow. The key elements to analyz-
ing fMRI data are the dynamics of the blood-oxygenated-level-dependent (BOLD)
signal. The BOLD signal is used to map neural activity in the brain by imaging
the change in blood ﬂow, which can help researchers identify the most active areas
in the brain during mental tasks. (Lee et al., 2008, Engberg, 2016).
For the analysis of neuroimaging data, python has become a popular choice (Mill-
man and Brett, 2007). This is due to the fact that python is a high-level pro-
gramming language, object oriented and able to deal with large sizes of data. And
also because it is very interactive and can easily facilitate the interaction between
diﬀerent programming languages (Millman and Brett, 2007, Spacek and Swindale,
2009).
When it comes to the techniques used for the data analysis, blind source separa-
tions (BSS) are one of the most commonly used tools for the analysis of fMRI data
to recover brain activation patterns (Dea et al., 2011). One of the most popular
BSS methods used today is Independent Component Analysis (ICA), which is a
computational method that assumes statistical independence of the source signals
and reveals hidden factors that underlie the data (Kim et al., 2006a, Lee, 1998).
Although very eﬃcient and much used, ICA has its limitations when it comes to
larger datasets and comparisons between subjects. For this reason an alternative
1

version called Independent Vector Analysis (IVA), which explicitly models subject
variability, came to light. IVA is an extension of ICA and part of the joint BSS
(JBSS) category. (Anderson et al., 2014). IVA assumes independence between
sources within separate datasets, while sources within one dataset are mutually
dependent (Lee et al., 2008, Kim et al., 2006a). IVA has been proposed with many
extensions throughout the years, one of those was proposed by Engberg (2016),
called IVA-Normal-Inverse-Gamma (IVA-NIG), IVA with a NIG source distribu-
tion which is based on independent Normal distribution, with a mean also drawn
from the Normal distribution and a variance drawn from the inverse Gamma dis-
tribution. (Engberg et al., 2016). The IVA-NIG method will be the main focus
of this thesis, where we will look into the relationship of the NIG prior and the
hyperparameter V0, and its eﬀects on the source distribution. The Multivariate
Laplace (MVL) extension of IVA will also be present for comparisons and analyses.
1.1
Problem Statement
As mentioned before BSS is a group of techniques used for estimating individual
source components from their mixtures.
This is not always a straightforward
and easy task. One of the main problems the BSS methods face is the so called
"Cocktail party problem", where scientiﬁcally speaking the goal is to be able
to identify the important and interesting variables from all the noise and non-
interesting variables.
One of those BSS techniques used for said task is IVA,
whose power is being able to retain the dependency across subjects while using
independence between subjects to identify latent sources. The IVA technique will
be the major focus of this thesis.
The overall aim of the thesis is to implement a ready to use decomposition algo-
rithm in python that relies on IVA with two diﬀerent sets of priors, NIG and MVL.
The eﬃciency of the IVA-NIG model will be the main research topic throughout
the thesis, where it will be tested against other methods and we will look deeper
into its limitations and concepts.

Chapter 2
Background
In our brain the activity of our neurons are constantly ﬂuctuating, as we per-
form diﬀerent tasks and activities. Tasks and activities that range from simple
to complex on a cognitive level. Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI)
is a non-invasive technique discovered and used for measuring and mapping this
kind of brain activity. The technique has gathered a lot of popularity in a variety
of neuroscientiﬁc investigations, to better our understanding of how the healthy
brain works, and how its normal function is disrupted in disease related cases (Lee
et al., 2008). Blood ﬂow to an area of the brain is very sensitive when it comes
to a change in neural activity. This amount of blood ﬂow to certain areas of the
brain, is what fMRI is based on. The so called blood-oxygenated-level dependent
(BOLD) signal changes is the key element to analyzing fMRI data (Ulmer and
Jansen, 2013).
2.1
Preprocessing
It is not possible to this day to acquire perfect and noise free fMRI images. There-
fore before retrieving the data from the images we need to make sure that the
signal from each voxel within the image contains the correct information. To do
this we use a series of preprocessing methods or data transformations, which are
all aimed at reducing the sources of noise, in order to ensure the validity of the
statistical model. The prepossessing pipeline applied to the motor task data used
in this project, can be seen visually in Figure 2.1.

2.1.1
Slice Time Correction
fMRI data is acquired in multiple slices. Each of these slices are at diﬀerent times
which can cause slice acquisition delays. To compensate for these delays Slice Time
Correction (STC) has been proposed (Calhoun et al., 2000). In STC a reference
slice is chosen and all other slices are then interpolated to match the timing of the
chosen reference slice (Sladky et al., 2011). For the temporal interpolation to be
correct, the order of the acquisition of the slices is crucial.
2.1.2
Motion Correction
When head movements or other motions of the subject occur during fMRI imaging,
it can have huge eﬀects on the data acquired. The data is extracted by applying
statistical time-series analysis, which assumes that a given voxel within the brain
does not change over time (Jenkinson et al., 2002). Therefore to make the data
ready for further analysis the motion must be corrected. By using corregistration
we attempt to align all the images acquired to one reference image using standard
rigid body realignment techniques. The shape and size of a subjects brain does
not change with head movements but only the position does.
Therefore rigid
body transformations can be used to model all the diﬀerent head positions of the
subject. How the brain moves along the x, y and z-axes and its rotation around
the axes, is what is used to model the head positions (Engberg, 2016, Ashburner
and Friston, 2007).
2.1.3
Normalization
The most common technique for spatial normalization, is using a reference image
such as the Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) brain template to achieve a
common spatial co-ordinate space (Engberg, 2016, Khullar et al., 2011). This is
done to make it easier to compare multiple subjects in ﬁnding common brain acti-
vations, by making it so that one location in one subjects brain image, corresponds
to the same location in another subjects brain image. And all of the subjects brains
are of equal size and shape. SPM12 (url:http://www.ﬁl.ion.ucl.ac.uk/spm/software/spm12/,
(Ashburner et al., 2014)) is a popular toolbox used for this task of normalizing.
The toolbox oﬀers normalization along the anterior and posterior commissure, by
==PAGE:19==

reorienting all images to be on the same plane. Then it oﬀers direct and indirect
normalization, that refer to spatial normalization by registering the images to the
MNI template mentioned above (Ashburner et al., 2014). Whichever method is
chosen, the important factor in all of this is to normalize the data in each voxel for
comparison. The data may diﬀer between diﬀerent voxels because of blood ﬂow,
signal amplitude diﬀerences or due to the distance from the receive coil.
2.1.4
Spatial Smoothing
Spatial smoothing is a method that averages across all the data point by taking into
account each of their neighbouring points. This has the eﬀect that high frequencies
in the signal get removed and the low frequencies enhanced. This makes the true
activations of the signal clearer by removing the level of background noise and
prepares the data better for further analysis. By removing the background noise
in the data improves the Signal to Noise Ratio (SNR), but in turn reduces the
resolution of the images. Therefore a balance needs to be found between improving
the SNR at the cost of loosing clarity (Clare, 1997). There are several smoothing
methods available, like Gaussian kernel ﬁlters and wavelets to name a few. What
most of these methods have in common, and which has shown to be problematic
at times, is that the same smoothing is applied to the whole image, which can lead
to some areas being under-smoothed, and others being over-smoothed (Yue et al.,
2010). Full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the Gaussian kernel has shown
to be a good estimate to the amount of smoothing that should be applied.(Chen
and Glover, 2015). But ﬁnding the balance between over smoothing and under
smoothing certain areas of interest is the tricky part.
2.1.5
Despiking
As mentioned before, fMRI images contain all sorts of noise artifacts. Mentioned
preprocessing methods can remove most of these secondary artifacts, but not all.
For instance when it comes to very subtle eﬀects, or spike like head movements,
the noise could either be hard to locate or correlate with the feature being studied.
For this problem wavelet despiking has been applied (Patel et al., 2014). So what
this method does is remove the residual motion artifacts still present in the data,
before processing the data.

2.2
Decomposition Methods
Decomposition methods, also known as blind source separation (BSS) techniques
are typically based on higher-order statistics.The term blind is an indication that
both the source signals and the way they are mixed is an unknown factor. The
goal of the BSS is to recover the independent sources without this knowledge.
(Lee, 1998). Simply speaking we allow the data to determine the components.
There are times when BSS solutions are not eﬃcient enough to solve the problem
at hand, but in turn joint Blind Source Separation (JBSS) techniques are. JBSS
solutions extend the BSS solutions to be able to solve simultaneously multiple
BSS problems at once by assuming the statistical dependence between the hidden
sources across the mixtures(Lahat and Jutten, 2014).
2.2.1
PCA
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a technique that ﬁnds underlying vari-
ables, or principal components that best describes the data and is based on the
second order statistics. It computes an orthogonal linear transformation that maps
the data from a high dimensional space to a lower dimensional space, by ﬁnding the
principal components and its accompanying dimensions in receding order (Suma
and Murali, 2007). The reduction of dimensionality helps reduce computational
requirements and eliminates redundancies in the data, especially when dealing
with large datasets. Each of the components is deﬁned in relation to the previous
one and are therefore uncorrelated with each other (Engberg, 2016). For fMRI
analysis, the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) method is mostly used in code
due to high computationally eﬃcient implementations available. The two algo-
rithms deliver essentially the same results when implemented, which are a set of
"new axes" that are constructed from linear combinations of the original feature
space axes.
2.2.2
ICA
Independent Component Analysis known as ICA is a method for solving the BSS
problem and can be seen as an extension of PCA since it works better on data that
has been preprocessed with PCA. ICA is based on higher order statistics, and tries
==PAGE:22==
Background
10
to recover hidden common components that underlie some multivariate data using
statistical and computational techniques (McKeown et al., 2003). This is done by
assuming that the components are non-gaussian and that they are statistically
independent from each other (Hyvärinen et al., 2004).
2.2.2.1
GICA
GICA or group Independent Component Analysis is as the name implies an exten-
sion of the ICA method. The goal of GICA is to identify group trends or mutual
components across the data (Allen et al., 2012). There exist many approaches to
GICA, which diﬀer in terms of how the data is processed before the ICA step,
how the estimates of the subjects are computed and what kind of output is pro-
duced (Erhardt et al., 2011). To name a few of these approaches we have one
where single subject ICA’s are combined. This is done by running separate ICA
analyses on each subject, which is then followed by correlation or clustering to
enable the group inference. Then there are temporal and spatial concatenation,
which have the advantage over the previous mentioned method, that only one ICA
is performed, which is then divided into subject speciﬁc parts. This makes the
comparison of a speciﬁc component across subjects easier (Calhoun et al., 2009).
The diﬀerence between the two is that the temporal concatenation approach al-
lows for unique time courses for each subject but then in turn assumes common
group maps (Calhoun et al., 2009, Zhang et al., 2010). The spatial concatenation
approach on the other hand allows for unique maps but then assumes common
time courses (Calhoun et al., 2009). Two other approaches are the tensoral and
pre-averaging methods, commonly used when it comes to group ICA. The pre-
averaging approach is very basic, but less computationally demanding than the
other mentioned approaches. The approach requires both a common time course
and common spatial maps where the data is then averaged before performing ICA
(Schmithorst and Holland, 2004). The tensoral approach involves the estimation of
a common time course and a common image for each component, but allows for a
subject speciﬁc parameter to be estimated (Hui et al., 2011). All of the mentioned
approaches here, have their disadvantages and advantages, and the approach to
go with depends on what results wish to be achieved.

2.2.3
IVA
Independent Vector Analysis (IVA) belongs to the category of JBSS solutions and
is an extension of the ICA from univariate components to multivariate components
(Lee et al., 2008). In ICA we are dealing with single variables, but in IVA we
are dealing with random vectors.
These vectors, usually called source vectors
contain elements we assume are dependent of each other.
(Kim et al., 2006a,
Engberg et al., 2016, Kim et al., 2006b). We also assume independence between
the multivariate source signals that are represented as random vectors. To sum
it up, IVA utilizes both the inner dependency of the source vectors and also the
statistical independence among the multivariate source signals (Zhang et al., 2012).
IVA is especially useful when it comes to fMRI group analysis, and has been shown
to capture activation patterns in functional areas better than other methods (Lee
et al., 2008).
Both GICA and IVA deal with multiple subject fMRI analyses. Comparison of
the two methods can be seen in Figure 2.2. Both methods have shown to perform
well, but IVA has shown to produce results that are closer to the ground truth in
simulations when inter subject variability is high (Michael et al., 2015)

3.1.1.1
Optimization by L-BFGS
The Limited memory Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shannon method (L-BFGS) is
an extension of the Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shannon (BFGS) method which
was introduces in 1970 separately by each of the researchers it is named after
(Broyden, 1970, Fletcher, 1970, Goldfarb, 1970, Shanno, 1970). L-BFGS however
was ﬁrst described by Nocedal (1980), where it was called the SQN method. The
method is almost identical in its implementation to BFGS, but the diﬀerence lies
in the matrix update. The L-BFGS method stores a certain amount of pairs that
==PAGE:31==
Method
19
represent the matrices in stead of the matrices themselves. After computing a new
iteration the oldest pair is replaced by the newest one. This way always the most
recent pairs are saved to memory to deﬁne the iteration matrix. (Byrd et al., 1994).
Limited memory methods are known for their simplicity, they do not require any
knowledge on the hessian, since it is not needed for their computations. They
neither need any knowledge on the separability of the objective function, only an
approximation of the search direction to be able to ﬁnd the next iterate to deﬁne
the matrix (Nocedal, 1980, Liu and Nocedal, 1989).
For the algorithm the bound method of L-BFGS is utilized. For this we use the
optimize method from the Scipy library to apply the optimization to our cost
functions.
And the search direction will be given here in this project by the
gradient.

Preprocessing of Motor Data
Before we are able to apply the IVA and ICA methods to the data, the data needs
to be preprocessed. For the preprocessing of the images we use the decomp class.
The decomp class contains functions that perform subject wise SVD along with
group SVD among other things.
We start by loading the data and supplying the decomp class with the ﬁles and
then start to run the subject wise SVD function. For the ﬁrst subject, we mask the
data by ﬁnding the mean of the data and threshold it with the mean multiplied by
0.8. This leaves the edges of the data out of sorts. We then reshape our subjects
data with this mask, and apply the standard deviation of the data to the array in
whole. The next step is to demean our data by subtracting the mean of the ﬁrst
dimension and multiplying a newaxis of size one to the data. The function then
returns an array containing the subject wise SVD results. For the IVA algorithms,
we apply the methods to this SVD data, but for the gICA method we ﬁrst need to
perform the group wise SVD to further reduce the dimensionality and allow the
noiseless group ICA algorithm to be used.

regard everything extra as noise, so there will be no need for the mean value. This
results in a low value of V0 being locked in and produces a bad ﬁt of the data.

Discussion
The results from two out of three experiments on fMRI like or real fMRI data
showcased that the IVA methods do outperform the gICA method when it comes
to estimation of spatial maps and components. In all experiments except the one
where data was drawn from the Laplace source distribution, IVA-NIG outper-
formed IVA-MVL at spatial map estimations. IVA-NIG-V0 performed as well as
the IVA-NIG model at spatial map estimations and in the simulated data experi-
ments it seems to outperform the IVA-NIG model by a small amount. Although
it was concluded that the hyper-parameter V0 does not signiﬁcantly increase or
decrease the IVA-NIG models eﬃciency. On real task fMRI data the IVA-MVL
model came to a halt at the estimations and only got one estimation right out of 10
on the task components. Also when the ten spatial maps produced by the model
were examined, it was clear that the results were not good enough and something
needed ﬁxing in the algorithm, as there were in some cases no visual task compo-
nents at all, but instead images with alot of noise and no way at distinguishing
any results.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
During this project an IVA algorithm based on the NIG source distribution and
an IVA algorithm based on the MVL source distribution were successfully imple-
mented in python. The performance of both models were evaluated by applying
them to both simulated data and real motor task fMRI data. The aﬀects of the
hyper-parameter V0 on the ability of the IVA-NIG model to estimate spatial maps
were put to a test. This was done by implementing an optimization step in the
IVA-NIG model, where the best ﬁt of the hyper-parameter V0 to the data being
analyzed was found, noted as IVA-NIG-V0 throughout the thesis. All three mod-
els were then applied separately to all 3 types of data, simulated, SimTB data
and motor task data. The results in all three experiments showcased that the
IVA-NIG and the IVA-NIG-V0 models provided very similar results in the spatial
map estimations for all thress. And also provided the best results according to the
mean correlation results, AUC results and even for the component estimations for
the motor task data.
It is thereby concluded that the IVA-NIG model was successfully implemented
and provides satisfactory results in comparison to the GIFT implementation that
was the inspiration to this project. And that by optimizing the hyper-parameter
V0 during processing of the data will provide a better ﬁt due to matching in the
data and therefore sometimes a tiny amount better results than without. But
the conclusion is that the hyper-parameter does not have a great signiﬁcance to
the model after the matching has been done correctly and the best ﬁt is acquired
through optimization.
