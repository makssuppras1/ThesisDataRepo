Abstract
This master’s thesis explores the development of a mathematical model to support the
transition towards a circular plastic economy, focusing on reusing plastic waste in chemical
processes to reduce environmental impact. The research specifically examines the process
of partial oxidation in a tubular reactor, one of several chemical processing steps used in
the transformation of plastic waste into useful chemicals for new plastic production. The
thesis outlines the formulation of a Newton solver designed to solve differential-algebraic
equations (DAEs), which are central to the simulation of the chemical processes involved.
By iterative expansions, the study constructs a non-stoichiometric thermodynamic equi-
librium model of increasing complexity for a chemical reactor. The modeling relies on the
assumptions of an ideal chemical reactor called the Plug flow reactor. The final model cul-
minates in a two-phase model accommodating solids and gases and incorporates stochastic
dynamics to simulate real-world uncertainty. The model is tested against a single data
set and used to explore the effects of varying chemical input concentrations. Further, the
thesis highlights challenges in parameter estimation, particularly in scenarios with multi-
ple parameters. The work contributes to the field by providing a detailed mathematical
approach for modeling tubular partial oxidation reactors. However, the work also forms a
strong foundation for modeling other chemical processes applicable to tubular reactors.

Introduction
In the recent century, humans have developed the industrial capability to consume our
planet’s resources at a steadily increasing pace. It is becoming evident that we are exploit-
ing the planet’s resources at a clearly unsustainable level. In areas like packaging, medical
equipment, consumer goods, electronics, etc., one common resource we cannot overlook is
plastic. Plastic is often a necessary or the best solution, resulting in enormous demand
for plastic products.
The plastic fabrication and application by itself is not the main problem; it is how we
dispose of it once its purpose has been served. Plastic waste is steadily accumulating
in both land-based environments and particularly water-based environments, leading to
human health risks and introducing lethal effects on sealife [1]. The production of plastic,
and in turn, plastic waste, is not slowing down either. An OECD report projects a rise in
plastic waste from 353 Mt in 2019 to 1014 Mt in 2060 [2].
Engineering a solution to the rise in plastic waste is an important endeavor. One way
to combat plastic waste is to build a circular plastic economy. Here, the goal is to take
the unrecyclable plastic waste and reuse its raw materials.
Reusing plastic waste will
enable an alternative to dumping it in landfills or burning it. This will subsequently lower
the impact of plastic on the environment. This thesis strives to contribute to developing
a circular plastic economy by implementing mathematical models designed to overcome
obstacles in realizing the circular plastic economy.
The specific process that sparked the interest in the work of this thesis is a chemical
process that takes plastic waste as input and outputs chemicals that can then be used as
feedstock in the production of new plastics. One company working toward making this
process a reality is SemperCycle ApS. This master thesis project was done in partnership
with SemperCycle, which has provided valuable input, especially regarding the chemical
aspect of the work.
Mathematically, the process is interesting as the input of waste plastics is a nonuniform
feedstock. Hence, optimal feedstock processing demands continuous adjustment of process
parameters. The development of a mathematical model simulating the chemical processes
can enable automated or assisted control of the process parameters. Further, in the pre-
liminary steps of building a large-scale industrial process plant, the mathematical model
can help plan the sizing of different components. Additionally, the model can help run
multiple experiments to guide which experiments should be performed in the real world.
Transforming plastic waste into chemicals used in plastic production involves multiple
steps. In this master’s thesis project, we have worked with one step of the process called
partial oxidation (POX). To build a mathematical model of a partial oxidation reactor,
we have worked towards answering the following problem statement:

• Develop and document a modeling framework to solve differential algebraic equations

To answer the above problem statement, the thesis is structured into four main parts:
Part 1 consists of Chapter 2, which contains a literature review on related work and a
review of experimental measurements of a POX reactor.
Part 2 has 8 chapters consisting of Chapters 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, and 10, which contain the
theoretical basis of the thesis. This part starts by describing the theory behind differential-
algebraic equations and presents a method to solve them using a Newton solver. Further,
the chemical reactor model is developed and iteratively improved until an adiabatic Plug
flow reactor (PFR) model with a gas and solid phase is reached.
Lastly, a stochastic
extension to the model is introduced using stochastic DAEs, which also gives rise to the
explanation of the Extended Kalman filter (EKF) and how this can be used to estimate
model parameters from measurements with uncertainty.
Part 3 consists of Chapters 11 and 12, which contain the experimental work and results
obtained from working with the model. This part includes a grid independence test that
explores how the Newton solver is impacted by the choice of grid resolution. Further, an
analysis is performed to explore the connection between numerical precision and compu-
tational load for the Newton solver, depending on the selected grid resolution. In this
part, a simulation study is also presented, including a comparison with data, a presenta-
tion of stochastic simulations, and an analysis showing how the model reacts to changes
in input parameters. The simulation study is followed by results showing how the EKF
is impacted by the selection of measured variables and how well it estimates the system
dynamics of our model. The part is finished by presenting parameter estimation results
using simulated data, where the focus is on estimating the heat capacities of the chemicals
in the model.
Part 4 consists of Chapters 13 and 14, which conclude the thesis with a discussion, highlight
of possible future work, and a conclusion.

2
Literature review
In this chapter, we will present related work describing the modeling of the partial ox-
idation reaction. Partial oxidation is a subset of the broader process of gasification [3].
Methods for modeling gasification processes are well described in the literature, and we
will present a summary of the methods used. We will only focus on processes in which
catalysts are not used, as this best reflects the partial oxidation we want to model. Fur-
ther, there are multiple different reactor types used in partial oxidation. In our work, we
will focus on tubular reactors. We will additionally present a look into the availability of
experimental data for partial oxidation.
2.1
Modeling review
In the literature, four primary approaches to model gasification are identified. These are
1) thermodynamic equilibrium models, 2) kinetic models, 3) computational fluid dynamic
(CFD) models, and 4) artificial neural networks (ANN) [4]. Each of these has different
advantages and disadvantages depending on the chemical knowledge of a given process
and the access to high-quality experimental data.
We will apply the thermodynamic equilibrium approach in this thesis. These models rely
on the modeling of mass and energy balances to predict the composition in a gasifica-
tion reactor. There are two subbranches of the thermodynamic equilibrium model, the
stoichiometric and the non-stoichiometric method. We will apply the non-stoichiometric
method, as it has the advantage of not relying on defining the specific reaction mechanisms
of a given process. Instead of utilizing reaction mechanisms, the equilibrium is attained
by minimizing the Gibbs free energy of the chemicals in a reactor.
Kinetic and CFD models are more sophisticated than our selected approach. However,
these two approaches depend on a far greater insight into the details of the specific reactions
in the modeled process. Further, they are more computationally demanding and might
produce an output that is far more detailed than required for the application.
CFD
models are especially advanced and can produce very detailed simulations for 3D geometry.
However, implementing a CFD model is, in most cases, a very demanding task, and a
CFD model is generally accomplished through commercial software. The artificial neural
network approach to modeling gasification relies only on mathematics, and a model can
be obtained without any knowledge of chemistry. However, this approach requires a large
volume of quality data that reflects the dynamics of the reactions. As we will describe in
Section 2.2, quality data is a challenge, and we believe black-box models are ill-advised.
For the specific case of partial oxidation, a non-stoichiometric thermodynamic equilibrium
model has been used by [5], [6], and [7]. These references have all performed their own
chemical experiments to obtain reactor data, which they test their models against. They all
conclude that their models are able to reproduce their experimental data with consistency.

Common for all three articles is that they do not describe in detail the equations used in the
modeling. For this reason, it is hard to verify the specific model they have used, and we can
only rely on the word of the authors that the short descriptions of their overall approach
are valid. All the articles also use established software where the models are implemented
to perform simulations. Our work will differ from these articles by rigorously describing
the equations and the numerical model used to solve them.
CFD models are applied to partial oxidation in [8] and [9]. Both articles highlight the
complexity of the method and demonstrate the need for a thorough understanding of the
process’s finer details to construct a CFD model. We have not been able to find any ANN
models described in literature working with the chemical process we are interested in. In
general, the literature is sparse for ANN models due to the limited data. In [10], [11],
and [12], the authors describe an ANN modeling approach for partial oxidation under
conditions different from those our focus lies on. However, what stands out in all three
articles is that they train their ANN using simulated data derived from models based on the
aforementioned principles. E.g., [10] uses a non-stoichiometric thermodynamic equilibrium
model to simulate reactor data. This is intriguing as it creates the opportunity for our
model to be utilized in a similar manner.
2.2
Data review
Access to data enables the use of black-box or grey-box modeling techniques in mathe-
matical model development. These two concepts cover the use of data to model a given
process, either by using only data or by incorporating the use of data in parts of the
model. For the black or grey-box approach to be successful, the data need to have a level
of quality that allows the dynamics of the process to be discovered. In this section, we
will describe the findings of a literature study on the availability and quality of data in
partial oxidation.
For the data to be applicable to our study, we need experimental data recorded in a tubular
reactor where hydrocarbon fuels are partially oxidized. In addition to those constraints,
the experimental setups should not include catalysts either. The most comprehensible data
set we identified was found in [13]. This article even outlines how, in literature, it is rare to
find quantitative experimental data for the demanding conditions of POX reactions. Given
our literature study, we can only confirm this to be the case. The primary reason for this
is most likely that companies that have the resources to perform large-scale experiments
or fund these experiments would likely keep the results internally.
In the data set found in [13], five different experiments have been made where the feed
stream has been changed. For each of the five experiments, the output stream compositions
were measured for a 200 K/h drop in temperature. In Figure 2.1, the data is plotted for
one of the five experiments.

3.3
Challenges with the Newton solver and possible
solutions
Newton solvers do not always converge. In this section, we will present four challenges we
should be aware of when using a Newton solver. If these four challenges are not addressed
when implementing the solver, a solution will not be found, or it will take an unnecessarily
long time to find one.
3.3.1
Providing a poor initial guess
One common challenge when working with Newton solvers is providing the solver with a
qualified guess of the solution of the next step. If the system is complex to solve and the
guess is poor, the method might not converge to an answer. A common approach is to use
the previous step as the guess for the next step. This approach works well if the timestep
is not too large. However, it does not work for the first step in the Newton algorithm. We
will call this the initial guess, and it needs to be found in another way.
One method to calculate an initial guess, which is highly effective, is to provide the Newton
solver with a steady-state solution as an initial guess. The steady-state solution will cause
the dynamics to stay constant if no parameters are changed. To obtain a steady-state
solution, the solution to the following root problem must be solved for given u and d
"
F(y, u, d)

Using the steady-state solution found by solving (3.22) is also an excellent method to test
an implementation. If no parameters are changed, we know the solution should not change
away from the initial guess. If it does so anyway, an error is present in the implementation.
If it is impossible to solve the root problem in (3.22), the second-best approach is to
solve the algebraic equations, G(w) = 0. Solving these independently of the differential
equations is simpler but does not provide as good an initial guess since it only complies
with the algebraic equations.
3.3.2
Undefined variables due to negativity
Numerous dynamical systems characterize quantities that are either non-negative, limited
to a certain range, or subject to other constraints. For the Newton solver, this can be
problematic as the solver can take a step into an infeasible region of the solution space.
A typical example of a constraint in a system is non-negativity on a set of variables, as
they have a physical association that does not permit negative values. In itself, negative
values do not pose a problem for the Newton solver. Still, some equations of a system
might not be defined for negative input values, leading to undefined calculations that will
return errors.
Later in this thesis, we will use the model we have introduced to model chemical reactions.
More specifically, we will have a quantity of chemical components measured in moles as

6
Adiabatic PFR model
So far, the models we have introduced have been developed to fit the ideal chemical reactor
called the continuous stirred tank reactor (CSTR). This idealized reactor works best for
modeling stationary processes. The partial oxidation (POX) reactor we want to model is
a cylindrical flow reactor. For this reason, modeling it as a stationary process will lose
some of the system dynamics.
To alleviate this loss of dynamics, we will, in this chapter, expand the CSTR model into a
new ideal chemical reactor called the Plug flow reactor (PFR) [24]. In the PFR, chemical
components flow through a cylindrical tube of length L [m] and cross-sectional area Aca
[m2]. For modeling purposes, the tube is divided into M different plugs, where a plug is a
section of the cylindrical tube, as shown in Figure 6.1. The chemical components react in
each plug, and the resulting products then progress into the next plug to react again until
they exit the reactor. The quantity sent along to the next plug is governed by the velocity
v [m/s]. In this way, the effect of chemical components reacting while flowing along in
a tubular reactor is captured. The assumptions of the PFR are no radial differences in
velocity, temperature, pressure, or density.
Figure 6.1: Illustration of the PFR model.
In each of the plugs, the chemical reactions are modeled as a CSTR. In other words, the
PFR model is a chain of CSTR models. This means that we have already described how
we can model the thermodynamic equilibrium in the plugs, as it will be identical to the
adiabatic CSTR model shown in Section 5.3. To complete the PFR model framework,
we must describe the transport between plugs. To do so, we will rewrite the differential
equations used in the previous chapters to describe material and energy transport between
each plug. To rewrite the material and energy balance, we will use partial differential
equations and flux, which we describe in the following two sections.

7.3
Thermodynamic equilibrium model
The thermodynamic equilibrium model will introduce some key differences compared to
previous models. The reason for this is that we will introduce a new optimization problem.
The new optimization problem will allow the selection of an active set, enabling the capa-

reactions, this means that we can add an assumption that a set of components is com-
pletely used up. In the inclusion of solid carbon, we found that applying this additional
constraint to carbon is necessary for the model to run. As we are pursuing scenarios where
carbon is not created, this is an acceptable modification to the thermodynamic equilibrium
model.

8
Stochastic DAE
The models we have introduced in the previous four chapters are deterministic models.
When modeling actual processes in the physical world, they are rarely perfectly deter-
ministic. Many processes in different areas of study behave randomly in small or large
ways. In this chapter, we will introduce a stochastic extension to the DAE model shown
in (3.1). A stochastic term can not only help the model perform better by modeling the
true behavior more closely. A stochastic term can be used as a substitute for modeling
too complex system dynamics. Instead, these complex behaviors are seen as random dis-
turbances we embed into the model. This approach is also associated with how stochastic
terms can be used to deal with incomplete information or lack of suﬀicient knowledge of
the parameters of a given system.
To obtain a stochastic DAE, we add a stochastic term to the differential equations de-
scribing the states, x, of our DAE [28]. This results in the following equations
˙x = F(y(t), u(t), d(t)) + σ(y(t), u(t))dω(t),
t ∈[t0, tf]
(8.1)
G(x(t), y(t), z(t)) = 0,
t ∈[t0, tf]
(8.2)
where ω(t) is a standard Wiener process and σ(y, u) is the standard deviation function
of the state variables. The initial value of the state variables is found using the initial
distribution of the states, which is
x0(t0) ∼N(x0, P0)
(8.3)
where x0 is the expected mean of the state variables and P0 is the covariance matrix.
Unless any knowledge suggests a value of the covariance matrix, it can be initiated as the
identity matrix.

8.2
Measurement uncertainty
Using stochastic differential equations to model the system dynamics is one method of
utilizing stochastic processes in our modeling. Another is to realize that depending on
the application, most sensors used in physical applications do typically not have perfect
accuracy. Manufacturers of sensors typically provide a variance in the measurements of
their equipment. We can use this variance to make our model more robust when working
with data received from measuring an actual reactor.
In our model, we are able to measure the algebraic variables and will denote a measurement
ym(tk), which is a discrete-time measurement, at time tk. We model the uncertainty of
the measurements using the following function
ym(tk) = H(y(tk)) + v(tk)
(8.5)
where H is a function mapping the full set of algebraic variables to the set of measured
algebraic variables. v(tk) is the measurement noise, which follows a normal distribution,
i.e., v(tk) ∼N(0, Tk).
Apart from improving the model compliance for the dynamics of an actual process, a
stochastic DAE also makes it possible to use a probabilistic approach to estimate the
system dynamics. In the next chapter, we will introduce the Extended Kalman filter,
which will enable parameter estimation from measurements with uncertainty.

9
Extended Kalman filter
In this chapter, we will describe the Extended Kalman filter (EKF), an advanced version
of the classic Kalman filter designed to work with nonlinear dynamics [29]. The EKF is
a method used to estimate the system dynamics in a stochastic nonlinear model. It not
only provides an estimate but also offers a measure of confidence in that estimate. The
EKF incorporates data into the estimation at discrete times. In this way, the method can
check if the estimated dynamics comply with the true system that has provided the data.
The EKF is central in using Maximum likelihood estimation to estimate parameters from
measurements with uncertainty. We will introduce the Maximum likelihood method in
the following chapter. The process of running an EKF can be described in three main
steps, two of which are repeated to perform the estimation. The first step is to initialize
the EKF. Next, the states of the current time step k are used to predict the states of the
next time step k + 1. This is called the time update. Lastly, the prediction of the states
at time k + 1 is adjusted using the obtained measurement of time k + 1. These last two
steps are repeated to run the EKF.
The explanation of the EKF introduced below is adapted to work with the stochastic DAE
seen in (8.1) [30], assuming a normal distribution of the initial states, x(t0) ∼N(x0, P0).
We will use a notation in the form: xk|l, which denotes the estimate of x at time k given
information obtained at time l.

11.1
Grid independence test

understand the effects of selecting the resolution of both our temporal and spatial dimen-

At some point, refining the resolution will not provide a relative improvement

In this section, we will investigate what

Extended Kalman filter estimations
In this section, we will show the results of the Extended Kalman filter. We will create
simulated data using our model to compensate for not having any time series data to
feed the EKF. Experimenting with the EKF using simulated data will nonetheless provide
us valuable insights into how the EKF performs depending on the number of measured
variables and the frequency of measurement.
When using the EKF in a real-world setting to estimate parameters or as a prediction
method, we can use sensor specifications to decide on the measurement uncertainty in the
EKF. As we will only be using simulated data, we do not have sensors we can use to decide
the measurement uncertainty. Instead, we have identified applicable industrial sensors and
will use the specifications to decide on the measurement uncertainty. We will use these
to decide both on the noise added to the simulated measurements, and also to select the
expected measurement noise in the EKF. This introduces a minor bias, as we use identical
uncertainties to create the measurements and as the measurement uncertainty in the EKF.
If we had had actual time series data, we would have used the data together with sensor
specifications to estimate the measurement uncertainty. Depending on the data volume
and data quality, such an estimate would measure up to the underlying measurement
uncertainty, and we accept the bias. We will now present the measurement uncertainties
we have decided to apply. For the temperature sensor uncertainty, we will use ±1.5◦C,
which is based on high-temperature sensors found in [36] and [37]. We will work with an
uncertainty of ±3% [38] for the pressure sensor. As we will mainly work with pressure in
the range of one to two bars, the uncertainty, using 1.5 bar, will translate to an uncertainty
of ±0.045 bar. When establishing measuring uncertainty of the different components in
the gas stream, we are faced with a significantly more complex problem. Some gases are
hard to measure, and the uncertainty in measuring them will differ. We will, in this work,
not go into detail on finding a sensor for each of the different components, as we deem it
out of scope. We will use a ±5% given [39] and [40]. Depending on the component, this
will correspond to different tolerance ranges for different simulation cases.
To use the uncertainties of the sensors in the EKF, we must transform the uncertainty
ranges to standard deviations. To do so, we will assume the uncertainty ranges to be
normally distributed. In a normal distribution, 95% of the time observations fall in the
range µ ± 1.96σ. We can find the standard deviation of our sensors as σ = r/1.96, where
r is the uncertainty of the sensor.

Discussion
In this chapter, we will discuss the application of the model and the results we have
obtained using the model. In addition, we will also present possible future work, which
discusses interesting continuations of the work performed in this thesis.
13.1
Model discussion
13.1.1
Strengths of First Principle Model
Overall, the limitations of the data sets provided and uncovered via the literature research
have influenced the work of this thesis. The approach and subsequent results we have
presented in this thesis are technical rather than applied.
The strength of the work,
therefore, lies in the strong foundation of the first principle model we have built. Our
model enables simulations independent of data, and it can also serve as a key component
within a comprehensive model framework that integrates both first principle models and
data-driven approaches. Having a well-formulated first principle model is a great asset,
especially in scenarios where data is sparse. It can act as a mean-value model computing
the best-known estimate without additional information. Extra information can then be
added in the form of data to model additional dynamics not discovered by the mean-model.
Such an application would be what we call grey-box modeling. Had we had better access
to data in this work, we would have been interested in investigating how incorporating
additional information could enhance the model.
An important implication of our work to build an advanced first principle modeling is
also that it can be used in other contexts than just a POX reactor. Partial oxidation is
one among several chemical processes that occur in a tubular reactor. As our model is
built using physical laws of chemical reactions, it is relatively simple to change the model
parameters to model another process in which PFR assumptions apply. Had the model
primarily been built using data-driven methods, more work would have been required to
get to a reliable model for a different process. The ability to quickly adapt the model to
new chemical processes also means that it has a greater value when designing a chemical
plant where many different processes are present.
13.1.2
Instantaneous equilibrium assumption
In our modeling work, one assumption that we have kept in all iterations of our model
is that the reactions find equilibrium instantaneously.
This assumption is accepted in
thermodynamic equilibrium models but is also the reason why they can be rough approxi-
mations for certain processes compared to CFD or kinetic models mentioned in Chapter 2.
It should be noted that the assumption of instantaneous equilibrium is most reliable for
high-temperature conditions in which chemical reaction rates are very high. Also, the
complexity of the chemical reactions plays a role, as more complex reactions can show
Stochastic data-driven grey-box modeling of power-to-X processes
81
==PAGE:91==
behaviors that are not simply captured in the maximization of entropy method of finding
the equilibrium. This is the case for, e.g., reactions including both gasses and solids. In
our work, we took a step towards modeling this complexity by modeling both a solid and
gas phase. However, it is still a simple approximation that does not capture interactions
between the two different phases. Since we simulate conditions where solid carbon for-
mation is assumed to not take place, we have accepted that a simple model is adequate.
If the objective was to observe whether solid carbon is formed in the chemical process, a
CFD or kinetic model would possibly be better suited.
13.1.3
Offline/online simulation
The level of complexity we have captured in our model is a good match for estimating
parameters and performing simulation studies of a POX reactor. This ability can help with
planning the construction of a chemical plant and its subsequent operation, tasks where
highly detailed reaction mechanics are not needed. As these tasks do not need to be solved
in real-time operation, they can be performed as offline simulations, which is what we have
done in our results. For this reason, the computational runtime of the model has not been
an area of concern, and the time to perform a simulation is longer than the time horizon
of the simulation. This fact will prevent the model implementation from being directly
applied in online simulation applications such as online parameter estimation or real-time
process control. If online simulation is desired, the work presented in this thesis is still a
solid foundation from which a model with online simulation capabilities can be developed.
Several means of reducing the computational runtime of the model exist; 1) model simpli-
fication, 2) re-factoring the model code for eﬀiciency, and 3) running the computation on
faster hardware. To obtain a simpler model, it would be possible to select one presented in
this thesis. Using a simpler model will naturally result in less accurate simulations. This
might not be crucial for online simulation, depending on the sampling interval of data
points. A model code re-factoring should investigate the option to use a sparse implemen-
tation of the iteration matrix MR as the number of zero elements gets large for increasing
M. Overall, MATLAB is an eﬀicient programming language in the development phase, at
the cost of runtime eﬀiciency. A refactoring onto a runtime-optimized programming lan-
guage such as Rust/C++ will likely show runtime improvements by several magnitudes.
The computational eﬀiciency of hardware is still on the rise. The computational hardware
used in this thesis is several generations of age in comparison with recent improvements
to computational eﬀiciency, in particular within the field of matrix operations (GPU’s).
13.2
Result discussion
In our work, we only had the possibility to evaluate our model against a single data set
with steady-state conditions. If we had a time series data set, with a selection of chemical
compositions and reactor conditions, we could and would have performed a comprehen-
sive model study to verify if the model reliably replicated the dynamics of a POX reactor
reflected in the data. Only testing the model against a single steady-state data set nat-
urally introduces uncertainty in the verification of the model. Whether this uncertainty
82

==PAGE:92==
will manifest as an unacceptable deviation between the model and the chemical process
can only truly be uncovered by testing the model with additional data. However, as our
model is built using physical laws of chemical reactions, we believe the uncertainty to be
manageable.
The estimation of parameters using our model is an ability we view as important for the
application of the model. In our work related to parameter estimation, we did not manage
to obtain very convincing results. The key learning was that it is a challenging task, and
thorough consideration needs to be applied to obtain convincing results. We believe that
credible results could have been obtained if the simulated data had been produced with
a higher degree of changes in the input parameters. Due to time limitations and long
estimation times, we unfortunately did not manage to peruse this thought. We believe
that more of the dynamics could have been uncovered had the simulated data included
additional changes. Further, adding additional measured variables we believe will make
the process of estimating parameters more manageable. Having the ability to perform
reliable parameter estimation also enables the option to estimate process noise from data
as a replacement for the manual selection we used in our work. Estimation process noise
from data would be a significant improvement to the current non scientific approach.
In the context of parameter estimation, process, as well as measurement noise, influence
the parameter estimation. In our work, we aimed to reflect the most realistic measurement
noise through research on industrial sensors. However, the detailed design of a chemical
reactor is not within our area of expertise, and it is not unlikely that the selected measure-
ment noise is not in line with the industrial baseline for this type of reactor and chemical
processes.
Gaining access to the hardware and software specifications, including mea-
surement uncertainty, of an active industrial reactor could undoubtedly have reduced the
uncertainty about whether or not the derived parameter noise is in line with the industry
baseline.
An additional aspect of the parameter estimation, which is concerning, is that we gave
the estimation favorable conditions by providing measurement in all finite volumes. This
reflects that we provided the estimation process with data in which measurements have
been made at multiple locations in a tubular reactor. It is technologically doable to mea-
sure at multiple positions along a tubular reactor, but from our research of experimental
setups, it seems unlikely. We envision that this class of data sets can realistically only be
produced by the industry and kept for internal use as well.
Finally, we will mention that our model produces pressure calculations that are not com-
pletely accurate due to the fixed gas velocity. Pressure and gas velocity are proportional,
and having the velocity fixed can produce simulations where the pressure is larger at the
exit compared to the entrance. This is not desired as it, in reality, would result in the gas
flowing the opposite way in the reactor. This observation is the primary reason for the
desire to expand with non-constant gas velocity, one extension mentioned in the following
section on future work.

13.3
Future work
In this section, we will present what we view as the logical next step to improve upon the
work presented in this thesis. As discussed in the previous section, an obvious improvement
in the insight into the model fit would be made if a high-quality data set was obtained.
With a high-quality data set, the model could also be extended to a grey-box model where
data could support the modeling of process dynamics which our first principle model is not
able to capture. To achieve a grey-box model, either an ARMAX model or a generalized
linear model [41] could be a candidate. In these models, our first principle model would
become an exogenous input to the model.
If we instead look at what can be done to improve the first principle model itself, we see
the following four as relevant.
1) Expand our model with non-constant gas velocity, as described in Section 6.5.
2) Expand the model with the capability of performing simulations with variable heat
capacities.
This extension would enable the possibility of performing more insightful
spline estimations of the heat capacities on simulated data.
3) In Section 7.3.1, we mention how we select the active set and fix it for a simulation. An
extension of our work is to implement an algorithm that selects the active set automatically.
Such an algorithm is described in [16]. By implementing the algorithm, the active set is
selected in the simulation and can change throughout the simulation as well. This is an
attractive improvement that will improve the model’s ability to reflect which components
are, in fact, not formed. In this way, we do not rely on a choice made before a simulation,
which is the approach used in our work.
4) Extend the EKF to accept measurement with variable sample periods of each variable.
In Section 12.2, we mention how the choice of a 2 second sampling period is conservative
for temperature and pressure sensors but optimistic for gas sensors. The ability to select
different sampling periods for the different variables would remove this problem. Further,
it will most likely be easier to work with obtained high-quality data sets, since they most
likely would reflect our finding of different sampling times for different sensor types.

Conclusion
In this thesis, we presented a differential-algebraic system of equations (DAEs) and demon-
strated how to rewrite a minimization problem into the form of algebraic equations.
Adding the minimization problem introduced advanced constraints to the dynamical sys-
tem model by the DAE. To solve the differential-algebraic equations, we employed a nu-
merical implicit approach, in which the dynamical system is discretized using a backward
Euler method. The discretized system was solved using an inexact Newton method. How
to implement the inexact Newton method was described in detail, and four challenges to
be aware of when using it to solve the discretized system were highlighted.
Utilizing the method of differential-algebraic equations, we build four different models of
a chemical reactor of increasing complexity. The first we described was an isothermal and
isobaric CSTR model. This model introduced the concept of components and elements,
material balance, and thermodynamic equilibrium.
These chemical concepts were the
foundational concepts used to build the more complex models. The second model removed
the assumption of constant temperature and pressure, which we achieved by adding an
internal energy balance to the model.
To ease the implementation of the model, we
derived all derivatives required to solve the model using the Newton solver. In the third
model, we extended the model from a CSTR model to a PFR model, which is a chain of
connected CSTR models. By doing so, we introduced a spatial domain, making our model
dynamics partial differential equations (PDEs).
To solve the PDEs, we used a Finite
volume discretization. To accommodate the new spatial domain, transport processes were
introduced into the model, changing the material and energy balance to model material
and energy flux. In the fourth model, we changed to a two-phase model where components
would either be modeled as solids or gases. To enable the two-phase model, we added
inequality constraints to the algebraic equations and introduced the active set method,
which is able to solve inequality constrained problems.
We expanded the model to a stochastic model, which enabled us to simulate stochastic
dynamics. To validate simulations produced by our model, we compared simulation results
with a data set, which showed conformity. Additionally, we performed dynamic simulations
showing the impact of changing the input concentration of chemical components. These
simulations showed that it is possible to obtain a (H2-CO2)/(CO+CO2)-ratio of 2.1 by
increasing the H2 concentration. Using the model to simulate stochastic data, we also
tested the potential of estimating model parameters. The estimation method we used was
Maximum likelihood estimation, relying on the Extended Kalman Filter to calculate the
likelihood. We found that estimating parameters using our model was a diﬀicult task. The
estimation of one parameter went well, but estimating multiple parameters at the same
time produced unreliable results.
