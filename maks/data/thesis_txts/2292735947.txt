ABSTRACT 
The recent years have seen a considerable increase in the use of concrete-
core activation, or Thermo-Active Building Systems (TABS) in Europe as an 
energy-efficient and economical cooling and heating solution for commercial 
buildings. This concept relies on using the building structure as thermal 
storage, allowing the use of renewable energy sources and providing stable 
thermal conditions indoors.   
However, challenges arise when this thermal system has to be combined 
with acoustic requirements. As a matter of fact, traditional fitting surfaces such 
as wall-to-wall suspended ceiling are inappropriate in this situation.  
This study focuses on quantifying the impact of soffit-hanging discrete 
sound absorbers on the TABS performances, assessed via its cooling capacity 
coefficient. The influence of various ceiling coverage ratios is studied using 
numerical simulations and full-scale measurements. The effect on thermal 
comfort is also assessed. Tests are performed at the scale of a two-persons office 
of ~20 m2. For this purpose, a water system supplying the active decks of the 
test facility with water at a given flow and temperature has been designed and 
implemented.  
It is shown that covering 60% of the ceiling surface with sound absorbers 
hanging at 300mm from the active deck is expected to reduce the cooling 
capacity coefficient of this latter by 16%. This drops to 25% when 80% of the 
ceiling is covered. The presence of acoustic panels also affects the thermal 
comfort in the room: the operative temperature increased by 0.9°C in the 
former case and up to 1.6°C in the latter. The results also show that comfort 
ventilation supplied to the enclosure has a considerable impact on shaving the 
peaks in temperature increase, indeed causing an operative temperature 
increase twice as big.  
 
Keywords: Thermo-active building systems; Concrete core cooling; 
Thermal comfort; Acoustic comfort; Cooling capacity; Cooling capacity 
coefficient; Sound absorbers; Free-hanging acoustic elements; TRNSYS Type.

1 INTRODUCTION 
The fundamental function of buildings is to provide a safe and healthy 
haven. When well designed and operated, buildings can truly make a 
significant difference on the daily life of their occupants; be it to help them 
learn better, be more productive at work, recover faster or simply to make 
them feel better in this place called “home”.  
At the same time, buildings are today the heaviest consumers of natural 
resources, and their influence on the environment cannot be overlooked 
anymore. In Europe, buildings represent more than 40% of the total energy 
consumption (EUR-Lex, 2002 and EEA, 2011) and approximately 25% of the 
greenhouse gas emissions (EEA, 2011). Therefore, finding innovative solutions 
for energy-efficient comfortable buildings is today’s main challenge in the 
building sector.  
	  
For this, state incentives are made both at national and European levels to 
reduce buildings’ environmental footprint. In Denmark, the Building 
Regulation 2010 targets a reduction of 75% of the energy used in buildings by 
year 2020 (Danish Ministry of Economic and Business Affairs, 2010). The 
European Commission targets “a reduction of at least 20% in greenhouse gases 
by 2020”, as well as “a 20% share of renewable energies in EU energy 
consumption by 2020” (COM, 2008). In practice, all new buildings shall be, by 
the end of 2020, nearly zero-energy buildings. Of course this transition will not 
happen without considerable efforts and innovations in the sector, and this is 
the frame in which the present work falls within.  
	  
Today, Thermo-Active Building Systems (TABS) are of special importance 
as they embody a promising solution for energy-efficient heating and cooling of 
buildings. Multiple case studies and research works emphasize that TABS offer 
strong potential for energy savings in commercial buildings (Kalz et. al, 2006; 
Lehmann et al., 2007; Kalz et al., 2014).  
Using the thermal mass of the building construction to ensure a 
comfortable indoor environment to the occupants, this widespread solution also 
features the disadvantage of requiring large uncovered hard surfaces indoors 
(typically concrete floor and ceiling). Consequently, acoustic comfort is often 
jeopardized in such an environment deprived of sufficient sound absorbing area.  
Hanging ceiling absorbers represent a viable solution to the acoustic issue, 
but their presence will interfere with the thermal performances of the active 
deck system. This influence has overall only been studied in a limited amount

2 BACKGROUND 
2.1 
Indoor environment 
Today, we typically spend around 90% of our time indoors. It is therefore 
crucial to design buildings providing high levels of comfort to their occupants. 
The perception of the indoor environment by an occupant is influenced by four 
basic factors, directly influencing comfort but also health and performances 
(both mental and physical).  
• 
Thermal comfort – embracing parameters such as temperature, 
air velocity, and moisture; 
• 
Indoor air quality – covering indoor air pollution, odour, fresh air 
supply; 
• 
Lighting quality – determined among others by the occupant’s 
view, illuminance and luminance levels, homogeneity; 
• 
Acoustic quality – influenced by the surrounding noise levels, 
propagation and reverberation of the sound waves, vibrations 
(Bluyssen, 2009). 
 
The present work will mostly focus on thermal comfort.  
2.1.1 
Thermal comfort indoors 
Variables influencing thermal comfort 
From a general point of view, thermal environment embraces parameters of 
the indoor environment having an influence on the heat balance of the human 
body. Among them are the following: 
• 
Air temperature [°C] 
• 
Mean radiant temperature [°C] 
• 
Relative air velocity [m/s] 
• 
Relative humidity [%] 
• 
Activity level (heat production of the body) [met] 
• 
Thermal resistance of the clothing [clo] 
Occupant thermal satisfaction 
Based on Fanger’s comfort equation (1970), and the standard ISO 7730 
that followed based on it, the predicted general thermal sensation and degree of 
discomfort of people exposed to an environment can be estimated, for 
sedentary office work and different types of clothing.  
This prediction is mainly based on two variables, namely PMV and PPD. 
==PAGE:16==

4 
• 
The PMV (Predicted Mean Vote) predicts the mean value of the 
thermal votes for a large group of people subjected to the same 
environmental conditions, but it does not predict how many people 
are going to feel uncomfortable (PPD). 
• 
The PPD (Predicted Percentage Dissatisfied) determines how many 
occupants will fall outside the limits of comfort, and thus 
determines how many people are thermally dissatisfied by feeling 
too cold or too warm.  
 
 
Figure 2.1 - Predicted percentage of dissatisfied (PPD) for a range of room 
temperatures (operative temperatures) and different clothing (sedentary 
activity). (Lehmann et al., 2007) 
Based on this figure, it can be seen that a total acceptance for a given room 
climate (i.e. 0% of dissatisfaction) cannot be reached. This is because each 
individual’s perception of temperature is different; and therefore each has a 
different conception of thermal satisfaction. Fanger (1970) shows that a PPD of 
5% corresponds to the optimal comfort condition.  
Therefore, comfort categories (A, B and C) are specified based on a 
maximum permissible rate of dissatisfaction (in practice: 6, 10 and 15% 
according to ISO 7730). Each comfort category implies a respective operative 
temperature range for comfort: the higher the category, the more restrictive the 
range. Standards and reports also specify in which ranges comfort indices such 
as PPD and PMV are able to fluctuate for each comfort category; as well as

Comfort ranges 
The recommended ranges suggest that thermal comfort indoor does not rely 
on a constant internal temperature, but rather on an acceptability range 
among which fluctuations are possible while keeping a satisfying comfort level.  
 
This is especially true in the case of buildings equipped with TABS, where 
as it will be described later, the indoor temperature does not remain constant 
over a day, but rather fluctuates within a certain range within the comfort 
zone. It is therefore crucial to understand the permissible temperature ranges 
for the design of such systems. The aim of a good TABS design is to maintain 
occupied space comfort conditions within the ranges specified by the standards 
during occupancy.  
2.2 
Theory of heat transfer 
2.2.1 
Heat transfer typology 
Heat exchange between two bodies at different temperatures or one body with 
parts at different temperatures can be achieved by means of three main 
physical actors, namely conduction, convection and radiation. Each of these 
modes of heat transfer plays a role in the modulation of thermal comfort, but 
==PAGE:18==
2 – BACKGROUND 
 
 
6 
not equally in the context of bodies and buildings. They will briefly be 
described in the following paragraphs.  
2.2.2 
Conduction 
Conduction consists of a microscopic transfer of internal energy within a 
body (or two bodies in contact) due to a temperature gradient. A bare foot on 
a cold floor is an example. This transfer is achieved by the diffusion of 
molecules, governed by Fourier’s law.  
Conduction is influenced by the material thermal properties: the thermal 
conductivity (usually noted 𝜆) characterizes the ability of a material to transfer 
heat energy throughout its body. Its value can vary considerably from one 
material to the other. For instance, aluminum conducts heat approximately 
1000 times better than gypsum boards such as the one used in the design of 
thermal applications; themselves 10 times more conductive than air.  
One-dimensional conductive heat transfer through a material can be 
assessed by (Cengel and Ghajar, 2014):

Typically, in a TABS scenario, heat conduction will be observed between 
the pipes embedded in the structure and the surfaces at stake. But while 
conduction heat transfer between a body and a building can be significant 
when it occurs, it is not as common as the other two modes of heat transfer. 
2.2.3 
Convection 
Convection consists of the transfer of heat from one place to another, by 
movements of fluids. The heat transfer occurs when a tempered medium heats 
or cools the adjacent fluid by conduction. The fluid will then initiate movement 
due to buoyancy forces will then be replaced by new fluid and the movement 
can repeat. In a building, convection is the movement of air molecules coming 
in contact with objects in the space. The convectional heat transfer can be 
increased in the case where an external source forces the fluid in motion – this

2.2.4 
Radiation 
Radiation relates to the heat transfer between the surfaces of two different 
bodies at different temperatures. Energy is transferred through electromagnetic 
waves (in the range of 0.8-400 um) from warmer objects to cooler ones. As 
opposed to conduction and convection, radiation heat transfer is not depending 
on an intervening medium; and is thus not influenced by orientation nor the air 
movements by the surface (Cengel and Ghajar, 2014). For bodies and surfaces 
to exchange heat energy by radiation, they do not necessarily need to be in 
contact, but simply in sight of one another.

depending on the type of metal and its surface condition (matt or polished).

In a situation where the source surface is exposed to multiple surfaces with 
different respective temperatures, radiation heat transfer depends on the 
orientation of each surface as well as their physical radiative properties. In the 
case of a typical rectangular office room for instance, the radiation from one 
surface depends on the room dimensions and the respective temperatures of the 
five exposed surfaces. This geometry dependence is expressed as a view factor, 
where the sum of the view factors of all facing surfaces should be one. View 
factor between one surface and another facing one can be expressed 
theoretically as described by Cengel and Ghajar (2014); but in practice for 
building applications one would refer to experimentally-established diagrams 
such as the extensive work of Fanger (1970).

To continue with the example situation of a TABS building, the long-wave 
radiation (non-visible light) from the active surface(s) - but also short-wave

2.2.5 
Heat exchange in TABS 
Influence of active surfaces 
Considering a typical TABS multi-storey building scenario, a given room 
will feature two active decks: floor and ceiling. According to Koschenz and 
Lehmann (2000), the larger part of the heat transfer will be through the ceiling 
active surface. The distribution has been found to be 2/3 through the ceiling 
and 1/3 through the floor. Another study (Hansen et al., 2002) concludes that 
85% of the heat transfer will be realized through the ceiling surface for cooling 
application. In reality, the actual distribution depends also greatly on the 
actual configuration of the deck construction with respect to floor covering and 
acoustic solutions on the soffit.

3.1 
Classification of building thermal mass activation 
concepts 
Based on the thermal storage capacity of their inherent mass, buildings can 
be classified in two primary types: lightweight thermal mass and heavyweight 
thermal mass (Price et al., 1998).  
Very responsive to any variation of thermal load applied, lightweight 
buildings react fast to changes, both in the outdoor environment, such as solar 
radiation and temperature but also in the indoor thermal living loads (Pavlov, 
2014). Heavyweight buildings on the other hand have a much slower 
evolution. Their indoor environmental conditions will react slowly to changes 
in the thermal load they are subject to. 
 
The present study will focus on this latter category of heavyweight 
buildings, or buildings with thermal mass activation. 
Among this concept, two main categories can here also be identified 
(Pavlov, 2014).  
• 
Passive thermal mass concepts, or surface thermal mass activation; 
• 
Active thermal mass concepts, or core thermal mass activation, or 
Thermo-Active Building Systems (TABS). 
If the passive thermal mass concept only relies on the capacity of the heavy 
thermal mass construction to absorb heat gains during the day and releasing it 
at night, building systems activating this thermal mass also exist and will be 
the main topic of this work.  
3.2 
The concept of Thermo-Active Building Systems  
Thermo-Active Building Systems (TABS) are a form of radiant zone 
thermal conditioning systems that exploit the thermal inertia of a structure as 
a medium to heat or cool the building. This is achieved by activating the 
building thermal storage capacity, often using a hydronic system with water-
carrying pipes embedded in the construction. Using water as heat carrier allows 
taking advantage of its high thermal capacity to minimize the temperature rise 
of the cooling medium required for conditioning a space. The water 
temperature can then be adjusted in order to heat or cool the surface, and by 
mean of consequence, the adjacent room by heat transfer (convective and 
radiative).  
==PAGE:24==
3 – TABS – THERMO-ACTIVE BUILDING SYSTEMS 
 
 
12 
 
Besides water-based systems, airborne systems were also developed, like 
TermoDeck in Sweden (Smith and Raw, 1999); but these solutions tend to be 
less common nowadays. The present work will focus on hydronic systems, since 
this concept is the most spread-out today. The broad range of TABS differs in 
dimension and spacing of the pipes, placement of the thermally-activated layer 
(surface-near or core), activated building component (ceiling, floor, wall) and 
implementation (Kalz and Pfafferott, 2014). 
 
By absorbing the heat from internal loads during the day and discharging it 
at a later time by a natural heat sink (such as ambient air, ground water or 
soil), TABS are today a key technology to reduce the primary energy demand 
for office buildings without jeopardizing thermal comfort (Kalz et al., 2006).  
3.3 
History of TABS 
Historically, this approach to radiant heating/cooling of buildings with 
pipes embedded in concrete slabs dates back in the beginning of the twentieth 
century, in Switzerland. In 1937, a system called “Crittall” consisting of 
embedded steel pipes in a concrete slab was implemented (Deecke, Günther 
and Olesen, 2003).  
From this point, this type of system has then grown and evolved in 
technology, efficiency and sustainability, now using PEX pipes for instance. 
Growing in use in commercial buildings since the 1990s, first in Central Europe 
(Germany, Austria, Netherlands, etc.), TABS are now spreading globally. As a 
matter of fact, modern buildings require effective building systems, and TABS 
represent a high-potential solution by cooling the slab, thus allowing the room 
to be cooled by the ceiling and floor. TABS are mostly used in multi-storey 
buildings, since the thermal exchange occurs both upwards and downwards (De 
Carli, 2002).  
3.4 
Dynamic principles of TABS 
Thermo-active building systems rely on the concept of a dynamic 
evolution, where the building temperature increases over the day as heat 
gains are accumulated in the active structure. This shifting should remain in a 
permissible domain to ensure comfort to the occupants.  
Figure 3.1, taken from Lehmann et al. (2007), illustrates a typical diurnal 
temperature cycle for a TABS building.

Figure 3.1 - Typical diurnal temperature cycle for TABS buildings

A minimum room temperature of 21°C is assumed for the morning point 
(1). Over the course of the day, the internal heat gains produced by the 
occupants, equipment and solar radiation will bring the air and room 
temperature up to approximately 26°C (1-3). The greater part of this 
temperature rise is due to the convective fraction of the heat sources (Lehmann 
et al., 2007). As soon as the heat gains are removed, this convective component 
disappears (3-4).  
As the building thermal mass constantly absorbs the heat sources energy, it 
undergoes a gradual temperature rise (1-4). For the system to function 
properly, it is necessary to bring down the activated surface temperature down 
to the initial starting point; i.e. to discharge the heat store. Otherwise, a higher 
morning point might, depending on the magnitude of the daily heat gains, lead 
to the room’s temperature comfort range to be exceeded.  
This recooling process (4-5), where the heat is rejected from the building 
structure, is typically achieved by the mean of heat exchange with plastic 
water pipes embedded in the slab core carrying cool water. If available in the 
building, and if the outdoor conditions allow it, free night cooling can also be 
taken advantage of, using the cool outdoor air to naturally ventilate the space.  
3.5 
Advantages of TABS 
Since the active slabs have the opportunity to store heat and transfer 
cooling needs to a different time with respect to thermal loads, TABS present 
various benefits.

heating/cooling effect, but, due to the thermal mass, the peak cooling power 
will be reduced and some of the cooling load transferred to outside the time of 
occupancy (typically during night-time, in office premises) (Olesen, 2012).

Energy reduction 
Shifting cooling loads to periods of low-occupancy also enables a reduction 
in energy costs using the lower nocturnal electricity rate to operate pumps in

Additionally, lowering the peak loads by distributing the cooling needs over 
a longer period allows the building to use conditioning plants of reduced sizes. 
According to Babiak et al. (2009), heat sources / sinks (chillers or heat pumps)

Besides, various studies have shown that an intelligent operation of TABS

operation time of the system. In practice, there is no need to operate the 
system continuously to ensure comfort. According to a study from Uponor 
Corporation featured in Babiak et al. (2009), operation cycles of TABS of 8h, 
with pumps on for 15min and off for the remaining 45min have led to similar 
results in terms of thermal comfort as a system with a 24h-round activation. In 
both cases, no comfort limit was exceeded. This is corroborated by results from 
Olesen (2000), showing that similar comfort results could be expected from a 
TABS functioning continuously or only when the building is unoccupied.  
3.5.3 
High temperature cooling / low temperature heating 
Due to the large thermally active surfaces at stake, substantial heat flows 
between the structure and the room can be achieved, even with modest 
temperature differences between the space and the heat-carrier fluid. Using 
heating/cooling systems with temperatures close to the room temperature 
increases the energy efficiency of the various systems used to adjust the fluid 
temperature (heat pumps, condensation boilers, solar collectors and ground 
heat exchangers for example) (Babiak et al., 2009).  
This contrasts with the high temperature heating and low temperature 
cooling paradigm of typical HVAC systems. In systems based on radiant 
transfer, the focus is on the modulation of the surface temperature. This offsets 
the air temperature to achieve a comfortable operative temperature. Whereas 
in an air-based heating mode, the air offsets low surfaces temperatures.  
This also makes possible the use of various different heating or cooling 
sources, and is therefore a special incentive to use renewable energy such as 
geothermal energy, groundwater or outside air with night ventilation 
(Meierhans 1993, Koschenz and Dorer 1999, Lehmann et al. 2007).  
 
Practically, cooling can be realized with high supply-water temperature 
between 16 and 22°C. Under steady-state conditions, cooling rates of 30-40 
W/m2 can be achieved. Near-surface systems can reach cooling rates of up to 
70 W/m2 under appropriate operating conditions (Babiak et al., 2009).  
3.5.4 
Substitution to full air conditioning systems 
Another advantage of TABS is to be found in partly substituting for a full 
air conditioning system.  
As a matter of fact, mechanical ventilation is an important share of energy 
consumption in multi-storey commercial buildings. Studies have shown that 
refrigeration and air conditioning account for approximately 15% of the total 
energy consumption worldwide (Confederation of International Contractors’ 
Associations, 2002). Its use should therefore be reduced as much as possible in 
==PAGE:28==

16 
order to cut on the building energy needs. In the case of TABS buildings, since 
the thermal conditioning of the rooms is made by means of hydraulic exchange, 
the air conditioning system can be downsized to provide only comfort 
ventilation to the occupied space. This is done based on standard 
requirements, such as EN 15251 or the report CR 1752; in order to provide 
rooms with a minimum amount of fresh outdoor air, ensure that the CO2 levels 
in the enclosure remain in an acceptable range, but also to remove latent heat 
from the occupants.  
3.5.5 
Cost-effectiveness 
Eventually, Babiak et al. (2009) emphasize that this system also presents 
the advantage of allowing low installation, operation and maintenance costs.  
3.6 
Precautions 
3.6.1 
Condensation on surfaces 
Condensation has been a major concern in the design of radiant cooling 
systems. In a real case, higher cooling capacities than the ones stated in the 
previous paragraph are limited by the dew point of the room air temperature, 
as condensation would otherwise occur on the active surfaces. Therefore, the 
temperature has to be carefully monitored in order to keep the slab surface 
temperature above the dew point. For a room air temperature of 26°C and a 
relative humidity of 50%, the dew point temperature is 15°C. However in 
practice, the thermally active surface temperature does not need to be 
anywhere near the dew point temperature. If the temperature of the ceiling is 
below the body temperature, then it is absorbing energy from the body and 
thus cooling it.  
3.6.2 
Soffit drilling 
In buildings with thermal activation of the slab, the presence of water pipes 
close to the surface of the concrete might interfere with the soffit fixing for the 
suspension of elements such as ceiling panels or luminaires for example. 
Notably, it has been identified as a constraint in the implementation of 
suspended acoustic treatments. Drilling a hole could lead to the perforation 
of the pipes and thereby to leakage. A special care should therefore be put at 
this stage, and several strategies can be used.  
A layout of the piping could be designed in such a way that a safe zone is 
left for drilling; potentially combined with the use of an infrared camera to 
localize precisely the water pipes in the structure. Alternatively, one can think 
==PAGE:29==
3 – TABS – THERMO-ACTIVE BUILDING SYSTEMS 
 
17 
of casting fixing profiles at the surface of the concrete in order to avoid having 
to drill into the construction itself. At the design stage, it can be decided to 
place the pipes in the slab at an increased distance from the surface. 
Eventually, selecting shallow or short fixings can help minimizing the risk.

4 ACOUSTIC ISSUE 
How to combine both high acoustic and thermal comfort in TABS 
buildings? 
4.1 
Acoustics in TABS buildings 
In order to be as efficient as possible, the thermal principle of concrete core 
cooling typically requires the active slabs to be as bare as possible for a 
better heat transfer with the room. But in practice a large surface of bare 
exposed concrete will reflect sound instead of absorbing it, acting as a sound 
mirror. (Le Muet et al., 2013). This can lead to a degradation of the room 
acoustic quality parameters, and consequently to a decreased acoustic 
comfort. The most used of these parameters being Reverberation Time, but it 
could also be Speech Clarity where such parameter is relevant, or Sound 
propagation in large offices. Concentration levels are affected, and it can 
become difficult to communicate without disturbing the other occupants.  
 
Therefore, challenges arise when TABS-equipped buildings are combined 
with requirements for good acoustics. The traditional solution of a fully 
covering, Absorption class A, acoustic ceiling is no longer an option (Ecophon, 
2014). The wall to wall, fully covering, ceiling would of course block the 
radiation (as well as the convection) from the concrete ceiling surface to the 
room, acting as a shield to the heat transfer. The same also applies in the 
opposite direction, with ceiling panels preventing hot air from heat sources to 
reach the soffit.  
 
In the literature, this issue is often put aside, specifying that buildings with 
thermally active buildings systems may require “special solutions for the 
acoustics” (Olesen, 2012), but without any guideline on how to actually achieve 
a specialized design solution. 
4.2 
The case of open-plan offices 
Typically, open-plan offices can cover an entire floor, including both 
individual desks and group areas. As a consequence, this workplace will 
accommodate both people working in teams and talking a lot to each other, 
but also single occupants in need of a quiet environment to fully concentrate on 
their tasks.  
If this flexibility in use can in theory lead to increase in productivity, 
research has often reported lower satisfaction, lower productivity, and poorer 
==PAGE:32==
4 – ACOUSTIC ISSUE 
 
 
20 
health among the employees working in open-plan offices compared to 
traditional offices (Navai & Vetch, 2003; Jahncke et al., 2011). Productivity is 
considerably impacted and degraded if not enough attention is put on the 
design of the acoustic environment, as the background noise increases, ensuing 
distraction (Jahncke et al., 2011). Banbury & Berry (1998) reported a 
productivity reduction of 67% for test subjects working in noisy office 
spaces.  
 
It is therefore important to stop the speech levels from escalading and to 
keep the sound from spreading in every direction, in order to minimize the 
disturbance of co-workers. This will ensure a comfortable acoustic environment 
to the occupants, which are then more likely to work better, as the physical 
environment affects their job perception, attitudes and job satisfaction (Lee & 
Brand, 2005).  
4.3 
Solutions for acoustics 
The basic principle for achieving good room acoustics is to introduce a 
sufficient amount of sound absorbing material in the room. The absorption 
area is an expression of the total absorption of the room. It is determined by 
multiplying the sound absorbing surface areas with their respective absorption 
coefficient; and summing the results for all surfaces (Jacobsen et al., 2009). The 
general rule of thumb is: the larger the equivalent absorption area, the less 
noisy the room.  
The acoustic design of office buildings is defined by some standards. Most 
of them are today performance-based (based on the research performed in 
Denmark, Sweden and France), but some prescriptive requirements still exist. 
Standard ISO 11690 (1996) gives recommendations for the acoustic design of 
low-noise workplaces; and recommends a minimum absorbing area higher than 
0.6 to 0.9 times the floor area. In Denmark, the Danish Working Environment 
Authority provides regulation about the acoustic conditions in offices. For an 
office used by more than one occupant and which volume is below 300 m3, the 
agency requires a minimum average value of the equivalent absorption area of 
at least 0.8 times the floor area. The Danish Building Regulations (2010) does 
not specify anymore any requirement for office buildings, considering that 
“project-specific noise provisions should be determined in each individual case 
to comply with the requirements for the acoustic indoor climate.” It does 
however state that, for open-plan teaching areas of educational buildings, the 
absorption area should be higher than 1.3 times the floor area. In the 
previous version (2008), recommendations for office buildings were 1.1 times 
the floor area. The German standard for acoustic quality in small to medium-
==PAGE:33==
4 – ACOUSTIC ISSUE 
 
21 
sized rooms, DIN 18041, gives guideline figures for the absorption area needed 
to fulfil basic acoustic requirements. For an open-plan office space, an 
equivalent absorption area of 90-100% of the floor space is recommended.  
But these requirements (or recommendations) are only minima, and are far 
from providing high acoustic comfort. In reality, according to a questionnaire 
investigation carried by C.M. Petersen in Danish open-plan offices (2008), even 
in offices meeting the above-mentioned requirements, employees report a 
certain dissatisfaction with the general acoustic conditions.  
 
If vertical acoustic panels can provide an efficient solution in some cases, 
they are not sufficient in a typical open-plan office situation, where the vertical 
walls to ceiling area ratio is very small. In this kind of offices, the ceiling is the 
most sound-reflective area.  
Therefore the limitation of a solution relying only on wall sound absorbers 
very quickly arises, since the usable vertical walls area (removing windows, 
doors and other surfaces not suitable for vertical panelling) only represents 
typically 40 to 50% of the ceiling area in open-plan premises. Therefore, 
ceiling acoustic absorbers will still be necessary to ensure a sufficient 
acoustic quality to the building occupants.  
 
One solution can be found in using hanging sound absorbers. In terms 
of acoustics, these units are very efficient since both sides of the sound 
absorbing panels are put to use. As sound is distributed around the panels, a 
larger area of the absorber is exposed to the sound field. Even if, from a strict 
acoustical point of view, these units will not be as efficient solutions as a 
complete wall-to-wall ceiling, they embody a promising solution for buildings 
with thermally active surfaces. The difference in performances between the two 
solutions is especially emphasized at low frequencies. This is why wall 
absorbers are recommended in addition of soffit-hanging sound absorbers, to 
improve the absorption quality in this range of frequencies.

advantage of enabling convective air movements both between their upper 
surface and the soffit, as well as between this layer and the room. They also 
allow some radiation from the soffit to reach the room, and hot air from the 
room to reach the soffit. This is of course in the case where the sound 
absorbers do not cover the entire ceiling surface.

Even if the radiation from the active deck to the room is partly blocked by 
the panels, this radiation will still cool down the upper face of the panels. By 
this way, the presence of the cooled surfaces of the panels will stimulate the 
convective heat transfer. But for the cooling system to function effectively, it is 
also important that the hot air arising from the room has access to the cooled 
deck. Once the TABS have cooled down this air sufficiently, it will naturally 
drop down back into the occupied zone. 
  
4.4 
Formulation of the problem 
The problem arising is therefore to understand the influence that the ceiling 
coverage ratio of sound absorbers will have on the thermal performances of 
TABS. What is the effect of various ceiling coverage ratios on the cooling 
capacity of the system? And, by means of consequence, what is their influence 
on the thermal comfort conditions in the room? 
 
The challenge is thus to combine thermal requirements with good room 
acoustics. This is a critical issue to tackle, not only in the case of TABS office 
buildings; but maybe even more as TABS expand to other building segments, 
such as education.

5 LITERATURE REVIEW 
A bibliographical literature review has been carried out in order to gather 
knowledge about the state of the art in the influence of the presence of discrete 
suspended panels (in opposition to a wall to wall, fully-covering ceiling) on the 
cooling performances of active ceilings.  
5.1 
Numerical studies 
S. Morey et al. (2010) conducted a computational fluid dynamics (CFD) 
study focusing on the perimeter gap between suspended ceilings and a room’s 
vertical walls. The aim was to understand the influence it would have on the 
air patterns in the room. The reason for this investigation was the will of 
architects or designers to access the building thermal mass, by leaving free part 
of the ceiling at the perimeter of the room.  
Morey et al. conclude that increasing the perimeter width results in a linear 
increase in the mean tangential speed of airflow across the underside of the 
ceiling. And the higher this air speed, the better the heat transfers between the 
ceiling and the room air.  
 
Continuing on these conclusions, T. Kershaw (2012) developed a further 
study based on CFD simulations to complete the work of S. Morey et al. Using 
similar methods and assumptions, Kershaw though focuses on rafts (large 
continuous acoustic horizontal panels) designs in office spaces. As Morey et al., 
he concludes that acoustic rafts allow convective heat transfer from the room 
to access the thermal mass above suspended ceilings. Moreover, his study also 
underlines the importance of the rafts orientation. In practice, Kershaw finds 
that, when possible, rafts should be orientated along the short axis of the room 
in order to enable convection currents. Finally, his conclusion is also that 
rafts should ideally be orientated so that they span the office in the direction 
perpendicular to the ventilation source. 
These conclusions provide extensive knowledge for suspended ceiling 
professionals as well as designers in general, for the implementation of effective 
ceiling solutions in TABS buildings.  
5.2 
Experimental studies 
P. Weitzmann, in his PhD dissertation (2004), focuses on the cooling 
capacity of active concrete decks with embedded water pipes (placed either 
under or in the decks hollow cores). He carried measurements in a TABS-
equipped test room simulating an office building. 
==PAGE:38==
5 – LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
 
 
26 
Based on his measurement series, Weitzmann concludes that the cooling 
capacity from the deck is proportional to the temperature difference between 
the fluid temperature and the room temperature. Therefore, the cooling 
capacity coefficient (Ucc, W.m-2.K-1) is a constant value for a given setup. This 
parameter will be further detailed in the next paragraph. This will set the 
ground for many further studies, using as a comparative tool the cooling 
capacity coefficient of TABS to evaluate the influence of external 
elements on the system’s efficiency.  
In the conducted measurements (without any suspended ceiling in the 
room), the cooling capacity coefficient of the ceiling surface has been found to 
be 5.4 W.m-2.K-1; and around 1.0 W.m-2.K-1 for the floor surface.  
 
Weitzmann also supervised the work of E. Pittarello’s MSc thesis (2006) on 
the impact of suspended ceiling panels on the acoustic and thermal 
performances in a TABS chamber. The test facility used was the same as the 
one used in the present study (at least for the geometric parameters). Pittarello 
used horizontal wood panels to simulate the presence of a suspended ceiling. 
Measurements were also performed using Rockfon acoustic panels, placed both 
horizontally and vertically (baffles). Different coverage ratios (from 0 to 100%) 
and dispositions were investigated.  
For the test setup with wood panels simulating a suspended ceiling, 
measurements have been done for coverage ratios of 0, 47, 67 (2 layouts), 83 
and 100%. As expected, Pittarello observes a reduction of the cooling 
capacity coefficient with the increase of ceiling coverage. The decrease 
ranges from 5.5 W.m-2.K-1 for a bare ceiling to ~2.5 W.m-2.K-1 for a fully 
covered surface. A 67% coverage ratio leads here to a reduction of ~29% of the 
cooling capacity coefficient. The cooling capacity of the free ceiling is here in 
accordance with the result obtained by Weitzmann in the same chamber.  
 
The test setup with Rockfon acoustic panels compared ceiling layouts 
covered with 0, 35 and 70% of ceiling panels. Here, an absolute cooling 
capacity reduction of 12% is noticed for 35% coverage, and 22% for 70% 
coverage.  
This test also led to conclusions regarding thermal comfort. Pittarello 
highlights a noticeable increase in the room operative temperature of 1°C and 
2°C when the ceiling coverage ratio increases respectively from 0 to 35% and 
70%.  
Regarding the geometry of the ceiling covering, Pittarello does not conclude 
on a significant influence of the panels layout on the heat output from the 
TABS. Nevertheless, for a same coverage ratio, the layout with stripes of 
panels distributed over the ceiling surface featured a more homogeneous 
==PAGE:39==
5 – LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
 
27 
temperature repartition in the room than the layout with panels clustered at 
the centre.  
 
B. Árnason studied in his MSc thesis (2011) the effectiveness of the cooling 
from TABS for different designs of suspended ceilings, with varying coverage 
ratios.  
In a test chamber equipped with a TABS ceiling, he evaluates the thermal 
and acoustic performances of different suspended ceiling designs, with regard of 
various coverage ratios. The coverage is here rather high (100%, 92%, 91%, 
90% and 87%) as it was a requirement from the architect team of the office 
building in the design phase that he was studying.  
Árnason highlights a linear decrease in the TABS cooling capacity 
coefficient with the increase of the suspended ceiling coverage, from ~4.9 W.m-
2.K-1 for a 87% coverage down to 2.8 W.m-2.K-1 for a fully covered ceiling. 
Coverage ratios of 90, 91 and 92% lead respectively to coefficients of 4.7, 4.5 
and 4.3 W.m-2.K-1.  
 
When comparing these results with Weitzmann’s and Pittarello’s, it 
appears that the values measured by Árnason were significantly higher than 
those of previous studies for similar and even higher ceiling coverage ratios. 
Nevertheless, his setup was differing from the previous both in room height and 
distance between the suspended ceiling and the soffit.  
 
Importantly enough, Árnason also concludes on the significant effect of the 
water mass flow in pipes on the TABS cooling capacity. Using two different 
flows (0.05 kg/s and 0.1 kg/s), he measured cooling capacities approximately 
6% lower with a low flow than with the high flow. Based on this observation, it 
will be important in the present study to keep a constant mass flow in the 
system, in order to be able to put into comparison the effect of the suspended 
ceiling layouts only. When it comes to a building in use, the flow used for the 
TABS operation would also have an influence on the pump’s energy use, which 
is also of important consideration.  
 
In 2009, H. Peperkamp and M. Vercammen quantified a reduction in 
cooling capacity of 20 to 25% for a ceiling covered with 50% of sound 
absorbers; and down to -40% for a coverage ratio of 85%. Overall, they 
conclude that the reduction of the radiative part of the thermal capacity is less 
than the percentage of coverage ratio of the ceiling.  
They also conclude that the size of the air gap between the soffit and 
the upper surface of the suspended ceiling does not seem to reduce 
significantly the TABS cooling capacity.  
==PAGE:40==
5 – LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
 
 
28 
 
S. Ruud (2008) measured the impact of sound absorbers on the heat 
exchange of a chilled ceiling consisting of radiant panels. Testing various 
coverage ratios and acoustic absorber types (horizontal panels and vertical 
baffles), the author concludes on a 20% reduction in cooling capacity when the 
ceiling coverage ratio increases from 0 to 60%.  
 
In 2013, the consultant group Peutz conducted a series of test in climatic 
chamber to determine the reduction in cooling power of TABS induced by the 
presence of acoustic suspended panels (Peperkamp, 2013). Measurements have 
been performed according to the procedure of the standard EN 14240 (2004).  
With an effective coverage of approximately 58%, achieved by panels 
evenly distributed over the ceiling surface, the experiment concludes on a 
reduction of the heat output delivery of approximately 33%.  
Interestingly enough, this reduction increases to 38% in the case of panels 
clustered altogether at the centre of the room, with the same coverage ratio. 
This shows that the coverage pattern actually has an influence on the heat 
output expectable from the system. This corroborates the results from Morey 
et al. (2010) and Kershaw (2012) previously described. Based on this, it has 
been decided to spread panels evenly over the ceiling for the measurements 
performed in the present study.  
 
All the above-mentioned studies have been carried out for a cooling 
situation in a TABS building. Regarding a heating situation, an assessment 
of the influence of suspended ceiling on the heating capacity of TABS has been 
performed by M. Álvarez (2013). The author concludes on a comparable effect 
of the mineral wool panels as in a cooling case. The heating capacity coefficient 
of the ceiling surface is reduced by 30% when the suspended ceiling coverage 
represents 70% of the total ceiling area; and by 45% when the coverage ratio 
increases up to 87%. Álvarez also concludes that the ventilation rate has a 
large influence on the convective heat capacity. When the ventilation rate is 
increased from 1.7 to 2.9 h-1, the heat transfer coefficient increases of 16% for 
the same occupancy and suspended ceiling layout.  
5.3 
Measurements in actual buildings 
Y. Le Muet and H. Peperkamp performed measurement in a TABS office 
building located in Lyon, France (2013). The focus was here mainly on acoustic 
and thermal comfort. The operative temperature was monitored both in a test 
office equipped with ceiling sound absorbers, and in a reference office with a 
bare ceiling. The conclusion is that a 50% ceiling coverage leads to an average

the operative temperature rose of 0.8 to 1°C.  
It can be noted that these values are significantly below the temperature 
increase measured by E. Pittarello in 2006. This will be discussed later on in 
the report in the Chapter 11, Discussion. 
 
The research institute Fraunhofer ISE performed over the summer 2014 a 
series of measurements taken in a TABS office building located in Freiburg, 
Germany (Fraunhofer ISE, 2015). It should be noted that this report has not 
yet been publicly published as of today –July 2015– but this will be the case by 
the end of the year. Parts of the results are available today in a paper by Le 
Muet et al. (2015). Here, different scenarii have been simulated, varying in 
coverage ratio and type of absorbers: horizontal coverages of 30, 45 and 60% 
have been assessed, as well as vertical baffles. Each measurement series was 
monitored in parallel with a reference office room, devoid of any sound 
absorber. Three campaigns have been run, comparing each type two by two. 
Campaign 1 monitored simultaneously rooms with 30 and 60% covered ceiling; 
30 and 45% in campaign 2. Campaign 3 compared a room with 60% of ceiling 
sound absorbers with a room equipped with baffles. Each campaign also 
featured a reference room.  
The thermal comfort evaluation has been done accordingly to the categories 
of the standard EN 15251. The conclusion of the study is that high coverage 
ratios (45% and up to 60%) are still able in most cases to meet the 
requirement for a category II indoor environment. Rooms with 45% of 
ceiling covered remained in the limit of the category II during 96% of the 
occupied hours among the campaign; and 95% of the time for rooms with 60% 
of ceiling covered.  
This counterbalances the intuitive idea that the lower the ceiling covering 
the better in TABS buildings, and that an effective compromise can actually be 
found.

measurements in actual buildings.  
5.4.2 
Constant heat load procedure 
As a matter of fact, these latters have approached the case from another 
angle. The heat loads in the room are kept constant, to simulate the actual 
loads of a building; and therefore the room temperature (operative 
temperature) will evolve when different scenarii are simulated. Typically, it has 
been proven that the room operative temperature will increase with the 
ceiling coverage ratio, all other loads being kept the same.  
The point is that, with temperature evolving in the room, the cooling 
capacity of the ceiling as defined in Eq. 5.1 cannot be easily compared between 
different scenarii. Typically, the cooling capacity will not necessarily decrease

5.4.3 
Conclusion 
One should therefore be attentive when analysing results of different 
studies, and understand whether the results are expressed in terms of TABS 
cooling capacity (W, or W/m2) or cooling capacity coefficient (W.m-2.K-1). 
Both of them have proven to evolve in a similar pattern, but with different test 
methodologies. Both parameters are generally decreasing with an increase of 
the ceiling coverage ratio, but it should be noted that certain simulations ran 
led to an increase of the cooling capacity with the ceiling coverage ratio. It can 
therefore be erroneous to directly compare both results, as they correspond to 
different realities and different procedures.   
In the current work, it has been decided to work with a constant heat loads 
methodology, as this allows simulating an actual office-building situation. 
This will therefore lead to results more easily applicable to the problematic of 
the industry world. Moreover, this methodology also presents the main 
advantage to allow quantifying the thermal comfort evolution when the 
ceiling coverage ratio evolves, which is judged of high interest in practice. 
Therefore, the cooling capacity coefficient of the TABS will be used in the 
results as an indicator of the TABS performances.

Test chamber 
The construction consists of two thermo-active decks (floor and ceiling)

Cooling mode 
In the cooling mode, if the supply temperature measured is higher than the 
setpoint, the valve opens to cool the water flow through the heat exchanger. In 
this mode, the electrical heater is not activated.  
 
The heat exchanger operates with the district cooling available in the 
laboratory, by the mean of a cold mix of water and glycol. Typically, the 
coolant temperature should be kept in a range around 10°C for improved 
efficiency of the cooling systems. Unfortunately, the coolant temperature in the 
workshop is kept constant at -20°C by the facilities management team. This 
has not been possible to change. Therefore, a special attention has been put in 
the system design to prevent any freezing in the heat exchanger.  For this, a 
loop has been integrated in the circuit before the heat exchanger, using a three-
way valve and a pump in order to mix the supply glycol with the temperate 
return until the glycol passing in the heat exchanger reaches a positive 
temperature.  
This valve is itself controlled by a dedicated controller, based on a 
measurement of the coolant temperature after the heat exchanger. As long as 
this temperature is not positive, the valve should close and recirculate the 
fluid, not allowing any -20°C fluid into the system.  
 
Heating mode 
In the heating mode, the heat exchanger is bypassed and the water only 
passes in the closed circuit, heated up by the electrical heater.  
 
To avoid any unwanted oscillations in the supply water temperature, a 
buffer tank has been installed before the supply to the decks. The aim is to 
make use of the tank to even out peaks in the water temperature, in order to 
ensure the system with a supply water temperature as constant as possible. 
The system used is the Metrotherm electrical heater 30L. It combines both the 
need for an electrical heater and also a buffer tank of small capacity. The 
choice of the pipes direction (facing up) has been made based on practical 
considerations to ease the system’s connections.  
The tanks are expected to feature a uniform temperature. If it happens that 
a fluctuation remains which is too consequent for the accuracy of the 
experiment, this risk could be minimized by an update. It consists of an extra 
circuit, circulating a large volume of water through the tank, and letting only a 
small amount out to the active slabs. By this way, the supply temperature 
could reach a very steady level.

6.2.6 
Guard 
As specified previously, an outer guard surrounds the test chamber. The 
guard is equipped with two fan coils allowing blowing and mixing the air in the 
enclosure. The air is blown to the top of the guard through two conduits placed 
on the sides of the guard; and is then naturally dropping back down. In 
practice, Weitzmann concluded on a reasonably homogeneous air mixing in the 
enclosure (2004). This is confirmed by measurements taken in six different 
locations in the guard: above and below the room, as well as on the exterior 
face of each of the chamber’s vertical walls.   
The fans are connected to the water pipes of the third flow unit, working 
on the same basis as the units supplying the active decks.  
 
Control system of the guard 
The temperature of the guard surrounding the test room is maintained at 
the inside room temperature by a PID-controller.  
The controller compares the room air temperature and the guard air 
temperature and controls the temperature of the water supplied to the fans 
accordingly.  
This relies on the assumption that the room and the guard air temperatures 
can be considered uniform, since the control is based on a temperature 
measured in one location. In practice, it can be that this assumption is not 
exactly correct due to possible temperature stratification in the room. 
• 
The room temperature is measured at the centre of the room, at a 
1.1m height; 
• 
The guard temperature is measured on the exterior side of one of 
the room walls; 
• 
These measurements are taken with Pt100 sensors placed in grey 
globes. 
 
The PID then controls the temperature of the water supplied to the fans, 
until the guard temperature reaches the room temperature. This is done by 
adjusting either the mixing valve for cooling the water through the heat 
exchanger, or the electrical heater when heating is needed.

6.6 
Measurement equipment 
6.6.1 
Temperature 
Temperatures in the test facility are measured using thermocouples (for 
absolute temperature measurement) and thermopiles (for temperature 
difference). This includes measurements in the test room itself, but also all 
around the enclosure (guard, decks, walls, etc.). This sums up to a total of over 
70 measurement positions, as described in Appendix 9 of Weitzmann’s 
documentation manual (2004). To process and display the measurements on a 
digital screen, an Agilent 34970A data logger connected to a computer is used. 
The temperatures are calculated internally in the instrument, using ITS-90 
software compensation. The equations at stake are presented in Appendix 5.  
An important part of the preparation of the test facility has been to 
identify and reconnect every loose thermocouple connection to the dedicated 
spot in the data logger. Before that, the wiring had been changed by previous 
users, and no documentation was existing allowing to have a clear overview of 
what each wire was actually corresponding to. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:65==
6 – FULL-SCALE MEASUREMENTS - METHODS 
 
53 
Thermocouples 
The thermocouples used are of type TT (copper/constantan). This type 
features an increased accuracy compared to type T thermocouples.  
The thermocouples’ accuracy is expected to be 0.3% of the reading 
(Weitzmann, 2004); and the data logger documentation gives an accuracy of 
±1K. The main reason for that is to be found in the fact that a common 
reference point in the data logger is used; and since the temperature across the 
instrument can fluctuate up to 1K, this sets the limit to the accuracy.  
 
Thermopiles 
Thermopiles on the other hand do not use the reference point in the data 
logger, and are therefore much more accurate. Additionally, in order to increase 
the measurement accuracy, the thermopiles in the setup are made of three or 
five serially connected thermocouples; and the average signal is simply found 
by dividing the measured signal by the number of thermocouples in the pile. 
Thermopiles have an accuracy of 0.5% of the reading and 0.4% of range 
(Weitzmann, 2004).  
 
Calibration of the thermocouples 
Sixteen thermocouples are used inside the test chamber, for measurements 
of surface and air temperatures, as well as operative temperature. These 
thermocouples have been calibrated to ensure a safe output.

Heat flux 
To complement the heat flow calculated as previously described, heat flux 
sensors are used on the TABS lower ceiling surface. The sensors used are RdF 
Micro-Foil Heat Flux Sensors. Very thin, these sensors provide minimal 
perturbations to the flow of heat. They allow measuring heat flows up to 95

in the centre of the room – again adjacent to the temperature measure point. 
The sensors are logged on the Agilent data logger with the other sensors.

Ventilation 
Even if the present work focuses on assessing the heat output of a TABS 
system, it has been considered important to supply the test room with fresh 
supply air, to recreate the conditions of an actual office building. Otherwise, 
the effect of the acoustic panels on the TABS performances might be 
unrealistic compared to an original daily-life situation.  
6.7.1 
Equipment 
Ventilation is supplied to the room by the use of an Air Handling Unit 
(AHU) with a cooling coil. The air supply temperature has been set to 20°C as 
a typical temperature that can be expected from a summer case for comfort 
ventilation.  
 
==PAGE:69==
6 – FULL-SCALE MEASUREMENTS - METHODS 
 
57 
 
The AHU is controlled by a RDT410 controller from SAUTER. The 
controller is used to set the temperature of the supply air. The airflow is 
manually adjusted using a regulator to control the fan of the AHU.  
Two thermocouples placed in the supply and return ducts allow measuring 
the respective air temperatures.  
The cooling coil of the AHU is connected to the district cooling system, 
providing cold fluid to the unit. A problem arose when realising that the fluid 
used here was the -20°C glycol. This temperature being much lower than what 
the unit requires to maintain a supply air temperature of 15-20°C typically 
used, the cooling coil was having trouble coping with this temperature. As a 
result, the supply air temperature observed was oscillating with an amplitude 
of ± 3°C.  
To fix this issue, a return loop has been introduced in the glycol system, 
mixing the temperate return fluid with the supply using a three-way valve and 
a pump. This allowed avoiding having a too cold supply fluid temperature to 
the coil.

In addition to the experimental investigation, a numerical model has been

8.1 
TRNSYS 
8.1.1 
Presentation of TRNSYS 
TRNSYS (TRaNsient SYstem Simulation) is a simulation environment 
initially dedicated to the numerical study of transient systems. The program 
originated 40 years ago at the University of Wisconsin Solar Energy Lab. 
Today, it is relatively widely used to model the dynamic thermal behaviour of 
buildings.  
TRNSYS features a modular structure in which the modules, called Types, 
each represent a specific component of the program (Jankovic, 2012). For 
example, Type 1 may be used to model a solar collector (TRNSYS manual, 
2014). A crucial component in TRNSYS is the Type 56, which models the 
thermal behaviour of a building.  
 
One of the strengths of TRNSYS is to be found in this modular structure. 
Since each TRNSYS Type is coded as an independent object compatible with 
the overall TRNSYS system architecture, new Types can be developed 
independently by third parties.  
8.1.2 
Type Ecophon Acoustic Elements 
Aware of this opportunity, and noticing that it was not possible in 
TRNSYS to simulate a suspended ceiling with a surface area different of the 
ceiling surface (i.e. anything else than a classic fully-covering suspended 
ceiling), P. Lombard conceived a new Type for TRNSYS allowing to simulate 
hanging sound absorbers with a certain coverage.  The new Type 
(Ecophon Acoustic Elements) considers convective exchanges of the sound 
absorbers with the room air, and radiative exchanges with the room inner 
surfaces. Linked to the Type 56’s room model, the component allows evaluating 
the impact of sound absorbers on operative temperature in the room and on 
the cooled ceiling efficiency, as a function of the ceiling coverage ratio 
(Lombard, 2015).

in the ceiling coverage ratio and to investigate its influence on TABS efficiency

8.7 
Remark regarding the Type Ecophon Acoustic 
Elements 
When looking at the code governing the Type Ecophon Acoustic Elements, 
one of the assumptions made is that the actual pattern of the suspended 
ceiling is disregarded. The only parameters at stake for the geometrical 
design of the panels are the distance between the soffit and the upper face of 
the panels, and the coverage ratio. In other terms, all sound absorbers in the 
==PAGE:85==

73 
room are considered as a contiguous and unique system, centred on the 
ceiling (Lombard, 2015).  
But, as highlighted by Peutz (2013), the suspended ceiling pattern does 
have an influence on the cooling capacity reduction: a reduction of 33% has 
been observed with evenly distributed panels, but 38% with clustered centred 
panels, for the same coverage ratio. Therefore, the above-mentioned 
assumption will probably lead to a slight underestimation of the cooling 
capacity (of around 5%, according to Peutz’s study).

the room, which is not modelled in TRNSYS.

10.1.3 Discussion 
One could therefore want to conclude that, to limit the shading effect of 
the acoustic panels on the TABS cooling power, these latters should be placed 
as far as realistically possible from the slab surface. Nonetheless, this is limited 
in actual situations by costs and efficiency of building height, as well as an 
optimum in noise absorption from the acoustic absorbers.  
 
According to Rindel (1982), the distance at which a porous material placed 
at a certain distance away from a hard surface will feature a maximum 
absorption is given by Eq. 10.1.

Hence, considering a typical frequency of 140 Hz (adult male voiced speech) 
and 210 Hz (adult female), the first optimum distances are respectively at 400 
and 600mm. Nonetheless, this frequency resolution is much higher than the one 
at which sound absorbing panels are actually designed to operate. Indeed, the 
Equivalent Absorption Area of a panel is calculated for an average of the 
frequencies in the range 500 – 4000 Hz, therefore blurring the differences 
between the different suspension heights.  
 
The major point in disfavour of large distance between ceiling and sound 
absorber is financial. For a multi-storeys building of a given height, a higher 
suspended ceiling thickness translates from a building owner point of view into 
less usable space for occupants; and therefore, less money when renting it. 
Increased building height also costs additional building materials and time 
when constructing the building. This explains why it can be observed that this 
distance is often kept to a minimal.  
 
Practically, according to Pierre Chigot from Saint-Gobain Ecophon, a 
distance of 300mm between the ceiling and the acoustic panels is the most 
realistic case encountered today in office buildings. This is why this height 
has been chosen for the setup in the test facility and the computer simulation.

10.3 Sensitivity analysis 
To put into perspective the above-displayed results, additional simulations 
have also been performed. They feature the same varying criterion – the part of 
the TABS active ceiling covered by acoustic panels – but with different inputs. 
This sensitivity analysis will allow understanding the weight of these various 
parameters on the results. Namely, the parameters at stake are listed here 
below.  
 
• 
Smaller internal heat gains: the heating mat simulating the 
solar gain has been removed from the simulation, leading to an 
internal heat gain of 34 W/m2. 
• 
The presence of only one TABS deck in the room: one 
simulation has been carried out with only the ceiling active.  
• 
The absence of comfort ventilation in the room.  
• 
An increased TABS supply water temperature of 17°C and 19°C.  
• 
A variable supply temperature of the ventilation system, both 
higher and lower than the reference: 22°C and 18°C. 
 
A study of the influence of the TABS water flow rate has not been 
performed, since this was estimated in Árnason’s work (2011). He noted that 
doubling the water flow rate (from 180 kg/h to 360 kg/h) resulted in an 
increase of cooling capacity coefficient of 6%. This can be explained by the fact 
that increasing the flow rate in the active decks will lead to a smaller 
temperature difference between supply and return in the system, and hence a 
more homogeneous concrete temperature.   
 
The other parameters of the study have been left unchanged if not 
mentioned.

11 DISCUSSION 
11.1 Cooling capacity coefficient 
The cooling capacity coefficient of the uncovered ceiling deck has been 
found to be 4.7 W.m-2.K-1 in average (for the eight tests conducted including 
the sensitivity analysis), which is slightly smaller than the value reported in E. 
Pittarello’s work (between 4.9 and 5.5 W.m-2.K-1 depending on the experiments 
performed). This is however very much in the same range, and the difference 
can be accounted on the difference in experimental setup and heat loads used. 
As illustrated in the model validation (Chapter 9), the cooling capacity 
coefficient obtained through the simulation model matches accurately the 
experimental values when the same conditions are recreated in the numerical 
model.  
 
From this initial value, the cooling capacity coefficient has shown a 
decrease of 16% when the ceiling surface is covered at 60% with sound 
absorbers, and of 25% when this coverage reaches 80%. Again, this is in 
accordance with the observations of E. Pittarello (2006). Two different batches 
of tests have been performed, using different types of panels and layouts. 
Overall, the author reports a cooling capacity coefficient reduction of 29% for 
67% coverage in one case, and 22% for a coverage of 70% in the other case.  
 
The graph here below (Figure 11.1) is taken from Pittarello (2006) and 
summarizes the results of the experiments performed, both by himself and 
Weitzmann (2004). On this graph have been added the results of the present 
work (grey hexagons). As the results of the sensitivity analysis emphasized, all 
results obtained in the simulations are very close, therefore only the results of 
the reference case have been plotted on this document.

It appears that the results follow the same overall trend as what has been 
previously quantified. One can however note that the TRNSYS model features 
a more linear evolution than the full-scale measurements performed. Indeed, 
a linear trendline can be drawn between the numerical model results with a

high coverage ratios. In fact, Pittarello concludes that “[…] covering up to 50% 
of the ceiling area has no significant influence on the cooling capacity 
coefficient”.     
11.2 Thermal comfort 
11.2.1 Operative temperature and ventilation 
The results show an increase of 0.9°C in operative temperature for a room 
equipped with a thermal active ceiling covered by acoustic panels on 60% of its 
surface. To put this into comparison, E. Pittarello measured +2°C in 2006 for a 
comparable coverage (70%). At first sight, this appears much higher than the 
increase observed in the present study; but one point is important to note: this 
is because the measurements have been performed without any ventilation in 
the room. And to compare comparable quantities, the same operative

temperature increase (+1.8°C for a 60% coverage ratio) has been registered in 
numerical simulations when the ventilation is turned off in the room.  
 
Measurements without ventilation are still valid and meaningful, but it 
should not be overlooked that this does not correspond to a real situation. As a 
matter of fact, even if the room conditioning is achieved by way of hydronic 
systems, a minimum amount of fresh air should still be provided to rooms 
by means of mechanical or natural ventilation. This is in order to account for 
the indoor air quality as well as removing the occupants’ latent heat. 
Standards and reports such as EN 15251 or EN 1752 provide guidelines and 
recommendations for the amount of air to be supplied by the ventilation 
system in buildings, based on their occupancy levels and the level of pollution 
to be expected from the building itself.  
Even in small amount to only account for indoor air quality, this temperate 
air supplied to the room will of course influence the thermal comfort in the 
enclosure, acting as a supportive cooling source. Indeed, it is observed that the 
fresh air will significantly reduce the peak in temperature (air or operative) 
increase due to the presence of acoustic panels hanging from the TABS ceiling. 
The operative temperature increase registered by Peperkamp (2013) in the 
WOOPA building in Lyon, France was only 0.3°C for a ceiling coverage ratio 
of 50%; and this is due to the presence of mechanical ventilation in the 
building.  
11.2.2 Occupants’ behaviour and adaptive comfort 
Horizontal sound absorbers hanging from the ceiling in a TABS building 
will, acknowledgedly, have an effect on indoor thermal comfort. At least 
appears so in computer simulations and in full-scale measurements in test 
facilities. But what about a real situation, in an occupied building? In practice, 
occupants’ behaviour has a major effect on thermal comfort, and it could 
be that this influence would at least weight as much as what is to be expected 
from the sound absorbers, if not outweigh it.  
 
This is confirmed by a study from the German research-organization 
Fraunhofer performed in a TABS office building located in Freiburg, Germany 
(2015). Occupied office rooms equipped -or not- with ceiling sound absorbers in 
different coverage have been monitored during three months in summer 2014. 
It appears that the occupant behaviour, by opening windows and doors or 
making use of solar shading, will greatly affect the indoor thermal environment. 
The study concludes that the short-term influence of the user behaviour was at 
least as high as the influence of sound absorbers on the thermal comfort 
(Fraunhofer ISE, 2015 – not yet published).  
==PAGE:110==
11 – DISCUSSION 
 
 
98 
Indeed, the results illustrate that some 4-persons offices showed higher 
operative temperatures and slightly lower cooling effect of the TABS with an 
increase in ceiling coverage ratio. But the influence of the occupants appears 
clear when the opposite result has also been noted in 3-persons offices. In these, 
thermal comfort measured with a ceiling covered at 60% by acoustic panels was 
better than in a room with 30% coverage ratio (Fraunhofer ISE, 2015).  
 
Practically, thermal comfort in buildings depends on many different and 
interrelated factors, governed – at least in most buildings – by the occupants. 
These latters can adapt to their environment in a number of small ways; using 
adaptive mechanisms or opportunities to achieve their personal desired 
conditions (Nicol and Humphreys, 2002). This is described for instance in 
Baker and Standeven’s work (1995), where they describe the model of an 
“adaptive opportunity” afforded by a building that will affect the comfort of its 
occupants. This can translate as the ability to open a window, draw a blind or 
use a fan, but also embraces occupants’ dress code. As a matter of fact, 
changes in clothing, activity level and posture and the promotion of air 
movement will change the conditions which people find comfortable. Adaptive 
behaviour embraces two types of actions:  
- 
Changing the conditions in accordance with occupants’ comfort, in 
order to suit themselves; 
- 
Changing the comfort temperature to accord with prevailing conditions. 
As Nicol and Humphreys conclude, the range of conditions considered 
comfortable is altered by the building’s characteristics and the opportunities 
for occupants’ individual adaptation.  
 
Overall, the cumulative effect of these adjustments can be consequent, and 
explains differences between adaptive thermal comfort and rational indices. In 
this context, it can be considered that an augmentation in operative 
temperature induced by the introduction of sound absorbers in a TABS 
building does not necessarily go in pair with a degraded thermal comfort. This 
is corroborated by the results obtained for the comfort criteria in the 
simulation (Table 10.2), notably the Predicted Percentage Dissatisfied (PPD). 
Indeed, the changes induced in the indoor conditions led to a PPD increase of 
3.14% - from 7.76% to 10.9% - when covering 60% of the slab surface. This 
latter value only exceeds by less than 1% the recommended value by the 
standard EN 15251 (2007) for a comfort Category II building.      
As long as the building allows the occupants to adjust the conditions to 
suit themselves and that the temperature increase induced remains moderate 
(such as the levels registered in this study), high thermal comfort levels are still 
achievable.

12 RECOMMENDATIONS  
Horizontal ceiling hanging sound absorbers have proven to have an 
influence on the TABS cooling performances, but without automatically 
meaning that their presence should necessarily be avoided. The opposite is 
true, and sound absorbers are an important feature in TABS buildings, 
featuring extensive amount of hard reflective surfaces. Absorbers will directly 
affect the sound levels in the rooms, leading to increased comfort for the 
occupants and improving their productivity by means of consequence.    
 
High performances in terms of cooling efficiency and thermal comfort can 
still be achieved in a room equipped with sound absorbers and conditioned 
with TABS. The present chapter will provide recommendations for a viable 
implementation of sound absorbers in this context.  
12.1 Air movement in the room 
For the system to be able to perform efficiently, it is important to select a 
solution of ceiling absorbers allowing a flow of air between the active soffit 
and their surface. Hanging absorbers should therefore be privileged rather than 
panels directly placed on the ceiling surface.  
Since the panels will impinge on the radiative cooling, it is important to 
balance it with convective movements in the room. An actual installation also 
features additional airflows due to ventilation, windows opening or simply 
activity in the premises, which will help the cooling process.  
12.2 Room acoustics 
The basic principle for achieving good room acoustics is to provide the 
room with sufficient amount of absorbing material. In TABS buildings, and 
aware of the thermal problematic, acousticians recommend a cumulative 
surface of suspended horizontal absorbers of 60% of the floor area (Ecophon, 
2014). Nevertheless, it is important to note that if this corresponds to a 
necessary amount, it is not necessarily sufficient. This is especially due to the 
relatively poor performance of hanging absorbers at low frequencies (Ecophon, 
2014). Consequently, use should be made of the other surfaces available to 
provide rooms with sound absorption. Ceiling absorbers should be 
complemented with wall absorbers where possible. When combined with the 
above-mentioned ceiling coverage, acousticians recommend covering at least the 
equivalent of 20% of the floor area with wall absorbers, depending on the usage 
that is made of the room. Since echoes are created when sound bounces back 
==PAGE:112==
12 – RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
 
100 
and forth on opposite walls, the optimum benefit from wall absorbers will be 
obtained when placed on two adjacent walls.  
12.3 Placement of acoustic units 
As the results show, the horizontal panels should be placed as far as 
possible from the soffit for improved TABS cooling performances. Practically, a 
distance of 200 to 600mm can be considered reasonable. It is also important to 
ensure air gaps on the panels’ sides and between rows of panels. This will 
support convective movements from the chilled slab, and allow the hot air from 
the internal gains’ plume to reach the ceiling thermal mass.  
12.4 The case of acoustic baffles 
Eventually, the solution of vertical absorbing baffles hanging from the 
ceiling is worth mentioning as it embodies a promising solution to provide 
acoustic comfort in rooms while having a minimal effect on the cooling 
performances of the TABS.  Their orientation is, by essence, more favourable 
to the radiative heat exchange from the ceiling and their masking effect will be 
less important than horizontal panels. A study from Ecophon (Ecophon, 2014), 
reports cooling capacity reductions of 12 and 16% respectively for TABS rooms 
equipped with baffles spaced every 400 and 200mm.  
 
Nonetheless, from the acoustic point of view, baffles will never be as good 
as horizontal panels. This is as the sound, when directed to the soffit, does not 
see directly the absorber (as in the case of horizontal units), but will first see 
the concrete surface, and will then only be absorbed by the baffles after 
reflexion. Acoustic tests performed by Ecophon show an important degradation 
in performances between the two solutions for an equal amount of acoustic 
material in use.  
 
But this solution is highly appreciated by architects, and it seems like the 
market shows enthusiasm for baffles-solutions. Hence, it can be expected that 
this solution will gain in spread in the upcoming years. Even if not the optimal 
acoustic solution, their improved thermal performances could allow placing in a 
room larger absorbing areas than what would be possible with horizontal 
panels (being limited by the thermal performances decrease), and consequently 
reaching equivalent levels of acoustic comfort.

13 – CONCLUSION

13 CONCLUSION 
Soffit-hanging discrete sound-absorbers, as opposed to a wall-to-wall 
acoustic ceiling, are a good solution to improve acoustic comfort in buildings 
where large surfaces of concrete are left exposed. This is typically the case in 
thermally activated constructions, which presence has considerably expanded in 
Europe since the 1990s. The number of Thermo-Activated Building Systems 
(TABS) buildings is expected to keep increasing in the future, as they 
represent an energy-efficient solution to provide steady thermal conditions 
indoors (Kalz et al., 2014).   
 
Adding hanging sound absorbers in a room conditioned by TABS will 
impact the performances of this latter, as the panels will shield some of the 
radiation from the chilled ceiling, as well as prevent some of the hot air plume 
generated by the internal heat gains to reach the soffit. By means of 
consequence, a temperature rise in the enclosure will follow. The present study 
focused on quantifying both of the above-mentioned phenomena, using a new 
thermal numerical model for the dynamic simulation software TRNSYS. The 
work also encompasses the renovation of a water system supplying the active 
decks of a TABS test facility, in order to pursue towards full-scale 
measurements.  
 
The numerical model has been validated based on an existing set of 
measurements, which showed a good consistency in the results. The simulations 
performed allowed quantifying a reduction of the TABS performances – 
represented by its cooling capacity coefficient – of 16% when 60% of the ceiling 
area is covered with floating acoustic panels; and of 25% when this coverage 
increases to 80%. A ceiling coverage of 60% is the typical level of performance 
recommended by Ecophon to achieve good acoustics indoors (2014). When 
complemented with wall absorbers to tackle low frequencies, this ensures an 
optimal acoustic comfort. In terms of indoor thermal comfort, this effect 
translates into an increase of operative temperature of 0.9°C in the former case 
under the cooling loads considered in this study (~40 W/m2), and up to 1.6°C 
in the latter.  
Besides, this solution still appears viable when TABS are used in a heating 
mode. As Álvarez (2013) concludes from her study, the heating capacity 
coefficient of a TABS ceiling deck is reduced of 30% when 70% of the active 
surface is covered with suspended mineral wool panels.  
 
 
==PAGE:114==
13 – CONCLUSION 
 
 
102 
Based on the results of the simulations performed, it can be concluded that 
high thermal comfort can still be achieved with such coverage. A sensibility 
analysis underlined the major peak-shaving impact of comfort ventilation on 
the operative temperature increase. All the tests conducted with the two active 
decks active in the room, by changing heat loads, TABS power or ventilation 
temperatures, led to an operative temperature increase under 1°C in the room. 
This study corresponds to an ideal case modelled using computer simulation; 
but a reduced effect is expected to be registered in an actual building, as the 
occupants’ behaviour might influence the indoor environment more than the 
actual change in ceiling geometry (Fraunhofer ISE, 2015, not published yet).  
When designing buildings, the goal is to provide optimal comfort conditions 
to the occupants. To make themselves fully comfortable, the users will make 
small adjustments to their environment through natural ventilation or 
adjusting solar shadings for instance; but also without changing the thermal 
conditions in the room, by adjusting their clothing level. These impacts are 
likely to be able to cope with the change in thermal conditions induced by 
introducing sound absorbers under the soffit surface.  
 
Overall, it appears now possible to combine high levels of thermal and 
acoustic comfort in TABS buildings, using hanging sound absorbers. 
Additionally, the use of the numerical tool developed can help integrating 
acoustic solutions better in the early design phases of a building design, when 
used by thermal consultants and architects.

14 – FUTURE STUDIES 
 
103 
14 FUTURE STUDIES 
Given the numerous delays and issues encountered with the flow units of 
the test facility, further work remains to be done to bring the study fully to 
shore. The control system of the flow units is being built and the active decks 
should hopefully be operational by September 2015.  
From this point, the full-scale measurements described in this report will be 
conducted in the test facility. Their results will then be analysed and compared 
with the conclusions of the numerical study performed. This could include 
additional experiments such as smoke tests for instance, to understand the air 
patterns in the enclosure, and how they might change depending on the ceiling 
coverage ratio.  
 
Additionally, further investigations will be carried out in the continuation 
of the present study. Experiments will be performed with acoustic vertical 
baffles, to assess the impact of various configurations for their layout, be it in 
terms of height, rows spacing or products type and to conclude on their 
respective effect on the TABS performances.  
 
Eventually, an interesting point that could be studied is a numerical study 
of the airflow patterns in the room. Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) 
simulations could be used to understand the influence of acoustic panels and 
various coverages on airflows and convection movements in the enclosure.  
As the present study concludes on the noticeable impact of comfort 
ventilation on the thermal comfort in a TABS building, it would be valuable to 
bring to a higher level the knowledge on the combination of different 
ventilation strategies and discrete hanging sound absorbers in TABS buildings. 
As a conclusion, this would allow being able to conclude on what 
ventilation strategy to recommend for a given TABS building depending on the 
comfort criteria for thermal and acoustic comfort indoors. Taking these aspects 
into account from the early phases of a building conception, alongside with 
energy efficiency and other comfort parameters, and influencing the design 
until reaching a more optimal solution is what integrated design should be 
about.

The present section displays internal equations used by the Agilent data 
logger to calculate the temperature of the thermocouples, based on their 
voltage measurement.  
The temperature is calculated by a built-in function in the data logger. 
When setting up the experiment, the desired thermocouples are registered in 
the program as “Temperature (T)” sensors.  
The method is based on the voltage measurement of the thermocouple, 
using the following equation to convert it to temperature:
