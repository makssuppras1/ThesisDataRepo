Abstract
In the following project the author has modeled the porosities in the Zo-
harim well between the calibration points obtained from the core samples
by Shitrit et al. 2016 [23]. Using these porosity values the author was
then able to calculate grain density, mass and volumetric fractions of or-
ganic matter and calcite, as well as hydrogen index of the organic matter.
All of those parameters were then used for assessing the lithology. Since
no data on the mud ﬁltrate resistivity (Rmf) was available, the approach
was to ﬁrst assume a value of Rmf using a constant cementation factor m
and then calculate m at the core depths. With these m values the author
was able to model cementation factor between the core depths using mod-
iﬁed gamma ray log, modiﬁed spontaneous potential log and trendlines
between the m core depths. Once the m values between the calibration
points were obtained it was possible to calculate porosity values through-
out the whole length of the investigated well section. The results of the
study indicate that all of the models follow the core porosity data with
reasonable precision. The only model which departs quite signiﬁcantly
from the calibration points and should not be used is the one calculated
with constant average cementation factor value of mave,core = 3.36. As for
the lithology, the authors guess is that there are two intervals with pre-
dominantly limestone matrix (330 m.d.-400 m.d. and 475 m.d.-550 m.d.)
and one with increased organic-rich shale content (400 m.d.-475 m.d.).

Chapter 1
Literature review
The Shfela basin oil shales reserves are a subject of great interest and have been
covered widely in both scientiﬁc articles and main stream media [5]. It is therefore
not a surprise that the number of papers published on the topic is fairly high. Below
I give a short summary of the scientiﬁc articles relevant to the organic-rich chalk from
the Zoharim well.
Shitrit et al. [23] has investigated the eﬀect of kerogen on the physical character-
istics of the core material from the Zoharim well. The studied parameters included
porosity, density, acoustic velocities, elastic moduli, tensile strength and compressive
strength of the rock. The results of the investigation indicated that the increasing
kerogen content and increasing porosity is correlated with decreasing dynamic bulk
modulus and decreasing Young modulus. Increasing porosity has also decreased the
Compressive and tensile strength of the samples. There was no correlation between
porosity and kerogen content.
Shitrit et al.
[24] has also measured acoustic velocities using water-saturated
core plugs in the bedding normal direction under simulated ﬁeld pressures.
The
results were compared between immature, early-mature and over-mature samples
and showed that S-wave velocities increased and P-wave velocities decreased upon
maturation of the samples. According to the authors of the paper, the possible reasons
for this change in velocities are (i) stiﬀening of kerogen upon thermal maturation
which results in S-wave velocity increase (ii) enhancement in kerogen porosity upon
maturation which decreases the P-wave velocity.
In 2017 Shitrit et al. published an article investigating physical properties of the
original immature rock at two pyrolysis simulated maturity levels: early-mature and
over-mature [25]. The authors found that porosity of the samples increases, P-wave
velocity decreases and density of the kerogen also increases upon pyrolysis of the
samples. According to Shitrit et al. bitumen is released under high temperatures
from the kerogen in the samples, which is linked with an increase in the kerogen
porosity which in turn increases permeability of the samples.
1
==PAGE:8==
Meilijson et al. [20] described the chronostratigraphy of the Upper Cretaceous
high productivity sequence of the southern Tethys. The paper gave useful insights
into the geological setting of the Shfela basin by providing correlation schemes and
petrophysical logs from other wells in the Shfela basin e.g. the Aderet well.
Organic-rich chalk from the Aderet well was also the subject to geomechanical
and petrophysical investigation done by Bisnovat et al. [10]. Bisnovat et al. has
studied the eﬀect of total organic carbon (TOC) on a number of physical parameters
including uniaxial compressive strength, tensile strength, Young’s modulus, poros-
ity and permeability. The authors found that (i) the uniaxial compressive strength
increases with increasing TOC content (ii) the eﬀect of TOC on Young’s modulus
and tensile strength could not be determined (iii) porosity and permeability of the
collected samples were independent of the variation in organic content, which is due
to co-precipitation of calcite minerals and organic matter during deposition. Further-
more, it was concluded that the chalk from the well displays both mechanical and
petrophysical anisotropy displayed by diﬀerent permeabilities and tensile strengths
at diﬀerent directions of the bedding.
Olsen et al. [22] has investigated the eﬀect of porosity, permeability and speciﬁc
surface on cementation factor for the North Sea chalk. Olsen et al. found that there
exists a positive relationship between cementation factor and speciﬁc surface. Paper
published by Olsen et al. proved to be especially useful in the case of chalk coming
from the Zoharim well, since it enabled calculation of cementation factors from the
speciﬁc surface data, which were later used to make an assumption about mud-ﬁltrate
resistivity values.
For the interpretation of results as well as theoretical background, the author has
heavily used Schlumberger log interpretation [6] handbook which provides practical
information in the ﬁeld of quantitative log interpretation, as well as overviews of
diﬀerent well logs. Among other materials the The Geological Interpretation of Well
Logs by Malcolm Rider [17] proved to be very useful, especially for the part concerning
geological interpretation of the investigated interval.

2.1.1
Resistivity log
Principle of measurement
Resistivity tool provides a measure of the formations resistivity [17, p. 42]. Most
of the rock materials are insulators so in practice the log is a reﬂection of formation
ﬂuids resistance, not the lithology [17, p. 42]. The highest resistivities will be asso-
ciated with oil and gas intervals while the lowest ones with salt water. Resistivity of
freshwater will be higher than that of the salt water, due to lower concentration of
ions. Low resistivities are also observed in shale zones due to inbound water between
the clay layers.
The basic principle of resistivity logging is to pass an electric current between
two electrodes located on a tool (Figure 2.1a). In case of a lateral device a constant
current is passed between A and B, and the potential diﬀerence between M and N is
measured [6]. The voltage drop between the electrodes M and N is proportional to
the resistivity of the formation [6]. Depth of investigation of the tool depends on the
spacing between the two electrodes; the larger the spacing the deeper the current will
pass into the formation [6].
As mentioned before, the rock itself does not play an active part in determining
the resistivity, however it does contribute through the so-called Formation Factor (F)
which is dependent on the rock texture (Figure 2.1b) [17, p. 44]. Rocks with low
value of F will conduct electric current with ease as opposed to high F rocks where
the current has to pass through a complicated path of pore connections.

Tools
Tools used in resistivity logging fall under two main categories: laterologs and
induction tools [6]. Induction tools are mainly used for non-conductive muds and are
most accurate in low- to medium-resistivity formations [6].
For the logging of Zoharim well, laterolog tool was used. Laterologs are recom-
mended for holes drilled with conductive muds and provide most precise measure-
ments in medium- to high-resistivity formations. The laterolog used in the Zoharim
well was a Schlumberger Dual Laterolog-Rxo system (DLL). DLL tool eﬀectively cor-
rects for borehole eﬀects by taking simultaneous recording of formation resistivities at
shallow and deep depth and is also capable of functioning in wide range of resistivity
values (0.2 ohm*m to 40 000 ohm*m) [6].
Invasion
Invasion refers to the advancement of the drilling ﬂuids into the pores of sur-
rounding formation under the pressure introduced by the drilling mud [6]. This will
only occur if the formation is permeable and porous enough to permit the drilling
ﬂuid inside [15]. Invasion results in the contamination of the virgin formation by the
drilling ﬂuids which alters well log parameters, especially resistivity readings.
4
==PAGE:11==
In order to overcome the problems introduced by the invasion of the drilling ﬂuid,
the resistivity tool is equipped with sets of electrodes that can measure the resistivity
at diﬀerent depths i.e. shallow, medium and deep resistivity measurements. Thus the
extent of invasion can be measured.
If the resistivity of the drilling ﬂuid (Rmf) is higher than the true resistivity of the
formation (Rt) than the resistivity readings at shallow depth of investigation (LLS)
will be higher than the deep resistivity readings (LLD). The opposite case i.e. LLS
< LLD is diagnostic of hydrocarbons [17, p. 49]. In the case of Zoharim well, the
deep and shallow laterolog readings have very similar low readings, with less than 10
ohm*m measured resistivity for most of the second run. Such low readings suggest
that (i) there is no oil in the formation hence the formation is only water saturated
(ii) there is no invasion into the formation.
Limitations
Dual Laterolog used for the Zoharim well does not provide very shallow penetra-
tion measurements which can be included in the resistivity tool [15, p. 251].
Other common problems with DLL measurements are Delaware eﬀect and Gronin-
gen eﬀect which occur in highly non-conductive beds underlain by conductive sections
[6]. Delaware eﬀect appears in such cases as abnormally high resistivity for around
80 ft below the resistive bed [6]. Groningen eﬀect occurs for about 100 ft below thick
non-conductive sections and involves circulation of the current through the mud col-
umn which creates a negative potential on the reference electrode [6].
Zoharim well
In case of the Zoharim well, resistivity tool is used for obtaining true formation
resistivities (Ro). LLD measurements have a deep range of investigation which can
measure Rt. In our case we assume that Rt = Ro. This assumption is based on two
conditions:
• Resistivity values in the investigated zone are low and do not exceed 10 ohmm
⇒The interval is 100 % water saturated
• LLD (Deep) and LLS (Shallow) resistivity measurements are almost the same
⇒No invasion of mud ﬁltrate into the formation.
Delaware and Groningen eﬀect are not an issue in case of Zoharim well since there
is no alternating non-conductive and conductive sections as seen from the resistivity
logs (section 3).

2.1.2
Gamma Ray log
Principle of measurement
Gamma Ray (GR) log measures natural radioactivity of the formation. The source
of the gamma radiation comes from radioactive elements within the earths crust:
Potassium, Thorium and Uranium [6]. The energy and the amount of gamma ray
radiation coming from each of the elements is shown on Figure 2.2. As seen from
Figure 2.2 there is generally a higher probability of emitting a low energy GR wave
than the higher energy one. It is also apparent that the decaying processes of Uranium
and Thorium are sequential and lead through a number of unstable isotopes with
various energy levels whereas potassium decays directly to a stable isotope.
When passing through the formation Gamma Rays become attenuated by Comp-
ton scattering where the photons collide with atoms in the formation and are gradually
absorbed by their nuclei. The absorption rate increases with density of the formation
which means that denser formations will appear as less radioactive on the GR log.
There are two main types of GR logs: Spectral Gamma Ray log (NGS) and
Standard Gamma Ray Log (SGR). The former one measures the gamma radiation
coming from Potassium, Uranium and Thorium separately whereas the primer one
accounts for the sum of radioactivities of K, U and Th [6]. Gamma radiation coming
from the Zoharim well was measured using SGR.
Figure 2.2: Gamma ray spectra from Th, U, K [6]
Tools
6
==PAGE:13==
The GR sonde consists of a detector held against the borehole wall that measures
the gamma radiation coming from the formation. For this purpose sodium iodide
crystal scintillation counters are used (Figure 2.3) [17, p. 70]. Gamma rays pass
through the crystal causing a ﬂash that is then collected by a photo-multiplier. The
higher the amount of gamma radiation coming from the formation the more energy
is accumulated over the time of measurement. The amount of energy is subsequently
translated into a value of the log.
Figure 2.3: Scheme of a gamma ray tool [17]
Limitations
• Poor repeatability ⇒GR tool is highly dependent on the logging speed because
radiations from the formation are counted by the tool over a ﬁxed period of time
(time constant) [17, p. 72]. If the logging speed is too low the bed boundaries
will mix between several lithologies. If the logging speed is too high the rock
being counted at the beginning of a time constant will be diﬀerent from a rock
counted at the end of the time constant. In order to prevent these eﬀects the
logging speed should be around 30 cm during a time constant [17, p. 73].
• Large dependence on hole conditions ⇒Cavings aﬀect the log signiﬁcantly by
lowering the GR reading (see section on Caliper log) [15, p. 103]
• Depth of investigation ⇒GR tool has a rather shallow depth of investigation
with 90% of the signal coming from 15 cm and 25c m for 3 g/cm3 and 2 g/cm3
formation densities [16] respectively. Depth of investigation increases for lighter
formations i.e. 1 g/cm3 formation will receive 90% of response from around
50cm [16].

Zoharim well
The amount of cavings in the Zoharim well is insigniﬁcant (see section 2.1.6) and
the ones that are present are rather shallow (around 1 inch) therefore the GR log can
be well relied on in order to provide information on shaliness of the formation.
The GR tool used for the Zoharim well can be also well relied on in terms of depth
of investigation. Density of the lower part of Zoharim well is around 2 g/cm3 which
given low diameter of the borehole (around 6.4 inch) should give gamma ray readings
of the true formation.
2.1.3
Neutron log
Principle of measurement
Neutron tools measure porosity of the formation by responding to the amount of
hydrogen in the formation. The neutron tool uses a radioactive source of americium-
beryllium (AmBe) or plutonium-beryllium (PuBe) to emit high-energy (4 MeV) fast
neutrons into the formation [17, p. 135]. These neutrons collide with formations
material and are slowed down to low-energy thermic state (0.025 eV) [6, p. 135]. The
rate at which the neutrons are slowed down depends on the type of atoms encountered
by neutrons. The atom having the highest slowing down capacity is hydrogen since it
has similar size to neutron [6] and the two collide in an inelastic manner that causes
the neutron to lose most of its energy. The velocity loss will therefore be the highest
in a hydrogen rich medium e.g. water or oil.
Neutrons at the thermic state become gradually absorbed in the formation i.e.
are captured by nuclei of other atoms [15, p. 153]. Absorption of neutrons causes the
nuclei of capturing atoms to emit gamma rays. The absorption capacity depends on
the so-called capture cross-section which is a measure of the eﬀectiveness in capturing
the neutrons. The most eﬀective neutron absorbers are gadolinium and boron, and
the least eﬀective are carbon and oxygen [17, p. 135]. The eﬀect detected by the
tool is the slowing down of electrons. Increased hydrogen content of the formation
will result in a lower number of neutrons reaching the detector. Measurement of the
gamma radiation from the absorbed neutrons can be also included in the modern
tools but is not obligatory.

2.1.5
Spontaneous Potential log
Principle of measurement
SP log measures the natural potential diﬀerence between an electrode in the well
and an electrode at the surface of the earth (Figure 2.6a) [17, p. 33]. The electrical
currents originate from the electrical disequilibrium created by connecting formations
vertically [17, p. 33]. Three factors are necessary to provoke an SP current: (i) having
a conductive ﬂuid in the borehole [15, p.218] (ii) having a porous and permeable bed
surrounded by impermeable formation [17, p. 33] (iii) diﬀerence in salinity between
the borehole ﬂuid (mud ﬁltrate) and the formation ﬂuid (formation water) [15, p.
218].
Two electrochemical eﬀects responsible for creation of SP currents are liquid junc-
tion (diﬀusion) potential and shale potential [15, p. 220]. Liquid junction potential is
created when two solutions of diﬀerent salinities are in contact through a permeable
barrier e.g. sandstone. The solutions in the formation and mud ﬁltrate are usually
assumed as sodium chloride NaCl [17, p. 33]. In liquid junction potential the smaller
and more mobile Cl−ions diﬀuse through a permeable barrier easier than larger Na+
ions. This creates a disequilibrium in charges between the borehole and the forma-
tion, resulting in a negative charge (Figure 2.6b, upper drawing). Shale potential is
created when two NaCl solutions are in contact across a semi-permeable membrane
such as shale [17, p. 34]. Shale consists of layers with large negative surface charge
which acts as a selective barrier permitting only the positive Na+ ions through (Fig-
ure 2.6b, lower drawing). As Na+ ions diﬀuse across the shale membrane, a positive
charge is created in the borehole. It is important to acknowledge that the change of
potential will occur only at the bed junction [17, p. 35]. Therefore the changes in the
SP reading indicate in fact diﬀerent lithologies.

2.1.6
Caliper log
Principle of measurement
Caliper tool measures the diameter of the borehole [15, p. 88]. The measurements
are made by two, four or more caliper arms that are pushed against the borehole wall
[15, p.88]. The caliper tool converts the mechanical movement of the arms to an
electrical signal using a variable resistance. These variations are then translated into
an output that is recorded on a caliper log.
The caliper log is presented as a variation of the hole diameter with depth. This
can be visible as a decrease in borehole diameter, increase in borehole diameter or On-
gauge reading (Figure 2.7). On gauge reading means that the drill bit size matches
the hole size [17, p. 28]. Decrease in borehole diameter indicates mud cake build-up
or rock sloughing [17, p. 28]. Sloughing occurs in clay intervals which swell during
the drilling due to uptake of the drilling mud. An increase in the borehole diameter
is usually caused by cavings i.e. chunks of rocks that separate from the wall due to
poor drilling technique or high brittleness of the rock. Cavings have a profound eﬀect
on other logs and are used for quality control. The eﬀect of cavings on other log
measurements is as follows:
• Gamma ray reading decreases ⇒Absence of rock material means that there are
no radioactive isotopes of Th, K and U to emit gamma radiation.
• Density reading decreases ⇒Density of air ﬁlling the cavings is negligible com-
pared to formation density which causes the density readings to drop. If the
drilling ﬂuid of lower density occupies the empty space analogous situation oc-
curs.
• Neutron reading decreases ⇒In case cavings occur density tool will measure the
hydrogen index of air which is usually very low. Thus the reading will decrease.
• Sonic reading increases ⇒Cavings create empty space which can be either
ﬁlled by drilling ﬂuid or air. In both cases the ∆t values will increase since
sonic velocities in ﬂuids are lower than in solids.
15
==PAGE:22==
• Resistivity reading increases ⇒If the caving is signiﬁcant the current will be-
come transmitted through air which is an electrical insulator.
The units of the caliper tool are usually inches.
Figure 2.7: Typical caliper log responses. Source: [17]
Tools
The standard version of caliper tool consists of two arms, but dual caliper tool
with four arms, rotated 90◦are also available [15, p. 88]. In such case the caliper log
contains two sets of data from two pairs of calipers. Using a dual caliper log a great
deal of more information can be gained because the tool measures the borehole size
along two perpendicular axes.

As mentioned before, the two arm version of the caliper tool gives the borehole

about the caliper log is needed e.g. measurement of borehole volume or required
cement volume [15, p. 91]. However, this does not apply to the Zoharim well where
the cavings are rather small and the two arm version of the tool is suﬃcient.

2.1.7
Acoustic log
Principle of measurement
The sonic tool measures the sonic velocity of the formation [14, p. 204]. The value
reported in the acoustic log is the reciprocal of the velocity i.e. the travel time (∆t)
required to traverse through 1 feet of the formation [6].
The time for a sound wave to travel through the rock depends highly on elastic
properties and porosity of the rock, thus sonic log is primarily used as a porosity
indicator and elasticity indicator [26].
When a sonic measurement is conducted the tool emits sonic pulses from the
transmitter. A pulse is ﬁrst emitted into the borehole. It travels in the borehole
until it reaches the mud layer where it becomes refracted due to diﬀerent densities
of drilling mud and the formation. The refracted sonic wave subsequently travels
through the formation and at critical (slower) velocity is refracted back towards the
borehole where it is detected by the tool again [17, p.
93].
The receiver at the
tool can detect either the whole spectrum of incoming waves (Compressional wave,
Shear wave and Stoneley wave) or only compressional wave. An example of receivers
detecting full spectrum of sonic waves can be seen on Figure 2.8 where eight diﬀerent
receivers record the sonic pulse and the time of the arrival depends on the location
of the receiver.
The speed of sound in the formation depends largely on porosity and lithology,
through matrix material [6].
The more dense the matrix material the higher the
sound velocity and the lower the ∆t. Porosity in turn decreases the velocity of sound
(and correspondingly increases ∆t) [6]. This is because increased porosity means more
empty space within the rock. Speed of sound decreases in ﬂuids therefore empty space
introduced by increased porosity will always slow down the sonic wave, no matter what
kind of ﬂuid will occupy the pores. This will apply to both Compressional (P-waves)
and Shear (S-waves) waves. Increase in ﬂuid content will not directly impact the
S-wave velocity since this kind of waves cannot be transmitted through ﬂuid. There
will be however an indirect impact since increase in porosity reduces the amount of
solid rock through which S-wave can be transmitted. The typical values of velocities
and sonic transit times ∆t are listed in Table 2.2.
Using sonic log and obtained ∆t values one can calculate porosity by using the
so-called Wyllie equation:

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
SP log
Interpretation of the SP log was the hardest part of the project due to (i) changing
sand and shale baselines (see section A.3) (ii) lack of prior knowledge about mud-
ﬁltrate resistivity (see section A.4).
The approach was to ﬁrst assume the mud ﬁltrate resistivity using cementation
factors obtained from speciﬁc surface measurements done in previous master projects
[11, 12, 29, 21] and then using those values obtain a set of Rmf measurements that were
averaged to obtain mean Rmf. This mean Rmf was then used in order to calculate
cementation factors at core depths (see section A.5). Those core depth cementation
factors were then used to model continuous m throughout the well which enabled
calculation of porosity.
The situation occuring in the Zoharim well might be an example of shale baseline
shifts, phenomenon described in Schlumbergers log interpretation principles [6]. Shale
baseline shifts occur whenever formation waters of diﬀerent salinities are separated
by shale beds that are not perfect cationic membranes. As a result the shale baseline
changes making it hard to determine the SSP. In the case of Zoharim well there is
however also a change in lower sandstone baseline, therefore the varying SSP cannot
be attributed to shale baseline shifts exclusively and both of the bases have to be
modeled.
5.2
Resistivity log
Resistivity log was mainly used to obtain formation resistivities (Ro) which were then
used to calculate porosities.
The spike in resistivity at around 560 m.d. might be due to source rock in a
mature state. As explained in The Geological Interpreation of Well Logs [17, p. 65]
presence of a mature source rock can cause a large increase in resistivity readings.
33
==PAGE:40==
5.3
Cementation factor
Chalk from the Zoharim well has a high value of cementation factor, with an average
of mave,core=3.36 at the core depths. This high m value is probably a result of organic
rich shale ingrained in the formation and it is slightly higher than the range of the
values encountered in reservoirs of practical interest i.e. from 1.4 to 3 [27]. Shales
have high speciﬁc surfaces which result in high value of m as compared to chalk
were cementation factor is around 2 [17, p. 57]. Therefore increased m values most
probably mean that our formation is shaly. At 560 m.d. the m reaches as high as 5
(Figures A.5, A.6, A.7) which might indicate an interval of pure shale.
5.4
Porosity
With an exception of porosity obtained from averaged cementation factor mave,core
all of the modeled porosities follow core data closely. There are however a few dis-
crepancies and the ﬁrst one can be observed between around 430 and 450 m.d. where
porosities coming from ﬁtting GR and ﬁtting SP separate quite signiﬁcantly (Figure
4.1). The porosity from ﬁtting SP seems to follow the core data more precisely. The
second interval which experiences such discrepancies lays between 500 m.d. and 520
m.d. (Figure 4.1) where porosity modeled with GR log dives to around 0.25 whereas
the decrease in porosity from SP log is much more gradual. Lower porosity from
ﬁtting GR comes from the abrupt decrease of GR value in this interval (Figure 3.1)
which might be due to sudden switch in lithology from a carbonate with increased
shale content to a carbonate with decreased shale content. This abrupt change can
be also seen on the SP log (Figure 3.3), therefore the conclusion is that the porosity
calculated from ﬁtting the GR log might be more accurate in the interval 500 m.d.-520
m.d.
Porosity from averaged cementation factor mave,core (Blue line Figure 4.2) corre-
lates poorly with the core porosity values especially between 500 m.d.-525 m.d. and
also 560 m.d.-600 m.d. The possible reason is that the m values at the calibration
(core data) points tend to ﬂuctuate quite strongly in these two intervals (Appendix
Figure A.7) while mave,core assumes a constant value of 3.36 in all intervals. This
discrepancy between constant mave,core and m calibration points creates large space
for imprecision.
There is a strong decrease in porosity (Figures 4.2, 4.1) at around 420 m.d.,
accompanied by an uptick in resistivity log (Figure 3.4), low value of SP (Figure 3.3)
and a small decrease in ∆t reading (Figure 3.5). Since this point lays in the high
organic matter content interval (Figure 4.5, 4.6), it might be a narrow layer where
34
==PAGE:41==
the organic matter content is much increased. High amount of bitumen and kerogen
within the pores might decrease the porosity and make make the sonic waves travel
faster.
The peak in porosity at approximately 560 m.d. points out towards shale oc-
curence. Shales have high porosities (up to 45 %) [15, p. 17], high GR [17, p. 77]
(Figure 3.1) and high m values that result from high speciﬁc surface [28, p.189].
Gradual decrease of porosity in the Zoharim well (Figures 4.2, 4.1) might indicate
compaction. This is supported by a simultaneous decrease in ∆t value of the acoustic
log (Figure 3.5).
5.5
Grain density
Based on Figures 4.4 and 4.3 grain density can be divided into roughly three intervals:
• 330 m.d.-400 m.d. ⇒Grain density around 2.4 g/cc which is probably linked
to high calcite content and low organic matter (section 5.7). The points from
this interval align themselves mostly along the limestone line (Figure 4.10).
• 400 m.d.-475 m.d. ⇒Low grain density, high GR (Figure 3.1) and high organic
content (Figures 4.5, 4.6) point out towards high organic-rich shale occurrence.
• 475 m.d.-550 m.d. ⇒This interval is quite diversiﬁed. There is a large increase
in ρgr at around 480 m.d. and 520 m.d. with simultaneous low reading of HIorg
(Figure 4.9) and low GR (Figure 3.1). This could indicate limestone also shown
by high calcite content at 480 m.d. (Figures 4.5, 4.6). At 530 m.d. the grain
density declines sharply to around 1.9 g/cc while GR reading spikes (Figure 3.1)
which can be due to high organic matter content (Figure 4.5, 4.6). As expected,
the neutron- porosity bulk density cross-plot in this interval is highly dispersed
and not very useful for lithology identiﬁcation (Figure 4.10).
Grain densities are calculated based on obtained porosities and bulk density log
(see section A.7). This introduces a restriction to the measurements because bulk
density log reaches only until 550 m.d., therefore the grain density values can be also
obtained only until this point.
5.6
GR log
Gamma Ray log values throughout the investigated interval are high and exceed 100
API (Figure 3.1).
Carbonates in their pure form are not radioactive [17, p.
80]
therefore increased GR readings are most probably linked to the increased organic
content of the formation [17, p.77]. GR can be roughly divided into following intervals:

==PAGE:42==
• 330 m.d. to approx. 400 m.d. ⇒Increased shale fraction visible from high GR
readings;
• 400 m.d. to 470 m.d. ⇒GR exceeds 150 API which may be indicative of a layer
of organic-rich shales. This is conﬁrmed by the Gamma Ray-Resistivity cross-

• 470 m.d. to 560 m.d. ⇒Values of GR lower than 150 API with an exception

5.7
Organic matter content and calcite content
Calcite content log and organic matter log are complementary to each other and both
add up to unity (according to equation A.16 in section A.8). Both logs (Figures 4.5,
4.6, 4.7, 4.8) follow the grain density logs (Figures 4.3, 4.4) very closely and in that
sense are rather supplementary for the interpretation of the grain density and porosity
logs.
Same as in grain density, there are roughly three zones into which organic matter
content log and calcite content log can be divided to:
• 330 m.d.-400 m.d. ⇒Low organic matter / high calcite. The calcite content is
fairly consistent in this interval and oscillates around 60% of the rock matrix.
There are however peaks at approximately 400 m.d. and 375 m.d. which are also
reﬂected on the grain density logs (Figures 4.3, 4.4) and can indicate intervals
of clean limestone;
• 400 m.d.-475 m.d. ⇒High organic matter / low calcite content. As predicted
by high GR reading (Figure 3.1) and low grain density value (Figures 4.3, 4.4)
the calcite content will decrease on the expense of organic matter (Figures 4.5,
4.6, 4.7, 4.8);
36
==PAGE:43==
• 475 m.d.-550 m.d. ⇒Predominantly high calcite content. The exception is a
sharp increase in P org and V org at 530 m.d. (discussed in section 5.5) and
the low-calcite content interval from 500 m.d. to around 525 m.d. that could
indicate increased organic matter content.
At 520 m.d. mass and volumetric fractions calculated using constant mave,core =
3.36 display negative and higher than 1 values of organic matter and calcite content,
respectively. This abnormal values are due to large discrepancy between constant
mave,core and m from calibration point equal to around 2.5 at 520 m.d. (Figures A.7,
A.6, A.5). This large diﬀerence can create errors.
5.8
Neutron porosity log
Porosity measured by the neutron tool reaches 50-60% in most of the depth (Figure
3.2) which seems too high both for limestone and shale [18, 15]. For the Zoharim well
the neutron porosity log serves mainly to calculate Hydrogen Index of organic matter
(HIorg). The organic rich shale eﬀect can be well observed on the Hydrogen Index
of organic matter log (HIorg) (Figure 4.9). The HIorg log reveals what is already
observed on the grain density and organic matter content logs (Figures 4.3, 4.4, 4.5,
4.6) i.e. concentration of organic matter in the rock matrix will increase in the interval
from 400 to 475 m.d.
5.9
Sonic log
Sonic log was not used for lithology identiﬁcation. The reasons are that (i) sonic log
has too much of a span in ∆t values for diﬀerent lithologies in order to be considered
indicatory [17, p. 99] (ii) sonic log from the Zoharim well gives porosity values that are
much out of the scale (Figure 5.1). Porosity values calculated using Wyllie equation
with limestone as the rock matrix approach 70% porosity at shallower depths (Figure
5.1), which far exceeds maximum limestone porosity of 40% [15, p. 17]. If we use
kerogene as rock matrix, the porosity values become negative (Figure 5.1).

==PAGE:44==
Figure 5.1: Sonic log calculated using Wyllie equation with kerogene as the rock matrix (purple
line) and limestone rock matrix (green line)
Despite erroneous porosity values sonic log can be possibly used as an indicator
of compaction [17, p. 102]. From the acoustic log (Figure 3.5) we can see that there
is a regular decrease in ∆t values as we move down the well, which means that the
velocity of the formation is increasing.
At 417 m.d., 450 m.d. and 560 m.d. there are sudden decreases in ∆t values. At
417 and 450 m.d. there is no associated change with other logs, so this might be due
to electrical noise around the borehole. At 560 m.d. there is a simultaneous abrupt
change in GR, SP, modeled porosity and ρb reading that might be due to organic-rich
shale. On the other hand the decrease in sonic transit values is not typical of a source
rock which would rather increase ∆t values [17, p.104].
5.10
Caliper log
The caliper log is quite consistent within log run 2 and does not exceed 6 inches
(Figure 3.1). The only exception is 530 m.d. where a caving of around 10 inches can
be spotted (Figure 3.1). This is reﬂected on the bulk density log as a drop to 1.6 g/cc
(Figure 3.2). Rest of the logs do not display any visible eﬀect due to the caving.
5.11
Sources of error
The largest possible source of error in case of porosity measurements is lack of knowl-
edge about mud ﬁltrate resistivity and the possibility that our guess on Rmf is incor-
38
==PAGE:45==
rect. If mud ﬁltrate resistivity has a much diﬀerent value than the one we assumed
then it would reﬂect on all the calculated cementation factors and porosities.
Using modiﬁed GR and SP to model cementation factor can also lead to error.
The literature discussing the link between GR, SP and m is rather scarce. The use
of the two logs to model m was not sophisticated. It was rather based on observation
that the two logs line-up with the cementation factor from the calibration points,
therefore the cementation factor might follow the same trend.
The possible source of error will be the core measurements done by Shitrit et al.
[23] on core porosity measurements. Although the core porosity values in the original
work were determined using Coreval30 poro-permeameter and they should be correct,
one cannot exclude the possibility that the data provided by Shitrit et al. is imprecise.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The following conclusions can be made in respect to the organic rich chalk investigated
in the project.
1. The most important logs were the SP log and resistivity log since they enabled
estimation of porosity from Archie’s equation. Neutron log was also useful as it
enabled assessment of the Hydrogen Index of organic matter. Bulk density log
was used for calculation of the grain density values. Sonic log was useful only
as a possible compaction indicator.
2. mave,core averages m over the whole log run 2. This results in erroneous volu-
metric fraction and mass fraction values, as well as porosities that do not follow
core porosity data (as described in section 5.4). Thus the data obtained by
using mave,core should be treated with caution.
3. The lithology is a mix between limestone and organic-rich shale with varying
fractions of the two. Based on the volumetric fractions the interval from 330
m.d. to 400 m.d. is mostly limestone whereas the interval between 400 m.d.
and 475 m.d. is predominantly organic-rich shale. Rock matrix in the interval
from 475 m.d. to 550 m.d. is mostly composed of limestone with a narrow layer
of shale at 560 m.d.
4. The large spike in resistivity reading at 560 m.d.
might be due to mature
organic-rich shale. This is further supported by a decreased SP reading, in-
creased GR reading and increased porosity reading.
5. All of the rock in the log run 2 contains shale, visible from the increased GR
readings. High concentration of organic rich shale is most probably linked to
the 400 m.d.-475 m.d. depth interval.
40
==PAGE:47==
6. Neutron porosity-bulk density cross plot is not very useful for lithology deter-
mination. One would expect that there would be a larger diﬀerence between
the intervals speciﬁed above which would indicate regions of high shale content
/ low shale content. In practice not much diﬀerence can be observed and all
the points are rather clustered together.
7. Gamma Ray-Resistivity cross-plot is useful for detection of the organic-rich
shale interval (400 m.d.-475 m.d.) shown by increased GR values. The two
intervals from 330 m.d.-400 m.d. and 475 m.d.-550 m.d. have lower GR values,
and vary mainly with respect to resistivity (Figure 4.11). This, however, should
be attributed to the change in water resisitivity (Figure A.9) rather than the
rock matrix.

In addition to organic matter content we can calculate the Hydrogen Index of the
organic matter HIo. We know that the total Hydrogen Index of an interval can be
calculated using the neutron-porosity log:
HI = φNHIw + (1 −φN)HIm
(A.17)
Where: φN is the porosity obtained from the neutron-porosity log; HIw is the
Hydrogen Index of water given as Hw = 1−0.4P where P is the salinity in ppm
106 ; HIm
is the Hydrogen Index of rock mineral which is in our case calcite, so HIm = 0 (Table
2.1).
The Hydrogen Index of water HIw will decrease throughout the well (Figure A.10)
since the salinity tends to increase (Figure A.8).

combination of Hydrogen Indexes of its components and obtained porosities:
HI = φHIw

(A.18)

rock forming mineral (in our case calcite); fo is the organic matter content calculated
using equation A.13; HIo is the Hydrogen Index of the organic matter.
