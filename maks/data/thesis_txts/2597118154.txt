Abstract
Thioreductin Reductase (TrxR) is a part in the hydrogen peroxide scavenging system, which is
a reducing system required for nucleid acid synthesis and redox regulation. TrxR regulates the
intracellular redox environment by reducing Trx and maintains the proper disulﬁde state, which
is crucial for cell survival. In this thesis, three diﬀerent bacteria (E. coli, L. lactis, and S. aureus)
are studied to determine the most stable conformation between the ﬂavin oxidising TrxR, ﬂavin
oxidising/reducing TrxR, or ﬂavin reducing TrxR using molecular dynamics simulations and
small-angle X-ray scattering guided molecular dynamics (SAXS-guided MD) simulations. The
diﬀerence between the states is due to the rotation of the NADPH domains. It is found that
using the SAXS-guided MD simulations to force rotation of the NADPH domain is a bigger
challenge than expected. Here, a violation of the structure might enable the SAXS-guided
MD simulations to change the conformation between ﬂavin oxidising and reducing. However,
coupling SAXS-guided MD simulations with center of mass pulling or enforced rotation may
enable the possibility of determining energy landscapes using computational chemistry. Using
MD simulations, it is found that L. lactis TrxR was the most stable bacteria, and that both the
ﬂavin oxidising and ﬂavin reducing TrxR are about equally stable as both are found to have
many interactions in the interdimeric interface and between the FAD ligands and the rest of
the protein.

SECTION 1
Introduction
Proteins are polypeptides consisting of typically ﬁfty to several hundreds amino acids and fold
into protein-speciﬁc three-dimensional structures in which the amino acid sequence determines
the native structure. A protein is not rigid, as it undergoes a variety of fast vibrations and
slower structural rearrangement, where the latter is often referred to as conformational tran-
sitions[1].
Proteins carry out most of the intricate biological processes required to sustain
life. Motions are often involved in the functionality of the protein out the biological processes.
The motions cause conformational dynamics in the proteins, and they play an immense role
in enzyme catalysis, allosteric regulation, and molecular recognition[2]. The conformational
dynamics can be summarised using an energy landscape, where each minimum corresponds to
a particular conformational substate structure; each of these minima corresponds to the low
energy state.
The project described in this thesis is based on the earlier work made by Nicklas Skjoldager on
his PhD project and the crystal structures reﬁned by Pernille Harris. The methods employed in
this thesis have been inspired by the PhD project of Line Abildgaard Ryberg in regards to using
SAXS-guided MD simulations to cause conformational changes. From here, a manual has been
written by Julie Bentzen on how to conduct MD simulations and SAXS-guided MD simulations
using the GROMACS simulation programme to guide new GROMACS users through the MD
simulations and SAXS-guided MD simulations.
In the project described in this thesis, the stability of the dimeric Thioreductin Reductase
protein in its Flavin oxidising, Flavin reducing, and a mixed conformational state in three
bacteria; namely, Escherichia coli, Lactococcus lactis, and Staphylococcus aureus, have been
studied.
Thioredoxin Reductase is a dimer in the pyridine nucleotide-disulﬁde oxidoreduc-
tase family, which are helping to maintain thioredoxins in their reduced state through dithiol-

SECTION 2
Theory: Molecular Dynamics Simulations
Computer simulations are performed to achieve a more in-depth understanding of the prop-
erties of molecules assemblies in terms of their structure and the interactions between them.
Computational simulations serve as a complement to conventional experiments in which knowl-
edge that cannot be achieved otherwise is achieved. The simulations act as a bridge between
microscopic length and time scales and the macroscopic world of the laboratory, as illustrated
in Figure 1. An estimate at the interactions between the molecules is used as input to the com-
putational simulation. Hereby, obtaining predictions about the properties of the protein. The
simulations act as a bridge between theory and experiments, by conducting simulations a theory
can be tested as the experimental results can be compared to those achieved from a simulation.
On the other hand, computational simulations allow performing theoretical experiments which
are diﬃcult or even impossible in the laboratory, such as working at extreme temperatures or
pressures. Furthermore, the experiments in laboratories might be time-consuming and costly.
Figure 1: Schematic overview of the usage of MD simulations.
There are two main families of computer simulation techniques, namely Monte Carlo (MC)- and

molecular dynamics (MD) simulations. Moreover, there exists a wide range of hybrid techniques
which combines features from both main techniques[4]. Molecular dynamics simulations were

Using MD simulations enables the understanding of dynamical
properties of a system. A simulated system can be represented in multiple levels of detail,
such as at atomistic representation or coarse-grained representation. Atomistic representation
is the best reproduction of an actual system, whereas coarse-grained representation is prevalent
if larger systems or long simulations are required. The models have been visualised in Figure

Additionally, solvent representation is also a key issue when deﬁning a system. The solvent
can be represented either implicitly or explicitly[5]. The implicit solvent method makes the
simulations faster than explicitly treated solvent. The simulation time can be decreased by ap-
proximating the discreet solvent as a continuum, thereby reducing the number of particles in the
system to keep track of. However, the speed of the simulation comes at the expense of the ac-
curacy[7]. On the other hand, the explicit solvent method recovers most of the solvation eﬀects
of the solvent. This includes the hydrophobic eﬀect from entropic origin[5]. When the solvent
is treated explicitly, then interactions between all pairs of solute and solvent atoms are explic-
itly computed. A method often employed is an algorithm PME (Particle mesh Ewald). PME
is needed to correctly calculate electrostatic forces of the atomistic MD simulation. Another

2.1 Ensembles
When performing MD simulations, diﬀerent ensembles can be used to describe the system.
If for instance, the goal is to study temperature induced unfolding of proteins, which require
temperature control, the simulation should reproduce an isothermal ensemble. The canonical
NVT ensemble can be used to reproduce such conditions in which the number of particles,
volume and temperature are ﬁxed and thereby under control. Diﬀerent ensembles and their
constant properties can be seen in Table 1. The state of a classical system at any time t is
completely determined by the location of one point in phase space. Phase space is the space
in which the phase will occupy, thereby sampling over all microstates in this phase.
The
position of an atom in space can be described by the spatial coordinates q1, q2, ..., whereas the
momentum of an atom can be described by p1, p2, .... Thus, the phase space is a conceptual
space constructed of a conceptual orthogonal space of 6N dimensions. One point in phase space
is the state of the system, a microstate. A collection of a large number of microstates is called
an ensemble of systems in phase space, which corresponds to the same macroscopic state. The
macroscopic state is the average of all the microstates. Hence, ensembles can be analysed to
derive thermodynamic properties of the system i.e. entropy or free energy[5].

2.5 Periodic Boundary Conditions
In simulations of bulk phases, the presence of explicit interfaces in a simulation box have a
profound eﬀect on the resulting properties of the system as interactions between atoms and
the wall are signiﬁcant for the small size of the system compared to bulk conditions. Instead,
periodic boundary conditions can be implemented to avoid the ﬁnite-size eﬀects of the boundary
box to imitate real space conditions. The idea is that the simulation disregards the walls in
order to eliminate the boundary eﬀect. Employing periodic boundary conditions implies that
the particles in the simulation are enclosed in a box, which is mentally replicated to inﬁnity
by translation in all three cartesian directions, as visualised in Figure 15. This leaves no ‘hard
walls’ between the boxes, hence allowing the atoms in the simulation box to leave/enter the
original simulation box. This has been visualised in Figure 16

3.1 Small/Wide Angle X-Ray Scattering
Small- and wide-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS/WAXS) is an experimental technique in which
structural information on biomolecules in solution can be obtained. SAXS experiments can
be used for detecting structural correlations, and extracting information on molecular param-
eters such as radius of gyration and molecular mass. SAXS can also be used to construct a
low-resolution envelope around the solute to get an overall idea of the surface of the protein.
WAXS, however, is used to probe the fold of proteins, detect ligand binding and to charac-
terise heterogeneous ensembles of peptides and proteins. WAXS can be used for detection of
conformational transitions of proteins either triggered by photodissociation or photon-induced
isomerisation of a dye using time-resolved WAXS. Both implicit and explicit solvent methods
have been developed for calculating a SAXS/WAXS scattering curve based on a structure. Im-
plicit solvent methods share a common feature in which the procedure requires the deﬁnition
of two or three free parameters. Those parameters could be the excess density of the solvation
shell, a free parameter for the overall excluded volume, and a scaling parameter for the radii
of the atomic groups. It should be noted that the scaling parameter is optional. Using an
implicit solvent method can be a risk, as there is a risk of overﬁtting as the parameters are not
easily measurable; hence the parameters between the solutes may diﬀer. On the other hand,
explicit-solvent MD simulations lead to a more realistic solvent distribution on the surface of
the solute and for the excluded solvent; however, at the consequence of a higher computational
cost.
The more realistic solvent distribution obtained from explicit-solvent MD simulation
avoids unknown ﬁtting parameters, thereby reducing the risk of overﬁtting as potentially ob-
served when using implicit solvent methods. For both implicit- and explicit solvent methods,
the excluded solvent term is either absorbed into the atomic form factors of the solute atoms
or is computed from a pure buﬀer simulation[23].

SECTION 4
Theory: Conformational Dynamics
Proteins are dynamic entities which orchestrate diverse cellular functions. The proteins do this
either by acquiring a speciﬁc folded structure or by adapting their conformation in response to
an external stimulus[33]. Proteins carry out most of the intricate biological processes, which
are required to sustain life[2].
To perform their function, proteins can undergo structural
rearrangements, namely conformational transitions (CT),[34].
The transitions which occur
between distinct structural (conformational) states are of interest as conformational motions can
control, enable, or constitute their speciﬁc biochemical functions2[2, 35]. A high-dimensional
energy landscape of conformational transitions has been sketched in Figure 25. The energy
landscape determines the dynamics of a protein, where the continuous ﬂuctuations of proteins
can cause them to fold, unfold, and to change conformations all the time leading to the existence
of an immense number of diﬀerent conformations. The stable sub-states can in some cases
be functional by being active/inactive, bound/unbound, or open/closed states[36, 37]. The
existence of sub-states can be sketched by a protein energy landscape, in which the protein
landscape is hierarchically organised with valleys within valleys. The conformational sub-states
at the lowest energy are stable conformations. The transition states in the energy landscapes
are the maxima[37].

4.1 Levinthal ”Golf Course” Landscape
Before the energy landscapes used by researchers nowadays, simpler models have been proposed
over the years.
At a time, many structural biologists and biophysicists were inﬂuenced by
Levinthal’s paradox3. Trying to solve the paradox, Levinthal suggested that a native protein
structure is not determined by stability such as thermodynamics, but by kinetics. Thus, the
protein follows a ’fast’ folding pathway, and its native fold is just the end of this pathway,
whether it is the most stable fold or not. In other words, Levinthal assumed that the native
structure corresponds to a rapidly reachable minimum of free energy rather than the global one,
which has been illustrated in Figure 29a. Levinthal ”golf course” landscape illustrates that a
random search could not ﬁnd the native state of the global energy minimum at the bottom

5.2.1
Conformations
TrxR can be crystallised in two formations, namely Flavin Reducing (FR) conformation and
Flavin Oxidising (FO) conformation[55]. The redox-active disulﬁde is adjacent to FAD and is in
a buried state, as such it cannot interact with the protein substrate Trx and the nicotinamide
ring of NADPH. Thus, a pathway for the electrons is lacking, and a conformational change
during catalysis is needed. Two domains rotate 66o relative to each other[44, 54]. This rotation
and counter-rotation place the nicotinamide ring of NADPH or the redox-active site disulﬁde
adjacent to FAD, which allows for the redox-active site dithiol to move from its buried state to
a surface-exposed state, in which it can react with the protein substrate Trx[54]. Hence, the
FR conformation is required for reduction of the protein substrate Trx by the TrxR dithiol.
This is a peculiar feature of prokaryotes, plants, and yeasts[44]. Thereby, the ﬂavin oxidative
conformation (FO) is the conformation in which the active-site disulﬁde, CXXC motif, is close
to FAD where it can oxidise FAD during catalysis and is in the proximity of Trx[54, 55]. The
reductive conformation (FR) is the conformation in which the nascent dithiol is solvent-exposed,
and the pyridine nucleotide-binding site is close, which allows for the reduction of FAD. In the
NADPH binding site, there are two cysteines residues in the range 135-140 as their position
diﬀers between species. For TrxR from E. coli, the two residues Cys135 and Cys138 are the
redox-active disulﬁdes, and it has two additional cysteines in contrast to S. aureus and L. lactis,
which links the FAD and NADPH domains. Additionally, Cys138 in TrxR from E. coli provides
thiolate for an initial attack to the S-S of oxidised Trx[54]. This is illustrated in Figure 34.

those two amino acids restricting their ﬂexibility. This restriction makes it diﬃcult to obtain
α- helical structure and they prefer to be in β-sheets[56].

6.3.1
Buﬀer Simulation
To be able to run a SAXS calculation and a SAXS-MD simulation, a simulation of the buﬀer
is a necessity. As in experimental SAXS experiments, it is important that the buﬀer exactly

excipient concentration (unless the excipients only bind to the protein and are not present in
bulk) A simulation box is created of a chosen size and is solvated. Ions (Na+ and Cl−) are
added to the water box to ensure an ionic concentration of 0.15 M to achieve physiological
conditions. Energy minimisation and MD simulations are performed as described above.

Radius of gyration (RoG) of a protein is the root mean square distance from each atom of the
protein to their centroid. It is used as an indicator of protein structure compactness. The tighter
packed the protein is, the smaller value of RoG; in which α-helical and β-sheet regions can pack
more tightly compared to loop regions. Thus, this can be used as an indicator of protein fold,
stable/unstable protein, and if the protein is whole due to PBC or if the protein has been split
into original protein and mirror protein in the simulation box. The RoGs calculated from the
MD-simulations have been plotted in Figure 52.

7.4 SAXS-guided MD Structure
It was attempted to force a conformational change within LlTrxR to study the energy needed
for transition using SAXS-guided MD simulations. Thereby, the most stable conformation can
be identiﬁed. Additionally, it can be seen if the transition between the two structures gives an
intermediate conformation, namely the FOFR conformation. The SAXS-guided MD simulation
protocol applies a force to make the structure align with the wanted structure using a SAXS
intensity curve. Everything that has been tried and tested will be gone through in this section.

Jochen Hub, who has written the SAXS-guided MD simulation package to GROMACS. The
email correspondence can be seen in Appendix 11.8 Email 1. It was found, that driving a
rotation using SAXS data can be quite tricky as the pair distance distribution function does
not change much during a rotation. Therefore, a large violation of the SAXS data needs to
be allowed before the protein can lock in to the correct conformation. That is, in state A, the
SAXS-derived force might not yet point in the direction of state B where the conformation

8.5 SAXS-guided MD Simulations
SAXS-guided MD simulations are quite useful when a conformational change is wanted. It
is seen in literature that SAXS-guided MD simulations performs well on structures with a
larger conformational change than a rotation such a conformational transition between open
and closed state. The methodology is used to investigate the role of said chain ﬂuctuations and
conformational sampling. SAXS was found by P. Chen et al. to be complementary with MD
simulations to probe conformational transition[23, 73]. However, the conformation change is
limited to open/closed structure and not to rotations. The conformational change is brought
on by forcing a change and thus resulting in an increase in potential energy. The potential
energy is going from 0 kJ/mol to 250 kJ/mol or less, which has been conﬁrmed by Jochen Hub.
The increase of energy is due to the breakage of interactions between the area being forced
to change and the part of the protein around the area. SAXS-guided MD simulations can,
however, be quite protracted as there is much trial-and-error to ﬁnd the right conditions for
Technical University of Denmark
Page 174 of 238
==PAGE:185==
J. Bentzen
s153065
Master Thesis
July 31, 2020
the SAXS-guided MD simulation to work such as the SAXS force and q-values. If too much
force is applied, the protein structure will start to unfold as was experienced.
To overcome the issue with the conformational change being a rotation of the NADPH do-
main, enforced rotation was tried. The idea to perform enforced rotation was found in litera-
ture. Here, C. Kutzner has studied how to drive rotary motions in atomistic simulations[22].
Enforced rotation, as with SAXS-guided MD simulations, also requires trial-and-error. The
rotation was achieved, but the only rotation achieved is similar to SAXS-guided MD structure
output where the NADPH domain started to come quite far away from the rest of the protein
and not to be in contact with the protein. This may suggest that enforced rotation also can
lead to unfolding of the protein. In theory, if the NADPH domains is forced to rotate, the
similar structure of FOFO and FRFR conformations may be changed. The conformations due
to a forced rotation might have changed in a manner enabling the usage of SAXS-guided MD
simulations. Thus, the SAXS-guided MD simulations can exert SAXS forces on the rotated
domain to change the conformation to the wanted rotation of NADPH, which is in similar
fashion to the opening and closing of a protein structure. When performing the SAXS-guided
MD simulation on the structure after enforced rotation, the NADPH domain would be guided
towards the SAXS curve given as target; thus, the SAXS force should be able to make the con-
formation change. When the interactions keeping the NADPH domain in place breaks when
enforcing rotation, this energy should be similar to the energy barrier observed in SAXS-guided
MD simulations. Thus, it should be possible to create an energy landscape and determine the
most structurally stable conformation.

SECTION 9
Conclusion
The MD simulations have been run for 200 ns. The RMSD stabilises after 40 ns, and the con-
formations look similar as the only diﬀerence is the rotation of the NADPH domains as seen
in Figure 49. From the geometric properties for all three bacteria strains, it can be concluded
that all bacteria in all their conformations are stable and show similar geometric properties.
The RMSF ﬂuctuates for all conformations around 2 ˚A, and the RoG ﬂuctuates between 26.5
˚A and 29 ˚A for all conformations. Here, the FOFO conformations ﬂuctuate around 27 ˚A and
the FRFR conformations around 28 ˚A. Moreover, the SASA for all three bacterial TrxRs in all
of their studied conformations are in the range 270-310 nm2. The RMSD, on the other hand,
diﬀerentiate slightly. The RMSD of L. lactis in all conformations lie around 2.5 ˚A, which the
FRFR conformation of E. coli also does. However, the FOFO and FOFR conformations lie
around 6.5 ˚A. S. aureus ﬂuctuates around an average of 4.5 ˚A.
The FOFO and FRFR conformations of L. lactis are both quite stable conformations. The
structures have low RMSD values of 2 ˚A. Many interactions support the low RMSD values
as they stabilise the conformations. The interactions stabilise both the interdimeric interface,
where all domains are in contact with the opposite chain, but also the interactions keeping the
FAD ligand in the FAD pocket. The FOFO conformation of TrxR oxidises the FAD ligand,
whereas the FRFR conformation of TrxR reduces the FAD ligand. Thus, it makes sense that
both structures are stable.
The stability of E. coli diﬀers between the conformations. The FRFR conformation of EcTrxR
is more stable than the FOFO and FOFR conformations, which is due to more interactions
between the interdimeric interface. The RMSD further supports the stability of FRFR with
the RMSD of 2 ˚A in contrast to FOFO and FOFR that have an RMSD of 6 ˚A. The FOFR
conformation is partly made of the FOFO conformation and the FRFR conformation. Thus,

Future Perspectives
For future work, it would be interesting to look at the crystal symmetries and determine if
they aﬀect the diﬀerence in crystal packing, and thus the distance between the monomers.
This especially counts for E. coli as the FOFO and the FRFR conformations have diﬀerent
crystal classes, namely P 63 2 2 and C 1 2 1, respectively. Regarding the distance between the
monomers, it would be interesting to redo the interface analysis with a cutoﬀof 4 or 5 ˚A so see
if the interactions observed in the FRFR conformation appear.
For future work, it would also be interesting to make a new homology model of FRFR confor-
mation as the current model has a loss of secondary structure. When a new model is made, the
analyses should be re-performed. S. aureus is, as L. lactis, predicted to have an oxygen pocket.
Therefore, this bacteria is of interest to see what the pocket means for the overall stability and
function of TrxR.

Center of mass
def center_of_Mass(selection):
’’’
DESCRIPTION
Calculates the center of mass of a given selection
’’’
model= cmd.get_model(selection)
x,y,z=0,0,0
totmass = 0
for a in model.atom:
m = a.get_mass()
x+= a.coord[0]*m
y+= a.coord[1]*m
z+= a.coord[2]*m
totmass += m
cM = numpy.array([x/totmass, y/totmass, z/totmass])
return cM

11.6 Analysis Script Used For Heatmap
The heatmap illustrates the interactions between two selections. Multiple steps are performed
in this analysis; counting number of interactions within a certain distance (VMD), sorting of
the counts, and generation of the heatmap (Matlab). The interactions can be made into a
b-factor ﬁle, which can then be illustrated on the structure (PyMOL). The scripts have been
made by L.A. Ryberg.
A PDB structure ﬁle from the MD simulation is generated containing 120 frames. The frames
are distributed over the simulation with a skip of 160 frames. Only 120 frames were chosen
as VMD could not handle more frames. The frames were chosen based on the RMSD plots
(Figures 50 and 76), and the frames chosen were from after 40 ns. The command used to
generate the PDB structure is:
PDB Structure Generation
echo 1 24 | gmx trjconv -s 04_*_ions.pdb -f 08_*_md.xtc -center -pbc nojump
-t0 40000 -skip 160 -n index.ndx -o prot_count.pdb
# 1: protein, 24: protein and FAD ligand

Hi Julie,
Driving a rotation with SAXS data is probably difficult, because the pair
distance distribution function does not change much during a rotation.
In such cases, you might even first need to allow a larger violation of the
SAXS data before your protein can "lock in" to the correct conformation.
That is, in state A, the SAXS-derived force might not yet point in the
direction of state B where you need to go (similar to a so-called
"free energy golf course landscape").
As counter example, when a protein is opening or closing with a large change
in Rg, the SAXS-derived force immediately pushes the protein in the right
direction, which makes things much easier.
Maybe you have to first help the simulation in some other way, e.g. by
pulling along some collective coordinate (rotation or so), so you get
close to you state B (close to the golf course minimum), and then turn
on the SAXS forces.
I hope this helps a bit.
