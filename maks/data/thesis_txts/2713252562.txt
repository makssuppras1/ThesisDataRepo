PHYTIC ACID IN PLANT-BASED “DAIRY” ALTERNATIVES 
Abstract 
 
This master thesis is a part of the project ‘REPLANTED’, which aim is to detect and remove 
undesired compounds in plant-based “dairy” alternatives by processing and fermentation. The 
project is in collaboration with the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and the following 
companies: Thise, KMC, Chr. Hansen, and FOSS Analytical. This master thesis takes part in 
the project in collaboration with FOSS Analytical. The aim of this thesis is to help the food 
industry to simplify the quality control of plant-based “dairy” products by establish the use of 
Milkoscan (supplied by FOSS) on plant-based products. The first task of the thesis is to identify 
relevant substances and specify the relevant substance(s) used for further analysis. The 
substance(s) will be selected on the note that they can be measured using near-infrared(NIR) 
spectroscopy. Afterwards detecting the substance(s) in plant-based “dairy” alternatives using 
High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) system at DTU, and lastly measurement 
on Milkoscan, to identify the substance(s) using a more simplified method. 
 
The relevant substance identified for plant-based “dairy” alternatives are phytic acid (PA). PA 
is a well-known antinutrient, since it has the ability to form complexes with e.g. minerals as 
zinc and thereby decrease the bioavailability of the desired minerals obtained from food 
consumption, and in worst case cause lack of important minerals. 
 
The phytic acid content was detected using reverse-phase HPLC system. Four different brands 
of oat drinks (Naturli’, Jörd, Spir, Oatly) with different content of oat (16, 13, 12 and 10%) was 
analysed, as well as a pea drink(2.5% pea protein) from the brand ‘Dryk’, and a plant-based 
drink containing both oat(8.7%) and pea protein(‘Alpro’). The results of the phytic acid content 
were with the highest content found to be 110.57 mg/g for Naturli’, 98.86 mg/g for Jörd, 100.51 
mg/g for Spir, 102.73 mg/g for Oatly, 104.6 mg/g for Dryk, and 95.86 mg/g for Alpro. 
However, the validation of the method showed a p-value among the groups at 0.18-0.22, 
meaning a significant difference among the plant-based “milk” alternatives was found. 
 
Further analysis of the plant-based “milk” alternatives was made using Milkoscan at FOSS 
Analytical, to test the possibility to detect phytic acid using near-infrared(NIR) spectroscopy. 
The result was promising with a distinctive peak around 1060 cm-1, meaning it seems that it is 
possible to measure phytic acid in plant-based “milk” alternatives using Milkoscan. However, 
the method requires more computer modelling, to estimate the content and obtain reliable 
results. Therefore, further investigations of the measurements of phytic acid in plant-based 
“milk” alternatives are needed to implement the method for quality control in the food industry.

1 Background 
 
There is a growing trend of plant-based diets as well as a growing population and an awareness 
of the climate crisis and how to contribute to minimize the CO2 emission. The food production 
is a source that have a high impact on the carbon-footprint and therefore, there is a need of food 
alternatives with less impact on the environment as well as alternatives for the growing trend 
of vegan and vegetarian diets. Furthermore, to contribute and take part in a more sustainable 
future and meet the sustainable development goals. In context to this project the goals number 
2, 3, 12, and 14 on zero hunger, good health and well-being, responsible consumption and 
production, and life on land, respectively, will be meet. (‘THE 17 GOALS | Sustainable 
Development’ n.d.) One of the food items that is a growing trend on the marked are alternatives 
to cow’s milk.  The market is expected to grow at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 
8.8% for 2021-2031, and CAGR of 6.0% for 2016-2020 for plant-based “milk” (PBM) was 
found. (‘Plant-Based Milk Market Size, Sales Analysis & Opportunity | FMI’ n.d.) This new 
alternative profit the population with lactose intolerance and lactose allergenicity, as well as 
the environment, and other concerns regarding intake of cow’s milk as hormonal contents, the 
use of antibiotics in cattle’s transferred to the milk and ethical considerations. (Reyes-Jurado 
et al. 2021) Therefore, there is a need of and a demand for an alternative to the highly consumed 
cow’s milk. A consumers demand that have increased 60% from 2012 to 2021. Cow’s milk is 
known for its nutritional value of minerals and vitamins such as calcium, kalium and vitamin 
B as well as protein, fat, and lactose. Plant-based “milk” alternatives (PBMA’s) does not 
contain the same nutritional values and therefore, there may be issues going from a diet 
consuming milk to a diet consuming PBMA’s, since cow’s milk is considered a complete food 
and plant-based “dairy” products are not. (Reyes-Jurado et al. 2021) This project aims to 
investigate content of relevant substance(s) related to PBMA’s. Furthermore, to investigate 
analysis of the content of the identified relevant substance(s) with the commercial equipment 
Milkoscan® from FOSS analytical A/S, to be used for the food industries to contribute to a 
user-friendly way of estimate the content of the relevant substance(s) in PBMA’s, and thereby 
simplify the quality control in the food industry.    
 
This leading to the hypothesis of this study, which are that: 
1) The relevant substance(s) chosen for investigation can be estimated quantitatively using 
HPLC systems. 
2) The estimated quantities of the substance(s) can as well be estimated with Milkoscan 
supplied by FOSS analytical A/S, thus be used as a simple method for the food industry 
to determine the substance(s) in relevant product of plant-based “milk” alternatives. 
 
1.1 Plant-based “milk” alternatives  
 
The global market for plant-based “milk” alternatives (PBMA’s) has become a multi-billion-
dollar business and a value of approximately 26 billion USD in 2023. (Tangyu et al. 2019) 
PBMA’s are plant extracts from e.g. legumes, oilseeds, cereals, vegetables, and nuts, and 
therefore come in many varieties. A general classification of the PBMA’s are made into five 
categories (no stated definition): 1) Cereal based: Oat milk, rice milk, corn milk, and spelt milk, 
2) Legume based: Soy milk, peanut milk, lupin milk and cowpea milk, 3) Nut based: Almond 
milk, coconut milk, hazelnut milk, pistachio milk, and walnut milk, 4) Seed based: Sesame 
milk, flax milk, hemp milk, and sunflower milk, 5) Pseudo-cereal based: Quinoa milk, teff 
milk, and amaranth milk. (Sethi, Tyagi, and Anurag 2016) The extract is water-soluble and 
with water it creates an emulsion that resembles cow’s milk. Since the main ingredient of plant-

The drinks are prepared by crushing the material and extraction of the soluble parts into water. 
However, before going through the extraction procedure, cereals are conditioned, which mostly

certain vitamins and minerals and contain bioactive compounds as antioxidants, dietary fibers, 
and phytoestrogens. Essential mono- and polyunsaturated fatty acids, minerals (Fe2+, Zn2+, 
Mg2+), and phytoestrogens are found in legumes. The soy-based drinks are the dominant 
PBMA’s in the Western world, but coconut-, sunflower seed-, chickpea-, lupine-, hemp-, pea-
, and rice drink are available on the market as well. However, the different drinks differ 
significantly in composition and flavour. (Tangyu et al. 2019)

and color, one would think that also the nutrients of PBMA’s are similar or even healthier since 
it is plant-based. However, this may not be the case. A study by Astolfi et al. have analysed 
and compared the elements in dairy milk and PBM. (Astolfi et al. 2020)

1.3.1 Tannins  
Plant tannins are a major group of antioxidant polyphenols found in food and beverages that 
attracts research interest with its multifunctional properties to human health. Tannins are 
oligomers of flavan-3-ols and flavan-3, 4-diols that are concentrated in the bran fraction of 
legumes. Tannins are categorized into four major groups: 1) Gallotannins, 2) Ellagitannins, 
3)Complex tannins, and 4) Condensed tannins. (Sieniawska and Baj 2017) Tannins exhibits 
antinutritional properties by impairing the digestion of various nutrients and preventing the 
body from absorbing beneficial bioavailable substances. Tannins can also bind and shrink 
proteins, and tannin-protein complexes may cause digestive enzymes inactivation and protein 
digestibility reduction caused by protein substrate and ionizable iron interaction. Tannins are 
represented in small quantities in legumes and no content are reported in grains, hence tannins 
are not of interest for further investigation in this study. (Popova and Mihaylova 2019)  
 
1.3.2 Trypsin inhibitors  
Trypsin inhibitors occurs in a wide range of foods like chickpeas, soybeans, red kidney beans, 
adzuki beans, mung beans and other representatives of the Leguminoseae, Solanaceae, and 
Gramineae families. Trypsin inhibitors redound to the loss of trypsin and chymotrypsin in the 
gut, thus preventing protein digestion. Excess trypsin synthesis and burden on sulfur-
containing amino acids in requirement of the body are due to the release of cholecystokinin 
triggered by trypsin inhibitors. Trypsin is reported with a content (6.7 g/mg) in legumes (e.g. 
peas). (Popova and Mihaylova 2019) Although trypsin inhibitors are found in most legumes, 
the levels present tend to vary, and low levels are reported in broad beans, peas, mung beans, 
lupins, and a few varieties of kidney beans. (Bhoola 1970) 
 
1.3.3 Alpha-amylase inhibitors 
Inhibition of alfa-amylase is considered a strategy for the treatment of disorders in carbohydrate 
uptake, by reducing insulin levels, as well as dental caries and periodontal diseases. Amylase 
inhibitors are substances that bind to alpha amylases making them inactive. The primary 
function of inhibitors is protecting the seed against microorganisms and pests. The other 
function is the inhibition of the endogenous alfa-amylase. The instability of this inhibitor under 
the conditions of the gastrointestinal tract and being a very heat-liable constituent results in its 
failure to be used as starch blocker. It is used to control human diabetes type two and finds 
several applications in the food industry. (Popova and Mihaylova 2019)  
 
1.3.4 Saponins  
Saponins are C27 or C30 natural product glycosides, which have surface-active properties and 
often foam strongly when shaken with water. They are distributed widely in plants, an can 
occur in very large amounts.  Saponins consist of one or more linear or branched sugar chains 
linked to an aglycone. Three main classes of these glycosides are reported: (1) triterpene 
glycosides, (2) steroid glycosides, and (3) steroid alkaloid glycosides. (Hostettmann and 
Marston 2005) Some saponins (steroid or triterpene glycoside compounds) can be used for 
food while others are toxic. Saponins with a bitter taste are toxic in high concentrations and 
can affect nutrient absorption by inhibiting enzymes (metabolic and digestive) as well as by 
binding with nutrients such as zinc. Saponins are naturally occurring, and in the presence of 
cholesterol, saponins exhibit strong hypocholesterolemic effect. Also, saponins can lead to 
hypoglycemia or impair the protein digestion, uptake vitamins and minerals in the gut, as well 
as lead to the development of a leaky gut. (Popova and Mihaylova 2019)

1.3.5 Oxalates  
Oxalate can be found in small amounts in many plants. Foods rich in oxalate are usually minor 
components in human diets but are present in higher quantities in seasonal diets in certain areas 
of the world. The highest levels of oxalates are found in the foods as kale, spinach, beet, 
soybeans among others. (Morrison C S 2003) Oxalic acid can form soluble (potassium and 
sodium), or insoluble (calcium, magnesium, iron) salts or esters called oxalates that are 
commonly found in plants i.e. leafy vegetables or synthesized in the body. Insoluble salts 
cannot be processed out of the urinary tract once processed through the digestive system. 
Calcium oxalates can have a deleterious effect on human nutrition and health by accumulating 
kidney stones. Cruciferous vegetables (kale, radishes, cauliflower, broccoli), as well as chard, 
spinach, parsley, beets, rhubarb, black pepper, chocolate, nuts, berries, and beans are some of 
the foods with high amounts of oxalates. Most people can induct normal amounts of oxalate 
rich foods, while people with certain conditions (enteric and primary hyperoxaluria) need to 
lower their oxalate intake. In sensitive people, even small amounts of oxalates can result in 
burning in the eyes, ears, mouth, and throat. (Popova and Mihaylova 2019) 
 
Considering the antinutrients mentioned above, as well as the content in legumes and 
grains(Table 2), the substance of interest in this study will be phytic acid in PBMA’s with 
sources from oat and peas. A detailed description of phytic acid as an antinutrient is found in 
section 1.5. 
 
1.4 Plant-based “milk” alternative: Oat drink  
 
Since the substance of interest – phytic acid – is specified, the PBMA’s for analysis are defined 
as well. Since oat drink is commonly consumed in Denmark and the content of phytic acid are 
reported in some of the highest concentration compared to other substances (Table 2), it seems 
reasonable to do the analysis of phytic acid in oat drinks. 
 
Oats is a good source to use for alternative plant-“milk”, since it contains dietary fibers, 
phytochemicals, and high nutritive value, which are associated with health benefits as 
hypocholesterolemic and anti-cancerous properties regarding beta-glucan, functional protein, 
lipid and starch components and phytochemicals. The proteins in oats are with a good amino 
acid balance and a source of antioxidants and polyphenols. B-glucan is a soluble fibre and can 
increase the solution viscosity and can delay gastric emptying time, increase gastrointestinal 
transit time. The hypocholesterolemic effect of oats are known to reduce the total and LDL 
cholesterol. The overall content of oats is 60% starch, 11-15% total protein, 5-9% lipids, 2.3-
8.5% dietary fibre and 0.54% calcium. All of it contributing to oat being a good and interesting 
source for a plant-based milk alternative. (Sethi, Tyagi, and Anurag 2016)

1.5 Phytic acid  
 
One of the antinutrients found in high concentrations in the plants are phytic acid, also found 
with the common names PA, myo-inositol hexakisphosphate and IP6, which is a natural plant 
compound with a unique structure that is responsible for its characteristic properties. (Oatway, 
Vasanthan, and Helm 2001) Phytic acid occur naturally in plants and is a primary storage form 
of phosphate in foodstuffs and can account for up to 60-80% of seed total phosphorus. (Dost 
and Karaca 2016) It is found in most cereal grains, legumes, nuts, oilseeds, tubers, pollen, 
spores, and organic soils. The phytic acid content can range from 5 to 30 mg g-1 and are found 
to reach up to 60 mg/g. From Table 2 the different concentration is found from a study review 
from 2019, and the given concentrations are found in smaller quantities (386-714 mg/100g). 
(Popova and Mihaylova 2019)  
 
The phytic acid is mainly localized in the aleurone layer (90%) and the remaining 10% is 
localized in the embryo. (Dost and Karaca 2016) Which means that phytic acid is primarily 
found in the husks of the plants. The form is found in phytin or phytate salts and are most 
concentrated in the bran of the grains. Phytic acid binds to minerals as iron, zinc, magnesium, 
and calcium and thereby inhibit the absorption of those minerals, resulting in highly insoluble 
salts that are poorly absorbed by the gastrointestinal tract and leading to lower bioavailability 
of minerals. Phytates also inhibit digestive enzymes like pepsin, trypsin and amylase. (Popova 
and Mihaylova 2019)

The chemical structure of phytic acid is seen from Figure 1. It is a six-carbon cyclic

Phytic acid is the hexaphosphate ether of myo-inositol. It is not a definite compound but a term 
of various salt forms of phytic acids in its natural state. It occurs frequently in the form of 
calcium magnesium salt, but some plant phytins contain more potassium than calcium, and 
elements as Mn, Fe, or Zn may be present. (Frank 2013) For example phytate is the calcium 
salt of PA and phytin is the calcium/magnesium salt of PA. These three forms: Phytic acid (as 
free acids), phytate and phytin have been used interchangeably in most of the literature linked 
to phytic acid systems. (Oatway, Vasanthan, and Helm 2001)

Minerals and proteins are positively charged components, which allows interaction between 
the negatively charged phosphate groups. Also, metals groups as Fe, may bind with one or

Phytic acid is formed from to different pathways; lipid-dependent and lipid-independent. In the 
lipid-dependent pathway, phospholipase C catalyses the hydrolysis of phosphatidylinositol 4,5-
biphosphate (PtdIns(4,5)P2) into myo-inositol P3 (InsP3 or IP3), which is then progressively 
phosphorylated to InsP6. And in the lipid-independent pathway, myo-inositol(3)P1 undergoes 
a series of phosphorylation until phytic acid is formed. (Silva et al. 2021)  
 
Phytic acid and its derivatives bind cations between pH 3 and 7 and are considered to be anti-
nutrients capable of forming insoluble complexes with minerals, proteins, enzymes and starch, 
plus it can interfere with calcium (Ca), magnesium(Mg) copper(Cu), iron (Fe) and zink (Zn) 
absorption by the gut, especially when there is inadequate ingestion of these minerals. Despite 
the antinutritional effects of PA, it is an important storage compound of phosphor (P) in seeds 
and play a role in germination and seedling vigour, in addition to other roles such as antioxidant 
functions. (Silva et al. 2021)  
 
In vulnerable groups such as children and pregnant women in deprived regions, who commonly 
ingest inadequate amounts of Ca, Fe, Mg and Zn, PA can magnify nutrient cation deficiencies 
in population with cereal- and legume-based diets. Feed that is corn- and soybean-based 
possesses high PA concentrations, and usually need to be supplemented with phytases or 
directly with P to meet the needs of livestock. Because of the presence of PA in faeces, these 
animals release high levels of P into the environment, and are therefore contributing to water 
pollution by eutrophication. (Silva et al. 2021)  
 
In cereals PA concentrations vary from 0.08-6% in ground fraction and protein products, in 
breads and other products, 0.05%-3.29% in ready-to-eat food products and 0.06-1.38% in 
infant cereals. In oat concentrations were observed between 0.6% and 0.8%. (Silva et al. 2021) 
Different techniques can be used to disable the antinutrients and/or other undesirable food 
components. It is essential to improve the quality of the food. The techniques that can be used 
are soaking, cooking, fermentation, radiation, germination/sprouting and chemical treatments.

1.6 Principles of High-Performance Liquid chromatography (HPLC) 
 
The principle of chromatography is using either solids or liquids to separate desired substances 
into different parts based on the polarity. There are different types of chromatography but 
having a liquid phase and a mobile phase is common for all the types. HPLC are working with 
very small quantities and the detector is much more sensitive. The different types of high-
performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) include normal phase, reverse phase, size 
exclusion and ion exchange. When operating with normal phase HPLC the stationary phase is 
polar, and the mobile phase is non-polar. Polar molecules are retained on the stationary phase, 
while the non-polar molecules moves down the column with the mobile phase. In case of size 
exclusion, the stationary phase particles are porous. As a result, small molecules enter the pores 
of the beads, and large molecules remain outside. Separation occurs based on the molecule 
size. For ion exchange the stationary phase has ion exchange resin/has an ionic charge. If the 
molecule of interest has a positive charge, the stationary phase is kept negative and the other 
way around. The method of this project is based on reverse phase HPLC. Here the stationary 
phase is non-polar, and the mobile phase is polar. The non-polar molecules are retained in the 
column and the polar molecules moves down the column with the mobile phase. (Harris 2015)  
 
 
Figure 3: HPLC system (Agilent 1200 series) (‘HPLC System - Atlantic Lab Equipment’ n.d.) 
When it is given the stationary phase is non-polar and the mobile phase is polar, it is indicating 
the stationary being weakly polar and the mobile phase to more polar than the stationary phase. 
And there will be a higher mobile phase strength when the solvent is less polar. 
 
As seen from Figure 3 the HPLC system contains different component. This include a solvent 
reservoir, where the mobile phase is found. In this project the solvent is 30% acetonitrile in 
water including 0.1 M HNO3 (polar solvent). Furthermore, it includes a solvent pump which is 
used to pump the mobile phase in the system/column with a specific flow rate. The high 
pressure makes the method much faster compared to column chromatography. (‘HPLC - 
ChemistryViews’ n.d.) The flowrate of the HPLC system regarding this project is 1 ml/min. 
(Dost and Karaca 2016) The injector/autosampler which are responsible for injecting the 
samples the system. 50 µL is injected to the system in case of this project. The column of the 
system is represented as the stationary phase. The column is normally located so the 
temperature can be controlled. Liquid chromatography are typically conducted with packed 
column, so that the a solute molecule does not have to diffuse far to encounter the stationary 
phase. (Diffusion in liquids is slower than diffusion in gases)

FOSS Analytical A/S is a company providing analytical equipment and measurements for food 
and agricultural production. Their mission is to “contribute to the sustainable use of our plant’s 
agricultural resources and thus to the nutrition and health of the people of the world”. (‘The 
FOSS Mission: Sustainable Use of Agricultural Resources’ n.d.) One of their products are the 
MilkoscanTM FT3, which can measure different parameters in liquid and semi-solid dairy 
products, including milk, cream, whey, yoghurt, creme fraiche, protein concentrates including 
whey protein, chocolate milk as well as plant-based drinks such as soy, almond, oat, rice, 
coconut and pea milk. (‘MilkoScanTM FT3’ n.d.)  
 
Since the Milkoscan are very user-friendly, and only a sample/product of e.g. plant drink is 
needed to obtain a lot of information of the analysed product. It may be of interest to have the 
knowledge of what the Milkoscan do. Therefore, a short introduction to NIR spectroscopy is 
given. 
 
The principles of the Milkoscan are based on near infrared (NIR) spectroscopy. NIR 
spectroscopy is a non-destructive method that can be used for analysis of samples at its original 
state, furthermore, explore inside a material. It permits non-contact analysis, and analysis using 
an optical filter as well as it is possible to apply NIR spectroscopy to samples in various states, 
shapes, and thickness. NIR spectroscopy is suitable for analysis of aqueous solutions since the 
water bands is weaker in the NIR spectrum compared to the IR spectrum. (Ozaki and Morisawa 
2021) 
 
NIR spectroscopy involves both electronic transitions and vibrational transitions. Regarding 
the electronic spectroscopy it is difficult to discriminate the NIR region from the  visible region, 
since the two regions are seamless in the electronic spectra. However, it is easier to distinguish 
the vibrational spectroscopy in the NIR region from the IR region, since NIR deals with bands 
arising from overtones and combination modes and IR deals mainly with bands due to 
fundamentals, and band appearing from overtones and combinations are weak. Bands in the 
NIR (electronic transitions and vibrational transitions) region are weak or very weak, which is 
one of the characteristics. The NIR region offers a highly transmitting window to radiation, 
which makes it valuable for application. (Ozaki and Morisawa 2021) 
 
1.8 Principal Component Analysis (PCA) 
 
A short introduction to principal component analysis (PCA) are needed to understand the 
theory behind the data obtained from FOSS Analyticals A/S. The information from FOSS is 
processed at FOSS’s software and PCA are performed from the data-output/spectra from the 
Milkoscan. (Cf. section 3.8)  
 
PCA is a method capable of providing an overview of complex multivariate data. It can be used 
for revealing relations between variables and relation between samples, clustered samples, 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
18
PHYTIC ACID IN PLANT-BASED “DAIRY” ALTERNATIVES 
detecting outliers, finding, and quantifying patterns, as well as generating new hypothesis. (Bro 
and Smilde 2014) 
 
PCA is a dimensionality-reduction method often used to reduce the dimensionality of large 
data sets. Meaning a large set of variables are transformed into a smaller set of variables, still 
containing the needed information in the large data set. The variables are standardized so every 
variable contributes equally to the analysis. The standardization is done by subtracting the 
mean and dividing by the standard deviation for each value of each variable. After the 
standardization the variables will be transformed to the same scale.  
 
The next that is needed is to see if there is any relationship between the variables, meaning how 
the variables of the input data set are varying from the mean with respect to each other. This is 
done with a covariance matrix, e.g. p x p, where p is the number of dimensions. It is a symmetric 
matrix that has as entries the covariance associated with all possible pairs of the initial 
variables. In short, the covariance matrix is a table that summaries the correlation between all 
the possible pairs of variables. 
 
The principal components (PCs) of the data are determined by computing the eigenvectors and 
eigenvalues. The principal component are new variables that are constructed as linear 
combinations or mixtures of the initial variables. PCA put the maximum possible information 
in the first component (PC1), then the maximum remaining information in the second 
component (PC2) and so on. These combinations are done so new variables (principal 
components) are uncorrelated. Since the PC are constructed as linear combination of the initial 
variables, they don’t have any real meaning, and one can say that they are less interpretable 
than the input data. However, there are as many PC’s as variables in the data. 
 
The PCs represent the directions of the data that explain a maximal amount of variance, 
meaning the lines that capture most information of the data. The larger the variance (carried by 
a line) the larger dispersion of the data points along the line and the more information it has. 
The PCs are new axes that provide the best angle to see and evaluate the data, so the differences 
between the observation are better visible. (‘A Step-by-Step Explanation of Principal 
Component Analysis (PCA) | Built In’ n.d.)

2.1 Determination of phytic acid in plant-based “milk” alternatives with HPLC 
 
Different methods of detecting phytic acid is found from literature. Methods which are based 
on complexometric titration of residual iron (III) after phytic acid precipitation, colorimetric 
methods based on the reaction between ferric ion and sulfosalicylic acid, as well as methods 
using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). (García-Estepa, Guerra-Hernández, 
and García-Villanova 1999; Latta and Eskin 1980; Lehrfeld and Morris 1992; CAMIRE and 
CLYDESDALE 1982) The challenge regarding detection of phytic acid is that there are no 
known specific reagents for detecting phytic acid and it has no spectrum in the ultraviolet(UV), 
100-400 nm, visible or infrared(IR), 700-1000 nm, region. (Frank 2013; ‘Infrared Radiation - 
Infrared Spectroscopy - Advancing Materials’ n.d.) Dost et al. (2006) have determined phytic 
acid in wheat and wheat products by reverse-phase HPLC and finds the method reproducible 
and accurate with a standard deviation and relative standard deviation between 0.72-1.20 µg/ml 
and 1.40-2.98%, respectively, with a sample size of 10 using 50 µg/ml. (Dost and Tokul 2006)  
 
Dost et al. (2016) is evaluating PA in tea and nut products by reverse-phase HPLC with some 
adjustments on the method from phytic acid determination in wheat and wheat product from 
2006. They found that the method is good with relative error between 4.52 and 8.00%. 
Therefore, the method used in this project will be based on the evaluated methods from the 
studies of Dost et al. (Dost and Tokul 2006; Dost and Karaca 2016)

2.3 Validation of the method 
 
Different parameters are used to validate the chemical analytic method. Common parameters 
to validate are selectivity/specificity, linearity/dynamic range, detection limit/quantification 
limit, measurement range, ruggedness, repeatability/reproducibility, trueness/bias, and 
measurement uncertainty. However, one should determine which parameters are relevant to 
validate on. The authors of the method (Dost et al. 2016) have found good linearity over the 
concentration range of 1-150 mg/L with a correlation coefficient value of 0.9938. The 
effectiveness is presented in terms of relative standard deviation (RSD) and found to 0.62% for 
5 mg/L phytic acid and 0.88% for 50 mg/L phytic acid. Repeatability in terms of RSD was 
ranging between 1.58-7.88% for a number of 10 samples of 50 mg/L and 0.98-4.63% for 10 
samples of 5 mg/L. Accuracy was found to be good with a relative error between 4.52 and 
8.00% (n=10, for 50 mg/L).

3 Results and discussion 
 
In following section, the results of the analyses will be presented. The results will be given, 
including test results from the test analysis that have been made to approach the chosen method. 
Furthermore, discussions of the results will be included.  
 
3.1 Test of column 
 
An initial test of the column is made regarding the set-up of the HPLC system (cf. section 
2.1.1). The result of the test is a good peak, with a retention time at around 2.8 min. According 
to the certificate toluene have a retention time at 3.054 min. The retention time is therefore 
approximately 0.2 min from the expected output. However, the result was read from the 
software (‘Instrument 1’) connected to the HPLC and not the specified in Chromeleon 7.2.10, 
which was the software used for data processing of the actual data. Therefore, the minor 
deviation was accepted. The result of the test from the supplier of the column are found in 
Appendix 1. 
 
3.2 Test analysis of standard solutions for calibration curve 
 
The first analysis is done to get an overview and an idea of the standard calibration curve, and 
thereby how the preparation of the standard solution is made. The peak of the iron(III)-phytate 
complex are detected at a retention time less than 3 min.  
 
The first results of the samples to be used for a calibration curve is not as expected. The 
standard solution is made according to the procedure from (Dost and Karaca 2016) with 1, 5, 
10, 25, 50, 75, 100, 125 and 150 mg/L phytic acid as the final concentrations. Since Dost et al. 
2016 have a linear calibration expressed by y=-0.8350x+127.7623(x104) it was expected to be 
a similar result obtained from the calibration curve of this project. However, this is not the case, 
and it is assumed that the preparation of the samples somehow went wrong. One of the issues 
could be that there was pipetted 1 µL to make the standard calibration with 1 mg/L phytic acid 
in the final concentration, since the calculation was based on a final volume of 1 mL. It may 
be difficult to pipette such small amounts and therefore, the final volume is increased to 5 mL, 
and the smallest amount pipetted are thereby 25 µL. (See Appendix 2 for calculations) 
 
Another issue could be the way of stirring the samples. As stated by Dost et al. 2016, the 
samples are stirred for 2.5 hour at 40C, and the equipment may not be sufficient. As the 
equipment for stirring is not specified in the method (Dost et al. 2016), it is not possible to 
follow the same procedure and the stirring unit are therefore chosen based on what is available 
in the laboratory, and most sufficient. Also, it is stated that the stirring is done in water bath at 
40C and not in an incubator as was used in this work. However, the stirring unit used for metal 
replacement is the only solution available in the laboratory and as an alternative the speed of 
the stirring are increased. The issue could therefore also be that the heat is not transferred to 
the sample tubes as it would be if heated in a water bath.  
 
Further adjustment of the method are the steps involving centrifugation and heating for 5 
minutes in water bath. The final centrifugation step in the sample preparation (Appendix 3) are 
excluded, since no precipitate occurs during the process and since the samples are prepared in 
glass tubes, the samples had to be transferred from glass tubes to plastic tubes suitable for the 
centrifuge. This could also be a potential error since the samples are filtered through air and

4 Conclusion 
 
The main objective of this study was to identify relevant substances regarding plant-based 
“milk” alternatives as well as detect the concentration levels of the substance in chosen plant-
based “milk” alternatives. Furthermore, to estimate the content of the substance on Milkoscan 
supplied by FOSS to make it a future simple and rapid method to be used for the food industry. 
 
The identified substances were phytic acid. The phytic acid content was detected using a 
reverse-phase HPLC system, and the four brands of oat drinks: Naturli’, Jörd, Spir, and Oatly, 
with different content of oat (16, 13, 12 and 10%) was analysed. Also, the pea drink from 
Dryk(2.5% pea protein), and a plant drink containing both oat(8.7%) and pea protein (Alpro) 
was analysed. The results of the phytic acid content were with the highest content found 110.57 
mg/g for Naturli’, 98.86 mg/g for Jörd, 100.51 mg/g for Spir, 102.73 mg/g for Oatly, 104.6 
mg/g for Dryk, and 95.86 mg/g for Alpro. However, the validation of the method showed a p-
value among the groups at 0.18-0.22, meaning a significant difference among the groups was 
found. Therefore, it is concluded that the results may not be reliable and further investigations 
on the method is needed to state any concluding remark on the content of phytic acid in plant-
based “milk” alternatives.  
 
The estimation on Milkoscan at FOSS analytical showed promising results. The spectra 
indicated that phytic acid may be found at a wavelength around 1060 cm-1 as well as previous 
studies using NIR also concluded distinctive peaks regarding phytic acid.  
 
 However, the hypothesis of the study can to some extent not be meet. The substance identified 
– phytic acid – can be detected quantitively using HPLC system. However, as the method 
showed results not in compliance as well as the validation showed significant difference of the 
results obtained, further work on the method is needed for adjustment of the method. The 
hypothesis stating estimation of phytic acid on Milkoscan may as well not be meet, since 
quantities of phytic acid has not been found from this study. However, the result from this study 
seems to indicate that a future method for detection phytic acid using Milkoscan is possible. 
 
Therefore, further investigations regarding the method for HPLC and NIR spectroscopy 
(Milkoscan) is needed to conclude on the specific method that may be the most sufficient to 
for quantification of phytic acid in plant-based “milk” alternatives as to be used as a simple 
and rapid method for the food industry.
