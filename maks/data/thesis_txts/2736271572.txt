Abstract 
 
This thesis investigates the integration of multi-source data for enhancing Traffic Analysis Zone 
(TAZ) delineation, with a focus on cycle mobility in urban environments. Traditional TAZ 
delineation methods often rely on limited data sources, which do not adequately capture the 
dynamic nature of urban mobility. By employing a novel methodology that integrates GPS data, 
OpenStreetMap (OSM) inputs, and European Space Agency (ESA) satellite imagery, this 
research proposes a more accurate and granular approach to defining TAZs. Using Copenhagen 
as a case study, this research applies a two-phase clustering strategy involving agglomerative 
clustering and Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN). This 
approach not only refines TAZ boundaries but also decreases the percentage of intra-zonal trips, 
enhancing the overall accuracy of traffic analysis. The thesis demonstrates how modern data 
sources can significantly improve the reliability of urban planning tools, providing insights that 
are crucial for sustainable urban mobility planning. The findings suggest that multi-source data 
integration can effectively refine TAZ delineation, contributing to more responsive and adaptive 
urban planning strategies.

Introduction 
Background 
Urban and transportation planning play crucial roles in enhancing the sustainability, efficiency, 
and liveability of urban environments. Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZs) are essential units in 
transportation modelling, aiding in the analysis of travel demand, land use patterns, and 
infrastructure development. Traditionally, the delineation and analysis of TAZs have relied on 
limited data sources, such as government surveys and manual traffic counts The transportation 
and land use modelling and planning professionals tend to consider the administrative boundaries 
as TAZ due to the readily available demographic data (O. Weerasinghe & S. Bandara 2000). 
However, these may not capture the dynamic and complex nature of urban mobility fully. 
 
Big data and advancements in data collection methods, have opened an opportunity to enhance 
the delineation and analysis of TAZs. With the use of multiple data sources, GPS tracking, 
OpenStreetMap (OSM), and satellite imagery, have the potential to offer rich insights into 
mobility patterns and urban dynamics. combining these diverse data streams can significantly 
improve the accuracy, granularity, and relevance of TAZ analysis, informing more effective 
urban planning and policy making. 
 
Problem Definition 
Despite the potential of multiple source data, integrating and analysing these varied data types 
within the context of TAZ delineation remains challenging. This research seeks to apply current 
knowledge of data-driven procedures for TAZ analysis to utilise the strengths of multiple data 
sources. The central research question is: Can multiple source data, with an emphasis on GPS 
tracking and land use characteristics, be effectively integrated to enhance the delineation and 
analysis of Traffic Analysis Zones for cycle mobility? 
 
The goal of this research is to apply and validate a multiple data source procedure for Traffic 
Analysis Zones analysis specifically for cycle mobility, that can support responsive and 
sustainable urban planning. Applying a systematic and objective method to delineate TAZs 
based on actual spatial data patterns, reducing the need of subjective decisions in the zoning 
process.

Prior Work 
Challenges and Optimization in TAZ Delineation 
The delineation of Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZ) is a pivotal element in travel demand 
modelling. Traditional methods aim to maximize the homogeneity of socio-economic and 
geographic characteristics of zones while minimizing intra-zonal trips. However, data collection 
technologies and the advent of big data sources have introduced new opportunities and 
challenges for TAZ definition. This development has prompted a re-evaluation of traditional 
models to incorporate additional land-use and spatio-temporal mobility features. (Martínez et al. 
2009) developed a comprehensive methodology for TAZ delineation, embedded in geographic 
information systems software, aimed at minimizing information loss when discretizing travel 
demand data. Their algorithm focuses on balancing statistical precision, geographical accuracy, 
and the percentage of intra-zonal trips in the resulting Origin-Destination (OD) matrix. This 
approach suggests significant improvements in OD matrix estimates compared to practices based 
on administrative parameters.

Multiple source Data Integration 
Recent studies highlight the importance of integrating multiple source data for TAZ delineation 
to capture homogeneous mobility behaviours and land use characteristics within each TAZ. 
(Castiglione, M, et, al. 2023) proposes a stepwise method to utilize features extracted from 
various data sources, including floating car data, OpenStreetMap and satellite imagery. Their 
methodology involves core Basic spatial unit clustering, and the optimization of zoning systems 
through information loss calculation, resulting in TAZs that more accurately reflect the spatial 
distribution of travel demand and land use characteristics. 
 
Agglomerative Clustering 
Agglomerative clustering is a hierarchical clustering method used to group objects based on their 
similarity. It's a bottom-up approach where each observation starts as its own cluster, and pairs of 
clusters are merged as one moves up the hierarchy. This method is essential for understanding 
data structures and has been applied in various fields, including spatial analysis and geographic 
information systems (GIS). (Tokuda, Comin, & Costa, 2022). In this context, agglomerative 
clustering has been employed to classify urban areas into clusters based on characteristics like 
land use type, aiding urban planning and decision-making 
 
Potential for Traffic Analysis Zone (TAZ) Delineation 
Agglomerative clustering presents a data-driven approach to TAZ delineation, offering the 
potential to define zones based on actual traffic flow data, land use patterns, or socio-economic 
characteristics, rather than solely on administrative boundaries or subjective criteria. This method 
can potentially improve TAZ delineation by integrating multiple data sources, providing a 
systematic and objective delineation method based on spatial data patterns, and adapting to 
various scales of analysis. 
 
Challenges and Future Directions 
While agglomerative clustering holds promise for enhancing TAZ delineation, it faces 
challenges: 
Scale Sensitivity: The scale of analysis can significantly influence clustering outcomes, making it 
critical to determine the appropriate scale for meaningful TAZ delineation. 
Data Quality and Availability: The effectiveness of the clustering approach depends on the 
quality and completeness of the underlying data. Inconsistent data can lead to inaccurate zoning.

Interpretation and Validation: Clusters produced by agglomerative clustering need interpretation 
and validation against actual traffic patterns and land use configurations to ensure they’re usable 
for transportation analysis. 
 
DBSCAN's Robustness in Traffic Data Analysis 
Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise or DBSCAN, is particularly well-
regarded for its ability to handle noise and outliers, which is crucial for traffic data often littered 
with anomalies due to irregular events. Unlike k-means or hierarchical clustering, DBSCAN 
does not force every data point into a cluster, thus allowing for the identification and exclusion of 
outliers. Another advantage of DBSCAN is its capacity to discover clusters of arbitrary shapes. 
This feature is particularly beneficial for traffic analysis zones, where data point distributions 
may not conform to a spherical pattern. DBSCAN's ability to identify clusters based on density, 
regardless of their shape, make it more versatile than other methods which assume spherical 
clusters. (Ester, M, et, al, 1996) 
 
How DBSCAN works 
DBSCAN iterates through each point in the dataset, undertaking the following steps: 
Classification of Points: Points are classified into three types: 
• Core Points: A point is considered a core point if it has at least MinPts (minimum points) 
are within distance ε (epsilon, a specified distance), including the point itself. 
• Border Points: These are points that lie within distance ε of a core point but have fewer 
than MinPts within their ε-neighborhood. 
• Noise Points: Points that are neither core nor border points. 
Cluster Formation and Expansion: 
• Starting from a core point, the algorithm explores each neighbouring point within ε 
distance. If these neighbours are also core points, the process recursively continues, 
allowing the cluster to expand. 
Handling Noise: 
• Points that do not belong to any cluster are labelled as noise and are typically discarded 
from further analysis, depending on the application.

Methodology 
This section outlines the approach taken for delineating and analysing Traffic Analysis Zones 
(TAZs). The methodology is structured into sequential stages, starting from data collection to the 
application of spatial analysis techniques, and finally, the implementation of clustering 
techniques to delineate TAZs. 
Data Collection and Preparation 
The study utilised Global Positioning System (GPS) data from bicycle trips, capturing mobility patterns 
within the urban environment. This data was sourced from Hövding users.  
Hövding is essentially an airbag for urban cyclists. This device is worn around the neck like a collar and 
is designed to deploy and inflate in the event of an accident, offering a helmet-like protection that 
envelops the wearer's head. 
GPS data, collected via Bluetooth sensors, was used to determine trip trajectories. The data was collected 
from 11223 users, containing 104063663 raw trips. Each data point within the GPS dataset contains 
timestamped geographical coordinates, denoting the trajectory of bicycle movements. These trips have 
been collected from Denmark as well as small regions within western Sweden and northern Germany. 
 
Spatial Indexing with H3 
To facilitate the spatial analysis, the case study area was transformed using Uber's H3 
hierarchical geospatial indexer. H3 partitions geographical areas into Hexagonal Spatial Units (HSU). 
Offering a unique approach to conduct spatial data analysis that is both scalable and consistent across 
different resolutions A principal advantage of the H3 system is that for a given scale, all hexagons are 
approximately equal areas. A second advantage is that with hexagons, the HSU are, continuous and 
compact with neighbours in all directions and are equidistant, this is not always true for other shapes. 
This discretization allows for the aggregation of GPS data points OSM land use characteristics and 
European space agency into identifiable spatial units, facilitating a granular analysis of the spatial 
distribution of cycle mobility patterns. 
 
OpenStreetMap Extraction 
OpenStreetMap (OSM) is a prime example of volunteered geographic information (VGI), which is a form 
of crowdsourced geographic data. 
OSM is a collaborative project to create a free, editable map of the world, built by a community of 
mappers who contribute and maintain data about the physical features of a region. 
OSM data is stored in a format that includes points (for specific locations), lines (for linear features like 
roads and rivers), and polygons (for areas like parks and lakes). Each of these elements can be tagged 
with detailed information, such as the name of a place, the type of road, or the nature of a land use, 
making the dataset versatile and extensive. However, the quality of the data can vary depending on the 
level of participation and the mappers' expertise (Neis, P., & Zipf, A. 2012). Despite these challenges, 
OSM continues to be a critical resource for spatial data. (Haklay, M. 2010)

European Space Agency World Cover Data 
The Integration of European Space Agency (ESA) Satellite Imagery for Land Cover Analysis introduces a 
robust layer of land cover information enhancing the granularity of urban land use analysis. This 
integration allowed for a more nuanced understanding of the urban environment, The ESA's Earth 
observation missions, notably the Copernicus Sentinel satellites, offer high-resolution imagery that is 
instrumental in accurate and up-to-date land cover mapping (Abdikan, S, et, al. 2016). These satellite 
images give an overview of the earth's surface, categorised into 10 classes. Satellite images were pre-
processed using QGIS to match the projection of the H3-indexed study area. 
 
Clustering Model 
Following the spatial indexing, features belonging to each HSU 
are quantified and aggregated. Features such as the density of 
bicycle trips from GPS data, and the proportion of different land 
use categories from OSM and ESA data. The model structure has 
been like that laid out in (Castiglione, M et al 2023) 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
To identify distinct TAZs based on the aggregated land use 
features, we introduce a hierarchical approach to delineate 
Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZs) through the integration of agglomerative clustering and Density-Based 
Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN). This two-phase clustering strategy aims to 
refine the spatial granularity of TAZs, focusing on the reduction of intrazonal trip percentages, thereby 
enhancing the accuracy of traffic flow analysis within urban settings. 
 
Phase I: Agglomerative Clustering for Initial TAZ Delineation 
Initially, we employ agglomerative clustering, a hierarchical bottom-up clustering technique, to group 
HSU into preliminary clusters or TAZs based on a set of land use characteristics. This phase leverages the 
spatial and functional similarities within urban areas to establish zones that reflect coherent land use 
patterns. 
 
Phase II: Intrazonal Trip Analysis and DBSCAN Refinement 
Subsequent to the formation of initial TAZs, we utilize GPS data to calculate the percentage of intrazonal 
trips journeys that originate and terminate within the same TAZ. Clusters exhibiting an intrazonal trips 
density off over 50%, are flagged for further analysis.  
High intrazonal trip percentages indicate a potential issue with the initial TAZ delineation,  
such as oversized zones or zones encompassing diverse land use areas that should logically be separated. 
To address this, clusters identified with excessive intrazonal trips undergo a refinement using DBSCAN. 
This density-based clustering method is good at identifying clusters of varying shapes and sizes and 
isolating outliers or noise. By applying DBSCAN to these select clusters, we aim to split them into 
smaller, more homogenous TAZs, thereby reducing the proportion of intrazonal trips.

Case Study Copenhagen 
 
The Copenhagen Metropolitan area, known for its progressive urban mobility strategies, relies 
significantly on cycling as a mode of transport, constituting 26% of all travel in 2020 resulting in 1.46 
million km cycled per weekday . These statistic highlights the city's dedication to sustainable 
transportation (Source: Author, Year). 
 
Copenhagen's commitment to enhancing its cycling infrastructure is evidenced by its ongoing investments 
in segregated cycle tracks. This infrastructure are key components of the city's mobility strategy, 
prioritizing safety and promoting cycling as a sustainable mode of transportation.  
 
The methodology described in this study has been applied to both Copenhagen and its neighbouring 
municipalities, including Dragør, Frederiksberg, among others. Utilizing the H3 geospatial indexing 
algorithm at a resolution level of 8, the total study area of 264.4 km² was systematically divided into 440 
hexagonal units. Each unit, measuring an average of 0.61 km² with an average apothem of 0.42 km

Cycle mobility Data 
The cycle trip dataset required additional processing to remove invalid entries. The Euclidean 
distances and travel times were calculated, which necessary to determine bike only trips. To 
refine the dataset further, trips with a Euclidean distance of less than 1 km were excluded, as 
were trips exceeding a duration of 120 minutes. 
 
The cleansed dataset was segmented into two, time blocks morning peak hours (AM, 6-9) and 
afternoon peak hours (PM, 14-18). This categorization resulted in the analysis of 52,640 morning 
trips and 66,724 afternoon trips. The rationale for this time-based segmentation was informed by 
established urban traffic flow patterns, which are documented in the literature as exhibiting 
distinct peaks during these hours (Smith, 2020). 
 
The data was collected between September 2019 to May 2021. This period was marked by the 
unprecedented global impact of the Covid-19 pandemic, which led to the implementation of 
curfews and lockdowns at various intervals. These public health interventions have been shown 
to significantly alter transportation dynamics Different studies have illustrated how pandemic-
induced changes in mobility (Francke A, 2020) Moreover, the temporary closure of recreational 
facilities and the shift toward remote work undoubtedly influenced the volume and nature of 
bicycle trips recorded in the dataset.

To advise the decision for the optimal cluster number, we employed two metrics:  
Dendrograms, which depict the hierarchical relationship between clusters. These visual tools highlight the 
dissimilarity levels between clusters at each hierarchical stage, with lower heights indicating greater 
similarity. Determining the appropriate "cut-off" point on a dendrogram helps determine a a number of 
good cohesive clusters by the number of vertical lines intersected by horizontal line. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The Silhouette Score, ranging from -1 to 1, assesses how well each object lies within its cluster relative to 
other clusters, providing an intuitive measure of cluster fit. In scenarios where boundaries may be non-
spherical, the Silhouette Score provides more detailed insights into the cohesion and separation of each 
cluster, making it more suitable for evaluating complex clustering scenarios.

Discussion 
In this thesis, we explore how to delineate Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZs) using a multiple 
source, data-driven approach that integrates both cycle mobility and urban land use 
characteristics. The objective was to utilize this methodology to enhance the accuracy and 
granularity of urban planning tools, particularly in the context of traffic analysis and 
infrastructure development. The methodological framework comprised several sequential stages: 
data collection and preparation, spatial indexing, and finally, the application of spatial analysis 
and clustering techniques. 
Data Collection and Methodological Approach 
The study utilized Global Positioning System (GPS) data from bicycle trips, provided by 
Hövding users, capturing mobility patterns across Denmark. This dataset included over 100 
million raw trips, offering a detailed snapshot of cyclist movements and behaviours. Utilizing 
Uber's H3 spatial indexing, we transformed this extensive dataset into a manageable set of 
hexagonal spatial units, or Hexagonal Spatial Units (HSUs), enabling a highly granular analysis 
of cycle mobility. OpenStreetMap (OSM) and European Space Agency (ESA) satellite imagery 
formed the backbone of our land use analysis. OSM’s contributed detailed urban features, while 
ESA’s World Cover data provided up-to-date land cover classifications, We applied 
agglomerative clustering to initially group HSUs into preliminary TAZs based on land use 
characteristics. This hierarchical approach was instrumental in reflecting the spatial and 
functional similarities within urban areas. To refine these clusters and ensure their relevance to 
actual traffic flows, we conducted an intrazonal trip analysis, where subsequent refinement was 
handled through Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise The effectiveness 
of our methodology was quantitatively assessed by measuring the global information loss, which 
stood at a notable 5.5%. This metric indicated a relatively low loss of trip information within the 
delineated zones, suggesting that the TAZs reflect actual functional areas within the urban 
landscape. 
The results of the global information loss of 5.5% in Traffic Analysis Zone delineation, align 
favourably with existing research while advancing the application of geospatial analytics in 
urban planning. 
Furthermore, this approach addresses several limitations noted in the literature, such as the static 
nature of TAZs and the generalization of travel patterns. For instance, (Zhao, Y & Kockelman,K. 
2002) highlighted the need for more dynamic zoning methods that adapt to changing urban 
landscapes and mobility patterns. By incorporating real time GPS data and detailed land use 
characteristics, our methodology not only captures a more accurate picture of current mobility 
patterns but also offers flexibility to update and refine TAZ delineation in response to urban 
changes.

However, our methodology also faces certain limitations that must be acknowledged. One major 
limitation is the reliance on the quality and completeness of the input data. For instance, the 
accuracy of the GPS data is dependent on the device's performance and user density, which can 
vary significantly across different regions and times. Additionally, while OSM data is incredibly 
rich and detailed, it is also subject to variability in coverage and accuracy, influenced by the 
uneven contributions of volunteers. Furthermore, the agglomerative clustering technique, while 
useful for creating initial groupings, may not always capture the optimal number of TAZs or the 
subtle nuances in urban dynamics without further refinement, which we attempted to address 
using DBSCAN for areas with high intrazonal trips. 
These strengths and limitations highlight the complexities involved in using data-driven 
approaches for urban planning. They highlight the need for continual refinement of data 
collection and analysis methods and suggest areas for further research, such as improving data 
collection or preprocessing techniques, exploring alternative clustering algorithms, and 
enhancing real-time data integration to better adapt to dynamic urban environments.

Future Research Directions 
The methodologies and findings presented in this thesis open several avenues for future research 
in urban planning and traffic analysis. One promising area is the enhancement of clustering 
algorithms to further reduce information loss and increase the accuracy of (TAZs).  
Investigating the use of machine learning techniques such as neural network’s ability to 
recognise patterns could provide deeper insights into complex urban dynamics and improve the 
precision of TAZ delineation.  
Additionally, incorporating more diverse datasets, socio-economic parameters, and 
environmental factors, could refine our understanding of the interdependencies in urban mobility 
and land use. Exploring the integration of dynamic data sources would also allow for the 
development of adaptive TAZs that can respond to temporal changes in urban landscapes, such 
as construction projects or large-scale events. Furthermore, comparative studies across different 
urban settings could further validate the robustness of the methodology. 
Conclusion 
This thesis has demonstrated delineating Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZs) using multiple data 
sources can significantly enhance urban planning and traffic analysis with regards to cycle.  
By integrating GPS data, OpenStreetMap (OSM) details, and ESA satellite imagery within a 
methodological framework utilizing H3 spatial indexing and agglomerative clustering, we 
achieved a global information loss of only 5.5%. This low percentage underscores the 
effectiveness of our approach in capturing the movement within urban areas.
