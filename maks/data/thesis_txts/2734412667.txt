Abstract
This thesis assesses the effects of governmental policies on electric vehicle (EV)
demand in Europe and provides a comprehensive description of policy interven-
tions that affect EV demand in Europe by looking at fiscal and monetary incentives,
infrastructure deployment and supplementary policies. This thesis also examines
these policies using a mixed-method approach, using a quantitative analysis of the
effect of these policies and a qualitative assessment of the supplementary poli-
cies and people’s perceptions. Findings reveal that targeted financial incentives
coupled with the co-development of charging infrastructure have a statistically sig-
nificant impact on the public’s preference for EVs. Considerable heterogeneity is
identified across Europe in the effectiveness of those policies and discusses the
different approaches that European countries will have to employ to maximize the
uptake of EVs in a European context. Overall, this study uncovers critical insights
about the design and implementation of policies that can facilitate the transition
to sustainable transportation systems; this work can be particularly used for by
governments across the world to assess their electric vehicle transition journey.

Introduction
Since the emergence of human life on this planet, most processes have been
across human life and human activity. One primary goal of the human race has
been to make human life as simple as possible, and the last century is quite an
example of this. With the advent of the Industrial Revolution, machinery and tech-
nology took over most mundane human tasks. They range from production to
cultivation to transportation, and now, using artificial intelligence, most complex
tasks can be performed in minutes.
Certainly, with these exponential technological advancements, life has seen a sig-
nificant transformation. However, due to its unprecedented splurge, its impact on
the environment, nature and living beings has been largely neglected. The ad-
vancements brought a sharp rise in the economy, leading to increased demand,
heavy production and carbon emissions. This also stands true for the transporta-
tion sector. More and more people travel by personal vehicles powered by fossil
fuels. This resulted in heavy CO2 emissions in the past century. Now the emission
of the transport sector accounts for nearly a quarter of the total world emissions
and can increase further in the future twice the times. [1]
Figure 1.1: Worldwide growth of CO2 emissions [2]
Governments worldwide have realized the repercussions of carbon emissions
and how much they have affected the planet.
The United Nations has char-
tered a plan to reduce emissions by individual nations by 45% by the year 2030
and attain net zero by the year 2050 in accordance with the Paris Agreement.[3]

The nations have also set up individual targets that would help them lower their
emissions. Considering that the transport sector is a very high contributor, the
biggest goal has been to determine alternative fuels for vehicles. Multiple propos-
als and alternatives have been established in the past decade. This includes using
compressed natural gas (CNG), a low-polluting agent, and electric or hydrogen-
powered vehicles.

Electric vehicles have been seen as the most viable alternative to Internal com-
bustion engine (ICE) vehicles. Electric vehicles have existed since the brim of the
19th century but have witnessed very little development. The first practical cars
were produced at the end of the 19th century. However, with the introduction of
gasoline-powered vehicles, the masses were shifted[5]. Gasoline-powered vehi-
cles were easy to produce due to technological developments in engine engineer-
ing. Ford’s moving assembly line fostered mass production of ICEs, reducing the
popularity of EVs. [6] [7] The resurgence was only observed in the 21st century
due to the worldwide gasoline shortages due to the Arab Oil Embargo of 1973 [5].
Adding to this was the looming dangers of climate change and emissions. Con-
sidering these factors, it became necessary to advance more into EV technology.
The first mass-produced Hybrid cars were put on the market in 1997. [8]
The surge in electric vehicle adoption is not solely a consequence of technolog-
ical advancements; an array of governmental interventions significantly drives it.
These interventions include, but are not limited to, financial incentives, tax bene-
fits, regulations on emissions, and the development of EV charging infrastructure.
Many nations across the world have come up with promotional incentives for Elec-
tric vehicles. Pertaining to the standards set by the United Nations for reducing
emissions and their set individual targets, global governments are pushing forward
the sales of Electric vehicles.

Literature review
To understand the relationship between electric vehicle sales and the policies,
it was necessary to conduct some research and identify some insights into this
relation. The literature review was conducted over 30 papers and government
documents. The collected information was divided into four core sections.
2.1
Electric vehicles - market and growth
Understanding the electric vehicle market and the factors that influence its sales
and growth is crucial. In 2021, global EV sales grew by 108%, with over 6.6 million
EVs sold, accounting for approximately 9% of the global automobile market. This
growth trend continued into 2022, with sales of 10.5 million electric passenger
vehicles, which is four million more than the 2021 levels.[12]
Globally, governments are working their best in the transition to electric vehicles
(EVs) to combat climate change and innovate the automotive industry. The Euro-
pean Union, India, Japan, China, South Korea, Chile, and New Zealand have im-
plemented varied strategies, including financial incentives, regulations, and ambi-
tious emission reduction targets. These initiatives range from funding for charging
infrastructure and research in the EU to incentives for EV purchases in India and
South Korea, China. Japan’s commitment to electrify all vehicles by 2050, Chile’s
focus on electrifying public transport, and New Zealand’s goal for a fully electric
fleet by 2050 and many more. Collectively, these efforts highlight a global com-
mitment to sustainable transportation.[13] Despite growing sales, EVs still require
support measures like financial incentives because of consumer have concerns
regarding the high initial costs and range anxiety.[14]
There are multiple factors that could influence the uptake of electric vehicles like
renewable energy usage, number of charging stations, education level, and pop-
ulation density positively affect EV demand. With one per cent increase in re-
newables within a country’s energy matrix, there could be a 2 to 6 per cent rise
in the demand for EVs can be predicted. [15] Battery electric vehicles and fuel
cell vehicles have the potential to reduce greenhouse gas emissions significantly,
especially when powered by electricity generated from renewable energy sources
such as wind or hydropower. The tailpipe emissions (tank-to-wheel or TTW emis-
sions) for these vehicles are zero. If EVs are charged with electricity from old
coal power plants, their total emissions could be higher than those from conven-
tional vehicles. Thus, the source of electricity is a crucial factor in determining the
environmental impact of EVs. [16]
There are several challenges that hinder the adoption of electric vehicles, making
it difficult for them to gain wider acceptance and big market penetration. One of the
significant barriers is the high initial purchase cost of electric vehicles compared to
traditional gasoline-powered vehicles. This high cost can be a discouragement for
consumers considering the switch to an electric vehicle. Another challenge is the
perception that electric vehicles have a limited driving range on a single charge,

2.2
Policies and implications
Governments have taken initiatives to deploy fast-charging stations, promote EV
uptake through greater investment, and establish supportive policies. These mea-
sures have significantly enhanced the growth of the EV sector. Policies focusing
on infrastructure construction support and production subsidies can positively in-
fluence EV adoption. Such policies can increase the uptake of EVs to 70%. Heavy
government support can play a crucial role in converting a substantial portion of
the global fleet to electric cars by 2040, with estimates suggesting that 11-28% of
vehicles could be electric with robust policy backing if looked at the projections. It
is recommended that governments should consider rolling out incentives not just
for personal electric vehicles but also for commercial EVs to promote technology
innovation, strengthen stakeholder relationships, and hasten the development of
charging infrastructure. [12]
The paper ”Comparison of Leading Electric Vehicle Policy and Deployment in Eu-
rope” examines the five largest EV markets in Europe: Germany, the United King-
dom, France, the Netherlands, and Norway. These countries represented more
than 80% of all European EV registrations in 2014. The paper highlights that
countries and cities with higher levels of fiscal incentives, which are tax-based
incentives and charging infrastructure, have higher EV shares. Cities generally
have higher shares of EVs and a higher density of charging infrastructure than
their respective countries. The paper used fiscal incentives, charging infrastruc-
ture density, and EV market shares as key indicators of a country’s readiness
for electric mobility. The analysis revealed that Norway had significantly higher
incentive levels and charging infrastructure, resulting in much higher EV shares
compared to other countries.”[18]
Econometric studies on the effect size of purchase incentives and analysis data
on PEV sales in relation to the impact of financial incentives show that financial
incentives positively influence PEV adoption, with estimates suggesting a 5 – 7%
relative sales share increase in PEVs for different model specifications. [19] Sub-
sidies appear to be beneficial mostly in the early stages of market introduction
but should not be relied upon to sustain market growth, emphasizing the role of
market mechanisms. Policymakers need to find the right balance and timing for
measures that promote EVs, such as implementing fleet emission regulations,
providing subsidies, and investing in charging infrastructure.[14]

Apart from having fiscal and non-fiscal subsidies which are related to the purchase
of electric vehicles, there should be some that are focused towards production and
infrastructure. This can lead to a sales rate increase by up to 70%. [24]
2.3
Modelling demand
The research paper titled ”How large is the effect of financial incentives on electric
vehicle sales? - A global review and European analysis” uses panel data regres-
sion to study the impact of financial incentives on plug-in electric vehicle (PEV)
sales globally and in Europe. The study reveals a complex relationship between
incentives, the availability of charging infrastructure, and the development of the
PEV market. Monetary incentives have a positive influence, access to high occu-
pancy vehicle (HOV) lanes has mixed effects, and charging infrastructure plays
a critical role in promoting PEV adoption. The study highlights the importance of
fixed effects models to control for unobserved heterogeneity and the consideration
of lagged variables to address S-shaped adoption curves. The research shows
that the effectiveness of incentives varies across contexts, with a notable quanti-
tative finding being a 0.6% increase in PEV registrations per $1000 of incentive.
This underscores the complex interplay of financial incentives, infrastructure, and
socioeconomic factors in driving PEV market penetration. [19]

One paper used Agent-Based Modeling to simulate the introduction of electric ve-
hicles and the impact of different policy interventions on their adoption within a
community. ABM is a computational modelling approach that allows for the sim-
ulation of interactions between autonomous ”agents” (in this case, potential EV
consumers) within an environment, capturing the complexity and heterogeneity of
individual behaviours and decisions. The model focused on consumer behaviour
and how it changed in response to public policies. Attributes such as socioeco-
nomic status, travel behaviours, and environmental attitudes were considered in
how they interact with vehicle attributes and policy features to influence purchase
decisions. The model illustrates that consumer decisions to adopt BEVs are in-
fluenced by a complex interplay of factors, including demographics, economic
considerations, personal attitudes towards the environment, and perceptions of
technology. [17]
These papers demonstrate that by using modelling techniques, clear and valued
insights can be found out about how policies or, in general, certain factors together
can influence one other factor that is related to them.

Electric vehicle technologies
The development of electric vehicles (EVs) is heavily reliant on advancements
in battery technology. The energy-saving potential of EVs is of great interest to
both investors and customers. Various battery chemistries, such as Lithium-ion,
Lead-Acid, Nickel-Cadmium, Nickel-metal hydride, and Nickel-iron, are being re-
searched. Emerging battery technologies like Iron-air, Aluminium-air, and Vana-
dium redox flow batteries are also under investigation.[12] Such advancement fos-
ters better production of cars that are more reliable and safe. The development of
EVs and associated technologies like fast-charging stations has escalated quickly
in previous years, largely due to government and private investment.[12]
The multifaceted advancements in electric vehicle (EV) technology emphasize the
critical role of battery innovation in enhancing energy density, reducing costs, and
improving safety. It underscores the importance of electric motors and drivetrains
in optimizing vehicle performance, alongside the necessity for a robust charging

Europe and potential
Europe has the second-largest stock of electric cars in the world after China, with
the European Union registering 2.3 million cars in 2021, which made up 34% of
the worldwide stock. The growth rate of EV sales in Europe was 64.3% in 2021.
[12] [20] Certain cities in Europe are playing a significant role in the electric vehicle
(EV) revolution. For instance, Oslo has an impressive EV market share, with 61%
of all new vehicles sold in 2018 being electric. This is considerably higher than
the Norwegian national average. Similarly, Stockholm’s EV market experienced
substantial growth in 2018, with an electric vehicle share that was 75% higher
than the Swedish national average. Amsterdam and Rotterdam - the Hague areas
also witnessed high EV growth rates, with their EV sales shares surpassing the
Netherlands’ national average. Additionally, these cities boast very high densities
of public charging points.
Different member states within the EU respond differently to EV policies, sug-
gesting the need for tailored approaches that align with EU regulations and con-
sider interactions between member states. [14] The European Green Deal is the
blueprint guiding the EU’s efforts to achieve carbon neutrality. The transport sec-
tor’s transformation is a significant part of this, and the paper discusses how the
EU is working toward replacing internal combustion engine vehicles with cleaner
technologies, such as EVs. [21]
Countries with a high share of renewable energy in their electricity mix—like Nor-
way, Sweden, and France can EVs significantly reduce GHG emissions. [16]
Policies that ensure that electric vehicle promotion measures must be made to
be sustainable in the future. Most of the promotion measures in place now do
not link the electricity used by EVs to the carbon content. However, it must be.
Future promotion strategies should be tied to the carbon content of the electricity
in use—making the most of the environmental benefits that EVs can offer. CO2-
based fuel- and registration taxes could be very important complementary policy
tools. Such measures would not simply promote the adoption of EVs; they’d also
help ensure that we make the transition to a cleaner electricity grid. That transi-
tion will be essential if the full benefits of achieving reductions in the overall carbon
emissions associated with EVs are understood.[16]

Policy data
The central focus of the topic is policy data and how it impacts different aspects.

used to perform a quantitative analysis by using a linear regression model. This
approach helps identify policies that cannot be quantified but can only be dis-
cussed in a qualitative manner. The policy data has been obtained from various
online sources, including government websites, academic studies, and research
papers. The data includes information on policy objectives, implementation time-
lines, and outcomes. The quantitative analysis of policy data involves using sta-
tistical methods to identify patterns and relationships between different policy vari-
ables. This approach assists in understanding the effectiveness of policies and
identifying areas where improvements can be made. Additionally, the qualitative
analysis of policy data involves examining the context, history, and stakeholders
involved in policy-making to gain a deeper understanding of the policy’s impact.

Methodology
The methodology for analysis has been divided into two distinct phases to pro-
vide a comprehensive understanding of policies across different nations. The
first phase of analysis aims to measure the number of policies in various coun-
tries and their impact over time. To achieve this, the initial methodology counts
the total number of policies within each nation and tracks how this number has
changed over time. Additionally, the analysis also aims to examine the extent to
which these policies have been enforced and their effectiveness in achieving their
intended objectives. By analyzing the trends and patterns in policy development
and implementation, this methodology provides valuable insight into the evolution
of policies over time across different nations.
In Phase 2 of the project, the primary objective was to thoroughly identify the
potential taxation reductions that could result from implementing policies that pro-
mote the use of Electric Vehicles (EVs) over Internal Combustion Engine vehicles
(ICEs). The objective was to identify specific taxes that, if reduced, would signif-
icantly contribute to an increase in the sale of electric cars in different countries.
This phase involved conducting broad research and analysis of various taxation
policies that directly or indirectly impact the purchase and use of EVs and ICEs.
By doing so, policymakers could be provided with insights and recommendations
on how specific policies can foster the adoption of EVs, reduce carbon emissions,
and promote sustainable transportation.
Phase 2 and the change in methodology were a direct consequence of the short-
comings of the initial method. Phase 2 also leads to a more quantitative and com-
parative analysis in comparison to phase 1. For both methods liner regression
was used to identify the potential trends.

The dependent variable in this study has been categorized into two distinct sec-
tions: national factors and policy factors. The national factors are more related
to the country as a whole, such as the number of people employed, the overall
population, the gross domestic product, and electricity prices. These factors can
provide valuable insights into how the general economic and demographic condi-

the most developed nations with strong economies, but if nations like Bulgaria or
North Macedonia are to be considered, it becomes imperative that the economic
state of the country is also measured. As it is possible that these regions could
not provide the same development as the economically booming nations.

tives changes the way people invest in electric vehicles.

method, which should be of utmost necessity.
One of the biggest flaws is that all policies and incentives are combined into one
variable, ”Tax benefits”. This leads to the consideration that all policies, regardless
of their practical benefits, still have the same value. This can not be true in real
life; for example, if the Dutch government waives off the Registration taxes, which
could be 20% of the total cost, while the Danish government provides special
access to the bus lanes access for EVs. This is considered the same. While both
are profitable in some way or another, getting a huge compensation while buying
the car is far more important for an individual as it would save a lot of money.
One policy is of monetary compensation background while the other is of special
privileges background. The monetary one can be quantified, but not the latter.
Thus, a qualitative analysis becomes necessary for such a policy, and a greater
focus on the quantitative analysis of the monetary ones is necessary.
This kind of calculation also hinders how effective these policies are. For example,
some nations impose ownership taxes which have to be paid by the people every
year for owning cars. If any sort of exemption is provided, this method cannot
analyse its actual effects. With the decimal method, it takes into account how
much change has occurred in the policy, but it does not identify the importance
of this said ownership tax. Thus, it should be considered as a separate variable
and not as one where all other policies are also taken into consideration. One
downside of the decimal method is that it changes scale according to the highest
and lowest value of that particular policy, which again undermines the actual effect
of the policy. It also indicates that although physical policies can be compared
according to the scale, each policy’s scales differ. This means that the comparison
will be a flawed one. An example of this can be imagining Norway providing a 30%
exemption on registration tax in the year 2019, and it is reduced to 20% in 2022.
This method can identify that a change occurred in Norway. But, at the same time,
Sweden has a 10% exemption in the year 2019 and goes on until 2025. The value

Considering the multiple issues with the first method, the second method was
more streamlined into a proper quantitative analysis across quantifiable policies
and a qualitative discussion over non-quantifiable policies. The data for the sec-
ond method for the quantitative analysis is taken from ACEA tax guides. These
tax guides range from 2012 to 2022, wherein all the taxes applied to vehicles are
detailed.
The documents contain information on all European Union nations and also in-

types of vehicles and all types of taxes. In the second methodology, all twenty-

The taxes within the tax guides are divided into Taxes on acquisition, Taxes on Mo-
toring, Taxes on Ownership, Private use of company cars and Periodic inspection.

5.1.2
Variance Influence Factor
The next test method is finding the Variance Influence Factor (VIF). The VIF is
interpreted to identify the issue of Multicollinearity in a regression model. When
two or more independent variables in a regression model are highly correlated,
it causes multicollinearity. This can make it difficult to accurately estimate the
relationship between each independent variable and the dependent variable. If
the VIF values are between 1 and 5, the correlation is moderate and there are no
corners. However, if the value is over 5 then there is a high multicollinearity.
Table 5.3 displays the VIF of all the variables and indicates that all values are
within the range of 1 and 5, indicating a moderate collinearity.

Q-Q plot
The next important task was to identify if there were any outliers and residuals.
A Quantile-Quantile (Q-Q) plot can be used to identify the existence of residu-
als. One of the most common applications of a Q-Q plot is to evaluate whether a
dataset is approximately normally distributed. To do this, the data’s quantiles are
plotted against the quantiles of a standard normal distribution. The points on the
plot should lie on or near a straight diagonal line if the data are normally distributed.
In addition to assessing normality, a Q-Q plot can also be used to compare the
distribution of a dataset against any theoretical distribution, not just the normal
distribution. This can be useful in determining if the data follow a specific theo-
retical distribution, such as exponential, uniform, or another specified distribution.
Points that are significantly far from the main trend line in a Q-Q plot may indicate
outliers in the data. [32]

account for potential correlations within clusters of observations in the data. When
data are collected in clusters, observations within the same cluster may be more

error terms to be correlated. Standard regression techniques usually assume that
observations are independent and identically distributed. However, when there
is clustering, this assumption is violated. Failing to account for this intra-cluster
correlation can lead to underestimated standard errors, resulting in overconfident
inferences.

6.1
Policies
From the results, it is quite evident that the introduction of policies by the gov-
ernment helps people to understand electric vehicles more. With policies, people
can take a leap of faith and decide to transition towards the not-so-common yet
sustainable transport methods.
One of the key issues with the adoption of electric vehicles is the cost of the ve-
hicle. However, there is a high likelihood that electric vehicles will be the most
cost-efficient option over medium distances and for all vehicle classes over long
distances. [33]. But to realise this, people need to adopt electric vehicles, try
them, and then understand for themselves how efficient electric vehicles can be.
This is where policies come into work.
Policies help bring in big transitions. Policies can range from the provision of aid
to the adoption of strict measures. With time, it has been evident how government
policies have shaped the world into what we are today. With the fragile ecological
state of the world, it is quite necessary to adopt sustainable methodologies, and
this can not be achieved till the world governments commit to this. In recent years,
more and more policies have been introduced to foster the change towards electric
vehicles. Within a span of a few years nations, there have been evident changes in
some of the national markets, from roughly 4000 electric vehicles in the European
Union in the year 2008 to roughly 8 million in the year 2023. This trend shows a
good increase and has been followed across the world. [1]
Most people prefer alternatives that are affordable and easily accessible. Thus, re-
ducing not just the cost of the electric vehicles but the overall cost is very needed.
Reduction of VAT and registration taxes has been shown to bring in people’s pref-
erence for electric vehicles. This can also be seen in the survey conducted in
Norway, where people indicated that the biggest factor that made them buy elec-
tric vehicles was the ”No VAT” policy. Norway and Iceland are the only two nations
that have VAT exemptions.
While Norway has a 100% VAT reduction from before 2012, Iceland has intro-
duced the same 100% reduction since 2020. Recently, Austria has introduced
certain deductions in the year 2023, [29], and Norway has observed the best elec-
tric vehicle market in the entirety of Europe. In 2019 it became the only country
where the registration for electric vehicles was higher than that of the gasoline
based. The cities of Oslo and Bergen display a market share higher than that of
the National average every year. [34]
Norway can be set as an example for other European nations for the amount of
development it has achieved. Apart from the reductions provided in VAT, Norway
has also implemented other policies like reductions in Import tax, special access
to bus lanes for EVs, etc. It is important to identify how more policies like these
could help develop an all-rounder electric vehicle market.

6.1.1
Scrapage schemes
While it is super necessary to have more Electric vehicle sales, it is also impor-
tant to eliminate the already existing gasoline vehicles. This will help reduce the
number of gasoline vehicles and dependence on them. As the overall goal is to
reduce the emissions reduction of emissions-producing vehicles, it will help fulfil
the goal.
After scrapping the old cars, people might buy cars that are not zero-emission
vehicles, but they would have lower emissions than the old vehicles. This is due
to the technological advances and policy implications in the past few years. [35]
Newly produced cars have lower emissions as there are more strict rules and
production emission checks. This shows that reducing the number of super old
vehicles is quite essential for nature.
Scrapping not only helps reduce emissions but also helps in reusing materials
from old vehicles for producing newer vehicles. [36]. This would also help re-
duce production and mining emissions, which are also big contributors to world
emissions, along with the transport sector.
Certain nations across Europe already have scrappage policies. France has a
scrappage policy for old vehicles. If a new vehicle or a second-hand vehicle with
emissions less than 50g CO2/km is being bought, then a bonus of 5000 euros is
provided based on income. [29]
In Greece, if a car which is 10 years or older is scrapped, 1000 euros is provided as
an added bonus. The same is 2500 euros if an old taxi is scrapped and replaced
by a new BEV taxi. Based on the emissions of the newly bought electric vehicle,
Italy has a certain bonus. This bonus can get an additional 2000 euros if an old
car is being scrapped.[29]
Lithuania provides scrappage bonus of 1000 euros if an old gasoline based car
is scrapped. Ireland introduced new legislation in 2022 where it provides certain
bonuses for old public service vehicles if replaced by new electric ones. [29]
Romania’s Rabla program, introduced in 2023, makes it mandatory to scrap an
old vehicle to buy a new vehicle to avail of the benefits of this policy. A bonus
of 51.000 lei is applied for a new purely electric vehicle. Twenty-six thousand lei

6.1.2
Public transport
When governments decide to lower the national emissions, they should not put
these laws and order on the public but also implement such changes in their own
possessions. Changing to electric cars will indeed bring in a lot of change and
promote low emissions but it will be incomplete without bringing such change in
the public transport infrastructure.
Many people across the world depend on public transport for their daily commute.
The usage of public transport systems is always seen as a more sustainable way
of transport as usually public transport can carry a large number of people in a
more concentrated space with lower emissions. Thus, it is also essential that the
public transport that we use should be 100% sustainable. While it is hard to bring
in electric alternatives for all types of public transport, like aeroplanes and ships,
the ones that can be changed must be done, like buses, trucks, taxis, etc.
The European Union, as a whole, has set a public procurement target for buses,
with up to 45% clean buses by 2026 and up to 65% by 2031 of a total share.
Denmark has set a target to make all urban public transport emissions-free by the
year 2030. 75% of public buses will be zero or low emission by 2030. [29]
Finland introduced legislation in 2019 where 50% of the public buses should be
low-emission. This should be increased to 100% by 2025. Big cities must at-
tain the 50% target by 2022. Ireland provides grants for public service BEVs,
which also include an added bonus for scrappage. Netherlands plans to have
100% public buses to be zero emission by the year 2030 and all sales to be zero-
emission buses by 2025. Poland proposes to have 100% public transport to be
zero emission by 2030 as well. [29]
Sweden, on the other hand, provides 20% grants for public transport authorities
and municipalities over the purchase price of electric buses.[29]
Different nations have different ways of introducing public transport policies. Every
nation can take ideas from nations that have already implemented these policies.
It is important that the entire transport sector becomes a low-emissions sector, and
that can be achieved with both private and public vehicles’ sustainable transition.

Road tax
The promotion of electric vehicles can occur if they are provided with special ben-
efits that are not attainable to gasoline-based vehicles. This can be in the form
of reduced tolls and road taxes. Even though the road tolls are not a very huge
amount and are catered to the public’s ability to spend, having additional bene-
fits like this where people save money can influence people to switch to electric
vehicles.
The Czech Republic has a policy wherein electric cars are exempt from paying
road tolls. Ireland has reduced tolls for low-emission vehicles. Most nations con-
sider tolls as part of the Road tax that falls under the Tax on ownership. However,
at certain times, tolls are not a part of this road tax, especially when it comes to
cross-country infrastructure like the Øresund link between Denmark and Sweden.
Tolls for this kind of structure are usually very high, and thus, reductions in such
tolls can be heavily beneficial for the people.[29]
6.1.4
Import tax
One kind of tax that was certainly not included in the main analysis was the im-
port tax. Import tax is the tax implied by nations if a product is imported into the
nation. Usually, the tax is a lot as it is applied by both the exporting and importing
countries.
Reducing or eliminating import tax on electric vehicles can be proven to be a big
help as most nations do not have enough available models of electric vehicles,
and thus, importing the vehicle the people want remains the only solution. This
would help people who really want to transition to a sustainable vehicle not to be
over-charged.
Currently, only two nations across Europe have policies related to reductions in
import taxes. Finland has 0% import tax on zero-emission cars from the year 2022.
While Norway has no import tax on pure electric vehicles from before 2012.[29][28]
Other nations outside Europe, like Angola, Brazil, Ecuador and Mexico, also have
import tax reductions on complete waiver. More European nations must follow
this trend and increase the number of their electric vehicles.

Special access

form of access to bus lanes for electric vehicles or by creating Low Emission Zones

movement of polluting vehicles is restricted either through priced or non-priced
strategies. [38] Through priced methods, cars or vehicles that are polluting have

the non-priced method completely restricts the entry of polluting vehicles.
Low Emission Zones (LEZs) are implemented to reduce the pollutants like Nitro-
gen oxides. As of 2022, there are about 320 low emission zones (LEZ) operating
within Europe. [38] LEZs are introduced in heavily populated regions with high
levels of pollution. Restricting such places for pollution vehicles and permitting
only electric vehicles would persuade people living in these regions to switch to
electric vehicles.

and takes less than 5 minutes. Plus, due to the high demand for cars, a sufficient

Conclusion
The analysis performed within this thesis across the final three models and the
test models provides significant insights on the impact of governmental policies
on the electric vehicle markets and the EV demand. The findings from the analysis
highlight that policies that aim to reduce the overall cost of the EVs like reduction
in the Value added tax (VAT) and registration taxes are highly effective along with
the polices where the government provides incentives for the purchase of EVs.
The study also highlights the importance of proper infrastructure and infrastruc-
ture policies particularly the ones catering towards chargers and charging infras-
tructure. This is quite evident as the results of the models also emphasize the
role of chargers in driving the electric vehicle market. The usage of the clustered
models is also highlighted here as the clustering process gives more insights on
national levels.
The thesis also identifies through results and literature review that Norway is the
best-performing market in Europe and also the most advanced in terms of policy
rollout. Comparing the three models also suggests that the addition of Norway
does not alter the fundamental results. Model 3’s exploration of policy-based fac-
tors, excluding chargers, points out the substantial influence of policy interventions
on EV demand. It also shows how some variables can influence the behaviour of
others pertaining to the heavy change observed in the Tax on ownership variable.
The thesis also emphasizes the importance of comprehensive policy frameworks
that encompass various fronts of EV adoption, including scrappage schemes,
public transport electrification, road tax adjustments, import tax considerations,
and special access provisions. Such policies not only encourage EV purchases
but also facilitate a broader transition towards sustainable transportation. Most na-
tions where such policies are adopted are generally the better-performing nations
in Europe. The study also briefly discusses the happening outside of Europe and
how these can be implemented in Europe and provides an overview of Denmark
and how it can enhance its policy making based on the data of other nations.
The study’s limitations, including the reliance on a single data source and the focus
on passenger cars to the exclusion of other vehicle types, suggest more enhanced
research can be pursued in the future. Expanding the dataset, incorporating a
wider array of vehicle categories, all nations across the globe and examining the
differential impacts of policies on BEVs and PHEVs could provide more refined
insights about policy effectiveness.
In conclusion, this thesis affirms the pivotal role of governmental policies in ac-
celerating EV adoption in Europe. By lowering financial constraints, enhancing
infrastructure, and implementing supportive regulations, governments can signif-
icantly influence consumer preferences towards more sustainable transportation
options. Future policies should be designed with a holistic view, considering the
multifaceted barriers to EV adoption and leveraging the insights provided by anal-
yses such as this study.
