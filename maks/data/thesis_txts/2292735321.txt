ABSTRACT 
 
Grasshopper/Rhino with the existing energy simulation plug-ins for Grasshopper can be as 
effective as the dynamic simulation and design tool IESVE. The purpose is also to determine if 
the Grasshopper plugins Ladybug/Honeybee can contribute to the validity and effectiveness of the 
design process, especially in terms of indoor thermal conditions and in terms of choosing the 
relevant design descions in the early design stage.  
This analysis will be executed in two steps. The first step is to recreate the design, to analyze the 
process of a 1- zone patient room in Rhino/Grasshopper with Ladybug/Honeybee, and to compare 
the analysis and simulation results, with that of IESVE. The second step is to parametrize the input 
variables of the model, and to determine if it is possible to create a design concept, that is more 
dynamic and user-oriented. The goal is to create a design concept with scenarios, which can be 
manipulated, according the userŌĆÖs needs.  
Do the parametric design process have possibilities, which the computer aided design process does 
not have, in order of validity, speed, holistic integration and in visualization and analysis results? 
What limitations are there, when not using one of the more conventional and known design 
simulation programs?

1.1 INTRODUCTION 
 
Buildings are responsible for 40 % of the total energy consumption in Denmark. It is therefore 
important to build low energy houses, and this calls for a better understanding of a holistic 
integration of the different building aspects. The two main aspects that must harmonize is an 
efficient thermal inner climate and a realistic, but low energy consumption. Today, we have a 
tendency to let the energy consumption overshadow the inner thermal comfort, but there are ways 
to make these two building aspects work together. A known issue is the number of overheating 
hours that often contradicts a low energy consumption. It is therefore important to carefully 
consider the building design strategies, and in which way they affect each other. The goal is, early 
in the design process, to make conscious decisions about passive energy initiatives, so that all the 
aspects has an appropriate amount of integration. In this way, we can build harmonic, low energy 
buildings with a good functionality, a healthy inner thermal climate and a low energy consumption. 
 
An existing building case from Grontmij, will be designed as a 1- zone model, so that it is possible 
to analyze and compare the results, first in IESVE and then secondly in Honeybee/Ladybug. How 
efficient are these two result sets compared to each other? Can the parametric grasshopper plug-
ins Honeybee/Ladybug contribute to more efficient design decisions?  
Today, engineers and architects use computer- aided tools to visualize their ideas. The concept of 
a parametric design is a result of the fast growing process in the CAD tools world, and that allow 
the designer to specify the key parameters in a building model and to make changes interactively. 
One of the challenges of todayŌĆÖs design process is to find and use a design tool, that at the same 
time, is able to combine a detailed and holistic design level, with a parametric approach? The 
different energy and thermal comfort models are difficult to understand, but an increased focus, 
lately has been on adaptive comfort models and the adaptive hypothesis.  
 
Is it useful to build a parametric approach, which can integrate the importance of energy 
consumption with the importance of inner thermal comfort?

1.2 AUDIENCE 
Scientific target 
Little research has been done, regarding Grasshopper plug-ins and the indoor climate in new 
constructions. The use of parametric tools in early design phases, has just recently, been integrated 
by the Danish engineering firms and architect offices. What is the potential and the possibilities of 
the new generation of parametric design tools? In this thesis, the research will focus on the 
simulation of the optimal indoor thermal climate in the early design phase by the use of parametric 
tools such as the grasshopper plug-ins; Ladybug & Honeybee and how it is possible to compare 
the results with the level of energy consumption in different scenarios and in two different 
programs.  
 
Developmental target 
This Thesis will show how it is possible to develop effective and flexible design decisions in the 
early design phase with the parametric energy simulation plug-ins Honeybee and Ladybug. It is 
important to evaluate the consequences of using a parametric design tool, such as 
Honeybee/Ladybug and to question the qualitative, quantitative, and economic consequences of 
a parametric design tool, compared to that of using a more conventional and well-integrated 
design tool as IESVE.

2.1 RESEARCH METHODOLOGY  
 
Analytical research  
This thesis is based on an existing building case, concerning energy consumption and inner thermal 
climate. The existing case model from the engineering firm Grontmij, will be represented, 
analyzed and compared with another approved building design tool, in order to make a critical 
evaluation based, on the introduction and research questions in this report.  In the creation of this 
thesis there will be used both processes of deduction and induction, which also can be described 
by the two methods, the water fall method and the hill climbing approach. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The waterfall method is often used in software engineering and computer science and describes a 
method that is linearly and sequential. There will be distinct goals for each phase. The advantage 
of this method is that it allows for departmentalization and managerial control.  
The hill climbing method also belongs in the world of computer science and is an optimization 
technique of local search. It is often described as an iterative algorithm, which begins with an 
arbitrary solution to a problem. The goal is to attempt to find a better solution by incrementally 
changing a single element of this solution. If a change produces a better solution, an incremental 
change is made to the new solution, repeating until no further improvements can be found.

2.4 PROFESSIONAL RELEVANCE  
 
The way buildings are created today, is to fit the occupantŌĆÖs demands. It is also primary a question 
about fitting the new energy demands to a new future climate with global warming aspects. The 
building design industry needs to consider a low energy consumption, but not at the cost of the 
inner thermal climate, because the number of overheating hours will not decrease, in the future, 
when the global warming raises the outdoor temperature, several degrees.  
We need to develop a new point of view at the building design code, and we need to utilize our 
knowledge about positive and negative feedback mechanisms. We cannot stop the positive climate 
feedback mechanisms that create enhanced levels of CO2 in the atmosphere, but we can learn to 
utilize the negative feedback mechanisms to stabilize the thermal conditions today.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The inner thermal climate will often come in a second place, because the use of solar gain often 
contradicts the inner thermal comfort. I believe it is possible to create harmony between the inner 
thermal climate and a low energy consumption, if occupants are actively integrated in the 
environment and in the design strategies and the occupant is considered an active player in the 
building process instead of a passive individual.

hosts different templates like constructions and inner thermal climate templates.  In the different 
sections like the thermal section, you can assign internal gain and air exchange schedules to your

4.1 THERMAL BALANCE 
Thermal balance of the human being 
The inner climate is divided in to 2 main categories. The thermal inner climate and the atmospheric 
inner climate. The thermal inner climate, which will be the focus in this thesis, has four classic 
main parameters: 
 Air temperature 
 Mean radiant temperature 
 Air velocity 
 Air humidity  
 
These four parameters has a significance for the heat balance of the human being. The purpose of 
the human inner thermal regulating system is to maintain a constant body temperature. A human 
being will feel thermal comfortable, when it feels thermally neutral, with no need to feel warmer 
or colder under stationary conditions. The human heat balance can be expressed as an inner 
production of heat that has to be exactly equal to the heat release to the surroundings. [10]

6.2 THERMAL FACTORS

temperature, where the person feels neutral and comfortable. Where he does not feel any cold or

physiological factors as clothing insulation and metabolic heat.

PMV method 
The Predicted Mean Vote method is developed by P.O. Fanger. It is a static model, which estimates 
the reactions people would have to a certain set of indoor comfort conditions, based on clothing 
and physical activity. The PMV method is today, criticized due to the fact, that people are placed 
in a climate chamber, where they have no influence or possibility to adapt to their environmental 
surroundings. The model is particularly suitable for winter simulations in that the inner 
environmental conditions will be more active.  
 
The PMV- index predicts the mean value of the subjective ratings of a group of people in a given 
environment. It is a 7- point thermal sensation scale, where 0 represents the thermally neutral 
condition and ŌĆō 3 and 3 represents cold and hot. The PPD index measures the percent of dissatisfied 
and the people who votes ŌĆō 2, 2, 3 and -3 is not satisfied with the thermal environment. That is 
why, the curve that shows the relationship between the PMV index and the PPD index never gets

models, and different scenarios. The Grasshopper plug-in Honeybee has Comfort Model Tools for 
both the static PMV comfort model and the Adaptive comfort model. With these tools, it is possible 
to connect your zone model and to calculate and visualize the model results to test thermal 
comfortability. Passive design strategies use the ambient air instead of purchased energy like 
electricity or natural gas.  
 
                                     Passive design strategies  
 
Evaporative Cooling  
A passive evaporative cooling system can be either a cooling tower or an indoor fountain, where 
the incoming air comes in contact with water inside. The result is that the air is cooled, as the water 
evaporates and this will automatically mix with the internal air cool it down.  
 
Internal mass + night ventilation  
The minimum temperature, to open the windows and naturally ventilate, will cool down the 
occupants, and decrease the number of overheating hours in the summer. The principle is to focus 
on night ventilation as to use the buildingŌĆÖs internal mass to store cooling energy. When it gets too 
hot during the day, the stored cooling energy, will be slowly be released, and lower the air 
temperature, even though there will come hot in, from outside.  
 
Occupant use of fans /natural ventilation 
A ceiling fan in the room can circulate the air through a set of rotating blades. Ceiling fans do not 
cool the room. They make the occupants feel cooler, because the air moves more quickly across 
the skin and accelerates the evaporation of perspiration. Ceiling fans do not lower the indoor 
heating building temperature and they should only be on, when occupants are in the room. Another 
just as effective strategy, mot depend on the outer temperature, is to open/close the windows when 
the occupant feels to hot.  
Internal heat gain  
When you want to control the internal heat gain, you are especially concerned with the envelope,

is governed by internal factors such as number of occupants per m2 floor area, equipment in W/m2

CONCLUSION 
A completely new building standard code is created with the concept of free running buildings, 
where people have the possibility to naturally ventilate and the option to move more freely around 
in the indoor environment. The occupant is no longer the means to an end, but is participated as 
an active co-creator of the desired indoor thermal environment and thereby also parts of the 
building functionality.  
Reducing the inner temperature, with passive strategies and creating an adaptable building 
environment, is a potential and economic way, to save energy. The building model has to be 
viewed as a parametric tool to the occupant that can be modelled to suit his /her needs. It can still 
be necessary to heat the building during winter season, but thinking in ways of how to make the 
building more adaptable with passive strategies, decreases the energy use of the building and will 
create a more suitable environment for the individual user, but also for the future.  
The future climate creates a lot of concern, in that 40 % of all energy use comes from the building 
category, but also because, the future temperature, because of global warming will rise, due to 
future changes of the earthŌĆÖs climate. It is necessary to create a change in the point of view, before 
we can break free from the evil cycle. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Almost, all the results are quite accurate, especially if you lower, the cooling point to be 10 below 
the temperature threshold for natural ventilation. The cooling limit is set to 0, but it seems that the 
Honeybee plug-in needs a stabilizing temperature in different situations. I am surprised that all the 
values had a percentage difference below 20 % and 10 %.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Honeybee/Ladybug are very useful tools to simulate and visualize energy simulations and 
simulations for thermal comfort and to look at different angles of the different scenarios. It sets 
demands to the user about learning many relations between building systems and learning how 
they affect each other. Because of this, I consider Honeybee/Ladybug both a holistic program with 
a lot of validity and many very inspiring building design components.
