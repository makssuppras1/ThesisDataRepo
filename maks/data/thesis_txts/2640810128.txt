Abstract
Conventional activated sludge systems, the mainstream treatment options for wastewater
are energy and resource intensive. Energy and nutrient recovery from wastewater treatment
plants is gaining prominence in the last few decades. High rate activated sludge (HRAS)
systems are used for redirecting organics for energy recovery. These systems are not
able to remove the nutrients due to low carbon level in the next stage of treatment and
either require additional carbon source or processes like anammox for treatment, which do
not work well in cold climates. A high­rate denitrification(anoxic) system is proposed as an
alternative system for wastewater treatment for achieving energy and nutrient recovery.
The system is run as a continuous stirred tank reactor (CSTR) at different solids retention
time (SRT) (5,3,1 days) with cultivation media. SRT 3 days gave better removal efficiencies
for harvested, redirected biomass. A high denitrification rate of 93,60% was observed
on average with the denitrification becoming worse in the lower SRT 1day (77,25%).
Phosphate removal averaged around 55%. Sludge volume index observed for all SRTs
was below 50 ml/g which is lower than observed in other studies for aerobic HRAS systems
(50­300 ml/g) and suggests that the sludge had good settling characteristics. Floc area
was highest for SRT5 with fewer flocs, SRT 1 had the smallest flocs in terms of area. All
the SRTs had similar fractal dimensions. The observed yield for chemical oxygen demand
(COD) and nitrogen and the nitrogen to protein ratio was highest for SRT5. Amino acid
content was averaged 25% of dry weight and was highest in SRT1 and this is in the range
observed for HRAS for aerobic systems.

Introduction
Domestic wastewater has various constituents like organic (biodegradable and
non­biodegradable) and inorganic compounds (metals like mercury or cadmium and
nutrients like nitrogen or phosphorus) and microorganisms (Henze M., 2002). Wastewater
systems have been used to safeguard downstream users and for environmental protection.
Wastewater treatment has been one of the most important technological developments
in the twentieth century improving life expectancy and reducing harmful environmental
impact (Mark C. M. van Loosdrecht, 2014). Activated sludge process has been the main
sewage treatment technology for more than 100 years, with numerous advancements
along the years.
The concept of resource recovery has been gaining speed over the last few decades,
with a shift from removal processes to recovery of resources from the wastewater. With
the increase in population and the rise in affleunce levels more sources are needed to
meet the demands of food and energy. With a projected population of 10 billion by 2050,
the world may need 70% more food calories than in 2006 (Ranganathan, 2013). High
usage of the already dwindling freshwater resources necessitate to look for alternative
sources of water for industry, drinking and agricultural purposes. Water stressed areas are
expected to increase in the coming years (Daigger, 2007). Fertilizer nutrients (nitrogen,
phosphorus, potassium) demands have an average growth rate of 1.9% per year (FAO,
2019).
Formation of phosphorus rock takes a large geological time to form naturally
(D.L. Childers, 2011) and thus recovery can be a good option. Countries that do not
have phosphate rock have to import this key mineral and thus become susceptible to
market variations in prices (World Bank, 2016). Nutrient recovery would also reduce fossil
fuel consumption used for manufacturing fertilizers (Perry L. McCarty, 2011). Moreover,
most conventional wastewater treatment plants have a high carbon footprint, as they get
their energy from fossil fuels and this could be reduced by adopting recovery strategies.
Owing to its composition, wastewater could be one of the resources for meeting future
energy, nutrient and water requirements. It is estimated that wastewater generated in
the world is around 380 billion m3, which can irrigate up to 31 million hectares of land
(Manzoor Qadir, 2020). Domestic wastewater contains 20% of synthesized nitrogen and
phosphorus (D.J. Batstone, 2015), (Matassa, 2015). It also contains about 3kWh∙kl­1
in the form of organic carbon (D.J. Batstone, 2015), which could be utilized to counter
energy intensive processes like aeration (0,3 ­0,7 kWh m3 of wastewater (Metcalf, 2003))
for carbon removal and for nitrification (which is 50% of energy required by the plant)
(Foley, 2010) in the conventional activated sludge system (CAS).

Wastewater contains 300­800 mgCODL­1 and around 30% of it is oxidized to carbon
dioxide if CAS systems are used (S.K. Lateef, 2013). By adapting carbon redirection
techniques, a highly biodegradable sludge can be obtained from the diluted influent
wastewater, which could be used as input to obtain energy (Sancho, 2018). The carbon
capture can be undertaken by the following means: chemically enhanced primary treatment,
high rate activated sludge (HRAS) process and anaerobic process (Junfeng Wan, 2016).
HRAS process developed by (A.M. Buswell, 1923) is very successful in redirecting carbon
(E. Chase, 1944). These systems have a short hydraulic retention time (HRT) (2­4 hours),
low solids retention time (SRT) (1­4 days) (Tchobanoglous, 2003) besides having a high
food to microorganism ratio (F/M)(2­10kg Biological Oxygen Demand BOD/kg VSS­1day­1)
(B Böhnke, 1998). Studies have shown that these systems remove particulate, colloidal
and dissolved organic matter (F.A. Meerburg, 2016). The high rate in this system allows
for directing the COD to the waste stream and its performance depends on the sludge
formation, bacterial storage and bioflocculation (Jose Jimenez M. M., 2015) .
These
systems can be either used to comply with secondary effluent standards or to function
as carbon adsorption system in a two stage process (A/B process) (Jose Jimenez M. M.,
2015).The low SRT and high F/M ratio in the A systems allow the bacteria to perform both
intracellular storage (which is proven by presence of storage polymers) and adsorption
(Sancho, 2018). The B stage then is used for nutrient removal as insufficient carbon does
not allow denitrification to take place. The schematic for the A/B system is shown in the
figure 1.1
Anaerobic digestion (AD), the commonly used process for energy generation from sludge
is able to convert 30­50% of wastewater COD for biogas generation since microorganisms
convert part of the organics into carbon dioxide or a large fraction of sludge is recalcitrant,
leading to a low efficiency of 20­50% in the anaerobic digesters (Junfeng Wan, 2016). AD
can occur either under mesophilic (35°C) or thermophilic conditions (55°C). It is possible
to produce methane via methanogenic stage or hydrogen via acidogenic stage. AD is
considered one of the most environmentally sustainable process for treating bio­waste as
it has a possibility to produce bioenergy and bio­products like soil bio­fertilizer (Monson
K, 2007).

Microbial protein (MP) or single cell protein (SCP) are high quality protein derived from
microorganisms and were initially produced using cheap hydrocarbon substances like
methane or methanol (Goldberg, 1985). However, the success of MP production was
hampered due to lower prices from conventional protein sources like soybean and fishmeal,
but this changed after 1970s as an increase in oil prices made the hydrocarbon industry
no longer a cheap source. Recently development and interest in MP is increasing as
soybean production puts pressure on the land and water usage and prices of fishmeal
are increasing (Øverland M., 2010). Fish farming provides 50% of the world’s fish supply
and is expected to grow as the natural fish resources are unable to keep up with the
increase in population. MP is comparable to fishmeal in terms of cost, essential amino
acid profile and industrial scalability of production (Øverland M., 2010). The interest to use
natural gas to provide MP has grown again in recent years and cultures of Methylococcus
capsulatus, are used to produce MP under the name FeedKind (Unibio, 2016). MP as a
feed is interesting as it can be a potential input for the aquaculture industry, which meets
majority of the fish supply and shows promising results with tests done on fish species
(Silvio Matassa, 2016). It has also been tested on livestock with success (Øverland M.,
2010). MP grown on wastewater can be a way harness recovery recovery for carbon,
nitrogen and phosphorus (W Verstraete, 2016). Wastewater from food and beverage
have been listed as a source for MP production (Muys, 2019).

In terms of resource recovery MP offers a wide range of advantages compared to other
means of removal of nitrogen sources from wastewater. Generally, the ammonia present
is removed by CAS by nitrification denitrification processes or by partial nitritation anammox
(PN/A). These processes are energy intensive and equal the energy required initially
to produce them artificially in the Haber Bosch process (Matassa, 2015). The removal
process also leads to production of nitrogen gas or loss as nitrous oxide which is a
greenhouse gas. The recovery of nitrogen and its usage to produce MP could be
economically and energy beneficial by closing the anthropogenic nitrogen cycle (Christiaens
M.E., 2017).
In view of these considerations for resource depletion and scarcity, better resource recovery
from wastewater, energy intensive CAS system , problems for the HRAS system for
nutrient removal and to develop newer methods for microbial protein production, the
thesis proposes a new system for treating wastewater.The research focusses on utilizing
wastewater for redirecting carbon for energy generation and nutrient recovery by growing
bacteria in an anoxic HRAS system to produce microbial protein. The objective of the
system was to find out challenges of such a system and if such a system would function
at high rates and if this system could be a superior way for the resource and energy
recovery from wastewater. To analyze the efficacy of this system, a continuously stirred
tank reactor inoculated with activated sludge from a local wastewater treatment plant and
using a synthetic cultivation media for the bacteria growth will be operated. The system
will be run at three different SRT (5,3,1 day) and a low HRT (2hours) to function it as a
high­rate system. The performance of the reactor will be accessed by measuring total
suspended solids (TSS), removal of TCOD, sCOD to see reactor performance in terms of
carbon removal and redirection efficiencies, removal rates for nitrogen and phosphorus ,
yields for chemical oxygen demand and nitrogen and calculations will be done for energy
production, floc structure and settleability of the sludge and the MP production for the
different SRT periods will be also analyzed. Thus answering the question if this system
could be a complete system for wastewater treatment by incorporating a B system for
further nutrient removal.

Materials and Methods
2.1
Reactor description and operation
The reactor consisted of a 4.8 l chamber followed by a laminar settler and having a
plastic and silicone lid on top to provide anoxic conditions. Lamella settlers are frequently
used in water treatment to make the rectangular settlers more cost effective as they
use less space (Hanan A. Fouad, 2016). The reactor was continuously stirred using a
magnetic stirrer (IKA Mag RCT, Germany). Prior to sampling, the floating sludge (formed
due to trapped nitrogen bubbles during denitrification) was removed by mixing , biofilms
removed and after two hours sampling was carried out .The measurements from the
reactor included total suspended solids (TSS) for reactor and effluent, pH and samples
were collected and stored in the freezer (­4 °C) for total chemical oxygen demand (TCOD)
and soluble chemical oxygen demand (sCOD) for effluent , nitrogen and phosphorus
measurements which were done at a later stage. Detailed sampling schedule is given
in the Appendix. Schematic and the pictures for the reactor are given in figure 2.1. The
HRT for the system was changed by changing the flow rate of the pumps while the SRT
was changed by manually removing particular volumes of mixed sludge to maintain the
SRT. Operating conditions for the reactor are given in table 2.1.

2.2
Cultivation Media and inoculum
Cultivation media was prepared using the formula given by (Sunny Aiyuk, 2004). The
details of the compounds used are given in the appendix. The concentrated media was
prepared by weighing the compounds and adding them to the bottle containing demineralized
water. This solution was thoroughly stirred and approximately 4L(out of the 12l prepared in
one batch) were connected to the experimental setup and the remaining stored at 4°C and
used within a week. Potassium nitrate (Merch Germany, Emsure ISO reagent 99%) was
added to reach a concentration of 50mg/L in the effluent. Details for the calculations can
be found in the Appendix. Peristaltic pumps (Longer Pumps, China BT300­2J for water
and BT100­1F for culture media with head YZ1515X) were used to introduce the diluted
synthetic media in the reactor. Prior to use the pumps were calibrated/flow rates checked
by using them for a longer duration (more than 12 hours) and by measuring the volume of
water pumped and recalculating the flow rates. The concentration of the prepared culture
media was tested for total chemical oxygen demand (TCOD), total nitrogen (TN) and total
phosphorus (TP) and the values are reported in Table2.2. The reactor was inoculated
with 2L of activated sludge from the Lundtofte WWTP (Kgs. Lyngby, DK) and filled up
with tap water.

Analytical Methods
TSS for the reactor and effluent were measured using glass microfiber filters (1,2μm,
42,5mm Whatman, USA) using the standard APHA methods (APHA, 1999). sCOD and
nutrient determination was performed after sample filtration through a 0.2μm Nylon filter
(Agilent, USA). Ammonium, nitrite, and nitrate concentrations were determined colorimetrically
by a continuous­flow auto­analyzer (SKALAR San++, Netherlands). Sludge volume index
(SVI) was analyzed by allowing well­mixed sludge to settle in a 1l cylindrical flask for 30
minutes and was calculated by dividing the volume of sludge by the TSS concentration
recorded. TCOD, sCOD, TN, TP tests were conducted using Test kits (LCI 400, LCK 138,
LCK 350 Hach Germany) and then analyzed using a spectrophotometer (Hach 3900,
Germany). Reactor pH was measured using a digital meter (WTW multi­3430, Germany)

Samples for microbial protein analysis were collected during steady states for each SRT in
50 ml falcon tubes and centrifuged at 17000g (Multifuge X3R, Thermo Scientific, Germany)

freezer at ­20฀C untill further analysis. The falcon tubes were freeze dried by placing them
for one day in the equipment (Sean Vac Cool Safe, 100­9, Denmark). Microwave assisted
protein analysis was carried out by adding 1ml of 6N Hydrochloric acid (HCl) in sample
tubes and 20ml of 6N HCl in the microwave tube. Argon was used to flush the tubes to
prevent oxidative degradation. A temperature sensor was also used to verify if the tubes
were heated at the right temperature. Once flushed the sample tubes were placed in a
microwave oven (Anton Paar Multiwave 3000, AT) for thirty minutes at 150°C and allowed
to cool for thirty minutues. The hydrolyzed samples were extracted to an Eppendorf tube
using a glass pipette and 6N HCl used to fill the tube to a 1ml volume. The tube was
centrifuged, and supernatant discarded, the sample analyzed using a HPLC (Thermo
Scientific Dionex UltiMate 3000 UHPLC, USA) and was stored in a freezer at 4°C until
analysis. Calibration was done with a commercial amino acid mix (AAS18, Sigma­Aldrich)
containing L­Alanine, L­Arginine, L­Aspartic acid, L­Cystine, L­Glutamic acid, Glycine,
L­Histidine, L­Isoleucine, LLeucine, L­Lysine, L­Methionine, L­Phenylalanine, L­Proline,
L­Serine, L­Threonine, L­Tyrosine and L­Valine).
Samples for bio­methane potential analysis were collected during steady state by allowing
the biomass to settle in a one­liter graduated cylinder, discarding the supernatant and
freezing the settled biomass at ­20°C in plastic bottles. Biomethane potential test could
not be carried out due to time restrictions for the project. Biomass collection was based
on (Christof Holliger, 2016) which proposes details for standardization of BMP tests and
suggests using a total (substrate and inoculum) concentration between 20­60gVolatile

Samples were collected for future PCR and Illumina sequencing, so microbial communities

sterilized Eppendorf tube and centrifuged (Minispan plus, Germany) at 13000g for 10
minutes. Supernatant was discarded and the biomass pellet was stored in a freezer at
­20°C for future analysis for each SRT period.

3.3
Energy recovery
Around 59.05% of TCOD is removed from the influent on an average in the reactor. This
removal is lower compared to the other studies as pointed out by (I Sancho, 2019) which
have COD removal rates from 70­ 80% for A stage systems. A lower removal could help
in providing be utilized in the next stage for the system for nitrification. Average ratio
between sCOD/TCOD in the effluent was 0,29 for the three SRTs with the ratio highest
in the lowest SRT and least among the highest SRT. Since biomethane potential tests
were not carried out owing to time constraints a theoretical value is proposed based on
literature (A Nielfa, 2015). (Meerburg F. B., 2015) carried out biomethane potential tests
for high­rate systems and found specific methane yield 1,10±0,13 g COD/gTSsludge for
a SRT of 1,31days. A lower SRT of 0,41 d gave a value of 1,36±0,04 g COD/gTSsludge.
The energy recovery for the SRT 1,31 and 0,41 days in their study was as 0,19±0,05 and
0,27 ±0,04 g CODCH4/g CODinfluent.

When amino acids like glycine, glutamic acid, aspartic acid and lysine are taken together,
they are lower than 50% of the total profile suggesting that they are inferior to other
protein sources. The biomass contains essential amino acids like threonine, arginine and
methionine, all of which are essential amino acids for chicken (F. Alleman, 2000). The
experiment cannot measure tryptophan and cysteine as these amino acids are degraded
during hydrolysis (cysteine being an essential amino acid for chicken). Essential amino
acids for fish like histidine, valine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, arginine, phenylalanine,
threonine and methionine are present (T Yamamoto, 2005), thus confirming the suitability
as a fish feed. While comparing to fish meal and soybean meal in terms of essential amino
acids the profile is better at all SRTs except for methionine and tryotophan (which are not
measured here) (Figure 2). The results of this system show that the amino acid content
is in the similar range measured for aerobic heterotorphic bacteria biomass (21­49% on
dry weight) reported by (Maarten Muysa, 2020) who measured the amino acid content for
food and beverage sectors.

Conclusion and Future Work
A HRAS anoxic system was run to check the if this could be an alternate option for the
resource recovery in terms of energy and nutrients.The reactor was run at different SRT
conditions and a cultivation media was used for growing bacteria.The reactor performed
in a similar fashion in all the SRTs except the lowest SRT where the removal efficiencies
like denitrification were poor. The sludge showed good settling characteristics with very
low SVI for all SRTs. The Microbial protein content obtained was in the range observed in
aerobic HRAS systems.The amino acid content was comparable to that of fishmeal and
soybean meal.
In terms of future prospects the system could be run using real wastewater to see if a
similar performance is observed.Biomethane potential tests could be carried out to find
the methane productivity levels and if the system performs better than the otehr HRAS
systems. Public perception towards using wastewater as a source could be challenge.
