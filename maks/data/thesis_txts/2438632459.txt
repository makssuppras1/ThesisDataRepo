ABSTRACT 
This study was primarily carried out to solve the performance degradation and cost problem caused by tem-
perature drop in the ground with conventional ground-source heat pumps. It investigates the daily performance 
of a multiple-source multiple-use heat pump system, with a residential thermal load pattern, during heating 
and cooling operation. The primary objective is technology verification and optimization, which is carried out 
through a series of prototype experiments and subsequently data analysis. The system consists of a sky source 
heat pump, a ground heat exchanger, floor heating heat pump, air conditioning heat pump and domestic hot 
water heat pump, which are all interconnected through a water loop. The novelty of this system is that the 
thermal state of the ground is maintained close to natural temperature, by the sky source heat pump, through a 
combination of complimentary renewable sources, and subsequently functions similar to that of a thermal 
reservoir. This process occurs in heat collection and dissipation cycles, and in an adaptable and synergetic 
interplay with the natural surroundings. Experiments are conducted in the Renewable Energy House, which is 
a newly constructed full-scale experimental building, located in Kashiwa Campus of The University of Tokyo.  
Heating operation performance evaluation investigates a shift in heat collection operation from morning 
(08:00) to noon (12:00), which was expected to increase system performance by exploiting increased availa-
bility of solar radiation. Results show:  
1) Increased average daily performance of the sky source heat pump, with new control due to beneficial 
weather conditions and lower energy use, as a result of higher evaporation temperature.  
2) No difference in performance for the whole system, due to longer operational duration of the system, 
influenced by a higher thermal demand during noon operation. 
A performance evaluation of the air conditioning heat pump during the cooling operation was carried out using 
the average daily coefficient of performance as an indicator. Results show: 
1) Air conditioning heat pump coefficient of performance was found ranging between 9 and 14 with an 
average daily value of 11.2. Further performance improvements are expected and will be investigated 
with similar weather conditions.  
2) The average daily system coefficient of performance including the domestic hot water heat pump was 
found to be 8.6. This performance degradation is caused by several factors including the high temper-
ature demand of the hot water tank, leading to a high energy use of the compressor. 
The multiple-source and multiple-use heat pump system has proven to effectively maintain ground tempera-
ture, for a residential thermal load pattern in both summer and winter situations, with system performance 
twice as high as conventional technologies. However, there is still a need to conduct a long-term performance 
evaluation, for reliable benchmarking. The system is scalable for larger projects such as peer-to-peer thermal 
networks, as well as other building types and climates, which has yet to be explored. The installation of the 
system, components and control have optimization potential. Switching from the special high-pressure diesel 
oil motor drills to electric motors can reduce capital cost. Integrating phase change material in the sky source 
panel could capture more heat while cooling the panel, consequently increasing efficiency. Finally, a more 
advanced control for heat collection control based on availability of solar radiation could improve performance.

1.  Introduction 
1.1 Research area 
The most noticeable current global threats, considering impact and likelihood, are related to climate change 
[1]. In addition to this, increasing interconnectedness is spreading negative externalities around the world, 
creating a knock-off effect, potentially threatening and destabilizing political and economic environments. 
This is a result of a drastically accelerating manufacturing process from the industrial revolution, which in-
creased the demand for resources, leading to atmospheric pollution from the burning of fossil fuels, hence 
leaving behind a remarkable footprint on the environment. Several environmental studies have proven signif-
icant human influence on changing climate patterns, mainly due to gas emission following extensive use of 
polluting natural resources [2]. Furthermore, extensive human health issues has been identified as a conse-
quence of this pollution [3]. International political consensus has led to a transition down a more sustainable 
path by slowly phasing out fossil fuels, while diversifying and increasing the share of renewable energy. How-
ever, this transition have created new challenges because of the intermittent and fluctuating nature of renewa-
bles. From an overall energy system perspective, this has resulted in a need for new technologies for energy 
conversion, storage, and demand-side management. For an effective transformation of the energy system, there 
is a need for synergetic, stable, flexible, smart, automated and integrated technical solutions, which can balance 
supply and demand in an efficient and reliable way [3].  
A high energy intensity in the building sector, has led the European Union to set a goal of all new buildings 
constructed becoming nearly zero-energy buildings by 2020. One essential task here is to improve the energy 
quality used for space conditioning through low-exergy sources and systems. This can be achieved with ther-
mal energy storage (TES) technologies that are suitable for renewable energies due to their load-shifting ability 
and compatibility with low-temperature heating and high-temperature cooling systems in buildings. One of the 
most promising TES technologies is borehole thermal energy storage (BTES), characterized by its high thermal 
capacity and stable temperature throughout the year. The barrier for increasing implementation is that initial 
cost is high for conventional ground source heat pump (GSHP) systems, since the ground heat exchangers 
(GHE) are buried deep and coils need a large surrounding area for avoiding any thermal disturbances between 
them. The system investigated in this study seeks to solve this challenge through daily active recovery (AR) 
of the ground thermal state, which allows for a more compact design due to lower thermal diffusion length [5].   
RE House is a newly constructed experimental building located in Kashiwa Campus of The University of 
Tokyo [5]. The aim of the project is to minimize cost and optimize performance for heating and cooling sys-
tems in buildings as well as solving the challenge of fluctuating renewable energy availability. The main nov-
elty of this system is that the ground thermal state is maintained through a combination of complimentary 
renewable sources. Various innovative mechanical components and system design are used to maximize utili-
zation of thermal energy in the surroundings. A sky source heat pump (SSHP) operates with a dual function-
ality, through heat collection and dissipation cycles, to recover the thermal condition of the ground, which 
subsequently serves as a thermal reservoir. Demand-side heating and cooling appliances are interconnected 
with supply-side technologies, using a water loop as a thermal network system and water-source heat pumps 
(WSHP), which are customized and decentralized thermal extraction technologies. This unique conceptual 
system design is named multiple-source and multiple-use heat pump (MMHP) system.

1.2 Objectives 
The overall objective of this thesis is technology verification through performance evaluation of a novel hybrid 
ground source heat pump system (HGSHP), also referred to as multiple source and multiple use heat pump 
system (MMHP) system. Previous research and development, mainly of individual components have shown 
promising, so this study will take a broader system level approach.  
The focus of the study is to compare different control strategies during heating operation, by investigating a 
series of prototype experiments, aiming to maximize the average daily system performance. A shift in fixed 
running time control (FRT) for heat collection operation from morning (08:00) to noon (12:00) was expected 
to increase system performance by exploiting increased availability of solar radiation. Two different days with 
similar weather conditions and heating demand were chosen for this purpose.

2.  Background  
2.1 Renewable energy utilization  
In recent times, increasing focus on renewable energy utilization has brought a new term to life, ‘passive 
design’. The history of passive design is in fact very long, and dates back thousands of years, and became a 
necessity due to the lack of mechanical components for regulating indoor climate conditions. This concept is 
one of the main ingredients in sustainable building design and implies all measures, which maximizes use of 
natural resources without the use of active components such as mechanical or electrical systems [6]. Through 
knowledge of thermal characteristics and the thermodynamic interplay between renewable energy sources in 
the surroundings and building components, exergy-efficient solutions can be realized. In most countries, it is 
still not possible to comply with indoor climate requirements through sole use passive design, leading to the 
use of hybrid systems with a minimal share of active components. In well-insulated buildings, low temperature 
heating and high temperature cooling systems are an alternative efficient solution, compatible with various 
renewable energies. Many of these can be found in the immediate environment such as the ones listed in table 
2.1 and depicted in Fig. 2.1. Solar and nocturnal radiation are abundant but only have intermittent availability. 
Air is more reliable but its temperatures have a high level of fluctuation. Ground is more stable but its thermal 
stock is limited. This is a challenge that can be solved through complimentary use. In coming chapters, a novel 
system concept that uses this concept, will be presented and investigated.

2.2 Thermal Energy Storage (TES) 
2.2.1. Purpose 
One of the main challenges for the transition to a renewable energy based energy system is the mismatch 
between supply and demand of energy. Supply depends on the energy availability and demand depends on 
consumers. By inserting a middle stage between the two, in the form of energy storage, it would level out the 
peak load and create a flexible and a financially beneficial solution to this problem. This increasingly popular 
energy management concept, is also referred to as load shifting. By designing integrated energy-buffers in the 
building, variations can be absorbed, and distributed in times with demand. Fig. 2.2 shows the load curve of a 
household, with demand peaking during morning and nighttime while solar availability peaks in the middle of 
the day. It illustrates a tendency that exist among many renewable energy sources, and thus a need for storage.

Sensible TES (STES) is the most widespread among TES technology. It functions by heating or cooling a solid 
or liquid storage medium. There is a linear correlation between thermal supply and temperature of the medium

Latent TES (LTES) is an increasingly trending TES technology, which stores thermal energy through a phase 
change of the storage medium. The thermal storage process involves a solidification stage where heat is ab-

[4]. This technology can have several benefits such as the compact design, stabilizing effect on surrounding 
temperature, reduction in peak load leading to reduced heating and cooling systems and an increase in the 
thermal inertia of a building.                 
Thermochemical TES is based on chemical reactions that are connected during the thermal storage and release 
process. It is not very widely used due to its infant development stage.

The underground is suitable for thermal energy storage, because of the high thermal inertia. Below a depth of

2.3 Heat pump and refrigeration cycle 
2.2.1 Concept 
Heat pumps are characterized by reversing the natural flow of heat, such that it flows from a lower to a higher 
temperature [18]. As implied by the name, the objective is to ‘pump heat’ from one place (low-temperature 
reservoir) to another (high-temperature reservoir). Similar to the principles of gravitational water flow in a free 
surface condition, heat can naturally flow from a ‘high position’ to a ‘low position’ (heat transfer) but the 
reverse demands active work by a water pump, or in the case of heat, a heat pump. Thus, metaphorically, heat 
pumping might be compared to water pumping, when replacing water height with the temperature difference. 
A heat pump is a machine, which is responsible for the mechanical process of moving the heat from one 
location (heat source) at a low temperature to another location with a higher temperature (heat sink). This 
process is also referred to as the refrigeration cycle [19].  
The heat pump is made up of four fundamental components working in a closed cycle: evaporator, compressor, 
condenser and the expansion valve. The thermodynamic principle of a heat pump is described by the Carnot 
cycle or the refrigeration cycle. The most representative type of refrigeration cycle used in most practical heat 
pump technologies is a vapor compression cycle, as shown in Fig 2.9. It works by compressing vapor of a fluid 
(typically refrigerant) to generate pressure difference and utilizing the saturation temperature difference and 
latent heat accompanying the gas-liquid phase. When a refrigerant evaporates or boils, it absorbs heat at a very 
high rate, which is why it works efficiently as a thermal carrier, thus latent heat exchange is one of the core 
principles of the refrigeration cycle. The three main stages of the cycle are evaporation, condensation and 
expansion. Heat is absorbed from the heat source in the evaporator, leading to vaporizing of the fluid refriger-
ant. The compressor transforms the liquid into a high-temperature gaseous phase, by compressing it and thus 
raising the pressure. Subsequently heat is released in the condenser (heat sink), leading to a drop in temperature 
and a phase change to liquid.      
                         
Fig. 2.9 Refrigeration process diagram (left) and ideal Carnot cycle (right) [11] 
A refrigerator is technically the same thing as a heat pump, but the objective is a bit different. The purpose of 
the refrigerator is to drain heat from a low temperature body (cool the heat source), while the heat pump seeks 
to provide heat to a high-temperature body (warm the heat sink). Metaphorically speaking a refrigerator can 
be viewed as a drainage pump, while a heat pump is a supply pump [19]. A heat pump with both a heating and 
==PAGE:25==
11 
 
refrigeration function is known as a reverse-cycle heat pump and use a four-way valve to change between its 
dual functionality. The theoretical highest achievable efficiency, expressed through a theoretical coefficient of 
performance (COP) is represented through an ideal thermodynamic model known as the Carnot cycle. It is 
found using the temperatures of the cold region and warm region, Tc and Th. The refrigerant temperature in the 
evaporator must be less than Tc and the temperature in the condenser must be greater than Th to allow heat 
transferring [20]. The real or practically achievable COP usually drops to about half due to various losses. The 
interesting aspect is the optimization potential, due to the large gap between achievable and commercially 
available heat pumps. For heating purposes in buildings, the current technologies have a COP of 5-6, while 
the estimated achievable COP is estimated around 10 [19].  
The critical parameter in optimizing the performance of a HP is the temperature difference between cold side 
(e.g. outdoor air or ground) and hot side (e.g. room). This can be achieved through use of stable heat sources 
with temperatures close to that of the heat sink, such as ground source heating/cooling in a combination with 
low temperature heating and high temperature cooling systems. In addtion to that heating and cooling loads 
and energy use of auxiliary components (pumps fans etc.) should be minimized [21].  
 
Fig. 2.10 – Carnot COP and temperature difference [60] 
Choice of heat source for heating and heat sink for cooling is crucial for performance. Moderate temperatures 
and large thermal quantity is desirable. Heating and cooling performance of ASHP are usually characterized 
seperately and evaluated in long-term operation. For heating, this rating is reffered to as seasonal performance 
factor, while energy efficiency ratio is used for cooling [22]. The COP of the heat pump varies with load factor, 
which is a ratio between operational and maximum capacity. Considering the heating and cooling load of a 
building, the operation is usually a long term operation at a low load rate. Therefore, to evaluate the actual 
seasonal or annual operational efficiency, the operation performance at partial load becomes important [19]. 
The importance of reducing the entropy production in order to avoid irreversible processes was recognized as 
early as 1824 by Carnot [23]. This has laid the foundation for the exergy term, which identifies thermodynamic 
inefficiencies, due to irreversible processes, and is the one of the main ideas behind the system design presented 
later.

2.2.2 Technologies 
 
Most HVAC systems use some sort of heat pump for heating and cooling. They possess a high potential to 
increase the share of renewable energy share, due to their thermodynamic utilization opportunities through its 
mechanical design with a high output/input ratio. Average input energy is equal to 25% of the output energy. 
This mean that for every 1 W of electric energy an additional of 3 W of ‘free’ usable energy is obtained. The 
primary source of thermal energy for heat pumps is ambient air, soil and ground water [24].

ASHP and GSHP have a long history in the HVAC industry, while hybrid solutions, especially hybrid ground-
source heat pump systems (HGSHP) have been increasingly popular in recent times, due to their attractive 
characteristics such as flexibility, efficiency and synergetic interplay. The choice of system depends on a wide 
range of decision variables from economic, environmental to the more practical such as availability of space.  
ASHP  
Air source heat pumps (ASHP) are both cheaper and more easily deployed and applied than other types of heat 
pumps, such as GSHP, which is the reason behind their widespread use. Most households use a separated 
ASHP (split) type in which the outdoor unit and the indoor unit are connected by refrigerant piping as shown 
in Fig 2.11.  
 
Fig. 2.11 Split-system type ASHP in (a) cooling mode and (b) heating mode [61] 
 
==PAGE:27==

The heat exchanger of an ASHP is typically composed of a finned-tube coil and a fan. The outdoor air flows 
over the coil in such a manner as to pass heat or cold to the refrigerant inside the coil with an acceptable 
difference in heat transfer temperatures. A fourway-valve is used for reversible heating/cooling operation.   
The drawback of this technology is the instable and inefficient operation due to wide-spanning temperature-
range and reverse correlation between ASHP performance and thermal demand. This is a result of two main 
reasons. Firstly thermal energy availability falls when the demand increases. In winter, when heating demand 
increases, outdoor temperature decreases, which reduces heat extraction opportunities. The opposite trend oc-
curs for summer conditions. Changing for a larger heat capacity and using an inverter (for adjusting compressor 
speed) would lead to excessive start and stop which degrades the performance. Secondly, a defrosting necessity 
of the coils in winter further reduces performance, since heating cycle needs to be reversed to remove frost.   
An ASHP generally has a COP of 2–4 for heating operation [25], which is quite low compared to the GSHP. 
This is clear according to the EHPA Quality Label for electrically powered heat pumps. ASHP (air/water) need 
a minimum COP of 3.1 in the testing conditions, while GSHP is either 4.3 (brine/water) or 5.1 (water/water) 
dependent on the fluid used in the refrigeration circuit [26]. This is mainly due to the comparatively low heat 
source temperature and large temperature difference between heat source and heat sink. In some climates such 
as Germany outside air (heat source) has a temperature range of 36 K and average temperature of 2.8 °C , 
whereas GSHP is just 9 K with 7.1 °C (using boreholes) [27]. In addition to that, energy usage of a fan for the 
heat exchangers is significantly higher compared to that of brine or water pump. In some conditions, such as 
moderate weather, ASHP can reach a COP close to 5. An example of an highly energy-efficient commercial 
air-to-water ASHP of the model Altherma ERLQ004CAV3 from Daikin, which was used in the EMBRACE 
building from Technical University of Denmark for Solar Declathon Europe 2014 [16]. The maximum COP 
for this unit is 4.72, which is reached with an ambient temperature of 7 °C, since this has been used as a 
standardized test condition, by the manufacturer, during performance evaluation.           
GSHP 
The most reliable source of natural thermal energy, can be found in the underground (soil and groundwater), 
due to its stable temperature throughout the year. The ground temperature is cooler than the outdoor air 
temperature in summer, making for a better heat sink, and warmer in the winter, making for a better heat source 
[28]. A wide  variety of renewable energies are thermally stored in the ground during natural atmospheric 
processes, which make them easily accesseable, if engineered and utilized properly. Ground source heat pumps 
(GSHP) or geothermal heat pumps are usually substantially more energy-efficient than even ASHPs, which 
uses the highly fluctuating air temeprature [29]. The principles of ground source heat pumps were first 
described by Lord Kelvin in the 1850s and continuous development since they were first used commercially 
in the late 1940s has greatly improved their efficiency [30]. One of the latest significant efficiency-enhancing 
aspect has been the active regeneration of the thermal state of the ground. The latest advanced system design 
include consideration of rates of regeneration found through a thermal respons test or TRT [62]. GSHP systems 
are especially common in colder regions, where ASHP are not performing well, such as USA, Canada, Sweden, 
Switzerland and Germany [30]. More than 30,000 GSHP units have been installed in Switzerland (2015) and 
over 250,000 in USA (2004) [31]. Even though GSHP has a wide range of attractive characteristics, its market 
share is still limited due to the shortcomings such as space requirements and higher initial investment cost. A 
conceptual diagram illustrating the vapor/compression cycle and main components is shown in Fig. 2.12.

Ground-coupled heat pump systems  
Since GCHPS transfers heat through a closed-loop ground heat exchanger (GHE), which has less regulatory 
barriers and is more reliable, this is the typical choice of system. The performance of a GSHP system is 
influenced by many variables, such as the type of GHEs, soil condition, local climate, building load 
characteristics, system design and control optimization [32]. Optimal design and advanced control of GSHP 
systems are essential to maximize their performance while maintaining indoor thermal comfort at acceptable 
levels. The control of these highly climate-dependent systems should be explored locally to find the best 
operation scheme in accordance with a specific location. A significant challenge for GSHP is the fact that 
continuous long-term operation will reduce the thermal quantity (stocked seasonal heat or coolness) in the 
ground, consequently deteriorating the system performance [31]. Source temperature has a high impact on the 
performance, and a decrease will lead to inefficient operation of the compressor and a lower COP during 
heating and cooling mode [38]. This is why a large soil volume is required, in the shape of deep boreholes or 
a wide ground area, increasing cost. Another aspect is the simplified control used in conventional GSHPs. 
They are designed to cover full load of a building and the compressors operates with on/off control. Since the 
maximum design load is only used for short periods of time, and normal operation is at substantially lower 
levels, capacity control is essential for performance. As the temperature difference changes, due to either heat 
demand or ground temperature, the evaporation and condensation rate of the refrigerant will be affected, 
leading to a need for regulating the compressor or other pumps in the system. This works by adjusting flow 
rate thorugh a variable-speed compressor or pump [39]. Furthermore it works most efficiently with a water 
loop, due to storage capacity, but in cold conditions close to ground surface,  an antifreezer (such as brine) is 
needed. A well-designed unassisted GSHP have been shown to typically achieve a COP of up to 4 depending 
on the heating system, climate and operating conditions [30]. High temperature heating sources such as radia-
tors can lower COP with around 50%, which is the reason for recommended use with low temperature heating 
systems, such as radiant floors.

Borehole

Performance of a typical well-designed solar assisted GSHP system can achieve a COP of up to 5.8 when

primary heat sink, while using the ground as a secondary heat sink for excess heat injection through bore holes

GEOTABS 
GEOTABS is a novel hybrid system concept combining a geothermal heat pump combined with a thermally 
activated building system [45, 46]. These complimentary systems allow for use of low temperature heating 
(27–29 °C) and high temperature cooling (18–22 °C). Through the integration of natural and reliable thermal 
energy sources and a stable distribution system, unpredictable loads can be met efficiently. It can operate in 
three modes: heating with a heat pump, passive cooling or active cooling with a reversible heat pump. Inde-
pendence of the system from the occupancy time, can increase flexibility and optimize control strategy based 
on electricity prices, available renewable energy etc. Model predictive control is an essential part of the system, 
which is using a coded objective function (such as energy price) with constraints (such as indoor temperature 
range) to solve a mathematical optimization problem.

Multiple-source and multiple-use heat pump   
A heat pump technology utilizing multiple surrounding renewable energy sources, such as solar radiation, 
nocturnal radiation, rain, wind and ambient air has been in development since 1974 [49]. This prototype de-
velopment was started by Toshiyuki Hino and originally named ambient-energy heat pump system [50]. It 
consists of storage units, compressor unit and panels with heat collection and dissipation capabilities. Through 
its reversible functionality, it can provide both heating and cooling. Refrigerant directly circulates in the panel, 
integrating it as a component in the refrigeration cycle. The idea behind this configuration is to better exploit 
the multitude of heat sources and sinks in the environment. In the heating cycle, the panels work as an evapo-
rator and a condenser in the cooling cycle. It showed promising performance with a seasonal performance 
factor of 3.5 [50]. One significant reason for this improved performance is the ability to operate despite of 
heavily defrosted conditions due to utilization of ambient air.

(SSHP). In 2011, an improved prototype of SSHP was tested in University of Tokyo [53]. It is estimated that 
the MMHP will be more than twice as efficient as conventional high-efficiency technology, in temperate cli-
mates.

3.1 System concept 
Multiple-source and multiple-use heat pump (MMHP) system does, as the name implies, flexibly utilize mul-
tiple thermal energy sources within and outside the system to satisfy a thermal demand through multiple de-
centralized (water-source) heat pumps. The novelty of the MMHP concept is that the ground thermal state is 
maintained at its initial natural temperature by the combination of complimentary renewable sources as shown 
in Fig 3.2. Similar to a battery the ground is rechargeable, once ‘depleted’. In a diurnal charge/discharge cycle, 
the SSHP works as a primary (dis)charger by regulating the ground temperature through either heat collection 
or dissipation depending on the need. The ground consequently behaves like a thermal reservoir with temper-
ature-restoration and thereby solves the challenge of decreased performance in GSHPs as a result of thermal 
imbalance in the ground. Thermal storage in the system is mainly concentrated on the source-side by utilizing 
building thermal mass and hot water tank, instead of supply-side. This further reduces thermal losses.    
 
Fig 3.2 Internal and external thermal exchange and the main components [54] 
Metaphorically speaking, the system concept can be perceived as an interconnected thermal network, from 
which heat is added and subtracted as needed with the objective of achieving a thermal balance. ACHP and 
refrigeration functions as heat sources, while space heating and HWHP works as heat sinks. The atmosphere 
and ground have dual functionalities, working as both heat sources and heat sinks depending on the need. Each 
cooling operation in the HPs adds heat to the water loop, while heating operations draws heat out.

3.2.1 Sky source heat pump 
Minor fluctuations of water loop temperature due to heating or cooling demand are compensated by GHE, 
while larger ones activates the SSHP. It consists of a heat pump unit and a sky source panel (SSP) connected 
through a refrigeration loop with manifold layout using R32 as a refrigerant. It operates with a dual function-
ality by evaporating the refrigerant in the panel for heat collection, while working as a condenser during heat 
dissipation. Top surface of the SSP collects thermal energy from surrounding radiation, while extruded fins on 
the back absorbs it from the ambient air, ensuring efficient operation at nighttime, during frost and in cloudy 
days. The panel is 8 m2 (2 m x 4 m x 0.2 m) with 30° tilt in southeastern direction and consists of aluminum-
extruded finned tubes, placed in parallel and integrated photovoltaic (PV) cells through a thin plate glass on 
top. Thermal capacity (maximum thermal output) for the SSHP is 5 kW, while electricity generation capacity 
is 1 kW. Aluminum was chosen as a material for its high thermal conductivity, light weight, inexpensiveness 
and recycling opportunities. It has a water resistant black coating on both sides for enhancing a wide spectrum 
of radiative absorbance (direct, diffuse and reflected) as well as emittance.  
The main part of the solar radiation is absorbed by the PV cells, while thermal collection is secondary [52]. 
Power generation efficiency is increased due to the cooling effect achieved by the thermal collector. It is esti-
mated that the production of PV power compliments the compressor electricity demand on an annual basis 
[51]. During night-time, cooling electricity is cheap, which is also beneficial. The compressor is variable-speed 
controlled with an inverter. Since expansion valves are typically instable, slow in response and have inefficient 
oil recovery, a drainer (gas trap) is used in the SSHP [8]. The drainer is a thermal expansion device that controls 
the flow and pressure of the refrigerant by draining condensed refrigerant and preventing the passage of high-
pressure gas to the evaporator. Fig. 3.5 shows the construction of the sky source panel in a cross-section.

3.2.1.1 Heat collection mode (charging cycle) 
In heat collection mode, the sky source panel functions as a refrigerant direct expansion evaporator. Both sides 
of the panel works for both convective as well as radiative heat exchange. First part of the actual heat output 
of the panel is determined by either the evaporator thermal capacity (maximum possible heat collection) or the 
actual heat collection (available surrounding thermal energy and collector efficiency). Second part depends on 
the ‘thermal lift’ from the compressor, which in total sums up the condenser output. The temperature of the 
collector is close to ambient air when insolation is sufficiently available. In this condition panel has shown to 
nearly meet evaporator capacity, of heat pump cycle [54]. As solar radiation decreases, the collector tempera-
ture naturally drops below that of ambient air and absorbs both solar heat and enthalpy of air to compensate 
for the deficiency [5]. During cloudy days and night-time, when solar energy is unavailable, panel temperature 
further drops as much as 10 K, leading to a full reliance on ambient air. The panel seeks an equilibrium state 
where the heating capacity of the compressor is equal to the quantity of heat flow from the air to the panel with 
free convective heat transfer [49]. Consequently the panel starts working similarly to an air-source heat pump 
but with no auxiliary boiler needed. Heat transfer coefficient of SSP is further increased by larger wind speeds 
and rain, which cools due to evaporation of latent heat [50].  On humid days latent heat of the water vapor in 
the air can be used, when temperature of the panel drop below dew point temperature leading to condensation.  
 
Air-source operation eliminates a need for defrosting, even though the panel can frost on cold days. Heat 
collection operation during sunny days is also beneficial for electricity use (from compressor etc.) and costs 
since free and naturally generated electricity from PV is available during day-time when electricity is expen-
sive. At night-time, operational energy use as well as electricity prices are low. An example of this daily dis-
tribution of heat source contributions and electricity sources is shown on Fig. 3.10. It illustrates the use of heat 
sources during a typical sunny day. In the morning and afternoon panel temperature will drop to collect heat 
in either partly air-source or full air-source mode. The collector efficiency can increase above 100%, as a result 
of heat absorption from ambient air, due to the natural below-ambient temperature of the refrigerant during 
times with low level of insolation. Fig. 3.11 shows the SSHP refrigeration cycle during heating operation for 
different levels of solar availability in a graphical form. Collected heat from air contributes less, which creates 
a shift in the evaporation temperature, and thus the heat output obtained from natural heat sources. The com-
pressor will then compensate appropriately in case there is a mismatch with the heat demand.

3.2.1.2 Heat dissipation mode (discharge cycle)  
The GHE and HWHP provide a significant cooling capacity in themselves, leaving the SSHP to function as an 
auxiliary cooler during summer days with extraordinary cooling demand. Sky source panel works as a noctur-
nal radiator by dissipating heat through night-sky radiation and convective heat transfer using natural draft and 
catalyzed by wind and rain [5]. This dissipation process occurs when high-pressure evaporated refrigerant 
condenses in the panel, releasing latent heat and raising the temperature by more than 10 °C above ambient and 
consequently exchanging heat with the air. Besides this rain works as an effective heat sink, by evaporating on 
the panel. Lastly, there is a radiant rejection to the night sky depending on the temperature difference between 
panel and sky. Despite its name of a ‘radiator’, radiation only consist of a small part of the heat transfer (up to 
30% with clear skies) [50]. One disadvantage is heat loss during heat collection when the sky is clear and its 
temperature is lower than panel temperature. Heat transfer coefficient of the panel largely depends on the 
weather conditions, which affects the condensing temperature and eventually COP. Most efficient (but not 
necessarily available) natural heat sinks exposed to the SSHP are in following order:  
1. Rain  
2. Ambient air (natural draft or wind) 
3. Night-sky radiation

3.2.2 Ground heat exchanger and water loop 
Using the ground for thermal storage serves two main purposes. The first one is to compensate for discrepan-
cies between heat pump output and heat demand of a building. Secondly, the natural high level of thermal 
content from renewable energies can be utilized and additionally added actively. Water loop has an essential 
function in the system, since it is responsible for the thermal flow and partly for short-term thermal storage 
between heat sources and heat sinks. Water has advantageous thermal characteristics such as large heat capac-
ity, which it uses to transport collected outdoor heat and indoor waste heat. In conventional GSHP, anti-freeze 
solutions are typically necessary, but since the ground loop in MMHP is maintained at a moderate temperature, 
and pipes are placed either underground or well-insulated, water can be used. Water circulation temperature 
in the GHE kept close to a natural ground temperature of 17 °C ± 5 K (for Tokyo area), avoiding any heat 
losses to surrounding as conventional TES technologies. This temperature range is also desirable since it is 
close to supply temperatures for heating and cooling, leading to an increase in heat pump performance. In case 
water loop temperature suits the purpose of the heat demand, heat pumps are bypassed. In order to reduce 
energy use for circulation of the water, a small water pump is used for each heat pump [55]. The water loop is 
made up of polyethylene (PE) pipes with a larger diameter size for reduced pressure loss. Decentralized water 
circulation pumps are of the type Alpha 2 (25-40, 180) from Grundfos. PE pipes are typically not applicable 
in GHE since antifreeze is necessary, but in this case, where water can be heated by SSHP, it is possible.  
The GHE is 15 m deep and is constructed with three high density and compact helical coils, made of high-
density polyethylene corrugated tubes shaped with an outer diameter of 0.7 m, and are installed at 1.5 to 2 m 
intervals. The diameter is larger than conventional GHE, due to the compact design. Theoretical capacity of 
each coil is 2.5 kW, but the total real capacity of the coils is 5 kW, due to various efficiency losses. Boreholes 
are typically separated by 5-6 m because of the thermal diffusion length, which is reduced to a shallow length 
of 20 cm in this case, due to daily cycle of regeneration and compact design, which is shown in Fig. 3.13 [53]. 
The depth of GHE is not common for vertical heat exchangers, however one scope of the project has been to 
reduce drilling cost by densifying the construction. This is currently challenged by the fact that special drilling 
equipment is required. The constructors used a high-pressure diesel oil motor drills, typically used for infra-
structure, but electric motors can reduce installation cost. Once the coilers were installed, the drillings were 
backfilled with silica sand. It can hold about 200 liters of tap water, which is similar to a DHW tank of a large 
size residential house [51, 56], proving the potential for thermal storage in the water loop.

1. Thermal extraction starts 
2. Storage gain through heat flow from surrounding soil (limited) 
3. Once surrounding soil is depleted temperature of ground decreases 
4. Once temperature is below control set point, SSHP operation starts

3.3. Control 
The goal of the control strategy is to satisfy thermal demands, while minimizing energy use, and maximizing 
performance of the system.  Control in cooling operation is based on a strict serial operation strategy, separat-
ing discharge and cooling operation, while heating can operate in both serial and parallel control, by heating 
and charging at the same time. Control strategy is divided into: SSHP, FHHP, ACHP and HWHP.    
Because of the limited thermal capacity of the ground, it cannot be considered as an infinite thermal reservoir. 
To utilize the geothermal energy at a sustainable level, the subsurface thermal state should be recovered to a 
certain level. In this system, this is achieved by utilizing the dual functionality of the SSHP for restoring the 
ground temperature close to the undisturbed natural temperature of 17 °C. This is achieved by regulating the 
circulating water flow in the ground heat exchanger to a similar level. The fundamental control strategy of the 
SSHP consists of two operational modes with the purpose of achieving a thermal balance in the system.  
 
Charging mode - Heat is transferred to the ground through collection from the SSHP   
 
Discharging mode - Heat is extracted from the ground and dissipated through the SSHP

setpoint temperature during cooling operation. The control strategy of the system consists of a flexible combi-
nation of fixed running time (FRT) and circulating water temperatures. SSHP runs on a fixed leaving temper-
ature (FLT) control, which is the return temperature of the GHE, while For the SSHP control of daytime, the 
deadband temperature ranges from 15–17 °C both of the investigated days. During nighttime, water of the

3.3.1 SSHP Compressor 
The compressor maintains a constant heat output of 6 kW, but is variable-speed controlled with an inverter 
which responds to an approximate refrigerant evaporation temperature, during both charging mode and dis-
charging mode. This temperature measured at compressor inlet, and corresponds to the outlet of the evaporator, 
which is the SSP during charging mode and heat exchanger during discharge mode. The frequency (rotations 
per second) of the compressor is adjusted according to heat exchange conditions of the panel depending on 
weather conditions. The control schedule in Fig. 3.26 illustrates three operational stages of the compressor for 
both heating and cooling mode: a constant high-speed stage for very low evaporation temperatures, constant 
low-speed stage for sufficiently high evaporation temperatures and finally a variable-speed stage in between 
these two states. This capacity control will beneficially lower the temperature differences between the evapo-
rator and the condenser by adjusting the thermal input relative to the demand of auxiliary heat and thus increase 
the performance. During heat collection cycles with low solar availability, panel temperature decreases, en-
hancing the heat extraction from ambient air. This lowers the evaporation temperature and enthalpy obtained 
from surroundings, consequently increasing compressor speed as shown on the control schedule, to achieve 
the desired condensation temperature. The compressor will only run in highest level in very extreme and rare 
weather conditions, such as when evaporation temperature is below -26 °C.  In order to ensure efficient radia-
tive and convective heat exchange during discharge operation, the SSP is raised about 10 °C above surrounding 
air temperature, with the help of the compressor. Since the outdoor temperature is usually not higher than 10-
20 °C on warm summer nights, the compressor starts decreasing its speed from an evaporation temperature of 
20 °C until it reaches 30 °C, which is sufficient and thus compressor runs on its lowest frequency. Dead band 
of operational temperature range is essential for efficiency and should avoid frequent activation/deactivation 
of the heat pump to secure stable and efficient operation and avoid wearing out compressor.

4.2.6 System COP  
The system performance during heating operation is evaluated through the system COP. This property is de-
termined from the ratio between the amount of useful thermal energy in the system, and the quantity of work 
used to produce this energy, which is also expressed as the total energy use from the system. This includes 
electricity use of compressor, water circulation pumps, fan and control equipment. The system COP for heating 
operation and cooling operation are calculated differently, because of the differences in its functional purposes. 
When looking at the heating operation performance, COP is the ratio between heat removed from the heat 
source plus the work of the compressor. For cooling operation, only the evaporated or extracted heat is con-
sidered, since this is the only useful heat for the purpose of this operational mode. Hence compressor heat must 
be subtracted from the cold output.

measured in W. It can be calculated as both an instantaneous value and as a time-dependent value, for example

5.1. Heating operation  
5.1.1 Solar-source collection and air-source collection contribution  
The SS Panel operates in three different modes: solar-source mode, air-source mode, and finally combined 
solar- and air-source mode. Temperature difference between ambient air and refrigerant determines which 
mode is in function. When the panel heats the refrigerant enough to exceed ambient air temperature, the solar 
source collection mode is in operation. A negative heat transfer to the ambient air occurs since heat is in excess. 
When evaporation temperature drops below the ambient air, but solar radiation is still heating the panel, a 
mixed operational mode consisting of solar and air is working. Finally a sole reliance on air-source heat comes 
to play when insolation is unavailable. Two of these functionalities are illustrated on Fig. 5.1, for heating 
operation during 13th of March. For simplification purposes average temperature of the refrigerant inlet and 
outlet to the panel is considered as evaporation temperature. Panel temperature has been heated by the solar 
radiation during morning hours and gets as high as 33 °C around 12 pm. Once SSHP starts running panel 
temperature almost immediately drops 15 °C, due to the heat absorption of the refrigerant through latent heat 
transfer during evaporation. This leads to an increase of evaporation temperature until thermal equilibrium is 
achieved, and then panel temperature approaches similar temperature as the refrigerant.

5.1.2 Collection efficiency  
Fig. 5.2 illustrates the correlation between collector efficiency and temperature difference between panel and 
ambient air on the 13th of March.  As it can be seen on the graph, efficiency exceeds 100%. The reason for this 
is that the air-source collection is included in the calculation, which is a gain in addition to the solar radiation. 
A noteworthy remark is that a typical solar collector only collects heat when the panel temperature is higher 
than the ambient [51]. As the temperature of the panel drops, heat absorption from air increases, due to lack of 
available solar radiation, which is the reason for the very high efficiency of almost 250% with a temperature 
deficit of 2 °C. This does not give a correct representation of the panel air collection efficiency, since only

5.1.4 SSHP - Heat output and electricity use 
The daily heat production and electricity use of the SSHP along with GHE return temperature is illustrated in 
Fig. 5.5. Heat output for both days reaches around the same level of around 5-6 kW, which expected since this 
is the heat capacity and only level it is controlled to provide. The reason for small fluctuations is partly adjust-
ments of compressor speed with the evaporation temperature as well as uncertainties in the measurement 
equipment, such as temperatures measured on pipe surface. GHE return temperature starts at the same temper-
ature for both days. Heating of the ground loop water is slower on the 13th, which leads to longer operating 
time for the SSHP. This is a cause of both an increase in total heat quantity delivered as well as a higher total 
energy use of the same day. One explanation could be the temperature of surrounding ground, which could 
have been lower due to heating operation and consequently heat extraction on previous day.  The SSHP 
switches off at a temperature of 17 °C but it reaches at least 20 °C in both cases. Instantaneous electricity use 
is lower on 13th of March due to more efficient heating of the refrigerant leading to higher evaporation tem-
perature and less compressor energy.  
Temperature is fluctuating 2-3 °C every 30 minutes outside of operation time due to measurement-related 
circulation as mentioned earlier. Around 10-11 pm, a large reduction in temperature is shown on the graph, due 
to floor heating operation, extracting heat from the water loop. In reality outlet temperature of the GHE was 
not measured at the investigated days, due to missing sensors at the time, however it has been simplified to be 
equal to SSHP water inlet temperature. This is possible since heat losses of pipes are very low since they are 
mostly buried underground. Since water loop temperature is highly related to and influenced by ground tem-
perature this parameter is depicted on the figure, measured 10 m deep. As it can observed temperature of the 
ground starts at the same level, however since SSHP starts later on the 13th, ground temperature reaches a lower 
temperature, which explains the reason for longer operation duration.  
Rate of heat transferred (slope of curve) to the ground over time is very similar for both days, which leaves 
only the small additional temperature drop on the second day as a reason for longer operation. The larger 
temperature difference between ground and water loop, the higher heat loss occurs. Once the ground starts 
heating up and gets closer to the water loop temperature then set point can be reached, since heat transfer to 
ground is low and thus a state of thermal equilibrium. It is important to notice that this value is measured in 
the center of the coil, but surrounding soil also has an important role to play, since heat is flowing both ways 
and might vary from this value. A lower surrounding ground temperature could explain why the thermal lift 
on the 13th takes longer. Thermal storage in the ground (used for FHHP), which is partly characterized by 
ground temperature is only slightly higher after SSHP operation on the 13th, which means benefits from in-
creasing evaporation temperature on the next day might not be much higher.      
Temperature loggers have recently been installed in GHE water loop, but until now return temperature from 
water loop has been assumed to be the same as SSHP input (this might have had some uncertainties due to 
heat losses). In addition to that temperature sensor for refrigerant is installed on the pipe so is does not actu-
ally calculate the exact value of the refrigerant but only an approximation.

5.1.5 FHHP - Heat output and electricity use 
Heat output, electricity use and supply temperature of FHHP is shown in Fig. 5.6. During daytime, there is not 
a high heat demand since sun is shining and heating up the room, but floor temperature and thus supply tem-
perature slowly start to decrease, due to heat losses. There is a steep drop of about 3-4 K in supply temperature 
after heat pump turns off, since no more heat is added to maintain this high temperature difference, and thermal 
storage capability is low (small volumes of water), thus a thermal equilibrium seeking process will begin. After 
this heat loss occurs, it approaches room temperature exponentially during daytime, until nighttime. Once dead 
band limit of 5 K below set point supply temperature of 27 °C is reached, the heat pump will start operating, 
until temperature is satisfactory. FHHP has very similar starting times and levels of heat output and electricity 
use for both days, but with longer operating times, on the 13th March. This is partly caused by an increased 
heat demand on the 13th, due to 5 K temperature difference in ambient air, as illustrated in Fig. 5.4. The shifted 
starting time of the SSHP is causing a significantly longer operational duration of the FHHP on the 13th. This 
leads to more heat extraction from the ground, decreasing ground temperature, which is the also a reason for 
longer operation time for the SSHP. One solution for avoiding this might be to stop the FHHP manually earlier, 
since the supply temperature has been very close to a satisfactory temperature level for a while. During the 
first investigated day, operation of FHHP continues for about 1 h into SSHP operation, leading to simultaneous 
parallel operation, while it stops before on the second day. This could also have influenced the increased energy 
use of SSHP (Fig 5.5), since heat is supplied to GHE as well as floor heating, consequently leading to more 
compressor work. One suggestion to avoid this type of inefficient process is to avoid parallel operation, since 
it could be argued that residents in this type of building will not be at home, during noon. Temperature of heat 
source, which in this case is the ground, has a crucial impact on the FHHP COP. A more accurate evaluation 
can be achieved by analyzing two days in a row, since benefits from SSHP thermal recharging is not reaped 
until the next day, and thus does not show as an effect on the system COP same day.

5.1.7 System COP 
Fig. 5.7 shows the system performance consisting of SSHP and FHHP for the two days. It depicts the instan-
taneous performance at every minute during the day. The system COP decreases during SSHP operation due 
to FHHP on stand-by mode, which results in a very low amount of usable energy being produced. Besides this, 
the system COP is stable with an average value of around 10 throughout the operation with minor fluctuations 
from the variations of pumping operation and flow rate. During the startup period of the FHHP, the system 
COP increases slightly above 12 due to increased heat output to heat up the floor. It is important to notice that 
system COP does not necessarily consider thermal energy collected by the SSHP and stored in the ground, but 
only the final usable heat at the FHHP. In this way all heat losses and inefficiencies throughout the system are 
considered such as piping and ground. Most influential parameters on the performance on the investigated 
days is panel temperature and ground temperature. Panel temperature is an essential indicator of the thermal 
gain from surroundings, and an increase will reduce the need for auxiliary heat from compressor. Varying 
weather conditions in previous days can have an effect on ground temperature. In case of cloudy or cold days 
ground temperature might take longer to recover.  FHHP continues operation for about 1 hour into SSHP 
operation, due to parallel operation. During SSHP operation energy use drops on the 13th due to increasing 
panel temperature and decreased compressor work. This is reflected on the curve with an increase and then 
slow decrease. It should be noted that this diurnal performance does not necessarily reflect season or annual 
performance. Operational variations and weather conditions throughout the year should be considered. Annual 
variations of climatic conditions (solar, ground temp etc.) might have an influence on seasonal performance. 
Increasing temperatures will lead to increasing demand but also increasing ground temperature. The high level 
of reliance of performance on climatic conditions, will need to be considered for long-term operations.

5.2. Cooling operation 
Cooling operation include HWHP and ACHP operation, and is thus divided into two parts, since operation 
occurs at different hours of the day. HWHP is controlled with a fixed timer, to run at 6.30 in the morning, with 
a duration of 30 min. It utilizes the excess heat previously extracted from ACHP and stored in the water loop 
and ground, for heating of the domestic hot water tank. ACHP is manually switched on and off during the 
afternoon, but can also be controlled through the exhaust air temperature. The thermal capacity during cooling 
operation quite high, since it involves multiple heat sinks including both the ground, HWHP water tank and 
the night sky through dissipation from the nocturnal radiator of the SSHP. This allows for a thermal buffer, to 
be maintained at all times, and alleviates auxiliary components such as the SSHP, for sole operation at peak 
demands, not able to be provided by ground and HWHP. In the following sections ACHP and HWHP will be 
investigated along with ground conditions to observe the behavior and performance of the system, under a 
typical sunny summer’s day in Tokyo.

5.2.2 ACHP operation 
Fig. 5.10 illustrates the different operating parameters of both primary (refrigerant loop) and secondary loop 
(water loop) connected to the ACHP along with room temperature at a height of 1.1 m. As shown by the flow 
rate of water pump there is a constant operation running from start to stop with a duration of 3.5 h. At the start 
of ACHP operation room temperature is 29 °C, but slowly decreases until reaching setpoint of 24 °C. The light 
construction of the building increases rate of temperature increase and decrease, due to faster heating and 
cooling with a low thermal capacity in the construction. This is a benefit for faster regulation of the temperature 
and heat exchange when outdoor temperature is lower, but a disadvantage in terms of utilizing thermal mass 
for peak-shaving as well as load-shifting.  
Once ACHP operation starts, air supply temperature quickly drops from 29 °C–12 °C. This low supply tem-
perature is a result of the very low evaporation temperature of around 8–9 °C, which is necessary for dehumid-
ification. Relative humidity in the supply air from outside is affecting the evaporation temperature, due to a 
need for lower temperature to absorb the significant amount of heat released during latent heat exchange from 
condensation of the moisture. Water inlet and outlet temperature slowly increases due to the heat exchange 
with the condenser. 
Condensing temperature is very close to the water outlet temperature showing efficient heat exchange in the 
condenser, while temperature difference between evaporating refrigerant and air temperature is around 2-3 K, 
showing room for improvement. Temperature difference on the water loop side is maintained at around 3 K 
during full evaporator capacity, for efficient operation with minimized energy use of water pump as previously 
shown in Fig 3.23. As cooling output decreases, so does the temperature difference, meaning less heat is ab-
sorbed from the air. Water inlet temperature of the ACHP can approximately be estimated to be same as GHE 
outlet temperature, which is what SSHP is controlled by. Since inlet temperature does not exceed 20 °C, the 
water loop still has a significant thermal buffer before reaching the SSHP setpoint temperature of 26 °C during 
daytime.

6.  Discussion 
MMHP achieves a high overall system performance compared with conventional technologies, which as pre-
viously mentioned in the background section, are all somewhere in between a COP of 3 and 5, with 5 being a 
considered highly efficient system. It does this through a combination of a wide variety of system solutions 
such as the thermal exchange efficiency of the environmentally adaptable SSHP, active recovery and stabili-
zation of the ground thermal state, direct-expansion type heat pumps, low-temperature spatial heating system, 
decentralized solutions (heat pumps and water circulation pumps), as well as the overall integrated network of 
available heat sources and heat sinks. With active daily recovery of the ground temperature, thermal capacity 
has been increased and thermal depletion of the naturally stored seasonal thermal energy has been prevented. 
This system is especially beneficial in temperate climates with wider temperature ranges throughout the year, 
and for long-term investment, due to the high capital cost. Before installation it demands some examination of 
the building, such as spatial requirements (room for system and installation), environmental requirements (leg-
islative barriers) and it is necessary to investigate ground conditions (soil type, thermal properties etc.) in order 
to explore whether it is suitable for the system.   
This study focuses on a residential building, however the system could be implemented in other building types, 
such as office-type buildings as well. As described in section 2.2.2., a previous version of the system was 
implemented in an office building belonging to Kajima Technical Research Institute, with positive results. 
Thermal load curve for other building types is different, thus control schedule needs to be customized. Parallel 
operation has been the focus of this study, but serial operation should be investigated, since demand curve and 
solar availability would occur on similar hours of the day, in the case of office buildings.        
SSHP was not in operation during this cooling operation, but it is a significant part of the active components 
during cooling operation. It can affect the system performance, so it is necessary to notice, that the system 
COP, found does not represent the whole system. Further experiments are planned during warmer days where 
the cooling load is higher, thus increasing the temperature above the current setpoint of the SSHP, consequently 
activating it. This would prove the real potential of the system in warmer climates. An interesting aspect is to 
consider whether the SSHP or HWHP is better for releasing the heat from the system. At the moment HWHP 
runs with a fixed time schedule, but a more flexible operation, based on demand patterns and performance 
would be optimal. Since the SSHP only activates in very warm weather conditions, with high thermal loads, it 
might be interesting to explore whether thermal capacity of the HWHP and ground could be sufficient for 
cooling in other more moderate climates. ACHP has a high performance, even with low evaporation tempera-
tures, customized for dehumidification due to the humid weather of Japan. In warmer but less humid climates, 
where dehumidification is not necessary performance might be improved even further.  
Since individual components have previously shown high performance, an attempt to validate a system-level 
performance, through a full-scale experiment, has been carried out. It is important to distinguish between these 
two types of performances, since they can vary a lot, as shown by the obtained results. SSHP has once again 
shown that multiple renewable energy sources can be utilized very efficiently through a combined air-, solar- 
and sky-source direct-expansion type heat pump technology that takes advantage of its latent, convective and 
radiative heat exchange capabilities to interact dynamically and beneficially with the surroundings. FHHP 
showed that low-temperature heat sinks can also achieve a remarkable performance. ACHP and its direct-
==PAGE:72==

expansion type technology very efficiently absorbed and delivered waste-heat from the supply air to the central 
water loop, which is clear from the performance. However the system-level performance during both opera-
tional modes was exposed to significant level of performance degradation. The high heat sink temperature of 
the HWHP was largely responsible for degradation during cooling, while efficiency losses in the multiple heat 
exchangers as well as heat losses throughout the system plays a role during heating operation.         
The prototype testing is currently happening with a lot of assumptions, such as occupancy behavior and indoor 
climate has not yet been taken into account, even though these are key aspects for implementation in buildings. 
Experiments should be carried out with real-life situations, possibly including occupants in order to determine 
whether comfort requirements, such as indoor climate and personal regulation of control can be fulfilled. All 
these previously mentioned technical, economic and social concerns should be addressed before the technology 
can be ready for market implementation.         
The investigations carried out in this study were only short-term performance evaluation on a daily basis. In 
order to give a more qualified and accurate picture of the system performance, long-term performance evalu-
ation are necessary. Seasonal performance is crucial for heat pumps, and this will be the next step for the proto-
type experiments. Besides when determining optimal control strategy for the system, not only one day should 
be considered but several continuous days should be observed since the ground temperature is affected over 
several days and thus influencing heating performance on the next day. Control strategies of the system include 
fixed running time and fixed leaving temperature, but they are tested based on heuristics. It is not known, 
which combination will work out for the best on a specific day. This also adds to the uncertainty of the con-
clusive results.   
It should be kept in mind, that the ground is not a conventional thermal storage solution, but more of a stable 
and reliable heat sink and heat source that can be balanced through active recovery. This is why heat losses 
can occur, depending on the subsurface conditions and only limited heat can be stored during beneficial 
weather conditions. Its characteristic high thermal inertia, not only maintains heat well, but also takes a while 
to recover, especially due to the large surrounding mass of the ground. Preheating of the ground above the 
natural ground temperature could be a possible solution on multiple continuous days with limited thermal 
energy in the environment, however this would lead to heat losses. One of the essential tasks for optimal 
control is to determine, when to collect and reject the heat, as well as where to store it. Since several compo-
nents function as active heat sources and heat sinks in the system, it should be considered to where the heat 
should be distributed, in order to minimize electricity use and heat losses.

7.  Conclusion 
This study was primarily carried out to solve the performance degradation problem caused by temperature 
drop in the ground with conventional GSHP. The performance of a novel MMHP system was evaluated during 
heating and cooling operation, by using the average daily system COP as a performance indicator. 
Heating operation was tested with different control settings in order to determine the optimal control strategy. 
Control settings were changed with an underlying hypothesis of increased performance by shifting fixed run-
ning time control of the sky source heat pump to a time with more available solar radiation. This hypothesis 
was partly confirmed with results showing higher performance of the sky source heat pump, but similar per-
formance for the system with both control settings. The reason for this is a longer operational duration of the 
sky source heat pump, caused by increased thermal demand of the building during the investigated day, due to 
weather conditions affecting the thermal conditions of the ground. Following conclusive remarks can be made: 
1. There is a clear correlation between COP of the sky source heat pump and short-wave solar radiation 
resulting in higher performance with shifted time setting from morning (08:00) to noon (12:00). This 
is due to higher evaporation temperature, leading to less compressor work. Another aspect is the pre-
heating of the panel, during morning hours, which adds to the thermal collection. Finally solar-source 
collection mode is longer during the noon operation, due to higher panel temperatures and lower am-
bient air temperatures.       
2. The system COP is at the same level for both control strategies, due to longer system operation, caused 
by differences in weather conditions affecting the thermal demand. In case the operational duration 
was similar, performance of the system would have shown more beneficial, by activating the SSHP 
charging function at noon.     
3. The MMHP can effectively maintain the temperature balance in the desired temperature range. 
It should be noted that this benefit of shifting time setting is specifically suited for sunny days. SSHP perfor-
mance depends on a several climatic variables and these weather conditions vary widely. This should be further 
investigated with different weather conditions, and adjusted accordingly. For cloudy days, air temperature have 
a higher impact and thus optimal control conditions might be different.  
Cooling operation performance evaluation showed that: 
1) A daily air conditioning heat pump coefficient of performance was found ranging between 9 and 14 
with an average value of 11.2. Further performance improvements are expected and will be investi-
gated with similar weather conditions.  
2) The average daily system coefficient of performance including the domestic hot water heat pump was 
found to be 8.6. This performance degradation is caused by several factors including the high temper-
ature demand of the hot water tank, leading to a high energy use of the compressor.

8.  Future investigation and improvements 
8.1 Planned improvements 
Even though the presented system is showing promising short-term performance, further long-term investiga-
tions (e.g. seasonal performance) and improvements are necessary. The experimentation is still in its infancy, 
hence the system need to be explored further for verifying results and to gaining a deeper understanding of the 
system properties and the parameters influencing these. Building a reliant energy simulation model can be 
used for more time-efficient and broader investigations. This could be beneficial for further verification opti-
mization and development of the system. Many suggestions have been listed in project related literature [50] 
[51, 52, 53, 54], such as:  
 
Applying an on-demand/instantaneous hot-water supply that takes advantage of the heat storage ca-
pacity of the loop water. A prototype has been developed and tested and a COP of about 8 was ob-
tained. 
 
Reducing helical coils from 0.7 to 0.5 m 
 
More advanced control using deep learning such as MPC based on weather conditions, electricity price 
and projected thermal demand etc. 
 
Spraying water on the panels for increasing the cooling effect 
 
Redesigning the compressor lubrication mechanism, to avoid the required minimum pressure differ-
ence for the compressor to lubricate itself. This could further increase COP.   
 
Redesigning compressor for adapting to lighter loads, as building envelope designs are optimized, 
causing demand decrease.  
 
Integrating additional waste heat recovery, to the MMHP, through e.g. waste water from taps and AC 
condensation.   
 
Using an electric battery, which would reduce or eliminate the need for electricity from the grid, could 
create self-reliance.    
 
Preheating and reheating of the dehumidification process. A new air handler that takes this advantage 
is under development.  
 
Maximizing/Improving heat-exchange capacity of an evaporator and a condenser. 
Optimal control strategy could be determined faster and with a higher precision, by utilizing the data obtained 
from the measurements. Comprehensive data collection can be used for different optimization objectives such 
as: thermodynamic (balancing the underground), thermoeconomic (cost and energy savings) and multi-objec-
tive (combining the two former). Using system COP (maximizing heat output while minimizing energy use) 
for optimization instead of conventionally used parameter energy use could be beneficial.  
Identifying process efficiencies and losses in the system, is essential and necessary for fully understanding 
where inefficiencies occur. This can be used to further improve system design as well as control.  
Due to a high reliance on stochastic weather conditions, it is important to design a flexible, intelligent and self-
learning system that can adapt to a wide variety of scenarios. Collecting and appropriately using data for op-
eration with a focus on climate (e.g. energy), comfort (indoor climate), economy (e.g. electricity prices) and 
sustainable life cycle is one of the essential tasks.

8.2. Additional suggestions  
PV and collector efficiency drops with increasing panel temperature. In addition to that a large amount of 
enthalpy from the environment is lost outside of operation. Integrating PCM in the sky source panel, would 
solve these challenges, and consequently boost performance. It has already proven potential by improving 
electrical and thermal efficiency in several studies (A. Gaur 2017, Stropnik 2016). However the solution is not 
simple, and many aspects need to be considered beforehand and experimental test should be carried out.     
Availability of short-wave solar radiation has shown to be essential for SSHP performance. Since there is 
already a radiation sensor installed, this could be integrated into a more advanced and intelligent algorithm in 
the control strategy of the system, which turns on the SSHP when detection of a specific level of insolation is 
reached. In addition to that model predictive control could predict the expected weather conditions in order to 
preheat or precool the ground to compensate for the expected thermal depletion.   
The RE House is built with a very light construction using metals, with very limited thermal insulation (50-
100 mm). This affects the heating and cooling demand, since thermal mass is limited, and thermal demand will 
be fluctuating and highly dependent on outdoor weather conditions. Utilizing building thermal storage capa-
bilities through constructions with suitable thermal characteristics and insulating better might be beneficial for 
performance, since supply temperature (as well as temperature difference between heat source and heat sink) 
can be lowered. One possibility could be to integrate PCM in the building construction, or alternatively use 
heavy material.  
Evaporation (heat source) temperature for ACHP is quite low due to a necessity of dehumidification. A sepa-
rate technology for dehumidification could increase air supply temperature, and thus decrease temperature 
difference between heat source and sink, resulting in a higher performance.    
Scaling the system for peer-to-peer use would enhance flexibility and efficiency by minimizing use of TES, 
and preferably directly balancing supply and demand. Temperatures could be decreased by creating a more 
advanced type of control, dependent on user presence. 
The use of decentralized heat pumps makes for a flexible and more customized design and operation. However 
thermal energy needs to travel through several heat pumps, making it the most crucial component of exergy 
destruction. Thus, further technology improvements, especially focusing on performance degradation of the 
heat pump, is a significant aspect of optimizing the system.      
Control strategies currently include fixed running time and fixed leaving temperature but it might be interesting 
to investigate how different control strategies such as a fixed temperature difference between inlet and outlet 
of the GHE and how it would influence the performance.   
A comprehensive feasibility study of the system, from different dimensions, such as technical, economic and 
environmental would be necessary to evaluate the system on a more general level. Such a study would give an 
impression of the market potentials. Energy simulations could find the suitable building types and climates, 
for the system, and gain a deeper understanding through a sensitivity analysis.           
Temperatures of domestic hot water tanks, have long been increased to high levels in order to avoid legion-
naires and other bacteria’s. However recent developments of disinfection technologies such as Oxitherm Pro 
from Grundfos [63], make is possible to reduce temperatures and thus increase performance.

This study investigates the heating operation performance of a multiple-source multiple-use heat pump system, consisting 
of a sky source heat pump, a ground heat exchanger and floor heating heat pump in an experimental facility named RE 
(Renewable Energy) House. The novelty of this system is that the ground thermal state is maintained by the combination of 
complimentary renewable sources. It operates to recover the thermal state of the ground, which functions as a thermal 
reservoir, through heat collection and dissipation cycles. A shift in heat collection operation from morning (08:00) to noon 
(12:00) was expected to increase system performance by exploiting increased availability of solar radiation. Results show: 
1) Increased performance of the sky source heat pump, with new control due to beneficial weather conditions and lower 
energy use 2) No difference in performance for the whole system, due to longer operational duration of the system.  
 
1. Introduction 
RE (Renewable Energy) House is a newly constructed 
experimental building located in Kashiwa Campus of The 
University of Tokyo. It is seeking to maximize utilization of 
thermal energy in the surroundings in order to solve the challenge 
of fluctuating renewable energy availability, through use of 
various novel mechanical components and system design. The 
concept is based on a renewable ground loop (ReGL) system, 
which utilizes surrounding environment, such as the ground and 
sky as thermal reservoirs and transfers thermal energy through a 
multiple-source and multiple-use heat pump system (MMHP)1). 
The components in focus are the novel sky source heat pump 
(SSHP) along with the ground source heat exchanger (GHX) and 
floor heating heat pump (FHHP). The primary objective of the 
study is technology verification. Through a series of prototype 
experiments, this MMHP system is tested and evaluated in order 
to verify its performance. This specific investigation focuses on 
heating operation performance during winter for two different 
control settings through prototype experiments. SSHP, which is 
controlled partly by a fixed running time (FRT) is shifted from 
morning time (08:00) to noon (12:00) in order to exploit the 
increased availability of solar radiation. Fig. 1 shows the active 
heating operation components consisting of the SSHP and FHHP, 
which are interconnected through a ground water loop. The 
system is connected by decentralized water-source heat pumps 
with a high efficiency AC water pump attached. The GHX is 15 
m deep and is constructed as a high density and compact helical-
coil, made of high-polyethylene corrugated tubes. The SSHP 
consists of a heat pump unit and a sky source panel (SSP) 
connected through a refrigeration loop using R32 as a refrigerant. 
The panel is 8 m2 with 30° tilt and consists of aluminum-extruded 
finned tubes, placed in parallel and integrated PV cells. SSHP has 
a dual functionality by evaporating the refrigerant in the SSP for 
heat collection, while working as a condenser during heat 
dissipation. Thermal energy is collected by the top surface of the 
SSP mainly from surrounding radiation, while fins on the back 
absorbs it from the ambient air, ensuring efficient operation even 
in cloudy days. Heat is dissipated through nocturnal radiation. 
Space heating is supplied through a floor heating distributor 
using polyethylene (PE) tubes embedded in the concrete floor.

2. Control 
Because of the limited thermal capacity of the ground, it 
cannot be considered as an infinite thermal reservoir. To utilize 
the geothermal energy at a sustainable level, the subsurface 
thermal state should be recovered to a certain level. In this system, 
this is achieved by utilizing the dual functionality of the SSHP 
for restoring the ground temperature to the undisturbed natural 
temperature of 17 °C. The fundamental control strategy of the 
ReGL system consists of two operational modes with the 
purpose of achieving a thermal balance in the system.  
 
 
Charging mode - Heat is transferred to the ground 
through collection from the SSHP   
 
Discharging mode - Heat is extracted from the ground 
and dissipated through the SSHP  
 
In this study, charging mode is the only operation in function, 
since the ground is heated up to supply the heat demand.     
The control strategy of the system consists of a flexible 
combination of fixed running time (FRT) and circulating water 
temperatures. SSHP runs on a fixed leaving temperature (FLT) 
control, which is the return temperature of the GHX, while 
FHHP is controlled by the supply temperature on the floor loop 
side with a desired temperature of 27 °C and an operational 
temperature range (deadband) of 5 °C, meaning a lower set point 
of 22 °C for maintaining a floor surface temperature of 23 °C. 
This supply temperature set point is manually adjusted according 
to weather conditions. For the SSHP control of daytime, the 
deadband temperature ranges from 15 °C to 17 °C both of the 
investigated days. During nighttime, water of the GHX is heated 
to a minimum of 8 °C, due to increased efficiency with higher 
temperatures. SSHP is turned off at 12 °C the 4th March, while 
this was decreased to 10 °C the 13th March (Fig. 2). Both SSHP 
and FHHP are set to run on specified time frames, but 
unsatisfactory temperatures can overrule time control.

3. Method  
3.1 Measurement equipment 
Several data loggers measure a comprehensive amount of 
data. The obtained data can be divided into two categories, 
system-related and environmental data.      
 
 
System-related data - Includes water loop temperature, 
flowrate, electricity use etc. 
 
Environmental data - Includes outdoor air temperature, 
solar radiation, humidity, velocity etc.      
 
All data loggers measure with a sampling interval of 5 s, however 
results are displayed for every 1 min. Furthermore, in order to 
sample average temperature of the circulating water and avoid 
incorrect measurements due to stagnation when SSHP unit is off, 
the circulation pump of the SSHP operates in 30-min cycles with 
a 27-min interval and stops after 3 min. 
 
From the collected data, we can calculate properties of the 
system such as heat output and system COP, which can be used 
to evaluate the performance of the system.   
 
3.2 Experimental method 
The experimental method for testing this system is known 
as a black box experiment. Since the technology is a prototype, 
the properties of the system are unknown, and must be found 
through experimental tests, in which input is changed, preferably 
one input variable at a time. The approach in determining which 
variables to adjust and how in the experimental tests is trial and 
error based on heuristics. In this experiment, two variables are 
different, however one is not of significant influence.  
 
Two different days with similar weather conditions were chosen 
in order to compare the system performance by a change of FRT.   
 
 
4th of March 2018 – SSHP starts operation at 08:00 
 
13th of March 2018 – SSHP starts operation at 12:00 
 
3.3 System COP  
The system performance during heating operation is 
evaluated through the system COP. This property is determined 
from amount of usable energy in the system, which in this case 
is the heat output from FHHP, and the total energy use from the 
system, including water circulation pumps and controls.

4. Results and Discussion 
 
The obtained results illustrate the measurement data of 
electricity use and heat output from the SSHP and FHHP for the 
two investigated days along with a comparison of calculated 
performance indicators including SSHP COP, the system COP 
and average daily system COP.  
 
Fig. 3 shows SSHP COP, outdoor temperature and short-wave 
solar radiation perpendicular to the panel of March 4th and March 
13th. Solar irradiance is almost identical for the two days, which 
make the change of conditions comparable. It is clear to see that 
the SSHP COP increases with more solar irradiance. There is a 
steep increase of the SSHP COP on March 4th from 16 to 33 
corresponding to 106% during the 3-hour operation. SSHP is 
running more efficiently 13th of March due to increasing 
availability of atmospheric energy. COP increases from 0 to 40 
in 20 min and maintains this high performance for over 1 h. Since 
SSHP collects thermal energy from both surrounding radiation 
as well as ambient air, the performance is affected by the outdoor 
temperature as well. On 4th of March outdoor temperature during 
operation is between 5 °C and 15 °C, while it is between 10 °C 
and 15 °C on 13th of March. This leads to a SSHP COP of 15 
during 600 W/m2 on the 4th of March and 25 on the 13th of March.

The daily heat production and electricity use of the SSHP along 
with GHX return temperature is illustrated in Fig. 4. Electricity 
use is lower on 13th of March due to efficient heating of the

utilization of available solar energy. Heat output is at the same 
level on both days, while duration of the operating time is longer 
on the same day, resulting in a higher total energy use. GHX 
return temperature starts at the same temperature for both days. 
Heating of the ground loop water is slower on the 13th, which 
leads to longer operating time for the SSHP. The temperature

5. Conclusion 
 
This study was carried out to evaluate the heating operation 
performance of a novel multiple-source multiple-use heat pump 
system with different control settings in order to determine the 
optimal control strategy by using the average daily system COP 
as a performance indicator. Control settings were changed with 
an underlying hypothesis of increased performance by shifting 
fixed running time control of the sky source heat pump to a time 
with more available solar radiation. This hypothesis was 
disproved with results showing higher performance of the sky 
source heat pump, but similar performance for the system with 
both control settings. The reason for this is a longer operational 
duration of the sky source heat pump, due to more thermal 
extraction from the ground, leading to slower heating of ground 
loop water. Following conclusive remarks can be made: 
 
1. 
There is a clear correlation between COP of the sky 
source heat pump and short-wave solar radiation 
resulting in higher performance with shifted time 
setting from morning (08:00) to noon (12:00).   
2. 
The system COP is at the same level for both control 
strategies, due to longer system operation.   
 
Further experimental and analytical investigations are needed 
and planned for verifying performance and gaining a deeper 
understanding the system properties and influencing parameters.
