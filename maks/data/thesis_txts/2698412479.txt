Abstract
People spend most of their time with buildings, however, volatile organic compounds keep
releasing from furniture and building components in some situations. Air purifying devices
are highly demanded in air­tight buildings. To investigate the potential of applying metal­
organic frameworks(MOFs) as novel adsorbents in controlling indoor air quality, this study
was implemented. One kind of typical VOC, formaldehyde, was used as the indoor pol­
lutant source in this research. Three kinds of MOF materials, MIL­100(Fe), MIL­160(Al),
and MIL­53(Al), were tested. Besides, one of the commonly used traditional materials,
Activated carbon, was also tested for comparison.
A preliminary static formaldehyde adsorption experiment and a further dynamic formalde­
hyde adsorption experiment were carried out to explore the adsorption performance of the
aforementioned four materials. In the dynamic adsorption experiment, the clean air de­
livery rate (CADR) was calculated. Additionally, a desorption experiment was conducted
after the adsorption experiments to investigate the desorption characteristics.
In the experiments, the internet of things was realized by a designed formaldehyde testing
device. Besides, in the dynamic adsorption, a small packing barrel was designed to serve
as part of the air purifying device.

Introduction
Chapter 1 briefly introduces the aim of this study and gives an overview of the thesis.
1.1
The goal of this study
This study is performed to investigate the potential of applying metal­organic frameworks
(MOFs) as novel adsorbents to purifying indoor air in residential buildings. One kind of
typical VOC, formaldehyde, was used as the indoor pollutant source in this research.
Three kinds of MOF materials, MIL­100(Fe), MIL­160(Al), and MIL­53(Al), were tested
in the experimental study, and one of the commonly used traditional materials, Activated
carbon, was also tested for comparison. A picture of these four adsorbents is shown in
Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: 4 adsorbents tested in this study. From left to right: MIL­100(Fe), MIL­160(Al),
MIL­53(Al), and Activated carbon.
1.2
Structure of the thesis
Figure 1.2 shows the structure of the thesis. Chapter 1 is an overview of the thesis;
Chapter 2 gives the background of the topic by literature review; Chapter 3 to Chapter
8 are the main part of the thesis, details of the experimental study are elaborated in this
part; Chapter 9 is the conclusion of the study and gives a brief prospect of the investigated
topic.
Chapter 3 presents the material characteristics of these four adsorbents via the testing
results from a gas sorption system. The nitrogen adsorption­desorption curve, pore size
distribution, pore volume, and BET surface area of each material are included. Appen­
dices I, J, K, and L are the reports from the associated operational software system of the
Quantachrome Instruments. Pore volume distribution reports are included in the uploaded
zip file.
Chapter 4 describes the formaldehyde testing device and the experimental setups of the
following experiments. On­site data transfer and online data transfer are integrated on the
device. Appendix B is the circuit schematic of the designed formaldehyde testing device,
Appendices C, D, E, and F are the coding part of the device. and Appendix G is the setting
procedure on different platforms to achieve the goal of the internet of things.

2
Literature review
Ofﬁce worker discomfort and medical symptoms related to buildings and pollutant ex­
posures, work organization, and personal risk factors are described as Sick Building
Syndrome (SBS). According to the response from the National Institute for Occupational
Safety and Health, the primary cause of occupant discomfort in buildings was attributed to
the ventilation systems (50%), followed by strong indoor pollution sources (tobacco, 3%;
others, 14%), pollutants entrained from the outside (15%), microbiological contamination
(3–5%), and the remainder unknown. The professional ventilation standard (ASHRAE
62­89) suggests two approaches to ventilation: a ventilation rate procedure and an air
quality procedure. (Hodgson 2005) The former one provides a minimum ventilation rate
per occupant, which is straightforward to implement. While the latter ventilating approach
is based on a certain exposure limit value of harmful substances in the air, it’s more ac­
curate. However, current relative metrics are not uniform yet.
2.1
Volatile organic compounds (VOCs)
IEA EBC Annex 68 SUBTASK 1 defines the metrics to evaluate the indoor air quality of
low­energy residential buildings. Indoor air is a complex mixture with typically more than
200­300 pollutants. These pollutants can be classified as gaseous compounds (inorganic
gases and volatile organic compounds), non­viable particles (PM10 and PM2.5), and bio­
contaminants (fungus and bacteria) (Abadie and Wargocki 2017). Volatile organic com­
pounds (VOCs) are gaseous pollutants with relatively small molecular weight. These
chemical pollutants are numerous, varied, and ubiquitous. While most VOCs are not
toxic, health problems, such as acute headache, allergy, respiratory problems, nausea,
and immunity in children/infants, may arise upon long­term exposure to some of these
VOCs even at low concentrations. Other VOCs such as formaldehyde, acrylonitrile, ben­
zene, toluene, ethylbenzene, and xylenes are highly toxic and/or carcinogenic (Sarkar
and Mulchandani 2015).
Thus, after integrating the acute short­term (<24 hours) exposure limit value, chronic
long­term (>1 week) exposure limit value and the probability of occurrence in the building
(Abadie and Wargocki 2017) into account, present work mainly focuses on six common
VOCs, namely formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, benzene, ethylbenzene, acrolein and 1,4­
dichlorobenzene.
VOCs abatement techniques can be divided into two categories, i.e., destructive (thermal,
catalytic, and photocatalytic oxidation, bio­filtration) and recuperative (adsorption, absorp­

2.2
Metal­organic frameworks (MOFs)
Metal Organic Frameworks (MOFs), consisting of metal or metal oxide vertices con­
nected by rigid or semi­rigid organic linkers, are served as excellent materials in gas stor­
age and gas separation applications. The most crucial advantage of MOFs over other
traditional nanoporous materials is the ability to be tailored their physical and chemical
characteristics during synthesis (Uzun and Keskin 2014).
2.2.1
State of the art
Acrolein
Bromberg, Su, and Hatton 2013 investigated the NH2MIL101(Al) and NH2MIL53(Al) ad­
sorption of three aldols, acetaldehyde, acrolein, and butyraldehyde. The uptake capacity
of Acrolein and butyraldehyde were less pronounced than that of acetaldehyde. By means
of polymerization and formation of solid cross­linked structures, higher­molecular­weight
compounds evidently blocked access of the incoming acrolein vapor molecules, resulted
in low adsorption capacity eventually at ambient temperature. However, it still exceeded
several­fold saturation adsorption capacity of heat­activated synthetic zeolite.
Formaldehyde
L. Wang et al. 2018 compared the formaldehyde adsorption capacity of γ­CD­MOF­K,
UiO­66­OH, and ZIF­8. γ­CD­MOF­K was explored as an effective formaldehyde adsor­
bent because of its high selectivity, speed, and capacity. Different adsorption duration
curves were developed, and the result showed that the performance degraded as tem­
perature increased. The Langmuir model was applied to simulate the exothermic and
spontaneous process and fitted the experimental data well. Whereas further adsorption
capacity values of the materials still remained to explore. Gu, G. Wang, and Yan 2010 in­
vestigated the MOF­5 adsorption capacity under different adsorbate inflow rate, adsorbent
volume and relative humidity, and the variation of recovery rate on different storage time.
In general, the uptake formaldehyde quantity decreased as the inflow rates increased.
Nonetheless, adsorbing capacity increased as the amount of MOF­5 increased and sta­
bilized at 200 mg. It’s noticed that adsorption capacity diminished significantly as relative
humidity increased over 45%. The recovery rate held 95% after 24­hour storage and 90%
after 72 hours at 20 degrees Celcius.
Liu et al. 2020 found that the adsorption rate of formaldehyde by the ZIF­67@PS­2 and
ZIF­67@PS­3 nanofiber membrane is significantly higher than other nanofiber membrane.
However, the main research was focused on the chemical synthesis process. There is
neither sufficient investigation into the characteristics and applications of the materials
nor information about the adsorption capacity. Chen, Yang, and Zhang 2014 worked on
the ZIF­67­based detection of formaldehyde vapor and mainly focused on the selectivity
on different adsorbates and the resistance of the ZIF­67 sensor to different formalde­
hyde concentrations. Consistent with the aforementioned conclusion, the selectivity de­
creased dramatically as relative humidity increased above 70%. Nandi et al. 2018 mainly
researched the intensity of the formaldehyde detection and collected detection results of
various MOFs applied to formaldehyde sensors.
Acetaldehyde
Chevalier et al. 2019 conducted their research on low­concentration VOCs adsorption
by HKUST­1. The adsorption performance of synthesized HKUST­1 was measured in
dynamic mode for mono­pollutant (toluene, o­xylene, ethanol, acetone, and acetalde­

relative humidity conditions, which resulted from the different molecular diffusion rates
in nanopores and demonstrated the high selectivity of toluene in a low relative humidity
environment. The regeneration ability was verified through cold desorption tests, and the
adsorption capacity seemed to stabilize at the third cycle. Whereas HKUST­1 merely pre­
sented the adsorption capacity of 1.4%wt for acetaldehyde. Vikrant et al. 2020 explored

Benzene
Gwardiak et al. 2019 explored benzene adsorption by a series of MOFs (MOF­520, MOF­

that the supermicropores, i.e., pores between 0.7 and 2.0 nm of the MOFs, were mainly
responsible for the effective benzene capture. Zhao et al. 2015 investigated the adsorp­
tion capacity of benzene on HKUST­1 and other traditional porous materials (activated
carbon, resin, zeolite, and silica). Benzene adsorption experiments were implemented
under different partial pressure up to atmospheric pressure, and the ideal adsorbed so­
lution theory (IAST) was proposed to predict the adsorption selectivity of benzene and
water vapor. The simulation results showed that benzene possesses a high adsorption
rate on the HKUST­1.
Belarbi et al. 2019 compared the benzene adsorption performance of MIL­101(Cr) and
HKUST­1. Because benzene only generated van der Waals interaction, the topology and
texture differences between these two MOFs are crucial. Therefore, with more favorable
pore size, MIL­101(Cr) showcased more significant adsorbing amounts of benzene. W. .­.

Ethylbenzene
Duan et al. 2013 researched the adsorption and diffusion properties of 3 xylene isomers
and ethylbenzene in MIL­53(Al). The breathing effect observed in MIL­53(Al) frameworks
resulted in the deviation from the normal Langmuir model of the isotherms at a higher
temperature. For 373 K, ethylbenzene showed two plateaus before 0.5 mbar and after
the equilibrium, which indicated that almost no adsorption appeared during these pres­
sure ranges. Amongst all the adsorbates, ethylbenzene possesses the smallest capacity.
Jangodaz et al. 2018 evaluated the ethylbenzene adsorption capacity on MIL­101(Cr),
MIL­53(Fe), and activated carbon at room temperature and atmospheric pressure. The
required saturation time and the effect of initial ethylbenzene concentration were investi­
gated.

2.2.3
Summary
From the perspective of acting force, the adsorption can be classified as chemical ad­
sorption and physical adsorption, driven by the forming of covalent bonds between co­
ordinating unsaturated sites (CUS) and adsorbates and van der Waals interaction, re­
spectively. At the molecular level, it is crucial for the adsorption performance that the
Metal­organic frameworks as novel adsorbents for volatile organic compounds in built environment
9
==PAGE:22==
structure, polarity, and relative molecular mass of volatile organic compounds (VOCs),
and the metal/metal oxide and pore size of metal­organic frameworks (MOFs). In addi­
tion, the breathing effect significantly influences the adsorption process, which appears
on flexible MOFs under gate­opening pressure.
A series of exogenetic factors contribute to the capability of MOFs materials’ adsorption.
Water vapor constitutes an essential part of the atmosphere. Furthermore, its concentra­
tion is usually thousands of times than VOCs in a residential environment. Consequently,
the relative humidity of inlet flow diminishes the VOCs uptake dramatically by stronger
interaction between water molecules and MOFs when reaching a certain value. Similarly,
there exists competence between different VOCs in case of multi­component adsorption.
Besides, the inlet concentration of VOCs impacts notably the final amount of adsorbed
pollutants as well. To exclude this effect, the partition coefficient value was proposed.
The 10% breakthrough performance (BTV10) was recommended to fit the real situation
instead of the 100% breakthrough concentration detected at the outlet. Furthermore, it is
interesting to notice that the flow rate of inlet stream also has a considerable influence on
the adsorption capacity.
To apply the MOFs to the indoor environment, the operational temperature and pressure
conditions should vary within reasonable ranges, which are merely employed in the cur­
rent study. In addition, the effect of potentially interfering species in the air, e.g., carbon
dioxide, should not be neglected when considering further applications.

a stable status.

to the evaporating dish. Subsequently, the mixing fan was turned on by the 5 V voltage
supplied by the power supply. The voltage drop at the mixing fan was checked by a
voltage meter to guarantee the constant end voltage. Stopped the fan and opened the
suction pump when the formaldehyde concentration inside the desiccator was stabilized.

9
Conclusion
As the microporous metal­organic frameworks materials, MIL­100(Fe) shows outstand­
ing formaldehyde adsorption performance among four investigated materials. In a typical
residential built environment, the air purifying device filled with MIL­100(Fe) powder dis­
plays higher formaldehyde adsorption capacity and quicker response speed to indoor air
pollutants.
The adsorbed formaldehyde on MIL­100(Fe) powder is less sensitive to a higher temper­
ature than Activated carbon. Thus, it is safer to apply this metal­organic framework to air
purifying devices in terms of auto­released formaldehyde from the adsorbents caused by
a local heat source.
Although the testing results of MIL­100(Fe) reveals the potential of applying metal­organic
frameworks for indoor air quality control, there are still some non­negligible weaknesses
of these novel adsorbents. The adsorption performance of MIL­100(Fe) is significantly
affected by the moisture when the indoor relative humidity is high.
More novel metal­organic frameworks with specific structures and metals/metals oxide
need to be synthesized for higher sensitivity to specific volatile organic compounds’ ad­
sorption and better storage performance after the adsorption in the built environment.
The structure of air purifying devices with metal­organic frameworks remains further in­
vestigated to have a higher clean air delivery rate.

Preliminary experimental research of metal-organic

Shan Chen, Kan Zu, Lei Fang and Menghao Qin 
Department of Civil Engineering, Technical University of Denmark, Lyngby, Denmark 
Corresponding email: menqin@byg.dtu.dk   
 
Abstract. Formaldehyde is a common emission from furniture and indoor decorations. Although 
the concentration of formaldehyde gas is not too high in the indoor environment, it is highly 
toxic and carcinogenic. The formaldehyde removal potential of a novel type of green and safe 
nano-porous materials, Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs), with a high surface-to-volume 
ratio, strong adsorption capacity, and low regeneration temperature was investigated. To date, 
researchers are mainly focusing on formaldehyde selectivity and detection using MOFs in low 
moisture circumstances. This study carried out a series of experiments to compare breakthrough 
curves of formaldehyde dynamic adsorption on MIL-100(Fe), MIL-160(Al), and aluminum 
fumarate with activated carbon. In experiments, the formaldehyde was evaporated from diluted 
formalin solution, dried to 30±5 % RH, and driven through different adsorbents by nitrogen. The 
results indicated that MOFs showed great potential for indoor air formaldehyde removal.  
1. Introduction 
People spent roughly 90% of their time indoors in the 1960s [1] and tended to spend more time inside 
buildings with the process of urbanization. Especially during the COVID-19 pandemic, half of the 
world’s population is under lockdown and works remotely [2]. Hence, the indoor environment quality 
plays a non-negligible role in people’s modern life. Sick-building syndrome (SBS) occurs more 
frequently after long-term exposure to airtight and energy-efficient buildings[3]. More strict standards 
on airtightness performance are implemented worldwide [4] to accomplish the energy-saving buildings, 
leading to less air infiltration in buildings. To compensate for this amount of reduced natural ventilation, 
mechanical ventilation is needed to supply sufficient fresh air at the inevitable cost of energy 
consumption. Therefore, when mechanical ventilation is not used in buildings, the air changes per hour 
(ACH) declines, resulting in the accumulation of indoor pollutants. Many indoor air contaminants could 
be significant causes of SBS, comprised of volatile organic compounds (VOCs), dust/fibers, bioaerosols, 
entrapped outdoor sources, and contaminants generated by human activity[3]. 
As one of the common indoor VOCs, formaldehyde is released continually from the products 
processed with formaldehyde-based resins, such as furniture, artificial boards, carpets, clothing, and 
even the wall with paintings. Furthermore, formaldehyde is also an ingredient of numerous consumable 
household products, such as cosmetics, dishwashing liquids, carpet cleaners, shoe-care agents, fabric 
softeners, glues and adhesives, lacquers, antiseptics, and even medicines. Moreover, formaldehyde is 
classified as a human carcinogen. For example, short-term exposure to formaldehyde can irritate various 
body parts, and sustained exposure can possibly lead to nasopharyngeal cancer, leukemia, and asthma.[5] 
Three approaches to control indoor formaldehyde concentration are pollutant source control, ventilation, 
and introducing pollutant sink. Due to the ubiquitous existence of formaldehyde in the air, ventilation is 
the most used manner to lower the formaldehyde concentration. However, when sufficient ventilation

cannot be guaranteed within the residence, introducing a pollutant sink is a meaningful way to remove

Activated carbon, clay, alumina, and zeolite are widely applied nano-porous materials to control the 
indoor formaldehyde concentration. However, their regeneration temperature usually is very high 
(>100 ℃). In this situation, a new type of porous material, Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs), comes 
into view. The MOFs are constructed by forming strong-chemical bonds to bridge inorganic metal-
related units with organic ligands, thus, creating open frameworks with ordered structures [6]. Due to 
the tunable structure and functional groups, many MOFs have a high surface-to-volume ratio, strong 
adsorption capacity, and low regeneration temperature. These characteristics give competitive 
advantages to MOFs over mentioned traditional materials. Furthermore, some applications of MOFs 
desiccants, like MOF-based precise humidity control material (MOF-PHCM), have been investigated to 
control indoor moisture[6]. Some MOFs’ VOCs adsorption performance has been tested, such as 
cyclohexane adsorption by HSKUST-1 and zeolite ZSM-5 (ZSM = Zeolite Socony Mobil), benzene 
adsorption by HSKUST-1 and ZSM-5/HKUST-1 foam material, and toluene adsorption by HSKUST-1 
and MIL-101. However, the concentration of tested toxic gases [7] is much higher than general 
residential indoor pollutants. Herein, some MOF materials were used to investigate the formaldehyde 
removal potential via physical adsorption. 
2. Material and methods 
The 4% formaldehyde aqueous solution, buffered, was diluted to 1/50 and then used as a formaldehyde 
source. Some absorbents such as MIL-100(Fe), MIL-160(Al), aluminum fumarate, and activated carbon 
were employed as formaldehyde sink in the experiments. The experiments were implemented at room 
temperature and atmospheric pressure. The Multi-gas Monitor Type 1302 with optical filter UA 0987 
(toluene as the reference gas) was employed to monitor the total volatile organic compounds (TVOCs) 
concentration. The instrument was calibrated by nitrogen gas to set the zero point. The interference from 
water vapor is compensated by the automatic compensation function and the monitored pure water vapor 
TVOCs concentration . As shown in Figure 1, nitrogen was used as the carrier gas to evaporate  the 
formaldehyde  gas from the solution . A regulator  controlled  the outlet nitrogen  gas at 1 bar, and the 
nitrogen flow was adjusted by the nitrogen flow meter at 1.2±0.2 L/min. The formaldehyde outflow of

4. Conclusions 
In this preliminary experimental research, MOFs showed great potential for formaldehyde removal in 
indoor pollutants levels. Under the experimental conditions, 30±5 % RH and room temperature, the 
competitive adsorption of formaldehyde and moisture was observed on some MOFs. Formaldehyde 
adsorption stability and formaldehyde adsorption isotherm of three MOFs used in experiments is 
remained to be investigated further. To exclude the influence of moisture on indoor formaldehyde 
removal, novel MOFs materials that have a high affinity for formaldehyde while less interaction with 
water vapor is under development.

Metal­organic frameworks as novel adsorbents for volatile organic compounds in built environment
129
==PAGE:142==
This study is performed to investigate the potential of applying metal­organic frameworks (MOFs) as
novel adsorbents to purifying indoor air in residential buildings. One kind of typical VOC, formalde­
hyde, was used as the indoor pollutant source in this research. Three kinds of MOF materials,
MIL­100(Fe), MIL­160(Al), and MIL­53(Al), were tested in the experimental study, and one of the
commonly used traditional materials, Activated carbon, was also tested for comparison.
