Abstract
Electromagnetic scattering problems are central in engineering applications
in various disciplines, from antenna analysis to biological imaging. One ap-
proach to such problems involves solving integral equations on a surface bound-
ary. This strategy generally requires discretizing integral operators known as
layer potentials. The integrand of layer potential is singular but integrable,
meaning special care should be taken when integrating them numerically. In
this thesis, we investigate recent developments in evaluating these integrals.
Specifically, we consider a method that utilizes generalized Gaussian quadra-
tures, i.e., quadratures that integrate collections of functions of a single variable
over an interval. Expanding the integrable kernel reduces the problem of eval-
uating a one-dimensional integral of functions on a particular form, which can
then be integrated using a precomputed generalized Gaussian quadrature. We
study the theoretical justification of the method and investigate how we can
generate generalized Gaussian quadratures in practice. Finally, we determine
the usefulness and performance of the method through numerical examples,
comparing it to already established methods.

Introduction
Electromagnetic scattering problems are central in engineering applications where an
accurate understanding of the interactions between electromagnetic radiation and an
object is necessary. An example of such an application is designing a reflector an-
tenna, where a solution to a scattering problem can be used to predict an antenna’s
radiation pattern and polarization. This ultimately determines whether the design
solves the problem it is supposed to solve. Electromagnetic scattering problems oc-
cur when modelling interactions of time-harmonic waves with Maxwell’s equations
and are approached by solving the Helmholtz equation with specific boundary condi-
tions. This problem is well studied in the literature, and classical methods exist that
reduce the problem to a problem of solving integral equations on the object’s bound-
ary [17, 8]. These equations consist of integral operators, known as layer potential,
whose integrand is weakly singular, i.e., singular but integrable. Special care should,
therefore, be taken when integrating them numerically.
Recent developments suggest that generalized Gaussian quadratures (GGQ) can be
used to discretize single-layer potentials [2, 3]. GGQs are efficient quadratures that
integrate functions in one dimension that are in the subspace spanned by a collection
of functions. The algorithm in [5] makes it possible to determine such a quadrature
for large collections of functions. An expansion of the integrable kernel by [21] allows
the authors of [2] to use precomputed generalized Gaussian quadratures of a specific
form in order to evaluate the weakly singular integrals of the layer potentials.
This work explores this idea further. We investigate the underlying theory of GGQs
and their application when evaluating weakly singular integrals. We consider aspects
related to implementation and investigate the method’s strengths and weaknesses.
Finally, through numerical experiments, we test the method’s performance and com-
pare it to alternative methods.
Section 2 introduces computational electromagnetics. The subject is vast, but this
serves as a motivation for our interest in layer potentials and boundary integral
equations. In Section 3, we introduce the reader to Gauss-Legendre quadratures and
define a generalized Gaussian quadrature. These are the main focus in the second
half of the section, where we present a method for generating GGQs for an almost
arbitrary set of input functions. In Section 4, we show how these can be used to
evaluate the weakly singular integrals studied earlier.
In Section 5, we illustrate
the methods in practice through a series of numerical experiments and comment on
the results. In Section 6, we discuss the methods in general and then end with a
conclusion and outlook in Section 7.
This work contains the following novel contributions
• We have supplied a unified and coherent introduction to the method for gener-
ating GGQs and their application in evaluating weakly singular integrals.
• We provide a small extension of the error analysis related to generating GGQs.

• We extend the method for evaluating weakly singular integrals to work for
surface panels parameterized by quadrilaterals and illustrate how they can be

• We have implemented the method for generating GGQs and the method used

are not evaluating Φ in the singularity. For the Duffy method, we split up the inner
integral into four triangles around the singularity and then use the method described
in Section 5.3 to evaluate the integral on each triangle. For GGQ, we also split up
the triangles, but instead use the method presented in Section 4. We test the GGQ

6
Discussion
The numerical example in Section 5.1 illustrates that the method for generating
generalized Gaussian quadratures (GGQ) presented in Section 3.3 works in practice.
The test showed that we can successfully build GGQs and obtain satisfactory results
when integrating functions within the family. There is still much work to do related to
a method for constructing GGQs. In this thesis, we have given error analysis results
on individual steps, but a unified perspective is still missing. An essential step for
this method to mature would be to provide a rigorous error analysis such that one
can give bounds when integrating functions from within the considered subspace V .
This would amount to predicting the errors seen in Table 1. With a rigorous error
estimate for integrating functions in V , one can start to consider functions slightly
perturbed from V . Here, like classical Gauss-Legendre quadratures, one would hope
that the GGQs remain stable. In working with this thesis, we have experimented
with whether the final point-reduction step in the optimization procedure improves
the stability of perturbations. However, so far, the results in this direction have been
inconclusive.
In Section 5.2 we presented timings for the generation of the GGQs used to evaluate
weakly singular integrals. The timings and accuracy obtained should be considered a
baseline. The method for generating GGQs is a framework where the individual steps
can be adapted. One could consider different representations in the discretization
step, different rank-revealing factorizations in the compression step, and different op-
timization algorithms in the point-reduction step. Altering any of these components
would change computation time and accuracy, and there is considerable potential in
tweaking the method for a given application. A significant improvement would be
to switch to precomputing the quadratures in extended or even quadruple precision.
In this thesis, we used the parameter εdiscretization = 1e-7, and it is impossible to go
much lower with double precision. This limitation is due to performing checks such
as ξj ≤1e-7 without hitting machine precision. Switching to quadruple precision
would allow computing discretization for εdiscretization = 1e-16, i.e., within machine
precision for double precision. Precomputing quadratures for higher precision with-
out specialized hardware would significantly extend the run time. However, this is
not a major disadvantage, as the quadratures can be tabulated and only have to be
computed once.
The example with the singular radial integral over a triangle in Section 5.3 illustrates
that utilizing GGQs for evaluating weakly singular integrals on triangles also works
in practice. Although simple, this test is useful as it allows for comparison with an
50
==PAGE:56==
6
Discussion
analytical solution. Compared to the Duffy transform, the GGQ method appears very
stable, even over degenerate triangles. To obtain a more complete picture, the next
step would be to compare against more advanced methods such as Sauter-Schwab
[19]. The results from the triangle tests also suggest how the choice of dividing the
intervals of r0 and θ0 influences performance.
The idea of using Gauss-Legendre
nodes comes from [2]. However, the choice is not motivated by the authors. The
endpoints were chosen arbitrarily. It should, therefore, be possible to improve on this
aspect by choosing more deliberately. A natural way to do so would be to consider
what triangles show up frequently in an application and focus the computational
effort around those. It might also be possible to interpolate between quadratures by
building a surrogate model. However, this project has not investigated the feasibility
of this idea.
The application to MoM in Section 5.4 and collocation in Section 5.5 illustrate the
method’s performance in a more realistic setting. The results of the MoM test are
positive, even showing that GGQ outperforms TICRA’s method when comparing
the number of quadrature nodes used. Furthermore, it is convincing that the method
is steadily decreasing, unlike TICRA’s method.
However, the methodology here
is somewhat flawed, as we do not have access to an analytic solution. A way to
circumvent this would be to build a complete MoM solver and then solve a PDE
with a known solution, as we did in Section 5.5. Although building a complete MoM
solver was outside the scope of this work, I believe this would be a natural next step.
Until then, the MoM example seen here is a convincing proof of concept that suggests
this line of thinking is worth investigating further. The collocation test presented an
attempt to use the method to solve an actual partial differential equation with a
known solution. Unfortunately, the limitation of representing the solution on a single
panel appears too limited to obtain convincing results.
There are some natural theoretical considerations one can consider when evaluating
weakly singular integrals on surface panels. In their article [2], the authors suggest
considering non-linear adaptions of ρ that would give rise to the same kernel expansion
while keeping the integration domain R. In the follow-up article [3], they also consider
an extension of the method that allows integrating kernels with stronger singularities.
An example of this is the operator NΓ appearing when solving the exterior Neumann
problem with a double layer potential as mentioned in Remark 2.4. This would extend
the range of applications for the method significantly.
Another approach to using GGQs to integrate singular integrals on surface panels
is to use the method for generating GGQs for R ∈R2 directly.
My experience
is that it is possible to get steps 1, 2, and 3 of the method for generating GGQs
quadratures working directly on R. Although the final step is challenging, we are
still left with a ˇCebyˇs¨ev quadrature, which could potentially be used to integrate
directly on the surface panel. This means we would not have to perform the splitting
and transforming seen in Section 4.2. However, even if this is possible, which functions
should be used to precompute the GGQ is unclear. This is the problem the expansion
in Section 4.1 solves for the method presented in this thesis.

7
Conclusion and outlook
Finally, the method for generating GGQs should be considered a method on its own.
The application in evaluating weakly singular integrals on surface panels is interesting,
but I think there is also much potential in finding applications for the GGQ method
outside discretizing BIEs. To my knowledge, research in this direction is sparse.
7
Conclusion and outlook
In this project, we studied classical methods for solving electromagnetic scattering
problems. This led us to the problem of discretizing layer-potentials which involves
numerically integrating weakly singular integrals over surface panels. We then stud-
ied classical quadratures and defined generalized Gaussian quadratures (GGQ), i.e.,
quadratures that integrate collections of functions under very weak conditions. This
was followed by an introduction to a method that makes the problem of generating
GGQs tractable for large collections of functions.
Utilizing that we can generate GGQs, we investigated a method for evaluating sin-
gular integrals over surface panels in R3. The method is built on an expansion of
weakly singular kernels into a polynomial, radial component, and trigonometric axial
component, which we derived. Utilizing this expansion comes at the price of having
to integrate over different reference domains. However, this problem is solved using
a large array of precomputed GGQs.
We then demonstrated the method’s effectiveness and compared it to alternatives
through a range of numerical examples.
The work presented here is a promising proof of concept for using GGQs in compu-
tational electromagnetics. The numerical examples illustrate the method’s potential,
and we have discussed suggestions to improve it further. The method is theoretically
supported but lacks a rigorous and unified error analysis. This is an essential step in
developing the method further and making it ready for applications.
