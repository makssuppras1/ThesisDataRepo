Abstract
Shake platforms have long been used to study the effects of induced vibrations from earthquakes
and high winds. The platforms can also be used to actively dampen structures in a wide range
of conditions.
Actively damping of structures such as wind turbines not only ensures safer operating conditions
but also reduces the materials requirements.
The objective of this project is to dampen a mock-up wind turbine structure using hydraulic sys-
tems. To achieve this, multiple mathematical models are developed, and tools such as Simulink
are employed for modelling purposes. From the linear model, a linear controller is created and
subsequently tested on a nonlinear model within Simulink. The hydraulic pump station and sys-
tem components are dimensioned and ordered from a 3rd party manufacturer, or if necessary,
custom-designed and fabricated in house to meet the test rig’s specifications. As the project
progresses, the mathematical models are refined, with important parameters being calculated
from experimental testing.
The test rig consists of a tower with six lumped masses connected via flexible beams, where two
of the top masses represent the turbine’s blades. The tower sits on linear rails and is coupled
to a hydraulic cylinder, where the movement is regulated by a servo valve. A hydraulic pump
station operates this valve. To capture the dynamic behaviour, accelerometers are fixed to each
lumped mass which are connected to Simulink via a dSpace controller.
Fast Fourier transformations are performed on the tower to validate parts of the mathematical
model. Extensive tests, including frequency response functions are performed on the servo valve
and sensors to find parameters and to study the dynamic behaviour.
The linear mathematical model is made in four steps.
Firstly, a simple model is developed
to find the necessary dimensions and specifications for various physical parts and components
to be able to build the hydraulic system. Secondly, a model is created in Simulink study the
behaviour of the servo valve coupled to an actuator using experimental parameters. Thirdly, a
linear state-space model is produced to construct a linear Kalman filter to act as an observer
and a linear PID and LQR controller. Finally, a non-linear model in Simulink with the use of
Simscape multibody dynamics are designed and coupled with a 3D model of the structure to
test the observer and controller.
The hydraulic actuator’s asymmetry, combined with the absence of position sensors, poses sig-
nificant challenges in the development of a linear model and controller. Even when studying the
theoretical performance of a PID controller, using a symmetric cylinder, including a position
sensor, indicated sub-optimal performance, due to a large temporal delay between the input
signal and the cylinder movement. In contrast, the integration of the Kalman filter/observer
and the LQR controller surpassed expectations, achieving an effective outcome.
Experimental data is obtained for the servo valve and gives a good indication of its characteristics
and is valuable for future students working on the test rig. With a symmetrical cylinder and
a positions sensor on the moving bottom plate, a linear model should capture the test rigs
dynamics well and is possible to be effectively damped with a linear controller. However, for the
current configuration with an asymmetric cylinder discussed in this report, a linear model does
not capture the systems dynamics. Without the a suitable position sensor mounted on piston,
the Kalman observer can not function, therefore the LQR controller does not work.

Preface
At the Department of Civil and Mechanical Engineering at DTU, there are specialised courses
that aim to equip engineering students with knowledge and skills in the field of dynamics. One
such course is “Dynamics of Machinery”, which delves into the study of the dynamic behaviour
of machines. Another related course is “Fluid Power and Motion Control”, which covers control
theory and how to control hydraulic systems. Both courses are under the guidance of Dr. Ilmar
Santos.
After immersing ourselves in the aforementioned courses, our team — consisting of Magnus
Stensbye, Rune Alcott and Daniel Egenes — was eager to deepen our understanding of the
concepts taught in the various assignments. We, therefore, arranged a meeting with Dr. Santos
with the objective of discussing the feasibility of undertaking a project that could serve as a
practical implementation of our master’s thesis. Our aim was to gain a more comprehensive
knowledge of the phenomena covered in the courses. After several meetings, the opportunity
arose for us to work directly under Dr. Santos on a project to design a servo-hydraulic controlled
platform for dynamic testing of structures (specifically, a flexible structure, which resembles a
wind turbine, that is used for educational purposes in the course of Dynamics of Machinery).
The project was completed in the spring of 2023, at the Department of Civil and Mechanical
Engineering (DCME) at the Technical University of Denmark, campus Lyngby. The experience
has been both exciting and educationally rewarding. We would like to take this opportunity to
thank our supervisor; Dr. Ilmar Ferreira Santos who has provided guidance and expertise every
step of the way, along with PhD student Bruno Resende Ferreira Rende. We would also like
to thank Torben Bender Christensen, Head of Workshop at DCME, and his team, especially
Morten, for assisting in purchasing and manufacturing custom-made parts. Lastly, we would
like to thank Benny Edelsten, for assisting with the wiring, assembly and providing us with
electrical expertise.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The need to dampen vibrations in large structures can be traced back to the 15th century
when Leonardo da Vinci observed that stiff materials did not always result in robust structures.
Instead, da Vinci discovered that flexible materials, such as ropes and chains, could be used to
evenly distribute the weight of a structure and mitigate the impact of wind-induced vibrations.
Ever since, engineers have sought innovative approaches to implementing flexible materials in
structures to make construction lighter and more resilient. One notable example of such in-
novation was demonstrated by Gustav Eiffel, who implemented tension cables and hydraulic
dampers when designing the Eiffel Tower in Paris. These types of dampers are classified as
passive dampers and are particularly useful in preventing resonance.
A big challenge in damping structures is the unpredictability and inconsistency of any impact.
If the frequency of the vibrations that require damping is consistent, dampers can easily be
pre-adjusted to mitigate vibrations — such as the dampeners installed on the Eiffel Tower.
However, when dealing with vibrations caused by a sudden and intense force, the vibrations tend
to exhibit a greater unpredictability. Anticipating such unexpected vibrations and implementing
an effective damping strategy for structures under such circumstances is a challenging task.
The motivation behind this thesis is to combine various engineering fields taught at the Techni-
cal University of Denmark into a unified project. This project aims to assist future engineering
students in visualising complex concepts and phenomena, such as mode shapes, natural fre-
quencies and resonance. Alongside this academic goal, there was also a motivation to engage in
hands-on physical experimentation, dividing time between the mechanical workshop and the lab-
oratory. Though initiating a project from the ground up, as opposed to continuing on previous
work, presents a more time-demanding task, it also provides insight into the development phase,
including brainstorming, conceptualisation, sketching, designing, modelling, researching and or-
dering parts. There was also a motivation to learn new analysis techniques, such as developing
complex state space models and incorporating these models into SimuLink and SimScape.
2
==PAGE:16==
1.2
Project Scope
The primary objective of this thesis is to reduce, and in an ideal scenario, neutralise the resultant
induced vibrations in as short a time period as possible, in order to preserve and restore the
functionality of the structure to its original state. More specifically, this thesis will study the
dynamic behaviour of a flexible structure (both theoretically and experimentally), where the
damping is governed by a hydraulic pump unit. The test structure will experience a sudden
blow, by manually striking the structure. By the use of sensors positioned on the structure,
the dynamic behaviour will be recorded and analysed. Through the implementation of control
techniques such as Proportional Integral Derivative and Linear–Quadratic Regulator, the struc-
ture will return to its position of rest. The recorded data will be processed and analysed using
DSpace as a digital acquisition system.
Damping flexible structures through the use of a dynamic platform is not a new concept and
is already a widely explored field and is well established in the industry. These dampers are
primarily electromagnetic shakers or some kind of damper that utilises electricity, rather than
hydraulic oil to govern the dynamics. While electromagnetic are praised for their rapid respon-
siveness and easy to control as they do not require to build-up pressure nor to circulate fluid,
they fall short when it comes to the formidable power and resilience to withstand harsh environ-
mental conditions offered by hydraulically operated dampers. The challenge lies in developing
a suitably linear model that can accurately govern the dynamics of the system. As physical
systems tend to be nonlinear, creating such a model, in conjunction with designing an effective
linear controller, can pose a demanding task. Hydraulic system is therefore mainly chosen for
educational purposes.
The thesis will be split into four main work packages: Hydraulic System Design (1), Exper-
imental System Implementation and Analysis (2), Dynamic Modelling and Simulation of the
Hydraulic System (3), and State Space Control Design (4).
Hydraulic System Design
This module introduce the conceptual framework supporting the design of the experimental
system used in this project. The system is designed based on calculations to dimension the
various components required for ensuring optimal system performance. Additionally, a simple
mathematical model based on calculated system dimensions and estimates is developed to verify
the system dimensions.
Experimental System Implementation and Analysis
This module focuses on the construction and testing of an experimental rig. Several compo-
nents require in-house manufacturing and installation, and the electrical instrumentation and
hydraulic components require tuning and calibrating. A series of experiments will be conducted
using Simulink and dSpace to obtain important experimental measurements, such as servo valve
characteristics and experimental modal analysis. The obtained data will then be processed to
provide a graphical representation of the system’s behaviour.
Dynamic Simulation of Hydraulic System
The objective of this module is to conduct a dynamic simulation of the hydraulic system to un-
derstand and predict the time-dependent interactions between the many components that make
up the system. This will be achieved by developing a mathematical model of the hydraulic
system, using MatLab. This mathematical model will also be used to validate the dynamic sim-
ulation created in SimScape and SimuLink. The main literature for the dynamic mathematical
model is ”Hydraulic Components and Systems” [1], also used as a curriculum in “Fluid Power
and Motion Control”.
State Space Control Design
The objective of the state-space controller is to eﬀiciently dampen the mode shapes of the
structure using an hydraulic system.

The theoretical model is analysed with respect to observability and controlability to ensue the
feasibility of the control design. A control strategy is subsequently chosen to effectively dampen
the structure with a combination of chosen filters.
Lastly, the filter and control design will be validated on the various mathematical models.
1.3
Literature Review
In the following section, an in-depth review of relevant literature regarding servo valve systems,
mathematical modelling, and control methods will be presented. This examination will shed light
on current understandings, established theories, and ongoing debates in these areas, providing
a solid foundation for subsequent discussions and analyses.
In the field of flexible structures, internal damping is often insuﬀicient and proves inadequate,
necessitating the incorporation of external dampers to assist in additional energy dissipation.
External dampers can be classified as being either passive or active [2]. Passive dampers are
the most common and simplest type of dampers, as they do not require an external energy
source to operate.
Examples of passive dampers are springs or dashpots, they are however
restricted to only work at specific frequencies and their damping characteristics are determined
by their physical properties. Active dampers on the other hand are more complex and require
an external source to operate as they encompass sensors, actuators, and a control unit. Active
dampers measure vibrations and apply forces in real time to counteract movement, resulting in
a versatile and adaptive solution to dampen flexible structures experiencing erratic vibrations
[3] [4].
Flexible structures can experience fractures and fatigue due to constant vibration, eventually
requiring maintenance or even failure. Consequently, engineers dedicate considerable effort and
research into the analysis of vibrations and the selection of the most suitable damping methods,
whether they be passive or active.
Issues related to vibrations in multi-story structures are extensively studied due to the threat
posed by earthquakes. Research into simulating earthquakes in all scales has been done for many
years to study how buildings can be safely built. Many of these studies utilise ”shaker tables”
— a technique that shares common goals with this report. However, this work deviates from
previous studies in its approach to utilising the hydraulic system. Rather than employing the
hydraulic system to simulate an earthquake, this report aims to leverage it to actively dampen
the vibrations within the structure during an actual seismic event. Although the goal for this
thesis deviates from conventional applications of shaker tables, there is still considerable value
in studying such methods, given that they can yield valuable insights for this thesis’s damping-
focused goal.
Tuned mass dampers in skyscrapers such as in Taipei 101 building in Taiwan has a 728-ton
tuned mass damper (TMD) that uses active damping to counteract the movement caused by
wind and earthquakes. The TMD is suspended at the top of the building and uses hydraulic
dampers to control the movement of the building. The TMD’s movement is controlled by sensors
that detect the vibrations and adjust the damping force to counteract them. [5]
Further advancements in the field are exemplified by the Large High-Performance Outdoor Shake
Table (LHPOST6) at the University of California, San Diego. The facility is the world’s largest
outdoor earthquake simulator, being built to test structures up to two thousand tons, and has
been enhanced to more authentically reproduce earthquake simulations in all six degrees of
freedom. This allows for the testing of large structures, including multi-story buildings, bridges
and wind turbines, providing critical insights for creating robust and safe structures. [6]
While the main focus of the research discussed above aims towards the simulation of seismic
activities and the implementation of active dampers in multi-story structures, the importance

Much like
the relationship between the sensors and the tuned mass damper in the Taipei 101 building,

Essential to the operational eﬀiciency of such shakers is the implementation of control techniques,
such as Proportional Integral Derivative (PID) and Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR), which
enable robustness and rapid movement, culminating in effective damping [7]. The objective of
control techniques is to swiftly restore the structure to its original state.
In the paper ”Advances in Shake Table Control and Substructure Shake Table Testing,” Matthew
Joseph James Stehman provides insight into the operation of a hydraulically controlled shaker
table, which utilises a conventional PID feedback controller to correct the error between a
measured process variable and a desired setpoint. By ’tuning’ the coeﬀicients in the P, I, and
D equations, the controller can provide the optimal control necessary for shake table systems.
This includes managing rapid movements, ensuring system robustness, and effectively damping
potential disturbances. [8]
The complexity and the specificity of the aforementioned active dampers necessitate precise
mathematical models. These models serve as the foundation for controlling and optimising the
damping process. Experimental data, obtained through various tests are key to developing a
realistic model and contributing to fine-tuning and eﬀiciently damping the model. [9]
The derivation of a mathematical model for a flexible structure is obtained by utilising key
principles and laws in physics, including Newton’s laws and Hooke’s laws. This culminates in
the derivation of what is referred to as the equation of motion (EOM) [10], which can be further
utilised to develop a state matrix. Once developed, the matrix can be manipulated and turned
into an eigenvalue problem, where the natural frequencies and mode shapes are extracted. These
parameters contribute to creating a suﬀicient mathematical model.

2.5.1
Signal Conditioning: Filtering and Windowing
The filtering methods employed for processing the experimental data are designed to address
the critical issues of aliasing. Aliasing is a phenomenon where high-frequency components in
the raw instrumentation signal may be incorrectly represented or ’aliased’ as lower frequencies
in the digitised signal due to inadequate sampling. This can result in distorted and misleading
data.
To mitigate aliasing and smoothen signals, a combination of low-pass filtering (anti-aliasing) and
windowing techniques (smoothing) are applied. Low-pass filtering is crucial in this context, as
it restricts the frequency content of the signal to ensure that no components exceed the Nyquist
frequency, which is defined as half of the sampling rate.
In essence, the Nyquist frequency
acts as a fundamental limit, determining the highest frequency that can be accurately captured
in the digital representation of the signal.
To prevent inaccurate date, a Butterworth low-
pass filter is employed as an anti-aliasing filter. The Butterworth filter’s characteristics allow
it to attenuate higher-frequency components eﬀiciently while preserving the lower-frequency
characteristics. This careful signal conditioning ensures that the experimental data remains free
from aliasing components and accurately reflects the physical characteristics of the unfiltered
signal by ensuring that the cutoff frequency of the filter maximum set to be equal the Nyquist
frequency.
The Butterworth filter is characterised by two key parameters: the cut-off frequency and the
filter degree. The filter degree dictates the sharpness of the filter roll-off, essentially defining the
filter’s response slope as it transitions between the pass-band and stop-band. Compared to other
filters, the Butterworth filter produces a gentle roll-off compared to other filters, but produce no
ripple effect that can cause distortion to the signals. Setting the signal degree too high can lead
to a reduction in signal amplitude. The cut-off frequency of a low-pass filter determine the max
frequency that is to be preserved. Setting the cut-off frequency too low can cause both signal
delay and unwanted filtering of data trends.
2.5. DATA ACQUISITION
22
==PAGE:36==
As some of the sensors produce a substantial amount of noise, an effective smoothing technique
using Savitzky-Golay filter and moving median filter is used to produce experimental plots with
minimal noise. These filters are windowing filters, meaning they utilises a window of a defined
length that slides along the data sample to reduce noise.
The Savitzky-Golay filter is suitable for data where preserving sharp edges and trends are im-
portant. The filter use polynomial fitting within a sliding window to remove noise and preserve
important features [11]. The filter do not eliminate random high peak noises, often referred to
impulse noise . To counteract the limitation, it is paired with a moving median filter, further
mitigating this phenomenon. A moving median filter is suitable for signals that contain signifi-
cant impulse noise. The filter computes the median value of the signal within each window. This
approach effectively mitigates the influence of high-magnitude impulse noise on the underlying
signal trend [12]. In contrast to a moving average filter, which calculates the average value, the
median filter is less susceptible to the disruptive effects of extreme outliers. This way, capturing
the median value of a windowed signal produce more accurate results for data with impulse
noise.
The filtering and windowing parameters are adjusted for each individual instrument and exper-
imental tests, as both instrument noise and data trends are not equal for every measurements.
For this reason, the filtering is applied post-processing to ensure the best trade of between raw
and filtered experimental data, where the focus is mainly to capture important trends and re-
ducing noise peaks. Visual example of the raw and filtered data used in this project can be
studied in Appendix D.
For data used in modal analysis, multiple samples, or chunks, of a discontinuous signal (such
as a multi-chirp sweep), a weighting function, commonly referred to as a window, is applied to
the signal. This is a method to avoid signal leakage, which is a phenomenon where fictitious
frequencies in a discontinuous signal appear in the frequency spectrum, that cause noise. In this
project, a Hanning (or Hann) window is used to avoid leakage. The Hann window is a bell-shaped
curve with vanishing tails (initial and final values), resulting in leakage being reduced.
It’s important to distinguish between the two distinct signal processing tools: the moving mean
window and the Hann window.
The moving mean window is primarily employed for data
smoothing, helping to reduce noise and fluctuations in various types of data. On the other hand,
the Hann window serves a specific purpose in spectral analysis within the frequency domain,
where it is used to minimize spectral leakage during operations like Fast Fourier transforms.

3.2
Modal Analysis: Experimental
Modal analysis is the process in structural dynamics used to describe the dynamic behaviour
of structures in the frequency domain. The analysis identifies a system’s natural frequencies,
associated damping rations and mode shapes. By extracting these vital parameters, it is possible
to predict how the structure will react under various frequencies. These values will assist in
validation of the mathematical models discussed in the subsequent chapters of this report.
3.2.1
Mode Shapes and Natural Frequencies
One of the purposes of the modal analysis is to deconstruct a multi-DOF system into its several
single DOFs parts, being the different mode shapes of the structure. To validate the mathe-
matical models through the natural frequencies, a Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT) analysis
is performed. This process involves manually inducing sudden excitation to the tower, causing
the structure to oscillate. The acceleration of each mass is recorded using the piezoelectric ac-
celerometers. Subsequently, an FFT analysis is conducted on the resultant data, which isolates
the damped natural frequencies of the structure. MATLAB is utilised for this analysis, taking
advantage of the built-in function fft.

3.4
Servo Valve Characteristics
When conducting experimental tests for the valve dynamics, dSpace is used to directly mea-
sure all crucial parameters in the discrete time domain. It is important to ensure quasi-static
equilibrium in the servo valve to avoid interference caused by the dynamics of the fluid (such as
turbulence) flowing through the valve. As the dynamic spool control is very sensitive to input
signals, even small disturbances from unstable flow can give faulty results.
Quasi-static conditions is achieved by transmitting a sine wave with an exceptionally low fre-
quency as an input to the servo valve to ensure quasi-static equilibrium. Unless stated otherwise,
all servo valve dynamics experiments are conducted with a servo valve spool frequency between
0.01 −0.005Hz. The signal sent to the servo valve is a sinusoidal signal uf = A · sin(ωt), where
ω = f2π, f is the spool frequency in Hz, and A is the amplitude of the spool opening in %.
To achieve a quasi static state, the spool frequency must be significantly small (f << 1[Hz]).
All instrumentation signals are transmitted and measured in Simulink and dSpace ControlDesk,
respectively, with a sampling frequency of 1kHz.
It is important to ensure a constant supply pressure during experimental testing which is one
of the main reasons for integrating an accumulator into the pump station. A fluctuating supply
pressure can introduce unwanted fluid dynamics in the servo valve.
The plots presented in this chapter are produced using more intuitive units (Pa, l/min, % of
maximum spool travel) to make the results more comprehensible. All the servo valve character-
istics plots are presented in Appendix B with SI-units. For implementing the linearized servo
valve characteristics in the mathematical model, the servo valve spool travel is converted from
% to m, where the maximum spool travel is ± 1.25mm.
3.4.1
Servo Valve Pressure Drop
The pressure drop is measured as a digital signal from the pressure sensors over the full range of
the servo valve. The experiment is conducted with a servo valve frequency of 0.01Hz to achieve
quasi-static equilibrium under no-load conditions. This way, the pressure drop is only influenced
by the steady-state characteristics of the flow through the servo valve.

of the flow gain plot very close to xv = 0% indicates that the flow is minimal or zero (with
a slight null-bias) when the servo valve spool is in the centre. When operating the hydraulic
system coupled to the actuating subsystem, a servo valve input signal of 0V produce no noticeable
movement in the actuator, indicating that the actual flow at 0% spool travel is practically zero.
The spool also experience approximately 1% hysteresis. For a servo valve, hysteresis of this
magnitude is common [18] [24]. The hysteresis occurs due to static friction (stiction) in the
valve. As the spool changes direction and moves quasi-statically, the input current is increased
to overcome the stiction forces, thus an increase in voltage is required for the spool to move.
Friction is a function of the spool velocity, i.e the friction force will increase as the amplitude and
frequency increases. Examining the flow gain for increased input signal frequencies in Figure 3.8,
it is noticeable that the mean hysteresis seems to be smaller for the test where f = 0.01Hz (b)
comped to f = 0.005 (a). This can be explained by consulting the Stribeck friction model, which
suggest that the friction forces increase for small velocities ranges as the velocity approaches
zero. For larger velocities, the friction forces is larger than the stiction force, which explain the
increase in hysteresis for a further increase in the frequency of the input signal.
A small pressure drop in the flowmeter is also expected, which can influence the hysteresis to
some extent. A common practice to compensate hysteresis is to add a dither signal to the valve
as an input, with an amplitude < 10% of the rated amplitude [24]. This is an intentionally

applied form of noise to transform correlated or cyclical error to random noise [25]. As the
hysteresis is within an acceptable range, dither compensation is not used in this project.
As previously mentioned, there also exist a small null bias, which is the voltage added to over-
come the fail-safe spring in the servo valve. The hysteresis is also dominant around this area,
which might be due to leakage being introduced on both sides of the spool pistons as it ap-
proaches the center. The viscous friction forces from the fluid introduce a friction force on the
spool, as the leakage flow in opposite direction of the spool. Therefore, the servo valve need
additional voltage to move the spool around its center position. From Figure 3.7(a), the flow is

Comparing the FRF results with the experimental results presented in Table 3.2, it is evident
that the Half-Power Bandwidth method yielded damping ratios approximately an order of mag-
nitude higher than those extracted from the experimentally obtained values. Such discrepancies
can be attributed to the inherent alterations introduced by the Butterworth filter applied to the
FRF analysis. Specifically, the filter can introduce phase distortions and magnitude alterations,
especially around the peak frequencies, which can influence the identification of the half-power
points. When the Butterworth filter is applied, even minor alterations in the FRF peaks can
greatly affect the estimation of damping.
Another potential explanation for the observed disparities could lie in the inevitable drift expe-
rienced by the hydraulic cylinder, as discussed in subsection 2.3.5. When a signal is introduced
into the hydraulic cylinder to excite the tower, an evident shift in the piston’s position occurs,
prompting it to move closer to the seal. In this case, a chrip signal is sent to the cylinder. When
the system does not oscillate around the zero point, it introduces deviations in the system’s dy-
namic behaviour. These deviations can significantly deviate from the assumptions underpinning
the Half-Power Bandwidth method.
This method is built on the assumption of symmetrical oscillations occurring around a zero
point. However, the presence of non-centralised oscillations due to the piston’s drift can lead to
corrupted data and lead to inaccurate damping ratios.
An alternative approach to enhance data accuracy could be employing an impact hammer cou-
pled with a force transducer.
By delivering a sharp disturbance to the tower, this method
eliminates the challenges associated with drift in the hydraulic cylinder. Subsequently, conduct-
ing an FRF analysis on the recorded data allows for more reliable data.

Theoretical Modelling
This section introduces the theory, procedures and equation used to build the mathematical
model of the system. The digital twins are made primarily to understand the behaviour of the
system and to be able to design a controller. Four different models of different complexity are
made;
1. Simple non-linear state-space model in MATLAB for the actuation subsystem.
2. Non-linear model of the actuator subsystem in Simulink using experimental parameters
3. Linearized state-space model in MATLAB of the entire system using experimental param-
eters
4. Non-linear model using Simscape library in Simulink of the entire system using experi-
mental parameters
The models 2-4 are validated with experimental data from the real-world test rig.
4.1
Digital Twin
A digital twin is a virtual replica of a physical system that can be modified and utilised to aid
an engineer to optimise a system. Moreover, a well-developed digital twin can provide real-time
insights into a system’s behaviour. To validate the system’s accuracy, engineers can compare
data from a digital twin with accurate data from a physical system. With a well-developed
digital twin, various scenarios and operating conditions can be tested without incurring the risk
and extra costs associated with implementing these changes on a physical system.
As a form of validation, four digital twins will be developed, each utilising a different modelling
approaches. However, all models will be based on the same fundamental equations: ordinary
differential equations (ODE).
The first model is built using a set of non-linear ODE equations in MatLab. This model will
serve as a valuable tool during the early phases of the hydraulic system’s development. The
model offers insights into the system’s dynamic behaviour in early phases of development.
Experimenting with the parameters and initial conditions facilitates the selection of physical
components and parts needed to assemble the unit. This initial model thereby plays a key role
in validating the chosen components and designing the hydraulic system.
Another approach to mathematical modelling is creating a block environment for simulating the
system’s dynamics in Simulink. This makes it easier to visualise the equations and allows for
easy implementation of limits using Simulink built-in function, such as the Saturation block to
comply with system restrict.
Lastly, an environment for modelling multi-domain physical systems is created using Simscape
add-on in Simulink. By utilising a library of pre-built components and fundamental blocks such
as ”spring-damper” and ”mass-blocks” a full system is assembled.
It is worth noting that the MATLAB and the Simulink models make several assumptions, in-
cluding a constant bulk modulus. This assumption has been made to preserve linearity and

hoses — rated at 330 bars — that theoretically can expand under high pressure, increasing the
overall inner diameter. Despite this being theoretically possible, a decision is made to continue
with a constant bulk modulus.

of reference is defined as “a coordinate system with respect to which the position of an object

interconnected, their positions can be described with respect to either a fixed ground or each
other. The former would be considered a global frame of reference, whereas the latter would be
a local frame of reference.

4.3.4
Damping Matrix
As mentioned above, the mass and stiffness matrices can be found by measuring the weight of
the structure and calculating the beam stiffness. Damping on the other hand is more abstract.
Assuming the damping matrix D represents viscous damping, the system can be described as
a linear combination of matrices M and K, governed by coeﬀicients α and β. There are three
possible cases that represent the dissipation of the vibration energy in different ways:
D1 = αM
D2 = βK
D3 = αM + βK
(4.18)
”The physical meaning of these three assumptions can be explained by comparing the structure
to a simple spring-damper system. D1 states that each lumped mass in a system has a damper
attached to it, and the damping coeﬀicient is proportional to the mass itself. The independent

Hydraulic States
The accumulator that is discussed in subsection 2.3.4, can be seen as linear in the operating

in the system as close to operating pressure as possible when there are pressure fluctuations.

Actuator Area Asymmetry
The hydraulic actuator used in this project features asymmetrical piston areas on the extend
and retract sides. However, for oscillating motion application, it is common to use a symmetric
piston to avoid the significant complications area asymmetry introduce.
Equation 4.44 assumes that one side of the servo valve is consistently connected to the same
side of the actuator. Consequently, the piston cannot exhibit the type of drift that a real system
with asymmetrical piston areas might experience. This is because the same flow gain Kq is
connected to the same cylinder area A at all times.
However, for a more accurate representation of the real system, this configuration can be switched
when the flow direction in the cylinder is reversed. This adjustment ensures that the actuator
receives the same flow for both extending and retracting the piston, regardless of the sign of the
servo valve opening.
As a result, the real system behaves in a piece-wise linear manner, introducing non-linearity
that is not captured in Equation 4.44.
In the research paper Piecewise Linear Control Pystems[36], it is argued that it is possible to
create a linear state space control system with Kalman filter and LQR controller with a piecewise
linear system. This is however outside the scope of this project due to the added complexity.
A more simple approach to the problems of piston asymmetry is presented in Appendix G,
however, this is not an accurate solution to the problem.

The tower is simplified to six lumped masses, where each lumped mass represents a degree of
freedom. The number of natural frequencies and therefore the mode shapes in such a simplified
tower equals the number of DOF. Each of the six modehapes corresponds to a pattern of motion
where the masses oscillate with a specific amplitude and phase to each other.

to extract the natural frequencies and mode shapes from the linear state space matrix A which
is a combination of the mass and stiffness matrices as seen in Equation 4.4. This involves the
identification of eigenvectors (u) and their associated eigenvalues (λ), which enable the extraction

5.1
Controllability and Observability
Before deciding on a control strategy and designing a controller, two main system properties
need to be addressed — the ability to observe a state and the ability to control a state, also
known as controllability and observability.
State Space Order of Magnitude
These properties are highly dependent on the order of magnitude, O(), of the state space matrix.
The state space matrix A has large variations that originate from the bulk modulus in the
pressure state O(109) and the servo valve position O(10−4).
This creates an ill-conditioned
matrix and makes it numerically diﬀicult to calculate. Rather than removing all units by non-
dimensionalization, some of the units are changed to increase the numerical precision.
The
pressure in Pa is changed to the unit of Bar, the valve position is changed to units of 10−4m,
and lastly, the cylinder velocity is changed to dm/s. The numerical precision is also increased
to include 100 decimals for these calculations instead of the usual 32 in MATLAB.
Controllability:
A linear system is said to be controllable if the system states can be moved from any initial
conditions to any desired condition in a finite time using the available control inputs. With a fully
controllable system and the correct control, all the eigenvalue poles of the system can be placed
in the desired location. The controllability of the system is calculated with the controllability
matrix, which is found using the function ctrb in MATLAB. By taking the rank of the matrix,
the number of controllable states can be found, which ideally should be the same as the number
of states to make the system fully controllable.
Co = [B, AB, A2B, ...A(n−1)B]
Co = ctrb(A, B)

Co_Rank = 18

controllable. The poles can therefore be chosen arbitrarily and the states of the system can be
placed at any desirable final state, which in this case is all states equal to zero in the tower.

5.1.2
Control Requirements and Limitations
Setting concrete requirements for the controller is necessary to be able to choose a preferred
type of control strategy.
The goal of the controller is to continuously dampen the vibrations in the tower, especially at
mass 4. This is done by regulating the input voltage to the servo valve which drives the actuation
of the actuator mounted to the platform. The vibrations originate from disturbance on mass 3,
which is initiated by manually disturbing the system.
There are three limits on the system: The input voltage to the servo valve which is limited
to ± 10 V, the actuation of the cylinder, which is physically limited to ± 40 mm and should
not be reached, and lastly, the actuation frequency is set to a maximum of 10 Hz, which is the
maximum frequency from the mode shapes. Getting close to these limits should be avoided, as
non-linearities will increase closer to the boundaries further from the linearisation point.
These requirements emphasize the primary need for a controller, highlight that tracking of the
target is unnecessary as the goal for mass 4 is to remain stationary, and suggest that constraints
handling would be beneficial, albeit not critical, as these limits can be managed through control
of the input signal magnitude, and the system’s hardware is inherently protected within these
limits.
5.1.3
Choosing Control Algorithm
When researching which control algorithm to use, extensive research was done on four different
levels of control strategies.
First, the Proportional Integral Derivative (PID) controller is a simple control algorithm which
can effectively handle a wide range of problems, making it a suitable starting point in many cases.
The controller is easy to implement, but tedious to tune to get the right gains (amplification
of proportional, integral and derivative terms) for the desired result. Minor alterations to the
system might render the current gains ineffective, necessitating their re-calibration. Moreover,
the PID controller requires the state’s position to be stabilized, but there is no position sensor
mounted on mass 4.
Furthermore, a considerable delay between input actuation and state
response could cause issues for the PID controller.
The Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) is widely used due to its simplicity, robustness, and
ability to handle complex, but linear, systems. The LQR controller utilises a cost function to
compare the input with the various output states and then minimises the total cost over time
using the Riccati equation (presented later in Equation 5.12). This is a simple algorithm to
implement in MATLAB where the cost function can easily be modified. The control strategy
only works on linear state-space, where it is the optimal controller. However it is not possible to
implement constraints, such as limit on cylinder stroke. Without constraints, the full range of

==PAGE:118==
control can not be effectively utilised. As this is a full-state feedback controller, all states need
to be observed. This can be accomplished with a Kalman filter.[39]
Model predictive control (MPC), is an advanced controller that optimises with the LQR repeat-
edly. It optimises for each new current time-slot, but also takes into account future time-slots
over a finite time-horizon and can therefore handle constraints. [40] MPC can be expanded to
handle infinite time horizons and non-linear systems. This however requires large computational
power and thus needs to be optimised accordingly, which generates an overly complicated con-
troller. An example is the Dual Mode Optimized Predictive Control (DMOPC), which is one of
the most advanced, but widely used, controllers as it incorporates infinite horizon, can handle
non-linear systems, and is computationally optimised, thus possible to use in real-time [41].
H-infinity control is a modern control technique that designs optimal systems by minimising
the effect of uncertainties, disturbances, and noise. It formulates the control problem as an
optimisation problem that minimises the H-infinity norm of a transfer function and is useful
in controlling complex systems with uncertain dynamics and high noise levels. As this project
consists of a single known system, this controller, which focuses on robustness, is not required.[42]

Theory Behind LQR
LQR is a full-state feedback controller, meaning that it utilises the entire linearised state space
model in its operation. All states are then multiplied with a chosen cost function as previously
mentioned. The chosen state cost matrix is often called Q and is multiplied with the state space
variables X. The chosen input cost vector (when there is only one input) is called R and is
multiplied with the state-space input u, which is the input to be calculated. By applying a cost
to each state, it is possible to compare them to each other and solve for the minimum cost over
time. This is done for an infinite-horizon, continuous time and outputs a gain matrix called K
to be used in the feedback loop. The cost function to be minimised is defined as:

input should be used over time, depends on the cost matrix Q and cost matrix R. If R is high
compared to Q, then the system will go faster to zero, but at the expanse of higher input.

5.2.2
Kalman Filter
As briefly stated earlier, in order to compute the optimal input signal, u(t), using the gain
matrix K, all states need to be accounted for. This is however usually infeasible in a real system
from a design and cost perspective as this will require many sensors.
A Kalman filter is used for two reasons: firstly, to estimate the signals which are not measured,
thus acting as an observer, and secondly, to reject as much noise from the measured signal as
possible. The Kalman filter utilises the linear state-space and compares it with the measured
signal to find the optimal average. In the same way as LQR, the disturbance covariance matrix
V d (similar to Q), and noise covariance vector V n (similar to R), decides if the state-space
model or measured signal should be trusted more in the comparison for each state.
Both the linear state space-model and the measurements from the sensors slightly deviate from
the actual and accurate state of the system.
Inherently, the state-space model tends to be
smoother compared to the sensors, but experience phase lag and deviate over time as it does not
account for disturbances from external forces. Disturbances can also come from the difference
in response because of errors in the state-space model. Conversely, the sensors exhibit a more
jagged response due to noise, but rapidly respond to disturbances and should not deviate over
time.
The Kalman filter is calculated much in the same way as the LQR controller and is therefore
a good combination and easy to implement. The Kalman filter is sometimes called a Linear
Quadratic Estimator (LQE) and it is normal to be combine it with an LQR in series, which is
called a Linear Quadratic Gaussian (LQG) controller. In MATLAB, the same function lqr can
be utilised with varied inputs and certain transformations:
KF = lqr(A′, C′, V d, V n)′;
(5.14)
If V d significantly exceeds V n, the filter anticipates a higher dominance of disturbances relative
to noise. As a result, the filter places increased reliance on the sensor readings.
The Kalman filter is in itself a state-space system that solves an ODE that can be expressed as:
˙x = (A −KFC)x + [BKF] u

5.3
Control Diﬀiculties
There are many problems when aiming to control a non-linear hydraulic system. To be able to
utilize the LQR-controller and not have to develop a non-linear controller outside the scope of
the project, the attempt to compensate for the non-linearities are done outside of the controller.
One possibility is to have two state-spaces where one is dedicated for extension and the other
for retraction of the cylinder. The difference between the state spaces would only be to connect
the change in pressure to the opposite side of the cylinder. This would also require two Kalman
filters and two LQR controllers. The problem, however, is that the motion would no longer
be sinusoidal, thus the high pass filter on the sensors would no longer work. On the real test
rig, this is still a problem. The only way to make it work, would be to have constraints on the
controller to exclude any drift. However, this would lead to an advanced non-linear controller
and additional experimental parameters would need to be investigated, such as the effective bulk
modulus.
A second option is to adjust the input voltage to have a larger valve opening (i.e larger amplitude)
in one direction to compensate for the different area. Due to the non-linearity of the flow through
the servo valve, this option is also a non-linear problem, as the extra fluid needed to fill larger
volume is mitigated by the extra force acting in the larger area at higher frequencies. This
method was tested on the system, but did not provide a good accuracy (see Appendix G).
Other linerization problems arise from the bulk modulus changing with changing volume from
the cylinder and flexibility in the hydraulic hoses. This causes nonlinear pressure change that
is transferred to the cylinder.

In the Simulink model, the grey system box is closely modelled to simulate the real world. It
is therefore transformed to the inertial frame with a gain matrix, which is simple in Simulink,

low-pass filter as integration in itself performed this action.
After obtaining the position and velocities, they are input into the Kalman filter to predict
and filter all states. Between the Kalman filter and the optimal full-state controller LQR, the
initial pressures are subtracted. This ensures that the controller does not attempt to bring the
pressures to zero as it does with all other states. After all the states are inserted into the LQR
gain, the resulting output from the LQR controller is subtracted from the zero reference signal
and fed back to the system as input voltage.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Test Rig and Experiments
The hydraulic pump station work flawlessly when combined with the soft starter. It preforms
well with constant operating pressure and flow well over the needed specifications.
The servo valve overlapped nature does ensure low enough leakage to not cause any problems.
With the frequencies operated with the LQR controller, the overlapped nature has observed to
have minimal effect.
Without an dedicated oil cooler, the oil temperature rises gradually during the course of the
experiments. The lack of a cooler has not been a significant problem, as the operation time of
the system is kept short, meaning each experimental test must be completed less then 5 minutes
before the pump station need to cool down. This is found to be an acceptable working time
length for a single test, but the oil takes significant time to cool down. This leads to the time
between tests can take up to several hours.
The cylinder on the test rig is not optimal when aiming to create a linear controller. It has
by far created the most problems in project, and is the main reason why the damping of the
tower structure was only possible in the simulations, not the real system. The cylinder should
be replaced with a symmetric actuator for this application. The cylinder static friction is also
high and therefore very non linear. This creates problems when the linear friction is modelled
as a constant.
7.2
Sensors
The position sensor associated with the hydraulic cylinder and mass 0 is essential for ensuring
system observability. It is therefore highly recommended that this is implemented for any future
studens working with the test rig.
The force transducer is found to not be working as expected and is most likely faulty. This
sensor is needed to reliably find the friction behaviour in the actuator.
The use of an high-pass filter when integrating acceleration to displacement is sub optimal when
the optimal solution to dampen the tower is to preform rigid body motion which the high-pass
filter can not handle. It did however help when integrating once to find velocities to use in the
Kalman filter.
7.3
Mathematical model
The test rig has many non-linearities that is hard to linearise accurately. The biggest diﬀiculty
is accounting for different piston areas, which is inherently non-linear even if it is piece-wise
linear. Other non-linearities include the overlapped valve, changing volume that effect the bulk
modulus, friction in the actuator and a servo valve that has non-linearity dependent on pressure,
flow and opening.
With the many diﬀiculties when modelling an hydraulic system, at one point, linear approxi-
mations does not satisfy the requirements. Hydraulic systems are found to be a great example
of where some terms of the system should be kept non-linear. This is not to say lineariation is
124
==PAGE:138==
not needed as for many parts of the system, this is still a valid method. Piece-wise linearisation
with sign dependency for the pressure connection between the servo valve and cylinder would
greatly aid the model. It is also possible to have an analytical non-linear approximation for the
piston friction and bulk modulus.
Steps have been made to mitigate the sign non-linearity of the actuator, but with this relationship
also being non-linear, it is in practice diﬀicult to model outside of the state space. Some of the
compensation methods have given various results, but nothing of a satisfactory level.
In total, the system is in practice too non-linear to effectively model accurately enough for a
strictly linear model. The changes recommended for the system, being an symmetric piston in
the actuator and a displacement sensor on mass 0, is an highly recommended upgrade to the
test rig for any future endeavours.
7.4
Simulink and Simscape
Even though Simscape in Simulink is an incredible tool to model almost any system, is it not
suﬀicient to simulate this real world system. This is for one because it is slow to compute when
having high enough accuracy, secondly, there is a lack of control when using the built in blocks.
When aiming to simulate a real system, the best option is to code it from the ground up with
full control and possibilities for debugging. Too much time went in to building the Simscape
model for it to be less accurate then the linear state space system. Simscape is therefore not
recommended for this purpose.
The aim with the Simscape model was to simulate the controller on a non-linear system. However
the non-linearities were too dominant for the controller to work, and it was very diﬀicult to match
the Simscape model with the real world. This was exaggerated when the model was finished,
coeﬀicients found and ready to be testes late in the project with little to no time to fine tune
the Simscape model.
While the idea of having a non-linear model to test the linear controller is good, the computa-
tional time for the model takes longer then preforming a real world test. Preforming a real world
test with a non-optimised controller does not hurt the system as it is protected with numerous
safeguards as long as the input is not exaggerated beyond reason. If the controller work on the
linear model, it should be safe to test it on the real test rig. This also makes it possible to tune
the controller close to real time.
Constructing the Simscape model gave a very good understanding of the system. Blocks, and
therefore parts, can easily be changed and replaced.
The initial model was quick to model
and quickly to compute if real life accuracy was not of a priority. Simscape can therefore be
a valuable tool to model an generic system. Another great use of Simscape are the resulting
animations. This makes initial building of the system easier, great for presentation purposes
and more rewarding as a engineering tool.
7.5
Control strategy
As the system is too non-linear for the model to depict the system behaviour very accurately, the
controller based on this system will also fail to accurately dampen vibrations on the experimental
test rig.
The PID and PD controller did not effectively work as there are too large delays between mass 4
and the servo valve input, not to mention the practical diﬀiculties with placing an displacement
sensor on mass 4.
The LQR controller worked perfectly on the linear state-space model it was designed for. The
LQR controller can not handle a non linear hydraulic system, thus creating a more accurate
liner model would not help in this regard. The same can be said for the Kalman filter which
7.4. SIMULINK AND SIMSCAPE
125
==PAGE:139==
effectively removed most noise, predicted the unobserved states and handled disturbances well
for the theoretical test.
Designing an LQE controller gives a large understanding of state space models, control the-
ory but also sensor noise and disturbance. It was practical to implement in Matlab, but also
mathematically rewarding to understand. It effectively fulfilled its purpose to the extend it was
designed for.
For future work, it could be better to build a non linear observer such as an augmented Kalman
filter with at least a sign-dependent mathematical model with the asymmetric cylinder. Even
better would be to change to a symmetric cylinder.
A piece-wise linear model could work, but at that point, it would be better to create a fully non
linear model with a non linear controller. Creating an controller such as an MPC and optimising
it in steps could be a great learning for a control design engineer.

APPENDIX G
Actuator Asymmetry Compensation
The actuator utilised in this project presents a significant challenge in dynamic applications
due to its asymmetric piston areas. It is crucial to control this asymmetry to achieve harmonic
cylinder motion, which is a requirement for optimal control. Using the Simulink model show-
cased in figure Figure G.1, an open-loop control technique is proposed, which is based on the
relationship of equal extension and retraction cylinder stroke:

gain (fitted polynomial equation) to calculate the required flow in chamber 1 ,Q1,(extension),
and consequently input signal amplitude, to achieve the same stroke length as for retraction
