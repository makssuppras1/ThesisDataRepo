Abstract 
 
 
 
Laser technology is continuously evolving and expanding to all kinds of fields and 
applications. One of these involves the use of the laser’s light characteristics to interact 
with the first two layers of the human skin in order to achieve different purposes, as could 
be the reduction of scar marks, removal of tattoos, hair removal, etcetera. The main focus 
of this project will be on skin resurfacing applications. 
 
Skin rejuvenation aims to use laser light to interact with the second layer of the human 
skin, the dermis, to trigger a mechanism where new skin cells replace old deteriorated cells, 
visibly reducing skin marks caused by age. Skin rejuvenation is realized by experts’ 
operators in knowledge of the application procedure, skin characteristics, laser settings and 
laser manipulation. 
 
The goal of this project is to reduce two main types of risks of injury. The first exists due 
to the need of manually handling the laser over long periods of time, which causes fatigue 
and potential muscular injuries on the operators. The second one is regarding skin burns 
produced as a result of bad manipulation of the laser, the difficulty to trace homogeneous 
paths over the skin, or as a consequence of the laser settings not being appropriate for the 
type and the conditions of the skin. 
 
For this purpose, a system will be developed making use of a robotic arm, a thermal camera 
and a depth camera as main components. The system will allow operators to achieve a 
homogeneous application of the laser over the desired areas of the skin automatically, and 
to receive thermal feedback of the laser-skin reaction to allow safer and more suitable laser 
settings for each person to treat.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
 
Since the creation of the first laser in 1960 by Theodore H. Maiman, laser technology has gradually 
been evolving and expanding it usage to many different fields and applications: from 
telecommunications, to photography, printing industry, etcetera. (Serena Gianfaldoni, 2017) 
 
One of these fields, is the medical. One year after the laser creation, in 1961, Charles Koester and 
Charles Campbell treated the first patient with a retina tumor using laser (Charles J. Koester, 2003). 
About a week later, Christian Zweng performed two successful similar operations; Since then, laser 
technology has expanded to many medical fields: Kaplan introduced it in plastic surgery; Abela in 
cardiology; Ascher in neurosurgery; Lynn-Power in dentistry; Apfelberg for the treatment of vascular 
lesions; Chekurov, Oshiro and Trelles in rheumatologic and in traumatology diseases (Serena 
Gianfaldoni, 2017). 
 
The first tests of laser for dermatology purposes uses were made in 1960 right after its creation by Leon 
Goldman, considered the father of the laser medicine. He tested the laser-skin effect on skin 
pigmentation and funded the first  biomedical laser laboratory at University of Cincinnati in 1961; In 
1963 he published the first article on the subject of laser-skin interactions and use of laser in medicine 
(Basics in Dermatological Laser Applications, 2011).

Nowadays, the usage of laser applications in dermatology is quite broad: they are used to treat Vascular 
Lesions, Tattoo Removal, Hair Removal, Wrinkle Removal, Scar Removal, Acne Removal, etcetera. 
 
For each of these applications, not only laser but different dermatology energy-based devices are used: 
radio frequency devices, IPL (intense pulsed light, LED technology, and a wide range of different laser 
devices (Consulting Industry News, 2018). 
 
The working principle that makes the use of these devices advantageous, if that different regions of the 
components of the skin, reacts to different types of energy or light spectrums. In this reaction, skin 
components that are willing to be removed, as for example could be a vascular lesion that react to visible 
light (at 418, 542 and 577 nm), which could be targeted in order to avoid damage on the surrounding 
tissues.  
 
Regarding the Laser devices, several subtypes has appeared over the years as laser technology has 
evolved to address different treatments and results: Fractional, Diode, InfraRed, Alexandrite laser, 
Nd:YAG, Pulsed, and Potassium Titanyl Phosphate (KTP) (Future Market Insights, 2016).

1.3 Proposed Solution 
 
One of the main reasons that motivated the realization of this project, were the existing risks of skin 
injury. After meeting professionals from the industry and knowing the risks and barriers that has been 
previously mentioned, two main solutions were proposed: 
 
1- Mechanical robotic system to manipulate the laser: having a system that could manipulate the laser 
automatically, with safety and accuracy, would allow to avoid the risk of injury that occurs in the skin 
due to overlapping laser shoots or laser paths, which happens often due to the difficulty of practitioners 
to trace paths on the skin and remember or distinguish already treated areas from areas to be treated.  
This will allow as well to open the door to the use of high precision laser that are not suitable for human 
manipulation due to the small diameter of the beam. 
Practitioners could also avoid injures, caused by the long periods of time they are required to apply the 
laser with precision over the skin of the people under treatment. 
 
2 - The use of thermal camera that could detect the laser shoot, get a measurement as a feedback, and 
represent the feedback information on the correct region of the skin where the reaction happened (as 
visual feedback system). Having a thermal map of the skin will allow to see which areas have been 
treated, and the temperature of each point, avoiding overlapping and providing feedback on the skin-
laser reaction to take measures on the laser settings if needed. 
 
The whole project should be able to be controlled and configured by professionals through an interface, 
where they should be allowed to decide which regions to threat, to see thermal feedback, to log data for 
future analysis, among calibration and other features that will be discussed on the development chapter.

Chapter 2: Theoretical Foundation  
 
2.1 Laser Technology: Definition and Applications 
 
A laser is a device that generates a beam of light with very different properties than the ones of the light 
coming from the sun and stars, or the other man-made lighting technologies as could be the bulb. Laser 
technology and applications has been evolving and expanding since the first laser was built in 1960 by 
Theodore H. Maiman (Engineering and Technology History Wiki, 2016). 
 
Laser technology is used in many fields, such as: welding and cutting, medical applications, 
communication, printing, barcode scanners, heat treatment, photochemistry, laser cooling, nuclear 
fusion, etc. 
 
 
Figure 2 - Comparison between torch light and laser light (Bhatt, 2018) . 
 
 
2.1.1 Laser Properties 
 
The properties that characterize laser light are: 
 
- 
Monochromatic: unlike light form the Sun of a torchlight, laser only emits light in a specific 
wavelength, i.e., it could only be of one color. The wavelength of light is determined by the 
amount of energy released when the electrons on the laser medium drops their energy level to 
a lower one. 
- 
Directional: whereas normal light disperses and spreads along its way on space and as it passes 
through lenses, becoming weaker and diffused. Laser light beams keep together, staying strong 
and concentrated for long distances. 
- 
Coherent: Inside a flash of light from conventional source, light travels in waves that are all 
mixed. Laser light is coherent, all the waves are exactly in perfect same steps (Figure 2).

- 
High intensity: Besides the previous properties, laser light is often characterized by the high 
intensity that can reach the produced beam, which makes it of extreme interest for applications 
nowadays. 
 
2.1.2 Principle of Operation 
 
The principle of operation of a laser can be separated in 4 different steps: 
 
1 - Spontaneous absorption and emission: 
when an atom is supplied energy, it can absorb this energy by raising its electrons’ energy states to 
higher ones, this is called spontaneous absorption. When the electron falls back to a lower energy 
state, it gives back energy releasing a photon equal to the difference between the two energy states. 
 
2 - Stimulated Emission:  
responsible of the organized, coherent effect of laser light and key process on laser light generation. 
When a photon is released from an electron that has drop its energy level from a state E2 to E1, if it 
interacts in its way with another excited electron on E2, it will be forced to lower its state to E1 and 
drop another photon with the same phase, frequency and approximately the same direction. 
 
3 - Population Inversion:  
The process that allows to have many atoms in excited states in the gain medium of the laser. 
Achieved through a process called pumping. There are different ways to achieve this, for example, 
using Xenon flash light from an external source, which provides the necessary energy for electrons in 
the atoms to jump to an excited state. 
 
4 - Optical resonation:  
it consists of a pair of mirrors that allow the Intensity to increment and to filter photons with 
undesired direction. They are placed in each side of the gain medium where excited electrons are kept. 
One mirror is fully reflective and makes all light escaping the medium to bounce back; The other 
mirror is partially reflective, part of the light will escape producing the output laser beam, and other 
part will bounce back to the medium. This allows the stimulated emission to constantly occur all the 
time. Since stimulated emission makes all photons to have same properties of phase, wavelength and 
polarization, but direction sometimes can be perturbed, only perfectly directional light will escape. 
 
2.1.3 Types of Lasers: 
 
There are many different types of lasers. They usually are classified according to the gain medium used: 
- 
Solid-state lasers: the lasing material is a solid matrix (ex. Infrared neodymium-Yag lasers) 
- 
Gas lasers: use gas a lasing material (ex. Infrared CO2 laser). 
- 
Excimer lasers (excited and dimers): reactive gases mixed with inert gases such as argon, 
krypton or xenon. Are ultraviolet lasers. 
- 
Dye lasers: they use complex organic dyes and are Characteristic for its flexibility to be 
designed in a large range of wavelengths.

Semiconductor (diode) lasers: can be very small and use low power. They may be built into

They can also be classified by the mode of operation:

2.2 Skin Rejuvenation and Resurfacing 
 
2.2.1 Definition and Applications 
 
The term of Skin Rejuvenation refers to the group of procedures to treat undesirable skin conditions 
and skin aging effects. Skin rejuvenation can be achieved through several methods: application or 
injections of chemical substances, thermal processes, mechanical processes, photo-rejuvenation, etc. 
(Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia, 2019) 
 
Skin resurfacing is one type of skin rejuvenation, it is worth noting that often both terms are used in a 
misleading manner or interchanged. Skin resurfacing are the skin rejuvenation treatments that 
specifically removes the two upper layers of the human skin, epidermis and dermis, in a controlled way 
to remove undesired conditions and promote optimal skin regrowth. Skin Resurfacing embraces as well: 
chemical, thermal, mechanical, and photo resurfacing methods. 
 
Laser resurfacing is a laser surgery technique that dissociates molecular bounds in a specific depth of 
the skin, it is used to treat acne scars, aging marks and spots, birthmarks, spider angioma, uneven skin 
color hyperpigmentation, vascular lesions, among others. One of the main advantages of laser 
resurfacing respect other as the chemical peels or dermabrasion, is the customizable capability that 
lasers offers to be adjusted to each person and the different areas of the skin. 
According to the goal and deep, some treatments are performed with topical anesthesia to keep the 
person to treat comfortable (Miami Herald, 2014).

2.2.2 Resurfacing Laser Classification 
 
There are different technologies of lasers used for skin resurfacing (Jason Preissig, 2012). They can be 
separated into: 
 
-Ablative lasers: it is a wounding laser that removes thin layers of skin. Ablative lasers were the first 
ones to be used on the skin care industry, since 1970, with laser models based on CO2. Other types of 
ablative lasers uses erbium, which is also common for resurfacing treatments. They require long 
recovery of the skin, several months, and special care after treatment. 
 
-Non-Ablative lasers: non-wounding laser, stimulates collagen growth and tightens the underlying skin. 
Includes many types of lasers as Nd:YAG or Diode based lasers, as well as intense pulse light (IPL) 
devices. Recovery time is faster than ablative lasers and results are less noticeable, usually require more 
sessions. 
 
-Fractional photo-thermolysis (Fraxel): is a type of laser that can operate in both ablative and non-
ablative scenarios. 
 
 
2.3.3 Risks and Dangers 
There are several risks in laser resurfacing treatments to be concern about (Medium, 2017). The main 
ones: 
 
- 
Scarring: when the lasers are not corrected manipulated or their settings are wrongly adjusted, 
risks exist to produce scarring. It's produced as a consequence of the high temperature of the 
tissues. Overtreating a spot or using a laser beam too intense for the type or region of the skin 
are the main consequences.  
- 
Redness, swelling and itching: specifically, with ablative lasers, skin will result itchy, red and 
swollen. It is directly related to the depth of the resurfacing being used.  
- 
Infection: ablative laser resurfacing can lead to infection due to bacterial, viral or fungal 
infection. The most common one being the herpes virus, that might have already been present 
but dormant in the skin.

2.3 Robot Arms 
 
Robotic arms are mechanical manipulator devices with similar functions to human arms, that can be 
programmed to achieve different purposes. The arm is made out a series of joints that allows rotational 
or translational displacements. The different links that forms the manipulator creates a kinematic chain. 
 
The end of the kinetic chain of a robot arm is referred to as ‘end effector’. End effector are designed to 
interact with the environment, and they can consist of a wide variety of devices, tools or sensors, of 
many different natures: grippers, welding torches, force-torque sensors, etc. At the time of programming 
robot arms, is common to use the term Tool Center Point (TCP) to the point of the tool that will interact 
with the environment, and thus, that will be used for the necessary kinematic calculations (Bouchard, 
2014). 
 
2.3.1 Classification of Robot Arms 
Robot arms are commonly classified according to the different configurations achieved according to 
the type of joints used to build the robot (RobotWorx - Industrial Robot Automation Integrator, 2008). 
The main types of robot arms, represented in Figure 3 are:

2.3.3 Important Considerations 
 
There are additional parameters that defines robots and are of high importance when comparing 
different models. Some parameters to take into consideration are: 
 
- 
The number of axes: at least two axes are needed to reach any point in a plane, and three in 
space. Roll, pitch, and yaw control is required for full control of the end effector. 
- 
Payload: amount of weight that a robot can lift or carry. 
- 
Accuracy and repeatability: define the reliability of the robot to reach different position, how 
important is the calibration, and how often should be recalibrated. 
- 
Compliance: distance or angle a robot joint will move under the action of a force. 
 
 
2.3.3.1 Robot Singularity 
 
According to The American National Standard for Industrial Robots and Robot Systems — Safety 
Requirements (ANSI/RIA R15.06-1999, 1999): Singularity is “a condition caused by the collinear 
alignment of two or more robot axes resulting in unpredictable robot motion and velocities.”   
 
When calculating robot positions and trajectories, a singularity will result in a zero Jacobian matrix. 
Physically, it is possible to appreciate how the robot loses one or more degrees of freedom and a change 
in a joint configuration does not reflect any change in the end effector. 
 
They might happen due to joint alignments, joint limitation to trace linear trajectories, target positions 
too close to the robot structure or too far from the working area (Universal Robots, 2018). They are 
crucial when considering the robot arm emplacement for the task to perform. Often developers offer 
measures to avoid them to some extent (The URScript Programming Language, 2016), as well as 
different calculation approaches and techniques might avoid some other.

at a certain position and with a precise orientation. Every position and orientation to reach a point is

2.4 Thermal Radiation and Thermographic Cameras 
 
2.4.1 Electromagnetic Spectrum 
 
Visible light is a type of radiation, which physically takes the form of a wave propagating through space. 
The wave of the visible light is characterized by a specific wavelength, between 380 and 760 nanometers 
(Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia, 2019). Every wavelength has a specific frequency. 
 
But in the environment, all types of radiation exist with different wavelengths and all that is around us 
is a source of radiation, the Sun, a rock, a tree, etc. 
 
 
Figure 5 - A diagram of the electromagnetic spectrum, showing various properties across the range of frequencies and 
wavelengths (Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia, 2019) 
 
 
All existing radiation found in the environment according to the frequency, from the lowest to the 
highest, form the so called the Electromagnetic Spectrum. It is common to separate radiation into 
different groups according to frequency bands. A classification of the Electromagnetic Spectrum is 
shown on Figure 5, it can be perceived that visible light is in the band of 400–790 terahertz (380 to 760) 
nanometers, infrared light in the band of 300 GHz to 30 THz (1 mm - 750 nm).  
 
 
2.4.2 Black Body 
 
All normal (baryonic) matter emits electromagnetic radiation when it has a temperature above absolute 
zero. The radiation represents a conversion of a body's internal energy into electromagnetic energy, and 
is therefore called thermal radiation. It is a spontaneous process of radiative distribution of entropy. 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
     Control System for Facial Laser Rejuvenation with Thermal Imaging and Robotic Arm Integration                   2019 | Technical University of Denmark 
18 
When a black body is at a uniform temperature, its emissions have a characteristic frequency 
distribution that depends on the temperature. Its emission is called black-body radiation. 
 
Real objects never behave as full-ideal black bodies, and instead, they emit radiation as a fraction of 
what the ideal emission (black-body radiation) would be, which defines the concept of emissivity. The 
emissivity of a material, specifies how well a real body radiates energy as compared with a black body, 
since the emission and temperature relation of the black body is known, by measuring the emissivity of 
any object, it is possible to know its temperature. 
 
2.4.3 Emissivity 
 
Emissivity is a ratio that expresses how much of the incoming radiation is kept inside a body. All 
existing objects exchange heat in the form of radiation, receiving, emitting, reflecting and transmitting 
(MoviTHERM, 2018). 
 
 
 
Figure 6 - Emissivity components of a body as seem by a thermographic camera (MoviTHERM, 2018)a. 
 
 
Emissivity formula: 1 = Transmission + Reflection + Emitted 
 
Emissivity, as shown in Figure 6 express in a range from 0 (perfect reflective body) to 1 (‘black body’ 
or theoretically a perfect radiation absorber) how much from the incoming radiation to a body, returns 
back to the environment considering: 
- 
 Emitted radiation: which depends of the inner body temperatures. 
- 
Reflected radiation: amount of radiation that bounces on the object surface and does not pass 
to its internal structure. 
- 
Transmitted radiation: the energy that passes through the internal structure of an object without 
being absorbed. 
 
When measuring body temperatures, it is interesting to focus on the emitted radiation, reflected and 
emitted radiation introduce errors in temperature measurements sensors and need to be taken care of for 
this special reason.

2.4.4 Thermographic Camera 
 
A thermal camera, colloquial term for infrared camera, is a device that makes use of environment 
infrared radiation to form an image, similar to how common cameras creates images using visible light. 
While common cameras use the range of 400 to 700 nanometer range of visible light that human eyes 
perceive in different colors, thermographic camera uses an infrared range of light (up to 14 micrometer), 
which is invisible to the human eye. Thermographic cameras use unusual materials for the lenses with 
high transmissivity, allowing most infrared radiation to pass through its structure (MoviTHERM, 2018). 
Thermal cameras are set to measure the intensity to which body emits infrared light, which determines 
their inner temperature, for a specific value of emissivity. Usually most bodies emissivity are close to a 
value of 0,95. So it is common to find this value to read temperatures already set by default in many 
thermographic cameras, but many of them also allow to be adjusted to different ones according to the 
object to measure and task to perform. 
 
 
 
 
Figure 7 - Image captured by a thermographic camera and different visualization post-process. 
 
Often, monochrome images (Figure 7.a) are pseudo-colored (Figure 7.b and Figure 7.c). Pseudo-
coloring is a set of techniques that allows to map color relationship from a region of the electromagnetic 
spectrum to other of different colors. This enhances the visualization and improves the interpretation of 
thermal images.

2.5 Depth cameras and 3D sensing 
 
The term 3D sensing, refers to the capability to obtain spatial information, usually in the form of an 
image or 3D model, that a device obtains from the environment it is exposed to. 
2.5.1 Types of Depth Cameras 
 
There are several technologies that uses of different operation principles to be able to obtain and 
represent the spatial information (Why is Occlusion in Augmented Reality So Hard?, 2018), the main 
ones are: 
 
 
Figure 8 - Passive Stereo Camera (Julien Morat, 2007). 
 
 
-Passive Stereo Camera: is a type of camera with two or more lenses, which principle of operation 
emulates the human binocular vision. They use triangulation as operation principle: using epipolar 
geometry calculations (Appendix), it is possible to trace a straight line from each camera view image at 
the position of a certain point or object, and both lines will collide at a distance related to the object 
depth as seen from the camera. One disadvantage of stereo cameras is that close objects that are not 
perpendicular to the camera view, can generate very different perspectives in each lens, making difficult 
to match the same point in the two different views to calculate its depth information. 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
     Control System for Facial Laser Rejuvenation with Thermal Imaging and Robotic Arm Integration                   2019 | Technical University of Denmark 
21 
 
Figure 9 - Structured Light sensor working principle (Why is Occlusion in Augmented Reality So Hard?, 2018). 
 
-Structured Light Sensor: They account with an infrared projector and two stereo cameras (some models 
use only one). A known pattern is projected in the scene, non-planar objects will create a distortion in 
the projected pattern, that can be used to obtain the spatial information and reconstruct 3D models.  
 
 
Figure 10 - Depth Camera (Active Stereo Camera) IR pattern projection (left). Pseudo-colored image to represent depth 
data (right) (Yinda Zhang, 2018). 
 
 
-Depth Cameras: also called Active stereo Cameras, use the principles of the stereo cameras and 
structured light sensor. The key point of their principle is that the projected pattern, usually a high 
density pattern of dots, does not need to be known (multiple cameras can work on a same scene), and 
allows to easily recognize and match hundreds of points (features) between the images of the two 
cameras to recreate accurate 3D models made out of points, usually denominated as ‘Point Cloud’. The 
advantage respect the passive stereo cameras, is that it can operate in environments of low contrast and 
planar surfaces where no many features can be detected. 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
     Control System for Facial Laser Rejuvenation with Thermal Imaging and Robotic Arm Integration                   2019 | Technical University of Denmark 
22 
 
Figure 11 - Time of Flight sensor operation diagram (Van Nhan Nguyen, 2018). 
 
 
-Time of Flight Sensor: unless previous devices, Time of Flight sensors does not estimate depth by 
triangularization, they measure distance directly. They account with an emitter and receptor of infrared 
light, and rely on the measurement of infrared light time offset or phase shift coming back to the sensor 
to know the distance at which objects are emplaced. It is commonly used in autonomous cars (measuring 
time offset) and Virtual and Augmented Reality Devices (measuring phase shift). 
 
 
 
2.6 Real-time Engine 
 
Real-Time Engines are software development environments that allows to process in real time (under 
milliseconds, and sometimes microseconds) software scripts, graphic rendering, lights and physics 
calculations, collisions, user interactions, etc. All under one same environment. They started to be used 
in the game and film industry and rapidly expanded to others as the automotive, architecture, 
engineering and construction (Wikipedia, 2019).

3.3.1 Expanding the Options of the Embedded Camera 
 
For the validation of the embedded thermal camera, it was necessary to develop a custom API expanding 
the limited characteristic of the open source SDK provided by the manufacturer. A series of required 
characteristics were developed in order to make the operation of this camera useful for the type of task: 
 
- 
Customizable and run-time editable color scale:  
the default library has a limited amount of pseudo-coloring options (color-maps). Open source 
libraries like OpenCV also account with limited amount of colormaps. But more remarkable is, 
these colormaps are static, that means that if the thermal scale would be from 0 to 10, and the 
color map uses a gradient of three colors (black for 0, red for middle value 5, and yellow for 
10) but the principal interest of a given task is to differentiate temperatures between 7 and 9, 
that task becomes more difficult as points between those values are using a blend of red and 
yellow in different proportions. Would be more convenient for the task to use all tree colors, to 
improve visualization and interpretation. This the thermal color scale could place the black 
color at 6, red at 8 and yellow at 10. Due to this reason, a customizable colormap tool (number 
of colors, colors itself, and the location of them in the scale) were created to adjust color scales 
according to the task to realize.  
This became a very helpful tool that otherwise would limit a lot the usage of the embedded 
camera for the project, since some laser settings cause minimal temperature increase on the 
skin, and it is needed a very well-adjusted color scale for the working values to be able to 
perceive the skin-laser reaction. 
 
- 
Custom selection of thermal range of measurement:  
the default SDK will display output images using the whole range of the camera. This 
produces a loss of information due to the conversion from raw data (14 bits) to image 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
     Control System for Facial Laser Rejuvenation with Thermal Imaging and Robotic Arm Integration                   2019 | Technical University of Denmark 
27 
visualizable data (8 bits). And makes the output images not to use the whole color map 
efficiently. If a measurement has most of its data from 30 °C to 40 °C and we use a colormap 
to apply pseudo-coloring using the whole range of the camera, the colors of the scale from -10 
°C to 30 °C will not appear on the image, having the output images less differentiate colors 
will make it more difficult to interpret.  
For this reason, custom conversion and normalization of the raw thermal data to 8 bits using a 
custom thermal range were made. The output images can be displayed in a range of interest 
(set 30 °C in an extreme of the scale and 40 °C as the other extreme), and thus, use the whole 
color map efficiently. 
 
Research was made on the importance of conversion from raw thermal data (14 bits) to visualizable 
data (8bits), where rounding methods can disturb the distribution of temperatures considerably, which 
could be of crucial importance according to the task to perform and interpretation of the data.

4.4 Orbital visualization of the model and focus on areas of interest 
 
A script was developed to allow user to interact with the model view: rotate the model and zoom in and 
out to focus on areas of interest. 
 
The approach for this was not to rotate the point cloud, since it would be computing demanding, but 
overall, it would change the point cloud position and orientation.  
Rather, the approach passed by calculating a middle point for the point cloud by averaging each point 
position parameter’s: x, y and z, when the model was loaded into the control interface.  
 
This middle point was used as reference for the camera control script, and reading dragging control 
inputs of the mouse, the camera will be displaced in a spherical trajectory around the model. The radius 
from the center point to the orbital sphere, i.e. the zoom, will be adjusted by reading the mouse wheel 
inputs.

4.6 Generating laser trajectories 
 
Even though the point cloud structure designed for the control interface is prepared to have the necessary 
information to generate robot commands, not all the points should be taken into consideration for laser 
resurfacing purposes; According to the task to perform, the laser type, area of the skin, etc., laser shoots 
should have a determined separation between them. 
 
For this reason, when an area is selected in the control interface, and the user click the option “Send 
Laser”, an algorithm was designed to generate laser trajectories according to the separations between 
laser shoots. 
 
The selected approach was to first calculate the distribution grid of shooting points in the selected area, 
according to the separation distance between them. 
Then, trajectories will be generated considering the best way to connect all shooting points regarding 
robot malleability: tracing either vertical or horizontal lines, according the which of the two requires 
less change of lines. 
 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
     Control System for Facial Laser Rejuvenation with Thermal Imaging and Robotic Arm Integration                   2019 | Technical University of Denmark 
36 
 
Figure 17 - Laser trajectory algorithm generation (Breath-first 3D adaptation) for specific shots’ separation distance. 
 
In Figure 17 is represented the working principle of the algorithm: 
 
- 
Stating from any point in de model, this point is considered the first laser shooting point. 
- 
Then the algorithm spreads as a breadth-first algorithm, four temporal points are placed 
orthogonally to the first calculated point, considering its normal. 
- 
A 2D matrix is created to map the algorithm search: all the matrix is set to”0” which means that 
any point has been calculated yet. then, the initial point is added and marked as valid with value 
“1”. If a point is not valid to be calculated, it is marked as 2 (edges of models, overlapping 
points or empty space). 
- 
Each of these four orthogonal points pass a process of validation: 
1. The point region should have not been calculated before, value “0” on search matrix. 
2. Points must exist in the area underneath and its surroundings. 
3. If it does not accomplish the previous, it is marked as invalid (empty, overlapping or 
edge) with a value “2” on the search matrix. 
4. If accomplished, then an average of the depth of the existing points underneath is 
realized. This is due to existing depth noise on the generated face models. 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
     Control System for Facial Laser Rejuvenation with Thermal Imaging and Robotic Arm Integration                   2019 | Technical University of Denmark 
37 
i. The point is placed in the averaged depth and it is added to the search matrix 
as a valid point, marked as “1”. 
ii. The point is added to a queue to repeat the spread algorithm on it later on. 
5. Then the algorithm repeats dequeuing points from the valid points queue, to keep 
spreading until the queue becomes empty. 
 
It is worth to mention that at a later stage of the project, a feature to fill gaps due to 3D curving was 
added: if the area has a “1” on the search matrix but there is not a calculated point in the surrounding, 
then calculated the point, and add it to process queue. 
 
Laser generated paths, are converted to robot pose commands using the custom-made point class. After 
this, commands are sent via server to be parsed to UR-script, and the robot start producing the given 
trajectories. 
 
 
4.7 Creation of a tool to calibrate clouds to robot frame 
 
Having the control interface able to manipulate the robot arm (select regions and generate robot 
trajectories). It was concluded that the point cloud model should be represented from the robot base 
frame, i.e., if the coordinates of a point of the point cloud match the coordinates from the robot base 
frame, when selecting a point on the cloud, the robot arm will go exactly to where that point is on the 
reality. 
 
Making a calibration process to match point cloud to robot frame would be necessary. 
 
Developing a tool to do the calibration by people without technical knowledge on these regards, 
introduce a challenge to face. It was achieved in the following manner: 
-

Interface. 
- 
The same routine is asked two more times. 
- 
Having three points, it is possible to define the position and orientation of the point cloud to be 
placed correctly respect the robot frame. 
 
Every time the user has to pick a point in the control interface, the point reference has been recorded. 
Then every time the use has click confirm when the robot was on the real space location of the point, 
the robot position was recorded (the robot position is read by the computer via server). Then a re-
localization of the cloud is realized and all points custom parameters class update with the new positions

4.9 Control and triggering of the laser 
The locations of the laser shooting points are known thank to the laser path generation algorithm. The 
end effector position of the robot is also known at all moment (via server connection). With this in mind 
an algorithm was designed to trigger the laser in the following manner: 
- 
The robot position and orientation read were constantly updating the position of a 3D cylinder 
on the visualization interface.  
- 
A rigid body component provided by the physics engine of Unity was attached to the moving 
3D cylinder; This allows to use features as 3D mesh collision detection, which provides 
information about which other 3D objects enter in contact with the cylinder, by generating 
events. 
- 
A script was subscribed to these events and in knowledge of the laser path that was sent to the 
robot. When events are received (and these events are filtered to only laser points, not the rest 
of the points of the point cloud), the laser shoot point object is checked to verify that it is the 
correct position and a simple Arduino command allow to activate a relay that triggers the laser. 
 
 
 
4.10 Providing thermal feedback, Detection of laser shoots 
 
The embedded thermal camera was installed on the robot end effector, this allows to know the position 
of the laser and to create a region of interest (ROI) to narrow down the area to process when looking 
for laser-skin reaction when laser shoots occur.

When a laser shoot is triggered, a server publish is realized. The thermal camera has a script subscribe 
to the server waiting for this signal. The content of the message provides a number indicating which 
shoot number has just been triggered. 
 
Then the thermal camera processes a copy of its buffer in a separate thread and send back the result to 
another server channel. The message includes the shoot number for which the thermal information has 
just been processed, the average temperature and maximum temperature of the buffer, an array with the 
maximum temperature by frame, and a color corresponding to the maximum temperature. 
 
 
4.11 Registration of thermal acquisition on point cloud

processing the buffer with the results of a laser shoot. This script takes the measured thermal information 
received, and finds the matching shoot number with the shooting position. Then it creates a point with 
the required color to provide thermal feedback to the user. 
 
It is worth to mention that the scale with the thermal temperature range used to apply color to the points

5.6 Shooting laser and Receiving external thermal process - Timing 
Issue 
 
After integrating the system, some attention was needed regarding the detection of the laser shoots by 
the thermal camera.  
 
When the laser is triggered, the thermal feedback algorithm searched in the buffer of the camera for the 
laser shoot. Due to the complexity of the project, working with two computers and a server, the limited 
technical capacity of the camera (in perfect conditions frequency of 8.7 Hz), it was discovered that a 
fast analysis of the buffer right after laser the laser was triggered did not include the shooting moment 
in the pulled buffer.  
 
Time stamping the camera frames was considered. Since the frame rate was not constant all the time 
(opening the thermal visualizer slowed it down, saving thermal images to disk as well, etc.) even using 
parallel threading, it was thought that time stamping the frames (they did not come stamped) would 
make this process prone to error. 
 
Instead, a non-deterministic approach was achieved: when launching the in-control interface thermal 
calibration (or using the last one by default), the user can shoot to a test material and adjust the buffer 
size if the shoot does not appear on the displayed shoot record. The adjusted buffer size if not extended 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
     Control System for Facial Laser Rejuvenation with Thermal Imaging and Robotic Arm Integration                   2019 | Technical University of Denmark 
44 
just to past frames, but also to consider mode current frames, willing to receive the shot on the middle 
of the buffer. 
 
Simple as it, the algorithm would search for the region of interest maximum of each frame and send the 
total average and maximum, plus a per-frame average and maximum, to the control interface. Thermal 
feedback color would be provided with the maximum of each frame maximum, i.e., the hottest sport of 
the laser-skin reaction when laser is triggered. The maximum and average of the region of interest of 
each frame would allow to do data analysis and see how the laser reacts with the skin, how was the raise 
of temperature produced and how it decays over time.  
 
A test will be provided on the test section to assess the validity of this approach.  
 
5.7 End effector integration: laser electromagnetic interferences 
 
Finally, one challenge worth mentioning in the integration phase, was the fact that the lasers 
electromagnetic nature did cause interferences with the robot arm, making this one to perform a security 
stop. 
 
The reason for this is that the robot arm needs of accurate and constant readings of its inner’s sensors 
for odometry; Having an electric interference on it, caused at some points, a loss in a package of the 
robot arm measurements. And for security, this one stops. 
 
The bridge piece connecting the robot and the custom developed laser-cameras case, was isolated using 
a special coating for electric leaks. Even with that, the interferences still happened sometimes, especially 
during laser excitation on start up. For this reason, was recommended to start the laser controller before 
starting the robot arm.

Chapter 6: Future Work 
 
Security 
Currently the system is working with only three securities: an active one provided by the robot arm 
manufacturer, with collision control providing a maximum of 150 Newtons on collision (Nubiola, 
2014); Fixed values of the robot speed and acceleration, hardcoded on the parsing script; And the fact 
that the robot can only be commanded to existing points on the cloud. 
 
Further security measures should be studied. 
 
Face Tracking 
One of the main lacking characteristics of the system is face tracking. Luckily, the system has been 
developed with this option in mind and at latter stages of the project, motion captures were installed on 
the system. The existing algorithm to recalibrate cloud to robot frame could be adapted to work on real 
time with motion capture cameras by:  
- 
Three markers’ position (no need of orientation), 
- 
or a single point with orientation. 
 
Increasing Speed 
If the security allows it, increasing the speed of the system should be another goal in further developing 
the project. At the current status, automatic laser application is realized slower than direct human 
manipulation. 
 
Future Data analysis 
This is the first system acquiring thermal data on laser resurfacing treatments. Not many studies have 
been realized on how the thermal reaction that the laser produces on the skin could be related to the 
cases where these treatments has produced undesired injuries. It is expected that after enough data has 
been gathered, and cases of undesired injuries reported and targeted, future data analysis could show 
hints between the thermal feedback and skin injures, allowing to develop measures to avoid these. 
 
Laser-Settings Controller based on Thermal Feedback 
Options for adjusting the laser settings should be included and provided by the control interface. But 
specially, the automatic settings control should be realized when evidence of incorrect or dangerous 
settings are detected. More information from the professionals and by the thermal measurements, should 
be able to provide in the future with safety ranges of operation, so that measures can be automatically 
undertaken.

The results of the project seem to prove feasible the automatic realization of laser tasks on humans 
relying on the benefits of robots. Robots can be more accurate than humans, they can realize almost 
perfect trajectories and smooths movements, and they do not suffer the risks of tasks where humans 
expose themselves to fatigue effects and possibilities of injury. 
 
For this reason, it is thought that this project supposes a move in the right direction to the avoidance of 
injures specifically in the laser resurfacing treatments. Both for the person receiving the treatment and 
the professional realizing it.  
 
Furthermore, it is expected that the registration of thermal data of these characteristics, could be of 
interest for future researchers, for better understanding of the nature of these treatments, and to improve 
the existing lasers technology being used.
