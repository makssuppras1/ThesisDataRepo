Preface
This thesis has been prepared over six months at the Department of Environmental and Re­
source Engineering, at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), in partial fulfilment for the
degree Master of Science in Civil Engineering, MSc Eng. The project accounts for 30 ECTS and
was done from January 2nd to July 23rd.
This project focuses on recycling wind turbine blade (WTB) remains in concrete as fiber rein­
forcement. The primary objective of this thesis is to explore the implications of using wind turbine
blade fiber (WTBF) in the construction industry and its potential positive contributions to envi­
ronmental sustainability. All the experiments were conducted at DTU. This thesis was written in
LaTeX, with calculations performed using Excel and Wolfram Mathematica, and plots generated
in Origin Pro. Additionally, AI tools such as ChatGPT were utilized to improve writing flow and
readability.

agement. I would also like to extend my sincere gratitude to my co­supervisor, Tao Liu, PhD, for
his essential training on the use of lab facilities and for his support during a challenging times.

Abstract
The disposal of wind turbine blades (WTBs), predominantly made from fiberglass (GF), presents
substantial environmental challenges due to their composite structure. With a typical operational
lifespan of 20­25 years, finding effective disposal solutions is essential for environmental sus­
tainability. This study aims to explore strategies for recycling WTB remains into mortar as fiber
reinforcement, offering a sustainable approach to mitigate landfill waste while enhancing the
mechanical properties of mortar.
Due to poor workability in the initial sample set, incorporating plasticizer emerged as a viable so­
lution to improve workability and potentially enhance the mechanical performance of the mortar.
As a result, two distinct mix designs were prepared: one with the inclusion of a plasticizer and
one without. The data collection methodology involved various lab techniques, including X­ray
diffraction (XRD) and X­ray fluorescence (XRF) analyses for material characterization, scanning
electron microscopy (SEM) for morphology studies, calorimetry to monitor the hydration pro­
cess. Mechanical properties were assessed through compressive and flexural strength tests.
Additionally, the flow table test was used to measure mortar flow and workability.
Samples with plasticizer consistently showed better performance compared to samples without
it. Among all formulations tested, samples with 0.5 vol% WTBF (W0.5) with plasticizer exhibited
optimal flow characteristics. The inclusion of WTBF generally reduced compressive strength,
especially at higher fiber contents, W0.5 samples with plasticizer showed minimal strength re­
duction and performed nearly as well as the reference sample. Additionally, samples containing
1 vol% WTBF (W1), both with and without plasticizer, demonstrated the greatest increases in
flexural strength. However, higher fiber content (>1 vol%) negatively impacted flexural perfor­
mance.
Recycling wind turbine blades (WTBs) into mortar presents a sustainable solution to reduce land­
fill waste and enhance the mechanical properties of mortar, especially when using an optimal
fiber content. This initiative has the potential for practical implementation in the civil engineer­
ing sector, presenting a substantial opportunity to contribute to environmental conservation and
promote the adoption of sustainable infrastructure solutions.

Introduction
The rapid increase in wind turbine (WT) production, driven by the wind industry’s positive envi­
ronmental impact and population growth, has led to an increase in the use of glass fiber (GF), the
primary component of wind turbine blades (WTBs). This escalation raises concerns about long­
term environmental repercussion [1]. As of 2023, the EU’s total installed wind power capacity
reached 221 GW, with a target of 425 GW by 2030 [2, 3]
Currently, around 85­90% of a dismantled wind turbine is recycled, including the towers, foun­
dations, generators, and gearboxes [4]. However, due to the composite nature of the blades,
segregating and reprocessing each component is challenging and time­consuming. This com­
plexity often leads companies to favor landfilling as the most economical waste management
technique, resulting in significant portions of GF waste ending up in landfills despite high tax
rates and negative environmental impacts [5]. In 2020, approximately 34,000 wind turbines in
Europe were 15 years or older, representing 36 GW of capacity, which should now be in the
process of decommissioning, given their 20­25 year lifespan [4].
In 2021, the European wind industry proposed a ban on WTB landfilling and committed to
reusing, recycling, or recovering 100% of decommissioned blades. Wind turbine manufacturers
have set targets for fully recyclable blades and developed blade recycling solutions [6]. There
are several approaches to handling WTBs waste, but the most cost­effective method involves
mechanical recycling techniques such as shredding and grinding. The waste resulting from me­
chanical recycling is commonly used as fillers, aggregates, or reinforcements in construction
materials[7, 8].
The construction sector is also experiencing a significant growth globally, driven by population
expansion [9]. While this industry plays a crucial role in societal development and economic
prosperity, it also contributes substantially to environmental degradation, accounting for approx­
imately 5­8% of the world’s CO2 emissions [10]. The main source of pollution originates from
the cement manufacturing process, which is a fundamental element of concrete production [10].
Cement production accounts for about 2­3% of global primary energy consumption, resulting in
an estimated 2.8 billion tonnes of CO2 emissions annually [10]. Therefore, the use of WTBs in
the concrete industry after decommissioning emerges as a feasible strategy for long­term waste
management, given their capacity to enhance construction materials. This approach not only
potentially enhances the mechanical properties of concrete but also contributes to reducing WTB
waste in landfills, promoting environmental sustainability. [4, 6].
For this study, the focus will shift from concrete to cement mortar. This change enables a more
controlled examination of the material properties and interactions, providing clearer insights into
the benefits and feasibility of incorporating WTBs into cement­based materials.
Because incorporating wind turbine blade fibers (WTBF) into cement mortar is a new and inno­
vative technique worldwide, it is crucial to provide companies with information on the practical
application of WTBF in mortar to effectively reduce WTB waste from landfilling. The aim of this
study is to identify potential strategies for securely recycling WTB remains into mortar as fiber
reinforcement to reduce WTB waste. This goal leads to the following objectives:
• To investigate the implications of using WTBF in cement mortar to minimize landfill waste.
• To evaluate the mechanical properties of WTBF­reinforced mortar.

Theory
This section provides a theoretical framework and a comprehensive literature review on the
innovative approach of WTB waste in cement mortar.
2.1
Cement paste and mortar
The main materials used to make cement paste and mortar are Portland cement (PC) and sand.
PC is the most essential component due to its binding properties. It is a fine powder that, when
mixed with water, forms a hard mass as a result of hydration [11]. Sand, a natural fine aggregate,
is widely utilized in construction, particularly in mortar [12]. River sand has been a fundamental
resource in the construction sector for building roads, buildings, bridges, and other infrastructure
projects [13].
2.1.1
Cement paste
The combination of PC and water leads to the formation of cement paste [14]. The cement paste
typically has 3 main functions which are binding, coating and lubricating [15].
2.1.2
Mortar
In the construction industry, mortar is one of the more versatile and useful building materials. It is
mainly used to bind bricks, tiles, and to render surfaces [11]. Mortar can be made by combining
water, PC, and fine aggregate (sand). Cement mortar possesses various physical properties,
including density, porosity, and strength. Additionally, it has thermal properties like shrinkage
and durability properties such as fire resistance. [16].
2.2
Fiber reinforcement in mortar
Fibre­reinforced mortar is characterized by incorporating short, discrete, and randomly distributed
fibres into its composition to help maintain structural integrity. In the civil engineering industry,
these reinforced mortars can be used for both structural and non­structural purposes [17].
GF, or steel fibers, are typically added to mortar to increase resistance to plastic shrinkage
and cracking [18]. It is important to note that these fibers are not intended to be the primary
reinforcing elements. When they are added during mortar production, they offer good resistance
to impact and vibration [17]. However, fiber­reinforced mortar can also reduce workability due to
its water absorption properties [19, 18]. Additionally, some investigations indicate that GF have
a brittle behavior and that an excess can lead to brittle failure [20, 21]. Therefore, it is crucial to
examine the optimal amount of fibers to minimally reduce mechanical properties while improving
workability. Figure 2.3 depicts the distribution of fibers within the specimen.

2.3.3.1
S­Glass
S­Glass, or Strength Glass (magnesium aluminasilicate glass), contains higher levels of ox­
ides and lower silica content. It provides 40% higher tensile and flexural strengths and 10­20%

Carbon Fiber
Carbon fiber is another widely used material in the wind industry due to its superior strength­to­
weight ratio compared to fiberglass. This enables the production of longer and thinner WTBs.
However, carbon fiber is costly and offers less resistance to compressive strength [29, 28].
2.3.3.3
Natural fibers
Natural fibers such as coconut, flax, hemp, and jute are eco­friendly alternatives available at a
lower cost in the market. However, their quality and high water absorption capacity can limit
their reliability [31].

Microstructure and morphology of the materials
SEM is a powerful technique used to analyze the microstructure of cementitious materials [40].
It utilizes a focused beam of high­energy electrons to produce multiple signals at the surface of
the sample. The signals resulting from electron­sample interactions provide information about

2.4.3.1
Interfacial transition zone (ITZ)
The ITZ is the region between the components of a composite material where they interact and
form a bond. It plays a critical role in the strength, durability, and workability of mortar [42]. Due
to its higher porosity, the ITZ between cement paste and aggregates in cementitious composites
is often seen as a weak link in the microstructure [43].
Figure 2.9 presents SEM images of GF reinforced mortar (RC) with a water/binder (w/b) ratio of
0.35. The analysis shows that the aggregates generally exhibit a smooth texture, whereas the
cement matrix appears rough. Furthermore, the cement matrix, glass fiber (GF), and aggregate
can establish a tight bond in the Interfacial Transition Zone (ITZ). The higher porosity of the ITZ
remains a potential weak point. However, the incorporation of GF can enhance the mechanical
properties of the mortar, especially when strong bonding between these components is achieved.

Heat flow analysis
Calorimetry, a fundamental technique for quantifying heat and heat production rate, plays a piv­
otal role in the construction industry, particularly in the study of cement hydration. When water
is added to cement it becomes a firm, hardened mass due to a chemical reaction between water
and cement, which generates a significant amount of heat — a phenomenon commonly known
as the hydration process [45, 46].
The primary objective of the calorimeter is to examine the kinetics of cement hydration reactions
[40], which essentially involves the study of the rates of chemical reactions, including factors in­
fluencing the rates and explanations of the rates concerning the reaction mechanisms of chemi­
cal processes [47] . This chemical process can influence various properties of mortar, including
workability, setting time, strength gain, and thermal cracking resistance [49].

(aluminate hydrate). The release of the energy from these reactions causes the initial
peak. This period can last about 15 min [50, 51, 52].
2. The dormant period (2nd): it refers to the induction or sleeping period, characterized by

The maximum load a specimen can withstand under compression is determined by the compres­

2.4.7
Measurement of flow and workability of mortar
Concrete workability, a critical parameter, describes the ease of manipulating, pouring, and com­
pacting the concrete mix with minimal effort. This metric may significantly influence the strength
and durability of concrete [58]. Workability can be assessed using the flow table test, a method
that evaluates the consistency and fluidity of the concrete mixture. In this test, the concrete mix
is placed on the flow table, and its flow across the surface is measured [66, 61], this parameter
is known as the flow of mortar. The higher the flow, the easier it is to pour and compact the
concrete mixture.
Figure 2.21, from the paper titled “Assessment of the Effect of Fiber Percentage in Glass Fiber
Reinforced Slag­Based Geopolymer,” illustrates how the incorporation of GF affects the work­
ability of the mix. The plot clearly shows that as the percentage of GF increases, the flow of
the mix decreases accordingly. At 0% GF, the flow measurement is approximately 158 mm,
whereas with 1% GF added, it decreases to around 50 mm. Since flow is directly proportional
to workability, a lower flow indicates that the mix becomes more difficult to mix and compact.
This theory finds support in another study on GF Reinforced Concrete (RC), indicating that higher
GF dosage inversely affects workability by reducing it significantly [61]. However, better worka­
bility can be achieved when plasticizer is added to the mix, as plasticizer enhances the workability
of mortar by increasing slump values, thereby facilitating better flow of the mortar [67].

2.4.7.1
Super Plasticizer (SP) in cement paste and mortar
A plasticizer is a substance used in concrete mixes to improve workability and compactness.
Since they do not incorporate air, super plasticizers can be used at high dosage rates without
affecting strength. The main objective of SP is to disperse cement particles very efficiently. By
adding SP, the slump increases, leading to better workability, strength, and durability [67].
The findings presented by Mehran Khan and Majid Ali [69] in their paper suggest that incorpo­
rating 1% of plasticizer by mass of binder can enhance the mechanical properties of medium­
strength coconut fiber reinforced concrete (MSCFRC). This recommendation is important for
civil engineering applications where both strength and workability are crucial factors.

4.3.5.2
Hydration stoppage
The morphology of samples can change over time, especially when they are stored for extended
periods (1 month or longer). To preserve the microstructure and prevent alteration of the hy­
drates, it is crucial to employ a hydration stoppage method that removes water from the pores
without disturbing the water within the hydration products. This process can be achieved using
either propanol or acetone [40].
In this study, propanol (see Figure 4.40 (a)) was used for the Ref and W2 samples without plas­
ticizer, as SEM analysis was scheduled for more than a month later. After cutting the samples,
they were soaked in propanol for 24 hours, as depicted in Figure 4.40 (b). Subsequently, they
were dried in an oven at 23oC for another 24 hours and then stored until the test day. This
drying process was unnecessary for samples with plasticizer (Ref and W2), as SEM analysis
was conducted 2 days after mechanical testing.

6.1
Cement Paste
6.1.1
Heat flow analysis without plasticizer
In subsection 5.2.1, for samples without plasticizer, it was found that the hydration process of all
the samples includes the five periods—initial, dormant, acceleration, deceleration, and stable—
consistent with previous studies. The peak of heat of hydration for all samples (Ref, W0.5, W1,
and W2) occurs at 12 hours. The stages of the hydration process for these samples follow a
similar timeline to each other. The heat of hydration curve shows a slight deviation compared
to previous studies, particularly in the dormant period, which is supposed to be shorter (about 1
hour) [55]. This shorter dormant period also affects the initial and final setting times. However,
the findings from this study are not entirely incorrect, as the dormant period for PC can vary and
last up to 4 hours [52]; in this study, the dormant period is approximately 4.5 hours, which is
within acceptable limits.
The decrease in heat flow with the addition of WTBF may be due to the ability of fibers to absorb
water [18], thereby reducing the water available for cement hydration. To observe an increase in
cement hydration, adjustments such as increasing the water­cement ratio and modifying curing
temperature conditions would be necessary [77], which were not implemented in this experiment.
6.1.2
Heat flow analysis comparison
The presented results in subsection 5.2.3 show a comparison between calorimeter analyses with
and without plasticizer. In terms of normalized heat flow, samples without plasticizer exhibit two
peaks within the acceleration period (III). The first peak corresponds to sulfate depletion at 11
hours, and the second peak corresponds to the silicate reaction at 12 hours. In contrast, sam­
ples with plasticizer are delayed by approximately 8 hours compared to those without plasticizer,
showing only one peak which corresponds to the silicate reaction at 19.5 hours. Additionally,
all these samples demonstrate lower heat flow compared to samples without plasticizer. These
findings contrast somewhat with the literature, where the sulfate depletion peak occurred during
the deceleration period for samples without plasticizer [71]. In this study, however, both peaks
took place during the acceleration period.
Interestingly, the findings of this study closely align with samples containing plasticizer from the
same study (aforementioned), as there is only one main peak which is the silicate reaction cor­
responding to the heat of hydration, possibly caused by the retarded silicate reaction. The high
heat evolution at the beginning suggests that sulfate depletion likely occurred during the initial
period, with only the silicate reaction being delayed [71]. Furthermore, the hydration peaks in
samples with plasticizer are delayed as expected from previous paper, which can be attributed
to the plasticizer’s characteristic of extending both initial and final setting times [72].
When comparing the normalized cumulative heat, samples containing plasticizer consistently
show lower heat release at 160 hours compared to those without plasticizer. For instance, Ref
decreases from about 260 J/g to 242 J/g, W0.5 from 258 J/g to 232 J/g, W1 from 264 J/g to 222
J/g, and W2 from 253 J/g to 221 J/g. This finding aligns with the observations of Harichane et
al. [72], who noted that adding plasticizer to mortar reduces final heat production as its dosage
increases. This reduction is attributed to superplasticizers delaying the initial contact between

6.2
Cement Mortar
6.2.1
Flow of mortar
The presented data in subsection 5.3.1 indicates a consistent trend: as the volume fraction of
WTBF increases in both mix designs (with and without plasticizer), the flow of mortar generally
decreases, implying a reduction in workability. W0.5 with plasticizer is the best sample com­
pared to Ref because it achieves a high mortar flow, almost as good as Ref (73% vs 80%). It
also shows a much bigger increase in flow (+180% vs +135%) comparing to samples without
plasticizer. This means W0.5 improves workability while keeping flow similar to Ref, making it
the best choice.
This findings aligns with prior studies and supports the notion that fiber dosage inversely affects
workability, with higher fiber quantities leading to poorer workability [61]. This effect may be
attributed to the shape and surface texture of WTBF particles, which generate internal friction
within the mortar mixture, necessitating additional cement paste to mitigate this friction [78]. Fur­
thermore, the hydrophilic nature of the fibers contributes to water absorption [18], resulting in
reduced water availability within the mixture particles as the percentage of WTBF increases.
On the other hand, plasticizer enhances the workability of mortar by increasing slump values,
thereby facilitating better flow of mortar [67]. This improved flow leads to higher compactness
and strength in the resulting mortar. When plasticizer was added, it was observed that the mortar
mix became easier to handle, compact, and mold compared to samples without plasticizer.
6.2.2
Correlation between flow of mortar and compressive strength
The provided data in subsection 5.3.2 explains how adding plasticizer enhances both mortar flow
and compressive strength at 28 days with respect to samples without plasticizer. The ”Strength
Gain” values in (Table 5.8) clearly indicate that plasticizer leads to stronger mortar across all
samples and that the workability is also enhanced compared to those without it. W0.5 without
plasticizer demonstrates an 11% decrease in strength (from 46 MPa to 41 MPa) compared to the
Ref sample. In contrast, W0.5 and W1 with plasticizer show minimal decreases of only 2% (from
65 MPa to 64 MPa) and 3% (from 65 MPa to 63 MPa), respectively, in compressive strength
relative to the Ref sample. These results highlight the effectiveness of W0.5 with and without
plasticizer and W1 with plasticizer formulations in maintaining strong performance, particularly
emphasizing the benefits of including plasticizer.
These findings alight with the literature review which states that super plasticizer improve worka­
bility which leads to better compactness and pores reduction which, consequently result in higher
strength. The improvement in compressive strength with plasticizer can be attributed to its abil­
ity to facilitate the pouring of mortar mixes with minimal compaction [67]. This enables easier
molding compared to mixes without plasticizer, resulting in greater uniformity within the mix and
ultimately reducing the percentage of pores in the sample [64], which might otherwise adversely
affect the compressive resistance. In addition, it is also important to recognize that higher doses
of glass fiber reduce workability, resulting in reduced mechanical performance of the mortar [61].

7
Conclusion
This chapter summarizes the key research findings in relation to the research aims and ques­
tions, as well as their value and contribution. The primary aim of this thesis was to investigate
the potential of recycling wind turbine blade (WTB) remains as fiber reinforcement in mortar.
The key points discussed include heat flow evolution, flow of mortar, and both compressive and
flexural strength. Additionally, this chapter will propose opportunities for future research to fur­
ther explore and optimize the use of WTB fibers in the construction industry.
The findings from this study offer valuable insights into the effects of incorporating WTBF and
plasticizer on the properties of cement paste and mortar. The investigation into heat flow anal­
ysis reveal that, for samples without plasticizer, the peak of heat hydration occurs at around
12 hours, with a dormant period of approximately 4.5 hours. Although this dormant period is
slightly longer than expected from previous studies, it remains within acceptable limits (4 hours).
Moreover, incorporating WTBF seem to decrease the heat of hydration peaks due to its water
absorption properties, which reduces available water during hydration.
The incorporation of plasticizer delays hydration peaks by approximately 8 hours and decreases
overall heat flow, which is consistent with prior research. This delay is attributed to the capability
of plasticizer to slow down the initial interaction between cement and water, thus prolonging the
setting times and lowering the total heat generated during hydration.
In terms of the flow of mortar, this study reveals that as the WTBF content increased, the work­
ability of the mortar generally decreased. However, the introduction of plasticizer notably en­
hanced both workability and strength, making the mortar mix easier to handle and mold. Specif­
ically, samples incorporating 0.5 vol% WTBF (W0.5) with plasticizer showed the best flow among
all tested formulations.
The compressive strength tests show that WTBF reduces the compressive capacity of mor­
tar, particularly at higher fiber contents. However, samples with plasticizer consistently exhibit
higher compressive strengths compared to those without, highlighting the role of plasticizer in
improving compactness and reducing porosity—key factors that directly influence compressive
strength. Notably, W0.5 samples with plasticizer show minimal strength reduction and optimal
performance, nearly matching the reference sample in compressive strength while exhibiting
lower total heat release.
Flexural strength tests reveal that WTBF enhances the flexural capacity of mortar up to a spe­
cific fiber content threshold. Both samples with and without plasticizer containing 1 vol% WTBF
(W1) exhibited significant increases in flexural strength. W1 without plasticizer showed a 30%
strength gain compared to W1 with plasticizer. However, excessive fiber content (exceeding
1 vol%) negatively impacted flexural performance due to poor fiber distribution and orientation
within the mix.
Overall, incorporating WTBF in mortar offers a promising method for WTBS, with the potential
to improve specific mechanical properties when optimized correctly.
