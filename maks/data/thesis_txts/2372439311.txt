Abstract 
The current study investigated the potential use of performic acid (PFA, CH2O3) as a disinfectant in 
recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS). The degradation rate of PFA, its impact on nitrification 
performance in biofilters, and its disinfection potential were investigated.  
The impact of temperature on the decay rate of PFA was examined in two water matrices. A positive 
correlation between temperature and degradation rate was found. Water from three different RAS systems 
were exposed to a concentration of 2 mg/L PFA twice to establish whether repeated exposure to PFA 
impacts the decay rate of PFA in RAS system water. The half-life of PFA in water previously treated with PFA 
increased by 32 - 128%.  
Identical batch reactors were used to examine the effect of PFA concentration (1, 2, 5 and 10 mg/L) on total 
ammonia nitrogen (TAN) and nitrite-nitrogen (nitrite-N) removal. After exposure to PFA, the rate of TAN 
and nitrite-N removal in the reactors was determined and compared to unexposed reactors. The rate of 
TAN and nitrite-N removal was determined both at a low substrate concentration (first order kinetics) and 
at a high substrate concentration (zero order kinetics). There was a negative correlation between the TAN 
removal rate and the concentration of PFA the bioelements were exposed to. The rate of nitrite-N was only 
inhibited at PFA concentrations of 5 and 10 mg/L. The impact of PFA on the TAN and nitrite-N removal rates 
was compared to that of another peracid, peracetic acid (PAA, C2H4O3). There was no difference between 
the two. The required concentration and contact time of PFA for achieving inhibition of the bacteria on 
active bioelements was examined by exposing the bioelements to two different concentrations of PFA (10 
and 100 mg/L). Bacterial activity was measured at three different time intervals (20 min, 2 h and 22 h) using 
hydrogen peroxide. Exposure to 100 mg/L PFA for 22 hours gave a 99% reduction in bacterial activity.  
In conclusion, PFA has promising properties for use as a disinfectant in aquaculture systems, but the long 
term effects on nitrification and fish welfare should be established before implementation.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Need for chemical disinfectants 
In aquaculture, there is an increasing need for water quality control and treatment with chemical 
disinfectants (Buchmann & Bresciani 1997; Jørgensen et al. 2009). Disinfectants are needed in egg 
disinfection, control of bacterial and fungal growth and general control of water quality as well as system 
sanitation. Microorganisms (i.e. parasites, bacteria, viruses and fungi) may be present in the inlet water and 
can cause disease outbreaks and increased mortality when entering a recirculating aquaculture system 
(RAS) (Jørgensen et al. 2009; Lekang 2008). Disinfection is needed when fish are removed from the system 
(harvested) or as new fish or eggs enter the system. This is especially important when introducing fragile 
juvenile fish (Waldrop et al. 2009). Aquaculture facilities that struggle with specific pathogens may require 
full sterilization of the system.   
1.2 Requirements for use of disinfectants  
Choosing a suitable disinfectant can be difficult, as the chemical should not compromise fish welfare, 
worker, food safety or environmental safety. In RAS, it is especially important that the chemical 
disinfectants do not impede the action of the nitrifying bacteria in the biofilters used to remove ammonia, 
as this can lead to toxic concentrations of ammonia and nitrite in the system (Bower & Turner 1982; Collins 
et al. 1975; Collins et al. 1976; Noble & Summerfelt 1996). Although the biofilms are robust and may 
survive treatment and disinfection procedures, potential new disinfectants should be tested on biofilters in 
small scale to determine safe dosages. Limiting the impact on the environment is also important and new 
chemicals should be examined in terms of their environmental effects and their rate of degradation 
(Jokumsen & Svendsen 2010).  
1.3 Chemical disinfectants 
Several chemical disinfectants have been used in RAS, such as; formalin, Chloramine-T, sodium chloride, 
ozone and hydrogen peroxide (HP, H2O2) (Boyd & Massaut 1999; Rintamäki-Kinnunen et al. 2005). Formalin 
is frequently used as the main disinfectant for such systems and its use has increased over time (Jokumsen 
& Svendsen 2010). There are unwanted side effects related to the amount of formalin released into the 
environment and its impact on human health and work environments (Masters 2004; Pedersen et al. 2007). 
Hydrogen peroxide is considered to be a sustainable alternative as it degrades rapidly compared to 
formalin and Chloramine-T (Bruun et al. 2007). Although ozone has been proven effective in controlling the 
bacterial load in RAS, there are challenges in the use of these in Danish outdoor aquaculture operations 
==PAGE:10==
 
10 
(Summerfelt et al. 2009). Ozone is a strong oxidizing agent and in some cases of overdosing ozone might 
reach culture tanks and have adverse effects on the cultured species (Bullock et al. 1997) since it is 
considered toxic to freshwater organisms at concentrations exceeding 0.01 mg/L (Gonçalves & Gagnon 
2011). Ozonation is suspected to form several harmful by-products, such as hypobromous acid in saltwater 
(Grguric et al. 1994; Wert et al. 2008) and carcinogenic aldehydes and carboxylic acids (Matsuda et al. 
1992). Ozone comes with high investment and maintenance costs and is not suitable for all disinfection 
purposes.  
Recent studies have been conducted on the use of peracetic acid (PAA, C2H4O3) as a sanitizer in aquaculture 
and results are promising, although PAA is not expected to become a universal disinfectant (Liu et al. 
2017b; Pedersen et al. 2013). PAA is an organic peracid that disinfects through the formation of highly 
oxidative radicals (Luukkonen & Pehkonen 2017). However, experiments on wastewater have shown that it 
degrades more slowly and requires more contact time for disinfection than performic acid (PFA, CH2O3) 
(Chhetri et al. 2014). 
1.4 Performic acid  
Performic acid (PFA) is an organic peroxide that, along with peracetic acid, has been tested and used in 
wastewater treatment and can potentially be used in aquaculture. PFA is a strong oxidizing agent and wide-
spectrum disinfectant that can inactivate viruses, bacteria, bacterial spores, mycobacteria and microscopic 
fungi (Gehr et al. 2009). In addition to wastewater treatment, PFA has used as a disinfectant in the medical 
field and food industry (Gehr et al. 2009), to control microbial growth in paper mill process water (Atkinson 
et al. 2014) and to control fouling on reverse osmosis membranes (Vance et al. 2013). Several studies found 
that for inactivating bacterial indicators in secondary effluents, doses of 0.4-2 mg/L of PFA for 5 - 10 min 
were required, while higher doses were needed to inactivate Clostridium and Giardia (Karpova et al. 2013; 
Ragazzo et al. 2013). Luukkonen et al. (2015) found that the action of PFA is almost instantaneous with a 
2.9 log reduction in e-coli (33 cfu/100 mL) after only 1 min contact time at a concentration of 1.5 mg/L. In 
studies carried out on combined sewer overflow, Chhetri et al. (2014) found that PFA requires significantly 
shorter contact time than PAA at the same dosage (2 mg/L) with 20 and 360 min required for each. For 
primary effluent wastewater, PFA was also found to be more efficient than PAA (Gehr et al. 2009). PFA has 
a higher efficiency than PAA and hypochlorite in full scale experiments, with the effects of PFA against fecal 
Enterococci suggesting that it will also perform better than PAA and hypochlorite against other pathogens 
(Ragazzo et al. 2013). Luukkonen and Pehkonen (2017) concluded that PFA requires lower doses (0.4-2 
mg/L) and contact time than PAA to achieve the same results in treatment of primary, secondary and 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
tertiary municipal wastewater effluents. Due to the promising results of low-doses (≤ 2 mg/L) in 
wastewater treatment, PFA should be considered for use in aquaculture and RAS.  
1.5 How to prepare PFA 
PFA cannot be purchased as a pure active product. The most common way of preparing PFA is by mixing 
formic acid and hydrogen peroxide in acidic conditions as shown below.

↔ 𝐻𝐶𝑂𝑂𝑂𝐻+ 𝐻2𝑂 
The resulting mixture contains PFA, formic acid and hydrogen peroxide. Filippis et al. (2008) found that PFA 
is quite unstable and easily decomposes into CO2 and H2O, while no decomposition of hydrogen peroxide 
was detected when studying the kinetics of PFA formation. While hydrogen peroxide oxidation is prone to 
catalase enzyme-induced decomposition, this is not the case for peracid-based oxidation (Rothbart et al. 
2012). Because of the unstable nature of PFA it should be synthesized in situ using a continuous system for 
use in an industrial scale (Leveneur et al. 2012). 
1.6 PFA compared to PAA 
Disinfection tests have shown PFA is a stronger disinfectant than PAA (Gehr et al. 2009; Lazarova et al. 
1998; Luukkonen et al. 2015). The rate of PFA degradation is faster than that of PAA (Luukkonen & 
Pehkonen 2017), which suggests that PFA might be preferable to use in RAS since lower concentrations will 
reach the biofilters and surrounding natural environments. PFA is cheaper than PAA in large scale facilities, 
however, it comes with certain investment costs since it should be synthesized in situ using a continuous 
system for use in an industrial scale (Leveneur et al. 2012). A system based on the safety requirements is 
available commercially (Desinfix, Kemira, Finland), which consists of the reagents storages for hydrogen 
peroxide (35–50%) and formic acid (70–90%), and diaphragm pumps feeding reagents to a tubular reactor 
submerged in a thermostatic bath (Ragazzo et al. 2013). The relatively high investment costs can be 
avoided by leasing the required equipment from a supplier, but the need for a constant disinfectant supply 
should be well established prior to its installation. In terms of the operational costs of disinfection, PFA was 
found to be cheaper than PAA with estimated costs of 0.0114 € for PFA and 0.0261 € for PAA per m3 of 
water treatment (once the equipment was installed) by Luukkonen et al. (2015). 
1.7 Toxicity of PFA 
The PFA mixture contains formic acid, performic acid, water and hydrogen peroxide and does not generate 
toxic by-products (Chhetri et al. 2014; Liberti & Notarnicola 1999). Formic acid is readily biodegradable and 
is not toxic to aquatic fauna at the dosages applied (Gehr et al. 2009). As of yet,  there is no consistent 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
information regarding the toxicity of disinfected effluents due to the few studies performed on the 
ecotoxicity of PFA (Meinertz et al. 2008; Svecevičius et al. 2005). However, Chhetri et al. (2016) found PFA  
to be very toxic to aquatic organisms at concentrations below 2 mg/L, in an experiment carried out on 
green microalgae (Pseudokirchneriella subcapitata). Ragazzo et al. (2017) found no evidence of a strong 
oxidative effect on organic matter from PFA, something that could suggest that it has a low tendency to 
form toxic by-products 
1.8 Aims and hypotheses 
The purpose of this study was to determine the selected characteristics of PFA for application in 
aquaculture. This included determining the rate of PFA degradation in water from RAS, the impact of PFA 
on nitrifying bacteria in the biofilter and the potential for using PFA to fully disinfect a RAS. The effect PFA 
has on fish was also assessed in a small trial. 
Aim 1: Determine the effect of temperature and successive exposure on the degradation rate of PFA in 
water from RAS. 
Hypothesis 1: The degradation rate of PFA in water will be positively correlated with the temperature, and 
the degradation rate of PFA will be reduced in a second exposure of PFA in RAS water.  
 
Aim 2: Determine the impact of PFA on the nitrification performance of the bacteria on bioelements, and 
compare it to that of PAA.  
Hypothesis 2: The nitrification rates will be negatively correlated with the exposure concentration of PFA. 
The impact of PFA on nitrification will be similar or slightly stronger than that of PAA.  
 
Aim 3: Evaluate the potential for using PFA to fully disinfect a RAS by determining the relative inhibition in 
bacterial activity on bioelements exposed to PFA. 
Hypothesis 3: The degree of inhibition of bacterial activity will be positively correlated with PFA exposure 
concentration and time.

2 Material and methods 
The degradation of performic acid (PFA) was investigated in aquaculture water samples of different origins 
and tested under different conditions.  PFA degradation and effects on nitrifying bacteria was further 
studied by a series of controlled experiments with colonized biofilter elements from a RAS.  
2.1 Origin of water samples and bioelements 
In the experiments, water from three different RAS systems were used, RAS A, B and C. Bioelements were 
only collected from RAS A. Different water sources were used to get a wider understanding of the 
degradation of PFA in aquaculture water. Milli-Q water was used as control. 
RAS A: Water samples and colonized bioelements tested were obtained from an experimental RAS at DTU 
Aqua Hirtshals, previously described by Pedersen et al. (2015), with stable operation for more than four 
months. The 8.5 m3 RAS containing 100-150 kg rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) was fed 1000 g of feed 
per day with a constant make-up water exchange at 1 m3 per day ensuring stability at temperature 14 ± 1 
°C. The feed loading was equivalent to 1 kg feed/m3 of make-up water. The four separate identical upflow 
biofilters (0.40 m3) contained 0.20 m3 carrier media (RK BioElements®, RK Plast, Denmark; Surface specific 
area = 750 m2/m3, density = 1 g/cm3) each receiving a water flow of 1.9 m3/h. The moving bed biofilters 
were supplied with 30 L/min of air. NaHCO3 was used to keep pH between 7.6 - 7.8 and alkalinity > 1.8 
meq/L. COD measurements were taken prior to each experiment in which water from RAS A was used 
(Table 1).

2.2 Batch scale experimental set up 
Identical batch reactors were used to examine the effect of PFA on TAN and nitrite-N removal in a 
standardized way, as described previously by Kinyage and Pedersen (2016). As shown in Figure 2, each 
reactor was connected to a RESUN Air Pump (RESUN®, LP40, China) using a tube with a control valve, a 
check valve and an air-tube in the bottom of the reactor. Water was circulated through the reactor by a 300 
L/h EHEIM universal pump (EHEIM 1046, Germany). The reactors were installed in a temperature 
controlled room to provide stable water temperature of 14°C, similar to that in RAS A, during the 
experimental trials. The water flow was adjusted to 60 L/h and the air flow was adjusted to 0.5 L/min.

2.3 Experimental protocols 
Effect of temperature on PFA degradation 
The decay rate of PFA at different temperatures in two water matrices was examined. 
The degradation rate of PFA was tested in Milli-Q water at 0, 5, 10 and 20°C. It was also tested in water 
from RAS A at 5, 10, 15 and 20 °C.  The temperatures were stabilized using water baths.  A 50 mL of sample 
water was placed in a Sarstedt tubes (conical base) and spiked to a concentration of 2 mg/L PFA. The 
concentration of PFA in the sample water was measured at fixed time intervals for 2 h with the RAS water 
samples and 4 h with the Milli-Q water. This ensured at least four time points for each sample. All 
experiments and measurements were carried out in duplicate.  
 
Effect of double exposure on PFA degradation in RAS water 
Water from different RAS systems (A, B, C) was exposed to a concentration of 2 mg/L PFA two consecutive 
times to establish whether a repeated exposure to PFA impacts the decay rate of PFA in system water. 
Water samples from RAS A, B and C (150 mL) were placed in plastic containers (250 mL) and kept at a stable 
temperature of 15°C using water bath, and spiked with PFA to a nominal concentration of 2 mg/L PFA. The 
PFA concentration in the samples was measured at fixed intervals for 3 h. After complete PFA decay, 5 h 
after the initial PFA addition, 100 mL of the water was transferred into new containers (200 mL), and the 
water was spiked to 2 mg/L PFA for a second time. The concentration of PFA in the water was measured at 
fixed intervals for 3 h. In the water sample from RAS C the concentration of hydrogen peroxide was 
measured alongside PFA to establish its degradation rate in the water samples. All experiments and 
measurements were carried out in duplicate.  
  
Effect of PFA (≤10 mg/L) on nitrification 
The impact of PFA exposure on the nitrification performance of biofilter elements (bioelements) was 
examined by exposing bioelements in batch scale reactors (Figure 2) to PFA under stable conditions. After 
exposure to PFA, the rate of TAN (NH4-N) and nitrite-N (NO2-N) removal in the reactors was determined 
and compared to unexposed bioelements (control). The rate of TAN and nitrite-N removal was determined 
both at a low substrate concentration (first order kinetics) and at a high substrate concentration (zero 
order kinetics).  
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
The impacts of PFA and PAA on the nitrification rates were compared. This was done by exposing 
bioelements in the reactors to a nominal dose of 2 mg/L PAA and PFA separately, and comparing the 
resulting rates of TAN and nitrite-N removal.  
Each of the batch scale reactors were filled with 5 L of RAS water from RAS A, and 2.4 L of active carrier 
media from the moving bed bio filter connected to it. Prior to the experiments alkalinity and pH was 
measured in the water used for the reactors to ensure that the pH remained between 7.6 - 7.8 and that 
alkalinity was > 1.8 meq/L. The water flow was adjusted to 60 L/h, the aeration was set to 0.5 L/min, and an 
HG40d multimeter (HACH Lange, Germany) was placed in one of the reactors to monitor temperature, pH 
and the dissolved oxygen throughout the experiment. After acclimatizing for 1.5 h the reactors were spiked 
with PFA to one of five concentrations (0, 1, 2, 5 and 10 mg/L). The concentration of PFA in the water was 
measured at regular intervals for the following 1.5 h. Two hours after the PFA addition, all the reactors 
were spiked to 1 mg/L NH4-N using 5 mL of an NH4Cl stock solution (1000 mg/L NH4-N) to determine the 
first order (substrate limited) TAN removal rate. Water samples of 8 mL were extracted (every 10 - 15 min 
for 2 h) using a syringe, and sterile filtered through a 0.2 m Sartorius filter to remove bacteria from the 
water sample. They were placed in 12 mL test tubes and refrigerated until analysis. 5 h after the PFA 
addition, each of the reactors were spiked with 5 mg/L NH4-N using 25 mL of a NH4Cl stock solution (1000 
mg/L NH4-N) to determine the zero order TAN removal rate. Water samples were taken every 10 - 15 min 
for 3 h.  
The experimental procedure was repeated to study the effect of PFA on the second step of nitrification i.e. 
the nitrite removal. For this experiment the reactors were spiked to 1 and 5 mg/L NO2-N, with 5 mL and 25 
mL of 1000 mg/L NO2-N solution, respectively. The same experimental procedures were also applied for 
comparing the effects of PAA and PFA, with a PAA concentration of 2 mg/L. All experiments were 
randomized and carried out in duplicate.

Effect of severe PFA exposure (10 mg/L) on biofilm 
The required concentration and contact time of PFA for achieving inhibition of the bacteria on active carrier 
elements was examined by exposing the carrier elements to two different concentrations of PFA (10 and 
100 mg/L). The bacterial activity was measured at three different time intervals (20 min, 2 h and 22 h).  
Under temperature controlled conditions (14°C), 50 carrier elements from RAS A were placed in buckets 
with 10 L of water from the same system. After acclimatizing for 1 h the buckets were spiked with PFA to 
achieve concentrations of 0, 10 and 100mg/L. At three different exposure times (20 min, 2 h and 22 h) 
triplicate samples were taken for each concentration. A sample consisted of five carrier elements that were 
removed from the buckets and placed in 50 mL Sarstedt tubes containing 42 mL of Milli-Q water. The 
relative inhibition of the bacteria on the carrier elements was estimated by measuring the rate of hydrogen 
peroxide degradation (at 22 °C) in each test tube after spiking with 10 mg/L hydrogen peroxide (HP). This is 
a newly developed method based on the work of Arvin and Pedersen (2015).  A blank sample was taken 
from each test tube before spiking with 400 L of 1000 mg/L HP solution. The concentration of HP in the 
test tubes was measured at 2, 15, 30, 45 and 60 min. The samples were kept in water bath for temperature 
control (22 °C) and inverted before every measurement of HP concentration.  
 
2.4 Analysis and chemicals 
The TAN and nitrite-N content in the water samples were determined using Danish Standard 224 (1975) 
and Danish Standard 223 (1991) respectively. All spectrophotometric analyses were performed using a DR 
2800 Spectrophotometer (HACH®, Germany). The alkalinity was determined by end point titration to pH 4.5 
with Mettler Toledo T50 autotitrator (Glostrup, Denmark). The pH was measured using a Hach Lange HQ40 
Multimeter (Düsseldorf, Germany) with IntelliCAL™ PHC1010 pH electrode. Total and dissolved chemical 
oxygen demand (COD) was measured from raw and filtered (0.2 µm) samples using a Merck test kit (Merck 
KGaA, Darmstadt, Germany), modified from ISO 15705. HP concentrations were determined using the 
method from Tanner and Wong (1998) modified by applying a four-fold strength fixating agent. 
The concentrations of performic acid and peracetic acid were determined as previously described by 
Chhetri et al. (2014). For analysis, 700 L of sample was added to 700 L of acetic acid (1.0 M, pH 3.50) and 
700 L ABTS (1.0 g/L) in a 10 mm disposable cuvette. The absorbance was measured after 10 and 20 min 
for PAA and PFA, respectively. The recalculated extinction coefficients for the oxidation of ABTS used in 
calculations were 5.23 mg L-1Abs-1 for PFA and 5.94 mg L-1Abs-1 for PAA. The coefficients were determined 
experimentally.  
==PAGE:18==

PFA, ABTS (Sigma) and the acetic acid buffer were prepared as described by Chhetri et al. (2014) before the 
experiment was performed. PFA was prepared in two steps. In the first step formic acid (85% w/w, 11 mL, 
VWR Chemicals) was mixed with sulfuric acid (95% w/w, 1 mL, Merck) in a test tube. Thereafter, 0.9 mL of 
the mixture was added to 1.1 mL hydrogen peroxide (50% w/w, VWR Chemicals) and left to react in a water 
bath (20°C) for 10 min. 1 mL of the resulting solution was placed in 99 mL of Milli-Q water on ice bath and 
used as stock solution of PFA. The stock solution was then immediately used for experiments.

4 Discussion 
4.1 Effect of temperature on PFA degradation 
The experiments on PFA degradation in water showed that an increase in temperature increases the rate of 
degradation (Figure 3, 4, 5). The degradation of PFA can be described using 1st order kinetics, as shown by 
Luukkonen et al. (2015). The results in the current experiment showed that PFA had half-lives of 6.93, 4.78, 
3.17 and 1.56 h at 0, 5, 10 and 20 °C in Milli-Q water and 0.94, 0.73, 0.47 and 0.30 h for temperatures of 5, 
10, 15 and 20° in RAS water. The results suggest that the effect of temperature on PFA degradation is 
independent of bacterial oxidation, and mainly caused by an increase in the rate of chemical oxidation 
since the rate of degradation increases both in Milli-Q and RAS water.   
While no previous studies were found on the temperature dependence of PFA degradation in RAS water, 
Luukkonen et al. (2015) found that PFA (15 mg/L) had half-lives of 1.65 h in tap water and 0.97 h in tertiary 
wastewater at 15 °C. Chhetri et al. (2014) observed a degradation of PFA up to 62% within the first minute 
for raw wastewater in artificial combined sewer overflow (CSO) for water spiked with 4 mg/L PFA. In 
another study on disinfection of CSO found PFA to be more than 50% degraded after 20 min (Chhetri et al. 
2015). Karpova et al. (2013) found PFA and its byproducts to be rapidly decomposing, with no PFA detected 
1 h after addition to secondary wastewater. These results are similar to those found in this experiment, and 
support a positive correlation between the amount of organic material in the water and the degradation 
rate of PFA. Studies on PAA have shown that the degradation rate of peracids is dependent on 
concentration, application mode, temperature, pH, amount of organic material, the presence of solids, 
salinity and water hardness (Luukkonen & Pehkonen 2017). The degradation rates of PFA in water showed 
no instantaneous decomposition of PFA as is seen with PAA, a finding that is supported by the results of 
Luukkonen et al. (2015) and Chhetri et al. (2015).  
The results show that PFA degrades rapidly in water from RAS at appropriate temperatures. The advantage 
of rapid degradation of chemicals used in aquaculture systems is that they pose a smaller risk of entering 
the biofilter and reducing nitrification performance. The rapid degradation also means that PFA will pose a 
smaller risk than more slowly degrading chemicals to the natural environment. 
The rate of degradation of PFA in the stock solution was examined prior to the experiments in order to 
assess the stability and determine whether the stock solution could be prepared in advance of the 
experiments. While Karpova et al. (2013) found that PFA was stable on ice for 5 h, the degradation 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
experiment showed that the stock solution had a half-life of approximately 8 h, which led to the conclusion 
that the solution should be prepared immediately in advance of the experiments. 
 
4.2 Effect of double exposure on PFA degradation in RAS water 
All water matrices in this experiment showed a reduction in degradation rate of PFA with the second 
exposure of PFA (Figure 6, 7, 8). The results suggest that PFA reacts with organic matter in the sample so 
that there is less material to react with in the second exposure. Peracids disinfect and oxidize micro-
pollutants in water through formation of highly oxidative radical species (Shi & Li 2007). The specific 
reactions for PFA oxidation of pollutants in water have not been reported. However, is it thought that the 
reactions are similar to those of PAA (Luukkonen & Pehkonen 2017). The water matrices in this experiment 
had different initial COD levels. The results were compared to assess whether there was a correlation 
between the initial COD levels of the RAS water and the degradation rate of PFA, yet no clear relationship 
was found. 
Because COD measurements cannot be carried out in water containing HP (Talinli & Anderson 1992), 
measurements on COD were not carried out after PFA treatment in the water used for this experiment. 
COD and total organic carbon (TOC) should increase as a result of peracid dosing (Luukkonen & Pehkonen 
2017), yet a decrease in COD has been observed (Baldry et al. 1995), which could have been due to the 
oxidation of organic material. Still, the results of Baldry et al. (1995) could have been impacted by the 
presences of HP in the water.  
The current study did not examine the level of disinfection in the water phase and no study on the types of 
microorganisms and inactivation of them has been carried out. BactiQuant® (Mycometer, Denmark) was 
tested as a potential method for examining the effect of PFA on the amount of bacteria in water, as used by 
Rojas-Tirado et al. (2016), but the results were variable and it was not possible to repeat the experiment 
due to time and financial constraints. While results from experiments in CSO show the action of PFA at low 
doses (2 - 4 mg/L) reduces the amount of the indicator bacteria E. coli and Enterococcus by ≥ 3 log units 
using a contact time of 20 min (Chhetri et al. 2014), little is known about the efficiency of PFA on the 
pathogens specific to recirculating aquaculture systems. HP in the peracid equilibrium solution has been 
found to have a synergistic effect on the disinfection mechanism, however, this depends on the initial 
inactivation of catalase by the peracid (Flores et al. 2014), as the enzyme can be used by microorganisms to 
protect against the action of hydrogen peroxide. 
==PAGE:31==
 
31 
When the concentration of HP was measured alongside the concentration of PFA in the PFA re-exposure 
experiment, no degradation of HP was observed. Pedersen et al. (2009) found that a high (3 mg/L) dosage 
of PAA/HP in water with low COD inhibited HP removal, yet decay of HP was still observed. Arvin and 
Pedersen (2015) hypothesized that the reduction in hydrogen peroxide removal in water with low COD 
could be caused by an enzyme deficit in the water, meaning that the decay is dependent on the presence 
of bacteria. The water from RAS C, in which the experiment was performed, had a very low undissolved 
COD content, which could be the reason for the inhibition of HP decay. The water from RAS C is treated 
with UV after exiting the biofilter, resulting in a reduced bacterial load in the water compared to the other 
RAS systems. An additional reason for the lack of HP degradation in the water could be that the rapid 
action of PFA limited the available bacteria. 
 
4.3 Effect of PFA (≤10 mg/L) on nitrification 
The results show that the rates of TAN and nitrite-N removal are negatively correlated with exposure to 
PFA, and the impact of PAA corresponds to that of PFA. The results from the batch reactor experiments on 
the impact of PFA on the nitrification rate of the biofilter showed that a dose of 1 mg/L had a small impact 
on the nitrification as a whole. While the surface specific TAN removal (STR) was reduced by 12 and 28% at 
concentrations of 1 and 2 mg/L, respectively, the surface specific nitrite-N removal (SNR) was not impacted 
(Figure 10, 12). This suggests that the ammonia oxidizing bacteria are more sensitive to PFA than the nitrite 
oxidizing bacteria. At higher concentrations (5 and 10 mg/L PFA) the effects of PFA where similar for both 
TAN and nitrite-N removal, with 72 and 71% reduction in STR and SNR in 5 mg/L PFA and 96 and 97% 
reduction STR and SNR in 10 mg/L PFA. The results for the TAN and nitrite-N removal rates at low substrate 
concentrations were similar. The TAN removal rate was reduced by 34, 46, 76 and 96% at concentrations of 
1, 2, 5 and 10 mg/L PFA respectively, while the nitrite-N removal rate was increased by 21 and 6% at 
concentrations of 1 and 2 mg/L PFA, and reduced by 87 and 101% at 5 and 10 mg/L PFA. While both TAN 
and nitrite-N can be impaired by the addition of a chemical disinfectant (Hagopian & Riley 1998), this study 
showed more inhibition on the ammonia oxidizing bacteria by PFA at low concentrations. This finding is 
similar to that of (Pedersen & Pedersen 2012) where they observed a stronger inhibition in TAN than 
nitrite-N for biofilters exposed to HP (64 mg/L). Pedersen et al. (2009) found that a concentration of 1 mg/L 
PAA caused only minor impaired nitrification, while doses of 2 and 3 mg/L of PAA caused a significant 
increase in nitrite levels over a prolonged period. The average TAN removal rates in systems treated with 2 
and 3 mg/L PAA were significantly reduced. This suggests that the effects of PFA on nitrification 
performance are similar to those of PAA and HP.  
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
In order to avoid damage to the nitrification process when using PFA in RAS concentrations exceeding 1 
mg/L PFA should not enter the biofilter. Higher doses could be used in other parts of the system due to the 
rapid degradation of PFA, this will, however, depend on the circulation time of the water. Based on their 
results Pedersen et al. (2009) suggested that a temporary bypass of the biofilter should be used when 
treating the water with PAA. Additionally, a temporary reduction in the treated water volume could reduce 
the need for PAA, and the potential risk of unwanted side effects (Pedersen et al. 2009). Liu et al. (2017a) 
found that simply adding PAA only to the fish culture tank and reducing the flow can avoid potential harm 
to the biofilter, with concentrations of PAA adjusted to the culture tank volume rather than the whole 
system volume. Long term effects of HP exposure was studied by Møller et al. (2010) who found that a 
single dose of HP only had a limited effect on the nitrification, while a prolonged HP treatment led to a 
severe inhibition of the nitrification process. Møller et al. (2010) concluded that great care should be taken 
when estimating the dosage of HP and the water volume.  
In terms of dosing strategy Liu et al. (2017b) found that pulse applications of PAA was associated with 
partially inhibition of nitrification, high oxygen concentration and stable pH in the RAS system it was 
applied. While continuous application of PAA caused a pH increase and intermediate oxygen 
concentrations. They concluded that, although pulse application of PAA caused minor stress in fish, it was 
preferable to continuous application as it provided better water quality. Different dosing regimens are not 
tested in this study, but the same results could be expected for PFA due to the similarities to PAA. In 
systems using PAA as a sanitizer there is a potential for combining the treatment with the addition of PFA 
to prevent pathogens from developing tolerance towards PAA. 
The ability of the system to recuperate from exposure depends on the degree of inactivation and the 
biofilm composition (Park & Noguera 2007). The extracellular polymeric substance that the nitrifying 
bacteria are embedded in can absorb and partly inactivate the biocide or oxidants (Mah & O'Toole 2001). 
Møller et al. (2010) found that the tolerance to HP was higher in the biofilters with the thickest biofilm and 
a high feed:water ratio, as is commonly found in commercial systems. This could also be the case for 
bioelements from commercial systems when exposed to PFA, where the thicker biofilm protects the action 
of the nitrifying bacteria.  
This study opens up the question of how long term exposure to PFA can affect the biofilter, but also what 
the recovery time is for biofilters that have been exposed to PFA. If PFA is to be used in RAS the long term 
aspects of its use should be examined.

4.4 Effect of severe PFA exposure (10 mg/L) on biofilm 
As expected, an increased dosage and exposure time to PFA lead to an increased relative inactivation of the 
bacteria on the bioelements (Figure 15). Addition of 10 mg/L PFA gave clear inhibition of the bacterial 
activity with contact times of 2 and 22 h, while inhibition was achieved using 100 mg/L PFA for all contact 
times. As Figure 15 shows, there is a large variation in the measured bacterial activity in the control 
samples. This can be caused by biological variation in the biofilm of the bioelements used for 
measurements. Since only five bioelements were used to measure bacterial activity in each replicate 
uneven biofilm distribution will lead to large variations in measurements. This can also be the reason for 
the relative increased in bacterial activity (negative relative inhibition value) for the control after 2 h (Figure 
15). The results for the control are based on six replicates while results 10 and 100 mg/L PFA are in 
triplicate. This could contribute to the larger variation observed in the control samples. Despite this 
variation, the results still show a positive correlation between PFA concentration and exposure time and 
relative inhibition of bacterial activity.   
The experiments show that exposure to 100 mg/L PFA for 22 h gives 99% inhibition in bacterial activity, this 
treatment could, however, be seen as a bit excessive and costly to manage in large scale. Additionally, 99% 
inactivation is not the same as full sterilization of bioelements, and the remaining bacteria can over time 
develop resistance towards PFA. This is always a risk when attempting sterilization. With complete 
sterilization it is important that no bacteria remain, especially in systems that are contaminated with 
pathogens. Furthermore, the use of system sterilization should be limited both because of the risk of 
remaining bacteria in the system and because of the time is takes to establish new populations of nitrifying 
bacteria in the biofilter.  
When PFA was added to the water containing the bioelements pH dropped to 6.5 and 3.0 in 10 and 100 
mg/L PFA, respectively. It is unclear whether the inactivation in bacterial activity was caused by the 
oxidizing activity of PFA alone or the reduction in pH as the growth and activity of bacteria decreases 
dramatically at pH levels below neutrality (Hankinson & Schmidt 1988). Other methods for sterilizing RAS 
like adjusting the pH to very acidic or alkaline conditions may be more cost effective as they require less 
equipment and cheaper chemicals.  Despite achieving good inhibition results it can be difficult to use PFA as 
the sole sanitizer when disinfecting a full scale biofilter. 
The method used to examine the inactivation of the bioelements is very useful in estimating the relative 
reduction in bacterial levels. However, it does not give any information on the type of bacteria inactivated 
by the addition of PFA, which means that resilient pathogens could potentially survive the treatment.

Methods providing more detailed information on the bacterial composition of the water and bioelements 
post and prior to the experiments were not used, but it is an interesting topic for further research. The HP 
method can be a useful tool in providing a quick insight into the bacterial load in water.  
4.5 PFA application in aquaculture systems 
A trial to test the effect and potential consequences of PFA (0.5 and 2 mg/L) on fish behavior was carried 
out in two tanks containing rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss). No mortality was registered within the 
24 h following the addition of PFA to the fish tanks. The fish were observed to change behavior as PFA was 
added into the tanks, with increased activity and movement towards the sides of the tanks and away from 
their normal position below the belt feeder. This suggests that the fish experienced some distress, but they 
returned to their initial behavior after adjusting to the stressor (PFA) and as water was exchanged from the 
tank.  
The observations from this trial are similar to the results of studies carried out on the effect of PAA 
exposure on fish. The effect of peracetic acid addition in culture tanks for fish has been studied for both 
carp (Cyprinus carpio) and rainbow trout (Liu et al. 2017a; Liu et al. 2017b; Pedersen et al. 2009). The 
results from these studies suggest that although the fish have a stress response to PAA application in the 
culture tanks, they can adapt to the stressor. Liu et al. (2017a) measured response in carp to repeated 
applications of peracetic acid (2 mg/L) in a RAS by measuring the cortisol in the water. Their results 
indicated adaptation in the carp to repeated disinfection with PAA, with less pronounced increase in the 
water cortisol and earlier decrease in the cortisol levels after repeated application. Liu et al. (2017a) also 
suggested that the carp would fully adapt to PAA applications and would show no or only negligible stress 
response with repeated application. Studies performed on rainbow trout in RAS showed an adaptation in 
the fish to PAA addition in doses of 1 mg/L, while continuous application of 0.2 mg/L PAA caused no stress 
to the fish (Liu et al. 2017b). No mortality or impact on growth was registered on the fish exposed to PAA 
(Liu et al. 2017b). Pedersen et al. (2009) found that PAA exposure (1 - 3 mg/L) resulted in altered behavior 
of the exposed rainbow trout at high dosages, but no deviations in the feed conversion ratio (FCR) and 
specific growth rate (SGR) were observed in the fish. No mortalities were recorded in any of the studies.

5 Conclusion and suggestions for further research 
PFA degradation depends on the water matrix and temperature, and repeated exposure of PFA increased 
the half-life due to consumption of organic matter, hence reducing the disinfection demand as the organic 
matter decreases. The rapid degradation of PFA and the lack of harmful by-products means that PFA poses 
a minimal environmental risk in outlet water. The kinetic properties of PFA also makes is suitable for 
treating inlet water. Especially marine water should be treated to prevent intake of undesirable organisms 
into a RAS. Tests on PFA degradation rates in seawater should, however, be carried out prior to investing in 
the required equipment.  
PFA at applicable doses (1 - 2 mg/L) limits the TAN removal rate of the bacteria in the biofilter, while the 
removal rate of nitrite-N is unaffected. The impact of PFA may be reduced in biofilters from commercial 
RAS systems with a thicker biofilm on the bioelements. Damage to the nitrifying bacteria in the biofilter can 
be prevented by the use of a bypass when PFA is added in the system. In order to use PFA in recirculating 
aquaculture systems further research should be carried out on the long term effects on nitrification of PFA 
exposure. This includes both the impact of repeated PFA exposure and the bacteria’s ability to recover from 
such exposure.  
The trial on fish response to PFA shows that the effect of PFA is similar to that of PAA, and that PFA can be 
used in RAS at concentrations  2 mg/L without causing immediate damage to the fish.  Further research 
should also be carried out on the long term impact of PFA on fish welfare. 
Results from testing PFA as a biofilter disinfectant show that a very high concentration (100 mg/L) of PFA 
inhibits the bacterial activity by > 99%, yet full sterilization was not achieved. It was not established 
whether the inhibition was due to the change in pH caused by the addition of PFA or by oxidation. Future 
research should compare PFA to acid or alkali disinfection.
