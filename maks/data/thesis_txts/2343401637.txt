Abstract
Increasing penetration of converter connected renewable generators in power system has led to new system
challenges. Short circuit power response from converter connected generators is different from that of tradi-
tional synchronous generators. Synchronous generators can provide short circuit current which is 3-4 times
more than that of its nominal current. On the other hand, power electronic converters can be designed for
over-current only up to 1.1-1.25 times of its nominal value. Therefore, short circuit power is low in power
system with large penetration of converter connected generators as compared to traditional power system
with synchronous generators.
Low availability of short circuit power can cause many challenges such as misoperation of distance relays.
The aim of this master thesis work is to study the effect of low inertia systems on performance of distance
relay. Backup functionality of the distance relay is major concern as miscoordination of backup relays in case
of cascading faults can lead to severe stress in system, which can develop into blackout.
In this thesis, response of relays in traditional system is compared with response of relays in low inertia, low
short circuit power systems. The impact of converter controls on fault current response of converter connected
generators is investigated and recommendations are provided for improved support from converter connected
generators during single and multiple faults. An interface with MATLAB is developed to control RSCAD
Runtime simulations and to facilitate improved processing of data obtained from RTDS in MATLAB.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter summarizes the motivation and background for this study. The project objectives, choice of simulation tool,
scopes and limitations as well as outline of this thesis are described.
1.1
Motivation
Environmental concerns together with increasing price of fossil fuels have led to decrease in fossil fuel
generated power in majority of electrical power systems [3]. This in turn has paved way for increase in power
generation by Renewable Energy Sources (RES). According to European Network Transmission System
Operators for Electricity (ENTSO-E) report, RES will play preeminent role with a forecasted increase in
installed capacity of wind and solar by 80% and 60% respectively by 2025 [4]. The Danish government has
set a target of achieving energy supply based on 100% renewable energy by 2050. Such huge penetration of
RES will lead to replacement of synchronous generators with RES which are mostly interfaced to the grid
through power electronic converters. The fault response of these converter connected generations differ from
that of synchronous generators. Thus the power systems which are traditionally operating with high inertia
will be eventually be operated with low inertia and low SCP.
High penetration of RES in future power system will pose various challenges for power system operators. One
major impact of decreasing SCP will be on the behaviour of protective relays, specially distance relays. Dis-
tance relays measure voltage and current to determine electrical distance from fault location. Therefore, both
voltage and current during fault plays signiﬁcant role in proper operation of distance relay. A synchronous
generator can provide 3-4 times more current than its nominal current during fault in system. However,
modern RES are mostly connected to the grid through power electronic converters. To ensure safety of power
electronic components, converters are designed to generate maximum current which is 1.1-1.25 times its rated
current. This implies that even during fault, converter connected RES can generate short circuit current which
is far less than that compared to synchronous generators. Hence a distance relay connected to a low inertia,
low SCP, may not have enough current for its accurate operation. This is a threat posed due to increase in
RES penetration.
On the other hand, presence of power electronic converter interface in modern day RES offers ﬂexibility in
control of short circuit current. Since short circuit current purely depends on control action of converter,
converter parameters can be tuned as per desired short circuit response. This gives a major advantage for
RES. Converter controls can be tuned for fast or slow reactive current response, delayed response, balanced
response in case of unbalanced faults etc. Nevertheless SCP remains low in system dominated by converter
controlled generators.
If a fault occurs in low SCP system which causes disconnection of an existing synchronous generator or a
part of the power system, the system gets further stressed. RES thus becomes the only available source which

has to provide for SCP in the stressed network. In such circumstances if another fault occurs in the system,
available power from RES may not be enough to trigger distance relays or may cause false tripping of backup
relays. This in turn can lead to blackout in the system. Several major blackouts in history have been caused
due to multiple or cascading failures and due to misoperation of protective relays [5], [6].
• Wrong relay operation initiated the ﬁnal cascade of blackout in US-Canada in 2003. The relay interpreted
undervoltage and overcurrent as a short circuit.
• On August 28, 2003 in Great Britain, a transformer was taken out of service. During this, wrong protection
relay setting led to tripping of an adjacent cable which in turn led to power outage.
• Denmark-Sweden blackout on September 23, 2003 occurred due to multiple outages of generating and
transmission capacities. Distance relays misinterpreted falling voltages as distant short circuit event sever-
ing connections between northern and southern Sweden, resulting in frequency and voltage collapse.
Above examples show multiple contingency events can cause transients which can be miscalculated by pro-
tective relays as backup zone faults and cause erroneous tripping of transmission lines and equipments. There-
fore it is of particular interest to study cascading or multiple faults on protective relay performance in low
inertia, low SCP system with converter connected generators. Specially if multiple outages cause discon-
nection of synchronous generator or a part of system with synchronous generators. Such event will cause
SCP to reduce further as power support from synchronous generator will reduce. Hence relay performance
will fully depends on SCP from WTG only. This in turn implies that relay performance will be dependent
on converter controls of RES. This provides motivation for this study where performance of zone 2 distance
relay is studied for different converter control parameters in case of multiple faults in low inertia and low SCP
systems.
1.2
Project Objectives
Main objectives of this project are :
• Development of a low inertia, low SCP power system model with high penetration of RES, with detailed
– modelling of Grid Side Voltage Source Converter (GSVSC) of RES.
• Comparison of distance relay performance in traditional power system with synchronous generators and
low inertia system with high penetration of RES.
• Study of relay response in power system with converter connected RES for various system parameters,
– Effect of different wind speeds on SCP response of RES.
– Effect of reactive current rise time on SCP response of RES.
• Study relay response in case of multiple or cascading outages for different reactive current rise time on
SCP response of RES.
• Development of a MATLAB interface to control simulations externally and export simulation data for
further processing.
1.3
Simulation Tool
The simulation tool used for this project is Real Time Digital Simulator (RTDS). RTDS is a power system
simulator which performs real time Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) simulations. RSCAD is JAVA based
graphical user-interface for RTDS simulator, which allows users to prepare and run the simulations and
analyze simulation results [7], [8].

1.4 Scopes and Limitation
In RMS simulations stator ﬂux transients are not considered. For study of power system protection system,
the stator ﬂux transients play an important role as these cause transients in the short circuit currents. EMT
simulation is required for protection studies [9]. As most of the RES are interfaced with power electronic
converters, study of switching events of these power electronics components is also required. Time step used
for RTDS simulations is in the order of microseconds, which enables study of switching of power electronic
components.
1.4
Scopes and Limitation
Scopes of the study are listed below :
• Study is done on a power system with very high penetration of RES. Such a system will be prevalent in
future when RES will be dominant energy producers.
• RES connected to the power system at a point of low Short Circuit Ratio (SCR) is considered in this study.
This will be signiﬁcant when a large offshore wind farm is connected to a weak grid with a very long
transmission line. In such a case, protective relay will depend solely on SCP available from WTG.
Limitations of the study are :
• Converter controlled RES can be wind or PV. Also large offshore wind farms can be connected to grid with
High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) links which use Voltage Source Converter (VSC) for independent
active and reactive power control. In this study only PMSG based WTG is used for study of distance relay
performance in presence of converter controlled generations. However, back-to-back VSC converters used
for Type 4 WTG is similar to that used for other converter connected generations. Therefore control and
performance of GSVSC will be similar.
• Various grid side VSC control strategies are suggested in literature. VSC can inject balanced current for
unbalanced faults in system if separate positive and negative sequence current control is employed. In this
study separate negative sequence current control has not been modelled. Thus the VSC of this model cannot
control negative sequence current as a result, controllability of fault current is lost during unbalanced faults.
Therefore unbalanced faults are not studied here. However, 3-phase balanced fault which is studied in this
thesis is of most severe nature.
• In real systems, various devices like synchronous condensers, FACTS, STATCOMS etc. are connected
which can affect the performance of distance relays. However, in this study relay performance is studied
only in presence of RES. Impacts of other power system components are not considered.
1.5
Thesis Outline
This master thesis is organized into 6 chapters.
Chapter 1 gives an introduction to this thesis. It describes the motivation, project objectives, choice of simu-
lation tool, scopes and limitations of this thesis.
Chapter 2 describes the state-of-the-art of Wind Turbine (WT) technologies and relay design fundamentals. In
this chapter WTG topologies, converter control methods, grid synchronization techniques, fault ride through
capabilities of Type 4 WTG is described. This chapter also describes different types of protective relay
schemes used for transmission line protection among which mho relay design is discussed in details.
Chapter 3 describes the system model used for simulation studies. The 9 bus benchmark power system as
proposed in literature is modiﬁed to meet the requirements of low inertia, low SCR and low SCP system.

Chapter 4 consists of simulation results for different case studies performed in this study. Firstly distance

Chapter 2
State-of-the-Art
This chapter describes existing WT technologies and protection schemes. Focus is mainly on the aspects of WT tech-
nologies that have been used in this study – WTG topology, existing converter control methods, grid synchronisation
techniques, fault ride through capability of WTG. Description of Danish grid requirements and recommendations which
have been implemented in the model are discussed in this chapter. Various types of protection schemes employed for
protection of transmission lines are discussed.
Effect of converter controlled generation on transmission line protection have been studied in [10], [11], [12].
Li et. al.[10] studied zone 1 protection performance of distance relay with varying degrees of RES. Roy et. al.
[11] studied the impact of full converter based Type 4 WTG on various protection schemes for both balanced
and unbalanced faults. It is observed in [11] that low fault current and absence of negative and zero sequence
current during unbalanced faults affects performance of protective relays. Investigation of transmission line
protection employing different protection schemes in a system with converter controlled generation is done
by Holder et. al. [12]. Hooshyar et. al. [13] has shown that the impedance seen by a relay on a line connected
to WTG does not represent true fault location during ﬁrst few cycles of a balanced fault. This can result in
zone 1 tripping of relays for faults occurring in zone 2.
He and Liu [14] describe the effect VSC based HVDC control on zone 2 setting of backup relays. Due to
fast reactive power support of HVDC, voltage at the bus where relay under study is connected, recovers fast
and the relay overestimates zone 2 fault as zone 3 fault [14]. He et. al. [15] shows when a fault occurs
near to Point of Common Coupling (PCC), backup protection can overestimate zone 2 fault. This can lead to
miscoordination of relay tripping. This may cause the fault to persist beyond its critical clearing time thereby
leading to cascading events.
Short circuit current contribution of converter controlled generation depends on reactive current output con-
trolled by the VSC. As total current output of converter is limited to 1-1.25 p.u., generations with converter
interfaces have a low short circuit contribution. This may cause problems in order to detect a fault situation.
Li et. al. [10] studied the impact of various converter parameters like current ramp rate, response delay,
output fault current level on zone 1 distance protection. He et. al.[14] shows that HVDC control can have
impact on protective relay schemes can prevent system instability.
Impact of cascading or multiple faults on protective relay performance in low inertia, low SCP systems with
converter controlled generations still remains unevaluated. In this study, impact of cascading or multiple
faults on protective relay performance with converter controlled generations is studied. For this purpose, a
low inertia, low SCP model is designed based on existing WT technologies and protection schemes. Different
WT technologies are discussed in the following sections.

2.1
Wind Turbine Technologies
WT topologies can be generally categorised into 4 groups [16]. These topologies mainly differ in power and
speed control methodology used. One of the widely used WT topology is Type 4 variable speed, variable
power WT with full-scale converter. Figure 2-1 shows schematic representation of Type 4 WTG. These type
of WTs are fully decoupled from the grid as these are connected to the grid through full scale converter. Pres-
ence of full scale converter implies that the converter rating should be same as the rated power of generator.
This makes Type 4 WTs more expensive than other topologies [17]. Type 4 WTs can have Wound Rotor
Induction Generator (WRIG), Wound Rotor Synchronous Generator (WRSG) or PMSG [16]. Type 4 WT
topology can be with or without gearbox. Topologies without gearbox uses generator with large diameter
directly coupled with WT shaft [16]. For e.g. - Enercon manufactures Type 4 WTs with multipole gearless
WRSG [16]. GE Wind has developed Type 4 concept with PMSG and gearbox [16]. Siemens Wind Power
has developed a Type 4 concept with Squirrel Cage Induction Generator (SCIG) and gearbox [16].
Figure 2-1: Schematic Representation of Type 4 PMSG based WTG
Baroudi et. al. [18] reviews various types of generator-converter topologies for wind energy conversion for
Type 4 WTG. One of the widely used methodology is to use two back-to-back VSCs for decoupling of the
stator from grid. This gives ﬂexibility of independent active and reactive power control [19]. Reactive power
is supplied by GSVSC and is determined by its size.
Few important components of Type 4 WTG topology are described in the following sections, like:
• Converter controls used for power and speed control.
• Grid synchronisation technique for generation of current and voltage from VSC which is synchronised with
the grid current and voltage.
• Fault ride through requirements and capability of Type 4 WTGs.
2.1.1
Converter Control Methodologies
The converter connected to the generator is called the Machine Side Voltage Source Converter (MSVSC) and
the other converter connected to the grid is called Grid Side Voltage Source Converter (GSVSC). MSVSC
converts AC signal to DC and is used to control torque and speed of the generator such that power from
wind energy is maximised. The GSVSC controls power ﬂow between between DC link and the grid by
maintaining the DC link voltage. Also reactive power ﬂow is controlled by maintaining PCC voltage. Three
different modes of control can be employed to maintain PCC voltage – V control, Q control and PF control
modes. In V control mode, constant V at PCC is maintained by absorbing or generating necessary reactive
power. In Q control mode the reactive power generation is controlled which can cause slight increase or
decrease of PCC voltage from set reference value. In PF control mode reactive power output is controlled to
maintain the reference power factor setting.
20
==PAGE:21==
2.1 Wind Turbine Technologies
Vector control strategy is commonly used control strategy for converters and is described in [20]- [27]. Con-
verter model employing vector control strategy is developed in a synchronously rotating dq frame which
is synchronised with three phase grid voltage. Decoupling of current into direct axis and quadrature axis
component allows independent control of active and reactive power. In MSVSC, the q-axis stator current
maintains the electrical torque in order to match the power requirements of WT. The d-axis stator current is
used to maintain reactive power at minimum by controlling armature ﬂux reaction. In GSVSC, the d-axis
current is used to control the DC link voltage and thereby maintaining active power ﬂow between DC link
and grid. The q-axis current is used to maintain the PCC voltage, thereby controlling reactive power output
of the converter. Two PI controllers are used to control the d-axis and q-axis components of the converter.
Two more cascaded PI controllers are used to generate reference values of current so as to maintain DC link
voltage and PCC voltage [28], [29].
2.1.2
Grid Synchronisation
Phase Locked Loop (PLL) is used to synchronise the synchronous reference frame of GSVSC. For optimal
performance of converter control, accurate information of grid voltage phase and frequency is required. Syn-
chronous Reference Frame - PLL (SRF-PLL) is commonly used for synchronisation purpose [30]. Though
conventional SRF-PLL works well under balanced conditions, under unbalanced condition its performance is
not satisfactory. This has led to development of advanced PLL structures like Decoupled Double Synchronous
Reference Frame PLL (DDSRF-PLL) [31], Dual Second Order Generalized Integrator - PLL (DSOGI-PLL)
[32], [33] and Three Phase Enhanced PLL (3phEPLL) [34].
DDSRF-PLL uses two synchronous rotating frames which are rotating at grid frequency but in opposite
directions. 3phEPLL uses enhanced PLL on each phase to generate two sinusoidal outputs with a difference
of 90 degrees. These are then computed to extract positive sequence voltage component.
DSOGI-PLL is a frequency adaptive PLL structure which uses dual second order generalised integrator based
Quadrature Signal Generator (QSG) to obtain ﬁltered and 90 degree shifted component of 𝛼𝛽voltage com-
ponents. These components are then used to calculate positive sequence 𝛼𝛽component. These are then
converted to positive sequence dq component which is then used in SRF-PLL to make the PLL frequency
adaptive and synchronised with grid frequency. Rodriguez et. al. [32] shows that performance of DSOGI-
PLL in case of unbalanced faults or in presence of distorted utility voltage is satisfactory. However, Karimi-
Ghartemani and Iravani [35] shows that DSOGI-PLL may result in output signals with incorrect phase or
magnitude if the system frequency varies too much from resonant frequency SOGI-QSG scheme.
2.1.3
Fault Ride Through Capability of Type 4 Wind Turbine Generator
Type 4 WTG has superior Fault Ride Through (FRT) capability as compared to other topologies [36]. When
a fault occurs in the grid, GSVSC provides reactive power support depending on the PCC voltage sag. Since
the power rating of GSVSC is constant, current delivered by GSVSC is ﬁxed. Generally GSVSC of Type 4
WTG are designed for 10% overloading capability [37]. Keller and Kroposki [38] depicts that, GSVSC can
provide fault current upto 1.25 p.u. During fault when reactive power delivery is ﬁrst priority, curtailment of
active power delivery to grid is needed. Since the generator is decoupled from the grid, it cannot see the fault
and hence generates active power which is same as pre-fault value. The excess active power which cannot
be delivered to the grid is dissipated through the chopper to maintain the DC link voltage and save the power
electronic components from heating up. When the fault is cleared, active power delivery to grid is restored
immediately.
During fault, reactive power delivery is speciﬁed by Transmission System Operator (TSO). As per the Danish
TSO Energinet.dk’s requirements [1], GSVSC control should be able to control the quadrature axis compo-
nent of current according to the voltage dip seen at the WT terminal. Figure 2-2 shows the reactive current
requirement curve during voltage dips. The reactive current control should follow the curve shown in the
ﬁgure, to provide the reactive power required to maintain PCC voltage during faults in the network. Total
reactive current can be limited to 1 p.u. of the nominal current output of Wind Power Plant (WPP). During

2.2
Distance Protection
Protective relaying deals with detection of abnormal conditions in power system and initiate control action
such that the system returns to normal condition as soon as possible [40]. Protective relays together with inter-
rupting devices such as circuit breakers ensures security and stability of power system and prevent blackouts.
Typically relay logic units consists of –
• Measurement units consisting of instrument transformers which continuously monitor system parameters
like voltage and current.
• Logic units which compares measured values with set reference values and decides if the abnormality seen
in the system corresponds to a fault.
• Action unit typically includes circuit breakers which are tripped by relay action for system protection.
An important aspect of designing protective relays is reliability which is deﬁned by IEEE Guide for Protective
Relay Applications to Transmission Lines [41] as “The ability of a protection system to operate correctly
taking into consideration both dependability and security.”. Dependability is deﬁned as the certainty that the
relay responds correctly to abnormal conditions that it is supposed to detect. On the other hand, security is
deﬁned as the certainty that the relay does not operate incorrectly for a fault which the relay is not supposed
to detect. Therefore it is difﬁcult to design a relay which is both dependable and secure. Dependability
depends on fast response of relay whereas security depends on accurate response of relay. As accuracy
cannot be guaranteed for fast responding relays, hence protective relay design gets biased on one of the two
aforementioned properties [42].
One of the following types of transmission line protection schemes is employed [43] depending on the func-
tion of the transmission line being protected, fault clearing speed required, type of circuit, etc.:
• Overcurrent relaying : Overcurrent relaying is the simplest form of relaying scheme which compares the
fault current with a preset reference value. Thus it works only when the minimum fault current exceeds

2.3
Summary
Operation of distance relay is based on measured impedance, i.e., ratio of voltage and current. Hence distance
relay performance is affected by available SCP in system. As more and more synchronous generators are
getting replaced by converter connected generations, availability of SCP is reducing. Converter connected
generations have limited short circuit current contribution which poses threat of misoperation of distance
relays. However presence of converter control gives ﬂexibility to control reactive power output during faults.
To study the effect of converter connected generations on SCP, Type 4 WTG topology is chosen. Back-
to-back VSC converters used decouples the stator from the grid. Generally space vector control is used to
control both converters. This control methodology allows fast and independent control of active and reactive
power output from WTG. Type 4 WTG can provide reactive power support depending on voltage drop at
PCC. However, the stator of WTG can not see the short circuit in the system due to complete decoupling of

on control strategy of Grid Side Converter (GSC). Considering over heating of power electronic components,
maximum short circuit current contribution from converter connected generations is 1.1-1.25 times.

Figure 3-3: Schematic diagram of PMSG with back-to-back NPC VSC
In this model, the PMSG used is a 2 MVA, 4 kV machine which is connected to a 2 MW WT. To transfer
power from PMSG to grid, back to back 3 level Neutral Point Clamped (NPC) VSCs are used. Both VSCs
are controlled using space vector control strategy [20]-[21]. A scalable interface transformer is used to scale
the model. Total 88 WTs are used to generate 163 MW power in this model. A shunt connected high pass
ﬁlter is used to ﬁlter the harmonics [23]. The chopper in the DC link is used to dissipate the surplus power
that cannot be supplied to the grid.
The PMSG is modelled in synchronously rotating dq reference frame. The stator ﬂux is aligned along d-
axis and q-axis leads it by 90 degrees. The main function of MSVSC is to optimize rotor speed to harvest
maximum energy from wind [23]. To attain this objective, MSVSC controls the stator current. The q-axis
component of stator current maintains the power requirement of WT. The d-axis component of stator current
controls the armature reaction ﬂux to maintain the reactive power at minimum (when output of machine is
<= 1 p.u.) [25]. As it is assumed in the simulations that the wind speed remains constant during the time of
simulation, control of MSVSC is not studied here. On the other hand due to complete decoupling of stator
from the grid, short circuit current contribution from WTG depends on control strategy of GSVSC. Therefore
the GSVSC is studied in details.
3.3.1
Grid Side Voltage Source Converter Control
The function of GSVSC is to maintain a constant DC-link voltage. Similarly to MSVSC, space vector control
strategy is also employed in GSVSC. The reference frame is oriented along the stator voltage vector position.
This enable independent control of active and reactive power ﬂow between the grid and the GSVSC. The
GSVSC control is shown in Figure 3-4. The parameters of the current control are described in Table 3.6.
The grid current is ﬁltered and decomposed into d and q axis components in this current control scheme as
shown in Figure 3-4. The d-axis current is used to control the DC link voltage and the q-axis current is used
to control the reactive power, hence the PCC voltage [25]. The PI controller, PI1 is used to generate reference
𝐼𝑑current. This is then compared with measured grid 𝐼𝑑and the error is passed to PI3. Similarly PI2 is used

3.3.3
Implementation of DSOGI-SRF PLL
For desired operation of WTG during system faults, the GSVSC control needs information about the grid
voltage for proper synchronisation with the grid. Performance of normal PLL under unbalanced conditions
and in presence of harmonics is not satisfactory [55]. In power system, SRF-PLL is most commonly used
technique for grid synchronisation [55]. SRF-PLL transforms the grid voltage into dq-reference frame. This
gives the dc value of the voltage, which makes it easy to control [55]. SRF-PLL is a frequency adaptive PLL
designed to perform well under balanced conditions without harmonics. When there is voltage unbalance and
distortions in the system, performance of SRF-PLL can be improved by using DSOGI-PLL. DSOGI-PLL uses
frequency adaptive positive sequence detection technique [32].

3.5
Summary
In this study, a low inertia, low SCP system with PMSG based Type 4 WTG is modelled in RSCAD to study
the performance of distance relay when available SCP is low in system. Detailed model of GSVSC is de-
signed where Danish grid code requirement for reactive current support during fault is implemented. Optimal
performance of GSVSC requires accurate information on grid side voltage phase and frequency. Thus dual
second order generalised integrator based SRF-PLL is used for grid synchronisation. Relay performance is
studied on mho type distance relay. For mho relay to generate trip signal three conditions are required to be
fulﬁlled – fault current should be higher than pickup current, measured impedance should be less than zone
reach setting and angle between operating and polarizing voltages should be less than characteristic angle.

Case Study : Effect of Reactive Current Rise Time for Single Fault in Modiﬁed
9-bus System with WTG
Different TSOs have different requirements for reactive current rise time. Generally, reactive current rise time
can vary from 10 ms to 150 ms [39]. Rise time calculation is inclusive of detection time. As the rise time
determines the rate of availability of reactive power from WTG, different rise time affects the PCC voltage.
If rise time is low, PCC voltage recovers from the initial voltage dip faster. As rise time increases, it takes
longer time for the PCC to recover from voltage dip. This can pose problems if the SCR of the system is very
low, for e.g., in cases where WTGs are connected to weak grid with long transmission lines. In such cases
there is only reactive power available from WTG to recover PCC voltage.
In this study the rise time is varied from 20 ms to 100 ms to study its effect on distance protection. Figure
4-16a and 4-16b shows reactive current and reactive power response for different rise times. With fast rise
time, reactive power reaches its steady state value faster. Figure 4-17 shows response of PCC voltage for
different rise times. When rise time is high, reactive power support from WTG is slow; resulting in increase
in voltage dip at PCC. However the steady state value is same in all cases. Therefore, in order to maintain
better voltage proﬁle in low inertia, low SCP systems, faster reactive current rise time is required.

Summary
In this chapter, performance of distance relay is studied and simulation results for various case studies are
presented. Weak power system with low SCP can have an impact on distance relay performance since fault
current seen by the relay decrease during fault. When a fault occurs in the system, PCC voltage drops initially.
Though due to action of GSVSC control PCC voltage recovers, locus of fault impedance for fault in zone 2,
moves closer towards zone 1 setting of relay. The locus is further affected when there is multiple fault in the
system. When a synchronous generator or a part of the system is disconnected due to a fault, short circuit

Reactive power output from WTG solely depends on capacity of GSVSC. Wind speed affects active power
output of WTG but not reactive power output. During fault, reactive power support from WTG is ﬁrst priority.
To provide maximum reactive power, active power output from WTG has to be curtailed during fault. Thus

Chapter 5
RTDS Runtime Control using MATLAB
This chapter describes how user can control RTDS simulation from an external application - MATLAB - by sending and
receiving data to simulation from the external program and vice-versa.
5.1
Introduction
RTDS simulations can be connected to external programs such as MATLAB or C# applications such that user
can control simulations from the external program. User can not only send data from the external application
to RTDS, but can also receive the simulated results back into the external program. This gives users ﬂexibility
to compare and process data received from various simulations using the external program. Simulations in
RTDS Runtime can be automated using script functions. One such script function called “ListenOnPort()”
can be used to control RSCAD simulations by an external program. The program can send script commands
to RSCAD over TCP/IP connection [61]. The syntax used for “ListenOnPort()” script command is
ListenOnPort(int portNumber, int passErrorsBack)
On execution of “ListenOnPort()” command, the RSCAD runtime acts as a TCP server. The server listens to
the port speciﬁed by the portNumber in the command. If passErrorsBack attribute is set “true” then errors are
passed back through the socket communication while execution of script continues. The external program
acts like a socket client, i.e., it can connect to the server at the speciﬁed port. On establishing connection
between the server and the client, a TCP stream is commenced between RSCAD Runtime and the external
program. Script commands can be read from this TCP connection. The connection between RSCAD and
the external program is maintained until the port is closed. The data ﬂow is bi-directional hence RSCAD
Runtime can also provide its own data back to the client.
At a time only one script command is read by RSCAD Runtime and executed consequently. As execution time
between the external program and RSCAD Runtime differs, it is needed to maintain synchronism between
the two. For this purpose “ListenOnPortHandshake()” script command is used. The syntax used is
ListenOnPortHandshake(string sequenceString)
The parameter “sequenceString” acts as a token which is exchanged between RSCAD Runtime and the ex-
ternal program. This script command can only be sent by an external program which has a pre established
TCP/IP connection using the “ListenOnPort()” command. Once the external program has send the token
string using “ListenOnPortHandshake()” command, the external program waits. RSCAD Runtime on the
other hand, echos the string back to the external program when this command is executed. Once the external
program receives back the token string, it resumes execution of further commands. This ensures synchroni-
sation of of execution between RSCAD Rutime and the external program. This command can also be used to
send data from RSCAD Rutime to the external program.

5. RTDS Runtime Control using MATLAB
5.2
RTDS Runtime Connection with MATLAB M ﬁle
To control the simulations of this study, communication between RSCAD Runtime and MATLAB is estab-
lished. RSCAD simulations are controlled by executing a MATLAB M ﬁle. The M ﬁle contains all the
necessary script functions required to control the simulation in Runtime. The M ﬁle requires an external
library ﬁle called “JTCP.m” for necessary I/O functions required for TCP communications. Both the library
and the M ﬁle for external control of simulation needs to be placed in the same directory.
All components that can be controlled in RSCAD Runtime environment, can be controlled using MATLAB.
This includes all sliders, switches, dials, push buttons, draft variables etc. can be controlled from MAT-
LAB. All outputs from meters, plots etc. can be send back from RSCAD Runtime to MATLAB for further
processing.
5.2.1
RSCAD Runtime Script
For MATLAB to communicate is RSCAD Runtime, “ListenOnPort()” script command has to be executed by
Runtime. Once the script is executed then Runtime awaits communication from the speciﬁed port. Thus to
connect RSCAD and MATLAB, ﬁrstly a small script is executed in Runtime.
string temp_string;
fprintf(stdmsg,“Initialization of RTDS simulation”);
ListenOnPort(4575,“true”);
fprintf(stdmsg,“Execution of script is done”);
The temp_string variable holds the token string generated from RSCAD Runtime and is transmitted to MAT-
LAB when “ListenOnPortHandshake()” is executed. Lines 3 and 5 of the script are used to print text message
in the “Message Area” of RSCAD Runtime environment. On execution of line 4 of the script, port 4575
opens and RSCAD acts like a TCP server waiting for communication at the open port. Therefore any further
execution of the script is blocked by this command. Next line of the script is not executed until “ClosePort()”
command is received from MATLAB.
5.2.2
MATLAB M File
To connect with RSCAD Runtime a connection request is send from MATLAB as following
JTCPOBJ = jtcp('REQUEST','127.0.0.1', portnum);
The port number used here should match with the port number in RSCAD Runtime script. The IP address
provided is the IP address of the TCP server. In this demonstration localhost IP is used as it is considered that
both client and server runs on the same machine. Location of client can be anywhere in a network as long as
communication can be set up between server and client.
“Start” and “stop” script commands are used to start and stop simulation respectively.
jtcp('writes',JTCPOBJ,'Start;');
jtcp('writes',JTCPOBJ,'stop;');
Scripts can be used to control the different input components like switches, sliders, dials, push buttons etc.
An example is shown below to demonstrate how a slider named “FLTDUR” in subsystem 2 of the RSCAD
Draft ﬁle is set to a value specieciﬁed by the variable “faultDuration”.
msg1 = sprintf('SetSlider "Subsystem #2 : CTLs : Inputs : FLTDUR" = %f;',faultDuration);
jtcp('writes', JTCPOBJ, msg1);

To update plots from MATLAB, “UpdatePlots” script command is used. To save the plot data such that it can
be read from MATLAB, “SavePlot” command is used. The variable “ﬁlename” speciﬁes the name of the ﬁle
into which the data is saved. “Id n Iq” speciﬁes the name of the plot in RSCAD Runtime environment that is

The ﬁlename.out ﬁle contains output data which can be read from MATLAB.
msg1 = sprintf('SavePlot "Id n Iq", "%s";',filename);
jtcp('writes', JTCPOBJ, msg1);
In order to read the data from MATLAB, execution of RSCAD Runtime and MATLAB has to be synchro-
nised. For this synchronisation “ListenOnPortHandshake()” is used. The code snippet used for synchronisa-
tion purpose is shown below.
jtcp('writes',JTCPOBJ,'temp_string = "sequence1";');
jtcp('writes',JTCPOBJ,'ListenOnPortHandshake(temp_string);');
rmsg = jtcp('read',JTCPOBJ);
rmsg2 = char(rmsg);
while (isempty(strfind(rmsg2, 'sequence1')) == 1)
rmsg = jtcp('read',JTCPOBJ);
rmsg2 = char(rmsg);
end
“ListenOnPortHandshake()” command sends the token string “sequence1” to RSCAD Runtime. MATLAB
then continues to read the incoming string from RSCAD Runtime until the token string “sequence1” is echoed
back by RSCAD Runtime. When the token string is received, MATLAB continues further execution of
commands. Once the execution is synchronised, the ﬁlename.out ﬁle can be imported to MATLAB using
“importdata” command.
To terminate the communication channel between the two applications, the port needs to be closed using the
following code;
msg2 = sprintf('ClosePort(%d);', portnum);
jtcp('writes',JTCPOBJ,msg2);
At the end of simulation, all data generated and saved in *.out ﬁles are imported to MATLAB and saved in a
*.mat ﬁle.

3. RSCAD Runtime script is loaded from “Script/Open” menu. When the script is started using “Run Script”
button, RSCAD Runtime becomes a TCP server and awaits signal at the speciﬁed port.
4. Next the M ﬁle is run from MATLAB.
5. Bi-directional communication between RTDS and MATLAB is now established.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
This chapter summarizes and concludes this study. Recommendations and future work are also suggested.
6.1
Conclusions
This thesis performs study of distance relay response in low inertia, low SCP system with high penetration of
converter connected generations, during multiple faults in the system.
The main conclusions of this thesis are as following:
• Distance relay performance is affected by low short circuit current in low inertia, low SCP system.
• Due to increased voltage dip during faults in low inertia, low SCP systems, initial fault impedance estimated
by distance relay moves closer to zone 1 setting of relay even for zone 2 faults.
• Fault response of distance relay is not affected by wind speed.
– Short circuit current contribution depends on GSVSC capacity. Since during fault, reactive current con-
tribution from GSVSC is dominant, variation in active power due to wind speed does not affect short
circuit current contribution from WTG. Moreover, reactive current output during fault is guided by net-
work codes.
• Fast reactive current rise time is essentially required for desirable relay performance in low inertia, low
SCP system. Therefore, GSVSC controller parameters must be tuned for fast reactive current response.
• If the system is already stressed due to unavailability of enough SCP, slow reactive current rise time fol-
lowing a fault can cause relay to trip falsely in zone 1 for zone 2 faults.
– With increase in reactive current rise time, rise in reactive power output becomes slower. This causes
larger voltage dips at PCC voltage. Since distance relay measures ratio of voltage and current, lower
voltages cause fault impedance locus to move closer to zone 1 setting of relay.
• MATLAB interface with RSCAD provides better control, ﬂexibility, analysis of simulation and data.
Future Work
• Relay performance has been studied here for 3 phase faults. Effect of control strategy and controller
parameters used for study of unbalanced faults on relay performance can be of interest.
59
==PAGE:60==
6. Conclusions
• Analytical relation between impedance seen by the relay and the converter parameters can be established.
This can enable in determining optimal PI control parameters for reactive power control at PCC, which in
turn will affect the impedance measured by relay during faults. Therefore false tripping of distance relay
for zone 2 faults can be prevented using proper GSVSC setting.
• Studies on solutions to provide the required SCP in low inertia, low SCP system should be performed.
Possibilities of obtaining SCP from synchronous condensers and other Flexible AC Transmission System
(FACTS) devices should be studied.
• Distance relay used in this study responds to fault in a predetermined manner. In future low SCP systems,
use of adaptive relaying technique can be used such that the relay adapts to available SCP in the system.
