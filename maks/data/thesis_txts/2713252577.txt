Abstract 
 
Given the fast growth of economies and the reduction of our planet’s limited resources, 
sustainability has become a mantra for the 21st century. This undeniable necessity for sustainable 
development implies the involvement of national and international organizations. Truly, disclosing 
non-financial information supports measuring, monitoring and managing undertakings’ 
performance and their impact on society, allowing more transparency on corporate environmental 
and social matters.  
In the European context, since 2014, companies within the scope of the Non-Financial Reporting 
Directive 2014/95/EU have been compelled to publish non-financial information. This supports 
stakeholders assessing companies’ value creation and impacts, and encourages society to take 
responsibility for social and environmental issues. However, despite the supplemental directive’s 
guidelines on non-financial reporting introduced in June 2017 and its supplement on climate-
related information in June 2019, there has not been a sufﬁcient improvement in the quality, 
quantity and relevance of this information. 
As a result, the European Commission is currently discussing a new Corporate Sustainability 
Reporting Directive (CSRD) proposal. This directive will require companies to disclose more 
detailed information on their impacts on the environment and society, and the sustainability risks 
and opportunities affecting the company, through reporting standards that foster comparability, 
relevance and reliability. The European Commission is aiming to have this directive applied from 
January 2024, with reports due in 2025.  
Thus, the purpose of this thesis was to investigate the readiness of large EU companies currently 
reporting on Corporate Social Responsibility topics for the upcoming CSRD, as well as the 
challenges in successfully integrating the disclosure changes of the projected CSRD on social 
matters. Indeed, the approach adopted in this thesis began with understanding what the CSRD's 
new disclosure requirements on the social topic would be. Afterwards, a sample of large EU 
companies from the Health Care & Medical Devices industry, Transportations and Logistic 
industry and Banking industry was analysed through the lens of the new CSRD disclosure 
requirements in their most recent Corporate Sustainability Reports (CSR) and annual reports. 
From this, a potential disclosing gap was identified. Indeed, this study showed that companies’ 
CSR mostly missed information regarding their sustainability risks and opportunities, along with 
the process of identifying and mitigating them. Moreover, companies also presented great 
difficulty in disclosing on workers in the value chain and affected communities, as it was harder 
to get access to detailed information on these stakeholders. It was concluded that companies find 
the new CSRD disclosure requirements too granular and, as a result, counter-productive to its 
goal. Based on this gap, recommendations on what companies could do to prepare for the future 
CSRD were made. Firstly, it is crucial that companies continuously follow the updates from the 
EU Commission and EFRAG on these matters, to be able to prepare the adapting strategy to 
these new requirements in advance. Furthermore, as these new standards are built on existing 
international frameworks and initiatives on sustainability reporting, following these also confers 
an advantage to the companies. This way, it is vital that companies start to deeply invest in their 
sustainability departments or outsourced services, so that the required assessment and data 
gathering processes are established and all EU disclosure requirements are complied with.  
Hence, this study helps companies anticipate and prepare for the upcoming CSRD changes. 
Indeed, fostering better sustainability reporting leads to more transparency in the companies’ 
value. This enables more sustainable investment, contributing to the Sustainable Finance Action 
Plan and the European Green Deal, which are cornerstones for sustainable development in 
Europe.

1. Introduction 
 
Due to the fast growth of economies, the connectivity technologies, and the fast reduction of our 
planet’s limited resources, the concept of Sustainable Development has risen as the most relevant 
principle in current times. In 1987, the United Nations Brundtland Commission defined 
Sustainable Development as “development that meets the needs of the present without 
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” [1] Indeed, Sustainable 
Development is a fundamental and overarching goal of the European Union, which aims 
to constantly improve the quality of life and well-being of present and future generations by 
connecting economic growth, environmental protection, and social justice. 
This undeniable necessity for sustainable development implies the involvement of national and 
international organizations. Truly, disclosing non-financial information supports measuring, 
monitoring and managing undertakings’ performance and their impact on society, allowing more 
transparency on corporate environmental and social matters. 
In the European context, since 2014, companies within the scope of the Non-Financial Reporting 
Directive 2014/95/EU (NFRD) [2] have been compelled to publish non-financial information. 
Hence, this directive aims to make non-financial information available to stakeholders and 
investors to assess companies’ value creation, opportunities and risks, and to encourage society 
to take responsibility for social and environmental issues. The non-financial information required 
to be disclosed under this directive concerns four issues: environmental, social and employee 
matters, human rights, bribery and corruption. Within each issue, five areas are covereed: 
business model, policies, the outcome of those policies, risk management, and key performance 
indicators (KPI), following the double material approach.  
However, despite the complementary directive’s guidelines on non-financial reporting introduced 
in June 2017 and its supplement on climate-related information in June 2019, there has not been 
a sufﬁcient improvement in the quality, quantity and relevance of this information, as referred by 
the European Commission [2]  In fact, there are several stakeholders interested in these 
improvements, both users and report preparers. Policy makers and environmental 
organizations use 
this 
information 
when monitoring environmental 
and 
social 
trends, 
for environmental accounting and to inform public policies. Non-governmental organizations and 
labor unions want companies to be held more accountable for their social and environmental 
impacts, and investors want deeper knowledge of the risks and opportunities that the 
sustainability matters formulate and the impacts of their investments on people and the 
environment [3]  In reality, most companies do not report non-financial information, and those that 
report some sustainability information, often do not report the relevant one. When the relevant 
information is in fact reported, it is often found not to be sufficiently reliable or comparable, hard 
to find by users and not in a machine-readable digital format [4] Indeed, this information’s 
demands have been increasing significantly in recent years, and will almost certainly continue to 
do so. This is due not only to an increasing awareness from investors in the co-relation between 
sustainability issues and financial performance, but also due to a growing market for investments 
that expressly pursue to follow sustainable standards. Yet another factor is regulation, such as 
the EU Taxonomy Regulation1 and the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR)2, that 
require more sustainable information from asset managers and financial advisors regarding 
investee companies [4] Additionally, companies preparing the reports are also struggling with 
meeting multiple and sometimes confusing external expectations, both from global initiatives and 
stakeholders [5] All the above represents a risk not just to financial stability, but also to the

Sustainable Finance Action Plan’s goal, as investments may not be oriented to sustainable 
businesses, to the European Green Deal 3 and to the achievement of the UN Sustainable 
Development Goals.  
Thus, the evolution of this reporting system is necessary and it is currently under discussion at 
the European Commission through a new Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (hereafter 
CSRD) proposal [4]. This directive will require companies to disclose more detailed information 
on their impacts on the environment and society, and on the sustainability risks and opportunities 
affecting the company through reporting standards fostering comparability. Indeed, there are 
notable changes such as the expansion of the current scope to all large companies and listed 
companies in the EU market (from 11 000 to 49 000 companies), the report under new disclosures 
requirements, the obligation of auditing the information reported and the introduction of a digital 
categorisation system for the financial statements and management report (so that the 
sustainability information universe is incorporated in the European Single Access Point4). The 
European Commission postponed the implementation of this directive and is currently aiming to 
have this directive applied from January 2024, with reports due in 2025.  
Indeed, the CSRD aims to develop on and collaborate with international sustainability reporting 
initiatives, starting with Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), the Climate Disclosure Standards Board 
(CDSB), Value Reporting Foundation (VRF) and the UN Guiding Principles Reporting Framework 
to build clear disclosure requirements, having relevant EU legislation such as the Taxonomy 
Regulation and the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation as a cornerstone.   
The European Project Task Force on Non-Financial Reporting Standards under the European 
Financial Reporting Advisory Group (EFRAG) 5  is responsible for developing the proposed 
draft standards. In the end of April 2022, the Project Task Force on European Sustainability 
Reporting Standards (PTF-ESRS) of EFRAG released ESRS exposure drafts on the first set of 
disclosure requirements and opened it for consultation.  
 
1.1. 
Purpose Statement and Research Goal 
 
As a result of previous requirements from the NFRD, most companies are already disclosing 
some information on the four issues described above through established KPI assessment 
methodologies. However, the European Commission's ambitions for the future evolution of CSR  
are further from what companies are currently disclosing. Thus, the qualitative and quantitative 
information to be assessed and the strategies used have also to be upgraded [2].  
This way, the aim of this master thesis is to investigate the readiness of large EU companies 
currently reporting on ESG topics for the upcoming CSRD, and the difficulty present in integrating 
the disclosure changes of the projected CSRD on social matters successfully.  
Previous investigation on this topic is limited due to the novelty of the CSRD topic at the present 
time of development of this thesis. As mentioned above, the official directive standards have not 
been published yet, and neither has a fitness check been performed, thus insights on this topic is 
very reduced.

Indeed, the approach adopted in this thesis goes through firstly understanding what will be the 
new disclosure requirements on the social topic from the CSRD. Secondly, a meaningful sample 
of large EU companies from different industries will be analyzed through their latest Corporate 
Sustainability Report (CSR) and annual reports under the lens of the new CSRD disclosure 
requirements. From this point, a potential disclosure gap could be identified and, thus, 
recommendations on what companies could do to adapt to the upcoming CSRD could be

The problem statement formulation of this master thesis has been established based on the 
general context presented above. It outlines the core question that will be addressed throughout 
the thesis.  
Hence, the purpose of this qualitative study is to investigate large EU companies’ readiness for 
the new upcoming CSRD regarding the social topic 
The state of “readiness” for this master thesis is defined through the gap between the current 
CSR disclosure status on the social topic by large EU companies, and the future CSRD 
requirements. 
Based on the general context of the master thesis, three research questions (RQ) have been 
established to create the foundation for further investigation of this master thesis: 
• 
RQ1: What will be the social disclosure requirements streaming from the new CSRD? 
• 
RQ2: What is the gap level between the current disclosures reported by large EU 
companies on their CSR and the future CSRD requirements? 
• 
RQ3: What recommendations can be given to companies to adapt to the upcoming

2. Research Methodology 
 
2.1. 
Research Strategy 
 
In this master thesis, the methodology adopted was primarily influenced by the research goal and 
by the sequential nature of the research questions, where each research question depends on 
the previous one. 
Within the research questions, a cross-sectional time horizon was established for the case study. 
In contrast to a longitudinal time study, a cross-sectional research design focuses on data 
collecting at a specific point in time [6]  The major contributor to this decision was the time horizon 
available to conduct the entire study. 
For this thesis, a sample case study of nine companies was selected to analyze the readiness of 
large EU companies regarding the new CSRD. Three health care and medical devices 
companies, three integrated transportation and logistics companies and three banking and 
financial services companies were chosen. These companies will be presented anonymously with 
respect to information privacy. Only one company from each industry was focused and 
collaborated actively (hereby primary companies). Besides having their latest CSR and annual 
report analysed, their CSR expert was also contacted for an interview. The selection of primary 
companies was based on the number of available large EU companies from Deloitte’s portfolio 
and from own research who were (i) under the scope of the NFRD and CSRD and (ii) had the 
resources and willingness to collaborate. The remaining companies were considered secondary 
companies, where only their latest CSR and annual reports were analysed without the 
accompanying interview. These companies were used to enhance the accuracy of the 
extrapolations made for the industries. They were selected based on their Bloomberg Social

In order to investigate research question 1 (RQ1), a qualitative data analysis and display approach 
was adopted on the social and cross-cutting ESRS Exposure Drafts. This was performed in 
parallel with an investigation on which international frameworks and legislation the disclosure 
requirements were based on. As described by Saunders [6], the data analysis and display 
approach include three steps: Data reduction, where the data is transformed and condensed; 
data display, where the data is organised and assembled into a visual display; and drawing 
conclusions. Indeed, this approach fits the goal of RQ1. The ESRS Exposure Drafts referenced 
above will be summarized and the disclosure requirements will be clustered by main thematics, 
allowing relationships between them to be established. Furthermore, this analysis was made with 
an inductive approach, where the codes arose during the analysis of the standards. These codes 
would later assist in the data analysis performed for RQ2, as they provided pre-defined key-topics 
to be focused on. It was ensured that the codes stayed meaningful in relation to the data as well 
as in relation to other codes. From this analysis, there were two out-puts documents. The first 
was a relationship map of the disclosure requirements clustered by main thematics covered. The 
second was a summary of all disclosure requirements on the social topic and cross-cutting 
standards, organized by ESRS. This contained the detailed disclosing points for each disclosure 
requirement and the respective cornerstone frameworks and legislation, which would contribute 
to answering RQ2 as explained below.  
Furthermore, to answer research question 2 (RQ 2), a content analysis under the lens of the new 
CSRD was performed on the latest CSR and Annual report from the selected large EU 
companies. This research method is used for determining the occurrence of specific words, 
thematics, or concepts in qualitative data and their meaning [6]. This analysis was supported by 
the codes that arose from the disclosure requirement’s analysis when answering RQ1.  
Although the standards used in the gap analysis are only exposure drafts conditioned by 
subsequent consultation, as it is the only available material at the time of the development of this 
thesis, they will be presented with a semi-binding nature whose limitations will be later discussed 
in Chapter 8.

Furthermore, this analysis is a 2-layer analysis that firstly focuses on the companies’ readiness 
through a holistic challenge. This takes into count both the disclosure requirements (DR) that are 
already disclosed and the ones that are missing, and a second layer that narrows into the actual 
challenge of closing the disclosing gap, which focuses only on the missing DR (cf. Figure 2). 
 
 
  
 
 
 
 
This analysis was performed using the disclosure requirements summary previously made, where 
each required disclosing point was checked to be present or missing on the company’s CSR and 
Annual Report. It can be noted that each disclosure requirement is composed of several 
disclosing points. Afterwards, a preliminary Challenge Level between 1 (low challenge) and 3 
(high challenge) for gathering the information needed to meet each missing disclosing point was 
assigned. In the case where the disclosing point was present in the CSR or Annual report, it was 
assigned a Challenge Level of 0, representing no challenge. On the topical standards (where the 
rebuttable presumption later explained can be applied), disclosing points that were not referenced 
as material by the company, and thus are not reported in the CSR, were assigned with Challenge 
1, connected to the activity of justifying why that topic is not material in the CSR. This information 
comes from the materiality assessment. 
Furthermore, the preliminary Challenge Level was assigned, taking into count: 
1) If the disclosure requirement had points that were already being required by EU 
legislation (NFRD), EU regulation (SFRD) or IFRS6, or if it was referred on international 
reporting frameworks, guidelines and principles (e.g.: GRI, OECD).  
2) Level of accessibility to that missing information by the CSR’s responsible (as they are 
the ones developing the CSR)  
This way, a Holistic Challenge Level could be calculated for each disclosure requirement, and 
thus a Holistic Challenge Level for the company could be determined. This Holistic Challenge 
Level reflects the readiness (or not) of a company regarding the new CSRD, where the higher the 
challenge, the less ready the company is ( lower Readiness Level ) .  
This analysis was then complemented and validated by an expert from each of the primary 
companies through a questionnaire and a follow-up electronic interview to discuss the answers 
provided.

3.1. 
Corporate Sustainability  
 
The literature suggests that there is no agreement on what constitutes corporate sustainability, 
with authors defining it in various ways. One illustrative definition consists on “adopting business 
strategies and activities that meet the needs of the enterprise and its stakeholders today while 
protecting, sustaining, and enhancing the human and natural resources that will be needed in the 
future” from ISSD [11]  Van Marrewijk [12] states that corporate sustainability refers to 
“demonstrating the inclusion of social and environmental concerns in business operations and in 
interactions with stakeholders”.  
As Wilson [13] argues, the concept of corporate sustainability incorporates elements from four 
established concepts: 1) sustainable development, 2) corporate social responsibility, 3) 
stakeholder theory, and 4) corporate accountability theory. A visualization is shown below in 
Figure 3: Elements of Corporate Sustainability [13] 
 
Figure 3: Elements of Corporate Sustainability [13] 
Sustainable development is a wide term that finds a balance between the need for economic 
growth and environmental protection, as well as social equity, with the influence of politics and 
laws. The term was first popularized in 1987 by the World Commission for Environment and 
Development in their book “Our Common Future” (WCED). The WCED described sustainable 
development as “development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the 
ability of future generations to meet their own needs” [14]. They recognized that achieving 
sustainable development could not be left solely to government regulators and policymakers and 
that the corporates played an important role. Wilson [20], argues that they must be more proactive 
in this drive towards social equity and environmental protection, partly because they are 
responsible for some of the unsustainable problems, but also because they have access to the 
resources needed to address them. 
Stuer [15] defends that although sustainable development is a social concept, it is increasingly 
being applied to corporations as “corporate sustainability”. Dyllick and Hockerts [16] go further, 
stating that when transposing this concept to the business level, corporate sustainability can thus 
be defined as “meeting the needs of a firm's direct and indirect stakeholders (such as 
shareholders, employees, clients, communities etc.), without compromising its ability to meet the 
needs of future stakeholders”. Like sustainable development, it has all three pillars creating a 
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
triple-bottom-line: economic efficiency, environmental performance and social equity, and these 
three dimensions interact.  
Similar to sustainable development, Wilson [13] refers to corporate social responsibility as a broad 
concept. Archie B. Carroll [17] draws a four-part definition, stated initially as: “corporate social 
responsibility encompasses the economic, legal, ethical, and discretionary (philanthropic) 
expectations that society has of organizations at a given point in time”. Carroll defined the first 
part as an economic responsibility, which includes, providing a return on investment to 
shareholders, creating jobs and fair pay for workers, discovering new resources and promoting 
innovation. The second regards legal responsibility, which entails society’s expectation on 
business to accomplish its economic mission within legal requirements. This connects with the 
third part, ethical responsibility. In this topic, Salomon [18] believes that this commitment must be 
seen as a proactive duty. That is, laws should not be seen as merely lined and where businesses 
can go round and cross if the punishment is low. Instead, responsible businesses recognize the 
regulations as a societal good and make good faith efforts to follow not just the law but also the 
principle of the rules. The last part is the philanthropic responsibility, in which corporations have 
the most discretionary judgment and option in terms of choosing on particular activities or 
charitable donations aimed at giving back to society. This way, corporate social responsibility 
contributes to company sustainability by providing ethical reasons for managers to work toward 
long-term development. 
The third element that corporate sustainability incorporates is the Stakeholder Theory of the firm. 
It is a concept first popularized by Freeman [19], who defines a stakeholder as “any group or 
individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organization's objectives”. Even 
though different stakeholder groups may and often do have different goals and priorities, Wilson 
[13] believes that goals such as economic stability, environmental preservation and social justice 
are shared between most stakeholders, even if in different priority levels. The contribution of 
stakeholder theory to corporate sustainability is the addition of business arguments as to why 
corporations should work towards sustainable development. According to stakeholder theory, 
working in this direction is in the company's best economic interest since it will enhance its 
connection with stakeholders, assisting the company in fulfilling its business objectives. 
The fourth and last element is Corporate Accountability. Wilson [13] defines accountability as “the 
legal or ethical responsibility to provide an account of the actions for which one is held 
responsible”. This theory contributes to company sustainability by defining the nature of the 
interaction between corporate leaders and the rest of society. It also explains why businesses 
should report on their environmental, social, and economic performance, rather than simply their 
financial performance. 
Indeed, corporate sustainability recognizes the importance of growth and profitability, but it also 
requires the company to achieve societal goals, particularly those related to sustainable 
development, such as environmental protection, social justice and equity, and economic 
development. 
 
3.2. 
Corporate Sustainability Reporting 
 
Regarding Corporate Sustainability Reporting, there are several definitions in published literature, 
without settlement on a universally accepted one. One representative definition is given by the 
World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD), which defines sustainable 
development reports as “public reports by companies to provide internal and external 
stakeholders with a picture of the corporate position and activities on economic, environmental 
and social dimensions” [20]. Boston College Center for Corporate Citizenship [21] defines 
Sustainability Reporting as the “disclosure and communication of environmental, social, and 
governance (ESG) goals as well as a company's progress towards achieving them”.  
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
As defended by Searcy and Buslovich [22], a company's commitment to sustainability is 
frequently reflected through a well-developed sustainability plan with goals, targets, and 
performance indicators. These goals, plans, targets and performances as well as the outcomes 
are made available to the public in the form of sustainability reports. In practice, several different 
terms may be utilized to allude to these reports, such as “corporate sustainability report”, 
“sustainability report” and "corporate social responsibility report”. Regardless the term chosen, 
the key is that these reports provide qualitative and quantitative data on the company's most 
important economic, environmental, and social concerns that are of interest to a variety of 
stakeholders. This way, it can identify and reduce risks, seize new opportunities, understand the 
companies' impact on the environment and society and take action towards becoming a 
responsible, trusted organization in a more sustainable world, as mentioned by the GRI [23].  
The majority of sustainability reporting is still done on a voluntary basis and by large companies. 
However, Searcy and Buslovich [22] state that an increasing number of countries are introducing 
regulatory reporting requirements on sustainability issues. This is being accompanied by a 
gradual tendency for companies to provide more information on their sustainability impacts in 
their annual reports to satisfy shareholders and customers, who are seeking more sustainability-
driven business decisions. As this demand goes up, so does the importance of creating quality 
sustainability reports for stakeholders. This requires stricter requirements, more reliable data 
systems and independent assurance.  
As Brown [24] alludes, the research of trends in sustainability reporting has been active. With 
time, there has been a diversification of potential target audiences, more integration with financial 
reports, and sharpened regional disparities in reporting and verification processes.  
From the literature, there are many approaches to the process of building a sustainability report. 
From PwC [25] and Gittell et al. [26] approaches, a proposed synthesized illustration of its main 
steps is found bellow: 
1. Define performance goals and metrics: Define the organization's goal in sustainability, 
and their target audience, overview their strategy and key activities, arising risks and 
opportunities along with the development of the KPI and targets used to measure the 
progress towards those goals; 
 
2. Measure performance: Gather, validate and store the required data. 
 
3. Evaluate performance: Transform raw data into actionable performance information and 
knowledge so that businesses can make better decisions. 
 
4. Reporting and Communication: Diffuse information to stakeholders in a form that they 
can understand results, their effects and what actions are needed to be taken. 
 
5. Manage Performance: React to sustainability performance with all the basic 
management 
functions: 
planning, 
organizing, 
controlling, 
and 
leading. 
 
6. Assurance: Improve the credibility of the content reported by referring to independent 
studies or external third-party assurance. 
Various standard frameworks exist and there is no universal method of external 
communication of sustainability performance. Until now, most businesses have relied on 
corporate sustainability reporting guidelines to provide a framework for their reports. 
Standards enable meaningful comparisons between sustainability information reported by 
different organizations using the same standard. Harvard Business Review [27] states that at 
least five well-known sustainability reporting frameworks and standards are available, both in 
the EU and international, supported by trustworthy organizations and with respected 
individuals on their Board. 
 
==PAGE:18==
11 
 
 
• 
CDP – Carbon Disclosure Project 
 
CDP is a non-profit organization with a global disclosure system enabling investors, 
businesses, cities, governments, and regions to manage their environmental impacts 
[28]. 
 
• 
CDSB – Climate Disclosure Standards Board 
 
CDSB is an international group of business and environmental non-governmental 
organizations. They are dedicated to advancing and aligning the worldwide mainstream 
corporate reporting model so that natural capital and financial capital are addressed. They 
accomplish so by providing businesses with a framework for reporting environmental 
information with the same precision as financial information. Consequently, this helps to 
provide investors with decision-relevant environmental information via the mainstream 
corporate report, leading to more efficient capital allocation [29]. 
 
• 
GRI – Global Reporting Initiative 
 
The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) is an international non-profit organisation providing 
a framework that aims to allow all organisations to report their economic, environmental, 
social and governance performance. It produces free Sustainability Reporting Guidelines 
[6]. 
 
• 
IIRC – International Integrated Reporting Council 
 
The IIRC Framework is a framework to set up guiding principles and elements for an 
integrated report. It enhances an integrated representation of a company's performance 
in financial and other non-financial relevant information [31]. 
 
• 
SASB – Sustainability Accounting Standards Board 
 
SASB was founded as a non-profit organization in 2011 to assist businesses and 
investors developing a shared language about the financial impacts of sustainability. This 
is achieved through standards developed for 77 industries identifying a group of 
environmental, social, and governance (ESG) issues most relevant to financial 
performance in each industry [32]  While the GRI Standards promote broad and 
comprehensive disclosures in order to gain a full understanding of an organization's 
economic, environmental, and societal impacts, SASB standards provide an industry-
focused perspective on a subset of financially material issues.  
In 2021, IIRC and the SASB officially announced their merger to form the Value Reporting 
Foundation (VRF). 
• 
United Nations Guiding Principles Reporting Framework 
 
This is the first complete guide for businesses to report on human rights issues in 
accordance with their responsibility to respect human rights. This responsibility is 
established in the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, representing 
the authoritative global standard in this field. The responsibility to respect human rights is 
neither a totally voluntary standard since it represents a worldwide expectation of 
business nor requires corporations to sign up to it as they would for a standard based on 
initiative membership. The UNGPs are considered soft law because, while the document 
itself is not legally binding, it contains a normative expectation of all businesses, 
==PAGE:19==
12 
 
regardless of size, industry or location, to prevent and address human-related 
consequences [33]. 
• 
Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) 
 
Recommendations adopted by governments to multinational enterprises operating in or 
from adhering countries. They offer non-binding principles and standards for responsible 
business conduct in a global context consistent with applicable laws and internationally 
recognised standards, across a range of issues such as human rights, labour rights, and 
the environment [34].  
• 
Charter of Fundamental Rights of the EU 
 
Created in 2000, it sets up the fundamental rights of EU citizens. It was established to 
bring consistency and clarity to the rights set up at various periods and in distinct ways in 
individual EU Member States [20]. 
Each standard addresses a different set of issues, ranging from narrow (an exclusive 
focus on greenhouse gas emissions, for example) to broad (all of ESG or the entire range 
of UN Sustainable Development Goals). The standards cater to a different set of 
stakeholders (primarily investors) to broader groups, such as customers, employees, and 
society as a whole. 
 
Besides these organizations, in 2022, the International Financial Reporting Standard 
(IFRS)7 announced the establishment of the International Sustainability Standards 
Board (ISSB). The ISSB's goal is to develop standards for corporate sustainability 
reporting that will be implemented universally internationally and serve as the foundation 
for national legislation, named IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards (ISSB 
Standards) [35]  The ISSB will collaborate with and build on the work of existing investor-
focused reporting initiatives such as the CDSB, the Task Force for Climate-related 
Financial Disclosures (TCFD), the VRF in order to become the global standard-setter for 
financial market sustainability disclosures. As these standards are strongly based on 
frameworks such as SASB, they describe materiality from a financial perspective and are 
mainly aimed at investors. As with the IASB's Accounting Standards 8 , it is up to 
jurisdictional authorities to decide whether the ISSB's standards must be applied. The 
standards have not been published by the time of the development of this master thesis, 
only exposure drafts by March 2022.  
 
Unquestionably, sustainability reporting is a topic in which its research and development are far 
from being assessed. At this moment, plenty of research gaps can be found, namely around the 
implementation of the new corporate sustainability reporting directive in the EU and how it will 
change the reporting panorama in the EU.

Under NFRD, companies are required to report on the impact of their sustainability issues 
on performance, position, and development (the "outside-in" view) as well as their impact on 
people and the environment (the "inside-out" perspective), commonly known as “double 
materiality” [38]  This directive had two primary objectives: to make non-financial information 
available to stakeholders and investors so that they could evaluate the company's value creation, 
opportunities, and risks, and to encourage society to take responsibility for social and 
environmental issues.  
Later, in 2017, as required by the directive, the Commission provided non-binding guidelines for 
companies on how to report non-financial information. In June 2019, the Commission announced 
additional guidelines on reporting climate-related information as part of the Sustainable Finance 
Action Plan, which incorporated the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related 
Financial Disclosures. 
However, despite these complementary directives’ guidelines on non-financial reporting, there 
has not been a sufﬁcient improvement in the quality, quantity and relevance of this information, 
as referred by the European Commission [4]. In fact, there are several stakeholders interested in 
these improvements, both users and report upgrades. This information is used by policymakers 
and environmental organisations to monitor environmental and social trends, for environmental 
accounting, and to guide public policy. Non-governmental organisations and labour unions want 
companies to be held more accountable for their social and environmental impacts, and investors 
want a better understanding of the risks and opportunities that sustainability matters create, as 
well as the effects of their investments on people and the environment [3]. In reality, most 
companies do not report non-financial information, and those that do report some sustainability 
information, frequently do not report the relevant one as the directive leaves of flexibility on 
content and disclosure mechanisms [3]. When relevant information is reported, it is often found to 
be insufficiently reliable or comparable, difficult for users to find, and not in a machine-readable 
digital format [4]. Indeed, the need this type of information has been increasing significantly in 
recent years and will very certainly continue to do so. This is owing not just to increased investor 
awareness of the relationship between sustainability matters and financial performance, but also 
to a growing market for investments that explicitly seek to follow to sustainable standards. Another 
aspect is regulation, such as the EU Taxonomy Regulation and the Sustainable Finance 
Disclosure Regulation (SFDR), which were proposed as part of the Sustainable Finance Action 
Plan. The EU Taxonomy constitutes a “classification system with environmentally sustainable 
economic activities where it requires companies under NFRD to disclose the proportion of 
taxonomy-eligible and taxonomy non-eligible activities” [39]  It aims to scale up sustainable 
investments and fight greenwashing of “sustainable” financial products. The SFRD is a framework 
for financial products and entities to disclose the extent to which financial products marketed as 
sustainable are aligned with EU Taxonomy (marking environmental sustainable economic 
activity)[40]. On top of that, companies preparing the reports face different and sometimes 
conflicting external expectations, both from global initiatives and stakeholders [5]. 
This way, with the Sustainable Finance Action Plan and European Green Deal development in 
2018 and 2019, and in their light, cornerstone regulations such as the EU Taxonomy and the 
SFRD, new sustainability needs emerged. As a result, in December 2020 an EU resolution on 
sustainable corporate governance committed to reviewing the NFRD and welcomed the 
Commission’s commitment to developing EU non-financial reporting standards [4].

requirements. Moreover, ESRS 1 does not present disclosure requirements, but rather general 
guidance and principles for the application of the fundamental concepts of the CSRD. 
Furthermore, ESRS 2 presents disclosure requirements that address reporting area 1 (Strategy) 
on general reporting issues, strategy & business model, governance and materiality assessment. 
Regarding the topical standards, each topical standard is divided into sub-topic standards 
covering sector-agnostic and sector-specific levels and reflecting an adequate granular approach 
to disclosures related to environmental, social and governance matters. Sector-specific standards 
are to be developed in the second set of disclosure requirements, along with the performance 
measurement and sub-topics disclosure requirements for the workers in the value chain, affected 
communities and consumers/end-user. Each sub-topic can then be subdivided in specific matters 
covered in the several disclosure requirements. Each standard is composed by several disclosure 
requirements, and each disclosure requirement is composed by several paragraphs (hereby 
called “disclosing points” that should be tackled. For example, on ESRS S1, under the disclosure 
requirement 3 – “Channels for own workers and workers' representatives to raise concerns”, there 
are several disclosing points to be tackled such as: “26. The undertaking shall describe: (a) the 
channels it has in place for own workers and workers’ representatives to raise their concerns or 
needs directly with the undertaking; and/or (b) the processes through which the undertaking 
supports the availability of such channels through the workplace of own workers; and (c) how it 
monitors issues raised and addressed.“.

3.4. 
ESG Disclosure Score 
 
Currently, there are several ESG rating agencies scoring companies on their performance on 
environment, social and governance metrics, such as MSCI11, Sustainalytics and S&P Global 
(which acquired RobeccoSAM in 2020). However, the number of ESG Disclosure Scores 
agencies is extremely limited. ESG Disclosure Scores focus on the ESG disclosing performance 
rather than the ESG general performance. They investigate sustainability reports, annual reports 
and related public information, and thus reflect on the transparency of the company on their ESG 
reporting through key ESG metrics. The weights of the key metrics are generally tailored to the 
different industries.  
From online and expert research, only two agency were found, Bloomberg ESG Disclosure 
Score[8] and London Stock Exchange ESG Disclosure Score [9]. Bloomberg ESG Disclosure 
Scores provides ESG disclosing performance for over 11 800 companies across more than 100 
countries. Examples of sources used for data gathering are annual reports, information published 
on company websites, CSR reports, and corporate governance reports. Their data is constantly 
updated and accessible through a Bloomberg Terminal. Bloomberg’s ESG data includes as-
reported data and derived ratios as well as sector and country-specific data points (around 90 
metrics). It covers 80% of company operations and workforce to represent a company’s 
operational impact. Their current offering of ESG scores includes climate change, management 
materials & waste, health & safety, compensation, diversity, board independence, structure & 
tenure and shareholders’ rights. The scores range from 0.1 for companies that disclose a 
minimum amount of ESG data to 100 for those that disclose every ESG-related data point

As mentioned in Chapter 2, this analysis is a 2-layer analysis that firstly focuses on the companies’

5.1. 
Companies’ Readiness through a Holistic Challenge Analysis 
 
The analysis started with two rounds of scanning of the latest CSR and annual report of the 
companies: 
1) A first scan of each CSR focused on highlighting segments of text that contained the 
previously documented codes or similar concepts; 
2) A second scan of each CSR and annual report focused on identifying the presence of 
each required disclosure.  
In order to document the results, an extension of the summary previously developed was made, 
adding a field for the presence (or absence) of each required disclosure point in the CSR and a 
preliminary assigned Challenge Level for each missing point under the DR. This allowed to 
calculate the Challenge Level for each DR. It should be noted that each disclosure requirement 
is composed by several disclosing points. 
As mentioned in Chapter 2, the preliminary Challenge Level was assigned to each disclosing 
point taking into account: 
1) If the disclosure requirement was already required by EU legislation (NFRD), EU 
regulation (SFRD) or IFRS13, or if it was referred to international reporting frameworks, 
guidelines and principles (e.g., GRI, OECD); 
2) Level of accessibility to that missing information by the CSR’s responsible (as they are 
the ones developing the CSR) . 
 
And four levels of challenge were defined:  
• 
Level 0 - Inexistent challenge: The company is already disclosing this information in 
their CSR or Annual report, so there is no extra use of resources; 
 
• 
Level 1 - Not substantially challenging: For example, the company already has this 
information in the system; 
 
• 
Level 2 - Intermediate challenging: For example, the information is not in the system 
but would be relatively easy to obtain (e.g.: contacting someone from a department in the 
company or a close stakeholder), OR it already has a plan to get the information OR have 
a team that is capable of getting it (function synergy);    
 
• 
Level 3 – Very Challenging: For example, the company would need to create a new 
process to investigate and gather this information requiring a special team and time OR

6. Extrapolation and Discussion of Results for Analysed 
Industries 
 
Before being able to draw any conclusions from the results obtained for the industries, it’s 
fundamental to notice that these three companies are only a small sample of the whole universe 
of their industry and of all large EU companies, and thus their readiness regarding the new CSRD 
may not represent the whole universe. This way, it is important to benchmark these companies 
regarding their latest ESG disclosing performance score, namely on the social pillar. Indeed, 
understanding where each company is positioned on the social disclosure performance score 
compared to its peers, and taking into account the already calculated company’s RL, it is possible 
to understand what the RL for these peer companies will (likely) be, and thus, for the industry. 
These results will then reflect on the readiness of each industry toward the new CSRD.  
The ESG disclosing performance score chosen was the Bloomberg Social Disclosure score 
(hereby “Bloomberg SD score”), which focuses on the amount of ESG data made publicly 
available by the company, rather than quantifying a company’s ESG performance. As previously 
mentioned, this scoring system goes from 0,1 for companies that disclose a minimum amount of 
ESG data to 100 for those that disclose every ESG-related data point collected by Bloomberg 
(high-quality disclosure), separated by sustainability pillars or aggregated. Moreover, the score is 
tailored to each industry.

company was conducted. As such, the previous calculated Readiness Level (RL) for the analysed 
companies could be extrapolated to each industry.

Null hypothesis Ho: ρ = 0

A p-value is then calculated, which shows the probability that the null hypothesis is true, so the 
probability that the correlation between the variables in the sample data occurred by chance.

rejected. If the p-value is bigger than the significance level (α =0.05), the null hypothesis in not 
rejected. This way, as the p-value is 0.004 (smaller than 0.05), then it can be concluded the

7. Recommendations and Reflection on CSRD  
 
Based on the analysis made on the CSRD and the meaningful insights from each company’s 
expert, overall recommendations were developed to help prepare large EU companies for the 
upcoming new disclosure requirements.  
Firstly, as investigated in Chapter 4, most of the disclosure requirements risen from the new 
CSRD are precedent from not only the previous NFRD but also from international initiatives and 
standards. This way, companies should continue putting efforts into following these voluntary 
frameworks and guidance. 
Secondly, as EFRAG is publicly publishing the working documents and exposure drafts on the 
disclosure requirements, it is allowing companies to be informed about the whole process and 
gradually start to get prepared for future changes. This way, it’s crucial that companies are alert 
and actively follow the progress published by EFRAG and the EU Commission so that they can 
define a fitting strategy from an early stage. 
Lastly, based on the results obtained in Chapter 5, strategies to tackle the disclosing gap that 
arises from the new CSRD depend on the Challenge Level as follows: 
• 
Challenge Level 1: Companies only need to assess their internal system and integrate 
that information in the next sustainability report 
 
• 
Challenge Level 2: Companies should contact the relevant colleague from another 
department that has the missing information or is able to get and integrate that 
information into the internal system and the next sustainability report  
 
• 
Challenge Level 3: Companies need to create a new process to investigate and gather 
this information requiring a special multi-disciplinary team and time  
This demands that companies start to deeply invest in their sustainability departments or 
outsourced services, so that the required assessment and data gathering processes are 
established and all EU disclosure requirements are complied with. 
This way, it is fair to state that the CSRD will bring new challenges to companies when reporting 
on their sustainability matters.    
Indeed, all experts state that the new requirements are not very decision-useful for the investors.  
They are also too resource intensive and counter-productive for the preparing companies, as it 
requires too many justifications on why the company is not disclosing each disclosure 
requirement. The three companies’ experts are hoping for a cut-down on the revised standards 
and a better alignment with ISSB standards in terminologies, definitions as well as objectives and 
disclosure requirements. There is a general encouragement for EFRAG and the EU Commission 
to build on ISSB standards and only strictly add necessary disclosure requirements that reflect 
EU’s higher ambitions with the double materiality perspective. 
However, as pointed by Schérer [46], the absence of certain metrics is also highlighted. Indeed, 
some indicators are stated to not be enough ambitious or comprehensive. For example, the ED 
ESRS S1 – DR 13 Worklife balance, only concerns the percentage of employees entitled to and 
taking family-related leave. However, work-life balance goes beyond family matters, as it also 
concerns free time for leisure activities. Besides, this metric does not concern a long-term 
measure as employees return from family leave after less than a year.  
Moreover, the concept of double materiality is in itself a complex principle to apply, as the 
company need to take into account not only the impacts that it has on sustainability matters, but 
==PAGE:52==
45 
 
also the effects that these sustainability matters have or may have on the different capitals of the 
company. Companies need to make an extensive analysis of their operations and value chain in 
order to identify these impacts, risks and opportunities, and their effects. This requires companies 
to collaborate with multiple departments and sometimes external stakeholders to perform a 
holistic analysis. 
Furthermore, due to differences in legislation, definitions and metrics within the EU, social data 
vary substantially from one nation to the next, which makes it difficult to compare between 
companies from different countries. As an example, in Denmark it is not legal to report on the 
number of employees with disabilities, which is one of the metrics presented in ED ESRS S1-19.  
Additionally, the banking industry expert shared that the 1st set of disclosure requirements already 
published was not very clear and in fact quite hard to disclose for the banking industry, as the 
value chain structure differs from the traditional one in the other industries. Truly, most of the 
impacts, risks and opportunities banks possess are not in their operations but on the side of their 
portfolio companies, which information is much harder to obtain. Indeed, even though the CSRD 
provides financial services companies with information that they can use for other EU 
requirements such as SFRD and the EU Taxonomy, it only covers a small subset of investments 
that European asset managers make. For European asset managers investing in non-EU 
equities, the new CSRD will not assist gathering information from this non-European equities as 
they may not be required to disclosure certain information.     
 
8. Limitations and Future Research 
 
Throughout this project, several limitations were found, some pointing to interesting future 
research. 
First, as the new CSRD is a brand new topic in EU, the literature on it is very scarce, which difficult 
to have a strong grasp on the CSRD and the research arising from it.   
Secondly, the draft ESRS standards themselves are exposure drafts that EFRAG mentions 
should only serve as a work-in-progress document exposing drafts, which were submitted to 
public consultation. Therefore, significant changes to the exposure drafts may arise from the 
subsequent steps of the due process. This introduces an uncertainty factor in this project and 
thus to the conclusions developed, as the thesis is based on these standards. This way, further 
work could be performed as a robustness check on the ESRS that here are considered 
assumptions.   
Alongside, during the analysis and understanding process of the ESRS, the possibility of punctual 
misinterpretation of EFRAG intentions when composing the disclosure requirements cannot be 
excluded. This could lead to errors when analysing the CSR and annual reports, thus, imprecise 
conclusions.  
Given the project time constraint and the unfeasibility of analysing all large EU companies, the 
conclusions on large EU companies' readiness were extrapolated from the analysis of nine case 
study companies. Indeed, the use of a small conditioned non-random sample [6] as the subject 
of this investigation and base for the extrapolated conclusions constitutes a clear limitation, as 
the sample does not contain all the industries, providing a narrow reflection of the EU reality. Also, 
the fact that only a single company was analysed from each industry also constitutes a limitation 
as it does not provide the most accurate result. This way, for future work, the analysis should be 
performed over a higher number of companies and a higher number of industries.   
When analysing each company’s CSR and Annual report, as there was no full knowledge of the 
company operations, it was not possible to state if all impacts, risks and opportunities were being 
==PAGE:53==
46 
 
identified by the company. Thus, it was not certain that the companies were reporting all of them 
as required, neither if what was not being reported was being excluded due to not being material.   
Moreover, due to the extensive size of the list of disclosing points, it was not possible for all of 
them to be validated by the companies’ experts.  
Additionally, different industry weights reflecting the different importance of material topics in each 
industry were not integrated into the HCL formula due to a lack of information. However, as the 
Bloomberg SD score includes these different weightings in count in their calculations, when 
performing the logarithmic regression between the HCL and the Bloomberg SD score, the 
resulting equation integrated these weights. Further work could research on these weights and 
introduce them in the HCL formula for a more accurate result.  
Furthermore, only one ESG Disclosure score (the Bloomberg ESG Disclosure Score, as it was 
the only one to which access was available) was used for the extrapolation of the Challenge Level 
results presents another limitation of this study. Indeed, it cannot be excluded the possibility that 
results would differ if another ESG disclosure scoring system was chosen with different variables 
and methodologies. Also, even though Bloomberg ESG Disclosure Score covers 11 800 
companies, it does not evaluate all the existing large EU companies ( it does not include any non-
listed companies). This limits the results of the benchmark and thus, of the industry extrapolation  
as the data used is only from these few companies.   
In this study, only social disclosure requirements (and related) were assessed. In future research 
the Readiness Level of the Environment and Governance spheres of the ESG could be analysed.  
Finally, during the companies’ expert-interviews, the impact of bias shall be evaluated closely. 
The interviewee and interviewer's biases should be highlighted here. Since the student is new to 
the expert, the responses may respond differently than they would to a well-known individual. 
Furthermore, personal bias related to, for example, cultural differences was acknowledged and 
carefully considered in the data analysis.  
 
9. Conclusion 
 
Given the fast growth of economies and the reduction of our planet’s limited resources, 
sustainability has become a goal for the 21st century. Organizations worldwide are involved to 
develop better instruments and policies to achieve sustainable development. 
As a result, a new CSRD proposal is been discussed by the EU Commission, requiring companies 
to disclose more detailed information on their social and enviromental impacts, and on 
sustainability risks and opportunities through reporting standards that foster comparability, 
relevance, and reliability.  
Therefore, the goal of this master's thesis was to investigate the readiness of large EU companies 
presently reporting on Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) topics for the future CSRD, as well 
as the challenges in successfully incorporating the planned CSRD disclosure changes on social 
concerns. Indeed, the approach taken in this thesis began with analysing what the CSRD's new 
social disclosure requirements would be. Following that, a sample of large EU corporations from 
the Health Care & Medical Devices industry, Transportations and Logistics industry, and Banking 
industry was examined through the most recent companies’ CSR and annual reports through the 
lens of the new CSRD disclosure requirements.  
These three industries showed to be generally similarly prepared, as most of the disclosure 
requirements were already being asked previously. However, some potential gaps were also 
revealed. This study showed that companies' CSR were mostly missing disclosures on their 
sustainability risks and opportunities, as well as the process of identifying and managing them, 
alongside social performance metrics. Furthermore, companies presented significant difficulty 
when disclosing on workers in the value chain and affected communities, as it is more difficult to 
==PAGE:54==
47 
 
obtain accurate information on these stakeholders. The banking industry show to have special 
difficulties, as its scope of assessment also covers its portfolio companies, since most of the 
impacts arise from them and not from the bank’s operations. In general, all companies agree that 
the new CSRD disclosure requirements are overly granular and, as a result, counter-productive 
to the aim. Based on this gap, recommendations were given for what companies could do to 
prepare for future CSRD. Indeed, it is critical that companies regularly monitor developments from 
the EU Commission and EFRAG on these issues in order to plan ahead of time adapting 
strategies to these new requirements. Companies should intensely invest in their sustainability 
departments, in order to perform holistic sustainability assessments and gather all the necessary 
information. Furthermore, because these new standards are based on pre-existing international 
frameworks and initiatives on sustainability reporting, corporations will benefit from adhering to 
them.  
As a result, this study assists companies in planning and preparing for future CSRD 
developments. Indeed, supporting superior sustainability reporting leads to greater transparency 
in the value of companies, allowing more sustainable investment, which contributes to the 
Sustainable Finance Action Plan and the European Green Deal, which are both cornerstones of 
sustainable development in the EU.
