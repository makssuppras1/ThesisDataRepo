Summary (English)
This thesis explores how data from eye movements may be used to estimate the level of
attraction for content in online dating applications, in the context of developing intelligent,
adaptive and personalized user experiences. In order to investigate this problem, two eye
tracking experiments were carried out, that tested face perception in regard to self reported
measures of attraction. Additionally, a prototype for an adaptive dating application was
developed, showcasing the feasibility of using the fndings in a commercial product.
Experiment I led to the conclusion that there is a diference between passive and active
viewing of visual stimuli such as faces. Experiment II confrmed this fnding and showed
that likable faces draw more attention and are noticed faster than faces that users rated
not likable: On average likable faces were fxated on faster and had a higher dwell time
associated. Equally, users returned more frequently to those images than to less likable
images. An investigation of this phenomena showed that the reason for such behaviour is
that users iteratively scan the other images before returning to the most likable ones.
Statistical exploration of the data revealed diferent correlations for diferent users in regard
to facial features and dwell time, pointing to the idea that attraction judgments vary across
users, e.g. depending on the person, some people might put more weight on trustworthy
looking faces, while other value cheek prominence. Based on these fndings, machine
learning modelling using SVM showed that it is possible to predict longer dwell times with
up to 66% accuracy. These results were implemented in a digital prototype that uses
probabilities from SVM classifcations to suggest new faces to the user.

Introduction
1.1. The revealing nature of the eye
"The eye is the window of the soul". These words, expressed by American sculptor Hiram
Powers (Jamison, 2013), denote the revealing nature of the human eye. Despite the obvious
romantic annotation, this statement points to a larger truth, which has been recognized by
the scientifc community for decades: The innate characteristics of eyes are able to reveal a
great deal about the physical and mental processes within humans, such as the state of the
autoimmune system (Perez, Saeed, Tan, Urbieta, & Cruz-Guilloty, 2013), sexual attraction
(Laeng & Falkenberg, 2007), personality traits (Rauthmann, Seubert, Sachse, & Furtner,
2012), decision making (Einhäuser, Koch, & Carter, 2010), cognitive workload (Just &
Carpenter, 1993) or even the identity of a person (Bednarik, Kinnunen, Mihaila, & Fränti,
2005). 
Practitioners in the software industry have long been embracing this fact in pursuit of
creating better user experiences, using eye tracking studies during development. By
comparing fxation patterns and dwell times of users looking at varying versions of an
application, designers are able to optimize interface elements based on the user's perception
(Bojko, 2013).

1.3. The case for intelligent dating applications
An interesting area of application that would beneft from such technology are online
dating sites. Through the promise of kindling romantic relationships, such services attract
millions of subscribers every year. The reasons are manifold. Finkel et al. (2012) explain
that the success is related to being able to access many diferent potential partners at once,
something that is not possible in the same way ofine, as well as providing a safe and
convenient way of communicating. Additionally, the authors state that, via the use of
smart matching algorithms, it may improve eficiency and efectiveness of the dating
process. While it has been speculated, to what degree these services actually hold up to
these promises (Slater, 2013), the author of this paper believes that eye tracking technology
could greatly enhance their potential in regards to intelligent user experiences.
One aspect of intelligent user interfaces is the ability to automatically tailor its content to a
specifc user and his preferences, without him disclosing it directly, such as the degree to
which a user is actually engaged with the content he is consuming and interacting with.
Given the fact that eyes are able to convey an abundance of information about a user, it

2. 
Literature Review
The following literature review will serve as a starting point for the theoretical
underpinnings of this discourse. It will be necessary to briefy outline a framework for user
engagement in order to examine how attraction relates to engagement in general. Since this
project presupposes a link between eye movements and attraction, this section will also
provide a short introduction into its anatomical and cognitive dimensions. Finally, an
overview of potentially relevant eye tracking measures will be presented.
2.1. User engagement
The research community has long been trying to defne user engagement for digital media.
Depending on the context, it can mean diferent things to diferent people. In his research
on fow-theory, M. Csikszentmihalyi has certainly laid the groundwork for studying
engagement, by describing fow as a state of immersion into a deeply satisfying activity,
while maintaining a balance between skill and challenge (Csikszentmihalyi, 1993). Although
his work was not focused on digital media as such, it can certainly be applied to it, as it
helps to explain engaging experiences.
One of the more recent frameworks is provided by O'Brien & Toms, who defned user
engagement as "a quality of user experience" characterized by a number of attributes like
e.g. control, afect, attention, fun or æsthetics (O'Brien & Toms, 2008) and three
consecutive stages: point of engagement, engagement and disengagement. The authors
further embedded their understanding of engagement into the work of McCarthy & Wright
==PAGE:14==
Literature Review
14
(2004) on experience threads. Engaging experiences, they argue, are composed of three
main threads: the sensual thread, the spatio-temporal thread and the emotional thread.
Sensual characteristics of an experience stimulate the user senses, such as æsthetic or haptic
properties of an application. Spatio-temporal aspects account for perception changes in
relation to physical surroundings and elapsed time during the interaction. The emotional
thread pertains to afective responses that are evoked by the experience, such as joy,
frustration or arousal.
In the context of dating applications, sensual and emotional aspects certainly prevail. One
aspect that touches upon both threads is attraction, a concept that is highly relevant in
such applications, as it refects the degree to which a person is physically drawn or even
aroused in regard to something or someone. Thus, from here on forth, this project will focus
purely on attraction as a subset of engagement.
2.2. The link between attractiveness perception and eye 
movements
Judging the attractiveness of faces appears to be something rather natural, as it has a high
infuence on who we choose as sexual partners (Alexander & Charles, 2009). Face
perception is processed, to a high degree, in the fusiform gyrus (Haxby, Hofman, &
Gobbini, 2000). While this region is rather important for processing and identifying faces, it
is not the only responsible region when judging their attractiveness. For example, studies
have shown that perceived attractiveness can activate areas in the brain that are linked to
reward, like the orbitofrontal cortex (Winston, O’Doherty, Kilner, Perrett, & Dolan, 2007).

Interestingly, it has been found in academic research that gaze selection is often closely

reward value, e.g. the æstetic value of a beautiful face. Or, as Hikosaka puts it, "the

and a faster speed, at the expense of a saccade that has not been rewarded" (Hikosaka,
Takikawa, & Kawagoe, 2000). 
2.3. Eye tracking measures
Bojko (2013) divides eye tracking measures into three main categories when it comes to
attraction: 
●
Area noticeability measures
●
Area interest measures
●
Emotional arousal measures
==PAGE:16==
Literature Review
16
All denote a diferent aspect of measuring attraction. While there might be several
potential measures in each category, one example for each is discussed below.
2.3.1 Area noticeability - Time to frst fxation 
Area noticeability measures can detect the degree to which an area is noticed. One example
for that is time to frst fxation. It provides information about how fast an area was noticed
from the time when the stimulus was entered. While it does not make any statements
about why something was noticed, potential reasons can include visual prominence (e.g.
elicited through contrast or color), or perceived importance (e.g. areas on websites where
navigation panels are usually located).
2.3.2 Area interest - Dwell time
Area interest measures can determine whether something maintained the user's attention,
meaning the level to which an area kept being visually interesting to the user for a
sustained period (or repeatedly). One such example is dwell time. It denotes the sum of all
time durations when an object of interest has been fxated on. It seems to be a rather
intuitive measure, as it provides direct insight into how long something was looked at. Even
more, according to Krupp (2008), dwell time "correlates with genital arousal and predicts
self-reported preferences for biological sex and age", making it an ideal candidate for using
it to measure engagement.
2.3.3 Emotional arousal - Pupil size
As the only measure in this category, pupil size can certainly be used to detect emotional
arousal (Bradley, Miccoli, Escrig, & Lang, 2008). However, there are numerous problems
associated with this approach. Even though positive arousal is connected to an increase in
the size of the pupil, the case for negative valence is not as clear, since such emotions could
cause dilation as well. The degree to which a pupil dilates or constricts is also highly
dependent on lighting conditions. In environments with low light, it is usually larger than
in those with bright light. Besides emotional arousal and light, pupil size changes could also
==PAGE:17==
Literature Review
17
be the result of cognitive workload, as seen in  (Just & Carpenter, 1993), who showed that
the pupil dilates when processing more complex sentences. Furthermore, a change in pupil
dilation may be attributed to visual fatigue (Ukai & Howarth, 2008) or the viewing angle of
the user (Sunkara & Pomplun, 2003). 
Certainly one of the most convincing reasons against using pupil size as a measure, is the
time it takes for a change to take place. The initialization (i.e. triggering dilation or
constriction) takes somewhere between 0.2 and 0.8 seconds (Bojko, 2013). To reach the
peak may require even more time, making it problematic to associate these changes with
specifc elements inside a user interface.

3.1. Eye tracker
The eye tracker used in this project was an Eye Tribe tracker, which is a stand-alone
remote device that, in its current development version, is placed in front of the participant.
It is using infrared light to illuminate the eye and obtain the position of the pupil via pupil-

experiments was operating in 30 Hz mode, but it also ships with 60 Hz. The self reported
accuracy from the manufacturer amounts to 0.5° – 1° in visual angle, with a spatial
resolution of 0.1°. Its latency is  less than 20ms at 60Hz. The device weighs 70g and comes
with a USB3.0 connector cable.
Prior to using it, participants need to undergo a calibration procedure, where a small series
of points have to be followed across the screen. Figure 2 shows the general setup of an

Experiment I
4.1. Theoretical considerations
Before it is possible to investigate individual preferences in regard to facial attraction, it is
necessary to answer if eye tracking can in fact detect whether or not users are attracted to
some faces more than others, i.e. without looking at the reasons behind such attraction.
The essential claim at this point is that attractive faces stand out among non-attractive
ones. In order to guide this exploration, the following sub-hypotheses are under scrutiny.
4.1.1 Users initially attend to attractive faces faster than to unattractive
ones (H1)
The frst hypothesis examines the noticeability of attractive faces. There is substantial
evidence in academic literature pointing to its validity. Maner et al. (2003) stated that,
although there is a diference between genders, attractive faces of the opposite sex generally
capture the attention of correspondingly men and women more than average ones. The
reason, so the authors, may deal with the fact that the human brain dedicates cognitive
resources to stimuli related to potential mating opportunities, so that ofsprings would have
optimal genes. As (Kościński, 2008) explains, "facial attractiveness is a reliable cue to the
owner’s biological quality". According to (Olson & Marshuetz, 2005), even when little visual
==PAGE:22==
Experiment I
22
information is given (due to masking or presentation speed), facial beauty is perceived
quickly and accurately, supporting the idea that there is a cognitive bias towards
attractiveness in humans. Given the truth of this assumption, there should be a diference
in the noticeability measure time to frst fxation of attractive targets as compared non-
attractive ones.
4.1.2 Attractive faces retain the use'rs interest longer than unattractive 
ones (H2)
Where the frst hypothesis considers the immediate selective attention that attractive faces
may receive, the second claims that users are also prone to devote their attention for a
longer period to faces that are visually appealing than to those that are not. The rationale
behind this idea stems from the earlier mentioned fact, that the judgment of attractiveness
is linked to the brain's reward circuitry, as proven by (Cloutier, Heatherton, Whalen, &
Kelley, 2008). It be could argued, that the brain generally attempts to seize experiences
that are rewarding and hence devotes attentional resources to attractive faces for a longer
time (i.e. to continuously reward itself). Furthermore, (Sui & Liu, 2009) showed that visual
stimuli of beautiful faces can compete with other cognitive tasks, implying that humans are
neurally predisposed to directing attention towards faces that are visually stimulating.
Consequently, these assumptions should be refected in the corresponding interest measure
dwell time.
4.2. Method
4.2.1 Participants and Setup
Data was collected from four individuals (two males and two females) in an indoor home
environment. Prior to these four trials, three other individuals had to be dismissed due to
poor calibration results (potential reasons may include droopy eyelids and contact lenses of
those participants). The participants were in the ages between 20 and 31. Two of them
were in a committed relationship, while two were single.
==PAGE:23==
Experiment I

Participants were seated in front of a MacBook Air (13"), at an unrestricted viewing
distance of approximately 50cm. The laptop was connected to the Eye Tribe eye tracker
located in front of them. Stimuli was displayed in a Javascript based browser application
that connected to the Eye tracker using Node.js, while sending the recorded data to a local
server, for storing it in a fle on the machine.
4.2.2 Stimuli
Two sets of stimuli were presented to each participants. In the frst set, female participants
viewed 15 x 4 male faces, while males viewed 15x4 female faces, preceded by a scrambled
mask (see fgure 3 below). The stimuli images portrayed either four Caucasian or four Afro-
American faces in a slightly smiling expression (with no teeth showing) between the ages of
18-40. The mask was created manually for each subset of 4 images using Adobe Photoshop.
Its purpose was to obtain a baseline value for the participant's pupil diameter for the same
luminosity before presenting the actual stimulus. Additionally, the mask contained an easy
mathematical problem. Both, images and mask, were 720 x 540 pixels big and located in
the center of the browser window in full screen mode.

4.2.3 Procedure
The experiment started by introducing the participants to the procedure of the study. The
background and goal of the experiment was not revealed until after the end of the
experiment. After a short introduction to the eye tracker, a calibration was performed using
the existing Eye Tribe user interface, where participants had to follow black points on a
white screen with their eyes. If successfully calibrated, participants were reminded of the
task and the fact that they should look at the screen at all times, and the frst test would
begin. Here, they would see the scrambled mask and the stimuli images in an alternating
fashion, each one for 5000ms. The task consisted of solving the mathematical problem,
displayed on the mask, and saying the answer out loud. Afterwards, so they were told, they
would see an image with faces to relax them. 
==PAGE:26==
Experiment I
26
The mathematical problem served three purposes. On one hand, it kept the participants
engaged with the task, so they would not become bored or distracted. On the other hand,
since they were located in the center of the screen, it could be assured that participants
always focused on the center before viewing the actual stimuli. Third, it served as a cover
story so that they would not be biased (which would be the case if they were told that the
point of the study was to look at the stimuli images).
After the frst test was fnished, the participants were informed about the relevance of the
images and instructed to judge each face individually according to its level of attraction.
Each stimulus image was displayed for exactly 500ms, so participants would not be able to
form a detailed view of the person, but rather form a quick impression. After each image,
they would be presented with the voting screen, featuring a six point Likert scale in which
0 = unattractive and 5 = extremely attractive. When the participant made his judgment
and pressed "Next", the next image would appear. This process would repeat until all
images were shown.
After this second test, the participant would be debriefed and informed about the purpose
of the study. There was no direct reward involved.
4.3. Results
4.3.1 Gaze points
Figure 5 below shows the general gaze plot for all participants over all trials. It only
contains gaze points for the times when participants actually looked at the images, and
hence excludes those for the scrambled masks. The visual appearance to be expected was a
roughly equal amount of gaze points that centered around four major areas, i.e. the four
quadrants where the faces were located. Surprisingly, a slightly diferent picture presented
itself. As can be seen, there is an unusual high amount of gaze points in the center of the
screen. This fnding is staggering because, as such, there was nothing particularly
interesting placed in that location. 
After careful deliberation, it can be concluded that the participants must have been so
focused on solving the mathematical problem (which was always placed in the center), that
==PAGE:27==
Experiment I
27
their anticipation of the next equation led them to focus their attention on the middle of
the screen instead of the faces. This theory is supported by two considerations. First, there
was no specifc task involved with the images. The participants were led to believe that
solving the mathematical tasks was the main purpose of the study and the faces should
relax them in-between stimuli. Since there was no competing task present, they devoted
most attentional resources to the math problems, even when they were not on screen.

One question that is worth asking deals with the high amount of center fxations. As
already touched upon, it is probable that the participants were so biased by the task that
they devoted less attentional resources to the faces. If true, it would result in a very
interesting implication: There appears to be a clear distinction between gazing at faces and
judging them for attractiveness in the human brain. Bradley et al. (2011) stated that 
"specifc tasks and instructions can alter eye movements to meet specifc goals". One reason
for that is that face perception is not located to one specifc area in the brain. Rather, it
depends highly on the type of processing that is needed. For example, face identifcation,
emotional expression processing and spatial attention of gaze are all associated with
diferent brain regions (Haxby et al., 2000), although all of them deal with the perception of
faces. It is therefore highly likely that judging a face for its attractiveness value is a
separate process that must be addressed specifcally in an experimental setup. Therefore,
what was apparently tested in this experiment, was a passive perception of facial
information that has no relevance in terms of the ultimate goal of this study, which is
potential mate selection using dating applications. That is because, in such applications,
people are actively looking at profle images. Future trials must therefore take account for
the type of face processing that is actually under investigation. For that reason, it is
advisable to not include any distraction tasks into the study but rather attempt to mimic
the behaviour that would be present in the intended use case, i.e. consciously judging
profle images for the sake of mate selection.

participants were not attracted to many of the faces presented, as most of the ratings were

5.1. Theoretical considerations
The previous experiment hinted towards the idea of goal-orientation, meaning a potential
diference between passively glancing at face images and actively browsing through them, a
concept that will be called 'browsing mode' in this chapter. If people perceive faces
diferently when they would scan them for attractiveness in a conscious manner, it would
be interesting if a change in experiment setup would lead to a diference in noticeability
and interest measures. For that reason, the following experiment was intended to build
upon the previous one while making specifc changes that would target these issues, as well
as exploring the browsing mode in more depth. While the three hypotheses from the
previous experiment will be revisited, the following three claims are under scrutiny as well.
5.1.1 With no competing tasks present, people will mainly focus on the 
center of the screen (H3)
The basic idea is that if the trials would not contain any distraction task like the
mathematical problem in experiment 1, and instead people were told to judge the faces,
they would focus their attentional resources on the images. Hence, the gaze points in the
center would mostly disappear.
==PAGE:37==
Experiment II
37
5.1.2 People return to those images that they fnd attractive more often 
(H4)
According to Kranz & Ishai (2006), when judging attractiveness, people use more time on
determining attractive face images than unattractive ones. It would therefore be reasonable
to assume, that such behaviour can be observed in eye tracking data when testing users in
browsing mode. But how exactly could this play out? The scenario suggested here is that
people, while scanning each face one by one, go back more frequently to those that they
fnd most attractive. Holmqvist et al. (2011) call this the "return to an area of interest".
Hence, it is postulated in this hypothesis that, on average, the returns for personally
attractive faces outnumber the unattractive ones.
5.1.3 There exists a general gaze pattern that people utilize to make 
judgments when in browsing mode (H5)
If hypothesis 4 holds true, it might indicate the presence of a larger gaze pattern when
making attractiveness judgments in browsing mode. To explore the diferent facets of such,
the purpose of this hypothesis is to investigate how users spent the time they had to browse
the faces. In order to do this, fxation patterns will be analyzed.

5.4. Discussion
The results of this experiment help to confrm the assumptions about the user behaviour in
the previous one and shed light on how users judge faces in the presence of diferent
alternatives. Some of these fndings shall shortly be refected upon.
The most striking fnding deals with the mysterious center gaze points from iteration one.
After changing the instruction to better mirror the actual user context, they decreased
drastically. This fact makes it clear that task demands can have a major impact on eye
movement behaviour. Or, how Einhäuser et al. (2008) put it, they can "override sensory-
driven saliency". If such tasks demands are too strong,  eye movements can potentially be
more controlled by top down processes than invoked by the actual visual stimuli. The point
here is not that one is more desirable than the other, but that the researcher has to
carefully determine the goal of the study. Hence, from an experimental point of view, it is
of extreme importance that the setup accounts for the type of behaviour that the research
is targeted at. If not taken into consideration, it might induce a behavioural bias that
renders the results of the experiment unusable.
As an obvious conclusion, one might be tempted to assert that the present study should be
focused on those top-down processes of the human brain that infuence visual mate
selection. Yet, the problem is actually more complex than that. As we will discuss in the
next section, sensory-driven mechanisms play a crucial role as to how we perceive other
human beings, especially in regard to facial features.
Furthermore, as a direct result of this experiment, it appears to be that dwell time is
related to how people rate faces in terms of likeability. Obviously, the assumption was
made that asking participants to judge likability is equal to judging the degree of attraction
in the context of mate selection in online dating applications. This is a reasonable claim
because mate selection is based on a variety of factors and not just physical attractiveness.
For that reason, a more complex concept was needed to assess the degree to which users
are drawn towards certain individuals more than others. The suggestion brought forth here
is the term likeability, due to its multifaceted properties. According to Reysen (2005), it can
encompass attributes like friendliness, warmth, approachability, attractiveness,
trustworthiness, similarity to oneself, knowledgeability and other similarly desirable
characteristics when selecting a mate for life. In the next chapter we will explore specifc

judgments about these characteristics.

6.1. Theoretical considerations
The previous experiment indicated the presence of a relationship between diferent eye
tracking measures like dwell time and visual attraction. In light of these fndings, the
question remains as to how one could pinpoint individual diferences between users in order
to gain insight into personal mate preferences. In particular, it shall be investigated how
these preferences can be mapped onto specifc features and characteristics in the face, in
order to predict what type of face the user prefers.
As one such example, let us consider facial width to height ratio (fWHR). Recent studies
have proven that fWHR is an indicator of behaviour and personality traits in humans.
Lewis et al.  (2012) showed how it can predict achievement drive in politicians. Mileva et
al. (2014) stated that fWHR is positively correlated with dominant social behaviour.
Welker et al. (2014) discovered the connection between fWHR and aggressiveness in
athletic performance. If a confgural feature like fWHR is linked to personality, it suggests
itself to assume, that humans may be subconsciously using it as a cue during the sexual
selection process. Indeed, Valentine et al. (2014) found that while men with a high fWHR
are attractive to women looking for short term relationships, the same is not true for
==PAGE:51==

women looking for long-term commitments. The authors argue that a high fHWR refects
dominance, which "is a sexually selected trait indicating genetic quality".
It is likely in this context that fWHR is not the only facial feature that can be linked to
how personality traits are observed and perceived. More than two decades ago, the work of
Cunningham et al. (1990) pointed to the importance of eye size, cheekbones and chin
prominence as well as emotional expression in the context of fnding mates who are
confdent, seem approachable and are of high social status.

features and eye tracking measures exist and, equally, how such relationships can be used
to predict specifc mating preferences. In order to delimit this discourse, dwell time was
chosen as a representative example.

6.4. The link between facial features and dwell time
As a general goal, it is highly desirable to fnd that the dwell times correlated diferently
among participants with the features. This is because it coincides with the major goal of
this study, which was meant to detect individual diferences. Based on these results, is it
reasonable to say that these not only constitute correlations, but actually point to a causal
relationship, i.e. the facial features determine the dwell times?
In order to disprove this assumption, one could try to fnd a third variable that can cause
both, the change in features and dwell time, or show how the opposite is true. Claiming
that the dwell times caused the change in features, however, is illogical and can be
dismissed. Before attempting to fnd a third variable, it seems necessary to distinguish
between concrete and abstract features. 
There are numerous physiological, genetical and chemical factors that could have caused
the concrete features, such as nutrition, hormones or mutation. None of them could
possibly cause the dwell times, because the stimuli were purely visual. Hence, the brains of
the tested participants had nothing else to go by except visual signals of the face, i.e. the
concrete features.
Concerning the abstract features, there is only one possible way in which they could be
infuenced by something else, and still have impacted the dwell times: It could be that one
or more concrete facial features determine how an individual (or humans in general)
perceives a characteristic like trustworthiness, femininity or threat. In fact, as we have seen
in the introduction to this chapter, fWHR has been proven to relate to dominance and
assertiveness. As this is only one of many examples in academic literature, it is highly likely
that the majority of such abstract concepts is strongly linked to concrete features in the
face. If they also cause the long dwell times, it would mean that we humans are
subconsciously picking up on them. And our brain must then be able to map the
combination of the features to these concepts to form an immediate impression of the
person. 
Figure 27 below displays the proposed model of causal relationships as it suggests itself at
this point. Current academic evidence strongly supports the idea that concrete measurable
features of the face are causally linked to abstract ones (A), as well as to attraction (B).
Equal evidence can be found for abstract features and attraction (D). The two experiments
==PAGE:60==
Predicting individual preferences
60
in this project intended to show the relationship between attraction and dwell time (C).
Based on the fact that we can rule out any alternative explanations, concrete features are
not only correlated to dwell time, but are even a possible cause for it (E). Whether abstract
features are equally causing the changes in dwell time is not entirely clear (F), but since
they are partly based on concrete features, it is reasonable to assume that these two
somehow work together.
Figure 27: Proposed model showing the links between facial features, attraction and dwell time. 
Arrows denote probable causal relationships.
Given the proposed model, it is certainly tempting to claim that the entire phenomena
relies on a top down process. The truth is, nothing could be further from the truth, because
the model only explains a small part of a larger problem domain, as facial features are not
the only factors that infuence dwell time. There are numerous other variables that could
impact how we perceive faces in regard to eye movements. Cultural ideals of beauty, past
==PAGE:61==
Predicting individual preferences
61
experiences, upbringing, expectations, goals and motivations are only some of the possible
factors in this context, all of which are bottom up infuences. The point being made is that
none of these are able to account for the correlations between facial features and dwell
time. The degree to which all these factors are contributing to why we gaze at some faces
more than others is not under scrutiny here, and should be examined in a separate study.
6.5. Predicting individual preferences
Since the link between facial features and dwell time appears to exist, it is important to
investigate how this link can be exploited for the purpose of predicting individual mate
preferences. The rest of this chapter will therefore shed light on the question if it is possible
to predict dwell times for a given face using supervised machine learning classifcation.
6.5.1 Method
Since predicting actual dwell times might prove to be very dificult, a binary approach was
chosen. The dwell times were divided into two categories, below 1000ms and above, which
were used as classes. The rest of the data, the metadata from the faces, was used as
features to predict whether an image would fall in one of the two categories: low dwell time
or high dwell time.
For classifcation, the support vector machine (SVM) method was chosen. SVM's are good
for binary classifcation tasks and are rather robust in regard to overftting as compared to
many other methods. Additionally, as will be discussed later, SVM's provide the possibility
of obtaining probabilities through Platt scaling, instead of simple class labels.
Optimal values for the soft margin parameter C and the infuence parameter ƴ were found
using exhaustive grid search via the python module Scikit-Learn (C=10.0, ƴ=0.1,
kernel=RBF).  The classifcation scores were computed separately for each participant
using 1/3 of the data for testing and 2/3 for training.

6.5.3 Discussion
Although the results seem quite low when compared to chance, it has to be noted that the
amount of data for training and testing was rather low. In light of that, the results are
actually not so bad, and it is with some confdence that we can expect better results if
given more data to train the model. For dating applications, this means that the classifer
would get better the more active a user is. 
In order for this to be used in an online dating application, however, one would need a list
of potential faces to present to the user and not a binary classifcation result. Thus, it is
important to feed the system a list of faces and receive the corresponding probabilities (that
it falls into either of the categories) as an output. This list could then be sorted according
to these probabilities and the ones with the highest results could be presented to the user.
Fortunately, the SVM implementation in Scikit-Learn provides such probabilities with its
classifcations results.
The author of this paper is aware that there are numerous other, potentially superior,
machine learning methods that could be used in this context. Yet, the purpose of this
project is more of an explorative nature in order to prove the concept. 
For now, only dwell time was used from the eye tracking data. In the future, numerous
other such measures like fxation count, blink rate, AOI return, etc. are imaginable to be
used in conjunction with dwell time. Additionally, one could use auxiliary features like
mouse clicks to improve the classifcation results, as well as trying to fnd ways to reduce
the number of facial features to only include the most relevant ones, using e.g. principal
component analysis. The latter would certainly be useful to avoid overftting problems.

Discussion
The explorations of using eye tracking for dating applications, as they were presented in
this paper, only mark the beginning of a wide feld of potential research. It is natural in
such a context that there are many diferent facets that could not be taken into
consideration. Yet, since many of them are still highly relevant, the purpose of this chapter
is to give a general overview of these issues.
8.1. Dating applications
For reasons of delimitation, this project necessitated a simplifed view of dating
applications, i.e. seeing them as a place where people fnd potential mates by browsing
through profle images. This perspective however, does not do justice to the dynamic
microcosmos that revolves around such services. 
For one thing, there are many diferent types of dating applications. While some promote
casual encounters and firting, others are intended to procure long term relationships. Even
others are either purely sexual or prompt people to have afairs. Depending on the goal and
target audience, the nature of these services can be highly diverse. 
Another issue deals with the fact that such applications are usually highly complex and
feature much more than just the ability to upload pictures and text about yourself. Many
==PAGE:75==
Discussion

are focused on incenting direct interactions in the likes of vast social networks, and provide
multiple ways of engaging users with the application and each other.
Looking merely at profle images, this dynamic and versatile nature of online dating
services provides another highly problematic issue for the research in this paper. It becomes
clear that people seldom upload avatar images that are as homogenous in terms of posture,
lighting, perspective and facial expressions as the ones used here. This is problematic,
because, as seen in this project, we are not dealing with precise measurements, but rather
very noisy data. Future research must hence be focusing on ways to overcome such data
problems in order to make this technology a feasible source of intelligence.
8.2. Auxiliary measures
Eye tracking is certainly just the beginning. Thanks to the personal data revolution of this
age, gathering sensor-based information about users is a trend that has yet to reach its full
potential. In the context of this research topic, one can imagine a whole range of additional

arousal in efect to certain stimuli. The company Kenzen with its Echo H2 Hydration

Since it covaries with pupil dilation (Bradley et al., 2008), it has a huge potential in terms
of gaining insight into levels of attraction.

neurological processes in the brain, i.e. at least to some degree. Using mobile
electroencephalography (EEG) like the Emotiv EPOC

8.3. Ethical issues
Given the huge public interest in minimizing the risk of privacy invasion nowadays, are
there any ethical concerns that are relevant when it comes to using eye tracking for web
applications? Certainly, when it comes to UCLA electrical engineering professor John
Villasenor, who believes that this data could be exploited in many diferent ways, as
"information could easily pass into the hands of advertisers or law enforcers" (Bleicher,
2013, p.10).
What are some of the potential risks involved? Let us imagine the following scenario. A
dating application uses eye tracking data to improve their recommendation algorithm.
Additionally, the company also displays advertisements on their website. In pursuit of
proft and unknowing to the user, the company could sell this data to advertisers. In turn,
these advertisers would be able to know when, how long and which users were looking at
what banners, increasing their marketing potential to a high degree.

9. 
Conclusion
This project set out to explore how data from eye movements may be used to estimate the
level of user engagement for content in online dating applications, in the context of
developing intelligent, adaptive and personalized user experiences. In order to investigate
this problem, two eye tracking experiments were carried out, that tested face perception in
regard to self reported measures of attraction. Additionally, a prototype for an adaptive
dating application was developed, showcasing the feasibility of using the fndings in a
commercial product.
 
A suboptimal setup in experiment I, providing unexpected results, led to the conclusion
that there is a diference between passive and active viewing of visual stimuli such as faces.
Experiment II confrmed this fnding and showed that likable faces draw more attention
and are noticed faster than faces that users rated not likable: On average likable faces were
fxated on faster and had a higher dwell time associated. Equally, users returned more
frequently to those images than less likable images. An investigation of this phenomena
showed that the reason for such behaviour is that users iteratively scan the other images
before returning to the most likable ones.
Statistical exploration of the data revealed diferent correlations for diferent users in regard
to facial features and dwell time. In turn, this points to the idea that attraction judgments
vary across users, e.g. depending on the person, some people might put more weight on
trustworthy looking faces, while other value cheek prominence.

Based on these fndings, machine learning modelling using SVM showed that it is possible
to predict longer dwell times with up to 66% accuracy. These results were implemented in a
digital prototype that uses probabilities from SVM classifcations to suggest new faces to
the user. 
Despite the fndings in this project, there is still a long way to go in terms of converting
such a concept into commercially feasible solutions.

10. Future Work
Undoubtedly, this research area points in many diferent possible directions. One of the
more promising ones presents itself in regard to auxiliary measures. For such an application
to be viable and feasible, it is vital to incorporate a variety of additional data input sources,
such as heart rate measures or facial expression detection, which would provide a more
diferentiated value to the eye tracking analysis. The degree to which these could
complement each other is certainly worth exploring.
Additionally, it would be exciting to pursue the idea of creating average face profles that
are based on the preferences of users. That way, it would help in generating
recommendations for additional targets, since one just has to compare other user profles to
a master profle. The closer the profle, the more likely it will resonate with the user's taste.
In that regard, it would involve the need to explore diferent machine learning approaches
to the problem. 
At last, an area with a lot of potential is the mobile experience. Here, it might be worth
investigating how an adapted version of such an application for smartphones and tablets
would fnd equally interesting results, and in particular, how it would accommodate for the
diferences in user behaviour.
