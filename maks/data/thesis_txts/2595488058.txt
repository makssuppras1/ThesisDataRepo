Abstract
As a mean to reduce their environmental impact, the company Social Foodies has initiated this project
to investigate alternative materials for their insulating ice cream container made of expanded polystyrene.
The needs and requirements for such alternative is established through stakeholder interviews, and used to
inform a systematic material selection process. Two diﬀerent container concepts is developed, one being
a biodegradable single use container made of mycelium based composites, and the other being a reusable
container made starch based polymers. Using Markov chain simulations and Life Cycle Assessment data,
the environmental impact is in terms of CO2 emissions and energy consumption through the stages of
manufacturing and use, evaluated for each concept and compared to the impact of the current container
made of expanded polystyrene. Results shows that the biodegradable single use container oﬀers the biggest
potential, though additional research is required to conﬁrm if it is a feasible solution. The behaviour of
Social Foodies customers has proven to be an essential parameter for the performance of a reusable container,
and analysis shows that such solution can represent a decreased environmental impact if a given customer
behaviour is met.

Introduction
The continuously increasing amount of packaging being used, is reason for environmental concern. Not only
is the manufacturing of packaging related to heavy amounts of emissions, energy use and resource consump-
tion, it is also a major source of waste. Due to the intended short lifetime of packaging, packaging waste
will quickly accumulate, and sometimes in inexpedient places such as oceans or nature in general, if a well
structured waste handling system has not been established. Though, even if such systems exists and the
packaging waste is collected, it is often found to be incinerated instead of recycled. Expanded Polystyrene
(EPS), commercially known as Styrofoam, used for food packaging is a particular example of problematic
packaging waste. EPS is widely used on a global scale for many types of applications, why it is found as waste
in great numbers. Being a polymer based on petrochemical processes, EPS is not a renewable material and
will not degrade in nature. With eﬃcient means of recycling EPS not being available, options for handling
EPS waste is limited to incineration and downcycling. In Denmark one of main uses of EPS is food packaging
due to its low cost and unique material properties of thermal insulation and shock absorption. That however,
complicates the possibility of recycling EPS even further, as it is close to impossible to completely separate
polymers from each other with the current waste collection systems. This means that other unintended
polymers and additives might end up in the recycled material, which is especially an issue in terms of food
packaging materials where food safety is critical.
Social Foodies is a Danish company selling quality ice cream and food, which allows them to invest in projects
helping social vulnerable people in both Denmark and Africa. They generate local jobs by e.g educating locals
about sustainable farming from where they purchase the ingredients required for the products they sell. So-
cial Foodies is thus committed to both social, economical, and environmental sustainability, and is constantly
seeking to improve in every aspect. Successfully selling ice cream, means that Social Foodies is responsible for
a large consumption of EPS. Ice cream bought from Social Foodies is packed in insulating containers of EPS
and is left with the customers once used. Social Foodies realize the environmental issues related to EPS, why
they have initiated this project to investigate alternative materials to EPS for insulating ice cream containers.
Insulating ice cream containers are generally characterized by being of low thermal conductivity, approved
for being in contact with foods, and only used once before disposal. This project presents two diﬀerent type
of ice cream container concepts, one being a single use biodegradable container and the other a reusable
container. The single use biodegradable container is intended to be used like the current single use EPS
containers, but with the possibility to be disposed as compostable waste. The reusable container is intended
to be used as many times as possible, which would decrease the amount of containers required to meet the
customer demand. The success of a reusable container is thus very dependant on customer behavior, meaning
their ability to keep using the same container. Many potential materials are explored and one is selected for
each concept. The environmental impact for each concept is evaluated using Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
1
==PAGE:9==
data, combined with customer behaviour models used to provide estimates of the lifetime for the reusable
container. Results shows that both concepts could be environmentally better solutions than EPS. However
a number of conditions related to scaling the manufacturing, meeting the requirements of an ice cream con-
tainer, and customer behaviour must be met for them to be viable solutions.
The project has been made in the times just before and during the Covid-19 related lock down, meaning
that activities of experimental work and prototyping initially planned, has not been possible to conduct as
no access to the required facilities was available. The scope has therefore shifted from aiming to develop
a working prototype, to a more comprehensive evaluation of environmental impact between concepts using
Markov chain simulations. How that has aﬀected the learning objectives developed prior to the project is
discussed in section 9 at the very end of the report.
1.1
Problem Statement
How can an insulating ice cream container of less environmental harm compared to those currently used, be
developed to lower the environmental impact of Social Foodies?
• What type of containers could be developed and what is the intended use cycle?
• What are the most suitable materials for containers representing diﬀerent use cycles?
• What is the environmental impact?
• What are the conditions and assumptions that must be met for each container type?

Chapter 0: Method and theory
In order to ease the process of reading the following chapters and ensure transparency of how the project
has been conducted, will this chapter brieﬂy present methods and theory used throughout the project. Only
having general descriptions of the methods and theory used in this chapter, allows the remaining chapters to
solely focus on activities driving the project forwards. References to this chapter will be made throughout
the report whenever a given method or theory is used. This chapter starts by explaining the general structure
used to guide the project which act as the report structure as well. This is followed by descriptions of the
speciﬁc methods and theories used.
2.1
Product development process
The project has in general been divided into four phases; (1) Problem exploration, (2) Problem Framing, (3)
Solution Exploration, and (4) Solution Selection, which as well represents chapter 1, 2, 3, and 4 respectively.
These phases are similar to the diverging and converging phases from the design thinking model ’Double
Diamond’ [1].
The project diverges in phase 1, as information about the problem of ice cream containers currently used
by Social Foodies is gathered. That is done through stakeholder interviews, desktop research and system
mapping, which together represents the overall situation and challenges related to developing a new container,
summarized in a ’Mission statement’ that indicates the general direction of the project [2]. The project then
converges in phase 2 where the speciﬁc problem to be solved is framed. That is done by selecting the ice
cream container type for which a new is to be developed, and its related user needs and requirements are
established. That sets the scene for phase 3 where potential solutions for a new container are explored, thus
diverging the project again. Diﬀerent concepts are developed and materials are chosen for each through a
systematic material selection process presented later in this chapter. Phase 4 compares the environmental
impact for each of the concepts developed in phase 3, which acts as the decision parameter for the selection
of a ﬁnal solution, representing the ﬁnal converging of the project.
2.2
Systems Engineering
The principles of ’Engineering Systems’, meaning systems of systems [3], is used during phase 1 to understand
the interactions between Social Foodies’ ice cream containers and the surrounding environment of stakehold-
ers. Multiple artefacts or actors interacting with each other, represents components of a system. Such system
might have a purpose, which can only be realized if every component is designed with that in mind. The
same system could be interacting with another system, thus becoming an engineering system, with multiple
purposes. The complexity of designing the single component is thereby increased even further, as the pur-
pose of the engineering system now also needs to be taken into account. One can imagine how changing a
4
==PAGE:12==
component may optimize the system it belongs within, but harm the interaction with other systems, thus
making the design change sub optimal. To mitigate such scenario when designing a new ice cream container,
the principle of Engineering systems is used to map the systems and actors in the surrounding environment
to understand the interactions. This represents one of the essential principles of systems engineering used in
this project. That is the principle of working in system levels of diﬀerent abstraction [4]. At the lowest level
of system abstraction is tangible artefacts such as components in a machine, at the level above is the system
that the artefact is placed within, and at the highest level is the engineering systems. Each systems level is
characterized by holding its own set of needs and will only function as intended if the system level below is
aligned with those needs.
To support the concept of working with systems of systems and layers of abstraction, the method of ’System
Demarcation’ is used to separate systems from each other and identify system levels. System demarcation is
a way of visually setting boundaries between systems and their environment [5] as shown on ﬁgure 1. Setting
boundaries between systems, makes it easier to explore the interactions between them, and thus understand
how changes on one system might aﬀect the other.
Figure 1: Principle of system demarcation
The concepts of systems engineering presented above is furthermore an essential part of how stakeholder
needs and requirements engineering is used in this project, which will be presented in the following sections.

2.3
Customer and Stakeholder Needs
Customer and stakeholder needs is in this project established using the process developed by Ulrich & Ep-
pinger, 2012 [2].
It is a four step process of (1) gathering raw data, (2) interpreting the raw data, (3)
organizing the needs into a hierarchy, and (4) establishing the relative importance of needs.
The ﬁrst step of gathering raw data is mainly done by conducting semi structured interviews with stake-
holders and users documented with notes, and supported with information found through desktop research.
Second step of interpreting statements from the raw data to formulate needs, is done trying to avoid indicat-
ing potential solutions by only expressing the needs in terms of what is wanted instead of how. The needs are
additionally expressed in a natural way that does not predetermine a level of importance, e.g. by not using
words such as must and should. In step three the needs are organized by placing them in groups of needs
representing a similar type of need. Organizing the needs provides an overview required in order to establish
a relative importance between them in step four. The method chosen to do so is the weighted objective
method [6]. The ﬁrst part of that method is to establish the priority of each need. Rank ’1’ is assigned to
the need of the highest priority, ’2’ to the second most important and so fourth. A need being prioritized
one rank higher than another, does however not reﬂect how much more important that need is, but only
that it is of higher importance. For that reason, should the relative importance of needs be determined once
the priorities has been assigned. This is done by rating the need of highest priority with ’1.0’, and then
evaluating the remaining needs of less important with the need of highest priority as reference by assigning
ratings of relative importance from 0.0 to 0.99 to them. A relative importance of 0.8 thereby means that
the need is 20% less important compared to the need of the highest priority. The speciﬁc values for priority
ranking and relative importance is determined iteratively through stakeholder discussions.
With the needs established, ranked and assigned with a relative importance, they are ﬁnally organized using
the three system levels mentioned in previous section. Each need is placed at a system level that reﬂects its
level of abstraction. A need speciﬁcally targeted the ice cream container itself, is thus to be placed at the
lowest level of abstraction, while needs concerning e.g. total environmental impact of the packaging industry
belongs to a system level of higher abstraction. Such organisation of needs simpliﬁes the development of
product requirements as shown in the following section.
2.4
Requirements Engineering
The customer and stakeholder needs is used to establish product requirements for a new ice cream container
and the system it is intended to be used within. The translation of needs into requirements was done by
constructing one or more metrics for each need as described by Ulrich & Eppinger, 2012 [2]. Doing so pro-
vides a strong way of ensuring that the requirements represents actual value for customers and stakeholders.
Attention should however be made of how the metrics are constructed. The metrics should reﬂect a technical
6
==PAGE:14==
measurable variable that corresponds to its related need. Metrics should furthermore be dependant variables,
meaning that it can be measured as a result of the design instead of dictating the design. Doing so ensures
the designer with the most freedom possible to develop a solution.
Following that procedure every need is translated into requirements, and organized using the system levels.
A requirement is thereby placed on the same system level as the need it is related to.
In requirements
engineering, stakeholders are initially interviewed to establish a set of requirements for the highest level of
abstraction, considered as the general objectives [4]. These could correspond to the requirements of an engi-
neering system. From those requirements is a sub-set of requirements of higher level of detail then derived, to
know what it takes for the engineering system requirements to be realized. Such a sub-set would correspond
to the requirements of a system. Finally could a third sub-set of requirements be constructed, to know how
the system requirements can be realized, which would represents the requirements of a component or single
artefact. This represents the same organization of requirements of using system levels as mentioned earlier,
and allows one to link requirements across system levels. A requirement should be linked to those require-
ments from the system level above that it satisﬁes. Doing so for every requirements enables the possibility
to trace requirements all the way from component level to engineering systems, and thereby gain insights of
how a new design aﬀects the remaining system.
The concept of deriving requirements from the highest level of abstraction down to the lowest and linking
them to allow for tracing, is used in this project to construct requirements not directly emerged from needs
and to investigate how design changes of the ice cream container might aﬀect the surrounding systems. The
requirements constructed from translating the needs was linked to the requirements on the system level below
that satisﬁes them. New requirements was derived if a higher level requirement was not satisﬁed by any of
the requirements already made. Doing so down through all the system levels made it possible to generate a
complete set of requirements by discovering where more requirements were necessary.
2.5
Outline of material selection method
Phase three of this projects develops two diﬀerent ice cream container concepts, each requiring a diﬀerent
type of material. The ﬁrst concept is a biodegradable single use ice cream container that can be disposed
as food waste, while the other concept represents a reusable container intended to be used as many times
as possible. The material selection method used to select materials for those concepts is that developed by
Ashby et al., 2014 [7]. The method consists of four steps; (1) Translation of requirements, (2) Screening using
constraints, (3) Ranking using objectives, and (4) Seek documentation.
Translation of requirements: Translation of requirements means expressing the component requirements
(the lowest system level of requirements) as the function, constraints, objectives, and free variables of the
7
==PAGE:15==
component. The function represents what the component is intended to do, the constraints are the conditions
the the component material must meet, objectives are those aspects of performance that are to be either
maximized or minimized, and the free variables are the parameters that the designer are allowed to change.
Screening using constraints: Every possible material is screened using the constraints from the trans-
lation of requirements. Constraints are considered as gates; a material either passes or fails. A constraint
could be that the material must be recyclable. A large range of materials can thus quickly be reduced to a
more manageable size.
Ranking using objectives: The range of material passing the screening is ranked using the objectives.
Objectives are performance parameters that can either be maximized or minimized. Cost is an example of a
objective that often are to be minimized, while strength often is an objective to maximize. The best ranked
material is thus the material that best manages to maximize and minimize the objectives important for the
given component.
Seek documentation: The highest ranked material can be expected as the best choice of material, however
not every aspect of the material is revealed in the selection process. Additional documentation of the material
should therefore be sought out to avoid any potential pitfalls. A material could e.g. have a bad reputation
because of its high environmental impact, which could make it a poor choice of material even though it
manages to optimize all the objectives.
With a material selected for each of the two concepts of ice cream containers, is the environmental impact
of each of them evaluated in order to decide which of them are to be the ﬁnal selected solution. The method
used for such evaluation is presented in the following section.
2.6
Markov chains and probabilistic sensitivity analysis for evaluation of envi-
ronmental impact
In Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) the environmental comparison is based on a ’functional unit’. A functional
unit is deﬁned as: "a quantitative description of the function or service for which the assessment is performed,
and the basis of determining the reference ﬂow of product that scales the data collection in the next LCA
phase, the inventory analysis" [8].
A functional unit should thus describe what the product is intended to do in a quantitative way that indicates
the amount of resources required. This is important, to ensure that the products or concepts of interest are
being compared on the same scale. The functional unit used for the evaluation of environmental impact of the
two concepts in this project is: ’1 year of consumption of insulating ice cream containers at Social Foodies’.
8
==PAGE:16==
Due to the scope of this project two environmental impact parameters are selected for the environmental
evaluation, instead of conducting a complete LCA. The chosen parameters are CO2 emissions and energy
consumption. The total environmental impact of a concept, is thus the amount of CO2 emissions and energy
consumption equivalent to 1 year of consumption of container types of that concept. The concept resulting
in the lowest environmental impact is the concept chosen at the end of phase 4.
The actual evaluation of environmental impact for each concept of ice cream containers, is done using Markov
chain simulations and probabilistic sensitivity analysis to incorporate the eﬀect of customer behaviour into
the evaluation. As the main source of CO2 emissions and energy consumption of ice cream containers are
related to manufacturing, the environmental impact can be reduced through a reduction in number of con-
tainers produced. For the concept of a reusable container, the amount of containers required for 1 year of
consumption are highly aﬀected by the customer behaviour, meaning how many times the average customer
reuses the container before it eventually gets disposed. Increasing the amount of times a reusable container
is used, would lower the amount of containers required to meet the demand of 1 year of consumption, and
thereby also lower the environmental impact.
Markov chain simulations are used to investigate how many times the average reusable container is used
before disposal. A Markov chain consists of three elements: a transition diagram, a transition probability
matrix, and a initial distribution. The transition diagram represents a given amount of states of a system
an object can be in, and what transitions between states that the object is allowed to make. The transition
probability matrix represents the likelihood of what transitions between states the objects are to make over
time. The initial distribution tells how the objects of the system are distributed between states at time
zero. Figure 2 shows a general representation of a transition diagram, transition probability matrix and
initial distribution. The arrows represents the transitions that objects are allowed to make, and the values in
the initial distribution and transition probability matrix are just arbitrary example values. From the initial
distribution it can be seen that 100% of the objects starts in state A, leaving no objects in the remaining
states.
From the transition probability matrix it can be seen that there is a 95% chance of an object
transitioning from State A to B, and only a 5% chance of transitioning from State A to D. Likewise does it
shows that there is a 95% chance of transitioning from State B to C and so fourth.
9
==PAGE:17==
Figure 2: General schematic of a transition diagram, transition probability matrix, and initial distribution
Following the probabilities stated in the transition probability matrix, objects in the system will transit from
state to state over time. States that are likely for objects to transit to, will thus hold more and more objects
as more transitions are made, why the initial distribution changes for every transition. An unique feature of
this is that the amount of transitions it takes for an object to reach a given state can be calculated. It is
however only possible with the technique used in this project for absorbing Markov chains, meaning that the
Markov chain contains an ’absorbing state’. An absorbing state is a state that an object can not leave once it
has entered it, meaning that every object will eventually end in that state. A number of deﬁnitions are thus
required to distinguish absorbing Markov chains from regular ones. An absorbing state of a Markov chain
is deﬁned as being absorbing if the row of the corresponding state has a 1 on the diagonal of the transition
probability matrix and 0’s elsewhere [9]. An absorbing Markov chain is deﬁned by containing at least one
absorbing state and allows objects to go from each non-absorbing state to at least one absorbing state in a
ﬁnite number of transitions [9]. Calculating the average amount of transitions it takes an object to move
from a given state to the absorbing state is done through a series of steps. First the transition probability
matrix is required to be transformed into its standard form. A transition probability matrix for an absorbing
Markov chain is in its standard form if the rows and columns are labeled so that all the absorbing states
precede all non absorbing states [9]. The standard form can generally be represented as so:

3.2
Sustainable Issues of EPP and EPS
To gain a holistic sustainability proﬁle of a product, assessment tools like Life Cycle Assessment (LCA),
evaluates the sustainable impact of a product on multiple impact categories such as, non-renewable energy
resource consumption, Global warming, Acidiﬁcation, Photochemical Ozone Formation and Depletion of
abiotic resources, throughout its entire life cycle [14]. In general, a product life cycle can be categorized into
four stages; Material acquisition, Manufacturing, Use, and End of Life [15]. To provide indications of the
sustainability proﬁle of EPP and EPS, following sections highlights primary sustainable issues throughout
the life cycle of EPP and EPS containers.
3.2.1
Stage: Material Acquisition
The styrene monomer, that after polymerization forms the backbone of polystyrene, is essentially a result
of cracking hydrocarbons from crude oil and is thereby based on a non-renewable resource [16]. Cracking of
crude oil is a process of braking the long hydrocarbon-chains into smaller molecules. From these molecules,
Ethylene is separated through distillation [17]. Ethylene and Benzene is the two main elements of styrene.
Ethylbenzene is made by reacting ethylene and benzene through alkylation, which after dehydrogenation is
transformed into styrene [18]. Once the styrene is formed, it can be polymerized into a polystyrene resin.
However, the resin can only be used to produce EPS once saturated with a blowing agent, such as pentane,
that allows it to expand. That is the main diﬀerence between EPS and PS. For EPS the blowing agent is
normally introduced during the polymerization, as it diﬀuses into the granules of the polystyrene resin [19].
15
==PAGE:23==
The result is small granules of polystyrene saturated with a blowing agent, which is the material resin used
for any EPS product.
The formation of expandable granules used for EPP, follows a similar procedure as mentioned above with
the main diﬀerence being the dehydrogenation of propane instead of ethylbenzene. Propane is as well a
by-product of cracking crude oil, why also EPP is based on a non-renewable resource [18].
LCA’s has identiﬁed multiple environmental issues related to the material acquisition process of virgin EPS.
Namely energy consumption during the process, was found to cause environmental concern [15]. In fact, the
material acquisition of virgin EPS has shown to be the most energy consuming stage of the EPS packaging life
cycle [16]. However, studies have shown that energy used for transportation can exceed that of the material
acquisition, if an ineﬃcient transportation mode is selected [16]. As processing of virgin EPP is so similar to
that of EPS, no sizeable diﬀerence in energy consumption is expected.
3.2.2
Stage: Manufacturing
Products of EPP and EPS is manufactured by steam-chest moulding, where the resin saturated with a blow-
ing agent is expanded inside a mould of desired shape and size. The process consists of ﬁve steps: closing the
mould, ﬁlling the mould, steaming (expanding), cooling, and ejection. Once the mould is closed and ﬁlled
with resin, is the resin steamed. By controlling the steam inlet ﬂow, the pressure inside the mould is normally
increased to a correspondent temperature of 150oC. Due to the blowing agent and elevated temperature,
the resin expands and bonds adjacent granules together until the mould is completely packed. Cooling ter-
minates the expansion and thereby lock the product in its ﬁnal shape, ready to be ejected from the mould [19].
Energy consumption from EPS moulding was found to be the second highest of its life cycle stages [14].
However, the amount of consumed energy is highly dependent on the quality of mould design, as most of
the energy is used to heat the mould and generate steam [15]. Additionally the use of pentane as a blowing
agent is found to contribute with a considerable amount of Photochemical Ozone Formation, as the agent is
released as emissions once the resin starts to expand [20]. As both EPP and EPS products are manufactured
by steam-chest moulding, the energy consumption and related emissions are once again considered to be
similar.
3.2.3
Stage: Use
Opposite to most products, has EPP and EPS packaging a very limited use phase, consisting mainly of
transportation. It is already mentioned that an ineﬃcient transportation mode can require more energy than
both material acquisition and manufacturing. This is likely the case of the EPP containers used by Social
Foodies as shown on ﬁgure 4, as it is highly reused. The EPS containers are however, only transported twice
16
==PAGE:24==
as shown on ﬁgure 6, why the related energy consumption is likely to be much less compared with that of the
EPP containers. To provide a completely transparent evaluation of how transportation contributes to the
sustainable proﬁle of EPP and EPS containers, the transportation mode and distance from resin producer
to container manufacturer, and from there to a local Social Foodies store, would have to be known. Such
evaluation is however not within the scope of this project. Instead emphasis on the fact that transportation is
the main sustainable issue during the use phase, and that already presented literature has shown that energy
consumption during transportation only becomes considerable if ineﬃcient transportation modes are selected.
The containers used by social foodies are all delivered by a Danish supplier situated outside Ejsbjerg, which
represents a much shorter distance compared with those used in the study of Madival et al. 2009 where
both resin and manufactured products are transported over a thousand kilometers by truck or train, without
exceeding the energy consumption of processing [16].
3.2.4
Stage: End of Life
The containers reaches their ’End of Life stage’, once their tasks are fulﬁlled. For the EPP containers that
means after 6-7 years when they are worn out, and for the EPS containers when the customer has consumed
the ice cream as shown on ﬁgure 4 and 6 respectively. At that stage Social Foodies (for EPP containers) and
customers (for EPS containers) are to select a disposal mode. As the waste handling system is organised in
Denmark, three options are in principle available: Recycling, Downcycling, or Incineration. Recycling repre-
sents the transformation of material used in an old product into a new product of the same type. Downcycling
utilizes material from old products to produce new ones of a lower quality. Producing EPS insulation panels
from EPS food containers, is a common example of downcycling. The third disposal mode, incineration, is
used to generate energy and support the district heating networks through incineration of waste.
It became clear through an interview with the environmental organisation Danmarks naturfredningsforenin-
gen [21], that the waste streams however, in reality are less transparent than indicated by the principle
organisation of disposal modes indicated above. As previously mentioned, EPS is not sorted together with
other polymers, but are instead recommended to be disposed as general waste for incineration. Because a
sizeable amount of EPS is used for food containers, the EPS industry has for a long period not been interested
in promoting recycling of EPS, why municipalities recommend incineration instead of collection and sorting
of the material. The reason being that by collecting EPS with other waste polymers, it becomes impossible
to completely separate the materials from each other again. Recycled material will thereby always contain
small amounts of other, often unknown, materials. That is an issue if the recycled material are to be used as
a food container, and high food safety are to be remained. For that reason the EPS industry has preferred
virgin EPS for the manufacturing of EPS food containers over recycled EPS.
17
==PAGE:25==
To accommodate the increasing demand for sustainable solutions by reducing the use of virgin EPS, the EPS
industry has recently developed a collecting container where only EPS are to be disposed in order to avoid
contamination from other polymers. The container can be bought by centralized recycling stations where
citizens can dispose their waste. However, as the containers are expensive and the recycling stations are not
obligated to buy them, such containers are not available on every station. The municipalities are additionally
still guiding citizens to dispose EPS for incineration, why the incentive to use the EPS collecting containers
seems limited for both recycling stations and citizens.
Another counter acting element for the development of a disposal system that motivates recycling rather
than downcycling and incineration, is the municipalities ﬁnancial liability to incineration plants. Due to
the relationship between the amount of incinerated waste and the economy, incineration plants have to keep
operating. In fact, not enough waste is generated in Denmark to meet the capacity of the incineration plants,
why waste are being imported from abroad [22]. Even compostable kitchen waste, collected in small contain-
ers publicly distributed to every household in many municipalities, was found to be incinerated for reason of
capacity [21].
Recycling and incineration can be considered as opposites, that on the one end closes the loop and stops
the use of non-renewable resources, and on the other end takes no action towards a reduced consumption.
Downcycling is an intermediate disposal mode, that does not close the loop but prolongs the life cycle of a
given material to reduce the demand for virgin material. Downcycling is thereby not sustainable on a long
term, but can act as an useful transition from incineration towards recycling. With the development and
reﬁnement of recycling technologies such as chemical recycling, the transition towards recycling is likely to
become easier with time. Chemical recycling makes it possible to transform polymer waste into the original
monomer or feedstock, and thus be used to generate new polymers again [23].
Such technology would
address the contamination issue faced when trying to separate multiple polymers and additives from each
other, however the technology is not mature enough for scaling [24]. Another element expected to drive the
transition, is the increasing regulatory pressure on, not only the management and priority of packaging waste
[25], but also on companies in the form of extended producer responsibility [26]. With an extended producer
responsibility, product manufactures becomes ﬁnancial and organizational responsible of handling the product
once it becomes waste in terms of both collection and separation of waste. With such regulations issued by
the European Commission, the incentive for companies to support recycling increases, which is expected to
demand changes of the current incineration based waste handling system [21].

3.4
Food packaging legislation
Legislation regarding Food Contact materials (FCMs) in Denmark is aligned with the harmonised legal EU
frame work, with a few exceptions where additional national regulations has been implemented for speciﬁc
materials [30]. In general, the EU framework Commission Regulation (EC) No 1935/2004 states, that FCMs
20
==PAGE:28==
should not release constituents into food at levels harmful to humans, or change the food composition, taste
and odour in an unacceptable way [31]. Furthermore, Commission Regulation (EC) No 2023/2006 provides
regulations to ensure good manufacturing practice of FCMs [32]. A substance not previously used in contact
with food, needs approval of the oﬃcial authority of the state before use. An application with all relevant
technical information must be submitted, in order to get such permission. The authority is obligated to state
whether or not the substance of interest is granted permission within six months, but has the possibility to
extent the process with up to additional six months [33].
These regulations represents an important aspect of the diﬀerences between the EPP and EPS containers.
The EPP transportation containers are packed with ice cream contained in smaller metal containers wrapped
in foil. In that use setting, the EPP container is thereby not exposed directly to the ice cream, why it is not
considered a FCM. This is opposite to the EPS container where no intermediate layers are found between
container and ice cream. The EPS containers are therefore obligated to comply with mentioned regulations,
whereas the EPP containers are not.

is estimated to be larger than that of the EPP container. This is due to the legislation’s related to food
contact materials which only the EPS container needs to comply with (See section 3.4). Speciﬁc develop-
ment activities would have to be conducted in order to establish such compliance, activities that would not
be necessary if the EPP container is chosen. However, despite the expected increase of complexity due to
legislation the EPS container is chosen to be the one that this project seeks to develop an alternative for,
with the reason being the much greater potential in terms of especially environmental sustainability. Re-
quirements for potential cost increases must however be set. The processing of EPS has been optimized for
years, and is as a result very cost eﬃcient. It is therefore unlikely that a newly developed alternative can be
price competitive right away. However the combination of a sustainable alternative and the great demand
for EPS containers, might make a slight cost increase acceptable. An additional trait of selecting the EPS
container, is the branding possibility it oﬀers for Social Foodies. Customers not only see the EPS containers,
but also bring it with them, whereas the EPP containers are only used and seen internally. An environmen-
tal sustainable alternative to the EPS container, would thus be a great way to turn a secondary product
into a platform that brands Social Foodies for their sustainable initiatives and justify potential cost increases.
Finally it should be noted that, though an alternative EPS container has shown to oﬀer the greatest potential
of impact, other settings might alter that. Some industries and businesses that operates in diﬀerent systems
23
==PAGE:31==
than that of Social Foodies, might have a larger use of EPP containers, why the global potential impact of
developing an alternative for the EPS or EPP container might not be as diﬀerent as it is at Social Foodies.
From the information presented up until this point, it can therefore not be concluded that all businesses using
both EPP and EPS containers, should start by implementing an alternative for EPS if the impact is to be
maximized as quickly as possible. That is only the case for Social Foodies or systems that highly resembles
that of Social Foodies such as other ice cream retailers.

Chapter 3: Solution Exploration
This section starts by presenting two diﬀerent concepts of ice cream containers and how they are diﬀer-
ent. Following is an exploration and selection of potential materials for each concept, including a material
characterization that provides data for later analysis of the environmental impact of the two concepts.
5.1
Concepts
The best material for a given application, is highly dependant on how the product is supposed to be used.
Thus, changing the way customers at Social Foodies are supposed to use the ice cream containers, might
broaden the solution space of alternative materials. Two fundamentally diﬀerent concepts of how the contain-
ers are supposed to be used are therefore examined. One concept being based around a single use container,
similar to the one currently in use at Social Foodies, and the other being based on a reusable container. The
two concepts are speciﬁed further in the following.
5.1.1
Single Use Compostable Container
To be as convenient as possible, this concept is aimed to resemble the concept currently in use as much
as possible, but with a container made of a compostable material instead of EPS. The idea is simply to
just change the material to a more environmental friendly alternative and maintain everything else as is.
Such concept would allow customers to stay within their current habits of disposing the container after
usage, knowing that Social Foodies have taken responsibility to minimize the environmental impact of the
containers by using proper materials. Figure 11 shows the states included for a container based on a single
usage concept.

5.1.2
Reusable Container
Whereas the concept of a compostable single use container does not change the product oﬀered by Social
Foodies (except for a lower environmental impact), a reusable container does. With a reusable container,
each container is brought back to Social Foodies and washed after use and can thereby be used again. This
changes the product of Social Foodies as the customers commits to the increased involvement of returning
the container. Various types of incentives (apart from reducing environmental impact), such as discounts
or other rewards, could be implemented to motivate customers to return the container. Such incentives are
likely to increase the performance of the concept, however the focus in this section will be on the exploration

5.2
Mycelium Composites for a Single Use Container
Mycelium composites have since its ﬁrst appearance in 2009 been gaining both academic [34] and commercial
[35] interest due to its environmentally friendly nature and unique material properties. It has often been
mentioned as the new replacement for EPS, because of its similar porous structure that provides the low
density and thermal conductivity and high impact resistance characteristic of EPS. Mycelium composites are
however still a new type of material only commercially realized by a few companies, which leaves a space for
investigations of potential areas of application. For those reasons, and the fact that mycelium composites
decomposes in nature, mycelium composites are chosen as the type of material used for the concept of a
single use compostable container in this project.
35
==PAGE:43==
5.2.1
The principle of mycelium composites
Mycelium is the hyphae network that makes up the root network of mushrooms, that if left to grow for
long enough will eventually start forming fruits of mushroom. However the hyphae network is so dense and
entangled with the substrate it grows within, that it can be used as a resin to bind particles together, and
thus form rigid materials of mycelium composites. Mycelium grows by decaying lignocellulosic material that
is uses as nutrition, why the substrate must be based on cellulose if a strain of mycelium are to colonize it.
Many organic waste streams from agricultural waste such as sawdust, straw and cotton are thereby suitable
substrates for mycelium to grow. The ability to use a living organism as a resin to bind an organic waste
material, is what makes mycelium composite so environmentally attractive. Not only is the resin an easily
grown renewable source, the particles used as substrate is also a resource that can be locally required from
various waste streams.
Producing a product of a mycelium composite, is unlike typical production processes found in industry, a
matter of growing. In general, the production/growth of a mycelium composite can be divided into six stages
[36]; Choosing a substrate, sterilization of the substrate, inoculation with mycelium, deﬁning geometry, grow-
ing, and termination of growth. Apart from being an organic material, the choice of substrate depends on
what is locally available and the intended application of the product, as studies have shown how material
properties of mycelium composites can be altered with diﬀerent kinds of substrates and particle sizes [37][38].
To avoid contamination of the material, meaning that unwanted bacteria or fungi starts forming in the sub-
strate, the substrate once chosen must be sterilized. Typical methods for doing so is through pasteurization
or steaming using an autoclave. Once sterilized, the substrate can safely be inoculated with mycelium, as-
suming that a culture of mycelium is available. Cultures of pure mycelium can be acquired in agar, and
then added to the substrate where it will start to spread. Or alternatively as a batch of pre-inoculated
substrate that can be dispersed into the new substrate. With the substrate inoculated with mycelium, it is
packed into a mold with the negative shape of the product to be made (similar to polymer injection mold-
ing). Leaving the packed mold allows the mycelium to grow, and will eventually bind together every particle
of substrate. The process of growing can be adjusted through parameters of the ambient conditions, such
as temperature, humidity [39], light level, and CO2 concentrations [40] but will approximately take 5 days
before fully grown. The many processing parameters, including type of substrate and mycelium species, can
be diﬃcult to control and, as shown later, highly aﬀect the properties of the ﬁnal material. No standard-
ized process have been developed yet to ease the production and control the processing parameters. Only a
few industry players have managed to scale the production and many details are kept as industry secrets [41].
The growth process is stopped by elevating the temperature to around 80oC for 30 minutes, after which
the material is dried out and the mycelium becomes inert. Doing so completes the production process of a
mycelium composite product. Figure 13 shows an example of a sample grown in a substrate of sawdust.

5.2.2
Sustainable Beneﬁts
Thinking holistic and considering systems of products instead of single products, closing material loops, and
recycling materials into products of equal or better quality, is just a few of the ideas embedded in the concept
of circular economy, which are to support a more sustainable way of production and consumption [42]. By
using organic renewable resources and waste materials to create entirely new products, mycelium composites
ﬁt the ideas of circular economy well. Not only that, but the production of mycelium composites are, as
indicated in above section, light on resource intensive machinery and chemicals. In fact, production related
energy consumption and CO2 emissions are mainly found during the smaller periods of heat treatment, where
the substrate is sterilized [43] and growth is inactivated [44]. The production of 1 m3 of mycelium composite
have been reported to require 652 MJ and emit 31 kg of CO2 [45].
5.2.3
Current Use
From a sustainable point of view, mycelium composites seem to be a suitable material, but only a few
companies have used it on a larger scale.
A patent review shows that more than 50% of all mycelium
composite related patents are owned by Ecovative Design or Ford [35]. Being the original inventor of mycelium
composite, Ecovative have the most experience with the material, and is perhaps the only company that have
scaled the production of products made of mycelium composites to a degree where they can supply companies
such as Dell [46] and Ikea [47] with packaging for some of their products. It shows that scaling the production
is possible, however with an approximate growth duration of 5 days, large scale production requires a great
amount of space in order to produce large batches. Combined with the fact that no standard automated
production process is available, might explain why only a few have implemented mycelium composites at this
stage. The current commercial use of mycelium composites are thus mainly packaging, however studies are
being conducted on how mycelium could be transformed into a less foam-like material in order to be used
for more structural applications [48].

EPS in terms of tensile strength, why tests would have to be made in order to determine whether mycelium
composites are strong and durable enough for an ice cream container. The range of each material property
is additionally much wider for mycelium composites than it is for EPS. This reﬂects the many diﬀerent
processing parameters aﬀecting the ﬁnal material mentioned earlier. As an example the density is highly
dependent on the density of the substrate used, and the ratio between mycelium and substrate once growth
has been terminated. This indicates that the processing parameters of a potential mycelium composite con-
tainer, could require a great amount of development and testing before it is possible to consistently produce
containers with the desired properties.
The CO2 emissions and embodied energy from production of mycelium composites is based on the measures
presented in 5.2.2 and the average density (232 kg/m3) of those reported by the 12 studies mentioned above.

6
Chapter 4: Solution Selection
Based on the two concepts and their selected materials presented in chapter 3, this chapter will conduct a
evaluation of the environmental impact, in order to chose between the two concepts. A general description
of the method used can be found in section 2.6. The environmental impact of the single use mycelium com-
posite container, is mainly a result of its manufacturing process. The environmental impact of a reusable
container is however additionally very sensitive to how many times it is used before being disposed. The idea
of a reusable container is to reduce the total amount containers required to meet the demand, and thereby
reduce the environmental impact. The more a container is reused the fewer containers is required to meet
the demand, why the goal is to maximize the number of times a reusable container is used. In the context
of Social Foodies, the maximum number of times a container can be used, is a result of two parameters: (1)
the durability of the container itself, and (2) the customers behaviour. The durability can to some degree be
controlled through product and material design, while the customer behaviour is much more intangible. The
concept of a reusable container is completely disrupted if the average customer chooses to dispose it as if it
were a single use container. Diﬀerent customer behaviour models has therefore been developed in order to
examine how sensitive the environmental impact of a reusable container is to the behaviour of the customers,
and what minimum of behaviour is required for the reusable concept to produce an environmental impact
smaller than a single use container concept.
The chapter starts by presenting the behaviour models and its related system of use cycle states for a reusable
container, which is then used to discover how many times the container can be expected to be reused before
disposal. That information is then combined with data regarding CO2 emissions and embodied energy, in
order to determine the environmental impact. With environmental impact known for each concept ends the
chapters by comparing them and making a ﬁnal selection.
6.1
Transition diagram and and Behaviour Model
The states of a reusable containers use cycle can be visualized with a state transition diagram similar to that
shown on ﬁgure 12 in section 5.1 where it was used to describe the concept of a reusable container. The
transition diagram consists of 5 states that the reusable container can lie within: It can be (a) Stored at
Social Foodies, be (b) In use by customer, be (c) Stored at customer, be (d) Disposed, or be (e) Forgotten.
How a container transits between these states is governed by a number of rules. As an example it is not
possible for a container that is disposed (state d) to transit back to being stored at social foodies (state a).
There is thereby a ﬁnite number of legal transitions for the container to take, and some of them are more
likely than others. How likely a container is to ’choose’ one transition over another, is what represents the
customer behaviour. It would e.g. be very unlikely that a customer with good customer behaviour disposes
the container right after using it only once, whereas it would be more likely to happen if the customer rep-
resented a bad customer behaviour.
49
==PAGE:57==
Three diﬀerent scenarios has been developed to represent three diﬀerent behaviour models of the usage of
reusable containers. Scenario 1 represents good customer behaviour, scenario 2 represents medium customer
behavior, and scenario 3 represents bad customer behaviour. The transition diagram and behavior model
alongside the transition probability matrix for each scenario is shown in ﬁgure 21. The initial distribution
d0 is also shown in ﬁgure 21, which indicates how the reusable containers are initially distributed between

happens. The three scenarios is designed to represent behaviours that range from being unlikely to dispose
the containers to being likely, which will provide insights on how sensitive the lifetime of a reusable container
is to customer behaviour.

One of the fundamental assumptions of Markov chain simulations is that the transition probabilities, or
customer behaviour model in this case, does not change. That is however very unlikely to reﬂect the reality,
as it would mean that every customer would be equally likely or unlikely to e.g. dispose the container after
use no matter the context. A more realistic case would be that the average customer has a behaviour that
represents a given model, such as scenario 1, 2, or 3, but with some customers deviating from the norm. It
additionally seems likely that a customer could deviate from normal behaviour because of a speciﬁc occasional
context that does not allow for usual behaviour. Being based on static transition probabilities, the ﬁgures
above is thereby unlikely to reﬂect the actual customer behaviour, even if any of the scenarios were a perfect
behaviour representation of the average Social Foodies customer.
To accommodate this issue a probabilistic sensitivity analysis is added to the Markov chain simulations.
The simulations are still based on the transition probabilities from the three scenarios, but they are used
as an average instead of ﬁxed values. Up until now only one simulation was executed for a given number

Input Parameters and Related Assumptions
Apart from the behaviour data presented in ’Lifetime Analysis of a Reusable container’, the data required
to conduct the evaluation of environmental impact for each concept is additionally, the CO2 emissions and
embodied energy from both primary production and processing, the total amount of containers, and the mass
of a single container. The data for CO2 emissions and embodied energy is already presented in section 5, and
the mass of the container for each concept is calculated to correspond to the medium sized 1000 ml container.
This is done by using the 37 g 1000 ml EPS container from Social foodies as a reference, with a density of

which is more suited for a later state and is beyond the scope of this analysis. Additionally, Thermoplastic

thermoplastic starch is estimated to reduce the density by at least 80%, which is a conservative estimate,
considering that the density of PS is reduced by approximately 98% by foaming it into EPS [50]. Similar
results has additionally been produced through foaming of starch based polymers [51]

Concept selection
From the analysis of environmental impact across concepts presented in previous section, it is clear that
the most environmentally friendly solution would be the concept of single use container made of mycelium
composites. Its material properties is additionally very similar to those of EPS, with only the tensile strength
not being as good as shown in section 5.2.4, making it likely to be a good option considering that EPS is the
industry standard. Considering the requirements developed for system level: Ice Cream Packaging in sec-
tion 4.4.3, meaning the container itself, shows the mycelium composites complies with several of them while
other are left unknown at current state. Many of the requirements requires testing of a prototype container,
why additional research would have to be conducted in order to fully explore the capabilities of mycelium
composites. The lack of experimental work in this project is further discussed later. Of the requirements
not relying on experimental work are no.: 3.3 ’The container is compatible with disposal modes available for
households’, 5.3 ’The material used is based on renewable resources’, 6.3 ’The manufacturing process is CO2
neutral’, and 7,3 ’The container has visual features that indicates sustainability’.
Being biodegradable in nature and made of renewable organic resources a container of mycelium composite
will comply with the disposal modes available in house holds, thus meeting requirement 3.3 and 5.3. The
manufacturing process is however not CO2 neutral as demanded by requirement 6.3, though it is the process
representing the lowest level of emissions as shown in previous section. The appearance of mycelium com-
posites indicates additionally that it is clearly not a polymer, opposite to thermoplastic starch, which has
proved to be important for some customers as they associates all polymers with being environmentally harm-
ful (see section 4.3.2). The concept of a single use mycelium composite container thus represents the lowest
environmental impact and fulﬁlls the most requirements, why it should be selected, as long as a container
that meets the remaining requirements, including scaling of the production, can be developed. Whether
that is possible is however unknown and represents a potential reason not to favour containers of mycelium
composites. Another element not favouring a biodegradable solution such as mycelium composite, is that
some stakeholders of the systems surrounding Social Foodies seems to prefer other types of materials. An
increasing regulatory pressure generates incentive for companies to support recycling, and the municipalities
have an interest in incinerating waste due to investments (see section 3.2.4 and 4.4). Neither of these are
aspects that aligns with a biodegradable container made of mycelium composite, which might make it harder
to realize and thus meet its true potential.
Alternatively the actual behaviour of the Social Foodies customer base could be investigated to discover how
it aligns with the required behaviour presented in previous section for a reusable container concept to func-
tion. If the behaviour proves to be suﬃcient, the concept of a reusable container would be a viable solution
as well, though it is unlikely to ever become as environmentally friendly as the container made of mycelium
composites.

Discussion
This section will discuss areas of the project where decisions critical for the result have been made. It is done
to emphasize how the result might change if more research was made in certain areas. First the environmental
parameters used throughout the project as decision criteria are discussed, followed by a discussion of how
stakeholders have been used as ’validation’, especially during the earlier phases of the project where needs
and requirements were constructed. Included in that, is also a discussions of how the requirements and
principles of systems engineering have been utilized. The limitations and potential improvements of how
customer behaviour of a reusable container is modelled is then presented, after which the section ends with
a discussion about feasibility of a single use container made of mycelium composites.
7.1
Life Cycle Assessment as a decision parameter
Social Foodies initiated this project in order to investigate sustainable alternative materials, that potentially
could be used to develop an ice cream container less harmful to the environment compared to those currently
in use made of EPS. Incorporating both social and environmental sustainability into everything they do,
sustainable solutions are very much the backbone of Social Foodies. They realize and acknowledge the need
for sustainable solutions, but also realize the business potential of the currents trends related to sustainability
in general. Environmental sustainability is thus the main driver of this project, which is also reﬂected by
how high they ranked the stakeholder needs representing sustainability in section 2.3. Sustainability is for
that reason naturally used as decision parameter for the selection of a ﬁnal solution.
Environmental sustainability is however a result of many diﬀerent aspects. In Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
environmental sustainability is represented by many impact categories such as: Climate change, Strato-
spheric ozone depletion, Photo chemical ozone depletion, Acidiﬁcation, Eutrophication, Ecotoxicity, Human
toxicity, Land use, Water use, and abiotic resource use. These are not every impact category, but those
most commonly used [8]. Using just footprints of CO2 emissions and energy consumption as done in this
project, which is mainly related to climate change, might therefore seem insuﬃcient. Using footprints for
environmental assessment instead of all the mentioned impact categories comes with a number of limitations.
First of all a footprint of e.g. CO2, does only focus on one environmental issue, meaning that it can be
used to identify the best option for that speciﬁc issue, but fails to support decisions between options where
multiple environmental problems needs to be considered. Using several environmental footprints, like it has
been done in this project (by using both CO2 and energy), makes it additionally diﬃcult to combine an
overall score of environmental impact and introduces the risk of creating a bias towards one of the selected
footprints being more important than the other. During the evaluation of environmental impact in section
6.3, that has been mitigated by only comparing concepts on one footprint at a time, and requiring a concept
to give the smallest value of both footprints if it were to be considered as the best solution.
68
==PAGE:76==
The main beneﬁts of using environmental footprints are: that the concept is intuitive and is easily commu-
nicated to a large variety of non-environmental experts, the required data is very accessible, and the task of
mapping a footprint is much more time eﬃcient than conducting a complete LCA taking every environmental
impact category into account. These beneﬁts are also why only footprints of CO2 and energy consumption
are used as decision parameters, as the scope of this project is more towards material development, meaning
that a complete LCA would simply be too time consuming. With that decision, it is important to emphasize
that the selected materials, chosen concept, and recommendations made, are subject to change if a complete
environmental assessment was made. However, due to the many unknown factors that still remains related
to realizing and implementing the concepts of interest, it could be argued that such complete environmental
assessment should not be made until it is certain that the concepts can be realized and implemented, why
using footprints as the initial analysis to indicate the direction of development is appropriate at this stage of
the project.
7.2
Requirements
The intended use of the requirements developed in chapter 2, was to establish criteria that an ice cream con-
tainer prototype of the selected concept and material could be tested against, and link them to requirements
from the above systems levels to ensure it would meet the needs of Social Foodies and their customers. The
requirements being linked across the system levels deﬁned in section 4.2, was additionally intended to allow
for investigations of how a new container in the system of Social Foodies, would align with the surrounding
systems to understand how the solution could be expected to be met and what implications that might arise.
However, with the lack of being able to do experimental work and prototyping, the requirements have not
been used entirely as intended. A single use container of mycelium composites, has shown to be the best
solution in terms of environmental impact, but it needs to meet the requirements developed in section 4.4 to
be a viable solutions, which has not been possible to determine without experimental work. The requirements
have instead been used for mainly two things: (1) deﬁning constraints and objectives during the material
selection for a reusable container concept, and (2) discovering how container design changes aligns with sys-
tems interacting with Social Foodies by linking requirements across system levels. It can however be argued
whether the latter aspect has been done to an suﬃcient extent. The systems interacting with Social Foodies
are represented by the stakeholders in the system level ’Packaging Resource Eﬃciency’. The main insight
gained by linking the requirements of Social Foodies to the requirements of ’Packaging Resource eﬃciency’
is, that only few stakeholders are advocating for biodegradable solutions why implementing them might be
met with resistance. That indicates that a compostable solution such as mycelium composites might not be
a viable solution. However, if more stakeholders from the system level of ’Packaging Resource Eﬃciency’ had
been included, more insights that could inform the decision making process of choosing a new container are
likely to have emerged. Including requirements for e.g. a plant handling compostable waste from households

to generate energy, could have changed the picture as they are likely to advocate for biodegradable solutions.

7.3
Behaviour Models for a reusable container
In section 6.1 was three diﬀerent scenarios developed, each representing a potential behavior model for how
customers could use a reusable container. The models was used to determine the expected lifetime of the
reusable container, from where the amount of containers required could be derived and the total environ-
mental impact evaluated. Had the actual behaviour of Social Foodies been known and expressed in the form
of a transition probability matrix, only one scenario would have been necessary for a successful evaluation.
However, since that has not been the case the three somewhat arbitrary behaviour models were developed
and used. The reasoning of doing so, is that by constructing diﬀerent scenarios of good and bad customer
behavior, both the potential risk and reward of a reusable container can be understood. Knowing pros and
cons of a solution is essential to any decision making process, however the result does not reﬂect the reality.
Without knowing the actual customer behaviour model, is it only possible to provide the potential of a
reusable concept and ﬁnd the required behaviour in order for it to be a viable solution.
An alternative or additional approach could have been to establish the behaviour model based on customer
data. Questioners about their expectations to the usage of a reusable container and tests with prototypes
could have been utilized to collect data suitable for setting the transition probabilities between states and
explore the deviations in behaviour, and thus provide an estimate of the actual customer behaviour. With
that, the expected environmental impact closer to the reality could have been evaluated and compared with
that from the required behaviour. Doing so, one could with more conﬁdence conclude whether or not the
solution is environmentally attractive. Had the use of Markov chains for evaluation of environmental impact
been planned from the beginning of the project, initiatives such as questioners and prototype testing to
determine actual behaviour would have been made in the early phases. As that was not an option, it is
instead recommended to conduct such data collection retrospectively. With the tool used to run the Markov
chain simulations now developed, it would be an easy task to feed it with a new behaviour model and complete
the evaluation of environmental impact for a reusable container.

7.4
Feasibility of mycelium composites
Assuming that a mycelium composite container can be made to meet the requirements of insulating ice
cream packaging constructed in chapter 2, such would solution represent the least environmental harm as
shown in chapter 4. However, that assumption would have to be conﬁrmed through material and prototype
development in order to conduct the necessary tests. Had the material been more established and used in
industry, ﬁnite elements analysis could perhaps have been used as an indicator whether the assumption is
correct. However, since that is not the case experimental work would be the best option. Experimental work
was as already mentioned, initially planned to enable the necessary material and prototype tests of mycelium
composite containers. With the facilities required to conduct such experimental work not being accessible
(due to the Covid-19 lockdown), the possibility to perform such tests was lost. Whether a mycelium com-
posite container can meet the requirements remains therefore unknown, and should be investigated before
any ﬁnal conclusions are made.
Another element of necessary investigation is that of scaling the production of mycelium composite containers.
This is important for mainly two reasons: (1) two understand if enough containers can be produced to
meet the demand, and (2) to know if it is a ﬁnancial viable solution. Because the production of mycelium
composites is a matter of growing, the time required to make one container would approximately be 5 days.
A lead time of 5 days would never come close to meet the yearly demand of 40000 containers. Multiple
containers would therefore have to be grown simultaneously, and thus provide a batch of containers every
ﬁfth day. To produce 40000 containers in one year, would the average batch size have to be 548 containers.
Since the demand of containers are higher during summer is it unlikely that the productions can be evenly
distributed throughout the year, meaning that the maximum production capacity should likely be more than
548 containers. Maintaining such production, without any automated solutions being available, might make
each container too costly even though the raw materials are of low cost. Producing batches of containers
additionally means that the amount of molds required would equal the batch size, which again is likely to
increase the cost. The molds for mycelium composite products is however fairly simple and cheap, unlike
molds for e.g. polymer injection molding, as they can be made through thermoforming of thin sheets of
polymer. Finally, the production of large batches requires space enough to ﬁt in every mold. Information
regarding a production scaled to meet the demand of containers, such as cost of raw material, mold expenses,
facility rent, salaries etc. should therefore be gathered in order to estimate the cost of mycelium composite
containers. It is likely to turn out to be more expensive than containers made of any polymer type, however
its environmental capabilities might add customer value enough to justify the increase of cost. Making such
evaluation is however only possible if the container cost is known, why the matter of scaling the production
is an important aspect to be considered.

By constructing requirements trough translations of customer and stakeholder needs, and utilize principles of
systems engineering to organize and derive additional requirements across all system levels, was the targets
for a new container set. It showed that the reduction of environmental impact was of highest priority, even if
it would require a solution of potentially higher cost. The reason being that reducing environmental impact
represents value enough for Social Foodies to justify some cost increases. Two new concepts of ice cream
containers was deﬁned as, (1) a single use biodegradable container and (2) a reusable container.
Using
the requirements was constraints and objectives set to guide the material selection process of choosing a
material for each concept. For the single use biodegradable container mycelium composite was chosen due
to its unique environmental properties, including the ability to be disposed as compostable wast. Polymers
based on starch, named as thermoplastic starch, was selected for the concept of a reusable container where
durability represents an important parameter.
The environmental impact of the two concepts and their
respective chosen materials was evaluated, in order to decide which could be expected as the best solution.
The evaluation assessed the CO2 and energy footprints, by calculating the total amount of CO2 emissions
and energy consumption to be expected from the production and use, of one year of container consumption.
It showed that the single use biodegradable container would provide the lowest environmental impact on
both parameters. The performance of the reusable container concept was through Markov chain simulations,
found to be sensitive to customer behaviour. The behaviour required for a reusable container to represent a
lower environmental impact than containers of EPS, was additionally discovered. The reusable container can
thus be a viable solution, if the actual behaviour of Social Foodies customers reﬂect the minimum required
behaviour. It can therefore be concluded that both presented solutions oﬀers an opportunity to lower the
environmental impact of Social Foodies. More research is however required to understand the technical and
ﬁnancial feasibility, of especially mycelium composite, required to realize such solution. Additional research
of the actual behaviour of Social Foodies customers is required to fully understand the expected performance
of a reusable container, is needed as well. Conducting that research would complete the information needed
to suﬃciently inform the ﬁnal choice between the two container concepts.

Post Project Reﬂections on Learning Objectives
Every project is likely to change as more information becomes available, however with the change of scope
due to the Covid-19 the skill set learned throughout this project has changed more than expected. This
section will discuss those learning’s and how they diﬀer from those originally set out to be made from the
learning objectives.
The original plan was to develop at set of requirements based on customer and stakeholder needs (chap-
ter 1 and 2), which were to be used to test the ﬁnal solution against. A comprehensive material selection
with an detailed classiﬁcation of all relevant factors was to be made, in order to fully understand the ca-
pabilities of the material to be chosen. With a material chosen, material samples and prototypes were to
be developed and tested against the requirements as a ﬁnal conﬁrmation of the solution. The original plan
thus reﬂects a project with more emphasis on the material science, compared to how the project ended being.
The learning objectives for this project are as follows:
• Identify, describe and apply relevant theory and methodology to conduct a holistic development process
• Analyze user needs and food packaging regulations and articulate multi-dimensional requirements for
the system surrounding insulated food packaging
• Outline a methodology for selecting materials for sustainable insulated food packaging
• Classify function, material, shape, processes, economic and environmental factors in the material selec-
tion.
• Assess potential materials for conﬂicting constraints and objectives
• Suggest a sustainable material for insulated food packaging
• Evaluate the potential and feasibility of the project and discuss the related implications
• Document the project results with a structured and adequate report in accordance with my ﬁeld of
study
• Conduct experimental research about thermal properties of mycelium based materials
The direction set by Chapter 1 and 2 was almost completed once the Covid-19 lockdown was initiated. Chap-
ter 3 has always been intended to represent an exploration of materials, while chapter 4 should have relied
on LCA data and experimental work in order to make a ﬁnal selection. With the latter not being available,
especially the learning objectives regarding evaluation of potential and feasibility and thermal properties of
mycelium based materials have not been achievable. It has additionally reduced the amount of resources
being allocated towards classiﬁcation of function, material, shape, processes, economic and environmental

==PAGE:81==
factors in the material selection, and the assessment of conﬂicting constraints and objectives.
The learning objectives of classifying function, shape, processes, economic and environmental factors, and
assessing potential materials for conﬂicting constraints and objectives, could however have been met to the
extent originally intended. Classifying more factors in the material selection would have meant a more de-
tailed selection process as more information would be included. More detailed constraints and, especially
objectives, could additionally have been made. Thus the only learning objectives directly aﬀected by the
lockdown are those of experimental work, and evaluation of feasibility. The inclusion of Markov chain sim-
ulations was meant as the replacement of the two latter learning objectives, however the resources allocated
to the two ﬁrst mentioned learning objectives was reduced in order to develop and implement the Markov
chains to the project in a valuable way. This means that the extent of the material selection process has
been smaller than originally intended as the scope shifted towards a comparison of two conceptually diﬀerent
solutions, using Markov chains. A total of four learning objectives has thereby been aﬀected either directly
or indirectly by the lockdown, and represents a general decrease of the extent of the material scientiﬁc related
aspects in the project. However, with those aspects being those I am the most familiar with prior to this
project, the change of scope has oﬀered a opportunity for additional learnings.
Comparing conceptually diﬀerent solutions using Markov chain simulations as a replacement for the more
material scientiﬁc aspects just mentioned, has presented a new challenge and allowed me to acquire skills
from a ﬁeld I am much less familiar with. I believe that the idea of using Markov chains to combine LCA data
for an object with the behaviour of its users to conduct environmental assessment, could be used in many
cases where environmental sustainability is part of the product development activities. The principle of the
tool developed in this project to make such assessments, could thus potentially be used outside this project
as well and represent value by itself. Knowing neither python nor the concept of Markov chains prior to
this project, represents the coding of the models required to run the Markov chain simulations both learning
aspects of programming, statistics, and data visualization. Getting to know these concepts to a degree where
the project could really beneﬁt from it, has been time consuming but nonetheless a valuable process for both
the project and the set of competencies gained from the project. Though being a set of competencies diﬀerent
from that initially planned to strengthen, it is a set of competencies I am satisﬁed having acquired. I see one
of the core competencies of Design & Innovation students, as the ability to work across many diﬀerent ﬁelds
and adapt to changes faced along the way. The changes made to this project provides an example of just that.
