Abstract
In the last decades, researchers and society have been increasingly aware of the threat
and predominance of microplastic pollution in the marine environment. Microplastic particles
are plastic fragments with length scales smaller than 5 mm. Even though it exists a large
scope of literature regarding microplastic pollution, there is a sizable lack of knowledge on the
understanding of microplastic transport behaviour in nearshore regions, where they interact
with a complex hydrodynamic system. In this study, over 18 microplastic particles of diﬀerent
density, shape and size are evaluated in a wave ﬂume in two diﬀerent beach conﬁgurations.
The experiments are carried out for more than 350 h. The results show a relationship between
the transport direction with the non-dimensional Dean number. Particles with high settling
velocities are likely to move towards the oﬀshore region, while lighter particles with lower
settling velocity tend to deposit in the beach. Besides, it is observed a strong dependency
on the shape and also the size, regarding their transport fate. The outcomes of the work
are discussed and it is stated that there is a need of approaching this problem in a more
extensive range of conditions in future studies.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nowadays, plastic has achieved a central and decisive position, with extensive
commercial, industrial, medicinal and municipal applications [Andrady, 2011]. The annual
global production of plastic products has increased every year since the starting point of plastic
in 1950. More than 367 M of tones of plastic were produced in 2020 [Hannah Ritchie, 2012].
In addition, more than 50% of global population inhabit close to coastal regions, which means
that is a potential hot spot for microplastic pollution [Cole et al., 2011].
The major danger of plastic pollution in marine environments is the outrageous re-
sistance of plastic to biodegrade. That makes the residence time of plastics in water extremely
high which can reach thousands of years. This problematic induces long term challenges for
the sustainability of ecosystems in aquatic regions [van Sebille et al., 2012]. Additionally, mi-
croplastic contamination is a huge concern due to their capacity to be transported over vast dis-
tances generating potential harm to diﬀerent ecosystems [Khatmullina and Chubarenko, 2019].
Furthermore, apart from the direct presence in open ocean and nearshore re-
gions, microplastics are easily swallowed by marine organisms which aﬀect the food chain
[Cressey, 2016, Chubarenko et al., 2018]. These plastic fragments are conceived as a risk to
marine wildlife because they can be a transport vector of toxic metals and may accumulate
organic pollutants[Isobe et al., 2014]. According to [Karthik et al., 2018] which studied the
microplastics along the southeast coast of India, microplastics were present in more than 10%
of the ﬁshes and highlight the ability to enter in the marine food web.
Due to this increasing problematic and an escalation in the proximity of the issue,
a great amount of coastal zones are potentially in risk and there is a need to approach
the situation in order to ﬁnd a solution. The understanding of the microplastic transport
behaviour will lead us to the potential hotspots, and thus, the possibility of managing or
even remove the pollutants from the zones in more danger.

Outline of the Thesis
The structure of the thesis is outlined in the following.
In section 2 the background research is described. The problematic of microplastics
in the marine environment is presented through a compilation of last years investigations.
Afterward, a brief summary of the main aspects of microplastic properties is introduced.
Then, an outline of the two main studies in microplastic transport in nearshore regions:
Forsberg et al., 2020, and Kerpen et al., 2020,; is developed.
In section 3 the methodology employed during the experiments is included. Firstly,
the model set-up and the conditions of the experiments is deﬁned. Moreover, a deep analysis
of the material properties adopted is carried out. Then, the procedure of the experiments is
presented and also how the data is stored. Finally, a wave analysis is undertaken in order to
strengthen the validity of the results.
In section 4 the results are presented divided in two sections. In the ﬁrst section a
discussion of the experiments using the equilibrium beach proﬁle is done. A cross-comparison
between cases is also achieved. In the second section, the main outcomes of the second layout
is presented. In this scheme a constant slope beach proﬁle is used.
In section 5 and section 6 the main conclusions derived from the thesis experiments
are presented, and future work recommendations is suggested.
In Appendix A the relevant plots used to carry out the analysis are shown.

Literature Review
2.1
General Scope
In the last years and due to the combination of increasing compiled data and
observed ecological consequences, microplastic have acquired a growing and justiﬁed re-
search interest [Andrady, 2011]. This concern has also reached the society and thus the
politicians, as the eﬀect of microplastics generate signiﬁcant consequences in human health,
economics, tourism and beach aesthetics [Thompson et al., 2009]. Diﬀerent investigations
carried out in the last decade have broadcasted the existence of microplastics in a wide
range of ecosystems and marine habitats [Zhang, 2017]. A large abundance of literature
states that microplastics are present in all kind of marine environments, including deep sea
zones, coastal regions, ocean surface waters, glaciers and also depositions in beach areas
[Ballent A, 2016, Kerpen et al., 2020, van Sebille et al., 2020]. The amount of microplastics
in the surface ocean was up to 528.000 t in 2018 [Lebreton, 2019] and it is estimated than it
is going to grow exponentially and in 2050 we will have more than 2.5 M of microplastics
in surface. Additonally, CSIRO agency estimates that more than 14 M t of microplastics
exist in the ocean ﬂoor. Therefore, marine pollution from microplastics has been stated as a
worldwide environmental and ecological hazard.
There is a huge number of literature treating the biological and chemical char-
acteristics of microplastics.
It has also been deeply studied their interaction with the
diverse elements of the marine habitat, the biological threat and the environmental im-
pacts of it. In terms of transport plastic behaviour in open oceans has been analyzed in
the last years [DiBenedetto et al., 2018]. However, it exists a considerable lack of investi-
gation on the understanding of characterizing the physical transport of microplastics and
the behaviour in nearshore zones and shallow waters [Zhang, 2017, Chubarenko et al., 2018,
Kerpen et al., 2020, Forsberg et al., 2020]. These coastal regions are contemplated as a tem-
porary sink for plastics because of the onshore transport induced by coastal hydrodynamics
[Zhang, 2017], but also an income source, particularly in extreme events [van Sebille et al., 2012].

Therefore, this gap appeals the need of experiments to explore and recognize the inﬂuence
of the huge amount of parameters involved in this process (i.e. particle size, particle shape,
particle density, sediment size, beach proﬁle and wave conditions).
Event thought the principal hotspot is recorded adjacent to the river mouth, the
leading source of microplastic presence in saltwater environments do not come from external
origins such as direct introduction with runoﬀ. It comes from degradation over long time peri-
ods from bigger portions (mesoplastics) by physical actions, combined with photo-degradation
and biochemical degradation and also including the abrasive consequences caused by waves
and currents interacting with the natural sediment [Andrady, 2011, Kerpen et al., 2020]. This
degradation is more likely to take place on beaches than in seawater as they receive more ultra-
violet radiation and exists a greater load of mechanical erosion [Gregory and Andrady, 2003].

Microplastics have diﬀerent densities, shapes and sizes, and all of these physical
properties change with time spent in marine environment due to processes such as biofouling,
weathering, mechanical degradation and other external factor [Kershaw, 2015]. Due to this
iterative fragmentation processes where larger plastics break and become microplastics, the
amount of them in the ocean is still increasing. That is the reason why their physical transport
characteristics are complex to determine and each microplastic is unique in terms of physical
properties. Thus, size and shape are considered as dynamic parameters [Zhang, 2017].
The comprehension of plastic dispersion is an essential knowledge void for plastic
litter management and even removal operations [Alsina et al., 2020]. An adequate man-
agement of plastic waste necessary in order to pause the growing negative eﬀects on the
marine environment.The study of microplastic transport fate diﬀers from the commonly
deduced modeling of conservative tracers, Lagrangian ﬂoats or oil spill patches used for

to sediment grains and other marine particles as sour pellets or phytoplankton. Transport
in nearshore regions is one of the main mechanisms controlling the environmental fate and
dangers from microplastics due to the regulation of spatial and temporal distribution among
diﬀerent aquatic habitats [Zhang, 2017].
Also, it has been observed that a predominant percentage of microplastics transferred
to the marine environment is retained in nearshore regions such as beach, estuaries and
wetland sediments [Zhang, 2017]. However, the physical processes involving this idea are
unknown and there is a need of further investigation. Additionally, coastal areas host both
the main sources and the main end users of the marine environment. Therefore, it is highly
beneﬁcial and challenging at the same time to reach an adequate comprehension of the general
features involving the transport fate of microplastics [Chubarenko et al., 2018]
Is commonly assumed that microplastics behave likewise to sediments. Nevertheless,
while the density of microplastics varies between 0,2 and 1,4 g/cmˆ3, natural sediment has
an average density of 2,65 g/cmˆ3 [Waldschläger and Schüttrumpf, 2019]. That means than
microplastic can be buoyant or non-buoyant. This matter do not inﬂuence the fact that more
than 50% of the microplastics are heavier than water, so they can go down and sink to the bed
without any other element regulating it. In addition, while natural sediment are considered
in a general perspective as spheres, microplastic shapes are substantially diﬀerent. As the
shape may change over time due to the processes mentioned before, the settling velocity and
hence the transport behaviour is altered during their lifetime.
Still, sediment transport is the basis to interpret and forecast the microplastic be-
haviour in coastal regions. The main idea is that the motion of sediment is driven by the com-
bination between the under-current ﬂow and the action of wave skewness [Grasso et al., 2011].
Consequently, when it takes place a change in the balance between these processes it appears
the know theory of onshore or oﬀshore transport. However, as microplastics are much lighter
than natural sediment grains, the response will be diﬀerent and will include processes such
as stokes drift, roller advection and wave-driven turbulence, in addition to the wind eﬀect
[Forsberg et al., 2020].
Is important to remark the diﬀerence between transport studies in deep water and
in coastal zones. Nearshore hydrodynamics are way more complex and the amount of factors
involved is increased. These multi-variable processes is not trivial and a signiﬁcant problem is
that are not currently well understood [Forsberg et al., 2020]. The balance between driving
forces is completely diﬀerent. In ocean waters, currents are in the geostrophic balance, so
pressure gradient balances the Coriolis force [Chubarenko et al., 2018]. But if we get closer to
the coast this balance becomes diﬀerent due to external factors such as wind or bottom friction.
In intermediate and shallow waters the fate of plastic transport is controlled by nonlinear
gravity waves propagating toward the shoreline [Cózar et al., 2014]. Therefore, the transport
and behaviour of microplastics in the nearshore environment is primarily determined by surface-
generated turbulence due to wave breaking processes [Alsina et al., 2020] . Additionally,
nearshore hydrodynamics alters the sediment transport and erosion-deposition processes.

2.2
Physical and Dynamic Properties
Microplastic properties, physical as well as dynamic ones, are the root and the
starting point to expand the knowledge of microplastic transport. In order to fulﬁll satisfactory
experiments which will lead us to the ﬁrst perspectives in the topic, an appropriate deﬁnition
of the properties is necessary. Below the principal and signiﬁcant microplastic properties are
described based in recent studies.
The term microplastic size has been described variously by diﬀerent researchers. The
interpretation of which range of sizes should include has not been clariﬁed yet and still there
is not a common universally accepted frame. Microplastics were ﬁrstly mentioned in order
to describe tiny fragments of plastics. [Gregory and Andrady, 2003] were one of the ﬁrst to
deﬁne a maximum threshold of 5 mm. The lower limit is more diﬀuse as it usually depends on
the mesh size used in the ﬁeld survey, which varies from 0,5 to 2 mm [Chubarenko et al., 2018,
Khatmullina and Chubarenko, 2019]. A common European categorization for size limits is
macroplastic (>200 mm), mesoplastic (5-200 mm), microplastic (1-5 mm) and nanoplastic (<1
mm). While macroplastics and mesoplastic predominant transport pathways are ﬂoatation
and suspension, with particles smaller than 5 mm, aggregation and sedimentation starts
becoming relevant [Zhang, 2017]. According to [Eriksen et al., 2014], almost 92% of the
plastics in marine climate refer to particles with linear length between 0.33 mm to 4.75
mm. Therefore, microplastic management is the main focus for partly erase the plastic
contamination in aquatic environments.

2.2. Physical and Dynamic Properties
The shape is one of the most diﬃcult parameters to characterize and quantify.
As mentioned before, microplastic range of shapes is remarkably wide. Moreover, the vast
majority of particles have an irregular form. The problem with irregular shapes is that their
characteristics diverge from the traditional approach established based on the hypothesis of
spherical particles [Chubarenko et al., 2018]. Thus it is important to classify by the shape
in an appropriate way in order to understand the basic patterns on microplastic transport.
This broad spectrum of shapes depend on two factors. Firstly, the original form when was
released which can be spheres, pellets, cylinders, ﬁbers, ﬁlaments, plastic ﬁlms, granules...
Secondly, the fragmentation processes experimented in their lifetime [Zhang, 2017]. Basically,
fragments from plastic products and pellets are the dominant shapes in the marine environment
[Cózar et al., 2014]. One common method to categorize the particles shape is deﬁned in
[Chubarenko et al., 2016] where three groups are constituted: quasi-one-dimensional (ﬁbers,
lines), quasi-two-dimensional (ﬁlms, thin disks, ﬂakes) and three-dimensional (pellets, ovoids,
cylynders, spheres). Finally, is important to take into account the surface texture or roughness
in the shape analysis as it may have an eﬀect on the transport fate [Chubarenko et al., 2018].
The density has an important role in terms of how the particle is transported as
it determines the buoyancy and hence the mobility in the water. It basically depends on
the polymer types and the manufacturing processes [Zhang, 2017]. However, the density of
microplastics can easily be varied from the original values due to the amount of processes
which is encountered in the water. Additionally, the adhesion of algae or other marine
elements can make a microplastic pass from buoyant to non-buoyant, and therefore dras-
tically change its behaviour. Plastics with density close to sea water density are the ones
more present in saltwater environments. In this group the most common are polypropy-
lene (PP)(0,85-0,90 g/cm3), polyethylene (PE)(0,90-0,95 g/cm3) and polystyrene (PS)(1-1,1
g/cm3) [Zhang, 2017, Chubarenko et al., 2018]. Then, high-density plastics are likely found
in benthic environments [Andrady, 2011, Hidalgo-Ruz et al., 2012] and include the following
types: polyamide (PA)(1,05-1,15 g/cm3), polyethilene terephthalate (PET) (1,30-1,45 g/cm3)
and polyvinyl chloride (PVC) (1,15-1,58 g/cm3). Lastly, plastics with low-density are less
common and are detected along shorelines and in the sea surface.
The sinking and rising velocities are the dynamic properties which governs the
transport fate of microplastics. They both depend on the size of the particle, the vari-
ation of density between the ﬂuid and the particle and the shape [Ballent et al., 2012,
Chubarenko et al., 2016, Khatmullina and Chubarenko, 2019]. From previous experiments
it has established that terminal settling velocity of diﬀerent microplastic ranges between 1-127
mm/s. For terminal rising velocities, less studies have been carried out, but the span goes
from 1 to 43 mm/s. From experimental data of sinking velocity of spheres and cylinders it has
been observed an adequate correspondence with the semi-empirical formulations developed
for natural sediment grains [Dietrich, 1982, Ahrens, 2000]. A concept which is relevant to
keep in mind is the fact that terminal sinking velocity is not the same as the one in real
conditions in the surf zone. Water turbulence near the surface and long-shore and cross-shore
currents may change the pace along the water column [Khatmullina and Chubarenko, 2019].
Furthermore, when a particle is falling, it does not follow a rectilinear trajectory and the

orientation depends mainly on the shape [Chubarenko et al., 2018]. This issue can imply that
the sinking velocity is not obtained straight-forward. Usually thin particles are more brushed
by oscillations and disk-shaped particles orientates by maximum drag force and tumbles side
to side following a zig-zag track [Chubarenko et al., 2016]. Referring to the re suspension of
the particle there are two key parameters: the ratio of the characteristic size of microplastic
to the bed roughness and the shape of the microplastic [Khatmullina and Chubarenko, 2019].
Lastly, even though it is not deeply treated in this study, it is signiﬁcant to reference
the importance of analyzing the critical shields parameter of microplastics in order to

small-sized microplastics it is also relevant to take into account the so-called hiding-exposure
eﬀect, which may aﬀect the starting point of the transport.
2.3
Previous Experiments
As stated before, there is a wide gap of knowledge referent to the transport fate of
microplastics in the surf zone. On one hand due to the lack of knowledge of how microplastics
behave in the marine environment, especially with the variation of properties during their
course. On the other side because still there is not a fully understanding of the complex
processes which occur in the nearshore zone and how they interact with microplastics. For
this reason there is an urgent need of undertake experiments simulating this complicated
system. In this subsection it is summarized the relevant results and outcomes of the two
microplastic experiments carried out up to the current date which are included in Forsberg et
al., 2020 and Kerpen et al., 2020. Firstly the experimental set up, materials and procedure is
brieﬂy introduced. Then, the main conclusions are described.
2.3.1
Forsberg et al 2020
In Forsberg et al 2020., the ﬂume adopted has a length of 6 m and the water level
is at 0.22 m at the deep end of the tank. In this case the bed proﬁle is without sand and
a constant slope of 1:18. The particles employed consist on three typical shapes; nearly
spherical 3 mm pellets, squared sheets with 5 mm sides and ﬁbres of 5 mm. For each type
it is placed 50 buoyant (LDP and PP) and 50 non-buoyant (PAI and PVC). Thus a total
of 300 particles are placed distributed in two points: shoaling zone and breaking zone. The
experiment procedure is divided in four cases. The ﬁrst three cases have a wave height of 9.2
cm and a wave period of 1.2 s. It is carried out one as a reference case, one with oﬀshore
directed wind and the third one with onshore directed wind. The fourth case is with non-wind
conditions and with a wave height of 8 cm and wave period of 0.7 s. The set-up is exposed
8
==PAGE:18==
2.3. Previous Experiments
to regular waves and the experiment length is minimum ﬁve minutes and it stops when the
equilibrium condition (no motion) has been achieved.
In all four cases almost all heavy particles were accumulated in the breaking zone,
which has a diﬀerent position in each case. First they moved oﬀshore but after a while
they start moving onshore due to wave non-linearity. The deposition in the breaking zone
happened due to local balance between return ﬂow and wave forcing. This goes in parallel
with the sand motion in the formation of bars. The only case which showed a quite diﬀerent
trend was the fourth one with short waves case which presented some particles remaining in
the shoaling zone. For light particles it is appreciated a stronger dependence on the shape.
For the reference case and the onshore wind the sheets remained in surf zone, the pellets
beached and the ﬁbres were predominant in the beach but some of them also concentrated in
the surf zone. For oﬀshore wind all light particles moved to oﬀshore due to surface stress of
the wind. The short wave case showed similar results as the reference and onshore case for
pellets and ﬁbres, however some sheets remained in the shoaling and the breaking zone.
Therefore, Forsberg et al 2020 conclude the following interpretations. It exists a
hotspot of heavy microplastic particles in the breaking zone as they react as natural sediment.
Light particles with low drag like pellets remain in the swash zone and then beach, while
particles with high drag like sheets are more susceptible to return ﬂow and thus stay in the
surf zone.
Lastly, it is necessary to include the subsequent comments. As they do not have a
sandy bed proﬁle, the eﬀect of the ripples is not present. Also the formation of bars is a key
aspect which inﬂuence the microplastic transport and is not taken into account. Another
feature is that they use regular waves in order to simplify the study, which creates a need
to deepen in the irregular waves environment. The last element to consider is the use of
freshwater. Working with salt water would increase the buoyancy forces which may aﬀect
the motion patterns of microplastics.
2.3.2
Kerpen et al 2020
In Kerpen et al 2020., 13 microplastic particles with diﬀerent density, shape and
size are studied. The experiments are carried out in a ﬂume of 6.7 m length and the water
level is at 0.3 m. The reference bed is made with ﬁne-sand of Dn=0.19 mm, and consists in
three parts: an initial 1:8 slope, then a ﬂat stretch and the ﬁnal slope to the berm of 1:5.5.
The particles, in general 100 for each type, are placed close to the shoreline. Around 40000
regular waves with mean wave height around 10 cm and mean wave period around 1.10 s
are generated for each of the two cases: the ﬁrst one of 23 h and the second one of 14 h, in
order to ensure the validity of results. The particles used cover the following range: nominal
diameter between 0.5 and 4.1 mm, buoyant and non-buoyant, and spherical and disk-shaped.
Then the microplastic distributions are analyzed in cross-shore and vertical direction.

Both cases are analyzed together. The main outcome of the test is the perception that
the location of the peak accumulation point moves in onshore direction as the microplastic’s
settling velocity increases. They state a dependency of the amassing microplastic position on
the orbital ﬂow velocity as it is reduced by increasing the water depth. Therefore, they assert
that particles with higher sinking velocity which are more dense follow a more streamlined
shape and vice versa. Additionally, they relate the decrease of microplastic density with an
increase of water depth, and thus the distance to the shoreline.
Therefore, from Kerpen et al 2020 is deduced that the accumulation peak is likely
to move towards shallow water (onshore) with increasing particle size or density. They have
only examined round-shaped particles so it emerges a require to approach the shape subject.
The vertical distribution analysis is relevant as they have substantially morphological change.
As in Forsberg et al., 2020, as they work in two-dimensional conditions there is a gap to ﬁll
in terms of eﬀects of long-shore transport.

3.1. Model Set-up
In order to reach the pursued induced waves, it has been employed a hydraulic
piston-type wave maker rigged with Active Wave Absorption Control System to the extent
of absorbing the reﬂected waves. The software used has been the Wave Synthesizer 2.30
developed by DHI Water & Environment. For all the experiments a 30 minutes irregular

factor of γ = 3.3. That leads to a spectral signiﬁcant wave height of Hmo=0.152 m and a
peak period of Tp=1.6 s at h=0.5 m measured in the wave paddle.
A total of 10 wave gauges are distributed along the ﬂume with the aim of obtaining
the respective wave measurements and be sure that the target waves are similar to the waves
generated by the wave maker. The wave gauges 1-5 are located in a ﬁxed position in the
ﬂat bed stretch measuring the conditions in deep water. Wave gauges 6-10 are set across
the rest of the ﬂume and the position of them is changed for every run in order to have
measurements covering all the coastal proﬁle and thus, have an adequate representation of
wave hydrodynamics.
Calibration of the wave gauges are conducted before every run such that the
robustness and credibility of the data is enhanced. The purpose of the calibration is to
guarantee a correspondence between the surface elevation and the voltage signal received
by the gauges. The procedure is based on moving the gauges in the vertical direction
for 5 known positions, which can be seen in ﬁgure 3.2a. With this process, the voltage
signal at the diﬀerent heights is acquired and is plotted with respect to the vertical motion.
The voltage signal is presented in ﬁgure 3.2b. Then, this voltage signal is translated to
a geometric elevation. Finally, the variation of the predicted distance with the measured
distance is obtained and limited to 1.5 mm. If the value is over this threshold the wave gauge
measurements are disregarded for the run in consideration.

3.2
Materials
As described before, in nature there is a huge range of microplastic sizes and shapes.
That makes the analysis of their transportation patterns and respective hot spots much more
complex. For this study it has been selected a total of 18 non-buoyant particles with diﬀerent
sizes and shape in order to cover the maximum possible scope.
There is not established a universal deﬁnition for the size range of microplastics,
but is commonly accepted between 1-5 mm [Andrady, 2011]. Smaller particles are considered
nanoplastics and if the size is higher than 5 mm then they are classiﬁed as mesoplastics. In
this research it has been employed sizes between 2-5 mm because particles with size lower
than 2 mm was not possible to track them in the ﬂume.
The particle’s shape is very relevant for understanding how is transported in the
coastal environment [Chubarenko et al., 2018]. Also, it is a parameter that is continuously
changing due to fragmentation processes such as mechanical abrasion and photo-degradation
[Zhang, 2017]. In general the shapes that are found in the sea are irregular. Nevertheless,
in order to simplify the analysis and have a ﬁrst general insight, for this study it has been
used regular shapes: 6 cubes, 5 spheres, 2 circular-disk, 3 square-disk, 1 square-cylinder
and 1 rectangular-cylinder. Following the classiﬁcation of [Chubarenko et al., 2016], in the
experiments have been employed 12 three-dimensional particles, 5 quasi-two-dimensional
particles and 1 quasi-one-dimensional particle.
In terms of the type of material of the microplastic, the spheres P7-P10 have been
obtained externally and are composed of Polyoxymethylene (POM) which is considered
a high-density plastic and its density range goes from 1,40-1,45 g/cm3. The sphere P11
have been acquired from another source and the material remains unknown. For the rest
of microplastics, they have been printed by a 3D printer provided in the laboratory with
ﬁlaments of Polylactic acid (PLA) which is also classiﬁed as a high-density plastic and its
density varies from 1,2-1,43 g/cm3.
Particle density is a key parameter in order to deduce the particle motion and its
buoyancy in water [Zhang, 2017]. For the density measurements it was implemented the
modiﬁed method from ISO 1183-1 (2012). For each particle it was selected 3 sets of 5 particles
and the ﬁnal density was the average of the 3 sets calculated separately using the following
procedure. The 5 particles were placed in a 100ml-plastic beaker ﬁlled with distilled water
with ρw = 0.9982g/ml and whisked in order to remove the air bubbles in the surface. Then a
small spoon of concentrated solution of zinc chloride (98.5+%) with ρz = 2.91 g/ml. The
outcome was mixed after every spoon. The process ﬁnished when the 5 particles reached the
neutral buoyancy, which means that they stayed suspended in the liquid without moving for
at least 1 minute. Once this point was achieved, the liquid was spilled in a graduated cylinder
and with a digital scale the weight, and thus, the density was calculated. As mentioned
before, all particles used in the experiments were non-buoyant and the range of densities
oscillated between 1.06 and 1.36 g/cm3.

Settling velocity calculation is essential for the understanding of hydrodynamics
and sedimentology. It is a key parameter in the processes of suspension, sedimentation and
sediment transport [Khatmullina and Isachenko, 2017]. Settling velocity was determined by
executing sink experiments using a circular glass column ﬁlled with distilled water. The glass
had a height of ... and on the sides were graph paper for the camera calibration. It was also
placed a light on the top in order to improve the visualization. Before the experiments it
was important to make sure that there were no bubbles to do not have extra drag. Particles
were allocated a little bit below the water surface so that the surface tension do not aﬀect
the motion, and then let fall freely in the column. The settling velocity was calculated as
the ratio between the distance and the duration of the sink when the velocity was constant
(gravity force and drag force in equilibrium). For the decision of the number of repetitions
necessary to reach an accurate result, the following procedure was implemented. The most
irregular particle was chosen due to its higher uncertainty. The measurement was repeated
for a great conﬁdent number, which was decided to be 100. Then, the results were analyzed
statistically in order to observe how many runs were needed to achieve a 95% of accuracy,
and the conclusion was 16 repetitions. To be in the safe side it was ﬁnally considered that
with 20 repetitions it was enough to state a suﬃcient veracity of the results.

Procedure
The experiments were developed in two diﬀerent beach proﬁles. The ﬁrst beach
proﬁle employed was based in previous experiments carried out in the ﬂume in which it was
observed that it was very close to the equilibrium proﬁle. Thus, this proﬁle was considered
more realistic and the starting point of the investigation. As it was unviable to add all the
particles in the ﬂume, this ﬁrst experiment was separated in four cases. Case 1 and Case 2
were undertaken using the same particles because the Case 1 had to be terminated sooner
than expected due to a problem with the wave generator. Though, that make the data more
reliable and create a path to perform a cross-comparison between cases. Also, Case 4 had to
be ﬁnished after 24 h due to a miss-calibration of the wave generator which create higher
induced waves. In this occasion, the particles were stabilized so it could be concluded that
the case duration was legitimate. The second beach proﬁle had a constant slope bed and due
to the thesis length, it was only possible to complete one case with seven chosen particles,
Case 5.
The procedure is described hereunder.
First of all, the particles were equally
distributed at 3 spots: at the shoreline, at the middle of the plateau and at the breaker bar.
A total of 90 particles of each type were used, so in each position there were 30 particles of
every microplastic. For the Case 5 in which it was not possible to identify the exact location
of the breaker bar, the decision of the third spot was the potential point where the breaker
bar will be arranged based on the previous nourishment’s experiments in the ﬂume. Here
is important to mention that due to a laboratory mistake, for Case 3 and 4 the particles
which were supposed to be located in the shoreline, were instead placed 1 m away in oﬀshore
direction. However, after analyzing the data obtained it can be stated that this issue did not
a major impact in the results.
Later of the particle setting, the beach proﬁle and microplastics were exposed to a
initial wave run of 30 min. After each run, the particle positions were written down manually
in 5 cm bins located along the ﬂume. In ﬁgure 3.5 it can be seen the bin partition every 5 cm.
Then the data was introduced in the system and stored in the corresponding Excel ﬁle. The
next run duration was also 30 min, then 1 h and ﬁnally 2 h. At this point, having achieved 4
h, the runs were performed for 4 h. For Case 5, in order to be in synchrony with the previous
nourishment experiments, the runs length were 5 h. The case duration was governed by the
non-motion of the particles, when the microplastics were completely stabilized.

4.1.1
Bed Proﬁle Evolution
The initial beach conﬁguration is chosen in this ﬁrst study because of previous
nourishment experiments as an equilibrium proﬁle. It is considered as a more realistic layout
and therefore, an acceptable starting point for the experiments.
In ﬁgure 4.1a it can be observed the initial bed scans for the 4 cases that comprehend
this ﬁrst study, with the water level at h = 0.5 m. The similarity between the proﬁles can be
appreciated. It starts with an oﬀshore slope m = 1:8 followed by the breaker bar located
at x = 11.75 m. Then, it comes the plateau zone (12.25 m < x < 20 m). From this point,
the beach slope begins with the beach bar at x = 21.5 m and the shoreline is located at x =
21.925m. The berm has a slope m = 1:5 and it ﬁnishes at x = 22.65 m.
On the other hand, in ﬁgure 4.1b the ﬁnal bed scans are represented. Due to the
ﬁnal polluted runs of Case 4 with higher wave heights it was decided to not consider its ﬁnal
proﬁle. It can be seen that a combination of a prevailing oﬀshore sediment transport from
plateau region to breaker bar, and an onshore sediment transport towards the beach bar and
the berm. The oﬀshore process leads to a slowly oﬀshore migration of the breaker bar which
after 80-84 h is located at x = 11.25 m. That mechanism also increases the sediment layer
in the oﬀshore slope by 7-8 cm. On the contrary, the onshore transport migrates the berm
slightly further from the shoreline, at x = 22.8 m, and also in a higher elevation. That issue
causes that less waves over-tops the berm as the bed proﬁle is exposed to more wave runs.
Another aspect to remark is the fact that the erosive part of the plateau is located only in
the zone closer to the beach (16 m < x < 20 m).

It is considered that the resistance of a particle to a ﬂow acting on it can be indicated
by the settling velocity [Kerpen et al., 2020]. As the wave height at deep water and peak
period are constant, the Dean Number is inversely proportional to the settling velocity. Thus,
the combination of Dean’s number and the particle shape is the key conjunction in the
analysis of particle distribution. In ﬁgure 4.3 microplastic transport disposal is presented as
a distribution along the ﬂume at the end of each case when the particle position is stable.
These distributions are arranged according to the associated Dean’s Number, from higher to
lower. The distribution evolution with respect to time for each particle, which is used in the
research of patterns, can be found in the Appendix A from ﬁgures A.1-A.18.
Wave hydrodynamics are very complex in coastal regions and small diﬀerences in
microplastic properties can drastically change its behaviour. In general, near-bed ﬂow is a
synergy of wave-induced oscillatory ﬂow, undertow currents and local wake-vortex systems
at sediment ripples and bars disturb and govern sediment and microplastic transport which
depends on bed roughness, sinking velocity and re-suspension velocity [Kerpen et al., 2020].
Also the scaling eﬀects and the inﬂuence of the ripples make the evaluation more complicated.
Thus, due to the wide range of diﬀerent shapes that is included in the study, it is necessary
to highlight the importance of particle shape in the analysis of patterns and hotspots. From
ﬁgure 4.3 it can be drawn two main conclusions. The ﬁrst one refers to the fact that particles
with high Dean Number (Ω> 1.19) do not go oﬀshore at all. The second outcome is that as
the Dean’s Number is decreased, the amount of particles that reach the beach is also reduced.

Chapter 5
Conclusions
In this report a ﬁrst insight related to the microplastic transport in nearshore
regions is achieved. Experiments in a wave ﬂume have been performed in order to expand
the knowledge in this enigmatic study area. The research demonstrates that it exists a
strong relationship between the microplastic properties and its behaviour in the surf zone.
This statement is supported by observing the principal hot spots for each microplastic and
grouping them for their physical and dynamic properties.
It was found that the accumulation points strongly depend on the settling velocity
of the microplastic, that in the analysis is taken as a non-dimension variable using the Dean
number concept. For low Dean number microplastics there is a tendency to travel oﬀshore
and it do not exist a deposit in the beach zone. Contrariwise, for high Dean number particles
the inclination is to go towards the beach and do not go further oﬀshore from the breaker
bar. However, this is not a ﬁxed and clear trend. For particles with Dean number values
close to 1 the shape and the size become more relevant. In addition, around 1 it looks like
the turning point where a particle have an onshore or an oﬀshore transport.
By having a case with a diﬀerent bed proﬁle, it emphasizes the perception that
the beach conﬁguration is a key element on the fundamental understanding of microplastic
transport fate. The positions of the sand bars, the berm height or even the ﬂatness of the
plateau region inﬂuence the mechanisms of particle motion.
Overall, it is demonstrated that the disposal of particles in the surf zone cannot be
simpliﬁed as the classic natural sediment transport. Microplastics are much more complex
and thus a higher number of potential hot spots are identiﬁed. Additionally, numerous studies
have presented the fact that low Dean number values for natural sediment means an onshore
transport. This statement is not fulﬁlled in the case of microplastics.
The conclusions of this report are of special interest for the subsequent numerical
studies on the dynamic behaviour of microplastic particles in the coastal region. Moreover,
47
==PAGE:57==
this study can contribute to the identiﬁcation of real life hot spots in the coastal environment
and is a key step for the sought management or even removal from the zones in harmful risk.
In the following lines, some limitations which have been encountered during the
project are described. The ﬁrst restraint is that due to the length of the experiments plus the
problems experienced with the wave maker, it has been diﬃcult to ﬁnd time to analyze deeper
the results. Also, the second proﬁle case is not ﬁnished yet at the thesis submission date.
Another limitation is the attainment of new particles. Due to the 3D printer constraints,
some particle properties have not been able to achieve and this reduced a bit the property
range of the microplastics. This is the case for low densities, irregular shapes and very thin
dimensions. Finally, there is a need of standardize the counting and the tracking of the
microplastics. The improvement in this are would allow to play with higher quantities and
therefore widen the knowledge.

Chapter 6
Further Research
As it is mentioned in the introduction of the thesis, approaching microplastic
transport from a coastal engineering point of view is relatively modern and rather-unique.
Thus, there is still a lot of work if we want to manage the microplastic pollution in the marine
environment.
Future experiments are necessary to establish an appropriate base for the under-
standing of the nearshore microplastic transport basics. First of all, up to this date, all
experiments have been carried out in a 2D wave ﬂume where processes like wave refraction,
rip-currents and long-shore currents are not taken into account. So there is a need to address
this problematic from a three-dimensional point of view. Also, as the experiments are carried
out in the laboratory, the so called scaling eﬀects are present. Furthermore, the current
studies have worked with freshwater circumstances. Thus, the water salinity is not taken into
account. If the experiments were carried out in real situation, as the water density would be
higher, the buoyancy forces will also increase and that could change the transport fate.
Due to the tracking system employed in this project, it was not possible to include
particles with size lower than 2 mm. This idea is relevant because small microplastics are
predominant in sea waters. In addition, from the conclusions of the thesis, it appears a
demand to investigate deeper the eﬀect of the shape for particles with Dean number close
to 1. Cube-shaped particles require a further research as they are very sensible to the size
and density. It would be useful to experiment with cubes with very low and very high Dean
number values. A concept to highlight is that the Dean number grouping also coincides with
the shape grouping. So it exists a need to work with spheres with high Dean number values,
and on the contrary, disk-shaped particles with very low Dean number values. Another
concept which claims for a standardization is the microplastic size for irregular particles.
Up to this date, still there is no a global approach to deﬁne the dimensions of microplastic
fragments.
49
==PAGE:59==
Event though in this study the beach proﬁle has been modiﬁed, it still needs a
deeper focus on this condition. For example, having the linear proﬁle without sand. Also, a
relevant approach is the variation of the natural sediment which conforms the bed proﬁle.
Investigate with higher grain sizes in order to aﬀect the shear stress of the microplastic. In
addition, the change of wave conditions such as the wave height and the wave period is more
than necessary to expand the range of situation. In accordance with this matter, another
aspect to take into account is the inﬂuence of extreme periods such as heavy storms. All
experiments have been conducted under normal wave conditions. But it is known that a
extreme period can drastically change the morphological proﬁle of the beach and thus the
related hydrodynamic processes. It would also be meaningful for increase the understanding
of mechanical abrasion in microplastic particles.
