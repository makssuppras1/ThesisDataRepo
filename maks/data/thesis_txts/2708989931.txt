Abstract  
The project aims at evaluating the possibility of integrating biochar in ventilation 
systems of pig houses, to remove pollutants and greenhouse gases.  
Air treatment systems for this purpose are commercially available, mainly for the 
removal of ammonia (NH3) via chemical or biological air scrubbers. These systems are 
also partially effective for the elimination of odour compounds.  
The integration of biochar in animal facilities ventilation systems is an interesting option: 
biochar can be inexpensive and produced on-site using locally available biomass and an 
effective mean to remove unwanted compounds from ventilation air of animal facilities. 
Indeed, biochar can have similar properties as active carbon and therefore have a high 
adsorption performance towards a variety of organic compounds. Therefore, biochar 
can be used as a dry filtering material to adsorb NH3 that escape the scrubbers.  
The work will start with a literature study to define the state of the art of animal facilities 
ventilation systems and identify areas where improvements are required. Afterwards, 
the most promising application of biochar in ventilation systems will be determined and 
suitable experimental setup will be arranged for a preliminary proof-of-concept of the 
proposed solution.  
Three different biochars are being tested in this study, pine, BGF and straw, with silica 
sand as the control and activated charcoal as a reference. Pine, biogas fibre and straw 
showed different profiles of adsorption capacities. The activated charcoal displayed the 
best profile of adsorption capability. The straw was generally the second best of the 
three biochars. While the BGF and Pine swapped position after a certain time from worst 
to best and vice versa.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Biomass and biochar: 
Biomass is an overall term that defines any organic material, including forest and crop 
residues, animal wastes and food wastes. It is in high demand ever since the depletion 
and rising cost of fossil fuels along with the environmental concerns that it raises. More 
specifically the CO2 emission and climate changes that follows. Biomass is a carbon 
neutral source when burnt and is therefore a great candidate to become a possible new 
gold standard of renewable energy sources, but further technologies need to be 
developed to efficiently convert this renewable carbon source to bioproducts that can 
be economically competitive with fossil resources1. Thermochemical conversion 
includes biomass pyrolysis in absence of oxygen which is another word for reinventing 
waste biomass into carbon-rich charcoal. This charcoal is defined as biochar which is a 
stable solid rich in carbon2. Pyrolysis can be done at temperatures between 300-800 °C 
(medium) and between 800-1300°C (high). It’s producing three products which are 
biooil (liquid), biochar (solid) and syngas (gaseous). The proportion of these products 
depends on different parameters including the composition of the feedstock and 
process variables like the rate of the heating. If the pyrolysis is done with quick heating 
(fast pyrolysis), the process is optimized to produce more bio-oil. However, if the heat 
rate is slow (slow pyrolysis) the major product will be the biochar3. The yield of biochar 
from pyrolysis lies between 15-25 wt.%.

exported to other EU countries, and the remainder is exported to outside of EU. More 
generally speaking the export goes to more than 140 countries and the largest markets

1.3 
Environmental effects / GHG emission 
Pig production in agriculture generates large quantities of pig slurry which emits gaseous 
waste into the air. The associated gaseous emissions consist mainly of ammonia (NH3) 
and other greenhouse gases (GHG) such as methane (CH4), carbon dioxide (CO2) and 
nitrous oxide (N2O). These GHGs come from the slurry management which represents a 
major concern due to the intensification of pig production and other livestock’s 
productions as well. Pig production was in 2015 considered to be the second greatest 
contributor of GHGs from the livestock sector6,7.  
The GHG emissions from animal breeding/production in general originates from 4 main 
categories, which are enteric fermentation, manure management, feed production and 
energy consumption. The energy consumption consists of all the machinery and 
electricity to keep the farm running. In this report the manure management is the one 
in focus. The manure contains two chemical components that leads to GHG emission 
during processing and storage, carbon in organic matter leading to CH4 and nitrogen 
leading to ammonia and nitrogen oxide emission which later is transformed into nitrous

1.4 
Current solutions - Literature Review 
Ammonia is a toxic gas that causes respiratory irritation, coughing, searing and lethargy9. 
A few different studies have been conducted to reduce the release of ammonia in 
ventilation system from pig production. The air scrubber method is one of the most 
widely used technologies, where liquid is sprayed through the air to drain and adsorb 
the gas. This method is thought to provide an effective solution to clean the air. 
However, the solution is also thought to have some drawbacks. First, the economy of 
having this high running cost solution, second is the ecological aspect, e.g., it is high 
energy demanding in the form of chemical use and discharge water. Lastly, the technical 
part of an unstable performance of bio-scrubbers11. 
A new system came along on top of the air scrubber method. The new solution was set 
to possibly overcome the drawbacks mentioned above.  It is called a Bio-trickling air 
scrubber, which implies the new application of microorganisms. This concept functions 
as gas treatment at low concentration using media for the microorganism to grow on 
and water spray to create moisture in the media. Therefore, the principle of bio-trickling 
system for the gas treatment from swine houses consists of ventilation system, liquid

able to maintain a controlled pH of >7.5, resulting in a 55-95 % ammonia removal

1.5 
Benefits of Biochar  
Biochar has been of global interest because of its potential carbon sequestration tool 
when incorporated into soil. Additionally biochar influences soil nitrogen transformation 
and has a recognized ability to take up ammonia16. Biochar is produced from different 
crop residues with a lot of possible applications. As mentioned above, carbon 
sequestration, soil amendment, and pollutant removal. Biochar possesses a variety of 
unique properties like high alkaline pH, fixed carbon content, stability against decay, 
cation exchange capacity and nutrient adsorption17. All these features contribute to an 
efficient, climate friendly, sustainable, and cost-effective material18.  
Furthermore, it can be grown locally, close to the farms, reducing and potentially 
removing the desire for purchasing from an external source. Which also reduces the 
need for transportation for the biomass.  
This study is going to showcase a run of ammonia through a bed of biochar in a flow 
reactor and into a gas analyser to see how much ammonia is adsorbed by the biochar. 
Different types of biochar are to be tested and compared. If the biochar can adsorb a 
good amount of ammonia, it would be advantageous to apply this as a natural soil 
fertilizer. It has the potential of becoming a renewable and easy source to collect 
ammonia to keep the soil fertile.

1.6 
Project description and objectives 
The project aims at evaluating the possibility of integrating different types of biochar in 
the ventilation systems of pig houses, to remove/reduce pollutants and greenhouse 
gases, more specifically ammonia. The project starts with theoretical part of finding 
what has been done before and up until now with regards to ammonia adsorption 
performances. 
Then the idea is to test the different types of beds, consisting of a variety of homemade 
biochars, in a fixed flow reactor. The focus in the beginning is going to be on testing how 
much the ammonia (NH3) is going to be reduced through a flow reactor. All the biochars 
will be compared to a control which consists of silica sand and a reference in the form 
of commercial activated charcoal. 
The process that is investigated is to run ammonia through a bed of biochar in a flow 
reactor and into a gas analyser to see how much ammonia was adsorbed by the biochar. 
Different types of biochar are to be tested and compared. 
Alternatively, the use of biochar as a packing material to support the development of a 
methane-oxidizing biofilm could be an attractive solution for reducing greenhouse gases 
emissions from stables and slurry storages, but the testing of this solution is beyond the 
scope of this project. 
The biochars are going to be produced using a pyrolysis oven. They are going to be 
produced from wood and agricultural waste as feedstock/biomass. 
The project is implemented in the following steps: 
• Use literature search to find state of the art and implement it towards a goal. 
• Set up experimental system for testing of biochar beds. 
o Produce different biochars for this purpose. 
• Test the performance of biochars in NH3 adsorption. 
• Use the more in-depth knowledge to contribute to research and development 
work. 
To plan and use methods to conduct qualified tasks within given frameworks and 
evaluate this work in the form of a report and a presentation/defence.

2. Materials and methods 
In this study, an experimental setup is used to assess the adsorption capacity of different 
biochars towards gaseous ammonia. The following materials are used to conduct the 
experiments. The control is silica sand, with particle size within the range of 355-500 
μm. The biomasses that are used to produce the biochars, are straw, pine, and biogas 
fibres. All the biomasses used come in pellet form. The reason for using pellets and not 
the raw material is that they are easier to utilize in industrial-scale processes, as they 
have a higher density, and they maintain their structure. 
Additionally, a commercial activated charcoal (Darco® 20×40 activated carbon (AC) 
produced by Norit (Norit Americas Inc.) was used as a reference material.  
 
2.1 
Pyrolysis 
The biochars used in the adsorption experiments were produced in a pyrolysis oven at 
DTU Risø. Aluminium foil containers were weighed out followed by a weighing of the 
biomasses with aluminium foil containers. These were then put into a reactor which was 
sealed with a lid and bolts, then it was going into the pyrolysis oven. 
The nitrogen inlet was connected to the reactor behind the pyrolysis oven, to ensure no 
oxygen in the reactor.  
The oven was set up to gradually increase with a 10 °C min-1 until it reaches 610 °C, to 
secure a temperature of 600±5°C inside the pyrolysis reactor. When the desired 
temperature was reached it would run for 1 hour. The temperature inside the reactor 
was monitored by LabView on a computer. When an hour had gone by, the oven door 
would be open, with a fan turned out to cool down the oven/reactor. When the reactor 
would reach a temperature which could be handled with oven gloves, it was taken out 
and the bolts unscrewed.  
The containers were then weighed out to calculate how much was left of the biochar 
sample. The biochar mass equals around 30 % of the biomass weight.

2.2 
Biochar preparation 
The collected biochars were grinded with a coffee grinder and sieved to achieve a size 
of 250-500 µm, to match the size of the control. Before the biochars were used they 
were set to be dried in an oven at 105℃ overnight. The samples were collected in jars 
with closed lids and stored in a desiccator.

2.4 
Flow reactor procedure 
The adsorbent sample is transferred into a separation funnel that sits on top of the flow 
reactor. 
Then the gas inlet is connected at the bottom of the reactor and secured by tightening 
the fitting with a wrench. The outlet of the flow reactor is then connected to the gas 
analyser and secured with a wrench. The outlet tube from the gas analyser is secured 
inside the ventilation, in this case with tape. 
When every connection is secured, they all need to be leak-tested with a leak detection 
spray on each connection to see if any bubbles would show indicating that there is a gas 
leak generating bubbles, if nothing happened it would mean that the connection is tight 
and would not leak. 
The gas inlets are opened to let the reactor fill up with a desired concentration of the 
gas, it should be achieved in 15-20 min. Since ammonia sticks everywhere in the reactor 
and inside the tubes, the flow needs to run for a while before atmosphere stable 
concentration is reached. When the gas analyser shows that the concentration is 
stabilised inside the reactor, the computer is set to collect the data and the biochar bed 
is dropped into the reactor by opening the valve on the separation funnel. 
When the gas analyser data logging on the computer indicates that the concentration 
of the gas has reached the starting concentration again, the experiment is done and shut 
off. 
When the experiment is done, the reactor is disassembled, and the bed is emptied. 
Compressed air is used to clean the reactor. 
Following the first control run, it was decided to go for 1 cm of height in the flow reactor 
rather than 3 cm, to limit the pressure drop.

2.8 
Elemental analysis 
An elemental analysis is a method to quantitively determine the elemental composition 
of a given sample. The experiment is carried out on a Euro EA Elemental Analyzer21, and 
the instrument is controlled with the EuroVector EA3000 software. The software also 
collects the data and prints it out in an Excel file at the end of the data collection. 
The samples must be prepared before the analysis begins. 0.5 to 2 mg sample is weighed 
into the provided tin capsules. The tin capsules are then folded into cubes with tweezers. 
The cubes are folded as compact as possible to remove air from the samples, to increase 
the accuracy of the test, by reducing the interference from external factors. 
The calibration standard is Alfalfa OAS. Six samples are analysed for each compound to 
ensure accurate values. The instrument heats up to 990 °C before the analysis starts.  
The way to prepare the elemental analysis is to prepare five blank samples, to ensure 
there is no background signals in the instrument. Furthermore, a blank is analysed 
between different samples to ensure that there are no remnants of the previous 
samples that could interfere.  
The order of the analysis goes like the following, 5 blanks, 6 standards, followed by the 
repetition of 3 samples and one blank between the samples. If the analyser is to be used 
for multiple samples when there is no more space in the disc. Five blanks are loaded into 
the machine before the samples are reloaded and the resume button on the software is 
pressed. The analysis is done to provide an overview of the carbon content, to see if the 
carbon content will potentially drop a bit when an unused sample is compared to a 
sample that has been through the fixed flow reactor.

4. Discussion 
This all leads to determine which of the biochars were performing better than the other. 
However, a few criteria’s need to be established to give a fair review and possibly 
consider every aspect of their individual performances. According to the adsorption 
experiment done at 100 and 300 ppm NH3 the commercially available activated charcoal 
was generally the best one. The silica sand as the control displayed a very poor 
performance at any kind of adsorption capacity towards the ammonia. 
The criteria will be: 
• Which of the biochar were better in the short run?  
• Which of the biochar were better in the full run?  
• Which of the biochar had a better adsorption overall per gram sample? 
o Does the surface area have relation to the performance? 
• Which one is more feasible with respect to how much is available? 
 
Pine was very good to stop the ammonia for a long time after the initial drop of the 
sample. The pine showed a small resemblance to the activated charcoal since these two 
were the only two samples that could completely adsorb the ammonia in the gas flow, 
before a slow breakthrough. The pine showed to adsorb up to 60 % for 200 seconds in 
the 300-ppm ammonia. However, for the 100-ppm ammonia, it managed to adsorb 
more than 90 % of the initial ammonia concentration for around 170 seconds. To 
compare to the other two biochars at the 200 second mark. The BGF manged to prevent 
75 % of the initial concentration to come through within the first 20 seconds, but within 
the next 80 seconds it dropped to only adsorb 50 % for the 100-ppm. For 300-ppm the 
BGF prevented 80 % of the initial concentration at 40 seconds and was at 50 % 60 
seconds later. The BGF was the worst of the biochar for the first 500 seconds. Afterwards 
it was the most stable and steady going curve. It took around 8000 seconds before the 
ammonia adsorption was back to the original concentration. This can be seen in 
appendix C. The last one is the straw, it dropped down to 90 % adsorption capacity for 
both the 100- and 300-ppm concentration for the first 20 seconds of the run. The

adsorption capacity fell to 50 % at 180 second mark, but after that the decreasing 
adsorption capacity had a similar pattern as the BGF and activated charcoal towards the 
end. So, for the first 500 seconds, pine was the best of the 3 biochars at adsorbing the 
ammonia, straw was performing in the middle of the three biochars. The worst one was 
therefore the BGF for the short run.  
After the 500 seconds mark the BGF and pine swapped their position. The pine showed 
the fastest decrease back to the initial concentration, while BGF showed the longest by 
far. The BGF took almost double the time than the activated charcoal to reach the initial 
concentration at 0 % adsorption capacity.  
It is quite remarkable that the adsorption capacities differ for the same samples at

pine, 1.59 [mg NH3 g-1] for BGF and 1.52 [mg NH3 g-1] for straw. The pine had similar 
values. The BGF were almost 1 mg off, and straw differed by 0.5 mg between the two 
concentrations.

5. Conclusion 
The aim of this project was to test the if it would be possible to introduce biochar beds 
in a ventilation system in pig stables to reduce the emission of ammonia. Different 
biochars were tested and their ability of adsorption capacity were monitored and 
compared to a control, which consisted of silica sand, and to a reference, which was 
commercially activated charcoal. The ventilation system in this project was a fixed flow 
reactor, connected to a mass flow controller which controlled the flow into the reactor. 
The flow passed through the bed in the reactor and onto a gas analyser to quantify the 
ammonia concentration going out of the system. 
The biochars showed different adsorption profiles, the pine was very good at the 
beginning of the experiment compared to the straw and the BGF. When the 500 second 
mark had hit, the pine turned out to be worst of the three. It showed a similar adsorption 
profile to the activated charcoal, but not nearly as good. The BGF was the worst one of 
the three until the 500 second mark, where it turned out to have a very steady and slow 
way back to their initial concentration. It showed the best potential for the long run. It 
took twice as long as the straw to come back to the initial concentration, and almost 3-
4 times the time that it took the pine. The straw showed the best drop of the three, 
preventing up to 90-95 % of the ammonia to pass through. However, the breakthrough 
came fast as it did with the BGF, and it stayed between the pine and BGF until the 500 
mark and until the end. Generally, the straw was the second best of the three 
throughout the whole experiment. 
However, as much as the promising profiles of adsorption capacities, the amount of 
biochar needed, and not to mention biomass to make the biochar, is ridiculously high. 
Further research is very much requested to reduce the needed amount. The activated 
charcoal showed the better results in the adsorption capacity and the porosity analysis. 
The amount of biochar needed for the reference biochar was 2,570 tonnes per year per 
farm, which was by far the lowest and better one. The BGF was the second to last with 
4,880 tonnes, straw was 5,400 tonnes and pine was almost 7,000 tonnes. Therefore, it 
does not seem to be a feasible solution yet, it needs more research.

6. Future Work: 
For future work, it would possibly decrease the demand of biochar by steam activation, 
to increase the porosity of each biochar. It could also be interesting to mix the biochars 
and see how the profiles would be different mixing ratios of two biochars, maybe even 
three. If the biochar gets feasibly enough to install in ventilation systems, the biochar 
could be collected after being used and then be utilised for soil amendment. It holds a 
free nitrogen source in the form of the adsorbed ammonia, which serves as nutrients 
for plants and for more fertile soil. The conversion from biomass to biochar makes can 
stabilize the captured carbon by plants, which makes it highly resistant to biological 
decomposition. Consequently, the biochar conversion also has a great potential to 
mitigate greenhouse gas emission and to store carbon in the soil. A sustainable biochar 
system is going to reduce the emission of greenhouse gas with about 12 % of the current 
CO2-C equivalent25. Another thing that would be interesting to do is to change the 
pyrolysis conditions, the sorption capabilities of the biochar would be altered25.  
The setup could also be used to test for methane and sulphur compounds, to better 
determine which of the biochars would be the better contender to mitigate the 
emission. The global warning potential for 1 kg of methane is CO2 equivalent to 84 kg of 
CO226.

How to operate BET NovaTouch (EN290) 12-10-2021  
grav@kt.dtu.dk  
 
 
 
Operation:  
• Weigh empty glass cell. Note down the cell weight and the cell number.  
 
• Add sample to the cell using the glass funnel. The amount of sample should 
be enough to have about 10-50 m2 of surface to measure. Make sure that the 
neck of the cell is clean from powder.  
 
• Insert filter at the top of the glass cell, to prevent fine particles  
elutriation.  
 
• Connect cell to a degassing port on the left side of the instrument. Up to 4 
cells can be connected simultaneously (2 for each station).  
 
• Fittings should be hand-tightened. Insert cells into the heating mantel.  
 
Degassing is started from the touchscreen. Press to see degassing stations. 
Insert operator name, sample IDs and choose the degassing program (e.g.  
==PAGE:59==

2/3  
4h, 350˚C). Make sure that you sample is stable at the selected degassing temperature. 
If necessary, select a lower T program (e.g., 6h, 150°C) 30 torr vacuum is usually ok for 
most samples. In case of fine powders 5 torr is advisable. If the samples ID have a red 
background, degassing won´t start! If they do, just cancel and re-type the sample ID.  
Remember to deselect stations that are not in use and to close with a metal plug the 
ports of an active station that are not in use.  
• Start degassing. Progress can be monitored by pressing  
• Cell can be removed after degassing when the cell temperature is below 80˚C  
• Weigh cell again and calculate sample weight after degassing  
• Add glass stick filler in the cell (carefully, not dropping it so the cell does not break)  
• Connect the cell to a measuring station by inserting it through the o-ring and tighten 
the fitting by hand. Write down which station is used for each sample. You can connect 
to two samples at a time (station 1 and station 2)  
• Fill the dewar with liquid nitrogen (available in the laboratory) using face shield and 
gloves. It must be full for a full isotherm measurement, half full for short BET program.  
• Measurement can be started from the touchscreen, but the software is more 
convenient. To do this, make sure software is connected to the instrument. Then click 
on “Analysis” and on the small gearwheel icon, then “Analysis parameters”  
 
• Click Load/Save and select the desired Preset (e.g., 6-points BET). Write sample Results 
file names in the admin tab for the samples connected to the stations in use. Always 
keep the first default part (St$(ST)_$(DT)_$(TM)_)  
 
• Deselect measurement stations if not in use. Click Start. A window will open where to 
insert the sample(s) weight at the sample(s) ID

How to read results  
For BET specific surface area  
Right click on open isotherm. Edit data tags. Micropore BET assistant. Ok. Ok.  
Right click again on open isotherm. Tables. BET. Multipoint BET.  
NB: Correlation coefficient should be around 0.999  
For porosity (only on full isotherm)  
Right click on open isotherm. Data Reduction parameters. Quick jump to→ DFT  
Choose model (e.g. QSDFT adsorption branch slit/cyl pores)  
Set min and max P/P0 (e.g. 0 and 1) + histogram bin width (e.g. 0.05nm). Ok.  
Right click again on open isotherm. Tables. DFT. Histogram (linear)  
NB: Fitting error close around 1%. Goodness of fit can be seen in: Graphs. DFT. Fitting 
comparison.  
How to change degassing programs  
On the software, go to NOVA → heater parameters  
Edit parameters then save program with new name not to modify the existing one.  
Click on “Send” to make the new program appear on the touchscreen
