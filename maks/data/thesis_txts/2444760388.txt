Abstract
Two-photon excitation fluorescence microscopy is a visualization tool that provides high-
resolution, depth-resolved imaging and that can asses morphological differences in bio-
logical tissue. A challenge is to deliver the pulses used for this imaging technique to
the hollow cavities of the body, du to influence from chromatic dispersion and nonlinear
effects. This project proposes an all-fiber based system to manage the dispersion. The
system was first proposed by Jespersen et al.[1] and is further characterized in a carefully
thought-out setup. A pulse duration of 46.28 fs is achieved after propagating 1.26 m in
a fiber. Furthermore the pulse is used in a microscope to image microspheres.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Two-photon excitation fluorescence microscopy (TPEFM) is a visualization tool that
provides high-resolution as well as depth-resolved imaging [2]. The technique can assess
morphological differences in biological tissues which makes it especially interesting for
cancer detection [3]. A key to such diagnostics is access to body cavities by non-invasive
means [4], which can be achieved by using an optical fiber in combination with an
endoscope [5].
Bladder cancer is one cancer type that would take advantage of the benefits offered
from TPEFM. The current method of detecting bladder cancer is white light cystoscopy,
where a flexible tube with a camera is guided to the bladder through the urethra. It
allows the physician to visually asses if any tumors are present, but the method struggles
to detect flat lesions, dysplasia, multifocal growth and microscopic papillary tumors [6].
Another issue with bladder cancer is its high recurrence rate. Approximately 60% - 70%
of patients experience recurrence [3] and up to 50 % of these cases were caused by pre-
exciting tumor that was missed during the first examination [6]. The specificity TPEFM
offers is beneficial for detecting tumors early, which is vital for effective treatment and
for reducing the mortality rate [3], as well as diagnosing the hidden tumor types at a
earlier stage. The imaging method can also help to establish the surgical margin, so the
entire tumor can be resected during the first operation [6].
TPEFM requires ultra-short pulses in the femtosecond regime to image effectively,
but it is difficult to propagate these pulses for even a short distance of fiber without
altering their properties. This is because femtosecond pulses exhibit broad bandwidths
and high peak intensities, which give rise to dispersion and nonlinear effects that has to
be compensated through a careful thought-out and specific designed experimental setup.
The challenge when implementing TPEFM through an endoscope is to minimize dis-
persion and nonlinear effects. Previously work suggests different types of pre-compensating
schemes to handle second-order dispersion.
An often found suggestion is the use of
GRISM systems [7][8]. A GRISM combines optical prisms and gratings to achieve con-
trol over second- and third-order dispersion and in some extent nonlinear self-phase
modulation. The drawback is the need for bulky optics that is sensitive to accurate
alignment [4]. Using a hollow core photonic band-gap fiber is a proposed solution that
removes the need for pre-compensating, since it can be operated at the zero-dispersion
wavelength. Furthermore its absence of nonlinearities makes it able to guide high en-
ergy pulses [9][10][11]. This type of fiber does struggle with third-order dispersion, since
wavelengths outside the zero-dispersion wavelength will experience significant broaden-

ing [1]. Another intriguing proposal is utilizing conventional single-mode fibers operated
at 1.58 µm and frequency doubling the light at the delivering end of the fiber [12]. Here
a concern is the frequency doublings sensitivity to the polarization of the propagating
light, which might change as the fiber is handled. Nonlinear effect are generally dealt
with by reducing the average power [13] or by increasing the core size, and thus the
effective area of the fiber [14]. Low power decreases the signal-to-noise ratio and a cer-

problematic, leading to high bending losses. This project proposes an all-fiber scheme
that can handle both second- and third-order dispersion and where nonlinear effect are
negligible.
The objective of the project is to experimentally characterize an all-fiber based de-
livery scheme for femtosecond pulses to be used for TPEFM. A module, consisting of a
standard single-mode fiber combined with an anomalous dispersive higher-order mode
fiber, is proposed to counteract second-order dispersion and to minimize third-order dis-
persion.
Chapter 2 will describe the specifics of the technology involved and cover the required
knowledge of ultra-short pulses, dispersion as well as nonlinear effects. The experimental
design and characterization of the optical components involved can be found in Chapter
3. Chapter 4 presents a thorough investigation of the pulse before and after the fiber
module. Aspects involving the pulse’ intensity distribution, polarization, spectrum and
duration are examined. Imaging with the pulse after characterization will be attempted
in Chapter 5. The results presented in Chapter 4 and 5 are further analyzed in Chapter
6, which also includes an assessment of the tradeoffs encountered throughout the work
and discusses the future development of the proposed scheme.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical background
and design considerations
This chapter presents carefully selected theory that is deemed necessary knowledge in
order achieve remote two-photon excitation fluorescence microscopy (TPEFM). Many
of TPEFM’s advantages were presented in the introduction and these will be elaborated
further on. Ultra-short pulses are the common approach to achieve two photon excitation
[15], and are also the technique used for excitation in this project. Therefore an overview
of these pulses and their properties are presented, as well as some of the accompanying
issues namely dispersion and nonlinear effects. A review of three specifically chosen
dispersion compensating methods is also included, with an enhanced focus on fiber
optics, since this is the compression technique proposed to guide ultra-short pulses into
body cavities.
2.1
Properties of two-photon excitation
fluorescence microscopy
Fluorescence is a process where an atom or molecule, known as the fluorophore, absorbs
a photon and reemits it again through spontaneous emission. The first step occurs when
light, often from the ultraviolet or blue spectrum, with a wavelength matching the en-
ergy gap of a fluorophore excites it up to a higher energy state. Here energy is lost
through the vibrational states before decaying down to the ground state again. The
loss of energy will cause the emitted photon to have a longer wavelength, often in the
visible spectrum. The wavelength difference between the maximum of the absorption
and emission spectra is called the Stokes shift. The excitation and emission processes
are displayed in the Jablonski diagram in Figure 2.1 [16].
Fluorescence is a common used technique in imaging because of its ability to target
specific fluorophores [17]. Some molecules will auto fluorescence with the correct exci-
tation light, and specific dyes are also used to label features of interest. Fluorescence is
even capable of detecting multiple fluorophores simultaneously, that differ in absorption
and emission properties. The process occurs in the scale of nanoseconds, and is thus
relevant for real-time imaging in vivo [18][6].

Two-photon excitation is similar to single-photon excitation, but instead two photons
are absorbed quasi-simultaneously by using a virtual state. The two photons’ combined

Usually red or infrared light is

It is, in contrary to single-photon excitation, a nonlinear process, so the
excitation spectra would not be completely identical. The emissions spectra are however
exactly alike, since the process of spontaneous emission does not change [16].
Two-photon excitation requires a high photon density; since the process is depended on
the probability of two photons simultaneously exciting the fluorophore. The probability
for the process to occur is quadratic dependent on the excitation light intensity [15].
TPEFM provide multiple benefits in comparison to using single-photon excitation
confocal microscope, especially when used on biological samples [15].
One of the most prominent features of TPEFM is the ability for it to selectively dis-
criminate structures in depth. This high photon density that is needed will only occur
in the focus of the beam, thus eliminating fluorescence signal in the cones outside the
focus, as displayed in Figure 2.2. The two-photon interaction can be contained in a
volume of approximately 0.1 fL (approximately 1 µm3) [16][19]. By scanning the focus
through a sample, a three-dimensional image can be created. In single-photon confocal
microscopy fluorescence occurs from the entire illumination area, so a pinhole is inserted
to reject out of focus photons. By eliminating the need for a pinhole, photons emitted

filter [20]. For single-photon microscopy a filter is used to block back-scattered excita-
tion photons, since there for some systems can be a partial overlap of the emission and
excitation spectra. The filter introduces yet another loss for the traditional fluorescence
microscope [17].

2.2
Ultra-short pulses
Ultra-short pulses are usually defined as pulses with a duration in the femtosecond
range and shorter, but sometimes also includes picosecond pulses. The standard laser
for producing ultra-short pulses for two-photon fluorescence is the Ti:Sapphire laser [18],
which has been shown to create pulses all the way down to 5 fs [22]. The shortest pulses
yet created are X-ray bursts with durations as short as 43 attoseconds [23].
The attributes ultra-short pulses posses are interesting in a variety of areas and are
especially relevant when looking at light/matter interactions. This is because of their
ability to reach high energy concentrations and peak powers giving rise to nonlinear
effects [24].
Nonlinear effects include the two-photon absorption just covered in the
previous section, but also effects like frequency mixing, second harmonic generation
(SHG) and anti-stokes Raman scattering. In biomedical optics, nonlinear processes are
used as a very selective scanning tool, that can discriminate cells from the outlying tissue
[15].

One way to create ultra-short laser pulses is by using a laser able to mode lock,
where it is easy to reach pulse durations under 100 fs [25]. The length of the laser cavity
determines what wavelengths are supported as standing waves, known as the cavity’s
longitudinal modes. Constructive interference occurs when coupling together the phases
of multiple longitudinal cavity modes, creating one circulating ultra-short pulse in the
cavity, as seen in Figure 2.3.
Having a cavity that supports many longitudinal modes, and thus many different frequen-
cies, is not enough for mode locking. The gain medium must have a broad bandwidth, so
that all the frequencies are amplified simultaneously [26]. Ti3+ ions exhibit a very broad

2.3
Chromatic dispersion
Chromatic dispersion is a major issue when utilizing pulses with very broad bandwidths
[15].
Chromatic dispersion occurs because the refractive index is dependent on the
frequency of light. This dependency causes different frequencies of light to experience
different velocities. Other types of dispersion include intermodal and polarization mode
dispersion, but they will only briefly be investigated here, since their effect are judged
minimal in comparison that of chromatic dispersion.
The wavelength specific refractive index can be calculated through Sellmeier’s equa-
tion [25], but for pulses containing many wavelengths it quickly becomes too complicated.
A travelling wave can be described by the propagation parameter γ that characterizes
how the amplitude and phase evolves as the light propagates:
A(z) = A(0)exp[γz]
(2.3)

part, β is the phase constant. β represents the change in phase per unit length and it
is the term responsible for dispersion. β is normally approximated by using the central
wavelength when dealing with longer pulses that have lower bandwidths. Complications
occur when dealing with ultra-short pulses, or short pulses that experience high influence
of dispersive media, such as when propagating through long fibers, or have many passes
in a laser cavity. In those cases, β is approximated with a Taylor series where the number
of terms used can be selected to obtain the desired precision. The Taylor series of the

2.3.1
Components for dispersion compensating
Dispersion clearly becomes an issue when dealing with ultra-short pulses and their broad
bandwidths. Almost all optical components, like mirrors and isolators, introduce some
sort of dispersion and by using a fiber to transport pulses into a body cavity even more
dispersion is introduced. Fortunately it is possible to compensate for the chromatic dis-
persion.
Dispersion compensating is about balancing normal dispersion with anomalous disper-
sion. The total amount of GDD is calculated by adding the individual contributions of
GDD from each component. To achieve no second-order dispersion the total GDD sum
must be equal to zero. This can be accomplished in a number of ways and three options
will be discussed in the following: chirped mirrors, the prism compressor and the use of
optical fibers.
2.3.1.1
Chirped mirrors
Dispersive mirrors, also called chirped mirrors are one way to introduce negative GDD
in the system. It functions by altering the different dielectric layers’ thickness of a Bragg
mirror, such that the distinctive wavelength component penetrate to different depth as
seen in Figure 2.5. The difference in penetration depth causes different travel lengths,
and thus the different frequencies experience different time delays. A chirped mirror can
be defined through the amount of GDD it introduces. Multiple mirrors can be combined,
such that the light bounces between them increasing the amount of introduced GDD per
bounce [31]. The number of bounces can then be adjusted to meet the system’s needs.
Chirped mirrors offer a high amount of customization [32], but when implemented in
a system they only provide discreet steps of GDD, thus removing the potential of fine-
tuning.

red light has the longest travel length, thus the largest time delay.

2.3.1.2
Angular compensation
A prism compressor utilizes angular dispersion to dispersion compensate and induce pos-

spatially separated through a prism, or a grating [25]. This way the different frequencies
travel in spatially different paths and can be manipulated individually causing different
time delays.
A prism compressor, as seen in Figure 2.6, consists of two prisms. The pulse enters
the first prism, which refracts the different wavelengths into different angles. When the
different frequencies hit the second prism they each experience different path lengths
through it. Longer wavelengths that are the fastest in normal dispersion now have to
travel further in a high refractive index compared to the shorter wavelengths. A mirror
folds the beam back, compressing the beam even more and collects the frequencies
spatially again [33].

2.3.1.4
Third-order compensating
So far the dispersion compensating methods have focuses on compensating for the second-
order chromatic dispersion associated with the phase constant. Compensating for GVD
does not take into account that a ultra-short pulse have different frequency components,
each with their own specific value of Dλ. To compensate the dispersion slopes, S, for the
normal and anomalous dispersion must also cancel each other out. For a fiber system
this could also be expressed as:
S1L1 + S2L2 = 0.
(2.11)
where L1 and L1 are the fiber lengths with the dispersion slopes, S1 and S1, respectively.
Numerical identical slopes may be possible to achieve by varying the design parameters
of the fiber. There also exists fibers that are dispersion flattened, thus having a very
small dispersion slope, reducing the value of β3. Prism compressors and gratings are
not ideal to use for TOD managing, since they lack free variables. Chirped mirrors on
the other hand can be designed very precisely to compensate for higher order dispersion
[32]. Typically numerical calculations are done on the system of interest to predict the
amount of TOD compensation needed.

2.4
Nonlinear effects in optical fibers
So far only the linear effects have been considered, but nonlinear effects must also be
taken into account since they posses the ability to distort the propagating pulse. Of in-
terest is especially the optical Kerr effect, which is the result of the refractive index being
modified by the propagating electric field of the pulse [36]. This refractive dependency
is described by:
n = n0(ω) + n2I(ω)
(2.12)
The refractive index, n0, is the standard refractive index used when not considering
nonlinear effects and a nonlinear addition consisting of the nonlinear refractive index, n2
and the pulse intensity, I, is added. The nonlinear index is dependent on the polarization
of the electric field which is normally assumed linear [36].
Nonlinear effects will become quite severe for high intensities, as the latter term will
dominate. Ultra-short pulses provide high intensity, especially when focused down to the
small effective areas of single-mode fibers. A consequence of the Kerr effect is self-phase
modulation (SPM), which results in a nonlinear phase shift in the time domain. This
effect will cause a positive chirp or up-chirp to the pulse, as also seen with the positive
GVD [24]. This positive chirp will cause GVD to influence the pulse even more. If SPM
occurs in a region of anomalous dispersion, the two effects can cancel each other out
creating a so-called soliton, which is a pulse propagating undistorted [29].
Four-wave mixing (FWM) is another nonlinear effect that is a consequence of the
Kerr effect. It is a process where two or three different wavelengths interact and generate
one or two new wavelengths. This effect requires phase matching to be efficient.
Stimulated Raman scattering (SRS) is the last nonlinear effect introduced here that
can disrupt the pulse and it is a result of light interacting with the vibrational modes
of the silica molecules. It occurs when different wavelengths propagate together. The
shorter wavelength will be attenuated as it acts as a pump for the longer wavelength.
As a consequence the frequency distribution of the spectrum will change.
There is also the possibility that other nonlinear effects will influence the pulse, but
they will not be covered here, since they are negligible compared to SPM, FWM and
SRS.

2.6
Summary
In this chapter the advantages of two-photon excitation fluorescence microscopy (TPEFM)
have been discussed. The depth-discrimination combined with a deep penetration depth
makes it possible for TPEFM to image the placement of cancer tumors. The depth dis-
crimination also confines photo damage and bleaching. Furthermore the used wavelength
for TPEFM is beneficial in comparison to single-photon excitation, since tissue has low
absorption in the near infrared region and it is easier to distinguish between excitation
and emission wavelength due to a larger stokes shift.
The ultra-short pulses used for TPEFM have high peak intensities and broad spec-
tra. Consequently chromatic dispersion and nonlinear effects become an issue. Three
examples to compensate for chromatic dispersion have been discussed, that will later on
be used. The fiber is preferred in this setup since it makes it possible to remove the need
for bulky optics. On the other hand the proposed fiber scheme is not yet tested and a
prism compressor is ideal for fine-tuning the system, while dispersive mirrors are great
for compensating elements that already are thoroughly characterized.
Third order dispersion will not be dealt with specifically through this work, and the
only way of reducing the influence is by shortening the light’s propagation length in the
fiber. The fiber length will be reduced so a pulse duration under 100 fs is theoretical
expected. Nonlinear effects are also not compensated for directly, but by lowering the
input power in the fiber the effects should be negligible in comparison to dispersion. If
the powers are too high a further broadening from self-phase modulation is expected, and
possibly a redistribution of wavelengths from Raman scattering and four wave mixing
will occur as well. The power limit will be examined further in the next chapter.

light from subsequent components to enter the cavity. When extrapolating on the iso-

considering a slightly shifted center wavelength a transmission of 25% is expected.
3.1.2
Prism compressor
The isolator introduces quite a lot of group delay dispersion and is responsible for broad-
ening the pulse duration. For the used Thorlabs isolator, a pulse broadening from 120
fs to 186 fs is expected [39]. For an even shorter pulse, as generated by the Ti:Sapphire,
the broadening becomes quite significant, as seen in Equation 2.7. As such a prism
compressor is introduced in the experimental setup to achieve a transform-limited pulse
that can be used for reference.
As explained in Section 2.3.1.2 the distance between the prisms have a deciding factor
on the amount of introduced negative dispersion. Since the induced GDD of the isolator
is not known, the prisms are placed at a random distance and the pulse is shortened
using the translation stage of the second prism. If the pulse clips, and the trace on the
autocorrelator becomes distorted the prisms are either moved closer together or further
apart dependent on what direction the prism was translated.
For the experimental
system the measured distance between the prims where 35.5 cm and the material of the
prisms is the SF10-800. The refractive index, n, and its derivatives used for the prisms
SF10-800 nm are given by Newport Application note, also found in Appendix A [33].
Thus the compensated GDD is calculated using Equation 2.8 to be -4236.53 fs2. A prism
compressor is chosen due to its low loss and excellent fine-tuning capabilities.
An extra half-wave plate can be inserted before the isolator to control the laser power
through the system, which is relevant when measuring nonlinear effects through the fiber
module.
3.1.3
Fiber module specifications
After the prism compressor the light is guided either through the fiber or directly to
the autocorrelator. The fiber module consists of a single-mode fiber and a higher order
mode fiber combined through a long period grating (LPG).
For practical applications a fiber is used to guide the pulse to the location of measure-
ment, but because of second- and third-order dispersion as described in Section 2.3 a
dispersion-compensating scheme is needed. The pulse is coupled into the SMF, a Clear-
Lite 780-11 (CL-780), in the fundamental mode, before being converted to a higher order
mode, namely the LP02 mode, using an LPG. The LP02 pulse then propagates in the
HOM fiber experiencing anomalous dispersion resulting in dispersion compensating.
As mentioned, the Ti:Sapphire lasers beam profile is approximately Gaussian as
displayed in Figure 3.2. The beam is focused down to match the fiber parameters of the
ClearLite 780-11 fiber so a coupling can be achieved. The fibers operation wavelength

After the taper a LPG converts the fundamental mode to the LP02 mode where the ad-
vantageous dispersion properties are found, that can balance out the normal dispersion
of the SMF as described in Section 2.3. A LPG works by introducing periodical pertur-
bation in the core of the fiber. The fundamental mode is through these perturbations
coupled to the cladding modes. Here they would normally be lost through scattering
and absorption, but since the HOM supports these higher order modes the LP01 mode is
simply converted to the LP02 mode. The conversion is fiber based, hence the efficiency
is quite high, about 97 % [35].
The refractive index profile of the HOM corresponds to the one previously shown in
Figure 2.7 [35]. It supports four modes, namely the LP01, LP02, LP11 and LP21. All
these modes are the ones shown in Figure 2.8. The LPG only converts the fundamental
mode to the LP02 mode, because of a similar group velocity between the LP01 and LP02
[35].

large Dλ parameter was preferred so only small lengths of the HOM would be needed.
2) A dispersion slope matching that of standard single-mode fiber. 3) The bend loss
was minimized for the LP02 mode. 4) A fiber supporting few modes, with a small V-
parameter, and with large gaps between the modes effective indices was aimed for, to

40 fs, a duration of 44 is expected for a fiber system with no GDD. The broadening the
pulse experiences is caused by third-order dispersion. Dispersion effects are more severe
for shorter pulses, where a 20 fs pulse would be broadened to 72.5 fs.

be evaluated to see which of the effects are more dominant, as described in Section 2.5.

the power should be carefully selected in regards to these limits.

amount of fiber length as traditional paddles does. The polarizer causes stress-induced
polarization by using a screw to induce pressure on the fiber. The screw can be rotated
such that the pressure can be applied from any direction onto the fiber.

3.1.4
Autocorrelator
The pulse is sent to an autocorrelator for characterization of the pulse duration. The
autocorrelator is operated in background free intensity autocorrelation mode [43]. In
this operation mode the incoming beam is divided using a beam splitter and the two
identical pulses are each sent down their own path. The beams first combine in the
second harmonic crystal (SHG) to interfere. By varying one beam path length using the
piezo-driven stage the amount of spatially overlap will vary and thus vary the amount
of nonlinear response. A filter right before the detector is used to filter out non-second
harmonic light. The detected signal is known as the trace. The pulse duration can be
calculated using the induced pulse delay and intensity measured by a detector. The
entire autocorrelator setup is displayed in Figure 3.6 [44].

The data received from the autocorrelator is calibrated with regards to the time
delay, since the output is measured in scanning time. When the piezo stage drives the
mirror back and forth small fringes will arise on the trace. The fringes are averaged over
an appropriate amount of scanning time. One fringe corresponds to a time delay of one
optical cycle (λ/c). A linear fit is also done for the piezo trace to acquire the scanning
rate. The time delay divided with the voltage change pr. fringe can then be calculated.
Multiplying this number with the measured FWHM of the pulse in terms of the voltage
change will result in a pulse duration. The calculation script is found in Appendix E.
The pulse duration is additionally multiplied with the autocorrelator conversion factor.
The conversion factor varies dependent on the estimated pulse shape, and is 0.6482 for
sech2 and 0.441 for Gaussian pulses [26].
The used autocorrelator can measure pulses from 5 fs up to 300 fs using the piezo-
scanner [43]. It is also possible to manually move the fixed mirror with the piezo mirror
locked in place. The mirror can be moved further than the piezo mirror and can thus
measure pulse duration up to 40 ps, corresponding to a translation of 6 µm. Manually
measuring the trace will cause a severe decrease in accuracy. Furthermore the autocor-
relator prefers a input power between 5 and 30 mW [43] and the polarization state of
the pulse is important, since the signal trace is generated utilizing a nonlinear process,
namely SHG [36].

3.2
Fluorescence microscopy
Instead of guiding the light to the autocorrelator, it can also be guided into the TPEFM
as indicated on Figure 3.1. The microscope itself is displayed schematically in Figure 3.7.
It includes a set of dispersive mirrors, called MOSAIC Pro, and a pinhole to compress
and clean the beam before entering the microscope.
MOSAIC Pro is a constellation of two chirped mirrors, which introduces negative
GDD. The number of bounces the light undergoes is adjusted to account for the added
component dispersion through the microscope. A pinhole is inserted after the compensa-
tion and is used as a spatial filter, cleaning the beam profile. Afterwards a lens collimates
the beam and it is guided to the microscope. If the pulse intensity needs to be regulated
an attenuator is also inserted.
Two mirrors are responsible for the XY raster scanning of the sample. A set of achro-
matic lenses is used to expand the beam, to completely fill the microscope’s objective.
By filling the objective a higher utilization of the numerical aperture (NA) is utilized
and a higher resolutions is achieved. The objective used in the microscope has a NA 1.0
of and a magnification of x20. Two-photon fluorescence is generated in the sample and
the same objective collects the emission signal. Even though two-photon fluorescence
is a nonlinear process it does not require a specific polarization of the light. This is
due to the randomness of the sample, in comparison to the fixed lattice of a nonlinear
crystal. The signal is sent back through the dichromatic mirror to a fiber. The fiber can
be coupled to a spectrometer or a photomultiplier (PMT). The spectrometer provides
the spectral information, which is relevant to determine what fluorophore has been de-
tected, but lacks sensitivity for weak signals. The PMT on the other hand has a very
high-sensitivity and is able to measure single photons, but is not able to differentiate
between their wavelengths. A short pass and long pass filter is also added to respectively
filter for excitation light and emission light.

duration can be measured. The pulse duration, which was important because of the

accurately measure pulse duration below 300 fs and can also be operated manual to
measure pulses up to 40 ps. In addition it is easy to switch between the pulse directly
from the laser or the pulse after the fiber for comparison.
The proposed fiber module was also described. It consists of a single mode fiber,
a long period grating and a higher order mode fiber with a total length of 123.6 cm.
Dispersion compensating occurs when the light propagates in the LP02 mode in the

Experiments with
compressed pulse imaging
The compressed pulses is guided to the microscope after being thoroughly characterized.
Here imaging with two-photon fluorescence is attempted, despite the relative low power
of the LP02 mode. This chapter discuss the focusing properties of the LP02 mode and
how it couples into the microscope. Afterwards experiments on imaging of fluorescent
microspheres are carried out, including measurements on intensity versus the input power
to establish the prescence of a nonlinear process.
5.1
LP02 coupling to the microscope
The beam utilized for two-photon excitation in the experimental setup’s microscope is
guided directly from a femtosecond laser. As such the beam path is optimized for a
standard Gaussian pulse. The only adjustment done to support the LP02 mode from the
fiber is by realigning the pinhole.
Even though the beam is collimated, it experiences divergence after the fiber. The
Rayleigh length of the beam is too short relative to the propagation length before the
MOSAIC Pro.
This causes a relative wide beam profile, that is not fully reflected
between the dispersive mirrors in the MOSAIC Pro, giving rise to a meassured loss of
approximately 70%. The Rayleigh length and thus the beam divergence can be adjusted
by choosing a different collimating lens after the fiber [25].
Achromatic lenses are used to expand the beam to completely fill the objective as
mentioned in Section 3.2. The magnification factor caused by the expansion is depen-
dent on the incoming pulse width [46]. The ideal expansion of the LP02 has not been
considered and should later on be characterized.
Previous work has shown that the focal distributions of the LP02 mode depends on the
filling ratio. The focusing properties are similar to those of the LP01 mode when over-
filling the objective [47], which makes sense since only the center peak, that is similar
to a Gaussian, will be present. Another article also suggest that the optical realiution is
enhanced when using LP02 instead of LP01, due to the center peak being more narrow
than that of a Gaussian pulse [48].

CHAPTER 6
Discussion and
improvement proposals
The delivery of ultra short pulses in the femtosecond regime has been demonstrated by
utilizing a fiber-based scheme. Additionally, two-photon fluorescence generation using
the guided pulses has been confirmed, despite a relative weak excitation power. This
chapter will cover some improvement areas for a more effective system. Especially the low
power and the multiple modes propagating will be discussed. Considerations regarding
the future work, after an all-fiber based scheme has been concluded, will also be discussed
and worked around. Finally an overall conclusion, of the work done in the project will
be presented.
6.0.1
Operating wavelength of the components
The first thing to evaluate is the alignment between the operating wavelength for the
optical components used and the center wavelength emitted by the Ti:Sapphire laser.
The emitted pulse has a center wavelength of 772 nm, but some of the setup elements
operate better at wavelengths that diverge from this value.
The isolator is a component that is optimized for incoming light at 800 nm, and
the wavelength offset results in a lower transmission efficiency (Appendix C). Power loss
through the system is a concern, and the isolator is responsible for a substantial amount.
If operated using the correct wavelength a transmission of 90% is expected instead of
the 75% achieved, which translates to an effective power reduction of 70 mW. Replacing
the isolator with one that offers a higher transmission at 772 nm is recommended.
For the prism compressor the wavelength offset only affects the amount of group
velocity dispersion (GVD) introduced per separation distance. The theoretical calcu-
lations for the amount of GDD introduced (Equation 2.8) and the associated values
from Appendix A are based on a center wavelength of 800 nm [33]. This will not have
any practical consequences and will only result in a mismatch between the theoretical
separation distance and the actual distance between the two prisms. It would however
become relevant should the prism compressor be removed in favor of an all fiber-based
scheme. The exact amount of GDD the compressor introduces is not known, making it
impossible to calculated the correct length of fiber needed.
==PAGE:56==
46
6 Discussion and improvement proposals
In contrast to the components just mentioned, the long period grating (LPG) has an
operating wavelength of 770 nm which correlates well with the center wavelength of the
pulses emitted by the laser. The LPG is thus unlikely to be responsible for introducing
the unwanted modes in the HOM fiber.
6.0.2
Propagation of multiple modes
The next challenge is the co-propagation of the LP01, LP02 mode LP21 modes in the HOM
fiber. Only the LP02 mode experiences the correct amount of anomalous dispersion,
and the two other modes will cause a broadening of the pulse duration as discussed in
Section 2.3. Two-photon fluorescence, and the autocorrelator, is highly dependent on
the excitation light’s peak intensity, which depends on the pulse duration, the repetition
rate and the average power. Having multiple modes travelling simultaneously will cause
power to be distributed between them, hence power is reduced in the LP02 mode.
The generation of these modes most likely originates from either the LPG, the splices
in the HOM fiber or bending of the fiber. The LPG has previously been shown to convert
efficiently to the LP02 mode and is operated at the correct wavelength. As such there is
no reason to believe the LPG does not convert correctly [1][50].
The splices are plausible candidates to influence the in-coupled modes. Especially the
first splice seems probable, since the intensity from both before and after the second
splice is unchanged, as seen in Figure 6.1. The small difference that is visible between
the two graphs is probably caused by a difference in length between the fiber and focusing
lens when the pictures were taken. The similarity suggests that the same distribution of
modes is present on both side of the second splice. Ideally a single splice should replace
the two HOM fiber splices, and by doing so their influence in regard to the in-couple
modes could be investigated further.
Finally, bending of the fiber also influence the propagating modes and the coupling
between them. The intensity profile changed slightly when carefully increasing the bend
of the fiber, suggesting that either power from specific modes is lost through the cladding,
or that power is being exchanged in-between modes. Forward work should include an
investigation of how the fiber bending affect the mode distribution.
Furthermore the dispersion properties of especially the LP21 mode is needed to char-
acterize the current system better. By knowing its properties a theoretical explanation
for the two pulse traces measured by the autocorrelator can hopefully be made, and thus
the origin of the additional modes can be identified.

3) modal dispersion and 4) polarization mode dispersion.

6.0.4
Optimization of transmitted power
Another key issue for the system is the low power output of the fiber. The achieved
power is barely enough to generate a trace on the autocorrelator from LP02 mode and it
is so low that scattering dominates during imaging. Components that can be improved
here are the isolator, the HOM fiber splices and the coupling efficiency through the
MOSAIC Pro.
The transmission efficiency of the isolator has already been covered. This is not the first
priority to improve, even though the power transmission can be substantially improved
here. The additional power gain will be coupled into the fiber increasing the nonlinear
effects.s The HOM splices also introduce a significant loss, even when not considering
the multiple propagating modes.
An increase of nonlinear effect is not expected by
substituting the two HOM fiber splice with a single, since the light already is propagating
in the fiber at this stage. The last proposal is to increase the power efficiency through
the MOSAIC Pro, by changing the collimating lens after the fiber. This is the most
promising alteration to make, since it is after the fiber and does thus not increase
unwanted nonlinear effects at all.
A higher amount of power enable a more elaborate investigation of the TPEFM,
despite the amount of nonlinear effects being introduced in the fiber. The influence of
nonlinear effects was determined through the nonlinear length in relation to the total
propagation length as shown in Section 2.5. The only free variable when determining
the nonlinear length for this system is the input power. The rest of the variables are
design parameters of the system that are not easily altered, like the effective area and
the used center wavelength.

6.1 Future work and challenges

6.1
Future work and challenges
The proposed fiber module does not include all components that enable it to be used
as an imaging endoscope. Aspects like signal collection, objective size and scanning
mechanism should also be considered.
Collection of the signal is an important factor for the system and a common approach
to achieve collection is by using a double clad fiber. Here the excitation light is guided
in the core and fluorescence signal is collected through the cladding [9]. Such a scheme
is better than using two different fibers, where the module size becomes larger [5]. This
collection method has already been shown to provide good imaging [7][9]. A requirement
of the collection fiber is for it to have a large core size and numerical aperture, so as
much signal as possible is coupled in [4]. In generally there is a tradeoff between the
collection efficiency and the overall diameter and thus size of the endoscope probe.
The objective lens also has to be developed. The same objective lens is used to
focus the excitation beam and collect the fluorescence signal. This duality sets high
requirements to the lens in regards to operating wavelength and size. Typically lenses are
set to operate at a specific wavelength but due to the large stoke shift between excitation
and emission light broadband focusing is required. The consequence of having a lens
not able to focus the two wavelengths correctly is either a reduction of the lateral and
axial resolution or worse signal collection due to chromatic aberration. Previous work
generally utilize a graded index objective lens that somewhat handles these complications
[7][9]. This lens typically can achieve a lateral and axial resolution of 1−2 µm and 5−20
µm, respectively and have a diameter in between 0.35-3 mm [4]. Usually the collection
efficiency is sacrificed over higher resolution [4].
6.2
Final conclusion
Extensive work has ben done to characterize the propagation of an ultra short pulse in
a fiber scheme consisting of a single mode fiber, a higher order mode fiber and a long
period grating. By letting the pulse propagate in the LP02 mode in the HOM fiber,
anomalous dispersion and achieved and the pulse is thus dispersion compensated. This
has resulted in a measured output duration of 46.28 fs on an autocorrelator, which is in an
acceptable result. The average power was measured to be approximately 22 mW which
is relatively low and is a challenge to obtain two-photon excitation fluorescence images
with. Nevertheless two-photon fluorescence was achieved when imaging microspheres
and there are many possibilities to optimize further on the system
