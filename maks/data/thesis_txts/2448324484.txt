Abstract 
Combustible dust and powder are flammable and can explode when in the form of a dust cloud, at 
critical concentration and exposed to an energy source. The risk is known and the desire to minimize 
the risk is seen in the extensive research and studies within this area.  
 
The present research focuses on the analysis of the chemical compounds released by pyrolysis of 
combustible dust. Knowledge in this field is scarce and data therefore few, despite the properties of 
the pyrolysis gases being essential for the reaction and energy of dust explosions. The aim is to iden-
tify the pyrolysis gases from dust and powders at different temperatures and to build knowledge and 
data to improve the modeling of dust explosions. 
 
The study focuses on dust and powders from the food industry, the furniture industry and the wood 
industry. In total, nine different dusts and powders have been analyzed. 
 
In the experiment the dust sample is placed in a reactor heated at temperature controlled on de-
mand. The atmosphere in the reactor is derived from oxygen to prevent oxidation products. The sam-
ple is analyzed at temperatures from 100 °C to 800 °C. For each temperature interval, two samples 
of the pyrolysis gases are taken from the reactor. The samples are subsequently analyzed in a gas 
chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) with helium and argon as carrier gas in two columns. 
 
Results from the GC-MC analysis showed that the primary pyrolysis gases produced are methane, 
carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide and hydrogen. The measurements of the primary gases from the 
different dust samples follows comparable trends.  
The initial pyrolysis gases are carbon dioxide and hydrogen. Hydrogen follow a steady increase with 
higher temperatures whereas the concentration of carbon dioxide has a peak at 400 °C followed by 
a decline towards 800 °C.  The proportion of methane have a steadily incline towards a peak at 600-
700 °C, after which it decreases. The maximum concentration of methane is measured up to 61,7 %.  
The concentration of methane is correlated to a Gaussian distribution with a good fit.   
Carbon monoxide increases with temperature. At 600 °C the incline become more steeply with con-
sequential high concentration of carbon monoxide at high temperatures. Three samples have a peak 
at 400 °C, before they follow the same trend as the other samples.  
At temperatures above 600 °C the reactions promote formation of hydrogen and carbon monoxide 
to an extent that seems unusual, but consistent with other studies of pyrolysis at high temperatures.   
 
As the results shows, even small amounts of flammable powder produce highly reactive gases, up to 
31,2 % hydrogen at 800 °C, and thus seen a combustible powder which is dispersed in air can react 
highly explosively.

1  Introduction 
Dust explosion have been known for almost 300 years and is a potential risk in any industry that is 
handling, manufacturing, or transporting combustible dusts. When a dust explosion happens, the 
damage is often severe and repairs expensive.  
 
Finely divided solids play a major and important role in modern technology. It is estimated that three-
quarters of all raw materials used in the chemical and process industries and half of its products (on 
a weight basis) are particulate solids. At least 1 % of the world's electricity is consumed in crushing 
and grinding [1].  
Dust explosions hazard continues to represent a constant threat to process industries that manufac-
tures, uses or handles dust and powder1 of combustible materials [2].  While it is obvious that some 
dust is combustible, like e.g. wood and paper dust, others are not that apparent, such as aluminum 
dust. 
 
A dust explosion begins when a cloud of combustible dust is ignited. The burning dust creates a high 
temperature volume of burning gas and dust, that will tend to spread into the unburned part of the 
dust cloud.  As the surface of the burning dust cloud expands, it creates a large volume of combustion 
products. It will accelerate rapidly, as the surface of the burning part of the dust cloud will continue 
to form an even larger volume of combustible products. If the dust cloud is confined in a vessel or 
structure, the high temperature of the combustion products will lead to a rapidly raise in pressure. If 
the rising pressure exceeds the strength of the confinement, it will rupture, letting the high-pressure 
gas expand into the surroundings [1]. 
 
When combustible dust ignite, there are often two explosions known as a primary and a secondary 
explosion. The primary explosion often occurs inside or near running machinery in processing opera-
tions when the dust cloud is ignited by an external source. The secondary explosion occurs when a 
primary dust explosion disperses dust which also ignites and hereby creates a secondary explosion. 
The secondary explosions can be particularly devastating as accumulated dust may be present around 
the equipment or in the building [1, 3]. 
 
Although the knowledge of combustible dust hazards has a long history, even today, combustible 
dust accidents surprise unsuspecting victims. As an example, in 2002 a Danish wood pellet factory 
was destroyed by a dust explosion, that began in a connecting warehouse. The factory was rebuilt

Dust explosions are a complex phenomenon, that is well researched, and the literature of the subject 
is extensive. Most of this emphasise the industrial safety perspective and the prevention and protec-
tion from dust explosions. As a consequence, most research has been focused on reducing the risk 
and managing the combustible dust hazard and thereby improving and ensuring the level of safety in 
the industries [3]. The procedure of risk management is to ensure that the hazards are systematically 
identified, evaluated and controlled.  
Though it is essential to limit the primary explosion, it is equally or even more important to limit the 
possibility of the primary explosion to ignite a series of secondary explosions. Possibly the most com-
mon method for fuel control strategy is housekeeping, as it is both effective and simple. Housekeep-
ing refers to the disciplined and systematic control of fugitive dust accumulation. Good housekeeping 
is one of fundamental importance to lower the effects of secondary explosions [1].  
 
However, dust explosions still occur and unfortunately relatively often, which is the reason there still 
is a need to expand the research in this area, and to include the properties of the dust explosion itself 
in the research. Knowledge of the pyrolysis products produced when dust is heated, is essential in 
order to understand the combustion phenomena of dust clouds. Extending this knowledge will pro-
vide data to simulate dust explosions and to improve the safety guidelines. 
 
The aim of this research is to identify and quantify the pyrolysis gases from dusts and powders at 
different temperatures. This gained data will be used in further works to determine the combustion 
and explosion properties of dust clouds as function of their physical and chemical properties. It will 
allow a better evaluation of the explosiveness and hazard of the specific dust or powder. The concen-
tration of the different volatiles species in the pyrolysis gases are an important factor in this evalua-
tion.  
 
The study focuses on powders and dust from the food industry (milk powder, corn starch, cocoa pow-
der etc.), the furniture industry (in the form of dust and other by-products) and the wood industry 
(dust and other by-products). The pyrolysis gases from nine different dust and powder samples have 
been experimental studied.  
 
In the experiment the powder sample is placed in a reactor and heated to temperatures between 
100-800 °C. The atmosphere in the reactor is oxygen deprived to prevent oxidation products. Samples 
from the pyrolysis products are collected at different temperature intervals and subsequently ana-
lyzed in a gas chromatograph mass spectroscopy (GC-MS).

2 Theory 
Combustible dust and powder are flammable and can explode when in the form of a dust cloud and 
at critical conditions. Nevertheless, finely divided combustible solids play an irreplaceable role in 
modern technology. By processing solids into powders, they are easier to handle, process, transport 
and store. However, converting a solid to a particulate material causes new hazards. Especially when 
it comes to combustible dust and powders, where the potential of flash fire, smouldering or dust 
explosion is an important risk factor to consider [1, 3].  
 
Dust explosion have been recognised as a threat to humans and property for almost 300 years from 
the end of the 18th century, where one of the earliest analysis of a dust explosion is described by the 
Italian natural philosopher Count Morozzo. The description of a grain dust explosion in this publica-
tion included the identification of the key elements necessary for a dust explosion: finely divided fuel, 
creation of a dust suspension in air, confinement of the dust cloud, and the presence of an ignition 
source. [1, 3, 5] As the industrial revolution created an increase in coal mining, there were also an 
increase in the number of fatal explosions in coal mines. At the same time an increase in the chemical 
and metallurgical industries have resulted in an increasingly number of new, finely divided combus-
tible solid materials and dust. [1] 
 
In this work, the chemical compounds released by pyrolysis of different combustible dusts and pow-
ders are analysed in order to collect data that allow better evaluation of the explosiveness and hazard 
of the specific dust or powder. The concentration of the different volatile species in the pyrolysis 
gases are an important factor in this evaluation. Knowledge in this field is scarce and data therefore 
limited, even though the properties of the pyrolysis gases are essential for the reaction and energy 
of dust explosions.  
2.1 Dust explosions 
A dust explosion begins when a cloud of combustible dust is ignited in a confined setting. In a dust or 
powder the surface area is very large and the volume of the particle is very small. Furthermore, the 
air entrainment is easier when a solid have powder form, as the surface/volume ratio is high, and the 
energy required to ignite the material is minimal. As a result, oxidation reactions, release a large 
amount of energy in a short period of time. This causes a drastic increase in temperature that support 
the burning of adjacent particles. If the dust cloud is confined in a vessel or structure, the high tem-
perature of the combustion products leads to a rise in pressure. If the rising pressure exceeds the 
strength of the confinement, the vessel or structure will rupture allowing the high-pressure gas to 
expand into the surroundings [1, 3, 6].

In both risk assessment and accident investigation it is helpful to conceptualise the necessary condi-
tions for a dust explosion.  
Combustible dust presents three types of combustible hazards: smoldering fires, flash fires and dust 
explosions.  For a dust explosion five different factors are necessary: fuel, oxidiser, an ignition source, 
suspension of the dust and containment of the dust cloud. Since five factors are necessary it is often 
symbolised as a pentagon (Figure 2-1) [1].

When the five factors of the pentagon are present, the risk of a dust explosion arises. Dust particles 
are strongly influenced by gravity; an essential requirement for a dust explosion is therefore suspen-
sion of the dust in a oxidant (for instance air) creating the formation of a dust/air mixture [1]. Once 
combustion of the dust/air mixture occurs, confinement (partial or complete) leads to the develop-
ment of overpressure, enabling the transition of a fast-burning dust flame into a dust explosion [7]. 
 
When a combustible dusts cloud ignites, there are often two explosions: a primary and a secondary 
explosion. The primary dust explosion occurs when a dust is dispersed into air forming an ignitable 
concentration in an enclosed area or within equipment, is ignited and the heat release results in a 
pressure rise. The pressure wave and flow induced by the primary explosion will disperse the deposits 
of other dusts and powders that have accumulated in the surroundings. Those new dust clouds can 
transport the oncoming flames, which can lead to a secondary explosion [3, 6]. 
 
Dust explosions are generated by the combustion of particles with the surrounding gas. The burning 
of combustible dusts, that consists of carbonaceous particles, will undergo pyrolysis creating a flow 
of fuel vapor from the solid particle into the surroundings. Flame propagation can occur when the 
fuel vapor, called pyrolysate, mixes with the surrounding air forming a flammable concentration [1, 
6]. 
 
The probability and severity of a dust explosion can be described as a function of a wide variety of 
parameters. Some of these parameters represent properties that are specific to the chemical prop-
erties of the exploding dust/air mixture, while others reflect the severity of the explosion to the flow 
properties and to the heat transfer to the dust cloud [8]. The key parameters used to describe

combustible dust hazards have their basis in fundamental combustion behaviour but are measured 
using standardised equipment operated to harmonised industry standards. The key parameters fall 
into three categories: explosion parameters, concentration limits, and ignition parameters [1]. 
 
The purpose of the various parameters, used to describe combustible dust hazards, is to provide the 
quantitative data for the various hazards related to dust explosions, and to be able to determine the 
right safety precautions.  
The tests are done at a laboratory; therefore, the results must be scaled up and applied to industrial 
situations. The conditions in the industry must be considered carefully to ensure, that the laboratory 
tests represent the actual scenario, and that the test sample is comparable to the material handled, 
with the same particle size distribution, chemical structure etc. This is one of the primary challenges, 
and the dust cloud in the production is likely to be different from a dust cloud created in the labora-
tory [3, 6].  
 
The explosion parameters are the explosion pressure, the maximum rate of pressure rise and Kst. 
These parameters give indication of the potential severity of an explosion. The parameters are typi-
cally measured in a vessel with the intent of achieving ignition in the centre of a uniform dust cloud. 
The explosion pressure is the final pressure attained in a test vessel following a dust deflagration. The 
maximum rate of pressure rise is directly related to the mass burning rate of the fuel. The Kst value 
(also called the deflagration index or combustible dust parameter), are numerically identical to the 
maximum rate of pressure rise in the standard 1 m3 ISO test. Kst is a central parameter used to eval-
uate the explosivity of a dust cloud but, as the maximum rate of pressure rise, the values of KSt is very 
sensitive to the size and shape of the test vessel [8, 1]. 
 
There are three important concentration parameters: the stoichiometric concentration, the minimum 
explosible concentration and the maximum explosible concentration. Both the minimum and the max-
imum explosible concentration have theoretical foundation, but their values depend on how they are 
obtained. They can be calculated using a theoretical model or the measured using a specific test 
method and laboratory apparatus. 
Measurement data for the maximum explosible concentration tend to have poor reproducibility and 
are generally considered not to be suitable for engineering design purposes [1].  
The stoichiometric concentration is a theoretical parameter determined by a mass balance assuming 
complete combustion of the fuel. At the stoichiometric concentration the concentration of fuel is 
exactly matched to the oxygen concentration required for complete combustion. Unlike flammability 
limits, the stoichiometric concentration is a theoretical parameter whose value is independent of any 
test apparatus. [1]. It has been found empirically that the maximum burning velocity or maximum 
explosion pressure for flammable gas mixtures typically is measured at or slightly on the fuel-rich side 
of stoichiometric, e.g. the stoichiometric concentration is slightly greater than 1 [1, 9].

There are two ignition tests for dust clouds, the minimum autoignition temperature (AIT) and the 
minimum ignition energy (MIE). In each case, the dust concentration is varied to achieve the lowest 
value of the ignition parameter. [1] 
 
Particle size and shape are also a primary determinant of explosion severity. The smaller the particle 
the greater the specific surface area, which is defined as defined as the total surface area of a material 
per unit of mass. The rate of combustion of particulate materials is dependent on the surface area 
available for mass transfer. [1] This is also confirmed by research and literature, among others the 
research by R. Zanzi, K. Sjöstöm and E. Björnholm [10] and D. Kunzu and M. Ali [11]. 
 
Combustible dust explosions undergo two alternative combustion mechanisms, homogeneous and 
heterogeneous. The homogeneous combustion of a single-particle explosion goes through different 
steps: heating of the particle, producing volatiles, mixing of the volatile gases with air and homoge-
neous combustion; all of which are influenced by the properties of the dust (i.e. particle mean diam-
eter, size distribution) [3]. At the same time the dust particle may react directly with oxygen diffusing 
towards the particle itself (heterogeneous combustion) [12].

For larger particles, the explosion process is dominated by the devolatilization process, due to the 
relatively inefficient heat exchange process between dust particles and gas around [13]. If the homo-
geneous combustion is the slowest of the different steps, increasing the devolatilization rate by de-
creasing the particle size does not increase the overall combustion rate.  
At very small particle sizes (generally <30 μm), the dust explosion is mainly controlled by homogene-
ous combustion rather than by heating and devolatilisation, as the heating as well as the pyrolysis 
steps are very fast [8, 14, 3]. 
 
Generally, the combustion of coal and carbonaceous dust are controlled by a homogeneous mecha-
nism, while metal dust burn through heterogeneous surface oxidation. This was confirmed by the 
research of B. Du et al. (2014) [13] where the dust explosions behaviour of sweet potato dust and 
magnesium dust were investigated.

2.2 Pyrolysis  
Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of organic (carbon-based) materials through the application 
of heat in an inert atmosphere and the first step in gasification and combustion. Pyrolysis generally 
consists in heating, breaking (“cracking”) the chemical bonds in its molecules.  
Many chemical reactions may take place in the particle during pyrolysis. Primary pyrolysis is when 
the material is heated above its decomposition temperature, and the initial material is degraded to 
gases and solid residues. The products of these primary reactions undergo secondary reactions where 
primary solid residues and gases may be transformed to other gases and solids [15].  
 
Pyrolysis of organic substances produces gaseous products (hydrogen, carbon monoxide, carbon di-
oxide, methane, acetylene, ethane and other organic compounds), liquid products (tars, high molec-
ular hydrocarbons and water) and solid products (char).   
The number and variety of decomposition products usually varies with the heating rate [16]. Hence, 
the set of decomposition products generated at a slow heating rate is usually different from the set 
of products generated by a fast heating rate. 
 
Pyrolysis can be controlled by heat transfer, mass transfer or chemical reactions according to exper-
imental conditions and the physical and chemical properties of the dust.  
Many papers have been published on the study of pyrolysis of organic materials. The physical condi-
tions of the pyrolysis of biomass and other organic material, such as temperature, heating rate and 
residence time have been shown to have an effect on the product yields and composition. Depending 
on the temperature and heating rate, different types of pyrolysis exists; slow, fast and flash. [17]

Slow pyrolysis is characterized by long residence times, low temperatures and slow heating 
rates. The heating temperatures ranges from 0.1 to 2 °C per second. Slow heating rates are 
often paired  with low maximum temperatures with the intent to maximize the yield of char.  
 
Several types of reactors are used in determining the pyrolytic behavior of combustible solids. These 
include wire-mesh, single-particle and entrained-flow/drop tube reactors, fixed and fluidized-bed re-
actors. Due to the nature of the dust some of these reactors prove difficult to use (e.g. fluidized-bed 
and flow/drop tube reactors). The residence time is difficult to controlled, because off different falling 
velocity/suspension, as dust are easily dispersed and. The effect of different falling velocities is dis-
cussed in the research by R. Zanzi, K. Sjöstöm and E. Björnholm [10], as it may have influenced the 
higher result of hydrogen in the test of straw as it had a visually lower falling velocity than the other 
tested agricultural residues.    
 
In the present work the dust samples are heated at a slow rate, ensuring local thermal equilibrium 
between gas and solid. A slow pyrolysis process can have several stages for separation of the products 
created in the process, this is controlled by creating an oxygen poor atmosphere, with limited com-
bustion and residence time to steady-state. This is an acceptable approach, as the trends of the re-
sults are only limited affected by the heating rate. 
 
As research of pyrolysis gases of dusts are scarce, the current work refers to other studies of combus-
tible solids and not just dusts and powders. The are several studies of the pyrolysis kinetics of com-
bustible solids at different temperatures, time intervals and different heat rates. The main aspects of 
the different studies are described in the following sections. 
As the heating of a dust or powder is very fast, the emphasis is on fast or very fast (flash) pyrolysis of 
larger sample sized combustible solids, as it is comparable to the pyrolysis of dusts and powders.  
2.2.1 
Pyrolysis of biomass 
Pyrolyzing biomass (such as biowaste) is a renewable energy source that can eventually replace fossil 
fuels. The research focusses on determined the pyrolysis gases in order to optimize the production 
of energy. This gives indication of the expected substances and trends when heating a combustible 
dust or powder, as it is comparable to heating biowaste, though particle size and heating rate varies.  
The smaller particle size of the dust samples result in a faster heating comparable of a fast heating of 
biowaste with a larger particle size.

2.2.2 
Wood pyrolysis 
In the research by Brink et al. [18] the mechanisms of pyrolysis-gasification of a gas suspension of 
wood particles is studied. The wood particles are investigated in a flow reactor technique over a tem-
perature range of 316 °C to 871 °C. The gaseous products are determined by gas solid chromatog-
raphy.  
The research showed three typical classes of products in the used temperature range: 
1. A gas mixture, containing primary water vapor, H2, CO, CO2 and CH4 
2. A condensable product 
3. A carbonaceous residue 
The results show that the sum of the condensable product and the carbonaceous residue decreased 
with temperature whereas the gas production increased with temperature. At low temperatures car-
bon dioxide is the main gas product, whereas the concentration of hydrogen, carbon monoxide and 
methane are low. At high temperature carbon dioxide decreases and the main product is carbon 
monoxide. Hydrogen increases with temperature, with a peak at 700 °C, after which it decreases. 
Methane follows the same trend as hydrogen with a smaller variation and generally lower gas pro-
duction than hydrogen.

2.2.3 
Pyrolysis of maize, sunflower, grape and tobacco residues 
In the research by Encinar et al. [17], four agricultural residues, with variation in reaction time and 
temperature, were studied in order to quantify their energy content. The temperature interval in the 
study is 400-700 °C.  
The research showed that an increase in temperature lead to an increase in gas production and a 
decrease in the solid fraction, with a plateau at 600 °C. The fraction of the liquid yield goes through a 
maximum at about 500 °C, and it is suggested that the gas formation increases from this point due to 
the cracking of the liquid fraction.  
The higher the temperature, the higher the maximum gas concentration and the lower the reaction 
time is needed to reach it. Temperature has a positive effect on the production of methane, carbon 
monoxide and hydrogen and a negative effect on the production of carbon dioxide. This is explained 
by the consumption of carbon dioxide with carbon (formula (2.2)).  
Since residue samples were fed once the reactor was at the reaction temperature, all experiments 
were carried out at isothermal conditions possibly in a fast pyrolysis regime.

2.2.5 
Reactivity of product gases in enclosed fires. 
The reactivity of product gases in enclosed fire environments are studied in several publications by 
W. Pitts [19, 20, 21, 22], with the focus on the carbon monoxide formation. The studies initiated with 
the purpose to investigate and verify the mechanism responsible for the surprisingly high carbon 
monoxide level found in enclosed fires. 
 
In the extensive review article “The Global Equivalence Ratio Concept and the Formation Mechanism 
of Carbon Monoxide in Enclosed Fires”  [21] W. Pitts summarise the research on formation of CO in 
fires up to 1994.  
The product gases generated for lean and rich conditions varied. As a result, the concentration of 
carbon monoxide can be correlated in terms of the global equivalence ratio2.  
For rich conditions carbon monoxide is produced in preference to carbon dioxide. For temperatures 
greater than 1100 K, the reactions of rich mixtures produce significant amount of carbon monoxide, 
hydrogen and water [19, 20].  
The results from the most recent study [22] indicate that rich gas mixtures only begin to approach 
thermodynamic equilibrium when the temperature increased to a value of about 1400 K. 
 
The comparisons of the calculated behaviours of the perfectly-stirred (fast) and plug-flow (slow) re-
actor for identical starting conditions showed only minor differences, indicating that the reaction be-
haviour is only weakly dependent on the mixing behaviour [21]. 
 
Furthermore, two alternative mechanism for increasing the formation of carbon monoxide have been 
identified. First the mixing of air or very lean gases directly into a rich upper layer at high tempera-
tures. Carbon monoxide is the primary oxidized products of those reactions, whereas the production 
of carbon dioxide is low [21].  
The second mechanism is the capability of wood, or other polymetric fuel containing oxygen as part 
of its molecular structure, of generating carbon monoxide directly by high temperature pyrolysis. If 
the pyrolysis gas does not come into direct contact with oxygen, the additional carbon monoxide will 
not be converted into carbon dioxide. When the layer is deprived of oxygen and if the temperature 
is > 900 K, then the pyrolysis of wood will lead to significant increase in carbon monoxide concentra-
tions [21].  
 
In the research by Leroy et al. [23] describing an experimental study of the combustion of pyrolysis 
gases in wildland fires, with similar results as the study by W. Pitts.  
The oxidation of a mixture of carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, hydrogen and methane are studied. 
The experiments were performed over the temperature range 773 – 1273 K and for equivalence ratio 
of 1 and 1,2.

2.3 Summary  
The study of the components pyrolysis gases, which are generated during the heating of dust and 
powder, can be used to gain greater knowledge of dust explosion, their hazardousness, their explo-
siveness and the precautions that can prevent them. And not least why dust and powder pose a spe-
cial danger. 
 
Many papers have been published on the study of pyrolysis of organic materials. Some of the notice-
able results are: 
 
- 
Gas production increased with temperature. 
- 
Gas production decreases with particle size. The smaller the particle the greater the specific 
surface area and the greater the rate of combustion. 
- 
The primary components of pyrolysis gas of combustible solids are methane, carbon monox-
ide, carbon dioxide and methane.  
- 
At higher temperatures there is an increase in proportion of hydrogen as a result of the in-
creased favoured cracking of hydrocarbon.  
- 
An increase in temperature will increase the concentration of carbon monoxide and hydro-
gen and a decrease the concentration of carbon dioxide. 
- 
At high temperatures and at rich conditions (e.g. under ventilated) carbon monoxide is pro-
duced in preference to carbon dioxide. The formation of carbon monoxide is further acceler-
ated by material containing oxygen as part of their molar structure.  
- 
The production of methane both showed an increase at decrease with temperature, depend-
ing on the temperature range.

3 Method 
The method was designed to analyse the gaseous products from the pyrolysis of combustible dust. 
The experimental setup has been focused on developing a technique that makes it possible to heat 
the dust/powder sample in an oxygen deprived atmosphere to prevent oxidation reactions. It was 
also important to be able to control the temperature, raise the temperature in controlled interval 
and have the possibility to extract samples from of pyrolysis gases without interrupting the experi-
ment and the heating process. 
In this way, it was possible to ensure samples of the pyrolysis gases at different temperatures for 
different dusts and powders. 
 
The gas samples were analysed using a gas chromatographer for determining and analysing the gas-
eous pyrolysis products.   
 
One challenges was to keep the experimental set up airtight and to ensure the test samples, the dust 
and powder which are very easily dispersed, remain inside the reactor.

3.2.1 
Procedure 
The dust sample was heated in a cylindrical metal reactor which was placed in an oven where the 
temperature could be controlled (tubular furnace). The reactor was connected to a pressure gauge, 
a valve for nitrogen flow (used to flush the system with nitrogen at the beginning) and a valve for 
sampling (with a needle to inject to the glass vials). The experimental setup is shown in figure 3-1 to 
figure 3-4. 
 
 
Figure 3-1: Experimental set-up 
To obtain an oxygen poor atmosphere, the reactor was filled with nitrogen before the valves were 
closed. Simultaneously, it was ensured that the experimental setup was completely airtight, as the 
reactor was pressurised with nitrogen to 80-100 psi. 
The nitrogen filled reactor was placed in the tubular oven. The temperature was measured by the 
oven as well as by a thermometer placed in the oven underneath the reactor, to ensure an even 
temperature measurement.  
 
==PAGE:31==
 
Method 
21 
Approximately 10-15 ml of the powder sample was placed in the reactor after which the installation 
was connected. Then nitrogen was circulated through the entire system to displace air throughout 
the system.  
After air was removed and replaced by nitrogen, the system was closed, and nitrogen filled to raise 
pressure and insure the installation was airtight. The atmosphere in the reactor was thus deprived 
from oxygen to prevent oxidation products and to ensure that the gas samples only contain pyrolysis 
gases.  
 
To begin of an experimental run, the oven was heated to 100 °C. After the temperature of both ther-
mometers reached the desired temperature and the temperature was steady (15-20 min), to ensure 
a steady state situation, two samples of the pyrolysis gases were obtained. The temperature was 
raised with an interval of 100 °C, where a new set of samples were collected. This was repeated until 
the maximum temperature of 800 °C was reached. 
 
To ensure a clean sample without residues from nitrogen and pyrolysis gases from previous sampling 
(at a lower temperature), the tubes and valves were evacuated by opening the sample valve for a few 
seconds before taking a sample.  
Samples were collected in glass vials. Air was removed from the vials before sampling, or the vials 
were equipped with an additional needle to drive out air as the gas filled the vial.

At the end of an experimental run, the oven was deactivated. After cooling down, the set-up was 
disassembled, and the individual components cleaned, before a new experimental run was initiated.  
 
At the beginning of every experimental run the pressure was between 80-100 psi, which was the 
pressure obtained from pressurising the reactor with nitrogen. When the experiment was running, 
the reactor was a closed air-tight system. As the temperatures rises, the pressure in the reactor was

3.2.2 
Gas Chromatography 
A gas chromatograph mass spectroscopy (GC-MC), Agilent Technologies 490 GC, was used to deter-
mine the volatile components in the gas samples.  
 
The GC-MS uses two column channels, channel 1: MolSieve 5A (MS5A) 10 m with backflush and chan-
nel 2: PoraPLOT U (PPU) 10 m also with backflush. The back-flushing system reverses the flow of the 
carrier gas after the target compound is detected to discharge residual components in the column 
from the injection port. Argon and helium were used as carrier gas. 
The two columns were set up to identify H2, O2, N2, CH4, CO and CO2, C2H4, C2H6, C2H2 and C3H8, re-
spectively. 
 
Quantification of the identified components were based on calibration standards. In this study the 
NIST (The National Institute of Standards and Technology) database were used. The volume propor-
tion of the gases in a sample was determined periodically by means of an integrator. 
 
The GC- MS was calibrated with standard gases at start-up. The calibration was verified continuously 
by running of a blank sample of atmospheric air, both at the beginning of a day’s runs, and between 
analysing a new set of samples. Additionally, the calibration of GC-MS has been regularly assessed by 
evaluating the data sets (whether all the substances are included in the analysis and/or whether there 
is a large deviation between the sum of the substances and 100%).

5 Discussion 
For each dust sample, concentration of different substances in pyrolysis gases was measured, at dif-
ferent temperatures ranging from 100 °C to 800 °C. 
Increasing temperature in the rector lead to a higher concentration of pyrolysis gases. The primary 
gases produced are methane, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide and hydrogen. The composition of 
pyrolysis gases varies with the different dust and powder samples; however, the measurements show 
that the development of the primary gases follows a similar trend, though not as clearly for carbon 
dioxide.  
 
The reactor was oxygen deprived with nitrogen at the beginning of the experiment. Therefore, at 
100 °C, measurements show high values of nitrogen.  
The initial gas formed is carbon dioxide and hydrogen, where the concentration of carbon dioxide is 
proportionally high. The concentration of methane and hydrogen is very low at low temperatures.  
 
Concentration of hydrogen is still low at intermediate temperatures but has a steady incline. In op-
posite to the concentration of carbon dioxide, where the concentration is high (up to 14,1 %) with a 
corresponding steep incline. Most dust samples have a maximum concentration of carbon dioxide at 
400 °C.  
At intermediate temperature the concentration of carbon monoxide is low for most samples, though 
three samples have a peak at around 400 °C (13/Wheat flour, 18/Corn starch and 3/Gypsum powder). 
The three samples have a similar chemical structure and a quit high initial moisture content, but it 
cannot explain the difference in trends completely.  
At 600-700 °C the concentration of methane peaked with a high concentration of almost 50 % in all 
samples.  
 
At high temperatures the measurements show high concentration of hydrogen (10,8 - 31,2 %) and 
carbon monoxide (13,8 – 39,0 %). The concentration of methane follows a decline matching the in-
cline at low temperatures.  
Carbon dioxide have low concentrations at high temperatures. At high temperatures there is also 
small concentration of hydrocarbon (mostly consisting of ethane). 
 
The results correspond with the research by Encinar et al. [17], where the temperature has a positive 
effect on the concentration of methane, carbon monoxide and hydrogen and a negative effect on the 
concentration of carbon dioxide. The study by Encinar et al. did not show an decrease in the produc-
tion of methane as the maximum temperature was limited to 700 °C. In the work by R. Zanzi, K.

5.1 Source of Error 
Four gas samples were omitted from the results, as the results from the GC-MS were inconclusive or 
showed a leak in the sample. It was the following samples: 
 
Sample 18_T600_1. 
The GC-MS analysis of the first sample of corn starch at T = 600 °C (sample 18_T600_1) was inconclu-
sive, as the sum of the different pyrolysis gases did not sum up 100, and all three analysis were dif-
ferent. The sample was omitted from the result. 
 
Sample 21_T700_1, 22_T700_2 and 22_T800_1. 
The glass vial of the first sample of cocoa powder at T = 700 °C (sample 21_T700_1), was not airtight 
as the GC-MS analysis showed an 20 % oxygen and 73 % nitrogen content and a very small amount 
of methane and hydrocarbon compared to the second sample. The three analysis by the GS-MC were 
all similar. The same was the case for the two samples 22_T700_2 and 22_T800_1. Both were samples 
of the pyrolysis gases from by-product of grain, from 700 °C and 800 °C, respectively. The samples 
were omitted from the result. 
 
Furthermore, the GC-MS analysis of the four samples from the sample of wheat flour at 300 °C and 
400 °C, did not detect all substances. The sample was normalised against the sum of detectible gases 
(varied from 65 % to 85 %). The data corresponding with this particular test, at these temperatures, 
must therefore be used with precaution.  
 
The pressure of the reactor was adjusted during the experimental run for safety, as a high pressure 
would cause a safety hazard. The pressure of the reactor was kept constantly but the release was not 
systematically and not registered. It is therefore not possible to detect the influence of this, especially 
as moisture would increase pressure at low temperatures and could have an influence on the three 
samples that deviated from the trend of carbon monoxide concentration.

6 Conclusion 
Pyrolysis of nine dusts and powders were studied at temperatures between 100-800 °C.  
The gas compositions were analysed to understand, which gases are produced during pyrolysis of 
dust and powders commonly found in the alimentary industry. 
 
Some specific conclusions can be drawn. Production of pyrolysis gases depends on temperature. The 
higher the temperature, the higher the proportion of pyrolysis gases. 
The main gases produced during the pyrolysis are methane, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide and 
hydrogen. At higher temperature there are also small concentration of hydrocarbons. The composi-
tion of pyrolysis gases was influenced by temperature. 
 
Hydrogen was one of the initial gases, with constant rise in concentration and a similar trend for all 
samples. At 800 °C the concentration was between 10,8 % and 31,2 %. 
Nearly all dust samples had a maximum concentration of carbon dioxide at 400 °C, with a concentra-
tion up to 14,1 %.  
The incline of the concentration of carbon monoxide increases with temperature. At 800 °C the con-
centrations are between 13,8–39,0 %, the maximum for most samples, though three samples (wheat 
flour, corn starch and gypsum powder) deviate from this trend with a peak at 400 °C. 
The maximum concentration of methane was measured at 600-700 °C with a concentration of up to 
61,7 %.  
 
The development of methane concentration was comparable for all nine samples and followed the 
Gaussian distribution, with very good confidence.  
Trends derived from the experimental data of the primary gases, agreed with existing literature of 
pyrolysis of combustibles at comparable conditions.  
 
Further work is necessary to extend the data and to obtain data from more dust and powders, as 
more data will give more detail information of the pyrolysis composition of dusts and powders. The 
characterisation of the physical and chemical properties of pyrolysis gases can be used in further 
works of modelling dust explosion and thereby be able to define the parameters that have the biggest 
impact on safety.
