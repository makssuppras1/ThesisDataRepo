Abstract 
Lyme disease is a tick-borne infection primary caused by Borrelia burgdorferi. However, 
other Borrelia species in different geographic regions can also cause infections, affecting 
various systems of the host. The pathogenicity of these species remains unclear, 
highlighting the need to investigate their genetic diversity and role in causing Lyme 
disease. Outer membrane proteins of the pathogen are known to be involved in disease 
virulence and bacterial survival mechanisms. Additionally, B. burgdorferi has been 
implicated in molecular mimicry, potentially causing autoimmune responses in chronic 
infections of Lyme disease. 
This study aims to distinguish the genetic diversity among various Borrelia species, 
exploring how protein variations influence Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) 
presentation, and to identify candidate proteins involved in autoimmune responses. 
Proteins from B. burgdorferi involved in Lyme disease were retrieved, and BLASTp was 
used to identify homologous protein sequences from other Borrelia species. High identity 
scores of the homologous sequences to B. burgdorferi indicated evolutionary 
relationships among the species. Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) was employed to 
identify conserved regions and explore differences in the proteins across species, which 
are likely due to different adaptations and antigenic mechanisms. NetMHCIIpan-4.3 was 
used to predict MHC-binding peptides covering HLA class II molecules for all selected 
species, and BepiPred-3.0 was used to predict B-cell epitopes, investigating the 
probability of the selected proteins to induce a humoral immune response. The analysis 
also identified homologous sequences in human proteins, revealing potential molecular 
mimicry by the multicopy lipoprotein MlpB of B. burgdorferi. 
While this study relies on computational data and requires experimental validation, it 
provides a foundation for future research into the genetic diversity of Borrelia species 
and their role in Lyme disease pathogenicity. Additionally, it offers insights into potential 
therapeutic and vaccine development.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Lyme disease or human Lyme borreliosis is the most prevalent tick-borne infection in 
temperate regions globally. It is an infectious disease commonly found in North America, 
Central Europe and Asia. However, it is widespread in multiple regions such as 
Scandinavia due to environmental factors [2],[3]. Lyme disease can be divided into three 
stages (early localized, early disseminated and late disseminated) based on the severity 
and duration of the symptoms. The first two stages are part of an early infection, and the 
third stage is part of a chronic disease.  Early signs of the disease can be distinguished by 
a ring – shaped rash which is followed by flu-like symptoms such as headache, fever, 
myalgia and arthralgia and it can be treated with a course of antibiotics. However, in some 
cases the stages might overlap, and the progression of the disease can be crucial if the 
infection is untreated. Untreated Lyme disease can turn into a debilitating illness affecting 
the nervous, cardiac and immune system of patients [4]. Hosts have evolved adaptative 
immune systems designed to respond to pathogen invasions and reduce the infection’s 
duration. However, the antigenic variations on the surface of the pathogens can challenge 
the immune system’s ability to recognize and effectively target these antigens [5]. 
Different species of the pathogen can elicit different immune responses in the host. The 
interaction between the host’s immune system and the pathogen is influenced by multiple 
factors such as antigenic variations, virulence factors and the host characteristics, which 
alter the dynamics of the disease. The pathogen’s proteins contribute to the bacteria’s 
ability to evade the immune system and influence the severity of the disease across the 
species [6]. Understanding the disease mechanisms and progression within the host will 
contribute to a better diagnosis and classification of the different species based on their 
pathogenicity. 
1.2 Immune System 
The immune system is the host’s defence mechanism against pathogens, including 
bacteria, viruses and abnormal cells that could lead to a disease, while protecting the 
host’s own cells [7]. The basis of the ability to distinguish self from non-self, is antigen 
presentation to immune cells. Antigens are molecular fragments on the surface of the 
pathogen, which are typically proteins, peptides or polysaccharides [7]. Once the immune 
system recognises the antigen the body launches an immune response to protect itself. 
The immune system is divided into two subsystems: the innate and the adaptive immune 
system[8].  This thesis focuses on the adaptive immune system. 
1.2.1 Adaptive immune system 
The adaptive immune system is responsible for providing a targeted and long-lasting 
defence to pathogens. The components of the adaptive immune system include two major 
groups of cells: T lymphocytes (T-cells) and B lymphocytes (B-cells). T-cells have a role 
when an infection has already occurred and are responsible for activating other immune 
cells (T helper cells) and destroying the infected or tumor cells [9]. T-cells have specific 
receptors on their surface (TCRs) where the foreign substance entering the host (antigen) 
can bind. The immune system is responsible for producing T-cells with receptors that 
match with the antigens for an effective elimination of the pathogen [9].

B-cells are responsible for the antibody production, proteins that will bind to the antigens 
and flag the pathogen for destruction. The B-cell activation requires both binding of the 
antigen to the B-cell receptors (BCRs) and interaction with the antigen specific T helper 
cells [7]. 
The activation of the adaptive immune system involves the recognition and presentation 
of specific antigens and activation of T-cells and B-cells. For the antigen recognition and 
presentation are responsible the antigenic presenting cells (APCs) and the major 
histocompatibility complex (MHC)[7]. Figure 1.1. presents the activation of the adaptive

1.3 Major Histocompatibility Complex 
The Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) is a group of genes that code for surface 
proteins of cells involved in the immune system for the recognition and presentation of 
antigens. In humans this complex is also called human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system 
[10]. MHC molecules are proteins found on the surface of the cells that present antigens 
to the T-cells. There are two major types of MHC protein molecules: MHC Class I (MHC-I) 
and MHC Class II (MHC-II). The MHC-I is responsible for presenting endogenous antigens. 
The focus of interest in this study lies on the MHC-II molecules.  
The MHC-II molecules are found on the APCs and are responsible for presenting 
exogenous antigens - such as Borrelia- to helper T cells. In humans, the genes that encode 
for these molecules display a high degree of polymorphism, as each gene has a large

number of alleles [11]. This polymorphism has a significant impact on the immune 
response as the MHC-II molecules can present a wide variety of peptides from pathogens 
facilitating a robust immune response. However, studies have shown that the 
polymorphism might cause implication for autoimmune diseases. Certain MHC-II alleles 
are more efficient at presenting self-peptides, which can lead to an autoreactive adaptive 
immune response [12],[13].  In the context of Borrelia infections, we will explore the 
possibility of molecular mimicry that would suggest a potential autoimmune response. 
1.4 T-cell and B-cell activation process  
T-cell and B-cell activation are significant processes in the adaptive immune system. Once 
an antigen is recognized and presented on the MHC molecules, T-cells and B-cells express 
TCRs and BCRs respectively that specifically recognize the presented peptides and initiate 
their activation [7]. T-cells are generated in the bone marrow and mature into naive CD8+ 
T-cells and CD4+ T-cells in the thymus. They differentiate into T-helper cells, which are 
responsible for activating other immune cells such as B-cells. B-cells are also generated in 
the bone marrow where they remain till their maturation into naive B-cells [7]. The 
activated B-cells they differentiate into plasma cells, responsible for the antibody 
production and memory cells. The large quantities of antibody neutralize the pathogen and 
mark it for destruction from other immune cells [14]. 
1.5 Structure and diversity of MHC-II molecules 
The structure of MHC-II molecules is composed by two chains: α-chain and β-chain, both 
are polymorphic and contribute to the formation of the peptide-binding groove.  
The α-chain has two external domains α1 and α2  as well as β-chain which contains the β1 
and β2 [15]. The peptide-binding groove is formed by the α1 and β1 domains and it can 
accommodate peptides of 13 to 25 amino acids long, with the peptides of 15 residues 
being the most common [7],[15]. The specificity of peptide binding to MHC-II molecules 
is defined by the interaction of the peptide with the four binding pockets within the 
peptide-binding groove, in positions: P1, P4, P6 and P9 as it shown in Figure 1.2. The 
pockets determine the presented peptides to CD4+ T-cells and show high variability.  
The peptide’s part interacting with the MHC-II molecule consists 9 amino acids and is 
referred to as peptide core, while the remaining segments are known as peptide flanking 
regions (PFRs)[7].

The peptide binding core is essential for the stability of the MHC-peptide complex, while

The MHC molecules present high polymorphism which contributes to the immune 
system’s ability to recognize a vast array of antigens from different pathogens. In humans  
MHC-II molecules are encoded by three major MHC-II genes (HLA-DR, HLA-DP, and HLA-
DQ) [11]. Each MHC gene variant (allele), can bind to a different set of peptides, allowing 
the immune system to have a defence mechanism against many pathogens. 
The primary focus of the thesis is on MHC-II molecules and their associated genes. 
1.6 Lyme disease: Etiology 
Lyme disease is caused by the bacterial spirochetes of genus Borrelia, which are bacteria 
transmitted from vertebrate reservoirs to human hosts through bites from the infected 
Ixodes spp. ticks [17]. The most common bacteria species causing the disease are  
B. burgdorferi, B. garinii and B. afzelii, with B. burgdorferi being the most prevalent 
causative agent in North America and Europe. There are various bacteria strains and 
genotypes with distinct characteristics, such as antigenic variation, virulence factors and 
geographic distribution [18],[19]. In Europe, B. burgdorferi sensu lato is more common and 
is associated with chronic skin conditions and central nervous system implications. In the 
United States, the most common variant is B. burgdorferi sensu stricto, causing arthritis 
[20]. B. garinii is a prevalent strain in Europe and commonly causes neurological 
complications, known as neuroborreliosis [21], while B. afzelii, mostly found in central and 
eastern Europe, causes a skin infection which being untreated can end up to a late stage of 
the disease with serious tissue atrophy [22]. The species, B. valaisiana has an unclear role 
in causing Lyme disease in humans compared to other species. Even though it has been 
detected in ticks and some vertebrate hosts, the specific symptoms and clinical 
significance of this species have not been extensively studied [19]. B. valaisiana is found 
primarily in Europe, in regions where Lyme disease is endemic.  
1.7 Evolution and genetic diversity of Borrelia 
Borrelia species exhibit significant evolution and genetic diversity driven by their life 
cycles and interactions with diverse vectors and hosts. The pathogen’s ability to transmit 
and evade the host’s immune system is accomplished by several mechanisms which 
differentiate the species [3]. These mechanisms include antigenic variation, where the 
bacteria alters its surface proteins to avoid the immune system detection and the 
utilization of specific virulence factors that enhance its survival within the host [6]. 
Antigenic variation is accomplished by recombination events that generate new variants 
of the surface proteins, preventing the host’s immune system from recognizing and 
destroying the pathogen. Figure 1.3. illustrates the antigenic variation tactic in Borrelia. 
 
Significant virulence factors that influence the pathogenicity and immune invasion of 
Borrelia,  include outer surface proteins (Οsps), such as OspA, OspB and OspC and other 
lipoproteins [23]. The OspC gene has been a focus of study, due to its significant role in 
causing Lyme disease. This gene encodes for OspC protein responsible for the bacteria’s 
interaction with the host, especially during the early stages of the infection. The high 
genetic variability of surface proteins increases the ability of Borrelia species to persist in 
different hosts and limit immune detection [24],[25].

1.8 Computational tools utilized in the analysis 
1.8.1 NetMHCIIpan-4.3 
NetMHCIIpan-4.3 is a useful tool for predicting peptide binding to MHC-II molecules using 
Artificial Neural Networks. It is designed to cover the three human MHC-II isotypes: HLA-
DR, HLA-DQ and HLA-DP. The tool’s pan-specific capability is due to its training on binding 
affinity and eluted ligand mass spectrometry datasets, integrating immunopeptidomics 
datasets for HLA-DP, HLA- DR, and HLA- DQ [27].  
The user provides the protein sequences and specifies the desired peptide length and 
MHC-II molecules. The tool can predict for any HLA class II (HLA-II) molecule of known 
sequence and provide predictions for peptides of any length, with a default size of 15 
amino acids (15-mer). The required input data should be specified in FASTA format. 
The prediction output for each selected molecule consists of detailed predictions about 
the: 
• 
position of the peptide in the protein sequence 
• 
peptide sequence and the 9-mer core of the peptide that binds to the MHC-II 
molecule 
• 
HLA-II alleles for which the predictions are made 
• 
binding affinity of the peptide-MHC-II complex 
• 
% of rank scores for strong and weak binders 
The default threshold for a strong binder is ≤ 0.01 and for a weak binder ≤ 0.05. A strong 
binder has a high affinity to the MHC-II molecules, ensuring stability of the peptide-MHC 
complex.  
The tool also provides the motif viewer feature which is a visual representation of the

binding motifs for MHC-II molecules. This feature shows the specific amino acids 
preferences at each position within the peptide-binding groove of the MHC-II molecule.  
To understand the molecular mechanisms underlying the adaptive immune system we

1.8.2 BepiPred-3.0 
BepiPred-3.0 is a tool used for predicting B-cell epitopes within the protein sequences. 
The B-cell epitopes are regions on the proteins that are likely to induce an immune 
response by binding to B-cell receptors or antibodies.  
BepiPred-3.0 predicts both linear and discontinuous B-cell epitopes from a protein 
sequence, using neural networks. Linear epitopes are arranged sequentially along the 
amino acid sequence, whereas discontinuous epitopes are formed by segments scattered 
throughout the antigen's structure, which come together in three-dimensional space due 
to the antigen's folding process [28]. 
This tool is trained to capture the features of the molecules, and non-epitope amino acids 
determined from crystal structures. An additional option the tool provides, is the use of a 
sequential smoothing, a technique to reduce the noise and variability in the data for 
predicting linear B-cell epitopes.  
The input data is protein sequences in FASTA format. The user specifies the cut-off 
percentage of B-cell epitope predictions for low to higher confidence predictions: higher 
confidence (top 20%), moderate confidence (top 50%), lower confidence (top 70%).  
There is a threshold for predicting B-cell epitopes, which is a classification threshold 
determining whether a peptide segment is predicted to be B-cell epitope or not, it is often 
protein-specific and the default value is 0.1512.  
The output data includes the B-cell epitope predictions illustrated with bar plots. 
Throughout our analysis, we used this tool to identify the protein regions of Borrelia that 
induce an immune response.  
1.8.3 BLAST 
Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) compares input sequences against a database 
of sequences to identify regions of local similarity. The program compares nucleotide or 
protein sequences and calculates the statistical significance of the matches. It is a tool used 
to identify homologous sequences and investigate potential functional and evolutionary 
relationships between species [29]. 
BLAST performs sequence alignments, and it uses heuristic algorithms. There are four 
basic algorithms: blastp, blastn, blastx and tblastn. In our analysis, we used blastp. Blastp 
compares amino acid query sequences against a protein database.  
The input data should include accession numbers of the sequences or be in FASTA format. 
There are features to specify the database used for the comparison and the taxonomic 
organism of interest. In addition, the tool provides advanced settings to specify the 
maximum number of aligned sequences to display, e-value threshold and scoring 
parameters.  
The output result displays a summary of the best hits, graphical representations of the 
alignments and detailed alignment for each hit. The results also include: e-value, identity 
score, query coverage, alignment length, gaps and bit score.  
In our analysis, we used BLAST to explore how similar are the different Borrelia species 
with each other in a protein level.

2 Objectives 
2.1 Literature review 
Recent studies on immune response against Borrelia species, particularly in the context 
of MHC presentation have significant suggestions in understanding the pathogen-host 
interactions. However, there is still need  for further study to identify Borrelia proteins 
that are important for the pathogen’s survival in the host and reveal novel features that 
impact the interactions with the host. Studies have facilitated T-cell epitopes derived 
from Borrelia proteins, such as OspA and OspC, indicating that hydrophobic residues in 
peptides such as cysteine, leucine and valine on the surface of the proteins tend to have 
antigenic characteristics [29],[23]. B-cell epitope prediction methods, suggest that 
continuous epitopes are more likely to be identified by antibodies. However, continuous 
epitopes can form folding that increases the complexity of epitope prediction. A study has 
demonstrated that certain HLA-II alleles, such as HLA-DRB1*0401, are associated with 
increased susceptibility to chronic Lyme disease and bind to a peptide from the OspA 
protein of B. burgdorferi [30]. Additionally, research into molecular mimicry has revealed 
how Borrelia peptides may cross-react with human tissue antigens. Specifically, T-cell 
epitopes from extracellular matrix proteins in patients with postinfectious Lyme arthritis, 
are identified as potential B. burgdorferi–mimic epitopes [31]. Moreover, a study 
investigating the complications of Lyme disease in patients with arthritis or carditis  
shows that OspA proteins of B. burgdorferi, may share common epitopes with humans 
which mimic self-proteins [32]. 
These findings present the complexity of Borrelia-derived peptides, MHC presentation, 
and the immune response to the pathogen, highlighting the significance for further study. 
2.2 Study objectives 
This study focuses on Borrelia bacteria, the causative agents of Lyme disease, 
investigating the genomic diversity of the species and its influence on disease 
susceptibility within populations.  
The primary objective of the study is to distinguish the genetic diversity among different 
Borrelia species, enabling their classification based on their pathogenicity in causing 
Lyme disease.  
Specifically, the study will explore how the variations in bacterial proteins differentiate 
the species, utilizing protein databases to extract protein information from Borrelia 
species and employing comparative techniques to detect similarities and differences 
between the species.  
Additionally, the study will investigate how the differences in the proteins and peptides  
influence the MHC-peptide binding and distribution between the pathogenic and non-
pathogenic species, understanding the immune response of the host.  
Finally, this study aims to identify candidate proteins that are involved in the disease 
pathogenesis that may potentially elicit an autoimmune response by mimicking human 
proteins.   
The findings of this study aim to enhance the understanding of the mechanisms 
underlying the pathogen and the dynamics between the host and the bacteria, 
contributing to future research.

3 Methods 
3.1 Literature review 
A comprehensive literature review was conducted regarding Lyme disease and its 
causative agents affecting different populations worldwide. The review was performed 
using PubMed database to identify pathogenic Borrelia species in humans. Advanced 
search terms and queries related to the disease included “Lyme disease”, “Lyme 
Borreliosis”, “Immune response to Lyme disease”, “pathogenicity of Borrelia species”, 
“causative agents of Lyme disease”, “diagnosis and treatment Borrelia species”, 
“evolutionary Borrelia species” and “virulence of Borreliosis”. The search encompassed 
articles, reviews, clinical studies with no restrictions on the publication date but 
prioritizing recent studies. 
3.2 Protein dataset collection     
Proteins from B. burgdorferi associated with disease pathogenesis were collected from 
UniProtKB database. The search and selection of these proteins were guided by the 
literature review on the types of proteins involved in disease virulence. To refine and 
specify the search, an advanced search was conducted utilizing the following queries:  
• 
(organism_id:139) AND (cc_scl_term: SL-0040): 9 reviewed 
• 
(taxonomy_id:139) AND (cc_function: lyme disease): 12 reviewed + 37   
        unreviewed 
The “cc_scl_term” corresponds to the subcellular location term where the “cell outer 
membrane” (SL-0040) was used. From the output of the first query, only the 9 reviewed 
proteins were selected. The unreviewed results were excluded from this analysis due to 
insufficient information on the pathogenicity of  the proteins. From the second query, 
both reviewed and unreviewed proteins were included as they met our selection criteria 
related to disease virulence. We ensured non-redundancy of proteins by eliminating 
duplicates from the combined results of the two query searches, which included 
removing one duplicate protein entry. The final selection included both Swiss-Prot 
reviewed and unreviewed proteins, which were downloaded in FASTA format for 
further analysis. The integration of the two searches provided a total of 57 proteins from 
B. burgdorferi.     
3.3 Identification of homologous sequences   
3.3.1 Protein sequences search  
The BLAST tool was used, with a focus on BLASTp. The analysis was performed on an 
online Python Notebook from Google Colab, using the BioPython package. FASTA files 
containing 57 protein sequences from B. burgdorferi were used as input for the analysis. 
For each protein, BLASTp searches were conducted against non-redundant protein 
databases, specifying B. garinii, B. afzelii (both pathogenic), and B. valaisiana (non-
pathogenic) as the organisms of interest. BLASTp output provided a list of proteins for 
each species matched to each input protein of B. burgdorferi. Specifically, the searches 
resulted in 1,587 homologous protein sequences from B. afzelii, 3,078 from B. garinii, and 
1,767 from B. valaisiana. After filtering the homologous sequences to include only the best 
hits corresponding to each of the B. burgdorferi input proteins, the final output was 57 
protein sequences per species.

3.3.2 Evaluation of protein search results  
The BLASTp results were filtered to avoid redundancy by comparing accession numbers 
and protein sequences. Duplicate sequences from the same species were identified and 
removed, retaining only the match with the higher E-value (E-value ≤ 0.01). The selection 
of the best hits for each protein involved choosing the most statistically significant match 
per species, based on E-value scores and alignment length. An independently created 
alignment length threshold of ≥100 amino acids was applied. For each species, 57 protein 
sequences were selected to match the 57 query protein sequences from B. burgdorferi. 
The dataset included the query protein sequences, similarity and coverage percentages, 
E-values, and the lengths of the matched proteins. 
3.4 Multiple sequence alignment  
The Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) was performed by utilizing ggmsa package. We 
used the 57 protein sequences from the BLASTp results, grouped by protein for each 
species, a gap penalty was set equal to -2.

3.6 Output data processing  
The output dataset of the MHC-binding predicted peptides was filtered to include only 
unique peptides and those characterized as strong binders, based on the predefined rank 
score threshold ≤0.01. We then merged this dataset with the full sequences of the 
corresponding protein sequences, including the starting and ending positions of each 
peptide within each protein sequence. This integration was performed for the following 
exploration of the peptide distribution across proteins and species. 
3.7 Visualization of peptide distribution across species  
3.7.1 Data preparation and processing  
For the visualization analysis, we used the dataset that included the predicted MHC-
binding strong peptides from the NetMHCIIpan-4.3, along with their corresponding 
protein sequences, the length of each protein, and the starting and ending position of each 
peptide within the protein sequence. Missing values were filtered out from the dataset. 
 
To visualize the presence of peptides within the protein sequence a protein vector was 
created and initialized with zeros, where each position corresponded to the position 
across the sequence length. This vector was filled based on the starting and ending 
position of each peptide in the corresponding protein sequence, by adding a +1 in the 
positions where the peptides were located. The protein vector was used to visualize the 
occurrence of peptides within each protein sequence.   
3.7.2 Data visualization using BepiPred-3.0 predictions  
For the prediction of potential B-cell epitopes we used BepiPred-3.0 server. For this 
analysis only the most prevalent pathogenic (B. burgdorferi) and non-pathogenic  
(B. valaisiana) species were used. Protein sequences from B. burgdorferi and B. valaisiana 
were used as input in FASTA format.

4 Results  
4.1 Borrelia proteins  
Through the literature review, we identified the most common Borrelia species 
responsible for causing Lyme disease, as well as one species lacking pathogenicity. The 
main causative agent in Lyme disease and most prevalent species in North America and 
Europe, is B. burgdorferi and was chosen for our analysis.  The species selection included 
two additional pathogenic species B. afzelii and B. garinii, which are both pathogenic but 
with distinct genotypes and geographic distribution compared to B. burgdorferi, 
affecting different systems of the host. The non-pathogenic species included in the 
analysis was B. valaisiana, which has been detected in ticks but there is no evidence in 
causing Lyme disease and its pathogenicity remains uncertain. 
 
A protein search in UniProtKB, a database of protein sequences and functional 
information, was conducted to identify potential proteins from B. burgdorferi involved 
in the disease pathogenesis, aiming to gain more insights about the molecular 
mechanisms underlying the infection. Based on the literature review, we prioritized 
proteins which are located on the outer membrane, as they are more likely to be involved 
in the disease. These proteins are directly involved in interactions with the host immune 
system and are critical for the bacteria's ability to invade host tissues, evade immune 
responses, and establish infection [24]. The protein search revealed 57 protein 
sequences in total from B. burgdorferi, 21 being reviewed and 36 being unreviewed 
based on our two query searches.  
4.2 Homologous protein sequences  
We aimed to collect the homologous proteins from the other Borrelia species for 
comparative analysis. The generated results from BLASTp offered a broader perspective 
on the protein landscape associated with Lyme disease across different Borrelia species. 
The data included information for the query and matched sequences in the database, 
alignment scores, E-values and identity and coverage percentages of the matched 
protein sequences. To ensure the accuracy of the findings and minimize redundancy 
from the same species, we filtered out duplicates based on the accession numbers of each 
protein match, which serve as significant identifiers. 
 
Moreover, the best hit for each protein per species was selected based on the E-value 
and alignment length of the matched protein sequence. The hits with E-values ≤0.01 
were selected as they indicate a match that is less likely to occur by chance. The 
alignment length represents the extent of the coverage between the query sequence and 
the matched sequence in the database. Choosing longer alignment length helped avoid 
selecting short sequences with high identity percentage by chance. Using these measures 
for evaluation of the matches we aimed for homologous sequences with high statistical 
confidence and more biologically meaningful similarity. From this process, we retrieved 
57 proteins from each species corresponding to the proteins identified in B. burgdorferi.

4.5 Peptide distribution  
MHC-II molecules present peptides derived from extracellular proteins of the pathogens 
to CD4⁺ T-cells.  In our analysis, we conducted predictions for the HLA-DP, HLA-DQ and 
HLA-DR molecules. We generated a column plot to visualize the abundance of the MHC-
binding predicted peptides across the proteins for each species. These visualizations 
aimed to identify pottential patterns in peptide distribution that could distiguish the 
species.  
 
An important parameter in MHC-binding peptide predictions is the binding affinity, 
which refers to the strength of interaction between the peptide antigen and the MHC-II 
molecules. The predicted peptides were classified based on their rank scores: peptides 
with a rank score of less than or equal to 0.05 were classified as weak binders, while 
those with a rank score of less than or equal to 0.01 were classified as strong binders. 
 
As presented in Figure 4.4., 30 proteins out of the total of 57 proteins analyzed, did not 
contain any predicted peptides. Among the remaining proteins, 2 included predicted 
peptides across all species, while 2 proteins included peptides only for B. valaisiana. 
Additionally, 7 proteins contained predicted peptides shared between B. burgdorferi and 
B. valaisiana. Furthermore, 9 proteins included predicted peptides for B. burgdorferi but 
not for B. valaisiana, while 8 proteins included for B. valaisiana but not for B. burgdorferi. 
There were 8 proteins which contained predicted peptides only for B. burgdorferi and B. 
garinii. The highest number of predicted peptides was observed in two proteins, each 
predicted to bind 45 peptides for B. valaisiana and B. burgdorferi in conjunction with B. 
garinii, respectively.

4.6 Homologous sequences in human proteins  
The peptides used to investigate homologous sequences in human proteins, were those 
with high likelihood of binding to specific MHC-II molecules based on their binding 
affinity. Identifying homologous sequences in humans using peptides derived from 
proteins of B. burgdorferi and B. valaisiana would be useful in understanding the 
relationship between Borrelia species and humans. The interactions between the 
pathogen and the host could reveal how pathogens adapt to their hosts and regions of 
similarity.  
 
Our investigation identified two significant matches between MHC-binding predicted 
peptides of B. burgdorferi to the human protein Ankyrin-1. These peptides derived from 
the MlpB lipoprotein and were found to align with both the canonical sequence and 13 
isoforms of Ankyrin-1. On the contrary, peptides from proteins of B. valaisiana did not 
present a significant match to human proteins.  
 
Ankyrin-1 has a significant role for linking cytoskeleton (protein complex) to the plasma 
membrane in erythrocytes, which is essential for maintaining cell stability and flexibility. 
Ankyrin-1 is expressed in both RNA and protein forms, ensuring proper function of 
various systems such as the cardiovascular, nervous, and immune system.

5 Discussion 
 
This study investigated the diversity among proteins from different Borrelia species, 
focusing on how these differences impact MHC presentation and immune response to 
Lyme disease. Additionally, we examined candidate proteins that might be involved in 
autoimmune responses. 
 
The comparison of the homologous protein sequences across species, revealed 
conserved regions at the beginning of the protein sequence, representing the signal 
peptides. These peptides are significant for the cellular function and intercellular 
communication, potentially influencing how Borrelia establishes infection. Signal 
peptides are often used by pathogens for localization, exporting virulence factors and 
surviving within the host [32]. Further conserved regions in the sequences across the 
species may indicate regions with essential functional domains for the protein activity, 
such as structural stability or protein-protein interactions. These conserved regions, 
also contained epitopes that could elicit an immune response, confirming the broad 
spectrum of the immune system to recognize and respond to different Borrelia species.   
 
The presence of gaps in various regions within the sequences could suggest areas that 
differentiate the proteins, representing regions with distinct functions between the 
pathogenic and non-pathogenic species. 
 
The outer membrane proteins used in this study have a significant role between the 
interaction of the host and the pathogen, being involved in the bacteria virulence and 
evasion of the host immune system [33] . The high frequency of hydrophobic amino acids 
observed within these sequences, provides the proteins with structural stability 
necessary to cause infections and be resilient to the host’s defence mechanisms.  
 
There were homologous sequences with high identity scores to B. burgdorferi proteins, 
likely, due to an evolutionary relationship and conserved functional regions across the 
species, as observed in the MSA. This conservation probably reflects a strong selective 
pressure to maintain specific regions in the sequence, which are possibly critical for the 
survival and the pathogenicity of the bacteria [34]. Specifically, high identity scores were 
observed in proteins such as OspA and OspC which are major surface antigens and 
targets of the host immune response [24]. The proteins interact with both the vector and 
the host adhering and surviving within the tick and then effectively transmitting to the 
host.  
 
However, some protein sequences exhibited low identity scores that point out the 
differences between the species that are likely due to their geographic distribution and 
adaptation mechanisms [18],[21]. As different Borrelia species are found in distinct 
geographical regions, they may develop adaptations to the local vectors and hosts. These 
adaptations could lead to variations in the protein sequence and surface exposed 
peptides which eventually will influence the pathogen detection from the immune 
system of the host.

The MHC-binding peptide predictions for certain proteins revealed no predicted 
peptides. This might be due to the absence of specific epitopes that can be recognized 
and bind to MHC molecules, preventing their presentation to T-cells and the pathogen 
destruction. These proteins evade the immune detection of the host, and they have high 
survival rates and persistence within the host. Borrelia species often use mechanisms to 
change the surface antigens, avoiding their recognition by TCRs [5] [35]. These antigenic 
variations are a major factor in the development of chronic Lyme disease causing 
symptoms such as arthritis [36], as the pathogen can continuously change its antigenic 
profile, leading to ongoing inflammation, escaping the immune system. 
 
In contrast, some proteins presented high density of MHC-binding peptides, which can 
stimulate the immune response through MHC-peptide binding and presentation to  
T-cells. These proteins are likely to be involved in significant interactions between the 
pathogen and the host as they are surface exposed proteins [37]. 
  
The variability observed between proteins that present MHC-binding peptides and those 
that lack peptides, reflects the genetic diversity across Borrelia species which present 
sequence variations of the same proteins. Examples such as OspA or Mlp proteins 
demonstrate variations in their sequences, likely due to mutations, evolutionary 
mechanisms and adaptation.  These variations can result in some proteins presenting 
peptides that are recognized by the immune system, while others do not, influencing the 
pathogen's ability to evade immune detection and persistence in causing an infection. 
 
Our further analysis focused on B. burgdorferi and B. valaisiana to detect possible 
patterns contributing to the pathogenicity of each species. Proteins that exhibit MHC-
binding predicted peptides for one of the species, may represent differences in the 
antigenic profiles between the species. The adaptation mechanisms or the different 
interactions of the species with the host might influence the presence and absence of 
epitopes recognized by the immune system. The differences in the protein seqeunces 
between the two species could be due to mutation events, or possible evolutionary 
mechanisms.  
 
In both species there were proteins presenting MHC-binding predicted peptides that can 
induce both humoral and cellular response. For B. burgdorferi, recognizing and 
processing these epitopes, enables the immune system to mount a robust defence, 
enhancing the overall immune response against the pathogen. However, proteins from 
B. valaisiana that induce an immune response, can potentially have protective effects to 
the host. This non-pathogenic species possibly shares antigenic or structural similarities 
with the pathogenic species stimulating the immune system without causing an 
infection. These proteins might be promising candidates for treatment methods such as 
vaccine develpoment against Lyme disease, stimulating the production of antibodies and 
activating T-cells that will recognize and target the pathogenic antigens. 
 
The peptide distribution across the protein sequences of B. burgdorferi and B. valaisiana 
revealed no distinct patterns that would differentiate the pathogenicity of the two 
species, indicating that their localization is possibly influenced by different factors that 
are not species-specific. The different geographic distributions and interactions with the

host might influence their protein sequences and presenting peptides, making the 
prediction of MHC-binding peptides distribution more challenging.  
 
Our further analysis included the identification of homologous sequences in human 
proteins, investigating candidate proteins that would induce an autoimmune response. 
Autoimmune diseases, such as Lyme arthritis (LA) caused by a persistent infection from 
B. burgdorferi, are strongly associated with specific HLA-DR alleles. However, the 
identity of the HLA-DR-presented peptides and their association with autoimmune 
diseases, remain unclear [38]. A recent study has shown the development of 
autoreactive CD4+ T-cells and autoantibodies to extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins in 
post-infectious LA patients, suggesting potential B. burgdorferi-mimic peptides or 
epitope spreading which is common in chronic inflammatory response  [38].  Unlike the 
traditional molecular mimicry, where the pathogen mimics the host proteins, the study 
suggests epitope spreading.  where the immune system reacts against the antigens from 
the pathogens and additional self-antigens that are structurally similar but not identical 
to the initial pathogen antigens [39]. Another study investigated the presence of cross-
reactive antibodies in the serum of patients with Lyme neurologic disease. The findings 
revealed that these patients’ sera contain antibodies that bind to human antigens on the 
axons of neurons and cross-react with B. burgdorferi [40]. This cross-reactivity could 
contribute to the different neurological symptoms observed in patients with Lyme 
disease.  
 
In our study we identified two epitope matches between B. burgdorferi MlpB lipoprotein 
and the human protein Ankyrin-1, with one epitope meeting the predicted binding 
preferences at the anchor positions for the MHC-II molecule. Ankyrin-1 is involved in 
different physiological systems including cardiovascular, nervous and immune system 
[41]. The sequence similarity between Ankyrin-1 and MlpB  lipoprotein of B. burgdorferi 
suggests a possible mechanism of molecular mimicry, where the MlpB lipoprotein 
resembles human proteins closely enough to trigger an autoimmune response. If the 
immune system mistakenly targets Ankyrin-1, aiming to the pathogen, it could lead to 
significant autoimmune responses. Such responses could potentially disrupt critical 
physiological processes that Ankyrin-1 is involved in, leading to conditions such as 
chronic inflammation, nervous system disorders, or cardiovascular complications.  
 
While our study investigates the immune response to different Borrelia species, several 
limitations should be considered. Firstly, the sample size of the protein sequences could 
influence the statistical power of our findings and possibly affect the reliability of our 
conclusions. A broader protein sample across the species, could encompass sequences 
with diverse variations, functions, and interactions with the host. The limited diversity 
among the initial protein sequences retrieved from UniProtKB, was influenced by the 
restricted available annotation for the proteins involved in Lyme disease. Expanding the 
sample size and incorporating a greater diversity of proteins could possibly reveal a 
larger array of MHC-binding peptides, that would differentiate the species based on their 
pathogenicity.  
 
Additionally, our study does not include the environmental factors or geographical 
distributions of the species. The interactions between the host and the Borrelia pathogen

evading the host’s immune system and influence its pathogenicity. 
 
Moreover, our findings on the potential molecular mimicry of B. burgdorferi provide 
theoretical insights that need experimental validation to fully understand the cross-
reactivity and the complexity of host-pathogen interactions involved in autoimmune 
responses. 
 
Our study relies on computational predictions which need to be integrated with 
experimental data to confirm our results and enhance the understanding of  the immune 
response mechanisms against Borrelia.

6 Conclusion 
Our study identified conserved regions in the homologous sequences across the different 
Borrelia species, which presented epitopes capable of inducing an immune response. 
MHC-binding predicted peptides in B. burgdorferi and B. valaisiana species could induce 
both humoral and cellular responses, enhancing the overall immune response.  
In B. burgdorferi, these peptides enhance the defence mechanisms against the pathogen, 
while in B. valaisiana, they may offer protective mechanisms and opportunities for 
possible vaccine development. The high  frequency of hydrophobic amino acids in the 
outer membrane proteins contributes to the structural stability enabling them to cause 
infections and avoid T-cell detection. The high identity scores observed in the 
homologous sequences to B. burgdorferi suggest an evolutionary relationship and a 
possible presence of conserved functional regions across the species. However, the low 
identity scores and gaps in the sequence alignments, indicate differences between 
species, which may be attributed to their distinct geographical distributions. Borrelia 
pathogens employ mechanisms such as antigenic variations to alter the surface exposed 
peptides avoiding immune detection by the MHC-II molecules in order to survive in the 
host’s environment.  Additionally, the identification of homologous sequences in human 
proteins revealed a peptide match between MlpB lipoprotein of B. burgdorferi and 
Ankyrin-1. This match suggests a potential molecular mimicry, possibly leading to an 
autoimmune response. 
Future research should aim to expand the protein sample size and diversity to gain a 
more comprehensive understanding of Borrelia mechanisms. This includes 
incorporating experimental data for the exploration of the autoimmunity mechanisms 
and developing potential therapeutic and disease preventing applications.
