Abstract
The carbon footprint of GPC is significantly lower than cement-based concrete and could
therefore pose as a part of the solution to lowering the CO2 in the building sector. With this
motivation, the report examined the material properties of steel fibre-reinforced geopoly-
mer concrete (SFRGPC). The objective was to investigate the effect of fibre content on
stiffness, compressive strength, tensile strength and post-cracking behaviour in geopoly-
mer concrete (GPC) and compare it to ordinary cement-based concrete. This effect was
studied through experiments and numerical analysis. Wedge splitting test (WST) cubes
samples and standard cylinders with GPC containing clay, slag and an alkaline activator
based on sodium silicate water glass and sodium hydroxide. Four GPC mixes were cast,
containing fibre-to-concrete volume fractions of 0.00%, 0.20%, 0.45% and 0.60%. Sam-
ples with reference (REF) CEM II cement concrete were also cast with a volume fraction
of 0.45%. Cylinders were used to test compressive strength, tensile strength and Young’s
modulus. The cubes were used in the WST to test tensile strength, and Young’s modulus
and to determine softening curves after 28 days. In the analysis of the experimental data,
constitutive relations for all the concrete mixes were developed and verified in a finite
element model of the WST.
The experimental results showed that similar material parameters could be achieved for
the GPC and REF cement concrete after 28 days. The workability of the GPC was sub-
stantially worse for the GPC, with more restricted flow and shorter setting time, thought
to be due to the high clay content of the GPC and the high alkalinity respectively. Similar
compressive strengths were obtained for all concrete mixes, ranging from 44 MPa to 55
MPa, with the strength of the 0.45% and 0.60% mixes being between 50 MPa and 51
MPa. The Young’s modulus across all GPC mixes was found to be in the range of 33-34
GPa using the cylinder tests. This was 14% to 18% lower than the reference concrete.
The same tendency was found using the WST, but with generally lower values of Young’s
modulus at around 24 GPa for GPC and 29 GPa for the REF concrete. The difference be-
tween the tests was due to the cylinders test being a compressive test and the WST being
a tensile test. The tensile strength of the GPC was generally found to increase, with in-
creasing SF contents. For the GPC without SF, the tensile strength obtained by the WST
and the cylinder test was the same. While the tensile strength of the SF-reinforced sam-
ples was larger using the cylinder test compared to the WST. This was attributed to the
Brazillian split test (BST) and the WST being developed for non-fibre-reinforced concrete.
The post-cracking parameters obtained through the WST showed that increasing amounts
of SF yielded a higher residual strength. This is explained by the fact that residual strength
is determined by the fibre reinforcement’s ability to create a stress bridge over the cracked
concrete. It was determined that the GPC utilised the SF-reinforcement to a higher degree
than the REF concrete, this was exemplified by a higher residual strength for the GPC
0.45% mix compared to the REF 0.45% mix. The general constitutive relations made for
the SF-reinforced concrete mixes showed varied success, when used in a finite element
model of the experimental WST, comparing the force-deformation curve of the simulated
and experimental data. The constitutive relations showed the best results for the GPC
0.45%, and 0.60% SF-reinforced mixes. Generally, the fit of the experimental curve was
lacking in the later plastic regime of the force-deformation curve. This was partially due
to the limitations of the general analytically based constitutive relation, that was used.

1
Introduction
The building sector is responsible for a large share of global greenhouse emissions, there-
fore a reduction of the emissions within this sector is necessary. The production of Port-
land cement is alone the source of up to 8% of global CO2 emissions [1, 2, 3], where 0.6
tons of CO2 are emitted per ton of Portland cement produced. Therefore, an obvious strat-
egy for reducing emissions within the building sector is to replace cement-based concrete
with alternatives with a lower carbon footprint. Geopolymer concrete (GPC) may be an al-
ternative to cement-based concrete where cement is replaced by alternative binders. By
replacing cement with calcined clay and slag, the CO2 emissions can be reduced by more
than 50% [3]. Since clay is readily available, and slag is a by-product of steel production
they are also viable materials in the future. However, further research and experience
are required for GPC to be accepted in the building codes and to be used widely in the
building industry. Similarly to ordinary Portland cement (OPC) concrete, GPC exhibits
brittle behaviour. To mitigate this, steel fibres can be added to reinforce the concrete [4].
There is however still limited experience with steel fibre-reinforced geopolymer concrete
(SFRGPC) although it is known that GPC benefits more from fibre reinforcement than
OPC concrete [5]. The general objective of this project is to determine the effects of steel
fibre reinforcement of GPC by experimental work and using this knowledge to develop a
constitutive relation to be used in a Finite Element Model (FEM). This gap must be closed,
for GPC to be a viable alternative to Portland cement-based concrete.
1.1
Scope of the project
The overall scope of the work in this project is to investigate the effect of the steel fibre re-
inforcement on the mechanical properties of GPC. This is done both through experimental
work and numerical analysis.
The experimental work includes testing of two types of concrete samples: 100mm x
200mm cylinders, and 100mm x 100mm x 100mm wedge splitting cubes. Both types
of samples were cast with a combination of clay, slag and alkaline activator as the binder.
Reference concrete samples were also cast with a reference (REF) cement as the binder.
Test samples were cast with varying contents of steel fibre (SF) reinforcement to investi-
gate the effects of different degrees of reinforcement. The following volume fractions, of
SF to concrete, were investigated: 0.00%, 0.20%, 0.45%, and 0.60%. Reference sam-
ples with REF concrete contained a volume fraction of 0.45% SF. Experimental testing of
the cylinder samples included compressive strength, Brazilian split, and E-moduli tests.
Wedge splitting testing was performed on the cube samples. The raw materials used
for the concrete casting were sand (0-1 mm fraction), limestone aggregate (2-8 mm and
8-16 mm fractions), water, super plasticiser, and steel fibres. The GPC was cast with
calcined clay and slag and with an alkaline activator composed of sodium silicate and
sodium hydroxide with a 1:1 M ratio of SiO2 to Na2O. For the REF concrete, Portland
cement containing fly ash was used.
The experimental data served as input for numerical analysis, where inverse analysis
was performed on the data from the wedge splitting test to derive mechanical and frac-
ture properties and a constitutive relation. A finite element model of the wedge splitting
experiment was made and used to verify the derived constitutive relation. Various other
constitutive relations were also devised and compared using the FE model, one being the

The main research questions to be investigated in this project are the following:

Theoretical Background and Literature
Review
This chapter is a review of the theory and literature relevant to the project. Especially
regarding geopolymer concrete, fibre-reinforced concrete, and finite element modelling of
fibre-reinforced concrete.
2.1
Geopolymer concrete
Geopolymer concrete (GPC) is a material that, unlike conventional Portland cement con-
crete, does not contain cement.
Geopolymer concrete is an alkali-activated material
(AAM) where alkali-activated concrete (AAC) is characterized by the four following con-
stituents: (1) A precursor, (2) an alkaline activator, (3) aggregates and (4) water. The
precursor consists of one or several combined mineral products that contain aluminosil-
icates and/or calcium silicates [7, p. 7]. It may be in the form of a solution or as a solid
[3]. The alkaline activator is necessary for the precursor to dissolve and for the polymer-
ization to happen. The choice of alkaline activator depends on the chosen precursor and
types of alkaline activators include alkali silicates, hydroxides, sulfates or carbonates [7,
p. 164]. In the context of research, the term geopolymer often refers to AAMs where the
binding phase consists almost entirely of aluminosilicates [7, p. 7] or generally has a low
calcium content [8, p. 3]. However, it must be noted that the term geopolymer is often
used universally to cover AAMs in general, which especially is the case in commercial or
industrial contexts and may lead to confusion [7, p. 7]. In this report, however, the term
geopolymer and AAM will be used interchangeably. This also carries over to more spe-
cific categories of materials where alkali-activated concretes and geopolymer concretes
will be used interchangeably.
Due to the variability in the composition of GPC, the mechanical properties may vary in a
wide range. However, generally, GPC has similar tensile properties to ordinary Portland
cement (OPC) concrete meaning that it suffers from a low tensile strength and is prone to
cracking. Therefore it is, for most purposes, necessary to reinforce the material to prevent
cracking, and to improve strength and durability [4].
2.2
Steel fibre-reinforced concrete
Steel fibre-reinforced (SFR) concrete is a composite material where steel fibres (SF) are
introduced into the mixing of the concrete. Steel fibres are commonly used in concrete for
applications such as tunnel elements, pavements and in industrial applications [4]. The
desired concentration of steel fibres in the concrete mix depends on the use-case and
is measured in kg fibres per m3 concrete or a volume fraction Vf of fibres to concrete.
Low concentrations of ∼1 kg/m3 (∼0.01%) concrete are used to mitigate shrinkage, and
light to moderate fibre concentrations of 25-75 kg/m3 (0.3% < Vf < 1%) is suitable for
increasing strength and ductility. Concrete with high concentrations of fibres >100 kg/m3
(1.3% > Vf) is known as ultra high-performance fibre-reinforced concrete (UHPFRC) are
designed for special purposes and may exhibit compressive strengths in order of magni-
tude 150 MPa [9].
Steel fibre reinforcement works similarly to conventional rebar reinforcement, i.e. by carry-
ing the tensile forces that the concrete is not able to. SFR has several benefits compared
to conventional reinforcement, one being that it enables faster casting. Another is that it

provides good impact and explosive spalling resistance, making it suitable for tunnel lin-
ings. One of the properties of SFR, which is studied in this project, is the residual strength
enabled by the fibres in the cracked state. This is due to the crack-bridging ability of
the fibres, meaning that the fibres are able to bridge a crack and transfer the stresses
when a crack is formed in the concrete. The stresses will be transferred by bonding shear
stresses along the fibre surface and at the fibre anchorage – depending on the geometry
of the fibre. The material fails when fibres begin to slip and are pulled out of the concrete
or the fibres rupture, the latter not typically being the case [9].

The residual strength of steel fibre-reinforced concrete will be different depending on the
degree of reinforcement, the distribution of fibres, and the bonding characteristics of fi-
bres with the concrete matrix [4]. On Figure 2.2.1 a comparison of the different types
of behaviour that may be exhibited in the cracked state of fibre-reinforced concrete is
illustrated. The main cases are strain-hardening and strain-softening where the former
material behaviour is characterised by increasing stresses for higher strains and the latter
exhibits lower stresses. Lightly fibre-reinforced concrete will to a higher degree show a
strain-softening behaviour, whereas strain-hardening behaviour will be seen for moderate
and higher reinforcement degrees. However, variability within the same material will also
occur since it depends on the randomness that lies in the distribution of the fibres [9].
2.2.1
Fibre distribution and orientation
The distribution and orientation of fibres affect the performance of SFRC in the cracked
state and is therefore important to address. Fibre distribution is a matter of ensuring a
proper mix of the components whereas fibre orientation is influenced by several factors,
including the formwork geometry, fresh concrete viscosity and vibration procedure [10].
The fibre orientation is important since the fibres only contribute to the residual strength
if they can bridge the cracks formed in the concrete, i.e. the fibres should not be parallel
with the cracks planes. The fibre orientation factor η ∈[0, 1] quantifies the efficiency of
the fibre orientation and is defined as [11]:
η = Nf Af

employed to determine the fibre orientation factor empirically, such as counting the fibres

2.2.2
Geopolymer concrete and ordinary Portland cement concrete
SFR ordinary Portland concrete is commonly used today and has been developed since
the 1950s, which results in decades of knowledge and experience within this field [9].
The same is not the case for steel fibre-reinforced geopolymer concrete (SFRGPC) where
there is limited experience, however, it has been observed that GPC benefits more from
SFR than OPC concrete. A study by Wetzel [5] has shown that the bond strength between
the concrete matrix and the steel fibres is greater for GPC than for OPC concrete, resulting
in a better anchorage and higher pull-out strength of the fibres. This is due to the high
alkalinity of the geopolymer dissolving the surface of the steel fibres and resulting in a
better cohesion of the fibres with the matrix.
2.2.3
Strength and workability of steel fibre-reinforced concrete
The introduction of SF to a concrete mix reduces the workability of the composite, which
can result in excessive void formation and poor compaction. The loss in workability is visu-
alised on Figure 2.2.2c, showing data from Ranjbar et al. [4] where the relative flowability
of the fibre-reinforced concrete as a function of fibre content. Conversely, the addition of
SF can increase the concrete strength. This can be seen on Figure 2.2.2a and 2.2.2b,
where the compressive strength and especially the flexural strength increases with in-
creasing SF contents. Here the increase in the flexural strength is a good indication of
the increase in tensile strength, that will be seen when increasing the SF content. A
balance between the workability of the fresh concrete and the material properties of the
hardened concrete must be found, via the choice of SF content. Bearing in mind that low
workability can have a negative influence on the material properties of the hardened con-
crete, due to higher porosity and poor compaction of the concrete with high SF contents
[4].
An optimal range for fibre content in geopolymer and cement composites has been sug-
gested to be within 0.2 vol.-% to 2.0 vol.-%. Anything larger than this interval can result in
uneven dispersion and balling of fibres in the concrete, where the matrix is not able to pass
through. Anything smaller than the suggested interval, would not contribute meaningfully
to the material properties of the hardened concrete [4].

2.3.2
Finite element modelling
An approach used in FE models of SFR concrete elements is assuming that the steel
fibres are sufficiently distributed in the concrete matrix, such that the resulting composite
material can be treated as isotropic. This has been done in research previously for SFR
Portland cement-based concrete in Abbas et al. 2010 [14, 15]. For this approach a simple
constitutive relation for the assumed isotropic material is needed.
Existing research has used the Lok & Xiao [6] constitutive relation for FE analysis of SFR
concrete where Abbas et al. 2016 [16] is one such case. Abbas et al. 2016 have analysed
FE models for a number of SFR concrete beams and the results from the FE model, using
the Lok & Xiao constitutive model, were then compared with existing experimental data for
the same elements in the same load condition. Abbas et al. 2016 had success in verifying
the FEM data with the experimental data. SFRGPC was not analysed in the research.
Another approach to modelling SFR concrete in a FEM program is to model each SF
individually, using the same matrix-to-reinforcement interactions for rebar reinforcement
in FE modelling. This method requires a scheme for distributing the SF randomly in the
concrete specimen as seen in [17, 18]. This approach with lead to different results for
each random distribution of the SF, meaning that multiple analyses are needed for each
material. Furthermore, the modelling of every single fibre leads to complexity and longer
run times of the FE model.

3.1.1
Slag
Ground granulated blast furnace slag (GGBFS), referred to as slag in this report, is a by-
product of steel production and a low-CO2 alternative to cement, making it it is a widely
used supplementary cementitious material. It improves the mechanical and durability
properties of concrete compared to pure OPC concrete. GGBFS is a fine glassy granular
material with rough microscopic edges. The main components of GGBFS are calcium
oxide (CaO) (30-50%), silicon dioxide (SiO2) (28-38%), aluminium oxide (Al2O3) (8-24%),
and magnesium oxide (MgO) (1-18%) [19, 20]. The slag used in this project was provided
by the German company Max Bögl, has a density of 2660 kg/m3, determined from testing
at DTU. Table 3.1.1 shows its chemical composition, and on Figure 3.1.1 the white powder
is a sample of the slag.
3.1.2
Calcined clay
Calcined clay can be used as a supplementary cementitious material. It is abundant in the
Earth’s crust and has relatively low production costs relative to alternative supplementary
cementitious materials. Calcined clay is obtained by heating clay at temperatures in the
range of 500–800 ◦C [21]. It is primarily composed of silicon dioxide (SiO2) with large
amounts of aluminium oxide (Al2O3) and calcium oxide (CaO) as seen in Table 3.1.1 with
data from XRF analysis. The analysis has shown a 9.4% LOI (Loss On Ignition) which
could be due to calcined clay being burnt at a lower temperature than slag and cement,
thereby containing impurities that are burnt off at higher temperatures. Additionally, it

3.1.4
Steel fibres (SF)
Steel fibres are used as concrete reinforcement in structural elements such as tunnels,
floors and precast elements. The substitution of traditional reinforcement with SF rein-
forcement in applications such as concrete tunnels, has been shown to significantly re-
duce CO2 emissions [26]. Additionally, the fibres improve the ductility of the concrete.
Many different types of steel fibres exist for different purposes of reinforcement in con-
crete [27]. The steel fibres used in this project were provided by the company Bekaert and
have the brand name Dramix 4D 80/60BGP. The fibres are uncoated and have a length of
61 mm and a diameter of 0.75 mm. They are mechanically hardened, resulting in a tensile
strength of 2200 MPa. The Young’s modulus of the steel fibres is 210 GPa. To increase
anchorage strength, the fibres have multi-hooked ends as seen on Figure 3.1.3. The fi-
bres are glued together in bundles of approximately 30 to 40 fibres to avoid fibre balling
during mixing and to ensure a homogeneous distribution of the fibres in the concrete. The
supplier’s instructions on how the fibres should be added to the concrete mix are is to add
it together with the aggregates or add it to the freshly mixed concrete. For more data on
the product refer to the product sheets in Appendix T and Appendix U. After finishing
the casting in this project, the manufacturer provided updated guidelines for mixing fibres
into alkali-activated concretes, which is described in the discussion. The emissions as-
sociated with the steel fibres can be expressed by the global warming potential in CO2,eq.
The value for the specific fibre used in the project is 1.94 kg CO2,eq per kg of Dramix 4D
80/60BGP steel fibres [27].

Wedge Splitting Finite Element Model
The objective of the finite element (FE) modelling campaign is to represent the material
properties of fibre-reinforced geopolymer concrete accurately in the model. A finite ele-
ment model (FEM) has been made as an attempt to replicate the experimental results of
the wedge splitting test (WST) and therefore the focus is on modelling the tensile proper-
ties. The program used was Abaqus Standard, which contains multiple concrete material
models where material properties gained from experimental data can be used to define a
concrete material. This allows the FE model to make a simulation based on the experi-
mental data.
The model created to replicate the fibre-reinforced samples in the wedge splitting test is
seen on Figure 4.0.1 and was a 2D shell model in a 3D modelling space. The model was
made to fit the WST sample measurements seen on Figure 3.6.2 where the cut notch
had a depth of 30 mm and a width of 3.6 mm. The overall model dimensions were 100
mm wide and 100 mm in height. The depth of 100 mm was represented by 9 thickness
integration points. The FE analysis is run as a static analysis to best simulate the quasi-
static load condition for the experimental WST.

4.2
Concrete material models
Several material models exist for finite element modelling of concrete. Two concrete mod-
els available in the FEM software Abaqus have been chosen, the smeared cracking model
(SC) and the concrete damaged plasticity model (CDP). The models, their assumptions,
strengths and weaknesses will be described in the following sections. Common for the
two concrete models is that they require a linear elastic isotropic material model, defined
by a single value for the Young’s modulus. This means that the elastic stiffness will be the
same in tension and compression [36, 37]. Both models allow modification of the material
behaviour in the plastic regime, making it possible to incorporate user-defined constitutive
relations as the one shown in Figure 4.1.1.
Smeared cracking (SC)
The smeared cracking (SC) concrete material model can be used for plain concrete, but
is primarily intended for reinforced concrete. It is intended for concrete that is subjected to
near-monotonic straining at low confining pressures. The material model uses an isotrop-
ically hardening yield surface. All these factors are true for the WST FE model, making
the SC model a viable choice.
The SC model uses a crack detection scheme to determine if a given integration point

been detected in an integration point, it will remain cracked for the rest of the calculation.
Without modelled reinforcement, mesh sensitivity can prove to be a problem, since the

issue if only a few discrete cracks form in the structure, which is the case for the WST FE
model. Considerations regarding this issue are described in Section 4.4 where the mesh
is described.
Once a crack has been detected the material properties in the integration point will be mod-

for the cracked concrete. More tension stiffening will make it easier to obtain numerical
solutions [36]. The tension stiffening parameters can, for example, be described via the

Concrete damaged plasticity (CDP)
The concrete damaged plasticity (CDP) concrete material model, is primarily intended for
reinforced concrete but can be used for plain concrete as well. It is intended for concrete
subjected to monotonic, cyclic, and dynamic loading under low confining pressures. It
can model isotropic tensile and compressive plasticity. These factors make it a viable
choice for the WST FE model. Furthermore, the CDP model allows the user to define
the stiffness recovery effect for cyclic load, as well as apply viscoplastic properties to the
material. These functions will not be relevant for the load scenario for the WST FE model.
The same mesh issues as for the SC model are present for the CDP model.
Under uniaxial tension, the stress-strain response follows a linear elastic relationship until
the value of the failure stress is reached. The failure stress corresponds to the beginning
of the micro-cracking in the concrete material. After the failure stress is reached the
behaviour of the concrete is governed by a stress-strain response that represents the
micro-cracks macroscopically. This induces strain localisation in the concrete. Just as for
the SC model, more tension stiffening helps to obtain numerical solutions [37].
On Table 4.2.2 the material model parameters needed for the CDP model can be seen.
These are the same as those suggested by Hafezolghorani et al. [35].

The meshing of the WST FE model was done with 1180 S4R elements, which are 4-node,

Discussion
6.1
Physical properties and casting of the concrete mixes
Density and air content
The densities of the GPC mixes were all measured to have a larger density than what
was expected based on the mix design, as it can be seen in Table 5.1.1. Assuming
the densities of the materials used in the mix design were correct, a deviation from the
expected density would be due to a difference in the assumed air content. However, the
air contents of the GPC mixes are in the range of the assumed 1.5% and still, the densities
are 2.1% to 4.1% higher than expected for the GPC mixes. If an unphysical case of an
air content of 0.00% was to be assumed, the measured density was still larger than what
would be expected. This suggests that the density of at least one of the materials used in
the mix design was erroneous. Since the density of the REF concrete mix is much closer
to that of the mix design, it is highly probable to assume that the error lies in the density
of the alkaline activator, slag or clay. Since the rest of the materials are used in both the
GPC and REF concrete mixes. The varying deviation from the expected density for the
GPC mixes can be attributed to the fact that only one sample was tested per concrete
mix. More samples would have to be tested to conclude a significant difference in the
deviation.
As seen in Table 5.1.1, the density of the REF mix was slightly lower than expected and
could be explained by a higher air content than the 1.5% which was assumed in the
mix design. Air content measurements of the fresh GPC lie within the range of what is
expected for Portland cement-based concrete, where air content does not typically exceed
1-2% [39]. The results obtained for the air content of the GPC, suggest that the amount of
air trapped in this alternative type of concrete does not pose a problem. The air content
measurement of the REF mix was so high that it was deemed invalid. This measurement
was probably due to an error in the air content measurement procedure, such as in the
vibration of the concrete in the air content measurement container, or an insufficient seal
of the container prior to testing.
Workability
It was observed qualitatively that the workability of the GPC was poor compared to the
REF concrete. The shovelling of the GPC posed quite a challenge already after 20 min-
utes of the initial mixing. This poor workability can be seen reflected in the slump mea-
surements in Table 5.1.1 where measurements were not possible to conduct for the SF-
reinforced GPC since the concrete mix stuck to the slump cone when lifting it. This was
not the case for the SF-reinforced REF concrete, where a measurement of 68 mm was
obtained, not far from the measurement of 95 mm for the non-SF-reinforced GPC mix. It
was possible to fill and vibrate the moulds with the reinforced GPC materials without any
issues, suggesting that when introducing energy into the fresh mix there was no problem
with the workability,
Multiple factors could contribute to an inferior workability for the GPC. Firstly the use
of calcined clay in concrete has been shown to affect the dynamic yield stress during
mixing, which is partly caused by the layered particle structure that adsorbs water from
the mix, as well as flocculation (charged attraction) of the clay particles [40]. This factor
might play a large role due to the high clay content in the GPC used in this project, at
40% of the combined slag and clay mass. An additional factor contributing to the poor
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
39
==PAGE:52==
workability can be the high alkalinity of the activation solution that speeds up the setting
time of the GPC [41], compared to the cement-based concrete, where the alkalinity is
developed over time. Furthermore, the REF cement is a commercial product developed
for industrial applications and therefore engineered to have an optimal setting time in the
concrete where it is applied. This is not the case for the geopolymer binder used in this
project. Therefore further investigations could be made into how a more favourable setting
time and workability can be achieved for the GPC.
Mixing of the steel fibres
As explained in Section 3.2.2, issues regarding bundling of the SF were found previously
for a GPC mix design identical to the one used in this project. This was though to be
due to the glue applied to the surface of SF from the manufacturer. According to the
manufacturer, the glue did not pose an issue when casting Portland cement concrete. It
was therefore thought that the glue was affected by the high alkalinity of the GPC mix and
caused the fibres to stick together.
To mitigate the issue in this project, the SF were submerged in water to dissolve the glue,
after which the fibres were separated mechanically. This approach could have introduced
new problems because the manufacturer has stated that the distribution of the SF not
will be optimal if the SF are separated before mixing [27]. An additional problem with
separating the SF before mixing is that not all the glue dissolved in the water, resulting in
some glue on the surface of the SF. This could worsen the SF to concrete matrix bond
strength.
An alternative method of how to mix steel fibres in an alkali-activated concrete was pro-
posed by the SF manufacturer. The recommended procedure was to make a dry mix
of the clay, slag and aggregates, then add water for a wet mixing. This would then be
followed by the addition of SF, and finally the addition of the alkaline activator to the mix.
This procedure would allow the glue in the SF bundles to be dissolved, before adding
the alkaline activator, which was suspected to be the cause of the SF distribution issue.
This recommendation was however not followed, since all the samples were already cast,
when the procedure was presented by the manufacturer.
6.2
Experimentally determined material parameters
Compressive strength
From Figure 5.2.2 it is seen that the highest compressive strength was achieved for the
GPC 0.00% mix at 54.9 MPa. The reinforced GPC mixes all had lower compressive
strengths, suggesting that the presence of fibres in the given volume ratios could im-
pact the compressive strength. This could be due to the introduction of porosity into the
concrete from the fibres, which weakens the matrix. However, in the literature, this phe-
nomenon is seen to be more common with higher fibre contents. Regardless of fibre type,
it is usually expected that the compressive strength of GPC is increased when adding fi-
bres in amounts up to 2%. This is because of the confinement and stress-distributing
properties of the fibres. Adverse effects from the fibres are more common for contents
above 2% [4]. It was observed during casting that that the fibres resulted in a lower work-
ability and could therefore be another reason for the lower compressive strength of the
fibre-reinforced samples. The low workability can lead to a less homogenous mix, poor
compaction and increased porosity.
The GPC 0.20% mix has the lowest overall compressive strength, suggesting a possible
local pessimum in the fibre content caused by the porosity and poorer workability reducing
the strength. At the same time, the fibre content is too low to improve the compressive

strength, via better stress distribution. Generally, the spread of the data is the largest
on the GPC 0.20% mix, suggesting that the sample size is too low to conclude that this
fibre content will have such a significant impact on the strength. More consistency in the
measurements could also be achieved by fine-tuning the manufacturing procedure of the
specimens, including vibration time and casting process.
When including the cylinder samples that were stiffness tested prior to being compressive
tested, the GPC 0.00% mix also features a large spread where the samples tested prior
featured a lower strength. This could be due to micro-cracking occurring during the load
cycles in the first test, which could lower the recorded strength in the compressive strength
test. The behaviour is however different for the reinforced samples where there is no
apparent tendency for the samples tested prior to have a lower strength than the remaining
samples. This could be entirely random or could be due to the fibres controlling the micro-
cracking, that could have occurred during E-modulus testing. The compressive strength
of the GPC 0.45% and GPC 0.60% mixes, as well as the REF mix generally feature a
lower spread. This could also be due to the fibres affecting the micro-cracking of the
concrete. However, this conclusion is uncertain because of the relatively small sample
size.
The compressive strength of the GPC 0.45% and REF 0.45% mixes are comparable,
showing that the REF mix serves as a good reference for the project. However, the com-
pressive strength of the unreinforced REF mix has not been tested. This would have
been useful to know, to rule out the effect of the fibres on the comparability in the strength
between the REF concrete and GPC.
Young's modulus
Looking at the results from the cylinder test on Figure 5.2.1 the Young’s modulus is seen
to be significantly lower for the GPC mixes than for the REF mix, where the Young’s mod-
ulus of the GPC mixes ranges from being 14% to 18% lower relative to the REF mix. The
same type of aggregate is used for both concretes, the difference in stiffness is therefore
likely due to the different stiffness of the binder. This corresponds with the general ten-
dency where GPC is seen to have a lower Young’s modulus than Portland cement-based
concrete when the compressive strength is comparable [42, 43]. The reason for the differ-
ence in the stiffness of the binders is likely complex. A possible contributing factor is the
difference in the phases formed in the binders of the two materials. In Portland cement
concretes the primary phase formed is C-S-H gel, whereas aluminium is substituted into
the primary phase in GPC. For GPC with high Ca contents, C-A-S-H gel is the primary
phase, whereas for low Ca contents N-A-S-H will be the primary phase [7, 42]. The stiff-
ness of C-S-H has shown to be similar to C-A-S-H, but significantly lower for N-A-S-H [7].
Since the calcium content for the GPC cast in this project is lower than the REF concrete
(compare the compositions on Figure 3.1.2), it could be expected that there will be a larger
share of phases in the GPC binder with a lower stiffness than in the REF samples.
A second possible contributing factor to the lower Young’s modulus for the GPC compared
with the REF is the slower development of mechanical properties associated with a low
calcium content [4]. Meaning a larger increase in the stiffness for the GPC mix could be
seen when testing the stiffness later than 28 days after casting, such as 56 days. Lastly,
the calcined clay in the GPC could also be a contributing factor to the lower stiffness.
For the GPC mixes in the cylinder test, the Young’s modulus is also seen to vary slightly
with the fibre content as seen on Figure 5.2.1, however with an overlap in the interquartile
ranges. The variation could therefore likely be reduced by fine-tuning the manufactur-
ing process as well as the measurement procedure. As described in Appendix G, the

measurements were performed with a new set of strain sensors that produced results

The Young’s modulus was also measured during the wedge splitting test (WST) and a

lower for the GPC mixes relative to the REF mix.
Softening curves
The horizontal part of the softening curves found for the concrete mixes with reinforcement
seen on Figure 5.3.2, are seen to be higher for concretes with a larger degree of steel
fibre reinforcement, reflected in the intercept values b2 seen in Table 5.3.2. This plateau
on the softening curve therefore represents the residual strength ftu of the concrete. This
aligns with the constitutive model proposed in Lok & Xiao 1999 [6], where the residual
strength is the plateau of the curve as seen on Figure 2.3.1. In the model from Lok &
Xiao 1999 the residual strength is proportional to the volume fraction of steel fibres (see
eq. (2.2)), since the fibres carry all the stress in the fully cracked zone. Interestingly,
the residual strength seen on the softening curves on Figure 5.3.2 is higher for the GPC
0.45% than on the REF 0.45% despite having the same reinforcement degree, w/b ratio,
and similar compressive strength. This suggests a difference in the composite behaviour
between the two materials which could be caused by a difference in the fibre to matrix
bond strength. In the model by Lok & Xiao, the residual strength depends on the fibre
bond stress τd. The higher bond strength for the GPC also aligns with previous studies,
that show a higher bond strength for alkali-activated materials compared to conventional
cement-based concretes [5]. During the WST of the REF samples it was noted, that the
force curves were significantly more jagged at higher CMOD values (this is seen on the
curves in Appendix J) which was attributed to slipping of the fibres, since it did not occur
for the GPC samples. Additionally, metallic pings or snapping sounds were observed
during the testing of the REF samples, further suggesting that fibres were slipping inside
the concrete matrix.
The softening curve for the GPC 0.20% mix seen on Figure 5.3.2 is the only curve to not
feature the above-mentioned plateau. This is the only unreinforced mix and the concrete
is therefore not able to bridge the stresses across the cracked zone for long and the
bridging stress approaches zero. This is also evident from the significantly lower fracture
energy seen in this mix compared to the others (see Table 5.3.2).
Tensile strength
The tensile strength has been determined for the mixes using both BST and WST. On
Figure 6.2.1 the tensile strengths found using the two methods have been compared. In
general, higher tensile strengths are reported when using the BST than the WST, however,
this is not the case for the unreinforced GPC mix where the strengths determined from
the two methods are comparable. It has been shown previously in other studies that
cylinder split tests provide estimates of the tensile strengths that are too high and that an
42

empirical factor of 0.6 to 0.9 is needed to reduce the reported strength [31, p. 80]. More
specifically for steel fibre-reinforced concrete, it has been reported that the BST estimates
an inaccurate and too large tensile strength, partly due to the failure being more ductile,
meaning that the tensile failure is registered too late during a conventional test , which
is designed for unreinforced concrete [45]. This could also explain why, on Figure 5.2.3,
the tensile strength is found to increase with higher fibre contents since more fibres would
increase the ductility. This points to a general issue with determining the tensile strength of
SFR concrete, since increasing the amount of fibres generally will make the initiation of the
cracking less apparent. This is especially the case at higher reinforcement degrees where
strain-hardening behaviour is observed directly after cracking [16]. Other factors may also
influence the amount of cracking, such as the porosity that is induced by introducing more
fibres into the mix [4].

Constitutive relations
On Figure 6.3.1 the plastic part of the constitutive relations can be seen for all the SFR

relation, meaning the point where discrete cracking begins to occur in the concrete. The
parameters used to generate the curves are tabulated in Table 6.2.1 and 6.2.2.
When comparing the abilities of the WST and BST to estimate the tensile strength of
SFR concrete it was decided to choose the BST tensile strength for representing the
tensile strength of the materials used in the Lok & Xiao constitutive relations, seen on Fig-

uncertainty of the WST tensile strength, induced by the unknown crack width parameter
and softening curve fitting, made it less reliable to use this value for the proposed Lok &
Xiao constitutive relations.

curve generated in Abaqus with the SC material model will fit well, at larger CMOD-values
only for the GPC 0.45% and REF 0.45%. where strain hardening is seen. A constitutive
relation that allows for a change in the residual strength slope is required to improve the fit
for other SF contents. This slope change is implemented in DS/EN 1992-1-1 [13]. How-
ever, the overall constitutive relation gained from this method is much too conservative to
replicate experimental data in a FEM program.
Influence of ultimate strain
Variations in the εtu only affect the end of the splitting force-CMOD curve as seen on
Figure 6.3.4. The curves for εtu = 0.1 and 0.2 are practically identical until the point at
around CMOD = 0.67 where the curve with the lower ultimate strain εtu begins to drop
rapidly, compared to the curve with the higher ultimate strain. This drop is attributed to the
elements of the WST FE model beginning to lose bearing capacity. This first occurs at the
top of the crack path and travels down the crack path, which leads to a continuous drop
in the splitting force. The ultimate strain εtu was chosen to be εtu = 0.1 since the value
only affects how far the simulation can run, and this value shows a good representation of
the beginning of the splitting force-CMOD curve in the approximate range CMOD = 0.0 to
0.7. The choice of ultimate strain should be determined for a given design situation. If the
later stage of the splitting force-CMOD curve is of interest, a larger εtu should be chosen.
A maximum value of the ultimate strain should be dictated by a fit of where the drop of
splitting force is in the experimental results, which occurs when the SF begin to fail. This
drop in the curve can be seen in the experimental WST data for the 0.45% and 0.60% in
Appendix J. The drop happens at different CMODs for all the samples, therefore some of
the drops are not seen in the interval that the experimental data was gathered.
It can be seen on both Figure 6.3.3 and 6.3.4, that some of the splitting force-CMOD
curves stop at smaller CMOD values than others. This is due to the FE model analysis
aborting, before its completion. As described in Section 4.2, the FE analysis will generally
become more stable for larger degrees of tension stiffening. This tendency can be seen,
for the GPC 0.20% mix, where the curves with the lower tension stiffening, i.e. the IA
curves, stop before the L&X curves with larger tension stiffening. This aborting of the
FE analysis poses a problem, that must be addressed if the later stage of the splitting

Conclusion
The steel fibre-reinforced geopolymer concrete (SFRGPC), that was produced in this
project, has shown to be a material that in several ways behaves similarly to conven-
tional cement-based concrete. However, it was not without challenges since the fresh
SFRGPC proved to have lower workability, likely caused by the high clay content and the
faster setting time of the material.
The achieved compressive strength of the geopolymer concrete (GPC) was satisfactory,
reaching 54.9 MPa for the unreinforced mix and 50.9 MPa for the GPC with 0.45% SF
(steel fibres) which was comparable to the reference concrete mix. The limitation in these
results is, however, the relatively small sample size. The Young’s modulus was found
from cylinder testing to range from 32.8 GPa to 34.3 GPa for the GPC mixes. There was
not found an influence from the fibre content on the Young’s modulus for the GPC within
the tested fibre volume fraction. A significantly higher Young’s modulus was found for the
reference concrete. Some of this difference could be due to the slower strength devel-
opment of the GPC compared to the reference cement mix. A lower Young’s modulus
could be a useful material property for increased damping in a structure. The values of
the Young’s modulus in the cylinder test are subjected to some uncertainty, due to the
limited sample size. The Young’s modulus found in the wedge splitting test (WST) was
found to be 25% to 34% lower, which was attributed to the different stress states observed
in the two tests. The WST being a tensile test and the cylinder test a compressive test.
The determined tensile strength of the materials also depended on the employed test
method, where higher tensile strengths were found when using the Brazilian splitting test
(BST) compared to the WST. Although this was not the case for the unreinforced GPC mix.
The higher tensile strength in the BST was linked to the test method not being well-suited
for SF-reinforced concrete, giving a possible explanation for why the same ft was found
in both methods for the unreinforced mix. Uncertainties exist for the WST too, however,
the main being the uncertainty in the hinge width parameter s and the small cube size
compared to fibre length, as well as the fact that the WST was initially developed for
non-SF-reinforced concrete.
The WST was successfully used to determine the softening curves, which were used to
determine the residual strengths of the fibre-reinforced materials. It was seen that the
GPC 0.45% mix had a 31% higher residual strength than the REF 0.45% mix, despite
having the same fibre content. This was thought to be due to the increased fibre-to-
concrete matrix bond strength, something that was seen in a previous study as well [5].
In this study, the increased bond strength was attributed to the alkalinity of the GPC mix
partially dissolving the steel fibre surface, thus giving a better cohesion between fibre and
matrix.
It was possible to create a constitutive law for the fibre-reinforced concrete that with partial
success recreated the results of the WST in a finite element model (FE). The deviation in
the FE results from the experimental results was partly due to the limitation of the general
Lok & Xiao [6] constitutive relation that was used. It allowed for an extrapolation of the
constitutive relation from one steel fibre volume fraction to another, but in the late plastic
regime of the experimental WST, the model proved inadequate, due to the limitation of a
constant residual strength value. The fit of the FE results to the experimental results was
more successful in the elastic and early plastic range of the experimental WST data. It

was however not possible to create a good FE fit for all the SF-reinforced mixes. A reason
for this could be explained by the fact that the SFR material showed high variability within
each mix, which means that the relatively small sample sizes used, could have provided
results that were not indicative of the results that would have been obtained with larger
sample sizes. It was generally proven difficult to model the samples with a relatively light
degree of steel fibre reinforcement in an FE program. The force-deformation curve for
such models is more complex than those of concretes with larger contents of SF because
the brittleness of the material is still pronounced at low fibre contents.
7.1
Further work
In the casting of the samples in this project, it was proven difficult to quantitatively deter-
mine the workability of the fresh concrete. If mixes like the ones in this project were to
be cast again. It would be beneficial to use alternative workability measurements to the
slump test used in the project. This could be the German concrete flow table test from
DS/EN 12350-5 [47], or using a torque meter in the mixer while mixing. Additionally, it
would be recommended to work with larger concrete samples or shorter fibres, to have a
better distribution of the fibres and to allow for testing of mixes with higher SF contents.
In future investigations of the SFRGPC material, it would be recommended to determine
the tensile strength more accurately, for instance by performing a uniaxial tensile test.
Another method could be the Brazilian splitting test, with measurements of the horizontal
displacement. This would be useful for calibrating the hinge model used for the WST.
If the FE modelling were to be done again for SFRGPC, then it would be recommended to
use an alternative FE software, such as DIANA. Which contains more concrete material
models and possibly some that would provide better FE results, than those obtained in
this project. To better model SFRGPC it would be recommended to expand on the gen-
eral Lok & Xiao constitutive relation used in this project. This should be done by adding
an additional material parameter that would dictate the residual strength slope of the con-
stitutive relation for the SFR concrete, however, this parameter should still be based on
experimental results. The parameters such as the fibre orientation factor η, fibre to matrix
bond strength τd and volume fraction of fibres Vf should still be used in the constitutive
relations to allow for a generalised analytical constitutive relation. Therefore making it
possible to extrapolate from one constitutive relation to another, simply by the knowledge
of the fibre content.
Lastly, to fully develop a constitutive law for SFRGPC it would be necessary to investigate
the material in different stress states to fully comprehend the tensile behaviour and to
describe the compressive and shear behaviour. This would be necessary if the model
should be verified for use in real structural elements.

Procedure
When calibration a strain sensor, the distance over which the calibration is done must be
determined. For the Single extensometers, this was 100 mm. The output from the strain
sensor is in Volts, this unit has to be converted to a strain by a factor called the gain. An
initial guess of the gain is made. The calibration setup is then adjusted to different values
of displacement, and the corresponding strains measured by the sensor using the initial
gain are noted. By inserting these pairs of measurements in the calibration sheet seen
on Appendix Q, a corrected gain is achieved. The initial gain is then changed to this new
gain value. A minimum of 10 measurements are made to make sure that the precision
of the measurement is sufficient. In the case of the EXD-021 seen on Appendix Q, the
precision was determined to be within 0.07%, which is deemed a sufficient accuracy. The
same procedure is used for every strain sensor, that was calibrated for Young’s modulus
testing, and wedge splitting testing, in this project.

G.1
Equipment
An overview of the equipment used for the experiments is seen in Table G.1.1. For both
experiments, the Toni Technik Servo Hydraulic test machine was used as the compressive
testing machine, capable of 2.8 MN compression. The test machine includes an interface
for connecting strain sensors for controlling the load frame and collecting data. A sys-
tem controls the test machine in which a loading procedure can be defined. For each
compressive strength test and stiffness test, a loading procedure has been pre-defined to
follow the respective standards for each method.
Two setups are used for measuring the elastic deformation in the stiffness test: (1) A
double extensometer setup consisting of an HBM DD1 and (2) a single extensometer
setup consisting of a pair of MTS-634.31F-24. The double extensometer measures the
strain using two extensometers – one on each side of the cylinder – where the output
signal is the averaged strain. The single extensometer setup consists of two individual
sensors mounted on each side of the cylinder. The benefit of two individual sensors is that
the strain measured by each sensor is known. This information could be important in the
case that the measured strain in one sensor deviates from the other. For instance, if there
is an error in the setup causing the sample to be loaded with eccentricity. This would not
be known with the double extensometer, since only one averaged strain is outputted. A
comparison of the Young’s modulus measured with each of the two extensometer setups
is therefore made in this sub-report.

Cylinder Testing: Brazilian Split Test
This sub-report describes the procedure followed and equipment used in the Brazilian
splitting test (BST) to obtain the tensile splitting force.
H.1
Equipment
Testing of the cylinders of diameter 100 mm and length 200 mm, required a compression
test machine, a BST test setup and some hardboard strips used for load distribution from
the BST test setup to the test specimen, see Figure H.1.1b.
(a) BST setup.
(b) Specimen in BST setup.
Figure H.1.1: BST setup configured for alignment of top and bottom part (a), GPC 0.45%
cylinder placed in BST setup (b).
The BST setup used is a bookable resource on EIS under the name FIX-039. It consists
of a top part, which is mounted via a steel pin to the test machine and a bottom part, that is
placed on the flat test surface in the test machine. The test machine used is the bookable
resource on EIS under the name EME-083, the link to the resource is here: https://eis.
dtu.dk/assets/details?id=12257. It is an electromechanical universal test machine with a
maximum load capability of 600 kN. An electric motor drives a linear actuator by a spindle
drive. A PC controls the motor of the machine via an input from the load cell, thereby the
displacement and force of the machine can be read and controlled. On Figure H.1.2 the
test machine with the computer that controls it, can be seen.

H.2
procedure
The procedure for testing can be seen below
• Place the BST setup in the test machine, by attaching the top part of the BST setup
to the test machine via a steel pin. The bottom part of the BST setup is simply placed
on the flat test surface of the test machine.
• Lower the top part of the BST via the test machine controller, unit the top and bottom
part barely touch, seeFigure H.1.1a.
• Align the top and bottom parts of the BST setup.
• Wipe off the specimen and the contact surfaces on the BST setup.
• Bring up the top part of the BST setup and place hardboard strips in the designated
slots.
• Zero the force readout on the PC.
• Place the specimen precisely in the middle of the test setup and lower the top of
the setup, until the force readout is around 200 N, such that the specimen is held in
place.
• Close the door to the test machine and initiate the test on the PC.
• Note the max force readout and save the load file generated on the PC.

This subreport describes the method, equipment, and procedure for carrying out the
wedge splitting test (WST) used for determining tensile strength, among other material
properties of the specimens. This subreport also describes the sawing of the test sam-

developed at DTU, to obtain the softening curve of the concrete tested. This procedure

I.2
Procedure for wedge testing
1. Before testing the wedge samples must have crack initiation notches cut into them,

2. Measure the dimensions of each wedge specimen, according to Figure I.2.1. Only

3. Mark the middle of the bottom for both sides, to more easily place the specimen
correctly at the support in the test setup seen on Figure I.0.1.
4. Mount the clamps, Figure I.1.1, on the specimen.
5. Place the wedge splitting sample on the support plate in the test setup. Ensure that
the marks drawn on the test specimen are aligned with the plate.
6. Lower the piston from the machine until it is no longer possible to easily rotate the
bearings on the clamps.
7. When the wedge splitting specimen is fixed and ready to be tested, please mount
the clip gauge in the middle of the test specimen, as seen on Figure I.1.3.

8. The loading rate is strain-controlled by the strain measured in the clip gauge and
the strain rate is set to 0.005 mm/mm per minute.
9. At a strain of 0.025 mm/mm (CMOD = 0.25 mm), the rate is increased by a factor of
10 to 0.05 mm/mm per minute which is visible on the force-CMOD curves from the
test. A ramp in the loading rate was implemented as the test would otherwise take
too long to perform and could exceed 20 minutes per sample. The ramp was set to
occur at this CMOD since it occurred after the unloading branch of the force-CMOD
curve. The ramping was not done for the GPC 0.60% mix since the force-CMOD
curve was more stretched out because of the higher ductility, however, this also
resulted in the test being terminated earlier.
10. The data extraction from the machine is set to 500 Hz.
11. The test is over when it is terminated manually or when the machine detects a failure.
For the unreinforced samples, an error always occurred which ended the test. For
the remaining samples, the test would run for 20 min to 30 min, resulting in a strain
typically in the range of 0.3 to 0.5 mm/mm (CMOD of 3 mm to 5 mm).
12. When the test is finished, release the piston from the test machine and remove the
clip gauge from the clamps. Save the test data. The machine is now ready for the
next specimen.

Before adding fibres
Maximum dosage depends on:
• Concrete composition
• Placing method

Bekaert recommendations:
• Preferably use a central batching plant 
mixer

Dosing
Plant mixers
• Introduce fibres together with sand and aggregates, OR: Add fibres to fresh 
mixed concrete
• Never add fibres as a first component
Truck mixers
• Add fibres continuously at a maximum of 40 kg/min 
• Never add fibres as a first component
• Never fill drum completely with concrete in order to achieve even fibre 
distribution
Mixing
Plant mixers
No special mixing requirements in terms of speed or time of mixing.
Truck mixers
• Run maximum drum rotation during the addition of fibres.
• After addition of fibres, continue mixing the concrete for 1 min./m³ with a

Mixing time depends on the efficiency of the mixing equipment

• Air content

WHAT TO DO IN THE EVENT OF AN ACCIDENT OR SPILLAGE 
Detail steps to be taken in the event of relevant accidents, the clearing up and disposal of spillages, the procedure for 
information on accidents etc. 
Accident 
Skin Contact: 
• Immediately remove any contaminated clothing and rinse the affected area with plenty of water. 
• Use a mild soap to wash the skin thoroughly. 
• If irritation persists or the skin appears burned, seek medical attention promptly. 
Eye Contact: 
• Rinse eyes with gently flowing lukewarm water for at least 15 minutes, holding your eyelids 
open to ensure thorough rinsing. 
• Seek immediate medical attention, even if there's no initial discomfort, as delayed symptoms 
can occur. 
Inhalation:  
• Move to an area with fresh air immediately. 
• If breathing is difficult or if symptoms such as coughing or shortness of breath persist, seek 
medical help promptly. 
Spillage 
1. Protect yourself: Put on appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) before attempting to 
clean up the spill. This may include gloves, goggles, and a lab coat or apron. 
2. Containment: Quickly try to contain the spill to prevent it from spreading further. Use 
absorbent materials such as sand, vermiculite, or commercial spill kits for chemical spills. 
3. Ventilation: Ensure proper ventilation in the area to disperse any fumes that may be generated 
by the spilled chemicals. Open windows and doors if possible or use fans to increase airflow. 
4. Neutralization: Depending on the quantity spilled and the specific circumstances, neutralizing 
the spill may be necessary. You can neutralize with a weak acid such as acetic acid (vinegar) or 
citric acid. However, be cautious when adding acids to a spill, as this reaction may generate heat 
and additional hazards. 
5. Cleanup: After containing and neutralizing the spill (if applicable), carefully clean up the area 
using absorbent materials. Place the contaminated materials into appropriate waste containers 
labeled for chemical waste disposal. 
6. Decontamination: Thoroughly clean and decontaminate the spill area and any equipment used 
in the cleanup process. Use water and appropriate cleaning agents to ensure that no traces of 
the spilled chemicals remain. 
 
Contact 112 in an emergency.

E.g. laser, vacuum, mixing of chemicals, other equipment – e.g. glass equipment under vacuum with risk of explosion. 
1. Risk of inhalation while weighing the material on a scale. 
2. When mixing the substances there is a risk of: 
a) Alkaline burn to the skin 
b) Slag dust development 
RISK OF EXPOSURE 
Assess the real risk in respect of the work process. The fact that the chemicals are dangerous if inhaled does not necessary 
mean that there is a risk of inhalation during this work process. Consider where in the work process the relevant risk 
resides: is it present throughout the process or only in a discrete sub-process. 
Dust from slag ash can be hazardous during measurement and mixing, especially if inhaled. Therefore, 
protective gear including gloves, an apron, safety goggles, and a dust mask must be worn. Particularly, 
the dust mask and goggles are crucial when pouring slag into the mixture. Additionally, when weighing 
the solution, there is a risk of getting it on the skin or inhaling the fumes. Hence, gloves and goggles are 
worn to ensure safety, and fume extraction is used when needed (during the whole process). 
Furthermore, safety shoes are essential when handling heavy test samples.

WHAT TO DO IN THE EVENT OF AN ACCIDENT OR SPILLAGE 
Detail steps to be taken in the event of relevant accidents, the clearing up and disposal of spillages, the procedure for 
information on accidents etc. 
Accident: 
Remove persons to safety. 
Inhalation: Move the person to fresh air and keep them at rest under supervision. In case of 
discomfort, seek medical attention and bring the safety data sheet. 
Skin contact: Remove contaminated clothing immediately and wash the skin with water and soap. 
With skin rash, wounds, or other skin issues: Seek medical attention and bring the safety data sheet. 
Eye contact: Do not rub the eye. Immediately flush with plenty of water for at least 15 minutes. 
Remove any contact lenses and lift the eyelid. If irritation persists, continue flushing while transporting 
to the emergency room. Bring the safety data sheet. 
Ingestion: Rinse the mouth immediately and drink copious amounts of water or milk. Keep the person 
under observation. Induce vomiting. In case of vomiting, keep the head low. Seek medical attention 
immediately and bring the safety data sheet. 
• In case of serious injury, call 112. 
• In case of minor injury with blood, call for an ambulance via emergency number 112. 
• In case of minor injury without blood, call 1813, and use a taxi voucher for transportation 
(available from management secretary Annette Frøhling or at the reception in building 118). 
 
In case of a spill: 
Keep the spill away from drains, surface and ground water. Remove the spill mechanically and dispose 
it in the designated container in the foundry.
