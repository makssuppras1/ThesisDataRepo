Abstract 
 
The attractiveness and competitiveness of the District Heating technology could be 
challenged under future market conditions, characterized by urban areas with low heat 
density, highly dependent on local conditions and available energy sources. The study 
develops and investigates an innovative District Heating & Cooling solution, based on a 
smart Double Loop concept, aiming to address the key technical challenges that the District 
Heating & Cooling industry will be facing in the future.  
The main features of the smart Double Loop concept are ultra-low operating temperatures, 
a loop configuration, robust and flexible connections of multiple local renewable and waste 
heat sources and the ability to integrate both heating and cooling in the same infrastructure. 
The concept was applied to a newly built urban area in Denmark. The network was 
optimally designed based on the peak heat demand in winter and its performance was 
evaluated. The analysis highlights promising results both from energy and economic 
perspectives. Heat losses are reduced by 30% and total costs by 5% when compared to a 
state-of-the-art tree-structured District Heating network. The innovative solution also 
results in a significantly reduced average return temperature. It was then demonstrated that 
the network designed on peak heat load was able to deliver a cooling load that could meet 
the demand for new buildings in Denmark during summer. 
The solution based on the smart Double Loop concept could therefore greatly enhance the 
District Heating & Cooling technology and could play an important role in future energy 
systems.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. 
Context 
Climate change can potentially have a significant impact on society, economy, human health 
and wildlife. The current warming trend is mainly the result of human domestic and 
industrial activities. Some individual and political actions must be taken to limit the 
greenhouse gas emissions in the atmosphere, and thus mitigate climate change effects.  
The European Union (EU) thinks and implements various strategies to reduce its 
environmental impact and tackle global warming. In 2016, the EU Heating and Cooling 
Strategy was launched. Its objective is to promote the development of renewable energy 
sources to decarbonize the energy supply and support the waste energy utilization [1]. In 
2018, the European Parliament voted an Energy Performance of Buildings Directive which 
aims at increasing the building energy efficiency and reaching a zero-emission European 
building stock by 2050 [2]. Both primary energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions 
must generally be reduced to meet the ambitious EU environmental goals. 
 
The building sector is responsible for almost half of the final energy consumption in Europe. 
The construction, renovation, operation and maintenance of buildings are also the largest 
contributor to global greenhouse gas emissions [3]. Given the massive growth in new 
constructions in a near future, the situation is not going to improve. Mitigation of 
greenhouse gas emissions from buildings must hence be a cornerstone of every climate 
change strategy [4].  
Low-carbon energy technologies for heating and cooling in buildings will play a crucial role 
in the essential energy revolution. The challenge of zero-emission building heating and 
cooling facilities is deeply relevant. However, such an environmental-friendly facility is not 
straightforward to implement in large urban areas with a dense energy demand and little 
available space. District Heating and Cooling (DHC) networks appear to be one of the most 
promising solution in this situation. 
 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
District Heating (DH) was first introduced in the late 19th century to make use of the heat 
generated during electricity production, albeit in an underperforming way [5]. Traditional 
DH systems are based on a centralized generation plant which feeds hot water into pipes 
to deliver heat to close urban areas. They operate with a high supply temperature (i.e. more 
than 70°C), which in the past was necessary to ensure thermal comfort in old and poorly 
insulated buildings.  
The use of such networks solely for DH is being questioned nowadays. The buildings are 
increasingly energy-efficient, resulting in reduced heat demand and urban areas with lower 
heat densities. High operating temperatures are therefore of no use and even lead to 
avoidable heat losses in the network. Heat losses could be reduced without compromising 
the end-users’ thermal comfort [6].  
The majority of DH networks are based on Combined Heat and Power (CHP) or fossil-fuel 
energy sources. This is not in line with the latest European Union energy directives which 
tend to increase the renewable and waste energy share in the energy system [7]. 
These are just a few examples showing that the economic profitability, the energy efficiency 
and the environmental sustainability of traditional DH networks could be challenged in the 
near future. Networks must hence integrate innovative concepts to meet the current 
building energy demand while remaining competitive. The main challenges for the future 
DHC competitiveness are detailed in the next section.  
 
1.2. 
Main challenges & Motivations 
The DHC market situation has changed and is now characterized by urban areas with low 
heat density, highly dependent on local conditions and available energy sources. The existing 
DHC technology therefore faces a number of critical technical challenges that could 
potentially affect its development and its economy.  
 
Operate the networks with lower temperatures constitutes one of the main challenges for 
the future DHC competitiveness. This is fundamental to increase the utilisation of low-
temperature renewable or waste energy sources, enhance the efficiency of heat generation 
plants and reduce the distribution heat losses. Due to improved building energy 
performances, DHC networks in new urban areas can be operated with ultra-low supply 
temperatures (i.e. 35-50°C) and ensure the thermal comfort [8].  
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
Current state of the art Space Heating (SH) solutions can comfortably heat buildings with 
as low temperature levels. However, specific technical solutions should be developed to 
guarantee the Domestic Hot Water (DHW) supply for end-users. Indeed, the Danish 
Standard DS 439 stipulate that the water must be heated to at least 50°C for DHW 
preparation to eliminate any risk of legionella [9]. The most efficient boosting temperature 
facilities must hence be investigated and implemented to safely deliver DHW.  
 
Moreover, the increasing building energy performances and the relative decrease of end-
users’ energy demand suggest that the profitability of new DHC networks will mainly be 
affected by the distribution losses, in particular in low heat density areas [8]. Innovative 
solutions enhancing the energy efficiency of DHC systems must be considered.  
The distribution losses are significant in summer periods when the heating needs are only 
composed of highly variable DHW demand [6]. Traditional DHC systems integrate street 
and substation bypass valves to allow hot water to circulate continuously in the network 
during low-load operations and always guarantee the required temperature for every end-
user. This has the negative effect of increasing the average return temperatures. Alternative 
solutions must be examined to limit thermal losses in summer. 
 
The attractiveness of new DHC networks will largely depend on their cost and 
environmental impact. In particular, they will have to comply with the targeted increasing 
share of renewable in the total energy consumption in Europe, and thus fully exploit local 
available renewable sources and excess heat. Their very low marginal price could definitely 
accelerate their integration either as central heat source or local heat recovery for ultra-low 
temperature DHC [7]. 
The DHC system flexibility also affects its profitability. Cooling needs are contributing 
increasingly to the building energy demand in various European regions [10]. To improve 
the DHC competitiveness with respect to individual energy solutions, a network which 
embeds heating and cooling in the same infrastructure needs to be investigated. New system 
design and control will be necessary to coordinate and optimally distribute heat and cool.  
 
Finally, Denmark and Europe are both heading to an energy system relaying on 100% 
renewable sources. The synergy between heating, cooling and electricity networks will hence 
become crucial in a hybridised energy system. The strategic use of electricity through central 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
heat pumps, boosting temperature units for DHW preparation and the integration of 
thermal storages needs to be investigated and documented [8]. The high share of volatile 
renewable energy sources in such hybridised systems will require improved control strategies 
to optimize the operation of the various network agents.  
 
1.3. 
Investigated Solution 
The innovative solutions for future DHC networks must address the challenges detailed in 
the previous section. This M.Sc. thesis will focus on a very promising alternative based on 
a new network layout, the smart double loop concept. It will embed heating and cooling 
networks in the same infrastructure as the main technical solution for new low-density 
areas, taking advantage of both the high share of renewable sources in the energy market 
and the local availability of waste heat. 
 
This novel DHC technology will be referred as the Double Loop Ultra-Low Temperature 
District Heating and Cooling (ULTDHC). Such a network is composed of both a supply 
and a return loops, operating at temperatures of respectively 45°C and 15-25°C. In winter, 
the end-users along the path use the supply loop to meet their heating needs. The network 
also includes the possibility to cover the cooling demand by using the ultra-low temperature 
of the return loop during summer. The description of the Double Loop ULTDHC concept 
will be discussed in Section 4.

2. Problem 
 
2.1. 
Problem Statement 
The M.Sc. thesis project will investigate the innovative Double Loop ULTDHC network as 
a novel solution with the ability to cover both the heating and cooling demands in dedicated 
areas. The key aspects to be examined about this technology are the integration of local 
low-temperature energy sources, its optimal design, its energy and costs performances and, 
finally, its crucial synergy with the electricity grid.  
 
2.2. 
Research Objectives 
The M.Sc. thesis project contains a combination of theoretical studies, synthesis, modelling 
and analysis. The main research objectives of the work are defined as follows. 
 
• Critical literature review focusing on current and future SHC market as well as newly 
proposed state-of-the-art solutions, 
 
• Description of the Double Loop ULTDHC concept and identification of the key 
challenges regarding dimensioning, connection of agents, operation and control,

3. Literature Review 
 
This section first discusses the evolution of DH systems and the future perspectives for the 
technology in the Danish market. It then focuses on the challenges that the latest 
generations of DH networks are facing. Finally, it details state-of-the-art solutions to 
enhance the competitiveness of DH systems in future market conditions.  
 
3.1. 
General Definition of District Heating 
 
3.1.1. Concept of DH 
District Heating is a system that distributes the heat produced by a central heating plant 
to consumers through a distribution network. Therefore, a DH system generally consists of 
three basic components: the heating plant, the transmission and distribution network and 
the consumer substations, as presented in Figure 1 [11]. 
 
 
Figure 1 - Key components of a DH system 
 
The central heating plant supplies energy to the consumers. A large number of heat 
resources can be used in DH networks, such as fossil fuel heat plants, CHP plants, solar 
stations or geothermal sources. 
A heat carrier is then used to transport the thermal energy from the heat source to the 
consumption locations via a pipeline network. The distribution network is often the most 
expensive part of the system. Heat losses during the distribution process can be significant. 
Finally, each end-user is equipped with a substation to extract thermal energy from the 
network and meet its SH and DHW preparation needs. 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
3.1.2. Benefits of District Heating 
DH networks presents many advantages over the use of conventional individual heating 
systems. They are more sustainable in the long term and have a lower environmental 
impact. For example, they can take advantage of renewable and excess heat sources more 
easily than conventional heating systems. DH networks are more efficient firstly for heat 
production through improved energy and exergy usage of fuel-based systems; but also, for 
heat distribution and consumption with through the collective efforts of the DH operator 
or the locals to manage the network efficiently [12]. 
DH networks are capable of covering large urban areas. They can therefore take advantage 
of the economies of scale for heat generation plants, resulting in lower investment and 
operating costs [13]. In addition, DH systems can readily exploit the available renewable 
and waste heat sources, whose production is more variable over time. The costs of 
renewable and excess heat are in most cases very competitive [12]. 
Finally, the consumer benefits from lower and more stable heat prices due to economies of 
scale. The DH network ensures high security of supply, with backup units in case of system 
failure. Moreover, the substations of each consumer are easier to control and operate 
compared to individual heating equipment. They also require less space in the building [12]. 
 
DH has reliable energy and economic performance. Figure 2 highlights the levelized cost of 
heat production at the current market conditions [14]. DH networks are economically 
competitive compared to individual heating solutions. 
 
 
Figure 2 - Levelized cost of heat production for DH and individual heating solutions 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
DH technology therefore has great potential, especially in dense urban areas. This is why 
reforms and policies for the development of DH have emerged from 1970 onwards in 
Denmark and later at the European Union level (e.g. Energy Efficiency Plan and Energy 
Performance of Buildings Directive) [12]. 
 
3.1.3. Introduction to 4th Generation DH 
The first-generation DH systems was introduced in the 1880s in the United States. Heat 
was conveyed in the form of steam through concrete pipes. The technology was initially 
designed to be used for industrial installations [15].  
Since then, four generations have been developed. The evolution of networks has always 
been oriented towards reducing operating temperatures and improving flexibility. The 4th 
Generation District Heating (4GDH), which is the most advanced concept currently 
available, is adapted to more energy efficient buildings and aims to integrate DH into future 
intelligent energy systems based on renewable sources [16]. 
 
The supply temperature in 4GDH systems is between 50°C and 70°C [16]. Operating 
temperatures are lower than for the previous three DH generations.  
This results in reduced heat losses in the distribution system, up to 33% compared to the 
3rd DH generation [16]. Heat generation plants, such as cogeneration, geothermal and solar 
power plants, then benefit from a higher output capacity [15]. The energy efficiency of the 
whole system is improved. In addition, reduced operating temperatures make it possible to 
integrate renewable or waste low-temperature heat sources, including waste heat from 
industrial processes or commercial buildings as well as geothermal and solar heating plants 
[11]. Due to variations in heat production of renewable sources, it may be necessary to 
temporarily store heat. Excess production can then be used to smooth peaks in demand. 
 
Finally, the overall investment costs of the 4GDH networks are almost identical to those 
related to the 3rd DH generation. The cost of additional equipment in the buildings (i.e. 
new thermostats for low-temperature radiators and new devices to prepare DHW) is 
compensated by the possibility of using cheaper flexible plastic pipes and less insulation for 
the distribution system [16]. The lower supply temperature also reduces the risk of leakage 
into the network due to high thermal stress, resulting in lower maintenance costs [15]. 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
In conclusion, 4GDH systems aim to provide heat from non-fossil sources at a very 
competitive price and can be an integral part of future smart energy systems. Research has 
demonstrated the effectiveness of this technology and has underlined the real interest of its 
large-scale implementation in Denmark [16]. 
 
3.2. 
Future Perspectives & Challenges for DH 
Today, there is great potential for heating networks because they are able to recover and 
use free heat sources and remain economically competitive compared to individual heating 
solutions. This section presents the prospects for the future use of DH in Denmark and the 
expected impact on the attractiveness of the technology. 
 
In Denmark, the number of households connected to DH is increasing every year. Today 
64,4 % of Danish households are connected to DH, especially in large urban areas with high 
linear heat density [17]. Figure 3 shows the trends in the distribution of heat production for 
the Danish DH sector [18]. In 2017, 60% of the heat in Danish DH came from renewable 
sources. More than 70% of the heat used in DH came from CHP. 
 
 
Figure 3 - Trends in the distribution of heat production for the Danish DH sector 
 
3.2.1. Increasing urbanization 
In the near future, increased urbanisation is expected in both the EU and Denmark. The 
European Commission forecasts an increase in the level of urbanisation in Europe of 10% 
by 2050, to reach a ratio of 84% of people living in urban areas [19]. 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
The municipality of Copenhagen projects a 20% increase in population by 2025. 45,000 
new housing units and 2.8 million m² of office, commercial, cultural and educational space 
are also expected to be built by 2025 [20].  
 
These trends show a growing need for heating and cooling of buildings in urban areas, thus 
a clear potential for DH. However, new low-energy buildings as well as deep renovation of 
old ones will improve the energy performance of the building stock. The linear heat density, 
which is the ratio of the annual heat delivered to the consumers and the pipes’ length of 
the DH network serving that specific area, will hence be lower in new urban areas. Figure 
4 illustrates the evolution of DH costs as a function of linear heat density [14]. Lower heat 
densities could have a significant impact on the competitiveness of the DH technology. 
 
 
Figure 4 - Evolution of DH costs as a function of linear heat density 
 
3.2.2. Further reduction of greenhouse gas emissions 
In order to combat climate change, there is an absolute necessity to reduce the 
environmental impact of human activity, mainly carbon emissions. The European Union has 
set a target of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 80-95% of 1990 levels by 2050 [2]. 
Denmark goes further and aims to be fossil fuel independent and carbon neutral by this 
date [12]. DH is one of the energy sectors most conducive to decarbonisation as it can 
largely benefit from local renewable or waste energy sources.  
 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
As shown in the forecast of the Danish Energy Agency in Figure 3, heat-only boilers and 
fossil-based CHP plants are set to be replaced by biomass plants in the coming years.  
This would make DH even more biomass-focused. In the long term, this does not seem 
sustainable, as biomass will compete with other uses. DH systems are therefore at a turning 
point and have to find technical ways to replace carbon-intensive heat sources with cleaner 
alternatives other than biomass (e.g. solar heat or urban waste heat). 
 
In conclusion, the implementation of new DH networks in urban areas with lower heat 
demand and the phasing out of fossil-fuel plants will surely call into question the future 
competitiveness of traditional DH technology compared to individual heating units. 
 
3.3. 
New Proposals to strengthen the future 
competitiveness of DHC technology 
The competitiveness and attractiveness of DH is expected to be challenged in the near 
future due to the lower heat density of the new urban areas to be equipped and the change 
of resources for heat production. S. Nielsen and B. Müller concluded a few years ago that 
DH is favourable for bulk heat transport but is not necessarily the best option compared to 
heat pumps for less dense areas, even in Denmark [21]. 
 
New DHC systems should therefore incorporate innovative concepts to meet most of the 
challenges they will face in the near future. Those new features that are presented in this 
section are expected to enhance the competitiveness and cost-effectiveness of DHC 
technology under future market conditions. 
 
3.3.1. Improvements in energy and cost performance 
This section details proposals that could help increase the overall efficiency of DHC systems, 
particularly in urban areas with low linear heat density. 
 
3.3.1.1. 
Operation of DHC with ultra-low temperatures 
By improving the energy performance of buildings, DHC networks can operate at ultra-low 
supply temperatures (i.e. 35-50 °C) and ensure indoor comfort [22]. 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
Lower operating temperatures are fundamental to DHC economics. They reduce heat losses 
in the distribution system and unlock the possibility to use more local and renewable energy 
sources and more efficient heat generation plants [15]. 
 
3.3.1.2. 
Reduction of heat distribution losses 
Due to lower energy demand in new urban areas, the profitability of the DHC networks will 
be greatly affected by distribution losses. The proportion of heat loss can reach 40% in 
areas with low heat consumption density [23]. 
Lowering operating temperatures as well as rethinking the way to design DHC networks 
seem to be promising solutions to reduce heat losses. 
 
3.3.1.3. 
More efficient heat supply during the summer periods 
The energy efficiency of the DHC networks is low when heat demand is small and 
intermittent, such as in summer. The network must ensure that every consumer, regardless 
of location, can be supplied with hot water within an acceptable time delay.  
By-pass valves are therefore usually installed in streets and substations. They form a direct 
connection between the supply and return lines [24]. In summer, a flow of hot water is 
constantly circulating in the network supply line, guaranteeing that warm water is always 
sufficiently available for each consumer.  
If the hot water has not been used before the last consumer, it is returned directly to the 
return line via a bypass flow. The return temperature of the system is then increased, 
resulting in additional heat losses and lower plant operation efficiency [24]. 
Technical solutions for removing the bypass valves and avoiding unnecessary contamination 
of the return temperatures must be implemented. 
 
3.3.1.4. 
DHW preparation with no risk of legionella 
With ultra-low supply temperatures (i.e. 35-50 °C), new solutions are needed to increase 
the supply temperature for DHW preparation and comply with the Danish Standard DS 
439 [9]. The Danish law stipulates that the temperature at any circulation point in the 
DHW circuit must be at least 50°C. If the water is stagnant, such as in hot water tanks, 
the temperature must be above 60°C. Those temperature levels prevent the proliferation of 
legionella bacteria and thus, ensure that DHW is safely delivered to the consumer. 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
3.3.1.5. 
Development of District Cooling 
District Cooling (DC) has been neglected for a long time, as the current narrow market in 
Europe shows. Less than 1% of the cooling demand in buildings is covered by DC despite 
its great potential, especially for Central and Southern European countries [25].  
However, the demand for cooling in buildings is increasing rapidly nowadays, as evidenced 
by the number of room air conditioners installed in European buildings, which has been 
increasing by 3% per year since 1990 [26]. This trend will continue in the coming years as 
the demand for Space Cooling (SC) is expected to pick up further. It is projected to triple 
in Europe by 2050 compared to 2006 values [27]. 
Furthermore, it has been established that DC leads to a 70% reduction in CO2 emissions 
and a 40% reduction in costs compared to individual cooling units, in Denmark [28]. This 
reinforces the potential of DC technology and positions it as one of the most promising 
solutions for cooling buildings. 
Taking into account the increasing demand for cooling and the energy and economic 
benefits that DC networks can provide, it seems profitable to consider distribution networks 
that combine the supply of both heating and cooling. 
 
3.3.2. Simplified connection and control of multiple heat sources 
Ultra-low temperatures make it possible to integrate a wider range of heat sources, including 
locally available renewable and waste heat. Both a detailed description and critical 
discussion of the various sources that can be integrated into ultra-low temperature DH 
networks is provided in Appendix I. In a nutshell, the local renewable and waste sources are 
mostly characterized by low capacity, low supply temperature and variable heat production. 
It therefore seems indispensable to combine them with each other or with heat storages in 
order to be able to meet the heat demand of the urban area at any time of the year.  
 
Future ultra-low temperature DH networks are therefore intended to couple a multitude of 
local energy sources. However, conventional network configurations lacks of flexibility and 
are not able to integrate and control a multitude of heat sources in a simple and robust 
manner [6]. New technical solutions must hence be investigated to allow flexible and 
efficient connection and control of a large number of heat sources, including prosumers. 
 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
3.3.3. Synergy with smart energy systems 
A study has analysed scenarios for the most efficient heating shares on the Danish market 
in 2060 [29]. It was found that the share of heat pumps will remain considerable and that 
DH will only be able to penetrate 65-70% of the market in the future.  
One of the main reasons for this is the surplus electricity production by the different wind 
farms in Denmark. This is the first problem of this kind to be examined in Europe. This 
reinforces the need to further develop the synergy between DHC networks and the electricity 
grid in the future, through intelligent and coherent energy systems. 
 
3.4. 
State-of-the-art Solutions 
This section reviews solutions explored in the literature that address some of the challenges 
identified in Section 3.3. 
 
3.4.1. Loop versus tree networks 
3.4.1.1. 
Tree structure 
The tree-structured network, as in Figure 5, is the common layout of DH systems [30].  
 
 
Figure 5 - Example of tree-structured network 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
They are characterized by a unidirectional flow pumped from the heat source to the end-
users. The main circulating pumps control the flows in the system. This is achieved by 
having a differential pressure controller at the most critical user in the network. The pumps 
receive the signal from the pressure controller and adjust accordingly the flow and pressure 
in the system to ensure the minimum differential pressure at the critical user [30]. 
 
During no-load periods and in summer (i.e. when only DHW is required), thermostatically 
control valves allow the minimum flow rate to be circulated and keep the supply line warm. 
This ensures that DHW can be taken within an acceptable time delay (i.e. less than 10s) 
and at a temperature of 50°C according to the Danish Standard DS 439 [9]. The negative 
effect is that the bypass flow is not cooled by the heat exchangers in the buildings but 
returned directly to the return line. This contributes to higher average return temperatures 
and higher distribution losses [24]. 
 
3.4.1.2. 
Loop structure 
Loops, as shown in Figure 6, are alternative network configurations and can currently be 
combined with tree-structured networks in many areas [30].   
 
 
Figure 6 - Example of loop-structured network 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
The advantage of loops is that the circulating flow does not have a unique path compared 
to the tree-structured network. This increases security of supply because loops serve a larger 
number of end-users. Even if a pipe breaks down in the network, alternative paths can 
ensure the necessary flow to end-users while the repair occurs, causing less disruption.  
Loops also allow the circulation of the supply flow, making the use of bypass valves 
unnecessary. As a result, lower average return temperatures and distribution heat losses can 
be potentially achieved in the network [30]. 
However, loops require longer pipelines. Innovative design strategies must be considered to 
avoid significant additional costs and heat losses [30]. 
 
3.4.1.3. 
Performance comparison 
As already mentioned, the average return temperature can be lower with the loop 
configuration. A recent study shows a considerable reduction in the average return 
temperature between both configurations. It is reduced from 25°C for the tree topology to 
20.4°C for the loop topology [31]. The cooling of the DH water in the users' substations is 
therefore greater with the loop configuration. This leads to lower flow rates in the system, 
resulting in lower pressure losses and required pumping power [32]. 
However, the tree-structured network is superior with respect to heat losses in the 
distribution system. This is mainly explained by its shorter total pipe distance [30] [31]. 
Overall, power losses (i.e. the sum of both heat losses and pumping power compared to the 
total heat consumption of the network) is lower for the loop configuration [32]. Loop-
structured networks thus lead to improved energy performance compared to tree-structured. 
The total costs (i.e. investment and operation) are almost the same for both configurations 
[32]. The study indicates, however, that some uncertainties remain and that the calculations 
involve many deep simplifications. It would therefore be interesting to carry out a more in-
depth investigation to assess the economic impact of the loop structure.  
 
A major advantage of the loop configuration is the equal pressure difference available 
between end-users, as shown in Figure 7. This allows for better control accuracy [32].  
The pressure difference varies greatly depending on the location of the end-user in the tree-
structured network [32]. Consumers close to the thermal power plant are more likely to 
receive more heat than they actually need and consumers far from the plant are more likely 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
to receive insufficient energy. This results in lower comfort for end-users and control 
difficulties during operations. 
 
 
Figure 7 - Pressure diagrams for tree and loop configurations 
 
3.4.2. Lower operating temperatures 
3.4.2.1. 
Ultra-low temperature DH 
Ultra-low temperature DH networks operate at even lower temperature levels (i.e. 45/25°C) 
than typical 4GDH networks. 
Ultra-low temperature networks pose two main challenges: (1) the need for a low return 
temperature to maintain a sufficient temperature difference between supply and return; (2) 
the sufficiency of the supply temperature to meet the heating needs of buildings [22]. 
 
This concept is well adapted to newly built urban areas. It was also shown that SH can be 
provided with ultra-low supply temperature to even old buildings if they incorporate suitable 
heating systems (e.g. low temperature radiators or floor heating) [33].  
In addition, a DH supply temperature below 50°C leads to complications in providing DHW 
according to Danish law. A temperature of at least 50°C must be reached at all points in 
the DHW system to avoid any risk of legionella [9]. Different methods of preparing DHW 
with ultra-low temperatures have been investigated [34]. Boosting unit using electricity (i.e. 
electric heater) can be an alternative to locally increase the DHW temperature according 
to standards of comfort and hygiene.  
The energy renovation of buildings as well as devices for DHW preparation induce additional 
costs for ultra-low temperature DH networks. 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
The ultra-low operating temperatures also result in significant energy savings. For example, 
heat losses can be reduced by 12% compared to the state-of-the-art 4GDH solution with a 
supply temperature of 55°C [22].  
 
Lastly, when the supply temperature is lowered below the temperature level required for 
DHW direct supply, the overall system costs increase. The additional investment exceeds 
the energy savings [22]. The ultra-low temperature DH therefore needs to integrate further 
innovations to become profitable. 
 
3.4.2.2. 
5GDHC 
Some DH concepts involve even lower supply temperatures. For example, 5th Generation of 
District Heating and Cooling (5GDHC) operates at temperatures close to the ground 
temperatures (i.e. 30/20°C). Those temperature levels are not suitable for direct use for SH 
and DHW preparation. The hybrid end-users’ substations should hence include heat pumps. 
They increase the temperature in the building side according to the needs [5]. 
 
The main benefits of such a DHC system are as follows: (1) heat losses in the distribution 
system are consistently reduced [22]; (2) the possibility to deliver heating and cooling 
simultaneously with the individual heat pumps; (3) the integration of all kinds of energy 
sources regardless of their feed-in temperature [5].  
The main limitation is the additional investment cost due to the reversible heat pumps in 
the substations and the larger pipe diameters required [5]. 5GDHC systems also involve 
more complex and advanced control strategies, which have not been investigated in depth 
in the literature at the moment. 
 
Early studies on 5GDHC technology indicate that both the extra investment costs and the 
high price of electricity compared to heat question the profitability and competitiveness of 
the solution. For the time being, it seems unrealistic from an economic perspective [22]. 
However, this solution remains promising to explore given the high penetration of electricity, 
especially for heat pump operation. This great synergy between DHC network and electricity 
could be the backbone of future smart energy systems. 
 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
3.4.3. Integration and control of multiple heat sources 
New DH networks tend to integrate a greater number and variety of decentralized heat 
sources, including renewable and waste sources. This implies innovative solutions for 
connecting and controlling the heat input of the multiple sources in the system. 
 
3.4.3.1. 
Heat source connection and control 
The purpose of heat sources is to feed heat into the network by means of a temperature 
increase. The two most useful feed-in principles for local heat sources are Return/Supply 
and Return/Return connections, as presented in Figure 8 [35]. 
 
The Return/Supply connection means that the water is taken from the return line, heated 
up and finally sent to the supply line. This connection can only be established if the feed-
in temperature of the source is greater than the supply temperature of the network [36].  
The Return/Return connection is appropriate when the heat source has a temperature 
below the setpoint of the supply line. The water is then taken from the return pipe and fed 
back into the same pipe after being heated. This connection is dependent on the flow 
characteristics in the return pipe where the heat source is located [36]. It should also be 
noted that cyclic temperature increases in the return pipe can create cracks in the steel 
pipes on the long term [35].   
 
 
Figure 8 - Example of Return/Supply and Return/Return connections 
 
Combined with robust control systems, temperature or flow control [35], those connection 
layouts ensure a stable supply temperature to the network, independent of variations in 
heat production of the source, differential pressure and return temperature in the network. 
 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
3.4.3.2. 
Agent-based control versus free floating 
The agent-based control was introduced to optimize the operation of multiple energy 
sources in a loop-structured network [37]. The novel control concept is based on two main 
steps. First, the optimal supply temperature profile over the year is defined according to 
the specific needs of the network. The study indicates that a constant temperature setpoint 
was sufficiently accurate for most of the urban areas [37]. 
The agent-based control then aims to track this setpoint. Temperature is measured in 
multiple points in the network in order to compute the average temperature. It is compared 
to the optimal setpoint to determine the requested thermal capacity adjustment. 
The energy producers bid for the capacity adjustment. The most cost-effective combination, 
which takes advantage of both individual exploitation and the interactions between multiple 
sources, is finally selected. This process is very similar to that currently used in the electricity 
market in Denmark. 
 
The study confirms that the agent-based control successfully coordinates various energy 
sources to maintain the optimal temperature setpoint in the DHC network. The temperature 
profile in the supply line is much closer to the setpoint than with the free-floating approach, 
which consists of keeping the supply temperature within an acceptable range based on the 
operation and actual heat output of the local sources [37]. The agent-based control strategy 
improves efficiency. Primary energy consumption is reduced by up to 52%, carbon emissions 
by 35% and energy costs by 53% [37]. 
 
3.4.4. Advanced control strategy based on Artificial Intelligence 
In order to optimize the energy and economic performance of the whole network during its 
operation, the control system must take into account a multitude of parameters such as 
the performance of the individual components, weather conditions and variations in primary 
energy prices [38]. Predictive feature such as machine learning models could therefore help 
increase the performance of the overall network. 
 
The integration of Artificial Intelligence in DHC networks is currently being investigated in 
the framework of the EU Horizon 2020 InDeal project [39]. The control method consists of 
three main steps. First, the short-term energy needs of the end-users are estimated using a 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
machine learning approach. The predictions rely on readings from smart meters and 
contextual information, such as weather and energy prices forecast as well as energy 
consumption historical data. The algorithm implemented provides very accurate estimations 
[39]. Short-term energy demand predictions allow the optimal anticipation and planning of 
the operation of the heat sources and energy storage facilities. 
 
Then, smart energy and flow meters distributed throughout the network communicate in 
real time via a central monitoring platform. The operating parameters of the system (i.e. 
the operating temperatures, local hot water flows, local pressure levels…) are continuously 
adjusted according to the data sent by the smart meters. The control system thus reacts 
in real time to end-user alerts, further optimising the overall energy efficiency.  
 
Finally, the operating data is stored and will be used to predict future network energy 
demands. The control mechanism is therefore part of deep learning: the system is constantly 
improving by integrating its historical operating data in order to achieve optimal energy 
and economic efficiency [39]. 
 
3.5. 
Why the Double Loop ULTDHC solution?  
Based on the literature review, the Double Loop ULTDHC solution aims to address some 
of the key challenges that were encountered in Section 3.3 and enhance the competitiveness 
of DHC technology in future market conditions. 
The Double Loop ULTDHC concept combines and links some promising solutions that were 
presented in Section 3.4, including ultra-low operating temperatures, loop configuration, 
flexible and simplified connection of various heat sources, and the ability to integrate both 
heating and cooling in the same infrastructure. 
Although the study was based on Danish conditions and data, the concept is designed to 
be adaptable to other climates. 
 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
4. Double Loop 
ULTDHC Concept 
 
4.1. 
General Definition 
The novelty of the Double Loop ULTDHC concept is to create a new ultra-low temperature 
network as an extension of existing systems for new urban areas, taking advantage of the 
local availability of renewable and surplus energy sources. The development of this concept 
is aiming to increase the competitiveness of DH in new low linear heat density areas. 
The conceptual schematic of the Double Loop is proposed in Figure 9. The network consists 
of two main loops, called the supply loop and the return loop, around which various agents 
(i.e. heat consumers and heat producers) are directly connected.  
 
 
Figure 9 - Double Loop ULTDHC network in winter 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
The objective is to cover the annual demand for both heating and cooling by using the 
flexible Double Loop infrastructure that efficiently recovers waste energy from local sources 
(i.e. data centres, supermarkets or industries). 
The system operates at ultra-low temperature levels. The temperature of the supply loop 
is expected to be within a range of 40°C to 50°C. In Figure 9, 45°C was assumed but 
defining the optimal setpoint will be part of the investigation. The end-users extract heat 
from the supply line to meet their heating needs.  
 
The temperature of the return loop is assumed to be 25°C in winter. As the Double Loop 
solution also include the possibility to cover cooling needs in summer, the temperature of 
the return loop must hence be maintained as low as 10°C during this period. This 
temperature level is suitable for cooling delivery in buildings. 
During the transitional seasons, the only demand on the system will be for instantaneous 
DHW preparation. The temperature in the return loop will then normally be 15°C (i.e. the 
temperature reached after optimal heat extraction via the DHW heat exchanger). 
 
4.2. 
Operating Modes 
One key element of the Double Loop ULTDHC network is that it combines both heating 
and cooling networks in the same infrastructure. To better present the concept and how 
the network is operated, winter and summer operating modes are detailed separately. 
 
4.2.1. Winter Operation 
Figure 9 presents the Double Loop concept during winter, when heat is supplied for both 
SH and DHW preparation. The combination of local heat sources, heat recovers and the 
connection to the existing DH system will ensure the optimal supply temperature in the 
network. The idea that all heat sources, regardless of capacity, can be randomly distributed 
around the Double Loop significantly increases the flexibility and resilience of the system. 
 
Central or local heat pumps can also be integrated into the network, representing one of 
the coupling points between the DHC networks and the electricity grid in future hybridised 
energy systems. 
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
4.2.2. Summer Operation 
Figure 10 illustrates the Double Loop concept during summer. The supply loop is only used 
to provide heat for DHW preparation while the return loop is utilized to meet the cooling 
needs of buildings. 
 
 
Figure 10 - Double Loop ULTDHC network in summer 
 
The temperature in the return loop is lowered to 10°C to be able to satisfy the cooling 
demand. The cold water from the return line is used by the end-users and then sent back 
to the same line. To prevent the temperature in the return loop from increasing too much 
along the network, ground water cooling systems with heat pumps are installed. They will 
locally guarantee the required temperature. 
 
Both hot and cold water are kept circulating separately in the supply and return loop, even 
during no-load periods. This allows to remove local by-pass valves from the end-user’s 
substations, preventing unutilized hot water from flowing back in the return loop. By-pass 
flows are one of the main causes of high return temperature in summer in conventional DH 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
systems. The possibility to circulate water in this configuration will lower the average return 
temperature, thus reducing heat losses and improving the efficiency of heat production. 
The circulating water flows in both supply and return loops will be adjusted to always 
guarantee the required temperature in the entire network. The optimal flow to minimise 
the distribution losses and the pumps’ energy consumption in summer needs to be 
investigated and documented. Novel control strategies to efficiently exploit local energy 
sources and intelligently balance summer heat supply and demand will also be addressed. 
 
4.3. 
Pump Configuration 
The use of two sets of pumps is envisaged for the Double Loop ULTDHC network. The 
main pumps will manage the operation of the Double Loop while local pumps will exclusively 
extract or distribute the energy to the network agents. 
 
4.3.1. Main pumps 
One pump is installed in each of the two main loops. They adjust the circulating flows to 
displace heat and cool in the network and meet the local demand, while maintaining the 
optimal temperature level throughout the network. This is the usual technology used in 
most of the DH systems. 
The key element is that the main pumps only regulate the flow in the main loops. Therefore, 
the hydraulics of the loops are not affected by the local conditions. However, both the 
water flow and pressure conditions along the supply and return loops will change according 
to the local heat consumption and production.  
 
The main pumps must also be controlled to always secure a water flow in the forward 
direction in all pipe segments of the Double Loop. This is necessary to avoid situations of 
counterflow or too low flow rate, which could be detrimental to the operation of the various 
connected agents, and therefore to the overall network performances. 
To guarantee the minimum forward flow throughout the entire system, the local flow will 
be monitored by flow meters in each segment of the loops. If a local heat producer starts 
pumping water from the return loop, the local flow conditions in the main loops will change. 
The flow meter associated to the heat producer will then send signals to the main pumps. 
==PAGE:39==
39 
 
They will be able to adjust their speed in order to maintain the local flows above the 
minimum acceptable value.  
To save cost, the local flow in each pipe segment will be obtained from a mass balance of 
flows from energy meters on all connections to the main loops. The installation of a local 
flow meter will hence be a prerequisite for the connection of an agent to the Double Loop, 
whether for the production or the use of heat. 
 
4.3.2. Local pumps 
Local pumps are used to locally extract and distribute energy from the various agents 
connected to the Double Loop (i.e. suppliers, consumers and prosumers). This way, the 
dimensioning of each auxiliary circuit is achieved independently of the Double Loop. The 
local operating conditions in relation to the agents are regulated by the local pumps.  
The local pumps will adapt their speed according to the specific needs of the agents. They 
will only act on the flow conditions in the auxiliary circuits and never take control of the 
flows in the main loops. It is the main pumps that will manage flows in the Double Loop 
based on the data collected from the local flow meters.  
This will simplify the efficient integration of new energy sources or consumers to the heating 
network, making it more flexible.

Different sources can provide heat to the Double Loop ULTDHC network. One of the main 
challenges of the novel system is to efficiently integrate local renewable or excess heat 
sources and use them as much as possible to cover the heat demand. However, most of 
them produce heat according to internal or environmental logics: they operate independently 
of the network demand. That is why the connection to an existing DH network, whose heat 
transfer can freely vary, is considered to secure the heat input in the Double Loop.

5. Methodology & 
Equations 
 
5.1. 
Aim of the investigation  
The aim of the investigation is to develop and implement a general design method for the 
flexible Double Loop ULTDHC solution, in order to evaluate its technical feasibility and 
overall performance compared to conventional DHC networks. 
The dimensioning, optimization and operation of such a system will be studied through the 
application to a case study. The results will be used to conclude on: 
 
• The technical feasibility of the Double Loop ULTDHC network, 
• The key design elements, 
• The energy performance, 
• The cost performance, 
• The environmental impact, 
• The reliability of possible control strategies. 
 
5.2. 
Investigation & Design Process 
The investigation and design process are divided into two main parts. First, the Double 
Loop ULTDHC network will be critically designed based on the peak heat demand in winter. 
Then, the adequacy and consistency of the network sizing in relation to the cooling delivery 
in summer will be verified. 
 
The heating demand is generally greater than the cooling demand for residential buildings 
under typical Danish climate conditions. This is because the cooling of buildings can be 
achieved mainly by passive means, such as opening windows in Denmark, when the outside 
temperature in summer allows it. 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
The heating season has the greatest influence on the annual energy consumption of a DHC 
network in Denmark. It was therefore decided to start dimensioning the Double Loop 
ULTDHC network by focusing on the peak heat demand. The main steps of the design 
process are briefly detailed below: 
 
1. Define the set of boundary conditions for the investigated Double Loop ULTDHC 
network. This includes details about pipes’ length in the system, operating 
temperatures, the distribution of heat sources (location, capacity and connection to 
the main loops), the distribution of heat consumers and the pressure constraints in 
the hydraulic circuits. 
 
2. Estimate the network design heat load (i.e. the highest instantaneous heat demand 
including SH, DHW and network distribution heat losses) that the system could 
face. This can for example be approximated by aggregating each individual peak 
demand of the consumers connected to the network. The design heat load is the 
design criteria for sizing the piping system. 
 
3. Determine the optimum pipe diameter for each section of the Double Loop ULTDHC 
network. This must be done by ensuring that each consumer can have sufficient 
flow in critical situations while limiting the total heat losses from the distribution 
network. The design of the main supply and return loops must also secure that any 
local heat source will be able to deliver heat into the network up to 100% of the 
total heat demand. 
 
4. Investigate and optimize the operation of the network. For example, develop 
strategies to ensure that flow remains unidirectional everywhere in the network 
throughout the year and calculate the optimal circulation flow needed to keep the 
supply line warm in summer. 
 
5. Based on the characteristics of the hydraulics and the network’s energy needs 
previously defined, determine the annual heat and electricity consumptions, the 
annual pumping energy and the annual thermal losses. This led to an economic 
analysis. The implementation of key indicators enabled the Double Loop ULTDHC 
solution to be compared with other DH systems and conclude on its competitiveness.  
 
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
The design and techno-economic analysis were firstly based on a Double Loop ULTDHC 
reference. Then, a comparison with a conventional tree-structured DH network was 
implemented. Finally, several alternative scenarios for the operation of the Double Loop 
ULTDHC network were investigated. This provides a more realistic and critical view of the 
proposed solution. More specifically, various heat sources distributions were studied, 
illustrating a greater variety of operating conditions. 
 
The second part of the work focused on the network operation in summer, when cooling is 
distributed to the end-users. The preliminary investigation consisted on verifying whether 
the Double Loop ULTDHC network previously dimensioned for the heating part can meet 
the cooling needs of end-users in summer period. Possible solutions to enhance the network 
performance in summer were finally discussed. 
 
5.3. 
Physics of the System 
This section presents fundamental theory to design the Double Loop ULTDHC network 
and used for modelling the different scenarios. 
 
5.3.1. Design heat load 
The design heat load, as presented in Step 2 in Section 5.2, represents the highest amount 
of thermal power that the DH network must supply to the end-users over the year. In order 
to be able to meet the end-users’ demand in any situation, the hydraulics of the network 
must be sized according to this load, which occurs in winter. The calculation of the peak 
heat load is hence a crucial element for the appropriate dimensioning of the DH network. 
The DH network has to meet both SH and DHW demands. They were estimated following 
Danish guidelines, as the investigation is intended to be implemented in Denmark.

6.1.1. Heat sources 
One key element of the proposed Double Loop concept is that a large number of heat 
sources, regardless of their capacity, can be combined to supply heat to the end-users. The 
novelty of the Double Loop network is its flexibility to integrate local sources available 
without problem in the hydraulics or conflict with circulation pumps. Conventional DH 
systems are much less adaptable to variations in local heat supply. 
The advantage of being able to connect many sources to the main loops increases the 
capacity to exploit local renewable and excess heat sources. The influence of having a large 
number of heat sources randomly placed and connected to the main loops was investigated 
in the scenarios. 
The connection to the existing DH network will guarantee security of supply for end-users. 
The amount of heat transferred to the Double Loop will be adjusted according to the 
network demand and the heat production from the local sources. 
 
The connection between the existing DH network and the Double Loop is achieved by two 
heat exchangers, as detailed in Figure 11 in Section 4.4.1. 
Local heat sources are connected either Return/Supply or Return/Return. The 
Return/Supply connection, as presented in Figure 12 in Section 4.4.2, is the most efficient 
and convenient for controlling flows in the system. It must be used only if the heat source 
is able to supply heat at the operating temperature circulating in the main loop. 
However, Return/Return connection must be implemented for ultra-low temperature heat 
suppliers (i.e. when the temperature of the source is lower than the temperature in the 
supply line). Heat will be first collected in the return loop, whose temperature will be locally 
increased. It will finally be used by a central heat pump to deliver heat at the operating 
temperature in the supply loop, while cooling down the return one. 
The reference case studies the Double Loop ULTDHC network configuration with a single 
heat source, the existing DH network. Other heat source distribution scenarios will be 
presented and investigated in Section 8.1. 
 
6.1.2. End-users 
The total length of the Double Loop is 10 km. It is assumed that two different types of 
end-users are connected to it: residential and commercial buildings. 
==PAGE:59==

6.1.2.1. 
Clusters of houses 
10 clusters of residential houses are placed around the Double Loop following the dimensions 
specified in Figure 18. Each single cluster is composed of 150 single-family dwellings with 
an individual heated area of 200 m². The houses are distributed along 3 parallel street pipes, 
as illustrated in Figure 13 in Section 4.5.1. 
 
The dwellings are connected to the DHC network via service pipes. The schematics and 
pipe lengths are presented in Figure 14. As the supply temperature of the Double Loop is 
below 50°C, each heat consumer must be equipped with a temperature boosting device to 
prepare DHW in accordance with the Danish hygiene regulations [9]. DHW will be produced 
via a heat exchanger, as shown in Figure 15. The boosting temperature unit consists of an 
insulated tank with electric heater at a cost of 400€ per unit. 
The end-users are connected with service pipes Supply/Return to the network. In case of 
heat demand, they take hot water from the supply line. The water is sent back to the return 
line once the heat has been utilized. When there is no heat demand, hot and cold water 
are kept circulating separately in the main supply and return loops respectively. This avoids 
having by-pass valves in the system, thus preventing contamination of the return 
temperature as in traditional tree-structured networks. 
 
6.1.2.2. 
Commercial buildings 
4 large commercial buildings are also directly connected Supply/Return to the main loops, 
as presented in Figure 16 in Section 4.5.2. They are distributed around the main loops as 
shown in Figure 18. Their heated surface is assumed to be 60000 m² each.  
Used as office buildings or shopping malls, the demand for showers and kitchen use is much 
lower than for residential houses. Their DHW needs will therefore be reasonably neglected 
when sizing the Double Loop ULTDHC network. Only SH demand is considered. 
 
6.1.3. Pump distribution 
The hydraulic of the Double Loop is separated from local situations. The water flows in 
both main supply and return loops are exclusively controlled by the 2 main pumps, as in 
Figure 18. This will simplify and avoid any hydraulics complications when the heat will be 
distributed to the different agents connected to the network. 
==PAGE:60==

Local boosting pumps are installed in the clusters. They are responsible for distributing heat 
to the dwellings. Typical pumping energy in DH network is 0.5-1% of the total energy 
consumption whereas heat loss in the distribution pipes account for more than 20% [23]. 
Therefore, it is a good design strategy to implement boosting pumps. The increased pressure 
head helps to further minimize both heat losses and pipe costs. 
 
The results of the hydraulic and pump design for the reference case will be provided in 
details in Section 7.3.1. 
 
6.2. 
Pipe References 
A wide range of insulated pipes can be used to transport thermal energy in DH networks. 
They are normally buried underground. They have to be resistant to chemicals, corrosion 
and physical impacts. Both physical properties (i.e. heat loss coefficient, the mechanical 
stresses that can be withstand, and the maximum operating temperature) and costs of pipes 
vary greatly depending on their application.  
The pipelines used in the investigated cases and their main technical characteristics are 
specified in this section. 
 
6.2.1. Pipe specifications 
The Double Loop ULTDHC network has almost the same piping layout for the supply and 
return lines. That motivated the choice to utilize twin pipes in the investigated cases. Twin 
pipes bring many benefits compared to a pair of single pipes, including lower heat loss up 
to 40% and reduction in service life cost by up to 22% [43] [44]. Pipe references from the 
manufacturer Logstor are used. Their service lifetime is estimated at 30 years. 
 
6.2.1.1. 
Distribution pipes 
The distribution piping system (i.e. Double Loop, branch and street pipes in clusters) is 
designed with Logstor steel twin pipes. Figure 19 from the manufacturer catalogue presents 
the main components of the twin pipe system [43].  
Supply and return pipes are placed within one single outer envelope. Both pipes are 
surrounded by a polyurethane foam insulation that ensures high heat retention over the 
entire lifetime of the pipes. A diffusion barrier may be added to keep the foam propellant 
==PAGE:61==
61 
 
gases inside the insulation and avoid any nitrogen or oxygen penetration into it. This 
prevents the insulation’s thermal properties from deteriorating over time. 
 
 
Figure 19 - LOGSTOR twin pipe schematic illustration 
 
The pipe diameters available in the Logstor catalogue range from 21.7 mm to 350 mm. 
 
6.2.1.2. 
Service pipes 
The service pipes which connect the houses to the street line are designed with flexible 
aluminium twin pipes. These are aluminium pipes coated with polyethylene on the inside 
and outside. Polyethylene makes the pipes flexible, allowing them to follow the easiest and 
most direct path during the DHC network construction phase. This is very useful for routing 
pipes inside buildings, especially when space is at premium.  
The cost of flexible aluminium pipes is slightly lower compared to those made of steel. 
The pipe diameters available in the Logstor catalogue range from 11 mm to 26.6 mm.

6.3. 
Network Duration curve 
This section presents how the network duration curve was built, assuming the heat demand 
distribution for the entire network over the whole year. From this, it was possible to 
calculate the annual heat consumption of the investigated Double Loop ULTDHC networks. 
 
6.3.1. Definition & Procedure 
The network duration curve illustrates the variation in the DHC network heat load over a 
specific time. The data is ordered in descending order of magnitude and not chronologically. 
The load profile of a network varies according to the climate of the region and the number, 
type and occupancy schedule of the buildings connected. 
This information is used to define the heat generating capacity requirements and the 
capacity utilization over the year. The network duration curve enables the calculation of 
the annual heat consumption of the system. 
 
The load profile can be determined in different ways. Data are mainly collected by meter 
readings or inferred from customer billing for existing urban areas. For new areas, as in the 
reference case, the instantaneous network heat demand was estimated with the building 
performance simulation software Polysun. The following method was implemented: 
 
1. Determine the individual heat load duration curve for each type of building 
connected to the DHC network (i.e. single-family dwelling and commercial building). 
This includes both SH and DHW demands. The load profile considers the local 
weather conditions for Denmark. 
 
2. Remove the contribution of electricity to the DHW preparation (i.e. the energy 
delivered by the insulated tank with electric heater). This should not be included in 
the heat demand profile. 
 
3. Assuming that the buildings of each type connected to the system are identical, 
deduce the heat load duration curve of the entire DHC network by gathering all the 
individual building load profiles. The heat loss from the distribution system is also 
included.

The efficiency of the heat production plants varies significantly depending on the

7.1.2. Cluster pipes sizing 
The total length of the service pipes is almost half of the total piping distance in the 
clusters. Those pipes suffer high heat losses in areas with low linear heat density. They 
therefore represent the critical part of the cluster circuit with respect to heat loss and must 
be dimensioned accordingly. 
It was assumed that they would be designed with the smallest twin pipe commercial 
diameter available. This will have the effect of keeping thermal loss to a minimum while 
locally increasing the pressure gradient in the cluster. 
 
The rest of the cluster pipes (i.e. branch and street pipes) are sized based on the pressure 
drop remaining after the design of the service pipes. An average pressure gradient is 
calculated for the critical path of the cluster and used in the formulas defined in Section 
5.3.3. The commercial diameters are finally selected to match the design calculations. 
 
The cluster pump must be able to provide 10 bar of head pressure, as this is the maximum 
level the Logstor pipes can withstand. 8-bar pressure drop is used to dimension the piping 
system. This is the pressure drop the critical user in the cluster will face under peak load 
conditions. If the pressure drop exceeds 8 bar, the cluster pump will not be able to deliver 
the necessary flow for the critical users in case of peak heat demand. On the contrary, a 
value well below 8 bar means that the piping circuit in the cluster can be improved to 
further optimize the heat loss/pumping energy ratio.  
2-bar are assumed left at the suction of the pump and ensure to overcome the local pressure 
drops in the valves, bends and heat exchangers. 
 
7.1.3. Double Loop pipes sizing 
To ensure optimal operation of the DHC network, the Double Loop and its main pumps 
must always be able to circulate the water flows needed to extract heat from the sources 
on the one hand, and to supply heat to the end-users on the other.  
The total pressure drop available for the piping system of the reference case was estimated 
at 8 bar in Section 6.2.2.1. The 8-bar value exclusively concerns the Double Loop, as the 
hydraulics of the connected agents are managed by local boosting pumps. 
 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
The selection of Double Loop pipes is based on critical operating conditions. The design of 
the main loops will hence respect the following constraint: a single heat source, randomly 
placed around the Double Loop, can provide 100% of the design heat load to the entire 
network. It is a key point of the design strategy as it gives to the specific network operation 
a high degree of flexibility. 
The constraint of random placement of the heat source makes it necessary to use the same 
pipe diameter for the entire Double Loop. The diameter selected is the one that matches 
the design calculations, resulting in a maximum pressure drop closest to the 8-bar available 
under critical operating conditions. 
As the number of commercial diameters is limited, the Double Loop piping system is slightly 
oversized. This does not pose any problem; it even leaves the possibility of increasing the 
capacity of the DHC network without having to modify the characteristics of the pipes in 
the main loops.

demand is affected by the simultaneity factor as described in Section 5.3.1.2.

7.5. 
Circulation flow optimization in summer 
This section deals with the circulation flow in the supply loop in summer. The concept is 
first defined and then the optimal circulation flow for the reference case of the Double Loop 
ULTDHC network is investigated. 
 
7.5.1. Definition 
The Double Loop layout gives the opportunity to circulate the supply and return flows 
separately. This is very worthwhile during periods of low heat demand as it avoids to have 
the by-pass valves to maintain the required temperature in the network. 
During summer, a flow is circulated in the supply loop to ensure the required temperature 
of 45°C for every end-user by compensating for the temperature degradation due to the 
distribution heat loss. If no heat consumption is needed, the water flow is sent back to the 
heat source for re-heating. 
In more conventional DH configurations, when the hot water is unutilized, it is returned in 
the return line via by-pass valves in street and end-users’ substations. By-pass flows are one 
of the main causes of high return temperature and significant heat loss in summer. The 
Double Loop layout allows to remove local by-pass valves from street pipes and end-user’s 
substations, resulting in a drastically reduced yearly average return temperature, as shown 
in Section 7.4.2. 
 
As part of the investigation, an optimization of the summer circulation flow was performed 
to minimize the energy consumption related to heat losses and pumping energy for both 
the clusters of houses and the main loops. 
 
7.5.2. Circulation flow in clusters 
The circulation flow is optimized to minimise energy consumption in summer. Both heat 
losses in the pipes and pumping energy are impacted by the flow rate circulating in the 
clusters. The higher the circulation flow, the lower the temperature difference in the supply

line between the entrance and the exit of the cluster. Heat loss is hence reduced whereas 
pumping energy is increased.

7.6. 
Cost Analysis 
This section presents the key elements of the economic analysis of the reference case, as 
described in Section 5.3.6. Both initial investment cost and annual cashflows are addressed. 
 
The initial investment includes the costs of pipes, pumps and micro tanks. It amounts to 
18.87 M€ for the reference case network, which supplies 1500 individual houses and 4 large 
commercial buildings. This does not include the money and time spent for the study and 
dimensioning phases of the project. 
 
This amount may seem high given the small number of buildings supplied with heat. 
However, this should not be problematic for the financing and bankability (i.e. the 
generation of sufficient cashflows to meet obligations created during capital outlay) of the 
Double Loop ULTDHC project. Indeed, several factors increase the attractiveness of the 
project to investors: 
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
• It is an innovative and flexible DHC solution whose technical feasibility was proven, 
• It integrates and is adapted to the key future issues, 
• It is eligible for public financial support as a renewable-based project, 
• It presents a limited investment risk, as heating buildings is a basic need in Denmark 
to satisfy the comfort of the occupants, 
• It can make use of renewable and excess local heat at very competitive prices, which 
greatly improves the profitability of the entire project.  
 
Figure 34 presents the distribution of annualized costs for the reference case. The annualized 
costs include both annualized investment (i.e. taking into account the lifetime of the 
different equipment) and operating costs (i.e. the costs of purchasing heat and electricity). 
 
 
Figure 34 - Breakdown of the annualized costs of the reference case 
 
The total annualized cost amounts to 2.134 M€ for the reference case network. 
It should be noted that more than 70% of this cost is related to the operation of the 
network, mainly to heat production. This shows that the final price paid by end-users for 
their heat supply largely reflects the initial cost of heat generation. The weight of heat 
transmission and distribution in the total cost is low. 
The cost of electricity is not negligible and represents 15% of the total annualized costs. 
The high contribution of electricity is due to the high electricity price in Denmark and the 
conservative interpretation for DHW delivery. 55°C was indeed assumed in the primary side 
of the heat exchanger. Further scenarios will be investigated in Section 8.2. 
==PAGE:87==
87 
 
The cost of the micro tanks with electrical heater in the end-user’s substations remains low 
in relation to the total cost. 
 
Finally, the use of boosting pumps in clusters has also a very small impact on the capital 
costs. On the contrary, this has the effect of reducing the diameter of the pipes in the 
clusters, which is beneficial for both capital cost and heat loss. It is one of the strengths of 
the design strategy.

8.2. 
Alternative solutions for DHW preparation 
The ultra-low supply temperature assumed for the Double Loop network (i.e. 45°C) does 
not allow direct delivery of DHW according to Danish Standard DS 439, where 50°C is 
recommended in the secondary side of the heat exchanger in 10 seconds [9]. Supplementary 
heating devices must hence be used to prepare DHW. 
 
A temperature of 45°C looks sufficient to meet the comfort requirements for the end-users. 
But favourable conditions for legionella proliferation are water temperatures between 25°C 
and 45°C and long-term stagnation [48]. That is why the Danish law requires a temperature 
of 50°C in any point with circulation in DHW systems.  
Nevertheless, 45°C is admitted during peak consumption periods because there is no water 
stagnation in the distribution pipes. 
 
The interpretation of the law may seem a little fuzzy regarding the required temperature in 
the secondary side of the heat exchanger for instantaneous DHW preparation. A solution 
where 50°C is assumed in the secondary side of the heat exchanger is hence used as a 
reference. An alternative solution for DHW preparation which provides 45°C in the 
secondary side of the heat exchanger was also investigated. Since the total volume of the 
DHW in the distribution pipes inside the house is very small (i.e. lower than 3L), the 
frequent use of water in residential buildings is enough to regularly renew the water in all 
the distribution pipes [49] [50]. It can then be assumed that there is no time for the bacteria 
to grow, so the risk of legionella is considered to be eliminated.  
 
Three different scenarios were therefore studied to assess the influence of the solution for 
DHW preparation on the energy and cost performance of the network. 
 
• Reference case DHW

Figure 43 - Schematic view of the Tree network

The total length of the distribution pipes is 10 km, as in the Double Loop cases studied. 
This will allow performance comparisons to be performed in the most objective way possible. 
 
The assumed operating temperatures were 55/25°C. The heat input is only provided by the

44. The insulated micro tank equipped with an electric heater is therefore removed. 
 
 
Figure 44 - DHW preparation unit for the critical user in the Tree network 
 
In summer, the Tree network must guarantee that each end-user, whatever its distance 
from the heat source, can be supplied with hot water within the acceptable time delay of 
10 seconds. Unlike the Double Loop system, the Tree configuration does not allow both 
supply and return flows to circulate separately. Thermostatic by-pass valves must hence be 
installed at each end-user’s substation, as presented in Figure 44.  
Thermostatic by-pass valves are a compromise between energy efficiency and quality of 
service. They form a direct connection between the supply and return lines [24]. If the 
temperature in the service supply pipe drops below the required 55°C, a by-pass flow directly 
returns the water to the return line. This way, the water in the service supply pipe is 
replaced by hot water from the distribution pipes with a temperature above 55°C.  
The use of the by-pass valves should be as limited as possible as it increases the return line 
temperature. This results in significant heat losses in the network. 
 
The Tree network includes one single main pump. It is assumed that the maximum pressure 
drop allowable is 8 bar (i.e. from 10 to 2 bar). The piping system is designed such that a 
pressure drop of 8 bar is observed for the critical route under design conditions. Note also 
that no boosting pump is used in the clusters.

The purpose of this section is to critically analyse the main results concerning the Double

9.1. 
Performance comparison of Double Loop 
ULTDHC & Tree networks 
This section presents the energy, economic and environmental performance of the Double 
Loop ULTDHC network. The reference case, where only one heat source is connected to 
the network, and Scenario 3, where the heat supply is secured by 4 local sources and the 
connection to the existing DH network, are analysed. It should be mentioned that only two 
scenarios are examined here, as all Double Loop cases lead to almost identical performance. 
Both cases are compared to the conventional Tree network introduced in Section 8.3. 
 
9.1.1. Energy analysis 
The energy analysis is based on key indicators detailed in Section 5.3.6 such as the annual 
heat loss and the yearly energy-weighted average return temperature. Figure 45 summarizes 
the energy performance of the investigated Double Loop scenarios and the Tree network.  
==PAGE:99==

Figure 45 - Energy analysis for Double Loop ULTDHC & Tree networks 
 
There is a consistent reduction in heat loss between Double Loop ULTDHC and Tree 
networks. They are 42% lower for the Double Loop cases. This is due to the lower operating 
temperatures and the closure of service pipes in summer when there is no heat demand. It 
is important to recall that only the street pipes are kept warm by the circulation flow in 
summer since the micro tanks installed in each end-user’s substation can temporarily take 
over the delivery of DHW. 
 
The pumping energy is higher for the Double Loop ULTDHC networks. This is due to (1) 
the smaller temperature difference between supply and return, resulting in greater water 
flows in the pipes, and (2) the use of boosting pumps in the clusters. However, this design 
strategy has led to a reduction in pipe size and a consistent decrease in heat loss. 
 
Finally, the yearly average return temperature is 22.8°C for the Double Loop ULTDHC 
cases, which is 10°C lower than the Tree network. Supply and return flows are circulating 
separately in the Double Loop network, avoiding by-pass flows in summer as in the Tree 
network. Therefore, there is no mixing of supply and return flows, resulting in lower average 
return temperatures. The low return temperature is the first step in the transition towards 
4GDH networks. It first reduces heat loss from the distribution system. Secondly, it 
increases the heat capacity of the network due to greater temperature difference between 
supply and return. Finally, it enhances the efficiency of heat production. For example, the 
heat extraction from flue-gas condensation is improved in CHP plants [15].

The more intensive electricity consumption in the Double Loop solution is explained by the 
higher pumping energy required and the use of micro tanks with electrical heaters for DHW

to deliver heat in the network. 
Heat pumps and supplementary heat devices, such as the electric heaters used for DHW

The total annual costs of the Double Loop ULTDHC solutions are reduced by 4% compared

The capital costs (i.e. pipes, pumps and possible micro tanks) are almost identical for both

9.1.4. Pressure Diagrams 
The purpose of this section is to demonstrate how pressure levels vary between the two 
network configurations. Figure 51 outlines the pressure levels in the distribution pipes of 
Scenario 3 of the Double Loop ULTDHC solution (with 4 local heat sources) and the Tree 
network.  
 
 
Figure 51 - Pressure diagrams for Double Loop ULTDHC & Tree networks 
 
First, the discussion is focused on a comparison of the pressure diagram for the reference 
case presented in Figure 27 and the pressure diagram for Scenario 3 shown in Figure 51.  
The pressure losses are lower for Scenario 3 which include a higher number of local heat 
sources. This results in a reduced total pressure drop. The main reason is that the Double 
Loop ULTDHC network was designed based on a single randomly placed heat source. This 
configuration is the critical situation from the hydraulics perspective, making the network 
thus sized as flexible as possible. In fact, the network would result oversized if heat is 
supplied from multiple sources distributed in the urban area and connected to the main 
loops. This explains the lower pressure losses observed for Scenario 3 of the Double Loop 
UTLTDHC network in Figure 51. 
==PAGE:105==
105 
 
For the Tree network, there are large variations in pressure difference depending on the 
location in the network, as illustrated in Figure 51. This can imply control difficulties during 
network operation. In fact, there is a risk of hydraulic imbalance and discomfort if the end-
users closer to the pump would withdraw more flow compared to the ones at the end of 
the network. 
The installation of differential pressure controllers in critical areas is a standard solution 
implemented in all networks to avoid any discomfort for the end-users. The differential 
pressure controller ensures the necessary pressure for the users. The price to pay is that, if 
the system is unbalanced, the DH operator has to increase the flow in the network, and 
eventually the temperature if there is not enough capacity to cover the demand. 
A higher supply temperature and flow rate would result in higher heat losses and a higher 
return temperature, especially if the end-user substations are not able to cool the supply 
temperature due to poor operation or control. 
 
On the contrary, the pressure differences are almost constant for all agents in the Double 
Loop ULTDHC network. This has the major benefit of making the system more stable 
hence easier to control and keep hydraulic-balanced. This will result in lower differences in 
comfort between end-users. 
In addition, the Double Loop ULTDHC network features hydraulic separation between the 
main loops and local interactions for supply and extraction of heat, which are controlled by 
local pumps. That gives more flexibility to the system and the possibility to simplify the 
connection of local heat sources compared to the Tree network.

9.2.3. Impact of the connection of local sources 
This section examines the influence of the connection of heat sources to the main loops on 
the operation and performance of the network. It was decided to investigate the differences 
between the Return/Return and Return/Supply connection of the local heat sources. Both 
Scenarios 1 & 5 are investigated. 
 
The local source is connected to the main loops with a Return/Supply connection in 
Scenario 1 because its feed-in temperature is greater than the network supply temperature. 
The feed-in temperature of the local source in Scenario 5 is 30°C, which is lower than the 
temperature in the supply loop. It is hence assumed to be connected the main loops with a 
Return/Return connection. The idea is that the local source should not reduce the supply 
temperature in the main loop. Therefore, the low-temperature heat will be delivered in the 
return loop, increasing the return temperature. A central heat pump would then be installed 
in the network to cool down the return temperature and deliver the heat in the supply loop 
at the optimal temperature. 
 
Figure 54 shows the distribution of water flows in the main return loop for Scenario 1 and 
Scenario 5. The local heat sources located 7000m from the existing DH network have the 
same heat capacity but different feed-in temperature and connection to the main loops. 
Their specific location is clearly highlighted in Figure 54.  
Another important aspect in relation to the Return/Return connection of a local heat source 
is the larger flow rates required in the main return loop to transport heat to the central 
heat pump, as shown in Figure 54 after the location of the investigated source. 
Increased flow rates in the main return loop result in greater pumping energy and higher 
operating costs. For example, annual pumping energy related to the main loop is raised by 
36% between the Scenario 1 and Scenario 5, only by changing the local heat source 
connection to the main loops.

Figure 54 - Mass flow distribution in the main return loop for the two different types of heat

The characteristics of the network and main pump define the maximum flow that can be 
circulated in the main return loop while complying with the pressure constraints in the 
network. This determines the maximum heat capacity that can be fed into the return loop. 
For example, the maximum capacity that can be extracted from the local source with a 
Return/Return connection in Scenario 5 is 26% of the design heat load. When this load is 
delivered, the temperature of the return loop reaches 30°C, which is the maximum possible 
temperature (i.e. feed-in temperature of the local source).  
 
The temperature increase is illustrated in Figure 55. The temperature of the return loop is 
higher than the setpoint of 25°C in Scenario 5 in order to transport the low-temperature 
heat to the central heat pump. When the local source is connected Return/Supply as in 
Scenario 1, the temperature in the return loop remains at 25°C everywhere.

Finally, the control system must ensure that enough flow is circulated in the main return

10. Cooling 
Investigation  
 
As mentioned in Section 3.3.1.5, the cooling demand is growing fast today and expected 
to pick up further in the future. Moreover, it has been established that DC leads to 70% 
lower CO2 emissions and 40% lower costs compared to individual cooling units [28]. 
The investigated Double Loop ULTDHC network could therefore enhance the DHC 
technology and improve its future competitiveness by delivering heat and cool using the 
same infrastructure. 
 
10.1. Methodology 
The objective of the research is to examine the feasibility and the benefits of embedding 
both heating and cooling in the Double Loop ULTDHC solution. The investigation process 
is divided as follows: 
 
1. Present the general concept of the operation of the Double Loop ULTDHC network 
in summer. Key features such as operating temperatures, water flow directions and 
cold sources connection were discussed.  
 
2. Propose solutions to the technical challenges posed by the simultaneous supply of 
heating and cooling to the end-users.  
 
3. Assess the additional costs associated with the implementation of the technical 
modifications for the reference case of the Double Loop ULTDHC network, which 
was presented in Section 6 and designed in Section 7. 
 
4. Calculate the maximum cooling load that can be delivered to the buildings in the 
reference case using the system previously dimensioned for heat supply. Verify 
whether it can satisfy a typical Danish cooling demand.  
 
==PAGE:114==
114 
 
5. Formulate and investigate changes in the operation of the network to improve its 
maximum cooling capacity. 
 
10.2. Summer Operation 
This section illustrates the description of the operation of the Double Loop solution during 
summer, proposed in Section 4.2.2.  
 
During the summer period, as presented in Figure 58, the Double Loop ULTDHC network 
was designed to meet both heating and cooling needs of end-users. This is possible because 
supply and return flows can circulate separately in the system. This eliminates the need of 
local by-pass valves at the end-user’s substations, preventing unused hot water from flowing 
back into the return loop. The temperature in the return loop is therefore not contaminated 
and kept as low as possible to satisfy the cooling demand.  
 
 
Figure 58 - Double Loop ULTDHC network in summer 
 
==PAGE:115==
115 
 
10.2.1. 
Operating temperatures 
The operating temperatures of the proposed DHC solution are 45/10°C in summer. The 
water flows in both supply and return loops will be adjusted by the main pumps to always 
guarantee the required temperature throughout the network. 
The temperature in the return loop must be kept at 10°C. This temperature seems adequate 
to satisfy the cooling demand in Denmark. However, the optimal setpoint may vary 
depending on the location of the DHC network and the availability of cooling sources, as 
investigated. 
The cold water from the return line is drawn by the end-users. After use, it is assumed that 
it is sent back to the same line at a temperature of 22°C. This is acceptable to maintain 
an indoor temperature of 25°C inside the houses during summer. The temperature in the 
return line is hence increasing along the streets in the clusters and locally when mixing with 
the return flow in the main loop. To prevent the temperature in the return line from rising 
too much, cooling plants such as free cooling systems or ground water cooling systems with 
heat pumps need to be installed at different locations in the network. They locally guarantee 
the required temperature of 10°C in the return loop. 
 
It is expected that the cooling capacity of the network will be lower than the heating 
capacity given the same pipe size, due to the lower temperature difference between supply 
and return for cooling purposes (i.e. 10/22°C) compared to heating purposes (i.e. 45/25°C). 
 
The temperature in the supply loop is maintained at 45°C by the local heat sources and the 
connection to the existing DH network. As in winter, this temperature level is sufficient to 
meet the heating demand of the buildings as temperature boosting units for DHW 
preparation are installed at the end-users’ substations. 
 
10.2.2. 
Control & Connection of cool suppliers 
Regardless of the type of cool source locally connected to the Double Loop network, either 
a free cooling system, an absorption chiller or a ground water cooling system with a heat 
pump, their purpose is to cool the water in the return loop. For example, the schematic 
and control of a ground water cooling system with a heat pump can be summarised as in 
Figure 59. 
==PAGE:116==
116 
 
 
Figure 59 – Connection to the Double Loop of a ground water cooling system with a heat pump 
 
The ground water is coupled to the network in two different ways. First, the ground water 
is cooled down to 10°C during the winter. The heat extracted from the ground water is fed 
into the network at the optimal temperature by the heat pump. 
At the beginning of summer, all ground water has a temperature of 10°C. Therefore, when 
the temperature T3 in the return loop is higher than the setpoint, part of the flow will be 
sent to the heat exchanger. The flow is cooled down in the heat exchanger using the 
groundwater and then returned to the same return line. The pump P2 will control the water 
flow passing through the cold source to obtain a temperature T5 in the return loop after 
mixing close to the required 10°C.  
Similarly, the pump P1 will adjust the flow rate of the cold ground water located on the 
primary side of the heat exchanger to efficiently cool the network return loop. 
 
10.2.3. 
Control & Connection of the individual houses 
The clusters piping configuration shown in Figure 13 of Section 4.5.1 allows both supply 
and return water flows to circulate separately to the residential buildings. 
However, it is not possible for the houses to meet their heating and cooling needs 
simultaneously (i.e. drawing DHW while the Space Cooling system is running) with only 2 
service pipes connected to the street pipes as shown in Figure 14 for winter operation. It is 
therefore essential to think about a new connection of the single-family houses to the 
Double Loop network. 
==PAGE:117==
117 
 
The proposed solution is to use service pipes with double return (i.e. one supply service 
pipe and two return service pipes) to connect the houses to the street pipes, as in Figure 
60. This solution has the advantage of not altering the connection established for the 
heating season. Only a new return service pipe is added.  
The use of a triple service pipe system has already been investigated for heating [56]. The 
arrangement can be adjusted in order to deliver cooling. 
 
 
Figure 60 – New connection of the individual houses to the street pipes 
 
Two heat exchangers must be installed in each end-user’s substation. One will be used for 
heating purposes, such as DHW preparation. It will be connected to the street pipes with 
a Supply/Return connection. The temperatures on the secondary side of the heat exchanger 
will be 45/15°C in summer. The other one will be used to deliver Space Cooling in the 
house. It will be connected to the street pipes with a Return/Return connection. The 
temperatures on the secondary side of the heat exchanger will be 10/22°C in summer. The 
output flows of both heat exchangers are finally combined in one pipe and returned to the 
return street line. A check valve will also prevent backflow from the return line. 
The two return pipes must be connected to the street pipe in such a way that the flow 
from the house does not return to the house, but is mixed the higher flow in the street pipe 
and flows forward to the next house. 
 
Finally, a pump will also be placed in each house. Its purpose will be to circulate the cooling 
water to the house and returned it to the street pipe. The pump will be sized to deliver the 
maximum flow of cold water that the house may require.

10.3. Cooling Capacity for the Reference case 
The principles necessary for the delivery of cooling are then applied to the new urban area 
used in the reference case and presented in Section 6.  
The objective is to determine the maximum cooling load that can be provided in the 
reference case of the Double Loop ULTDHC solution, which was designed based on the 
peak heat demand in winter. The sizing results were summarized in Section 7.2. 
 
10.3.1.

It was assumed that 4 cooling plants are distributed on the Double Loop network. They are

10.3.2. 
Maximum cooling load 
After defining the network boundary conditions, including piping and installed pumps, the 
operation conditions were defined. The maximum cooling load to be supplied to each 
individual house was investigated. A critical analysis was made to evaluate if the network 
designed on peak heat load was able to deliver a cooling load that could meet the demand 
for new buildings in Denmark during summer. 
 
10.3.2.1. Method 
The study focuses on the return pipes in the clusters, as cooling is delivered to the houses 
via the return line. In order to maximize the cooling load supplied to the buildings, the flow 
of cold water in the cluster should be as high as possible. The maximum flow that can be 
circulated in the return line of the cluster is determined according to the cluster pump (i.e. 
total pressure drop of the critical path below 8 bar). The maximum flow rate that can flow 
through one street is then calculated. 
==PAGE:120==
120 
 
The temperatures in the return pipe at the beginning and end of the street are assumed to 
be 10°C and 20°C respectively. This leaves a temperature difference of slightly more than 
2°C for the last user in the street to satisfy its cooling needs. The maximum individual 
cooling load is thus determined. The load is assumed constant for all houses in the street. 
 
The average temperature in the street return pipe is about 15°C. Heat loss with the ground 
is therefore not taken into account. The impact of the DHW flows is also neglected. The 
DHW flows will be smaller than the DC flows. That is why the mixing of both flows is not 
taken into account. Moreover, the typical demand profiles for DHW and SC do not overlap, 
as DHW peak demands occur in the morning and the evening, while SC peak demand would 
typically be expected in the early afternoon.  
 
10.3.2.2. Results 
The maximum cooling load that can be provided to each house in the cluster is 1.4 kW. 
This represents the critical situation due to hydraulics in the return line of the cluster. The 
temperature and mass flow profiles along the street for the peak cooling load is represented 
in Figure 62. 
The temperature increases along the street pipe, from 10°C to 20°C. In order to ensure that 
all houses have the same cooling load, the water flow through their individual heat 
exchanger increases accordingly. The maximum water flow rate of 0.28 kg/s is reached at 
the last house of the street. 
 
 
Figure 62 - Temperatures & Mass flows along one street during peak load delivery 
==PAGE:121==
121 
 
The calculated maximum cooling load of 1.4 kW supplied to the houses is 47% of the 
winter design heat load. The cooling capacity is limited because of the combined 
heating/cooling infrastructure, where the network was design for peak heat load.  
The cooling capacity of the network seems to be satisfactory for recently-built houses, 
taking into account the typical summer weather conditions in Denmark. In Denmark, the 
cooling of buildings can mostly be achieved by passive means, reducing the peak cooling 
demand. DC is therefore used in addition to passive means to further guarantee a 
comfortable indoor climate. 
However, if the cooling demand would temporarily exceed the critical value of 1.4 kW, the 
strategic rooms in the house can be prioritized. This is the case, for example, in bedrooms 
so that the occupants can experience a high-quality indoor climate at night.  
The conclusion is that it is feasible to deliver a cooling load in the clusters by using almost 
the same network as the one dimensioned for the heating part. 
 
Finally, the influence of the water flows returning from the clusters on the temperature in 
the main return loop was assessed. The water leaving the clusters has a temperature of 
20°C in the critical situation. The temperature in the main return loop will therefore increase 
at each connection with a cluster.  
The cooling plants reduce the temperature in the return loop to 10°C, as explained in Figure 
59. A cooling unit is located near each commercial building. It therefore supplies a maximum 
of 3 clusters. 
The return flow from 2 consecutive clusters increases the temperature in the main return 
loop by 1.01°C. The impact on the maximum cooling load that can be supplied to the 
houses in the last cluster will therefore be small. 
 
10.3.3. 
Domestic pump selection 
The model of pump installed in each house must be selected according to the critical 
conditions of flow and pressure drop in the return service pipes. It should be remembered 
that the service pipes in the reference case are 18m and 12m long and have a diameter of 
11mm, as indicated in Section 7.1.2. 
 
Since the maximum flow in the return service lines varies from house to house along the 
street, different pumps will be installed in each substation. 
==PAGE:122==
122 
 
The critical building with respect to operating flow rates is the last house in the street, as 
indicated in Figure 62. The Grundfos CR 1-15 pump meets the specific flow and pressure 
requirements. Its electrical power is 250 W when delivering the peak load [42].  
The houses at the beginning of the street require a more limited flow. They will then be 
equipped with a domestic pump with a reduced capacity, leading to a considerably lower 
pumping power. This results in a street average pumping power of 55 W per house to 
deliver the maximum cooling load. Further solutions to reduce and optimise the pumping 
power will be investigated and discussed in Section 10.4. 
 
10.3.4. 
Extra costs 
This section assesses the additional investment costs required for the Double Loop network 
to be able to provide heating and cooling simultaneously during summer. The extra 
investments concern the purchase and the installation of a third service pipe and a domestic 
pump for each of the houses connected to the system.  
 
For the reference case of the Double Loop network, the cost of the third service line is 
evaluated according to the Logstor pipe catalogue prices presented in Table 2 in Section 
6.2.2.3. The linear cost of an individual 11mm diameter pipe is assumed to be 105€/m.  
The cost of domestic pumps varies from 800€ to 2400€ depending on the location, and 
therefore flow requirements, of the houses in the street [42]. An average cost of 1200€ per 
installed domestic pump is thus used. The additional investment costs for the 1500 houses 
in the reference case of the Double Loop network therefore total 4.16M€. This represents 
22% of the initial investment of the Double Loop solution when it was assumed that it 
provides heating exclusively in Section 7.6, as illustrated in Figure 63.

The impact on the investment cost remains limited compared to the significant benefits to 
end-users of embedding heating and cooling in the same infrastructure. 
 
10.4. Discussion to improve the system 
This section presents possible solutions to improve the Double Loop solution by increasing 
its cooling capacity and energy efficiency during summer operations.  
 
10.4.1. 
Lower the energy consumption of the domestic pumps 
Due to the large flows to be circulated in the return service pipes for cooling delivery, the 
required domestic pump power is high. Solutions to reduce the energy consumption of those 
pumps should therefore be examined. 
 
The most promising solution is to increase the diameter of the two service return pipes. 
This will reduce the pressure drop experienced by the service pipes, resulting in a lower 
energy consumption of the domestic pumps. Figure 64 shows the variation in the average 
electrical power of the domestic pumps of a whole street when the critical cooling load is 
supplied as a function of the diameter of the installed return service pipes. 
 
 
Figure 64 – Average electrical power of the domestic pumps of a street for peak load supply as a function 
of the diameter of the return service pipes installed 
 
As expected, the pumping power decreases significantly as the pipe diameter increases. 
Using a diameter of 16mm instead of 11mm will reduce the energy consumption of the 
domestic pumps by 78%. 
==PAGE:124==
124 
 
However, the larger the diameter of the return service pipes, the higher its cost and the 
larger the heat losses. Nevertheless, the additional heat losses will remain very small due to 
the fact that the water temperature in the return line is close to the ground temperature 
throughout the year. 
The installation of return service pipes with larger diameters should therefore be preferred. 
It is possible to investigate the problem further to define the point of optimisation, leading 
to the best compromise between pumping energy, heat loss and return service pipe 
investment cost. 
 
10.4.2. 
Increase the cooling capacity of the network 
As presented in Section 10.3.2, it was found that the maximum cooling load in the reference 
case network looks acceptable for meeting the cooling demand of a new Danish urban area.  
However, a higher cooling demand may be required in other Danish urban areas with other 
types of buildings. This section therefore presents and investigates ways to improve the 
design and increase the cooling capacity of the Double Loop solution. 
 
10.4.2.1. Increase the diameter of the return street pipe 
One possibility to increase the maximum cooling load that can be provided by the Double 
Loop network is to widen the return street pipe in the clusters. The larger the average 
diameter of the street line, the greater the flow of cold water that can be circulated while 
meeting the pump constraints, the higher the cooling capacity for the houses.  
 
Figure 65 shows the variation in the maximum cooling load for a house as a function of the 
average diameter of the return street pipe. 
As expected, the cooling capacity of the network increases significantly as the pipe diameter 
is expanded. The available cooling load doubles, for example, when an average diameter of 
70mm is used instead of the 37mm in the reference case.  
Here again, the larger return street pipes will cause greater heat loss throughout the year. 
However, the additional heat loss will be limited as only the return pipe is widened. The 
supply pipe, which experiences the highest temperatures, can remain identical to the 
reference case without affecting the cooling capacity.  
 
==PAGE:125==
125 
 
 
Figure 65 - Maximum cooling load for a house as a function of the average diameter of the return 
street pipe 
 
10.4.2.2. Lower the operating temperature in the return line 
Another possibility to increase the maximum cooling load that can be delivered by the 
Double Loop network is to lower the operating temperature in the return line. The lower 
the temperature in the return pipe, the larger the temperature difference available to the 
buildings, the greater the maximum cooling capacity in the network. 
 
In the reference case, the heat pumps cool down ground water to 10°C during winter, as 
shown in Figure 59. This cold water is then used in summer to supply the network with 
cooling. However, the temperature of the ground water can be effectively lowered to 5°C 
in winter by modifying the operation of the heat pumps. Ground water at a lower 
temperature would therefore be available in summer to cool the return loop whose 
temperature could be reduced to 5°C. 
 
Figure 66 shows the variation in the maximum cooling load for a house as a function of the 
temperature in the return line.  
The cooling capacity increases linearly as the temperature in the return line decreases. 
Lowering the temperature of the reference case to 5°C increases the maximum cooling load 
available for one house by 50%. 
 
==PAGE:126==
126 
 
 
Figure 66 - Maximum cooling load for a house as a function of the temperature in the return line 
 
In conclusion, a decent cooling demand for new urban areas in Denmark can be met by the 
Double Loop network designed on the peak heat load. The optimal temperature in the 
return line has to be defined according to the peak cooling needs of the urban area, as well 
as the available cooling sources. If ground water cooling systems are used, the cost of 
electricity and the coefficient of performance of the heat pumps must also be considered. 
If a higher cooling load is required in the urban area, the initial design of the Double Loop 
can be slightly modified and improved to achieve a greater cooling capacity. For example, 
larger return pipes can for example be installed in the clusters. 
Finally, if the concept were to be used in a different temperature climate or in southern 
European countries where cooling is dominant, a different approach would be used for the 
design of the network. It would be sized according to the peak cooling demand. Then, it 
would need to be investigated if the network is capable of delivering the typical peak heat 
demand requested by the urban area.

11. Conclusions 
 
District energy is an efficient solution for providing cities with heating and cooling. Its key 
benefits include using local and renewable resources improving the efficiency of energy 
production (e.g. free heat from CHP plants), and reducing greenhouse gas emissions and 
air pollution. However, its attractiveness and economic competitiveness are beginning to be 
questioned in the new energy market situation, characterized by new urban areas with low 
heat density, based on high share of renewable sources and local availability of waste heat. 
 
This study proposes and investigates an innovative DHC solution, based on a smart Double 
Loop concept, aiming to address most of the main technical challenges that the DHC 
industry will be facing in the future. The general concept, design, optimization and 
operation were addressed through the application to a newly built urban area in Denmark. 
 
One of the main advantages of the Double Loop network is that it can be operated with 
ultra-low temperatures. In fact, in new urban areas, indoor comfort can be guaranteed with 
supply temperatures in the range of 30-45°C. This makes it possible to fully exploit and 
recover locally available heat, including low-temperature renewable and waste sources. 
 
Both the ultra-low operating temperatures and the improved design strategy of the network 
help reducing distribution losses and costs. In fact, heat losses are typically one order of 
magnitude higher than pumping energy in DH networks. The use of boosting pumps in 
clusters of houses has therefore led to an optimal design by minimizing pipe diameters.  
In the investigation, the annual heat losses were reduced by 36% when comparing the 
Double Loop solution to a more conventional Tree network, thus enhancing the energy and 
economic attractiveness of the proposed concept. 
 
Due to ultra-low temperatures, to safely deliver DHW at 50°C and avoid any risk of 
legionella growth, the end-users’ substations must be equipped with temperature boosting 
devices using electricity. In the perspective of the future energy system, electric boosting 
==PAGE:128==
128 
 
devices can be the coupling point between DH networks and electricity grid. This synergy 
is expected to play a key role in future energy systems based on 100% renewable. 
 
The Double Loop configuration also allows for more efficient heat supply during the summer 
periods. Supply and return flows can circulate separately, so they are never mixed. Street 
and substation by-pass valves can hence be removed, ensuring lower average return 
temperatures. In the investigation, the Double Loop solution leads to an average return 
temperature of 22°C, which is 10°C lower than the Tree network. This has the direct effect 
of increasing the efficiency of energy production and distribution. 
 
Furthermore, the Double Loop configuration provides a robust and simple way to connect 
and control multiple heat sources, including prosumers in the network. This is a clear benefit 
for future DH systems designed to take advantage of a multitude of local sources. 
 
Finally, the new added value of the Double Loop concept is its ability to combine heating 
and cooling within the same infrastructure. The temperature of the water in the return pipe 
is lowered in summer and the connection of end-users to the network has been redesigned 
so that they can meet their heating and cooling needs simultaneously. 
The investigation revealed that a satisfactory cooling demand for new urban areas in 
Denmark can be met by the Double Loop network designed on peak heat load. If the 
concept were to be used in southern European countries where cooling is dominant, a 
different approach would be used for the design of the network, sizing it according to the 
peak cooling demand. The Double Loop solution can enhance the transition of DHC 
technology towards the 4GDH, opening up to new market opportunities in Denmark and 
the rest of Europe. 
 
In a nutshell, the study carried out in this project suggests that the smart Double Loop 
concept improved the energy efficiency of DH networks, making them attractive even in 
urban areas with low heat density. This seems to be a promising solution also from the 
economic perspective as the performed analysis indicates for the same urban area annualized 
costs 5% lower than those related to the conventional Tree network. 
==PAGE:129==
129 
 
12. Future Work 
 
The Double Loop solution looks very promising as it addresses the main challenges for the 
competitiveness of DHC networks in the future. It needs to be further investigated in order 
to obtain a more detailed picture of its actual economic and energy performance. 
 
Future work that can be developed are listed as follows: 
 
• Investigate the alternative design based on the peak cooling load that can be used 
in climate where cooling demand is dominant. 
 
• Study and document the efficient integration of local energy sources, including the 
possibility of connecting heat pumps, having thermal storages and optimized control 
strategies (i.e. due to the digitalization of DHC networks) as well as the use of 
mixing loops instead of heat exchangers to connect local sources to the network by 
implementing the innovative Grundfos iGrid solution [57]. 
 
• Perform a more detailed economic analysis, taking into account cooling costs as well 
as the variations of electricity price. The price of heat could also be further 
investigated, as mentioned in Section 9.1.2.  
 
• Compare the overall performance of the Double Loop solution with the use of 
individual heat pumps in buildings.

1.1. 
Low-temperature heat sources 
Operating the DH networks with ultra-low temperatures allows a wider range of heat 
sources to be integrated into the system. Locally available heat, including low-temperature 
renewable and waste sources, can be fully exploited. 
This section reviews the available heat sources that could be connected to DH networks 
operating at ultra-low temperature levels. The main advantages and limitations of each 
source in typical Danish DH situations are highlighted.  
 
1.1.1. Available heat sources 
 
1.1.1.1. 
Heat sources considerations 
The main concern when integrating a heat source into a DH network is its supply 
temperature. A producer can directly provide heat to the network only if it is able to achieve 
a feed-in temperature close to the supply temperature of the network. Thus, the lower the 
network operating temperature, the larger the range of heat sources which can be directly 
integrated into the system.  
If the temperature of the heat source is lower than the setpoint in the network supply line, 
boosting temperature units such as heat pump can be used beforehand to make use of the 
free heat. This operation remains, however, more complex and costly [58]. 
 
Both heat availability and production flexibility are also important factors to consider. The 
greater the total amount of energy a heat source is able to deliver to the network, the more 
attractive it is. The more the heat producer is able to adapt its production to the demand 
in a flexible way, the more advantageous it is for the operation of the DH system. 
 
==PAGE:136==

The cost of producing and transferring heat plays a decisive role in the heat sources selection 
for a DH network. The more cost-efficient the energy production, the greater the system 
profitability. For this reason, the use of cheap waste or renewable heat with competitive 
prices seems very attractive. 
 
What is more, the energy supplier location greatly influences the overall performance of the 
DH system [59]. The closer the heat source is to the urban area, the lower the transmission 
heat loss, hence the greater the energy efficiency. 
 
Finally, environmental considerations are an integral part of the selection of the most 
promising heat production alternatives. The use of non-renewable resources and greenhouse 
gas emissions have a large impact on the environment and should be reduced to a minimum. 
Figure 1 presents the energy hierarchy that classifies heat sources according to their benefits 
for society and the environment [7]. The most environmental-friendly situation is to take 
advantage of primary products that are considered as a pollutant or a disposal for the 
society. The use of fossil fuel resources is obviously the most detrimental to society and the 
environment because of resource depletion and substantial greenhouse gas emissions.

1.1.1.2. 
Ultra-low temperature DH systems 
The supply temperature of the new DH solutions varies from 35°C to 50°C through the 
year. Heat producers in ultra-low temperature networks can be divided into two main 
categories: balancing and non-balancing plants [58]. Balancing plants are very similar to 
peak load plants of older DH generations. They flexibly adapt their heat generation to the 
network demand. Non-balancing units operate independently of the network needs, they 
can hence be considered more decoupled from the network. Most of the excess and 
renewable heat sources are classified in this category because they behave according to 
internal or environmental logics [58].  
The variety of heat sources that can be connected to ultra-low temperature DH networks 
are discussed below. 
 
• Urban waste heat 
Low-temperature urban waste heat can be provided by shopping malls, data centres and 
commercial buildings. Those constructions generally release heat into the environment to 
meet their specific cooling and refrigeration needs. The idea is to recover and inject the 
heat into ultra-low temperature DH network. 
The most promising sources of urban waste heat are shopping centres and especially food 
stores, as they are widespread in urban areas. Those shops indeed require a relatively large 
and constant daily refrigeration load. Almost all the waste heat from the refrigeration 
system could be recovered by the DH network. A study estimates this amount of heat at 
about 5% of the annual heating consumption of a typical Italian urban area [58]. Note that 
this ratio could be greater for small thermal grids located near large shopping malls.  
 
However, the temperature of this inexpensive and environmental-friendly waste heat is 
generally too low to be directly used in DH networks. B. Zühlsdorf et al. therefore 
investigated some strategies for recovering excess heat from supermarkets and efficiently 
increasing its temperature. The main conclusion is that waste heat recovery only becomes 
economically promising if the recovery and transmission equipment standard and 
inexpensive [60].  
 
• Industrial waste heat 
Large amounts of waste heat are released into the environment during industrial processes. 
High-temperature waste heat can be directly reused by plants to improve the efficiency of 
their industrial operations. However, waste heat at lower temperatures (i.e. below 150°C) 
==PAGE:138==
138 
 
is economically and technically difficult to reutilize internally [59]. The connection of those 
heat sources to the DH networks could hence be very attractive. Recycling waste energy 
would promote the circular economy in cities [14]. 
 
 
Even if large quantities of industrial excess heat are available, their distribution over time 
is not always adapted to the residential heating demand and usually involves large 
fluctuations from day to day. Indeed, most of the plants are closed on weekends and only 
operate during working hours [58]. It is hence difficult to consider ultra-low temperature 
DH networks fully based on waste heat from industry. Unless large energy storages are 
implemented to mitigate time mismatches between heat production and consumption. 
 
• Solar thermal 
Solar thermal technology feeds heat produced from the solar radiations into DH networks. 
This solution is very relevant and has a great potential in the future. The major advantages 
of this resource are its high, and relatively unlimited, availability in almost all places 
worldwide and its steadily improving economics [61]. 
Solar thermal energy is mainly exploited in DH through large fields of solar collectors. These 
are devices that absorb heat from the sun to heat a circulating heat-transfer fluid. The 
collector fields can be installed either on free ground or, due to lack of available space in 
urban areas, integrated into building roofs [14]. There are different types of solar collectors: 
flat plate, evacuated tubular, concentrating and tracking. The first two are the most 
common solar thermal technologies. The others lead to increasing performances but remains 
currently less economically profitable [58]. 
The integration of such a renewable and emission-free heat source into ultra-low 
temperature DH network is facilitated. Indeed, the lower the solar collector field operating 
and supply temperatures, the higher its efficiency [58]. 
 
However, solar radiation is not a guaranteed resource and suffers from high daily, weekly 
and annual variations. Solar fields are therefore generally connected to storage facilities 
that store the large amount of solar heat in summer and redistribute it in winter. Thermal 
storages also allow to take advantage of the solar fields’ economy-of-scale. The levelized 
cost of solar heat is indeed lower for large solar plants than for smaller systems [62].

• Biomass 
 
Biomass has a very limited environmental impact as long as its fuels are produced and used 
locally. Moreover, it makes use of resources that are generally considered as pollutants for 
the society. It is hence a cost-effective way to manage and recycle waste. However, biomass 
plants can potentially pollute local air. They must be located far from the city [14]. 
 
• Geothermal 
Geothermal technology consists in using the Earth’s natural thermal energy. The heat is 
stored below the Earth’s surface. The deeper, the higher its temperature. Ground heat is 
absorbed through wells whose depth varies according to the hydrothermal potential of the 
area. For traditional high-temperature DH applications, only geothermal systems installed 
in locations offering the most attractive underground temperatures are economically 
profitable [63].  
Lower-temperature geothermal heat sources can now be used in novel ultra-low temperature 
systems. It results in a couple of advantages. Shallower wells could be constructed, which 
will considerably reduce the investment cost. Moreover, geothermal heat utilization could 
be extended to urban areas with lower underground thermal potential. Geothermal energy 
could thus become a promising local, abundant and renewable heat source for ultra-low 
temperature DH. In addition, geothermal installations benefit from a long service life [14]. 
 
• Heat pump 
Heat pumps can extract heat from the air, water and ground. The use of heat pumps is 
required when extracting heat from the top Earth’s layers. Indeed, the ground temperature 
is not high enough at this depth to feed directly DH networks [14]. If available near the 
urban area, alternative ambient-temperature sources such as sea, lake or river water can 
also be used [58]. The more stable the source temperature is, the higher the heat pump 
performance. 
Lowering the DH network operating temperature has a positive impact on the performance 
of heat pump systems. The lower the network temperatures, the higher the heat pump 
coefficient of performance. The UK Department of Energy and Climate Change estimates 
==PAGE:140==
140 
 
that the performance of heat pumps is improved by 34% by lowering the temperature of 
the heat distribution network from 80°C to 50°C [64].  
 
Moreover, DH networks mainly based on heat pumps have a significant potential for 
reducing greenhouse gas emissions. Those electricity-driven systems appear to be 
particularly advantageous in regions where penetration of renewable and low-emission 
electricity is high, such as Denmark.  
Heat pumps can be used to convert electricity to heat at high efficiencies in times of surplus 
electricity generation [14]. 
Finally, one of the key drivers for the implementation of heat pumps in DH networks is 
their ability to provide both heating and cooling. Indeed, the ground or water-basin can be 
used as a heat source in winter and as a heat sink in summer. Using a single heat pump 
system to flexibly supply heat and cool can help increase the energy and economic efficiency 
of the DHC networks [64].

can be exploited. New heat sources such as urban waste heat and heat pumps can therefore 
be used only in ultra-low temperature DH networks. Others such as solar thermal and 
industrial waste heat can be used more efficiently than in traditional DH networks. In

Renewable and waste heat sources, which typically supply low-temperature heat, are now

reduce the environmental impact of heat production. Lowering the network operating 
temperature thus offers more cost-effective opportunities to reduce greenhouse gas

Most renewable and excess heat sources have inflexible production. Solar heat production 
is intermittent and depends entirely on solar radiation. Industrial excess heat follows 
production schedules of factories. It is therefore essential to integrate a few balancing 
sources to be able to meet the network demand in moments of low renewable and waste 
heat production. 
 
Finally, the combination of local heat sources which offers the best compromise between 
cost, energy performance and environmental impact will be preferred. It must also, as far 
as possible, take advantage of the availability of local heat sources in the considered area. 
 
1.2. 
Cooling sources 
While conventional networks are limited to meeting heating needs only, ultra-low 
temperature DHC systems are able to provide both heating and cooling through the same 
infrastructure. The available cooling sources are discussed below. 
 
1.2.1. Available cooling sources 
 
• Free cooling 
Bodies of water are frequently used as cooling sources in DC networks. Most DC projects 
in coastal areas are connected to cold water from oceans, lakes or rivers via heat exchangers 
[25].  
Free cooling should only be used if the chilled water source is reasonably close to the urban 
area. This avoids too much heat exchange with the outside environment in the transmission 
line and thus poor energy performance. 
Recent studies have shown that the use of such a cooling source in DC systems leads to 
low annual costs, significant energy savings and environmental benefits [65]. Moreover, it 
guarantees a high level of security of supply to end-users.  
 
• Absorption and adsorption chillers 
Absorption and adsorption chillers rely on thermo-chemical process. Both devices use heat 
to separate and recombine two fluids, creating a cooling effect. Their utilization appears to 
be particularly advantageous in ultra-low temperature DHC networks. 
Indeed, heat demand in summer is almost exclusively composed of DHW preparation. Many 
heat sources used in winter are therefore no longer fully exploited in summer days. Some 
of these sources can hence be used to operate absorption and adsorption chillers [25]. In 
==PAGE:143==
143 
 
particular, this makes possible to take advantage of non-balancing excess or renewable heat 
production in summer. The usage of inexpensive heat further makes this cooling solution 
economically attractive [66].  
 
• Ground source heat pump 
The energy flexibility of reversible heat pumps greatly favours their implementation in ultra-
low temperature DHC networks. Indeed, such a device absorbs heat from the ground and 
transfers it to the network in winter. In summer, the thermal flow is reversed, the ground 
is used as a heat sink to cool down the return loop [64]. 
The possibility for reversible heat pumps to deliver both heating and cooling improves their 
energy efficiency. However, energy savings are mostly offset by higher investment and 
operation costs, especially in countries with high electricity prices. 
This is why coupling reversible heat pumps with large thermal storages can ensure 
substantial economic benefits. Indeed, a cooling peak demand is usually accompanied by an 
overall increase in the electricity demand. The opportunity to store cool energy would 
therefore enable to shift the heat pump operation to periods with low electricity price, 
enhancing the system profitability.

1.3. 
Thermal energy storages 
Thermal Energy Storages (TES) will play a significant role in ultra-low temperature DH 
networks. TES are used to counterbalance the variations in energy demand. Thermal energy 
is stored when supply exceeds demand and is delivered later when extra thermal requests 
occur. It makes the mismatch between supply and demand smoother through the year. It 
allows to optimally exploit the local energy sources connected to the network.  
 
E. Guelpa et al. presented several benefits of coupling heat storages with DHC networks 
[67]. The utilization of storages helps to manage shifts in time between heat production 
and consumption as well as variations in renewable heat production such as solar. The 
system further gains in flexibility and primary energy efficiency when it integrates a TES. 
However, implementing thermal storages requires additional investment costs, often large 
dedicated spaces and a challenging connection design with the network.  
The promising perspective of combining cold TES with DHC network is also pointed out in 
this scientific paper. The cooling demand in buildings varies a lot in summer as it is mainly 
affected by the solar radiations. Cold storages could hence help the cooling sources to cover 
the peak load. This will avoid the heavy use of electric-driven cooling devices during peaks 
in electricity demand and price. 
 
1.3.1. Available thermal energy storages 
TES commonly used in DHC networks can be classified into three categories: sensible, 
latent and chemical storages [67]. Sensible storage is a widespread and mature technology. 
It consists in storing energy by means of temperature increase or decrease of a storage 
medium. Water is commonly used as storage medium because it offers the best compromise 
between physical properties, availability, environmental impact and price.  
Sensible TES can either be water tank for short-term purposes or large water pit for long-
term ones. This kind of storage is economically advantageous but suffers from high heat 
losses. Its low energy density usually leads to large storage volume, which requires a 
==PAGE:145==
145 
 
substantial land availability. To get rid of those negatives, underground TES such as aquifer 
or borehole can be considered, only if the geologic conditions are favourable.  
 
Latent heat storages are based on the phase change process of judiciously selected materials. 
Latent heat storages provide higher energy densities than sensible storages. Therefore, they 
allow to store the same amount of energy in reduced volumes.  
The material used should be chosen so that the phase change temperature is close to the 
network supply temperature. Coupled to ultra-low temperature DH networks, heat losses 
in latent heat storages are therefore minimized. Despite their higher investment cost, latent 
TES seems to be the most promising solution for ultra-low temperature DH networks, 
especially when land availability is reduced. 
 
E. Guelpa et al. briefly described the characteristics of chemical energy storages. It 
constitutes a very efficient way of storing thermal energy. However, the required 
temperature to activate most of the chemical processes is often unacceptably high compared 
to the ultra-low operating temperatures of the DHC networks.  
 
Other alternative storages in DHC systems are also identified in [67]. The network pipeline 
could be itself considered as an energy storage facility. The low temperature in the network 
nonetheless does not allow to store a lot of energy.  
Otherwise, the buildings envelope properties could be improved to smooth their heating and 
cooling demand profiles. This can be achieved by integrating phase change materials into 
the building envelope. Walls, floor and ceiling will hence be able to store excess heat or 
cold from the indoor climate and release it at the appropriate time. This technology seems 
very promising but remains very costly and unexplored for the moment. 
 
1.3.2. Optimal selection & sizing 
The most appropriate type of energy storage for a DHC network depends on many 
parameters such as the operating temperature of the system, the land availability in the 
urban area or the maximum amount of energy to be stored. Each DHC network has 
therefore its own most efficient storage solution [67]. 
 
Some articles deal with the optimal sizing of TES in DHC networks. M. Wirtz et al. 
proposed a new methodology for the design and optimization of the main energy conversion 
units, including hot heat storage, of DHC networks [68]. 
==PAGE:146==

The application to a real use case proved the key role of heat storage. The optimal size of 
the storage was determined in order to minimize the overall costs of the DHC network.  
In winter, the heat storage system is operated with multiple charge/discharge cycles to 
smooth out peaks in heat demand. It was also observed that the storage is used less 
frequently in summer, when heat demands are lower.
