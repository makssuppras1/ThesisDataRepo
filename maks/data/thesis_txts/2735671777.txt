Abstract
Quantum optical frequency conversion (QFC) is crucial in advancing quantum communication and
computing. Coherent frequency conversion facilitates an interconnection between quantum optical
systems operating at different wavelengths while maintaining their inherent quantum properties.
Four-wave mixing Bragg scattering (BS) has been proposed as an ideal candidate for QFC due to
its ability to imitate the similar three-wave mixing process, by having the advantage of being a
quadratic nonlinear interaction that is highly tunable and not constrained by material properties.
However, challenges arise in fiber-optical implementations due to noise photons induced by Raman
scattering.
This thesis theoretically studies the impact of Raman scattering on BS-induced QFC, present-
ing a full-vectorial model that encompasses instantaneous and noninstantaneous four-wave mixing
interactions. With continuous wave pumps, analytical solutions are obtained, giving rise to a
simple expression of the second-order correlation of the frequency-converted quantum field. This
study reveals a significant temperature dependence on the quality of the converted single-photons,
and it is found that a sufficiently low temperature and an appropriate choice of field polarizations
can mitigate the impact of Raman scattering. With a perturbative analysis of the quantum state
density matrix, the impact of a single phononic scattering event is studied, and a calculation of
the quantum state fidelity supports the findings of the second-order correlation.
A numerical model based on the split-step Fourier method is derived in a pulsed pump config-
uration. The temporal dynamics of the resulting Green functions are studied through a Schmidt
mode decomposition, which reveals how the noninstantaneous Raman response and higher-order
dispersion affect the frequency-converted modes. The numerical model lays the groundwork for
further studies involving Raman scattering and dispersion effects. Overall, this investigation con-
tributes to a comprehensive understanding of Raman scattering effects on BS-induced QFC, paving
the way for improved applications in quantum communication and computing.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Quantum frequency conversion (QFC) holds immense potential for the future of quantum com-
munication and computing. In quantum communication, it is crucial as it enables the transfer
of photonic quantum states in existing optical networks with minimal loss, protecting the frag-
ile quantum states [1, 2] and facilitating interconnection between systems operating at different
wavelengths [3]. In quantum computing, the manipulation of single photons at the quantum level
is foundational [4, 5], and QFC thereby offers an extra degree of freedom for altering the quantum
states [6]. QFC generally relies on two methods [7]: three-wave mixing through sum-frequency
generation (SFG) and four-wave mixing through Bragg scattering (BS), being a second- and
third-order nonlinear interaction, respectively. Both methods translate a single photon through a
beamsplitter-like interaction, which is essential for quantum state preservation of the photon, as
this does not introduce noise [8]. Additionally, the beamsplitter-like interaction of the two QFC
methods holds the potential to mediate interference between photons of different frequencies,
which is an essential building block within linear-optical quantum computing [9].
SFG translates a signal photon through a nonlinear interaction mediated by a high-power
pump, whereby the signal photon (s) and a pump photon (p) are annihilated in favor of creating
an idler photon (i), such that energy conservation ωp = ωs−ωi is obeyed. SFG benefits from being
a second-order process, as χ(2) is usually prominent in common semiconductors, which gives rise
to high conversion efficiencies within short interaction lengths [10, 11]. However, SFG lacks spec-
tral tunability since the frequency of the pump limits the translation distance and is restricted to
materials that do not possess inversion symmetry [12]. BS, on the other hand, relies on four-wave
mixing in which two pumps mediate the nonlinear interaction, whereby a signal photon and a
pump photon (q) are eliminated in favor of creating an idler photon, and a second pump photon
(p), such that energy conservation ωp −ωs = ωq −ωi is obeyed, allowing arbitrary translation
intervals [13]. Bragg scattering has been studied extensively in the context of QFC demonstrat-
ing preservation of single-photon statistics [7], high conversion efficiencies in both microring res-
onators [14] and optical fibers [15, 16], and quantum interference between spectrally separated
single-photons [17, 18]. However, fiber-optical implementations suffer from noise-photons induced

wave (CW) pumps, and numerical analysis is performed in a pulsed pump configuration afterward.
In this chapter, we start by giving a brief introduction to FWM BS, Raman scattering, and the
properties of single photons.
1.1
Four-wave mixing Bragg scattering
Four-wave mixing is a nonlinear interaction between four optical fields mediated by the electrons of
the material in which they propagate. The interaction induces a material polarization, described
by the third-order expansion of the polarization in the displacement field
P(3) = η(3) : DDD ,
(1.1)
where D is the displacement field, η(3) = χ(3)/ε3 becomes the well-known third-order suscepti-
bility tensor in an amorphous material [24], and ε is the material permittivity. The polarization
can be interpreted as the interaction between three displacement fields that generates a fourth
field through the induced polarization, effectively creating an interaction between the four fields.
Several types of interactions can happen depending on the configuration of the four fields. Here,
we are only be concerned with interactions that are phase-matched and energy-conserving in a
Bragg scattering configuration, as depicted in fig. 1.1. These interactions are nonlinear phase
modulation (NPM), covering self-phase modulation (SPM) and cross-phase modulation (XPM).
Bragg scattering is the photon-conserving process in which a signal photon (s) is annihilated to

1.2
Raman scattering
Raman scattering is the inelastic scattering between photons and phonons, with the phonons being
the assessed material’s atomic or molecular thermal vibrations. A single scattering event can result
in either absorption or emission of a phonon, leaving the photon with a new frequency that is larger
or smaller than the initial frequency. These two events are conventionally referred to as anti-Stokes
and Stokes scattering, respectively, as illustrated in fig. 1.2(a). Raman scattering can be classified
into spontaneous, stimulated, and Raman-induced FWM. Spontaneous and stimulated Raman
scattering are similar processes, as these arise from a photon with frequency ω, interacting with
a phonon that transfers energy equivalent to ±Ωto the photon, which ends up with frequency
ω ± Ω. However, the spontaneous process is stochastic, and the stimulated scattering is excited
by a photon already present at ω ± Ω. Raman-induced FWM is when a phonon excited from a
previous scattering event is eliminated under a new scattering event, acting as an energy messenger,
effectively mediating the scattering between four photons. Through Raman-induced FWM, all of
the same processes as described in the previous section are possible, with the significant difference
being that Raman-induced FWM is a noninstantaneous process due to the relatively slow response

1.3
Properties of single-photons
Several methods for generating single-photons have been studied, in particular spontaneous para-
metric down-conversion (SPDC) [30], spontaneous FWM (SpFWM) [31], and quantum dot (QD)
emission [26]. SPDC and SpFWM are based on a nonlinear interaction between a pump field with

a χ(2) and χ(3) material, respectively. These methods are spontaneous and, therefore, not deter-
ministic; however, this may be accounted for by heralding techniques. Since SPDC and SpFWM
both rely on the instantaneous nonlinear electronic response of a continuous medium, the temporal
and spectral properties of the emerging single-photons are virtually tunable [32, 33]. QD emission
is based on the discrete energy levels in a three-dimensional quantum well, for which, at cryogenic
temperatures, only the ground state is populated. Through electrical or optical stimulation, a
ground state electron is excited, after which the electron decays back to the ground state within
a time scale of nanoseconds [34], and in the process, emits a single-photon. Thereby, the QD
emission happens deterministically. However, the spectral and temporal properties are limited by
the decay mechanism of the QD.

advanced than others, the produced single photons must meet specific criteria to truly qualify as
single photons. Most importantly, a single photon is not correlated with other photons. This is
characterized by the second-order correlation function [35]

1.5
Thesis outline
This thesis aims to develop a model that describes quantum frequency conversion by Bragg scat-
tering in an optical waveguide under the influence of Raman scattering and to use this model to
understand how the presence of Raman scattering alters the BS QFC.
Chapter 2 derives the equations of motion for the four interacting fields from the appropriate
quantum field Hamiltonian. Chapter 3 solves the field equations in a regime where the pumps are
treated as CW sources, which gives rise to analytical solutions that can be analyzed rigorously.
Additionally, a perturbative density matrix is derived, which gives rich insight into the behavior
of a single scattering event. Chapter 4 derives a set of classical Green function equations from

3.1.2
Summary
This section has found analytical solutions to the equations governing the signal and idler. The
solutions found are similar to results from the literature but adapted to the presence of Raman
scattering, introducing an inherent frequency dependence. The propagation length resulting in
complete conversion was derived, as it was shown that Raman scattering can either increase or
decrease the required length due to Raman-induced BS depending on the real part of the spectral
Raman response.
Furthermore, an analytical expression of the frequency-converted spectrum
was derived, from which we could discern the physical effects contributing to the idler field. In
particular, the effects are spontaneous and stimulated Raman scattering and Raman-induced BS.
It is important to note that stimulated Raman scattering of the spontaneously scattered light
takes place, which gives rise to an exponential increase (Ω> 0) or decrease (Ω< 0) of the
spontaneous Raman photons as a function of the nonlinear interaction strength γP0ℓ. Using the
developed intuition regarding Raman scattering, we analyze the photon statistics characterized
by the second-order correlation function in the following.

3.2.1
Summary
In this section, we determined the second-order correlation of the frequency-converted single-
photon. The resulting g(2)(0) was shown to be bounded by a value of 2 and, more importantly,
goes towards 2 for ℓ→0, indicating that spontaneous Raman photons initially dominate the idler
field. As the photon is converted, the second-order correlation drops significantly, implying the
presence of a single photon. The g(2)(0) value depends considerably on the choice of temperature,
frequency detuning, and polarization configuration. We showed that by choosing an anti-Stokes
configuration and decreasing the temperature, the g(2)(0) value can be decreased substantially.
Furthermore, a simple semi-asymptotic expression for the second-order correlation was derived.
This expression can readily estimate a particular configuration’s expected g(2)(0) value. Finally,
it was shown that second-order correlation depends highly on the time-bandwidth product of the
single-photon, indicating that the single-photon shape impacts the accumulated Raman photons.

Boundaries of causality
Lines of causality inherently bound the cross-Green function for a pure electronic response due to
the instantaneous nature of the electrons. In the following, we use this to visualize the impact of
noninstantaneous Raman response and temporal broadening caused by second-order dispersion.
The causality lines are given by
ts = tin + ¯β1sℓ,
(4.42a)
ti = tin + ¯β1iℓ,
(4.42b)
where tin is the input time, and ts and ti are the corresponding output times defining the lines.
The first causality line ts can be interpreted as the output time for a photon that travels as a
signal throughout the fiber and is converted at the fiber end. The second causality line ti can be
interpreted as the output time of a photon that is converted at the fiber input and thereby travels
as an idler throughout the fiber. ts is, therefore, the latest time at which a photon can arrive, and
ti is the earliest time at which a photon can arrive due to the arrangement of the group velocities.
From the causality, it is possible to determine the expected time deviation in the arrival time of
a photon, which is given by δtout = |β1s −β1i|ℓ. Thereby, it is also clear that when the signal and
idler fields co-propagate, the cross-Green function is proportional to a delta function, implying
that all signal shapes are converted equally well.

4.3.3
Second-order dispersion
With pulse durations on the order of the Raman response, dispersion effects play a crucial role
in the propagation dynamics.
In particular, the combined effects of dispersion and nonlinear
interactions increasingly complicate the frequency conversion.
Here, we focus on the effect of
second-order dispersion described by β2 and use the parameters given in fig. 4.1.
The pure
effect of β2 is pulse broadening due to different frequency components having different velocities.
In combination with nonlinear phase modulation, new effects arise, in particular optical wave
breaking (broadening in the regime before breaking) [48], pulse compression [49], and XPM-
induced temporal reflection [50]. Due to our choice of sign for γ > 0, we expect pulse broadening
in the anomalous dispersion regime (β2 < 0) and pulse compression in the normal dispersion
regime (β2 > 0), i.e., for the q- and p-pump respectively. XPM-induced temporal reflection is
a phenomenon in which a high power pulse, with a steep slope, modulates the refractive index,
which causes a second pulse to reflect off the index modulation [50]. Since it is both a temporal
and spatial reflection, the process requires the presence of β2 to obey energy and momentum
conservation [51].

4.3.4
Summary
In this section, we computed the cross-Green function using the SSF model derived in the previous
section. We initially showed that the model correctly predicts the Green function in the low con-
version regime. Knowing that the model works, we started investigating the qualitative features of
the BS Green function in three regimes. First, we investigated a pure electronic response. Within
the high conversion regime, an interference pattern emerges due to a difference in the acquired
phase of the fields. The interference could be interpreted as vacillations in the frequency conver-
sion direction. Next, we included Raman scattering and observed that pure phononic scattering
was necessary to observe a significant effect. Here, the noninstantaneous response revealed itself
as a delay in the frequency conversion. However, increasing the pulse duration reduced the delay.
Finally, we included second-order dispersion, for which the pump propagation was considerably
distorted for short pulses, an effect that also became apparent for the cross-Green function. How-
ever, the effect was reduced substantially for longer pulse durations due to decreased dispersion
length. More importantly, we observed that second-order dispersion does not reduce the conver-
sion probability by any significant amount, as the primary frequency conversion happens on a
length scale sufficiently shorter than the dispersion length.

Conclusion and outlook
In this chapter, we wrap up the efforts undertaken in this thesis. We begin by discussing some of
the limitations of the derived model, proceed to a general conclusion, and conclude by exploring
potential applications of the current model in future research.
5.1
Model limitations
The model presented in this thesis is general in that it incorporates both instantaneous and non-
instantaneous NPM effects, arbitrary dispersion, mode profiles, and field polarizations. However,
the approximations applied throughout impose some limitations of the model, which is relevant
to consider. Firstly, the derivation of the Raman Hamiltonian assumed that the two sets of fields
(s,p) and (i,q) are sufficiently separated such that Raman scattering between q and s, and p and
i is not possible. The model is, therefore, limited to describing a far-conversion configuration and
extending the model would require an alternative derivation of the equations of motion.
The semi-classical approximation was found to eliminate cascaded Raman scattering, which is
a genuine effect observed in a full quantum model [40], in particular in a regime with a significant
nonlinear interaction, as is BS. The exclusion of this effect in the model is potentially an important
drawback, as our model implies the absence of spontaneous Raman photons outside the Raman
bandwidth. With knowledge of the behavior of cascaded Raman scattering, the model could be
extended by including the effect appropriately in the spontaneous Raman spectrum. Otherwise,
treating the problem with a full quantum model is necessary.
Additionally, the model has neglected intrinsic losses, which could significantly impact long
propagation distances, resulting in loss of the single photon or dephasing of the quantum state.
The impact of frequency-dependent loss within the pumps also introduces an NPM contribution
to the phase-matching condition, as was shown in the CW regime, thereby reducing the conversion
efficiency. However, since we have been concerned with silica fibers, we do not expect the losses
to have a considerable impact, and even further, the loss can be mitigated by decreasing the
interaction length through a higher pump power.

5.2. CONCLUSION
The model presented in the CW regime incorporates arbitrary dispersion effects; however, in
deriving the spectrum and second-order correlation, we assumed that the dispersion for the signal
and idler is identical. This approximation is only valid, assuming that second- and higher-order
dispersion is negligible, such that the fields co-propagate.
5.2
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have investigated the impact of Raman scattering on quantum frequency conver-
sion induced by Bragg scattering four-wave mixing. Starting from the appropriate Hamiltonian
and a mode-field expansion containing the four fields of interest, we derived the quantized Hamil-
tonian describing Bragg scattering and Raman scattering. Through the Heisenberg equation of
motion, we derived the evolution of the phonons, which was found to depend on the optical fields,
which in turn gives rise to a noninstantaneous FWM contribution. In deriving the pump equa-
tions, we performed a semi-classical approximation, which decouples the classical fields from the
quantum fields. Additionally, to incorporate higher-order dispersion effects, we found it necessary
to perform a dispersion transformation of the differential operators, such that the slow variations
were transformed from the temporal coordinate to the longitudinal spatial coordinate. The re-
sulting classical equations were found to be the well-known nonlinear Schrödinger equations with
temporal dispersion. The quantum field equations were similarly derived through the Heisen-
berg equation; however, with the aid of Feynmann diagrams, we developed intuition on how the
coupling between phonons and photons gives rise to the Raman scattering effects.
We started the investigations by treating the pumps as continuous waves. We found it possible
to derive analytical solutions of the quantum fields, from which we could determine the frequency-
converted spectrum and the second-order correlation function. From the analytical expressions,
we could discern the physical effects contributing to the idler field. Even though stimulated Raman
scattering causes a significant loss on the anti-Stokes side of the pump, we found that the primary
deterioration of the quantum state originates from spontaneous Raman scattering. We showed this
based on a semi-asymptotic approximation of the second-order correlation function, which only
incorporated spontaneous Raman scattering and approximated the exact expression reasonably
well. Thereby, the impact of Raman scattering is mitigated primarily by lowering the tempera-
ture of the material. Additionally, the semi-asymptotic expression revealed that the second-order
correlation depends on the time-bandwidth product of the single-photon shape. Moreover, we de-
termined the density matrix of the resulting idler field in the perturbative regime, corresponding
to the quantum state resulting from a single scattering event in a nonlinear fiber. This approach
gave detailed insight into the phonon-photon interactions. Using the density matrix, we deter-
mined the fidelity of the resulting quantum state, which exhibited the significance of the Raman
gain function. In particular, this derivation showed that the second-order correlation function
sufficiently describes the effects that a perturbation approach can explain.
Finally, we investigated the frequency conversion dynamics in a pulsed pump configuration
with pulse durations on the order of the Raman response. To do so, we derived a Green function

model, which could easily be solved using the well-known split-step Fourier method. This model
is a general method for studying the dynamics of the quantum frequency conversion, including
Raman scattering and higher-order dispersion. Using the model, we computed the Green function
in a symmetric collision scheme, from which we gained intuition regarding a pure electronic re-
sponse. Including Raman scattering, we saw that the shape of the Green functions was inherently
changed due to the noninstantaneous response. However, as the pulse duration was increased, the
impact became less significant because the noninstantaneous response started to mimic an instan-
taneous response. Finally, we implemented second-order dispersion, which significantly changed
the pump propagation. The Green functions were affected by this through a complicated interfer-
ence pattern. Moreover, temporal broadening of the signal and idler fields resulted in the Green

by increasing the pulse duration.
5.3
Further work
The model derived in this thesis serves as a starting point for future investigations. In particular,
including other parasitic effects, such as fiber diameter fluctuations, is relevant. We showed that
the effect of Raman scattering can be considerably reduced by increasing the frequency detuning
beyond the Raman bandwidth. However, fiber diameter fluctuations induce a significant sensitivity
on the conversion efficiency in this regime [28]. Therefore, understanding how the combined effects
of Raman scattering and dispersion fluctuations impact the QFC process is of utmost importance.
The model derived in this thesis is easily extended to include these effects and can serve as a
starting point for future investigations of parasitic effects.
The Green functions studied in the numerical model only incorporate stimulated Raman scat-
tering, while spontaneous Raman scattering enters through the Heisenberg operator eq. (4.16).
It is, therefore, relevant to study how the quantum fields are affected by spontaneous Raman
scattering in the pulsed regime.
Since it enters as a convolution between the Green function
and the Raman response, the shape of the Green function could impact the amount of scattered
light. Previous studies have quantified the shape of the Green function through the selectivity
Schmidt modes [53, 54], which describes the number of Schmidt modes necessary to capture the
Green function. Therefore, One relevant study could explore how selectivity affects the amount
of spontaneous Raman photons and, if it does, whether it is possible to mitigate the effect by
appropriately engineering the pumps.
The combination of the analytical and numerical models presented in this thesis is a general
tool for future investigations. Even though the investigations here have focused on an optical fiber
of silica and linearly polarized modes, the model is easily adapted to describing other materials
and arbitrary polarization modes by adequately choosing the parameters and response functions.
The analytical expression may serve as approximations and guidelines for designing a proper
frequency conversion experiment, while the numerical model can correctly predict the resulting
frequency-converted modes.

E.3. SECOND-ORDER DISPERSION
E.3.1
Symmetric behaviour of cross-Green function
The extra frequency components can be understood by considering the phasematching condition,
β(ωs) −β(ωp) −β(ωi) + β(ωq) = 0
(E.1)
which we know is fulfilled for the above frequencies. However, now we search for other frequencies
that are phasematched for the same pump configuration. This is done by adding δω to the signal
and idler frequencies,
β(ωs + δω) −β(ωp) −β(ωi + δω) + β(ωq) = 0
(E.2)
Taylor expanding β to second order around the respective center frequencies, i.e. ωs, ωp, ωi, and
ωq, it is found that
β1sδω + 1
2β2sδω2 −β1iδω −1
2β2iδω2 = 0
(E.3)
Solving for δω we find,
δω = −2(β1s −β1i)
β2s −β2i
(E.4)
Thereby it is found that there is phasematching for a second frequency configuration displaced by
δω with respect to the signal and idler center frequencies.
