Abstract 
The identification of biological targets of small molecules is an important task in drug discovery. 
Some of the most used target identification methods have shortcomings, for example that they usually 
can only be performed on cell lysates and not live cells, so the discovery of new methods can lead to 
breakthroughs within pharmaceutical research. A potential new target identification strategy is based 
on proteolysis targeting chimeras (PROTACs). A PROTAC is a molecule made up of a ligand that 
can bind to a protein of interest, a ligand that can bind an E3-ligase, and a linker coupling the former 
two together. By bringing the protein of interest close to the E3-ligase, the PROTAC can facilitate an 
accelerated and selective degradation of the protein of interest. A target identification strategy based 
on PROTACs can be applied to live cells, and the degraded proteins can be identified using mass 
spectrometry-based proteomics methods. 
Using PROTACs for target identification can be applied to a wide range of small molecules. One 
class of molecules for which target identification could prove interesting is oxysterols. Oxysterols 
have been known to play a role in cholesterol homeostasis, but in recent years they have been found 
to be implicated in many other biological functions, and there are still new functions to be discovered. 
Carrying out PROTAC-based target identification for oxysterols would help discover new biological 
functions of these essential molecules as well test PROTAC-based target identification in practice. 
In this project, the concept of using PROTACs for target identification was tested in practice by syn-
thesizing a range of PROTACs based on the VHL E3-ligase ligand and a sterol, either cholesterol or 
4𝛽-hydroxy cholesterol, linked together via alkyl or polyethylene glycol linkers. Due to previous 
research showing cholesterol-based PROTACs to degrade the protein GPP130 via lysosomal degra-
dation, the synthesized PROTACs were tested by western blot to assess their ability to degrade 
GPP130. This was done to figure out what part of the PROTAC molecule was responsible for the 
lysosomal degradation. All the cholesterol based PROTACs downregulated GPP130, while this was 
only true for one of the 4𝛽-hydroxy cholesterol based PROTACs. This, in combination with previous 
experiments, led to the conclusion that the sterol-linker part of the PROTAC was likely responsible 
for the GPP130 degradation.

Resumé 
Det at identificere hvilke biologiske mål, som små molekyler binder til, er en vigtig opgave indenfor 
lægemiddelforskning. Nogle af de mest brugte målidentifikationsmetoder har dog nogle ulemper; for 
eksempel kan de kun bruges på cellelysater og altså ikke hele, levende celler. Så nye metoder ville 
potentielt kunne føre til gennembrud inden for lægemiddelforskning. En mulig ny målidentifikations-
metode er baseret på såkaldte proteolysis targeting chimeras (PROTACs). En PROTAC er et mole-
kyle, der består af en ligand, som kan binde til et givent protein, en ligand der kan binde til en E3-
ligase, og en linker der binder de to førnævnte sammen. Ved at bringe det givne protein og E3-ligasen 
i tæt kontakt kan PROTAC-molekylet facilitere en accelereret og selektiv nedbrydning af det givne 
protein. Målidentifikationsstrategien baseret på PROTACs kan bruges på hele, levende celler, og de 
nedbrudte proteiner kan identificeres ved brug af massespektrometri-proteomics metoder. 
Målidentifikation via PROTACs kan bruges på en bred vifte af små molekyler. En bestemt klasse af 
molekyler, hvor målidentifikation kunne vise sig interessant, er oxysteroler. Man har vidst, at oxyste-
roler spiller en rolle i cholesterol homøostase, men i de seneste år har forskning vist, at oxysteroler 
også spiller en rolle i mange andre biologiske processer, og det er langt fra sikkert, at alle oxystero-
lernes egenskaber er opdaget. PROTAC-baseret målidentifikation af oxysteroler ville kunne bruges 
til at opdage nye biologiske funktioner for disse essentielle molekyler og samtidig teste PROTAC-
baseret målidentifikation i praksis. 
I dette projekt blev PROTAC-baseret målidentifikation testet i praksis ved at syntetisere en række 
PROTACs baseret på VHL E3-ligase liganden og en sterol, enten cholesterol eller 4𝛽-hydroxycho-
lesterol, bundet til hinanden med alkyl eller polyethylen glykol linkere. Da tidligere forskning havde 
vist, at cholesterol-baserede PROTACs kunne nedbryde proteinet GPP130 via lysosomal nedbryd-
ning, blev de syntetiserede PROTACs testet via western blot for at vurdere deres evne til at nedbryde 
GPP130. Dette blev gjort for at finde ud af, hvilken del af PROTAC-molekylet, der var ansvarlig for 
GPP130-nedbrydningen. Alle de cholesterol-baserede PROTACs forårsagede nedbrydning af 
GPP130, mens det kun var tilfældet for én af de 4𝛽-hydroxycholesterol-baserede PROTACs. De re-
sultater, sammenholdt med resultaterne fra tidligere forsøg, førte til den konklusion, at det sandsyn-
ligvis var sterol-linker delen af PROTAC-molekylet, der førte til nedbrydning af GPP130.

1    Introduction 
In this section, the theoretical background for this project is presented along with previous work car-
ried out by the Laraia group. Specifically, the ubiquitin-proteasome degradation of proteins is exam-
ined along with proteolysis targeting chimeras, which utilize this system for targeted protein degra-
dation. Oxysterols are examined as well, and target identification is briefly investigated, with affinity 
chromatography and thermal proteome profiling being highlighted. All these topics are then com-
bined to discuss how PROTACs can be used for target identification and how the Laraia group has 
tried to use this concept to identify the protein targets of oxysterols. 
1.1    The ubiquitination-proteasome degradation of proteins 
Protein degradation is an essential process in human cells. It is a process that is required for the 
removal of misfolded or damaged proteins as well as proteins that the cell does not need under given 
circumstances. Essentially, protein degradation together with protein synthesis is what regulates the 
protein concentration in all tissue to maintain protein homeostasis1. While there are several different 
protein degradation processes such as degradation by lysosomes and cysteine proteases to name a 
few, most intracellular proteins are degraded via the ubiquitin proteasome system (UPS)2. 
The UPS is a pathway in which three types of ligases, an E1-, an E2-, and an E3-ligase, work together 
to tag a protein substrate with ubiquitin molecules in an enzymatic cascade2-6. Ubiquitin is a peptide 
consisting of 76 amino acids which can be covalently linked through its C-terminal glycine to a lysine 
residue on a protein substrate. Subsequently, a lysine of this ubiquitin can be linked to another ubiq-
uitin molecule to form a ubiquitin chain5. When the ubiquitin chain has grown to contain four or more 
ubiquitin molecules, the protein substrate can be recognized by the 26S proteasome which is a large 
proteasome found in the cytosol and nucleus of the cell7. In an ATP (adenosine triphosphate)-depend-
ent process, the 26S proteasome removes the ubiquitin chain from the substrate protein, releasing free 
ubiquitin back into the cell medium to participate in ubiquitin tagging again, followed by linearization 
and digestion of the target protein into small peptide chains. These smaller peptide chains are lastly 
broken down into the constituent amino acids by other proteasomes in the cell or used in antigen 
presentation mechanisms of the immune system2. 
The tagging of proteins targeted for degradation by ubiquitin is important, but how does this happen 
in practice? The process is illustrated in Figure 1, but a written description is as follows: An enzymatic 
cascade is carried out by three ligases, the first of which is the E1-ligase. The E1-ligase activates the 
==PAGE:14==
Page 2 
 
ubiquitin at its C-terminal glycine by forming a thioester between the catalytic cysteine residue of the 
E1-ligase and ubiquitin glycine in a reaction that requires ATP3. The E1-ligase then transfers the 
ubiquitin to the catalytic cysteine residue of an E2-ligase, and lastly an E3-ligase can bind to this E2-
ligase as well as the target protein forming a complex in which the transfer of ubiquitin from the E2-
ligase to the protein can take place2,3. This whole process is repeated until four to five ubiquitin mol-
ecules have been attached to the protein being targeted for degradation since the 26S proteasome, as 
already mentioned, recognizes and breaks down proteins with at least four ubiquitin molecules.

ligase is followed by transfer of ubiquitin to an E2-ligase which via an E3-ligase transfers the ubiquitin to the 
target protein. The process is repeated to yield a ubiquitin-chain. Reproduced from D. Nandi et al5.

It is worth mentioning that the mechanism of ubiquitin transfer from the E2-ligase to the target protein 
differs depending on the type of E3-ligase catalysing the process. The E3-ligases can be divided into 
three subcategories: The Homologous to E6Ap Carboxy Terminus (HECT) ligases, the Really Inter-
esting New Gene (RING) ligases, and the UFD2 homology (U-box) proteins6. The HECT ligases 
catalyse the transfer of ubiquitin from the E2-ligase to the target protein via a thioester intermediate 
formed by the active-site cysteine of the E3-ligase and the C-terminus of the ubiquitin. Meanwhile, 
the RING ligases and U-box proteins orient the E2-ligase and target protein to mediate the direct 
transfer of ubiquitin from the E2-ligase to the protein8-11. The two different mechanisms are shown 
in Figure 2, and one can note that while the HECT ligases participate directly in the ubiquitin-transfer, 
RING ligases and U-box proteins act more like a scaffold bringing the E2-ligase and protein together 
in such a way as to facilitate the transfer. 
 
Figure 2: The different mechanisms for ubiquitin transfer between E2-ligase and the target protein/substrate. 
a: The HECT E3-ligases carry out the transfer by binding the ubiquitin to an E3 cysteine residue followed by 
transfer to the substrate. b: The RING E3-ligases mediate direct ubiquitin transfer. Note that U-box proteins 
follow this mechanism as well. Reproduced from R. J. Deshais and C. A. P. Joazeiro11. 
From the above description of the UPS process, it is not apparent how the whole process is controlled. 
It could seem that all proteins are degraded equally, but this is far from the case as the ligases are very 
specific enzymes. In mammals, there are two known members of the E1-ligase family, approximately 
==PAGE:16==
Page 4 
 
40 members of the E2-ligase family, and more than 600 members of the E3-ligase family3. The large 
variation of the E2- and E3-ligases is what brings about the specificity of UPS process. Specific E3-
ligases will only bind certain proteins, and specific E2-ligases will only conjugate to certain E3-lig-
ases in a way that can bring about the ubiquitin transfer. Cells can therefore adjust the concentration 
of a protein by up- or downregulating the expression of the ligases responsible for its degradation2. 
1.2    Proteolysis-targeting chimeras: Hijacking ubiquitination for therapeutic use 
While the traditional drug development process, focused on identification and optimization of mole-
cules exhibiting activity towards a biological target, has yielded many pharmaceutical breakthroughs, 
the inherent problems with this process has meant that other approaches are being examined. Even 
for well-described classes of protein targets, the identification of a therapeutic agent is seldom 
straightforward, and the issue of maintaining a drug concentration that is sufficient to provoke a ther-
apeutic effect is also a challenge. In response, other technologies such as monoclonal antibodies and 
protein agents have been designed for pharmaceutical use, but as is the case with most biomolecules, 
these agents cannot traverse the cell membrane and can therefore only be used on extracellular tar-
gets12. While research into overcoming this limitation of biomolecular therapeutics is ongoing and 
has resulted in useful therapeutics based on smaller biomolecules, as evidenced by the use of lipid-
based nanoparticles for RNA-based therapeutics13 such as the mRNA-vaccines used against Covid-
1914, small-molecule based drugs are currently the best option when it comes to inhibiting or agonis-
ing intracellular targets. However, can small molecules be used to take control of the efficient bio-
logical processes in cells like the UPS? The answer is yes: Using so-called proteolysis targeting chi-
meras (abbreviated PROTACs) makes the selective degradation of protein targets possible. 
PROTACs as a concept has seen significant development in the 2010’s, and PROTACs are now con-
sidered a class of molecules which has the potential to produce highly selective therapeutics. 
1.2.1    PROTACs: What are they conceptually and structurally 
PROTACs are molecules that can bring a target protein and an E3-ligase close to each other so that 
ubiquitin transfer can be facilitated15-18. Without the PROTAC, protein degradation proceeds at a 
normal rate but bringing the target protein and E3-ligase together in a ternary complex will accelerate 
the ubiquitination leading to the protein’s degradation by the 26S proteasome. The principle is show-
cased in Figure 3, and as the figure suggests, the PROTAC is catalytic in nature. When one target 
protein has been degraded, the PROTAC is released again after which the cycle can be repeated. 
==PAGE:17==
Page 5 
 
From a chemical point of view, a PROTAC is a bifunctional molecule consisting of three things: A 
ligand that can bind to the target protein, a ligand that can bind to an E3-ligase, and a chemical linker 
binding the former two together16-18. While the importance of the two ligands is somewhat self-ex-
planatory, the importance of the linker should not be underestimated. The choice of linker is very 
important for the formation of the ternary complex since the wrong type of linker can make the ubiq-
uitination geometrically impossible12,16,19,20. Furthermore, the linker also effect the pharmacokinetic 
properties of the PROTAC like aqueous solubility, stability, and cellular uptake12. An example of a 
linker used in a PROTAC can be seen in Figure 4a, where the structure of the bromodomain-contain-
ing protein 4 (BRD4) selective PROTAC, ARV-825, is shown21. A polyethylene glycol linker was 
chosen based on its high conformational flexibility which would increase the chance of the PROTAC 
associating with both the target BRD4 and the E3-ligase cereblon (CRBN). The degradation of BRD4 
is desirable when treating cancers seeing that BRD4 plays a pivotal role in gene expression, so ARV-
825 is an anticancer PROTAC.

close to a protein of interest (POI) ubiquitinating the POI. After degradation of the POI, the PROTAC is re-
leased and can repeat the cycle. Reproduced from M. Toure and C. M. Crews18.

In relation to the PROTAC ARV-825, it was mentioned that ARV-825 recruited the E3-ligase CRBN. 
The nature of the E3-ligase targeted by the PROTAC is a little interesting in that even though more 
than 600 E3-ligases are known, only a few E3-ligases can currently be recruited by PROTACs since 
not a lot of E3-ligase ligands have been identified. The four E3-ligases most commonly targeted by 
PROTACs are murine double minute 2 (MDM2), Von Hippel-Lindau (VHL), cell inhibitor of apop-
tosis protein (cIAP) and CRBN15,18,19,22, although most reported PROTACs have targeted primarily 
VHL or CRBN17,20. The respective ligands of the four E3-ligases are shown in Figure 4b.

1.2.2    Advantages and disadvantages of PROTACs 
There are several advantages to using PROTACs as opposed to using traditional inhibition-based 
drugs. As has already been mentioned during the conceptual description of PROTACs, they are cat-
alytic in nature. Traditional small molecule therapeutics typically work in a 1-to-1 mechanism in 
which one small molecule can only bind to (and thus inhibit) one target molecule. To drive the full 
inhibition of enough target molecules to produce a therapeutic effect, small molecules are therefore 
administered in high doses; something which increases the risk of side effects12. PROTACs on the 
other hand lead to the degradation of the target molecule after which the PROTAC is released again. 
This ensures that the PROTAC can turn over several target molecules. As a result, PROTACs are not 
dependent on high concentration: even a low concentration of PROTAC can maintain a sufficient rate 
of protein degradation which theoretically should limit the risk of side effects12,15,20.  
Another advantage of PROTACs is that they may have an increased duration of action. The duration 
of action for small molecule therapeutics depends on the equilibrium between the free drug and drug-
target complex. As the drug is metabolized by the body, the equilibrium will be shifted so that fever 
drugs participate in a drug-target complex leading to a diminished therapeutic effect over time. This 
is not the case for PROTACs. Since the target has been degraded, the duration of action for PROTACs 
depends on the recovery rate of the target protein. If the PROTAC can degrade the target protein 
faster than it is resynthesized in the cells, then the therapeutic effect will remain. If the protein is 
resynthesized slowly, even a small concentration of PROTAC may be able to maintain the degrada-
tion, and therefore the diminishing concentration of PROTAC over time is not necessarily a big issue. 
Even when the PROTAC concentration is too low to degrade the target protein faster than it is syn-
thesized, it may still take some time for the target protein concentration to reach its prior level12,15. 
It is also worth noting that PROTACs offer great selectivity and broad applicability. As mentioned in 
section 1.1, the UPS system, which PROTACs utilize, is a degradation system found in all tissue. The 
fact that the UPS system is at play in all cells means that PROTACs as a technology is widely appli-
cably, but more importantly the selectivity of the E2- and E3-ligases can be used to make PROTACs 
that selectively degrade one target protein. Designing selective therapeutics is a key focus in pharma-
ceutical development since high selectivity equals minimized binding to off-target molecules which 
may lead to unwanted side effects12. 
An additional advantage of PROTACs is that they may be used to treat drug targets that have other-
wise proven difficult to treat with conventional therapeutics. A small molecule inhibitor is dependent 
==PAGE:20==
Page 8 
 
on the target having a functionally active site which can be blocked. Finding a ligand which can 
selectively bind to the functionally active site may prove difficult, but it is not necessarily an issue 
for a PROTAC. A PROTAC can potentially bind to any site of the protein to facilitate the degrada-
tion12. Furthermore, some proteins may have several different functions and active sites and inhibiting 
one functionally active site may not prove sufficient. Once again, PROTACs overcome this issue via 
the complete degradation of the target protein15. 
There are also disadvantages when it comes to using PROTACs. For one, the PROTAC molecules 
are rather large and in general they break the rulesets established for orally available drugs such as 
Lipinski’s rule-of-five23. PROTACs often break most of Lipinski’s rules and as a result most studies 
carried out regarding PROTACs have involved parenteral delivery24. Seeing that oral delivery is the 
preferred administration method, designing orally bioavailable PROTACs is a challenge. And it is 
not only a challenge that must be solved due to oral absorption: the non-adherence to Lipinski’s rule-
of-five may also prove problematic with i.v. administration if the PROTACs cannot pass the cell 
membrane (and thus be taken up by the cell). 
 
Figure 5: Ternary complex formation as a function of PROTAC concentration. At too high PROTAC con-
centration, the system is saturated with PROTACs so that only binary complexes are formed. This is called 
the hook effect. Reproduced from M. Pettersson and C. M. Crews16. 
Another potential disadvantage is one which is also its advantage: The selectivity. Although the in-
herent selectivity of PROTACs is a desirable property for a therapeutic, it also makes PROTACs 
were challenging to design. Apart from finding a suitable ligand binding to the POI, the PROTACs 
==PAGE:21==
Page 9 
 
must also be designed to utilize the right E3-ligase (i.e., one that can degrade the POI), and the 
PROTACs must be able to form a ternary complex with the right geometry for ubiquitin transfer, 
something which is dependent on the linker. So, designing a PROTAC is not as simple as it may 
initially sound25,26. 
An additional challenge to designing PROTACs stems from the fact that the thermodynamics of a 
trimeric ternary complex is more complicated compared to the formation of the “simple” binary tar-
get-ligand complex seen for conventional small molecules. For PROTACs (and other bifunctional 
molecules in general), the formation of a ternary complex as a function of therapeutic concentration 
follows a bell-shaped curve as illustrated in Figure 5 in which the cause of this phenomenon is also 
shown. At high concentrations of PROTAC molecules, the system is saturated so that all E3-ligases 
and target proteins are complexed with PROTACs in binary complexes. With no free E3-ligase or 
target protein, these binary complexes cannot form the trimeric ternary complex resulting in no ubiq-
uitination nor any subsequent protein degradation12,16,17,20,26,27. 
1.3    Oxysterols 
Oxysterols are oxidized derivatives of cholesterol (CHO): in other words, they are sterols that have 
had a hydroxy group, a keto functionality or a third oxygen-containing functional group introduced28. 
Although oxysterols were initially thought to be simple metabolites of CHO, bringing about its met-
abolic elimination, and only playing a minor role in CHO homeostasis, recent research has shown 
that oxysterols are bioactive molecules themselves28-30. Oxysterols can be divided into two categories: 
Primary oxysterols are side-chain-modified or ring-modified oxysterols having undergone one mod-
ification, while secondary oxysterols have undergone more than one modification28. The modifica-
tions seen for secondary oxysterols are not necessarily two separate additions of oxygen-containing 
functional groups, it can also be modifications of the functional groups of primary oxysterols (e.g. 
oxidation of a hydroxy group to a ketone)31. 
==PAGE:22==
Page 10 
 
 
Figure 6: An assortment of different oxysterols based on CHO. The general numbering scheme for sterols 
has been applied to CHO. Reproduced from W. J. Griffiths, and Y. Wang29. 
CHO, as well as sterols in general, can in theory be oxygenated at many different carbon atoms of the 
sterol backbone and the side chain. In practice, the oxygenations typically occur at carbons 7, 24, 25, 
and 26/2728 (the carbon atoms of CHO are numbered in Figure 6), but the many possible oxygenation 
sites means that a lot of different oxysterols exist. An assortment of oxysterols based on CHO is 
shown in Figure 6, but as mentioned oxysterols can be derived from other sterols as well. All these 
oxysterols can potentially bind to selected targets29, and while the targets for various oxysterols have 
yet to be discerned30, some oxysterols have already been found to play key biological roles. 
Although CHO is central in regulating its own concentration, oxysterols have been found to play a 
significant part in CHO homeostasis, both on a transcriptional and post-transcriptional level31. On a 
transcriptional level, CHO homeostasis is governed by two regulators: sterol regulatory element bind-
ing protein (SREBP) and liver X receptor (LXR)28,32. SREBP controls the expression of multiple 
genes playing key roles in the synthesis of CHO while LXR regulates genes responsible for removing 
CHO from the cells. Oxysterols have been found to inhibit SREBP and activate LXR, in both cases 
==PAGE:23==
Page 11 
 
leading to diminishing CHO levels; an example of a specific oxysterol having this effect is 24S,25-
epoxycholesterol28. On a post-transcriptional level, oxysterols regulate CHO homeostasis by altering 
the stability of enzymes involved in CHO synthesis. An example is that side chain oxysterols promote 
the degradation of 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase31, which is the first rate-limiting en-
zyme of the CHO synthesis pathway28. 
Oxysterols have also been found to act as receptor ligands. For example, oxysterols have been found 
to interact with the Hedgehog signalling pathway29,31. Many oxysterols have been found to bind to 
the G protein-coupled receptor, Smoothened, which leads to downstream effects that are essential in 
embryonic development32. However, incorrect activation of the Hedgehog signalling pathway by 
oxysterols is undesirable as that can lead to cancer29. Similarly, some oxysterols have been found to 
act as ligands for estrogen receptors31, some of which can stimulate estrogen receptor dependent 
growth in breast cancer33. 
Lastly, oxysterols have been found to play a significant role in both the innate and adaptive immune 
system31. There are a variety of mechanisms by which oxysterols mediate the immune system34,35, 
one example being the antiviral effect of 25-hydroxycholesterol. During a viral infection, macro-
phages increase their expression and secretion of 25-hydroxycholesterol28 which has been shown to 
inhibit the CHO synthesis pathway and in turn inhibits the viral replication mechanism of infected 
cells34,35. 
1.4    Target identification for small molecules 
In the past, drugs have been used even though their precise mechanisms of action were unknown, but 
in recent decades more research has been carried out in the field of identifying the targets of bioactive 
molecules. This has been done to get a greater understanding of how bioactive compounds exert their 
therapeutic effect as well as identifying off-target binding which may lead to side effects36. In this 
section, two approaches to target identification based on different principles are presented. 
1.4.1    Affinity chromatography 
Affinity chromatography, also known as the pulldown method, is one of the most used and well-
established methods to target identification. The principle of affinity-based chromatography is shown 
in Figure 7. A pulldown probe is synthesized by immobilizing the ligand, for which the protein targets 
are determined, to a solid matrix via a linker. The probe is exposed to a protein extract, like a cell 
lysate, and the target proteins will then bind to the ligand. A stringent washing will remove all proteins 
==PAGE:24==
Page 12 
 
not binding to the ligand leaving only the target protein(s) behind37,38. The target protein(s) can then 
be liberated by denaturation through heating or via specific elution in which the matrix-linker-ligand-
bound proteins are treated with free ligand36. The proteins liberated are then identified using sodium 
dodecyl sulphate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) where the probe sample is run 
against a control sample. Comparison with a control probe is essential to differentiate between pro-
teins binding specifically to the ligand and proteins that bind non-specifically to the matrix itself (that 
is, proteins binding to the linker or resin used to immobilize the ligand). The control probe can be 
either the linker alone or an inactive ligand closely resembling the bioactive ligand, like an enantio-
mer36. 
 
Figure 7: The principle of affinity-based chromatography. Reproduced from S. Ziegler et al36. 
As mentioned, affinity chromatography is a well-established target identification method which has 
proven its worth several times, an example being in the identification of the primary targets of Tha-
lidomide which caused a significant number of birth defects in children back in the 1950s39. However, 
affinity chromatography has its challenges as well. While characterising high-affinity, high-abun-
dance proteins is rather straightforward, proteins that are either found in low abundance or have 
lower-to-medium binding affinity to the ligand are harder to characterise38. It is also worth noting that 
affinity chromatography cannot be used on live cells but require the cells to be lysed beforehand36. 
Furthermore, immobilizing the ligand so that it retains its binding affinity for the targets is also a 
challenge. The linker must be attached to the ligand in such a way that it does not interfere with the 
binding of the protein, something that can prove especially tricky if the ligand interacts with different 
proteins with different faces of the ligand surface36,37. 
 
==PAGE:25==
Page 13 
 
1.4.2    Thermal proteome profiling 
A newer method used for target identification is thermal proteome profiling which is based on the 
concept that a ligand-protein complex will exhibit a greater resistance towards thermal denaturation 
compared to the free protein40,41. As a result, the apparent melting temperature of the target protein(s) 
will shift to a higher temperature when treated with the ligand. This principle is shown in Figure 8. 
 
Figure 8: The principle behind thermal proteome profiling. The target protein is thermally stabilized by the 
ligand. Reproduced from A. Mateus et al41. 
The thermal stabilisation of target proteins can be used to identify ligand targets by exposing a cell 
culture or cell lysate treated with the ligand to a range of temperatures. At higher temperatures, the 
proteins will denature as shown in Figure 8, and these denatured proteins will aggregate and be in-
soluble in the medium. The proteins in the lower-temperature samples on the other hand will not 
denature and will remain in solution. The aggregated/insoluble proteins are removed using a centri-
fuge and the remaining proteins are digested and labelled using tandem mass tags. The labelling is 
carried out so that all the protein mixtures can be analysed by mass spectrometry (MS) simultaneously 
instead of having to carry out several MS experiments. The data gathered from the MS analysis can 
be used to construct melting curves for the various proteins in solution, and these melting curves can 
be compared to melting curves obtained from a control experiment in which the cell cultures or cell 
lysates were not treated with the ligand. The whole workflow of the thermal proteome profiling is 
shown in Figure 9. An important advantage of thermal proteome profiling which sets it apart from 
affinity chromatography is that it can be used on live cells. This allows for monitoring of a lot of 
processes that affinity chromatography cannot, for example thermal proteome profiling can be used 
to monitor uptake and activation of prodrugs41.

Page 14 
 
 
Figure 9: Scheme showing the workflow of thermal proteome profiling. Reproduced from C. Rossetti and L. 
Laraia42.  
1.5    Using PROTACs for target identification 
Given that PROTACs can bring about the selective degradation of protein targets, there is a theoret-
ical possibility that PROTACs can be used for target identification. If cells are treated with a 
PROTAC based on a bioactive ligand, the PROTAC should in theory cause the degradation of the 
protein targets of this bioactive ligand. So, if the targets of the bioactive ligand are unknown, a full 
proteomic study of the cells exposed to the PROTAC can be used to identify the targets. As elaborated 
upon in section 1.4, there are well-defined methods for target identification available already, but 
PROTAC-based target identification could be used to address the short-comings of these methods. 
For example, PROTAC-based target identification can be carried out on live cells, something which 
is not possible for affinity chromatography. 
==PAGE:27==
Page 15 
 
It should be noted that although PROTACs can theoretically be used as a tool in target identification, 
it should be seen as a supplement to other target identification methods like those described in section 
1.4. As mentioned in section 1.1, the UPS is a very specific process mediated by around 600 E3-
ligases, so degradation and subsequent identification of protein targets is dependent on the PROTAC 
binding to the right E3-ligase and forming the right ternary complex. A full characterisation of ligand 
targets would therefore require the synthesis of PROTACs based on all E3-ligases with different 
linkers. That is too an extensive task for PROTAC-based target identification to be the sole method 
used for target identification. 
Regardless of whether target identification using PROTACs can be the sole target identification 
method or not, it is interesting to examine whether the theory aligns with reality. The Laraia group 
has therefore started researching PROTAC-mediated target identification, more specifically using 
PROTACs for oxysterol target identification. This project is a continuation of previous work within 
this avenue of research, so it is important to know about the previous work of the Laraia group. 
1.5.1    Previous work by the Laraia group: Pomalidomide-based CHO PROTACs and the dis-
covery of selective GPP130 degradation 
As elaborated upon in section 1.2.1, only a few E3-ligase ligands are currently known, and it was 
decided to base the first PROTACs on the CRBN-recruiting ligand pomalidomide. Furthermore, the 
first sterol to be examined would be one resembling CHO, with the only difference being that the 
sidechain had been modified to allow for coupling to a linker by adding a carboxylic acid. Although 
this would increase the polarity of the molecule, the unchanged nature of the steroidal core meant that 
it could still serve the purpose of identifying targets of CHO43. Four CHO-based PROTACs were 
synthesized (Figure 10) as well as a control compound lacking the linker and pomalidomide. These 
five compounds had their degradation profiles examined by adding them to HeLa cell cultures and 
subsequently carrying out MS proteomics analyses of the cells. The results of the proteomics analyses 
are shown in Figure 10, and one thing that all the PROTACs had in common was that they caused 
downregulation of the Golgi integral membrane protein 4 (GOLIM4) also known as Golgi phospho-
protein 130 (GPP130). At first it was believed that the PROTACs had caused the degradation of 
GPP130 via a UPS-mediated mechanism, but further study revealed that not to be the case. The ability 
of the PROTAC C3 (see Figure 10) to degrade GPP130 was tested in combination with two inhibitors 
of the UPS degradation process: The neddylation inhibitor MLN4924 and the proteasome inhibitor 
MG132. A western blot analysis was carried out showing that coincubation of the cells with 
==PAGE:28==
Page 16 
 
MG132/MLN4924 and C3 still led to GPP130 degradation (see Figure 11a), so the degradation was 
not caused by UPS.

PROTACs cannot be used for target identification. More PROTACs based on other E3-ligase ligands 
and other oxysterols must be synthesized to properly test the concept and potentially identify targets 
of said oxysterols.

A SeO2 mediated allylic oxidation was used to synthesize 4-BHC. A hydroxy group can be introduced 
at the allylic position of an alkene functionality by the use of the Se(IV) compound, SeO2, which is

The first step of the allylic oxidation is a [4 + 2] cycloaddition similar to an ene reaction between the 
alkene and the SeO2 which forms a seleninic acid. This seleninic acid can undergo a [2,3] sigmatropic 
rearrangement to form an unstable intermediate which rapidly decomposes to the allylic alcohol. In

the 4𝛽 C-H 𝜎-orbital is parallel to the 𝜋-orbitals of the alkene, which is a prerequisite for the overlap 
with the molecular orbitals of SeO2 to occur.

2.4    Western blot analysis of GPP130 degradation 
As explained in section 1.5.1, the previously synthesized CHO-based PROTACs had caused signifi-
cant degradation of GPP130, likely via lysosomal degradation. It was unknown which part of the 
PROTAC molecule was responsible for this degradation, so the newly synthesized PROTACs could 
help elucidate whether the nature of the E3-ligase ligand had any impact on the degradation of 
GPP130. Furthermore, comparing the results for CHO-based PROTACs and 4-BHC-based 
PROTACs would help elucidate whether the GPP130 degradation was unique to CHO or whether 
other sterols could cause the same effect. 
==PAGE:46==
Page 34 
 
The PROTACs synthesized in this project were therefore tested in HeLa cell cultures and examined 
by western blot to ascertain whether they could degrade GPP130. All compounds were tested thrice 
to obtain results with reasonable statistical significance, and the compounds were tested at concen-
trations of 1 μM and 100 nM respectively. An example of how the western blot results looked like is 
shown in Figure 29. Although all PROTACs were tested simultaneously, the results are analysed 
separately for each oxysterol.

2.4.1    CHO-based PROTACs 
The data obtained for the PROTACs based on CHO is shown in Figure 30. The graph shows that all 
the PROTACs cause significant degradation at a concentration of 1 μM while also causing some deg-
radation at 100 nM. It seems that 21 is more potent since it causes a rather large degradation even at 
100 nM. As such, a hydrophobic linker may be more favourable towards GPP130 downregulation as 
opposed to the more hydrophilic polyethylene glycol linkers of 23 and 25. Regardless, these results 
suggest that the nature of the E3-ligase ligand does not matter for the degradation of GPP130, and 
that one must look to the cholesterol, linker or both to find the part of the PROTAC molecule pro-
voking the downregulation of GPP130.

2.4.3    Concluding on the western blot results 
There is an interesting conclusion that can be drawn based on the results of the western blot analyses. 
Back in section 1.5.1, it was noted that the CHO control compound C5 could not in itself cause 
GPP130 degradation nor could pomalidomide. So, what part of the PROTACs cause this degradation? 
The western blot results presented here offer an indication: The results for the CHO-based PROTACs 
all showed GPP130 degradation despite having a different E3-ligase ligand compared to the previ-
ously synthesized PROTACs, C1 – C4. Meanwhile, changing the oxysterol has a significant effect 
which the results for the 4-BHC-based PROTACs show. Lastly, both CHO- and 4-BHC results indi-
cate that the linker, more specifically its hydrophobicity, influences the degradation. All these things 
tied together seem to suggest that the part of the PROTAC molecules causing the GPP130 degradation 
is the sterol-linker part. It is worth underlining the fact that this is so far only an indication. Further 
studies will have to be carried out to prove it definitively.

3    Conclusion and future work 
In this project, six PROTACs have been synthesized for the purpose of using them for identifying the 
targets of CHO and 4-BHC. This was carried out by first synthesizing the sterols CHO (9) and 4-
BHC (10) in a five-step synthesis followed by the synthesis of the three VHL ligand-linkers, 18 – 20, 
by coupling the VHL ligand to three different linker molecules in amide coupling reactions. Lastly, 
the VHL ligand-linkers were coupled to the two sterols using amide coupling reactions resulting in 
six PROTACs of the general structure shown in Figure 32. 
 
Figure 32: The general structure of the six PROTACs synthesized in this project. 
Previously synthesized PROTACs based on CHO and the E3-ligase pomalidomide had shown a pro-
pensity for downregulating the protein GPP130, so the PROTACs synthesized were tested in HeLa 
cells to see if this was also the case for PROTACs based on the VHL E3-ligase ligand, but also to 
examine if 4-BHC would cause the same GPP130 degradation. All CHO-based PROTACs showed 
degradation of GPP130 while that was only the case for one of the three 4-BHC-based PROTACs 
(compound 22). Given that the E3-ligase ligand had no effect on the degradation while the opposite 
was the case for the oxysterol, it was concluded that the sterol-linker part of the PROTAC was likely 
responsible for the GPP130 downregulation. 
As outlined in the aims and objectives, a goal of this project was to carry out proteomics analysis of 
cells that had been treated with the six PROTACs to determine potential targets of the oxysterols. 
Cell lysates of cells treated with the PROTACs have been prepared, but they are yet to be analysed 
==PAGE:50==
Page 38 
 
by MS, so that task should be completed in future work. Furthermore, other PROTACs based on 
different oxysterols and different E3-ligase ligands should be synthesized and examined to discover 
oxysterol targets and to figure out if using PROTACs is a viable strategy for target identification.

4.1    General directions 
Commercially available reagents were used without further purification and all solvents were of 
HPLC quality. All reactions were monitored by TLC and/or reversed-phase ultra-performance liquid 
chromatography low-resolution mass spectrometry (RP-UPLC-LRMS; abbreviated as LCMS). 
Quantification of impurities and yield corrections were based on quantitative 1H-NMR spectros-
copy51. Analytical TLC was conducted on Merck aluminium sheets covered with silica (C60). The 
plates were either visualized under ultraviolet light or stained using KMnO4 [3 g in water (300 mL) 
along with K2CO3 (20 g) and 5% aqueous NaOH (5 mL)]. Flash column chromatography was per-
formed using Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 μm) silica gel. 
All new compounds were characterized by NMR, RP-UPLC-LRMS positive electrospray ionisation 
(ESI+) mass, RP-UPLC-LRMS (retention time), and optical rotation. Structural assignments were 
made when possible for new compounds using COSY, HSQC, HMBC, and H2BC spectra. For the 
recording of 1H-NMR and 13C-NMR spectra, a Bruker Avance III 400 spectrometer with a Bruker 
Ascend 400 magnet and a Prodigy CryoProbe (operating at 400 MHz for proton and 101 MHz for 
carbon) was used. The chemical shifts (δ) are reported in parts per million (ppm) and the coupling 
constants (𝐽) in Hz. Spectra were referenced using the residual solvent peaks of the respective solvent; 
DMSO-𝑑6 (δ = 2.50 ppm for 1H-NMR and δ = 39.52 ppm for 13C-NMR), CDCl3 (δ = 7.26 ppm for 
1H-NMR and δ = 77.16 ppm for 13C-NMR), and CD3OD (δ = 3.31 ppm for 1H-NMR and δ = 49.00 
ppm for 13C-NMR). The following abbreviations were used to report peak multiplicities: s = singlet, 
d = doublet, t = triplet, q = quartet, dd = doublet of doublets, dt = doublet of triblets, ddt = doublet of 
doublet of triplets, m = multiplet. 
Analytical RP-UPLC-LRMS (ESI+) analysis was performed on a S2 Waters AQUITY RP-UPLC 
system equipped with a diode array detector using an Thermo Accucore C18 column (d = 2.6 μm, 2.1 
x 50 mm; column temp. = 50 °C; flow = 1.0 mL/min). Eluents A (0.1% HCO2H in H2O) and B (0.1% 
HCO2H in MeCN) were used in a linear gradient (5% B to 100% B) in 2.4 min and then held for 0.1 
min at 100% B (total run time = 2.6 min). For compounds with retention times exceeding 2.6 min, a 
longer 5 min program was used. The LC system was coupled to a SQD mass spectrometer. Optical 
rotation was carried out using a PerkinElmer Polarimeter 241 using the D-line from sodium-vapor

dioxane/water to make a 1 M HCl solution. The solution was set to stir at rt under N2 atmosphere until 
judged complete by LCMS (typically 1-2 hours). This produced the deprotected amine as a precipi-
tated HCl salt. A solution/suspension of the carboxylic acid (1.3 equiv.), HATU (17, 1.4 equiv.), and 
DIPEA (16, 5 equiv.) in DCM was set to stir for a few minutes before dissolving the amine and some 
DIPEA (5 equiv.) in DCM and adding this amine solution to the acid-HATU solution. The mixture 
was set to stir at rt under N2 atmosphere until judged complete by TLC and LCMS (2 to 18 hours). 
The mixture was diluted 4-fold and washed with equal volumes (1:1 ratio between the organic and 
washing phase) of sat. NaHCO3, 0.5 M HCl, and brine respectively. The organic solution was dried 
with anhydrous Na2SO4, filtered, and evaporated to dryness on a rotary evaporated. The crude prod-
uct was purified by flash chromatography and dried under vacuum.
