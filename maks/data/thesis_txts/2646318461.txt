Abstract
The accumulation of dust and soiling on the PV cover glass decreases the transmittance
and reduces power output. These losses due to soiling on a solar farm can add up into a
significant number which can translate to financial loss as well.
The company Photocat A/S has developed an anti­soiling coating called ShineOn for the
PV systems. The ShineOn coating is composed of titanium di­oxide(TiO2), the coating
uses its hydrophilic and photocatalytic property to reduce the accumulation of dirt and
thus improves the PV performance during dry and arid conditions.
The aim of this thesis is to comparatively evaluate the ShineOn coating by carrying out
various tests with coated and uncoated PV systems. In order to achieve that, ShineOn
was applied on PV arrays in the solar farm at Risø, to single cell PV coupons for lab and
outdoor coupon tests.
For the farm test the ShineOn was applied to half of the arrays on a single tracker while
the other half was used as reference arrays. Hourly current ratio was calculated from
the current output of these arrays and was analysed at different test period to compare
the performance. It was observed that after the application of the coating the PV arrays
show a transmittance loss of 0.38%. As the rate of soiling is very low in Denmark the PV
arrays were artificially soiled to observe any improvement on the coated arrays. Initially
the performance of the coated arrays increased due to the increased soiling compared
to the uncoated arrays but with increased precipitation the artificial soiling was washed
away and the coated PV array performance reverted back. Finally, T­test was carried out
to verify the reliability of the statistical analysis in this test.
To study the effect of the coating in a controlled small scale, single cell solar coupons
were fabricated and installed on an outdoor solar tower of which four of these coupons
were coated and two uncoated. It was observed that the rate of soiling in the test period
was 0.11% and coated coupons showed transmittance loss of 0.49% when compared to
uncoated. Due to the low soiling rate any effect of it on ShineOn is inconclusive.
The optical characterisation of the ShineOn was achieved by the indoor lab tests such as
IV, IAM and transmittance test. Similar single solar cell coupons were used in this test
as well. The IV test were carried out on the coupons to compare the before and after
coating PMPP losses, this translated to a transmittance loss of 1.14% due to the coating.
The IAM test showed a transmittance loss of 0.025% suggesting that the coating has no
effect on AOI. Finally, the transmittance test was carried out in two stages, linear and total
transmittance test both showed transmittance loss of 1.015% and 1.37% respectively.
In the end, due to various degrees of uncertainty in the measuring instruments for the lab
tests an uncertainty analysis was performed to verify the reliability of these losses.

Introduction
As the world is shifting its focus to more sustainable methods to use and manage our
resources, renewable energy is at the helm of this change as renewables are not only
”green” but also a great source of inexhaustible energy which is growing constantly as
a competitive sector thus creating jobs and access to energy in developing nations. As
of 2018 renewables made up 26.2% of the electricity generated globally, that share is
projected to rise to 45% by the end of 2040 [1]. Solar energy is one such energy source
that helps to fulfill these needs. According to the International Energy Agency’s (IEA)
world energy outlook 2020 the world’s best solar power schemes now offer the cheap­
est electricity in history with the technology cheaper than coal and gas in most major
countries. A maturing technology and support mechanisms that stabilise revenues, while
promoting competition, have had a substantial impact on the prevailing financing costs for
utility­scale solar PV energy thus the accelerated growth in the sector. Falling costs and
increasing competitiveness of solar PV push its installed capacity beyond all the other
technologies worldwide as also supported in the Figure 1.1. [2]
Figure 1.1: Cumulative installed solar capacity, measured in gigawatts(GW) [3]
Worldwide grid connected solar capacity reached 580 GW at the end of 2019 along with
3.4 GW of off­grid PV according to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IREA)
and the growth is expected to follow the same rate in the future. Since 1992 the worldwide
growth of PV has been exponential as the industry took its place as a mainstream source
of energy. The world added 97.1 GW of new capacity in 2019 and the power generation
from PV have been estimated to be increased by 22% in 2019. With this increase, the
solar PV share in global electricity generation is now almost 3%. This surge in growth has
been recently driven by the Asian countries such as China, Japan and India. [4] [5]
Denmark installed 109 MW of solar capacity in 2019 and had at the end of 2019 a cumu­
lative installed capacity of 1.4 GW. 3.8% of the countries electricity demands have been
met by solar energy. The country utilizes solar power in two different ways, to produce
district heating and to produce electricity. The country has set its sight on meeting 100%
of the demand by renewable energy and solar power contributes towards this goal. Den­

1.2. NEED FOR ANTI­SOILING COATING
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Soiling is a general term used to describe the accumulation of dirt on the surface of the
panels, the dirt comprises of snow, inorganic and organic dirt, dust, and even bird drop­
pings. This dirt can either form a thin layer on the surface of the panels or shade a small
portion of the solar panel in the case of bird droppings. Due to both these cases the light
transmitted to the solar cell is reduced and thus cause power loss. The location of the
panels largely determines what kind of soiling will take place.
The Figure 1.2 depicts the dust intensity map of the world which is divided into 4 zones, the
darker the zone the more dust intensity and thus more soiling on the panels. The region
in and around the equator have more dust intensity when compared to the regions near
the poles. This is due to the dry and arid regions around the equator. Other factors such
as pollution and presence of active volcanoes also contribute to soiling. Annual power
loss due to the soiling depending on the location varies from 1.5% to 6.2% within the four
zones [8]. If not cleaned regularly or regularly checked the soiling on these panels get
aggravated with time and the losses also increase substantially.
1.2
Need for anti­soiling coating
Figure 1.3: Global photovoltaic power potential of the world [9]
Power loss due to soiling in large scale solar farms can translate to financial losses along
with power loss, the cost of the cleaning is generally labour intensive and in order to
constantly increase the revenue the spending on cleaning increases as well. Application
of anti­soiling coating on the solar panels in such instances prevents the accumulation
of dirt on them which lowers the cleaning cost and increase the performance of the solar
panels.
Figure 1.3 shows PV potential around the globe and overlaying the Figure 1.2 of dust
intensity to compare it with the PV potential map we can infer that the regions with high
dust intensity are the regions with the highest PV power potential which is a good motiva­
tion to work towards the reduction of soiling losses and also suggests that the anti­soiling
coatings have a huge potential today as well as in the future. The ShineOn coating is
being tested in Denmark which comes in the zone two of the dust intensity map and thus
have low soiling rate, due to this factor the full potential of the coating can’t be judged but
with the in­lab optical tests the optical performance can be estimated.

1.3. SHINEON COATING
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.3
ShineOn coating
The ShineOn coating is an anti­soiling coating developed by Photocat using titanium di­
oxide(TiO2) which is coated as hydrophilic nano­crystal layer. The coating is coated using
the spray coating method. Along with the hydrophilic property of the coating, ShineOn also
portrays photocatalysis characteristics.
1.3.1
Photocatalysis
Figure 1.4: Mechanism of TiO2 photocatalysis [10]
The process of photocatalysis is a chemical transformation initiated by the absorption
of light in the form of photons and then accelerated in the presence of a catalyst. This
photoreaction depends on the ability of the catalyst to generate electron­hole pairs and
also subsequently generate free radicals to undergo secondary reactions.
TiO2 is the common material of choice for hydrophilic self­cleaning surfaces. Not only is
titanium dioxide highly efficient at photocatalysing dirt in sunlight it is also non­toxic and
relatively easy to handle and deposit into thin films. AS the coating is done on mono crys­
talline silicon solar cells, silicon which has an indirect band­gap needs additional phonons
to be involved in absorption and emission processes in order to provide the required mo­
mentum change of electrons to generate power. The solar cells utilize light above the
band gap, therefore sun light with long wavelength can’t be utilized. Thus, the usage of
TiO2 is again justified as the films obtained after coating exhibit a transmittance higher than
80% in the visible region, making it highly transparent in the visible region and opaque in
the UV region. This high transparency is one of the properties that explain the interest of
TiO2 thin films in solar cells applications. The high refractive index of TiO2 also leads its
common use in thin film optics. Typically, TiO2 is one component of alternating layers of
high and low refractive index materials, this technique can be used to make optical filters,
reflectors. TiO2’s index can vary due to density variations caused by different deposition
processes, and the index reduces with the increasing wavelength.
As the light from the sun is incident on the coating the TiO2 absorbs the energy from the
light in the form of photons. After absorbing energy, light itself acts as the catalyst. The
Figure 1.4 visually represents the mechanism of photocatalysis for TiO2.
The absorption of this energy leads to a charge separation due to promotion of an elec­
tron from the valence band of the TiO2 to the conduction band which leaves a hole in
the valence band. This freed up negative charged electron combines with the oxygen

1.3.2
Hydrophilic
Figure 1.5: Hydrophilic property of the coating
Hydrophilic property as the name suggests, is the material which attracts (philic) wa­
ter(hydro).
Thus the hydrophilic property of the ShineOn coating develops a surface
which attracts water to the coated solar panel surface. Due to this attraction on the water
molecules the contact angle is as low as possible as depicted in the Figure 1.5, in an ideal
case the contact angle of the water molecule is at 0°, in average cases the contact angle
is less than 10°. The coating having higher surface energy is the other leading reason for
the attraction of water molecules and the low contact angle.

Scope of the work
There has been no previous literature studies on the ShineOn coating by Photocat A/S but
the literature has studied the application of a hydrophilic TiO2 coating for soiling related
losses reduction in PV systems and the various outdoor, indoor tests carried out on the
coating to asses its performance. Although majority of the anti­soiling coating studies
have taken place in the temperate region or where the rate of soiling is high as to aid
these studies. Therefore this thesis is comparatively a different study.
The initial objective is to understand soiling and there are several factors that effect ideal
yield of a PV system and the environmental factors are one of the crucial contributors.
Studies have investigated the partial shading of PV module by soil which as been accu­
mulated on the surface of these modules and the effect it has on the voltage (DC) and
current (DC) due to soiling. A literature study done by Mohammad Reza Maghami and
others published in the Renewable & Sustainable Energy Reviews journal studies the ef­
fects of soiling on PV panels in Iraq, the literature suggests that there are two types of
shading due to soiling which are hard shading and soft shading. Soft shading are caused
by a hindrance between the PV panels and the sun, this hindrance can be due to fog,
smoke or even smog. Whereas the hard shading occurs when the dust or soil particles
accumulate on the surface of the panels and blocks the sunlight. The results of these
study show that the soft shading only affects the current of the module whereas the volt­
age remains same. For the hard shading the accumulated particles can shade few cells
or all the cells in the module, and in the case of few cells being shaded the voltage of the
PV modules decrease. At last the study recommends to carry out a weekly cleaning of
the modules especially in the dry periods.

uncoated reference modules. The PV modules were installed outside in the laboratory of

Coupon fabrication:
Single cell coupons were fabricated, in the PV hall at Risø using the lamination technique.
The fabrication process of the coupons is outlined in the steps below, various components
are used in the fabrication of the coupons as they are layered over and then laminated
together to get the result.
• The first component is glass, the type of glass used is soda lime float glass with
very little iron oxides. The low iron glass used has consistent high direct energy
transmission even at higher wavelength when compared to normal float glass. The
glass used doesn’t have any anti­reflective properties.
• Below the glass we place a encapsulant cut out to the size of the glass. The encap­
sulant acts as an adhesive that glues all the layers together. The standard encap­

polymer which hold the layers together. The EVA also exhibits properties such as
electrical insulation for the open conductors on the cell and improves transmission
of energy. Although the main advantage of EVA’s is that it provides good adhesion.

a P­type mono­crystalline cell of H­pattern with 2 bus bars. The efficiency of these
cells are around 16%. Before placing the solar cell the bus bars of the cell are sol­
dered with tin coated copper ribbons as to act as an interconnection. The soldering
is done manually and is done on every cell individually.
• After placing the solar cell we now place the second layer of encapsulant, the en­
capsulant used are the same EVA used for the first layer thus providing insulation on
both the sides of the solar cell. Slits were cut on the second layer of the encapsulant
as for the interconnecting ribbons to come out.
• The final layer is the backsheet placed below the second encapsulant layer, the
backsheet used is a sandwich of an adhesion promoting layer on the inside, Polyethy­
lene terephthalate (PET) or Polyphthalamide (PPA) in the middle and Polyvinyl flu­

Transmittance test
The optical transmittance of the glass material is a key characteristic for their use in PV
modules. Changes in transmittance affects the module performance. Thus, transmittance
is important in the manufacturing of the modules and the yield of these modules over time.
Therefore by coating the glass of the solar modules with a coating such as ShineOn can
have its effect on the transmittance. This effect of the ShineOn coating on transmittance
is discussed in this section.
As, the glass used for the solar modules and coupons are not completely transparent and
cause diffusion to the incident light, both linear transmittance and total transmittance test
is carried out on the coated glass in order to have a better understanding. Two coated
glasses are tested for both the tests they are labelled as coated glass 1 & coated glass 2
along with a reference petra glass which is the glass used in solar modules and coupons.
Testing two coated glasses will test for the variability in the transmittance caused by the
manual coating method and then comparing it to the reference petra glass will test the
change in transmittance due to the coating.

3.1.1
Factors affecting PV performance
As soiling is an environment condition that has an effect on the performance of PV arrays,
there are other environmental and physical conditions which can have a similar impact.
Since, the soiling rate is very minimal in this case it is hard to determine if the changes
seen in the current ratio is caused by soling or by other conditions affecting the PV work­
ing condition. Therefore, in the farm test four arrays are compared in order to calculate
the current ratio and the environmental/physical conditions can affect different arrays dif­
ferently, hence this creates a difference in the working condition for the arrays under test
which can result in the changes observed in the performance of these arrays. Some of
these conditions are discussed below.
Array temperature difference
PV devices are very sensitive to temperature. The output of the PV modules decreases
with increase in its temperature, due to the increase in internal carrier recombination rates
caused by increased carrier concentration of the solar cells. The temperature of the PV
arrays can be increased by a increase in solar radiation and also the air temperature of
the environment. During the summer noon time in Denmark the PV array temperature
could reach upto 40­45°C. In the farm the temperature differences in the arrays could be
due to insufficient gap between the arrays and the ground, an inverter is close or below
one of the arrays which then has a impact on the current output of the arrays.
The positioning of arrays also contributes to these minor temperature differences. At the
Risø farm the string hub has a height of 1.95 m to which the four arrays are attached.
Two are adjacent to the north of the string hub whereas two are adjacent to the south.
These arrays are mounted at a landscape position, the length of each array is 1.65 m
and since the arrays are mounted on the string hub with a tilt angle of 25° it results in
the southern arrays to be closer to the ground than the northern arrays. Knowing the tilt
angle, the height of the string hub and the length of the arrays we can easily calculate
that the northern arrays reach upto 2.647 m from the ground whereas the southern arrays
are 1.252 m from the ground. The difference in elevation is around 1.39 m at the ends
of the northern and southern arrays, therefore the solar arrays at the upper and lower
edges of arrays observe very slight changes in environmental conditions, such as wind
and irradiation intensity, different distance from the ground allowing convective air flows
to cool the arrays at higher elevation which affects the temperature of the arrays. Hence
these difference in the positioning can contribute to the small scale changes of the current
output from the arrays.

The IV test measures the the relationship between current and voltage whilst varying the

Pre­lam coupon IV test
Figure 3.10: IV curves of the pre­lam coupons tested after various stages
In the Figure 3.10 above the IV curve of the pre­lam coupons at two different stage of
testing is plotted. In this case we test the coupon after the coating and after it has been
removed from the outdoor coupon test setup which lasted for three months. The before
coating test could not be done on these coupons as they are coated before the lamination
an fabrication process.
We compare the two IV curves as shown in the plot, it can observed that the coupons when
tested after the coating traced a higher curve compared to the coupons when they were
tested after the three months of soiling period. Suggesting that the three months of low
soiling rate degrades the performance of the coupons. This can be reasoned by the light­
induced degradation (LID) phenomenon, which suggests that the solar cells degrade at
their highest rates just few hours after there installation after which the degradation slows
down, the initial loss can be 1­3% in the initial stage. Thus explaining the performance
reduction while testing the coupons after three months of soiling.

are carried out manually this combination can lead to uncertainties in the tests. As the lab

devices and normal devices, therefore the reliability of these values and their uncertainties
are of crucial importance to the test results. Thus the uncertainty analysis determines
which test results can be relied upon and to what extent and which results are caused by
an uncertainty in the measurement rather than the result of the ShineOn coating.

along with that the repeatability or test­retest reliability of these tests are also performed
to remove any anomalies in the measurement.

5
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to evaluate the ShineOn coating and asses the improvements
caused by it on PV systems. On that account, ShineOn was tested on various scales
such as farm test, indoor lab tests and outdoor coupon test. A comparative evaluation
was achieved by comparing coated and uncoated PV systems in all the tests.
PV arrays were coated at the Risø solar farm to test the ShineOn on a large scale outdoor
conditions. The test period was divided into three period, the first period was where the
arrays work under normal conditions, the data from this period was also used to analyse
the array bias if any present among the four arrays under test. Of which one array was
negatively biased while the other three were positive. In the second period two of the
arrays were coated with ShineOn, in order to compare the performance, hourly current
ratio of the arrays were calculated comparing the coated arrays to the uncoated ones. Due
to the coating the arrays suffer a transmittance loss of 0.38% , also it was also observed
that the rate of soiling was very low. This low soiling rate contributes to the transmittance
loss. In the third and last period artificial soiling was applied to the arrays, due to this
increase soiling the performance of the coated arrays improved as the soiling loss was
now being reduced by the ShineOn coating compared to the uncoated arrays. Although,
the increased precipitation in this period washed the artificial soiling away which reverted
back the performance of the coated arrays. As the differences in the performance were
minimal the statistical analysis was verified by the T­test which showed that the differences
in the performance for different period are not just by chance and thus verified the reliability
of these tests. The current output data from the arrays and the weather data from the
weather station was used to filter the data and remove outliers. Other than the soiling
effect, changes in POA irradiance, albedo effect or the array bias could contribute to
the changes in the performance along with such low rate of soiling in Denmark it is not
conclusive how the ShineOn will perform against soiling.
In order to test ShineOn in a controlled small scale outdoor test, single cell solar coupons
were fabricated and installed on a solar tower facing south at a tilt of 25°. The coupons
were coated before and after the lamination process, to check the effect of the laminating
process on the coating. Six coupons were fabricated, out of which four were coated and
the other two were used as reference. The two of the four coated coupons were pre­lam
and two were post lam. In order to calculate the soiling rate one of the reference coupons
were cleaned regularly and then compared to the uncleaned reference coupon. The data
from coupons and the weather station were filtered according the requirements and the
isotropic transposition model which was generated for this purpose. The current data from
the coupons were corrected at STC and then normalized to compare the performance.
It was observed that the soiling rate was just 0.11% which was very low for a soiling
related study, also the coated coupons suffered a transmittance loss of 0.49%. At the
latter stages of the test the coated coupon performance improved but due to the low
soiling rate however it is inconclusive if the improvement is due to soiling.
In the final stage of the ShineOn testing the optical characterisation of ShineOn was tested
in lab tests such as IV, IAM and transmittance. The lab tests were carried out to verify
if the constant outdoor transmittance losses due to the coating on PV systems. Similar
single solar cell coupons were used for the tests.
The IV tests were performed at three stages before coating, after coating and after three
months of outside exposure. The test shod that due to the application of ShineOn the

coupons suffer a transmittance loss of 1.14%. The PMPP output from the IV curves at dif­

transmittance test showed an uncertainty of around 1%.

is not suitable of places with low soiling rate. The lack of literature study for effects of ant­

5.1
Future Work
The ShineOn coating from Photocat A/S is an anti­soiling coating developed for the pur­
pose of improving the performance of the PV systems in dry and arid regions, therefore in
order to improve the work of this thesis, the testing should be carried out in a location with
high soiling rate which will allow ShineOn to utilize its potential. Also the application of
the artificial soiling shouldn’t be applied during the rainy season as the soiling is washed
away and conclusive results are not formed.
Furthermore, the thickness of the applied coated layer and how its variance effects the
performance should be studied and an optical thickness value should be modelled. Fi­
nally the relationship between the ShineOn coating and anti­reflective glass should also
be studied to see if the coating erodes the AR layer or weather the AR layer helps the
ShineOn coating to reduce its transmittance loss.
