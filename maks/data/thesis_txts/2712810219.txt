Abstract 
Innovation has become one of the key competences of companies if they want to stay 
competitive in today’s world. Driven by an increasing interconnectedness and 
complexity,  the business world has undergone fundamental changes due to technological 
developments, such as artificial intelligence (AI). These emerging digital technologies 
provide an environment of radical change in the field of innovation, but many companies 
often lack the knowledge and capabilities to take advantage of these great opportunities. 
With the growing significance of strategic partnerships and knowledge-sharing, 
intermediary organizations play an increasingly important role in the domain of 
innovation and entrepreneurship. Innovation intermediaries (InIns) refer to organizations 
designed to enhance innovation and entrepreneurial capabilities of companies and 
therefore have a strong impact on innovation processes and ecosystems. As many 
companies in various industries are already adopting and are affected by digital 
transformation, there are also strong immediate implications for innovation 
intermediaries to adjust existing services or create new ones accordingly. Disruptive 
technologies, such as AI, have a strong transformational character and can provide 
benefits to intermediary organizations through e.g., information processing or support in 
decision-making processes. However, there is a gap between the opportunities described 
in literature and the question on how these can be utilized by innovation intermediaries 
today. To close this gap, this thesis deals with the questions how innovation 
intermediaries can be defined and categorized, which potentials AI technologies hold for 
these organizations, as well as how a first approach to a prototype based on machine 
learning can be conceptualized, which can support innovation intermediaries in offering 
better or entirely new services. 
 
Keywords: Innovation intermediaries, intermediation, digitization, artificial intelligence

1 
Introduction 
1.1 
Background 
In today’s globally connected and competitive world, innovation has become one of the 
key factors for the competitiveness of a business (Tohidi & Jabbari, 2012). Businesses 
are “required to increasingly innovate in order to obtain a competitive advantage” 
(Mohalajeng & Kroon, 2016). Especially during the last decades, the business world has 
undergone fundamental changes due to technological developments, such as artificial 
intelligence (AI) and advanced analytics (Yoo et al., 2012). These emerging digital 
technologies provide an environment of radical change in the field of innovation, e.g. they 
create new markets, new products or services, but many companies often lack the 
knowledge as well as the capabilities to take advantage of these opportunities 
(Kamalaldin et al., 2021; Musiolik et al., 2020; Rossi et al., 2021). In order to gain access 
to wider resources and knowledge, companies increasingly open up to their environment 
and embrace “open innovation” (Chesbrough, 2003; Markovic et al., 2021; Obradović et 
al., 2021). Open innovation approaches, however, often bring additional challenges, such 
as integration issues (Gurca et al., 2021; Huang & Su, 2019; Klerkx & Aarts, 2013), 
especially in the context of digital technologies which require the integration of various 
components often developed by a number of different external parties (suppliers, 
developers, etc.) (Coombs, 2003; Park, 2018; Teece, 2018). 
Following Mohalajeng and Kroon (2016), open innovation involves three core processes: 
outside-in, inside-out and coupled processes, from which the latter is a combination of 
both outside-in and inside-out processes resulting in not only bringing in external 
knowledge from partners and customers, but also externalizing the business’ innovation 
and knowledge to bring ideas to various markets. Companies that establish this coupled 
open innovation process are able to “co-create with corresponding partners through joint 
ventures, alliances and cooperation and in doing so, jointly develop and [commercialize] 
innovation” (Mohalajeng & Kroon, 2016). With the growing significance of open 
innovation, innovation intermediaries (InIns) are pressed to interact with a greater variety 
and number of external partners to remain connected and resourceful (Enkel et al., 2020; 
Laursen & Salter, 2006). InIns refer to organizations, or initiatives within organizations, 
designed to enhance innovation and entrepreneurial capabilities of individuals, firms, or

groups of firms (Dalziel, 2010). Various types of innovation intermediaries provide a 
wide range of services to their members, such as technology brokering, knowledge 
transfer, intellectual property rights management, among others (Howells, 2006). The 
intensified interface with external partners and other stakeholders not only complements 
an innovation intermediary’s limited internal resources, but also contributes to the 
competitive and impactful services offered to its members (Y. Wang & Li-Ying, 2015). 
Meanwhile, industry firms have widely adopted digital transformation (Gong & Ribiere, 
2021; Verhoef et al., 2021), which imposes strong immediate implications for innovation 
intermediaries to adjust existing services or create new ones accordingly. As the 
availability of data and information has increased rapidly throughout the last years, more 
and more companies start reconsidering the way they innovate and tend to look for new 
ways of strengthening their level of competitiveness. Observing these trends over the last 
years, it looks like competitiveness at least partially depends on a company’s information 
and problem-solving capabilities (Hajli & Featherman, 2018). One technology that is 
imposing strong implications for innovation intermediaries to adjust or rethink their 
services, as Gong and Ribiere (2021) as well as Verhoef et al. (2021) call it, is artificial 
intelligence. Similar to technologies like the steam engine or electricity, AI has a strong 
transformational character that is already influencing several industries from retail to 
agriculture, healthcare, and manufacturing (Quan & Sanderson, 2018). As Haefner et al. 
(2021) argue, innovation management today plays a key role in companies’ capacity to 
reinvent themselves through exploratory initiatives, and AI can provide “instrumental 
assistance beyond the scope of humans”. One reason for this is that information 
processing can be seen as a “key component in innovation in organizations” and decision-
making as a central activity, requires processed information from managers in the 
innovation process (Haefner et al., 2021; McNally & Schmidt, 2011; van Riel et al., 
2004). These developments show that ”the way innovation is organized needs to be 
challenged by introducing AI and machine learning because of their cost advantages in 
information processing” (Haefner et al., 2021). 
This thesis deals with the questions how innovation intermediaries can be defined and 
categorized, which potentials AI technologies hold for these organizations, as well as how 
a first approach to a prototype based on machine learning can be conceptualized, which 
can support innovation intermediaries in offering better or entirely new services to their 
customers and partners.

1.2 
Relevance 
Innovation intermediaries can facilitate and help companies, and especially start-ups, to 
generate new ideas and grow their business much faster than the market “by identifying, 
accessing, and transferring solutions to problems in various stages of the innovation 
process” (Hargadon & Sutton, 1997; Nambisan & Sawhney, 2007; Sawhney et al., 2003; 
Verona et al., 2006). This value comes from the rich service portfolio that InIns are 
offering, and their impact on innovation processes and ecosystems, which has been 
studied by a number of researchers (Howells, 2006; Lin & Wei, 2018; Russo et al., 2018; 
Silva et al., 2018). Emerging digital technologies, such as data technologies and artificial 
intelligence, have the potential to automate specific processes of intermediaries, such as 
the process of selecting suitable companies or individuals (matchmaking), providing 
more accurate matches and pricing (Geliskhanov & Yudina, 2018), just to name one 
example where digital technologies can support InIns in offering better or new services 
internally as well as externally for their customers and partners. 
1.3 
Structure & Objectives 
The structure of this thesis is connected to the research questions (chapter 3.2). First, a 
brief theoretical background on InIns, their functions and services (2.1), AI and ML 
foundations (2.2, 2.3, 2.4, 2.5), as well as the emerging trend of service digitalization of 
InIns (2.6) is provided. Second, an overview of the developed definitions of the types 
innovation intermediaries and their categorization is given (4.2), followed by a potential 
analysis for matchmaking (4.3), a promising component of the service portfolio of InIns, 
is presented, based on various expert interviews and literature analysis. Third and lastly, 
a brief proposal for a conceptualization of a prototype is given (4.4). This prototype has 
the potential to support InIns in their service offerings and shows how machine learning 
can be used in matchmaking processes. The thesis closes with a discussion and limitations 
part (chapters 5 and 6), as well as a conclusion and outlook on further research (chapter 
7). 
This master thesis has the following objectives: First, based on empirical data from 
Denmark, this thesis intends to answer the question of how innovation intermediaries can 
be defined and systematically clustered. This is being addressed by screening what the

current scientific literature provides, and by showing the geo-sectoral distribution of 
innovation intermediaries’ current and intended future services that rely on or are enabled 
by data and AI technologies. Second, this thesis intends to fill the research gap of 
identifying the potentials that artificial intelligence technologies have for the service 
portfolio of innovation intermediaries against the background of a paradigm shift towards 
open innovation in the industry and the emerging opportunities offered by data and AI 
technologies. This need is being addressed by showing what the current scientific 
literature provides, what the analysis of the questionnaire dataset tells about the provided 
and intended AI-based service level, as well as what selected experts in this field are 
saying about that topic. Third, these information on innovation intermediaries’ service 
portfolio and expectations of AI technologies are used to conceptualize a prototype which 
is based on machine learning algorithms, that is able to support specific components of 
the service offering of Danish innovation intermediaries.

2 
State of the art 
2.1 
Innovation intermediaries 
2.1.1 Definition 
Historically, firms have been developing new products and technologies merely 
internally, keeping their knowledge and expertise secret from the competitive 
environment. However, increasing complexity in global markets and networks, as well as 
the steady technological progress requires companies to open up their innovation 
processes and collaborate with external organizations and communities (Agogué et al., 
2017). These external organizations and communities act as "nodes and links" in the 
innovation process and have been broadly termed in literature as "intermediaries" who 
perform a variety of tasks and roles in the innovation process (Howells, 2006). 
In scientific literature, there are various notations for these innovation intermediaries, 
such as “third parties” (Mantel & Rosegger, 1987), “intermediary firms” (Stankiewicz, 
1995), “networkers” (Agogué et al., 2017), ”boundary organizations“ (Keating, 2001; 
Sarewitz & Pielke, 2007), “bridgers” (Bessant & Rush, 1995; McEvily & Zaheer, 1999), 
“brokers” (Hargadon & Sutton, 1997; Provan & Human, 1999), “information 
intermediaries” (Popp, 2000), or “superstructure organizations” (Lynn et al., 1996). 
However, a formally accepted definition of the term “innovation intermediary” is still 
lacking in scientific literature. For example, one definition of the term developed by 
Howells (2006) states that an innovation intermediary is an “organization or body that 
acts as an agent or broker in any aspect of the innovation process between two or more 
parties”. According to Dalziel (2010), innovation intermediaries are “organizations or 
groups within organizations that work to enable innovation, either directly by enabling 
the innovativeness of one or more firms, or indirectly by enhancing the innovative 
capacity of regions, nations, or sectors”. This research understands the term 
“innovativeness” as broadly as Schumpeter’s definition of innovation as “the introduction 
of new technical methods, products, sources of supply, and forms of industrial 
organization” (Schumpeter, 2000).

The roles of these innovation intermediaries can be quite heterogeneous in nature: As 
brokers, they are supporting problem solving (Gianiodis et al., 2010; Hargadon & Sutton, 
1997) or transfer of technology (Bessant & Rush, 1995) by utilizing different strategies 
such as „informing, consulting, matchmaking, engaging, collaborating and building 
capacity” (Michaels, 2009). As networks, they can “establish a more [supportive] context 
for the realization and durable embedding of […] innovation projects” (Klerkx et al., 
2010). In general, intermediaries “connect companies to external sources […] of 
innovation and mediate their relationships with those actors” (Nambisan et al., 2012). As 
Agogué et al. (2017) mention, these intermediary organizations facilitate the 
„identification of external knowledge providers“ and „make external knowledge 
accessible“. In that sense, intermediaries act as agents which “improve connectivity 
within and among innovation networks” (Agogué et al., 2017; Stewart & Hyysalo, 2008). 
Sarewitz and Pielke (2007) refer to intermediation as a „process of reconciling the 
capabilities and aspirations of knowledge producers and knowledge users“ and state that 
these intermediary institutions, as they call them, may “enhance the pursuit of well-
ordered science by mediating communication between supply and demand functions”. 
Especially as brokers, intermediaries are one means to “lessen the information deficit of 
users that may result from particular information needs not being fulfilled or that 
potentially useful information exists that users do not know about” (McNie, 2007; 
Michaels, 2009). According to Agogué et al. (2017) and Michaels (2009), the recent 
scientific literature stressed that the intermediary role can be “critical to the exploration 
of new opportunities and the development of new ways to address shared issues, such as 
sustainability and environmental issues”. They explain this critical role by stating that 
intermediaries can “initiate change” (Callon, 1994; Lynn et al., 1996), “build networks” 
(McEvily & Zaheer, 1999), and define “where to look in the first place” (Howells, 2006). 
The literature often distinguishes between two cases intermediaries can operate in: cases 
where “problems are known, actors can be recognized and [in which] there is sufficient 
knowledge available to solve the problems […], and cases where the problems are ill-
defined, the role of actors is not given, and where not even the art of knowledge needed, 
is known […]” (Agogué et al., 2017). In these complex and ill-defined situations, learning 
processes become more necessary (Klerkx et al., 2010). This is why, in recent literature, 
innovation intermediaries are being described as “architects” or “colleges of the 
unknown” (Agogué et al., 2013; Le Masson et al., 2012), as Agogué et al. (2017) note.

In the following sub-chapter, the different types of innovation intermediaries will be 
examined. According to Agogué et al. (2017), there seems to be four “core functions” 
being fulfilled by all types of innovation intermediaries: (1) connecting actors; (2) 
involving, committing, and mobilizing actors; (3) solving, avoiding, or mitigating 
potential conflicts of interests; and (4) stimulating the innovation process and innovation 
outcomes. 
2.1.2 Types of innovation intermediaries 
In previous studies on intermediaries, researchers have identified and described several 
types of innovation intermediaries. Following Agogué et al. (2017), intermediaries can 
be utilized (1) for problem solving, (2) for technology transfer, or (3) as coordinators of 
networks. The authors stress that these three types might not be exhaustive. After 
exploring other studies, this typology seems to match the majority of the analyzed studies: 
Lopez and Vanhaverbeke (2009) have also grouped innovation intermediaries into three 
types, describing each one by giving examples of their main functions: (1) Connection 
group (gatekeeping, brokering, demand articulation), (2) Collaboration and support 
group (knowledge processing and combination, commercialization, foresight and 
diagnosis), and (3) Technological services group (intellectual property, testing and 
training, assessment and evaluation, regulation). Similarly, Vidmar (2021) has developed 
another typological model consisting of four types: (1) Enabling the ecosystem (removing 
barriers, close involvement), (2) Equipping the players (encourage innovation, systemic 
investment), (3) Shaping the common vision (creating motivation, soft leadership), and 
(4) Moving the development (enacting visions, strong mandate). Kivimaa et al. (2019) 
have focused more on “transition intermediary types” arising from a more systematic 
view. They have distilled the following five different types of transition intermediaries: 
(1) Systemic intermediary (system level between multiple actors), (2) Regime-based 
transition intermediary (system level between multiple actors, mandate given by regime 
actors), (3) Niche intermediary (local projects, and/or higher level of aggregation), (4) 
Process intermediary (experimental projects or specific processes contributing to 
transitions), (5) User intermediary (technology and use, and/or niche technology and 
dominant configuration). However, in more recent research papers, there seems to be a 
common understanding that, with respect to the field of innovation, deals with the 
typology of innovation intermediaries described in the table below (Table 1).

Accelerators 
By opening up to both internal and external resources (open innovation), most established 
companies are building programs to generate innovative ideas and start-ups (Kohler, 
2016). Instead of seeing start-ups as “agents of disruption”, companies are now 
collaborating with them to use them as “engines of corporate innovation” (Weiblen & 
Chesbrough, 2015). Further, Weiblen and Chesbrough (2015) differentiate between 
“inside-out” and “outside-in” programs. Corporate incubators, as they call these inside-
out programs, are means to create and launch start-ups based on ideas that ”are born in 
the corporate environment, but do not fit with the current [business model]” (Weiblen & 
Chesbrough, 2015). An example for outside-in programs are the corporate accelerators, 
which have their roots in incubator organizations (Weiblen & Chesbrough, 2015). The 
term corporate accelerator highly depends on the context, but in the corporate context, it 
usually refers to “a time-limited program that start-ups can apply for […], in which a 
cohort of start-ups is selected from the applications and receives support, funding, 
coaching, and co-location” (Weiblen & Chesbrough, 2015). Kohler (2016) goes into a 
similar direction by stating that corporate accelerators are “company-supported programs 
of limited duration that support cohorts of start-ups during the new venture process via 
mentoring, education, and company-specific resources”. Another form of accelerators is 
the non-corporate form, which includes independent accelerator programs such as “Y 
Combinator” (Kanbach & Stubner, 2016). According to Kim and Wagman (2014), the 
modern form of accelerator programs was established by Y Combinator, which was 
founded in 2005. Further, they argue that accelerators “make a small equity investment 
in a group of start-ups and require a relatively early exit” (Kim & Wagman, 2014). 
Pauwels et al. (2016) see accelerators as a “new generation incubation model” that 
responds “to the shortcomings of previous generation incubation models, which are 
primarily focused on providing office space and in-house business support services” 
(Bruneel et al., 2012). An “incubation model” thereby is defined as “the way in which an 
incubation entity provides support to [start-ups] to improve the probability of survival of 
the portfolio companies and accelerate their development” (Pauwels et al., 2016). Further, 
Pauwels et al. (2016) define an accelerator as “an organization, which aims to accelerate 
new venture creation by providing education and mentoring to cohorts of ventures during 
a limited time”.

The accelerator, as an innovation intermediary, covers some of the provided services of 
an incubator, such as networking and mentoring, but has some specific features that set 
them apart (Isabelle, 2013). First, they do not primarily provide physical resources and 
second, they are typically offering early investment (as opposed to incubators), usually in 
exchange for equity (Pauwels et al., 2016). Furthermore, accelerators seem to be “less 
focused on venture capitalists […], but are more closely connected to business angels and 
small-scale individual investors” (Pauwels et al., 2016). Another specific feature of the 
accelerator model is the emphasis on “business development and the development of 
[start-ups] into investment-ready businesses” (Pauwels et al., 2016) by offering mentoring 
and networking opportunities, a supportive peer-to-peer environment and entrepreneurial 
culture  (Christiansen, 2009). 
However, empirical knowledge about the different characteristics of this new generation 
incubation model is scarce (Birdsall et al., 2013). Furthermore, the scientific literature on 
business incubation “lacks a theoretical lens to [analyze] and explain the heterogeneity 
among different incubation models, with the majority of published studies being largely 
descriptive in nature” (Pauwels et al., 2016). According to Dempwolf et al. (2014), the 
differences between the service offerings of, for instance, incubators, venture 
development organizations and accelerators are often not clear. An important aspect of 
accelerators, considered by Dempwolf et al. (2014), is that accelerators are often 
businesses themselves meaning that their overarching goal is self-sustainability. 
Clusters 
Innovation and knowledge are widely regarded as important drivers of successful 
businesses and a growing economy. According to Moreno et al. (2006), such drivers 
highly depend on the “process of creation, accumulation and diffusion of knowledge 
which is often strongly localized into clusters of innovative firms”, sometimes 
collaborating with public institutions, like research centers or universities. Furthermore, 
innovation processes can be “sensitive” to geographical distances as well as technological 
spillover effects, one reason why local innovation clusters emerged (Moreno et al., 2006). 
The success in a cluster, as stated in Sallet et al. (2009), “creates effects in the regional 
economy, as specialists look to the area for next generation of information technology life 
sciences or other form of innovation”. Cluster formation has had a “major impact” on 
regional development (Li et al., 2016) because they are able to drive innovation

(Oerlemans et al., 2001) and facilitate knowledge spillovers on the local businesses 
(Baptista & Swann, 1998; Jaffe et al., 1993; Knott, 2003). Following Scott et al. (2019), 
clusters can improve companies’ access to “information, knowledge, resources and 
institutions […], but the cluster’s effectiveness is always dependent on the “processes 
through which relationships form within and among individuals at the micro-level of the 
networked cluster”. 
Research provides various heterogeneous definitions for the intermediary type “cluster” 
composed by a variety of authors and publications. Martin and Sunley (2003) talk about 
a “chaotic concept”, and Hamdouch (2008) states that different notions, such as 
“network”, “value chain”, “industrial district”, “local production system”, “innovative 
milieu”,

clearly distinguishable in literature. When looking at the different definitions themselves, 
it can be said that already in 1998, Harvard business professor Michael E. Porter defined 
clusters as “geographic concentrations of interconnected companies, specialized 
suppliers, service providers, firms in related industries, and associated institutions that 
compete but also collaborate” (Porter, 1998). Two years later, Porter has worked on 
another definition, including a spatial context: “A cluster is a geographically proximate 
group of interconnected companies and associated institutions in a particular field, linked 
by commonalities and complementarities. The geographic scope of a cluster can range 
from a single city or state to a country or even a group of [neighboring] countries” (Porter, 
2000). In 1999, the OECD developed its definition of a cluster: “Clusters are 
[characterized] as networks of production of strongly interdependent firms (including 
[specialized] suppliers), knowledge producing agents (universities, research institutes, 
engineering companies), bridging institutions (brokers, consultants) and customers, 
linked to each other in a value-adding production chain” (OECD, 1999). Further, the 
OECD mentions that the clusters “are based on a sophisticated infrastructure in which 
knowledge is developed, shared and exchanged, and are [characterized] by highly 
concentrated and effective links between entrepreneurs, investors and researchers” 
(OECD, 1999). This notion of linked elements is also being used by Lange et al. (2010) 
as they state that “clusters are taking advantage of the connection capacities of the ‘Triple 
Helix’ model, which combines government, business, and public research in the 
development of knowledge-based innovation systems”. Clusters can be seen as a “driving

force” for start-ups “with advanced research and for accelerating entry of products and 
services into the market” (Lange et al., 2010). Further, “[cluster-based] businesses usually 
invest more in training and education than is typically done in their industries and 
countries. Consequently, firms within the innovation hub have a higher ratio of R&D to 
sales, effectively transforming research projects into new commercial products and 
services” (Lange et al., 2010). 
There are also scholars who are viewing clusters from a knowledge-based perspective: 
Hans-Dieter (2008) understands a knowledge cluster as a “local innovation system 
organized around universities, research institutions and firms which intend to drive 
innovations and create new industries”. They form “central places within an epistemic 
landscape”, in a wider structure of knowledge, in which e.g., universities, colleges, 
research institutions, think tanks, government research agencies or knowledge-intensive 
firms are embedded (Hans-Dieter, 2008). Through their “agglomerations of 
[organizations]” they have the organizational capability to “drive innovations and create 
new industries” (Hans-Dieter, 2008). 
Clusters have specific benefits for both the networked parties but also the environment 
surrounding it. As stated in Sallet et al. (2009), innovation clusters are “a critical 
component of national competitiveness”. Geographic regions which are connected in a 
network “create virtuous cycles of innovation that succeed by emphasizing the key 
strengths of the local businesses, universities and other research and development 
institutions, and non-profit organizations” (Sallet et al., 2009). 
According to the OECD, clusters can take “a variety of forms”, depending on their main 
areas of specialization, the most frequent of which are at regional, national and 
international level (OECD, 1999). In this work, however, an innovation cluster is being 
defined as a geographically close group of connected organizations or companies aiming 
at fostering innovations and creating new industries. 
Digital Platforms 
According to Srinivasan (2017), there are three different kinds of intermediation 
platforms: matching platforms, transaction platforms, and info-mediation platforms. The 
platform intermediary type that this thesis will cover is the “digital” or “online” platform 
intermediary and can be instantiated as each of Srinivasan’s platform types. The matching 
==PAGE:22==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
22 
 
platforms, as Srinivasan (2017) defines, are those where users look for “one-to-one 
matches”, and the platform then provides them with a matching solution based on the 
information given by the user. This matching principle can also be found in “traditional” 
matchmaking intermediaries that “bring together different parties” on multi-sided 
platforms (Helfat & Raubitschek, 2018). The special aspect of this kind of transactions 
for the users is that it is the only transaction they make on this platform: “Once the 
transaction is done, the two parties would not engage with the platform [anymore]” 
(Srinivasan, 2017). A matching platform usually does not want to have returning 
customers as that “would mean that they have not been matched properly the first time” 
(Srinivasan, 2017). A transaction platform, on the other hand, facilitates the transactions 
between two parties, or two sides, of that platform, e.g. the buyers and the sellers 
(Srinivasan, 2017). According to Srinivasan (2017), an example for a transaction platform 
would be a website where patients can find doctors and directly book appointments with 
them on that platform. The last platform type, the info-mediation platform, “gathers a 
database of information about one side of customers and provides it to the other side” 
(Srinivasan, 2017). Unlike on a matching platform, Srinivasan (2017) explains, both sides 
of the platform “keep coming back to [that platform to do] more business”. A fourth type 
of a platform intermediary is given by Troll et al. (2019) in the context of crowdsourcing. 
They explain, that crowdsourcers can set up their own platform, or they can hire an 
intermediary that provides “a technical infrastructure and access to a crowd” (Troll et al., 
2019). These types of intermediaries could be considered as “brokers, insuring that 
crowdsourcing enterprises do not only connect with a suitable crowd by providing the 
necessary skills and resources, but also shift risks, efforts and overhead related to crowd 
and process management” (Troll et al., 2019). Further, Troll et al. (2019) argue that 
“handing over all or part of the power to an intermediary may mean that risks like losing 
valuable contributors or gaining a reputation damage due to perceived negative 
experiences run out of their control.” 
Various definitions of platforms, in the sense of digital platforms as institutions, could 
not be found in great number in the literature. The digital platform as an intermediary 
organization is defined by Geliskhanov and Yudina (2018) as a “new economic institution 
functioning in a hybrid reality that has the characteristics of an intermediary transaction 
and organizational institution”. The main task of this intermediary transaction institution 
is to “accumulate information (digital and non-digital), tangible, intangible, financial, and 
==PAGE:23==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
23 
 
other resources and provide various entities with access to these resources” (Geliskhanov 
& Yudina, 2018). The form of the intermediary institution is currently developing and, 
according to Geliskhanov and Yudina (2018), is implemented “in the form of new virtual-
network organizational forms, including in the form of digital platforms”. Digital 
platforms can be defined as “hybrid structures (organizations, systems, and technologies) 
that focus on value creation by providing and facilitating direct interaction and exchange 
between two or more groups of external users within a single digital ecosystem of 
algorithmic relationships” (Geliskhanov & Yudina, 2018). It is therefore very closely 
connected to the concept of a network and some overlaps can be noticed. Geliskhanov 
and Yudina (2018) further argue that digital platforms “allow to connect people, devices, 
and various cyberphysical systems into a single intellectual and information space. In 
addition, [digital platforms] allow for interoperability and transactions in a discrete mode, 
without the need for users of different parties to have a simultaneous access to each 
other”. The benefit of a digital platform, according to Geliskhanov and Yudina (2018), 
lies in its ability to “significantly reduce various transaction, transformational, and other 
types of costs, including through the use of […] digital technologies [such as] artificial 
intelligence […], and digital devices”. 
Due to the fact that more detailed definitions towards digital platforms as innovation 
intermediaries could not be found, the definition of a digital platform throughout this 
work will be the following: A digital platform, in the context of innovation 
intermediation, is a hybrid structure that provides value for its participants by enabling 
and facilitating direct interaction between two or more groups of participants. 
Incubators 
Incubators are organizations “providing small new ventures with resources […] that 
improve their chances of foundation and survival” (Allen & Mccluskey, 1991; Dettwiler 
et al., 2006; Hackett & Dilts, 2004; Löfsten & Lindelöf, 2002) as Barbero et al. (2014) 
explain. Their objective is to “improve the probability of survival of the firms supported 
[…] and accelerate their development” (Barbero et al., 2014; Escorsa & Valls, 1996; 
Mian, 1996; Schwartz, 2009, 2013; Smilor & Gill, 1986). The main services that an 
incubator provides include “financing, goals and structure, resources and support and 
entrepreneurial spirit” (Barbero et al., 2014; Dettwiler et al., 2006; Löfsten & Lindelöf, 
==PAGE:24==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
24 
 
2002; Patton et al., 2009). These offerings include e.g. physical facilities, assets such as 
management, legal services, or networking (Barbero et al., 2014). 
A variety of different incubator typologies exists in literature, however, no clear 
description of them could be found. One classification can be done by distinguishing 
incubators based on the different market failures they cope with (Aernoudt, 2004). 
Following this typology, Aernoudt (2004) distinguishes the incubator types “mixed 
incubators”, “economic development incubators”, “technology incubators”, “social 
incubators”, and “basic research incubators”.  Becker and Gassmann (2006a) differentiate 
between non-profit and for-profit incubators on a higher level, and Becker and Gassmann 
(2006b) then differentiate on a deeper level within these two groups: “government” and 
“non-government” (non-profit), as well as “independent” and “corporate” (for-profit). 
However, what is worth noting, for Becker and Gassmann (2006b), science parks are seen 
as a sub-type of an incubator – a non-profit, governmental institution. Science parks, 
another type of innovation intermediaries, will be described and explained further below. 
Following a four-types classification, Allen and Mccluskey (1991) differentiate between 
“for-profit property development incubators”, “non-profit development corporations”, 
“academic incubators”, and “for-profit seed capital incubators”. Beyond these four types, 
the also mention two other types: “public-private partnerships” (a hybrid or combination 
of at least two of the previously mentioned types), and “corporate incubators” (in which 
economic value is added back to the company) (Allen & Mccluskey, 1991). Grimaldi and 
Grandi (2005), on the other hand, are clustering incubators into “business innovation 
centers” (BICs), “university business incubators” (UBIs), “technology incubators”, 
“independent private incubators” (IPIs), and “corporate private incubators” (CPIs). As a 
another classification of incubators found in literature, Zedtwitz and Grimaldi (2006) 
distinguish incubators between “economic business”, “university”, “independent”, 
“company internal”, and “virtual incubator”. 
Benefits of using incubators as innovation intermediaries include the reduction of 
“economic disparities by maintaining a local/economic network […], to provide space, 
infrastructure, communication facilities, visibility […], represent pioneering economic 
development incubators […], support newly created technology firms” [as well as to 
provide] rent reductions, access to capital and shared office space, […] laboratories, 
technology transfer services, […] faculty acting consultants […], basic research […]

creation of new businesses, […] [to] gain quick profits from successful [start-ups], […] 
[and to] focus on the specific needs of the entrepreneurial community”. Incubators play a 
significant role in innovation, yet there are “more than subtle differences” in the type of 
innovation that comes from each type of incubator (Barbero et al., 2014). 
In this thesis, however, an incubator is defined as an organization providing new ventures 
with resources with the goal of improving their chances of foundation and 
competitiveness. Benefits of using incubators as a start-up or a company include the 
reduction of economic risk by maintaining a local network, to provide space, 
infrastructure, facilities, or visibility, to support newly created ventures as well as to 
provide further benefits such as rent reductions, access to office space, laboratories, 
technology transfer services, basic research and consultants, as well as to focus on the 
specific needs of the entrepreneurial community. 
Innovation Hubs 
In some scientific papers, innovation hubs are defined as “small operations” aiming to 
“enable individual technologists, entrepreneurs, and freelancers to innovate, network, and 
start businesses” (Friederici, 2019). In others, they support “collaboration, innovation, 
and entrepreneurship” and offer services ranging from “networking-oriented social 
events, pitch nights, and mentoring sessions” to “more traditional services like pre-
incubation, incubation, and acceleration support” (Jiménez & Zheng, 2021). The concept 
of an innovation hub can differ depending on the research field, the scholar’s discipline 
and the term itself (Mwantimwa et al., 2021). According to Chirchietti (2017), innovation 
hubs historically developed out of incubator organizations that, due to increasing start-up 
requirements and needs, were forced to “diversify their offer” but they still “share the 
same overall traits as incubators”. Just like incubators, innovation hubs provide services 
“that help [entrepreneurs] to get through initial hurdles in starting a business”, such as 
helping with the basics, networking and market research activities, or assistance in 
marketing (Chirchietti, 2017). However, what sets them apart from traditional incubator 
organizations is the fact that innovation hubs, according to Chirchietti (2017), are based 
on the concept of open innovation and collaboration. However, there are some loose 
definitions and overlaps in the different terms of hubs with several connotations. The 
word ‘hub’ often “has become a misnomer for many [organizations] where the label [does 
not] quite fit” (Friederici, 2014). For instance, hubs can also take the form of a “policy 
==PAGE:26==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
26 
 
initiative”, which is called a “Digital Innovation Hub (DIH)” by the European 
Commission in its communication on “Digitising European Industry” (Ríssola & Sörvik, 
2018). In this report, a DIH is defined as an organization helping “companies in the region 
become more competitive by improving their business/production processes as well as 
products (and services) by means of digital technology” (Ríssola & Sörvik, 2018). 
Then, there are scholars who are viewing hubs from a knowledge-based perspective: 
Hans-Dieter (2008) uses the term of a ‘knowledge hub’ and connects it to the “knowledge 
sharing and dissemination aspect”. Knowledge hubs are “local innovation systems that 
are nodes in networks of knowledge production and knowledge sharing […] 
[characterized by] high connectedness and high internal and external networking and 
knowledge sharing capabilities” (Hans-Dieter, 2008). They fulfill three major functions: 
(1) “to generate knowledge”, (2) “to transfer knowledge to sites of application”, and (3) 
“to transmit knowledge to other people through education and training” (Hans-Dieter, 
2008). The authors Mian and Hulsink (2009) go towards the same direction by stating 
that a hub is “a networked platform and hallmark of innovation ecosystem”. 
A deeper focus on a collaborative context of hubs is brought by Bachmann (2013) 
defining a hub as a space that was designed “to provide a collaborative environment in 
which people could work, meet, and learn within a modular structure”. Following the 
UNICEF guide (2012), hubs “bring together diverse stakeholders, lower barriers to 
solutions from co-creators, are embedded in their local context, and encourage 
collaboration through shared physical space”. This aspect of collaboration is also found 
in Bastion (2013) stating that a hub is providing a space which serves as a “focus point 
[or center] of certain activities”, “a meeting point, for the local […] community”. A hub, 
therefore, creates a “structure where people […] interact with others they would not 
typically meet” (Chirchietti, 2017). Hubs do not only provide access to fast and reliable 
internet connections, but also electricity, information and networks” (Bastion, 2013). 
Having access to these different resources and sharing these with the different participants 
in the hub “not only saves costs, but working together in one space with my co-workers 
is seen as a source of inspiration and creates collaborative possibilities” (Bastion, 2013). 
In this thesis, hubs are viewed from the perspective of innovation and entrepreneurship. 
Therefore, throughout this work, a hub is being defined as a physical space that supports 
start-ups as well as individuals at various stages of their entrepreneurial development. It

is an umbrella term covering aspects of other innovation intermediaries such as 
incubators, networks, or science parks (Chan et al., 2009; Chan & Lau, 2005; Sambuli & 
Whitt, 2017). 
Innovation Networks 
Innovation is often considered the result of “networking and interactive learning among 
a heterogeneous set of actors” (Hall, 2006; Klerkx et al., 2010; Leeuwis, 2007; Röling, 
2009). Some of the most innovative companies are part of “collaborative networks of 
alliances” (Winch & Courtney, 2007). According to network theory, different network 
members play different roles, one of which being a “third party providing some sort of 
linkage between two or more other firms”, such as buyers and sellers in the context of, 
for example, stock brokers (Winch & Courtney, 2007). In this work, these broker 
organizations are understood as sub-groups of network intermediaries. They are 
connecting companies to different innovation providers using website platforms 
(Chesbrough, 2006) or by turning “old knowledge into innovative solutions” (Hargadon, 
2002). Intermediation is often occurring between “multiple network partners […] or 
system actors […] and aid in negotiating change by assisting in the building of alliances 
[…] and bring in supporters from [different parties]” (Kivimaa et al., 2019). In this 
negotiation context, “intermediaries act as brokers between multiple priorities, interests 
and knowledge pools for creating a shared vision and activities to facilitate transition” 
(Kivimaa et al., 2019). However, there is no consensus on the term of innovation brokers, 
or innovation intermediary itself. Some notations associated with brokers are “technology 
brokering” (Hargadon & Sutton, 1997), “innovation brokering” (Klerkx & Leeuwis, 
2009), and “network brokerage” (Sverrisson, 2001). 
Building contacts and networks is said to be the prime factor in determining the success 
of any company, even though the literature on networks in relation to small businesses is 
“very sparse” (Birley, 1985). The entrepreneur’s use of innovation networks relates to 
“the extent to which [the entrepreneur seeks and receives] help from the various potential 
sources” (Birley, 1985). Dhanaraj and Parkhe (2006) see innovation networks as “loosely 
coupled systems of autonomous firms […] [which] orchestrate network activities to 
ensure the creation and extraction of value, without the benefit of hierarchical authority”. 
Furthermore, they argue that the network form of organization” has profoundly impacted 
how companies innovate […] [making it] possible for different activities along the value 
==PAGE:28==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
28 
 
chain to be carried out efficiently by different firms” (Dhanaraj & Parkhe, 2006). 
According to Billington and Davidson (2013), the growth of innovation intermediary 
networks “enables the rapid pairing of firms seeking knowledge to address a wide range 
of business challenges (seekers) with other firms or individuals who already have relevant 
knowledge (solvers or knowledge brokers)”. These networks can bring “new ideas 
through open innovation […] and create competitive advantage” (Billington & Davidson, 
2013). Aernoudt (2004) also mentions “business angel networks”, which can be seen as 
a subtype of networks. These specialized networks “provide, through their angels, 
financing and hands-on management to starting companies” (Aernoudt, 2004). This level 
of granularity, however, is beyond the research goal of this work. One advantage of 
innovation networks is given by S. Lee et al. (2010) by arguing that because companies 
are usually relatively specialized, being a part of a network may enable them to gain 
access to greater markets as well as various resources to become more competitive in their 
fields. Another advantage of networks is that those with a higher capacity for learning 
and knowledge transfer are able to “out-innovate” those networks with less sharing 
capacity (Billington & Davidson, 2013; Hippel, 2007). Following Håkanson et al. (2011), 
intermediaries are important due to “the ‘systemic’ and ‘distributed’ nature of innovation 
involving the interplay of multiple actors connected through social, industrial, scientific 
and technological ‘networks’”. This interplay of actors produces “network participation 
costs” which often hinder “the development of shared innovation strategies” and “could 
be minimized by the work of intermediaries” (Iturrioz et al., 2015). In the context of 
innovation networks, “knowledge is the chief currency and is dispersed”, so ensuring 
“knowledge mobility” is essential (Dhanaraj & Parkhe, 2006). Knowledge mobility 
thereby refers to the “ease with which knowledge is shared, acquired, and deployed within 
the network” (Dhanaraj & Parkhe, 2006). Another key task of orchestrating innovation 
networks involves managing “innovation appropriability” (Pisano, 1990; Teece, 1986, 
2002). According to Teece (1986), appropriability refers to the “environmental factors 
[…] that govern an innovator’s ability to capture the profits generated by an innovation”, 
as measured by “the nature of the technology, and the efficacy of legal mechanisms of 
protection”. The third task of orchestrating networks, according to Dhanaraj and Parkhe 
(2006), is fostering the network stability. This stability “aims for a [non-negative] growth 
rate while allowing for entry and exit of network members” (Dhanaraj & Parkhe, 2006). 
According to Powell et al. (1996), when the knowledge base of an industry is “both 
==PAGE:29==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
29 
 
complex and expanding” and the “sources of expertise are widely dispersed” then 
entrepreneurial networks are truly innovative. 
Birley (1985) categorizes these entrepreneurial networks into informal and formal 
networks. An informal network includes “family, friends, […] colleagues […], a group 
which whilst it may be less informed about the options and schemes open to the 
entrepreneur, is more likely to be willing to listen and to give advice” (Birley, 1985). A 
formal network, on the other hand, includes “all the local, state, and [federal] agencies 
such as banks, accountants, lawyers […]” (Birley, 1985). Furthermore, it is stated that an 
efficient network is one “in which, no matter where [it is entered] […], [the 
entrepreneur’s] needs are diagnosed and [the entrepreneur] is passed round the system 
until […] the necessary information and advice [are gathered]” (Birley, 1985). In this 
thesis, innovation networks are understood as a resource in themselves through which 
entrepreneurial individuals or organizations gain access to resources and capabilities such 
as capital and advice. The network itself  can bring new ideas through the concept of open 
innovation and create competitive advantage for its participants. 
Research and Technology Organizations (RTOs) 
For research and technology organizations (RTOs), various wide-ranging notions exist, 
such as “technology institutes”, “technology centres”, “technology demonstration and 
application centres” (Barge-Gil & Modrego-Rico, 2008). They further distinguish 
between “public research organizations”, which includes both universities and public 
research centres, and “technology institutes” (Barge-Gil & Modrego-Rico, 2008). This 
level of granularity, however, is beyond the research goal of this work. 
Albors-Garrigos et al. (2010) define them as “organizations whose main business is 
[research and development], with the aim of enhancing the innovative performance of 
their customers”. Leitner (2005) states that these organizations can be characterized by 
their different kinds of “owners, […] legal status, missions, [organizational] structures 
and outputs”. For Barge-Gil and Modrego-Rico (2008), RTOs are a tool for technology 
and innovation policy and their main role is to “foster creativity”, “develop firms’ internal 
capabilities” and to deal with “market failures” such as “appropriability problems, 
information imperfections […], and uncertainty”. The European Association of Research 
and Technology Organisations (EARTO) defines RTOs as “non-profit [organizations] 
with public missions to support society. To do so, they closely cooperate with industries, 
==PAGE:30==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
30 
 
large and small, as well as a wide array of public actors” (EARTO, 2022). For Zylberberg 
(2019), this “involves bridging the gap between basic research and applied research and 
development”. RTOs can also be defined as networks of research institutes being part of 
several industrialized economies’ national systems of innovation (Martínez-Vela, 2016; 
Rush et al., 1995; Utterback, 1975). Successful RTOs leverage both public and private 
funding to conduct research and development competences which are needed to support 
companies and build broader capabilities within a region or a country (Zylberberg, 2019). 
These RTOs often build relationships with universities and other research institutions 
(Zylberberg, 2019). The role of RTOs, according to Albors-Garrigos et al. (2010), is that 
“they provide the necessary organizational dynamism for the innovation process by 
generating knowledge, by acting as a repository of human resource generation and 
utilization and as a source for networking within industry, universities and other 
institutions.” Other authors describe the tasks of RTOs as providing knowledge-intensive 
services, and supporting connectivity between the innovation agents (Barge-Gil & 
Modrego-Rico, 2008; Martinez-Gomez et al., 2010; Tann et al., 2002). In accordance 
with Loikkanen et al. (2011), RTOs’ functions are to “provide research and development 
[…], technology and innovation services to firms and clients”. Mrinalini and Nath (2000) 
state that RTOs “provide technological services to the industry and at the same time invest 
in research activities, keeping in view the future requirements of the industry”. According 
to Cohen and Levinthal, RTO tasks include not only to generate innovations, but also to 
develop learning capabilities (W. M. Cohen & Levinthal, 1989, 1990). Martínez-Vela 
(2016) states that RTO activities can be seen as “the vehicle to help translate [research 
and development] outcomes into innovations and in that way materialize the economic 
benefits of public investment in research”, as well as “to support new business formation

Science Parks 
In literature, a generally acceptable definition of a science park could not be found. 
However, there are various similar terms used to describe this type of intermediary, such 
as “research park”, “technology park”, “business park”, “innovation centre”, and many 
more (Monck et al., 1988). According to Macdonald (1987), these terms are used 
synonymously and all refer to the following core concepts: “a property-based initiative 
close to a place of learning, and [...] one which provides high quality units in a pleasant 
environment”. Most science parks have a strong relationship with universities and other 
institutions which are housing the facilities, and therefore “spare the search of appropriate 
facilities elsewhere for on-park firms” (Dettwiler et al., 2006). Westhead and Batstone 
(1999) further differentiate between managed and non-managed science parks, but this 
differentiation is beyond the research goal of this work. In a critical assessment paper on 
science parks, Amirahmadi and Saff (1993) found different motivations for governments 
supporting science parks, such as the belief that a science park “would promote economic 
growth at a regional and/or national level” (Löfsten & Lindelöf, 2002). On a more 
conceptual level, a science park can be considered as “a geographical distinct 
environment in which social and institutional processes can emerge, and the […] 
environment is expected to become more integrated through a texture of social and 
institutional networks take place over time” (Dettwiler et al., 2006; Johannissson, 1998). 
When looking at the added value that science parks provide, it is “difficult to appraise 
[their] effectiveness”, because the objectives of the different partners involved may differ 
significantly (Löfsten & Lindelöf, 2002; Monck et al., 1988). As Löfsten and Lindelöf 
(2002) further argue, a university, for example, may be interested in “achieving a 
satisfactory level of income from the [science] park by promoting business activities 
closely linked to its own research interests”, while private sector organizations, on the 
==PAGE:32==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
32 
 
other hand, might have more commercially motivated objectives “towards investments in 
the [science] park or its constituent firms”. However, one of the main strengths of science 
parks probably lies in a higher attractiveness level to groups of entrepreneurs “than off-
park locations” (Löfsten & Lindelöf, 2002). The researchers’ results show that science 
parks have a higher “rate of job creation” of firms located inside the park than off-park 
firms. 
In this thesis, science parks are defined as organizations aiming to create an environment 
for growth. They are described as intermediaries that play an incubator role, supporting 
the development and growth of new ventures, facilitating the transfer of know-how, and 
stimulating the development of innovative products and services.  One of the main 
characteristics of science parks is the assessment of academic knowledge and expertise 
by businesses located on-site.

2.2 
Artificial Intelligence 
Technological innovations are often seen as one of the most fundamental drivers of 
economic growth (Brynjolfsson & McAfee, 2017). So-called “general-purpose 
technologies”, just like electricity and also AI, are the most important category of these 
drivers, according to Brynjolfsson and McAfee. The most influential general-purpose 
technology of the 21st century, however, might be AI, and particularly machine learning 
(ML) (Brynjolfsson & McAfee, 2017). The history of AI reaches back to the 1950s, when 
computer scientist John McCarthy first coined the term “artificial intelligence” at an 
academic conference in 1956 (Smith et al., 2006). The journey since the 50s has not been 
a steady rise with a lot of setbacks, and even today, this technology still creates a lot of 
hype leading to unrealistic and often false expectations towards it. A lot of start-ups have 
used the term “AI” in their business plans or marketing leading to higher funds, but they 
often do not even use the technology in their business (Purnell & Olson, 2019). However, 
AI started to settle down and now begins to disrupt different industries due to the 
development of deep learning and its neural networks (see chapter 2.4). This new rise of 
AI has two major reasons. First, the availability of vast amounts of data (mostly big data), 
and second, the rising efficiency and capacity of computer components like GPUs (Soni 
et al., 2019).  
The following chapters and subchapters will provide basic definitions of the terms 
artificial intelligence (2.2.1), its various subfields (2.2.2), machine learning and its

different techniques (2.3), as well as deep learning and its neural network architectures 
(2.4). A clear delimitation between these different disciplines will also be subject

The research field of AI can be seen as an area in computer science and studies the 
development and design of such intelligent computer systems. One of the key challenges 
of intelligent machines, many scientists struggled with back in those days, was to define 
formal rules that are accurate enough to describe the world and its complexity. Due to 
these difficulties, computer scientists suggested that proper AI systems would need the 
==PAGE:45==

ability to acquire their own knowledge, by “deriving organized knowledge from 
experiments and observations” (J. G. Carbonell et al., 1983, p. 7). This learning approach 
today is known as machine learning, often abbreviated with “ML”. The introduction of 
ML has enabled computer systems to solve problems including the collection of 
information about a system’s environment, e.g., a self-navigating robot in a production 
hall. This approach makes a human intervention redundant as the machine does not have 
to be explicitly programmed to execute tasks but can change and adapt its behavior 
autonomously. As soon as the term artificial intelligence is used in the further course, it 
has the following broad meaning, which can be regarded as a combination of the various 
definitions listed in Table 3: Artificial intelligence is the ability of a machine to learn and 
adapt to a changing environment. 
2.2.2 Subfields of AI 
Artificial intelligence can be understood as an umbrella term, covering various subfields. 
The literature usually distinguishes between machine learning and deep learning as the 
two main fields of AI (Goodfellow et al., 2016). 
Kavlakoglu (2020), program manager at IBM Cloud, states that the terms machine 
learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) are often used interchangeably, “leading to some 
confusion around the nuances between them”. In her blog post, she compares the 
differentiation of AI, ML, DL and neural networks (NN) with Russian nesting dolls: Each 
being a component of the prior term (Kavlakoglu, 2020). Kavlakoglu (2020) argues in 
this way: “[…] machine learning is a subfield of artificial intelligence. Deep learning is a 
subfield of machine learning, and neural networks make up the backbone of deep learning 
algorithms”. Deep learning and neural networks are introduced and further explained in 
chapter 2.4. When looking at artificial intelligence as the development of “intelligent 
agents” (Buxmann & Schmidt, 2019), various application fields can be discovered, in 
which these intelligent agents can operate: Russell and Norvig (2010) describe the fields 
of “problem-solving”, “knowledge, reasoning, and planning”, “uncertain knowledge and 
reasoning”, “learning”, as well as “communicating, perceiving, and acting”. Examples 
of these application fields of AI include speech recognition, game playing, spam fighting, 
and machine translation. Kaplan (2016) also mentions robotics, computer vision, speech 
==PAGE:46==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
46 
 
recognition, and NLP as some of the more prominent application fields of AI. These 
various concepts are further explained in chapter 2.5. 
In this thesis, the research field of AI and its subfields is approached from an applied and 
practical perspective. Most practical books on artificial intelligence concepts and tools 
break AI down to the main areas of machine learning and deep learning, in which the 
most prominent domains are explained (Géron, 2017). This means that throughout the 
next chapters, the most prominent theoretical concepts and algorithms of machine 
learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) are first explained, and then applied. When 
approaching ML, and especially DL, from an applied perspective, the most commonly 
preferred programming language is Python (Hao & Ho, 2019), and its most popular 
frameworks usually are Scikit-Learn and TensorFlow (Géron, 2017; Hao & Ho, 2019). 
These frameworks are further described in the research design (chapter 4.1). In the 
following chapters 2.3 and 2.4, the terms machine learning and deep learning are 
explained in further detail. 
2.3 
Machine Learning 
The current accelerating boom around AI was fueled especially by breakthroughs in the 
area of machine learning in the last decades. Machine learning is about “programming 
computers to optimize a performance criterion using example data or past experience” 
(Alpaydin, 2010, Preface, xxxi). It can be described as a subfield of computer science that 
is concerned with building algorithms that “rely on a collection of examples of some 
phenomenon” (Burkov, 2020). As Luger says it correctly in his book, building intelligent 
systems is about “[…] [answers] that are neither exact nor optimal, but are in some sense 
'sufficient'. This is a result of the essential reliance on heuristic problem-solving methods 
in situations where optimal or exact results are either too expensive or not possible" 
(Luger, 2005, pp. 30–31). 
2.3.1 Definition 
Generally speaking, machine learning is the discipline of training computers to take over 
tasks based on data from examples rather than on explicit programming. After decades of 
development, ML systems are now used in clinical medicine, for example for screening 
==PAGE:47==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
47 
 
images of a patient’s retina for signs of diabetic retinopathy, which can lead to vision loss 
if diagnosed too late (Wong & Bressler, 2016). Computer programs using ML techniques 
are very efficient in identifying and generalizing patterns in datasets based on a relatively 
small set of examples (Mohri et al., 2018, pp. 1–2). For example, computers are being 
used to automatically tag people in photo collections (e.g. Facebook, Instagram). Other 
aspects of machine learning are regression, clustering, or dimensionality reduction. In 
general, these fields of ML can be categorized into supervised, unsupervised, and 
reinforcement learning (Alpaydin, 2010, pp. 4–13), depending on datasets the models 
learn from. Most of the ML algorithms process an entire dataset in order to learn from the 
examples, but some do not just experience a fixed dataset. For example, reinforcement 
learning algorithms interact with an environment, so there is a feedback loop between the 
learning system and its experiences. 
In a supervised learning scenario, the model receives a set of labeled examples as training 
data and calculates predictions for new, unseen data points. This is the most common 
scenario for classification or regression problems (Mohri et al., 2018, p. 6). 
When there are no labeled examples available in the dataset, the model exclusively learns 
from unlabeled training data, and makes predictions for the unseen data points. Clustering 
and dimensionality reduction are examples of this unsupervised learning scenario (Mohri 
et al., 2018, p. 6). 
Reinforcement learning is a scenario where the model actively interacts with the 
environment, and in some cases even affects the environment, while receiving a 
feedback/reward for each action steps. The objective of the model is to maximize its 
reward over iterations with its environment (Mohri et al., 2018, p. 7). 
2.3.2 Learning process 
The learning process itself is split into different stages. Before the model can learn, it first 
needs example data. In the case of supervised learning, this sample dataset needs to be 
correctly labeled, so that the model can understand and learn from correct examples. The 
example data is a collection of pairs (input, output), in which the inputs can be e.g., 
images, sensor data, voice records, etc. Relevant features are very important for the 
algorithm to concentrate on the correct and relevant details when learning from the 
==PAGE:48==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
48 
 
examples. Features are a set of (numeric) attributes, often represented as a vector, 
associated to an example. The output of the machine learning model can differ depending 
on the objective of the model. In binary classification models, for example, the output is 
usually either 0 or 1, which could represent e.g., “spam” or “not spam” in email spam 
filtering. 
In supervised learning scenarios, the inputs and outputs are therefore known. The central 
challenge of machine learning is to model relationships between the target output and the 
input features, so that the model can predict the output for new data based on those 
relationships it learned from the previous example datasets. The example dataset will then 
be divided into the categories “training dataset”, “validation dataset” and “test dataset”. 
The training sample is usually the largest dataset and varies for different learning 
scenarios, as described previously. The validation and test datasets are approximately the 
same sizes and smaller than the training sample. The rule of thumb is to use 70% of the 
dataset for training the model and 15% each for the validation and test sample (Burkov, 
2019). 
After the model was trained within the first stage, its abilities are usually evaluated with 
the validation dataset to check its accuracy. If the performance of the computer reaches a 
certain accuracy level, the ML system then predicts the labels of the examples in the test 
sample during the testing stage. The output label of the learning system is compared to 
the known label included in the test dataset. If the results are not satisfying, then the ML 
system might be trained with new or more data to improve its prediction model. As seen 
in Figure 1, the process of machine learning can be divided into the four parts learning, 
training, validation, and testing.

2.3.3 Classification 
In this subchapter, one of the more popular supervised learning techniques are being 
presented that play a role for the prototype phase of this thesis work. Classification is 
about learning from experiences so that new inputs can be evaluated by “matching them 
with the predefined predictive behavioral pattern” (Awad & Khanna, 2015, p. 21). 
“Machine-based classification algorithms follow [supervised learning] techniques, in 
which algorithms learn through examples […] of accurate [decision-making], using 
carefully prepared inputs. The two main steps involved in classification are synthesizing 
a model, using a learning algorithm, and employing the model to categorize new data” 
(Awad & Khanna, 2015, p. 21). Three of the more popular classification techniques are 
decision trees, support vector machines, and k-nearest neighbors. 
 
 
==PAGE:50==

50 
 
Decision Tree 
The decision tree is one type of learning algorithm that breaks the input into “regions” 
having separate parameters for each region (Goodfellow et al., 2016, p. 146). Decision 
trees, if they are relatively small, are “fast to construct” and “produce interpretable 
models” (Hastie et al., 2009, p. 352). They are able to handle different kinds of input 
variables, such as nominal, numerical and alphabetical types (Somvanshi et al., 2016). 
Decision trees are used to classify data which can be then stored and classified further. 
Applications include medicine (diagnostics of various diseases), intrusion detection, or 
image processing (grouping of 3D features in aerials images) (Somvanshi et al., 2016). 
Support Vector Machine 
Support vector machines (SVM) are used for prediction and classification problems. They 
work by calculating a separation line or decision boundary that is maximizing the 
separation margin between the datapoints belonging to different classes. A support vector 
machine can also be used for classifying nonlinear data when using “kernel functions” 
(Somvanshi et al., 2016). For this thesis, it is sufficient to know that SVMs are prediction 
tools for classification that are separating the datapoints by drawing a decision boundary, 
known as a hyperplane. Applications include handwriting recognition, face analysis, or 
pattern classification (Somvanshi et al., 2016). 
K-Nearest Neighbor 
K-nearest neighbor classification (kNN) is one of the most fundamental classification 
techniques. This algorithm, just like the previous ones, is used for classifying new, 
unlabeled observations by assigning them to the class of the “most similar labeled 
examples” (Zhang, 2016). It is based on the idea that the nearest/closest patterns to a 
target pattern, that needs to be classified, gives some useful insights on the labeling 
(Kramer, 2013). Therefore, in order to classify new datapoints, kNN looks at the k nearest 
neighbors of the training set and uses the class of the nearest one for determining the class 
of the new point. It is often useful to take more than one neighbor into account, hence the 
name “k-nearest neighbor” (Cunningham & Delany, 2022). The variable k, therefore, 
stands for the “neighborhood size”, which means the choice of k defines the “locality”. 
For example, for k=1, “little neighborhoods arise in regions, where patterns from different 
classes are scattered, and for larger k values, the algorithm “generalizes ignoring small 
==PAGE:51==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
51 
 
agglomerations of patterns” (Kramer, 2013). For example, a k value of 3 on a two-class 
problem (labels “O” and “X”) leads to the visualization seen in Figure 2. As Cunningham 
and Delany (2022) explain, all three of the nearest neighbors of 𝑞1 are of class “O”, 
therefore, it is also classified as “O”. For 𝑞2, on the other hand, the labelling is not so 
obvious, given the two neighbors of label “X” and one of label “O”. Here, deciding the 
class can be done by either “majority voting” or by “distance weighted voting” 
(Cunningham & Delany, 2022).

2.3.4 Clustering 
In this subchapter, one unsupervised learning technique is being presented that is also 
used for the prototype phase of this thesis work. Clustering is a “knowledge discovery” 
process, which can be seen as a special area of machine learning (Awad & Khanna, 2015, 
p. 21). Generally speaking, it is the process of grouping items from a collection, based on 
similar attributes or characteristics (Awad & Khanna, 2015, p. 22). This process involves 
==PAGE:52==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
52 
 
“an iterative algorithm of trial and error that operates on an assumption of similarity (or 
dissimilarity) and that stops when a termination criterion is satisfied” (Awad & Khanna, 
2015, p. 22). One of the most popular clustering technique in the field of machine learning 
is the DBSCAN clustering algorithm. By checking the documentation page of the 
DBSCAN module provided by the popular machine learning library scikit-learn, the 
abbreviation “DBSCAN” stands for “Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications 
with Noise”, and works by finding core samples of high density and expanding clusters 
from them (Scikit-learn developers, 2022). A more detailed explanation of how this 
algorithm works is given by Mahesh Kumar and Rama Mohan Reddy (2016) stating that 
“DBSCAN obtains clusters by finding the number of points within the specified distance 
from a given point. It involves computing distances from given point to all other points 
in the dataset.” The DBSCAN algorithm in scikit-learn has two required parameters, 
“eps” and “min_samples” necessary for implementation. 
“eps” (ε) refers to the “maximum distance between two samples for one to be considered 
as in the neighborhood of the other” (Scikit-learn developers, 2022). It specifies how close 
the datapoints are to each other so that they are seen as part of the cluster. 
“min_samples” refers to the “number of samples (or total weight) in a neighborhood for 
a point to be considered as a core point”, which includes the point itself (Scikit-learn 
developers, 2022). It basically is the minimum number of datapoints in order to form a 
cluster region. 
With these two parameters, the DBSCAN algorithm aims to find those areas, “which 
satisfy this minimum density, and which are separated by areas of lower density” 
(Schubert et al., 2017). All neighbors within the ε radius of a core point are considered 
to be part of the same cluster as the core point, and if any of these neighbors is again 
a core point, their neighborhoods are transitively included (see Figure 3) (Schubert et 
al., 2017). 
In Figure 3, a small example of the DBSCAN technique is visualized: The 
min_samples parameter is set to 4, and the ε parameter is indicated by the circles. The 
point N is called a “noise point” (outlier), point A is a core point, and B and C are so-
called border points.

When looking deeper into how the algorithm behind the DBSCAN technique works, 
Schubert et al. (2017) explain that the process begins “with an arbitrary point and retrieves 
its ε-neighborhood”. If this is a core point, then a new cluster is created that is expanded 
by assigning all points in its ε-neighborhood to this cluster (compare points A, B, C in 
Figure 3). If an additional core point is found in the ε-neighborhood, then the search is 
expanded to include also all points in its ε-neighborhood (Schubert et al., 2017). If no 
more core points can be found in this expanded ε-neighborhood, then the cluster is done. 
After processing all points, those which could not have been assigned to a cluster are then 
considered to be noise points (just like point N in Figure 3). This clustering technique will 
be used to cluster various start-up organizations in the further work of this thesis (see 
chapter 4.4). This subchapter has the goal to clarify the theoretical foundation behind this 
technique, as well as to give an overview of the parameters for the respective module in

2.4 
Deep Learning 
Many machine learning problems become exponentially difficult when the number of 
features is too high. For example, classification examples usually deal with two 
dimensions (e.g., for classifying if an email is spam or not). However, if there are more 
than two dimensions, it is getting more and more difficult for the model to solve these 
kinds of problems. Such multidimensional tasks often imply high computing power 
(Goodfellow et al., 2016, p. 282) and increasing costs. The development of deep learning 
was motivated by those challenges. Deep learning is another approach to develop AI, and  
is a particular subtype of machine learning “that achieves great power and flexibility by 
learning to represent the world as a nested hierarchy of concepts” (Goodfellow et al., 
2016, p. 8). In easier terms, deep learning tries to mimic the human brain and its complex 
network of neurons (Goodfellow et al., 2016, p. 13). A neuron is a nerve cell, working as 
the building block of the nervous system, and is designed to transmit information to other 
cells via connections called synapses. “By receiving external stimuli via sensory neurons, 
[the nervous system processes] the signal [and] […] gives the related response (e.g. 
muscle contraction)” (Namazi & Kulish, 2012). Each individual neuron can form tens of 
thousands of those connections with other nerve cells, creating a neural network. Similar 
to the biological neuron (Figure 4 (A)), the artificial neuron (Figure 4 (B)) takes some 
values 𝑥" as inputs, processes these values in a mathematical function 𝑓(𝑥) and returns 𝑦" 
as an output value.

Source: Edited, based on Goncalves et al. (2013)

An artificial neural network is a web of many artificial neurons (so-called “nodes”) that 
are connected within three types of layers, that are themselves interconnected. Each 
neuron’s output is the input of the next neuron in the layer structure. During the training 
phase, these networks are optimized to solve specific tasks, such as detecting faces. In 
Figure 5, the imitation of biological synapses through different layers is visualized. Just

results (compared to other AI techniques),  and it generally performs well on unstructured 
data, such as image pixels or text. However, deep learning also requires more training 
time and more resources.

2.6 
Digitalization of innovation intermediaries 
The adoption of digital technologies by the industry, circumstantially accelerated by 
COVID-19 (Amankwah-Amoah, 2021), presents an opportunity for innovation 
intermediaries to assume a novel role as providers of digitization and digital 
transformation services. Industry firms have already widely adopted digital 
transformation (Gong & Ribiere, 2021; Verhoef et al., 2021), which imposes strong 
immediate implications for innovation intermediaries to adjust existing services or create 
new ones accordingly. Linked to their function as knowledge and technology diffusors 
(Howells, 2006) and thanks to their network-oriented nature, innovation intermediaries 
are the perfect candidates to become “digitalization champions” in innovation networks. 
One example in this regard is the “Public Innovation Intermediaries” (PII), which channel 
and disseminate governments’ strategies on digital transformation of firms and other 
organizations in several countries (Rossi et al., 2021). Digitalization of innovation 
intermediaries as organizations themselves has also been studied briefly by scholars 
looking at the rising role of “Online Innovation Intermediaries” (OII) (Dong & 
Pourmohamadi, 2014; Randhawa et al., 2017). Observing these trends over the last years, 
it looks like competitiveness at least partially depends on a company’s information and 
problem-solving capabilities (Hajli & Featherman, 2018). As Haefner et al. (2021) argue, 
innovation management today plays a key role in companies’ capacity to reinvent 
themselves through exploratory initiatives, and AI can provide “instrumental assistance 
beyond the scope of humans”. One reason for this is that information processing can be 
seen as a “key component in innovation in organizations” and decision-making as a 
central activity, requires processed information from managers in the innovation process 
(Haefner et al., 2021; McNally & Schmidt, 2011; van Riel et al., 2004). These 
developments show that ”the way innovation is organized needs to be challenged by 
introducing AI and machine learning because of their cost advantages in information 
processing” (Haefner et al., 2021).

4.1 
Research design 
The research design chapter is logically structured in three parts: (1) scientific literature 
analysis, (2) quantitative study (survey) and its analysis, and (3) qualitative study (expert 
interviews) and its analysis. Research question 1 (RQ1) is being answered by a 
combination of the scientific literature analysis for the interpretation and definitions of 
the various types of innovation intermediaries (InIns), as well as the analysis of the 
quantitative study to investigate the distribution of these InIns. Research question 2 (RQ2) 
is being answered by a combination of the scientific literature analysis and the analysis 
of the expert interviews and the statements of each expert. Research question 3 (RQ3), 
which is about the conceptualization phase, is being answered by building on the potential 
analysis developed in RQ2. The most promising AI technology is being identified by this 
potential analysis and then implemented in a small prototype scenario. In the following, 
each research methodology is explicitly described. 
The scientific literature analysis was done by analyzing various papers, journals and 
books (3.3.1) in order to understand the different existing AI technologies, their general 
potentials, as well as their specific potential to improve InIns’ current services related to 
data and AI technologies. This scientific literature analysis covered more than 200 papers 
and journals on the fields of innovation intermediaries, their different types, open 
innovation, and artificial intelligence. 
The quantitative study (survey) was conducted in 2021 and is now analyzed as part of this 
thesis. The analysis was split in different parts: First, in order to understand the data and 
the distribution, some basic descriptive statistics methods were applied to the dataset. It 
was investigated how the data is distributed through various diagrams, calculations of the 
statistical location parameters mean, variance and standard deviation, and the distribution 
of the various services offered by the investigated innovation intermediaries. A cluster 
analysis of the typology, geographical and sectoral distribution, among others, can be 
studied in chapter 4.2, which deals with answering RQ1. 
For preparing, processing and analyzing the dataset, the programming language Python 
was used. The online notebook “Deepnote” (https://deepnote.com/), as well as the 
==PAGE:66==

66 
 
“Jupyter Notebook” offered by the graphical user interface “Anaconda Navigator” 
(https://docs.anaconda.com/anaconda/ navigator/), were used as development 
environments for writing Python code. 
The qualitative study (expert interviews) was conducted in April 2022 by interviewing 
different managers in leading roles of innovation intermediaries. The interview guides 
consisted of the four groups of questions “Expectations”, “Potential fields”, “Challenges” 
and “Implementation”. First, the expectations towards the use of AI were asked in order 
to have no bias included in the way of thinking about the potentials of AI. It was 
investigated how AI technologies are perceived and understood by the interview partner 
and in the two following questions, this understanding is tested on the current internal and 
external activities of the InIn: What are specific fields where AI can be applied today, and 
how could it benefit the organization? A short explanation by the interviewer of a small 
scenario serves as a creativity boost and is supposed to open the creative space for new 
potential fields of AI in the respective organization. In the challenges part, it was asked 
which current barriers hinder the organization to apply AI technologies. It was asked what 
specific reasons there are, e.g., organizational structure, culture and mindset of the 
employee/management, data quality or data management. In the last question, the focus 
was put on the implementation aspect of the thesis, where it was asked if the InIn was 
interested in a collaboration and what kinds of data there are in the organization and which 
ones could be shared. The quality of an interview is generally determined by various 
dimensions, such as design and implementation. While collecting information for a 
research project, there are various ways by which bias can appear in the study, for 
example by selecting certain organizations based on personal preferences, which is called 
a “selection bias” (Simundić, 2013). The interview partners were selected in the following 
way: First, a short-list with InIns was created based on the most “promising” innovation 
intermediaries that filled out the quantitative study. This “promising” level refers to a 
number of criteria, such as the size and growth stage of the InIn, the depth of services 
provided (niche service or holistic approach?), level of data resources (this is a guess 
based on e.g., personal contact to these InIns, size of InIn and partner network). Then, 
this short-list was contacted via email, and asked for a 30-minutes interview. The profile 
for the interview partner was either a program manager, innovation manager, acquisition 
manager, “Head of” position, CXO, or managing director. To ensure that the selected 
sample is representative of the statistical population, in case some of the contacted InIns

By scanning the scientific literature and analyzing the dataset derived from the 
quantitative study, several hypotheses were developed in order to support answering RQ2 
and RQ3. For the development of those hypotheses, the principles of the qualitative 
content analysis developed by Mayring were applied (Mey & Mruck, 2010, p. 601 ff.). 
This qualitative content analysis has the aim to make basic forms of interpreting text 
describable and verifiable using content analytical rules (Mey & Mruck, 2010, p. 602).  
The content analysis consists of the following eight steps: 
1. Clarification and theoretical justification of the research question 
2. Selection and characterization of the material 
3. Classification of the material in communication model; determination of the 
direction of the analysis 
4. Determination of the analysis units 
5. Definition of categories 
6. Formulation of the coding guide 
7. Start of deductive category application 
8. Revision of the category system 
The chapters 1.2, 1.3 and 3.2, in which the research questions together with the overall 
relevance of the research topic are described, are resulting from step 1 of the analysis 
(clarification and theoretical justification). The hypotheses aim at answering the research

object of the analysis, which has been done in the chapters 3.3.1, 3.3.2, and 3.3.3 as the

Research Result 1: Definition and clusters of innovation 
intermediaries 
In the various studies on intermediaries, researchers have identified and described several 
types of innovation intermediaries: accelerators, clusters, digital platforms, incubators, 
innovation hubs, innovation networks, research and technology organizations, and 
science parks. By collecting and combining various heterogeneous definitions in the 
scientific literature, this thesis has developed and synthesized a definition of each 
innovation intermediary type as presented in the following (Table 6).

Type 
Definition 
Accelerator 
Accelerators are organizations that aim to support and 
accelerate venture creation by providing specific incubation 
services, focused on education and mentoring, during an 
intensive program of limited duration. In addition to these 
educational services, accelerators often provide counselling 
services, training programs, investment services, as well as 
demo days or investor days. 
Cluster 
A cluster is a geographically close group of connected 
organizations or companies aiming at fostering innovations 
and creating new industries. This connected group can be 
seen as a driving force for start-ups and for accelerating the 
development of products and services. 
Digital Platform 
A digital platform, in the context of innovation 
intermediation, is a hybrid structure that provides value for its 
participants by enabling and facilitating direct interaction 
between two or more groups of participants. 
Incubator 
Incubators are organizations providing new ventures with 
resources with the goal of improving their chances of 
foundation and competitiveness. Benefits of using incubators 
as a start-up or a company include the reduction of economic 
risk by maintaining a local network, to provide space, 
infrastructure, facilities, or visibility, to support newly created 
ventures as well as to provide further benefits such as rent 
reductions, access to office space, laboratories, technology 
transfer services, basic research and consultants, as well as to 
focus on the specific needs of the entrepreneurial community. 
Innovation Hub 
In this thesis, hubs are viewed from the perspective of 
innovation and entrepreneurship. Therefore, a hub is being 
defined as a physical space that supports start-ups as well as 
individuals at various stages of their entrepreneurial 
development. A hub creates a structure where people interact

Innovation networks are understood as a resource in

organizations gain access to resources and capabilities such as 
capital and advice. The network itself  can bring new ideas

(RTO) 
RTOs are research and development organizations, with the

customers. Characteristics of RTOs are dependent on their

creativity, develop firms’ internal capabilities, as well as to

Science Park 
Science parks are defined as organizations aiming to create an 
environment for growth. They are described as intermediaries

growth of new ventures, facilitating the transfer of know-how, 
and stimulating the development of innovative products and 
services.  One of the main characteristics of science parks is 
the assessment of academic knowledge and expertise by

In the following, each of the AI technologies mentioned are being explained a bit further 
in order to understand how they can bring a value-added in the support of innovation 
intermediaries. 
Natural language processing (NLP) 
The AI technology NLP can support innovation intermediaries in different ways. For 
example, NLP can enable a semantic understanding of input text, e.g., of surveys that 
were sent to potential new lead start-ups. The survey input of these start-ups could be 
processed more easily with NLP methods, such as information extraction, discourse 
analysis, NL question-answering, commonsense interfaces, commonsense thinking and 
reasoning (Chowdhary, 2020; Khurana et al., 2017). Companies such as Cauliflower.ai 
are offering these kinds of solutions by providing various NLP techniques, such as topic 
identification, sentiment analysis, keyword extraction, etc. 
Robotic process automation (RPA) 
In RPA, one potential field of AI technologies is text extraction. According to Baidya 
(2021), various business documents could be processed and formatted through process 
automation by combining optical character recognition and NLP systems as described by 
Baidya (2021) or Ling et al. (2020). This would save resources, such as time and money, 
as innovation intermediaries are often struggling with data collection and processing (e.g., 
#E04: 9-14; 26-28). Another potential field within the domain of RPA is the updating of 
customized CRM systems, as experts #E01 and #E06 suggested (#E01: 144-145; #E06: 
52, 70-71). Following the paper by Kokina and Blanchette (2019), robotic process 
automation could help reducing human errors in systems, because “bots can perform non-
cognitive tasks with greater accuracy than human employees”. Especially for highly 
customized systems, innovation intermediaries could then utilize RPA tools, such as the 
ones provided by UiPath, to support them in updating customer data in their systems. 
Expert systems 
For the domain of expert systems, another field where AI and machine learning could be 
beneficial was the process of team formation, as experts #E08 and #E09 stated. According 
to scientific literature, various analytical models can be used to facilitate this process of 
team formation, e.g., in projects (Strnad & Guid, 2010). By deriving personnel attributes 
from quantitative data, innovation intermediaries can benefit from expert systems by 
==PAGE:91==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
91 
 
finding and forming teams and e.g., aligning entrepreneurs with investors more easily. 
Another similar potential field is matchmaking, as stated by experts #E01, #E02, #E03, 
#E05, #E06, #E07, and #E09 (78% of all experts interviewed). Scientific literature states 
that in situations, “where technological problems and situations are well understood and 
articulated, [innovation intermediaries] are able to mobilize large numbers of agents into 
market-like exchanges that produce low costs for searchers at low risk of opportunistic 
behavior […], [but there are situations], […] where these conditions do not hold”: One of 
these situations is classified as “matchmaking” (Håkanson et al., 2011). Matchmaking 
refers to a situation in which the innovation intermediary is able to connect parties “who 
were not previously aware of one another’s existence and/or of the potential synergies in 
combining one another’s knowledge” (Håkanson et al., 2011). Especially the machine 
learning domain of ‘prediction’ and its sub-domains ‘classification’, ‘analysis’ and 
‘recommendation’ (see Figure 6) could have high potentials for automating some of 
services offered in innovation intermediaries’ portfolios such as matchmaking, as a paper 
by Foner and Crabtree (1997) shows. Matchmaking itself is often a manual process, tuned 
by hand, as stated in the expert interviews (#E01: 47-48, #E09: 44-49). On the other side, 
in machine learning, the algorithms are being automatically tuned from the data 
(Alpaydin, 2010, Preface, xxxi). According to Foner and Crabtree (1997), there is a 
number of reasons why matchmaking people and introducing them to each other could be 
interesting. One of the most relevant for innovation intermediaries could be the following: 
“It is often the case that people need to find an expert in some field, but finding such an 
expert can be difficult and time-consuming. Those who are not well ‘plugged-in’ via word 
of mouth can find this even more difficult” (Foner & Crabtree, 1997). The same concept 
could also be extended to matching team members, start-ups or investors. 
Visual recognition 
In visual recognition methods, optical character recognition (OCR) can be used for 
automatic document scanning (e.g., a Word or PDF file) and further processing in invoice 
and account management, as Becker and Gassmann; Howells (2006b; 2006) explain. 
Another domain of visual recognition could be information extraction from websites and 
platforms. For example, websites such as Crunchbase hold vast amounts of information 
about corporates and start-ups that could be valuable for innovation intermediaries, e.g., 
for scouting new start-ups. There are also various scraping tools, such as “Grepsr.com”,

with which InIns can automatically extract interesting public data on these start-ups and 
use them for further internal processing. 
Deep Neural Networks / Support decision-making processes 
As Haefner et al. (2021) put it, information processing “is a key component in innovation 
in organizations, […] and a central activity […] is the “process of decision making, which 
requires information processing by managers involved in the innovation process”. 
Furthermore, organizational information processing can be “supported or, in some cases, 
taken over by AI systems” (Haefner et al., 2021). According to Haefner et al. (2021), 
current AI systems “excel at overcoming humans’ information processing constraints” in 
the area of opportunity development. AI systems and their neural network structures 
thereby rely heavily on vast amounts of data (Ng, 2017). Knowing this, Haefner et al. 
(2021) argue that AI systems can be “able to support humans in the development of ideas, 
opportunities, and solution approaches by processing a much larger amount of 
information than is humanly possible and unearthing interesting areas for investigation”. 
The potential of AI to contribute to collaborative work, group support and creativity is 
also stressed by Siemon et al. (2015). Following an article by Roose (2019), artificial 
intelligence and automation are already creating a significant economic value for 
companies.

The results of the scientific literature analysis show a strong evidence that AI technologies 
in fact have the ability to improve or even to add value to the existing service portfolio of

intermediaries to adjust or rethink their services, has a “transformational” character (Quan

The Pearson correlation coefficient is usually used for normally distributed data, and 
ranges from –1 to +1, where 0 indicates no linear relationship between the variables, and 
the closer it gets to 1 the stronger the relationship gets and ultimately approaches a straight 
line (Schober et al., 2018). In this case, the two distributions are not really normally 
distributed. However, Schober et al. (2018) recommends that both variables “follow a 
bivariate normal distribution in the population from which they were sampled”. As both 
variables originate from survey with N=86, a normal distribution in the population 
(innovation intermediaries in Denmark), in this case, is considered given. Another 
requirement of the Pearson correlation coefficient, according to Schober et al. (2018), is 
that there are “no relevant outliers” as “[extreme] outliers may have undue influence on 
the Pearson correlation coefficient.” In both variables, no extreme outliers can be 
detected. 
After running the correlation analysis, the output is a Pearson correlation coefficient of 
0.5564 (=55.64%), which can be considered as at most a “moderate” linear relationship. 
It is, nevertheless, to be tested if the corresponding p-value is statistically significant or 
not. A p-value of 0.05 or lower is usually considered statistically significant. Here, the p-
value equals 0.0000000266, so it is far lower than 0.05. 
However, in this case, with a Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.5564, H05 cannot be

1. Foresight and diagnostics 
During literature research, the concept of matchmaking (in the broadest sense) in the field 
of foresight and diagnostics was often connected to health and medicine research. As 
described in literature, matchmaking in this domain is used to help diagnosing patients 
(Brokamp et al., 2021) and matching cases with similar phenotypic and/or genotypic 
profiles (Lambertson et al., 2015). Transferred to the innovation and entrepreneurship 
domain, a similar concept of foresight and diagnostics can be observed in the process of 
“foresight through strategic planning” (Gliedt et al., 2018). According to them, an 
important function of InIns is “demand articulation”, which includes “scanning for 
information/opportunities, foresight through strategic planning, diagnosis through 
needs/knowledge gap assessment” (Gliedt et al., 2018). As it can be seen, foresight and 
==PAGE:103==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
103 
 
diagnostics are important functions of InIns. Matchmaking can be one technique to offer 
this service, which leads to a high assessment regarding that performance potential. 
2. Scanning and information processing 
In institutional environments in international matchmaking, potential partners from 
different countries are likely to bring “asymmetric conceptions of trust to the business 
relationship because the institutional implication for understanding business [behavior] 
can vary across different national context” (Ma et al., 2014). In these situations, 
“innovation intermediation is critical for collaborative innovation and open innovation. 
From a new institutional economics perspective, intermediation [and matchmaking] is a 
way to [minimize] asymmetric information in the market” (Ma et al., 2014). 
The aspects of “foresight and diagnostics” and “knowledge processing, generation and 
combination” also fit to one of the three types of innovation intermediaries, that Lopez 
and Vanhaverbeke (2009) have defined: “Collaboration and support group”, including 
knowledge processing and combination, commercialization, foresight and diagnosis. 
Especially as brokers, intermediaries often “lessen the information deficit of users that 
may result from particular information needs not being fulfilled” (McNie, 2007; 
Michaels, 2009). To conclude, matchmaking can have a positive influence on scanning 
and information processing. However, compared to the field of foresight and diagnostics, 
not so many papers on the field of information processing could be found, which is why 
an assessment level between medium and high was chosen. 
3. Knowledge processing, generation and combination 
In the domain of semantic matchmaking in the military field, matchmaking “will exploit 
knowledge about […] decision intent, the value of information, and features of the 
coalition communication network in order to support the efficient exploitation of coalition 
information assets” (Smart et al., 2011). Using matchmaking for scanning and 
information processing is also described in the entrepreneurship domain: Agogué et al. 
(2017) mention that innovation intermediaries „make external knowledge accessible“, 
while Stewart and Hyysalo (2008) state that they “improve connectivity”. Again, not so 
many literature was found regarding knowledge processing through matchmaking, so the 
assessment level was set between medium and high.

4. Gatekeeping and brokering 
Regarding the tasks on gatekeeping and brokering (facilitating negotiations, deal 
making), matchmaking can probably bring the biggest benefit as the core idea of 
brokering is to arrange or negotiate an arrangement. An example of the advantages of 
matchmaking and brokering is the cross-border business matchmaking facility supported 
by GreenCape and the Climate Technology Program, whose goal it is to “stimulate and 
catalyze the growth of local and regional green markets” (Baboolal & White, 2022). 
Another advantage of matchmaking in the domain of gatekeeping and brokering is 
described by Rong et al. (2021), who focus on the sharing economy (SE) domain. Here, 
matchmaking “can enhance the liquidity of SE [platforms]” and can lead to “lower 
transaction costs” and “removes the need for institutional […] overhead” (Rong et al., 
2021). As seen by these two examples, the potential of matchmaking in gatekeeping and 
brokering is high. 
5. Testing and validation, 6. Accreditation and standards, 10. Assessment and 
evaluation 
For the domains of testing and validation (modelling, validation, and training of new 
analytic methods) as stated by Howells (2006), as well as for accreditation and standards 
(standard setter, providing advice, developing references), and for assessment and 
evaluation (general assessment of performance and technologies, specific evaluation of 
products and technologies once in the market), no specific use cases of matchmaking 
could be found in scientific literature. That is why for these tasks and functions, the 
potential assessment level is set to low. However, this does not mean that matchmaking 
has no potential in these areas. More research needs to be done in these three functions 
described by Howells (2006). 
7. Regulation and arbitration 
The concept of matchmaking is not new, nor is the concept of platform models based on 
intermediation. What has changed in the digital era today, however, is the scaling effect 
and the higher complexity in the market for matchmakers (Demertzis, 2021). According 
to the Demertzis (2021), technology-based services can “be scaled-up without big 
investment”, which leads to the increasing growth of intermediary organizations and 
platforms. Thus, it is relatively hard to make sure that a few of these organizations do not 
==PAGE:105==

105 
 
concentrate their market power, and regulation “is only just emerging” (Demertzis, 2021). 
However, especially in the domain of banking, regulations could “intensify competitive 
pressure from non-bank dealers” and, therefore, encourage bank dealers to shift their 
business towards matchmaking leading to a “more efficient form of financial 
intermediation” (Saar et al., 2019). Therefore, it seems that matchmaking and regulation 
could potentially benefit each other to some extent, which is why the assessment is 
between low and medium. 
8. Intellectual Property 
Intellectual property right (IPR) protection, according to Ma et al. (2014), is “a great 
concern” with 56% of the interviewed organizations expressing their concern regarding 
IPR theft. One of the biggest problems in this domain is that companies usually invest “a 
great deal of effort and financial resources in developing instruments to motivate people 
to submit ideas to companies” (Harland & Nienaber, 2014). However, external 
contributors, such as innovation intermediaries, are often “not willing to submit their 
ideas” because they are afraid that they will not be compensated for them afterwards” 
(Harland & Nienaber, 2014) . With this development, the question arises as to how 
beneficial relationships for both parties can be built without the obstacle of a potential 
IPR theft. A potential solution to this dilemma was developed by Harland and 
Nienaber (2014). They have built a theoretical model which is called the “trust-based 
intermediary model”, which allows “efficient matchmaking between both partners” 
making it “possible for the idea contributor to make sure that the company is not lying 
about its knowledge of a submitted idea” (Harland & Nienaber, 2014). This example 
shows, that even in the critical field of intellectual property, it is possible for matchmakers 
to keep their intellectual property rights protected and still ensure a mutually beneficial 
matchmaking process. Thus, the assessment is set to a medium level. 
9. Commercialization 
The task of commercialization, which according to Howells (2006), includes aspects, 
such as market research, business planning, support in selling and commercialization 
process, early stage capital, and IPOs, is also found in other papers in scientific 
literature: Lopez and Vanhaverbeke (2009) talk about three types of InIns, one being the 
“collaboration and support group” dealing with commercialization, among other tasks. 
On the other hand, Martínez-Vela (2016) states that the core tasks of InIns include 
==PAGE:106==

106 
 
ideation, research, development, demonstration, production, IP protection and 
commercialization, business and market development, collaboration and networking, and 
funding. Especially in the context of open innovation, innovation intermediaries stimulate 
entrepreneurial activities through the commercialization of ideas (Iakovleva, 2013). 
According to Holzmann et al. (2014), matchmaking in innovation has been studied 
extensively and is essential “to find partners with ideas […], technologies […], or to find 
‘lead-users’ for new product development”. According to Gao et al. (2021), business 
incubators, a form of innovation intermediaries, provide “efficient mechanisms for start-
up clients to generate, complement, and ultimately commercialize their proprietary 
knowledge, which in turn enables their international growth”. Further, Gao et al. (2021) 
also state that business incubators “facilitate the generation of knowledge […] and 
support their start-up clients surviving from market selection. They replicate the 
successful incubation through matchmaking.” With these examples, it becomes clear that 
matchmaking for facilitating the commercialization process of start-ups has a significant 
potential to support innovation intermediaries, hence the high assessment level. 
 
This evaluation of matchmaking shows that this domain can help with many of the tasks 
and functions that innovation intermediaries, according to Howells (2006), are offering 
in their service portfolio. Matchmaking is therefore chosen as the focus domain for the 
development of the prototype (as described in chapter 4.4).

4.4.1 Goal and intention 
The goal of this conceptualization phase of the thesis is to build a prototype based on 
machine learning that has the potential to support the matchmaking process of innovation 
intermediaries in Denmark. During the interviews conducted, it became evident that the 
field of matchmaking could have the biggest potential of the service portfolio of the 
Danish InIns. Matchmaking as such is a very broad field so there can be different 
meanings and forms. This is why in this chapter, the focus will be put on matching given 
start-ups in Denmark with other Danish start-ups that can be considered similar based on 
different criteria. The main idea behind the prototype is to compare and match Danish 
start-ups within the same industries and with the same business models based on their 
variety of skills and competences. For an innovation intermediary manager, for example, 
it is important to know which start-ups to scout for and which ones seem promising 
compared to competitive start-ups in the field (#E02: 144, 167; #E05: 4-6, 44-47). As 
expert #E04 said in the interview, it would be very beneficial to “have the system just 
shoot out a lot of different sort of conclusions” (#E04: 169-170). So, the idea is to build 
a tool that takes information about a start-up that an InIn manager wants to have analyzed 
and compared to other start-ups, filters out all start-ups in the database that have similar 
characteristics, extracts valuable information on the start-ups’ current skills and 
competences, and outputs an overview of the various competences of both the given start-
up and its competitors in the industry. With this insight, the manager can directly see in 
which competency fields his/her start-up needs to improve to catch-up with competition.

Next, this filtered selection is then being clustered using the DBSCAN algorithm 
explained in chapter 2.3.4. In order to be able to perform a clustering algorithm on a 
dataset that has both categorical (e.g., “Employees”) and numerical values (e.g., 
“Founded”), there are various ways to transform the dataset. According to Lasaosa 
(2021), categorical features could be transformed into numerical values using techniques 
such as one-hot encoding. However, these transformations could then lead to a 
misunderstanding of the algorithm, and could create meaningless clusters (Lasaosa, 
2021). This is where Lasaosa introduces the Gower Distance measuring similarity and 
dissimilarity, first defined by J. C. Gower in 1971 (Gower, 1971). By not going into more 
details as this would go beyond the scope of this thesis, to calculate the so-called Gower 
similarity (GS) between observations 𝛼 and 𝛽 (e.g., two start-ups), GS is computed as the 
average of partial similarities across the 𝑛 features of the observation. GS is a non-
Euclidean and non-metric value meaning that clustering algorithms such as K-mean may 
not be used (Lasaosa, 2021). In this project, the Python module “gower” 
(https://pypi.org/project/gower/) is being used, as well as the DBSCAN clustering 
algorithm with its “metric” parameter set to "precomputed", as recommended by Lasaosa 
(2021). When applying the gower module to the dataset, the result outputted by the 
module is a distance matrix that can be used as the input for DBSCAN. Setting up the 
DBSCAN algorithm, the value for “eps” (epsilon) was set to 0.2 (to ensure a decent level 
of cluster variety), while “min_samples” was set to 20 (to ensure a decent level of 
significant clusters). The result of DBSCAN with the clusters can also be seen below. As 
shown by Figure 18, the clusters are well distributed giving a decent clustering of the 
start-ups.

4.4.4 Interpretation of model output 
The model’s output should be interpreted in the following way: For each start-up listed 
(first value), the listed skillset of this start-up contains various skills and competences of 
the people working for that organization. The format of the skills has the shape of 
“[Number] [Skill]”, e.g., “2 Microsoft Word”. The number 2 corresponds to the number 
of employees having that skillset within that start-up; in this case: two employees. So, 
this machine learning model outputs start-ups, their skillset, and the employees 
distribution of each skill listed. 
The research question that the development of this prototype answers investigates how 
the implementation of an AI technology (in this case matchmaking by clustering) can be 
conceptualized. It is important to emphasize that this prototype must be interpreted as a 
first draft towards the development of a fully developed AI tool that can support the work 
of innovation intermediaries. There are some limitations that this tool has (see chapter 5 
for details). Most importantly, the functionality level of this tool can be extended by 
considering the following potential features.

Discussion 
The research topic about innovation intermediaries and their different forms and shapes 
can be complex and opaque due to the high heterogeneity in their definitions and 
differentiations. In this thesis, only some of the forms of InIns were considered and 
investigated in order to at least get an overview of their various types and functions. Of 
course, the functions explained are by no means exhaustive and more tasks and focus 
areas could probably be found in further and more extensive research. For this thesis, it 
was sufficient to focus on the defined typology and to get an understanding of how these 
innovation intermediaries could benefit from new digital technologies, such as artificial 
intelligence and machine learning. It was discussed that in the corporate and start-up 
world, many organizations already adopted digital technologies (Gong & Ribiere, 2021; 
Verhoef et al., 2021), and that these can impose heavy implications for innovation 
intermediaries to adjust existing services or create new ones accordingly. It was also 
stated that due to these trends, the competitiveness level of a company probably, at least 
partially, could depend on its information and problem-solving capabilities (Hajli & 
Featherman, 2018). One of these technologies is artificial intelligence (AI). Just like 
technologies like the steam engine or electricity, AI has a strong transformational 
character that is already influencing several industries (Quan & Sanderson, 2018). As 
Haefner et al. (2021) argue, innovation management today plays a key role in companies’ 
capacity to reinvent themselves through exploratory initiatives, and AI can provide 
“instrumental assistance beyond the scope of humans”, because information processing 
is often described as a “key component in innovation in organizations” (Haefner et al., 
2021). For managers, decision-making, as a central activity in innovation and 
entrepreneurship, heavily depends on processed information (McNally & Schmidt, 2011; 
van Riel et al., 2004). These developments have showed that AI and machine learning 
could, at least from the theoretical side, can bring strong benefits and cost advantages in 
information processing (Haefner et al., 2021). These benefits were underlined by various 
expert interviews, where the majority confirmed the advantages of AI in the fields of 
innovation and entrepreneurship. Another aspect of the research design of this thesis was 
to find out how Danish innovation intermediaries are positioned with regards to this 
technology today and what kinds of data- and AI-based services they intend to provide in 
the future. For this, the survey with 86 usable responses were analyzed and the two count 
==PAGE:120==

variables of provided and intended data- and AI-based services defined. These variables 
had the goal to understand how InIns could develop more AI capabilities, and to better 
understand what the current limitations of implementing AI technologies are today. The 
two hypotheses here were that today, 1) Danish InIns would show a low level of provided 
data- and AI-based services, and 2) that they would show a high level of intended data- 
and AI-based services. The idea was that the InIns would not be able to provide a high 
level of AI-based services today, as this technology is still young and being tested in the 
industry, but that the majority of them would at least be open to try it out and would plan 
to offer such services in the future. However, as it can be seen in Table 14 (descriptive 
statistics), the mean value for the provided (intended) data & AI services of 1.5116 
(1.7907) is relatively low looking at the possible value range of minimum 0 and maximum 
14. This leads to a confirmation of the first hypothesis, but a rejection of the second one 
assuming a threshold of 50%. These facts show clearly that AI and machine learning, as 
well as the digital transformation in general, are not yet fully diffused into the start-up 
and corporate world. This can be explained by some of the expert statements: Some 
experts stated that they are not sure yet what AI can actually do for them (#E03: 61-64; 
#E04: 5-6, 61-63; #E09: 4-6), and that AI technologies are still developing and need more 
time to accelerate to show their true transformative character (#E03: 10-33). 
This thesis shows, that the scientific literature already started an extensive discussion on 
the possibilities to connect innovation and entrepreneurship to artificial intelligence. In 
Table 13, it can be seen that there are some very specific use cases for AI technologies 
that can bring a real value-added for organizations, such as innovation intermediaries. 
With the help of literature and industry examples, it was demonstrated that this discussion 
is in full swing and new ideas and concepts are developed frequently. 
One small contribution to this ongoing discussion is made not only by screening what 
kinds of various potential fields are being described in literature (chapter 4.3, Table 13), 
but also to go one step further and to try to outline how these potentials of AI and machine 
learning could practically be implemented and utilized. The conceptualization phase 
(chapter 4.4) also showed that using machine learning techniques requires processed data, 
the right quality, and especially a clear vision on what problem the ML product should 
solve. Here, those responsible have to always weigh up and decide whether machine 
learning is the only right means to solve this problem or if another technique or already 
developed product can achieve the same goal. Technology, in the end, is only just the

means to solve a problem. In the conceptualized prototype, for example, it can be 
discovered that the actual machine learning part (supervised and unsupervised machine 
learning) is rather small compared to the whole process. A big portion of the work is the 
data collecting, cleaning and processing part. 
Nevertheless, it is important to say, that with regards to AI and all its various forms, a lot 
of research and development will be necessary to fully exploit its potentials in the future. 
Industry and society are just beginning to understand how this technology works and 
which tasks it can solve and which ones might be not suitable. Again, not everything that 
can be solved with machine learning should be solved in this way. There are always 
different considerations to be made before deciding to choose AI for a given problem. 
This is also underlined by expert #E07 (23-40). by saying that the start point of reaching 
artificial intelligence is actually finding out: “What do we want to find out?”.

Limitations of work 
This thesis can be considered a work of explorative nature. Different topics that are still 
being researched themselves and where there is great heterogeneity towards definitions, 
limitations and subfields (such as innovation intermediation, artificial intelligence, and 
digital transformation). The limitations of this work are categorized into three 
components. 
6.1 
Subject-specific limitations 
When looking at chapter 2.1, it was stated that there are many heterogeneous notations 
and terms for innovation intermediaries. A formal, widely accepted definition of the term 
“innovation intermediary” is still missing. That is why, a clear and exhaustive typology 
of innovation intermediaries is not possible to give, which is why an own typology was 
developed in this thesis (Table 6). This typology, however, only considered a few of the 
various types of InIns and depending on other researchers’ perception, completely 
different types could be defined and categorized. 
Another subject-specific limitation, surely, is the sole focus on those services of InIns 
concerning data and AI technologies. Because the overall focus area of this thesis is 
innovation intermediation and digital transformation, it was of course interesting to 
investigate those specific services of InIns. This goes hand in hand with the distinct focus 
on AI and machine learning in chapter 2, which should be seen as only a small component 
of the broad term of digital transformation. This transformation should be rather 
understood as an umbrella term covering various components, one being the field of AI 
and intelligent agents. 
A third subject-specific limitation that should be mentioned in this context is the focus on 
the InIn functions developed by Howells (2006) used for the potential analysis (Table 
15). Here, of course, other functions and definitions could be used in further research. In 
this thesis, those functions described by Howells (2006) were detailed and specific 
enough to develop the potential analysis for matchmaking. Surely, other potential fields 
next to matchmaking can be considered to investigate further. Here, matchmaking was 
==PAGE:123==

123 
 
the domain for which the majority of experts saw the biggest potentials (compare 
validation of hypothesis H06). 
6.2 
Research-specific limitations 
Focusing on limitations regarding the research environment and design, it must be said 
that the analysis of the survey is based on 86 usable responses derived from an overall 
size of 171 innovation intermediaries surveyed. In order to validate and further deepen 
this research field, this number should be increased. The more organizations there are in 
the dataset, the higher the accuracy and reliability of the research results. Another point 
here is that the focus market was set to Denmark, because this master thesis is co-
developed and co-supervised by DTU in Denmark, together with TUM in Germany. The 
innovation intermediation market in e.g., Germany could be very different to the Danish 
counterpart. 
Another aspect that is worth mentioning is the number of the expert interviews conducted. 
Due to the scope of this thesis, it was sufficient to conduct nine interviews in total, as no 
new insights were found after the nineth interview. However, in further research, the 
number of experts should be increased, and it should be tested whether the results still 
apply. 
6.3 
Prototype-specific limitations 
With regards to the conceptual prototype that was developed in this thesis, it was based 
on a very specific use case, which is matching a start-up with other start-ups based on 
similarity. In a more extensive research project, this prototype could be developed for a 
broader spectrum of use cases, potentially including matching start-ups with suitable 
investors or other companies who could be interested in a partnership. Other business use 
cases could surely be developed here (compare chapter 4.4). Entering a more technical 
level, the underlying structure of the prototype is rather basic in its functionality. The 
algorithm, depending on the inputted Excel sheet and the chosen industries, sometimes 
has problems scraping all necessary information from LinkedIn. For example, it has 
happened quite often that the information listed on the profile pages of a start-up could 
==PAGE:124==

124 
 
not be extracted due to random security checks prompted by LinkedIn. If a website is 
opened in a short interval using the same IP address, LinkedIn detects this behavior and 
assumes a potential spam action. This is why, a security check is implemented that asks 
to solve a random problem, e.g., by choosing the correct image fitting to a word. This task 
is supposed to be hard to solve by computers but easy to solve by humans. However, after 
checking the documentation of the scraper and different forums discussing this topic, no 
solution was found. One idea, which could be tested in further research, is to try it with 
changing IP addresses, e.g., by using a VPN (virtual private network), or by extending 
the waiting intervals before the prototype starts opening the respective website. Further, 
it should also be mentioned that the algorithm is very rough and simple. There are still 
lots of bugs and improvements to be made. However, as the scope of this thesis was to 
build a conceptualization of a prototype, and explicitly not to build a ready-for-rollout 
prototype, this simple algorithm is considered sufficient. 
Also, when looking at the data foundation that the algorithm and the machine learning 
model had for training, it must be pointed out that all data came from The Hub. However, 
this platform is already pre-processed and classified, meaning that it does not represent 
the whole start-up ecosystem in Denmark at all. This database should be extended, as 
described in 4.4, by e.g., more public platforms and websites holding information about 
Danish start-ups. Furthermore, by taking other platforms into consideration, the number 
of features and datapoints can also be extended by e.g., looking at parameters such as the 
start-ups’ own websites, newspaper websites, number of jobs posted, scraping Google 
Trends and other websites that hold valuable information about start-ups and companies. 
In general, the more information there is about a start-up, the more accurate and useful 
the model could be. 
However, the goal of this thesis was to develop a conceptualization of a prototype, and 
this prototype provides more functionality than only using The Hub, but there are, of 
course, many different and extended outputs of the model possible.

7 
Conclusion and outlook 
This master thesis had the aim to provide a deeper understanding about the interplay 
between innovation intermediaries and artificial intelligence technologies. The intention 
of answering RQ1 was to clarify the heterogeneity of the definitions of innovation 
intermediaries and their typology that can be found in scientific literature. The various 
definitions of the subtypes were synthesized using various existing definitions in 
literature, as well as analyzing empirical data from Denmark, to create uniform definitions 
and clusters for this thesis. It was shown that by consulting innovation intermediaries, 
companies and especially start-ups can develop new ideas and grow much faster than by 
trying to innovate and grow by themselves. Furthermore, innovation intermediaries can 
create value for companies “by identifying, accessing, and transferring solutions to 
problems in various stages of the innovation process” (Hargadon & Sutton, 1997; 
Nambisan & Sawhney, 2007; Sawhney et al., 2003; Verona et al., 2006). This value 
comes from the rich service portfolio that innovation intermediaries are offering, which 
has been studied by a number of researchers (Howells, 2006; Lin & Wei, 2018; Russo et 
al., 2018; Silva et al., 2018). Nevertheless, little is known about the potentials and 
opportunities for innovation intermediaries, that are emerging from digital technologies 
and how they can benefit these organizations to improve their service offerings, or even 
create new ones. This gap is important to explore, as several researchers argue that 
“digital technologies and algorithms” (Geliskhanov & Yudina, 2018) have the potential 
to automate specific processes of InIns, such as the process of  matchmaking, that can be 
used to find new customers and partners. That is why the second research question looked 
at the potential that AI and machine learning technologies have for innovation 
intermediaries and their services. For this question, different expert interviews with 
various innovation intermediary managers were conducted, and different potential fields 
in which AI and ML offer advantages for innovation intermediaries were identified and 
mapped to these technologies. The third and last research question was answered by a 
practical example of the previously mapped out advantages that AI can bring. This 
conceptual prototype that was developed showed how machine learning techniques can 
be used to extract and analyze various start-up data and automatically derive conclusions 
for the innovation intermediary managers. This prototype can help the managers by 
==PAGE:126==
Potential evaluation and conceptual implementation of AI-infused service offerings of Danish intermediary organizations 
126 
 
saving valuable resources such as costs and time that they would otherwise spend for 
manually acquiring and accumulating information on various start-ups. 
Digital technologies, such as artificial intelligence, can improve or even create completely 
new services for innovation intermediaries today. These intermediary organizations are 
facing radical changes and new opportunities for their businesses as disruptive 
technologies are entering the market and provide a set of advantages if intermediaries are 
able to utilize them correctly.
