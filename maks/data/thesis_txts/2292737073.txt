Abstract
Infective endocarditis (IE) is a serious disease with a mortality at 20-25% in western
countries [34]. Treatment of IE is composed of long term intravenous antimicrobial
therapy (4-6 weeks), and in many cases surgery with implementation of valve pros-
thesis is necessary. The viridans streptococci have long been recognized as one of
the major group of bacteria causing 20-60% of all IE globally [10, 66, 89]. Fast and
targeted treatment of the infection is of high importance for the patient to recover
from the infection. However, identiﬁcation of these species has always caused great
challenges using phenotypic methods, and the pathogenicity of the strains are far from
fully understood [14].
The Department of Clinical Microbiology, Slagelse Hospital, have collected and whole
genome sequenced 80 Streptococcus strains belonging to the Mitis group streptococci
from patients with IE. This thesis is a part an ongoing Ph.D. study by Louise Hes-
selbjerg Rasmussen. The aim of this project was to perform bioinformatics and com-
parative genomic analysis on the strains, to identify virulence and potential antibiotic
resistance genes. To enable this analysis, the genomes have been assembled, anno-
tated, and diﬀerent phylogeny models have been tested on the strains to establish
their correct taxonomic classiﬁcation. The protein domains architecture in the strains
have been identiﬁed and based on those, a pan - and core genome has been gener-
ated. Analysis on the core genome has been performed to identify potential virulence
genes by involving related non pathogenic reference strains. Based on the phylogeny
performed in this study four Streptococcus strains were re-annotated. The genomic
comparison analysis revealed 15 potential pathogen speciﬁc gene families. Several po-
tential species speciﬁc gene families of four Mitis group Streptococcus species analysed
in this study were furthermore identiﬁed. Several potential virulence gene families
were observed in both the pan - and core genome of the analysed genomes. Lastly,
55 antibiotic resistance genes were identiﬁed in ResFinder. Additional studies of the
identiﬁed gene families are necessary to suggest possible to drug targets and establish
the true function of the identiﬁed genes. Therefore, funding for a Ph.d. project has
been applied.

Preface
Infective endocarditis (IE), is an infection of the inner heart valves with 1.7-6.2 recorded
cases per 100,000 patients every year [34, 74]. Mitis group streptococci, normally com-
mensal bacteria of the human oropharynx, often cause the infection, and very little is
known of how they become pathogens. Treatment of IE often involve long term intra-
venous antibiotic treatment, and in approximately 50% of the cases heart surgery [34, 74].
Fast and correct identiﬁcation of the bacterial strains in the blood contribute to a more
eﬃcient treatment and prognosis of the infection. This project will attempt to identify
potential virulence factors in Mitis group streptococci which might explain the changing
face of Mitis group streptococci: going from being a commensal to a pathogenic strain.
This project will address this issue using high quality DNA data of known pathogenic
Mitis group streptococci and Mitis group streptococci not related to an infection. The
project will focus on six species of the Mitis group Streptococcus: S. cristatus, S. gordonii,
S. infantis, S. mitis, S. oralis, S. sanguinis, which all are known as commensal species in
the oral cavity, but have been isolated from blood cultures from patients with IE. Potential
virulence - and antibiotic resistance genes will be investigated in the streptococci genomes,
and funding have been be applied to continue the master project as a Ph.D. project.

Perspectives and speciﬁc aims
Bioinformatic analysis of the whole genome information will give a better understanding of
the pathogenecity of Mitis group streptococci, normally a commensal bacteria, which may
cause serious disease as infective endocarditis. Identiﬁcation of core genes that are spe-
ciﬁc for the pathogenic strains can potentially be used as target for therapeutic treatment
related to streptococci infections. Therefore this project has the following aims:
- High quality assembly of the Streptococcus genomes.
- Comparative studies of pathogenic and commensal Mitis group streptococci to identify
potential virulence genes.
- Identiﬁcation of genes which could contribute to faster and easier separation of the Mitis
group streptococci to species level.
- Identiﬁcation of antibiotic resistance genes in the Mitis group Streptococcus genomes.

To understand how Mitis group streptococci are able to attach and infect the heart valves,
it is important to understand how the bacteria grow in their normal niche. Therefore, this
section will go into detail on how Mitis group streptococci grow in the oral cavity and
how they interact with their surroundings. Last in this section, the pathogenic face of
the Mitis group streptococci are commented on, along with a more detailed explanation
of their role in IE.
3.1
The Streptococcus bacterium
Streptococci were described for the ﬁrst time in 1683 by Leeuwenhoek, when drawing its
microscope image after having examined material removed from between his teeth [65].
In 1879 the Streptococcus made its main entry into history, when Louis Pasteur studied
the reason for puerperal fever in diseased women. His work lead to the introduction of hy-
giene and aseptic practices in hospital wards after proving that the infections were caused
by transfer of streptococci from the staﬀto the women when doing assistance of child
birth[65].
Streptococci are gram-positive bacteria, belonging to the phylum Firmicutes and Lacto-
bacillales order. The word Streptococcus comes from the Greek strepto (twisted) and
coccus (spherical), drawn by the shape of the Streptococcus bacterium, Figure 1,[65].
Streptococci divides along its axis, forming chains or pairs of streptococci cells.
The
Streptococcus genome is between 1.8 Mb and 2.4Mb in length, has a GC content of ap-
proximately 40%, and encodes about 2100 genes [17]. Today, more than 100 streptococcal

3.2
Bioﬁlm formation in the oral cavity
Streptococci are found in natural environments often growing upon surfaces, and many
species of streptococci that colonize mammals exist naturally within communities of bac-
teria growing bioﬁlm [65]. Mitis group streptococci have their origins from the human oral
cavity or nasopharnyx, where they grow on the surface of the tissue[28]. The environment
of the oral cavity is challenging for the long-term persistence of bacteria. The constant
changes in nutrient supply, temperature, pH, and the ﬂow of saliva have selected for a
specialized bioﬁlm community[19, 47, 65]. The diﬀerent anatomical sites in the oral cavity
have their own unique microenvironments, pushing the evolution of the diverse micro-
biome [47].
Colonization of the oral cavity begins within a few hours after birth with bacteria trans-
ferred from the mother, nurses, or environment [55, 81]. The oral streptococci are known
to be involved in the initial colonization of the oral cavity and make up over 80% of the
early bioﬁlm [47, 61]. Studies have shown that S. sanguinis start colonizing the oral cavity
by attaching the surface of the teeth, around the age of nine months, when the teeth starts
to emerge [12].
The bioﬁlm formation is a complex process, where attachment to the surface is the ﬁrst
step. In the oral cavity, a saliva ﬁlm containing albumin, glycoproteins, acidic proline-rich
proteins, mucins, sialic acids, and other compounds covers the tooth surface [39, 80]. This
saliva ﬁlm is also called acquired pellicle and provides attachments sites for the oral strep-
tococci [39, 65, 80].
In general streptococci can attach the surface of the oral cavity in three ways [65]:
- Direct binding to the host; Mitis group streptococci have shown to adhere to glycopro-
teins and glycolipids in the host, Figure 3b.
- Long range adherence using pili, Figure 3a.
- Fibrins to penetrate mucus, Figure 3a, or to adhere to surface proteins, Figure 3c.
Fibrils and pili, known as ﬁmbriae, are in particularly important for the growth and sur-
vival in the oral cavity [65, 74].
Surface-attached bacteria are believed to respond to their new physical state, thus their
metabolic status and gene expression changes as a result of the altered surface properties
[19]. When the ﬁrst streptococci have attached to the surface, development of a complex
community of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria can take place [65]. The later
bioﬁlm formation involves coaggregation and coadherence of oral bacteria, and if undis-
turbed develops into a stratiﬁed, complex bioﬁlm, Figure 3. If the teeth is cleaned the

3.2.1
Cell to cell interaction
A mixed community of diﬀerent species may develop within 24h [65]. Many environmental
conditions, such as pH, temperature, oxygen availability, and nutrition, control the devel-
opment of the community, by inﬂuencing the cell-to-cell communication for population
control [65]. Studies have shown that certain species tend to colonize in symbiosis with
each other (e.g. by nutritional cross-feeding) while others competes with one another (e.g.
by production of bacteriocins) [47, 65]. The bacteria interaction can have great inﬂuence
for the bioﬁlm composition, and can have an impact on the well-being of the host. Studies
have identiﬁed a correlation between the compositions of species and dental health of the
host; individuals with a healthy oral cavity have shown to have a signiﬁcant higher con-
centration of S. sanguinis and S. gordonii, while individuals with caries have a signiﬁcant
higher concentration of S. mutans and almost no detectable levels of S. sanguinis [6].
An example of competition between strains in the oral cavity is the direct competition
between the species S. mutans (which is responsible for tooth decay) and S. gordonii or
S. sanguinis. S. mutans are able to secrete bacteriocins that inhibits the growth of S.

then promotes the growth of V. atypica [26, 65].
3.2.2
Horizontal gene transfer
The dynamic environment in the oral cavity is stressful for the bacteria, where the diver-
sity, strain enrichment, and cell density is found to be high. It is believed that exchange
and acquirement of genes, also called horizontal gene transfer (HGT), is a crucial factor
for adaptation in this speciﬁc nice. HGT has been demonstrated to occur between a wide
range of bacteria, which inhabit in the oral cavity of humans [45, 79].
In general, genes can be transferred from one bacteria to another in three ways: 1) conju-
gation; direct cell-to-cell contact or by forming a bridge-like connection between the two
cells. 2) transformation, direct uptake and incorporation of extracellular DNA (eDNA).
And 3) transduction, injection of foreign DNA by a bacteriophage [40, 85]. Even though
observations of transduction in the oral cavity have never been reported, bacteriophages
are found in high abundance in the oral cavity, making the event likely to happen[79]. A
recent study by A. Roberts and J. Kreth, 2014, has furthermore observed membrane vesi-
cles carrying virulence genes, and plasmids with antibiotic resistance genes, being released
from the cell surface of a donor bacteria and transformed into an acceptor bacteria. The
release and uptake of DNA can contribute to diversity in a community, which could favour
establishment of new phenotypic traits given the right selective pressure. It is believed
that diversity in a community is most likely achieved by incorporating heterologous DNA
in closely related strains [79].
Streptococcus pneumoniae, the fourth most frequent cause of fatal infections worldwide
[11] and a close relative to the Mitis group Streptococcus strains, Figure 2, is know to be
highly geneticly transformable and recombining [2, 15]. Several studies have shown, that
genes are frequently exchanged between strains belonging to the Mitis group streptococci
and S. pneumoniae [2, 20, 45]. A study by Killian et al., 2014, suggests that S. mitis
is an evolutionary linage of the pathogenic S. pneumoniae, explaining the great genomic
similarities between the commensal Mitis group streptococci and the pathogenic S. pneu-
moniae[45].
Competition between strains can furthermore induce gene exchange due the stress on the
bacteria, and in some cases to lysis of the cell. To acquire extracellular DNA, especially
lysis of other bacteria can come in handy. Expression of speciﬁc encoded genes in Streptoc-
cocus strains have been observed to be involved in lysis of other bacterial cells [45]. The
release of DNA into the environment has furthermore been closely associated with the

to host epithelia cells in lungs, cervix, nasopharynx, tonsils, and intestine and have been
recognized as a virulence factor [65, 74]. The ability to bind to choline and collagen is asso-
ciated with colonization in the oral cavity as well. These binding proteins are recognized as
virulence factors in Mitis group streptococci and Staphylococcus aureus, as they contribute
to platelet adhesion and can facilitate binding to damaged heart valves [43, 53, 65, 67].
Adaptation to the environment
To successfully colonize and persist within the host, bacteria must adapt their gene expres-
sion proﬁles in response to environmental changes [59, 65]. Streptococci utilize two main
types of transcriptional regulators: TCSS (two component signal transduction systems)
and Stand-alone regulators. Several TCSS are well characterized and some of them have
been observed in strains belonging to Mitis group Streptococcus, and have shown to be
involved in bioﬁlm formation. Theses regulators are however believed to be contribute
the the pathogencity of the strains as well[59, 65]. Another way for bacteria to adapt and
persist is the acquirement of new genes as explained in section 3.2.2. Acquirement of new
genes is not a virulence factor, however the new genes could potentially be associated with

3.3.2
Host modulation
Due to the harsh environment of the oral cavity, the Mitis group Streptococcus species have
developed the ability to interact and modulate the cells of the immune system of the host,
as well as with other bacterial species. Receptor recognition by bacterial surface proteins
is an essential step in mediating attachment to host tissues. These events can also trigger
host cell signalling cascades to modulate host cell behaviour [65]. The outcome of this can
result in internalization of streptococci by host cells, induction of cytokine release, and
induction of apotosis [65]. Some Mitis group streptococci are able to cleave IgA receptors
of the immune system in the host, which enables the bacteria to escape detection and
hinder an immune response provided by the immunoglobulins [59, 65, 71]. Bacteria which
express the M protein on their cell surface are furthermore able to avoid immune response
in the host [58, 65]. The protein is known to facilitate plasminogen binding which can lead
to plasmin activation. Plasmin activation can result in a decreased coagulatory response
at the site of infection which makes it easier for the bacteria to spread [58]. The M protein
in Mitis group Streptococcus strains is therefore highly associated with virulence [58, 65].
3.3.3
Platelet binding
When bacteria escapes from its niche and enters the bloodstream an interaction between
platelets and the bacteria can occur.
If such interaction happens, three scenarios can
occur. Firstly, an increase of inﬂammatory cytokines is mediated due to the presents of the
bacteria, which can lead to platelet activation. Secondly, the bacteria may secrete products
that activate platelets, and thirdly bacteria may bind to the platelets [43]. The bacteria
can bind directly to surface receptors on the platelet or bind indirectly by using proteins
(usually plasma proteins), which can bind both to the bacteria and the platelets. When
bacteria binds to platelets, two types of response can take place; platelet lysis or platelet
activation.
Activated platelets secrete anti-microbial peptides that act to kill bacteria
[43]. However, Mitis group streptococci have shown resistance to such peptides, and the
activated platelets can under certain circumstances trigger the pathological processes [43].
Mitis group streptococci are known to adhere to platelets which is thought to be crucial
for the colonization of heart valves, which can lead to infective endocarditis (IE) [43, 65].
Furthermore, some Mitis group streptococci are able to induce activation of platelets,
which can lead to the formation of blood clots in the blood vessels, due to an increased
platelets and clotting factor consumption [65].

MGmapper
MGmapper is a pipeline that maps the raw reads to diﬀerent databases chosen by the
user [70]. In this study MGmapper was used to check for primers and phages that are
used doing the sequencing, and have not been removed from the raw reads. To do so the
PhiX library containing the bacteriophage PhiX174 genome, and the Cutadapt database
containing four adapter sequences was used, [38, 56]. Therefore, the program was used to
check if the majority of the raw reads were bacterial, or if the DNA has been contaminated
in the laboratory. The raw reads were therefore mapped against the following databases:
Bacteria, Human Microbiome, Bacteria Draft, Meta Hit Assembly, Plasmid, Virus, Fungi,
and Human. The program was run in chain mode, meaning that the reads were mapped
to the databases in the mentioned order. If a read gave a signiﬁcant hit in one of the
databases it was removed from the data pool, and would not be mapped against the next
database. This means that each read only could give a hit in one database. In some cases
one read was mapped to several sequences in the same database, all with the mapping
same score. In these cases it is random which hit was chosen. Therefore, it is more im-
portant that the reads gave bacterial hits in general, rather than hits to the Streptococcus
genus.
4.3
Assembly methods
Today, several diﬀerent assembly programs are available, using diﬀerent assembly strate-
gies, and the choice of program often depends on the characteristics of the data being
assembled [60]. In this study single genome reads were used, it is sequenced by Illumina
technology, and the reads are paired-end reads. An assembler should be able to consider
all these factors when processing the reads. Next, it is important to consider in which
way the program organizes the reads in order to assemble them, and which settings to
use. Especially the choice of k-mer value can have great eﬀects on the construction of
the de Bruijn graph and the resulting assembly: small values of k will result in a more
tangled graph due to more repeats will be collapsed together. Large values of k may in
low coverage regions can fail to detect overlaps of reads resulting in a more fragmented
graph [4]. The choice of k-mer is therefore a balance between sensitivity and speciﬁcity
when assembling reads. Please note that the value of k has to be odd, to ensure that each
k-mer can not be its own reverse complement.
This study has used two diﬀerent assembly programs, which both have generated de novo
assemblies from the raw data using the de Bruijn graph. However, very diﬀerent mathe-
matical models are used to construct this graph to organize and assemble the reads. The
two programs, Velvet and SPAdes, will be explained in further detail in the following two
sections.
4.3.1
Velvet
Velvet is an assembly algorithm using the de Bruijn graph for genomic sequence assembly.
The program is developed to handle short reads and paired-end reads for both probiotic
and mammalian DNA, sequenced by, among others, Illuminia technology. In this study

Velvet was executed as a pipeline, applying diﬀerent values of k to the program. One ﬁnal
assembly with the most optimal k-value, was identiﬁed based on ranking of contig N50
and the number of contigs in the assemblies.
The program has been developed to manipulate the de Bruijn graph, to eliminate errors
in the assembly and to resolve repeats. First, Velvet hashes the reads according to the
predeﬁned k-mer length. A hash table is formed containing each k-mer observed in the
dataset, recording the ID of the ﬁrst read containing that k-mer and position of the k-
mer, Figure 5a2. Furthermore, reverse complements of each k-mer is recorded as well.
Using the hash tables of a roadmap is generated, where original k-mers are combined with
overlaps with previously hashed reads. In a second database nodes are recorded for each
continuously, uninterrupted sequence of overlapping k-mers. Finally, the reads are traced
through the graph using the roadmaps, and the nodes are connected in a graph.
To decrease the number of errors the de Bruijn graph is simpliﬁed by merging nodes that
points to each other. When the graph is simpliﬁed, assembly errors are further decreased
by removing tips, bubbles, and erroneous connections in a majority consensus mode, Figure
5a3-4. The simpliﬁcation decrease redundancy in the assembly and results in long straight
nodes and short nodes that can not be simpliﬁed due to low-complexity sequences in the
dataset, ﬁgure 5b. Velvet work with a ﬁxed coverage cut-oﬀthreshold in the de Bruijn
graph to avoid low-depth and likely erroneous regions in the assembly. This operation
reduces the complexity of the de Bruijn graph. To learn more about Velvet please see
Zerbino et al., 2008, [97].

4.3.2
SPAdes
SPAdes (St.
Petersburg genome assembler) is designed to assemble small bacterial or
fungal sequences, either as single-cell genomes or standard multi-celled bacterial genomes.
It is a universal A-Bruijn assembler, as it uses k-mers for building the initial de Bruijn
graph, as described in section 4.3.1, and then use them no further.
However, instead
of constructing the de Bruijn graph using one predeﬁned k-mer as the Velvet assembler,
SPAdes constructs a mulitsized de Bruijn graph using varied set of k-values. The multi-
sized de Bruijn graph enables the assembly of low coverage regions with a smaller value
of k, while high coverage regions can be assembled with high values of k.
SPAdes performs graph-theoretical operations on the graph, which does not need labelled
k-mers. The operations are based on graph topology, coverage and sequence length, but
does not uses the sequences themselves. Furthermore, SPAdes deals with issues as sequenc-
ing errors, non-uniform coverage, insert size variation, and chimeric reads and bireads.
SPAdes comes in several separate modules, of which can be selected by the user. In short,
the construction of the multisized de Bruijn graph is a complex process, thus SPAdes can
be divided into four (simpliﬁed) stages:
1 Assembly graph constructions: The construction and simpliﬁcation of the de Bruijn
graph. However, the simpliﬁcation of error removal diﬀers from the one described in
section 4.3.1.
Instead, a new algorithm is developed to detect and remove chimeric
reads, aggregates bi-read information into distance histograms, and then backtrack the
performed graph operations.
SPAdes also includes a module with a modiﬁcation of
BayesHammer[63] for error correction and quality trimming.
2 k-bimer adjustment: the accurate distance estimates between k-mers in the genome (the
edges in the assembly graph) are derived using joint analysis of the distance histograms
and paths in the assembly. The inexact distances between pairs of k-mers which were
estimated in stage 1, are then substituted with the adjusted exact (or nearly exact)
distances.
3 Construction of the paired de Bruijn graph: mulisized de Bruijn graph is constructed
using the accurate distances between pairs of k-mers.
4 Contig construction: the DNA sequences are constructed as contigs, and the reads are
mapped to the contigs by backtracking graph simpliﬁcations.
To learn more about SPAdes, please see Bankevich et al., 2012 and Mendvedev et al.,
2011, [4, 57].

CD-HIT-EST
CD-HIT-EST clusters similar protein sequences into clusters. The user can deﬁne the
similarity thresholds for how the sequences will be clustered. The clusters are created by
all - against - all comparisons using an algorithm, which ﬁlters the sequences by length.
The longest sequence becomes the representative of the ﬁrst cluster, and the remaining

the similarities of the sequences by word counting; if the similarity is greater than then
required value, an alignment is performed to conﬁrm their sequence identity and the
respective sequence is added to the cluster. Otherwise a new representative of a cluster
is found. This method is a fast way of clustering sequences since unnecessary pairwise
alignments are avoided. An illustration of how a cluster could look like using CD-HIT-

UCLUST
UCLUST is an algorithm that divides a set of sequences into clusters. In this program a
cluster is deﬁned by one representative sequence, also called the centroid. To cluster the
sequences, a similarity between the centroid and a sequence has to be above a given iden-
tity threshold, which can be viewed as the radius of the cluster. To compare the sequences
with the centroids, UCLUST uses the USEARCH algorithm. The input sequences are
sorted by length, and longest sequence is identiﬁed as the ﬁrst centroid. The remaining
sequence is then split up in short words or k-mers. UCLUST uses the count of matching
k-mers to the centroid to prioritize the database search in the indexed table of k-mers. If
a sequence in the database is higher than the identity threshold, it is considered as a hit.
Unlike other programs that use k-mer counting, UCLUST does not attempt to estimate
sequence identity from the number of matching k-mers. This is because identity correlates
only approximately with the word count and does not give an accurate estimate, especially

4.4.3
OrthoMCL
OrthoMCL is a clustering program that clusters genes based on orthology. Homolog genes
sequences are genes with very similar sequences; two major types of homologs are orthologs
and paralogs. Orthologs are genes evolved from a common ancestor by speciﬁcation, thus
are believed to have retained identical function over evolutionary time. Paralogs one the
other hand arose from ancient duplication events and are believed to have diverged in
function over evolutionary time. However, recent paralogs are considered to retain the
same function and will be grouped with the orthologs.
In Figure 7, the work ﬂow of the OrthoMCL pipeline is found. In OrthoMCL pairs of
orthologs are identiﬁed by reciprocal BLAST; an all versus all BLAST, where gene Se-
quence1 ﬁnds gene Sequence2 as the best hits and vice versa. If the best reciprocal hit
of Sequence1 and Sequence2 is found within the same genome it is considered as recent
paralog. A p-value cut-oﬀif 10≠5 is used for separating orthologs and paralogs from each
other. The detected relationships between the orthologs and paralogs are converted into
a similarity graph. Each node in the graph represents a the protein sequence, while the
weighted edges represents the relationship between the sequences(nodes). The weights in
the similarity graph are normalized for each genome to reﬂect the average weight for all or-
thologs between genomes and recent paralogs within the genomes. The resulting graph is
represented by a similarity matrix, which will be the subject for Markov clustering (MCL)
[27]. The MCL algorithm separates the diverged paralogs, distant orthologs mistakenly
assigned with week reciprocal best hits, and sequences with diﬀerent domain structures.
The identiﬁed orthologs and recent paralogs are then combined into gene clusters. The
tightness of the gene clusters is determined by the inﬂation parameter in the MCL algo-
rithm. In general, a lower inﬂation value result in the inclusion of more sequences in fewer

sequences from at least two diﬀerent species.

PanFunPro
Sequence similarities and presence of a shared domain within a group of proteins does
not necessarily imply that these proteins perform the same biochemical function[27] .
However, similarities in the domain architecture can be a more correct way to classify
gene families. A diﬀerent gene clustering method was therefore applied to this thesis:
clustering based on functional domain architecture. To do this, the program: PAN-genome
analysis based on FUNctional PROﬁles (PanFunPro) was used. PanFunPro is a program
that provides homology detection and genome annotation by three Hidden Markov Model
(HMM) collections[22].
Furthermore, the pan - and core genome within the provided
dataset was estimated. The program can be divided into four steps:
1) Genome selection and gene prediction: The program imports the genomes provided by
the user. Each genome is represented as a fasta ﬁle of amino acid sequences. The pro-
gram then predict gene sequences in the selected genomes using Prodigal Software[37].
21
==PAGE:23==

2) Identiﬁcation of functional domains: all predicted proteins are scanned against three
collections of HMMs: PfamA[30], TIGRFAM[32], and SUPERFAMILY[94] using In-
terProScan software[96].
3) Construction of functional proﬁles: only HMM hits with an E-value<0,001 are consid-
ered as signiﬁcant, and used for the functional architecture of the genes. The functional
proﬁles of each protein sequence, are determined by non-overlapping functional domains
(HMMs). Only one database is considered at the time, for each sequence. The HMM
collections are searched in the following order: PfamA, TIGRFAM, and Superfam-
ily. Proteins which share the same functional proﬁle are considered as a gene family.
Sequences with no signiﬁcant matches to any of the searched HMM-databases, are col-
lected from each of the analysed genomes and clustered with CD-HIT. The clustering
uses a 5 amino acid window search (-n 5) and a identity threshold of 60 % (-c 0.6)
(see section 4.4.1 to learn more about CD-HIT). The HMM based and clustering-based
protein families for each genome are joined together to form a whole genome proﬁle
collection.
4) Analysis: based on the identiﬁed gene families several analysis can be conducted. In
this project the pan - and core genome was determined. This was done by combining
all the functional proﬁles from all the analysed genome. The pan genome in PanFunPro
is deﬁned as the complete collection of all unique functional proﬁles found in a set of
genomes. The core genome are only the unique functional proﬁles present in all the
analysed genomes.
By determine the functional proﬁles of each gene, and combining the genes which share
the same proﬁles into gene families, the number of unique gene families per genome are
decreased compared to the initial number of protein sequences. The deﬁned pan genome
was used to generated a gene matrix which listed all the unique functional proﬁles and
in which Streptococcus genome it was observed. The identiﬁed core genome was used to
build a consensus tree. This tree would show the phylogeny based on the core functions
of the Streptococcus genomes. To learn more about PanFunPro please see:[54]

5.1
Preparation of the strains
All the isolated Streptococcus strains were grown in BA at 35oC ON in 5% CO2 and the
DNA extraction was conducted by boiling at 98oC in 10 minutes.
To prepare for sequencing, the strains were grown over night in BA at 35oC ON with 5%
CO2. One loop full colony material was inoculated into 40 ml Todd-Hewitt (SSI) in blue
cap bottles and incubated at 35oC with 15 % CO2 under shaking for approximately 15
hours. After incubation the cells were harvested from 7 ml of cultures by centrifugation
(5000 rpm) in 10 minutes. The strains had no more than three passages, however strains
prepared at Rigshospitalet had four passages. The DNA extraction was performed with
MasterPureTM Gram Positive DNA Puriﬁcation Kit (Epicentre) with some modiﬁcations.
The incubation steps were prolonged for a better result and beside this, Mutanolysin from
Streptomyces globisporus ATCC 21553(Sigma-Aldrich) were added in the ﬁrst lysis step
as suggested by Epicentre. The mutanolysin was diluted in potassium phosphate buﬀer
pH 6.2 (SSI) and 50µl of 5000U/ml mutanolysin was added pr. extraction.
The DNA was stored at -20oC and shipped on dry ice to BGI-TECH Hong Kong for
quality check, library construction and sequencing. The strains were sequenced with high-
throughput Illumina sequencing by Illumina Hiseq 2000 to conduct paired-end sequencing.
The sequencing depth was aimed to be 100X.
The species identiﬁcation based on ITS sequence and gdh analysis was performed as de-
scribed by Nielsen et al. [62]. The sequences of the ITS region and gdh gene were compared
to sequences deposited in the NCBI database[17] by using the BLAST search engine and
by taking into consideration the percentage and number of identities, the maximum score,
and E values for the best and the second best taxon matches.

5.5
Assembly
After the quality check of the raw reads, each Streptococcus genome was assembled, ﬁrst
by using Velvet and then using SPAdes; two assemblies of each genome were therefore
generated. The velvet assembler was used as a pipeline tool that assembled each genome
multiple times, each time with a new value of k. The most optimal assembly with a speciﬁc
k value, was chosen based on genome size and N50. The tested values of k in the Velvet
assembler were: 65, 57, 59, 71, 73, 75, 77, 79, 81, 83, 85, 87, 89, 91, 93, and 95.
SPAdes on the other hand assembled the genomes using multiple values of k at once,
resulting in only one assembly. The option –careful were used to decrease the number of
assembly errors. The choice of values of k were: 25, 35, 45, 55, 65, 75, 85, and 95.
Both assembly programs are explained in further detail in section 4.
5.5.1
Quality check of the assemblies
To compare the most optimal k value automatic selected by Velvet with the theoreti-
cally best k value for each assembly, a program called KmerGenie was used [13]. This
program tests which k-mer would be optimal for the speciﬁc set of fastqc ﬁles. The pro-
gram generates abundance histograms and estimate how many distinct k-mers are genomic
(error-free). Finally, it identiﬁes the k-value that maximizes the number of genomic k-
mers.
To be able to compare the assemblies generated by Velvet and SPAdes, statistics of each
assembly were generated using Assemblathon [21]. Furthermore, REAPR was run on each
assembly to determined how many assembly errors it contained [36]. REAPR identiﬁes
errors in assemblies by mapping the raw reads back to the assembled genome. In short,
an assembly error is identiﬁed either by lack of - or irregular - fragment coverage. REAPR
produce, among others, a summery of identiﬁed errors and warnings in the assembly, and
a new ”broken” assembly, from where the errors are removed.
The quality analysis of the assemblies showed that some of the SPAdes assemblies had
many scaﬀolds of a low assembly depth. Therefore all scaﬀolds having a mean assembly
depth lower than 2x were removed from the SPAdes assemblies. Comparisons on the six
diﬀerent assembly versions: SPAdes, SPAdes broken, SPAdes trimmed, SPAdes trimmed
broken, Velvet, and Velvet broken, were conducted using the N50 and number of errors.
From these comparisons the trimmed SPAdes assemblies were found to be the best rep-
resentatives of the Streptococcus genomes, and will be used for further analysis in this
study.
5.6
Phylogeny
Due to the diﬃculty in diﬀerentiating Streptococcus strains on species level, phylogenetic
trees were generated to determine if the strains were correctly annotated. Three phyloge-
netic trees were generated; two trees based on identiﬁcation of single nucleotide polymor-
phisms (SNPs) in the fastqc ﬁles and assemblies, respectively, and one based on variations
in the 16S rRNA regions in the assemblies. The SNP trees were generated on the web
based CSIPhylogeny server [41]. Here the input sequences was mapped to the selected

==PAGE:28==

reference, Streptococcus mitis B6, using BWA and SAMTools [41]. All genome mappings
were compared and all positions containing SNPs were validated. Based on the SNP call-
ing a Maximum Likelihood trees were created using FastTree [41, 75].
To generate the phylogeny based on predicted 16SrRNA regions in the strains, several
tools were used: ﬁrstly the 16S rRNA regions were predicted by a program called species-
ﬁnder [49]. Speciesﬁnder generates a fasta ﬁle containing one or more predicted 16S rRNA
sequences. If more than one 16S rRNA sequence were predicted for each strain, the shorter
region was removed from the fasta ﬁle. The fasta ﬁles containing one 16S rRNA sequence
for each strain, were then merged together and aligned against each other by a multiple
aligner called RDPAlligner [16]. The multiple alignment was converted to phylip format,
and the program jModelTest [18, 72] was used to predict the most optimal model for a
phylogeny tree, by testing 24 diﬀerent nucleotide substitution models. Four diﬀerent ﬁt-
ting methods were applied to each model: Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), Bayesian
Information Criterion (BIC), corrected Akaike Information Criterion (AICs), and Deci-
sion Theory criterion (DT). The substitution model that describe the variance best in the
sequences was found, and a tree was generated with each of the ﬁtting methods by the
program PhyML which was implemented in jModelTest. The PhyML algorithm is based
on the maximum-likelihood principle. The best phylogeny model was found based on the
summary given by jModelTest. The distance matrix given by PhyML, was copied into
a text ﬁle and opened in Figtree[76]. Based on the phylogeny results, four strains were
re-annotated according to the species clusters they were observed in.
5.7
Analysis of gene content
So far all analysis have been performed on DNA level of the genomes. To get information
on protein level, genes were predicted in the strains using Prodigal [37]. The genes were
predicted both as nucleotides and amino acid sequences, and only genes with closed ends
were considered. In some cases the predicted genes had to be analysed at DNA level, and
then translated to protein. Translation from nucleotide to amino acids were carried out
by the 6pack program [78], in reading frame 1.
To evaluate the similarities between the Streptococcus genomes, the codon usage was de-
termined in the predicted genes. The codon usage was derived from the predicted genes
using the package CMGbiotools v.2 [92] in Virtual Box. A description of the the CMG-
biotools package and how to determine and visualize the amino acid usage can be found
in section 1 and 11 in the exercise document: [91] and is explained in detail in the study
by Vesth et al., 2013 [92].

One of the aim of this study was, to identify potential pathogen speciﬁc genes, as well as
species speciﬁc genes. To enable this, several analyses have been performed, which can
be divided in to two parts: analysis based on sequence identities, and analysis based on
protein function.
The focus point was to:

- Identify a pan - and core genome of the Mitis group Streptococcus genomes.

5.8.1
Comparative genomics based on sequence identity
The aim in this part, was to divide the genes of the Mitis group Streptococcus strains into
gene clusters, to decrease the total number of genes. These gene clusters would be used
to create a matrix of the genes represented in the 88 genomes and eight references. This
would give an overview of the gene content of each Streptococcus strain. Furthermore, the
overall function of the gene clusters would be found by BLAST the clusters against the
eggNOG database, and then using the eggNOG annotations [73]. The eggNOG database
contains non-supervised ortholog groups constructed from 1,133 organisms. The groups
are annotated with a function description based on the functional annotations of each
protein member within the group. The functional annotations obtained in this study were
based on the 25 functional categories provided by eggNOG and are found comparable with
those provided by the COG and KOG databases [73].
To cluster the genes, the fasta ﬁles containing nucleotide gene sequences were used. The
gene clustering caused great diﬃculties - therefore, several methods have been used. Each
of the listed programs are found in more detail in section 4.
- UCLUST
- CD-HIT-EST
- OrthoMCL clustering
The approach using UCLUST and CD-HIT-EST were very similar, and will be described
together, while the clustering using OrthoMCL will be described separately:
UCLUST and CD-HIT-EST
The approach for using UCLUST and CDH-HIT-EST were the same; all predicted genes
were merged together in one fasta ﬁle and gene sequences with a high similarity were clus-
tered. The programs were run three times with diﬀerent settings; an identity threshold
at 90%, 95%, and 98%. The CD-HIT-EST program were additionally set to: maximum
alignment coverage for the longer sequence (-aL) = 0.90, minimal length similarity (-s) =
0.90, word length (-n) = 8. The resulting ﬁles were a fasta ﬁles containing all the gene
clusters, each with one representative DNA gene sequences.
To see which gene cluster was represented in which strain, and how many times it was
represented, the raw reads were mapped to the fasta ﬁles containing the gene cluster rep-
resentatives using BWA mem [50].
OrthoMCL

A diﬀerent method of clustering the genes of the Mitis group Streptococcus strains was to
identify gene orthology by the pipeline of OrthoMCL. This method includes many diﬀerent
parts, including population of a SQL database. The input to the program was the fasta
ﬁles containing the predicted genes of the Mitis group streptococci strains. The reference
S. sanguinis SK36 was excluded from the datapool due to complications when populating
the database.
Before the genes were applied to the database each genome was assigned with a unique
three digits code, and each gene in the respective genome was assigned with a unique
gene number, e.g.: aaa—gene1. A translation table was created to combine the renamed
genomes and genes with the original names. The renamed genes were ﬁltered to remove
low quality protein sequences from the fasta ﬁles, and to combine the individual compliant
proteome fasta ﬁles into a single ”goodProteins.fasta” ﬁle. A BLAST database was created
containing all the gene sequences, and was used for reciprocal BLAST. The BLAST output
was parsed to get the right input format to the OthoMCL database, and the BLAST hits
were applied to the OthoMCL database. The OrthoMCL groups were then used in the
database and the ortholog and recent paralogs were predicted. Diﬀerent inﬂation values
(-I): 1.2, 1.3, 1.4, 1.5, 2, 3, and 4 was tested to ﬁnd the best possible clustering of the
genes. The -I value of 1.2 was found to be most optimal for this dataset. Then the unique
gene codes present in each identiﬁed ortholog group was extracted from the database and
put in the ”groups 1.2.txt ﬁle”.
To be able to make statistics on the grouped orthologs, the information ”groups 1.2.txt”
ﬁle was used. From this text ﬁle statistics were made by extracting the number of genes
in each group, and determine which speciﬁc genes were located in each ortholog group.
This information could be used to extract genes of interest from the assemblies. A gene
matrix was generated, with all the identiﬁed clusters and which genome was observed in
each gene cluster.
All ortholog sequences were extracted from the assemblies and located in folders with the
unique ortholog group name. Furthermore, sequences not located in ortholog groups, sin-
gletons, were extracted from the assemblies as well.
Gene annotation
To annotate the ortholog gene groups, the genes from each orthologe cluster was extracted
from the assemblies as described above, and blasted against the eggNOG database v.3 and
annotated by eggNOG categories [73]. Using the annotated genes, a matrix was gener-
ated, containing the singletons ortholog groups with the annotations observed in the genes
belonging to the gene cluster.
5.8.2
Comparatative genomics based on domain architecture
To make a comparative analysis based on protein domain architecture in the genomes,
rather than gene sequence identity, the program PanFunPro was used. An description of
the program can be found in section 4. The program was used to:
- Identify domain architecture of the genes and group them according to their function
- Identify the pan- and core genome based on functional proﬁles.

- Create a pan-genome matrix based on the identiﬁed protein families
- Generate a phylogenetic consensus tree based on the core genes
The input to program was the fasta ﬁles with predicted proteins generated in section 5.7.
The program proﬁled each genome by identifying non-overlapping functional domains in
the gene sequences, and scan them through the three databases: PfamA, TIGRFAM, and
Superfamily, [30, 32, 94]. The functional proﬁles where then used for genomic comparison
of the Streptococcus genomes, by a pan-core analysis.
Based on the pan-core analysis the abundance of each functional proﬁle was determined,
and from that a gene family matrix was generated. The matrix contained a list of all
unique functional gene families and in which genome they were observed. The pan-core
analysis were furthermore used to determine the core genome of the Streptococcus genomes.
The gene sequences of all the observed core gene families were extracted from each genome
to generate a consensus phylogenetic tree. The consensus tree was generated by aligning
the extracted sequences using muscle v3.8.31 [24], resulting in 767 multiple alignments of
96 genes sequences. The format of the multiple alignments were converted to NEXUS and
a phylogenetic tree was build from each multiple alignment using PAUP (Phylogenetic
Analysis Using Parsimony v4.0b10 [87]). The 767 PAUP trees were merged into one single
ﬁle, and the consensus tree was generated using PHYLIP (PHYLogeny Inference Package
v3.695 [29]). The ﬁnal consensus tree was visualized in Figtree [76].

Identiﬁcation of speciﬁc genes
Sine no clear core genome was found using the OthoMCL grouping, and very few functional
categories were assigned to the ortholog groups, only the results generated in PanFunPro

families of interest could be extracted from the dataset.
The gene families of interest
would be those only found in the pathogenic strains (potential pathogen speciﬁc genes).
Gene families only represented in the respective species were furthermore extracted from
the pan genome (potential specie speciﬁc gene families). The identiﬁed core genome was
used to extract some genes families related to immune modulation, toxin production, and
antibiotic resistance.

7
Discussion
The most important results obtained in this thesis will be explained in further detail in
this section. To be able to to achieve reliable results, it is of high importance that the data
which make up the foundation of the project is of high quality. Much eﬀort was therefore
invested to ensure the quality of the raw reads and to generate as high quality assemblies
as possible.
7.1
Quality check of the reads
To achieve reliable results obtained in this study, it is of high importance that the data
which make up the foundation of the project is of high quality. Only few fastqc ﬁles failed
when they were processed by FastQC, Figure 23. The module Sequence duplication levels
passed non of the processed reads, and gave the most fail messages of the three modules.
Very few fails was observed in Per base sequence quality, and they were all located either
in the beginning or end of the respective read. The same fail pattern was observed in
the Per base sequence content module. The failed modules in FastQC could suggest that
trimming of the reads were necessary, however no adapter or over expressed sequences
were recognised.
Furthermore, a high sequence duplication level is often triggered by
contamination of the DNA or by a very high sequencing depth. When including the result
from MGmapper, it was seen that less than 1% of the mapped reads were detected as
non-streptococcal DNA. Furthermore, the sequencing was aimed for a 100x depth, which
is very high. The warnings and fails messages in this module were therefore believed to
be due to high sequencing depth, rather than contamination of the reads. Approximately
10% of the reads were not mapped to any of the databases in MGmapper, this implies
that a part of the Mitis group Streptococcus genomes is unknown, Figure 10. However, it
could also be a result of lack of bacterial sequences in the databases. Very few adapters
were detected in the MGmapping, but due to the high numbers of Streptococcal genus
hits, and few failed modules in FastQC, removal of adapters was not found necessary. In
fact removal of DNA from both ends of the reads could decrease the overall quality of the
data, since there is a ricks of removing relevant bacterial DNA rather than adapters or
primers.
7.2
Assembly methods
The 88 Streptococcus genomes were assembled using two diﬀerent assemblers. The statistic
based on number of errors, N50, and number of scaﬀolds showed that the SPAdes assem-
blies had fewer errors than the Velvet assemblies, but a higher number of scaﬀolds, Figure
11 and Figure 12. One of the few downsides of the SPAdes assembly algorithm is that the
scaﬀolds and contigs with a low assembly depth are not removed from the de Bruijn graph,
as in they are in the Velvet algorithm [4]. In some cases, this step is not necessary to get
high quality assemblies, however, some of the assemblies in this project contained many
low depth scaﬀolds. Removal of scaﬀolds which had an average assembly depth below 2x,
solved this issue. The trimmed SPAdes assemblies, showed to have a higher N50 and fewer
assembly errors than the Velvet assemblies. This shows that by using a dynamic assembly
tool, that includes diﬀerent values of k, rather than a set k-vaule, enables more correct

and high quality assembled genomes.
The genomes sizes of the trimmed SPAdes assemblies showed to range between 1.8Mb and
2.5Mb, which is consistence with the genome sizes of known Mitis group streptococci in
the NCBI database [17].
7.3
Phylogeny
The phylogenetic trees based on 16S rRNA showed high similarities between Mitis group
Streptococcus strains. Especially the S. oralis, S. Mitis, and S. infanits species were found
extremely similar and a clear separation of the species was not possible, Figure 13. The
species of S. gordonii and S. sanguinis were observed to be more distinctive from the rest
of the species and were found in separate species speciﬁc clusters. The high similarities
based on 16S rRNA sequences are in consistence with previous phylogenetic analysis based
on 16S rRNA [65]. This result is furthermore supported by the study by Killian et al.,
2008, who suggests that 16S rRNA sequences might not be suﬃcient to separate Mitis
group Streptococcus species from each other [44]. When including the phylogenetic trees
based on fastqc ﬁles and the assemblies for analysis, four of the Streptococcus species were
observed to be located in diﬀerent species speciﬁc clusters than expected. This implied
that the four strains were assigned with a wrong species annotation. The four strains were
therefore re-annotated according to the phylogenetic group of species they were located
in. The diﬃculties in precise identiﬁcation to the species level could be explained with the
known genomic similarities between the Mitis group Streptococcus. Especially S. oralis,
S. mitis, and S. infantis species have shown to be extremely similar based on 16S rRNA
comparisons [42, 44]. All the 88 genomes from this study, were annotated using the 16S-
23S intergeneic spacer region and gdh analysis, which have shown to be more eﬀective than
16S rRNA analysis [62]. However, this result implies that the method is not suﬃcient,
and more species speciﬁc gene region might contribute to more precise identiﬁcation of
the respective Mitis group Streptococcus species.
Besides the four outliers in the 16S rRNA phylogentic tree, a ﬁfth strain also stood out:
the reference strain S. oralis SK143. However, the strain showed to cluster with the S
oralis species in the two CGIphylogeny trees, Figure S25 and Figure S26. Based on these
observations and the fact that the strain was downloaded from the NCBI databse, the
reference was not re-allocated.
7.4
Analysis of gene content
The number of predicted genes in the 88 assemblies and eight reference strains, showed
that S. gordonii and S. sanguinis encode for more genes than the remaining four species,
and the S. sanguinis species in average encodes for more genes than S. gordinii, Figure
14. This is in consistence with the gene content of known S. gordonii and S. sanguinis
reference genomes found in NCBI [17]. Furthermore, the number of encoded genes in the
S. cristatus, S. infantis, S. mitis, and S. oralis species was found to be very similar, in
consistency with the gene content of other known reference strains belonging to the same
species [17].
No clear correlation of amino acid usage was found, when analysing the percentage of
identiﬁed amino acid in the predicted gene, Figure 15. This analysis was therefore found

7.5
Comparative genomics
The identiﬁcation of similar genetic traits across the 96 Streptococcus genomes were found
to be an important step, to be able to compare the respective strains. The correct com-
parison method was believed to be crucial for correct identiﬁcation potential pathogen
speciﬁc genes as well as species speciﬁc gene variations. This identiﬁcation of homolog
genes caused great diﬃculties, and several approaches to combine genes with similar traits
were tried out.
7.5.1
Comparatative genomics based on sequence identity
Fist, the genes were clustered together using UCLUST and CD-HIT-EST. These clustering
tools only decreased the number of gene sequences with approximately 50% when using
the identity threshold of 98%, and even when using identity threshold of 90% the number
of genes were reduced to approximately 25,000 and 23,000 gene clusters in CD-HIT-EST
and UCLUST respectively, Table 5. Very few clusters were found to contain a gene from
each of the strains, and no clear implication of a potential core genome was observed.
This result was diﬀerent than expected, considering high similarities observed in the 16S
rRNA phylogenetic tree and implied a lot of false negative gene clusters. Several studies
have shown horizontal gene transfer between diﬀerent species of Mitis group streptococci,
and DNA uptake from the environment is well known [44, 45, 65]. The lack of clustered
genes could therefore suggest that each strain contained several unique genes that was not
shared across the species, due to the genomic recombination, however, it does not explain
the lack of observed core genes. A new approach of comparing and grouping the genes
was therefore necessary.
By using OrthoMCL, the gene sequences were grouped together based on orthology in-
stead of global sequence identities.
The OrthoMCL grouping of the genes resulted in
4,838 ortholog groups and 46,705 singletons. The high number of singletons supported
the suspicion of a high number of unique genes in each strain, and does imply that these
strains are very diverse when considering the whole genome. Only one group containing
a gene from all 95 Mitis group Streptococcus genomes was found using the OrhtoMCL
clustering method, Figure 16. This result diﬀers from the results obtained in the study
by Song et al., 20013, where a clear core genome of 1,373 gene groups was identiﬁed in 67
related Streptococcus mutans genomes [84]. The two diﬀerent result were expected, since
this study has included six diﬀerent species while the study by Song et al., 20013 only
have included Streptococcus mutans species [84]. However, the lack of identiﬁed ortholog
core gene groups in the 95 Streptococcus genomes, suggest false-negative clustering in Or-
thoMCL. Several clusters containing 70-80 genes from unique strains were found, and the
core genes of the 95 Mitis group Streptococcus genomes are likely to be found within these
gene groups.
A Pearson correlation analysis of the 4,838 ortholog groups revealed a clear separation of

7.5.2
Comparative genomic based on domain architecture
A functional proﬁle was generated on each of the Mitis groupStreptococcus genomes, based
on their protein domain architecture. The genes with similar domain architecture were
grouped together in a gene family, and were assumed to carry out the same function. In
total, 6,850 gene families were derived from the 96 Streptococcus genomes. The pan-core
analysis suggested an open pan genome, as it increased in size every time a new genome
was applied for analysis, Figure S27. The open pan genome can be considered as a dynamic
set of genes, which changes due to evolution. Furthermore, the increasing pan genome size
can be caused by acquirement of new genes in the analysed genomes [84]. The implication
of an open pan genome is in consistency with the biological knowledge of the Mitis group
streptococcus, since they are known to be recombinant due to the harsh environment they
grow in [45, 79]. Furthermore, the majority of the analysed genomes have gone through
an environmental change which can be coupled with an an evolutionary change in their
genomic composition [84].
A Pearson correlation analysis was performed on the identiﬁed gene families, and based
on the gene pattern of the 96 strains a matrix was generated. The Pearson gene cor-
relation matrix showed a clear separation of the species, and the correlation within the
species groups were found to be higher than observed in the OrthoMCL gene correlation,
Figure 18. In spite of all six species groups were found in separate correlation clusters,
the S. oralis species diverged into two subgroups in the Pearson gene correlation matrix.

Furthermore, a week correlation within the S. mitis species was observed compared to the
correlation observed in the other species clusters.
Based on functional proﬁles, a core genome was found containing 767 gene families. The
sequences belonging to those gene families were extracted from each genome, and used
to generate a consensus phylogenetic tree. This tree shows a clear separation of all six
species, which implies that each group of species carries unique versions of the identiﬁed
gene families, Figure 19. These gene families could therefore be used for future analysis
to reveal gene variations unique for the respective Mitis group Streptococcus species. This
result could have great importance in improving the separation of the Mitis group Strep-
tococcus strains at species level, which could contribute to a more targeted treatment of
patients with Mitis group Streptococcus related infections.
The consensus tree does however show a similar separation of the S. oralis genomes as
observed in the 16S rRNA tree and in both Pearson correlation matrices, Figure 13 and
Figure 18. This separation could suggest that the S. oralis species contain genomic vari-
ation, and could be split into two sub groups of S. oralis.
Eight of the Streptococcus strains were sequenced twice. Both versions were included in
this study to examine how similar the assembled genomes were. Based on the 16S rRNA
phylogeny, the A and B versions of each strain were found to be identical. However, the
consensus core tree implied small genomic variations between the A and B strains, which
was conﬁrmed in the CSIphylogeny trees, S25 and Figure S26. These small genomic dif-
ferences between to identical biological samples, implies sequencing variation or variation
in the assembler caused by e.g. assembly errors.
When comparing the results from the three diﬀerent gene comparative methods, the anal-
ysis based on domain architecture was found to be the most useful for this dataset. The
functions derived in this analysis were therefore used to extract information which could
be unique for the pathogenic strains, as well as for each of the four species: S. gordonii,
S. mitis S. oralis, and S. sanguinis.

Identiﬁcation of speciﬁc genes
To identify potential pathogenic genes the pan genomes of gene families were analysed.
In total, 15 gene families containing more than 40 pathogenic strains (and non of the
non-pathogenic) were observed in the pan genome, Table 6. The gene family observed in
the highest number of genomes, 52, contained 5 domains with the respective functions:
- Gram positive anchor.
- YSIRK type signal peptide.
- G5 domain.
- M26 IgA1-speciﬁc Metallo-endopeptidase N-terminal region.
- M26 IgA1-speciﬁc Metallo-endopeptidase C-terminal region.

Discussion
Several of the functions in this speciﬁc gene family can be related to known virulence fac-
tors among streptococci. To be able to infect a host and to cause Infective Endocarditis,
the bacteria have to be able to adhere to platelets or the surface structure of the heart
valves. The gram positive anchor is a surface protein, and it is known to share the same
functions as the M protein [82]. The M protein is a virulence factor identiﬁed in S. pneu-
moniae and provides protein adhesion to host [65]. The YSIRK type signal peptide is a
surface protein known in S. pneumoniae and is positioned within a signal peptide. This
domain is related to LPXTG sortase pathway, however not related to protein anchoring
nor to virulence [3]. The G5 domain is found in a wide range of extracellular proteins and
belongs to the M26 family as does the two last mentioned domains [5]. The M26 family
is known to cleave human IgA, and can be involved in metabolism of bacterial cell walls
suggesting this domain may have an adhesive function and bioﬁlm formation [5, 58]. S.
pneumoniae strains are known to produce an IgA1 protease which might interfere with
host defence at mucosal surfaces [1]. It may also facilitate attachment to epithelial cells
by cleaving sialic acid from the host glycolipids and gangliosides [1].
Considering the known functions of these ﬁve domains, this speciﬁc gene family is likely
to be involved in pathogenic behaviour of the strains carrying this gene. However, these
functions are based on predicted protein domains, and to determine the true functions of
the gene, more analysis are necessary, including in vivo studies. It is also important to
keep in mind that the gene has to be expressed in the bacteria before it has any function at
all. To determine if the remaining 14 gene families identiﬁed have a potentially pathogenic
function in the bacteria, more investigation of the predicted domains, and the expression
of the genes are necessary.
Potential Mitis group Streptococcus species speciﬁc gene families were furthermore ob-
served in the pan genome, however only three and ﬁve were identiﬁed as unique for the S.
mitis and S. oralis species respectively, Figure 20. As observed in the Pearson correlation
matrix, neither was the group of S. oralis nor the group of S. mitis species as strongly
correlated as the group of S. gordonii and group of S. sanguinis species. Both the ma-
trix and the consensus tree did furthermore imply, that the strains belonging to S. oralis
species consisted of more than one cluster. This could explain the lack of S. oralis speciﬁc
gene families, and implies that the S. oralis species are more diverse than the rest of the
species. The low correlations observed among the S. mitis species could explain the low
number of species speciﬁc gene families for the S. mitis species. Several functions were
annotated to the identiﬁed 59 S. gordonii and 31 S. sanguinis species speciﬁc gene fami-
lies, Table 7. Among those, one speciﬁc gene family was of special interest; it consisted of
three domains, and were uniquely observed in the S. sanguinis species:
- Gram positive anchor
- Collagen binding domain
- Cna protein B
Gram positive anchor has the same functions as described above [82]. Both the collagen
binding domain and the Cna (Collagen adhesion) protein B are involved in binding to
collagen and have been associated with surface adhesion in Staphylococcus aureus [53, 67].

Discussion
In the study by Patti et al., 1994, collagen adhesion proteins, similar to those observed
in the S. sanguinis species, were recognized as a virulence factor in Staphylococcus aureus
[67]. Presence of such binding proteins showed a signiﬁcantly higher infection rate than
non carriers in mouse models [67]. It is furthermore suggested that collagen is presented
on the surface of damaged platelets and heart valves, and a study by Hienz et al., 1996
observed a connection between collagen adherence of S. aureus and endocarditis in a rat
model [33]. This potential S. sanguinis speciﬁc gene family is therefore considered as a
potential virulence factor even though it is observed in S. sanguinis strains not related to
infections. The Mitis group streptococci are opportunistic pathogens, and it is therefore
very likely that strains that are isolated from healthy individuals also carry virulence genes
as the one described above. It has been suggested that collagen meditates adhesion to
dentin in the oral cavity and is believed to be an important factor in caries development
carried out by S. mutans [86]. The fact that the S. sanguinis species all carries an addi-
tional collagen adhesion gene could explain why S. sanguinis species have a greater natural
prevalence in the colonization of the oral cavity compared to S. gordonii species[65].
A closer inverstigation of the core genome revived a few gene families which were found
interesting, Table 8:
- Penicillin binding protein
- Bacteriocin related domains
- Competence
- Host binding domains.
The presence of the three penicillin binding domains in the core genome could suggest
that all the strains are sensitive to penicillin. However, few mutations in such antibiotic
target genes could make the antibiotic compound ineﬀective [93]. Antibiotic resistance
can furthermore be achieved by pumping the antibiotic compound out of the cell or to
break down the compound by enzymatic activity [93]. Further gene sequence analysis is
necessary to determine if these genes are wild type or mutated, and antibiotic sensitivity
can furthermore be established by phenotypic tests.
Bacteriocins are toxins produced
by bacteria to kill or inhibit growth of other, often closely related bacteria. The LytTR
binding domain is found to be regulation important virulence factors such as extracellular
polysaccharides, toxins and bacteriocins. This domain has been recognized as a part of the
two-component signal transduction systems (TCSS) and is involved in the regulation of cell
autolysis [64]. The Mitis group streptococci which grow in the oral cavity are often found
in bioﬁlm where nutrition and space competition is well known [47, 48]. The ability to
lyse other related bacterial cells does give the strains an advantage in regard of nutrition-
and space competition as well as acquirement of new genes [45, 47, 48].
Competence
or the ability to take up and incorporate foreign DNA can be regulated and promoted
by competence speciﬁc genes [83]. The CoiA like domain is an uncharacterised protein,
however it is known to belong to the competence-speciﬁc operon in S. pneumoniae which
is involved in genetic transformation [46, 69].
Presence of both the LytTR and CoiA
like domain in the core genome can act as promoters for genetic diversity in the Mitis

the pan genome. The analysed Mitis group Streptococcus strains are all assumed to be
able to attach to the surface of the oral cavity. The majority of the analysed genomes
were furthermore related to colonization and infection in the heart, and thereby assumed
to be able interact with the host. The interaction and adherence to cell surfaces of the

Mitis group Streptococcus species uses diﬀerent mechanisms to adhere to the cell surface
of the host. It is believed that the pathogenic behaviour of the Mitis group streptococci
is carried out by several mechanisms, some which might compensate the lack of others.
Therefore, more gene families which could be involved in virulence are believed be found
in the pan genome. To be able to say more about the predicted functions of the gene
families further analysis of the gene sequences are necessary.
7.6
Resﬁnder
Patients with IE are treated with long term intravenous antimicrobial therapy. The an-
tibiotics have a lethal eﬀect on the Streptococcus bacteria, however, some bacteria are
able to become resistance to those antibiotic compounds as described in section 7.5.3. In
this project the ResFinder search tool was used, and the BLAST against their database
with known antibiotic resistance genes. The search showed that 30 of the 96 genomes
were carriers of such genes, Figure 21. The detected antibiotic genes were: Tetracycline ,
Macrolide, Aminoglycoside and Macrolide, Lincosamide, and Streptogramin B resistance,
and are not directly related to the antibiotic treatment usually provided to patients with
IE [77]. It is a known fact that bacteria can become resistance to antibiotics by diﬀerent
evolutionary mechanisms, this result does therefore not imply that the remaining 65 are
susceptible to antibiotic treatment [93]. The presence of resistance genes are furthermore
only an implication of antibiotic resistance to the speciﬁc compounds, since it is unknown
if the genes are expressed. To determine if the analysed genome are susceptible to antibi-
otic treatment, and to which antibiotic compound, more genetic analysis are necessary, as
well as an antibiotic sensitivity study.

Conclusions
8
Conclusions
The overall quality of sequenced genomes were high and trimming or adapter removal was
not found necessary. The SPAdes assembly algorithm was found to be the best suited for
our type of data, however scaﬀolds with a low assembly depth had to be removed from
the assembly to achieve the best possible assemblies.
Great similarities within the 16S rRNA region were found in the Mitis group Streptococcus
genomes, and 16S rRNA was found insuﬃcient in regard to separate the S. mitis, S. oralis,
and S. infantis species. Combining the phylogeny trees, the functional gene correlation
analysis and the consensus core tree, four of the strains were originally allocated to a false
species. Clustering of the Mitis group streptococci genes based on homology showed to
cause great diﬃculties, and a great genomic variation was observed within the dataset.
However, grouping based on functional protein domain architecture was found to give the
best results. A pan genome of 6,580 gene families was identiﬁed including a core genome
of 787 gene families. The consensus core tree did suggest the presence of Mitis group
streptococci core genes with unique species speciﬁc variations.
Based on the functional protein domain architecture in the Mitis group Streptococcus
genomes, it was possible to identify 15 potential pathogenic protein families, as well as
several potential S. gordonii, and S. sanguinis species speciﬁc gene families. Potential
virulence and competence functions were observed in the core genome, which could be
responsible for pathogenic behaviour and genetic recombination. Three penicillin binding
domains were observed in the core genome, however, antibiotic resistance genes were found
in 30 of the 96 analysed Mitis group Streptococcus genomes. Based on these ﬁndings the
antibiotic sensitivity of the analysed strains could not be established. More genotypic and
phenotypic investigations of the strains are needed to determine if the identiﬁed genes are
involved in pathogenic behaviour, as well if the analysed strains have evolved resistance
to speciﬁc antibiotics.

Future perspectives
9
Future perspectives
The results from this study revealed that it was possible to identify potential pathogenic
gene families, potential species speciﬁc gene families, and potential antibiotic resistance
genes using a biotinformatic approach. However, more investigations are needed to deter-
mine the true functions and species relation of the genes.
Pathogenic genes
To determine if the identiﬁed pathogenic genes are virulence factors in the Mitis group
streptococci more gene analysis are necessary. Furthermore, an in vivo knock out study
would potentially determine the true function of the genes. However, it is believed that the
virulence of the bacteria is multi-factored, and several diﬀerent genes could be responsible
for the pathogenic behaviour. The identiﬁed potential pathogenic gene families could be
subject for a future therapeutic vaccine. However, to be able to target all pathogenic Mitis
group streptococci and not commensal oral bacteria, more more non-pathogenic Streptoc-
cocus genomes needs to be included, as well as other related species.
Species speciﬁc genes
This study was able to reveal species speciﬁc gene families as well as core genes with
species speciﬁc variations. These result could contribute to a more correct separation of
Mitis group streptococci genomes to the species levels, which could be beneﬁcial for the
healthcare system and will lead to faster and more precise diagnostics of IE. The species
speciﬁc gene variation could also be subject for a future therapeutic vaccine, making the
treatment extremely target speciﬁc. This study observed a lack of S. oralis and S. mitis
species speciﬁc gene families. More bioinformatic investigations are necessary to be able
to determine the cause of this result. The analysis could furthermore establish if the S.
oralis species should be divided into separate groups of subspecies.
Antibiotic resistance
Potential antibiotic sensibility were observed in the core genome of the Mitis group strep-
tococci. However, several antibiotic resistance genes were observed in the genomes of 30
the Mitis group streptococci. To be able to determine if the analysed strains are antibiotic
sensitive or resistant, and to which antibiotic compound, more bioinformatic analysis and
phenotypic test are necessary.

Kort beskrivelse af projektet
Infective endocarditis (IE) is an inﬂammation of the inner tissue of the heart caused by
infectious organisms. Usually the inﬂammation is caused by a streptococcal- or staphylo-
coccal bacterial infection, and tends to be caused by the patient’s own commensal strains.
[4]. Between 1.7- 6.2 cases of IE are recorded per 100.000 patients every year, and the
mortality rate is as high as 40% [2,5]. In spite of improvements in healthcare and higher
awareness of hygiene the incidence of IE has remained the same over the past two decades
[2]. This study will cover IE caused by viridans streptococci, which are the leading cause
of this type of infection among streptococci [2,4]. The Mitis group streptococcus strains,
belonging to the viridans streptococci, are normally commensal mouth bacteria with a
beneﬁcial eﬀect in the host. The isolation of bacteria in blood culture and species identi-
ﬁcation is of high importance for diagnosing IE, and for correct and accurate treatment
of the patient. Discrimination of the strains to the species level has always been a great
challenge [2, 5].
This study will focus on improvements of fast and accurate identiﬁ-
cation of the bacterial strains, evolutionary aspects as well as identiﬁcation of speciﬁc
target genes important for disease development and progression. This will be achieved
through computational comparisons and the aim is to further characterize the genomes of
strains of Mitis group streptococci with the perspective of recognizing genes controlling
important pathogenic pathways. Doctors at Slagelse Hospital have previously sequenced
80 Mitis group streptococcal strains isolated from IE patients, which are available for
analysis in the study. Additionally, collaboration with Presidential Research Professor
Christina Warinner at the University of Oklahoma (USA) has been established, who has
pioneered research of extracting bacterial DNA from ancient dental calculus. These an-
cient bacterial genomes will be used to assess the recent evolution and acquisition of genes
in these strains, which allows them to infect human heart valves. The project will be
carried out at the Department of Systems Biology at the Technical University of Denmark
in close collaboration with medical doctors at Slagelse Hospital. These doctors have had
long-lasting interests for these groups of bacteria, including Mitis group streptococci, and
perform routine diagnostics of IE. The ﬁndings in the projects can therefore relatively
fast be translated into beneﬁts for the healthcare system and will lead to faster and more
precise diagnostics of IE. Additionally it will try to elucidate which genetic components

INTRODUCTION
Infective endocarditis (IE), is an infection of the inner heart valves with 400 cases a year in
Denmark. Mitis group streptococci, normally commensal bacteria of the human orophar-
ynx, often cause the infection, and very little is known of how they become pathogens.
Treatment of IE often involve long term antibiotic treatment, and in approximately 50%
of the cases; heart surgery [2]. Fast and correct identiﬁcation of the bacterial strains in the
blood contribute to a more eﬃcient treatment and prognosis of the infection. This project
will cover the changing face of Mitis group streptococci: going from being a commensal to
a pathogen strain, with the aim of more speciﬁc and targeted treatment of patients with
IE. This project will address this issue using state-of-the-art bioinformatics on high qual-
ity DNA data of known pathogenic Mitis group streptococci, ancient streptococci isolated
from ancient dental calculus, and known commensal Mitis group streptococci. Further-
more will a unique gene expression study be performed to illuminate virulence factors
responsible for the heart infection.
PERSPECTIVES AND SPECIFIC AIMS
Bioinformatic analysis of the whole genome information will give us better understanding
of the pathogenicity of Mitis group streptococci, normally a commensal bacteria, which
may cause serious disease as infective endocarditis. Besides, Mitis group streptococci, es-
pecially Streptococcus mitis, are close relatives of S. pneumonia; the fourth most frequent
cause of fatal infections world-wide. Identiﬁcation of core genes that are speciﬁc for the
pathogenic strains can potentially be used as target for therapeutic treatment related to
pneumococcal diseases and other streptococci infections. Therefore this Ph.D. project has
the following aims:
1.Assembly of ancient Streptococcus species from dental calculus
2.Comparative studies of pathogenic, commensal, and ancient streptococci to identify ge-

BACKGROUND
Diagnostics and bacterial phylogeny
Identiﬁcation of streptococci and related gram positive cocci based on conventional phe-
notypic reactions has always been a challenge in the routine microbiological laboratories
[3]. Molecular diagnostic methods, mainly 16S ribosomal rRNA gene sequencing has rev-
olutionized bacteriology in the last 2-3 decades [5]. However, for some of the species, 16S
rRNA gene sequences are too similar to be able to discriminate them to species level. The
16S-23S Intergenic Spacer (ITS) region has been found useful in separating most of the
clinically relevant NHS [3]. However, in some cases this method is not precise enough, and
a more speciﬁc taxonomic classiﬁcation method is necessary. The precise species iden-
tiﬁcation gives the clinicians important information related to suspected primary focus,
choice of treatment and prognosis. We therefore ﬁnd it of interest to identify more speciﬁc
gene sequences that could be used to make a precise molecular characterization of clinical
isolates causing IE.
Mitis group streptococci, opportunistic pathogens
Mitis group streptococci are normal commensals of the human oropharynx. Colonization
of the oral cavity begins within a few hours after birth with bacteria transferred from the
mother, nurses and the environment [5]. The oral streptococci are known to be involved
in the initial colonization of the oral cavity and make up over 80% of the early bioﬁlm.
Studies of the oral cavity have shown a connection between a high concentration of strains
belonging to the Mitis group and a healthy oral cavity, while individuals with tendency of
carries have almost non-detectable levels of the speciﬁc strains [5]. This implies that the
strains of the Mitis group streptococci have a beneﬁcial eﬀect in the host. However, Mitis
group streptococci can escape from this niche and cause a variety of infectious complica-
tions including IE [2,4]. The question is: How a commensal organism with few identiﬁed
toxins and virulence factors can be a successful pathogen in the human host. To make
a signiﬁcant impact on the treatment of infections carried out by Mitis group strepto-
cocci there needs to be a greater understanding of how the bacteria colonizes the human
oropharynx, survive the phagocytosis, invade the epithelium and establish standing infec-
tion.
The genome of strains belonging to the S. mitis group
The key to the changing face of Mitis bacteria; going from being a commensal to a pathogen
strain might be found in its genetic composition. The genomes contain a GC content of
40%, are between 1.8 and 2.1 Mb in length, and encodes between 1800-2200 genes. This is
similar to genomes of S. pneumoniæ, which also belongs to the Mitis group (please see the
enclosed ﬁgure: ”phylogeny”). S. pneumoniæ is the fourth most frequent cause of fatal in-

Gene expression
Identiﬁcation of potential virulence genes in a strain does not necessary make the strain
pathogenic. For the commensal oral bacteria can cause IE, the bacteria have to enter the
bloodstream, and the bacteria therefore undergo an environmental shift. The environmen-
tal conditions may change from aerobic to anserobic conditions and several factors as pH,
temperature, and potential attachment sites may also be diﬀerent at the infection site.
These factors can have a great inﬂuence on the gene expression of bacteria. We therefore
ﬁnd it of great importance to analyze the expression of the identiﬁed virulence genes under
diﬀerent environmental conditions. This will enable us to map the genetic pathways of
genes that we expect to be crucial for the colonization of heart valves. This insight could
lead to identiﬁcation of gene targets relevant for speciﬁc treatment.
HYPOTHESES
-Whole genome comparisons of pathogenic Mitis group streptococci with non-pathogenic
Mitis group streptococci, will show diﬀerences in gene content that will give information
about the pathogenic potential of the strains.
-Identiﬁcation of pathogenic pathways will lead to gene targets, relevant for speciﬁc treat-
ment of the patients.
-Information from of ancient Streptococcus species from dental calculus will give insights
of how the species have evolved though time, and which genes are involved in host colo-
nization and pathogenesis.
METHODS
This project is a further extension of an ongoing PhD project, which is about identiﬁcation
of virulence genes and resistance genes. 80 strains of Mitis group streptococci (belonging
to 6 species), have been collected from patients with IE, and deep sequenced. These strains
are therefore both available as sequenced genomes, and as stored bacteria, which can be
used for in vitro studies in the laboratory. More genomes of Mitis group streptococci iso-
lated from healthy persons will be retrieved from international databases (e.g. NCBI) and
included in this study. Whole sequenced genomes of ancient streptococci will furthermore
be included in the study. To achieve the previous listed aims of the study will be divided
into the following three parts:
a) Bioinformatic and comparative studies of the DNA sequences
- This part will include aim 1 and 2, and will involve a bioinformatic approch: Assembly of
genomes from metagenomic environment such as the oral microbiome will be carried out
using a novel method based on gene co-occurrence and abundance correlations which the
group has developed and published in Nature Biotechnology [1]. When the Strepotococ-

assembler SPAdes. Comparative studies of pathogenic, commensal and ancient strains will
be carried out using functional analysis of the protein sequences in the strains using the
program PanFunPro. We will reconstruct the phylogeny of the Mitis group streptococci
using the ancient samples and determine and with OrthoMCL to identify orthologous

b) Gene expression studies of the pathogenic strains
- This part will include aim 3 and 4, and involve laboratory work and DNA sequencing:
Expression of virulence genes and toxins will be estimated using real-time PCR. Analysis
of up-and down regulation of virulence genes in the pathogenic strains when grown under
diﬀerent environmental conditions will be performed. RNA sequencing and in vitro knock-
out studies of potential virulence genes will be performed by a comparative transcriptome
analysis. Messenger RNA from selected strains will be extracted and then sequenced using
an Illumina Miseq. The data will be aligned to the pan-genome using Burrows Wheeler
Aligner (BWA) and gene abundance estimated using htseq. Diﬀerential expression will
be identiﬁed using DEseq and the transcriptional responses will be correlated with the
metabolic networks and the pathogenic potential.
c) Characterization of metabolic pathways
- This part will include aim 5 and will combine the results gained from a) and b) and fur-
thermore include bioinformatic analysis: Characterization of metabolic pathways, poten-
tial virulence and resistance genes in Mitis group streptococci, will involve bioinformatics
analysis and comparisons of predicted genes in the streptococci strains. The analysis will
among others include annotation of all genes and proteins with their metabolic, virulence
and resistance potential. The metabolic reconstruction will be carried out by mapping the
functionalities to metabolic pathways and use this to reconstruct the metabolic potential
of the bacteria. Virulence genes will by similarity to other virulence genes and novel vir-
ulence genes predicted by protein domain occurrences and pathogenicity prediction such
as PathogenFinder.
