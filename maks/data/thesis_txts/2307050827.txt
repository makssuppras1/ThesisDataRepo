Abstract 
 
According to European Incentives, there is urgency to a more environmentally friendly approach towards 
energy consumption. In the building sector, this means reduction in energy consumption and improvement 
of energy efficiency, without compromising indoor climate and healthy indoor environment. Thus, the aim 
of this research is to investigate the potential of a new heating and cooling system, integrated with diffuse 
ventilation. 
 The investigation is conducted in the context of a Danish office building, with indoor climate category A, 
according to Building Regulations 2015. Energy demand is calculated with simulations in IDA ICE of a three 
zone building (office-corridor-office) with both north and south oriented offices. 
The system is, then, simulated in Comsol in a microenvironment, in order to calculate new convective heat 
transfer coefficients. Results in Comsol confirmed the increase in heat transfer from the system to the 
plenum air, as well as, to the air that passes through the perforated ceiling and enters the room. 
Contribution of the concrete slab in the plenum is also taken into consideration; thus, it is simulated and 
proven to have a positive effect in the whole system. 
Compared to the state of art systems, the new solution is proven to be more effective in both heating and 
cooling mode, excluding the heat exchange of CATC in heating mode, which is marginally higher. 
The pipe system used in this investigation was calculated to have very small pressure drop. As a result, 
smaller pipes can be used to provide the required heating and cooling demand. 
Variations of this system for further work are presented and limitations, that are not part of this project, 
are discussed.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
 
1.1 Background 
 
Countries around the world have established new energy regulations and policies, in an attempt to 
ensure future prosperity and face current threats, such as global warming or emissions of greenhouse 
gases. Until recently, energy was dependent on fossil fuels; the introduction of new sustainable sources 
has made the energy approach more environmentally friendly. More specifically, Denmark’s 
government has announced in the report ‘’Voresenergi’’ (Our energy), that its target is to base their 
energy supplies on 100% renewable energy by 2050, while the European Union has set as a goal a 20% 
reduction in primary energy consumption by 2020 (European Commission 2011). 
Around 40% of Danish total energy, as well as in other European countries, is consumed by the building 
sector, mostly by heating and operating equipment(1). Thus, a primary goal is to minimize consumption 
of these areas, firstly by reducing their energy demand and secondly, by exploiting new technologies 
based on renewable sources, which will make low temperature heating and high temperature cooling 
feasible. 
In Denmark, the first step of this approach is covered by new regulations on construction, such as 
BR2010/2015/2020(2)(3), which increase the thermal insulation and air-tightness of the building 
envelope in new buildings or in renovated old ones. 
In the second part, Denmark, as other countries, have introduced regulations on the energy 
performance of the whole building, which require an increase in efficiency of heating and cooling 
systems, ventilation and other equipment, while suggestions about renewable energy sources are 
introduced. New energy requirements for Denmark are presented in chapter 4.

In general, though, the sustainable direction, as introduced by the United Nations, requires the 
collaboration of all three aspects: society, economy and environment; one cannot achieve this goal

by spreading information and  by adapting a sustainable way of living, economy represents the

sources (such as solar, wind, geothermal etc. energy).  
 
1.2 Problem Statement 
 
With the aforementioned taken into consideration, research has been made on new heating and 
cooling systems. Old buildings in Denmark, due to their poor building envelope, have high demand in 
heating, while other non-residential buildings need higher cooling capacity, because of high internal 
gains from technological equipment and air-tightness in case of new buildings. Such problems have 
been treated well in the past by floor heating and ceiling cooling; but in many cases, a building requests 
both solutions and their combination is not economically feasible. To that end, recent research has 
focused on investigating new combined systems, such as floor heating and cooling or ceiling heating 
and cooling. A combined system can decrease the construction materials needed, as well as, the 
installation time required. On the other hand, it has some challenges to overcome; heating has been 
proven more efficient when placed on the floor, because it is enhanced by buoyancy flows, while when 
it is placed on the ceiling, comfort may be compromised and the effect of ‘’warm head’’ can be 
experienced. At a similar note, cooling performs better on the ceiling, while on the floor, it may cause 
local discomfort due to ‘’cold feet’’. Another challenge new systems face is their application with 
renewable energies, such as low temperature district heating, solar heating, heat pumps etc.; new 
systems should be able to provide the required heating and cooling energy with low temperature for 
heating and high temperature for cooling, meaning that the heat transfer from the system to the room 
is increased. These challenges can possibly be confronted, if the new combined system is combined 
with ventilation, which is the subject of this thesis. 
The aim of this project is to investigate such a system. A heating and cooling system placed on the 
ceiling, combined with diffuse ventilation. This system will be simulated with regard an existing Danish 
building office, in Comsol, fulfilling indoor climate category A requirements. 
The simulation’s results will be of quantitative nature and will give a perspective of the system’s 
potential for further exploration and use, which may result in more energy efficient buildings.

1.4 Limitations 
 
In this project, humidity and condensation in the plenum or the contact surface are not investigated. 
Ventilation effectiveness and air velocity is also not included. 
Moreover, the effect on the radiant heat transfer of the new system is not part of this report.  
In addition, the system is simulated in a microenvironment, while room simulation was proven not 
feasible, due to geometry complexity in Comsol simulations. 
More analytical assumptions of the model are analyzed and presented in Chapter 5 (Discussion of 
validity).

Chapter 2 
Literature review 
 
Air-based heating and cooling systems have be proven to be insufficient; Increased energy consumption, 
bad indoor air quality, noise, draught, Sick Building Syndromes(SBS) and high initial costs are some of their 
disadvantages. Thus, the development of water-based radiant heating and cooling systems attracted the 
focus of scientific research. These systems consist of water-circulating pipes (water is used as a medium), 
which can be placed on the ceiling (radiant panels) or be embedded in the building structure (embedded 
systems) or in the concrete core (TABS). Initial application of these systems took place at residential 
buildings, where free space was the primary issue. For this purpose and due to their ability to reduce the 
peak load, water-based systems were applied also in commercial and industrial buildings. 
 
2.1 Radiant heating and cooling panels(4) 
 
Radiant panels are systems consisting of pipes, placed under the suspended ceiling, insulated on the other 
side, in order to avoid heat losses towards the plenum. Panels are made from high-emissivity materials, 
while above them, high heat conductive materials contribute to the heat exchange between water and the 
ceiling. In order for such a system to be referred to as radiant, 50% or more of the heat transfer should 
occur by radiation. 
These systems are proven to be very efficient in removing sensible heat, by both radiation and convection. 
Radiation occurs between the ceiling and the other surfaces exposed to the ceiling. At the same time, 
convection takes place between the ceiling and the air that moves on its surface. In cooling mode, due to 
the fact that cold air drops down, moving by downward plume, radiant panels are more efficient, compared 
to heating mode, when air usually stays close to the ceiling area and convective heat transfer is minimized 
(5). Heat transfer coefficients from literature are being presented here.

2.2 
Embedded heating and cooling systems 
Embedded radiant systems consist of a plastic water piping, which works with low temperature for heating 
and high temperature for water; in general, the water temperature is very close to the room temperature. 
These systems are usually insulated from the building structure, which results in the heat transfer taking 
place mainly between the water system and the room.

Embedded systems work both by convection and radiation (varying from 50% to 90%). The radiant heat 
transfer is 5.5 W/m2K, while the convective has a range between 0.5 and 5.5W/m2K, depending on the 
placement of the system and its mode (heating or cooling). The total heat transfer coefficient for each 
system is presented in the table below.(6)

TABS are water-based radiant surface heating and cooling systems, consisting of embedded pipes in the 
concrete core of the construction. Its effectiveness is a result of the great thermal coupling of the emitting 
element and the main building structure, which makes the use of water near room temperatures possible. 
During daytime, ventilation extracts part of the heat from the occupied zone, while the remaining heat is 
stored in the concrete slab, which is then removed, during the night, by the circulated cold water in TABS. 
As a result, the required peak load is reduced and, thus, energy savings is achieved(7) 
 In addition, the fact that water close to room temperature is used increases the energy efficiency of the 
heat pumps, boilers or any heat exchangers.(8)

2.7 Free air cooling 
 
In some situations, the external temperature of a room can be much cooler than the indoor, due

external temperature can be used to cool down the air; external sources could be the outdoor air, 
groundwater, seawater etc. 
This unique effect has been recently proven very efficient for a novel system (12), which uses only 
natural ventilation, combined with building thermal mass activation and diffuse ventilation. In this 
research, new well-insulated and air-tight buildings, which need cooling almost all year round, are 
exploiting the external free air, which comes in contact with the thermo active concrete slab and 
gets cooled or heated. This system has the potential to reach up to even 50% of energy savings, 
for a typical office with internal heat loads of 30-40 W/m2.

3.2.2 Turbulent modeling 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
15 
Turbulent flow or turbulence is a time-dependent flow regime with a chaotic (unsteady, irregular) 
behavior, with fluctuated transported quantities, which is commonly seen in many fluid flows. Its fluid 
properties include low momentum diffusion, high momentum convection, as well as high variation of 
pressure and flow velocity in space and time. (15) 
An important characteristic of this specific flow is the ability to enhance mixing of fluid mixtures and to 
increase rates of mass, momentum and energy transports, called diffusivity. In addition, a turbulent flow 
contains a wide range of turbulent eddy (swirling or reverse current of a fluid) sizes, where large eddies 
derive energy from the mean flow, thus a constant energy supply is required. This energy is then spouted in 
smaller eddies, creating a hierarchy in eddies, which finally, results in such small structures that molecular 
diffusion becomes significant enough to convert turbulent energy into internal by viscous dissipation.

3.2.3 Computational approach 
 
==PAGE:28==
 
16 
The laminar flow can be easily calculated by solving the steady-state Navier-Stokes equations, which 
predict successfully the velocity and pressure of the fluid. 
The turbulent flow, though, is time-dependent, so the problem needs to be solved in the time domain.  In 
addition, the existence of small eddies and the short timescales of oscillations make N-S equations 
computationally unfeasible to be solved. Thus, Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) were formulated, 
which are time averaged equations of fluid motion and give approximate-averaged solutions. 
Consequently, new unknowns are added to the system and approximations for the near-wall flow is 
introduced. 
3.2.3.1 Wall Law 
 
In the turbulent flow near the wall, four different flow regimes occur. At the wall surface, velocity is zero 
and for a very thin layer over this surface, velocity increases linearly to the distance from the surface, this 
layer is called ″viscous sub layer″ (or laminar). Above this layer, ″the buffer layer″ is created, where the flow 
starts to transition to turbulent. In the region where the flow is fully transitioned to turbulent, the velocity 
is proportional to the logarithm of the distance of the point from the wall surface and is known as ″log-law 
region″. If the distance of the buffer surface from the wall is δ, then the extension of the log-law region 
reaches 100δ from the wall. After the region of log-law, the flow is described by a free-stream flow. All the 
above are depicted in the Figure below. 
 
 
Figure 11. The four sub layers that characterize turbulent flow. 
 
RANS models can compute the flow in all four sub layers. Models, that use wall functions, ignore the buffer 
layer and compute a non-zero velocity at the wall. In case a more accurate solution is required, then 
models that solve the entire flow region is required. 
3.2.3.2 Turbulent models (RANS) in Comsol 
 
==PAGE:29==
 
17 
All of seven models in Comsol differ in how they solve the wall function, as well as, in the number of 
variables that they use, how they solve these extra variables and how they compute the turbulent viscosity 
term. All models’ differences are described below; models are presented in order of increase 
computational cost per iteration.(16) 
1. L-VEL and y-plus 
These models compute the flow motion, taking into account only the local fluid velocity and the 
distance to the closest wall, without additional variables. Their results represent good 
approximations for internal flow, but they are the least accurate. 
2. Spalart-Allmaras 
This model is a one-equation model, which means it solves for only one additional variable, the 
Spalart-Allmaras viscosity and it does not solve any wall functions. Even though, it is considered to 
be stable, with good convergence, it does not perform satisfyingly in case of shear, separated or 
decaying flow. 
3. k-ε 
The k-ε model is the most widely used model in industrial engineering. It is a two-equation model, 
which solves for k, the turbulent kinetic energy and ε, the rate of dissipation of kinetic energy. This 
model uses wall functions, which means that the buffer layer is ignored; this property of the model 
makes it inadequate for flows that involve adverse pressure gradients, strong curvature or jet flow. 
Meanwhile, its results for external flow around complex geometries are considered accurate. 
4. k-ω 
The k-ω model differs from the k-ε, only in the second variable it solves, which is ω, the specific 
rate of dissipation of kinetic energy. This model also uses wall functions, but faces more difficulty in 
converging (compared to k-ε). It is usually used together with the k-ε model, which computes the 
initial conditions to set as input in this model. Moreover, k-ω can be used in the cases, mentioned 
above, where k-ε is facing difficulties. 
5. Low Reynolds Number k-ε 
This model is also similar to k-ε, but it does not solve the wall functions, which means that it solves 
for the whole flow motion and gives more accurate results for lift and drag forces, as well as for 
heat flux. 
6. SST 
The SST model is a combination of k-ε and k-ω; it solves the free stream flow based on the k-ε, 
while it uses the k-ω model for solving near the wall, without using the wall functions. This model is 
the most accurate when it comes to near-wall results, but it is the most computationally costly, 
since it requires more iterations to be solved.

5.1 Functional requirements 
 
The new product idea should have specific functional requirements, which will fulfill our sustainable aim. 
New regulations obtain low energy consumption, lower operational and maintenance costs, while 
construction management requests simple and easy installation and reparation systems, in order to save 
time and minimize costs. 
With that in mind, the new system is designed to integrateventilation with a heating and cooling system, in 
the plenum of the room.   
With regard to heating and cooling, radiant panels are chosen instead of conventional air heating and 
cooling systems, which have the potential to cause discomfort and incidents of draught. Water is chosen as 
energy medium due to its high conductivity (0.58 W/(mK)) compared to air (0.024 W/(mK)), a property that 
also allows the system to work in small temperature differences and satisfy the needs for low temperature 
heating and high temperature cooling. Another general qualification for water is the fact that it is an 
abundant, non-toxic and safe material to be used in a system, where leakages might occur.

5.3 Modeling of solutions 
5.3.1 General description 
 
The central concept behind the water piping is the easy and fast construction and installation, which lead to 
place the pipes in the suspension profile system. This restricts the piping distance to 600mm, but allows 
free space on top of the diffusing surface. Due to this restriction, pipes should have big enough diameter 
(>16mm) to provide the required mass flow. No insulation is placed on top, in order to allow the ventilated 
air to pass through and be heated or cooled by the pipe system.  
Moreover, in order to increase the heat transfer from the pipes to the ceiling surface, an aluminum plate 
was chosen to be placed around the pipe, which was spread along or embedded in the surface. However, 
other new conductive materials (polymers) might constitute a more effective solution (but lack of more 
precise data does not allow us to use them in our model). 
The ceiling surface would be gypsum board, with perforations of 10mm, while another option could be an 
alu -plate with 2.5mm perforations.(24)

5.5 Discussion of validity 
 
The model described above faces some limitations. First and most importantly, due to its complex 
geometry (perforations), it cannot be simulated in a macro-environment. For that reason, the plenum had 
to be divided in small representative parts, which would be sequential simulated one after another. 
In the design of the system, the real suspension system, that hangs from the slab and creates an 
obstruction to the air flow, is not simulated. 
In addition, full mixing is assumed in the flow; meaning that the average time spent of each air particle 
equals to the average time of a particle that takes one round in the plenum. Thus, it was assumed that the 
average temperature of the exiting air particle would be equal to the one who has made one full round 
inside the suspended ceiling; as a result, in order to simulate the average plenum temperature, a number of 
simulations equal to the times this particle would go over the pipe is selected. 
Another limitation faced with Comsol is the inlet velocity. In order to have more accurate results, the outlet 
velocity profile of each simulation represents the inlet velocity of the next one. This velocity is parabolic, 
but inserting a (parabolic) polynomial function of the height (U(z)) was not possible in Comsol. As a result, it 
was decided to split the inlet height in three representative parts (based on the parabolic outlet velocity)

6.1.1 Mesh 
Sensitivity analysis is firstly conducted with regard to the mesh. Any mesh coarser than “normal”   could not

“extremely fined” mesh.

phenomena were affected negatively. On the other hand, a finer mesh (extra fine) presented the same 
results as the used one.

the flow and not the “Wall Law” of turbulent flow.

6.3 Discussion 
 
Comparing to the state of the art, the new solution system shows great potential. The convective heat 
transfer coefficient of the new system is increased, in both heating and cooling mode. Especially in the 
heating mode, the new convective heat transfer coefficient can reach up to 5 W/m2, which is more efficient 
than the radiant panels for heating which have a convective HTC of 0.5 W/m2. 
Similar positive results were also acquired for cooling mode, where the new solution has a new convective 
HTC of 6.9 W/m2, which is also increased compare to the HTC of the traditional radiant system of 3.1-
4.4W/m2. 
In both modes, the contribution of the slab is significant at 28-29%. But even without the slab, the heating 
coefficient is increased by 3 W/m2 for radiant panels, which makes the new system very energy efficient. 
While in cooling, HTC is increased by 1.8W/m2. 
If the radiant HTC is assumed to be 5.5 W/m2, as it is for conventional radiant panels, in both heating and 
cooling, the total HTCs would be 10.5 W/m2 and 12.4 W/m2 respectively.

Chapter 8 
Conclusions 
 
Heat transfer coefficients:  
 Investigation of a new heating and cooling system by simulations in Comsol show a great potential in heat 
exchange between the system and the air of the plenum, as well as, with the air that crosses the perforated 
ceiling.  
 
Heat transfer coefficient to the plenum is 1.5 W/m2 for heating and 1.97 W/m2 for cooling. While the heat 
transfer coefficient cross-ceiling is 2 W/m2 for heating and 2.9 W/m2 for cooling. 
Contribution of the concrete slab is also taken into consideration, because there is heat exchange between 
the slab and the moving air in plenum. Thus, the slab heat transfer coefficient was calculated to be 1.5 W/m2  
for heating and 2.1 W/m2 for cooling. 
 
Adding in the above the potential radiant heat transfer coefficient of conventional radiant ceiling panels, the 
total HTC of the new system reaches up to 10.5 W/m2 in heating mode and 12.4 W/m2 in cooling mode. Both 
results show a big increase in its heat exchange ability. 
 
When compared to other existing systems, which are mentioned and analyzed in the literature review, the 
new system has the highest performance in cooling, while, in heating mode, only CATC seem to be 
marginally better, but this could possible change if the ventilation rate is increased. 
 
In a Danish Context: 
Moreover, the new solution proved that it is able to cover the energy needs of an office building of 
2015/2020, with efficiency. The current system’s pressure drop was calculated to be very small for the study 
case of “intend”, as a result a smaller pipe can be used to cover these needs. Furthermore, the new heating 
and cooling system, integrated with ventilation can provide the required operative temperature for an office 
to be classified as Category A, with the appropriate temperature set-points. 
 
Further work: 
The focus of this project, though, was only on the convective heat transfer. Its radiant heat transfer should 
be evaluated or confirmed as assumed. In addition, control of Relative Humidity should take place, inside 
the plenum. The effect of the suspension hangers on the air flow in plenum should be investigated, in 
further work. 
Furthermore, due to lack of computational time, ideas with a variation of the placement of the aluminum 
plate or the height of the pipe in the suspension system were not simulated. While, alternatives in more 
conductive materials such, as super conductive polymers, should be integrated in the system and tested.
