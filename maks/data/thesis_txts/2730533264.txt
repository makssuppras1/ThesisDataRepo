1
Introduction
In recent decades, the arrival of Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) technology, particu­
larly SAR Interferometry (InSAR), has revolutionized the measurement of ice velocity over
glaciers and ice sheets. While InSAR offers high accuracy and resolution, it faces signifi­
cant challenges, notably in phase unwrapping. Phase unwrapping errors, mainly arising
from decorrelation due to temporal changes, topographic effects, and large velocity gra­
dients, significantly affect the accuracy of ice velocity measurements. Traditional InSAR
implementations, which rely on pixel masking based on coherence thresholding or manual
inspection, are often inadequate in reliably detecting these errors, especially in complex
ice flow regions [1].
This thesis seeks to address the critical challenge posed by phase unwrapping errors
in InSAR ice velocity measurements. The work presented here revolves around develop­
ing and evaluating an Artificial Intelligence (AI) algorithm designed specifically to detect
InSAR phase unwrapping errors, by segmenting erroneous regions in processed InSAR
data. This is particularly crucial for ensuring the integrity and reliability of ice velocity data,
which is vital for understanding and monitoring glacier dynamics and climate change im­
pacts. In Chapter 2, the theoretical background is provided, focusing on the core concepts
of InSAR and neural networks. This chapter lays the groundwork for understanding the
methodologies used in the study, particularly those related to InSAR data processing, as
well as the general theory of neural networks, as well as convolutional neural networks,
and their applicability to segmentation tasks in detailed imagery like InSAR data. Chap­
ter 3 outlines the model development strategy. It starts with the initial model architecture
and proceeds to describe the modifications and testing phases. This chapter is key to
understanding the progression from the initial AI model design to its refined versions,
providing insights into the iterative process of AI model development tailored for InSAR
unwrapping error detection. In Chapter 4, the simulation of phase unwrapping errors is
discussed. This includes the methodology used, the data considerations, and specific
examples of simulated phase unwrapping errors. This chapter is crucial for comprehend­
ing the challenges in simulating phase unwrapping errors, in order to make a suitable
training data set for AI model development. Chapter 5 delves into the progressive de­
velopment and fine­tuning of the AI model. It covers the data preparation process, the
optimization of training, and the impact of additional input layers and training set sizes.
This chapter highlights the practical aspects of enhancing the AI model’s accuracy and
reliability in detecting InSAR unwrapping errors. Chapter 6 presents an analysis of the
model’s performance. It includes a detailed examination of the model’s effectiveness in
different scenarios, specifically focusing on datasets from two relevant regions, namely
the North East Greenlandic Ice Sheet (NEGIS) and Hagen Isbræ, as these regions are
prone to phase unwrapping errors due to low coherence areas [1]. Comparative analyses
of model iterations across these datasets provide empirical evidence of the model’s capa­
bilities and limitations in real­world applications. Finally, Chapter 7 concludes the thesis,
summarizing the key findings, implications, and potential future directions for research in
the field of AI­assisted detection of InSAR unwrapping errors.

Theoretical Background
In this chapter, the theory behind the approaches used in this study is described. The
first section focuses on InSAR, and provides the formulations and terms relevant in order
to carry out simulation and modelling tasks based on InSAR data. The second section
delves into the general theory of neural networks, and which networks are suitable for
segmentation tasks in detailed images such as InSAR data.
2.1
InSAR
In this section, a brief introduction to the concept of SAR will be introduced, followed
by a presentation of the techniques and formulations regarding InSAR relevant for this
study, including interferometry, phase unwrapping and the errors that occurs in phase
unwrapping.
2.1.1
SAR
Synthetic aperture radar is a specific radar technology used to create high resolution im­
ages of the Earth’s surface. SAR images are indifferent to day and night conditions, as
well as weather, which makes it suitable for a wide range of applications such as geo­
science, climate change research, mapping and change detection [2].
A SAR generates images by sending out a radar pulse and measuring the reflected sig­
nal. The ’synthetic aperture’ refers to the fact that SAR utilizes the motion of the antenna
along­track over a target region to effectively increase the antenna size, which in turn
creates a finer resolution of the radar image.
The satellite trajectory is called the azimuth direction, and the other direction is called
range, which in terms of SAR with a side­looking acquisition is a slanted range due to
the look angle, called slant­range. Furthermore, the region illuminated by the radar pulse
is called the footprint, and the width in the range direction of the acquisition is called the
swath. The images acquired for InSAR are called Side Looking Complex (SLC) images,

effectively reducing noise through local averaging. As a result, the fringes become more
visible, although the number of pixels and hence the spatial resolution decreases after
multilooking.

Phase Unwrapping
In order to create deformation and velocity maps that extend beyond one full phase cycle,
the wrapped phase undergoes a process called phase unwrapping. Phase unwrapping
is essential to retrieve the actual phase differences from a wrapped interferogram, where
the phase is confined within a full phase cycle of ±π. An example of successful phase
unwrapping for a 1D case is shown in fig. 2.4.

2.2.2
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a type of NN which is primarily used for pat­
tern recognition in image data. A key difference between a CNN and a standard NN, is
the way the network organizes neurons into three dimensions: height, width and depth,
where the depth dimension corresponds to the third dimension of an activation volume
[7]. As an example: An input image of dimension 64x64x3, where the depth dimension of
3 corresponds to e.g. three color channels, could result in a final output with dimension
1x1xn, where n is the number of possible classes. The overall architecture of a CNN is
illustrated in fig. 2.10a, and comprises of convolutional layers, pooling layers and fully­
connected layers.
Convolutional layers are the main building blocks of a CNN. They perform convolutions by
applying kernels to the input data, which extract spatial hierarchies and features such as
edges, textures and other complex patterns. The parameters in the convolutional layers
consists of learnable kernels which are small in size but are applied to the full depth of
the input volume. An illustration of a convolutional layer is depicted in fig. 2.10b.
Pooling layers are used to reduce the spatial dimensions in width and height of the in­
put for the next convolutional layer. This is done by downsampling the data, which in
return reduces the computational complexity and provides an abstracted form of the rep­
resentation. Common types of pooling is max­pooling, which picks the maximum value in
a kernel, and average­pooling that averages the values in the kernel [7].
Fully connected layers resemble those in a standard NN and are often found near the
end of a CNN. Each neuron is connected to all the activations of the previous layer, and
interprets the features extracted by convolutional and pooling layers to make the final
output prediction [7].

2.2.3
Optimization Techniques in Neural Networks
An important process with neural networks is iterative optimization (training) of the model
weights. This process involves two main components: The backpropagation algorithm
which includes the concept of gradient descent, and the backpropagation algorithm will
be described in this section.
Backpropagation
Following the same terminology from the examples in section 2.2.1, the process of back­
propagation can be described as follows:
1. General Scenario: The weights in the network are initially set to small random
numbers [6]. During training, each example is forward­propagated through the net­
work, and the output is compared with the target vector. The difference informs how
weights should be modified.
2. Gradient Descent: This technique is used to adjust the weights to minimize the
mean square error. The goal is to find the set of weights that correspond to the
global minimum of the error function, which can be visualized as a landscape with
valleys representing local minima as seen in fig. 2.11.
3. Weight Update Formulas: An updating algorithm is then applied to update the
weights based on the gradient descent.
4. Procedure: The training process involves iterating over these steps, adjusting the
weights based on the computed gradients until a termination criterion is met.
Backpropagation aims to minimize the error in the network’s output by adjusting the weights
in a direction that reduces the overall error. The learning rate plays a crucial role in the
extent of weight modification, with typical values being less than 0.1 [6].

2.2.4
Regularization Techniques
Regularization is a technique in optimization problems that involves adding a regulariza­
tion term to the original error function. This technique adds a penalty against complexity
of the original error function to improve the stability of the solution. Regularization is cru­
cial in managing the trade­off between fitting the training data accurately and maintaining
a model simple enough to generalize well to new data [8].

to smaller and more manageable weights [8].

2.2.5
U­Net
U­Net is a deep learning model architecture proposed by Ronneberger et al. in 2015 [9],
which was specifically designed to effectively segment biomedical images. U­Net showed
a unique ability to train on small data sets of 30­40 images with a resolution of 512x512
pixels, and achieve a high solution accuracy [9]. This makes U­Net especially useful in
situations where training data is sparse.
The U­Net architecture, which is depicted in fig. 2.13, is named after it’s characteristic
symmetrical ’U’ shape. The ’U’ shape consist of two main paths: A contracting path and
an expansive path. The contracting path resembles that of a typical CNN, which focuses
on capturing the condensed features in the image. The architecture employs a series of
3x3 kernels for filtering, and 2x2 max­pooling for down­sampling in the contracting path.
The expansive path conversely enables precise location, when the small resolution fea­
tures are up­sampled to a higher resolution image for a detailed segmentation [9]. This
up­sampling (or up­convolution) is a distinct feature for the U­Net in contrast to a normal
CNN.
Another distinctive aspect of U­Net is the ’copy and crop’ feature, which involves copying
features from the contracting path and concatenating them with the up­sampled feature
map. This is necessary due to the loss of border pixels in every convolution, and also
ensures that the network retains important contextual information and localization [9]. An
example of a biomedical image segmentation of neuronal structures is shown in fig. 2.14.

while a low rate may result in slow training.

3.2.1
Learning Rate and Early Stop
After the initial model architecture and configurations are set, the first parameter to deter­
mine is the learning rate.
The learning rate, denoted as α in the optimization algorithm, is a critical hyperparam­
eter that determines the size of the steps the optimizer takes during the training of a deep
learning model. This parameter significantly influences the model’s ability to converge
to a good solution. If α is set too high, the model’s parameters may fluctuate erratically,
potentially causing the optimization process to diverge or oscillate around the minimum.
Conversely, a learning rate that is too low may lead to very slow progress, causing the
training to take an impractically long time or stall completely before reaching the optimal
solution [13].

3.2.2
Residual Connections
Subsequent to determining the learning rate, the incorporation of residual connections
is explored as a means to counteract two commen issues in deep neural networks: the
vanishing gradient problem and the degradation problem.
The vanishing gradient problem is characterized by gradients that diminish in magnitude
as they are backpropagated through the layers, leading to negligible updates in the net­
work’s earlier layers and, consequently, poor learning outcomes.
Conversely, the degradation problem is an unexpected decrease in training accuracy
as network depth increases. This decline is not attributed solely to data drift or model
complexity but is a distinct phenomenon where model performance unpredictably dete­
riorates over time, even with stable underlying data. Some models can remain effective
over extended periods, while others may suddenly exhibit increased error and variability,
independent of data changes [16].
Residual connections solve the problem of training deeper neural networks by enabling
the training of layers to learn residual functions with reference to the layer inputs [17].
As depicted in fig. 3.1, a residual block consists of a shortcut that bypasses one or more
layers. In practice, the input x is added to the output of the weight layers, creating an
identity shortcut that allows gradients to flow directly through the network. This design
mitigates the vanishing gradient problem and enables the training of very deep networks.
The identity mappings help not only in forwarding the input to deeper layers without at­
tenuation but also in the backward flow of gradients during training, making it possible to
train much deeper networks by addressing the degradation problem effectively.

3.2.4
Batch Normalization
Batch Normalization (BN) is a technique to accelerate the training of deep neural net­
works and improve their performance. This method addresses a fundamental issue in
the training of deep networks, known as the ”internal covariate shift”. By normalizing
layer inputs, BN makes the training process more efficient and stable, enabling the use of
higher learning rates and reducing the dependency on careful parameter initialization [19].
Internal covariate shift refers to the change in the distribution of network activations dur­
ing training due to changes in the network weights. This shift complicates the training
process which makes it slow [19]. The training of the network is done in batches of the
training data, also called mini­batches, due to computational resource limits. This means,
that a random subset of the training data is used for each training epoch. BN incorporates
a normalization step over the mini­batch that fixes the means and variances of the layer
inputs, making the network less sensitive to the scale of the parameters and reducing the
need for careful weight initialization.
BN improves the gradient flow, and has shown remarkable results in various experiments,
including image classification tasks, where model accuracy was improved [19]. Thus, BN
is implemented in the model development.

work, during each training iteration, effectively creating a different ”thinned” network at
each step. This stochastic regularization technique encourages the development of more
robust features. An illustration of a neural network with it’s corresponding dropout imple­

4.2.2
Data Pre­Processing
This section describes the preliminary data processing before the simulation algorithm
can take place. The simulation algorithm takes as input the coherence data from a pre­
liminary interferometric post processing using the IPP described in section 4.1.3, where
the original SLCs from the NEGIS dataset described in section 4.2.1, have been multi­
looked by a factor of 4 and 20 in the azimuth and range direction respectively.
The coherence files from the IPP are stored in a binary format, with each complex data
point consisting of a pair of 32­bit floating­point numbers representing the real and imagi­
nary parts. The data is arranged in a row­major order, meaning that each row of complex
numbers is stored sequentially. Moreover, the floating­point values are encoded in a big­
endian byte order, where the most significant byte (the highest positional value) of each
number is placed first in the file. This information, together with the knowledge of the
coherence matrix shape in rows and columns, corresponding to azimuth and range re­
spectively, are used to reshape the binary files into interpretative matrices.
A high and low coherence example from the 2019 NEGIS dataset is used to demonstrate
the simulation processes through this chapter, and the same examples will be used in
Chapter 6 and 7 as well for comparison. The high coherence example is derived from the
SLC image pair captured between February 10th and February 16th, 2019. Conversely,
the low­coherence example is taken from the SLC image pair acquired between January
5th and January 11th, 2019. The reshaped initial high and low coherence matrices re­
trieved from the initial IPP processing are shown in fig. 4.5a and fig. 4.5b respectively.
In order to make the simulation and later steps more effective, and the calculations less
computationally heavy, a 1500 x 1500 pixel square area of the initial coherence matrix
from the downstream part of NEGIS, which is known to exhibit features associated with
phase unwrapping errors, such as high levels of shear, ice­bedrock transitions, and fre­
quent loss of coherence [1], are cropped and used for further processing. The coherence
matrices for both low and high coherence examples are cropped from the initial coher­
ence matrices. Specifically, they include rows 1500 to 3000 and columns 0 to 1500.
These cropped matrices are depicted in fig. 4.6a and fig. 4.6b respectively. It is noticed
that the first columns from the left contains dark pixels, which correspond to Not a Number
(NaN) values which have been exchanged with the specific value 1×10−20 during the IPP
processing.
To prepare the data for the simulation accurately reflecting the full­resolution SLC, the
cropped coherence matrices undergo 2D bilinear interpolation with a factor of 4 and 20
for the rows and columns respectively. This process involves a linear interpolation first
in one direction (e.g., along columns) and then in the other direction (e.g., along rows),
effectively estimating pixel values on a regular grid from the multilooked lower­resolution
data. The resulting interpolated matrices serve as the input for the subsequent model sim­
ulation steps, and the interpolated cropped high and low coherence examples are shown
in fig. 4.7a and fig. 4.7b respectively.
The handling of flat is similar to how the initial coherence was handled, as the file is
saved in a similar fashion. Likewise, EVR undergoes comparable processing, but with an
additional step unique to EVR. Post­IPP processing, the EVR data exhibit a reduction of
two rows and columns. To address this reduction and attain the target matrix size of 6000
rows and 30000 columns, the EVR data are padded with NaN values around the edges.

Progressive Model Development and
Fine­Tuning
In this chapter, the stages of developing a neural network model specialized in segmen­
tation of erroneous phase unwrapping regions are described. Beginning with the foun­
dational steps of data preparation and the initial model architecture, progressing to the
modifications that gradually optimize the model performance, and concluding with a sum­
mary of the main results during the development.
5.1
Data Preparation for Model Development
From the simulation stage described in chapter 4, three collections of data matrices from
the 2020 data described in section 4.2.1, are retrieved and prepared for the training of
the model: Coherence, unwrapped phase and the threshold­masked errors after median
removal.
5.1.1
Model Inputs
The coherence and unwrapped phase matrices serve as the two inputs for our model. The
coherence matrices are the cropped coherence from the initial IPP processing, which was
described in section 4.1.3, and an example of high and low coherence is shown in fig. 5.1a
and fig. 5.1b respectively. Coherence, which was described in section 2.1.2, is chosen
as the main input for the model, as phase unwrapping is particularly challenging in areas
with alternating high and low coherence, and unreliable phase unwrapping is present in
areas with low coherence [27], which can lead to unwrapping errors.
The unwrapped phase matrices, which is seen in fig. 4.12, are used as a supplementary
input to the coherence as it is a product of the interferometric processing that indicates
which areas have been unwrapped. The unwrapped phase data is processed by replac­
ing NaNs with 0 and the unwrapped phase with 1, which produces a binary masked input
that guides the algorithm to focus only on detecting unwrapping errors in areas that have
been unwrapped. The resulting unwrapped phase mask for the high and low coherence
examples are shown in fig. 5.1c and fig. 5.1d respectively.
The ground truth labels are based on the simulated errors, where the median has been
subtracted, and afterwards the errors between ­1.5 and 1.5 [m/yr] have been removed
as well. The threshold of ± ­1.5 [m/yr] is chosen as a conservative limit which excludes
the errors that lie within ± a full phase cycle, and this was described in greater detail in
section 4.1.4. The thresholded masked errors with the median removed, where exam­
ples of these are seen to the right in fig. 4.14, are converted into binary masks, with NaNs
being replaced with 0, and 1 everywhere else, which will give us a binary masked ground
truth label matrix indicating the areas in which the phase unwrapping errors occur. The
corresponding ground truth masks from the high and low coherence examples are shown
in fig. 5.1e and fig. 5.1f respectively.

5.1.2
Data Split
The processed data is saved with corresponding ID tags between the three collections to
make sure the training inputs and the ground truth match, and is then split into a training
set, validation set and small test set. The size of the data set depends on the available
SLC pairs for a year, with a baseline of 6 days, and that the quality of the SLC pairs were
sufficient for interferometric processing. The main data set used for the model develop­
ment is the 2020 set, and it consists of 61 matching file sets of inputs and ground truth
masks. The training set consists of 70% of the total set size, which is floored to the near­
est integer, the validation set consists of 20% of the total set size floored to the nearest
integer, and the test size consists of the rest.
trainsize = ⌊0.70 × totalsize⌋
= ⌊0.70 × 61⌋= 42
valsize = ⌊0.20 × totalsize⌋
= ⌊0.70 × 61⌋= 12
testsize = totalsize −trainsize −valsize
= 61 −42 −12 = 7
(5.1)
A larger portion of the data is dedicated to training to allow the model to learn better
while separate validation and test sets ensure robust evaluation and avoid overfitting.
The validation set is not directly trained upon, but is used to evaluate and set check­
points during the training, and the small test set can then be used after training for a brief
independent analysis. The data split is randomly selected, due to the fact that the quality
of SLC pairs can vary greatly from one baseline pair of 6 days, to another. Additionally, a
general test set is created, comprising 7 matching file pairs from the 2019 and the 2018
data, for a total of 14 matching file pairs. This set, hereafter referred to simply as the
”test set,” offers a consistent and independent model evaluation for each modification.
The prepared data sets gets processed in batches in order to manage computational
resources efficiently, and due to the large size of each matrix, the batch size is set to 2.
5.2
Training Optimization
During the progressive development of the deep learning model, validation loss is em­
ployed as the primary evaluation metric. Later in this section, visual illustrations of the
model outputs for a couple of test examples will be assessed qualitatively, and a quanti­
tative analysis with statistical metrics of these model outputs will be described in chapter 6.
The optimal model is identified and preserved at the point where the minimum of the val­
idation loss curve is observed. Consequently, it is imperative that the learning rate (LR),
delineated in section 3.2.1, is established initially in the developmental phase. This allows
for consistent comparison of training outcomes across different experimental runs. When
analyzing the loss curves, attention must also be paid to potential signs of overfitting, indi­
cated by a divergence between training and validation loss, and persistent fluctuations in
the loss curves during later training epochs may indicate the model’s overfitting to noise
within the training data, reducing its ability to generalize to unseen data. The gap be­
tween the lowest training and validation loss can provide insight into how well the model
generalizes. A small gap suggests good generalization, whereas a large gap suggests
the model may be memorizing the training data. Furthermore, the epoch corresponding
to the best validation loss indicates how effective the training is.

in section 4.1.3, and an example of its raw form was displayed to the right in fig. 4.9.
The inclusion of external range velocity data into the AI model provides a complementary
physical measurement that enriches the input feature set. This additional context can
enhance the model’s ability to discern underlying phase variations due to surface motion
from those resulting from unwrapping errors. Moreover, the external range velocity offers
a coarse baseline against which the unwrapped phase can be compared. This is par­
ticularly useful in regions where the coherence is low, and the phase information is less
reliable, allowing the model to maintain high detection sensitivity without being misled by
noise.
The masked EVR from the simulation is retrieved and integrated with the additional input
layers, where NaN values are replaced with the specific value 1 × 10−20, for consistency
with the NaN handling of coherence. The original EVR values are velocities up to ±
several hundred [m/yr], but due to the nature of the sigmoid activation functions in the
network, mentioned in section 2.2.1, values between 0 and 1 are preferred for consis­
tent learning between all inputs. Hence, the EVR data is processed through a modified
min­max normalization, with the maximum value being 1000 [m/yr] to make sure the most
severe unwrapping errors are taken into account. The process is as follows:
1. Normalization: Each value in the EVR data is divided by 1000 to bring it to a more
manageable scale.
EV Rnorm = EV R
1000
(5.2)
2. Shift Calculation: The code identifies if there are any negative values in the normal­
ized data.
shift = min(0, min(EV Rnorm))
(5.3)
3. Adjusting for Negative Values: If negative values are present, the entire data set
is shifted upwards so that the smallest value becomes 0. This ensures all values are
non­negative.
EV Rprocessed = EV Rnorm −shift
(5.4)

at threshold 0.5, and for the critical task of detecting unwrapping errors, the priority given
to recall by the F2 score is more aligned with the operational requirements.
This prioritization is not just a theoretical consideration but is grounded in the practical
implications observed in the model’s performance. The visual and quantitative analyses
across the thresholds guides towards a threshold selection that maximizes recall with­
out excessively compromising precision, ensuring a comprehensive detection of potential
unwrapping errors.
When the performance of various model iterations are compared in the last section of this
chapter, the F2 score provides a crucial metric for evaluating and determining the most
effective threshold for this application.

This section presents a comparative analysis of the various model iterations described

Threshold Adjustments
• NEGIS: Threshold values varied across model iterations, with the models with high­

• Hagen: Similar to NEGIS, threshold fluctuations were significant, but with the final

• NEGIS: A general upward trend in accuracy and IoU, particularly with the final mod­

• Hagen: Contrasting NEGIS, accuracy and IoU peaked in middle iterations, particu­

F2 Score Dynamics
• NEGIS: An increasing trend in the F2 score with model complexity, highlighting en­
hanced inclusive error detection.
• Hagen: The F2 score did not consistently improve with model complexity, indicat­
ing a nuanced relationship between model enhancements and recall capabilities
between the two datasets.

• NEGIS: The introduction of high dropout rates negatively impacted performance,
while the final model with EVR and a larger dataset showed the best results.
• Hagen: Dropout levels had varied effects, but in general, the dropout modifica­

in contrast to the NEGIS dataset, where dropout resulted in the lowest F2 scores.
Furthermore, the most complex models yielded the worst performance, suggesting
over­fitting to the training set with increased model complexity.
General Observations
• Model Generalization: The divergence in model performance on the two datasets
underscores the challenge of creating a universally optimal model. While enhance­
ments like EVR and expanded datasets significantly improved performance on NEGIS,
they did not translate to similar gains on Hagen.
• Threshold Strategy: The variance in optimal thresholds between datasets high­
lights the importance of evaluating the output from the model on the specific dataset,
in order to determine the best classification threshold.

7
Conclusion
This thesis presents a comprehensive study on the development and evaluation of an AI
algorithm designed to detect InSAR phase unwrapping errors. An algorithm for simulat­
ing phase unwrapping errors was developed. This involved creating a simulated InSAR
dataset for the training of an AI model. The simulation was based on the generation of
SAR SLC image pairs, with per­pixel coherence levels provided by real datasets, and mo­
tion contributions based on average ice velocities, projected onto the radar line­of­sight.
A U­Net based AI model was successfully evolved through various stages, integrating en­
hancements such as residual connections, rotation augmentations, batch normalization,
dropout variations, learning rate scheduling, multiple inputs, and an expanded dataset.
Each modification was rigorously tested, leading to a model with a low error in the es­
timated probabilities of erroneous unwrapped phase regions. The model’s performance
was extensively analyzed across various thresholds of the probability output, highlighting
the critical balance between precision and recall. The F2 score, which emphasizes recall,
was particularly vital in scenarios demanding the minimization of false negatives, crucial
in the context of ensuring the integrity of phase unwrapping error detection. The model’s
generalizability was tested on two different datasets from two different regions ­ NEGIS
and Hagen Isbræ. While the model displayed consistency in its precision­recall trade­off
across thresholds, it also revealed the necessity for a nuanced threshold approach when
applying the model to diverse InSAR landscapes. The model demonstrated higher pre­
cision at higher thresholds at the cost of recall, a pattern observed in both datasets. A
comparative analysis on the NEGIS and Hagen datasets of the performance of the model
at different development stages was conducted, pointing out modifications, which aid in
model generalization.
Limitations and Future Work
Dataset Sensitivity: The model showed varying levels of performance on the NEGIS and
Hagen datasets, indicating a sensitivity to specific dataset characteristics. This suggests
a need for further research into model robustness and adaptability across diverse InSAR
data environments, which could include a more diverse training dataset with varied rela­
tionships between input data such as EVR and coherence.
Overfitting in Complex Models: The most complex model configurations, particularly with
the Hagen dataset, indicated potential overfitting to the NEGIS dataset. The dropout
modification added during the development stage decreased the performance of model
estimations on the NEGIS dataset, but had the opposite effect on the Hagen dataset,
which indicates that dropout could have a potential beneficial value for a more general­
ized model. Future work should explore strategies to mitigate this, possibly through further
experimentation with dropout rates, or the addition of other enhanced regularization tech­
niques.
Computational requirements: Due to the detailed nature of high resolution SAR data,
the training of the model on the datasets are computationally heavy.
A resolution of
1500x1500 for each set of inputs resulted in training sessions containing only 2 input sets
(batches) per training epoch, due to the memory requirements when handling datasets of
this size for training deep neural networks. The small batch size can induce higher fluctu­
ations in training loss curves, due to the relatively small representational quality of a small
sample size. Furthermore, a small batch size can also invalidate statistical optimization

modifications, such as batch normalization, due to the small sample size. Future work
could include experiments with coarser resolutions, allowing for increased batch sizes.
Additionally, utilizing more advanced computational hardware could enable the process­
ing of larger batches, mitigating the issues posed by memory constraints and supporting
more robust training.
In conclusion, this thesis investigated a promising approach for the field of InSAR phase
unwrapping error analysis, paving the way for more accurate, efficient, and reliable de­
tection of unwrapping errors through AI algorithms. The findings and methodologies de­
veloped provide a solid foundation for future research and practical applications in this
domain.
