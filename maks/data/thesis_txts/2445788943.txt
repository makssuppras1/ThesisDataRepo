ABSTRACT
In 2018 the global CO2 emissions have exceeded the record high amount of 37.1 billion
tonnes, setting an increase of over 2.7 % compared to the plateau 2014-2016 [1]. These
are very alarming data that show how the Paris agreement is struggling to be actuated.
California has set the ambitious goal to be economy-wide carbon neutral by 2045. The
electricity sector accounts for 16 % of the total CO2 emissions [2]. Solar generation
accounted for the 11.79 % of the overall in state generation in 2017 and the solar industry
is in constant expansion [2]. Moreover, a signiﬁcant amount of the solar generation is
produced by behind the meter (BTM) PV panels. The variability of solar generation
combined with the unobservability of BTM installations is challenging power systems
operators on several fronts. This thesis aims to address two of these challenges:
• Solar disaggregation
• Short term solar forecasting
In the case of BTM PV generation, power system operators have only access to the metered
net load at the individual customer site. In order to accurately plan how to manage their
grid assets and perform real time operations such as voltage regulation and switching, they
need to have access to the solar generation both historically and in real time. Installing
more sensors would be prohibitively expensive. This thesis expands the proposed method in
[3] to separate the solar generation from load consumption at the individual customer level
using only existing AMI measurements and solar proxies, such as irradiance or monitored
solar generation at a speciﬁc site in the same region. The disaggregation is performed both
historically and real time.
Furthermore, the increased solar penetration is challenging power system operators to
manage the variability of power generation, especially during ramp events (rapid and
signiﬁcant changes in solar generation). In order to book the correct amount of ancillary
services, power system operators need accurate forecasts of renewable energy generation.
In the third chapter of this work diﬀerent models are developed and compared against
each other to deliver accurate solar forecasts. Finally, a method to directly estimate the
uncertainty on the solar forecast is proposed and evaluated.

1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background and motivation
For many years the grid operators have been running the power system reliably keeping
three main concepts in mind:
• Demand and Supply of electricity must match at each point in time
• The electricity demand is very inelastic
• Power plants can be dispatched and their level of generation can be controlled
Nowadays, only the ﬁrst principle is still valid. The power system is facing a great revolution
in terms of how it is run, who are the agents involved and how they behave. The main
concern of power system operators is to run the power system reliably, minimizing the
cost of the energy supply. In order to do so, the ﬁst principle has to hold at each point
in time, otherwise dangerous and detrimental power outages and blackouts may occur,
causing potentially enormous ﬁnancial losses.
The advent of renewable energy sources has drastically changed the third principle.
Renewable energy resources are variable by nature and intermittent.
They are hard
to control and cannot be turned on if the natural resource is absent. No electricity can
be produced by a photovoltaic (PV) panel is there is no sun nor by a wind turbine if the
wind is not blowing. This increased variability on the generation mix is aﬀecting power
system operations reliability in many ways, raising the overall uncertainty. The two ﬁrst
countermeasures that have been adopted so far are: curtailment and increase in the amount
of ancillary services booked. The ﬁrst one consists in curtailing part of the renewable
energy generation when it overpasses a certain threshold to avoid large ﬂuctuations or
steep ramps in the generation supply. The second one consists of booking more energy
and power reserves to ensure to be able to successfully overcome a large unexpected loss
in generation or major fault. The latter strategy is strictly correlated to the ability to
accurately forecast the variable generation and quantify the level of uncertainty associated
with that. Curtailing renewable energy and overbooking ancillary services is deﬁnitely non
optimal and costly. It is commonly acknowledged that improving the forecasts is the ﬁrst
step to accomplish in this direction.
Renewable energy resources have added an ulterior characteristic at the energy mix: PV
panels can be installed in the houses’ rooftops, creating distributed generation assets. The
power system topology has been built under the main key assumption that the energy ﬂows
from the big power plants to the ﬁnal users in a radial conﬁguration. Distributed energy
resources are changing this historic paradigm by introducing reverse power ﬂow for certain
levels of solar penetration. Moreover, the majority of these systems are not monitored,
meaning that the distribution system operator has no visibility on their generation proﬁle.
1
==PAGE:16==
1. Introduction
The only information that is accessible is the net load, i.e. the algebraic sum of generation
and electricity consumption. This is creating many operations challenges that will be
addressed in this work. Furthermore, consumers are transitioning to become prosumers,
agents who can consume or produce electricity locally, depending on the availability of the
natural resource. Participation in demand response programs and the growing diﬀusion of
batteries and electric vehicles (EVs) is reinforcing this epochal paradigm shift, making the
energy sector one of the most challenging and sexy to work in this century.
1.2
Problem overview and thesis objective
This work focuses on solving two problems that aﬀect power system operations namely:
• Solar disaggregation
• Short term solar forecasting
In order to increase the ability to host new solar capacity at the distribution feeder level
and to perform power system operation such as switching and voltage management a
deeper level of visibility is required. Grid operators have only access to the aggregate
net load measurement at the single house level, that is the algebraic sum of electricity
consumption and generation. The objective of the ﬁrst part of this thesis is to separate the
aggregate signals into the two separate components improving already existing algorithms.
This objective is also known with the name of solar disaggregation and this name will
be used to reference it. Contextually supervised source separation is used as a general
framework to perform the signal processing task and an optimisation problem is cast to
provide the solution.
The second part of this work focuses on short term solar time series forecasting, up to 3
hours ahead. This information is really valuable to the grid operators in terms of deciding
which is the most cost eﬀective dispatch as well as how the reserves should be managed and
scheduled to cope with steep ramp events. A ramp event can be categorized as a large and
fast deviation in the electricity generation, due to a signiﬁcant variation in the intensity
of the primary resource. For example, if the level of solar penetration is very high, the
approaching sunset can cause a ramp event. Other weather events can also cause a major
variation in the solar output. The objective of this study is to compare the accuracy of
diﬀerent models as well as assess their complexity, advantages and disadvantages. Machine
learning models are compared to traditional ones and the impact of data preprocessing
methods is outlined. In the last bit of this work, the deterministic forecasting framework
is expanded to estimate the uncertainty around the forecast. This approach gives the
power system operator more information about the probability distribution of the solar
generation prediction.

2
SOLAR DISAGGREGATION
2.1
Overview
2.1.1
Problem
Thanks to the dramatic decrease in the manufacture and installation costs as well as to
the incentives schemes designed for this technology, rooftop solar photovoltaic (PV) panels
have largely proliferated in California. Most of these have been installed behind-the-meter
(BTM). For these systems the power system operator (PSO) does not have access to the
solar generation directly, but can only measure the net between the electrical load and PV
output. At the end of June 2018 the number of non-utility BTM rooftop solar installations
has reached 6,200 MW in the Californian ISO’s balancing area, with over 2,500 MW
installed since 2016.[8].
The lack of visibility of real time PV generation is an important issue. Fundamental
operations such as switching, state-estimation and voltage management needs this
information in order to be performed reliably and eﬀectively. [3] What is typically available
to the grid operators is the installed capacity of solar behind each meter, though in some
cases even the presence of BTM solar is unknown or the databases are not up to date or
there are some reporting errors. These diverse types of issues contribute on increasing the
uncertainty on estimating BTM solar generation.
In order to manage the grid reliably, the distribution system operator (DSO, in this work
interchangeably used with PSO) has to plan potential upgrades to the infrastructure
to meet increasing demand or improve reliability metrics and resiliency. The inability
of observing the real load can lead to planning errors. Moreover, unmonitored BTM
generation poses increasing challenges in load forecasting. The main four ones have been
individuated and reported in [9].
• Challenge 1
If PV panels, battery energy storage systems (BESS) or other distributed energy
resources are installed behind the meter, what is measured is the net between them
and the electrical load. This translates in the impossibility to exactly quantify the
contribution of each piece of equipment separately. The demand for electricity services
cannot be isolated. This issue has translated in reduced accuracy of day-ahead and
general short term load forecasting as the stochastic generation piece is hard to
predict. The challenge is therefore to create more accurate models that try to take
into account these other variables to deliver more accurate forecasts.
• Challenge 2
The relationship between load and weather temperature has become more uncertain.

models need to take into account cloud coverage too. Having two or more variables
makes it more complicated to uniquely deﬁne extreme weather events, especially if
the service territory includes diﬀerent weather regions.
In this work the attention will be focused on the DSO perspective. What is available
in the current metering infrastructure is the net load, that is to say the algebraic sum
between the BTM solar generation and the electrical load at the consumer connection
point. This information is usually referred to as advanced metering infrastructure
(AMI) data. The objective of this work is to disaggregate the net load into these two
signals to provide the DSO with the two separate time series. The disaggregation is
performed both historically (on past data) and real time. The former aims to provide
information to plan future upgrades whereas the latter is crucial for power system
operations such as switching, state estimation and voltage management.

2.1.2
Literature review
Solar disaggregation is quite a recent topic in the literature. For relatively low solar
penetrations power system operations can be run reliably without requiring much
visibility at the individual customers level. Over the course of the last few years,
the dramatic increase of BTM PV panels have stimulated the research community
to address this problem.
Most of the publications on this topic are in fact no
more than 2-3 years old. The two main approaches to perform this task can be
categorized in: model based and data-driven based. The former usually requires
more information about the system. A crucial input is the plane-of-array (POA) [10],
[11] irradiance which is obtained using solar-earth geometry models, information
about the geometry of the PV array, and on-site measurements of direct normal
irradiance (DNI), diﬀuse horizontal irradiance (DHI) and global horizontal irradiance
(GHI). Precise measurements of both DNI and DHI are costly to obtain, and usually
only GHI is directly measured and DHI and DNI estimated afterwards [12]. In the
case study reported in [12] the achieved average RMSE is approximately 10 % or
13 % depending on the application. A drawback of model-based methods is the
signiﬁcant amount of information needed which is typically unavailable to the power
system operator or costly to obtain. The data-driven approaches are divided into
two categories depending on the level at which the disaggregation is performed:
feeder or individual customer. In [13] the authors adapt contextually supervised
source separation to the solar disaggregation problem. The signals separation is
performed at the feeder level using only information already available from the
current infrastructure: SCADA measurements, solar proxies and weather data. The
proposed methodology achieves an RMSE of 6 % across all sky conditions [13]. [3] is
the main reference for this thesis and expands on the approach presented in [13] to
perform the solar disaggregation at the individual customer level, both historically
and real time. The proposed method is able to recover the solar generation proﬁle
with 15 minutes granularity with root mean square errors between 20 % and 50 %
of average daily PV generation. Translated in terms of RMSE relative to installed
capacity the numbers are close to 11 % on average. The proposed approach shows
the same performance both historically and in real time. In [14] the disaggregation
is performed at the individual consumer level. A clustering method is performed
to classify houses with and without BTM solar generation before injecting the data
into the main algorithm. Moreover, a consumer mixture model is used for the load

model instead of a hourly and temperature based one. In [15] the authors expand a
geometric model of behind-the-meter solar production with weather and AMI data,
leveraging fundamental relationships between location, weather, solar irradiance,

at creating a US database with houses with BTM solar using satellite images and
computer vision. The information coming out of this project can then be easily
retroﬁtted in almost every approach presented above.

The objective of this work is to ﬁrst autonomously reproduce the modelling framework

Fig 2.3 shows the system structure. It represents a classic radial feeder where many

2.2.3
Contextual information: load model and solar model
In this work it is assumed that there are some monitored PV systems in the vicinity of
the area of interest where the customers are located. We refer to these measurements
as solar proxies. Other information relevant for solar generation could be exploited
such as irradiance, etc. In this case each component Xi represents a solar proxy. In
general the monitored PV systems will have diﬀerent geometric properties, such as
tilt and orientation compared to the ones we want to reconstruct the signal for. As
a result there will be nonlinear errors translated into our model, as it is assumed
that the physical characteristics are unknown. These errors can be regarded as
“transposition errors” because they result from the transposition of the irradiance
beam from the proxy’s plane to the planes of the unobserved PV systems. However,
collecting more proxies should help the algorithm to ﬁnd a suitable base for the PV
signal of interest.
The aggregate load is mainly a function of the hour of the day and the outdoor
temperature. In fact, load patterns usually show a periodicity of 24 hours, in line
with the human cycle. At the same time heating, ventilation and air conditioning
accounts for approximately 40 % of the total residential load [18] justifying the
importance to take the outdoor temperature into account. The time of day (ToD)
dependency is included using a hour of day dummy variable, HOD ∈{0, 1}T,24 ,

Tuning α
As mentioned above, the α parameters have the purpose to weight how the information
of each model, load and solar, is taken into account to perform the signal separation.
An heuristic is presented in [3] to tune α assuming the power system operator
have access to the solar generation of at least one household, referenced as the
tuning system in the following. This system is used to estimate the uncertainty
of the aggregate load and solar models for each customer. The algorithm is quite
complicated and statistical heavy. The main steps are reported here. It is intuitive
that a good choice to weight each model would be the inverse of the expected standard
deviation of each model’s errors αi,t = Var (ϵi,t)−1/2 and αL,t = Var (ϵL,t)−1/2. The
problem is that this choice would require to observe the true signals which we do
not. The main idea behind the algorithm proposed in [3] is to ﬁrst estimate the error
variance of solar models at the tuning systems and then scale this variance to all
other systems using the coeﬃcients from the untuned optimization. Subsequently,

2.4.1.1
Aggregation eﬀect and weekday-weekend dependency
The formulation of the solar disaggregation problem cast above requires to estimate

It is intuitive that
aggregating more houses can improve the accuracy of the aggregated load model, as
the variations of diﬀerent agents tend to balance out more than at the individual level.
Figure 2.11 shows the boxplot of the MAPE for the load model as a function of the
number of houses. The average MAPE can be reduced signiﬁcantly by aggregating

validation across the full data set. Figure 2.11 shows that the average MAPE for
a single house is around 80 %. However, increasing from 5 to 160 houses the load
model error can be reduced from 32.5% to 14.5%. 160 customers is a more realistic
number for a substation. Utilities have conﬁrmed that the number of customers at
the same substation can easily be on the thousand range. Moreover, the original
load model has been extended including the weekday/weekend dependency. The
load model already implements HOD (hour of the day) as a regression term in

implemented with 48 (24 · 2) dummy variables, allowing 24 hours for weekday and

variable indicating if the day in examination is a weekday or weekend. The 48 hours
allow instead to have a totally diﬀerent load shape for weekdays and weekends. It
can be observed that the average MAPE is reduced to 13 % for 160 houses, using
the weekday/weekend formulation. Modelling weekday/weekend dependency did not
provide a signiﬁcant improvement, but it was deﬁnitely worth trying. These results
lead to trying to implement the same idea in the full solar disaggregation algorithm.

2.4.2
Sensitivity of the training size
In section 2.4.1.2 it has been shown that having a perfect load model can improve the
disaggregation performances of around 10 % in terms of CV, when a 10 days training
window is chosen. The objective of this section is to investigate what happens if
we increase the size of the training data. The idea is that increasing the training
size lets the model observe more instances of not correlated variations in the load
consumption and solar generation, leading to a better estimation of the capacity
installed at the solar houses. It has been found out that when the training size
is expanded to one year the advantage of having a perfect load model drastically
reduces to only 1 %. This is a very important result saying that when we increase
the training size to one year, even if we have a perfect load model, the maximum
reduction that can be expected on average is 1 % in terms of CV. This leads to
consider that the eﬀort should not be put in improving the load model, but more
on including more data points. In this analysis all the parameters have been ﬁxed
beside the training size. Nhouses = 85, Nproxies = 1, Ntunsys = 2. Moreover, a full
year of data has been considered and three diﬀerent optimization problems in terms

consumption if the solar generation is greater than the load, i.e. if the generated

installed smart meter around the world so this assumption should be checked.

Real time solar disaggregation
As mentioned in the introduction, the power system operator is interested in getting
access to the historical solar generation as well as to be able to estimate it in real
time. The streaming problem builds on top of the training one to achieve the latter
objective. In this section the mathematical formulation is presented along with the
main results.
The mathematical formulation of the streaming problem is in theory identical to
the historical training. The problem is that the AMI data are usually received by
the power system operator once a day. Therefore during the real time estimation
the algorithm cannot access this information. The optimisation problem for the
streaming task is cast ignoring constraint 2.10, that is enforcing the solar generation
to be lower than the net load, and assessing how diﬀerent is the performance of the
relaxed version. Moreover, the aggregate net load PN
i=1 Yi is also not available in
real time and constraint 2.8 is also aﬀected. However, the data upstream at the
substation level are typically sent to the power system operator every 4 seconds,
meaning that a pretty good estimate can be achieved in real time if the power losses
are taken into account. It was part of the original plan to develop a data-driven
model to map the SCADA measurement to the actual aggregate AMI netload at the
individual customers level. However, the data necessary to do it did not come on

The impact of neglecting constraint 2.10 is assessed performing the real time

Conclusion
In this chapter the solar disaggregation problem has been largely explored and
the algorithm proposed in [3] has been further validated and expanded. The main
outcomes of the analysis are reported following a bullet points structure.
• The tuning alpha routine showed to successfully remove nighttime generation
which was assigned to some solar houses and signiﬁcantly improve the
performances of the algorithm at non solar houses.
• A sensitivity analysis has then been performed. The results showed increased
performances with aggregating more houses up to 8 and slightly decreasing for
a higher level of aggregation.
• Increasing the number of days in the training period also proved to reduce
the error metrics, with 30 days the best compromise. Including a full year of
observations proved to have the same overall performances as 30 days, while
improving for customers with low signal to noise ratio (SNR).
• Having a perfect load model showed to reduce the error metric CV of around
10 % at solar houses if only 10 days were used for training. It had little to no
improvement when a full year of data was used.
• Having 2 proxies resulted to be the best choice. Additional proxies enrich the
diﬀerent cloud patterns and geometry conﬁgurations accessible to the model.
However, having more than 2 proxies resulted in a worse performance at non
solar houses.
• 15 minutes data resolution proved to convey the same performance as the hourly
one at houses with solar, while outperform the latter at houses without solar. If
using hourly data at non solar houses tuning the model enormously improve
the performances.
• The model proved to perform much better at the feeder level than at individual
customers, as expected. The average CV dropped from 37 % at the individual
level to 21.7 % at the feeder level.
• The alternative approach using the coeﬃcients of a linear model to predict the
solar generation proved to have the same average CV performance as the tune
model at solar houses. The tuned model does a better job at identifying non
solar houses instead. For large number of customers the linear model has a
signiﬁcantly lower computational burden.
• Clustering the individual sites based on the average daily load shape proved to
work very well for this data set with 94 % accuracy, 100 % precision and 90 %
recall.
• The estimated SNR (solar to noise ratio) proved to be an accurate proxy to
estimate the quality of the signal separation after having performed it. Sites
with lower SNR experienced a higher error. Intuitively, it is harder to extract
the true solar signal when the energy of this one is lower compared to the load
one.
• The streaming problem proved to perform as well as the training one for both
historical and future data. It has been pointed out that this could be an artifact
of this data set where the solar signature is fairly strong in most houses.

TIME SERIES SOLAR FORECASTING
3.1
Overview
3.1.1
Problem - why is solar forecasting important
Solar generation is intermittent, variable and hard to predict. Given the large and
increasing amount of PV systems installed in California, accurate forecasts of solar
panels’ electricity output have become crucial for the grid operator to reliably and
cost eﬀectively run the power system. It is worth refreshing that at each point in
time generation has to match demand of electricity. In order to be able to face
unexpected failures such as a generator or transmission line outage, the Independent
System Operator (ISO) has to book particular types of generators or other energy
resources as reserve. These participants constitute part of the ancillary services. In
general the more inaccurate is the forecast, the more ancillary services need to be
booked (and paid) to ensure enough reserve power. The general idea behind this
reasoning is that better forecasts can signiﬁcantly reduce the costs of energy supply.
It is beyond the scope of this work to recap on how electricity markets work, but
it is worth mentioning that power system operators are mostly interested in two
diﬀerent time scales for solar generation forecasting. The ﬁrst one is day ahead and
the second one is closer to real time. The day ahead forecast is useful to clear the
day ahead market and commit the generators to produce what they have agreed
on. The intraday forecasts are useful to optimize the dispatch of the already booked
generation resources to minimize the costs, taking into account the most recent
information. This work focuses more on the second type of challenge to help PSOs
better schedule the dispatch and foresee and act in case of solar ramp events. Ramp
events are those sharp and large changes in solar generation that occur in a very
short time.
Renewable energy generation is variable and uncertain. Deterministic forecasts are
helpful to run the power system reliably and cost eﬀectively, but are unable to
quantify the uncertainty around the point prediction. In the ﬁnal part of this work
an uncertainty estimation framework for solar power is explored, aiming to give the
power system operators a working tool to estimate the conﬁdence of the forecast and
make better decisions.
3.1.2
Literature review
Solar forecasting has been explored quite extensively in the literature,
as
comprehensive works such as [21] and [22] demonstrate. Almost all the papers
on this topic are categorized based on the time horizon they focus on. For intra-hour
33
==PAGE:48==

horizons the state of art is using total sky images to predict the cloud coverage and
movements [23]. For time horizons between 1-6 hours satellite images have been
used in diﬀerent works such as [24] and [25]. In order to predict further than 6
hours ahead numerical weather prediction models (NWP) are usually necessary. In
fact the cloud movements have to be modeled to get meaningful results [26]. While
intra-day solar power forecasting is mainly useful for the power system operator to
ensure grid stability and dispatch resources in the real time market, day-ahead solar
forecasting is mostly used for trading generation in the day-ahead electricity market.
Examples of work in this context can be found in [27] and its literature review. This
work diﬀerentiates from previous ones for two main aspects: the complexity of the
forecast and limitation of feature data. The selected forecast horizon is 3 hours with
5 minutes resolution. The variance of PV signals signiﬁcantly increases reducing the
temporal granularity making the 5 minutes resolution forecast a particularly hard
problem. Moreover most of previous works concentrated on one or two steps ahead,
performing multi-step ahead forecasts only on hourly or daily data [28]. In this work
a 36 steps ahead forecast is issued. The approach taken here is time-series based,
meaning that the only information used to issue the forecast is the time-series itself.
The 3 hours horizon ﬁts in between the temporal granularity where sky images and
satellite data represent the state of art. Satellite images are becoming more available
and easier to get access to and it is expected they will provide further improvement
to the forecasting models in the next future. However, these two extra inputs come
together with an increased computational burden to process and issue the forecast.
In the case of sky images, ﬁsh eye cameras are still quite expensive to manufacture
and maintain. The aim of this work is to extract as much information as possible
from the time-series data themselves with the belief that this setup would be easier
to scale for many PV systems in many diﬀerent regions.
3.1.3
Objective and scope deﬁnition
The problem that this work is aiming to solve is to provide the most accurate forecast
for the aggregate power of multiple PV systems located in a speciﬁc region, only using
the single sites’ generation time series. As mentioned above, 65 sites are considered
and the data are available with 5 minutes resolution. The objective is to predict the
aggregate solar generation for the next 3 hours with 5 minutes granularity. In other
words the setup is a 36-steps ahead forecast. Diﬀerent models have been ﬁtted and
evaluated to quantify the performances as well as the computational costs and model
complexity.
Persistence and ARIMA exploits the aggregate power generation time series to issue
the forecast. On the other hand k-nn regressor, neural networks and LSTMs exploits
the time series at each site to forecast the aggregate one. Finally the deterministic
forecast is expanded to the probabilistic framework for the neural network approach.
This part of the thesis is the continuation of pre-existent work performed as a ﬁnal
project [28].

The data used for this project have been provided by SunPower Corporation under
NDA. The dataset is part of a larger DOE-funded project called VADER (Visualisation
and Data Analytics for Distributed Energy Resources) [28]. The dataset consists
of all the PV sites in a speciﬁc region in South California. The data were raw and
unclean ﬁles. The ﬁrst step of the analysis is therefore to understand the quantity
and quality of the data provided by SunPower, in order to identify which sites can
be used. All the selected sites have at least two years of data and more than 100,000
non-null points in the considered window. 62 sites out of 218 were chosen with this
process. Some sites present multiple inverters associated with them making 73 the
total number of systems. Because of data errors 8 systems have been disregarded at
a later point. The input data to the forecasters are therefore the time series of each
one of the 65 systems.
3.2.2
Train-Dev-Test splitting
The ﬁnal dataset spans two years from 7/15/2015 to 7/14/2017 (mm/dd/yyyy). The
data have been chronologically split into training, development, and test sets, with
500 days (7/15/2015 - 11/25/2016), 110 days (11/26/2016 - 3/15/2017), and 121
days (3/16/2017 - 7/14/2017) respectively. Other choices could have been made,
moreover K-fold cross validation could have been applied to the full dataset to exploit
all the data and possibly reach the most unbiased estimate of the generalized error.
However, this was not the main focus of this work and while being aware of this
possibility another choice has been made in the interest of time. In fact, training
the most complex networks architecture is computationally expensive and a broader
exploration of the diﬀerent models was of more interest for this dissertation. Training,
development and test performances are all reported for completeness.

k-nearest neighbors algorithm (k-NN)
k-nn is one of the simplest yet commonly used machine learning algorithms. The
regression variant will be presented here, however it can be used for classiﬁcation

to the input X.

3.5.1
Clear sky model
As mentioned in section 3.4, it has been observed that preprocessing the raw data,
by removing the periodic trends before injecting them into the modelling algorithms,
could improve the overall accuracy. The idea is to ﬁt a statistical clear sky model to
the aggregate solar power and then predict the diﬀerence between the clear sky and
the aggregate solar power, instead of the original time series [28]. The objective of
the forecaster is now to understand how much the aggregate solar power diﬀer from
the clear sky signal. In the following, this time series will be called the "detrended
time series" to accentuate the fact that the periodic trends have been removed.
Clear sky conditions are generally deﬁned as the absence of visible clouds in a speciﬁc
location [7].
Clear sky models traditionally rely on atmospheric and geometric
modeling techniques. The statistical approach proposed in [7] has the advantage to
only use the observed power output time series without any assumption regarding
knowledge of weather, irradiance data, or system conﬁguration metadata, proving
to be robust to missing or incorrect data. The statistical clear sky ﬁtting (SCSF)
algorithm take the observed power output as input and estimates the clear sky power
output of the PV system over the course of the year.
The details of the algorithm can be found in [7]. Fig. 3.12 shows the output of
the SCSF algorithm compared to the observed power output. It can be visually
appreciated how the curve ﬁtting constitutes an upper bound of the measured power.
Two days are represented, one with some cloud coverage and the other one mainly
sunny.

3.6
Solar generation uncertainty estimation
Uncertainty estimation and probabilistic forecasting are becoming more and more a
need for all those players who have to face the variability of renewable generation
in their daily operations. These actors could be power system operators, energy
traders, aggregators, etc. In this section the deterministic forecasting framework
is adapted to generate a probability distribution around the single-point forecast.
Prediction intervals are generated and the main results are highlighted. Due to the
many topics that have been touched on this thesis there is still room to expand on
this one for future work. A probabilistic forecast gives the decision maker more
information compared to a single point one. In fact it is informative of what is
the probability distribution around the expected value. Optimisation frameworks,
such as stochastic optimisation, embrace the innate uncertain nature of the problem
they are meant to solve and optimise based on potential scenarios weighted by their
probability. It is not in the interest of this work to go into the details of stochastic
optimisation. However, if the uncertainty of the decision variables can be quantiﬁed
the solution given by this framework is more optimal than just optimising for the
expected value. The artiﬁcial neural network has been selected as the model to
implement the uncertainty estimation part.

3.6.1
Direct variance estimation
In the literature, several methods for indirect prediction intervals estimation are
reported and discussed. [32] and [33] are two very good references to further explore
this topic. Two of the most common approaches are the mean variance estimation
(MVE) and the bootstrap method. In the former two neural networks are trained
jointly to estimate both mean and variance of the prediction. In the latter Nb neural
networks are trained from the original resampled dataset to provide a more unbiased
estimate. The second part of the process is similar to the MVE method and a new
loss function is deﬁned. It would have been interesting to explore these two methods,
but the time available did not allow that.
In this section a method to directly estimate the variance of the solar forecast is
proposed. The core idea develops by observing that in the presence of clouds the
power output is much more volatile and diﬃcult to forecast. Therefore the idea to
model the uncertainty of the forecast by predicting the amount of variance expected
in the output. If we consider a one hour window, the higher the variance of the
detrended time series (ﬁgure 3.14) in this window, the harder it is to predict the solar
power in that window. Moreover, assuming that the errors are normally distributed
it is possible to quantify the uncertainty at each point in time and construct the
prediction intervals. Two neural networks are trained. The ﬁrst one is exactly the
same as described in section 3.4. The second network has a reduced structure. The
input are the last 5 hours at ﬁve minutes resolution of the aggregate time series and
the output is the variance of the signal one hour, two hours and three hours ahead.
In order to construct the output vector for training the network 3 windows of one
hour in length are created on the forecasting horizon. The variance of the signal in
those windows is calculated and constitutes the target variable. To sum up, the new
model should predict the variance of the aggregate solar power for the next three
hours, based on the observed signal over the last 5 hours. Equation 3.13 explains the
concept in mathematical terms. Time of day and day of the year are also included
as explanatory variables. Figure 3.25 shows the forecast against the actual power for
one hour ahead prediction. At the bottom of the plot the time series of the predicted
standard deviation over time is illustrated. Assuming that the errors follow a normal
distribution and choosing a 95 % conﬁdence interval, the upper and lower bound
are estimated. The method shows consistent results. When the day is clear, the
uncertainty on the forecast is almost zero and the prediction follows the actual power
very tightly. When the day is partly cloudy the uncertainty increases as soon as
more clouds patterns inﬂuences the solar power. This behaviour can be observed in
the last day presented in the plot, where the upper and lower bounds expand and
then close back together at the end of the day.

In this chapter many diﬀerent time series and machine learning based models have
been developed and tested against each other. The multi-step ahead forecasts span a
3 hours horizon with 5 minutes resolution. The feed-forward neural network and Long

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
In this work two important problems for power system operators have been presented
and studied, multiple solutions have been implemented and the results have been
shown and discussed. The conclusions on each individual problem, namely solar
disaggregation and short term solar forecasting, are highlighted at the end of the
respective chapters. The aim of this paragraph is to provide an overview on possible
future work.
Solar disaggregation:
The solar disaggregation problem has been studied in depth in this master thesis.
However, the data set available for this work was limited to 167 houses in
Austin, Texas.
While this has allowed researchers to validate their algorithms
and assumptions, it is believed that a much larger data set, including diﬀerent types
of customers and climate regions would be very beneﬁcial to extend the results
obtained in this master thesis and the cited research papers.
Short term solar forecasting:
The time series based modeling framework proved to give good results especially if the
detrended time series is fed to the machine learning models. The preprocessing step
using the statistical clear sky model reveled to signiﬁcantly improve the performances
of each forecaster. However, other inputs besides the time series itself are relevant for
solar forecasting. A very valuable extension of this work would be to include weather
information in the artiﬁcial neural network. Satellite images are becoming easier
to access and the recent developments in computer vision would deﬁnitely make it
easier to extract information from the images with a convolutional layer and then
merge that with the time series itself. This is believed to improve the accuracy at a
certain computational and data management cost.
Another interesting path to explore would be to expand on the uncertainty estimation
of the forecast and build a complete probabilistic forecasting framework.
The
continuous increase in solar generation will deﬁnitely require an uncertainty estimation
together with more accurate forecasts.
The power system is facing a very fast and disruptive transition and needs more and
more researchers and engineers to deliver eﬀective solutions to complicated problems.
