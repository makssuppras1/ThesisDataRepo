Abstract
In this pathfinder work, experiments were carried out to assess the
possibility of performing a C2 radical coupling between unprotected 2-
deoxyaldonic acids and terpenes in environmentally-friendly solvents. Ex-
periments were carried out using a blue light/Ir[dF(CF3)ppy]2(dtbpy))PF6/
quinuclidine system in acetonitrile/water with D-(+)-glucose as the sub-
strate. A multitude of olefins with varying electronic characters and sol-
ubilities were examined. Following a careful assessment of experimental
results and research, it was contrived that blue light/[Ir(dtbbpy)(ppy)2]PF6/
DABCO system in acetonitrile/water facilitates a reaction between sodium
acrylate and D-glucose.

1. Introduction
1.1
Photoredox catalysis
Photochemistry describes chemical transformations that occur from an
excited state by harnessing of photon energy. The range of wavelengths of
interest is limited between 200 and 1000 nm and the limits are determined
by practical considerations [1]. Photochemical transformations were likely
responsible for the synthesis of first organic molecules from basic gases
such as methane and ammonia, and are central for the evolution of life
from simple organic building blocks to the fascinating variety of life forms
found on Earth. Moreover, it is central in the photosynthetic process for the
biosynthesis of carbohydrates. As most organic molecules have a relatively
large band gap between ground and excited states, high-energy UV irradi-
ation is often employed to promote the excitation of an electron. However,
the use of high energy photons is rather problematic because it also pro-
motes side reactions.
The interest in synthetic organic photochemistry surged in the 1960s,
leading to a variety of useful transformations being discovered that fa-
cilitated the manufacture of compounds inaccessible by established het-
erolytic transformations [2][3]. In recent years, this field has experienced
a further increase in interest due to the maturation and adoption of pho-
toredox catalysts, LED technology that provides efficient, durable, high-
intensity monochromatic light sources of various wavelengths, and devel-
opment of photochemical reactor technology that promises to carry out
such transformations at production scale [1]. The use of photoredox cat-
alysts permits to cleave bonds and induce reactions by harnessing less-
energetic, visible light which—in combination with parallel evolution of
related technologies—has prompted chemists to completely rethink the
5
==PAGE:7==
assembly of organic molecules. Such transformations can be very con-
venient because organic molecules rarely absorb visible light, meaning
side-reactions are minimised. Furthermore, visible-light driven reactions
proceed efficiently at room temperature and have good functional group
tolerance, which permits coupling of the photoredox catalytic cycle with
hydrogen-atom-transfer or transition-metal catalytic cycles. Additionally,
it is possible to combine photoredox catalysis with biocatalysis, enantios-
elective catalysis, and electrocatalysis, thus allowing to form bonds using
a variety of unconventional coupling partners [1].
Moreover, photoredox catalysis permits efficient C-C bond formation
at hydridic C-H bonds, such as formyl, α-amino, or α-hydroxy [4]. This is
very synthetically useful because such reactivity facilitates further explo-
ration of the chemical space for manufacture of higher potency pharmaceu-
ticals, value-added compounds, and acquiring alternative synthetic routes
that can be more mild, efficient, and environmentally friendly. Moreover,
photoredox catalysis can be used to cleave bonds, such as C-C or C-O
bonds, or to oxidize/reduce functional groups. These qualities in com-
bination with the opportunity to harness sunlight is a promising way for
the chemical industry to adapt to the changing environment by developing
more efficient, sustainable, and less-polluting synthetic transformations.
There are many opportunities for research and development, but major
challenges in utilising sunlight have to be overcome. This includes the
polychromatic nature of solar light, the day/night cycles, the variable light
intensity, and diffuse nature caused by scattering and reflections [1].
Molecules that exhibit light-induced reactivity can generally be divided
into three classes: transition-metal chromophores (iridium and ruthenium
polypyridyl complexes), organic dyes (benzophenone, phenothiazine, acri-
dinium), and semiconductors (titanium, tungsten, or cadmium compounds).
Following photoirradiation, these compounds can exchange electrons (pho-
toredox catalysis), energy (photosensitisation), or hydrogen atoms (hydro-
gen atom transfer photocatalysis) [5]. The structural diversity offered by
transition-metal chromophores, organic dyes, and a wide array of semi-
conductor materials give rise to a diverse set of excitation lifetimes and
oxidation/reduction potentials [3]. As a consequence, photoredox catalytic
6
==PAGE:8==
systems have good potential to be optimised for a particular desired trans-
formation, and therefore have found many applications, such as traditional,
dye-sensitised, and organic solar cells, photochemical water splitting, or-
ganic synthesis of complex molecules, CO2 reduction and valorisation, and
degradation of organic pollutants in waste treatment.
The initiation of the catalytic cycle begins with promotion of electrons
from the HOMO to the LUMO, and photogeneration of holes in HOMO
[2]. The excited species often has a greater potential to participate in redox
reactions than the ground state species, with the excited electrons and holes
having certain reduction and oxidation potentials, respectively [6]. Follow-
ing photoirradiation, transition-metal chromophores generally undergo an
excitation of an electron from the split metal d-orbital (t2g orbital) to the
ligand π* orbital, which is termed metal-to-ligand charge transfer [7]. To
facilitate this transfer, the ligands are dominated by highly conjugated sys-
tems. Photoredox transformations often occur from such a singlet or triplet
metal-to-ligand or ligand-centered excited state [5].
In more detail, the initial excited state is generated by a vertical tran-
sition and has opposite-spin electrons in the HOMO and LUMO orbitals.
Thus, the intermediate attains a Franck-Codon non-minimal energy geom-
etry because the excitation happens on 10-15 s timescales and nuclear po-
sitions effectively do not change in that period of time [2]. Higher excited
states can also be populated, but the excited intermediate extremely rapidly
relaxes to the minimum energy geometry of the lowest singlet excited state
(S1) with release of excess energy to the solvent, which is termed as in-
ternal conversion or a non-radiative decay. As the last step, the S1 in-
termediate can undergo spin inversion by intersystem crossing to yield a
triplet excited state (T1) in which both unpaired electrons have the same
spin, or relax to the ground state (S0) either by fluorescence (radiative de-
cay) or dissipation of thermal energy (non-radiative decay) [2][5]. The
T1 state also adopts a new minimum energy geometry. In comparison to
the S1 states with lifetimes in the nanosecond range, the T1 states exist
in the milisecond range because decay to the S0 state must take place by
phosphorence, which is a spin-forbidden pathway. Therefore, redox trans-
formations occur from either of these states, with both being capable of
7
==PAGE:9==
electron or energy transfer to the substrate [3]. If intersystem crossing is
fast relative to reaction, reaction will occur through the T1 state. If reac-
tion is faster than intersystem crossing, the reaction will occur from the S1
state [2]. Additionally, photosensitizers such as organic dyes usually react
from the T1 state [2].
Following excitation by direct or sensitised energy transfer, the excited
electron or hole may participate in oxidative or reductive quenching cy-
cles [3]. Both cycles proceed by single electron transfer (SET) events
that reduce/oxidize the substrate, and depending on the net redox trans-
formation of the substrate, three classes of activation protocols exist: net-
oxidative, net-reductive, or redox-neutral [5][8]. During net-oxidative and
net-reductive reactions a stoichiometric amount of sacrificial oxidizing or
reducing agent is required to restore the photoredox catalyst to the active
state through an additional SET step, whereas during redox-neutral reac-
tions two SET steps occur at disparate points of the reaction mechanism
[5]. The purpose of sacrificial agents is to consume unused photogener-
ated electrons or holes [6]. Alternatively, the excited state of the photore-
dox catalyst may be quenched by direct hydrogen atom transfer, energy
transfer as in the case of organic dyes, proton-coupled electron-transfer, or
by generation of reactive oxygen species [5][6]. Notably, the process of
energy transfer from a T1 state of a photosensitizer to a substrate proceeds
with net conservation of spin, and the energy of the T1 state must be higher
that the energy of the substrate for exothermic free energy change [2].
Furthermore, SET events provide radical cation/anion intermediates
with reactivity patterns different from their ground or excited state coun-
terparts. This can be utilised to better control the transfer of electrons
occurring between the excited photoredox catalyst and the substrate by the
use of an electron mediator, such as tertiary amines, thiols, or conjugated
ring systems. Depending on whether the photoredox cycle proceeds by
oxidative or reductive quenching mechanisms, an electron mediator can
participate as an electron donor or acceptor, thereby resulting in coupled
photoredox and organocatalytic cycles. The use of a mediator may be nec-
essary to promote a desired reaction pathway over side reactions, as well
as increase selectivity and enhance reaction kinetics. To facilitate such a
8
==PAGE:10==
mechanism, it is necessary to make sure the potential of the excited pho-
toredox catalyst is more reducing or oxidizing with respect to the electron
mediator. Additionally, since photochemistry is chromoselective, it is nec-
essary to use either a light source whose emission band overlaps with the
absorption maximum of the photoredox catalyst or use a frequency range
where no side reactions occur, i.e. chromatic orthogonality [1]. In this
regard, LED sources are particularly attractive due to a sufficiently narrow
spectrum which reduces competing side reactions. Furthermore, the posi-
tioning of the lamp source is a crucial aspect because irradiance, the radiant
flux received by a surface per unit area, follows the inverse-square law. If
UV radiation is used, it is necessary to use transparent reactor materials as
well as take into consideration the increased degradation of reactor mate-
rials owing to higher luminous flux [1]. During a photochemical reaction
not every excited molecule will react because of a multitude of compet-
ing processes, such as radiative or non-radiative decay. The fraction of
molecules that react relative to those that are excited is called the quantum
yield ϕ, which is a measure of the efficiency of the absorption of light in
producing reaction product [2]a. This means that ϕ is often less than unity.
However, when free radicals are generated in the photochemical process, a
chain reaction can be initiated leading to apparent ϕ values which are much
larger than unity. From the perspective of energy cost, such photoinduced
radical-chain processes can actually be advantageous as less photons are
required to reach full conversion [1].
1.2
Photoinduced spin-center shift transforma-
tions
Radical processes have long been employed in C-C bond construc-
tion for manufacture materials our societies have grown to be dependent
on, to explore the chemical space, and to provide access to reactivity that
is complementary to the established heterolytic synthetic transformations.
In radical reactions, homolytic bond breaking occurs by the absorption of
thermal or electromagnetic energy, and the resulting intermediates with an
unpaired electron take part in chain reactions. The use of phototoredox
catalysis facilitates efficient generation of radical intermediates. This field
9
==PAGE:11==
of chemistry presents an opportunity to be creative when designing the re-
action conditions. For instance, the photoredox catalytic cycle can be cou-
pled with an organocatalytic, or transition metal cycle to facilitate novel
cross-coupling reactions [4]. As a consequence, these types of reactions
can incorporate and build on existing knowledge, which leads to alterna-
tive ways to perform cross-coupling reactions. In recent years, interest in
photoinduced transformations has steadily grown to be a well-established
and formidable synthetic tool for manufacture of complex molecules [8].
Most often, photoredox catalysis generates radicals at α-oxy, -hydroxy,
-alkoxy, -amino, -thio moieties through one of a number of hydrogen ab-
straction pathways [8]. A heteroatom with an unshared electron pair in the
π orbital can delocalise the lone pair into the antiperiplanar σ* orbital of
the adjacent geminal C-H bond [2]b. This conjugation of electron density
causes decrease in the bond dissociation energy (BDE) and increases the
bond length, which effectively causes the bond to be activated for HAT.
Furthermore, this process is aided by deprotonation of the amine or alco-
hol by a base [9]. Moreover, the same effect takes place with aldehydes
whereby the electron density in the σ orbital of C-H bond delocalises into
the adjacent carbonyl π* orbital [4].
If a leaving group (chloride, bromide, hydroxide, or their activators) is
present on the carbon adjacent to the generated radical, rapid elimination
takes place by 1,2-delocalisation of spin density [10]. This transformation
is regarded as the spin-center shift (SCS) concept and it considerably ex-
tends the scope of some radical reactions [11]. The SCS transformation
was discovered to be widely used by nature and central to the existence of
life. For instance, during DNA biosynthesis the SCS pathway is utilised by
ribonucleotide reductase class (I to III) for the formation of deoxyribonu-
cleotides that are the building blocks of DNA [10][11]. In its narrowest
sense, this concept describes the generation of radical cations or oxoallyl
radicals depending on whether alkoxy or hydroxy groups are tethered to
the α-carbon, respectively. In its broadest sense, the SCS concept may
be applied to transformations that preserve radical character, such as rear-
rangement, addition, or elimination, as during the course of these reactions
the spin density is likewise shifted to neighbouring atoms [11]. Owing
10
==PAGE:12==
to its efficiency and straightforward approach, the SCS concept has been
widely applied in organic synthesis, and some of the most eminent appli-
cations include functionalisation of C-F bonds of acetates and acetamides
[10], C-H bonds of aldehydes [4], and C-H bonds of alcohols [12].
1.3
Biomass valorisation by the application of
the SCS concept
Biorefining concerns itself with sustainable processing of biomass,
a renewable and carbon-neutral resource, for the production of biofuels,
chemicals, food, feed, and other materials. Humans have been practis-
ing this process since ancient times with production of ethanol from sugar
and starch by fermentation. A reasonable historical marker of the modern
biorefinery process could be the point at which the exploitation of cellu-
lose as an alternative to sugars and starches was put forward. Therefore,
biorefining has not arisen recently to confront modern environmental and
economic challenges, but rather is a historical and cross-disciplinary move-
ment that has experienced a surge of interest in modern times [13]. Indeed,
biorefinery research output parallels that of historical gasoline prices in
the US with major stimulus coming from a range of federal programs to
promote renewable energy and material production [13]. While biorefining
has not arisen to deal with the tremendous population growth and industrial
development that began with the industrial revolution, it is certainly one of
the solutions to the increasingly high consumption of fossil fuels. Over
the centuries, our societies became heavily reliant on crude oil for manu-
facturing, transportation, heating, electricity generation, and maintaining
economic growth. With increasing acceptance that combustion products
have caused a man-made global warming crisis and that alternatives are
needed, researchers have pursued novel ways to obtain resources normally
derived from crude oil. Various kinds of biomass are available for valori-
sation, for example wood, agricultural, food processing waste, agricultural
crops, litter from animal feedlots, and sludge from water treatment plants.
Bioethanol is produced from food crops rich in starch and sugar, which
can interfere with the food and feed supply chain [14]. This poses a major
challenge to the sustainability aspect of biomass-derived ethanol. Alterna-
11
==PAGE:13==
tive sources of biomass are available, such as lignocellulose that is widely
available, inexpensive, and inedible. In general, this carbon-neutral renew-
able feedstock consists of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin, which ac-
counts for about 15-30% (dry weight) of lignocellulosic biomass [14]. This
biomass source has a high potential for valorisation because biorefining of
lignocellulose can produce furfural, monoaromatic molecules, glycol, and
acids. Now, lignin is an amorphous, complex polymer lacking a primary
structure, and is made up of aromatic structural units interlocked into a
three-dimensional mesh structure through C-O bonds, such as β-O-4, α-
O-4, 4-O-5, and C–C bonds, such as β-β, β-5, and β-1 [15]. The building
blocks of lignin are methoxylated phenylpropanes, which can generally be
divided into three components: H (p-hydroxyphenyl), G (guaiacyl), and S
(syringyl) [15]. In natural lignin, the β-O-4 is the most common linkage
and accounts for 43-65% of all bonds, and the abundance of this linkage
may be increased to 89% by bioengineering [14][15].
The lignin component provides support to cellulose fibers by primar-
ily through covalent binding of cellulose and hemicellulose, as well as
hydrogen bonding and ionic interactions. Such a complex structure of
lignocellulose complicates the biorefining process because it inhibits effi-
cient enzymatic hydrolysis of polysaccharides to platform molecules [14].
Therefore, pre-treatment of lignocellulose is required, which can be done
by a variety of methods: physical, such as grinding or milling, chemical,
such as acid or alkali treatment, biological by utilisation of degrading en-
zymes, or a combination of these methods, such as steam explosion. How-
ever, chemical methods stand out due to suitability for large-scale indus-
trial degradation [14]. Additional procedures for chemical degradation of
lignocellulose include rapid pyrolysis, catalytic hydrogenolysis, catalytic
oxidation, and ionic liquid depolymerisation. Despite being more scal-
able, it is a challenge to make chemical degradation methods cost-effective
and non-polluting, as these routes require high temperatures, pressures, or
a source of H2. To make matters worse, the aforementioned routes are al-
ways accompanied by the generation of unsaturated, reactive compounds
or carbonium ions that promote condensation reactions with products be-
ing extremely recalcitrant for further conversions, and significantly reduc-
12
==PAGE:14==
ing the yield of aromatic monomers from depolymerisation of lignin [14].
As a consequence, photocatalytic depolymerisation has gathered increas-
ing attention as an alternative to the established protocols because it is has
the advantages of low cost, high yield, and environmental friendliness [14].
The photocatalytic depolymerisation of lignin can be generally divided
into reactions catalysed by homogeneous (such as iridium or ruthenium
chromophores) or multiphase catalysts (metal oxides, nitrides, metal sul-
fides, and their composites). Excited states of homogeneous photoredox
catalysts possess large redox windows and long lifetimes, but these chro-
mophores are susceptible to devitalisation in the complex reaction environ-
ment and tolerate a small number of functional groups. Therefore, multi-
phase catalysis has been the primary focus of research efforts. In the lignin
scaffold, a number of C-C and C-O bonds can be targeted by photore-
dox catalysts. The photocatalytic bond breaking of β-O-4, whose cleavage
largely affects the extent of total lignin depolymerisation, by and large oc-
curs either by a two-step or one-step methods. The two-step method uses
two different photocatalysts for oxidation of α-hydroxy group to a ketone
and photoinduced reduction of the ketone, which yields the ketyl radical
anion that then undergoes a 1,2-spin density shift to eliminate a phenol.
In the one-step method, the generated hole leads to the abstraction of the
α-hydrogen from the benzylic alcohol to yield a ketyl radical, that is then
further oxidised to yield the ketone. Subsequent reduction of the ketone
with the excited electron completes the photoredox catalytic cycle, and
yields a ketyl radical anion that is primed to undergo a SCS process to
eliminate a phenol group [8].
1.4
SCS-mediated radical coupling between car-
bohydrates and terpenes
Terpenes and terpenoids are natural products of plants, being present
in leaves, flowers, and fruits [16]. They are most readily obtained from
turpentine that is a major by-product of the paper and the pine resin indus-
tries. Terpenes can be considered as essential oils and are used as sources
for fragrances, flavors, fine chemicals, and polymers. What is more, their
structure is suitable for catalytic conversion into fuels and fuel additives
13
==PAGE:15==
[16]. Consequently, catalytic conversion of terpenes and terpenoids has
steadily gained research interest over the past decade in search for bio-
derived, high-density fuels that could transform the aviation industry [16].
In this regard, this project aims to investigate the feasibility of a di-
rect photocatalysed coupling between unprotected aldoses and terpenes to
create ampiphilic, hybrid molecules with hydrophilic and hydrophobic sec-
tions. By the use of a photoredox catalyst, various sugars can undergo HAT
to generate nucleophilic radicals at various points of the carbohydrate scaf-
fold, which can then undergo coupling reactions with electrophilic olefins
[8]. In contrast, the substrate investigated by this study is D-(+)-glucose
that undergoes HAT and SCS using a visible light/quinuclidine/iridium
catalyst system to generate an electrophilic β-carbon radical, which subse-
quently undergoes reduction by the IrII species to give 2-deoxyaldonic acid
salts [17]. The primary goal of this work was to intercept the electrophilic
β-carbon radical generated following the SCS step. The SOMO (p-orbital)
of such a radical is strongly stabilised by orbital mixing with the adja-
cent π* orbital of the carbonyl, thus making it more close in energy with
the HOMO of nucleophilic olefins present in compounds such as terpenes.
Therefore, reactivity along such pathway was investigated because it could
represent a new research direction with many promising applications.

General information
All reagents were bought from commercial suppliers Sigma-Aldrich,
TCI Chemicals, Combi-blocks, Thermofisher, and were used as received.
Technical solvents were bought from VWR Chemicals and used as re-
ceived. Organic solutions were concentrated under reduced pressure on
a B¨uchi rotary evaporator using a water bath. Flash column chromatogra-
phy was performed by using VWR Chemicals Silica gel Si 60 (40-63 µm).
Thin layer chromatography (TLC) was carried out on VWR Chemicals Sil-
ica gel 60 pre-coated aluminum sheets, and visualised under a UV lamp or
by staining using cerium molybdate stain. 1H-NMR and 13C-NMR spectra
were recorded at 400 MHz and 100 MHz on a Bruker instrument using
relevant deuterated solvents, such as D2O, MeOD, or CDCl3. Coupling
constants are given in Hz.
1H-NMR and 13C-NMR signals are reported
in chemical shift (δ ppm), multiplicities are reported as: s = singlet, d =
doublet, t = triplet, dd = doublet of doublets, m = multiplet, compound m
= combination of multiplets, b = broad.

Compound Preparation
2-deoxy-D-arabino-hexono-1,4-lactone (5)
An HPLC vial was charged with 2.2 mg (Ir[dF(CF3)ppy]2(dtbpy))PF6,
8.9 mg quinuclidine, 36.1 mg D-(+)-glucose, 42.5 mg K3PO4, and a stir
bar. 0.5 mL MeCN and 0.5 mL DI H2O were syringed in. The vial was
sealed tightly with a screw on cap, and N2 was bubbled through the solu-
tion for 5 minutes. The reaction mixture (RM) was stirred under 3.25W
blue LED lamp (Thorlabs M470L4 470nm Mounted LED with a Thor-
labs COP1-A OLYMPUS collimation adapter) irradiation with cooling by
continuous flow of compressed air. The reaction was stopped after 41 h,
and the RM was worked up by adding 0.13mL 2M HCl and stirring for
approximately 1h. Thereafter, the solvent was removed in vacuo and the
crude mixture was purified by column chromatography.

3.66 (m, 1H), 3.56 (dd, 1H, J 11.7), 3.38 (dd, 1H, J 1.9 8.16), 2.41 (m, 2H).

15 mL THF. 400 mg 1,2:5,6-di-O-isopropylidene-α-D-glucofuranose was

layer was washed with water, dried over Na2SO4, and the solvent was re-
moved in vacuo. The residue was purified by column chromatography (9:1
Hex:EtOAc) to yield 234.5 mg of desired product. Yield = 50.8%.

3. Results
3.1
Introduction
Photocatalysis provides the means to form novel C-C bonds using
mild conditions.
This is particularly useful in the biorefining industry
where standard approaches lead to significant side product formation. In
this work, C2 radical coupling of unprotected D-(+)-glucose and various
olefins was investigated because reactivity along this pathway could pro-
vide new ways to utilize biorefining products to make energy-dense fu-
els. As intramolecular processes often have accelerated kinetics relative to
intermolecular reactions, intramolecular coupling experiments were car-
ried out using 3-O-allyl-D-glucopyranose as the substrate. Additionally,
D-fructose and 1,4-dioxane-2,5-diol were subjected to photocatalytic con-
ditions as alternative substrates.
3.2
Radical coupling with D-(+)-glucose
2-deoxyaldonic acid was synthesised according to a previously re-
ported procedure by using the combination of 1 and 2 as the catalytic sys-
tem, followed by an acidic workup to yield 2-deoxy-D-arabino-hexono-
1,4-lactone [17]. It was found that the reaction proceeds well. The sug-
gested mechanism for the formation of 2-deoxyaldonic acid by Murakami
and coworkers proposes a radical intermediate following a SCS and elim-
ination of β-hydroxy group, therefore various olefins were added to the
reaction mixture to attempt to intercept it. As a result, it was found that no
radical coupling occurs with a multitude of electron-deficient and electron-
rich olefins, and that addition of the olefin partner to the reaction mixture
generally prevents the formation of 2-deoxyaldonic acid. Nevertheless, it
was observed that formation of 2-deoxyaldonic acid is not significantly af-
22
==PAGE:24==
fected when vinyl cyclohexane (LL35) and sodium acrylate (LL40) were
used as coupling partners. Subsequently, 1 and 2 were exchanged with
3 and 4—which were reported to work together [18]—and reacted anal-
ogously with styrene (LL44) as well as vinyl cyclohexane (LL45) and
sodium acrylate (LL46). It was found that the control reaction (LL43),
produces no lactone product. Furthermore, no conversion was observed
for LL44 and LL45. However, LL46 experiment yielded a radical coupling
product as indicated by TLC and NMR (Figure 3.1). LL12 is a radical
coupling experiment with N-methyl-N-vinylacetamide using a combina-
tion of 1 and 2 that gives no formation of an 2-deoxyaldonic acid, i.e., the

3.3
Radical coupling experiments with other sub-
strates
Moreover, radical coupling with various substrates that resemble D-
glucose were investigated using the 1 and 2 combination. Curiously for
LL30—which contained D-fructose and styrene—white, insoluble, foam-
like material coalesced at the phase boundary interface. NMR samples of
both soluble and insoluble parts were prepared, and it was revealed that
23
==PAGE:25==
styrene was hardly present in the reaction mixture, while the foam-like
material contained aromatic protons. However, no carbohydrate scaffold
could be observed for the insoluble sample. In contrast, LL32 reaction
that utilised 1,4-dioxane-2,5-diol as the substrate and styrene as the olefin
yielded a cis/trans mixture of 1,2-diphenylcyclobutane as a result of a [2+2]
cycloaddition.
3.4
Intramolecular coupling experiments
Since intermolecular capture with an olefin was not occuring, it was
decided to perform intramolecular experiments as such processes often
have faster kinetics and may be faster than single-electron reduction of the
C2 radical by the photoredox catalyst. For this purpose, 7 was synthesised
and reacted with a photocatalytic system containing 1 and 2. However, no
bicyclic product could be observed.
3.5
Summary
Altogether, it was found that using 1 and 2 is not appropriate to achieve
C2 radical couplings. Furthermore, it was ascertained that formation of 2-
deoxyaldonic acid is inhibited by a number olefins while being unaffected
by others. Furthermore, intramolecular interception also did not yield a
coupled product. However, the use of 3 and 4 combined with a water-
soluble coupling partner yielded reactivity in the right direction.

4. Discussion
No conversion for the control reaction LL43, but product formation
for LL46 indicates that DABCO radical cation is sufficiently oxidizing to
cleave the anomeric proton, but thereafter catalyst 3 is not sufficiently re-
ducing to reduce the radical intermediate formed following a 1,2-spin cen-
ter shift transformation. Reactivity with sodium acrylate, which is predom-
inantly water soluble, and no reactivity with vinyl cyclohexane, which is
soluble in organic solvents, provides proof for the formation of a C2 radi-
cal as proposed by Murakami and coworkers because such a species would
favour dissolution in the aqueous phase [17]. Following reduction of the
radical coupling intermediate, an enolate species would be generated that
should cyclise to a 5-exo-tet ring upon acidic workup. Additionally, the
radical could perhaps abstract a C3, C4, or C5 hydrogen. Such an inter-
mediate may be primed for an additional SCS step in a basic reaction en-
vironment. Following the initial SCS step, the resulting C2 radical has an
electron-withdrawing group on the adjacent carbon that facilitates radical
stabilisation due to orbital mixing, as well as a neighbouring electron-poor
C3 carbon. The intermediate formed following radical coupling would
have an electron withdrawing group and a neutrally/negatively charged
neighbouring carbon. This could have an influence on the stabilisation
of the intermediate, and the subsequent step in the reaction mechanism.
Now, neighbouring groups can have a substantial effect on the reaction
rate. The anomeric effect is a fundamental example whereby in a six-
membered sugar structure the oxygen lone pair has good overlap with the
σ* of the C-X bond on the adjacent atom. This decreases the bond dissoci-
ation energy and increases the bond length. Furthermore, the anomeric ef-
fect can be promoted further by deprotonation of α-hydroxy group because
the charged oxygen can donate electron density into the neighbouring car-

of the C-H bond [2]c[9]. This facilitates cleavage of the C1-H bond us-
ing DABCO, which is a HAT catalyst similar in molecular structure to
quinuclidine. By the use of a base, anomeric hydrogen abstraction using
DABCO should be on the edge of being favourable, thus creating a window
for HAT [19].

5. Conclusions
In this work it was demonstrated that interception of a 2-deoxyaldonic
acid radical species is possible in acetonitrile/water by the use of an ap-
propriate photoredox catalyst/HAT catalyst combination together with a
water-soluble coupling partner. This is a big step forwards in sustainability-
oriented organic synthesis with promising applications in the biorefining
industry for the manufacture of biofuels and chemical building blocks.
