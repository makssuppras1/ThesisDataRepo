CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and literature review
Denmark’s pledge to be fossil fuel free by 2050 [1] (i.e. the penetration of renewable
energy sources, intermittent and non-controllable for most of them), along with the mas-
sive automation of domestic appliances [2] and the penetration of electric vehicles will
put serious stress on the power grid. Consequently, both production and consumption
are expected to be more uncertain and with greater fluctuations. Accurate forecasts
will likely prove increasingly valuable to tackle these new constraints and support grid
stability.
Smart meters could be beneficial for this purpose, since they provide load measurements
at a more granular level that what was previously possible using high-voltage level data.
Hence, smart meters can either be used to better profile individual loads or to offer more
accurate forecasts at aggregated levels [3].
Danish and European targets of equipping all consumers with smart metering [4] opens
up new opportunities for Distribution System Operator (DSO) to better understand
their customers and foresee bottlenecks in their grids. In Denmark, 60/10 kV transform-
ers are typically the lowest voltage level component with online measurements. There
are hundreds of thousands of lines and transformers at 10 and 0.4 kV with no measure-
ments at all, and equipping them with online meters would be prohibitively expensive.
Using known network topology, smart meter data and advances in computational power
presents a cheap and effective alternative to estimate the loading of these components.
Knowing and forecasting low and medium-voltage power flows will be important in the
coming decade as electrification of the power system creates new challenges for the DSO.
In power systems, different needs and applications require different forecasting horizons.
With regards to load forecasting, three time horizons are usually defined [5]:
• Short Term Load Forecasting (STLF): few hours to few days ahead. STLF
is critical as input to unit commitment problems and to properly plan electricity
distribution.
• Medium Term Load Forecasting (MTLF): few weeks to few months ahead.
==PAGE:14==
2
1 Introduction
MTLF assists in power system maintenance and energy pricing strategies.
• Long Term Load Forecasting (LTLF): from one to several years ahead. LTLF
supports decision-making in planning new production units, transmission lines, or
any other long term investments pertaining to the grid, at both transmission and
distribution levels.
STLF is especially valuable for DSOs because it enables to generate forecasts that either
support the pricing strategy used in the electricity markets, or to predict lines congestion
and re-dispatch the power flows accordingly.
Several models have been successfully applied to forecast the load few hours ahead and a
distinction is usually drawn between two types of algorithms. On the one hand, statisti-
cal methods such as Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) models have
always been extensively applied to time-series applications. On the other hand, Machine
Learning (ML) models have thrived in the last two decades thanks to the availability
of larger and larger datasets that enabled the training of high-performing algorithms
comparable to traditional models in terms of accuracy [6].
Although ML major breakthroughs concern topics in the field of classification and pat-
tern recognition, such as marketing personalization, fraud detection, natural language
processing or image detection, ML models show significant improvements when applied
to time-series forecasting as well [7].
In particular, several types of artificial neural
networks have shown strong performance at forecasting both electric consumption [8]
and production [9] using conventional neural networks along with other networks more
suitable for time-series, such as the RNN [10]?
1.2
Problem statement
The main research goal of this project will be to investigate methods to forecast the
power flows in Evonet’s distribution grid on the short term. The dataset made available
can be exploited to either forecast the load at different voltage levels and build on the
latter results to estimate the power flows in the grid, or forecast the power flows directly
from historical measurements.
Two sub-questions arise from this problem statement:
• Do ML methods outperform traditional statistical algorithms for predictive mod-
eling regarding Evonet’s dataset?
• Is the network topology useful to improve the forecasting accuracy of the power
flow?

The dataset will be introduced in the first chapter, focusing on a preliminary description

CHAPTER 2
Data Presentation
This chapter aims at introducing the dataset in terms of summary statistics and data
visualization to better understand the type and number of customers supplied by Evonet.
This preliminary analysis will be valuable to support the choices that will be made in
the later chapters.
2.1
General information
The data is provided by Evonet, a distribution company subsidiary of SE, and clusters
all their customers and portfolio, i.e. 8% of the total Danish electricity consumption
located in the Southern Jutland, as seen on Figure 3.3.
Figure 2.1: Area covered by Evonet distribution grid in Denmark.
The original dataset (non-aggregated) has about 900 GB of data and is composed of one
year of measurements from smart meters with a frequency of 15 minutes, between 1st
January 2017 and 31st December 2017.

In any data-science related field, it is common practice to get a better insight into
the dataset by extracting relevant summary statistics and by plotting the observations.
Summary statistics help describing large amount of information with few indicators,
while graph visualization is useful to understand patterns, seasonality, tendency and
dispersion, or to spot spurious data points for example. The next two sections focus on
wrapping up the relevant results that were obtained from this first analysis and to give
the reader a good overview of the dataset.

2.3
Time-series of consumption and
production
This section provides the reader with time-series useful to gain further insights in the
dataset. Figure 2.3 displays the net consumption (i.e. the difference between the gross
consumption and the gross production) with respect to time, aggregated over all smart
meters.
Figure 2.3: Aggregated net consumption over all smart meters throughout the year
(blue) and corresponding moving average over the 300 neighboring values
(red).
By definition, a negative net consumption refers to an actual production of electricity
for that hour. The net consumption is subject to huge power variations, from -523 to
+452 MWh/h, i.e. a dispersion of almost 1 GW. The large negative peaks support the
observation made above that the production is significant. The red line on the same
figure represents the moving average (using the 300 neighboring data points) of the net
consumption in blue, highlighting that most of the year, the consumption is greater than
the production for the given area. The latter observation makes sense for a distribution
grid whose main goal is to connect the transmission network to the end consumers, large
production units being usually connected directly to the transmission grid.

Figure 2.4 provides the reader with two time-series of the consumption from the regu-
lar consumers only (thus excluding the prosumers). As expected, this consumption is
composed of three different seasonality levels:
• A yearly pattern with less consumption during summer and more during winter,
due to more space heating during winter to keep the indoor temperature to a

CHAPTER 3
Load Forecasting
This chapter investigates several models to forecast the electric load along with the data
preprocessing steps building up to the forecasting stage per se, such as the choice of the
explanatory variables, dimensionality reduction, normalization, the choice of the perfor-
mance metrics, etc. Though not directly part of the predictive model implementation,
the latter steps usually require a considerable amount of time and are key to lead up to
a well-functioning and effective algorithm.
Two types of forecast were implemented and will be discussed here: the forecast of the
electric gross consumption (aggregated over all the smart meters) which is unequivocally
cyclical on a daily and weekly basis, and the forecast of the electric gross production (ag-
gregated over all the smart meters as well) which is much more uncertain and fluctuating,
and thus expected to be more difficult to predict. The consumption and production were
forecast separately because they require different inputs: for instance, the solar radiation
might be relevant for the electric production to have prior information on solar produc-
tion, but it is not necessarily useful to predict the aggregated consumption. This choice
of a breakdown between consumption and production at an aggregated level will happen
to be meaningful for a direct forecast of the power flows at HV level, which aggregates
several thousands of meter measurements. Moreover, the law of large numbers ensures
smoother and more predictable time-series for large aggregation levels, and forecasting
at a more granular level is generally a harder problem, for instance at an individual level
(individual households, individual solar panel, etc.).
3.1
Feature engineering
3.1.1
Why dimensionality reduction?
The ”curse of dimensionality” is a common problem in machine learning [11] and occurs
when the number of attributes gets too large compared to the number of observations in
the dataset. Dimensionality reduction is the process of simplifying the feature space by
reducing its number of dimensions. Reducing the number of attributes can bring three
main advantages:
• It reduces overfitting by preventing the model from identifying spurious correlation
==PAGE:28==
16
3 Load Forecasting
from residual noise in a too small dataset.
• It improves the accuracy of the predictive model.
• It reduces the training time.
Although the initial dataset has a very large number of observations overall, the ag-
gregated time-series are only made of less than 9000 data points (one measurement for
each hour of the year 2017, i.e. 8760 observations): a predictive model training on that
amount of data would be prone to overfitting for a relatively small number of features.
Finally, reducing the size of the feature space can also ease the understanding of the
model and its interpretation.
3.1.2
Dimensionality reduction methods
Dimensionality reduction methods can be split in two categories: feature selection and
feature extraction (see Figure 3.1) [12].
Figure 3.1: Dimensionality reduction methods.

While feature selection simply consists of excluding irrelevant or redundant features
without changing the remaining ones, feature extraction transforms the feature space
to find a smaller combination of attributes that better captures the information of the
dataset. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is the most common method for feature
extraction and a very simple example is provided in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Simple example of Principal Component Analysis.
In this example, the dataset is originally represented by two features X1 and X2, but
most of the variance of the dataset can actually be captured by a single feature PC1
(green line) without any significant loss of information. The main drawback of feature
extraction turns out to be the delicate interpretation of the new features generated. The
new features can always be represented by their components in the original feature space,
but they do not necessarily have an understandable physical meaning. For example, a
new feature with a component of 0.5 from the attribute temperature and 0.5 from the
attribute wind speed is not straightforward to interpret. As will be detailed later in
the report, the total number of features will exceed 50 and the interpretation of the new
features becomes intractable in that case.
In this project, feature selection will consequently be preferred. As detailed in Figure
3.1, three main types of feature selection methods exist:
• Filter methods: These methods do not build on any predictive algorithm and
simply ranks the features using statistical scores that evaluate their correlation
with the output variable. A good measure of correlation strongly depends on the
context and discarding attributes necessarily relies on an arbitrary threshold. The
Mutual Information and the F-test are two scoring methods that are relevant for
the case of regression and continuous variables.
• Wrapper methods: These methods generate subsets of the feature space and
evaluate them relative to a given predictive algorithm, until the subset giving
the best predictive accuracy has been found. For example, the forward selection
==PAGE:30==
18
3 Load Forecasting
starts with an empty set (no feature) and looks for the best feature to add at each
iteration. When the predictive performance stops improving, the optimal subset
has been found.
• Embedded methods: With these methods, all the features are used as input,
and a ”penalty cost” is applied to the objective function of the algorithm to direct
it towards models of lower complexity. L1 and L2 regularization are well-known
methods that constrain the number of coefficients that a predictive algorithm can
use, thus filtering out the least relevant attributes.
The wrapper methods has two main shortcomings: the optimal subset depends on the
algorithm used to assess the performance of the different subsets, and the algorithm has
to be trained for each subset, which is computationally expensive for a large number
of features. Because several predictive algorithms will be used in this project, the fea-
ture selection method which will eventually be applied needs to hold true irrespective
of the given algorithm. For that reason, wrapper methods are discarded in the first place.
Embedded methods do not filter the features before applying the predictive algorithm
and are consequently heavier than filter methods. In this project, filter methods will
firstly be applied as they are faster and simplify the feature space beforehand, and
embedded/regularization methods will be applied (if deemed necessary) to improve the
performance of the algorithm afterwards.

3.1.3.1
Explanatory variables
The choice of the explanatory variables significantly affects the performance of an al-
gorithm. [13], [5], [14] and [15] provide a good overview of the features that could be
useful if available.
Many variables have an influence on the electricity consumption and below is a list of
some of them:
• Weather variables: temperature, daylight time, precipitation, etc.
• Calendar variables: human activity is largely driven by social conventions and
local customs that directly affect the electricity consumption. In most Western
countries, people go to work from Monday to Friday and enjoy free-time during
the week-end. In addition, special days (either public holidays, school holidays,
sporting events, etc.) also heavily impact the load.
• Electricity price: demand response builds on the flexibility of customers and
their capacity to react to price signals (i.e. to shift their consumption in case of high
electricity price). Though today, demand response is mainly used at the industrial
level and still not widely applied to individual households, the growing number of
smart meters now enables individual customers to react to price incentives, thus
increasing the price-sensitivity of the load.
• On the long term, several other features could come into play, such as energy
efficiency (more efficient fridges, TVs, etc. leading to a lower consumption), urban
development [15] (load consumption is affected by urbanization, keeping in mind
that cities provide electricity-intensive services), air quality level (which impacts
outdoor activities and thus electricity consumption), socio-economic factors such
as industrial production level, population, GDP, etc.
In Denmark, the demand response is still relatively limited and the electricity price will
not be taken into account as explanatory variable for the consumption. What is more,
long-term features (corresponding to timescales of several months to several years) can
be assumed constant within a couple of days and especially for short-term load forecast-
ing. Consequently, those features will be disregarded.
In addition to the smart meter dataset from Evonet giving the hourly load and described
in Chapter 2, features will be extracted from other time-series:
• Denmark national load: forecast of the evolution of the national load consump-
tion.

• Public holidays: flag for the public holidays in Denmark, since those particular

3.1.3.2
Handling categorical features
For categorical features such as public holidays for example, the standard approach is to
apply ”one-hot encoding” to transform them into as many binary features as there are
labels/categories. This process prevents the algorithm from interpreting the numerical
value of one label as higher or lower than another label, thus keeping the label ”neutral”.
However, different aggregation levels can be applied on a given categorical variable
when using one-hot encoding. To stick to the example of the feature public holidays
that provides the name of each public holidays that occurred in 2017 (New year’s Eve,
Christmas, etc.), two choices arise:
• Create one binary feature for each label/category.
For example, a first binary
feature would be assigned to Christmas Eve (with 1 for the time steps on that
very day, and 0 for all the other time steps), a second binary feature for New
Year’s Eve, etc.

• Create a single binary feature that flags all the public holidays with a 1, and

In that sense, all the public holidays are attached to
the same data type. In that situation, a single feature would assign 1 to both

independently. On the contrary, the second method boils down to consider the effect
of all public holidays on the electric consumption as the same, which is not completely
realistic. Nevertheless, since the dataset covers only one year of measurements, only one
single occurrence of each public holiday is available (only one occurrence of Christmas
Eve and one occurrence of New Year’s Eve in 2017) and the second method has to be

features characterizing week-days and week-ends would result in 5 × 52 observations for
the week-days feature, and 2 × 52 observations for the week-ends feature.
3.1.3.3
Influence of temperature on load
Among the weather variables, temperature was found to be the most influential param-
eter for the estimation of electricity consumption in the literature [15].
In [15], the
author adds the 2nd and 3rd order polynomials of the temperature to forecast the load
consumption, because the load and the temperature appear to be non-linearly correlated
for the specific location of the study (Southern region of the United States). In that
particular location, most households are equipped with air conditioning, meaning that
below 20°C, the consumption decreases with the temperature (less electric heating when
the temperature increases), but above 20°C, the consumption tends to increase with the
temperature due to air conditioners being turned on.
However, the influence of temperature on load consumption highly depends on the loca-
tion, for two reasons:

3.1.3.4
Mutual information and F-test for feature selection
In this project, the filter methods are two correlation scores which are applied and com-
pared on our set of features. Those scores measure the correlation between each feature
and the output variable. In that case, the output variable is the (gross) consumption.
The two correlation scores were calculated using the built-in functions f_regression()
and mutual_info_regression() from the ScikitLearn package in Python and are de-
scribed below:
• F-test: The F-test is a linear regression test that calculates the Pearson’s corre-
lation coefficient (i.e. the standard correlation coefficient) between each attribute
and the output variable. This coefficient is then provided as an F-score along with
the corresponding p-value.
• Mutual information: The mutual information measures the dependency be-
tween each attribute and the output variable. Two variables are independent if
and only if their mutual information equals zero, and the higher the mutual infor-
mation, the more correlated the variables.
By definition, the F-test is only able to capture linear dependency while the mutual
information can spot non-linear relationships as well, as shown in the four examples
provided in Figure 3.5.
Figure 3.6 and 3.7 show the scores corresponding to the 55 attributes including both
weather variables for Esbjerg, Kolding and Ringkoebing (marked with suffix ’_E’, ’_K’
and ’_R’ on the figures), public and school holidays flags, and summer time flag. As ex-
pected, for both scores, the temperature is highly correlated to the electric consumption.
The solar radiation also appears to be a valuable attribute in the three cities of interest
and belongs to the top graph (features with the highest score) for both scores. The
lowest F-scores and mutual information scores are obtained for the attributes snowfall,
along with medium cloud and high cloud. It seems reasonable that these features
are not highly correlated to load consumption, and they will be discarded in the rest of
the project. Besides, the features medium cloud and high cloud probably provide
redundant information, since the two weather features cloud cover and low cloud
already exist, supporting the aforementioned choice.

3.2
Outlier detection
3.2.1
Objective of outlier detection
Anomaly/outlier detection is typically part of the exploratory analysis and is informative
on its own. An outlier is defined as a data point lying ”far away” from the mass of the
other data points. The latter definition depends on a ”distance” metric and suggests
that there is no ground truth whether to categorize a given data point as an outlier. The
decision would ideally be made on a case-by-case basis, but the size of modern datasets
is prohibitive for this approach to be practically used, and data scientists usually apply
an arbitrary threshold dividing up the normal observations from the so-called erroneous
ones.
An outlier either suggests a measurement error (sensor fault, typo, missing data point,
etc.) to be discarded for the sake of forecasting accuracy, or it is simply an occurrence
of the underlying phenomenon to be kept.
==PAGE:42==
30
3 Load Forecasting
For datasets consisting of independent observations, several effective statistical methods
exist to spot outliers ranging from simple boxplots/interquartile method and Z-score
to the more advanced density-based anomaly detection such as K-nearest neighbors,
relative density or Gaussian kernel density estimation. However, due to the sequential
nature of time-series, outlier detection is not as straightforward in this project and
it should ideally include considerations about trend, seasonality and more broadly time

on the residue once the time-series has been decomposed in three components:
• Trend: the long-term evolution of the time-series.
• Seasonal: the regular/repeated cycles in the time-series.
• Residue: the remaining noise, supposedly random.

3.3.2
Single-step vs multi-step forecasting
In time-series forecasting, predictive models can either output a single step forecast, or
directly a multi-step forecast. For instance, forecasting the observation at the next time
step alone relates to single step prediction, while forecasting the next 10 observations
at once is a multi-step prediction. All the models that are implemented in this project
can do both types of forecasting. However, in our case, the explanatory variables belong
to both measurement category (consumption measurement, etc.) and forecast category
(weather forecasts, electricity price forecasts, etc.). For that reason, to make the most
out of the forecast information, the models will be set to single step prediction only.

• Optimization algorithms (such as gradient descent) converge much faster.

folds and no difference was noticeable above that. For that reason, all the models were
trained with a 10-fold cross-validation in this project.
3.3.6
Grid Search
A machine learning model can have two types of parameter:
• Hyperparameter: Its value needs to be set before the learning process begins.
In a neural network, the number of neurons and the number of layers are two
examples of hyperparameters.
• Parameter: Its value is derived during the learning process. In a neural network,
the node weights are examples of parameters.
For the predictive models, hyperparameters were tuned using a method called grid search.
A grid search consists of exhaustively searching through subsets of hyperparameters in
order to find the optimal ones. A grid search compares several values of a given hyper-
parameter and picks the one that minimizes the forecasting error. The latter error is
estimated using a 10-fold cross-validation for all the models.
Because applying 36 different grid searches (one for each lead time) is computationally
unfeasible, the grid search was only carried out for a single lead time (fixed to 36 hours).
The optimal hyperparameters were then re-used for all the other lead times as well.

3.4.1
Benchmark models: persistence and climatology
To ensure the quality of the forecasting methods, two very simple models were used as
a benchmark, namely the persistence and the climatology forecasts:
• The persistence forecast consists of using the last measurement available as
our forecast. For instance, for a lead time of 5 hours, the persistence forecast for
t=12:00 AM is the true observation at t=7:00 AM. The persistence model is based
on the assumption that there is inertia in the quantity to predict, that is, the
quantity does not fluctuate too rapidly from one observation to another, and the
last measurement available is a good estimation of the values to come. For that
reason, the persistence forecast typically performs well for short lead time or for
highly inertial phenomena.
• The climatology forecast consists of using the average of the last n measure-
ments available as our forecast. The term ”climatology” stems from meteorologists
and climatologists who use this approach to generate simple weather forecasts. In
this project, the number of averaged measurements n was arbitrarily set to 168
(i.e. one week of hourly measurements). For instance, for a lead time of 5 hours,
the climatology forecast for t=12:00 AM is the average of the last 168 observations
prior to t=7:00 AM. The climatology model is especially relevant for stationary
==PAGE:51==

time-series, where the average of the last measurements provides a good approx-
imation of the next observations, assuming that the short-term fluctuations are
not too large. For example, the climatology would tend to underestimate the true
values for a non-stationary time-series with an increasing trend.
The two latter models only build on historical data and do not make use of any other
explanatory variables. They were chosen for their ease of implementation and because
any more complex algorithm is expected to outperform them.

3.4.2.2

Holt-Winters method is also known as triple exponential smoothing because it applies

• Level: the value of an observation. This corresponds to the simple exponential
smoothing: for the next time step, the level is estimated as the exponentially
weighted average of the past observations’ level.
• Trend: the difference between two consecutive observations. For the next time
step, the trend is estimated as the exponentially weighted average of the past
observations’ trend.

3.4.5.2
RNNs with LSTM
Up to now, all the models described previously (MLR, SVR, NN and DNN) handled
single-step inputs. The temporal dependency was only included through the choice of
the features, by adding for instance lagged features such as the average temperature
over the previous week, the consumption measurement of the day before at the same
time, etc. (see Table 3.1). Alternatively, RNNs (RNN) can explicitly handle sequential
observations, by dealing with multi-step inputs: a point observation is replaced by a
temporal sequence of several observations, as illustrated in Figure 3.19. In that respect,
RNN removes the burden of manually diagnosing the temporal information that needs
to be added in the model through feature engineering. Note that a new hyperparameter
now needs to be tuned, namely the number of time steps n in the input.
Figure 3.19: Handling of the input data as point observation (top) and as sequences
(bottom) for a single-step output h.

forecasting is not feasible with a mix of measurement features and forecast features. Con-
trary to the previous models where the mapping function between inputs and outputs
was fixed once the training completed, the RNN adds a new dimension to the mapping
function, which becomes dynamic with respect to time. The main drawback of the RNN
approach is that handling sequences instead of point observation makes the algorithm

3.4.5.3
Hyperparameter tuning
The three types of Neural Networks were designed using Keras API, and the following
hyperparameters were tuned using a grid search:
• Batch size: Number of samples seen by the model before updating the parameters
(weight, bias, etc.). The training set is usually divided into several batches, so that
the model already improves after a few samples while walking through the dataset.
The optimal batch size was typically between 20 and 100.
• Number of epochs: Number of times the model works through the whole dataset.
The performance of the learning algorithm tends to improve with the number of
epochs but levels off after a while. Consequently, a suitable number of epochs
would stop the training shortly after convergence.
The optimal number of epochs was typically around 100.
• Optimizer: Optimization algorithm used to train the model.
Several optimizers were tried out: Stochastic Gradient Descent, RMSprop, Ada-
grad, Adadelta, Adam, Adamax, Nadam.
The best optimizers were Adagrad, Adadelta and Adam.
==PAGE:67==
3.4 Models overview
55
• Weight initialization: Initial values taken by the weights in the network. All the
weights were initialized in the same way. An informed choice for the initial weights
helps speeding up convergence and mitigating the common problem of ”vanishing
gradient” that prevents neural networks from learning and updating their weights
VanGrad The latter issue is mainly relevant for neural networks with a large
number of layers (deep networks). In this project, the neural networks were only
restricted to a couple of layers and the problem of ”vanishing gradient” was very
limited.
Several initialization methods were tried out: uniform, lecun_uniform, normal,
zero, glorot_normal, glorot_uniform, he_normal, he_uniform.
• Activation functions: Non-linear function applied after each neuron and which
controls when to fire. In this project, a single activation function was optimized
for each layer.
Several activation functions were tried out: ReLU (rectified linear unit), tanh,
sigmoid, hard_sigmoid, linear.
• Dropout rate:
Percentage of neurons which will be randomly dropped out.
Dropout is a regularization technique which prevents overfitting by randomly leav-
ing some neurons out of the training phase. For each training set, each neuron
has a probability p of being used and a probability 1 −p of being ignored. The
dropout rate refers to the probability 1 −p. Consequently, the network will tend
to avoid assigning very large weights to specific neurons since these neurons could
be randomly discarded in the next training phases.
In this project, the optimal dropout rate was chosen to be the same for the whole
network, though it could be different for each layer in theory. The optimal dropout
rate was typically around 20%, meaning that one neuron out of 5 was dropped out
at each training stage.
• Weight constraint: Maximum magnitude allowed for the weights. Along with
dropout technique, constraining the weights alleviates overfitting.
• Number of neurons: Number of neurons in each layer.
In this project, the number of neurons was optimized for each layer independently.
The optimality was typically found for numbers ranging from 50 to 200 neurons.

CHAPTER 4
Power flow forecasting
This chapter investigates two methods to forecast the power flows in the distribution
grid: a full data-driven approach with a direct forecast of the flows from historical flows,
and a minimum cost flow approach including forecasting of the loadings at low voltage
followed by a linear program to find out the cheapest path for the electricity.
In the following, the visualization of the output of both strategies will focus the flows
between the transmission grid and the HV nodes of the distribution grid, because the
limited amount of HV nodes ensures a clear interpretation of the results and also because
this information engages both the TSO and the DSO who are interested in having a
prediction of the power flowing in and out of their grid area.

Power flow forecasting strategies
Once the system scope is set, two strategies can be implemented, both of them building

• Minimum cost flow strategy (top diagram of Figure 4.7): This approach follows

regarding the power flows can be factored in to improve the predictive accuracy.

forecast flows in all the lines for the next time steps. The main advantage of this
second method is that it does not require any in-depth knowledge about the grid

4.3.1
Minimum cost flow
The first strategy to forecast the power flows in the network is to build on the load
forecasts for nodes located at the boundary of the system, to then run a minimum cost
flow model. Other well-known electrical modelling approaches, such as the Direct Cur-
rent Optimal Power Flow (DC-OPF) or the Alternative Current Optimal Power Flow
==PAGE:91==
4.3 Power flow forecasting strategies
79
(AC-OPF), can also be applied to figure out the power flows. However, both of these
models require specific information about the lines (lines resistance and reactance for in-
stance) that were not available for this project. In addition, the AC-OPF is a non-linear,
non-convex problem which is harder to solve and with no guarantee to find a global
optimum. On the contrary, the minimum cost flow model is a linear problem which is
fast to solve and thus easily scalable to complex networks and multiple time steps.
The minimum cost flow builds on concepts from the field of graph theory. In graph
theory, a network is composed of vertices (or nodes) connected by edges (or lines). If
the edges have a direction, the graph is said to be ”directed”. An example of a directed
graph with two nodes i and j is given in Figure 4.8:
Figure 4.8: Example of a simple directed graph with two vertices and one edge along
with its characteristics.
Applying graph theory to our distribution grid, the vertices represent the nodes (trans-
formers) while the edges represent the distribution lines. The edges of a graph can have
different characteristics, such as a maximum capacity u(v, w) and a travel cost c(v, w).
The capacity u(v, w) is a power capacity (in kW) limiting f(v, w), the amount of power
flowing from node v to node w. The travel cost c(v, w) (in DKK/kW or øre/kW) models
the cost related to the power losses per unit of power transported in the line connecting
v to w. The vertices are assigned a demand b(v) that corresponds to power flowing
towards the node v (or consumption) if b(v) > 0; and to power flowing out of the node
(or production) if b(v) < 0. The demand represents the power exchanges with the exter-
nal environment. Therefore, nodes without any connection to the external environment
have a demand of zero.
The minimum cost flow model consists of finding the optimal (or cheapest) path for the
power while satisfying both the demand and the capacity constraints. To mathematically
formulate this linear program, two ensembles are introduced:
• E is the ensemble composed of all the edges

For this problem to be a linear program, the objective function needs to be linear and
the power losses must be proportional to the power flowing in the line. In reality, the
losses are not proportional to the power flow, and include for example ”no-load” losses
that occur to energize the transformers even with no electricity flow, or resistive losses
that increase exponentially with the line current [21]. However, constant travel costs is
adequate as a first approximation. Hence, the objective function represents to total cost
of the power flowing in the network.
Then, the purpose of the first constraint in Equation 4.1 is to enforce the Kirchhoff’s
law for each node v by ensuring than the net power flowing in node v equals the demand
b(v) of this node.
Finally, the last constraint makes sure that the power flow does not exceed the capacity
of the line. One can notice than in this problem, the edges are directed. In particular,
the lower bound of the flow f(v, w) is 0, meaning that the power can only travel from v
to w. However, in a genuine electric grid, the power can flow both ways in a line. For
that reason, two directed edges with the same characteristics (same cost and capacity)
but opposite directions were defined for each line. The cost minimizing objective func-
tion ensures that only one direction is utilized for each time step.

solve the linear program.

4.3.2
Full data-driven
This second strategy consists of directly forecasting the power flows from the historical
measurements and does not require any knowledge about the network topology. To be
comprehensive, this strategy would require a Big Data model able to forecast all the flows
at once. However, due to time constraints, this model could not be developed and the
analysis will be limited to the independent forecasts of the flows in the 14 lines connect-
ing the TSO to the HV nodes (depicted by the gray lines in Figure 4.1). The latter will
provide us with a rough estimate of the accuracy that could be achieved with this model.
To forecasts these 14 power flows, the model that performed the best for load forecast-
ing (in Chapter 3) was trained again here, namely the SVR model. Considering that
in Chapter 3, all models were applied to forecast both aggregated consumption and
production, it makes sense to leverage the output of this analysis to forecast the power
flows from the TSO to the HV nodes, which also aggregate the information of numerous
smart meters and are thus expected to behave in a similar way.
The feature set was chosen to be the one used for the production forecast in Chapter 3,
since it includes most of the critical features used for consumption.
Finally, the optimal hyperparameters of the SVR (namely the kernel type, kernel coeffi-
cient, penalty cost and tolerance) were obtained by means of a grid search.
This concludes the presentation of the two strategies to forecast the power flows in the
distribution grid.

5.1
Lessons learned
First of all, several lessons can be learned by taking a step back and reflecting on the
working process per se. A 6-month long project such as this Master’s Thesis offers a
wealth of teachings that should be outlined:
• Data preprocessing can be a time-consuming task and should be properly planned
because it directly affects the quality of the outputs. Data preprocessing is com-
posed of all stages prior to the actual implementation of the models, and includes
tasks such as data wrangling, data mining and visualization, data cleansing with
outlier detection, feature selection, etc. “90% of data science is wrangling data,
the other 10% is complaining about wrangling data.” [23] (David Robinson, Chief
Data Scientist at DataCamp)
• Outlier detection is straightforward and well documented for datasets composed of
independent observations, while applying outlier detection on sequential observa-
tions necessarily involves modeling the temporal evolution of the process in some
way, which leads up to more sophisticated methods.
• Feature engineering is a crucial step in designing an effective predictive model and
requires to combine sector knowledge and data exploration to be valuable.
• The choice of an algorithm should be made considering the computing resources
and the time available.
• To achieve the implementation of a model over a large dataset, it is good practice
to first produce a dummy dataset with the same characteristics but a smaller size.
The latter scale model enables fast prototyping, sanity checks and debugging, and
it usually gives a better grasp and follow-up of the outputs.

5.2
Conclusion
Key findings and analysis of results from this project are presented below:
• The size of the dataset, covering a single year, might be limiting to accurately
forecast rare events with a frequency of occurrence below one event per year, such
as the consumption changes related to holidays and bank holidays (Christmas Eve,
New Years’ Eve, etc.). However, a larger dataset over several years would have
probably required making the time-series stationary before applying our models,
regarding the rapid evolution of both the electric consumption (electrification of
devices, penetration of electric vehicles, etc.) and production (penetration of re-
newable energy sources).
• The number of cross-validation folds greatly affects the performance of the neural
networks and 10 folds happen to be a good trade-off between a reasonable com-
putation time and a stable performance of the models. These machine learning
models are indeed very sensitive to the amount of observations they take as input
and perform particularly well on very large datasets.
• The electric production comes increasingly from renewable energy sources, which
is stochastic in nature.
Consequently, production forecasts show worse perfor-
mance than consumption ones and are highly dependent on the quality of weather
observations and forecasts.
• The SVR turns out to be the best fit for electric consumption/production forecasts
at an aggregated level, both for forecasting accuracy and fast prototyping and
computation time.
• With a relevant feature selection, the MLR provides good predictive performance
while being particularly fast to tune and run, thanks to very few hyperparameters.
On the contrary, the large number of hyperparameters for neural networks lead to
a highly time-consuming tuning stage.
• In the scientific literature, the RNN-LSTM is predominantly praised for multi-step
forecasting. Applied to single-step forecasting, this model turns out to be much
less beneficial due to an overly lengthy computation time.
• Distribution networks are hardly meshed and mainly radial from HV to LV levels,
making interpretation and visualization of networks as a graph easy.
• For power flow forecasting, the main challenge is to develop a model able to predict
the loadings of all LV nodes. However, to access the flows between the TSO and
the HV nodes, the loadings of the MV nodes are enough and lead to a much lighter
process (about 200 forecasts instead of 12000).

Future work
With more time, several topics could be further investigated, such as:
• A comprehensive ”Big Data” outlier detection model applicable to each individual
smart meter signal to filter out spurious observations directly from the measure-
ment sources.
• A systematic analysis of the features weight given by the predictive models could
be valuable.
• Instead of directly forecasting the aggregated consumption/production at HV
nodes, it could be interesting to forecast the load at LV nodes, and then aggregate
the forecasts to see if the accuracy can be further improved.
• For the full data-driven model applied to power flow forecasting, a comprehensive
and more complex Big Data model could forecast all the network flows at once. To
this end, caution should be used when tackling two issues: flows at different voltage
levels behave quite differently and might require various additional attributes to
be effective. In addition, to ensure consistency of the flows, a proper aggregation
of the forecasts needs to be carried out. In other words, seen from a node, the
forecast flows of the incoming lines should always add-up to the forecast flows of
the outgoing lines.
• With access to the line characteristics (capacities, cross-section area, length, etc.),
the travel costs could be redefined and the minimum cost flow strategy could be
run again in a more realistic set-up.
