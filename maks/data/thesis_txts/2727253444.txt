Abstract
This project investigates the method of converting a lightweight block cipher into an LP-
model and solving it using a MILP-solver to find differential characteristics with high prob-
abilities. The paper covers the theoretical process of converting such a cipher specification
into an LP-model and also presents a software solution in Python, that can perform this
process automatically for different, lightweight SPN block ciphers.
The paper focuses on testing the lightweight cipher CRAFT and how the method used
manages to find characteristics for this cipher. Characteristics are found for up to 10 rounds
of CRAFT using different search strategies based on an optimized configuration of the MILP-
solver Gurobi and the constructed LP-model targeted. The distribution of probabilities for
characteristics is also investigated and characteristics and differentials for 12-, 14- and 16-
rounds are also found.
Results show that average characteristics can be found fast and optimal characteristics can
be found slower, depending on the search strategy. Results also hint at combining the search
strategies can be the most efficient option. The characteristics found for CRAFT showed that
specific patterns could be used to avoid diffusion over 10 rounds. This hints at a weakness
in the design of CRAFT, which further could be exploited to create better characteristics in
future works.
In general, the method used proved to be correct with regard to the characteristics found and
is a relatively simple and efficient tool to automate the search for differential characteristics.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In today’s modern age, the world is widely digitized, and many TBs of information are
transferred all over the world. Some of this information contains sensitive and private data
and the senders can employ a wide range of security measures to prevent it from falling into
the wrong hands. In cybersecurity, one of these aspects is Confidentiality, which in practice
is implemented using Cryptography. Since ancient times when cryptography was more an
art than a field of science, it has been used to safeguard critical information, both in civilian
and military contexts. [Sch22]
Throughout the years, cryptography has evolved and especially the last 30 years has seen
a rapid development of both cryptographic designs and cryptanalytic tools to attack such
designs.
Academically, for both cryptographers and cryptanalysts, it is in their interest
to develop new and more secure cryptography constructs and help in testing and attacking
them, to ensure their security and guarantee that only the very best cryptographic constructs
are standardized and recommended to the public. This project seeks to contribute to that
agenda. [Sin99]
One relatively new method of performing differential cryptanalysis is by modeling a block
cipher as a linear-programming model and solving this model using a MILP-solver. This
allows for the automation of finding characteristics, which can be combined into a differen-
tial to perform a practical attack. This method is often used in cryptanalytic papers, but
depending on the hardware, solver, and cipher used, the results can vary quite a lot. This
paper seeks to look into this method and present new insights into its efficiency and use
cases. [Mou+12]
Over the last couple of years, an increased focus has fallen upon lightweight block ciphers
and CRAFT is one such design. CRAFT has been developed with a limited hardware size
and an increased resistance against differential-fault attacks in mind. CRAFT is a relatively
new cipher that has not seen much wide investigation yet and thus will be the target of
differential cryptanalysis using a MILP-solver in this paper.

Project Objectives & Structure
The primary objectives for this project are as follows:
• Investigate the lightweight block cipher CRAFT according to its resistance against
differential cryptanalysis and try to find certain patterns among differential character-
istics, that hint at weaknesses in CRAFT.
• Use the MILP-solver on CRAFT to perform the differential cryptanalysis and evaluate
it as a tool in relation to its speed and precision, as well as try to optimize the tool.
This project will seek to combine these two primary goals by performing differential crypt-
analysis on CRAFT using a MILP-solver. The project will cover the theory and method
involved, as well as the modeling of an SPN-cipher as CRAFT into an LP-model. A flexi-
ble software solution will also be developed, which given the specifications of a lightweight
SPN-cipher can find characteristics using a MILP-solver. Lastly, tests will be run against
CRAFT to find possible weaknesses in its differential resistance, as well as gain insight into
the efficiency of the chosen method to use. An overview of the sections can be seen below:

Chapter 2
Background Theory
This project heavily draws theory from two fields within mathematics; namely differential
cryptanalysis and mixed-integer linear programming (MILP). This section will cover the
required knowledge from these mathematical fields to fully understand this project. It is
assumed that the reader has a basic knowledge of cryptography and block ciphers.
2.1
Differential Cryptanalysis
The field of differential cryptanalysis deals with trying to break block ciphers using differences
between plaintext pairs. The method was originally developed by IBM and NSA around 1974
when DES was developed. [Cop94] [Lev01] The method was rediscovered publicly by Edi
Biham and Adi Shamir and published in their 1991 paper Differential Cryptanalysis of DES-
like cryptosystems, where they used this method to find a theoretical attack on DES. [BS90]
Over time, this cryptanalytic attack has been enhanced and multiple variants of the method
have been developed, one of the latest examples being quantum differential cryptanalysis
to work on quantum computers. [Kap+16] In parallel, cryptographers have had to improve
the design of block ciphers, specifically by implementing substitution boxes (or S-boxes) to
counter this cryptanalytic method. Nevertheless, differential cryptanalysis remains to be
one of the stronger techniques in a cryptanalyst’s toolkit and will be used in this project to
attack the lightweight block cipher CRAFT.

Differential cryptanalysis in practice
As previously mentioned, the S-box is the most difficult part to overcome in an attack using
differential cryptanalysis. We want to use the transition with the highest probability, which
in the case of table 2.1 is 3 →4 with probability 8/8 = 1.
We can show a practical attack on the cipher in figure 2.1, which contains two S-boxes and
3 keys. First, we make use of all plaintext pairs with the fixed input difference inδ = 3.
Thus we choose the first value x ∈B3 and create the second plaintext by XOR’ing with
inδ, therefore making this a chosen plaintext attack. We also get the ciphertexts to the
equivalent plaintexts. We then make n = 8 different counters for the unknown key and try
to push each of the ciphertexts from each pair backward through the S-box s2 by testing

of the unknown key, calling the test i. If we get the expected difference of outδ = 4, the

is encountered. Thus the sum of all counters after the n · n run is expected to be n.
After such an attack, we can peel a round off the cipher and attack it again, now with only
r −1 rounds. This can be done until all of the round keys have been found, which allows us
to recreate the initial key and thus break the cipher.[KR11]
2.1.2
Characteristics and differentials
When we make use of a transition in the DDT, we call it a characteristic and write it as
X →Y . In fact, multiple characteristics can be combined to make longer characteristics
to attack multiple rounds. Using the same DDT from table 2.1, one could make a second
characteristic that continues from the first, like 3 →4 →7. This would be called a 2-round
characteristic. The probability of both will be multiplied to get the total probability, in this
case, (8/16) ∗(4/16) = 2−3 Thus, after 2 rounds, if we start with a difference of 3, we expect
there to be a difference of 7 with a probability of 2−3.
In practice, when it comes to attacking block ciphers with a lot of rounds, we only care
about the input- and output differences, and nothing in between. Thus the difference of 4
would be completely irrelevant in our previous characteristic. By doing this, we accept all
possible differences that can happen between the input and output differences, thus allowing
the use of more transitions and not restricting ourselves to a single r-round characteristic.

2.1. Differential Cryptanalysis
We call these differentials and they therefore act as a collection of characteristics. Each of
the r-round characteristics in a differential contributes a probability to the probability of the
whole differential. Thus it is in the interest of the cryptanalyst to find the combination of
input- and output differences, thus a differential, which has the highest possible probability
over r-rounds based on the sum of the individual characteristics covered by that differential.
Based on the complexity of the cipher, there can be millions of different characteristics and
trails, but with a good (or the best) one, it may be possible to perform an efficient attack
to recover a round key from a cipher, especially compared to an exhaustive search. [KR11]
2.1.3
Differential resistance
Until now, we have worked with a single input to the cipher. In practice, multiple 4-bit
or 8-bit inputs are normally used in lightweight ciphers. Each of these nibbles undergoes
the same operations over a round, thus making the round function a so-called Bricklayer-
function, as it works on groups of 4 bits. [DR01]. The multiple nibbles result in the need
to combine the probability of multiple characteristics per round. Considering a block cipher
with 4 nibbles, we can have a 2-round transition look like this:
(0, 9, 0, 0) →(0, e, 0, 0) →(3, 5, 0, a)
(2.2)
In this case, the permutation layer helps spread the single input to multiple positions in the
output state. Thus we have multiple transitions and have to multiply these to get the actual
probability for this 2-round characteristic. As the probabilities are under 1, the probability
is further reduced for each transition and that makes it more difficult for the cryptanalyst
to use this characteristic, thus the need to use multiple in a differential to increase the
probability.
From the view of the cryptographer, it is of interest to have as many transitions as possible
be used in a round. Specifically, this means maximizing the number of active S-boxes each
round, e.g.
an S-box with a non-zero difference as input.
In general, a cryptographer
makes use of two concepts to make their cipher as efficient as possible: Confusion and
Diffusion.[KR11]
Confusion & Diffusion
The terms confusion and diffusion originate from Claude Shannon’s groundbreaking paper
A Mathematical Theory of Cryptography from 1945. [Sha93] The original meaning of the
terms has changed a lot, but both still describe important concepts for block ciphers used
today.
Confusion is about hiding the relationship between the key and the ciphertext so that having
a large number of plaintext-ciphertext pairs does not help in guessing the key. Confusion
thus means that each bit of the ciphertext should depend on all bits of the key so that a
change in one bit of the key would lead to changes in most or all of the ciphertext bits. This
is known as the avalanche effect

2.1. Differential Cryptanalysis
Diffusion on the other hand is about hiding the relation between the plaintext and the
ciphertext, while also ensuring the avalanche effect between them. This is in practice done by
permutation operations, which diffuses the input of the cipher each round. This increases the
number of active S-boxes and thus is the main aspect of the block cipher that cryptanalysts
"battle" against when performing differential cryptanalysis. [Sha49] [LRL18]
Differential branch number
Based on the diffusion caused by the permutation layer, multiple S-boxes are activated each
round to thwart the attempts of differential cryptanalysis on the cipher. Essentially, this is
caused by the differential branch numbers from the functions used in the permutation layers.
Definition 2 Given a linear function F : Bn →Bn and an input, X, the branch number
β() is the smallest non-zero value of the hamming weight W() of both the input and output
of the F, e.g:
β(F) = min
X̸=0(W(X) + W(F(X)))
(2.3)
The branch number has an upper bound of n+1, which means that a single non-zero compo-
nent in the input ensures that all n components in the output will be non-zero. Essentially,
a linear function with branch number β(F) will cause the sum of the hamming weights of
the input and output to be exactly β(F), as long as at least one component of the input is
non-zero. [LS16]
Having a high branch number in a function thus guarantees a high diffusion of non-zero
inputs and it is therefore vital for the cryptographer to include such functions in their block
cipher, as to maximize the number of active S-boxes.
In the end, both cryptanalysts and cryptographers have different tools and techniques they
use to thwart each other’s work and this academic struggle is beneficial for advancing the
field of cryptography as a whole and for improving the next generation of encryption systems
and cryptanalytic tools.[KR11]

Linear Programming
Linear programming is a major sub-field within the area of mathematical optimization, which
deals with the problem of finding an optimal solution from a set of solutions, given specific
requirements. This normally involves either finding the maximum or minimal solution from
the given set.
Linear programming specifies the requirements as being linear equations,
which help define the solution space.
2.2.1
Fundamentals of Linear Programming
A problem P within Linear programming consists of a set of decision variables X =
x0, x1, ..., xn, which each can take upon values from R. These decision variables serve as
input parameters to a function f : Rn →R, which is called the objective function. It
has the form f(X) = c0 · x0 + ... + cn · xn where xi, ci ∈R|i = 0..n . The decision variables
together make up a solution array to the problem P and thus with n decision variables, we
can have an arbitrary solution i where Xi ∈Rn.
For this kind of problem, the goal is to find a solution X0, which either maximizes the
function f, meaning that ∀X∃X0 : f(X) ≤f(X0) or minimizes the functions, so that
∀X∃X0 : f(X) ≥f(X0). Note it is possible for there to be multiple solutions Xi, that all
optimize the object function f : f(Xi) = f(Xj).
In most cases, a linear programming problem also has constraints, which limit the search
space of where to find a solution.
We distinguish between valid/feasible solutions for
which the constraints hold and which geometrically can be found in the solution space, and
invalid/infeasible solutions, which are located outside the solution space and for which
one or more constraint do not hold. Additionally, depending on the number of constraints
and what they constrain, it is possible for there to exist no optimal solution. This happens
if the solution space is unbounded, meaning for example that limx→∞f(X) →∞. The
opposite of this is a bounded LP-problem, where the solution space is constrained.
The constraints of an LP-problem are normally modeled as a set of linear inequalities. De-
cision variables and their constants are on the left side and a single constant is on the right
side. With these constraints, it is possible to visualize the solution space geometrically, as
long as the number of decision variables is at most n = 3. An example with n = 2 can be
seen in figure 2.2.
Lastly, one normally denotes an LP-problem in a matrix notation. For the objective function,
we denote the n×1 matrix (array) of constants C and the decision variables as a 1×n matrix
D. Thus, the objective function can be denoted as C × D, which becomes a single value.
Equally, the constraints can be described in the same way: With m inequalities, the left-side
constants are denoted A ∈Rn×m as a n × m matrix and the right-side constants as a 1 × m
matrix denoted B ∈Rm. Finally, we can then describe an arbitrary problem within linear
programming in the following manner:

2.2.2
Mixed-Integer Linear Programming and Hardness
Mathematical optimization and specifically linear programming is used every day in different
contexts. This can be in minimizing raw materials for production or maximizing employee
workdays by optimizing the work schedule. In practice, the optimal solutions to such prob-
lems do in most cases not consist of fractions or irregular numbers, but integers. In such
cases, it is called Integer Programming (IP) and there exists an additional constraint, that
all decision variables only can take integer values, so that an arbitrary solution Xi ∈Zn. An
illustration of such a solution space can be seen in figure 2.3
Such a restriction increases the hardness of the problems significantly: Instead of consisting
of continuous elements in a restricted subspace, solutions are now discrete points in the same
space. This forces optimization algorithms to make use of rounding to be able to find the
closest valid integer solution to a given IP-problem. By only considering integer solutions, one
can further reduce it to only considering binary solutions and thus convert it to a problem of
satisfiability of logical formulas, which is an NP-Complete problem. [Coo71] An IP-problem
is therefore also NP-Complete and thus relates to the hardness and complexity of breaking
an encryption scheme.

Solution spaces and convex polytopes
To better visualize the solution space, which is important for explaining the method used
in this project in section 4.2, we introduce a more formal notion of describing the solution
space of an IP-program. To do this, we introduce the notion of convexity.
Convex sets and hulls
Definition 3 A set of n-dimensional points M ∈Rn is said to be convex, if for every two
arbitrary points x, y ∈M and for all t ∈[0, 1], the point (1 −t) · x + t · y ∈M
If one considers a set of points in the 2-dimensional plan R2, the convex set consists of all
the points existing in the area between them, in which a straight line can be drawn from
every single point to every other point in the set. This can be expanded to more dimensions,
so fx. every ball consists of a convex set, while every sphere does not, as they are hollow.
Furthermore, we can define the related term convex hull below:
Definition 4 The smallest convex set, that can be created to contain a set of points M ⊆Rn
is called the convex hull of M and is denoted conv(M)
Thus a convex set specifies a set of points with a feature, while the convex hull specifies the
minimal convex set of a set of points, e.g. a polyhedron in 3 dimensions. Note that the
convex hull of a set M of points is itself the intersection of all convex sets that contain M,
therefore the convex hull is the minimal of those sets. [MG07]

2.2.4
LP-solvers & Algorithms
For solving LP-problems and other problems within mathematical optimization, specialized
software has been developed, called optimizers or solvers. These software programs make
use of some of the existing algorithms for solving programs and optimizing them to run
as efficiently as possible.
For the case of LP-problems, these are primarily the simplex
or interior-point algorithms. [Foua] Shortly, the simplex algorithm works by visiting the
"corners" and edges of the polytype of the solution space, which normally contains the best
solutions. It then visits them in a random order depending on the starting solution and so-
called pivot rules, which determine which corner to jump to next, until an optimal solution
is found. The interior-point algorithm also jumps from solution to solution but instead uses
internal points in the solution space to get to the optimal solution. In general, the interior-
point method is most efficient for larger problems, while the simplex method is good for
smaller ones. [MG07] The simplex method’s running time depends on the pivot rules used,
but it generally has polynomial average time complexity, with a worst-case time complexity
of O(n2). [Sch98] [Gre03] The interior-point method consists of different algorithms, but in
practical cases, they are bounded by O(log(n)). [MG07]
As examples of LP-solvers, open-source solvers include SCIP and GLPK, while commercial
examples include IBM’s CPLEX or the Gurobi Foundation’s Gurobi software.

Chapter 3
Specifications of CRAFT
CRAFT is a lightweight block cipher developed by Beierle et al. and presented in the IACR
journal Transactions on Symmetric Cryptography in 2019. CRAFT is a tweakable block
cipher, meaning that an additional tweakey is used to XOR values onto the cipher state, as
in a standard addRoundKey-operation. Additionally, CRAFT has specifically been built to
withstand differential fault attacks (DTA). These attacks seek to inject faults into the hard-
ware running the cipher, like high voltages or temperatures, to make the processor output
faulty results and use such unusual behavior to recover the key.[BS97]
CRAFT is a classical SPN-block cipher, consisting of involutory components. It takes a
64-bit data block as input, as well as a 128-bit key and a 64-bit tweak. The 128-bit key is
split into two 64-bit half-keys, which are used to generate four different tweakeys. [Bei+19]
3.1
Architecture and round operations
CRAFT uses 32 rounds. A standard round in CRAFT consists of a MixColumn, AddCon-
stant, AddTweakey, permuteNibbles, and an S-box operation, in that order. The last round
omits the subBox and permuteNibbles operations. An overview can be seen in figure 3.1.
Note the absence of a addRoundKey-operations, as the addTweakey-operation instead is
used.
3.1.1
S-box
CRAFT uses the following S-box, which is the same as used in MIDORI. [Ban+15] This
S-box has been chosen for the following reasons:
• Being involutory, e.g. that f(f(x)) = x, so that both the encryption and decryption
process can use the same S-box function and hardware.
• Having a low Gate Equivalence (GE) size of 11, which measures the hardware required
to realize the construction. In lightweight cryptography, 1 GE unit corresponds to 1
CMOS NAND gate. [GB21]

3.2
Strengths and focus of CRAFT
Being a lightweight cipher, a big focus of the design of CRAFT has been to limit the GE size
of the implementation in hardware. This has primarily been done by choosing involutory
components, as to prevent adding separate components for either encryption or decryption.
Additionally, CRAFT can be implemented in a round-based architecture using only a single
clock cycle per round. Specifically, the method for generating the round constants, as well
as the tweakey schedule relates to the goal of having as small overhead as possible, to better
work in lightweight scenarios.

3.3. Reasoning behind targeting CRAFT
When it comes to preventing DFA, redundancy is necessary as extra hardware is needed
for concurrent error-detecting schemes. For CRAFT, the authors make use of binary linear
codes known from telecommunication, to detect faults injected into the hardware.
This
requires at most a duplicate hardware implementation of the cipher, to be able to detect
faults in all parts of the circuit. Note that such a construction does not alter the security
of the cipher, but adds a larger overhead to the complete hardware implementation of the
design.
The choice of linear layers was primarily influenced by the security level that could be
attained. The requirement here was to find a binary matrix for the MixColumn-operation
and permutations so that at least 32 S-box were active in both differential and linear attacks,
as well as achieving the minimum possible number of rounds to attain full diffusion in both
the forward and backward directions. The chosen candidate for PermuteNibbles fulfilled
both requirements with the chosen MixColumn-matrix, as full diffusion was attained after 7
rounds and 32 S-boxes were active after 9 rounds of both differential and linear attacks.
On the other hand, due to the prioritized simplicity of the architecture to reduce the foot-
print, the cipher ends up with a lower level of diffusion than what could have been possible to
achieve. For example, the MixColumn-operation leave the last two rows untouched; Having
to mix these would require more gates. Thus compromises have been made between security
and hardware complexity. [Bei+19]

Methodology
In this section, the primary method used to attack CRAFT is presented. Firstly, the his-
torical development of the method is described, giving credit to the original and influential
authors behind it.
4.1
History of MILP-based Differential Cryptanalysis
4.1.1
Original paper
The origins of using MILP for differential cryptanalysis start with Mouha et al.’s paper from
Inscrypt 2011.[Mou+12] Here, the authors presented a method to take an arbitrary cipher
consisting of an S-box, linear layers, and XOR operations and make constraints equivalent
to these operations. These constraints could then be used to create an LP-problem, which
then subsequently could be solved by a MILP-solver. The authors applied this method to
a simplified version of Enocoro-128v2 to perform both differential and linear cryptanalysis.
The results proved that 38 rounds were sufficient to protect against differential cryptanalysis
(as the probability would exceed 2−64, making it impossible to perform with the 264-input
pairs available) and 61 rounds to resist linear cryptanalysis.
4.1.2
Popularized method
In 2014, Sun et al. expanded upon the work done by Mouha et al. and proposed how to bit-
oriented model block ciphers as MILP-program using a generalized method. This allowed
for the automatic search of the best differential trail for a given number of rounds r for
a specific cipher, as well as the best characteristic with pre-defined properties (Fixed input
or/and output state). Additionally, Sun et al. also proposed a method to model the behaviors
of the S-box, which ensured that the search space containing all valid characteristics was
included in the MILP-program. Essentially, this method works on all block ciphers, as long
as they only contain the following types of operations:
• Bit-wise XOR-operations
• Bit-wise permutation

4.2

4.2.1
Purpose of the method
The overall goal of the method is that given an arbitrary block cipher, to perform differential
cryptanalysis on it by modeling the process as an LP-problem and solving it using a MILP-
solver. To do this, multiple objectives have to be defined:
• An objective function has to be defined, so the MILP-solver knows what to solve for
and what the "best case" is in an attack using differential cryptanalysis
• A set of linear constraints, that correctly represent the structure of a single round of
the targeted cipher.
Point 2 will be presented in the following sections, where the modeling of the S-box and the
linear layers are presented. Point 1 is shown in section 4.2.4.
4.2.2
Modeling the S-box
The modeling of the S-box is the most difficult part of this method, compared to the simplic-
ity of modeling the linear layers seen in the next section. The challenge here is specifically,
how to represent the behavior of the S-box as a set of linear inequalities, which can be used
in an LP-program.

set of binary strings. For an arbitrary S-box with the function S : Fn

The inequalities generated by this method can be massive, fx. 220 inequalities are needed
for the S-box used in CRAFT. This number can be reduced tremendously, as far fewer
inequalities are needed to define the same space. This can be realized by observing that
a lot of the half-spaces in Hconv(B) overlap with each other. Additionally, the space also
contains many additional points, which represent the invalid transitions of the S-box. Thus,
it is also in our interest to shrink the space to remove as many of these invalid transitions
as possible.
Sun et al. proposes a simple, greedy algorithm to do this, which we will call the Reduction
algorithm. This algorithm goes through all the linear inequalities in Hconv(B) and tests all
possible invalid transitions, to see how many linear inequalities are satisfied by each. For
each loop, it then picks the linear inequality which is satisfied by the least number of invalid
transitions and adds this inequality to a set Os. The specific invalid transitions that now can
be "avoided" by using the picked inequality are removed from the set of invalid transitions,
and the next loop is then run. Ultimately, we are left with the set Os of inequalities, which
satisfy the original points that represent the valid transitions of the S-box, but now contain
far fewer points representing the invalid transitions, as well as consisting of fewer inequalities
than Hconv(B). This set Os will then be combined with the new constraints for the linear
layers in the next section, to completely model the targeted block cipher. Pseudocode of the
algorithm can be found in appendix E.

Combination of rounds
With the knowledge from the previous sections, it is now possible to attack a single round of
the block cipher. To extend it to multiple rounds, one simply has to include the additional
variables in the objective function, as well as add similar kinds of constraints for the S-box
and linear layers, just with new variables. Lastly, it is important to chain the rounds together,
e.g. the variables from the end of the constraints representing the linear layer in section 4.2.3
has to be the same binary variables, which are used to represent the input differences in the
following S-box-operation. In this way, the whole structure of the cipher is defined by the
constraints, which ensures that there is a direct relation between the input differences and
output differences after r rounds.
Lastly, note that the MILP-solver can only alter the
decision variables found in the object function, these being the initial input difference and
either the input differences for the S-box and/or the probability bits, depending on the
objective function used. In both cases, they model the transitions at each S-box, so these
are the only variables that the MILP-solver can modify. All other variables are changed,
as they all are linked together by the constraints created.
From LP to MILP
Considering all the constraints needed for the method to work, they all consist of binary vari-
ables in practice. This thus makes it an Integer-Programming problem, which as mentioned
in section 2.2.2 is NP-Complete.
To improve the method and make the runtime faster, many of the variables are normally
instead set to be continuous variables and thus turning it into a MILP-model. Experimental
results show that this relaxation speeds up the method, as it is now closer to a normal LP-
model than a pure IP-model. And as mentioned in section 2.2.2, LP-models are easier than
IP-models.
In the case of this model, only the 64 input-bit variable and 64 output variables, as well as
the variables representing these 2 · 16 nibbles, are forced to be binary. As all the variables
are linked based on the presented constraints, all the variables will in the end become binary
variables. Tests will be performed later in section 6.2, to determine what variable types
the other non-binary variables should have, to be most efficient for the solving of these
LP-models.
This method of relaxing binary variables was first suggested by Borghoff et al. and Mouha
et al. also reference that paper and mentioned it in their original paper on this method.

5.3.4
Gurobi module and solving algorithm
Gurobi reads the supplied LP-file and creates a model-object of the LP-problem. For solv-
ing MILP-models, Gurobi makes use of the branch-and-bound algorithm. This algortihm
starts with taking the original MILP-problem and performing a LP-relaxtation, meaning
that all constraints forcing the variables to be integers are removed, resulting in a "normal"
LP-model. Upon solving this model with the standard algorithms (E.g. simplex or interior-
point algorithms), the solution will normally contain decision variables with fractional values.
The overall algorithm will then branch, creating two additional LP-models with additional
constraints, one having a lower bound and the other one an upper bound on this specific
decision variable. As an example, say that the decision variable z6 had a value of 13.4. The
two resulting LP-models will then have the constraints z6 ≤13 and z6 ≥14 respectively.
This tells the solver, that the optimal value for z6 is either 13 or 14 for current variants of
the models. This branching then continues for the two new LP-models and new constraints
are slowly added to narrow down the optimal solution. Note that for each new LP-model
created, the model will be easier to solve than its parent, as an extra constraint has been
added and thus the solution space has been further restricted. Essentially, this is a binary
search tree, with the nodes as LP-models.

When solving large models, the solver will make use of multiple threads to run concurrently
to solve the LP-models in different nodes of the search tree. Thus increasing the number of
CPUs and threads results in a faster runtime.
In the output files created by Gurobi, there is a so-called Gap-value. This gap represents
the difference between the current upper and lower bounds. For an LP-model requiring
maximization, one will always know the lower bound as being the current best solution. The
upper bound, also known as the best bound, can be calculated by taking the minimum of
the current discovered leaf nodes, which represent the worst current solution. When the
difference between these is 0, we know that the optimal solution has been found.[Foub]

This chapter will focus on the modeling, testing of the models, and analysis of the test re-
sults. First, the complexity of the targeted LP-models will be described, to give an overview
of how CRAFT is represented as an LP-model. Secondly, optimization tests will be done for

developed software solution. The tests will involve finding the best possible characteristics
for r ≤10 rounds and observing how the complexity of the corresponding LP problems

to give an insight into the efficiency of this method of using a MILP-solver to perform dif-

Additionally, some tests against a larger number of r > 10 rounds will also be performed,
to hopefully find characteristics with high probabilities. Lastly, these characteristics will
be used to find differentials with the same input and output values, in hopes of finding a
significant, high-probability differential to use in a practical attack against CRAFT.
The tests performed will use either of the objective functions defined in section 4.2.4: Either
the MILP-solver will try and minimize the number of active S-boxes or it will try to prioritize
taking the best transitions with the highest probability. We will call the former method for
the o-method for "original" and the latter method for the p-method for "probability".

6.2
Optimization
During the developing and testing phase of the software, it was found that the data and
time complexity of solving the LP-models increased way faster than expected. To improve
the performance and reduce the solution space, a couple of optimizations were implemented,
as described in the following sections.
Page 35 of 53
==PAGE:43==
Differential Cryptanalysis of CRAFT using a MILP-solver
6.2. Optimization
Restricted input/output states
The input and output states of the characteristics were restricted, so that these states at most
could have 8 active S-boxes. The reason for this restriction is that most characteristics
start and end with a low number of active S-boxes. This reduction greatly improves the time
complexity of the LP-program, as far fewer combinations of input and output differences to
the cipher now exists in the solution space.
Tuning of Gurobi parameters
Gurobi has several parameters, that can be modified to customize the solver’s behavior.
Three of these parameters have been modified, to improve the capabilities of the solver for
this type of LP-problems.
• Improved search strategy: The most important modified parameter is the MIPFo-
cus-parameter. This parameter controls the high-level search strategy for the solver.
[Gur23b] It has 3 different priorities:
– 1: Find good feasible solutions.
– 2: Prove optimality.
– 3: Find better bound (=better solution)
Of these, option 3 seemed to be the best, as we exactly were looking to find the best
optimal solution as quickly as possible. This would force the solver to always try to
improve the solution during its traversing of the solution space.
• Cutting plane strategy: "Cutting Planes" refers to an advanced sub-strategy within
the overall algorithm used by the solver to find solutions. This strategy can be set to
behave as either "Conservative" or "Aggressive". Gurobi recommended using aggres-
sive for complex solutions, so this option was chosen. [Gur23b]
• Presolve strategy: The solver performs a Presolve before solving the actual program.
Presolving consists of simplifying the model by removing redundant variables, normally
by combining similar variables into a single one. This parameter also has two main
options, either "Conservative" or "Aggressive". The more aggressive approach can
lead to a tighter and thus faster model, so the aggressive option was also chosen for
this parameter.
For testing the parameters, a couple of 5-round attacks using the p-method were performed.
The MIPFocus option tested was 3, while both the "Cutting Planes" and "Presolve" pa-
rameters were tested with their "Aggressive"-setting. The performance results can be seen
in table 6.2, which shows that setting all parameters to the previously suggested values was
the most efficient configuration.

6.3
Tests
The developed software solution was used to perform different tests, based on the four
different categories below. The overall goal of the tests was to find as good characteristics
for as high rounds r as possible and use the collected characteristics and data to evaluate the
methods and optimization applied, as well as common patterns found in the characteristics,
that could reveal weaknesses in CRAFT.
• Low-round tests For rounds r ≤10, tests were performed using all 4 combinations of
methods and tuning. It was expected that the duration to solve the LP programs would
rise exponentially and therefore a point would be reached when finding a solution will
be unfeasible time-wise, in relation to the project deadline. Thus this limit was set to
r ≤10 and the goal of these first tests was to try to solve as many of these LP-models
as possible.
• High-round tests For rounds r > 10, tests were also performed to find good charac-
teristics, but not nearly as many tests as for rounds r ≤10 were run, as the tests for
high-level rounds had large runtimes. Only the o-method was used here, as it was the
only one that could complete the tests within the time limit.
• Distribution of characteristics Thirdly, an interesting observation would also be
to see how quickly the probability of the characteristics at certain rounds r degrade.
Given the top 100 000 characteristics and their probabilities, insights could be gained by
observing how many "good" characteristics there were at that many rounds. Collecting
additional solutions other than the optimal one increases the time complexity of the
program, so this was only done for r ≤10 rounds using the o-method.
• High-round differentials Based on the best characteristic from each attack against
high r rounds, differentials were made for each of them based on their individual input
and output states to evaluate their efficiency. Ultimately, the goal here was to see how
high the probability of these differentials could get.
• Combination of methods Lastly, a few tests were also performed to test the effi-
ciency of combining both methods. This was done by first using the o-method to find
an optimal solution and afterward continuing from that solution with the p-method.
Regarding these characteristics, note that this was a single-key attack. Thus there was no
modification of the tweakeys to do a related-key attack.

ensure that xδ = yδ to keep these patterns throughout multiple rounds, while also picking
transitions with probabilities 2−2.

7.2.1
Probability distribution over individual characteristic
An interesting observation, is that all the characteristics found for CRAFT start and end
with a lot of active S-boxes. Normally for characteristics from other lightweight block ci-
phers, the number of active S-boxes peaks either midway or only towards the end. [ZZP18]
[Ban+15] [KY22] This can be because it is easier for the solver to set up an input state, so
that reductions in active S-boxes can be achieved in the first half, as opposed to reaching
such a state midway through the characteristic and doing the reduction in the latter half.
Ultimately, this is based on the components and logic implemented in CRAFT.

7.2. Discussion on Gurobi and results
7.2.2
Determinism of the Gurobi solver
Another interesting observation is that the algorithm employed by the Gurobi-solver always
finds the same optimal solution, even though there exist multiple solutions with the same,
optimal objective value. This hints at the solver having some other parameters to decide
which set of optimal solutions is the preferred one. In addition to this, based on the tests
concerning the distribution of characteristics, the distribution is always the same for the
same method/search strategy used, e.g. no different pool of solutions is found, no matter
what path through the solution space is taken. This would further suggest, that the solver
has an internal ranking of solutions, even though their objective values are equal.
Concerning the runtimes, there seem to be minor variances, which become larger as the LP
model becomes more complex. Thus the solver is not fully deterministic and there exists
some random element. Concerning the individual solving of an LP model, this could be
a difference in the starting solution, which would result in a longer or shorter path to the
optimal solution. This difference could also be due to a change in the pivot rules, though it
does not make much sense to randomize that component. The randomness could also exist in
the larger branch-and-bound algorithm: As the process of finding the best optimal solution
is represented as a search tree, differences in the search paths taken by the individual threads
could also lead to significant differences in run times.
7.2.3
Gurobi and Differential Cryptanalysis
During the test and the results, a couple of points were noticed regarding Gurobi and its
efficiency in solving these types of LP-problems.
One interesting observation is that none of the characteristics have any case, where three
non-zero nibbles are XOR’rd together to output 0. This can either be because the solver
based on the objective function never considers such a situation to be necessary to find the
optimal characteristic or, in the worst case, that the modeling of the cipher does not correctly
capture such a situation. This will require further research.
Additionally, the solver was not good at finding diverse characteristics. For a cryptanalyst,
it can be more favorable to have the top characteristics be more different from each other to
analyze different patterns and discover other weaknesses, though they still would be ranked
according to their number of active S-boxes or probability. From the point of the Gurobi
solver, it makes sense that all the top solutions found are almost equal, as these solutions are
found nearby the optimal one. Nevertheless, one could possibly tinker with the display of
the results to ensure a more diverse ranking of the results, than the current way of ranking
that Gurobi uses.
Concerning the differences in running times of the methods, it makes sense that the p-method
is slower, as it contains more variables and constraints than the o-method, thus being more
complex (See section 6.1). Still, it was unexpected that it would be that much slower than
the o-method. Based on this, each method still has its strengths and weaknesses, with the
o-method being faster and the p-method being more precise. As shown in section 6.3.3, an
ideal way would be to combine them to utilize both of their strengths and thus improve the
whole method of using MILP-solvers to perform differential cryptanalysis.

Apart from the modeling, the specific MILP-solver also played a big part in the efficiency of

7.5
Future work
Further optimization
Gurobi has a lot of different parameters, that also could be tested and tuned to find the best
configuration. One could also force the Gurobi-solver to try other algorithms for solving
LP-problems, to see if one would be more efficient for this specific type of model. Gurobi
also supports the concurrent solving of LP-models, so by having access to more powerful
computing nodes that could work together, one could achieve significant performance im-
provements.
Only the Gurobi solver was used in this project, but there exist many more different solvers
that could be used. Some of these might be better suited to solve these types of LP-models,
as they might make use of other algorithms and search strategies.
One could also try to optimize the LP-model itself to try and tighten the solution space.
Some papers have come up with further optimizations, but none that seem as groundbreaking
as the original papers by Mouha et al. and Sun et al.. [YK21]
Program for finding differentials
This project mainly sought to find good characteristics, but an extension could be done to
specifically focus on finding good (possibly optimal) differentials. The easiest way would
be to add input and output constraints to fix these states and find all possible solutions in
the solution space. The sum of all these probabilities would then represent the efficiency of
using such a differential. The running time should be way faster, as the solution space is
greatly reduced compared to the tests in this report, but as there are both 1616 input and
output states, there are approximately 3.4 · 1038 different combinations. Thus finding the
best differential is a very challenging task, but if a cryptanalyst has an assumption regarding
some input/output combinations that might work, such an implementation could be made
on a smaller scale.
A second, but way more complex way, would be to change the whole LP-model to instead
represent finding the best differential is in over r-round. This would require a way more
complex LP-model and just designing such a model would be a project in itself.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This project sought to perform differential cryptanalysis on the lightweight block cipher
CRAFT using a MILP-solver. For this, the method initially developed by Mouha et al. and
further improved by Sun et al. was used to model CRAFT as an LP-model, that could be
solved using Gurobi. A software solution written in Python was developed to automate this
modeling process and also make it flexible, so a user only had to supply simple information
regarding the specifications of the cipher to model it. Additionally, the software solution
also supported the solving process and presented the differential characteristics found in an
easy-to-read format. All LP-models created and characteristics found were valid with respect
to the specifications of CRAFT.
For the efficiency of the method itself, it was found that minimizing the number of active
S-boxes was faster at finding characteristics, than maximizing the probability of the char-
acteristics. In contrast, the latter method was better at finding characteristics with the
optimal probability, than the former method. Results also hinted at a combination of the
two methods being the fastest at finding high-probability characteristics. Additionally, tun-
ing the parameters of Gurobi to perform a more aggressive search led to faster runtimes,
but led to characteristics with even worse probabilities. Depending on the search strategy
used, the pool of characteristics found would differ from one another, as the ranking of the
characteristics would be different. Nevertheless, the method is an efficient tool to quickly
and automatically find characteristics for lightweight block ciphers.
Regarding CRAFT, the best characteristics found were for 12-,14- and 16-rounds with proba-
bilities of 2−88, 2−108, and 2−120, respectively. Multiple of the best characteristics found were
made up of the same patterns throughout multiple rounds, as well as only using a small set
of transitions with probabilities 2−2 between differences. In the best case, this combination
allowed characteristics where no diffusion happened for 10 rounds. This property was mainly
caused by the PermuteNibbles-function not properly diffusing all nibbles in a column to all
other columns. Ultimately, this weakness is caused by the interaction of all components in
CRAFT and thus is a result of its design.
