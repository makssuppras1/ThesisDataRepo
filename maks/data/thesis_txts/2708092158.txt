Species-specific effects of car tire leachates on zooplankton 
 
Evanthia Bournaka 
National Institute of Aquatic Resources-DTU Aqua, Kemitorvet, Building 202 DK-2800 
Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark 
 
Abstract 
 
Tire wear is a major contributor to microplastic pollution. Thousands of tons of Tire Wear Particles (TWP) enter 
the aquatic environment every year. In contact with water, TWP can leach plastic additives such as  organic 
compounds and metals. However, little is known about the toxicity of TWP leachates to plankton and their 
environmental risks. In this study, we tested the lethal effects of different TWP leachate concentrations (0.005-
5 g L-1) on five species of marine copepods: Acartia tonsa, Temora longicornis, Centropages hamatus, 
Oithona davisae and Amonardia normanni. Furthermore, we assessed the effect of a low TWP concentration 
(0.05 g L-1) on feeding and pellet production of T. longicornis, C. hamatus and O. davisae at high and low 
food  (Rhodomonas salina) concentrations. TWP leachates were toxic to all the studied species, with LC50 
(72h) ranged from 0.3 to 3.2 g L-1 We observed dose and time-dependent responses and species-specific 
toxicity, with the calanoids being the most sensitive, followed by O. davisae. A. normanni, showed high 
tolerance even in high concentrations. Copepod’s response to TWP leachates was contingent on a) 
swimming/feeding behavior, b) increased sensitivity under starvation stress, and (c) drinking of water (oral 
intake). Clearance and ingestion rates were lower in the most of leachate treatments, but pellet production 
rates were relatively higher indicating possible stress. Nonetheless, we cannot assume that a 24 h  exposure to 
0.05 g L-1 can have a substantial effect on marine copepods. Our results indicate that copepod’s behavior and 
physiology should be considered in ecotoxicological studies. Given the importance of plankton in the 
functioning of marine ecosystems, more research on the effects of TWP and their associated additives on 
plankton communities is crucial to evaluate the environmental risks of TWP pollution.    
 
 
Keywords: tire wear particles, leachates, copepods, lethal and sub-lethal effects 
 
1. Introduction 
 
Plastic pollution is one of the major global 
environmental concerns. Global plastic production 
reached 368 million tons in 2019, with Europe 
producing 58 million tons (PlasticsEurope 2020). It 
has been estimated that 8 million tons of plastic 
litter enter the ocean every year (IUCN 2018) and 
the amount of plastics in the aquatic ecosystems is 
projected to triple by 2040 (UNEP 2021). The mass 
plastic production that began in 1950s resulted in 
plastic accumulation in the open ocean, coastlines 
and deep sea over the years (Barnes et al. 2009), 
with the highest densities found in the Northern 
Hemisphere (Barnes et al. 2009). Furthermore, 
considering plastic’s persistent nature, it can 
endure many decades in natural environments 
(Barnes et al. 2009) and therefore marine 
organisms that are exposed to plastic can be 
affected through entanglement and ingestion of 
particles (Derraik 2002).   
 
Physical processes taking place in the oceans 
result in the fragmentation of plastic into small 
particles 
(size 
<5mm) 
 
forming 
so-called 
microplastics 
(GESAMP 
2012, 
2015). 
Microplastics (MP) could also stem from polyester 
fibers, polyethylene (O’Brine and Thompson 
2010), polystyrene fragments (Davidson 2012), 
==PAGE:2==
 
 
2 
 
and synthetic polymer products used in cosmetics 
(Lassen et al. 2015). MP vary in size, shape and 
color due to their origin and the physical processes 
that can modify them, and these properties are what 
determine their fate in the oceans (Lehtiniemi et al. 
2018). MP usually reach estuaries and open oceans 
via freshwater systems, which makes the ocean the 
primary MP sink (Luo et al. 2019). Moreover, 
coastlines and mid-ocean gyres can be considered 
significant areas for MP accumulation, as they have 
been found to have the highest abundance of MP 
(Cole et al. 2011). For instance, in the North 
Atlantic gyre the highest concentration was 
mapped in the open ocean, showing ca. 20000 
pieces km-2 (Lavender 2010).. 
 
Micronized rubber particles released from car 
tires are a major source of microplastics 
worldwide, but only a few studies have considered 
them as part of the microplastic pollution (Sundt et 
al. 2014; Lassen et al. 2015; Kreider et al. 2010; 
Knight et al. 2020; Tamis et al. 2021). Tire wear 
particles (TWP) were first studied in 1960s, where 
tire rubber was identified in road dust using a 
combination of pyrolysis and chromatography 
techniques (Thompson, 1966). In the 1990s, 
research focused on the impact of the leaking 
products coming from TWP (leachates) on aquatic 
biota (Day et al. 1993, Boxall and Maltby 1997). 
Recently, there is an increased focus on TWP as 
part of microplastic pollution (Halle et al. 2021). 
 
TWP can form by the mechanical abrasion of 
car tires with the road surface (Rogge et al. 1993). 
They can be emmited in the air and spread and also 
be transported with runoff into water bodies, soil 
and sewers (Kole et al. 2017).  According to a 
recent 
review 
(Wagner 
et 
al. 
2018), 
an 
approximation of generated TWP was 1.3 x 106 
tons year-1 within the European Union (2014). 
Sundt et al. (2014) stated that a substantial portion 
of TWP could reach water bodies and the ocean. It 
is estimated that 11 x 103 tons year-1 could enter 
water bodies only from the German highways. 
Also, TWP were found in natural environment in 
abundances ranging from 0.6 ± 0.33 to 65 ± 7.36 in 
5 mL, while the abundances decreased with 
distance from the road (Knight et al. 2020). In 
Norway, almost 4000 tons year -1 might end up in 
the ocean (Peter Sundt, Per-Erik Schultze 2014). 
However, in general TWP distribution and 
abundance lacks of empirical data ( Wagner et al. 
2018; Knight et al. 2020). 
 
Car tires consist of complex materials, 
containing a variety of chemical substances such as 
synthetic and natural rubbers along with black 
carbon, mineral oils, antioxidants and antiozonants, 
plasticizers and many more components (Turner 
and Rice 2010). More specifically, Capolupo et al. 
(2020), identified organic compounds such as 
acetophenone, 
benzothiazole, 
n-
cyclohexylforamide, phthalide and bisphenol A in 
high concentrations and also inorganic compounds 
such as zinc (Zn), manganese (Mn), cobalt (Co), 
antimony (Sb) and lead (Pb). Moreover, Halsband 
et al. (2020) identified a few polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons 
(PAHs) 
along 
with 
the 
abovementioned 
organic 
and 
inorganic 
compounds. 
 
Compounds found in TWP leachates can be 
potentially toxic for aquatic organisms but are still 
not thoroughly investigated (Wagner et al. 2018). 
Some studies have shown that TWP leachates can 
affect a wide range of marine organisms by causing 
toxic effects (Day et al. 1993; Turner and Rice 
2010; Bejgarn et al. 2015; Beiras et al. 2019; Halle 
et al. 2021).  Specifically, Capolupo et al. (2020), 
showed that TWP rubber leachates inhibited 
growth of two phytoplankton species and affected 
the embryotic development of mussel Mytilus 
galloprovincialis. Moreover, Koski et al. (2021) 
observed a reduction in feeding and pellet 
production rates of two common copepods, Acartia 
tonsa and Temora longicornis, when exposed to a 
high concentration of TWP and/or their leachates. 
Two recent laboratory studies found acute toxicity 
of TWP leachates on  adult and nauplii of Acartia 
tonsa (Mejlholm 2021) and three phytoplankton 
species (Page 2021).  
 
Besides the direct effects on copepods’ vital 
rates, there is also evidence that they experience 
oxidative stress leading to activation of various 
==PAGE:3==
 
 
3 
 
antioxidant enzymes, when exposed to rubber 
leachates (Lehtiniemi et al. 2021). This evidence 
agrees with studies focusing on copepod exposure 
to chemicals such as endocrine disruptor chemicals 
(bisphenol A) or heavy metals (Zn, Pb etc.) that can 
be found in TWP leachates. When copepods were 
exposed to these compounds a series of defense 
mechanisms was activated (detoxification enzymes 
and proteins; Seo et al. 2006; Lee et al. 2007)). 
 
Marine zooplankton constitutes the link 
between primary producers and higher trophic 
levels (Turner 2004; Steinberg and Landry 2017), 
with copepods as the dominant zooplankton group 
representing 
70-90% 
of 
mesozooplankton 
abundance (Mauchline et al. 1998; Steinberg and 
Landry 2017). Furthermore, copepods are crucial 
components in the biological pump as they 
contribute to carbon sequestration, recycling of 
nutrients and food web productivity (Steinberg and 
Landry 2017). Thus, copepod’s exposure to TWP 
and their leachates might result in particle ingestion 
and further chemical assimilation in the gut 
passage, potentially leading to bioaccumulation of 
harmful substances through the food web. Also, the 
effects can be direct by influencing copepod’s vital 
rates, resulting in changes in ecosystem functioning 
(Koski et al. 2021). Furthermore, copepods can be 
considered appropriate indicators of environmental 
stress of ecosystem’s health (Hays et al. 2005) and 
represent model species in ecotoxicological 
experiments (Kwok et al. 2015). Their life history 
traits (Kwok et al. 2015) and their sensitivity to 
fluctuations in environmental factors (Siokou-
Frangou 1996) makes them appropriate for toxicity 
evaluation related studies. 
 
The main objective of this study was to 
investigate the effects of TWP leachates on 
different zooplankton species and evaluate whether 
the effects are modulated by their size, physiology 
and behavior. The work consists of two parts: First, 
acute toxicity experiments were carried out in the 
laboratory by exposing adults of five copepod 
species (the cyclopoid Oithona davisae, the 
harpacticoid 
Amonardia 
normanni 
and 
the 
calanoids T. longicornis, Centropages hamatus and 
A. tonsa) to different leachate concentrations to 
estimate the median lethal concentrations (LC50). 
Second, T. longicornis, C. hamatus and O. davisae 
were exposed to leachates from 0.05 g TWP L-1 
under saturating and limiting food concentrations, 
and the sub-lethal effects on feeding, pellet and egg 
production were estimated. The five species differ 
in size, feeding mode and reproductive strategies, 
allowing for the investigation of the mechanisms 
behind the species response. Moreover, the 
selected experimental copepod species/genus are 
ecologically relevant.  O. davisae, can be found all 
over the global oceans and usually is the dominant 
species amongst the metazooplankton (Böttger-
schnack et al. 1989; Hay et al. 1991; Nielsen et al. 
1993), the three calanoids are usually dominant 
coastal species (Fransz et al. 1998; Nielsen and 
Munk 1998) and the harpacticoid represents a semi 
benthic copepod species (Koski et al. 2005), thus 
they cover a wide distribution range in the oceans. 
 
We hypothesized that copepod’s response to 
TWP leachates will be influenced by the leachate 
concentration and exposure times, copepod 
properties 
and 
environment. 
Therefore, 
we 
hypothesized that the copepod’s response will 
depend on 1) the concentration of TWP leachates, 
2) the size and feeding mode of copepods and 3) 
food concentration.  
 
2. Methods 
 
2.1 Copepods and algae 
 
The five copepod species, A. tonsa, T. longicornis, 
C. hamatus, O. davisae and A. normanni, used in 
the experiments were reared in the laboratory of the 
National Institute of Aquatic Resources in 
Technical University of Denmark. The cultures 
were kept in 30 L buckets filled with filtered sea 
water (FSW), with constant aeration, 28 ‰ salinity 
and temperature of ca. 16 oC. 
 
A. tonsa, T. longicornis and C. hamatus were 
fed with a mix of the cryptophyte Rhodomonas 
salina, the diatom Thalassiosira weissflogii and the

week. O. davisae was fed three times a week with 
the dinoflagellate Oxyrrhis marina and A. 
normanni was fed twice a week with T. weissflogii. 
All phytoplankton cultures were grown at 18oC and 
approximately 28 ‰ salinity, in B1 media (Hansen 
1989) with constant light exposure. 
 
The copepods used in experiments were 
maximum 5-6 weeks old, to ensure similar ages 
among species. The prosome length (PL) of 30

2.2 TWP and leachate extraction 
 
A new car tire (Imperial 145/70-13 71T- 
Snowdragon HP-Vinterdæk) was bought from a 
commercial store (thansen.dk) in August 2021. To 
generate leachates for the acute toxicity tests and 
chemical characterization, a standardized protocol 
was used (Beiras and Almeda 2021, unpublished). 
Firstly, the tire’s outer layer was cut into bands 
(micronization process). Subsequently, TWPs were 
produced by grinding the strips using a stainless-
steel pneumatic milling cutter and were collected in 
a stainless-steel tray.  Subsequently, liquid nitrogen 
was added in the tray for the size-fractioning 
process where the TWP were sieved through a 
standard steel sieve with a certified 250 μm steel 
mesh. The process was performed by hand, using a 
paint brush. 
 
Fractioned particles’ size was confirmed by 
measuring a subsample suspended in a Tween-80 
0.01% solution, with a Beckman Multisizer3 
Coulter counter (CC). The results confirmed that 
98% of the particles had a diameter <250 μm (Fig.  
1).  
Afterwards, 5 g of TWP were incubated in 1 L 
glass 
bottles, 
with 
screw 
caps 
with 
polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) protected seal, 
containing autoclaved FSW with a salinity of 28 ‰ 
and pH of 8.17. The bottles were covered with 
aluminum 
foil 
to 
ensure 
dark 
conditions. 
Additionally, the bottles were placed in a rotating 
plankton wheel (1 rpm) for 72 h in a 20 oC room. 
During this process, called lixiviation the leachates 
(solutes) separate from the TWP. 
After 72 h, the incubated water was filtered 
(filtration process) in a vacuum filtration system 
using glass-fiber filters (Whatman GF/F filters 0.8 
μm). The filtration process is not very effective 
==PAGE:5==
 
 
5 
 
regarding nanoplastics (<1 μm), therefore, they 
might have been present in small quantities in the 
leachate water (Beiras and Almeda 2021, 
unpublished).

coulter counter and a photo showing TWP under stereo

Finally, 3 L of the produced leachates were 
stored in -20 oC in dark for 4 months until the 
chemical analysis (organic compounds) and 
another 3 L was sent directly after production for 
metal analysis. The chemical analysis of TWP and 
leachates was conducted at SERTOX “Servicio de 
Toxicología Clínica y Analítica”   of the University 
of Las Palmas de Gran Canaria (ULPGC). For 
extraction of selected organophosphorus flame 
retardants (OPFRs), organochlorine pesticides 
(OCPs), polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and 
polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) the 
method described in Camacho et al. 2019 was used. 
TWP and their leachates were analyzed by using a 
gas chromatograph coupled to a mass spectrometer 
GC System 7890B and Triple Quad 7010 (Agilent 
Technologies, Palo Alto, CA, USA).  
 
2.3 Experimental setup 
 
Acute toxicity tests and sub-lethal tests under 
different food conditions were carried out (Table 
2). First, acute toxicity tests were executed by 
exposing the five copepod species to different 
leachate concentrations, in the absence of food, 
using mortality as an end point. All the experiments 
were conducted in triplicates with a range of TWP 
leachate concentrations (0.005, 0.05, 0.5, 1.25 and 
2.5 g L-1) originating from dilution of the 100% 
stock solution corresponding to 5 g L-1. For T. 
longicornis, an extra concentration of 0.0025 g L-1 
was added. Salinity of the tested solution was 28 ‰ 
and the pH was 8.07, declining from the initial pH 
of autoclaved FSW by 0.1. The experiments took 
place in 18 ± 0.5 oC temperature-controlled room 
in total darkness.

Approximately 20 adults were placed in 30 mL 
bottles containing the test solutions. The mortality 
was checked every 24 h over the course of 78 h by 
pouring the bottle content into a PYREX Glass 
evaporating dish and inspecting it using a 
stereoscoping 
microscope. 
A 
copepod 
was 
considered dead if it was not moving for over 30 s, 
after touching it with a glass pipette. Copepod 
mortality rate was calculated as a percentage of 
dead individuals from the initial number. 
Based on the outcome of the acute toxicity 
tests, the concentration used for estimating the sub-
lethal effects was selected to be 0.05 g L-1 for all 
species. Three species were chosen for this 
experimental series as their feeding modes differ. 
T. longicornis is a feeding current feeder (Kiørboe 
==PAGE:6==
 
 
6 
 
2011), while C. hamatus is a cruise feeder (Tiselius 
and Jonsson 1990; Kiørboe 2011) and O. davisae is 
an active ambush feeder (Kiørboe 2011). The 
experiments were run with two concentrations of R. 
salina that corresponded to high (10000 cells mL-
1) and low (2000 cells mL-1) food, and the lethal 
(mortality) and sub-lethal (feeding and fecal pellet 
production) effects were measured.  
Firstly, approximately 75 copepods, were 
added to 1000 mL bottles with the targeted 
experimental 
food 
concentrations 
for 
acclimatization. In addition, a grazing control was 
included (absence of copepods) for following 
cryptophyte’s growth and cell concentration under 
leachate exposure. The bottles were placed on a 
rotating plankton wheel turning at ca. 1 round per 
minute in a 18oC room with no lights. After 24 h of 
acclimatization, the content of the bottles was 
filtered onto a submerged sieve > 100 μm. 5-10 
actively swimming female copepods and 2 males 
were added in triplicate 500 mL bottles with the 
experimental treatments. All experiments run in 
triplicates. After 24 h, the treatment bottles were 
filtered onto a 200 μm to remove the copepods 
which were rinsed into a petri dish to check their 
mortality. The filtered water was collected into a 
beaker from which 10-20 mL were taken for 
measuring the phytoplankton concentration in the 
CC. The rest of the beaker content was filtered onto 
a 20 μm sieve. The sieve content was rinsed into a 
petri dish for counting faecal pellets (FPP, pellets 
ind.-1 d-1). 
Approximately, 30 pellets were measured 
(diameter and length) and their volume (VFP, μm3) 
was calculated by assuming cylindrical shape. To 
estimate the carbon in fecal pellets (CFP, μg C 
pellet-1), a carbon to pellet volume relationship was 
used: CFP = 4.3 × 10-8 × VFP (Swalethorp et al. 
2011). Afterwards, the specific fecal pellet 
production was calculated using the following 
formula: SPP = FPP x CFP / CFEM, where CFEM 
is the female carbon content (Swalethorp et al. 
2011). Grazing rates (clearance; mL ind.-1 d-1 and 
ingestion; μg C μg C d-1) were estimated according 
to Frost (1972). Specific ingestion rates have been 
calculated using R. salina carbon weight=58 x 10-6 
(Amin et al. 2011). 
 
2.4 Statistical analysis 
 
Data from the acute toxicity tests was tested for 
differences 
in 
mortality 
among 
leachate 
concentrations, species and between sexes for each 
species, using non-parametric analysis of variance, 
Kruskal-Wallis test when the data was not 
following 
a 
normal 
distribution. 
Two-way 
ANOVA was used when the data was normally 
distributed. The differences in mortality between 
the control and leachate concentrations, as well as 
among species, were tested using Dunnett’s or 
Tukey’s (normal distribution) post hoc tests. 
Moreover, the differences in clearance, ingestion 
and pellet production rates among the tested 
treatments were tested using One-way ANOVA 
and Tukey’s post hoc test.  For the null hypothesis 
rejection, a level of significance of  α ≤ 0.05 was 
set. Normality and homoscedasticity were tested 
using 
Shapiro-Wilk 
and 
Levene’s 
test, 
respectively. The analysis was performed in 
RStudio V.1.3.1056 and SigmaPlot 14.0.  
 
3. Results 
 
3.1 TWP 
and 
leachate: 
Chemical 
characterization 
 
A range of compounds was found in TWP (Table 
3), including organochlorine pesticides (OCPs), 
OPFRs, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon (PAHs) 
and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs). High 
concentrations of PAHs were present in the TWP 
where pyrene had the highest, 10973.33 ng g-1. 
Other PAHs found (ranked from highest to lowest 
concentration) were: fluoranthene, phenanthrene, 
benzo[ghi]perylene, 
naphthalene 
and 
acenaphthene. OCPs, OPFRs and PCBs were in 
lower concentrations compared to the PAHs (Table 
3). Most of the compounds found in the TWP, were 
not detected in the leachates. Naphthalene and Tris 
(2-chloroisopropyl) phosphate were found in

mL-1, 
respectively.

3.2 Acute toxicity tests 
 
Substantial differences in tolerance to TWP 
leachates among copepods species, specifically 
between calanoids, cyclopoids and harpacticoids, 
were observed. The most sensitive species were A. 
tonsa and T. longicornis. In general, zooplankton 
mortality 
increased 
with 
higher 
leachate 
concentrations and longer exposure time (Fig.  2). 
In the highest concentration all individuals died 
after 48 h (Fig.  2 A, C and D), apart from T. 
longicornis where all individuals died after 72 h 
(Fig.  2 B) and A. normanni where mortality 
reached 96.2% after 72 h (Fig.  2 E). The 
relationship between mortality and TWP leachate 
concentration for A. tonsa and T. longicornis was 
relatively similar, described by the sigmoid dose 
response curve for each time point (Fig.  2 A and 
B), although T. longicornis seems to more tolerant 
during the first 24 h. The response curve was 
different for C. hamatus, showing a smoother slope 
(Fig.  2 C). The dose response relationship for the 
other two species, O. davisae and A. normanni, was 
also well described with a sigmoid curve (Fig.  2 D 
and E).  
 
LC50 varied among species and generally 
decreased with exposure time. A. normanni had an 
average LC50 of 3.6 ± 0.6 g L-1 which was the 
highest among the species (Table 4). C. hamatus 
and O. davisae exhibited relatively similar values, 
although C. hamatus had a slightly higher LC50 
after 24 and 48 h, and O. davisae had a slightly 
higher LC50 after 72 h. However, O. davisae data 
has better fit (r2=0.94), compared to C. hamatus 
(r2=0.72), indicating that the LC50 after 72 h for C. 
hamatus might be overestimated.  A. tonsa and T. 
longicornis were the least tolerant species with a 
low LC50, of 0.3 ± 0.1 and 0.4 ± 0.1 g L-1 
respectively.

In general, the sensitivity of the different 
copepods ranked as A. tonsa > T. longicornis > C. 
hamatus > O. davisae > A. normanni (Table 4). The 
sensitivity was not size dependent (Fig.  3), as the 
smaller O. davisae had higher LC50 compared to 
the calanoids. Also, A. normanni has similar size 
range with A. tonsa and T. longicornis but was the 
most tolerant species. A. tonsa and T. longicornis 
experienced 100% mortality in concentrations 
≥1.25 g L-1  (Table 5). For concentrations between 
0.0025-0.5 g L-1, the three calanoids showed 
similar mortality ranging from 30.6 to 64.4%, 
whereas O. davisae exhibited a much lower 
mortality, ranging between 9.7-10.9% (0.0025-0.5 
g L-1). A. normanni had the lowest mortality in

Clearance and ingestion rate for T. longicornis 
were not significantly different between the control 
and leachate treatments (One-way ANOVA; p >

average ingestion rates in the HF were 1.2 and 0.6 
μg C μg C-1 d-1, for the control and leachate 
treatment, accordingly. In the LF treatments 
ingestion was 0.4 μg C μg C-1 d-1 for the control and 
0.3 μg C μg C-1 d-1 for the leachate treatment. On 
the contrary, C. hamatus (Fig.  4 and Table 6)

In general, clearance and ingestion rates were

4. Discussion 
 
4.1 Chemical compounds in TWP and leachates 
 
The focus of this study was to investigate the effect 
of TWP leaching compounds. Compounds found in 
TWP and its leachates are very complex, including 
organic additives and metals (Turner and Rice 
2010; Capolupo et al. 2020; Koski et al. 2021). 
Also, our chemical analysis showed high 
concentrations of PAHs, OPFRs, OCPs and PCBs 
in the TWP. In leachates, only one PAH was 
present in our leachates (naphthalene) and one 
OPFR (Tris (2-chloroisopropyl)phosphate). Metal 
analysis was not available on time; therefore, we 
will use data found in the literature for the 
discussion.  
 
It is challenging to compare the chemical 
composition found in our study with others, as for 
example, Capolupo et al. (2020) and Halsband et 
al. (2020) focused on acetophenone, benzothiazole, 
bisphenol A (BPA), n-cyclohexylformamide, 
phthalide 
and 
phenols, 
phthalates, 
PAHs, 
benzothiazole, respectively.  Our results agree with 
a recent study (Halle et al. 2021) that found 
relatively similar concentrations of PAHs in TWP 
suspensions including other organic compounds 
such as benzothiazole (3.90 ± 0.91 μg g-1). In TWP 
leachates, Halle et al. (2021) detected naphthalene 
in concentrations <0.01 ng mL-1. The rest of PAHs 
that were not detected in our study, were also found 
by Halle et al. (2021) in concentrations <0.01 ng 
mL-1, except for acenaphthene and phenanthrene 
(10 ng mL-1). Its important to mention that leachate 
production methods differ between our study and 
Halle et al. (2021) therefore that could have 
affected the concentrations. Our results suggest that 
the organic compounds used for the chemical 
analysis did not leach out of the TWPs, or their 
concentrations were below the detection limit. 
However, other organic compounds, not measured 
here, could be present. Therefore, this limitation 
does not allow us to identify the exact composition 
of TWP leachates. 
 
In addition, several different metals are found 
in other studies, however, Zn is the dominating 
metal in all of them. Halle et al. (2021) found Zn in 
concentration of 5.53±0.54 μg g-1, while Halsband 
et al. (2020) and Capolupo et al. (2020) found 
almost 20000 mg kg-1  and over 5000 μg L-1, 
respectively. Other metals found were aluminum 
(Al), iron (Fe) , cobalt (Co), Cu, Mn, chromium 
(Cr), nickel (Ni), strontium (Sr), cadmium (Cd), Sb 
and Pb. Interestingly, Halsband et al. (2020) 
showed that Zn kept increasing over the course of 
30 d, compared with the compounds which 
concentrations stabilized after 14 d. 
 
In general, studies focusing on TWP leachates 
tend to use different extraction (TWP type and size, 
TWP-water ratio, temperature, salinity and time of 
incubation) and analytical methods, as well as units 
for data representation. Halle et al. (2021), 
Halsband et al. (2020) and Capolupo et al. (2020) 
used different leachate production methods, 
therefore, that creates high variability when 
comparing studies. Also, Wik and Dave (2006), 
showed a wide range of LC50 when using 25 
different types of tires, indicating differences in the 
chemical composition of tires. For example in 
Capolupo et al. (2020), where 80 g L-1 were used, 
high 
concentrations 
of 
acetophenone, 
benzothiazole, n-cyclohexylformamide, phthalide 
and bisphenol A were found after 14 days. In our 
study we used lower TWP concentrations (5 g L-1) 
and shorter incubation times (72 h) as proposed by 
Beiras and Almeda (2021, unpublished). Also, as 
mentioned before, our chemical analysis focused 
on different compounds than in Capolupo et al. 
(2020). 
Hence, 
when 
comparing 
chemical 
compounds found in TWP leachates and their acute 
toxicity among studies it is important to keep in 
mind 
variations 
between 
initial 
TWP 
concentration, type and size of TWP and methods 
(leachate production and analytical, as well as 
acute toxicity test protocols). As we lack 
knowledge on TWP leachate effects on various 
marine organisms, it is important to establish 
standardized methods for leachate production and 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
14 
 
characterization of chemical composition for more 
conclusive and comparable results among studies.  
 
4.2 Lethal and sub-lethal effects of TWP 
leachates 
 
Moreover, the toxicity was concentration and time 
dependent, with lower LC50 values for all species 
after each 24 h that passed. Acute toxicity tests with 
TWP leachates have been performed for various 
marine organisms (Gualtieri et al. 2005; Wik et al. 
2009; Halle et al. 2021; Tian et al. 2021), including 
phytoplankton (Turner and Rice 2010; Capolupo et 
al. 2020; Page 2021) and copepods (Hall et al. 
1993; Halsband et al. 2020; Lehtiniemi et al. 2021; 
Mejlholm 2021). TWP leachate toxicity has been 
previously reviewed by Wik and Dave (2009) and 
recently by Halle et al. (2020) where they point out 
significant 
differences 
in 
median 
lethal 
concentrations among studies, probably due to a 
high variability in methods, tire material and types 
used, as well as species tolerance. Considering the 
low number of studies focusing on the toxic effects 
of TWP leachates and the variability in methods, it 
is difficult to compare among studies and to draw 
meaningful conclusions. 
 
In one of the first experimental studies 
(Halsband et al. 2020) focusing on the impact of 
TWP leachates on two different species of 
copepods, species-specific toxicity was detected. 
Their results indicated size-dependent response, 
where the small species Acartia longiremis showed 
higher sensitivity with LC50 values < 5 g L-1 after 
48 h, compared to the lipid-rich Calanus sp. which 
had a LC50 of 35 g L-1. Calanus sp. has lipid storage 
that could possibly buffer the leachate effect 
compared 
to 
A. 
longiremis. 
The 
lethal 
concentration for A. longiremis was higher than in 
our experiment (0.3-1.6 g L-1) for all species except 
for A .normanni (3.2 g L-1). The differences in LC50 
could be explained by the fact that A. longiremis is 
an Arctic species (Halsband et al. 2020) and also by 
the colder temperatures (8 oC) used in their 
experiment that could have acted as a buffer for 
toxic compounds. In addition, we need to consider 
that Halsband et al. (2020) used different leachate 
production methods and experimental approach 
(lower temperature, mutliwell plates etc.), where 
the experiments were run with a food concentration 
> 5000 cells mL-1, while our acute toxicity tests had 
no food. Mejlholm (2021), also found lethal effects 
on A. tonsa when exposed to high concentration of 
TWP leachates under starvation, with an LC50 of 
2.5 g L-1, which is more than 8 times higher 
compared to our values for A. tonsa (0.3 g L-1). 
Even though the same brand tire was used, 
Mejlholm (2021) run the acute toxicity tests in petri 
dishes, while our experiments were done in 500 mL 
bottles. In both studies, mortality in the controls 
exceeded 10% for A. tonsa, therefore, the 
differences in the LC50 results could be due to 
differences in organisms’ handling, their initial 
physical state and differences in acute toxicity 
protocols.  
 
Usually, acute toxicity tests with copepods are 
carried out under starvation for 48 h (International 
Organization for Standardization 1999). Our lower 
LC50 values might indicate that there is an 
increased sensitivity to toxic leachates under 
starvation but also under extended exposure period 
(72 h), especially for copepods with low-lipid 
reserves such as O. davisae (Saiz et al. 2009). 
Starvation could also explain part of the relatively 
elevated mortality in the controls, as it increased 
with time, for A. tonsa, T. longicornis, C. hamatus 
and O. davisae, considering that copepods with low 
lipid content could experience weakening under 
acute toxicity tests in absence of food (Tlili et al. 
2016). When it comes to A. normanni, there is no 
information about its lipid content, neither of lipid 
content of harpacticoids in general (Werbrouck et 
al. 2016). A. normanni is a semi-benthic species 
(Koski et al. 2005) which has high tolerance for 
environmental changes (temperature and salinity; 
Castel 1979). This makes it a good candidate for 
inhabiting stressed coastal zones (Castel 1979). We 
can postulate that benthic and semi-benthic 
harpacticoids such as A. normanni could show a 
similar high tolerance under pollution stress, which 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
15 
 
could explain their low sensitivity to TWP 
leachates.  
 
TWP leachate effect on grazing rates and pellet 
production 
varied, 
contingent 
on 
food 
concentration and copepod species. Clearance and 
ingestion rates in the leachate treatments were 
lower compared to the control, with some 
exceptions for O. davisae. However, no statistically 
significant differences were detected. The lower 
rates in the leachate treatments could indicate 
stress. The only different trend was seen for O. 
davisae where clearance and ingestion rate were 
elevated in LF leachates compare to the control.  
Furthermore, 
pellet 
production 
for 
T. 
longicornis and C. hamatus had some interesting 
patterns. The higher pellet production in the 
leachate treatments could  indicate another possible 
effect of TWP leachates. Results from a recent 
paper showed that TWP leachates in high 
concentrations, induced decreased ingestion and 
pellet production rates (Koski et al. 2021). Our 
results were not significantly different and also 
could be considered contradicting due to high 
variability, therefore, we cannot assume that a 
short-term exposure to low leachate concentration 
under excess and limited food concentrations can 
cause substantial effects on marine copepod’s vital 
rates. 
 
4.3 Species-specific toxicity  
 
Our results suggest species-dependent sensitivity to 
leachate toxicity. In particular, the effect of 
leachates was related to taxonomy rather copepod 
size, with calanoids experiencing elevated lethal 
effects 
in 
intermediate 
and 
high 
leachate 
concentrations compared to the smaller cyclopoid 
and harpacticoid species. Our initial hypothesis that 
copepod’s response would be size-dependent, 
expecting smaller species to be the most affected 
due to larger surface to volume ratio, was thus 
declined. We could however speculate that the 
sensitivity to leachates was size-dependent within 
the calanoids, since the largest C. hamatus (≈4.8 µg 
C ind.-1) showed higher tolerance than A. tonsa 
(≈2.5 µg C ind.-1) and T. longicornis (≈3.3 µg C 
ind.-1). 
 
Besides the effect of surface area, species-
specific sensitivity could be explained by 
differences in (a) feeding/swimming behavior, (b) 
increased sensitivity under starvation stress, and (c) 
drinking of water (oral intake). The three calanoids 
used in this study are feeding current feeders, 
meaning that their foraging strategy is “active” 
compared to the other two taxonomic orders which 
are ambush feeders (passive foraging strategy) and 
therefore less motile (Tiselius and Jonsson 1990; 
Kiørboe 2011). However, A. tonsa, can switch from 
active to passive feeding strategy depending on 
food availability (Kiørboe et al. 1996, 2018). van 
Someren Gréve et al. (2017) who studied the 
swimming behavior of copepods with different 
foraging techniques, showed that T. longicornis 
adults were swimming constantly and C. hamatus 
spent more than half of the time swimming. In 
contrast, Oithona nana, which uses the same 
feeding strategy as O. davisae used in this study, 
had very low swimming activity and spent most of 
the time sinking. 
 In our experiments, the calanoids were the 
most active compared to the other two species, 
especially A. tonsa and T. longicornis. A. normanni 
had a similar behavior as O. davisae as an ambush 
feeder. A recent study (Holm et al. 2019), 
investigating the starvation tolerance of copepods 
having different foraging strategies, showed that 
active feeders like T. longicornis have higher 
sensitivity to starvation compared to ambush 
feeders like O. davisae or copepods like A. tonsa 
which can switch from an active to passive foraging 
strategy, indicating high plasticity under starvation. 
We could thus speculate that O. davisae and A. 
normanni minimized the energy spent on 
swimming and invested it, for example, in 
activating their defense mechanisms, however, that 
was not the case for A. tonsa. In contrast, we could 
assume that the calanoids spent their energy 
reserves into active swimming/foraging food and 
were therefore more affected by the leachates’ 
toxicity.  
==PAGE:16==
 
 
16 
 
Additionally, the main pathways that toxic 
environmental substances can enter the food web 
are through ingestion (along with the food) or 
diffusion from the water (Wang and Fisher 1998). 
Studies have shown that the accumulation of toxic 
substances, such as heavy metals, in marine 
organisms is lower through ingestion compared to 
absorption from the water (Wang et al. 1996; Wang 
and Fisher 1998; Gomes et al. 2004). However, a 
recent study (Kadiene et al. 2019) confirmed that 
the uptake of dissolved metals also happened by 
oral intake (by ‘drinking’) in the copepods 
Pseudodiaptomus annandalei and Eurytemora 
affinis, and demonstrated that this pathway was 
significantly higher compared to dietary uptake. 
Therefore, toxic substances in their dissolved phase 
could also enter organism’s system through oral 
intake and not only through diffusion or together 
with ingested prey. We speculate that the calanoids 
were more vulnerable to uptake of the leachates by 
oral intake due to their feeding mode, while O. 
davisae and A. normanni were less likely to pass 
large amounts of water through their system. 
Therefore, feeding/swimming behavior could have 
played a role in the elevated mortality observed in 
calanoids. Also, it should be considered when 
studying the effects of TWP leachates and other 
environmental pollutants on marine organisms. 
 
4.4 Defense mechanisms 
 
The species-specific toxicity could also be 
explained by differences in defense mechanisms, 
however we did not investigate this aspect in this 
study. Copepods exposed to various chemical 
stressors, such us chemicals contained in TWP 
leachates, could react with various defense 
mechanisms. It is likely that the chemical 
compounds in leachates induced oxidative stress 
through generation of reactive oxygen species 
(ROS) in all species, especially in high 
concentrations. Oxidative stress can be caused by 
the excess production of ROS leading to cellular 
damage (Pizzino et al. 2017). Many studies have 
shown that copepods exposed to stressors such as 
heavy metals and plastic experienced increased 
intercellular oxidative stress (Kim et al. 2014; 
Vuori et al. 2015; Jeong et al. 2017).  
 
Organisms can develop an antioxidant defense 
system (ADS) for protection against ROS induced 
oxidative stress. ADS consists of enzymes like 
catalase (CAT), superoxide (SOD), glutathione 
peroxidase (GPx), glutathione reductase (GR; 
Lushchak 2011; Pizzino et al. 2017) and non-
enzymatic components as glutathione (GSH; 
Lushchak 2011). We could speculate that the 
considerable tolerance of the cyclopoid and 
harpacticoid was due to increased enzymatic 
activity compared to the calanoids. O. davisae and 
A. normanni spent less time swimming compared 
to the calanoids, possibly thereby saving energy 
that could have been invested in the ADS. 
One recent study found high concentrations of 
trace metals such as Zn in leachates from rubber 
products (Lehtiniemi et al. 2021). In that study, 
copepod’s ADS were activated by producing 
antioxidant enzymes like GPx, GR and CAT. We 
could assume that calanoids experienced similar 
effects, but ROS exceeded the antioxidant 
enzymes, leading to elevated mortality.  
Other heavy metals such as Cu, Mn , arsenic 
(As) and Cd have been detected in TWP leachates 
(Halsband et al. 2020; Koski et al. 2021). A study 
using the marine copepod Tigriopus japonicus, 
proved that GR expression increased significantly 
during exposure to Mn and Cu (Seo et al. 2006). 
Furthermore, another study, showed that a sigma 
class of glutathione S-transferase (detoxification 
enzyme) can have a significant role in copepod’s 
protection against heavy metals when testing them 
in T.japonicus (Lee et al. 2007). It would be 
interesting 
to 
investigate 
which 
defense 
mechanisms the different zooplankton species use 
when exposed to marine TWP leachates. This will 
help us develop a better understanding of why some 
species are more tolerant than others.

4.5 Which compounds can be responsible for 
TWP leachate toxicity? 
 
It is challenging to identify compounds that are 
responsible for the toxicity in our experiments. In 
our study, naphthalene was the main organic 
compound found in the leachates. We should keep 
in mind that the chemical analysis was done with 
10 g L-1 and our experiments run with 5 g L-1, 
therefore naphthalene concentrations could be even 
lower in the experimental leachates. Also, other 
organic compounds, not analysed here could be 
present. Saiz et al. (2009) tested the effect of 
naphthalene on adult O. davisae with the results 
showing no significant mortality at concentrations 
≤ 10 μg L-1 after a 24 h exposure. Barata et al. 
(2005) found an LC50 of 0.8 μmol L-1 of 
naphthalene for O. davisae, which is much higher 
than the concentration of naphthalene found in our 
leachates. It seems that O. davisae its relatively 
tolerant when exposed to single compounds such as 
naphthalene. In general, LC50 values for copepods, 
found in literature, range from 2.54-3.8 mg L-1 (Ott 
et al. 1978; Neverdal et al. 2006; Calbet et al. 
2007), concentrations far above the detected  
naphthalene concentration in our leachates. 
OPFRs, such as tris (2-chloroisopropyl) phosphate, 
caused alterations in early stages of zebrafish, 
Danio rerio (Dishaw et al. 2014). Also, in another 
study they found that environmentally realistic 
concentrations of OPFRs negatively affected 
Daphnia magna development and reproduction. To 
our knowledge there are no studies testing OPFRs 
in marine copepods, even though OPFRs have been 
detected in the marine environment (Hong et al. 
2021).  
 
Studies have shown that metals, if in excess, 
can be toxic for marine organisms (Verriopoulos 
and Dimas 1988; Hagopian-Schlekat et al. 2001; 
Fleeger et al. 2007; Mohammed et al. 2010; Jeong 
et al. 2019; Zidour et al. 2019). Zidour et al. (2019) 
showed that Eurytemora affinis had lower 
tolerance in Cu compared to Cd and Ni. In that 
study, in the mixture treatments (Cu+Cd+Ni), Cu 
accumulation rate was higher than the other two 
metals, indicating complex interactions among 
them. Overall, Zidour et al. (2019), showed that 
exposure to individual metals and their mixture, 
negatively affected the population of E.affinis. In 
addition, metals (Cd, mercury (Hg) and lead (Pb)) 
and 
PAHs 
mixtures 
(phenanthrene 
and 
fluoranthene) have been found to have synergistic 
effects on two harpacticoids, suggesting that their 
combination can be toxic (Fleeger et al. 2007). 
 
The low concentrations of organic compounds 
found in our leachates and the elevated mortality in 
some species indicate that there is a cocktail effect, 
meaning that not only one compound was 
responsible for the elevated mortality. Even though 
we do not know metals (and other compounds) 
concentrations found in our leachates, we could 
assume that similar metals with studies like 
Capolupo et al. (2020), Halsband et al. (2020) and 
Halle et al. (2021) would be present. Zn was the 
dominant metal in these three studies, therefore, the 
possibility of happening in our leachates is 
substantial. However, we would expect that the 
concentrations would vary along with the different 
extraction methods. It is possible that the organic 
compounds and metals had complex reactions that 
led to the observed toxicity. Therefore, chemicals 
found in our leachates could have additive, 
synergistic, potentiating or antagonistic effects but 
it is very challenging to assess that. Further studies 
needed to identify which of the compounds drive 
the toxicity and make possible a reduction or 
removal from car tires and other rubber products.  
 
4.6 Copepods species as reference organisms in 
ecotoxicology 
 
The environmental risk assessment (ERA) of 
chemical substances is based on toxicity tests using 
various organisms (European Commission 2003; 
European Medicines Agency 2006) such as 
crustacean, mollusk, fish (Lin et al. 2018) and 
specifically zooplankton (Koski et al. 2021). These 
studies can give predictions on how marine 
organisms might be affected under different 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
18 
 
environmental pollution scenarios (Smeti et al. 
2007; Gorbi et al. 2012; Brooks et al. 2019).  
Considering the importance of copepods in the 
marine food web (Bron et al. 2011) it is crucial to 
understand how they can cope with various 
chemical 
substances, 
entering 
marine 
environments. Their life strategies (Bron et al. 
2011), their diversity (Boxshall and Halsey 2004) 
and abundance  (Raisuddin et al. 2007) makes them 
appropriate reference organisms in ecotoxicology 
(Raisuddin et al. 2007). In addition, their ecological 
importance and their easy handling under 
laboratory conditions contributes into making 
copepods good candidates used in ecotoxicology 
(Raisuddin et al. 2007). In the 1990s, the use of 
marine copepods in ecotoxicological studies 
increased (Kwok et al. 2015), with harpacticoids 
(Nitocra spinipes) being one of the first orders used 
(Barnes and Stanbury 1948). There are at least 30 
marine copepod species frequently used in 
ecotoxicity tests (Kwok et al. 2015), including 
calanoids and harpacticoids such as A. tonsa 
(Sverdrup et al. 2002) and Tigriopus spp. 
(Raisuddin et al. 2007).   
In our study we have used the three most 
important 
orders 
of 
copepods, 
Calanoids, 
Cyclopoids and Harpacticoids (Bron et al. 2011), 
which cover an extensive range of biogeographical 
distributions. A. tonsa, T. longicornis and C. 
hamatus, are representative species in coastal 
waters (Fransz et al. 1998; Nielsen and Munk 
1998), where pollutants such as Persistent organic 
pollutants (POPs) and inorganic pollutants such as 
heavy metals can enter the water via many routes 
(atmospheric deposition, run off etc.; Crain et al. 
2009). O. davisae is a ubiquitous species and 
probably the most common copepod genus found 
in coasts and oceanic environments (Gallienne and 
Robins 2001). Finally, A. normanni was used as a 
benthic representative as marine sediments in 
coasts and open oceans can be considered sinks for 
pollutants, like heavy metals, due to increased 
human pressure (Yoo et al. 2013). All species used 
in our study, can be considered suitable reference 
organisms, as they satisfy all the necessary 
requirements. However, our results indicate that 
different taxonomy will be diversly influenced 
under chemical pressure coming from TWP 
leachates. Therefore, it is challenging to compare 
ecotoxicological studies as there are species-
specific differences and also frequently, different 
endpoints are being used.  
 
5. Future studies and conclusions 
 
The concern of the effect of TWP and their 
leachates is growing, as there is strong evidence 
that they can impact marine organisms. Firstly, it is 
important to establish protocols for leachate 
production and chemical characterization. Also, 
future studies should focus on quantifying the 
concentration of TWP in the ocean. That will allow 
us to test environmentally relevant concentrations 
and investigate the long-term effect on marine 
organisms. Furthermore, TWP leachate toxicity 
should 
be 
investigated 
along 
with 
other 
environmental stressors (increased temperature, pH 
changes etc.). The organisms tested here are reared 
under optimal conditions but, in situ they can 
experience a range of other environmental 
stressors. In addition, in ecotoxicological studies, 
except for acute toxicity tests, exploring the 
defense mechanisms (detoxification process) of the 
organisms could give a better understanding on 
how different species react when exposed to toxic 
compounds in a molecular level. 
 
This study represents one of the first 
experimental studies focusing on the effect of TWP 
leachates on different mesozooplankton. We 
showed dose and time-dependent responses and 
indicated species-specific differences. Our study 
indicates that some species are less tolerant, 
perhaps due to differences in feeding/swimming 
behavior, elevated stress under starvation and oral 
intake of water. Therefore, our study suggests that 
copepod behavior should be considered when 
testing toxic substances, along with the TWP type, 
leachate concentration and other environmental 
variables. The 24 h-exposure to low concentration 
did not significantly affect copepods’ vital rates, 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
19 
 
indicating that leachates in low concentrations are 
not harmful in a 24 h-period. However, long-term 
experiments are necessary. Comparing our results 
with other studies was challenging as we lack 
standardized protocols. 
Moreover, one of the main goals of Marine 
Strategy 
Framework 
Directive 
(Directive 
2008/56/EC) is to achieve good environmental 
status in EU, where  contaminants should be at a 
level that do not affect the marine environment 
(Directive-EC, 2008). The question that rises is: 
Can TWP be considered as a big concern that 
could cause limitations for reaching this status? To 
answer this question, we need extensive research 
focusing on estimating TWP concentration that 
reaches 
the 
marine 
environments 
and 
understanding the processes that take place once 
the TWP come in contact with the water. There are 
still major research challenges to overcome, and in 
that framework, it is crucial to estimate the impact 
of TWP leachates to marine organisms.
