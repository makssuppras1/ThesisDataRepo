Abstract
In the search for alternatives to fossil fuels, ammonia is a promising candidate by being
carbon-free and the production being a well-established industry. Yet the use of ammonia
as a fuel for engines causes some challenges with its poor combustion characteristics. To
be able to design engines, it is very important to develop a reliable chemical kinetic model
for the oxidation of ammonia.
In this study a ﬂow reactor with two diﬀerent reactor designs has been used to examine
the ammonia oxidation with respectively varying temperature or varying residence time.
The experimental results are interpreted using a detailed kinetic model of the ammonia
oxidation developed at DTU Chemical Engineering. It should be noted that after comple-
tion of all the experiments, it is found that a leakage problem has been present through
all the experiments resulting in a higher inﬂow of air diluting the gas ﬂow entering the
reactor. Unfortunately it has not been possible to resolve the leakage problem within the
time frame of this study. Especially the residence time experiments are sensitive to the
leakage and the results are therefore unreliable.
There is some diﬀerences between the experimental results and the model for both tem-
perature experiments and residence time experiments, which could indicate that surface
reactions are taking place initiating the chemistry of the induction period. Furthermore
the ammonia oxidation seems to be inhibited by the presence of water as a higher tem-
perature is needed for reaction as well as the oxidation rate is slower.
Because of the uncertainty of especially the residence time experiments, the model va-
lidity is examined using previous experiments performed by others. Here it is concluded
that temperature experiments are not very useful in studying the ammonia oxidation.
Furthermore the modelling indicates the presence of surface reactions. However the need
for time shift of the model to match experimental results depends on reactor conditions
as this is what inﬂuences the surface reactions. Based on the results from modelling of
previous experiments, the model is validated.

Background
Ammonia has the potential of being used as a carbon-free fuel or energy carrier in the
search for sustainable alternatives to fossil fuels. The advantage of using ammonia is
the already well-established industry of ammonia production, storage and transportation.
However the use of ammonia as a fuel in Diesel engines proposes some challenges since
it has a low combustion intensity, which means a narrower ﬂammability, low caloriﬁc
value and a low laminar burning velocity compared to carbon-based fuels. In studies
examining ammonia as an engine fuel the poor combustion characteristics have been
diﬃcult to overcome making it important to develop reliable models for the oxidation of
ammonia for use in engine design. However, gas-phase ammonia oxidation is challenging to
characterize experimentally, because ammonia reacts easily on reactor surfaces. Available
experimental studies on ammonia oxidation in ﬂow reactors reported in literature may
have been aﬀected by heterogeneous reactions. An early study by Dean, Hardy and Lyon
(1982) [1] indicates that the time for initiation of reaction is aﬀected by the reactor surface,
while the post-ignition oxidation rate is not. Experiments on ammonia oxidation in a ﬂow
reactor, where the ignition stage can be separated from the post-induction oxidation is
desirable.
Project objectives
The objective of this project is to investigate the oxidation of ammonia. For this purpose
a ﬂow reactor setup is to be developed and used, which detects reactants and products
as a function of residence time. This way it will be possible to decouple the initiation
from the post-initiation part of the oxidation. Apart from residence time the ammonia
oxidation will be examined based on stoichiometry and temperature. The results will be
interpreted using a detailed chemical kinetic model, where the reaction mechanism will
be based on work of amine chemistry already performed at DTU Chemical Engineering.

Introduction
The demand and need for sustainable solutions to combustion processes become more and
more important. The challenge is to ﬁnd alternatives to fossil fuels and in the process
minimize the emission of greenhouse gasses. One of the alternative fuels of interest is
ammonia as it has several advances. It is carbon-free and does not have a direct green-
house eﬀect [2]. Furthermore the production is a well known and established process since
it is already being used for several purposes. The main usage is fertilizer but it is also
used for other purposes such as raw material for various industrial products and as a
chemical additive for selective catalytic reduction (SCR) of NOx. Ammonia thereby has
the advance of being a well-established industry of production, storage, transportation
and utilization. Replacing fossil fuels with ammonia would therefore result in minimal
investments [3]. Even though ammonia is primarily produced using fossil fuels it can also
be produced using biomass or renewable sources.
Especially in the marine sector the interest in using ammonia as a fuel in the nearby
future is high in an attempt to become more sustainable, where batteries is not an op-
tion. It is not possible to manufacture batteries with a big enough capacity to sail the
large ships. However the main disadvantage and challenge of using ammonia as a fuel
is its poor combustion characteristics compared to carbon-based fuels. The lower burn-
ing velocity and higher auto-ignition temperature prevent ammonia from being used in
existing engines as a single fuel [4]. It is therefore important to understand the kinetics
and the reaction mechanism behind the oxidation process to be able to develop a reliable
model, which can be used for engine design. Over the last four decades extensive research
has been performed in the area however there is still discrepancies between experimental
results and the developed model needing further investigation as ammonia reacts easily
on reactor surfaces. The purpose of this project is to examine the ammonia oxidation by
performing experiments and examine the present model’s compatibility and validity with
experimental results. Especially the inﬂuence of residence time on the ammonia oxidation
as it is desirable to be able to separate the induction time and post-induction time of the
oxidation.

2
Literature study
This literature study covers the advances made in using ammonia as sole or dual fuel for
engines as well as the characteristics of ammonia combustion. Furthermore it describes
the advances made in determining the kinetics and a reaction mechanism for the oxidation
of ammonia. In the oxidation of ammonia NO is formed which is also covered here as well
as how the emission of NO can be reduced.
2.1
Ammonia as fuel for engines
In order to minimize the emission of greenhouse gasses, research has looked into replacing
hydrocarbon fuels among these diesel with alternative carbon free solutions such as am-
monia. In a review from 2020 made by Dimitriou et al. [5] on ammonia as a compression
ignition (CI) engine fuel. they found that literature in CI engines running on only ammo-
nia is very limited. Furthermore the literature that does exist is from the 60’ties. This is
due to the unsuitable fuel properties for compression ignition. However some researchers
managed to operate CI engines on only ammonia under speciﬁc engine conditions, but
because of the very high compression ratios required it will be challenging to implement
ammonia in a single fuel concept [5].
Apart from the scarce literature on using ammonia as a single fuel for CI engines, re-
search has also been investigating the possibility of partially replacing diesel in CI engines
with ammonia in a dual fuel operation. Reiter et al. [6] tested combustion and emissions
characteristics of a CI engine using a dual fuel consisting of ammonia and diesel. Under
constant engine power a 40-60% diesel fuel fraction on energy basis resulted in the best
fuel eﬃciency whereas increasing the ammonia concentration above the 40% resulted in
longer ignition delays and lower peak combustion pressures. Reiter et al. found that
with decreasing energy supplied by diesel and replacing it with ammonia resulted in an
increasing reduction of CO2 emissions. However the emissions of CO and other hydrocar-
bons in the exhaust gas were higher than using only diesel as fuel when the same power
output should be met. The emission of NOx depended on the fuel combination which
could be reduced by using less than 40% ammonia since this gives a lower combustion
temperature resulting in lower thermal NOx. If ammonia was the main fuel the NOx
emission would increase. Furthermore the emission of unconverted ammonia was between
1000-3000 ppmV even though the ammonia conversion eﬃciency was very high and the
unconverted ammonia requires further after-treatment.

Based on simulations made by Lasocki et al. [7] of ammonia combustion in a dual-fuel CI
engine using diesel as initiation for ignition of the NH3/air mixture, they found the use of
ammonia feasible. It resulted in lower emissions of CO2 and CO as compared to running
on only diesel. Diﬀerent ammonia-diesel ratios can be used to achieve maximum power
and engine torque. However the eﬃciency of an ammonia-diesel engine is lower than for
the diesel engine because of the low heating value of ammonia.
Gill et al. [8] examined co-fuelling a diesel engine with ammonia to achieve a reduc-
tion in CO2 emissions.
This was done by injecting gaseous ammonia into the intake
manifold of the engine with fresh air charge since ammonia is diﬃcult to ignite. Their re-
sults agreed with the other two studies of using ammonia as dual fuel ﬁnding a signiﬁcant
reduction in CO2 emission when ammonia was used as partial replacement of the diesel
fuel. However the lower combustion temperature of ammonia made it diﬃcult to ignite
and burn eﬀectively. Furthermore Gill et al. also found increased hydrocarbon emissions
as well as increased NOx emissions including an increase in N2O formation.
Because most of the research ﬁnds that relatively high concentrations of unburned am-
monia are present in the exhaust gas as well as the combustion results in relatively high
emissions of NOx, the implementation requires after-treatment systems. This makes am-
monia as a CI engine fuel suited for marine application, power generation and heavy-duty
applications since these applications have no signiﬁcant space constraints [5].
Based on found literature ammonia is not suitable for single fuel solutions in a CI engine
because of its unsuitable fuel properties for this type of engine, whereas it shows promise
in dual fuel operations of CI engines where it is mixed with diesel. Using ammonia for
dual fuel operations results in lower emissions of CO2, however it also results in higher
emissions of NOx. Moreover not all ammonia is undergoing combustion in the engine lead-
ing to emissions of unconverted ammonia. Therefore further studying of after-treatments
for the unwanted emissions of NOx and ammonia are needed before ammonia can be used
for dual fuel operations.
2.2
Ammonia production
Ammonia is mainly produced by the Haber-Bosch process using an iron-based catalyst
for industrial purposes [5]. The production is based on reformed hydrogen from natural
gas (CH4 + 2 H2O −−→4 H2 + CO2) or lignite and nitrogen separated from air [3]. The
reaction for the Haber-Bosch process is [5]:
N2 + 2 H3 −−→
←−−2 NH3
(1)

This is a very exothermic reaction and the production happens at temperatures between
723 and 873 K and at pressures of between 10 and 25 MPa. The structure of the nitro-
gen molecule is very stable with a large binding energy of 911 kJ/mol, which is why high
temperatures are needed to crack the molecule. Furthermore the equilibrium is facilitated
towards the product side by using high pressures. The conversion degree for every run of
the process is not very high being between 15 and 25% but by recycling the unreacted
gas an overall conversion of 97% can be obtained [5].
Obtaining hydrogen from steam reforming of natural gas results in emission of CO2,
therefore research is focusing on producing ammonia free of CO2. This can be done by
getting the hydrogen needed from the electrolysis of water using renewable energy [5].

been studied. Lee et al. [16] found the laminar burning velocity to increase with in-

the kinetics determined by Miller et al. [17]. Especially for fuel-rich ﬂames the increase
of laminar burning velocity is substantial. Lee et al. explain the higher laminar burn-
ing velocity by an increase in ﬂame temperature because of the hydrogen substitution
since hydrogen is more reactive compared to ammonia making the ﬂame stronger. The
increased ﬂame temperature then results in higher radical concentrations increasing the
reaction rates which thereby increase the laminar burning velocity. Furthermore Lee et al.

found based on the Markstein number that the increase in hydrogen substitution seems
to stabilize the ﬂame more at fuel-rich conditions while at fuel-lean conditions the ﬂame
seems to be more unstable with hydrogen substitution. For stoichiometric conditions the
Markstein number indicated that the ﬂame also become more unstable with hydrogen

increasing equivalence ratio, where the maximum burning velocity for diﬀerent mixtures
of NH3/H2/air is around an equivalence ratio of 1.1. Moreover Li et al. examined the
emission of unburned ammonia and did not detect any in the exhaust gas for diﬀerent
equivalence ratios and diﬀerent initial ammonia concentrations.
2.4
Experimental techniques
Diﬀerent experimental techniques can be used for studying combustion. In this literature
study of the kinetics of ammonia oxidation the articles are based on experiments using
either a jet-stirred reactor, which has the design of a continuously stirred tank reactor
(CSTR), a plug-ﬂow reactor or laminar ﬂames.
The concept of a CSTR is that the rate of mixing of the reactants with products is
very fast compared to the rate of chemical reaction. This results in an uniform content
of the reactor.
Furthermore diﬀerent residence times can be obtained when the inlet
mass ﬂow is changed. Because of the rapid mixing the composition is entirely kinetically
controlled making them a great tool when studying reaction mechanisms. However the
problem in practice regarding the CSTR is to obtain suﬃciently rapid mixing [19].
The plug ﬂow reactor also have rapid mixing of reactants but it also operates in a fast-ﬂow
mode where time can be replaced directly by distance in the kinetic equations describing
the reaction. The problem with a plug ﬂow reactor in practice is to maintain the consis-
tency in composition, temperature and ﬂow velocity over the cross section of the reactor
[19].

The laminar premixed ﬂame is a macroscopic reactor where the ﬂames is usually sta-
bilized on top of porous metal cylinders. It is here the reactants are fed. In order to
measure the detailed spatial distribution of temperature and composition, the ﬂame is
normally operated at a low pressure of between 10 and 100 torr so that the reaction
zone is spread out. Out of the common laboratory devices the laminar ﬂame replicates
the conditions of practical combustors best in the most cases in that transport eﬀects
and temperature history play important roles in the combustion process. The limitation
of the laminar ﬂame is that its mathematical modeling requires knowledge of molecular
transport properties. However there exists a considerable amount of information on gas
transport properties especially those that impact the structure of ﬂames [19].
2.5
Kinetics and reaction mechanism for ammonia ox-
idation
Ammonia can be oxidized either through complete oxidation which forms nitrogen and
steam as shown in the following equation [20]:
2 NH3 + 1.5 O2 −−→N2 + 3 H2O
(2)
Or partial oxidation of ammonia can take place, which yields nitric oxide and steam [20]:
2 NH3 + 2.5 O2 −−→2 NO + 3 H2O
(3)
However the oxidation of ammonia is much more complex and has to be explained through
reaction mechanisms, which have been modelled and studied by many. Glarborg et al.
[21] have established a model for the oxidation of ammonia based on a wide range of ex-
perimental data found in literature which is shown in ﬁgure 2. In the reaction mechanism
set up by Glarborg et al. ammonia reacts with the radicals H, O and OH all forming the
radical NH2. The NH2 reactions inﬂuence the consumption rate of ammonia as well as
the selectivity for formation of NO and N2. The reaction of NO and NH2 is very fast and
can be separated into two reactions forming diﬀerent products:
NH2 + NO −−→
←−−NNH + OH
(4)
NH2 + NO −−→
←−−N2 + H2O
(5)
where the reaction shown in equation (4) is chain branching while the reaction shown in
equation (5) is terminating.

Furthermore the radical NH2 can react with NO2. The oxidation of NO to NO2 is facili-
tated when the radical HO2 is formed in rather large amounts at large excess air ratios:
NO + HO2 −−→
←−−NO2 + OH
(6)
The reaction of NH2 and NO2 can be separated into two reactions forming diﬀerent
products as is the case with the reaction of NO and NH2:
NH2 + NO2 −−→
←−−N2O + H2O
(7)
NH2 + NO2 −−→
←−−H2NO + NO
(8)
NH2 can also form the radical NH by hydrogen atom abstraction which can react in two
ways with oxygen:
NH + O2 −−→
←−−NO + OH
(9)
NH + O2 −−→
←−−HNO + O
(10)
Besides reacting with oxygen, NH can react with NO leading to two diﬀerent product
combinations:
NH + NO −−→
←−−N2O + H
(11)
NH + NO −−→
←−−N2 + OH
(12)
Especially at high temperatures the competing consumption reactions of NH shown in
equation (9)-(12) are important for the overall selectivity of the formation of NO and N2
in the oxidation of ammonia. Furthermore of the two product channels for the reaction
between NH and NO, the formation of N2O and H is dominating at temperatures between
300 and 2200 K contributing 70-90% while the formation of N2 and OH contributes the
remaining 10-30%. This value only increase very little with increasing temperature [21].

Glarborg et al. Miller et al. have included a reaction path from NH to HNO, where
NH reacts with either OH or O2 to form HNO. Otherwise the reaction paths are similar.
According to Miller et al. the primary reaction for converting ammonia to the radical
NH2 is through OH. Yet at lean conditions the reaction with O has an impact on the

For lean conditions NO is primarily formed from reactions of HNO and NH whereas the
Zeldovich mechanism barely contributes to the formation of NO since the ﬂame temper-
ature is around 1720 K which is comparatively low. Apart from being formed the NO is
consumed by reactions with the radicals NH2 and NH. This happens early in the reaction
zone. At the latter part of the reaction zone the formation rate of NO is higher than the
consumption rate which yields a total formation rate of NO that is positive.
For rich conditions NO is formed from reactions of HNO and NO2. Also the Zeldovich
mechanism contributes to the formation of NO just at the latter part of the reaction zone.
As for lean conditions NO is consumed by the radicals NH2 and NH but for rich conditions
the inverse Zeldovich mechanism also contributes to the consumption of NO by reacting
with N. The overall NO formation becomes negative in the latter part of the reaction zone.
According to Kobayashi et al. [3] based on literature of ﬂames studies the ammonia reacts
mainly with the radical OH of three radicals OH, H and O independent of the equivalence
ratio. Depending on the concentration of O and H radicals, the radicals NHi can either
through oxidation mostly form NO via the intermediate HNO or they can reduce NO by
reacting with the NO molecules. Therefore at lean conditions the concentration of NO
is high because under this condition there is an abundance of the O and H radicals. At
rich conditions the concentration of O/H radicals decreases yet the concentration of H
increases in the O/H radical pool. This leads to reactions with NHi resulting in the pro-
duction of N atoms. The increase in N atoms at rich conditions promotes the Zeldovich
mechanism.

is happening rapidly and then more slowly hereafter. There also seems to be a diﬀerence
in the rate of NO formation depending on the presence of water. If the ammonia is dry the
formation is greater than for the wet ammonia. The water thereby inhibits the ammonia

2.6
Formation and reduction of NO
In combustion the formation and consumption of NO depends on the radical pool, which
is usually controlled by the oxidation chemistry of the fuel in this case ammonia. In some
cases at speciﬁc conditions the trace species chemistry also impacts the radical pool for
example the oxidation of the fuel can be promoted or inhibited by the nitrogen oxides
present [21].
NO is as mentioned before produced in the oxidation of ammonia. One of the ways NO is
produced is through the Zeldovich mechanism which involves the following reactions [3]:
N2 + O −−→
←−−NO + N
(25)
N + O2 −−→
←−−NO + O
(26)
N + OH −−→
←−−NO + H
(27)
The reaction shown in equation (25) is the rate-limiting reaction, which necessitates the
cleavage of the covalent N-N bond in N2. However the Zeldovich mechanism may be neg-
ligible in the overall formation of NO. Furthermore in the ammonia oxidation there is a
lot of NO and N atoms present which favors the backward reaction, thereby reducing the
==PAGE:22==

NO concentration. At rich conditions the reverse of the Zeldovich mechanism is promoted
resulting in a reduction of NO, where it is mainly the reaction shown in equation (25)
that takes place [3].
The concentration of O and H radicals increases with increasing temperature since the
temperature sensitive chain branching reactions are promoted. Yet this may not inﬂuence
how the Zeldovich mechanism contributes to the NO concentration since this increases
the rate of reduction of NO through the reaction shown in equation (25) while it at the
same time increases the formation of NO through the reaction shown in equation (27) [3].
Furthermore NO can also be formed by N2O or NNH. The formation of NO via N2O
can be signiﬁcant under lean conditions at high pressure and moderate temperatures
whereas the formation of NO via NNH is important under slightly reducing conditions
and higher temperatures. This may in particular happen in diﬀusion ﬂames where on the
fuel-rich side of the ﬂame sheet NNH can form and hereafter inside the ﬂame sheet react
with O [21]. The mechanism of N2O forming NO is:
O + N2 ( + M) −−→

(28)

←−−NO + NH
(29)

(30)

NO forming N2 or N2O [21]. The rest of the reactions of the N2O mechanism are:

←−−N2 + OH
(31)
N2O + O −−→
←−−N2 + O2
(32)
It is the branching fractions of the reactions between N2O and H and O respectively that
control the selectivity for forming NO or N2. Of the two product channels for the reaction
between N2O and H, it mainly yields N2 and OH [21].
The mechanism for the formation of NO through NNH is:
H + N2 ( + M) −−→
←−−NNH ( + M)

←−−NH + NO
(34)

3
Experimental
The oxidation of ammonia is examined in a laminar ﬂow reactor using two methods.
One of them is where the temperature is varied while the residence time is kept constant
to see the inﬂuence of temperature.
The other is where the residence time is varied
and the temperature is kept constant to see the inﬂuence of residence time. These two
methods have been conducted using two diﬀerent reactor designs. For the temperature
experiments, the reactants, ammonia and oxygen, are premixed while for the residence
time experiments, they enter the reactor at diﬀerent positions, thereby getting mixed
inside of the reactor. Furthermore water might inﬂuence the oxidation of ammonia which
is why some of the temperature experiments and residence time experiments have been
performed with water vapour present in the gas ﬂow.
3.1
Description of setup
The setup consists of a reactor feed system for the gasses and the H2O addition, a laminar
ﬂow reactor, an oven, a condenser and a analysis system for the outﬂow of gas from the
reactor. The part of the reactor where the reaction can take place is 60 cm long, which is
the length of the electrical oven. The electrical oven can be set to the wanted temperature
for reaction. To make sure that the oven is heating up to the correct temperature with
a minimal temperature diﬀerence as well as determining the isothermal zone in the oven,
a temperature proﬁle has been measured before the start of the experiments. The tubes
connecting the feed system to the inlet of the reactor as well as the tubes leading from
the reactor to the FTIR are made from teﬂon to minimize the adhering of gasses to the
tubes. To get a better understanding of the setup ﬁgure 6 later in the text shows a PI
diagram of the system.
The reactor feed system consists of gas cylinders, mass ﬂow controllers, valves and a
piping system. The gas cylinders used for the setup are ones with pure nitrogen and pure
oxygen as well as one with 4000 ppm ammonia in nitrogen. There is one mass ﬂow con-
troller connected to each of the gas cylinders. The gas cylinder containing a concentration
of 4000 ppm ammonia is connected to a mass ﬂow controller called MFC1, which has a
ﬂow capacity of 500 ml/min. The nitrogen cylinder is connected to a mass ﬂow controller
called MFC3 with a ﬂow capacity of 2000 ml/min. These two mass ﬂow controllers are
regulated through a little machine near the set up. The oxygen cylinder is controlled
through a mass ﬂow controller called MFC4 which is regulated through a computer con-
nected to the setup. The MFC 4 has a ﬂow capacity of 100 ml/min. Furthermore there

3.3.3
Calibration of ﬂow
Every time experiments have to be conducted the ﬂow needs to be calibrated. This is
because of the varying set point of the mass ﬂow controllers. The ﬂows are calibrated by
estimating a given valve opening percentage or ﬂow and then use a ﬂow meter to control
if it results in the wanted ﬂow. Otherwise the value of the mass ﬂow controller is adjusted
and checked again with the ﬂow meter until the right ﬂow is obtained. The measurement
with the ﬂow meter has to be performed around 5 times to give an average of the ﬂow
since this is ﬂuctuating. It should also be noted that when the gas ﬂows from the diﬀerent
gas cylinders mix before entering the inlet of the reactor, it results in a small uncertainty
of 5-10 ml where the smaller ﬂow is reduced by the larger ﬂow. The overall uncertainty
in the ﬂow measurements is around 2%.

3.5
Leakage problem
After the conduction of the experiments, it is found that during the experiments a leakage
has been taking place on the gas panel as the metal nut around valve V-2 close to MFC2
has not been properly sealed. This is before the inlet of the reactor. The exact location of
the leakage can be seen in ﬁgure 12 of the setup, where it has been marked. This leakage
has been aggravated by the pump connected to the FTIR. Because of the loose metal
nut, air is entrained into the system resulting in an inﬂow of air diluting the gas ﬂow
entering the reactor. The leakage problem results in a ﬂow at the outlet of the reactor
of 1.6 l/min, while the wanted ﬂow through the system, if it had been tight, should have
been 1 l/min. This means that there is an inﬂow of air of 600 ml/min. The reason that
the leakage has not been detected is that it has been attributed to the FTIR leakage as
well as it is expected beforehand that there would some deviation between experimental
results and the model due to reactions happening on the surface, which will be discussed
further in a later section. Moreover the pump leads to the experiments being conducted
under a sub-atmospheric pressure enhancing the problem with leakage. The pressure has
been estimated to be around 0.7 bar for the temperature experiments while it has been
estimated to be 0.8 bar for the residence time experiments. The diﬀerence in estimated
pressure between the experiments is due to the reactor design where it is assumed that
the resistance is a bit higher in the reactor used for the residence time experiments.

Chemical kinetic model and Chemkin
The ammonia oxidation has been simulated using the simulation program ANSYS Chemkin-
Pro 17.1 [29]. The chemical kinetic model uploaded to the program is based on an updated
version of the mechanism proposed by Glarborg et al. [21]. The updated version is pro-
posed by Dai et al. [4]. In the updated version they have adopted the theoretical rate
constants for the reactions NH3 ( + M) −−→
←−−NH2 + H( + M), NH3 + HO2 −−→
←−−NH2 +
H2O2, NH3 + O2 −−→
←−−NH2 + HO2 and HNO( + M) −−→
←−−H + NO ( + M) determined by
Stagni et al. [30]. Stagni et al. studied these reactions by ab initio methods and they
veriﬁed these over a wide range of conditions. They found that NH3 + O2 −−→
←−−NH2 +
HO2 proceeds very fast in the reverse direction making it a fast chain-terminating step.
Yet Dai et al. state that this reaction is faster at room temperature than found by exper-
imental data and it should be examined further. Moreover Dai et al. also included rate
constants for the HONO/HNO2 subset determined theoretically by Chen et al. [31] in
the updated version as well as a few rate constants for H2NO reactions are modiﬁed. The
mechanism consists of 225 reactions and 36 species. The chemical kinetic model has been
tested against ignition delay time experiments performed in a rapid compression machine
(RCM).
The type of reactor for the simulation is chosen. The chosen reactor used to conduct
the simulations is a batch reactor as this is a simple reactor type where the reaction
proceeds as a function of time. Under the tab "Reactant Species" the reactants are put
into the program as well as the reactant fractions. All simulations of the experiments
are conducted with an ammonia concentration of 563 ppm and an oxygen concentration
of 10.4% to take the estimated leakage into account. Under the tab "Reactor Physical
Properties" the problem type "Constrain Pressure and Temperature" has been selected

4.1
Surface reactions
According to experiments and modelling of the ammonia oxidation performed by Dean,
Hardy and Lyon [1] surface reactions are taking place inﬂuencing the ammonia oxidation.
The surface reactions can be both ammonia oxidation reactions taking place on the surface
or thermal decomposition reactions taking place on the surface.
The induction time,
which initiates the chemistry of the reaction, is surface dependent since it is inﬂuenced
by the variation of the surface area or the surface pretreatment. However for the post
induction time the ammonia concentration gradient is independent of the surface since
this is unaﬀected by changes of the surface area or the pretreatment. Dean, Hardy and
Lyon’s results are based on extensive research where experiments have been conducted
with various reactor surfaces to volume ratios as well as various pretreatments of the
quartz reactor surface. In ﬁgure 14 some of their results are shown:
(a) Inlet composition: 900 ppm ammonia, 4%

line is modelling with time shift.
By time shifting the modelling of the ammonia oxidation, the modelling and the exper-
imental results are in very good agreement. This is the reason for the assumption of an
induction period that is initiated by surface reactions as it can be disengaged with a time
shift.

4.3
Residence time experiments
In this section the inﬂuence of residence time on the ammonia oxidation is investigated
with the results found from the residence time experiments as well as modelling of the
ammonia oxidation. The results from the residence time experiments are not reliable
and comparable with earlier experiments and modelling because of the sub-atmospheric
pressure and the leakage problems. Within the time frame of this thesis, it has unfor-
tunately not been possible to resolve the problem, yet the results are shown and described.
The reactants are not pre-mixed, which means that the reactants are preheated before
encountering each other. However only 40 cm of the reactor is considered isothermal
starting 10 cm inside the reactor and ending 10 cm before the end of the reactor. This
means that some of the reaction is taking place at a lower temperature than wanted.
From the temperature experiments as well as literature [1], it is found that it is very
likely that surface reactions are taking place, which starts the chemistry. However it is
the oxidation rate after the induction period that is of interest and not the surface chem-
istry and therefore the residence time in the reactor for the modelling of the oxidation is
shifted until the model matches the experimental data. This way the induction period is
disengaged.

Conclusion
Page 54 of 61
5
Conclusion
The ammonia oxidation has been examined by conducting experiments varying temper-
ature or residence time. The reactor design of the two types of experiments has been
diﬀerent where the reactants have been premixed and not heated for the temperature ex-
periments, while the reactants are preheated before meeting each other in the reactor for
the residence time experiments. Some of the experiments have been repeated and espe-
cially the residence time experiments showed a very good repeatability. The repeatability
for the temperature experiments has not been as good but still acceptable. Furthermore
the ammonia oxidation has also been modelled to test how well the oxidation can be
described by modelling. It has also been compared with the conducted experiments.
Unfortunately it is found that there is two leakage problems happening in the setup. One
of the them is an internal leakage of the FTIR, which is being used to analyse the con-
centration of the products for the experiments. This was already known at the start of
the thesis as it has been found by an earlier user of the FTIR. However it was thought
that the oﬀ-set is only caused by the internal leakage and that it could be adjusted for by
performing calibrations of the FTIR adjusting the concentrations of ammonia and NO.
However after all the experiments have been conducted a leakage was found in the setup
on the gas panel. This leakage has resulted in air being entrained into the system resulting
in an inﬂow of air diluting the gas ﬂow before entering the reactor. Of the two reactor
designs the residence time reactor is more sensitive to this leakage as the inﬂow of air is
in the ammonia gas ﬂow. The ammonia and air can then react before meeting the oxygen
making the experiments unreliable since the exact residence time and inlet composition
are unknown and diﬃcult to estimate. Furthermore the experiments have been conducted
under a sub-atmospheric pressure as a result of the pump connected to the FTIR, which
function has been to reduce the resistance inside the FTIR.
From the temperature experiments it is found that the oxidation takes place in a larger
temperature interval for the experimental results than it does for the model. This could
indicate that surface reactions are taking place initiating the ammonia oxidation chem-
istry. Furthermore it seems like the ammonia conversion starts at a higher temperature
when water is present, which could indicate that water is inhibiting the reaction. When
the NO concentration is examined it is found that it is increasing with increasing tem-
perature. It is concluded that the temperature experiments are not very useful in the
studying of the ammonia oxidation.
==PAGE:60==
Conclusion
Page 55 of 61
Since the residence time experiments are quite unreliable as a result of the leakage prob-
lem not much can be concluded about these. When the residence time experiments are
modelled there is some diﬀerence between the model and the experiments however when
the modelling includes a time shift to disengage the induction period, the agreement be-
tween the model and the experimental results are good. Moreover the addition of water
seems to slow down the oxidation.
Finally the compatibility and validity of the model is examined by comparing it to exper-
imental results performed by others as the residence time experiments are too uncertain.
The agreement between the experimental results and the model is very good. However
it is found that for Wenli’s experiments time shift is needed whereas it is not necessary
for Dean, Hardy and Lyon’s experiments. This suggests that it is possible that surface
reactions are taking place but that they are dependent on the reactor conditions. It is
concluded that based on these results the model can be validated.
