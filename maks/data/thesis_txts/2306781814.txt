Preface
This thesis was submitted in partial fulﬁllment of the requirements for completion of
the Master programme in Engineering Design and Applied Mechanics (mechanical engi-
neering) to achieve the degree Master of Science in Engineering (MSc) at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU). The thesis work was conducted from January to June
2016 and represents a workload of 30 ECTS points.
The purpose of this thesis is to conduct a feasibility study on the manufacturability of the
geometry enhanced ultrasonic ﬂow meter, a novel technology developed at the Technical
University of Denmark in a collaboration between the Department of Applied Mathemat-
ics and Computer Science (DTU Compute), the Department of Photonics Engineering
(DTU Photonics) and the Department of Mechanical Engineering (DTU Mechanical En-
gineering). Using robust design methodologies, the thesis combines both analysis and
synthesis to understand the eﬀects of variation and to improve the originally proposed
ﬂow meter design to make it more robust, and ultimately, more manufacturable. The
thesis also proposes a calibration scheme, that may be implemented directly into a qual-
ity assurance procedure to reduce calibration complexity and costs in a production setting.
The author would like to issue a big thanks to Thomas J. Howard, thesis supervisor,
for arranging and guiding the thesis work, and for understanding and supporting the
entrepreneurial context. Also thanks to co-supervisors Tobias Eiﬂer and Jes Broeng for
helping scoping the thesis work, and ﬁnally a big thanks to Peter Nørtoft, now business
partner and colleague, for including me in the technology and business development.
The reader is assumed to have an educational background at the same level of the author,
but does not require particular knowledge or expertise within mechanical engineering to
get a good understanding of the contents.

1
Background
The geometry enhanced ultrasonic ﬂow meter (“enhanced ﬂow meter”) is an interdisci-
plinary technology development project between DTU Photonics, DTU Compute, and
DTU Mechanical Engineering formerly under the development name “FlowWise”. The
technology is expected to provide a signiﬁcant improvement to ﬂow rate measurement
precision in ultrasonic ﬂow measurements (tenfold), speciﬁcally in laminar ﬂows. Prior
to this thesis, the technology had already been in development for four years, in which
period the team has developed a highly detailed theoretical and numerical model. As of
the beginning of this thesis research, experimental validation has not been concluded, and
all intermediate results treated in this thesis are derived from the numerical model based
on isogeometric analysis, developed by Peter Nørtoft [1]. Experimental validation was
conducted by a third party (COPAC ApS) during the ﬁrst half of 2016, with results ini-
tially scheduled for the end of May 2016. The experimental testing was delayed however,
and the testing was still not concluded by the submission of this thesis.
1.1
Ultrasonic Flow Measurement
The enhanced ﬂow meter works by measuring the transit time of an acoustic signal (ul-
trasonic pulse) between two transducers in a pipe section. If there is a ﬂow, the acoustic
signal will travel faster downstream (with the direction of the ﬂow) than upstream (against
the ﬂow). The diﬀerence in transit time, or “phase shift”, is proportional to the ﬂow ve-
locity and can thereby be used to determine the (volumetric) ﬂow rate because the cross
sectional area is known. Compared to regular transit time ultrasonic ﬂow meters, this
Figure 1: Ultrasonic ﬂow measurement based on the transit time principle
technology diﬀers by the geometry enhanced pipe section. The geometry enhancement
may vary with for diﬀerent applications, but for the particular ﬂow meter treated in this
thesis, the geometry enhancement consists of a bulge in between the ultrasonic transduc-
ers. The theory is, that a trapped acoustic mode will exist inside this “chamber”, which
1
==PAGE:7==
aﬀects the acoustic signal traveling through the pipe. The trapped mode is highly sensi-
tive to the ﬂow velocity, and thus inﬂuences the acoustic signal in addition to inﬂuence
from the ﬂow itself [2] [3].
1.2
Experimental Validation
Prior to the experimental testing by COPAC, the technology was experimentally tested
by two separate research groups, both during 2015. The ﬁrst experimental testing was
during project work on the course ”Innovation and Product Development”, by a group
of Msc. Eng. students in the spring of 2015 [4]. The demonstration was based on a test
bed with an interchangeable pipe section, that allowed comparisons between a geometry
enhanced pipe section and a straight pipe section. The student’s conclusion was that the
geometry enhanced technology was delivering a factor 5 improvement in phase shift sen-
sitivity compared to a traditional straight pipe. However, this conclusion is questionable
due to a large presence of measurement noise and lack of reproducibility.
The second occasion was part of a Bsc. thesis, in which two Bsc. Eng. students con-
ducted testing on a test bed using water as the medium [5]. The experimental results
were compared to data from a reference ﬂow meter. The experimental results obtained in
this thesis were also aﬀected by noise, but reproducible to some extent. The experimental
results could not identify an increased phase shift in the enhanced ﬂow meter. The test
setup did, however, have some error sources in the transducer setup, medium (theoretical
model used air instead of water) and the pipe run-in length before the ﬂow meter. The
Bsc. students concluded that the technology could neither be proved or disproved based
on the experimental results.
In addition to the known error sources, there is no reference on the dimensions of the
geometry enhancement feature, nor any references on the sensitivity of the prototype ﬂow
meters. It is therefore a possibility that the questionable test results can be related to
robustness or manufacturing issues.
1.3
Entrepreneurial Setting
In parallel to the technology development, the FlowWise team has been working on ﬁnding
a market to commercialize the technology. As of the of beginning of the thesis research,
the business model revolved around selling high precision ﬂow meters to the biogas indus-
try, which is a small market niche with a small sales volume. The commercialization later
pivoted towards application in smart water metering business, which led to the founding
of Aqubiq, consisting of the original FlowWise team and the thesis author. In this new
application, measurement precision is a second priority value proposition, and develop-
ment of the biogas ﬂow meter is canceled. The plan is to introduce the technology in a
second generation smart water meter.

Product Development
Prior to this thesis, little to no work has been done on evaluating the application re-

is available on use conditions, installation and ambient conditions, desired manufacturing
processes and volume, tolerances or calibration. Without this information, it becomes im-

Thesis Scope and Introduction
With the background and previous development covered in section 1, the following sec-
tion will focus on deﬁning the scope of the thesis, as well as describing the fundamental
assumptions.
2.1
Assumptions
As implied in section 1, the basis for development is a theoretical and numerical model of
the ﬂuid and acoustic behavior inside a geometry optimized pipe section. The practical
usability of this thesis is therefore entirely dependent on the assumptions that:
• the acoustic phenomena supporting the increased ﬂow meter sensitivity can be ex-
perimentally veriﬁed, and:
• the numeric model calculates - or can be calibrated to calculate - phase shift values
that accurately predict real world performance
In the positive event that the acoustic phenomena is experimentally veriﬁed, some dis-
crepancies between the numerical model and experimental results are still expected due
the underlying assumptions of the numeric model: Most signiﬁcantly the assumption of
rotational symmetry. This implies that ultrasound is emitted and recorded around the
entire circumference of the pipe, while it in reality is in a single “point”. This (and other)
theoretical assumptions are likely to require some adjustment of the numerical model for
the numerical and experimental results to be directly comparable.

2.2
Scope
The purpose of this thesis is to support the ongoing development of the enhanced ﬂow
meter. This includes conducting a manufacturing feasibility study and to identify and
implement optimizations to both mechanical design and the numeric model. The scope
of the feasibility study shall be:
• to analyze to what degree the enhanced ﬂow meter is manufacturable, including;
– to determine required manufacturing tolerances, and
– to identify suitable manufacturing processes
• to identify sources of variation (noise factors) that may inﬂuence the ﬂow meter
performance;
• to analyze the relative sensitivity to these noise factors;
• to identify to what degree control factor(s) may be used to reduce the impact of
noise factors (i.e. calibration);
2.3
Research Questions
To further guide the thesis research the following research questions are deﬁned, based on
the previously discussed scope and background:
1. Can the predicted ﬂow meter performance be obtained with the proposed geometry,
based on common manufacturing processes (IT9 to IT13)?
2. Can the predicted ﬂow meter performance be obtained with another geometry?
3. How does dimensional variation inﬂuence the performance output of the geometry
enhanced ﬂow meter?
4. To what extend can control parameters (such as transducer frequency) be used to
reduce performance loss from dimensional variation?

3
Design for Manufacture
The initial focus of the thesis was to determine sources of variation, and to investigate
for which of these sources the sensitivity could be determined. It became apparent that
deﬁning and verifying dimensional variation in the originally proposed geometry would
be very diﬃcult, due to reasons discussed in this section. As a result, the scope of the
thesis was extended to not only include manufacturing analysis, but also synthesis.
3.1
Revised Geometry
The originally proposed geometry enhancement feature was based on a six-point spline
proﬁle with a proprietary calculation methodology. The beneﬁts of this geometry is that
it has no sharp edges that potentially could disturb the laminar ﬂow, and it may be easier
to clean which could be relevant in applications with a hygienic requirement. The origi-
nally proposed spline-based geometry is illustrated in ﬁgure 3.
Figure 3: Original 6-point spline geometry
From a robust design perspective, however, the spline-based geometry poses large chal-
lenges with geometry speciﬁcations, determining dimensional variation modes and phys-
ically verifying and measuring the geometry. To improve upon the design, a geometry
based on integral elements was proposed. The beneﬁts of this design is that the geometry
is split into individual, unambiguous features, thereby becoming both easy to specify,
e.g. with GPS methodology (geometric product speciﬁcation) [6] and more convenient
to physically verify. Among the evaluated options, a geometry consisting of two cones
connected by a cylinder was selected, which showed identical performance characteris-
tics with the spline based geometry. The revised geometry is illustrated in ﬁgure 2, and
the pressure distribution characteristics are shown in ﬁgure 4. Both geometries have a
pressure distribution that is almost completely symmetric around a middle plane. The
revised geometry enhancement feature can be unambiguously deﬁned by two lengths and
one diameter as illustrated in ﬁgure 7.

3.2
Performance Indicator (ﬂow meter output)
The original performance indicator used in the numerical model, called the acoustic asym-
metry, is a unitless number calculated by integrating and comparing the sound pressure
in both halves of the ﬂow meter. The acoustic asymmetry gives a qualitative ﬁgure for
the impact of the geometry enhancement, but its functional meaning and design criteria
are very ambiguous. Furthermore, the acoustic asymmetry is a completely artiﬁcial value,
and cannot be physically measured or calculated from experimental data.
Similarly to the ﬂow meter geometry, having an unambiguous and quantitative perfor-
mance indicator was a requirement for further analysis and product synthesis. This lead
to the implementation of the phase shift calculation, which besides being completely
quantitative, also is measurable on a physical ﬂow meter prototype. The revised perfor-
mance indicator furthermore allows the enhanced ﬂow meter to be directly comparable
to existing, traditional ultrasonic ﬂow meters.
3.3
Noise, Variation and Inﬂuencing Factors
An overview of factors that inﬂuence the ﬂow meter performance is a basic requirement

others). The parameter diagram is presented in ﬁgure 5 (in a slightly simpliﬁed version),
and contains variation from diﬀerent product life cycles, but mainly manufacturing and

inﬂuencing variation parameters cannot be simulated by the numeric model. Due to the

4
Sensitivity Analysis
This section, along with the results presented in section 5, will discuss how sensitivity
analysis may be used for determining the manufacturing tolerances and requirements.
These requirements especially depend on how sensitive the enhanced ﬂow meter is to
dimensional variation.
If the ﬂow meter is more sensitive to certain parameters, the
analysis be furthermore be useful in determining the location scheme for interfaces between
parts, if the enhanced ﬂow meter cannot be made in a single part.
4.1
Manufacturing and Assembly
Because the enhanced ﬂow meter features an internal geometry, manufacturing becomes
fairly challenging due to the fact that the geometry is inaccessible. Diﬀerent manufactur-
ing processes may, in addition to aﬀecting tolerance capability, also require the ﬂow meter
to be assembled from multiple parts, because the internal geometry needs to be accessible
for machining. Milling and turning are processes that will likely require the ﬂow meter to
be split into multiple parts, while processes such as injection molding or hydro forming
may form the entire geometry as a single part.
(a) Longitudinal assembly interface
(b) Radial assembly interface

variation in other
Compared with a single part, an assembly of multiple parts is exposed to additional vari-
ation in the assembly process because multiple interfaces, each with variation, is mated
together. Placing interfaces strategically can be an eﬃcient method of reducing variation
in the product output. For the enhanced ﬂow meter, the optimal position of this / these
interface(s) depend, among other factors, on the sensitivity to variation in speciﬁc dimen-
sions. As previously mentioned, the revised geometry may be unambiguously speciﬁed
by the three dimensions illustrated in ﬁgure 7. Knowing the relative sensitivity to each
dimension, allows the designer to place the interfaces such that the most sensitive dimen-
sions become least aﬀected. An example of two diﬀerent assembly schemes is illustrated
in ﬁgure 6.

Besides precision requirements, selecting a manufacturing process is a compromise be-

material, volume, economy, ﬁnish and appearance, preparation for additional manufac-
turing steps etc. These factors will not be discussed in this thesis, because the thesis does
not focus on detailed design of the enhanced ﬂow meter.
4.2
Design of Experiments
A series of experiments is designed to investigate how the ﬂow meter performance (mea-
sured phase shift) denoted “output”, is aﬀected by dimensional variation (manufacturing
precision or tolerances) denoted “input”. The sensitivity analysis is conducted as a full
factorial experiment with four parameters; three dimensional variation parameters, and a
fourth “frequency” factor corresponding to the frequency control factor in the P-diagram
(ﬁgure 5). Frequency variation is included to investigate the possibility of using frequency
calibration to compensate for dimensional variation. The design of experiments does not
include variation in transducer position. The three dimensional factors are illustrated in
ﬁgure 7.

single phase ﬂuid, by adjusting the geometry (form and dimensions) and transducer setup.

The numeric model solves the Navier Stokes’ equations (for a laminar ﬂow) to obtain the
velocity ﬁeld, in which the propagation of the acoustic signal is subsequently simulated.
The most important output from the simulation is the numeric value of the phase shift
(in radians), because it quantitatively represents the performance of the ﬂow meter as
previously discussed. A naturally occurring large transit time diﬀerence (phase shift) is

less aﬀected by measurement noise, and therefore wanted over a small phase shift. A large
phase shift allows the ﬂow meter to detect low ﬂow rates with high precision, which is
exactly the idea with the geometry enhancement. The ﬂow meter geometry is subjected
to a rough optimization on single parameter optimization, to form the basic geometry

Interaction Eﬀects, IT13
At nominal transducer frequency, it is very apparent that the ﬂow meter performance

In few variation

In most of the cases the performance is reduced. For inner length (l2) dimensions smaller
than nominal (variation levels -4 to -2) the performance is reduced with between 0.2%
and 20.1% (worst performance in combination with smaller diameters). For inner length
dimensions (l2) larger than nominal (variation levels +2 to +4) the performance is re-

with the actual phase shift value being reduced to 0.066 radians.
At IT13 level of dimensional variation, the analysis has identiﬁed the following factors as
having the largest eﬀect on performance:
• Combined diameter (d) and inner length (l2) variation
• Inner length (l2) variation

ﬂow meter output. For dimensional parameters smaller than their nominal values, de-

frequency. Running another experiment with that particular parameter combination as
the nominal could potentially reveal another local optimum.

may improve the output for certain parameters combinations, but to a smaller extend
than the other case. It is possible that the frequency could be increased beyond the range
outlined in table 4 to gain a stronger eﬀect. The ﬂow meter is clearly more sensitive to
decreasing the frequency, than increasing it.

Calibration Scheme
In addition to providing fundamental insight into the ﬂow meter performance, the large
amount of information from the full factorial analysis may also be useful in product
calibration. In the previous section, it was concluded that changing transducer frequency
can eﬀectively be used to compensate for dimensional variation. This information is used
to propose a calibration scheme based on dimensional variation, as opposed to manual
calibration in a testbed. Installing each ﬂow meter in a test bed may be inconvenient,
especially if the ﬂuid to be measured is dangerous, and furthermore it may require a more
complex setup than a measurement / veriﬁcation station.
6.1
Example
Assume a situation where the theoretical model has been ﬁtted to experimental data,
and provides matching results in a speciﬁc variation range, e.g. IT11. A ﬂow meter is
manufactured, which has identical performance characteristics to the ﬂow meter in this
thesis. As concluded in section 5.3, the ﬂow meter requires calibration to avoid large
unit-to-unit performance variation.
The proposed calibration scheme uses the actual (measured) dimensions of the inter-
nal geometry, to compare with the performance characteristics determined in the full
factorial analysis. Because the performance of every single combination of dimensional
variation is known beforehand, the calibration process becomes a simple procedure identi-
fying the dimensions in the factorial dataset, and looking up the corresponding frequency.

6.2
Advantages
The proposed “pre-determined” calibration scheme is advantageous in situations where
measuring the dimensions is more convenient than experimental calibration in a testbed.
This would likely be the case if the enhanced ﬂow meter is assembled from multiple
parts, and the internal geometry is directly accessible for physical measurements (probe).
Potential advantages over manual calibration in a test bed includes:
• Complete integration with regular quality assurance procedures
• Fast and convenient calibration of each unit
• Cost savings due to time savings and no requirement for test bed / lab

Discussion
The purpose of this thesis was to conduct a manufacturing feasibility study on the en-
hanced ﬂow meter. The study is conducted entirely on basis of the numerical model [1],
because the ﬂow meter technology has not yet been supported by experimental testing. It
was concluded that the originally proposed geometry would have issues with speciﬁcation
and veriﬁcation, hence a new simpler geometry was proposed and implemented. This
answers research question 1 (RQ1): Because the originally proposed geometry may not
be unambiguously speciﬁed or veriﬁed, there is no way to reliably obtain the predicted
performance. In addition to the geometry synthesis, the numerical model was revised to
output a quantitative and measurable performance indicator; the phase shift. Finally,
an overview of factors that can inﬂuence the ﬂow meter performance was created and is
presented in the parameter diagram (ﬁgure 5). Based on the revised geometry, a sensitiv-
ity analysis was conducted to determine the manufacturing requirements. The sensitivity
analysis is used to estimate how the ﬂow meter performance (measured phase shift) is
aﬀected by dimensional variation at diﬀerent levels, speciﬁcally IT grades IT13, IT11
and IT9. Because manufacturing volume and tolerance precision is inevitably related,
knowledge of the performance eﬀects of diﬀerent manufacturing processes allows the de-
velopers to connect the technology to certain market segments, based on product volume
or business model, e.g.
• High volume, low price
• Medium volume, medium price
• Low volume, high price
The sensitivity analysis concludes that the enhanced ﬂow meter performance is highly
sensitive to dimensional variation, and could suﬀer from high unit-to-unit variation if
manufacturing processes worse than IT9 are used. However, by using IT9 capable pro-
cesses, the performance of the revised geometry is very consistent even without adjusting
any control parameters. This answers RQ2: The predicted ﬂow meter performance may
indeed be obtained with another geometry.
The sensitivity analysis also concluded that frequency calibration can counteract nega-
tive eﬀects from dimensional variation, and even improve the output beyond the reference
value in some cases, thus answering RQ4. This may have an advantage in situations
where calibration is more cost eﬃcient than precise manufacturing processes or even re-
work. Calibration is only limited by the transducer’s frequency range and supporting
electronics, which practically means that the calibration can cover quite a large range
of dimensional variation. To avoid a large unit-to-unit variation, present at tolerances
above IT9, the ﬂow meter will probably require a unit speciﬁc calibration if processes
worse than IT9 are to be used. Among common manufacturing processes, IT9 is typically
associated with turning processes, however, some milling processes may also be able to
deliver IT9 capability [9] [10]. At IT11 and above, the analysis predicts the unit-to-unit
variation to become very signiﬁcant. For such processes, additional veriﬁcation (dimen-
sional measurements) and calibration is a requirement. If a suitable metal is used, most
23
==PAGE:29==
machining processes (milling and turning) are reliably IT11 capable. High volume pro-
cesses such as injection molding and some casting processes are typically IT13 capable [9].
When comparing the sensitivity to variation in each parameter (section 5) to the full
factorial analysis with all interaction eﬀects (sections 5.2, 5.3 and 5.4), it can be con-
cluded the results in section 5.1 can be generalized to a large extent. This implies that
the sensitivity to each parameter can be prioritized as follows (list going from most sen-
sitive to least sensitive):
• Inner length variation (parameter l2)
• Frequency variation (parameter f)
• Diameter variation (parameter d)
• Outer length variation (parameter l1)
This list eﬀectively functions as a prioritization list when designing assembly and position-
ing features to govern the dimensions of the above mentioned parameters, i.e. interfaces
should prioritize minimizing variation in the longitudinal / axis direction of the geometry
enhancement feature. Of the two interface location examples in ﬁgure 6, a longitudi-
nal assembly interface (ﬁgure 6a) is better suited to reduce the eﬀects of dimensional
variation.

8
Conclusion
During the thesis research, the performance characteristics of the geometry enhanced ul-
trasonic ﬂow meter has been thoroughly analyzed. Although the analysis is based on the
same physical conditions and parameters as the experimental proof of concept, it is only
to be considered applicable at a methodical level until further experimental validation is
completed.
A new geometry based on integral elements was introduced to simplify geometry spec-
iﬁcation and veriﬁcation. This was found to have no implications or adverse eﬀects on
the ﬂow meter performance, but is expected to improve its manufacturability consider-
ably. The performance indicator was changed from the artiﬁcial ”acoustic asymmetry”
to actual phase shift, being less ambiguous, easy to verify experimentally and enabling
quantitative comparison of diﬀerent ﬂow meter geometries (including simulated variation).
The thesis has investigated the use of sensitivity analysis in technology development, both
with respect to general technology development and optimization, but also with respect to
commercialization and manufacturability. Simulated dimensional variation at IT13, IT11
and IT9 levels has been used to construct a guideline for the manufacturability of the
enhanced ﬂow meter. The sensitivity analysis was also used to identify optimal interface
locations to minimize the eﬀects of dimensional variation. Additionally, the analysis has
concluded that frequency calibration can be used to compensate for dimensional variation.
With the contributions from this thesis, the geometry enhanced ultrasonic ﬂow meter
technology has become signiﬁcantly more manufacturable, and the contributions may be
applied directly to the proof of concept / prototyping work, and going forward.
