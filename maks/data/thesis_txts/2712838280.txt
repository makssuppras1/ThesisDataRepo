Abstract 
The construction industry is constantly evolving, processes and tools are becoming more 
digitalized than ever before. due to a greater competition and demand for more efficient, 
sustainable, and digital construction. VDC has been a tool that has pushed the construction 
industry in the right direction. VDC is a digital method that can ensures greater collaboration 
and increases quality in the construction industry. 
In this thesis we will investigate the current digitalization stage of the construction industry, by 
a larger construction company, NCC Denmark A / S and a larger digital knowledge 
organization, Molio A / S. The investigation includes international case studies and own case 
study in collaboration with NCC Denmark A / S to analyze and discuss the use of VDC in the 
planning and execution of larger foundation projects.

Chapter 1  
Introduction 
1.1 Motivation for this thesis  
The building standards have increased significantly in the recent years, and therefore clients 
have a greater demand for quality and control of the construction processes. Sustainability, 
digitalization, technology, and increased quality assurance are the main focus of any 
construction project in the modern building industry. According to Molio’s surveys on digital 
development in Denmark (2020), it turns out that 96% of the contractors believe there is a 
benefit in digitalization and 65% believed that the most significant gain is increased quality of 
projects.(A. 2) The studies show that the barriers within digitization are scattered, and this 
involves, i.e. a lack of digital culture in the workplace, ineffective forms of collaboration, and 
a lack of digital competencies in contractors organization. This thesis aims to gain a deeper 
insight into the challenges within these barriers and examine the internal mechanisms based on 
qualitative and quantitative methods and tools.  
1.2 Problem definition 
Molio’s barometer show that the clients and AEC have the greatest desire to increase the use of 
VDC in the construction industry. The contractor uses VDC to optimize the construction 
processes and increase earnings and quality. The studies show that VDC contrasts with its 
current role in the construction industry. 1 The lack of synergy between virtual and physical 
construction creates an inappropriate barrier in streamlining the entire production, from 
planning to operation. Based on these statements, a problem definition is defined. 
Problem definition: “Today's construction industry suffers from productivity issues due to a 
lack of integration of VDC in execution and planning of foundation projects. “  
The following hypothesis will be examined throughout the report and will be discussed and 
evaluated based on empirical data. 
The hypothesis “Increased use of VDC in planning and execution of foundation projects can 
lead to increased time optimization, profits and quality.”

3.1 Virtual Design and Construction (VDC) 
VDC stands for Virtual Design and Construction and is a technology that provides opportunities 
to work more digitized by planning, simulating, and scrutinizing the construction digitally 
throughout the design and execution. Thereby minimizing the risk of errors and creating an 
optimal execution with a focus on sustainable solutions, quality and safety. VDC is used to 
handle digital information throughout the project lifecycle. First, the project is simulated, 
predicted and analyzed, and later in the production phase by performing follow-up, 
documentation and visualization of the project's progress and the final product.2  VDC are 
multi-disciplinary as it represents the Architect, Engineering, Contractor (AEC) and project 
owner, as well as relevant sub-disciplines. VDC is divided into three key elements, BIM, ICE, 
and PPM, which provide a more straightforward approach to analyzing the entire execution 
from start to finish. During construction projects, there will always be a risk of human error. 
VDC is thus a tool that reduces the risk of errors and streamlines and increases quality. VDC 
optimizes design, processes and material waste by tracking the progress of a construction 
process.3  
 
Figure 2 – VDC key elements 
3.1.1 ICE (Integrated Concurrent Engineering) 
Integrated Concurrent Engineering (ICE) is an interdisciplinary method of handling 
interdisciplinary meetings using visualization, making it easier to discuss and introduce virtual 
changes. ICE is a way to organize a design team that enables stakeholders from multiple 
disciplines to participate concurrently to develop integrated project designs very rapidly.  
==PAGE:15==
Technical University of Denmark | M.Sc. Civil Engineering 
 7 of  91 
 
The overall benefits of using ICE correctly are saving time, increasing quality, and getting the 
best collaboration internally and externally across different professional groups.  Time is 
reduced by: 
- 
Involve all decision-makers in the meetings to get faster decision-making. 
- 
Increased communication to ensure the design is re-evaluated fewer times. 
- 
The problems are clarified more effectively with the active involvement of the partners 
- 
Deadlines follow the plan by integrating BIM modelling, processes and organization 
- 
The meetings are shortened, but the quality of the daily control is increased.4 
 
3.1.2  PPM (Product Production Management) 
Product Production Management (PPM) establishes a production process and sets the daily 
workflow and the necessary resources. PPM is based on a LEAN construction method. Lean 
construction is based on the Toyota Way’s four broad principles – Philosophy, process, people 
and problem-solving (A. 8). This processing method gives contractors substantial advantages 
in cooperation, process management and communication. The central theme is to establish a 
system that prevents and minimizes wastage of materials, time and effort to generate the 
maximum possible value for the client. The transfer from lean construction to lean production 
has limits, as the focal point is coherence within industrial work processes and production. Lean 
production is carried out under controlled conditions, making identifying the processes and 
steps easier.  Manufacturing outputs consist of a precisely engineered parts that are produced 
in large numbers and with few variations. Lean construction has a greater variety of processes, 
materials, and trade in construction than in manufacturing. The components are numerous and 
wide-ranging, as each project has unique physical, environmental and social characteristics. 
The complex issues such as weather conditions and interdisciplinary activities create 
unfavourable conditions to establish the perfect synergy between lean production principles and 
lean construction principles. 5

3.1.3 BIM (Building Information Modeling) 
BIM (Building information model) is a method tool for digitizing some typical design and 
planning processes. BIM has significantly improved the AEC industry by including the 
schedule and model-based cost estimate in the BIM model 4D and 5D. BIM allows you to better 
coordinate trades and subcontractors, detecting any MEP, internal, or external clashes before 
construction begins. In addition, by implementing BIM, you can visualize the risk analysis and 
plan the construction site to increase safety on the construction site.6 In current years, an 
intelligent integration process is built on reliable information models and coordination from the 
design, production, and operation phases. The BIM model is a common platform for key 
stakeholders, creating greater transparency and knowledge sharing in the design phase among 
the external and internal partners. The consistency of visual communication ensures a safe 
framework for solid decision-making.  Figure 3 illustrates the various processes to consider 
when advanced BIM is used in projects.  
 
Figure 3 – BIM cycles 
In the early phases of a BIM project, the team agrees on the design criteria and approves the 
process. The model is closely coordinated with those involved in a relevant phase from idea to 
execution. In the event of changes in the design, revisions are required so that agreements and 
changes are registered. After the project is completed and ready for commissioning, the 
information from the model can be actively used to drive what has been built.

3.1.4 Dimensions of BIM 
Project materials' format and information level, from design to operation and maintenance, has 
changed significantly in a few decades. 3D modelling is the new construction standard when 
current construction projects are to be built. The development stems from increasing the quality 
of the project and intercepting future construction technical errors that are more difficult to 
register on a floor plan or elevation. The BIM principle is constantly evolving; currently, several 
contractors are trying to implement one or more new dimensions in their services to the client 
and the internal collaboration. In the research area, the BIM dimensions have been defined and 
extended to 4D, 5D and 6D modelling, connecting the 3D model to the schedule, finances, and 
facility management. The purpose of connecting all dimensions is to illustrate the total cost of 
ownership through a model that provides a connection between design and maintenance. Thus, 
the model can be used to assess alternatives, so the financial consequence of the design 
decisions regarding cleaning, procurement, operation and maintenance. Implementing the new 
BIM dimensions requires changes in contractor structures, digital competencies and culture, 
which has meant that all work processes have not been streamlined yet. The BIM dimensions 
are a tool that aims to complement each other based on the project’s scope, complexity and 
challenges. 2D is not considered an outdated method but should be used in the contexts that 
create the best value for the client, consultants and executives in terms of resources, time and 
productivity, which is compatible with the digital agenda and the sustainable transition. That 
can be done by reducing printed drawings and saving expenses on services for architects and 
suppliers.

3.2 Forms of contractors 
Organizational structure, contract basis and division of responsibilities between the client and 
the contractor depending on the chosen form of contract. There are 3 different types of contract 
which will be explained in the following sections. 
3.2.1 Main contractor 
The main contractor is one of the private clients' most widely used contractor forms. The main 
contractor is responsible for the entire construction management, time planning, finances and 
quality assurance. Using the main contractor, the client has left all the risk and responsibility to 
the contractor. That means the client does not influence the overall construction management 
and employment of specialist contracts. Service descriptions and contractual conditions are 
specified in the contractual basis AB18 and ABR18. AB18 is the agreement basis between the 
contractor and the client, and ABR18 is the agreement between the contractor and consultant. 
A chart of the main contractor is shown in Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5 – Main contractor 
For this type of contract, the main contractor will have all the responsibility during execution. 
That means if something goes wrong during the execution due to a lack of construction 
management, it is the main contractor responsible for the unpredictable extra costs for 
correcting errors, expenses for facility management or ordering new materials.  
==PAGE:20==
Technical University of Denmark | M.Sc. Civil Engineering 
 12 of  91 
 
The disadvantages of the main contractor are that the client pays an extra expense to manage 
and take care of the execution of the project. With the main contract, the client is still 
responsible for the design of the construction project, which involves a building permit, 
descriptions and all the necessary drawings, and delivering a buildable project to the contractor.  
 
3.2.2 Specialist contractor 
In specialist contract cases, the client is responsible for the entire construction. The client is 
responsible for the design, drawings, obtaining a building permit, managing the construction 
process, etc. The client thus designs the tender material himself, obtains tenders, draws up a 
contract with the selected contractor, supervises and manages the entire process. In the case of 
a specialist contract, the client assumes legal and financial responsibility for the project. The 
specialist contract places great demands on the client. It should only be chosen if you have 
experience with this or the qualifications to familiarize yourself with the entire project from 
start to finish. 7 
A specialist contract requires the client has qualifications and experience in construction 
management. Specialist contracts are most often used in small private projects. The contractual 
basis between the client and the contractor is AB18. The benefit for the client with a specialist 
contractor is that he can save a lot of resources by having fewer links in the organization. It also 
means that there is a great responsibility and requirement for the client since he needs to deliver 
all the required materials, permits and construction management.8

3.2.3 Turnkey contractor 
The turnkey contractor is responsible for the initial planning of the project and the subsequent 
execution of the construction. The turnkey contractor's services are a complete architectural 
design, obtaining excavations/building permits, contacting authorities, carrying out the 
construction and handling associated planning/management tasks during the entire construction 
project. The Contractual basis used for a turnkey contractor is ABT18.9 Turnkey contractors 
are often used on major construction projects, where the project’s complexity, scope and 
duration require strong competencies within management.10  Several large contractors such as 
NCC, MT Højgaard and Aarsleff have worked purposefully to streamline work processes in 
several aspects of the construction process by increasing digitalization. A new business concept 
emerged, where several departments were launched to develop technological tools such as 
further. VR, VDC and 4D/5D relate to the design phases. The demand for quality assurance and 
collaboration in the construction industry has led to greater competition in the market. The 
public guidelines have positively affected the private sector, as there is a desire for monotonous 
communication channels, such as ICT management.

3.3 Process-based planning – from generic to construction 
LER 2.0 
LER is a cable register created to prevent damage to the cables during excavation work.12 LER 
contains information about the owner’s supply lines and aims to reduce the number of 
excavation damage to supply lines. The purpose of the register is to ensure that contractors and 
other excavation operators can get in touch with the owners of the wires found in a current 
excavation area. Since 2016, the Danish Agency for Data Supply and Efficiency has worked to 
further develop the wire owner register to LER 2.0.  
LER 2.0 was launched on 3 January 2020 and aimed to ensure that users of LER can access 
uniform wiring information within 2 hours.13

owners will need a system that can ensure an automatic exchange of cable information. The 
conversion deadline is June 30, 2023, requiring a conversion process for all cable owners13.

Excavation 
Before the excavation begins, an investigation of the soil and the area must be carried out. The 
needed information is the slope of the plot, the type of soil, the distance to neighbouring 
buildings, underlying structures and other conditions. This information is used to plan how the 
work will be performed and which machines are best suited to perform the work within the set 
time frame. 14.  
 
Figure 8 - touch screen placed in the excavator 
Local plans and soil samples are analyzed to locate old wires, soil conditions, and soil 
contamination. Various machines are used for the excavation, such as excavators, cranes and 
trucks14. For medium-sized projects, excavators such as "Doosan DX300LC-7". The excavator 
has low noise and vibration, perfect for densely populated areas. The excavator is built with a 
360-degree camera system, anti-slip steps, and a platform. A touch screen with the latest 
technology provides the necessary information and adjusts digging and lifting settings. In 
addition, the excavator is equipped with an ultrasonic sensor to avoid obstacles during 
excavation15.

Agreement basis and responsibility 
The client is responsible for preparing a safety plan and conducting start-up meetings, safety 
rounds, etc., at the construction site. That applies when at least 2 companies or 11 people are 
simultaneously on the construction site16. The contractual basis for earthworks is AB18. In the 
event of excavation damage, the contractor can only be held liable if they have acted negligently 
about the location of the cables, cf. LER. In addition, it is the client's responsibility to ensure 
that no excavation work is carried out on surrounding cables. The client must inform the 
contractor about the soil conditions on the building site and provide adequate tender material 
for extra work associated with soil conditions. Such work is considered extra work for which 
the contractor is entitled to payment, cf. AB 18 § 26, para. 3. 
 
Figure 9 - The client's responsibility 
It’s important to have clear communication between the client and the contractor to prevent 
unforeseen expenses, as unforeseen soil conditions can be very costly for the client. The client 
can cancel the contract if the circumstances result in public orders or prohibitions that will 
prevent the work from continuing or make it unreasonably burdensome for the client. By

Foundation 
Foundation work is characterized by having fixed deadlines, as the prefabricated concrete/steel 
elements must be installed as soon as the base plate is cast. The work processes must be planned 
from generic to construction as a lean construction principle. 
Foundations can be modelled with simple generic 3D models, where primary geometries and 
substantial volumes can be measured. To optimize the initial planning of obtaining offers and 
deliveries, contractors implement complete services within 3D modelling, which shows the 
reinforcement details, number and lengths of the foundations. It ensures an efficient and 
automated workflow for the construction manager, who submits cut & bend lists to the suppliers 
with total quantities. To perform reinforcement work, many processes such as formwork, 
casting and a proper surface treatment must be streamlined. Although foundations mainly 
consist of simple materials, it still requires a lot of planning. 
 Working hours and rental prices for equipment make up a large proportion of costs related to 
foundation projects. Therefore, processes related to labor and machinery must be integrated 
into production. As described in section 3.1.2, the lean construction principle is governed by 
varying processes in an uncontrolled environment. Working on-site requires the right 
conditions, safety measures, and protective equipment while complying with strict tolerances 
according to the project material. The existing conditions often mean that the work areas are 
varied and that the construction parts cannot be assembled due to lack of space. Lack of 
planning and buildability often occurs when artisans are assigned a work task or when they are 
in the process of performing the task. That hampers the production, as it will result in production 
coming to a standstill or future planning having to be reorganized. The increased process-based 
planning creates risk diversification, as the accumulated risk depends on factors such as high 
wages and destructive resources, including cranes, excavators, and concrete pumps.

3.5 International case study 
This section is based on an international case study. It aims to give an insight into the global 
digitalization development in VDC and identify success experiences and challenges in the 
implementation. Creating a common virtual platform for the various phases of construction is 
the future solution to reduce errors and shortcomings and thereby increase the quality of 
construction projects. For this reason, VDC is an overall digital strategy that can be found 
worldwide. The background for these studies is to uncover the external borders of VDC and to 
get a better understanding of the development outside Denmark’s borders.  
VDC through CIFE 
Virtual Design and Construction (VDC) was introduced in 2001 by John Kunz and Martin 
Fischer through the Center for Integrated Facility Engineering (CIFE) at Stanford University.18 
The United States pioneered their hands-on BIM development and adoption in the construction 
industry. The goal of VDC in the building design phase is to use the models to understand the 
complexity of a project and predict potential challenges before a significant commitment of 
time or money is made to the project 
Case study – Target Field 
This case study provides an insight into how some specific digital tools have streamlined the 
initial production phase of the Target Field project. This section describes how VDC has 
increased the productivity of the foundation project, including VDC impact on the construction 
site, foundation work, and in-situ castings. The case study also includes challenges associated 
with using VDC and its significance for the project.19

3.5.1 Construction Site 
The project was in a densely trafficked area, with various infrastructural obstacles in the form 
of 2 highways north and south to the construction site and a railway station in the western 
direction. The contractor knew before the start of the project that the geographical location 
would pose a risk in terms of logistics on the construction site and planning of future deliveries. 
Therefore, a 4D ballpark model was created during the preconstruction phase using design 
models to determine the crane scheduling. A total of 3 cranes have been used for cast-in-place 
structures, and three primary crawler cranes were used for precast concrete elements, steel 
structures, enclosures, and precast concrete stadia. Due to the job site's complexities, bordering 
on the North and South by bridges, East by an active highway, and Westside by an active 
railroad, the stadium needed to be built from the inside out. Coordinating trade flow for up to 
five of these cranes simultaneously within a tight site required a virtual review of each crane’s 
pick locations and their ability to reach the proper building elements. Involving construction 
workers in the coordination activities improved the constructability and resulted in a leaner 
meeting with a better understanding of upcoming work.

3.5.2 Foundation 
As the soil's load-bearing capacity was insufficient to support the main structure, pile 
foundations were used to secure the vertical load. One of the significant challenges of the 
foundation work was the active underground stormwater culvert, Bassett Creek, which ran the 
entire length of the project. The detailed profile of the structure was unknown, and instead of 
completely removing the subsoil down to the branch line, drilling was conducted at 90’ 
increments. From this information, the design team created a virtual model, which was used in 
conjunction with the design parameters to define the foundation of the ball field. 24 large 
transfer and 29 smaller grade beams were required to disperse building loads beyond the edge 
of the structure by up to twelve 100’ deep steel piles per beam. The geometry of each derived 
from the model was shared with the steel reinforcing and temporary shoring subcontractors for 
their shop drawings.

3.5.3 Casting 
The foundation project contained many in-situ deck castings, where more than 5000 sleeves 
and openings had to be placed. This required high accuracy and coordination between different 
design groups, as the fully dimensioned interior plans were not projected. To ensure deck 
sleeving and layout were able to work in real-time with the evolution of the design, a new 
innovative solution was developed to increase the quality and minimize the risk of delays and 
financial losses. The mechanical contractor could place the sleeves and openings highly 
efficiently using a total robotic station and dynamic tags created within the 3D model. This 
aspect was essential to ensure accurate layout while at the same time reducing the time and 
labor required, as the traditional methods of measuring locations from a marked grid were a 
time-consuming task with a low degree of accuracy.

Challenges  
The project team's challenges were mainly concentrated on storing and sharing data and

contractual relationships with suppliers and third-party users, and as a result, the synergy

3.5.4 Distribution of empirical data 
After conducting a methodological study of available international case studies on experiences 
with the use of VDC in foundation projects, there is insufficient empirical data in the field to 
provide an overall picture of how development is going. The studies show that the digitization 
of the construction is centered around BIM and its properties to create an overview of quantities, 
dimensions, and collisions, but not how VDC can optimize time and make potential challenges 
visible in foundation work. The empirical data indicate a dividing line between the digital 
development in construction and civil work. The difference between the two types of work is 
characterized by identifying building parts and work processes.

4.2 Interview with the project manager, Hassan Mohammed, VDC Aarsleff 
Which digital tools are most beneficial related to civil work and foundation projects?  
I can think of 2 specific tools/software. One is Civil3D, a program combining existing 
terrain conditions and buildings with new project material. It’s possible to analyze 
existing conditions more efficiently and make more collision checks than by drawing 
them in AutoCAD. In Aarsleff, the program is also used to simulate excavation rates, 
excavations, soil subdivisions and wires in soil. The other program that also creates a 
lot of value in Aarsleff is the BIM viewer. The BIM viewer is used to coordinate the 
project. This tool allows contractors to assemble all project parts into a single 3D model. 
How does Aarsleff use VDC in soil management, and what benefits does it create? 
Based on existing foundations, data from drones, surveyors, laser scanning, 
geotechnical drilling, and other types of soil samples are generated to provide an 
accurate picture of soil conditions and surrounding obstacles. The foundation is 
transferred directly from a PC to the excavator's computer. The information the 
excavator receives are in a 1:1 ratio.The excavator has a built-in sensor, that can transfer 
data to the computer. The advantages are to ensure accurate operation, and the relevant 
actors can quickly and more efficiently follow the excavation flow and respond on time. 
It will also be easier to keep an eye on soil volumes, which is very relevant when 
deciding on soil balance. 
Is the potential for VDC in Civil work as great as the construction over terrain? 
I would say that the potential is the same, but VDC creates a different value in civil 
work than for construction over terrain. In buildings above ground, there are far more 
objects of many different sizes, types and dimensions. VDC use in Civil work is more 
process-controlled, whereas it is more object-based with construction over terrain. That 
requires a lot of internal coordination. On the other hand, large Civil work projects have 
simple elements, such as a bridge or a railway, but the complexity and work processes 
are often more complicated. the process demands more focus, but I am sure that the 
potential is so great that the new initiatives and technologies can drive development.

On what parameters has Aarsleff received the highest benefits from increased VDC?

Has increased VDC resulted in less material waste during concrete/reinforcement work?

construction manager can focus more on planning the foundation work and spend less 
time on the 2D details. The savings can be felt when Aarsleff makes offers, the costs 
related to reinforcement work have decreased, and thus gained the company financial 
benefits.

Root cause 
The root cause analysis indicates that VDC plans to secure digital construction by streamlining 
item- and process-based planning. Civil work is based on traditional items with simplified 
geometries and material types; therefore, the need for digitization has been a second priority. 
Both international and Danish case studies point out that BIM development has streamlined

civil constructions projects are carried out more frequently, it has set higher standards for

5.2 PESTEL 
The PESTEL analysis has been chosen as a methodological tool for examining the external 
forces that impact VDC and the construction industry. VDC's function is to assist the design 
and planning of projects using digital tools. Therefore, VDC's development depends highly on 
clients, contractors, and other important stakeholders within AEC to add more projects to their 
order list. There is thus a direct connection between the situation in the construction industry 
and VDC development. PESTEL is an appropriate method for analyzing the market situation 
by examining the political, economic, social, technological, legal and environmental factors. 
5.2.1 P – politics 
Political strategy and level of education 
In January 2019, the Danish Transport and Building Ministry prepared a strategy to digitize 
public projects. The report emphasizes that having a wide range of courses and continuing 
education is vital to ensure a high digital level. The supply of education must cover the digital 
competence needed in construction. In 2018, the Institute of Technology (TI) studied 
development trends and competence needs within the construction industry, focusing on 
building designers. TI points out that the digital competencies are generally behind compared 
to other industries. That is due to the older generation of building designers having more 
difficulty adapting to the new IT requirements and competencies. Only 13% of the building 
designers have been offered a new IT qualification. in comparison, other industries have a 
percentage recruitment of 25%. IT qualification involves a digital exchange of drawings and 
other documents using advanced BIM and software that synchronises financial management, 
time planning and risk assessment. There is a considerable need for competence in the 
construction industry to participate in digital tendering, project management and collaboration 
internally and externally.22 
Suppose the digital requirements and competencies are not raised, and a more comprehensive 
range of continuing education is not offered, it will result in smaller contractors being less 
competitive and unable to participate in the tender phase. During the hospital project in Aalborg, 
the management agreed with The Danish Construction Association and TEKNIQ to offer their 
members courses in digital offer and BIM so that ICT requirements in construction projects do 
not scare the smaller contractors to bid on these types of projects.23

5.2.2 E – Economics 
Financial benefits of using ICT  
In 2012, DTU byg measured financial gains from digital construction, covering the ICT 
concept. The study shows that ICT creates financial gains from the design and up to the delivery 
phases. The most significant gains are found in tenders/offers, where DTU-byg has assessed 
that increasing the use of 3D modelling and access to precise quantities and areas can give a 
reduction corresponding to 15% of the contract price. The study also shows that the gains 
associated with the progress on the construction site give rise to interest savings corresponding 
to DKK 300,000 on a total contract sum of DKK 50 million. With increased ICT, the client can 
systematically register the tasks performed on the construction site, thus better controlling the 
contractors' payments.24 
Current market situation 
A new report from Statistics Denmark shows that building materials have risen in price since 
the end of 2020. The price index has risen from a price index of 103 to 111 which is a result of 
the corona pandemic (A. 6). Price increases for steel and iron have been noticeable for the entire 
construction industry, as steel production is mainly imported to danish construction projects. 
The statics also shows that from 2020 to 2021, the material and labor costs for concrete works 
have increased by 5.9% and 4.0%, respectively.25 
According to “Bjarke Roed Frederiksen”, chief consultant at SMVDanmark, the two most 
important factors for the price increases are related to Corona. Firstly, restrictions have made 
citizens unable to spend money on travel experiences and hotel stays. As a result, more people 
have chosen to renovate or build new properties, which has led to a higher demand for building 
materials. Secondly, the global supply chains have also been affected by Corona, as transport 
and logistics have been hampered by closed ports and borders and a shortage of containers. 
That has reduced the supply of building materials and led to rising prices.26 The high price 
increases have made it more difficult for the contractors to submit tenders, as there is a great 
deal of uncertainty associated with the expected prices when the project starts. That means there 
is a greater probability that the budget will not be kept or that the contractor will have a large 
deficit, as the original offer deviates too much from the market price.27

Distribution of construction projects 
In 2021, Byggefakta published statistics for initiated construction projects from 2017 to 2021 
in Denmark. 2021 was a record year for the construction industry, with projects for 103.5

In the third quarter of 2021, there were approximately 188,000 artisans that were employed in

considerable price spiral, creating significant market uncertainties. There is a great need to 
recruit foreign labor, and generally strengthen the construction segment.

5.2.3 S – Social 
Qualifications and competencies 
The Danish Construction Association (DCA) states that 139,000 artisans are over 60 years old 
and that in the future, there will be a shortage of young workers in the construction industry.29 
Fewer young people are taking a VET education related to the construction industry. Therefore 
DCA demands that there must be a targeted effort to get more young people into the industry. 
An analysis from TI concludes that artisans are lagging in digital competencies. Still, according 
to the foreman of the construction group 3F, Kim Lind Larsen, the Danish artisans have the 
suitable profiles to embrace the digital tools. Through the apprenticeships, the young people 
have worked purposefully with IT solutions to use the digital competencies on the construction 
site.30 
Communication methods on site 
The concrete worker usually works in a work gang. They perform tasks in a specific area or 
perform specific phases of the foundation project. The work gang is led by a foreman, who 
communicates directly with the project managers and enters into agreements on future 
milestones and deadlines. After agreeing with the construction management team, the foreman 
negotiates chords, participate in safety rounds, and delegate tasks to the group members. 
Modern Danish construction sites are characterized by international cooperation, which has led 
to increased foreign labor. Therefore, a more significant requirement is to ensure better 
communication in the execution phase by implementing communication methods, such as 
illustrations, drawings, and models. 
Social structures  
The social aspect of being in a work gang means that there are agreed norms and values that 
members must conform to thrive optimally. The social structures on a construction site facilitate 
a social identity that is connected to the work gang but not necessarily the companies. Studies 
show relations to the work gang are weighted higher than the relation to the construction site31. 
It emphasizes that social conditions are an essential part of the production and pose a real risk 
to the level of production, working environment and safety. The work gang increase their self-
image and status through a social categorization, dividing the construction site into “them” and 
“us”. This perception can result in contradictory cooperation with the management team, 
affecting the work gang’s commitment.

5.2.4 T – Technology 
Denmark needs a productivity boost in the Construction Industry. In 2014, the IDA engineering

5.2.5 E – Environment 
Sustainable strategy 
 Construction work accounts for 30 percent of CO2 emissions in Denmark and constitutes to 
35 percent of all waste products. Therefore, climate requirements are necessary to ensure an 
efficient green transition in the construction industry. There is a political agreement to introduce 
requirements for the maximum C02 emissions per m2 per—year for new construction over 
1000m2 from 2023 and for all new construction from 2025. The C02 requirements are expected 
to lead to increased use of wood in construction, as wood has a lower C02 footprint than 
concrete and steel. Concrete is still expected to play an essential role in the construction 
industry, but with a focus on developing C02 neutral cement and concrete. Therefore, the future 
offers more hybrid buildings composed of materials based on the most sustainable complete 
solutions.35 
DGNB-certification  
DGNB is a sustainable building certification based on the UN’s definition of sustainability. 
DGNB is originally a German sustainability certification, and in the spring of 2012, the first 
Danish version was launched. DGNB places equal emphasis on the environment, economy, and 
social conditions and aim to promote sustainable, innovative solutions in the construction 
industry. 36 Points are awarded for the environment, economy, and social conditions. The total 
number of points determines the certification class, ranging from bronze to platinum. 
LCA – Life Cycle Assessment 
LCA is an analytical method where you assess the potential environmental impacts and resource 
consumption associated with a product or service. The method is key in demonstrating that the 
CO2 requirements are met. The documentation requires a unique digital infrastructure where 
the relevant stakeholders can access and share relevant environmental declarations, generic 
data, and data sheets. Improving data sharing can ensure a better decision when comparing 
different building materials. Currently, the environmental declarations are scattered in various 
national and international databases, making it more challenging to perform LCA. There are 
two international standards related to LCA, ISO 14040, which describes principles and structure 
and ISO 14044, which specifies requirements and guidelines.37

5.2.7 PESTEL Analysis – Summary 
The PESTEL analysis indicates that the macro-environmental factors require special attention in 
future solution proposals. Steel and concrete prices are rising, resulting in foundation projects 
becoming more expensive to carry out. There is a lack of digital competencies for artisans and 
building designers, and these are not optimal conditions for using VDC in foundation projects. The 
digital agenda can lead to social challenges, as the virtual competencies must be combined with the 
physical competencies on the construction site. The political aspect of digitalization gives rise to new 
and innovative guidelines and strategies in the public sphere. The strategic plan secures the country’s 
interests and has resulted in many more companies becoming more digital in the form of increased 
use of ICT and BIM. The current market indicates that digital construction and VDC are progressing, 
but the construction market suffers from high price increases due to the high demand. Inflation is 
rising and thus creates more significant uncertainty at all levels of the construction value chain. That 
implies there will be more requirements for managing deliveries, material waste and more automated 
work processes to minimize socio-economic problems in the future. The uncertain market situation 
affects risk management through imprecise offers, smaller profits and a labor shortage. Digital 
development is flourishing through greater collaboration among the most important Danish 
stakeholders and organizations, as several standard specifications are published. The analysis shows 
a lack of a standard working basis for digital models in the construction industry. The analysis also 
shows great potential in making construction more sustainable and protecting the environment by 
minimizing material waste and developing a solid digital infrastructure that can ensure accurate LCA 
when the client has to choose building materials.

6.2 Solution space 
1. Monitoring material consumption and deliveries 
The current geopolitical conditions and market situation have led to longer delivery times and higher 
purchase prices for critical building materials such as reinforcement, steel and cement. There is a need 
to increase safety around future deliveries to run operations on the construction site. There is a direct 
correlation between the complexity level of the project and the uncertainties associated with the 
project material. That means that deliveries and orders must be constantly adjusted according to the 
latest revisions, as the collaboration between the AEC is more dynamic for these types of projects. 
The conditions require better facility management and increased use of automated processes to handle 
material consumption and ensure good logistics on the construction site. The economic situation 
indicates that there will be a labor shortage in the construction industry and that high inflation will 
remain a long-term problem. There is a greater need to create innovative solutions and a working 
environment that suits the lean construction principle. 
2. Extended collision controls at hotspots in the BIM model based on process-driven work 
The methodological analysis from section 5.1 indicated that VDC’s development focused on item-
based digital construction planning. As the foundation project is managed by process-based planning, 
new automated solutions must be implemented to ensure efficient planning related to existing 
conditions, resources, and tight work areas.  Collision controls are a simple and effective digital tool 
for item-based planning. It has provided excellent value for the AEC to optimize the BIM model in 
the design phase. Process control of critical work areas or building parts must be determined so that 
the 3D model can detect possible collisions before, during and after the work has been performed. 
3. Optimize internal collaboration and knowledge sharing by raising digital competencies. 
There is a need to acquire the right competencies in each organization. There must be better 
opportunities for knowledge-sharing, as technological development is ahead of the digital 
competencies in the construction industry. VDC must be organized in aspects of construction 
processes and negotiations that require the right digital skills. According to Martin Vinding from 
NCC, only a few employees know what kind of digital tools the VDC department can offer in the 
planning phase. Project managers on the construction site have far greater insight into buildability 
and process management. Therefore, this target group must get a better insight into VDC services and 
their properties so VDC can be better integrated. The solution area is characterized by behaviour

change and higher utilization of internal knowledge sharing. These approaches are closely linked to 
the company’s image and future strategies and require long-term plans and strategies.

Ensuring the digital development of the future requires strategic actions, which involve legal 
regulations and broader cooperation between the client, the contractor and the AEC. Mikkel Rønne

6.4.1 Solution proposal 1 
The first identified solution to ensure efficient planning and production involved increased 
monitoring of materials at the construction site. The solution is based on strengthening the contractor's

6.4.2 Solution proposal 2 
Extended collision controls in process-related management 
The extended collision control is a solution proposal that supports process-controlled planning using 
VDC. This solution aims to ensure efficient planning methods in critical work areas, known as 
hotspots. The contractor identifies these hotspots early in the design and planning phase. The collision 
control is performed at these hotspots. Ensure that the selected working method does not collide with 
other building parts before, during or after the work has been performed. 
The workflow for the extended collision control is illustrated as a flow chart in Figure 26. The first 
step is to collect the necessary data input to determine the required geometry for hotspot areas. This 
information is the working curves for machines, safety distances and other necessary specifications. 
The second step is to record the number of possible collisions and locate them in the shared model. 
The data analysis must then evaluate whether the work processes risk exceeding the budget or the 
schedule. If collisions pose a real risk, the project manager will reevaluate the processes to find other 
solutions and working methods that do not pose a process collision. Once the evaluation is done, the 
new re-evaluated budgets and work methods are presented to the scheduler and then forwarded to the 
client.

large digital building systems such as Dalux, which can include the app in their services so that more 
companies can access the service more efficiently. 
Legislation – Handling personal data must live up to the updated legislation within GPDR. The

Chapter 9  
Discussion 
Based on insights retrieved from the previous chapter, this chapter will discuss the empirical findings 
concerning prior research and knowledge explored through interviews, method analysis and literature 
review. This section will be centered around the identified sub-questions, discussing the key insights 
from our study related to the problem definition.  
“Today's construction industry is suffering from productivity issues due to a lack of integration of 
VDC in execution and planning of foundation projects. “  
The construction industry has been transforming digitally since the turn of the millennium. It is 
leading to a change in the construction agenda. VDC's purpose is to build virtually before building in 
real-time, and that has required all links in the construction industry's value chains to work more 
virtually and automated. The research methods, from both interviews and analysis tools, indicate that 
VDC is still a relatively new concept in Denmark, with risks associated with increasing 
implementation, as the industry is generally lagging in digitization. From the interview with Mikkel 
Rønne (Molio), there was a clear statement that VDC would be able to benefit the client in terms of 
time optimization, finances gains and image. However, the client is unwilling to take the risk of 
paying extra expenses associated with multiple VDC services as the contractor would make a 
financial profit by implementing VDC. The client's focus is on ensuring that the initiatives that are 
generally actively used in the construction industry, such as the ICT agreement and BIM, are 
improved, which is also part of the VDC concept, as described in section 3.1 
The interview with Hassan Mohamed (Aarsleff), VDC has been beneficial for both the contractor and 
the client in some situations. It has mainly been modelling the reinforcement, and processes 
associated with quantities, cut and bend lists and orders to suppliers. Aarsleff has been able to generate 
a positive cash flow and, at the same time, become more attractive to the client. VDC's influence on 
the foundation project has resulted in time optimization, less material waste, increased automated 
planning and improved collaboration between the client and the supplier. 
==PAGE:97==
Technical University of Denmark | M.Sc. Civil Engineering 
89 of 91 
 
 
From the Interview with Martin Vinding (NCC), there was a clear perception that VDC is not 
streamlined to the most critical link, the executive part. Therefore there is a lack of utilization of 
productivity and time optimization of foundation projects. The VDC concept thrives at the head office 
along with the AEC, but construction managers and artisans tend to work according to classical 
planning. As a result, the processing in the design phase is lost in the execution phase. 
A prerequisite for using VDC in planning is access to the right digital competencies. Based on the 
pestle analysis and interviews, it is the construction industry's biggest challenge, especially among 
contractors and artisans. The development of technology within digital construction has been shifted 
concerning the digital competencies among the most important stakeholders, which is also evident in 
the PESTEL evaluation, as the technological aspects do not pose risks in the implementation. The 
empirical data indicate that competencies within a general digital understanding and international 
BIM standards are lacking in the Danish construction industry. There is a common desire to increase 
the level of ambition through digital strategies at the company level and politically. The legal 
framework must reflect the ambitions through common guidelines and concrete objectives at all 
strategic levels. 
The foundation project is characterized by process-controlled planning and has had a secondary role 
in the digital transformation. The methodological analyses indicate the root cause of why VDC has 
not streamlined design and production in the initial phase of construction. The foundation work of 
our case study Kontorværket, highlighted some work processes that required attention from the 
construction management in the planning phase. Our data collection indicated that the benefits of 
using VDC and simple automated work processes could optimize the foundation work time by 2 days. 
In addition to time optimization, there will also be a financial gain, as costs can be saved for machines, 
construction site operation and extra wages. CO2 emissions will be reduced, and this will benefit the 
sustainable agenda. Initially, in section 1.2 the hypothesis for the thesis was presented. The hypothesis 
stated that increased VDC use in planning and executing foundation projects could lead to time 
optimization, profits, and quality. The data reported in the thesis appear to support the hypothesis, as 
data from our case study analysis and interviews indicate that VDC can streamline work processes 
related to the foundation project and time-optimize common work tasks. 
From the interview with the foreman, the overall statement was that VDC does not always result in 
financial gains for artisans, as piecework salaries have not had the same development as digital 
development. On the other hand, it has become easier to build as the project material becomes more 
==PAGE:98==
Technical University of Denmark | M.Sc. Civil Engineering 
90 of 91 
 
 
visual. That is an advantage for the artisans, as communication with the construction management is 
based on a common platform that everyone can understand. The artisans need to have faster access 
to information in the model, as this makes it possible to focus on building faster and, at the same time, 
work digitally.  
Recommendations for future research 
The implementation of this thesis framework demonstrates the potential of VDC for the essential 
stakeholders in the construction industry. However, since VDC is still a relatively new concept in 
Denmark, several aspects can be considered in future research. The thesis focused on the initial start 
of a construction project and how to increase productivity by exploiting the potential of VDC in the 
planning phase. As the developed proposals are primarily a proof of concept, it is assessed that the 
suggestions below will assist in further evaluation. 
• Investigate how VDC can be better integrated into sustainable construction 
• Develop an implementation strategy for how to increase digital competencies 
• Examine how VDC can improve safety during the execution 
• Investigate how the client can handle the digital information from the contractor

Chapter 10  
Conclusion 
The project's main outcome is a conceptual and practical framework for using VDC as a digital tool 
throughout the planning and execution phase of the foundation project. This thesis has set out to 
answer the following problem definition: 
“Today's construction industry is suffering from productivity issues due to a lack of integration of 
VDC in execution and planning of foundation projects. “  
Based on the collected empirical data and assessments from professionals within VDC, we have 
discovered that VDC in foundation projects is used to a minimal extent. Through the interview with 
Hassan from Per Aarsleff and Mikkel Rønne from Molio, we have ascertained that increased use of 
VDC in foundation projects can increase productivity and time optimize work processes. Still, the 
full potential has not been reached due to the lack of integration of process-based planning tools. The 
focus in the construction industry has been to become proficient in existing digital tools and working 
methods before using new innovative methods such as VDC, as it requires that all stakeholders in the 
construction industry also follow the strategy. There is a great deal of financial uncertainty in the 
construction industry, which has meant that contractors are careful when dealing with VDC. The 
reasons are, among other things, that there is a lack of digital skills among contractors and artisans. 
We can conclude that VDC is suitable for time optimization of foundation projects and reducing 
errors related to the execution. However, due to a lack of standardizations and digital competencies. 
The contractors have been challenged to integrate VDC in the planning and execution of the initial 
phases.
