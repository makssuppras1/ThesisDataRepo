Abstract
The beaming effect of loudspeakers with increasing frequency is a commonly known issue. The goal
of this thesis is to use topology optimization on a fully acoustic finite element model of an existing
compression driver to create a lens to be put in front of the driver in order to achieve equal sound
pressure levels over a 90 degrees listening space. The constructed finite element model is based on
measurements of the compression driver via a velocity matching method on the diaphragm to capture
the electroacoustic behavior of the driver.
A set of optimal parameter settings for interpolation, filtering, projection and volume constraints in
the topology optimization is found via a single frequency dual objective problem on the well-described
piston in an infinite baffle problem. The method is later used for multiple frequency optimization of an
acoustic lens on the compression driver model. Through examples using different objective functions,
the importance of clearly defining the goal and proper formulation of the objective is highlighted.
The results show that via topology optimization, it is possible to improve the directivity in most of
the desired frequency range. This however comes with the drawback of altering the on-axis response
of the driver.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In professional audio systems at concerts it is desired and important to have an even sound field, thus
equal sound pressure levels across the audience. The most commonly addressed issue in large sound
reinforcement systems is the ability of being able to focus low frequencies, thus not waste acoustic
energy where no audience is located. A well known approach to direct low frequency sound is stacking
loudspeakers in a J-shaped array. The J-shape is to be able to point the loudspeakers at the front
and back of the audience. The vertical stacking, due to the large baffle seen by the loudspeaker,
allows for direction control in vertical directions down to a lower frequency than for one loudspeaker
alone. This configuration has no stacking in the horizontal direction, thus the lower limit of directional
control is that by one loudspeaker alone. This is however regarded as ”OK” since the audience is
most often spread over a wide horizontal angle. However, it is commonly known that loudspeakers
become more directional with increasing frequency, thus difficult to achieve an equal sound field with
increasing frequency over a wide listening area. Horns are often used because of their ability to control
directivity and their increased efficiency over direct radiating loudspeakers. However, also for horns
a beaming effect is observed with increasing frequency.
The hypothesis in this work is that via topology optimization it is possible to construct an acoustic
lens to be put in front of a loudspeaker to enhance its off-axis performance, thus achieve equal sound
pressure levels over a listening area of 90 degrees spread.
With simulation techniques such as the finite element method it is possible to approximate solutions to
complex problems in the fields of many physics ranging from structural mechanics, heat transfer, fluid
flow to acoustics and much more, where analytical solutions do not exist. Using the finite element
method with mathematical optimization techniques, it is possible to find optimal solutions to the
given problem at hand and is widely used in the engineering industry. Topology optimization builds
on mathematical methods to obtain optimal material distributions within a given domain, subjected
to some defined objective and constraints. Topology optimization was first presented by Bendsøe and
Kikuchi [4] in 1988 within the field of structural mechanics and has later been used with numerical
solutions to the Helmholtz equation governing the propagation of acoustic waves. Within the field
of acoustics, topology optimization is fairly new and not much literature that deals with exterior
acoustics is to be found where solutions have been implemented and tested on actual products in the
industry. The literature mostly deals with noise reduction via introduction of reflecting or absorbing
material in rooms and design of sound barriers [14] or interior acoustics in ex.
exhaust systems
2
==PAGE:18==
presented in [30]. Regarding loudspeakers, most examples in the literature are not realized. However,
Berggren et al. [31] show that a combination of topology- and shape optimization can be used to
obtain a horn flare and lens combination that successfully achieves frequency independent directivity
up to 1 kHz. Christensen [9] shows that topology optimization of a tweeter phase plug can be used to
achieve a flatter on-axis response over its entire operating range. Similarly, Bezzola [7] shows, however
with realization and measurements, that with the use of topology optimization, one can improve the
high frequency response while maintaining a smooth response at lower frequencies and maintaining a
satisfactory directivity in a tweeter phase plug. In this work, an existing compression driver mounted
in a coaxial driver unit is measured and a numerical model is made, based on the measured response.
Due to the lack of information regarding the electro-mechanical properties of the driver, the model
is a purely acoustic model, in such that the diaphragm is excited by a complex velocity forced to
match the measured on-axis response. Prior to performing topology optimization on the compression
driver and lens combination, a study on interpolation, penalization, filtering, projection and material
volume constraint is carried out on a numerical model of the well described circular piston in an
infinite baffle. Based on those findings, various examples of topology optimization are shown on the
compression driver with lens model. All numerical simulations and optimization are carried out using
the commercial software COMSOL Multiphysics [1].
1.1
Motivation
In my work as an acoustics student assistant in the DTU based company Kombo Audio ApS, a coaxial
driver is used in our guitar, bass and vocals amplifier. The coaxial driver has a compression driver
for high frequency reproduction. The speaker is to be used for small pop-up concerts. The speakers
are meant to be stacked vertically, and most amateur bands will only use one stack most likely placed
centered relative to the band. Since the speakers are placed in height with the audience, the audience
will most likely spread wider to have the direct sound coming towards them. Thus, we want to address
the beaming of high frequencies by placing a lens in front of the compression driver unit to be able
to cover a wider listening area.
1.2
Scope
In this work, an existing compression driver mounted in a coaxial driver unit is measured and a
corresponding FE model is constructed. The impact on the frequency response and directivity by the
introduction of the lens found via topology optimization is only evaluated in the FE model. However,
since the model is based on measurements, it is made possible for future work to realize and measure
the response of the compression driver and lens combination.

than the far-field limit in Equation 2.18, the difference in path lengths from the different places on
the piston to the observation point P must be considered, as cancellations may occur at certain
frequencies. This consideration of path lengths is referred to as Fresnel diffraction [24]. A highly

stemming from different points on the piston can be made. Consider the piston as being divided into
rings. These rings represent areas (also referred to as Fresnel areas) on the piston that vibrate in equal

into three equal phase areas. If the distances are chosen such that d1 = z +λ/2 and d2 = d1 +λ/2, the

second area is 180 degrees out of phase the that from the first area. Similarly, the pressure arriving
from the third area will be 180 degrees out of phase with that from the second area, thus in phase with
the contribution from the first area. In this scenario there are two areas radiating in phase and one
area radiating out of phase, thus two areas cancel out and one area is said to be the effective radiator
and a maximum in the pressure at the observation point occur. For an even number of radiating
areas, there are an equal number of areas radiating in and out of phase, thus a cancellation occurs at
the point P resulting in a pressure minimum. Thus, for an odd number of areas, maximum pressure
occur and for an even number of areas, a pressure minimum occurs. The total number of equal phase
areas on the piston is dependent on the radius a, wavelength λ and the distance [24].

2.3
Loudspeakers
Loudspeakers, or often referred to as transducers, are electro-mechanical devices that convert electrical
signals into acoustic output through mechanical movement. Loudspeakers come in different shapes
and types.
In this project, a coaxial loudspeaker unit is used.
A coaxial loudspeaker is a full-
range loudspeaker consisting of two individual loudspeaker units, namely a traditional moving coil
loudspeaker and a compression driver, where the lower frequencies are produced by the moving coil
loudspeaker and the high frequencies are generated by the compression driver.
2.3.1
Moving-coil loudspeakers
The cross-section of a regular moving coil loudspeaker is shown in Figure 2.5. It consists of a diaphragm
which generates sound with movement in the vertical direction. Two suspensions, the spider and the
surround, are made of a flexible material and ensures movement only in the vertical direction. The
suspensions also serve as movement limiters and provide a restoring force which keeps the diaphragm
in place when no signal is applied. The dust cap prevents dust from entering the electrical motor
system [25]. On the diaphragm, a voice coil former is attached which has a voice coil wound around
it. When an electrical signal is applied to the loudspeaker, current runs through the voice coil and a
force is created due to the interaction between the magnetic fields of the voice coil and the permanent
magnet. To maximize the force, the top plate directs the magnetic field to be perpendicular to the
flow of the current [24]. Since audio signals are AC signals, the direction of the force changes with
the alternating direction of the current, which causes the diaphragm to move up and down.
11
==PAGE:27==
Figure 2.5: Cross sectional view of a moving coil loudspeaker. [25]
2.3.2
Compression drivers
The cross section of a compression driver is shown in Figure 2.6. Similar to the moving coil loud-
speaker, a force is generated via the interaction of the magnetic fields from the permanent magnet and
the voice coil which causes to diaphragm to move back and forth with the alternating direction of cur-
rent. As seen, the diaphragm has a sector of a sphere shape and the sound is radiated into the throat.
The throat is a tube with a smaller cross section than the diaphragm, thus the air is compressed at
the throat, hence the name of the driver. A phase plug is located between the diaphragm and the
throat to ensure equal distance from diaphragm to throat, thus a constant phase across the cross
section of the throat [24]. Usually a horn or waveguide with an increasing cross section is attached to
the throat. The purpose of the horn is to provide an impedance match between the low impedance
medium of air and the high impedance of the loudspeaker [22] and thereby achieve greater efficiency.

2.3.4
Loudspeaker phase
Frequency response
The sound pressure produced by a loudspeaker is complex, i.e. it has a magnitude and a phase.
How the magnitude and phase of the incoming signal is affected at each frequency is described by its
transfer function or frequency response [28]
Y (ω) = H(ω)X(ω).
(2.29)
where Y (ω) is the output spectrum, X(ω) is the input spectrum and H(ω) is the frequency response.
The frequency response can be decomposed into its magnitude |H(ω)| and phase ϕ(ω) = ∠H(ω).
When expressed as a complex exponential, the amplitude and phase appear explicitly [28]
H(ω) = |H(ω)|ejϕ(ω)
(2.30)
In general, the phase can be described as a function of the gradient of the magnitude waveform [13].
Thus, if the gradient is flat, the phase is zero. If the gradient is positive, also the phase will be positive
and if the gradient is negative, the phase will be negative. This concept is shown in Figure 2.9. If
the phase can be predicted only from the magnitude, i.e. no more phase shifts than those dictated
by the gradient of the magnitude response, the system is said to be minimum phase. In fact most
loudspeakers are minimum phase [13].

2.3.4.1
Measurement distance and time delay
As seen in Section 2.1.2, the real part of a plane wave is cos(ωt+kx+ϕ). It is seen that the wave has
phase components related to frequency, time and distance as well as the initial phase or phasor ϕ. As
aforementioned, far from the source in free field, a spherical wave can be regarded as locally plane.
Thus, the waves arriving at the microphone in a measurement setup can be regarded as plane. So,
the signal picked up by the microphone holds information of both the phasor ϕ (here, the loudspeaker
phase) and distance travelled. The phase change stemming from the distance from loudspeaker to
microphone is known as the phase lag and is related to the frequency, as the phase changes a full cycle
(2π radians or 360 degrees) when travelling one full wavelength. In complex notation, the distance is
affecting the phase by ejkr, where k is the wavenumber and r is the measuring distance. Usually a
loudspeaker’s performance is measured at one meter distance with an input power of one watt. Thus,
the phase response will be dominated by the phase lag and it is therefore necessary to remove the
phase lag and one should be left with only the loudspeaker phase. In fact, the phase to be removed
may exceed the measuring distance as only at the acoustic center of the loudspeaker, no phase lag is
present. The acoustic center of a loudspeaker is not easy to determine, and is in most cases found by
increasing the distance bit by bit until only the loudspeaker phase is left. It is however not an easy
task to get the exact distance to the acoustic center as the distance changes with frequency [15].
In the case of measuring loudspeaker cabinets with two or more units, it is important to find the
acoustic center of each unit as the relative phase shift caused by the time delay from the distance
differences from unit to microphone between the units can cause peaks and dips to occur in the
15
==PAGE:31==
crossover region. For example, a tweeter often has its acoustic center closer to the baffle front compared
to a woofer. By determining the distance differences between acoustic centers, it is possible to delay
the signals to match the latest arriving driver.
2.3.4.2
Group delay
A delay does not alter the perception of sound if the full signal spectrum is delayed. One will still
hear the audio sample as unchanged but arriving later. However, if only parts of the signal is delayed,
the perception is altered. This concept of groups of frequencies experiencing relative delays is called
group delay, and is defined as the derivative of the phase with respect to frequency [8]
Tg(ω) = −dϕ(ω)
dω

(2.31)
That is, for nonlinear phase systems, the phase/time delay is different for different frequency bands,
thus not only the magnitude change from input to output of a given system is frequency dependent,
also the time of arrival is. Thus, the output signal is said to have experienced group delay distortion
with respect to the input signal [8].
All analogue filters cause group delay by the introduced change of phase (time delay), whether it is an
electrical crossover network or an electro-mechanical filter created by a loudspeaker and the enclosure

Topology optimization
Topology optimization uses mathematical methods to obtain material distributions within a given
design domain to optimize the performance of a system subjected to a predefined objective and
possible constraints [5, 30]. Inside the design domain, a variable χ that can take values between zero
and one is introduced. In structural mechanics, a value of zero is often associated with no material
and a value of one corresponds to a solid material. In the field of acoustic topology optimization, the
extreme values of χ are associated with air and some solid material. In this work, the density method
is used. In the density method, the material properties are constant within each element inside the
design domain. For each step in the optimization, a gradient-based sensitivity analysis with respect
to the optimization variables is carried out, and the design is updated based on its outcome.

Measurements and compression driver
FE model
In this chapter, the measurement setup and measured responses of the compression driver under
various baffle conditions are presented. Based on the measurements, an FE model is constructed and
compared to the measurements. Basing the FE model on the measurements will allow for evaluation
of the impact of an acoustic lens in future work.
The compression driver is to be measured in an infinite baffle under anechoic conditions 1. In reality
it is not possible to have an infinite baffle. However, to approximate infinite baffle behavior, the size
of the baffle has to be at least half the wavelength. Thus, to achieve infinite baffle conditions at 50
Hz, the distance from speaker center to the edge of the baffle must be around 3.5 meters [6]. However,
as the directivity only plays an important role at mid and high frequencies it is decided to measure
the response from 500 Hz - 10 kHz. Thus, the required baffle size to achieve infinite baffle conditions
is 34.3 cm. The compression driver is only measured and compared to the FE model up to 10 kHz,

3.1
Diffraction
Moreover, one has to be aware of diffraction effects from the baffle edges causing ripples in the
measured response. Diffraction is the concept of a change in the direction of propagation of a wavefront
due to the encounter with an obstacle or discontinuity [32]. Thus, in the case of a baffle, the wavefront
travelling along the baffle faces a discontinuity at the baffle end and the 2π radiation expands to 4π (see
Figure 3.1). This can be modelled as small point sources on the baffle edge. At the listening position,
the direct waves and diffracted waves are summed up and since the diffracted wave is travelling a
longer distance to reach the listening position, the relative phase shift gives rise to peaks and dips in
the response. At the baffle edge, the phase of the diffracted wave is inverted [32]. Thus, at frequencies
where the extra travelling distance for the diffracted wave compared to the direct wave is an odd
multiple of half the wavelength, the two waves add up and gives rise to a peak in the response. At
frequencies where the extra travelling distance for the diffracted wave is a multiple of the wavelength,

CHAPTER 4
Topology optimization of acoustic lenses
This chapter shows examples of topology optimization of acoustic lenses in front of a loudspeaker.
First, a study on interpolation, penalization, filtering, projection and material volume constraints is
carried out on an FE model of the well-described piston in an infinite baffle in which the pressure can
be solved for analytically. The piston case is chosen as the basis for the study due to its close proximity
to a modern loudspeaker and as described in section 2.2, the phase contributions from different places
on the piston due to differences in path lengths result in cancellations in the near field on-axis and
in the near and far field off-axis. Thus, it is deemed possible to insert material that could change
the path lengths of the main contributions to the cancellations and ensure constructive interference
instead. Later, the optimal topology optimization parameter settings found from studying the piston
is used on the compression driver FE model in order to improve the off-axis response.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion and conclusions
This thesis aimed to use topology optimization to improve the off-axis sound pressure level (SPL) of
an existing compression driver mounted in a coaxial driver and thereby achieve an even sound field
over a 90 degrees spread. A fully acoustic finite element (FE) model including diaphragm, phase
plug, throat and horn section was created. Its response was forced to match the measured on-axis
response under a non-baffled condition via matching the velocity on the diaphragm. A comparison
between the on-axis response of the FE model when mounted in an infinite baffle and the measured
on-axis response when mounted in an IEC baffle to approximate infinite baffle conditions showed a
good match with some discrepancies originating from some of the suppressed acoustics in the velocity
matching method and the finite baffle versus infinite baffle. It has been highlighted via a comparison
of off-axis measurements to the FE model that care must be taken in a measurement environment
when trying to capture the response at the desired angles.
Prior to performing topology optimization on the compression driver FE model, a study on the effects
of interpolation, penalization, filtering, projection and material volume constraints was carried out on
an FE model of the well described circular piston in an infinite baffle. It was shown that with proper
settings for the above mentioned parameters, it is possible to achieve binary topology solutions, and
via insertion of sound hard material in front of the diaphragm (an acoustic lens) possible to alter the
sound field to achieve constructive interference both on-axis and 45 degrees off-axis at a frequency
where without the presence of an acoustic lens, a cancellation at 45 degrees off-axis occurred. With
the optimal filter settings found, topology optimization of an acoustic lens was performed on the
compression driver FE model. Here it was shown possible to increase and achieve a flatter response
on-axis compared to the initial response. However, when evaluated off-axis, it was realized that the
performance had decreased, thus referring to the original goal of achieving an equal sound pressure
level in a 90 degrees listening space, the compression driver had in fact become more directive with the
optimized lens. A general trend of more improvement on-axis compared to off-axis was experienced in
all optimization examples with an objective of maximizing the SPL. Inspection of these geometries,
revealed a horn-extension like design which indeed increased the SPL, however lead to an increase in
directivity. Thus, on-axis improvements can be made, but with the cost of some off-axis performance.
By setting the on-axis response free to be altered and minimizing the squared difference between on-
axis and off-axis SPL, it was possible to improve the directivity of the compression driver. However,
this resulted in an alteration of the on-axis response which had greater peaks and deeper dips, which
depending on the usage of the driver may not be worth the improvement in directivity. However, if the
77
==PAGE:93==
alternative is to place the loudspeakers pointing in different directions to cover a given audience at a
venue, constructive and destructive interference between the contributions from different loudspeakers
may cause the overall response at the listening positions to have even greater peaks and deeper dips
compared to implementing the topology optimized lens and placing the loudspeaker in the center of
the stage.
A comparison between performing the optimization at 1 m distance from the horn mouth and in
the near field at 0.2 m distance and evaluating the response at 1 m distance showed similar results
in terms of optimized geometry and directivity, with each of them performing slightly better than
the other in different frequency areas. Thus, it was shown reasonable to perform the optimization
closer to the driver and thereby saving computational time by not having to extend the air domain
to include the points at 1 m distance.
In conclusion, it was not found possible to achieve the ultimate goal of achieving an equal sound
pressure level over a 90 degrees spread with the chosen objectives. However, some improvements can
be made and it was shown that care must be taken when formulating the objective, as this is the only
target seen during the optimization and the quality of the results depends on this.
It should be mentioned that the FE model was a fully acoustic model with all boundaries modelled
as fully reflecting, however in reality, the material will not be fully reflecting and some loss of energy
will be present when impinging the material. Thus, the introduction of material will result in less
acoustic energy propagating into space.
Furthermore, in this work it was chosen to study the effect of interpolation schemes, penalization,
filtering, projection and material volume constraints on the piston in an infinite baffle and directly
apply the best performing parameters on the compression driver model with different objective func-
tions. The optimal parameter settings was found based on a single frequency objective function on
a well known structure where a success criterion could easily be defined. If another objective was
chosen, the optimal settings may have been different. Thus, it is in general a good approach to play
around with different parameter settings when dealing with a new problem. In addition, both for the
topology optimization on the piston in an infinite baffle and on the compression driver, the placement
of the design domain was chosen arbitrarily and examples have only been shown using that placement,
however it was shown in one example that the choice of design domain and objective function must
be taken with care, as one objective with one design domain can give good results, whereas with
another design domain one may end with a highly undesired design (e.g. a complete blocking of the
driver). Thus, the placement of the design domain is a factor that should be experimented with before
deciding on a solution to implement.
In this work, only the influence on the response of the compression driver unit was investigated by
the introduction of an acoustic lens. However, since the driver is mounted in a coaxial driving unit,
the impact on the low frequency driver response should be evaluated and perhaps included in the
optimization before implementing the lens.
In total, it has been shown that topology optimization is not a magical tool that can solve all problems.
It is shown that the optimization will only try to do what is asked and disregard everything else. Thus,
it can never replace proper engineering knowledge as the engineer must state proper objective functions
78
==PAGE:94==
and constraints that can lead to the wanted result. However, if the objective and constraints are stated
clearly, topology optimization can be a very good tool to come up with solutions that would have
taken a very long time to arrive at if the traditional trial and error approach was the only possible
path. In addition, the manufacturability of optimized geometries play a role in the usage of topology
optimization. Most of the geometries obtained in this work are difficult to realize. However, again the
topology optimization can contribute to knowledge regarding the problem at hand and the results can
be used as a good starting point in experimenting with similar solutions either via a trial and error
process or via other optimization techniques such as shape- and/or parameter optimization leading
to designs that can be manufactured and implemented in the product.
5.1
Future work
The results obtained in this work have only been evaluated in a numerical simulation environment.
Since the FE model is based on actual measurements of the driver, an obvious task for future work
would be to manufacture and implement the lens in order to compare the impact and investigate the
sensitivity to manufacturing tolerances and placement of the lens. Such study on tolerances could be
carried out in both environments for further comparison.
In this work, the topology optimization was carried out on a 2D axisymmetric FE model of the
compression driver, thus the stated directivity not only covers the horizontal distribution of the
sound field. Dependent on the position of the loudspeaker at a given venue, it may be undesirable to
have the same dispersion both horizontal and vertical. Therefore, it would be interesting to expand
the model into 3D and optimize for different horizontal and vertical directivity.
Furthermore, since the compression driver is mounted in a coaxial driving unit, the impact of the
lens on the low frequency driver should be investigated as, depending on crossover region between
the two drivers, its response may have been altered as well. For this, similar measurements should be
performed and matched to the FE model in a similar way to what has been done with the compression
driver, and combining the model to get the response of the full coaxial driver including the crossover.
To this, topology optimization of the acoustic lens should be performed and evaluated on the total
response of the coaxial driving unit.
