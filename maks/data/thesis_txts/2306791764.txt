Abstract
Many emerging quantum technologies, such as quantum computation and quantum cryp-
tography, require reliable single-photon sources producing on-demand photons of high pu-
rity. Optical nonlinearities provide a promising way of generating such photons for use in
optical systems. Certain nonlinear eﬀects produce pairs of photons and the detection of
one of the photons in the pair then heralds the presence of the other, which can then be
used for the required purpose.
In this thesis in-waveguide photon-pair generation through spontaneous four-wave mixing
is considered, where a photon pair is generated symmetrically in frequency around one
or two strong pumps. In the ﬁrst part, an analytical model, describing the state of the
generated photon-pairs, is developed in the case where the phononic contribution to optical
nonlinearity is included for all possible pump conﬁgurations. The model is used to predict
the impact of spontaneous Raman scattering on the probability of producing a photon-
pair, depending on the frequency-separation of the pump and photon-pair and the ﬁber
temperature. The inclusion of the phononic response reveals a strong frequency dependence
of the pair-production probability and a somewhat weak temperature dependence. The
ratio of photon-pairs to noise photons generated by spontaneous Raman scattered is also
investigated and found to have a signiﬁcant temperature dependence.
In the second part of the thesis, a numerical scheme, based on the well-known split-step
scheme, for propagating the state of produced photon-pairs, is constructed. Using this
numerical model, the purity of heralded single photons is investigated. It is found that for
pulses shorter than ∼1.2 ps (FWHM) the inclusion of spontaneous Raman scattering has
an impact on photon purity. Additionally, the change in purity is found to have a signiﬁcant
dependence on the frequency separation of the ﬁelds. Finally, it is found that higher-order
dispersion eﬀects combine in a trivial way with the inclusion of phononic eﬀects, giving rise
to the same behavior of the photon purity as in the case of a purely instantaneous material
response.

Preface
This master’s thesis deals theoretically with the impact of including Raman scattering on
the process of photon-pair production by spontaneous four-wave mixing in a waveguide.
The work was carried out in the timespan from January 25th, 2016 to June 25th, 2016 at
the Department of Photonics Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark under
supervision of Ph.d.-student Jesper Bjerge Christensen and Prof. Karsten Rotwitt.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Single-photon sources
The ability to prepare single photons is vital to many emerging quantum technologies such
as quantum metrology[1] and especially within the ﬁeld of quantum information science
including quantum communication[2–4], quantum cryptography[5, 6] and quantum com-
putation[7, 8]. Quantum computation, using so-called qubits to process information, has
potential to eﬃciently solve certain classes of problems using quantum algorithms such
as Shor’s prime factorization algorithm[9] or Grover’s database search algorithm[10]. One
promising candidate to encode quantum information (the qubit) is the single photon due
to its weak interaction with the environment and its ease of manipulation through linear
optics. Quantum cryptography promises secure data exchange, through e.g. quantum key
distribution protocols such as BB84[11] based on fundamental properties of quantum states
such as the impossibility of copying them[12] without changing the state.
Currently, many ways of generating single photons are being investigated[13]. They fall
into two categories: The ﬁrst is the deterministic sources, which have been realized by
coupling individual cesium atoms to a cavity[14], using nitrogen-vacancies in diamond[15]
and through quantom dot systems[16]. The second category consists of the probabilistic
sources. An important subset of these utilize weak optical nonlinearities to convert photon
from a strong pump to another frequency. A convenient way to prepare single photons is
by nonlinear optical processes that generate photon-pairs. Detection of one of the photons
in the pair then testify to the existence of the other - a process called heralding[17,18].
Mainly two approaches to generation of photon-pairs through optical nonlinear processes

are currently taken. The ﬁrst is by spontaneous parametric down conversion[19–21]. This
method has the disadvantage that, since the photon-pairs are created in a χ(2)-nonlinear
crystal, signiﬁcant degradation and loss of single photons can occur in coupling if they are
needed in a ﬁber network.
The second method, which has recently been the subject of much research, is based on spon-
taneous four-wave mixing (SpFWM)[22,23]. This eﬀect relies on the Kerr χ(3)-nonlinearity
and is dominant in optical ﬁbers which. for symmetry reasons, do not posses second-order
nonlinearities. This opens the possibility of generating one of the photons in the pair at
communication wavelengths, which can then be spliced to conventional communication
ﬁbers and propagated with a low loss of photons. This second method, using SpFWM to
generate photon pairs, is the subject of this thesis.
While spontaneous Raman scattering (SpRS) is known to be a source of signiﬁcant noise
in experimental realizations of photon-pair generation through SpFWM[24], the impact
of including the time-delayed phononic response, responsible for Raman scattering, on
the actual generation of photon-pairs and their quantum mechanical state has only been
studied in the case of a continuous wave (CW) pump[25] and the pulsed case turns out
to be qualitatively very diﬀerent. Inclusion of the full nonlinear response leads to a very
generally applicable model for photon-pair generation through pulsed SpFWM.
A powerful way to analyze the state of the generated photon pairs is through considering
of the joint temporal amplitude (JTA) of the created photon-pairs[26, 27]. The JTA is
essentially a two-particle wave function holding information about the temporal states of
the correlated photon-pairs.
As for quantum mechanical wavefunctions, the amplitude
of the JTA determines the probability of generating a photon-pair. Another important
parameter is the factorability of the JTA, which is an expression of the correlation between
the two photons in the pair - mathematically quantiﬁed through a Schmidt decomposition.
The factorability determines the quantum mechanical purity of the prepared single-photon
states[28]. Proposed schemes for linear optical quantum computation relies on interference
between photons from diﬀerent pairs[29], which is limited by state purity[30], making this
an important property for single-photon sources. Throughout this thesis, all numerical
calculations are performed on an example of a standard silica communication ﬁber, but
the developed model is applicable to any waveguide provided the Raman response is known.

The goal of this thesis is to analytically and numerically analyze the impact of including
spontaneous Raman scattering, and hence the full nonlinear response, on the photon-pair
generation process.

Fundamental Concepts
This chapter sets the scene for the quantum mechanical description of spontaneous photon-
pair generation. Compared to classical FWM brieﬂy discussed in Sec. 1.3 the following
elements are introduced:
1. The signal and idler ﬁelds are quantized
2. All ﬁelds are pulsed and thus given a temporal proﬁle
3. The non-instantaneous phononic part of the nonlinear reponse is included
A general model including all these elements has not yet been developed. In order to include
these elements, the quantization of ﬁelds and pulsed propagation is discussed in Sec. 2.1,
the joint amplitude is introduced in Sec.
2.3 and Hamiltonians governing spontaneous
scattering are constructed in Secs. 2.4 for FWM and 2.5 for SpRS. Sec. 2.5 also discusses
the noise operator formalism for treating SpRS and correlation functions are calculated for
later use.

4.3.3
Degenerate case with orthogonal polarization
The only remaining conﬁguration to consider is the degenerate case with the signal/idler
cross-polarized compared to the single pump. This case is fairly straightforward. Consid-
ering the full nonlinear response, it is clear that this case is obtained from the co-polarized
degenerate case by simply setting hR(t) = Rb(t)/2, instead of hR(t) = Ra(t) + Rb(t), and
making the substitutions γs →γs/3 and γr →γr/3. As mentioned, this case allows phase-
matching further away from the pump, but at the cost of reducing the number of produced
pairs by 32. There is the additional advantage that the anisotropic Raman response Rb(t)
is much smaller than Ra(t), resulting in much fewer Raman noise photons.

4.4
Chapter summary
In this chapter, analytical expressions for the JTA were obtained through consideration
of the evolution operator obtained as a second-order perturbation from the spontaneous
scattering Hamiltonian developed in Secs. 2.4 and 2.5. The derivation was carried out
in detail for the simplest case of a degenerate setup with a single pump pulse and a
co-polarized signal and idler ﬁeld.
Two new response functions, R(t) and gR(t) were
introduced describing the phononic eﬀect on SpFWM and SpRS, respectively. Finally, the
expressions were considered in the long pulse limit and expressions for the pair-generation
rate and the Raman contribution to pair-production were obtained. Finally, the results
were generalized to the case of non-degenerate FWM for all possible combinations of linear
polarizations, resulting in a very generally applicable model for photon-pair generation
through SpFWM.

5.5
Numerical results
In previous chapters, the main focus was on the impact of SpRS on the amplitude of the
JA. This chapter discusses the result of using the numerical model to analyze the single
photon purity through the factorability of the JA. The numerical model is implemented in
Matlab. The code is included in Appendix C.
Until this point, the JTA has only been plotted in the long pulse limit due to the in-
tractability of the integral in the general case, Eq. (4.9). In this approximation, and when
the Raman response is completely neglected, the two photons in the photon-pair are cre-
ated at exactly the same instant. This is what gives the JTA its very sharp edges, see
Fig. 5.2. This very rapid variation of the JTA introduces a degree of correlation since
knowing, for example, the idler arrival time for a photon-pair created at either the start-
or endpoint of the ﬁber, gives exact information as to the temporal state of the signal
photon. The introduction of the retarded phononic response means that the photon-pair
is not created at the same instant any longer which eliminates the step-like edges. Hence,
one might expect the inclusion of the Raman response to slightly increase the purity of
generated single photon states. Since this is mainly an edge eﬀect, the degree of impact
is, loosely speaking, determined by the ratio of the temporal extent (temporal ’area’) of
the JTA to the amount of edge (temporal ’length’).
In other words, the eﬀect is ex-
pected to be largest for short pump pulses propagating short distances in the waveguide.
All numerical calculations are based on a silica waveguide at room temperature, so that
fR = 0.18 and T = 300 K and the nonlinear coeﬃcient is assumed frequency-independent
at γs = γr = γp = 2.0 × 10−3 m−1W−1.

means the eﬀect of smoothing out the edges is very small for states of high purity. A
Gaussian pump with a low pump power of Pp = 0.1 kW is considered to avoid distorting
eﬀects from nonlinear phase-modulation.

The ﬁgure shows some clearly visible ripples near the edge of the JTA that seem to introduce
a degree of correlation since the ripples in the JSA are brighter on one side than in the

is too large, the oscillations of the response functions become too rapid, which causes the
ripples in the JTA. As the frequency increases even further these oscillations become more

Remarks
This is the concluding chapter of this thesis. Before concluding the thesis however, a short
discussion of the limitations and approximations of the models developed in the previous
chapters is appropriate.
6.1
Discussion of model limitations
While the developed analytical model is fairly general, incorporating the phononic contri-
bution to the nonlinear response as well as SPM and XPM eﬀects, in both the degenerate
and non-degenerate pulsed pump setup in all combinations of linear polarizations, it has
several limitations.
First of all, to obtain analytical expressions it was necessary to neglect higher-order disper-
sion. While dispersion eﬀects are not expected to have a signiﬁcant impact on generation
rate, it could have an impact of photon purity for some setups. Higher-order dispersion is
however included in the numerical model.
Additionally, both the analytical and the numerical model are based on a perturbative
approach, which means neglecting stimulated and multi-pair scattering. This is reasonable
for low generation rates. If necessary, more terms could be included in the perturbative
expansions, allowing the possibility of two pairs being emitted. This would give contribu-
tions where two pairs are created independently at diﬀerent points in the waveguide and
likely also higher-order scattering events, where four pump photons are scattered into two
81
==PAGE:100==
82
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUDING REMARKS
signal and two idler photons through interaction with a single phonon mode.
All forms of ﬁber losses are neglected is both the analytical and numerical model. This is
reasonable for most setups since propagation distances are short. Inclusion of ﬁber losses
is however straight-forward in both the analytical and numerical model. Like in the case of
higher-order dispersion, the loss would have two eﬀects: It would reduce the amplitude for
pairs created near the start of the waveguide, due to linear absorption of single photons,
and near the end of the waveguide, due to pump decay.
It has been assumed that the three ﬁelds involved in SpFWM are spectrally well-separated.
For some of the shorter pump pulses considered in this work, this may not be a good approx-
imation for some of the lower frequency separations. Relaxing this assumption introduces
severe complications in both the analytical and numerical model.
It has throughout this thesis been assumed that all ﬁelds are in a single spatial mode in
the waveguide. If required, the model can straight-forwardly be generalized to the case of
several modes, similar to how it was generalized to several conﬁgurations of polarizations.
The eﬀect would be to change the nonlinear coupling between diﬀerent spatial modes,
depending on the intensity overlap between them.
The change would likely largely be
uninteresting since the nonlinear coeﬃcient would be scaled uniformly in this case and the
relative strength between the electronic and phononic response would be unchanged.
Lastly, all calculations have been based on an example of a silica waveguide. There are many
proposed materials for photon-pair generation through SpFWM, both where no Raman
scattering is present, such as gas-ﬁlled photonic crystal ﬁbers, or where the Raman response
has a diﬀerent character, such as silicon waveguides[53]. Such systems can be analyzed with
the model developed in this thesis (although the case with no Raman scattering has been
covered in the literature) on a case-by-case basis.
6.2
Conclusion
In the ﬁrst part of this thesis, an analytical model was developed for analyzing the quantum
mechanical state of photon-pairs generated through pulsed spontaneous four-wave mixing
in a waveguide. The model incorporates the full nonlinear response with contributions from
both the instantaneous electronic response and the retarded phononic response, which has
not yet been covered in the literature for the case of a pulsed pump.
The model was
developed by ﬁrst constructing Hamiltonians governing spontaneous four-wave mixing and
Raman scattering. From these a two-photon evolution operator based on time-dependent
perturbation theory was derived.
This evolution operator was then used to obtain an
analytical expression for the joint temporal amplitude for generated photon pairs.
==PAGE:101==
6.2. CONCLUSION
83
This result was considered in the long pulse limit which is often applicable in experimental
pair-generation and found to agree with existing literature when the phononic contribution
was not included. It was found that, even in the long pulse limit, the phononic contribution
still had a potentially signiﬁcant eﬀect of the the pair-generation eﬃciency for a large range
of frequency detunings between the pump and the signal/idler. In addition, the ratio of
produced photon-pairs to noise photons from single-photon spontaneous Raman scattering
was calculated.
It was found that this ratio can vary signiﬁcantly over a large range
of frequency detunings between pump and signal/idler, and is worth considering when
designing experiments.
The results were generalized to the case of a dual pump conﬁguration. This gives a very
general model for photon-pair generation based on four-wave mixing. The model is able
to describe the case of two pulsed pumps, which propagate at diﬀerent group velocities,
in all combinations of linear polarizations, while also including both the electronic and
phononic contributions to the nonlinear response. One of the few remaining eﬀects, which
is not included, is higher-order dispersion which is traditionally diﬃcult to incorporate in
analytical models.
In the second part of the thesis a numerical model for propagating the joint amplitude
along the waveguide length was developed. The method is based on the well-known split-
step scheme for nonlinear propagation of pulses with the additional eﬀect of spontaneous
scattering. The evolution of the joint amplitude due the diﬀerent eﬀects was found and it
was determined in which order to apply the steps in the split-step algorithm in order to
achieve a precision equal to the usual symmetrized split-step scheme for nonlinear pulse
propagation.
The numerical model was used to investigate the impact of including spontaneous Raman
scattering on the heralded pre-ﬁltering photon purity. It was found that for pump pulses
below a FWHM of ∼1.2 ps, inclusion of the phononic contribution has an impact on the
pre-ﬁltering purity of heralded single photons in a silica waveguide. Additionally, a fairly
strong dependence on the pump and signal/idler frequency separation was found in the
deviation from the instantaneous response purity. For frequency separations below the
Raman resonance the purity was found to slightly increase whereas frequency separations
above the Raman resonance decreased the purity slightly. Possible reasons for this behavior
was discussed. Lastly, it was found that the deviation in purity from the case of instanta-
neous response was approximately proportional to the inverse pump pulse length T −1
p
for
longer pulses. This gives a way to estimate if inclusion of the full nonlinear response, with
the phononic contribution included, is necessary for a pump pulse of a given length.

6.3
Further work
The framework developed in this thesis oﬀers many opportunities for further research.
The very general nature of the model, allows analysis of many systems for diﬀerent pump
conﬁgurations, given knowledge of the material response.
One intersting candidate for an in-depth analysis is the silicon waveguide, where the Raman
peak is signiﬁcantly narrower[53]. One consequence of this is that the phonon lifetime is
much longer, which means that the eﬀect on photon purity may exist for longer pulses than
in the case of silica.
The generalization of the numerical model to the dual pump setup for diﬀerent polarizations
is straight-forward and thus a numerical analysis of similar systems is also possible. One
interesting feature, introduced by including spontaneous Raman scattering, is that the
two processes, where pump photons are scattered from one of the two pumps into the
signal, may have diﬀerent amplitudes depending on the polarization conﬁguration. This
could potentially slightly reduce the purity in schemes relying on such symmetry. Such
a generalization would yield a numerical toolbox which could be used to obtain the two-
photon state, through the joint amplitude, by simply supplying the material response, the
polarization conﬁguration and the ﬁber parameters, for almost any experimental setup for
photon-pair generation through spontaneous four-wave mixing.
