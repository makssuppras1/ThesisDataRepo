Abstract
Background and Purpose: CFD simulations have emerged as a potential alterna-
tive to other non-invasive imaging methods since they allow to access data not available
with other techniques. However, complex biological CFD models, such as the human
heart, can be difficult to implement and validate. Even more, these models are usually
validated in a qualitative fashion and not quantitatively. Therefore, this thesis aims to
establish a simplified CFD cardiac model of the left ventricle and to qualitatively and
quantitatively validate it, by means of a phantom model.
Materials and Methods: Four CFD studies were carried out, under different dis-
placement conditions, where the velocity field and the pressure field were investigated.
The four studies comprise (1) Steady-state, (2) Compression displacements, (3) Rota-
tional displacements (4) Compression and rotational displacements. In the experimental
setup, the velocity field was measured using US vector flow imaging (VFI), across several
planes and regions of interest (ROIs). The pressure field was measured in selected points
via intravenous catheters. Servomotors were used to impose the displacements to the
phantom heart, and the US transducer was held in place with a robotic arm. The results
from the simulations and the experiments were systematically compared for validation of
the CFD model.
Results: The qualitative comparison of the VFI measurement and the CFD simu-
lation showed similar flow patterns in the examined planes. The quantitative comparison
of the velocity, performed over 8 regions of interest, revealed mean relative error values
under 18% for Study 1. For Studies 2, 3, and 4, the error values are found under 10%
in two regions. In the remaining ROIs, the mean error ranges from 10% to 28%, with
some exceptions. The results for ROI 4 were deemed non-representative. The assessment
also showed good agreement between simulation and measurement throughout the cycle,
in terms of the trend of the curves. The comparison of the pressure values exhibited
correspondence of the hydrostatic and total pressure values. However, large differences
were found when comparing the pressure oscillation due to the imposed displacements.
Conclusion: The co-registration of simulated and measured data showed a good
agreement of the flow patterns and acceptable correlation of the velocity and pressure
magnitude. Although some refinements are still needed in both the CFD simulation and
the experimental setup.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Approximately 17.9 million people die per year from cardiovascular diseases (CVD),
making it the leading cause of death around the globe [25]. It primarily targets people
over 70 years old. CVD constitute a group of different disorders that affect the heart and
blood vessels, i.e., the cardiovascular system. Some of these disorders are coronary heart
disease, congenital heart disease, cerebrovascular disease, peripheral arterial disease, etc
[25]. In spite of continuous improvement and application of state-of-the-art solutions, the
ideal therapeutic approach for some of these disorders remains an open matter [33].
Therefore, it is imperative to implement innovative technologies and tools to study
the heart and the blood flow inside it, so that existing treatments can be optimized and
new ones can be developed. Such tools demand a combination of imaging techniques
and numerical modeling and simulations of real scenarios [33]. Particularly, phantoms,
i.e., physical models, are a valuable and widely used tool [6]. Phantoms are essentially

2.1
Physiological background
Developing an experimental setup and establishing a numerical model, revolves around
making and evaluating assumptions and simplifications. Hence, it is imperative to clearly
understand the system that is intended to model, i.e., the left ventricle of a human heart.
2.1.1
Human heart anatomy
The human heart is basically a four-chambered pump, the chambers being the left
atrium (LA), the right atrium (RA), the left ventricle (LV) and the right ventricle (RV),
as shown in Figure 2.1. It possesses two main functions: (1) send collected unoxygenated
blood from the body to the lungs, and (2) forward oxygenated blood to the body from
the lungs [34]. The right and left atrium work as blood-collecting spaces, while the right
and left ventricles function as pumps. The heart can be split into two hemispheres: the
right hemisphere which is in charge of fulfilling the first above-mentioned function; and
the left hemisphere which serves the second function of the heart [34].
This means that the blood flows in a unique direction in and out of the heart, which
is maintained by a group of four valves. These are a tricuspid valve and a bicuspid valve
(right and left atrio-ventricular valves (AV), respectively), and a pulmonary and an aortic
semilunar valves. The atrioventricular valves allow the blood to enter the ventricles and
the semilunar valves permit the blood to flow out of the heart [34]. A healthy heart from
an adult is capable of pumping roughly 5 l/min at a heart rate of 72 beats per minute
when it is at rest. This gives a stroke volume of 70 ml/beat, every 0.8 seconds, on average
[34][27].

2.1.2
Cardiac cycle
The term cardiac cycle corresponds to the two-phase rhythmic pumping action of the
heart, as seen in Figure 2.2. The systolic phase is the period where the ventricles contract
and the diastolic phase is when the ventricles relax and fill with blood. The former takes
up to one third of the cardiac cycle, while the diastolic period occurs during the remaining
two thirds. An entire cycle has a duration of one heartbeat [27].
Two periods can be identified within the systole phase. These are isovolumetric con-
traction and ejection. The closure of the AV onsets the isovolumetric contraction period,
which lasts 0.2 seconds, approximately. There is a sudden increase in pressure since this
action stops blood from leaving the ventricles. The ventricles contract until the pressure
reaches a value above the aortic pressure (left ventricle) and pulmonary artery pressure
(right ventricle). When this occurs, the semilunar valves open, beginning the ejection
period. Once the ejection has ended and the blood has been ejected, the ventricles relax,
resulting in an abrupt drop in intraventricular pressures. The aortic and pulmonary valves
close due to the back-flow of the blood coming from the large arteries [27].
The diastolic phase is characterized by three periods: isovolumetric relaxation, ven-
tricular filling, and atrium contraction. Immediately after the semilunar valves snap shut,
the ventricles continue to relax for 0.3 to 0.6 seconds. During the isovolumetric relaxation
period, the volume of the ventricles remains unchanged, while the pressure drops to the
point of becoming lower than the atrial pressure. At this point, the blood that has been
accumulating in the atrium flows into the ventricles due to the opening of the AV. At last,
the right and left atrium contract, providing the last 20% of the ventricles filling [27].

2.2
State of the Art
As previously described, the heart is a complex organ, governed by the interplay
of fluid, structural, and electro-dynamics [31]. Hence, performing a precise quantitative
assessment of the flow motion inside it can be exceptionally difficult [20][22]. Various
non-invasive techniques have been developed to this end, such as Ultrasound imaging
(US), Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), and catheter-based techniques [18] [20] [22].
However, all of these methods have their limitations, for instance, US provides useful
velocity estimates in real time, but it is dependent on the angle between the flow and
ultrasonic beam, and the signal is lost in the presence of vortices. The catheterization
techniques are invasive and not practical for follow-up controls. MRI gives the possibility
to conduct a 3D assessment of the flow, although it requires prolonged measuring time
and low spatio-temporal resolution [20] [22].
Alternatively, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has emerged as a non-invasive
imaging method, since it gives access to data not available with other techniques. CFD is
7
==PAGE:24==
2.2. State of the Art
a numerical method that allows to characterize fluid behavior by providing the solution
to a non-linear system of equations of motion [3] [7]. Attempting to understand how fluid
behaves, in an intuitive manner, is extremely challenging. It is in these kind of situations
where tools such as CFD prove to be very beneficial [7]. For instance, in the field of cardio-
vascular diseases, CFD has been crucial for the development of diverse medical devices,
such as, heart valves, stents, and ventricular assist devices [3] [7]. Additionally, it has
contributed to the understanding of how flow disturbances might affect the development
of atherosclerosis as well as the growth and rupture of aneurysms [7].
Calculating magnitudes of interest within a region, such as velocity and pressure, via
CFD implies defining very detailed initial conditions and boundary conditions. The latter
being imposed values of the quantities of interest at specific locations and times [3] [7].
Several studies have implemented MRI and US data to specify the boundary conditions
of the flow model [20]. This shows the potential of CFD to be implemented in future
clinical applications, such as patient-specific models [20]. For instance, MRI can be used
to obtain accurate anatomical measurements of the heart’s ventricles during the cardiac
cycle from a specific subject, which can then be combined with CFD to get subject-specific
simulations [22] [18]. As an alternative, US can be implemented to overcome the spatio-
temporal limitations of MRI [18]. All of the above meaning that, CFD could be used as a
semi-empirical method to accurately estimate the velocity and pressure fields in biological
models [3] [7].
In simple models, like blood vessels in hemodynamics, a CFD analysis could be per-
formed applying a rigid-wall approach [33]. In the case of more complex models, such as
the human heart, the walls are not rigid and their movement results from the imposed
forces of the surrounding tissue. Additionally, it is clear that the fluid motion cannot be
estimated without knowing the cardiac tissue motion. This implies that both the fluid and
solid need to be computed simultaneously, which is the reason why biological CFD can be
exceptionally complex [16]. In such models, it is necessary to implement Fluid-Structure
Interaction (FSI). This approach uses the solid domain to estimate the stresses and dis-
placement within the tissue, whereas the fluid domain is used to calculate the pressure
and velocity fields of the flow. At the boundary surface between the two domains, the
boundary conditions for both the solid and the fluid must be met. The latter is solved by
implementing a computational grid capable of deforming in such a way that fulfills both
structures, i.e., a moving-wall geometry [33] [31]. The expressed difficulty of biological
CFD models suggests that their validation is a pressing subject. As a matter of fact, most
of the validation carried out on these models is rather qualitative and not quantitative [18].
The observations, and limitations above-discussed, show that quantifying the flow
motion inside the heart, in an accurate and precise way, is not an easy task. In fact,
currently, in the clinical field, several imaging techniques need to be used to obtain the
full picture of the heart’s motion and hemodynamic changes [22].
Furthermore, even
though new methods are being developed, their validation still remains a complex problem.
Thus, it is the objective to establish a simplified CFD cardiac model of the left ventricle,
with its correspondent phantom counterpart. Then, the latter is used to qualitatively and
quantitatively validate the computational model, which ideally will serve as the foundation
for further research on more anatomically correct models.

2.5
Pressure sensors - Intravenous catheters
Measurements of intravenous pressure are carried out routinely in intensive care units,
laboratories, and operating rooms. Fluid-filled catheters, inserted into the vessel of in-
terest, are often used to perform these measurements. These pressure catheters are a
thin, hollow tube, which needs to be filled with a column of fluid so that the pressure is
transmitted to a transducer located at the end of the tube [32] [9]. This working principle
implies that the correct combination of material compliance, diameter, and length must
be used to obtain the highest accuracy possible. Hence, measurements bias using this type
of catheter can be a result of dynamic disturbances due to the presence of air bubbles, a
poor catheter-to-vessel ratio, and distortion of the pressure wave inside the probe, among
others [32].
Wire catheters are another type of instrument, which consists of a thin thread with a
miniaturized pressure transducer placed at the tip [9]. Due to their comparatively small
thickness and the transducer placement, pressure wires can significantly reduce the effect
of the artifacts present when using fluid-filled catheters [32]. The frequency response of
these kind of catheters is extremely flat, which means that the measurements could be con-
sidered a true reflection of the intravenous pressure if the samples are taken with sufficient
precision and sufficiently high rates. Therefore, pressure wires allow to obtain high-fidelity
pressure measurements at the location where they are inserted [9]. These characteristics
make them ideal for research work. In fact, it is possible to insert them through small
incisions and position them at the desired location with high precision, without disrupting
the flow. This has great importance when it comes to the co-registration of measurements.
Nonetheless, the presence of particles or sudden movements of the tip of the wire, e.g.
when placed into a high-velocity stream, could introduce noise into the measurements. [9].

3.1.3
Experimental measurement methodology
The single ventricle heart phantom was subjected to four experiments, under differ-
ent displacement conditions. Each of these experiments was carried out to extract flow
velocity, direction and pressure data, which were then used for validation of the numerical
study. The four studies are:
I. Study 1: fixed flow rate and no displacements prescribed
II. Study 2: fixed flow rate and compression displacements prescribed.
III. Study 3: fixed flow rate and torsion displacements prescribed.
IV. Study 4: fixed flow rate and compression and torsion displacements simultaneously
prescribed.
For all studies, the measurement protocols found in appendix A were strictly fol-
lowed. The Cole-Parmer pump was set up to supply a continuous flow rate of 2.4 l/min.
The fluid used for the experiments was a blood-mimicking fluid. The properties of this
fluid deviate from those of the blood inside a human heart since the blood-mimicking
fluid is water-based with the addition of erythrocytes imitating particles. Its properties
can be observed in Table 3.1. A BK5000 ultrasound scanner and a BK9032 (8L2) linear
array transducer were implemented to measure the velocity and direction of the flow.
The transducer was attached to the end effector of the UR3 robot arm via a customized
3D-printed support. The robot arm was used to accurately position the transducer at
different predefined locations. Hence, its implementation allowed to know, with a high
degree of certainty, the position and orientation of the transducer in relation to the SVHP.
The movement of the robot arm was done with a program created with the user interface
software provided by the manufacturer. During the measurement process, a wide VFI box
(as wide as possible) and a narrow VFI box (1x1 cm, approximately), were used to pro-
vide an overall visualization of the flow and a higher resolution measurement, respectively,
at each target region. An example of the VFI boxes used can be seen in Figures 3.7 and 3.8.

3.2.5
Mesh
In previous analysis carried out by R.T.J. Lauwers [19] and F.H. Poulsen [8] on this
model and on more complex ones, it was defined that a normal mesh size for both do-
mains and at the boundary surface is accurate enough. However, the complexity of the
simulations and the implementation of a Low-Re RANS model required an overall finer
mesh. Hence, a fine and a finer mesh were used for the solid domain and the fluid do-
main, respectively. An extra-fine mesh was used for the inlet and outlet of the heart. The
denominations normal, fine, finer, and extra-fine, are predefined mesh sizes embedded in
the software.
As presented in Figure 3.12 A, the mesh applied in the solid domain is essentially
composed of free tetrahedral elements (in gray), with the exception of the surfaces ad-
jacent to the inlet and outlet pipes of the SVHP. The latter are composed of radially
organized pyramid elements (in magenta). In the apex, the mesh size was refined to fa-
cilitate the convergence of the model, since in this area is where the larger displacements
are imposed. In Figure 3.12 B, the selected mesh for the fluid domain is displayed. Here,
the overall volume is composed of free tetrahedral elements (in gray). The outer surface
is composed of prismatic elements, shown in yellow. In the inlet and outlet, an organized
and uniformly distributed mesh was constructed (hexahedron elements, displayed in cyan).
The skewness of a mesh is a quality measure that indicates how good it is, i.e., it is
a value from 0 to 1, being 1 the highest quality achievable. When using the skewness as
a quality parameter, the mesh constructed in this model gives an average element quality
value of 0.68. Thus, the mesh is of high quality, which reduces the computational time
and the residual error after solving.

3.2.6
Study steps setup
The finite element simulations were set up in COMSOL Multiphysics 6.1® following
the methodology suggested by R. Hvid in his Ph.D. dissertation [13]. The simulation
pipeline is presented in Figure 3.13. Since the first experiment consisted of the investiga-
tion of the fluid dynamics under a steady-state condition, "Simulation 01" includes only
one stationary study step, where only a stationary CFD is solved.
In the numerical models 2 to 4, the solid deformation, the stationary CFD, and the
dynamic CFD are computed in separate study steps. The displacement field of the solid
domain is solved in a stationary study with a parametric sweep (Figure 3.13, in yellow)
over one cardiac cycle. The reason being that the solid deformation is the same in every
cycle, thus, it is enough to do the calculation only once. In a second step, the stationary
CFD is solved, which is then used as the initial value for the following simulations. In the
subsequent studies, the dynamic CFD is solved in an individual time-dependent study
step for each cardiac cycle, i.e., for "n" cycles, "n" study steps need to be set up. In
this thesis project, 5 cycles were selected, to ensure that the initial conditions do not
have influence over the results. Each of these steps is taking as input the solution of the
Solid Mechanics calculation and the last result of the previous CFD step. This manner of
establishing the numerical models, although more time-consuming, leads to a more robust
convergence of the solver in less time.

Solver configuration
COMSOL Multiphysics 6.1® has two different solver configurations available, i.e.,
the segregated solver and fully coupled solver. The latter includes all the unknowns and
their interactions in one single large system of equations, that solves at once in every
iteration. Whereas the segregated solver subdivides the problem into two or more steps
that are solved individually in every iteration. Here, to solve the displacements of the
solid domains, a GMRES solver combined with a Fully Coupled model is used.
The
number of iterations of the Fully Coupled solver was increased from 4 (default) to 50
and an Automatic (Newton) nonlinear method is implemented, as the problem to solve
is highly non-linear. For the CFD simulations, a Segregated model, combined with a
GMRES solver was used instead, which help to reduce the computational time.
The
time-dependent studies were set up to compute from t=0 s to t=0.8 s, with a time step
of 0.01 s.

The quantitative comparison consists of a thorough analysis of the correlation be-

Chapter 5
Discussion
CFD simulations of the human heart are of great clinical interest since they can
be used as an alternative diagnosis technique. Even more, a CFD simulation could also
be the means towards state-of-the-art medical devices, such as heart valves, stents, and
pacemakers, among others. The first step to make this possible would be to establish
an accurate simulation, with its respective boundary conditions, that can provide a good
understanding of the fluid’s behavior inside the heart. In this master thesis, flow direction,
velocity, and pressure are estimated via a CFD simulation, and the data obtained are
validated with US and intravenous catheter measurements in a phantom heart. This is
done over four studies: (1) Steady-state, (2) Compression displacements, (3) Rotational
displacements (4) Compression and rotational displacements. The agreement of the fluid
direction between the experiment and simulation is carried out on a qualitative assessment.
This is followed by a quantitative assessment of the velocity and pressure magnitudes
between CFD and phantom.
5.1
Qualitative assessment
A qualitative comparison between simulated and measured data was performed, based
on the in-plane velocity field. This was done in selected cross-sectional planes, that al-
lowed to get an overview of the fluid motion inside the heart phantom, particularly around
the apex region. The results shown in Figures 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, and 4.4 indicate an overall
correspondence between simulation and experiment. Although, there are discrepancies
present in all the slices. This is especially the case of the transversal slices, and to a lesser
extent, on the longitudinal slice, where the main stream is contained.
In the LS (Figure 4.1), it can be seen how the fluid follows the expected path on both
sides of the image. In the bottom box both, CFD and VFI, depict the formation of a
vortex around the same coordinates, alongside some fluid re-circulation next to the wall.
In the middle box, on the inlet side, there is also good correspondence between simulation
and experiment. In this position, it is possible to observe part of the flow following the
expected path, while the rest is moving towards the center. However, on the outlet side
of the heart, the VFI fails to accurately capture the fluid motion that the simulation pre-
43
==PAGE:60==
5.2. Quantitative assessment
dicts. This could be because of the high complexity present in this area, which can result
in aliasing since the US cannot keep track of the particles. Another possible reason could
be the depth at which it is being measured. The scanner’s manufacturer suggests not to
perform VFI measurements beyond 50 mm in depth. The point in question is located
between 40 mm and 60 mm away from the transducer, so it is likely that the quality of
the measurement is diminished in this area.
Similarly, in the transversal slices, there is a good degree of agreement between CFD
and VFI. For instance, in the slice TS01 (Figure 4.2), the flow motion is characterized
by the same movement tendency towards the upper right corner on both sides of the
image. In slice TS02 (Figure 4.3), the overall flow motion seems to resemble, between
simulation and experiment, to some extent. Nonetheless, the split flow direction that is
observed around coordinates (x, y) = (0, 0) mm on the CFD side of the figure, it is not
seen in the VFI measurement. This is also the case with the vortex around coordinates
(x, y) = (3, −10) mm estimated by the simulation. Lastly, in slice TS03 (Figure 4.4), the
rotational flow predicted by the simulation, around coordinates (x, y) = (0, −3) mm, is
not seen in the VFI measurement. It could be argued that it is present based on the flow
direction, although it would be desired to observe it with more clarity. The discrepancies
found in these three planes could also be due to an impairment of the US to track the
position of the particles in chaotic flows.
Other studies have performed similar experiments, either on the carotid artery [15]
[26] or on a heart phantom [14] [20]. They have found great similarities between the
simulated and the measured flow patterns, with some discrepancies as well. These results
demonstrate that there is good correspondence of the overall flow patterns between CFD
and US data. Even more, this qualitative assessment over several cross-sectional planes
showcases the complexity of the flow motion inside the heart and the necessity for imaging
tools that allow a holistic visualization. In fact, there are some techniques of 3D ultrasound
imaging [4] [11], alongside techniques applied to complex flow [35], that would allow to
obtain the full picture of the velocity field in all directions.
5.2
Quantitative assessment
A quantitative correlation of the velocity magnitude, between CFD and VFI data,
was carried out in several ROIs. In addition, the pressure magnitude was compared in
defined points of interest inside the cavity.
5.2.1
Velocity magnitude
For the following analysis, it should be highlighted that the CFD simulation gave
an underestimation of the peak velocity in the inlet. Given that the flow inside the inlet
pipe is laminar (as established in Section 3) and the length of the pipe is larger than
the calculated entrance length, it was expected to obtain a fully developed flow prior to
entering the cavity of the phantom. This would give a peak velocity of 0.738 m/s in the
experiment. As seen in Figure 4.1, the maximum velocity estimated by the simulation is
0.704 m/s, which results in an underestimation of 4.6%. Hence, it is expected that the

CFD results will always lay under the experimental velocity data.
Analyzing the results exhibited in Table 4.1, there is a good agreement in 7 out of
8 ROIs of the averaged velocity for Study 1. The exception is ROI 4, where the mean
relative error is 28.68%. This region happens to be located beyond the limit of 50 mm in
depth suggested by the scanner manufacturer. Hence, it is assumed that this discrepancy
is due to low quality of the measured data. In the remaining ROIs, the mean relative

closer to the wall where the transducer was placed. Additionally, in the majority of the

the flow inside the heart phantom is highly chaotic. Thus, the in-plane velocity measured

than expected. In addition, the high turbulence of the flow might result in the impossi-
bility of the scanner to track the position of the particles at all times, giving lower values
than expected.
Now, looking at the results in ROI 5 (Figure 4.5), which is placed next to the inlet,
a generalized underestimation of the CFD throughout the entire cardiac cycle can be ob-
served. This is a direct consequence of the lower peak velocity calculated by the simulation
with respect to the theoretical value. Additionally, since this region is located far apart
from the apex, it is not largely affected by the imposed displacements, which is reflected

during the better part of the cycle and generally on top of the VFI curve. Meaning that
the previously observed underestimation is not present here. A possible explanation is a
slightly lower VFI velocity values due to the turbulence in area, caused by the compress-
ing displacements. A highly turbulent flow will present out-of-plane velocities at times,
which cannot be measured with ultrasound since it only allows to measure the velocity
across a 2D plane. This results in an underestimation of the overall velocity magnitude.
Also, there is a great similarity between the curves of these two studies, which suggests
that the effect of the rotational displacements is less significant. This would also explain
why in Study 3 the CFD curve lays under the VFI curve through the better part of the
cycle.

similar observations can be made. Especially in ROI 8, which is further apart from the
apex and thus less affected by the displacements. As seen in Figure 4.7, the reduced
effect of the disturbances led to higher accuracy of the measurements in Study 2, with
mean relative errors of 10.63%. However, in Studies 3 and 4, the same behavior as in
ROI 1 is observed. This indicates that the chaotic nature of the flow in this region still

studies are within a similar range to the results presented in this project.
5.2.2
Pressure magnitude
The results of the hydrostatic and the total pressure obtained for Study 1, shown
in Table 4.3, depict a close correlation between CFD simulation and experiment in the
measured points. Nevertheless, the calculated near-zero discrepancy of the hydrostatic
pressure in the three points was expected. The reason is that these values are closely
related to the boundary conditions of the simulation. As a matter of fact, one of the
boundary conditions is, precisely, the reference point that the simulation should use to
estimate the effect of gravity on the fluid. Another boundary condition extracted from
the experiments was the outlet pressure, which was measured to be around 3300 Pa when
the pump is switched on. The latter is linked to the total pressure values. The CFD
estimated a slightly larger increment in the three selected points than the intravenous
catheter. The largest difference lies around 3.57% (<5%), which is an acceptable margin
since it is within the uncertainty of the instrument. Additionally, since the position of
these points does not change from study to study, the total pressure values are maintained.
Looking at the results displayed in Figure 4.8, there are some similarities and more
significant differences. For starters, it is clear that the imposed rotational displacement
does not alter the pressure in an appreciable manner in either the simulation or the mea-
surement. On the contrary, in Studies 2 and 4, a sine pressure oscillation is generated in
accordance with the compression displacements. In the three studies, the averaged val-
ues of both, the CFD and the catheter curves match the total pressure previously found
in each point. This means that the pressure variation, due to the deformation of the
phantom, occurs around this value, as one would assume. Additionally, the gap between
the two averaged pressures, CFD and catheter, corresponds with the differences found
in Study 1 for the total pressure. In Studies 2 and 4, the periodicity and shape of the
curves nicely agree. However, substantial deviations are observed in the amplitude of the
sine wave. The CFD simulation estimated a maximum amplitude of approximately 50
Pa, whereas the measurements give a variation roughly four times larger. Considering
46
==PAGE:63==
5.3. Limitations
the CFD simulation as the golden standard in this case, the larger pressure amplitude
measured with the catheter can be attributed to the precision of the instrument (± 1.5
mmHg ≈200 Pa). If the variation is in fact 50 Pa and the uncertainty of the intravenous
catheter is wider, then it is not possible to perform the measurement. This was also
observed by Haslund et al. in their work [10]. Another source of noise could come from
the simulation itself. In the experiment the catheter moved along with the phantom, so
to replicate this in the simulation, it was necessary to attach the points to the nearest
boundary. This potentially results in a lower pressure variation.
The methodology followed in this project to perform the pressure measurements al-
lowed to obtain good results in terms of hydrostatic and total pressure values. Nonetheless,
the discrepancies found in Studies 2, 3, and 4 calls for the implementation of either more
precise catheters or other technologies. For instance, Haslund et al. [10] and Olesen et al.
[24] have obtained precise pressure estimations by means of US measurements. Although,
these were obtained in laminar flows and it is only possible to measure relative pressure
and not absolute pressure, as it was done in this project. Nevertheless, it might be worth
investigating this method.
5.3
Limitations
There are some limitations in the work presented here that can be attributed to both,
CFD simulation and the experimental setup. The main limitation of the simulation is its
underestimation of the peak inlet velocity, which definitely has an impact on the velocity
magnitudes, flow direction, and location of vortices.
In relation to the experimental setup, the validation of phantom models only reveals
the performance of the methodology used [13]. In the experiment, the impeller pump was
set to supply a constant flow, while the cardiac cycle was simulated by the displacements
imposed to the SVHP. Since the phantom was designed without inlet and outlet valves, it
is possible that the incoming flow rate was affected by the displacements. In addition, due
to the limitations of the pump, the flow rate used in this study (2.4 l/min) is considerably
lower than the physiological flow rate (5 l/min). Another limitation is the positioning of
the transducer. This was done with a robotic arm, which provided stability during the
measurements and allowed for more accurate co-registration of values. Nonetheless, the
positioning of the probe is strongly related to the relative position of the tank with respect
to the robot. Since the acrylic tank does not have a fixed position within the setup, it is
very challenging to re-position it if moved. Also, handling the transducer with the robot
arm required special support to hold it. This support posed some physical inconveniences
to acquire the velocity data at some point. This is why, for example, the transducer was
rotated 15◦to measure the inlet velocity.
In relation to the measuring instruments used, the ultrasound presented some disad-
vantages to estimate velocities in highly turbulent regions. This is assumed to be because
of the difficulty to track the position of the particles in between emitted sound waves.
Furthermore, high emission frequencies need to be used to avoid aliasing during the mea-
surements. A consequence of this is that the particles that move relatively slowly, in
relation to the chosen frequency, appear to be static. Additionally, the ultrasound only

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This project focused on the validation of four CFD simulations of a single ventricle
heart phantom, under different displacement conditions. This was done via co-registration
of results between experimental measurements and simulation. Hence, an experimental
setup was established, alongside the correspondent measurement protocol, so that the
necessary velocity and pressure data could be acquired. Ultrasound imaging was used to
obtain the flow direction and velocity magnitude across several planes and ROIs, while in-
travenous catheters were implemented to attain the pressure magnitude in selected points.
The correlation between experiments and simulations was assessed by means of a quali-
tative comparison of the flow motion and a quantitative comparison of the velocity and
pressure magnitudes.
The qualitative comparison showed an overall agreement between CFD simulation
and measurements. In all four studies, there are several similarities observed in regard
to the flow motion and the location of the vortex formation. Although there are some
differences present in the different selected cross-sectional planes, particularly in the areas
that are further away from the US transducer. The quantitative assessment of the velocity
magnitude exhibited good correspondence in most of the investigated ROIs. In many re-
gions, the mean relative error lies under 10%. The remaining ROIs show discrepancies in
the range between 10% and 28%, with some exceptions. The results from ROI 4 were not
considered. On the other hand, the comparison of the pressure magnitude revealed a gen-
eral correlation of the hydrostatic and total pressure values (<3% difference). However,
large differences were found when comparing the amplitude of the pressure oscillation due
to the mechanical excitation. This was consistent throughout the studies.
All things considered, it has been proven that there is an acceptable correspondence
between simulations and measurements. Although some refinements are still needed in
both the CFD simulation and the experimental setup. Nonetheless, these results provide
a great foundation for further improvement of both, the numerical model and the ex-
perimental setup, towards more anatomically accurate models, which can be used in the
clinical field and to develop state-of-the-art medical devices.

Appendix A
Experimental measurement protocols
This appendix contains the measurement protocols used to collect both ultrasound
and pressure data for Studies 2, 3, and 4. Such protocols are a collection of steps to
follow to accurately measure flow direction, velocity, and pressure, in defined planes and
points. This allows to correlate the results to finite element simulations, for validation of
the latter.
A.1
Before the measurement
Follow the following steps to correctly prepare the experimental setup. Preferably,
do this procedure a day before the measurement needs to be carried out, this will allow
the air bubbles in the tank to escape.
i. Calibrate the gear pump according to the instruction in the user manual.
ii. Mount the ultrasound probe to the UR3 robot.
iii. Turn on the UR3 robot and execute the program "SVHP_US_measurements".
iv. Fill up the phantom heart with fluid according to the step established in the Filling
procedure.
v. Place phantom inside the acrylic tank.
vi. Attach the rod to the phantom with the respective screws.
vii. Fixate the other end of the rod to the servo "arm", with the corresponding screws.
viii. Fixate the phantom to the bottom plate of the acrylic tank. Do not tighten the
screws yet.
ix. Position the robot end effector at the point "AV_01".
x. Verify that the center of the probe holder is aligned with the black mark on the
phantom’s support.

A.1. Before the measurement
xi. Position the robot end effector at point "AV_02" and repeat step number 10.
xii. Tight the screws to secure the position of the phantom.
xiii. Move the robot’s end effector to the "home position".
xiv. Add distilled water to the tank. Make sure that the level of water is approximately
10 mm above the support of the phantom heart.
A.1.1
Filling procedure
The purpose of the filling procedure is to ensure that there are not air bubbles trapped
inside the phantom or the pump’s pipes, which would interfere with the ultrasound mea-
surements.
i. Turn off the gear pump.
ii. Fill up the reservoir with either demineralized water or blood-mimicking fluid.
iii. Attach one end of the inlet pump to the pump and the other one place it inside the
reservoir.
iv. Turn on the pump. Set a flow rate of 200 ml/min and start circulating the fluid.
These will eliminate the air inside the pump and the pipe.
v. Steadily increase the flow rate, up to 1500 ml/min, to ensure there is no more air
inside.
vi. Stop the pump and set a flow rate of 200 ml/min.
vii. Position the phantom in a vertical position. This will allow the air to escape more
easily during the filling.
viii. Connect the free end of the inlet pipe to the phantom.
ix. Start the pump.
x. When the phantom is almost completely full of liquid, stop the pump. Connect the
free end of the outlet pipe to the phantom.
xi. Make sure that the level of the reservoir is almost at the top.
xii. Start the pump and steadily increase the flow rate up to 1500 ml/min. This will
eliminate the remaining air.
xiii. Gently massage the phantom to remove any possibly trapped bubble.
xiv. Stop the pump.

A.2
Ultrasound measurement protocol
This protocol includes four different measurements as follows:
I. Study 1: fixed flow rate and no displacements prescribed
II. Study 2: fixed flow rate and compression displacements prescribed.
III. Study 3: fixed flow rate and torsion displacements prescribed.
IV. Study 4: fixed flow rate and compression and torsion displacements simultaneously
prescribed.
The first study is intended to give a good understanding of how is the flow inside the
phantom in a static position (laminar flow, vortices formation, and flow direction). Based
on previous studies, the flow rate to apply is 2400 ml/min. The rest of the studies are
thought to provide insights into how the flow behaves when different displacements are
applied. The concept is to try to isolate some variables and simplify the analysis.
Additionally, two different VFI box size is going to be used. A wide size to give
an overall visualization of the flow inside the phantom and narrow VFI boxes in specific
points to get better resolution and use these values for validation of the experiment later
on.

Appendix E
Updated Project Plan for the Master
Thesis
This appendix contains a reevaluated Project Plan for the master thesis "Experimen-
tal validation of heart phantom model". It includes the purpose of the project, a summary
of the task to carry out, and the learning objectives. Additionally, the initial time plan
for the project is attached, together with a revised version and an overall evaluation of
the project.
E.1
Introduction
This project plan outlines the purpose, the learning objectives, and the time schedule
of the master thesis "Experimental validation of a heart phantom model". The thesis
accounts for 30 ECTS points and covers a period starting the 2nd of January 2023 and
finishing the 2nd of June 2023. It is conducted as the final requirement to obtain the Master
of Science degree in Biomedical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The
master thesis is conducted in collaboration with the Department of Health Technology at
the Technical University of Denmark, under the supervision of Marie Sand Traberg and
Lars Emil Haslund.
E.1.1
Purpose
The purpose of this master thesis project is to develop and establish the experimen-
tal procedure for the acquisition of ultrasound imaging data (both direction and velocity)
inside a single ventricle heart phantom. The acquired data are used for validation of
the numerical simulation model of the phantom, which is set up in Comsol Multiphysics.
Currently, the model has been established in simulation.
The project is envisioned to include the following objectives:
1. Establish the experimental setup
101
==PAGE:118==
E.2. Work Tasks
2. Establish the protocol for the acquisition of ultrasound images with the robot arm
3. Perform co-registration of simulation and measurement on the heart phantom
4. Do validation of the numerical model using the acquired ultrasound data
5. Investigate the pressure inside the phantom
E.2
Work Tasks
The work tasks are the different activities that have to be done to achieve the above-
mentioned goals.
E.2.1
Literature research
The main focus of this project is to validate a numerical simulation of the fluid
dynamics inside a phantom heart, by means of a physical experiment.
Therefore, it
is required a thorough understanding of fluid behavior, especially in relation to vortex
formation. In addition, it is also needed a deep understanding of hyper-elastic materials
and solid mechanics. This goes hand in hand with a deep comprehension of the heart’s
anatomy and physiology, since that is what we are attempting to model.
E.2.2
Experimental setup
To successfully acquire ultrasound data, it is required to select the appropriate ultra-
sound probe, which will allow us to characterize the flow inside the phantom heart. This
means, that both fluid velocity and direction will be observable. Furthermore, pressure
sensors are intended to be implemented as well, so that the pressure field inside the heart
cavity can be investigated.
Once the experimental setup has been established, a measurement protocol has to be
put in place. This way, data can be obtained in a systematic manner. What is more, the
use of a robot arm to control the ultrasound probe during the measurements will provide
repeatability and stability, besides allowing for co-registration of the obtained results.
E.2.3
Model optimization
The software used to simulate the heart phantom is Comsol Multiphysics 6.0. A
static simulation of the phantom has been already successfully carried out. Hence, in
this project, the aim is to optimize the existing simulation. It is also desired to establish
another simulation where an external displacement is applied to the heart.
E.2.4
Validation study
The data acquired from the physical experiment will be then used to validate the
finite element simulation performed in Comsol Multiphysics.
This will be done via a
comparative analysis of the results from both experiment and simulation. Additionally, a
data co-registration methodology will be developed.

E.6. Evaluation of the Project process
E.6
Evaluation of the Project process
A thorough understanding of fluid dynamics and solid mechanics, as well as some
physiological knowledge, has been used to obtain the results presented in this master
thesis. All the objectives established at the beginning of this project were accomplished.
After careful consideration of the problem, it was decided to perform four studies, un-
der different displacements applied. Hence, the plan schedule was reassessed to fit this
new necessity. The experimental setup was prepared to carry out the four experiments,
while the correspondent numerical models were set up. Additionally, the existing model,
together with the new ones, were optimized to obtain the desired results. A measure-
ment protocol was carefully designed and put in place to facilitate the repeatability of the
experiments throughout the project.
