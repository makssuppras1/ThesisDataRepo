Abstract
This report is written on the basis of a newsletter from the Danish work envi-
ronment agency (WEA), where they wrote that throughout three years, they
should inspect companies with a recognised work environment certiﬁcate. Why
did WEA ﬁnd it necessary to carry out these inspections when companies with
a recognised occupational health and safety certiﬁcate are generally exempted
from inspections as it is assumed that the companies work purposefully with
the working environment.
If the implementation of a working environment system is considered as a sig-
niﬁcant organisational change, how is an implementation performed then?
In order to answer the questions, it was necessary to examine the Danish leg-
islation in the area, the working environment standards and the change man-
agement theories. The Danish authorities have also conducted studies on how
companies with a work environment certiﬁcate performs compared to companies
without a work environment certiﬁcate. It turns out that certiﬁed companies
have more accidents than companies without a certiﬁcate.
Through a comparison between OHS systems and change management theories,
it turns out that a working environment system can easily coexist with an or-
ganisation’s activities without any interaction whatsoever.
It also turns out that our authorities’ conﬁdence in systems is considerably
larger than can be justiﬁed.
A recognised working environment system does not contribute to an improved
working environment.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Certiﬁed Company inspections
In April 2018 the Danish Working Environment Authority (WEA) issued an
online newsletter stating that the WEA will visit ﬁve hundred companies with
a work environment certiﬁcate over a three years period. The purpose of the
inspections is to investigate whether the certiﬁcation scheme is suﬃciently ef-
fective to ensure that certiﬁed companies control their working environment.
The newsletter stated that the background for the investigation is that: An
analysis of occupational accidents and occupational diseases in certiﬁed compa-
nies has not shown that the working environment of the certiﬁed companies is
better than the non-certiﬁed companies. There have also been many examples
of occupational diseases in certiﬁed companies. The analysis resulted in, a po-
litical agreement on ”Enhanced Work Environment Certiﬁcation”[8] that was
signed in November 2016, which will strengthen the work environment certiﬁ-
cation and ensure greater control of the working environment of the certiﬁed
companies. As part of the policy agreement, the WEA must inspect the working
environment of ﬁve hundred certiﬁed or recertiﬁed companies to gather knowl-
edge about whether the certiﬁcation scheme results in the absence of signiﬁcant
occupational health and safety issues when a company is certiﬁed or recertiﬁed.
When companies have obtained or applied for a recognised health and safety
certiﬁcate, the inspections will initiate.

After reading this newsletter, an application for access to records regarding

certiﬁed companies, that led to the mentioned political agreement was sent

that the records are public.

Chapter 3
The Law
3.0.1
Danish work environment legislation
This chapter describes the Danish work environment legislation[20], a large part
of the legislation is available as unauthorised compilations of consolidated acts
with later amendments. The Danish health and safety legislation is extensive
and in some areas very detailed. In the Danish legislation, common European
regulatory requirements have been implemented. This chapter will provide a
short resume of the legislation without going in-depth with the individual para-
graphs, with the aim of giving an overview of the legislation and how companies
are obliged to organise health and safety work.
The purpose of the legislation
In Denmark, the working environment is regulated by framework legislation that
aims to prevent accidents and illnesses at workplaces. The main areas of law
are the execution of work, the design of the workplace, technical aids, materials
and substances, rest periods and young people under the age of 18 in the labour
market. The purpose of the law is to be consistent with developments in society
at all times.
Organisation
Health and safety work is carried out through cooperation between employer and
employees, and are regulated by ”Bekendtgørelse om samarbejde om sikkerhed
og sundhed”[3]. Depending on the size of the organisation the health and safety
are either through regular direct contact (1-9 employees), organised in a health
and safety organisation with one or more supervisors and elected representatives
of the employees (10-34 employees) or organized in two levels: the one level
being responsible for the overall tasks related to health and safety. The other
level is responsible for the daily tasks related to health and safety (35 or more
employees). The employer is also responsible for ensuring that everyone who is a
member of a health and safety organisation is on a statutory three-day working
environment course.
5
==PAGE:12==
General
The general duties of an employer are to provide healthy and safe working con-
ditions. The basis for this is to carry out workplace assessments. The assess-
ment is performed by mapping the organisation’s health and safety conditions,
describing and assessing health and safety issues, prioritise and establishing a
plan to address health and safety issues. The employer’s actions must be aligned
with legislation. The implementation of measures must take into account the
educational background of the workers and their general ability. The employer
must train and instruct employees so that they can do the work without risk
to their safety and health. The employees are obliged to report any risk they
encounter, and at all time safely perform the work, employees have the right
to leave their workplace or danger zone in the event of severe or imminent
non-avoidable danger.
Planning
Work shall be planned, organised and carried out in such a way as to ensure
health and safety. Applicable and approved standards of importance to health
or safety shall be complied with. There are in Denmark more detailed regulation
on speciﬁc parts of the working processes, e.g. noise, or risks of explosion, ﬁre,
or to health from gases, fumes, vapours, dust and smoke, heat, cold, odours,
infections, or incorrect working postures, movements or strains.
Equipment & materials
Machines and other technical equipment and personal equipment must be de-
signed and used in such a way that they are safe and do not endanger health.
The employer shall e↵ectively secure employees against accidents and illness
when using substances and materials which may be dangerous or otherwise
compromise health or safety.
Recognised occupational health and safety certiﬁcate
An enterprise with employees may be issued a recognised occupational health
and safety certiﬁcate.
For a company to obtain a recognised work environ-
ment certiﬁcate, including for an associated production unit, the company must
document a good working environment.
Requirements
The work environment of the company or production unit must be in accordance
with the requirements of the working environment legislation with regard to
signiﬁcant health and safety problems in a number of areas. The company or
production unit must have fulﬁlled the requirements for its e↵orts in connection
with the working environment. Documentation must be available for both the
company’s or production unit’s e↵orts to meet the requirements and partly
the results of this e↵ort. In this connection, it must be documented that the
employees or their representatives have been involved in the work to fulﬁl the
requirements.
6
==PAGE:13==
The issuer
A recognised work environment certiﬁcate can be obtained through either an
accredited inspection or accredited certiﬁcation. Accredited inspection is done
by an inspection body while accredited certiﬁcation is performed by a certiﬁ-
cation body. The bodies concerned must be accredited for this by DANAK.
DANAK is the national accreditation body in Denmark appointed by The Dan-
ish Safety Technology Authority, under the Ministry of Business and Growth
Denmark. As the national accreditation body DANAK is responsible for accred-
itation of laboratories and companies. DANAK, an abbreviation of the Danish
Accreditation Fund, is established as an independent non-proﬁt business fund.
Economic context
Inspection and certiﬁcation bodies pay to be accredited according to rules set
by the accreditation body. Companies pay for inspection and certiﬁcation, in-
cluding certiﬁcate issuance.
Validity
A recognised occupational health and safety certiﬁcate is valid for three years
from issue.

Chapter 4
The Smiley Scheme
4.0.1
The scheme
The purpose of the smiley scheme is external publication and thereby the vis-
ibility of the individual company’s working environment with a green, yellow,
red smiley or a crown smiley. They are awarded on the basis of the Danish
Working Environment Authority’s assessment of the company’s working envi-
ronment e↵orts and compliance with the working environment regulations. The
legal basis for the publication of companies’ working environment comes from
Bekendtgørelse 1276 of 08/12/2011 with later changes.[4]
4.0.2
Crown Smiley
Companies with a work environment certiﬁcate can request the Danish Working
Environment Authority to have a crown smiley published. The Crown smiley
show that the company has a high standard of the working environment.
Withdrawal
The crown smiley is withdrawn if the company receives a yellow or red smiley, if
the Working Environment Authority ﬁnds important problems in the company’s
working environment during an investigation visit, or when the company’s cer-
tiﬁcate is deprived, suspended or cancelled.
Reassignment
The company gets the crown smiley assigned again when the established working
environment problems are rectiﬁed.
4.0.3
Green smiley
The green smiley is given to the companies that comply with the working envi-
ronment rules and do not have anything outstanding with the Danish Working
Environment Authority.
8
==PAGE:15==
Award
A green smiley is awarded when the Danish Working Environment Authority
has carried out risk-based inspection, and either has not detected violations or
has found one or more formal violations that the company has since rectiﬁed.
The green smiley is displayed for three years unless violations are found in the
company before. At the end of the publication period, the company has the
opportunity to request the Danish Working Environment Authority to carry
out new risk-based inspection.
4.0.4
Yellow smiley
The Danish Working Environment Authority gives a yellow smiley when a vi-
olation of the Working Environment Act is found during an inspection.
At
the Danish Working Environment Authority’s website, the date of the decision
and the work environment issues that have triggered the yellow smiley are also
published.
Removal
The yellow smiley is removed when the Danish Working Environment Authority
has received feedback on the decision, but only after a minimum of six months.
4.0.5
Red Smiley
The Danish Working Environment Authority allocates a red smiley in cases
where the company receives a prohibition or an advisory order. At the Danish
Working Environment Authority’s website, the date of the decision and the
work environment issues that have triggered the red smiley are published at the
same time.
Removal
The red smiley is removed when the Danish Working Environment Authority
has received feedback that the prohibition or advisory order has been complied
with, but only after a minimum of six months.

Chapter 5
Safety Management
Systems
A working environment certiﬁcation is an expression of the company work-
ing systematically with the working environment. It can be an advantage for
the company’s image and increase the possibility of attracting new customers
and employees.
Many companies today experience increased demands from
customers that the company must be health and safety certiﬁed. One of the
premises of having a system is that the companies are self-controlling and that
there are direct demands for continuous improvements in the working environ-
ment. In a work environment management system, there is a large documenta-
tion requirement, since all processes must be documented.
5.0.1
Occupational Health and Safety Management sys-
tems
In this chapter, the OH&S management system descriptions are based on ISO
45001[14] since the basic principles of the systems are more or less the same as
in other OH&S management systems.
The purpose of an OH&S system is to establish a framework for managing the
health and safety of an organisation. Organisations are responsible for protect-
ing its worker’s and others who can be a↵ected by its activities, physical and
mental health and safety.
The aim is to provide healthy and safe workplaces; therefore organisations strive
to eliminate hazards and minimise risks through preventive and protective mea-
sures.
5.0.2
Implementation
Implementation of an OH&S management system is both a strategical and op-
erational decision. If an OH&S management system is successful, it must be
supported by leadership commitment and collaboration from all functions and
levels in the organisation.
According to ISO 45001[14] There is a number of critical factors on which im-
plementation and maintenance of an OH&S management system are dependent
10
==PAGE:17==
on:
a) top management leadership, commitment, responsibilities and account-
ability;
b) top management developing, leading and promoting a culture in the or-
ganisation that supports the intended outcomes of the OH&S management
system;
c) communication;
d) consultation and participation of workers, and, where they exist, workers
representatives;
e) allocation of the necessary resources to maintain it;
f) OH&S policies, which are compatible with the overall strategic objectives
and direction of the organisation;
g) e↵ective process(es) for identifying hazards, controlling OH&S risks and
taking advantage of OH&S opportunities;
h) continual performance evaluation and monitoring of the OH&S manage-
ment system to improve OH&S performance;
i) integration of the OH&S management system into the organisations busi-
ness processes;
j) OH&S objectives that align with the OH&S policy and take into account
the organisations hazards, OH&S risks and OH&S opportunities;
k) compliance with its legal requirements and other requirements.
5.0.3
Minimising risk
The concept of minimising risk is based on the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)[21]
cycle; the PDCA is a four-stage approach for continually improving the health
and safety and for resolving related problems. PCDA is systematic testing of
solutions, assessment of result and implementing solutions that are shown to
work.
1) Plan phase; problems or opportunities are identiﬁed and analysed, and
there are developed hypotheses about issues and decided which should be
tested.
2) Do phase; the potential solutions are tested and results are measured.
3) Check phase; the results are studied their e↵ectiveness measured, and it
will be decided if the developed hypotheses are veriﬁed or not.
4) Act phase; solutions will be implemented.

others’ requirements and expectations that are relevant to the system and an
understanding of whether some of these requirements and expectations are or

Audits
The primary reason to do an audit is to give organisations conﬁdence that their
management system is functioning e↵ectively.
In organisations, several rules and requirements are established, so there is a
need to know whether they are being followed, this is called auditing for con-
formity.
An audit can also be performed to check for compliance with legislation or reg-
ulations.
Auditing is not just for conformity and compliance it is also about having an
e↵ective system.
An e↵ective management system has typically measurable
objectives, to what extent are the objectives of the organisation met, can be
audited.
6.0.1
Types of audit
There are three types of audits, ﬁrst party auditing is where an audit is ordered
in the organisation; these are internal audits.
Second party audits are when checking on the performance of external providers;
it may be a particular supplier of goods or services. Second party auditing can
be done at the supplier’s premises; it may be possible to do remote audit through
video conference telephone or by reviewing documentation.
Third-party auditing: A certiﬁcation body typically performs third-party au-
diting; the body is independent of the other two audit forms. They will do an
audit to determine whether the organisation are to be awarded an ISO certiﬁca-
tion or whether the organisation can keep the ISO certiﬁcate that they already
have. These three types of auditing are performed to gain conﬁdence that the
management system is working and working e↵ectively.
6.0.2
Audit execution
An audit can be carried out with di↵erent goals. They can be requirements in a
management system standard, requirements and policies deﬁned by stakehold-
ers, legislative demands, parts of a management system deﬁned by the organi-
sation or others, plans in the management system that must produce a speciﬁc
output.
This section will describe how audits are carried out according to the ISO 19011:

6.0.3
Seven principles
Audit builds on seven principles designed to make audits a useful and reliable
tool to support management policies and to exercise control to provide informa-
tion that an organisation can act on and thereby improve its performance. It is a
prerequisite that audits be carried out following these principles to provide suf-
ﬁcient and relevant claims which, under similar conditions, could be generated
by other auditors.
a) Integrity: The basis of professionalism. Auditors and the person or per-
sons managing an audit program should:
- Do their work ethically with honesty and responsibility
- Only carry out auditing activities if they are competent to do so
- Carry out their work in an impartial manner, i.e. stay fair and impartial
in all their relationships
- be sensitive to any impact that can be exercised in their judgment when
conducting a review.
b) Fair presentation: The duty to report truthfully and accurately audit
ﬁndings.
Audit ﬁndings, and audit reports must reﬂect truthfully and
precisely the audit activities. Signiﬁcant obstacles during the audit and
unresolved divergence. Statements between the audit team and the au-
ditor should be reported.
Communication must be truthful, accurate,
precise, objective and complete.
c) Conﬁdentiality: Information Security. Auditors must exercise discretion
in the use and protection of the information acquired during the perfor-
mance of their duties. Audit information may not be used for personal
proﬁt of auditor or audit client or used in a manner that damages the
legitimate interests of auditors.
This concept also includes the proper
handling of sensitive or conﬁdential information.
d) Independence: the basis of the impartiality of audit and the objectivity of
auditor conclusions. Auditors should be independent where possible and
should in any case act in a way that is free of obliquity and conﬂicts of
interest. For internal audits, auditors should be independent of the func-
tion being audited, if practicable. The auditor should maintain objectivity
throughout the audit process to ensure that audit ﬁndings and conclusions
are based on audit evidence only. For small organisations, it may not al-
ways be possible for internal auditors to be entirely independent of the
activity being audited, but everything needs to be done to eliminate bias
and promote objectivity.
e) Evidence-based approach: The rational approach to achieving reliable and
reproducible audit conclusions in a systematic audit process. Audit ev-
idence must be veriﬁed. It should generally be based on samples of the
available information, as an audit is conducted for a limited period and

linked to the conﬁdence that can be placed in the audit conclusions.
f) Risk-based approach: An audit method that takes into account risks and
opportunities.
The risk-based approach should signiﬁcantly a↵ect the
planning, implementation, and reporting of audits to ensure that audits
are focused on issues that are essential to the audit client and to achieve

7.1
Concept
Change management as a concept entered the business world in the 1990s where
there was a need to develop processes that could lead to successful changes
in organisations.
Since methods have evolved and are adapted to nowadays
business, but there is a still a common perception that seventy per cent of all
changes fail[11], in spite of countless books and theories about how changes must
be managed.
7.1.1
What is it
Change management is an organised, structured way to ensure that changes are
fully implemented throughout an organisation. Organisations changes due to
external or internal pressures.
7.1.2
Why change
Below are listed some of the external and internal conditions that the company
must relate to
Externally
Organisations have to react if they will survive, to changes in the market sit-
uation such as new customer demands, suppliers, unions etc.
can force the
organisation to pursue new goals. Technological development can create new
needs for customers and increased competition. Organisations do not inﬂuence
social and political changes in their surroundings but may have to make changes
to exist still.
Internally
Often organisations change due to change in the executive management, or due
to the company are taken over, merged with or have taken over other compa-
nies. Sometimes changes are launched due to deﬁciencies in the organisation,
to change the organisation can be the only way to remove ﬂaws, e.g. due to a

There can be many other factors that make a company change e.g.
imple-
mentation of management systems, demands for salary raise or establishment

7.2
Change models
There are several change models all attacking the process of implementing a
change.
The four most popular are Lewin’s 3 stage model, Kotter’s 8-step
change model, the Je↵rey Hiatt ADKAD model and the Kubler-Ross change

7.2.1
Lewin’s three-stage model
Kurt Lewin (1890-1947) was a German-American psychologist. His three stage
model[15] can be compared with ice that is melted to water which moves until
it is again frozen and becomes a new solid form again. The model requires that
the change is scheduled because it is linear and does not contain a feedback
element or other elements where the change can be changed or rejected. The
three stages are:
Unfreeze:
Employees must understand why changes must take place, and they have to be
unfreezed. Clarify why the status quo is no longer suﬃcient to pave the way
for the new way of doing things. In major changes, ensure top management
support. Emphasise why the change is essential and necessary, and be open
to the possible concerns and doubts of employees. Convince employees that
changes can be seen as a challenge that will bring energy to the organisation
and motivate employees and managers.
Change:
Communicate e↵ectively and convincingly and let the employees take on the
new initiatives.
Old systems are disbanded and new ones introduced.
New
routines, new solutions and new collaborators are introduced.
Refreeze
Stabilise the group to ensure that the new behaviour is relatively safe from
regression. The new behaviour must to some extent coincide with employees’
norms and values; otherwise, changes can lead to discouragement and lack of
commitment.
One can argue that the model is too simple and that it does not allow for
changes and adaptations after it is put into operation. The model also does not
respond to how the individual elements must be performed. It can be used as a
framework for carrying out organisational changes, under the premise of being

close to the organisation and aware of workers’ reaction patterns when changes
occur.
7.2.2
Kotter’s eight-step model
John P. Kotter, Harvard Business School, Konosuke Matsushita Professor of
Leadership, Emeritus[12] The model was introduced in the May-June 1995 issue
of the Harvard Business Review in the article ”Leading Change: Why Trans-
formation E↵orts Fail”[18], followed by the 1996 book leading change[17]. The
model is later being revised, and the latest edition is an ebook from the company
Kotter inc.[16]
The method was in the ﬁrst article written as eight errors describing why
transformation e↵orts fail. In the latest edition focus is more on an up to date
approach namely how to lead a change, and relate to a fast-changing world
where organisations must continuously seek out new markets and capitalise on
them. Therefore the organisation must always be ready to change and adapt
to new conditions and react to opportunities. A traditional business structure
needs to change towards more network-based architecture to be able to change
and not get stocked in rigid structures. The eight steps are now called eight
accelerators and four change principles.
The four change principles
Leadership + Management
In order to capitalise on windows of oppor-
tunity, leadership must be paramount- and not just from one executive. It’s
about vision, action, innovation and celebration, as well as essential managerial
processes.
Head + Heart
Most people aren’t inspired by logic alone, but rather by
the fundamental desire to contribute to a larger case. If you can give greater
meaning and purpose to your e↵ort, extraordinary results are possible.
Select Few + Diverse Many
More people need to be able to make change
happen - not just carry out someone else’s directives. Done right, this uncovers
leaders at all levels of an organisation; ones you never knew you had.
”Have To” + ”Want To”
Those who feel included in a meaningful op-
portunity will help create change in addition to their normal responsibilities.
Existing team members can provide the energy...if you invite them.
The eight steps
Step 1, Create a sense of urgency
Top leaders must describe an opportunity that will appeal to individuals heads
+ hearts, and use this statement to raise a large, urgent army of volunteers.
The idea is that opportunities bring people together in creating an outcome of
an opportunity that might not be available later. You must be able to identify
opportunities, communicate them so that the organisation catches them. You
are explaining the beneﬁts of the change and the consequences of failing.

Step 2, Build a guiding coalition
The Guiding coalition must consist of members from multiple layers of the
hierarchy, represent many functions, receive information about the organisation
at all levels and ranks, and synthesise that information into new ways of working.
Members must have an ability and a willingness to work across hierarchies, and
with people across all levels and functions. And be dedicated to the change.
Step 3, Form a strategic vision and initiatives
Strategic initiatives are actions that, if carried out well enough and quickly
enough, will ensure that your strategic vision becomes a reality. A strategic
vision must motivate people to act. It must ensure that their handler is coor-
dinated and adjusted. It must clarify how the future will be di↵erent from the
past and how the future will become a reality. It must be directly linked to the
created urgency.
Step 4, Enlist a volunteer army
To build an army of volunteers people must be able to choose whether to par-
ticipate and be allowed to participate voluntarily. The army of volunteers is not
required to include external; there is enough energy and power in the organisa-
tion. People must have a reason and motivation to participate. A strong vision
is a good foundation.
Step 5, Enable action by removing barriers
First, you must identify barriers and obstacles; barriers include silos, aﬃliations,
complacency, inherited rules or procedures, and limited access to key stakehold-
ers and leaders. Statements like; it can’t be done that way, we have tried that
before, it won’t work, can also identify barriers. Comments like these can kill
any initiative before even trying to change anything. By removing barriers such
as ine↵ective processes and ancient standards, the necessary freedom is given to
employees to work across borders and create real inﬂuence.
Step 6, Generate short-term wins
A win is something that helps you move towards your opportunity. They can
be, actions, knowledge gained, an improved process, a changed behaviour, etc.
Wins must mean something to other people than the winners, be it members
of your team, another team, customers, stakeholders, etc. Wins must be clear,
visible and tangible so that people can replicate or adapt it.
A win that is
scaled across the organisation has the most signiﬁcant inﬂuence. It is important
to celebrate victories to show people that it is an essential and appreciated
mission they participate in, it is also necessary to communicate these victories
so that progress is demonstrated throughout the organisation.
Step 7, Sustain acceleration
After the ﬁrst wins, there may be a tendency to slow down, but it is at that
time that you have to push harder and use the wins as momentum to further
accelerate the change. Communicating urgency again. The ultimate goal of

The Prosci ADKAR model
The ADKAR model[22] was developed by Je↵Hiatt in 2003 and it was intro-
duced as a practical tool by Prosci. The model is built over an adapted Kurt
Lewin model, consisting of three steps and ﬁve building blocks. The three steps
are:
Current
Individual needs Awareness of the need for change and Desire to participate.
Transition
Moving through the transition state requires Knowledge and Ability to imple-
ment the necessary skills and behaviours.
Future
Individuals need ability and Reinforcement to sustain the change. The Building
blocks are:
Awareness
Building understanding is about explaining why change and how, as well as
explaining the consequences if the change is not made.
Desire
Employees must have the desire to participate and support the change, some of
the motivators are; being a part of something, willingness to follow the leader
or fear of consequences.

Knowledge
Employees need knowledge of what to do during the change and knowledge on
how to perform in the future.
Ability

Reinforcement

7.2.4
The Kubler-Ross Grief model
Elisabeth Kubler-Ross describes in her 1969 book On Death and Dying[19], ﬁve
stages a terminal ill patient undergoes. The book was written to medical schools
due to too little knowledge in the ﬁeld.
The ﬁve stages
Denial
As a defence mechanism people have while the bad news is processed.
Anger
When the person realises the gravity of the situation, anger and looking for
someone to blame is a natural reaction.
Bargaining
Persons try to ﬁnd ways to postpone and ﬁnd the best way to be in the situation.
Depression
Persons have negative emotions, sadness, fear, regrets and despair.
Acceptance
In this stage people realise that it is useless to ﬁght against and accept their
ﬁnal.

7.2.5
Kubler-Ross in Business context
In a business context, the Kubler-Ross can be used to understand the reaction
patterns of employees who must participate in a change process.
Stage one
In the ﬁrst stage, employees may be in shock, and It can be hard to believe that
one has to go through a change process and adapt to something new.
Stage two
In the second stage, employees understand the situation and its impact. If you
come from a safe environment, an now you have to change and adapt to new
can trigger anger.
Stage three
In the third stage, employees understand the change and strive to ﬁnd the best
way they can adapt to and ﬁt into the new scenario. There will be employees
that will try to negotiate a status quo.
Stage four
In the fourth stage, in this stage employees can have low morale and energy, this
period is usually a period where employees are trained, and one must expect
lower productivity.
Stage ﬁve
In the ﬁfth stage, employees ﬁnally understand the necessity of the change and
resign to the new situation. Some surrender because there are no other possibil-
ities and others because they sincerely ﬁnd the new opportunities exciting and
rewarding.
Kubler-Ross used for business purposes is a good tool for explaining and predict-
ing employee response patterns. Once the patterns are known, change leaders
can plan and take into account these.
7.3
Why do transformation e↵ort fail
According to McKinsey & Company[11], seventy per cent of change programmes
fail to reach their goals. The main reasons for this are employee resistance and
lack of management support.
Resistance
Employee resistance to change may be due to many things: if a proper com-
munication strategy is not laid down, employees may not be informed about
the necessity of change or the beneﬁts that the employees might receive from
the change. If the change is led by a far down in the company Hierarchy, there
32
==PAGE:39==
may be problems with middle managers taking the change e↵ort seriously and
therefore not encouraging their employees to participate in the change actively.
If employees perceive the change as unfair, there is no willingness to participate.
Even if employees recognise that there are beneﬁts to them for a change, they
may object to fear that they cannot develop the necessary skills required of
them. Other times the employee understands and accepts the change but still
resists without knowing why.
Lack of Senior management support
Lack of support from senior management is another factor that can destroy a
change e↵ort; if senior management does not communicate the importance of
the change, how should the rest of the organisation believe that a change is
essential. A change manager must be placed high enough in the hierarchy for
the organisation to take him and the change seriously. Senior management must
help ensure momentum in the change process by continuing to communicate the
importance of the change.
Organisational setup
Despite all the knowledge and models in the ﬁeld, organisations have a signif-
icant error rate when making changes, perhaps due to traditional organisation
structures where organisations are built in departments, function-based, prod-
uct based, market-based, or geographic. In such organisations, employees do
not have a particularly high incentive to worry about anything other than their
department and the department’s goals, which can even arise tribal relation-
ships where departments see other departments as enemies. So having to be
part of a change can also result in employees being afraid that their adherence
needs to be changed. In a networking organisation, employees do not have the
same aﬃliation with departments but communicate and work across organisa-
tions. Employees who work in network organisations will be able to adapt more
quickly to a new situation. Moreover, the communication ﬂow is more substan-
tial and also unﬁltered as one is used to communicating directly with others in
the network, and not through managers as one is in a traditional line organi-
sation. With workers working in project organisations, most often organised as
a matrix organisation, participating with changing frequencies in projects, and
looking at projects, it can be seen from employees as a change each time they
become a participant in a new or di↵erent project. Such employees will also be
very adaptable, as changes are their everyday life.

Change Management

The missing link

this has is done because they have been aware that up to seventy per cent of all
change initiatives failed.
It is therefore doubtful that an OH&S system is implemented just because
it is audited.

9.0.1
Companies with certiﬁcate
In Denmark, there are approx three thousand companies with a recognised
work environment certiﬁcate. According to the Danish Working Environment
Authority (WEA), a recognised work environment certiﬁcate shows that the
company works systematically and purposefully with the working environment.
Inspections of health and safety certiﬁed companies
The WEA Quality Procedure DT-15[5] describes how inspectors should relate
to companies with a work environment certiﬁcate.
The WEA assumes that
companies that have a health and safety certiﬁcate, to a large extent, can and
will solve their health and safety problems themselves, this means that the
WEA uses limited supervisory resources on health and safety certiﬁed compa-
nies. Companies with a work environment certiﬁcate must not have risk-based
inspections[6]. The veriﬁcation of whether the company meets the conditions
for obtaining and maintaining the working environment certiﬁcate is carried out
solely by the certiﬁcation bodies. The WEA receives knowledge that a company
has a work environment certiﬁcate in that the certiﬁcation body sends a copy of
the certiﬁcate to the WEA. If the WEA on a risk-based inspection[6] becomes
aware that the company has a recognised work environment certiﬁcate, the in-
spection will be interrupted. Speciﬁcally, the inspector must ensure that it is
a recognised occupational health and safety certiﬁcate, this is done by examin-
ing whether the certiﬁcate is one of the recognised models, as well as whether
DANAK’s or SWEDAK’s logo is printed on the certiﬁcate.
Risk-based inspections
Risk-Based inspections[6] is an audit based on an index model.
The model
contains some parameters that together identify the companies where it is ex-
pected that there is the most signiﬁcant risk of problems with the working
environment. A sample also selects companies. Companies can also be selected
manually based on suspicion.

9.1
Accidents
Looking at WEA’s attitude towards companies with occupational health and
safety certiﬁcates, an assumption that comparing accident statistic for certiﬁed
and non-certiﬁed companies would show a lower number of accidents at certiﬁed
companies is not unrealistic.
Analyse
The WEA has never automatically compared comparisons between accident
statistics on certiﬁed and non-certiﬁed companies. Before the unveiling of the
poor working environment at Siemens in Denmark on May 1, 2016, even though
Siemens has a recognised work environment certiﬁcate. Moreover, the subse-
quent political interest where many questions were raised in the Parliament’s
Employment Committee, no studies have been conducted on whether companies
that have an work environment certiﬁcate has a better working environment and
fewer accidents than companies without a certiﬁcate.
This comparison was ﬁrst made in connection with a response from the Min-
ister to a question posed by the Parliament’s Employment Committee in 2016.
Where the minister was asked:”How many work injuries have work environment
certiﬁed companies reported in the past ﬁve years and is it relatively fewer or
more than comparable companies that are not work environment certiﬁed?”[1]
Findings
A descriptive analysis of the frequency (number of cases in relation to the num-
ber of employees) of reported work accidents and occupational diseases in cer-
tiﬁed production units compared to non-certiﬁed shows that:
• Certiﬁed production units overall have a higher frequency of both reported
work accidents and reported mental illnesses, muscle-skeletal and skin
diseases than non-certiﬁed units.
• For reported work-related severe accidents, with an expected absence of
more than three weeks, there is also a higher frequency of certiﬁed units,
but the di↵erence between the certiﬁed and non-certiﬁed units is consid-
erably smaller.
• A right frequency of reported work accidents and occupational diseases
for certiﬁed production units is still seen when the account is taken of the
size of the production units.

Conclusion
When reading the working environment standard and the standard for perform-
ing the audit the ﬁrst impulse is that companies with an audited system must
have a signiﬁcantly better working environment than companies without a work
environment system.
The standards are through thought, detailed and well-written and it is diﬃcult
to imagine that an implemented standard will not contribute signiﬁcantly to
the working environment.
It just does not seem to be the case; studies show that companies with a recog-
nised work environment certiﬁcate have more accidents than companies without
a work environment certiﬁcate. This report shows that a work environment sys-
tem can work in parallel with the organisation’s activities.
There are also no models for implementing OH&S systems in the same way as
when companies have to make changes when it comes; the change management
models come into play.
The report also conﬁrm that lead auditor programmes could be optimised.
The original question, why would WEA perform audit on ﬁve hundred compa-
nies with a recognised occupational health certiﬁcate has changed into a wonder
why the WEA has not long been auditing all companies with a recognised oc-
cupational health and safety certiﬁcate.
A recognised working environment system does not by it self contribute to an
improved working environment.
