ABSTRACT 
 
The objective of this thesis is to assess the value of UAV based SHM systems and compare them 
with the conventional SHM systems. To reach this objective, the Value of Information from UAV based 
SHM systems is quantified through a case study and compared with the Value of Information from a 
conventional SHM system. Taking basis in structural benefit and risk assessment and Bayesian Decision 
Analysis, probabilistic models to represent structural systems with deteriorating resistance and the 
extreme load acting on them over their life-cycle are developed. The performance of the monitoring 
systems is modeled probabilistically through the development of Probability of Detection (PoD) models. 
A novel approach to model the Probability of Detection for a structural system with many components is 
developed. A case study for the Value of Information analysis is developed taking basis in the economic 
and decision scenario as observed in a Wind Park Operation. The case study deals with a cost-benefit 
analysis considering the decision to extend service life of the wind park with and without the additional 
information provided by different SHM systems/strategies. The Decision Analysis to quantify the value of 
SHM includes all consequences associated with the life-cycle performance of the Wind Park along with 
the costs model for the associated monitoring systems. The expected utility gain from the use of UAV 
based monitoring systems is quantified and compared to a conventional monitoring system. The study 
concludes that the UAV based SHM systems provide information with higher value as compared to 
conventional systems.

1. Introduction 
 
 
1.1 Introduction  
The objective of this study is to investigate the applicability of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles 
(commonly referred to as drones) in Structural Health Monitoring (SHM). Recent developments 
in UAV technologies offer new opportunities for Structural Health Monitoring. UAVs, especially 
the compactly sized micro-UAVs, may be readily employed for monitoring and inspection of 
different types of structural components in a variety of locations, some of which may presently 
be hard or unsafe to reach via conventional methods. A limited number of UAVs may also 
replace the need for multitude of stationary sensors installed on a structural system, thus 
lowering costs associated with initial investment for SHM systems and their operation.  
However, the precision of UAV based SHM systems in the delivery of damage detection 
type information may differ from that of conventional systems due to the effects of the hovering 
motion of the UAV and novelty of technology. As SHM systems are intended to assist 
infrastructure operators in managing their facilities, an exhaustive cost–benefit analysis is 
required to show that benefits outweigh costs. Hence, a comprehensive cost-benefit 
comparison of UAV based and conventional SHM systems is required. This can be achieved by 
performing a Value of Information analysis and quantifying the monitoring benefits obtained 
from UAV based SHM systems. Further, different UAV based SHM systems may be 
differentiated by different measurement strategies. 
The reliability of structural health monitoring systems is defined by their Probability of 
Detection and appropriate models are developed in this study, taking into account the effect of 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
 
uncertainties associated with UAV based measurement. Taking basis in structural risk 
assessment and Bayesian Decision Analysis, this thesis involves development of probabilistic 
models to represent structural systems over their life-cycle, the loads acting on them and the 
limit states. A Bayesian Decision Analysis facilitates the decision maker through building a 
probabilistic model capturing the uncertainties which might affect decision making and 
producing a utility function. The decision maker can calculate the expected utility associated 
with each viable decision and identify the best decisions as those having the highest expected 
utility score. The Decision Analysis to quantify Value of Information from SHM includes all 
consequences associated with the life-cycle performance of the structure along with the costs 
modeled for the associated monitoring systems. 
1.2 Organization of Thesis 
Chapter 2 presents an overview of structural health monitoring systems and damage 
detection type information. An introduction to the scope of UAV based SHM technologies is 
further provided. Chapter 3 deals with the development of a generic structural system model 
that describes the reliability of a deteriorating structural system under extreme loading. The 
system model also takes into account the correlation between the component resistances and 
deteriorations. The chapter further presents Structural Reliability Methods commonly used in 
calculating structural reliabilities and their implementation. Chapter 4 describes the reliability 
of structural health monitoring systems through Probability of Detection models. The models 
developed for a single component are first presented from literature. A method for determining 
PoD for a multi-component system is developed and described. Chapter 5 presents an overview 
of UAV based SHM systems and their applications in industry. The costs associated with a UAV 
based system are presented. A model to describe the accuracy of UAV based SHM based on 
measurement and operational uncertainties is defined. The framework for performing a Value 
of Information analysis is presented in Chapter 6 along with the various consequences 
associated with life-cycle monitoring of structures.  A decision scenario in the context of normal 
and extensive form analysis is further presented. The Bayesian updating approach for updating 
the structural reliability based on damage detection information is also presented.  Finally, the 
developed models and frameworks are implemented in a case study in Chapter 7 and the study 
is analyzed and concluded with results. The case study is based on the operation of a Wind Park 
and the associated costs are modeled along with the risks and decision scenario. The expected 
==PAGE:12==
 
11 
 
utility gain from the use of conventional and UAV based monitoring system in the context of 
life-cycle performance of a Wind Park is presented. The thesis is summarized and concluded in 
Chapter 8.

2. Structural Health Monitoring Systems 
 
 
2.1 Introduction 
           The process of implementing a damage identification strategy for aerospace, civil and 
mechanical engineering infrastructure is referred to as structural health monitoring (SHM). This 
process involves the observation of a structure or mechanical system over time using 
periodically spaced measurements, the extraction of damage-sensitive features from these 
measurements and the statistical analysis of these features to determine the current state of 
system health.  
At the simplest level, recurrent visual observation and assessment of structural condition 
(cracking, spalling and deformations) could be viewed as SHM, yet the aim of present-day 
research is to develop effective and reliable means of acquiring, managing, integrating and 
interpreting structural performance data for maximum useful information at a minimum cost 
while either removing or supplementing the qualitative, subjective and unreliable human 
element (Brownjohn, 2007).  
2.2 SHM for Engineering Structures 
Civil Infrastructure such as bridges, tunnels, power plants, offshore installations, etc. are 
of immense importance to the society owing to their functional value. Due to their importance 
and the potential risks and hazards associated with them, such structures invite the need for 
monitoring programs. While structural design codes have proven to be successful in preventing 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
 
catastrophic global failures, structures commonly experience varying levels of structural 
damage over their operational lives.  
Damage, in this context, can be defined as changes to the material and/or geometric properties 
of structural systems, including changes to the boundary conditions and system connectivity, 
which adversely affect the system’s performance (Farrar & Worden, 2007) . There are many 
causes of structural damage such as corrosion, fatigue, extreme loads due to wind, seismic 
activity or waves, moisture absorption, thermal stress, fire or even extreme loads from a 
combination of live loads and poor structural maintenance.  
The effectiveness of structural health monitoring systems depends on their ability to timely 
detect damage type information and hence provide basis for remedial action. The majority of 
SHM research conducted over the last 30 years has attempted to identify damage in structures 
on a more global basis. Damage identification is carried out in conjunction with five closely 
related disciplines that include SHM, condition monitoring (CM), non-destructive evaluation 
(NDE), statistical process control (SPC), and damage prognosis (DP). 
Significant development in SHM related research had originated from major construction 
projects in for example, the oil industry, hydropower generation and highway & bridges 
agencies. During the 1970s and 1980s, the oil industry made considerable efforts to develop 
vibration-based damage identification methods for offshore platforms. 
The civil engineering community has studied vibration-based damage assessment of bridge 
structures and buildings since the early 1980s. Modal properties and quantities derived from 
these properties, such as mode shape curvature and dynamic flexibility matrix indices, have 
been the primary features used to identify damage in bridge structures.  (Farrar & Worden, 
2007). 
With the development in the renewable industry in the recent years, solutions for providing 
structural health monitoring for such facilities is gaining attention. Among the different options 
being considered, wind energy emerges as a strong contender because of its maturity in 
technology, good infrastructure and relative cost competitiveness. As the wind turbines grow 
in physical size, the need to improve safety considerations while minimizing down time and 
associated maintenance and logistic costs requires that the wind turbines be monitored from 
time to time to ensure that they are in good condition. With a reliable SHM system, a promising 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
 
maintenance and repair strategy for wind turbines, especially those offshore, can be planned. 
Maintenance and repair actions can be worked out on demand and during suitable weather 
conditions. Further, mobilization costs for staff, materials and craning equipment can be 
optimized.  
Finally, many portions of the existing infrastructure are approaching or exceeding their initial 
design life. As a result of economic issues, these structures are being used in spite of aging and 
the associated damage accumulation. Therefore, the ability to monitor the health of these 
structures is becoming increasingly important.  
The monitoring process involves the observation of a structure or mechanical system over time 
using periodically spaced measurements, the extraction of damage-sensitive features from 
these measurements and the statistical analysis of these features to determine the current state 
of system health. For long-term SHM, the output of this process is periodically updated 
information regarding the ability of the structure to continue to perform its intended function 
considering the inevitable aging and damage accumulation resulting from the operational 
environments. Under an extreme event, such as an earthquake or unanticipated blast loading, 
SHM may be used for rapid condition screening. This screening is intended to provide, in near 
real-time, reliable information about system performance during such extreme events and the 
subsequent integrity of the system. (Farrar & Worden, 2007) The prompt reoccupation of 
buildings, such as manufacturing units, factories, etc. can significantly mitigate economic losses 
associated with major seismic events. 
It can therefore be established that strong interest exists in using structural response 
measurements to identify the existence of damage in civil structures. Structural health 
monitoring systems offer facility managers the opportunity to adopt condition- based 
maintenance strategies in lieu of schedule-based maintenance approaches and to evaluate 
structural integrity immediately following a major extreme loading event, such as seismic 
activity (Lynch, 2007).  
As SHM systems are intended to assist infrastructure operators/owners in managing their 
facilities, an exhaustive cost–benefit analysis is required to show that benefits outweigh costs. 
Apart from the initial outlay in planning, purchase and installation, operation and maintenance 
costs of the system should be low.

2.3 SHM Methodology 
 Sohn et al. (Sohn, Farrar, Hemez, & Czarnecki, 2002) regard the SHM problem as 
fundamentally being one of statistical pattern recognition. The statistical pattern recognition 
paradigm can be described as a four-part process: (1) Operational Evaluation, (2) Data 
Acquisition, Fusion and Cleansing, (3) Feature Extraction and Information Condensation, and (4) 
Statistical Model Development for Feature Discrimination.  
Operational Evaluation defines how the damage is being monitored, the operational and 
environmental conditions under which the monitored system functions, the limitations on 
acquiring data in the operational condition being considered and the economic benefits of 
performing the monitoring. 
Data Acquisition involves decision regarding selection and placement of sensors, including the 
number of sensors to be used and the data acquisition, storage and transmittal hardware. One 
of the major focuses of SHM research has been in advanced sensor development, and sensors 
developed within other engineering disciplines are now finding their way into civil applications. 
Particular examples are fiber optic sensors (FOS), GPS and piezo-impedance transducers (PZT 
patches). It is not uncommon to find hundreds of sensors installed for monitoring of large 
structures, such as long-span bridges. For example, the Tsing Ma suspension bridge in Hong 
Kong, with a span of 1377 m, was constructed with over 350 sensor channels. Such sensory 
systems often produce several megabytes of data per hour. It may be agreed that such a vast 
amount of data is useless unless it is distilled into manageable amounts of information.   Hence, 
either the signals are acquired in a selective fashion or the data is converted to information 
through pre-processing in the data logger. For example, time-series of acceleration are 
converted to mean and variance values, discrete Fourier transforms to determine peak 
frequencies or auto-regressive model coefficients. Also, data fusion is used to integrate data 
from a multitude of sensors to make a more confident damage detection decision than is 
possible with any one sensor alone (Sohn et al., 2002).  
Feature extraction is the process of the identifying damage-sensitive properties, derived from 
the measured system response, which allows one to distinguish between the undamaged and 
damaged structure. Modal-based methods are among the most common damage detection 
methods used, principally because they are simple to implement on any size structure. 
Structures can be excited by ambient energy, an external shaker or embedded actuators, and 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
 
embedded strain gauges, piezo ceramics or accelerometers can be used to monitor the 
structural dynamic responses. The basic idea behind this technology is that modal parameters, 
notably frequencies, mode shapes and modal damping, are functions of the physical properties 
of the structure (mass, damping and stiffness). Therefore, changes in the physical properties, 
such as reductions in stiffness resulting from the onset of cracks or loosening of a connection, 
will cause detectable changes in the modal properties. Another type of modal-based damage 
detection, capable of detecting local damage, utilizes piezoelectric materials. The basic concept 
of this approach is to monitor the variations in the electrical impedance of piezoelectric 
materials, which is directly related to the mechanical impedance of the host structure, and will 
be affected by the presence of structural damage. The localized nature of the sensing region 
provides an advantage in that the impedance sensor is less sensitive to boundary condition 
changes or any operational vibrations, which usually affect lower order global modes. (Ciang, 
Lee, & Bang, 2008). Vision-based sensing systems are most commonly used for measurement 
of displacement responses from full-scale civil engineering structures. Vision-based approaches 
may incorporate Laser Doppler Vibrometers, global positioning systems, and camera-based 
systems with digital image processing techniques.    
The algorithms used in statistical model development usually fall into two categories. When 
data are available from both the undamaged and damaged structure, the statistical pattern 
recognition algorithms are referred to as supervised learning algorithms. Unsupervised learning 
refers to class of algorithms that are applied to data not containing examples from the damaged 
structure. The availability of reliable algorithms that accurately identify structural damage, 
embedded within structural monitoring systems would highly automate the task of assessing 
structural health.   
Examples of supervised learning methods include response surface analysis, linear 
discriminants, neural networks and genetic algorithms. Unsupervised learning methods include 
control chart analysis and novelty detection methods. 
2.4  Emerging Technologies and their impact on SHM 
As the rate of growth of infrastructure development slows in the developed economies 
and the existing infrastructure ages, the need for growing support to research programs in 
structural health monitoring has been realized by research communities. Approaches to SHM 
being developed involve the whole spectrum of the problem from sensors to data mining via 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
 
advanced sensor technology, embedded systems, communications, Information Technology, 
expert systems and Artificial Intelligence. They involve collaborations across disciplines and 
institutions, as well as across national boundaries, and are ideal cases for industrial support. In 
the European Union, the SIMCES project was funded under the fourth framework of the EU 
CORDIS research program, while the US National Science Foundation continues to fund research 
in ‘smart structures’ (Brownjohn, 2007).   
The transition of the traditional SHM techniques from the research community to the practical 
field implementation still needs to overcome difficult challenges due mainly to technical and 
economic considerations. 
Conventionally, structural monitoring systems consist of sensors installed in a structure, with 
response measurements communicated by coaxial cable to centralized data repositories where 
data are stored and processed. Direct consequences of using cables to communicate sensor 
measurements to a centralized data repository are the high system costs and labor-intensive 
installations. Further, cabling is also associated with the practical problem of establishing 
reliable communication for data transmission and control.  
One example of the application of a monitoring program is of the Lehigh River Bridge, SR-33. It 
was reported that during the three-year monitoring program, the direct cost of the equipment 
made up 40% of the total monitoring cost. For a long-term monitoring program, this direct cost 
must be addressed several times during the lifetime of the structure, due to software or 
equipment obsolescence. The other 60% of the cost is related to labor and other related items. 
Some of the challenges of the direct approach include: labor intensive sensor deployment and 
maintenance, sensor system cost, powering the sensors system, data transmission and data 
interpretation.  
The development of wireless local area networks and public radio systems mitigated these 
problems, lowering set-up and maintenance costs.  
As a consequence, wireless monitoring emerged in the as a promising technology with an 
impact on the field of structural monitoring and infrastructure asset management. Wireless 
sensing units integrate wireless communications and mobile computing with sensors to deliver 
a relatively inexpensive sensor platform. A key design feature of wireless sensing units is the 
collocation of computational power and sensors; the tight integration of computing with a 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
 
wireless sensing unit provides sensors with the opportunity to self-interrogate measurement 
data. Particularly, wireless sensing units that could interrogate structural data for signs of 
damage are of great interest.  
Unlike tethered monitoring systems where electrical power is delivered to sensors through the 
same coaxial cable intended for communication, a wireless structural monitoring largely 
depends upon battery power. With battery supplies limited in their operational life expectancy, 
the usage strategy of the wireless monitoring system is strongly influenced by a desire to 
conserve battery power. (Lynch, 2007).   
 
Figure 1 Wireless Sensing units and their architectural design. (Image Source: Lynch, 2007) 
 
The use of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles equipped with wireless sensors may also alleviate the capital 
and operational costs associated with structural health monitoring systems by avoiding the need 
for the structure to be covered with a large number of stationary sensors. Over the past few years, 
UAVs have become cheaper because many control functions can now be implemented in software 
rather than having to depend on expensive hardware. This has allowed single or multiple UAVs to 
be employed for real-world applications.  
Recently, the application of camera-equipped Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) for visually 
monitoring construction and operation of buildings, bridges, and other types of civil infrastructure 
systems has exponentially grown (Ham, Han, Lin, & Golparvar-Fard, 2011). These platforms can 
==PAGE:20==
 
19 
 
frequently survey construction sites, monitor work-in-progress, and inspect existing structures, 
particularly for hard-to-reach areas where it is either difficult to measure or risk to human safety. 
The potential applications for such unmanned aircraft in structural health monitoring focus on the 
tasks of state detection, damage analysis and condition monitoring.  
 
Figure 2 Use of UAVs for monitoring of civil infrastructure (Image Source: http://www.asctec.de) 
 
The field of unmanned aircraft is categorized according to different classifications, lined up by size 
restrictions, weight limits or the respective area of operation (operating radius, flight duration, 
etc.). These common categories include both aircraft and helicopters, as well as any other type of 
aircraft. The aircraft used for structural inspections are categorized as micro-UAVs (MAV or 
𝜇UAV). These are lightweight and easy to maneuver. With the advancement in measurement 
technologies, the sensors and equipment used for conducting non-destructive evaluation (NDE) 
are becoming smaller, lighter, and cheaper. Also, UAV technology is evolving rapidly and 
inspection-specific UAV features are just coming into the marketplace that will increase their 
effectiveness. Although NDE techniques can be considered to be well established in general, their 
implementation into a flying platform is still a significant challenge. Another influencing aspect is 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
 
the request for stable hovering characteristics to ensure a planned and detailed damage 
inspection. 
Thus, with the new concept of equipping a drone with NDE equipment, possibilities are created 
for new research areas in the field of remote sensing-based structural health monitoring.

3. Reliability of Structural Systems 
 
 
3.1 Introduction 
In the context of probabilistic modeling, structural analysis is the formulation of a 
mathematical model to arrive at the probability of a structure behaving in a specific manner given 
that its material properties and/or the actions on the structure are of a random or incompletely 
known nature. It is possible to formulate many different probabilistic models for the evaluation 
of the reliability of a structure. These models may be differing in the elaboration of detailing and 
may therefore be differing in flexibility with respect to handling new information. It is common to 
all probabilistic reliability models that the measure of reliability with respect to not experiencing 
some specified adverse behavior is uniquely related to the probability that the adverse behavior 
will take place within a specified time interval as, for example, the assumed time of existence of 
the structure (the design life time) (Ditlevsen & Madsen, 2005). This probability is denoted as the 
probability of failure. 
3.2 Limit States  
The concept of limit state related to a specified requirement is defined as a state of the 
structure including its loads at which the structure is just on the point of not satisfying the 
requirement. Often the requirement is verbally formulated. However, usually the requirement 
will be interpreted and formulated within a mathematical model for the geometric and 
mechanical properties of the structure and for the actions on the structure. Let 𝑋1, 𝑋2, . . . , 𝑋𝑛 be 
those variables that independently contribute to that part of the mathematical model that 
concerns geometry, strength properties and actions. The values of the variables can be chosen 
==PAGE:23==
 
22 
 
freely and independently within a given domain. To each choice of values corresponds a uniquely 
defined structure with uniquely defined loads. This structure with its loads is a pure mathematical 
object that does or does not satisfy a given limit state requirement.  
Therefore, a given limit-state requirement divides the domain of definition of the model in two 
sets, the safe set and the failure set, in which the requirement is satisfied and not satisfied, 
respectively. The boundary of the safe set (which, of course, is also the boundary of the failure 
set) is called the limit state. In the models considered herein the limit state is of sufficiently simple 
structure to be represented as the set of zero points for a piecewise differentiable function 
𝑔(𝑋1, … , 𝑋𝑛) which is defined everywhere in the domain of definition of the model and which 
takes positive values in the internal of the safe set and negative values in the internal of the failure 
set. Thus, the limit state is given as the set of values of the input variables (𝑋1, … , 𝑋𝑛) for which 
𝑔(𝑋1, … . , 𝑋𝑛) = 0 
(3.1)

3.6 Structural Reliability Methods 
The integral presented in Equation 3.2 is non-trivial to solve and hence numerical 
approximations are required. Several methods for the solution of the integral have been proposed 
including numerical integration techniques which, however, become increasingly inefficient with 
the increasing size of the vector of random variables. To this end, approximation methods, such 
as the First Order Reliability Method (FORM) and Second Order Reliability Method (SORM) have 
been developed in the area of structural reliability. These methods have since then been 
significantly refined and extended and are by now widely applied for reliability evaluations. Two 
steps are involved in these approximation: (i) simplify the integrand so that its contours become 
more regular and symmetric, and (ii) approximate the integration boundary. After the two steps, 
an analytical solution to the probability integration is easily found. 
The integral may also be estimated by so-called simulation techniques. It is noted that simulation 
techniques have proven their value especially for problems where the representation of the limit 
state function is associated with difﬁculties. Such cases are for example, when the limit state 
function is not differentiable or when several design points contribute to the failure probability 
(Michael Havbro Faber, 2012). All simulation techniques have their origin in the so-called crude 
Monte Carlo Method, the principles of this simulation technique are brieﬂy outlined below.

3.6.2 Random Number Generation (RNG) 
Random numbers can be generated via physical mechanisms such as the timing between 
successive events in atomic decay, thermal noise in semiconductors, and the like. However, “Real” 
realizations of random variables based on physical devices are difficult to get. In practice, random 
variables and other random objects are simulated by deterministic algorithms. Ideally, the 
physical RNGs may be used for selecting the ‘’seed’’ of an algorithmic RNG or the ‘’seed’’ may be 
==PAGE:31==
 
30 
 
created by observing a random process (for example: measurement of radioactive decay). A good 
algorithmic RNG whose seed is selected at random can be viewed as an extensor of randomness, 
stretching a short random seed into a long sequence of pseudorandom numbers (L’Ecuyer, 2004). 
Hence, computer generated realizations of random variables are denoted as “pseudo random 
realizations” because the results are not random in a strict, mathematical sense. Software 
applications such as MATLAB, use algorithms that make the results appear to be random and 
independent. The results are also required to pass various statistical tests of randomness and 
independence.

4. Reliability of SHM systems 
 
 
4.1 Introduction 
Non-destructive testing methods employed in monitoring systems are able to retrieve 
information on the geometry of flaws; on states of material degradation; on material parameters; 
and on stress related material states. This information is required for damage assessment 
concepts, especially those related to the applied design concepts. In order to detect damage, the 
following NDT methods, among others, have been applied in SHM: ultrasonic inspection, electro-
magnetic inspection, radiographic inspection, and visual inspection. Devices for traditional NDT 
methods are usually complex and expensive. In recent years, several new monitoring methods 
have been proposed, including Lamb-wave-based PZT sensors, fibre Bragg gratings (FBGs) and 
intelligent coating monitoring (ICM). Different sensors offer different features in damage 
detection. (Wang, He, Dong, Liu, & Zhang, 2016) 
A quantitative consideration of NDT methods in assessment procedures requires quantitative 
statements about the reliability of the applied NDT method. The determination of the Probability 
of Detection (PoD) is one possibility to quantify the probability to detect a specific flaw with an 
NDT method. Each NDT measurement is influenced by a variety of factors: the stage of 
development and the quality of NDT equipment, the quality of written procedures, the skills and 
attitude of the operators, the geometry and material of the component, the environment in which 
the inspection takes place, the location, orientation and size of flaws, as well as the material state. 
Therefore, reliability information about an NDT method requires defining a Probability of 
Detection. 
==PAGE:34==
 
33 
 
The knowledge of the Probability of Detection of a certain flaw allows assessing the consequences 
of this flaw in a probabilistic manner. The PoD is usually expressed as a function of flaw size or 
damage size, although in reality it is a function of many other physical and operational 
parameters, such as, the material, the geometry, the flaw type, the NDT method, the testing 
conditions and the NDT personnel (e.g. their certification, education and experience). 
4.2 Probability of Detection of SHM systems 
The Probability of Detection expresses the probability of detecting a damage or defect of 
a particular size and is the common measure to evaluate the capability of a monitoring system. 
Generally, the Probability of Detection increases with the size of the damage and eventually 
reaches a maximum value at which no detection is governed by other complicating factors (for 
example, human/operator errors) that may dominate the detection process (Zhang & 
Mahadevan, 2001). Many studies have been reported to develop the PoD for different methods 
of inspection. Several PoD models in the form of a cumulative distributive function (CDF) of the 
damage size have been suggested, including exponential distribution, lognormal distribution, and 
Weibull distribution.  
The recommended practice for the experimental sequence of events for generating data to 
produce PoD curves and to ‘certify’ (i.e. validate) an NDT method or procedure can be broadly 
summarized here (Georgiou, 2006) :  
• Manufacture or procure damaged specimens with the required large number of relevant flaw 
sizes and flaw types 
• Inspect the flaw specimens with the appropriate NDT method  
• Record the results as a function of flaw size  
• Plot the PoD curve as a function of flaw size 
During the last two decades, the Modeling of NDT capability has increased and improved 
substantially. The savings in carrying out Modeling of PoD, as opposed to the experimental 
determination of PoD, has been a strong motivation in developing models. The development of 
Modeling PoD has focused on NDT methods such as ultrasound, eddy currents, X-ray radiography, 
etc.

In the present study, Probability of Detection models are developed for an individual component 
of a structural system following which a method is developed to obtain the Probability of

5. Unmanned Aerial Vehicle based SHM  
 
 
5.1 Introduction 
An Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) is defined as an aircraft flown with no pilot on board.  
They are sometimes referred to as drones and the name can be used interchangeably. The vehicle 
is controlled either autonomously or with the use of a remote control by a pilot from the ground 
and can carry a wide range of technologies including cameras for still or video imaging, 
instruments for remote sensing/detecting, infrared sensors, etc. UAV technology is evolving 
rapidly and inspection-specific UAV features are just coming into the marketplace that will 
increase their effectiveness with respect to structural health monitoring. Frost & Sullivan pegs 
today’s global market for nonmilitary Unmanned Aerial Systems at $2.5 billion, and projects it will 
reach $10.8 billion by 2020 (Weng & Enbar, 2015). Reports suggest that, in Europe alone, the 
combined revenue from unmanned platforms; system and platform support and services; 
application services and enabling technologies reached approximately $88 million in 2015. This 
anticipated demand is likely to stimulate further technical innovation, and thus improve the 
technology’s fundamental value proposition for O&M and other applications. However, some 
challenges must be addressed to fully unlock the technology’s commercial appeal for SHM and 
other applications. The novelty of the technology, operator inexperience and concerns about 
safety are among the main restrictions in the widespread adoption of UAV based SHM. The 
technical capabilities of UAV have steadily improved over the years and additional feature such 
as ‘sense-and-avoid’ safety protocols are needed to further improve their autonomous flight 
function and consequently, the economic proposition.

5.2 Current UAV Technology 
Today, a wide variety of commercial UAV models are available on the market that range 
in size, weight, shape, material makeup, fuel capacity, flight technology, control mechanism, and 
functional capability. These systems typically include a host of sensors and instruments used for 
navigation, imaging, data collection, and remote sensing/detecting. A small, programmable 
computer system controls the onboard equipment.

architecture enables greater controllability and precision of movement. While hovering up-close 
over modules and other system equipment, close-up inspections using visual, infrared, and other 
specialized sensors can capture exceptionally high-resolution imagery and unique data, which is 
more difficult to obtain at longer distances.

5.3 UAVs and Safety Issues 
UAVs have come under scrutiny for privacy and safety concerns. Most UAVs have safety 
features to reduce the risk involved. The first is the lightweight design: to have a longer battery 
life, all the components are designed to be as light weight as possible. The light weight reduces 
the damage potential if there were a collision. There are also several fail safes built into the device 
==PAGE:52==
 
51 
 
including a return to home function if communication with the remote is ever lost. Further, the 
propeller motors are also programmed to stop running if an object is struck. Apart from the 
safeguards adopted by UAV manufacturers, most countries also have a regulatory framework to 
minimize the risks involved in UAV based operations. For Europe, the European Aviation Safety 
Agency started to develop a proposal for an operation centric, proportionate, risk- and 
performance-based regulatory framework for all unmanned aircraft (UA) establishing three 
categories with different safety requirements, proportionate to the risk.  
The agency has taken into consideration the developments in the international arena e.g. work 
done in the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO); in the Joint Authorities for the 
Rulemaking of Unmanned Systems (JARUS) and in the USA (Federal Aviation Administration- FAA). 
The proposal provides a framework to safely operate drones while allowing this industry to 
remain agile, to innovate and continue to grow. The risk posed to people on the ground and to 
other aircraft as well as privacy, security and data protection issues created by such drones are 
also considered. 
5.4 Costs associated with a UAV based SHM System 
UAV based inspection provides opportunities for economies when viewed as an end-to-
end process, the upfront investment can be greater than with a traditional inspection. However, 
this needs to be carefully evaluated against the costs of a traditional inspection, as well as the 
safety of personnel. Also, UAV based inspection delivers a key benefit over traditional inspection 
methods in the minimization of the need for a shutdown, planned or unplanned. 
Determining the cost-benefit of UAS is a difficult proposition given the early stage nature of the 
market and a general lack of available data. While hard cost components, such as capital costs 
and O&M, can be reasonably estimated, soft costs are less clear-cut. For example, the extent and 
means by which industry participants train personnel, obtain certification, test aircraft, set up 
operations, and acquire insurance varies widely, as does their associated costs. 
There are many UAVs currently on the market with GPS and imaging capability starting at around 
$1000. The wide range in price is attributed to features and length of battery life. A lower cost

5.6 Future Applications and Technologies 
UAVs can be affixed with a variety of advanced sensors and, in turn, collect an assortment 
of data simultaneously. Some of these developing applications are discussed below: 
• 
Image processing based/Optical 
Lightweight optical sensors are commercially available for airborne applications with on-
board image pre-processing tools. The availability of digital information in camera systems 
nowadays has made inspection possible even at very high resolution. In terms of damage 
inspection, tests have been done based on high resolution areas allowing crack sizes down into 
the millimeter range to be characterized (Ham et al., 2011). The digital image data stream supplied 
by the imaging sensors of a UAV could be analyzed by automated techniques. Using software like 
‘eCognition’, fuzzy logic reasoning can be implemented to identify possible defects in the captured 
imagery and transfer the information to an alarming system (Fernandez Galarreta, Kerle, & Gerke, 
2015). 
Digital image correlation (DIC) can be used as an alternative to traditional bridge response 
measurement instruments such as strain gages or linear variable differential transformers. DIC is 
an optical measurement technique that captures displacement data in both two and three 
dimensions through high-resolution digital photography (Peddle, Goudreau, Carlson, & Santini-
bell, 2011). 
==PAGE:57==
 
56 
 
The digital image correlation technique can be adapted as a modal-based method for wind turbine 
too. This method utilizes a digital video camcorder to capture the video of an area of interest on 
the target structure. The area of interest is patched with pattern of white spots on a black 
background. A digital image correlation algorithm is then used to compare the video frames of 
the pattern with a baseline image. The displacement of the area of interest can be obtained in 
real time and the global structural integrity can be known using modal analysis. This method has 
been applied to flexible bridges to determine their dynamic displacement and natural frequency 
with the results showing that it is comparable to the resolution of a laser vibrometer but much 
more cost effective (Ciang et al., 2008). 
• 
Piezoelectric Transducer (PZT) based NDE  
The idea is for the drone to temporarily attach the Piezoelectric Transducer onto a specific 
region where the excitation and data acquisition occurs simultaneously. This eliminates the need 
to have hundreds of permanently attached sensors on the structure. A study investigating the 
effects of hovering state of the drone on the measured impedance signatures is made. It is 
concluded that, due to the high frequency usage of the NDE method, the effect of hovering on 
the impedance signature can be ignored. (Na & Baek, 2016). The study concludes with results that 
validate the drone SHM concept but affirms the need for further research into this.

Laser-based Methods 
Laser interferometry makes use of the intensity distribution pattern formed from space 
interference, generated by illumination of coherent light onto rough object surfaces. Laser speckle 
interferometry can be used in inspection of metal, ceramic, glass, rubber and composite 
materials. The surface stress can be also measured for highly accurate measurements of 
deformation. The idea could be to equip the UAV with generator and sensor for laser 
interferometry and use them to perform inspection. The data measured, in the form of 
displacement response or velocity response time series, could be used for vibration based modal 
analysis. Laser based Ultrasonics (LBU) involves the generation of ultrasound by laser illumination 
and the detection of ultrasonic signals by laser interferometric techniques. LBU has many 
advantages for applications to quantitative nondestructive evaluation including non-contact 
generation and detection, remote placement of equipment using fiber-optics, easy scanning, 
absolute displacement calibration, both broad band and narrow band signal generation, wide 
frequency band measurement, and applicability to curved surfaces.

Active infrared thermography has been successfully applied as a nondestructive testing

duration energy pulse (flash light, ultrasonic vibration, etc.) and the surface thermal images are 
collected via an infrared camera. There are already commercial models of UAV equipped with

5.7 Uncertainty in Measurement for UAV based SHM system 
Regarding the UAV based SHM system, two strategies for measurement are proposed. 
The first strategy involves the UAV to be in flight operation while taking the measurement 
corresponding to a relevant NDT method through an on-board sensor. The second strategy 
involves the UAV temporarily affixing itself, and hence the sensor, to the measurement location 
on the structure followed by detachment and moving on to the next target, when the 
measurement has been completed. The effect of measurement uncertainty when using either of 
the above-mentioned strategies is considered by applying an appropriate human error factor to 
the ‘PoD’ model as developed in the earlier section (see section 4.2) in accordance with (Wall et 
al., 2009). In the case of the first strategy, an operator error in taking measurements due to 
difficulty in maneuvering the UAV due to its hovering motion is considered along with an operator 
error due to lack of familiarity with the equipment procedure and data analysis. The effect of the 
errors is considered by using a factor of 0.95 for hovering effect and 0.95 for lack of familiarity. In 
the case of the second strategy, the effect of error due to hovering motion is disregarded and a 
factor of 0.95 is used to account for lack of familiarity with the procedure and data analysis.  
In practice, the actual effect of human reliability on the PoD of UAV based systems may be 
obtained through trial data and it may be expected that the human error is lower for larger 
damage sizes and also that the error decreases with increasing operator familiarity and skill.

6. Bayesian Decision Analysis 
 
 
6.1 Introduction 
Bayesian decision theory is a prescriptive or normative model for decision making under 
uncertainty (Benjamin & Cornell, 1970). The decision maker draws on the specific information 
relevant to the problem at hand and use this probabilistic information to help make the best 
decision possible (i.e. which maximizes the benefit or minimizes the associated undesirable 
consequences).  
Standard Bayesian inference and decision analysis is an operational reality in a wide range of 
applications where alternative theories - for example those based on belief functions or fuzzy logic 
are less well developed. When looking for a subjective methodology which can systematically 
incorporate expert judgements and preferences the obvious prime candidate to try out first is 
currently the Bayesian framework. As the dominant types of decision problems has begun to shift 
away from small scale repeating processes to larger scale one off Modeling and high dimensional 
business and phenomenological applications where data is sparse and often observational and 
not experimental, the Bayesian framework has gained importance (Smith, 2010).  
6.2 Framework for Decision Making 
A Bayesian analysis facilitates the decision maker through building a subjective probability 
model capturing the nature of uncertainties about features of the model which might affect 
decision making and producing a utility function with due regard to the needs of the various 
stakeholders. The decision maker can then calculate the expected utility associated with each 
==PAGE:61==
 
60 
 
viable decision. The best decisions are identified as those having the highest expected utility 
score.  
In the context of applied decision theory according to (Raiffa & Schlaifer, 1961), we are, 
in this study, concerned with the logical analysis of choice among courses of action when (a) the 
consequence of any course of action will depend upon the “state of the world’’, (b) the true state 
is yet unknown, but (c) it is possible at a cost to obtain additional information about the state. It 
may be further assumed that the person responsible for the decision has already eliminated many 
possible courses of action as being unworthy of further consideration and thus has reduced the 
problem to a choice within a well-defined set of contenders.  
A formal definition of the basic data involved in the decision analysis may now be made: 
•

of potential acts. 
•

action 𝑎 depends on some “state of the world” 𝑋𝑖 (which cannot be predicted with certainty) 
in the domain of space of possible states.  
•

outcome of an experiment before performing an action. They may select an experiment 𝑒

6.5 Value of Information Analysis 
Decisions are often required to be made under uncertainty and risk with the possibility to 
gather further information prior to making the decision. Obtaining the information reduces the 
uncertainty and hence the risk associated with the decision. However, collecting information 
comes at a price which may not always be justified by its benefit. If the cost of this information is 
small in comparison to the potential Value of Information, the decision maker may make the 
decision of performing the experiment (or monitoring system). A Value of Information analysis is 
a decision analysis having a certain format to quantify the utility or benefit increase due to 
additional or unknown information. It is possible to distinguish four types of Value of Information 
analyses: 
• 
Expected/Conditional Value of Sample Information (EVSI/CVSI) 
▪ 
EVSI: Difference of utilities in pre-posterior and prior decision analysis 
▪ 
CVSI: Difference of utilities in posterior and prior decision analysis 
Sample Information refers to information with a finite precision (uncertain). 
• 
Expected/Conditional Value of Perfect Information (EVPI/CVPI): Analogous to EVSI/CVSI 
but with infinitely precise information, i.e. without uncertainties. 
==PAGE:67==
 
66 
 
The Value of Information analysis thus enables the optimization of the decision regarding choice 
of experiment/monitoring system before any action is performed. The most optimal system 
would be the one for which the information acquirement is most cost-efficient i.e. with the 
maximum expected Value of Information.

7.4 System Model 
A system model for the Wind Park including Wind Turbine structures as its components, 
subjected to extreme loading and accounting for deterioration in the form of stiffness loss is 
developed in accordance with section 3.5. 
Considering a Wind Park Operation, the failure of the system can be attributed to the inability of 
the Wind Park to generate the requisite power. It should also be considered that the occurrence 
of damage or failure at the individual components i.e. Wind Turbines, does not necessarily lead 
to the loss of the Wind Park’s ability to generate power. Hence, for a system with several turbines, 
a degree of redundancy exists such that failure of one or few turbines would not lead to complete 
failure of the system (Michael H. Faber, Thöns, & Val, 2017). However, if there are a number of 
component failures sufficient to cause loss of functionality of Wind Park, system failure can occur. 
Hence, the system can be modeled as a Brittle Daniels System.

7.5 Consequence and Cost Modeling 
Following an exposure event, the components of a structural system can either remain in 
an undamaged state as before or change to a damage state. A damaged component can then 
either lead to the failure of the component or no failure. Consequences are associated with each 
of the possible damage and failure scenarios, and are classified as either direct or indirect. Direct 
consequences are considered to result from damage states or failure of individual component(s). 
Indirect consequences are incurred due to system failure (or loss of system functionality).  
Both types of consequences are expressed as costs in this study.  
The direct consequence of the system state being intact is the benefits from power production 
(according to Table 3 and Figure 30). 
Direct consequence of component in the state of damage is a cost of repair of the component 
whereas a direct consequence of failure is the replacement of the component. The replacement 
cost of a component can be approximately estimated as being equal to the unit price of the 
component. In addition, the losses in power production ability due to the activity of repair and 
replacement is also considered. 
Indirect consequence corresponding to the cost of wind park investment are incurred because of 
system failure. In addition, the monetary loss of power production from time of failure to end of 
service life is also considered. 
With SHM systems, the consequence related to component and system failure would also include 
the cost of SHM investment associated with that component and with the system, respectively. 
Further, a reduction in the monetary consequence of loss of power production ability is 
considered when the SHM system is able to acquire information about damage (damage 
indication). It is assumed that due to SHM information, it is possible to optimally plan repair and 
replacement activity, thereby reducing down-time costs.

8. Conclusion 
 
 
The applicability of UAVs for the purpose of Structural Health Monitoring has been investigated 
in this thesis in the context of structural expected benefit and risk assessment and Bayesian 
Decision analysis. A system model has been developed to represent a deteriorating structure 
(undergoing stiffness reduction) under the action of extreme loading, while accounting for the 
correlation between system components. The precision of SHM systems has been modeled with 
Probability of Detection curves and a system Probability of Detection model has been developed 
to account for the reliability of a SHM system monitoring a structural system. This facilitates to 
update the structural system reliabilities based on the SHM system information. A review of 
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) has been presented with an emphasis on their application in 
structural inspection and monitoring activities. This enables the formulation of measurement 
strategies combining the use of UAVs and NDT sensors. The precision of UAV based SHM systems 
has been modeled considering the effects of operational uncertainty, caused by the hovering 
motion of the UAV and operator inexperience, on damage detection. Further, the costs associated 
with UAV based monitoring are also presented. The analysis of the expected costs and benefits of 
conventional and UAV based SHM systems has been conducted through the quantification of 
Value of Information in the context of a study on the operation of a Wind Park. A decision scenario 
to extend service life of the Wind Park with and without additional information provided by 
different SHM systems/strategies was developed taking basis in Bayesian Decision Analysis. The 
performance of the Wind Park system has been modelled under extreme events through 
representative system reliability models which also account for degradation over time for the 
==PAGE:88==
 
87 
 
complete service life of the Wind Turbine structures. The developed system model captures the 
redundancy with respect to the functioning of a Wind Park system. Further, monitoring strategies 
have been described and their precision depicted along with their life-cycle costs. The expected 
benefits over the service life of a Wind Park have been quantified accounting for the capital 
expenditure, operation costs and power production. Further, the structural risks were quantified 
by modelling the consequences of Wind Turbine damage, Wind Turbine failure and Wind Park 
failure and expressing them with a monetary value. As a result, the expected utilities over the 
service life of the Wind Park were obtained with the optimal decisions of extending the service 
life if SHM is utilized and of keeping the originally intended service life if no SHM is utilized.  
               With respect to the results obtained from the case study, it can be concluded that: 
1. The expected Value of Information from UAV based SHM systems is higher as compared 
to Conventional SHM systems. 
a. This difference is considerable (23% for UAV based SHM Strategy 1 and 25% for 
UAV based SHM Strategy 2) and could be expected to increase with increasing 
precision of UAV based SHM systems.  
2. If the Value of Information obtained from SHM systems is compared with the cost of 
obtaining the information, then it may be observed that the UAV based systems give 
considerably higher results. For the service life of 25 years of the Wind Park from the case 
study, the Value of Information from UAV based systems is expected to be in the range 
of 2,000,000 Euros and the corresponding costs are around 200,000 Euros. Whereas, for 
the conventional SHM system, the Value of Information is expected to be approximately 
1,500,000 Euros and the cost of obtaining the information is 600,000 Euros.  
3. Of the two measurement techniques considered in the UAV based SHM system, the one 
with the higher modeled precision expectedly gives higher benefits, although this 
difference is marginal (2% when comparing Value of Information). This may lead us to 
conclude that for the case study considered, the costs of the SHM systems were 
dominating the benefits from precision of monitoring information.  
4. A higher precision of UAV based measurement can be obtained if the effect of hovering 
motion on the measurement uncertainty is reduced. 
It should be noted that a conventional and simplified approach has been utilized in Modeling the 
system PoD (section 4.2.5) and that the resultant PoD value is lower than what would have been 
==PAGE:89==
 
88 
 
obtained following the developed ellipsoid model (section 4.2.4). Similarly, a simplified approach 
has been followed to model the effect of operational and measurement uncertainties on PoD of 
UAV based systems. Also, an estimation has been used in Modeling the operating costs of UAV 
based systems. Hence, a groundwork for future research may be laid in the: 
i) 
Calibration of system PoD models following the approach described in the mathematical 
model developed in section 4.2.4  
ii) 
Using actual trial based data to model the measurement and operational uncertainties 
related to UAV based monitoring systems. Such data may then be utilized in a more 
accurate description of the precision of UAV based monitoring systems. Also, it can be 
postulated that the effect of human reliability factor due to lack of operator familiarity 
decreases with time and repeated measurements. Hence, modeling the precision of UAV 
based systems with actual trial based data recorded over time can reduce the limitations 
of the models developed in the present study. 
iii) 
Substantiating the actual operating costs of UAV based monitoring activities. This may be 
done based on industry or market data and would aid in the economic comparison 
between UAV based and conventional monitoring systems.
