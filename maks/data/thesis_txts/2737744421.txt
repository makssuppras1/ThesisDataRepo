Abstract
With rising sea levels, accurately mapping the bathymetry of shallow coastal regions has
become essential for coastal preservation. This thesis proposes a workflow for spatially
modeling bathymetry using green LiDAR data and image segmentation of orthophotos.
The workflow is adapted to and tested in a shallow coastal region with high turbidity in the
Wadden Sea. It involves two main steps: seafloor extraction and spatial modeling.
The seafloor extraction uses point clouds based on a full waveform processing by the
company RIEGL. The point clouds contain reflections from the sea surface, the water
column, and the seafloor and are affected by outliers. The outliers were filtered using
a density­based clustering algorithm, DBSCAN, to isolate the seafloor. Additionally, it
was found necessary to modify the seafloor extraction to the surface type through image
segmentation of orthophotos.
Alternative unsupervised machine learning algorithms were tested against RIEGL’s raw
full waveform processing. However, the point cloud extraction was found to be superior,
yielding a 5 cm error margin to a multi­beam recording.
A Gaussian Markov Random Field (GMRF) is recommended for creating spatial models of
the bathymetry, which showed good agreement with validation datasets. An advantage of
the GMRF is that it provides a standard deviation for the bathymetry estimates, which can
help determine the depth limitations of future LiDAR surveys. Although including image
segmentation in the spatial models was tested, it did not show significant differences from
the original GMRF model.

1
Introduction
Mapping of seabed topography, called bathymetry, is essential in many different applica­
tions, including navigation, morphological mapping [2], and the growing interest in offshore
wind farms [3]. Bathymetry maps of shallow coastal regions have become especially crit­
ical due to rising sea levels, aiding in coastal preservation [4].
A typical bathymetric surveying method is Multi­Beam Echo Sounders (MBESs), an echo­
sounding technique using the two­way travel time of sound waves to map the subsurface
topography. MBESs are typically mounted on ships that cannot enter shallow waters [5].
This is where the alternative LiDAR (Light Detection And Ranging) offers an advantage.
Compared to the MBESs, the LiDAR sensors can operate from an airborne platform by
emitting green laser beams for bathymetry measurements.
This thesis aims to develop a workflow for spatial modeling of the bathymetry in the Wad­
den Sea using green LiDAR data and incorporating image segmentation of orthophotos.
The investigations are based on two LiDAR surveys conducted in November 2023 by
NIRAS: one performed during low tide and the other during high tide. The LiDAR mea­
surements were recorded from a gyro­helicopter equipped with a laser scanner from the
company RIEGL[1] and a camera that recorded images.
The test area in the Wadden Sea is a shallow coastal region. The water level in the
Wadden Sea changes with a semi­diurnal tidal cycle, causing sediments to be mixed in
the water column during high tide, decreasing the water transparency[6]. This makes
mapping the Wadden Sea environment challenging with LiDAR, as the returned signal
strength of each emitted laser pulse depends on the local turbidity [7]. Therefore, this
thesis focuses on adapting the workflow to the turbidity conditions of the Wadden Sea.
The workflow for creating the spatial models is split into two parts:
1. Extracting the seafloor point estimates from the LiDAR surveys
• Two approaches are tested for extracting the bottom echoes: from the returned
energy spectra (full waveforms) and from point clouds based on RIEGL’s pro­
cessing of the full waveforms. The first approach investigates if unsupervised
machine learning techniques can extract more information about the seafloor
from the raw full waveforms than RIEGL’s processing. The second approach
extracts the bottom echoes from RIEGL’s processed point clouds. This in­
volves investigating if bottom echo extractions can be improved by modifying
the point cloud extraction based on image segmentation of orthophotos.
2. Creating a spatial model of the bathymetry in the Wadden Sea
• Based on the best­performing seafloor extraction approach, a probabilistic spa­
tial model (Gaussian Markov Random Field (GMRF)) is used to create contin­
uous bathymetry profiles. This includes a test of whether including the im­
age segmentation of orthophotos affects the bathymetry mapping. The final
bathymetry profiles are validated against a multi­beam recording by the Dan­
ish Coastal Authority[8] and two DTMs of the Wadden Sea created by NIRAS.
This study can help understand the challenges and potentials of conducting LiDAR bathy­
metric surveys in highly turbid water areas and demonstrates the value of orthophotos in
mapping shallow coastal regions.

Theory
In this project, it is sought to map the bathymetry in the Wadden Sea. The mapping is
based on a LiDAR (Light Detection And Ranging) survey performed at high and low tide
combined with semi­simultaneous image recordings. This section describes the theory
behind bathymetric mapping with LiDAR in shallow water areas (section 2.1 and 2.2) with
a comparison of different bathymetric sensors (section 2.3). The Wadden Sea can be
particularly challenging to map with LiDAR due to its large tidal influence and varying
morphology. To understand these challenges, a description of the Wadden Sea is made
in section 2.4.
2.1
Airborne LiDAR Bathymetry (ALB)
LiDAR is an active remote sensing technique where laser pulses are emitted. The sensor
records the backscattered energy for every emitted pulse once the pulses are reflected
from a surface. The backscattered energy spectrum is called the full waveform. Us­
ing the two­way travel time from when the pulses are transmitted to being received, the
elevation of the surface below can be found with an ellipsoid model. A typical applica­
tion of LiDAR data is bathymetry mapping, where bathymetry is a measure of the sea
bed topography. This means that, opposite to topography, bathymetry measures water
depth below sea level, whereas topography is a measure of elevation above sea level[9].

ceived echo signal is delayed from the transmitted, where the shape of the returned signal
depends on the encountered surface. Specifically for bathymetric measurements, the re­
ceived echo signal has three main returns from the [13]:
1. Sea surface
2. Water column
3. Seafloor
The sea surface and seafloor backscatter often follow a Gaussian distribution, as depicted
in figure 2.1. In the water column, absorption and volume scattering of the signal occurs
with interactions with water molecules and sediment particles. This scattering broadens
the laser beam footprint in the water [14] (see figure 2.1). The absorption and scattering
of the laser beam are highly dependent on the turbidity of the water, where turbidity is a
measure of clarity ­ the less clear the water is, the higher the turbidity. The effects of the
turbidity can be expressed through the volume exponential decay function given as [14]:
f(x) = a · e−k·x
(2.1)
where x is the distance the laser has traveled in the water column, a is the backscat­
ter, f(x) is the strength of the reflected signal, and k is the volume exponential decay
coefficient. Hence, the larger the turbidity, the more dampened the returned signal will
be. Since the intensity of the laser pulse is exponentially dampened with distance, the
outermost part of the sensor’s footprint is more decayed than the central part of the foot­
print [15]. At larger distances, beam divergence occurs where the beam diameter in­
creases with distance. With a larger beam divergence, the pulse energy covers a larger
area, resulting in a lower Signal­to­Noise Ratio (SNR). Note that the beam divergence is
instrument­specific [16].
As for other remote sensing techniques, LiDAR uses a translation into a range from the
time of the two­way travel (from the laser beam being transmitted to being received). The
translation into range is shown in equation 2.2 [17]:
R =
c
2 · n · T
(2.2)
where T is the time of the two­way travel, c is the speed of light with which the laser
travels, and n is the average of refractive indices. Note that when the beam encounters
the boundary between air and water, the reflected signal changes due to the refraction
differences between the two mediums. If this is not corrected, the water surface will ap­
pear higher than in reality, resulting in a skewed measurement [18] ­ Hence, why the n is
included in equation 2.2.
Next, the elevations can be found by subtracting the refraction­corrected ranges from the
altitude of the aircraft w.r.t. an ellipsoid­ or a geoid model. The geo­referencing of the
aircraft is performed by using a Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) receiver to
find the coordinates of the aircraft and by using an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) to
find the orientation and altitude [19]. The processing workflow for translating the two­way
travel time into an elevation is summarized in figure 2.2.

Bathymetric waveforms in shallow waters
As stated, the three main returns in a bathymetric waveform are from the water surface,
water column, and seafloor. However, it should be noted that the reflections from the water
column typically are much weaker than the surface and seafloor reflections in shallow
waters. Therefore, two waveforms are typically seen in shallow waters (see figure 2.3
from the article by Yang et al.). The surface and bottom echoes are either blended into
each other (left image), or the two Gaussian peaks are separate (right image). The overlap
between the two echoes is typically due to very shallow waters where the LiDAR sensor
cannot distinguish the echoes[13].

2.3
Bathymetric sensors
There are two main types of LiDAR sensors: the discrete LiDAR and the full­waveform Li­
DAR. LiDAR records reflections from each encounter that the laser beam has with different
mediums. Depending on the type of LiDAR sensor, these encounters will be recorded as
either discrete points (peaks) or as a continuous energy spectrum of the entire backscat­
tered signal (full­waveform). Aside from LiDAR, another typical sensor that can be used
for bathymetric measurements is a Multi­Beam Echo Sounder (MBES).
Each sensor type is described below, along with its advantages and disadvantages.
2.3.1
Discrete LiDAR

peaks of a returned signal, for exam­

[21].

The advantage of the discrete LiDAR system is that it requires less memory than the full­
waveform LiDAR system (described in the next section) since much of the returned signal
is filtered out during flight [19].
2.3.2
Full­waveform LiDAR
The full­waveform LiDAR system records the complete backscattered signal for each emit­
ted pulse. The memory requirements are much higher than for the discrete LiDAR system
[21], and it requires additional post­processing to determine which feature each peak cor­
responds to [22]. An illustration of a bathymetric full­waveform is seen in figure 2.5a.
As stated previously, the intensity of the transmitted signal is exponentially decayed with
distance; hence, the surface peak is always greater than the bottom echo. The deeper the
water, the more attenuated the signal is, and the higher the intensity difference between
the surface and bottom echo peaks. Moreover, the attenuation, e.g., caused by high
turbidity, often results in less well­defined bottom echo peaks shown in figure 2.5b.
The signal strength also depends on the surface type that is investigated. As observed in
the article by Kastdallen et al.[23], the signal strength of the green LiDAR can be signifi­
cantly decreased with turbulent water (white wave crashes) and darker vegetated water
bottoms. Therefore, the local morphology is important when investigating the LiDAR sen­
sor’s signal loss.
The main benefit of full­waveform LiDAR is that it contains more information than discrete
LiDAR. If using the discrete LiDAR system, the five largest peaks in the spectra do not

the points are combined into a 3D density point cloud[36].

4.1
Typical full­waveform decomposition methodologies
The typical characteristics of a LiDAR full­waveform include the emitted and received
waveforms and target characteristics, e.g., the refractive index of the water surface. These
waveform characteristics are included in different ways to determine the bottom echo in
a bathymetric full­waveform.
Different methodologies for locating the surface and bottom echoes in the raw full­waveforms
are described in an article by Wang et al. [41]. These are listed as:
1. Echo detection
2. Mathematical approximation
3. Deconvolution
Echo detection is a methodology that does not include information on the encountered
surface. A typical echo detection method could be simple thresholding or peak finding,
which is investigated shortly in this thesis in section 4.3.1.
The mathematical approximation methodology demands pre­knowledge of how the reflec­
tions in the waveform can be modeled. A typical example is the Gaussian decomposition
of the waveform, where it is assumed that the full waveform can be modeled as a sum of
Gaussians. In this thesis, the unsupervised machine learning algorithm Gaussian Mixture
Model (GMM) is investigated along with a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) with Gaussian
emissions (section 4.3.2 and 4.3.3).
The deconvolution methodology uses deconvolution to remove the emitted waveform from
the received waveform. However, these methods will not be further investigated since the
transmitted waveform is not provided.

Peak Detection
Assuming that the two highest peaks in the waveform will correspond to the surface and
bottom echoes, a simple peak­finding technique can be used. This is implemented based
on the Python peak­finding package from scipy[45], which locates the local maxima in
the waveform. The two largest peaks can be found by specifying the minimum distance
between the peaks. In this thesis, the minimum distance is set to 100 points after inter­
polating the stacked waveforms using 1000 points.

4.3.3
Hidden Markov Model (HMM)
Another unsupervised learning method that can be used to model sequences is the Hid­
den Markov Model (HMM). The HMM has previously been used to identify different modes
in a time series, e.g., to split a time series into different flying modes for birds in the article
by Conners et al. [48]. This technique is adapted to the LiDAR raw full­waveforms.
A HMM is a type of graphical model. The ”hidden” refers to the latent states, events that
cannot be directly observed. However, in the HMM, the observable sequence of events
is used to uncover the underlying hidden events[49].
The main parameters in a HMM are [49]:
1. N states
The total number of states that a data point can be in.
2. Transition probabilities, A
A matrix of size N × N, describing the likelihood of moving from one state to
the next.
3. Emission probabilities, B
A matrix of size N×nobs describing the likelihood of a data point being generated
from a given state.
4. Initial state probabilities, π
A vector of size N describing the probability of starting in a given state.
where nobs is the length of the observed sequence. Note that each of the rows in the
matrices (A and B) should sum to a probability of 100 %, and the sum of π should be a
probability of 100 %.

When applying the Hidden Markov Model (HMM) on a LiDAR dataset, the observations
are the amplitudes of the LiDAR backscatter (the full waveforms), and the sought­after
”Hidden” states are categorized as:
1. Bottom echo
2. Water column
3. Surface peak
Therefore, the HMM is used to determine which waveform pattern is most likely when
observing an energy spectrum. The HMM depends on the initialization of A, B, and π
and can only locate the local maximum likelihood [50]. Hence, to account for the lack
of robustness in the method, the HMM is tested with different initializations, where the
likelihood of each model is compared. The final model is chosen to be the model with the
largest likelihood using Gaussian emission probabilities.
To test the initialization, the following parameters are varied:
1. Number of Gaussian states (tested between 2 and 7 Gaussians)
2. 50 different random initializations
where the random initializations are done by changing the parameter ”random_state” in
the Hidden Markov Model implementation using the hmmlearn library in Python [51]. The
number of necessary hidden Gaussian states is then found using the BIC criteria. Yet
again, the maximum number of Gaussians is set to 3.

4.4
Filtering point clouds with image segmentation
Another approach for locating the bottom echoes is using the point clouds processed by
RIEGL.
When using the point clouds, it is necessary to include information on the local morphology
of the area. In the Wadden Sea, some areas are dried out during low tide (see section
2.4). Therefore, to create a continuous bathymetric profile, it is necessary to map both
the dry and wet areas. The extraction of the bottom echoes will vary depending on the
surface type. The classification of the point cloud w.r.t. the surface type is achieved with
an image segmentation of the orthophotos. This is done with a probabilistic approach,
which is described in this section.
4.4.1
Image segmentation: Markov Random Field (MRF)
To segment the orthophotos described in section 3.2.2, the Markov Random Field (MRF)
method is used. It is a probabilistic approach where labeling the given pixel depends on
the surrounding neighborhood. The probabilistic approach infers that the more pixels in
the neighborhood with a given class, the higher the probability of the pixel being labeled
that class [52].
The MRF implementation incorporates a factor to ensure smooth transitions between the
segmentations. This is done by giving a larger probability to the configurations (label
setups) with a neighborhood with the same labels[52].
The final segmentation is chosen as the Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) solution. This is
found by maximizing the sum of a likelihood and a prior probability. Through the Markov­
Gibbs equivalence, the probability of the label configurations can be expressed as neg­
ative exponential energy functions[53]. This is advantageous since energy terms can be
added, whereas probability terms are multiplied [52]. This means that maximizing the
probability of the label configurations (the MAP solution) is the same as minimizing the
energy of the label configurations.
The likelihood energy is determined from the sum of 1­clique potentials, defined as [52]:
Plikelihood(fi) = (µ(fi) −di)2
(4.3)
where di are the intensities of the image, fi is the label of the pixel, and µ is the mean
pixel intensity of the label. Equation 4.3 shows that the likelihood potential is calculated
as the squared distance between the class’s mean and the given pixel’s intensity.
The prior energy deals with smoothing and penalizes the configurations where the neigh­
boring pixels differ in labeling. This is given as the sum of 2­clique­potentials, defined as
[52]:
Pprior(fi, f′

where each neighboring pair differing in labeling is penalized with a β value.

In the K­means algorithm, it is necessary to choose the number of clusters the dataset
should be split into. This number is determined from the Within­Cluster Sum of Squares

4.6.1
Gaussian Markov Random Field (GMRF)
Gaussian Markov Random Field (GMRF) is a probabilistic graphical model that can esti­
mate the field values in spatial or temporal dimensions. The GMRF is used in this project
to estimate a bathymetry map, where the gaps between the bottom echo point estimates
are filled out using a probabilistic approach.
The Gaussian Markov Random Field (GMRF) satisfies the following for a Gaussian Ran­
dom Field following a normal distribution (x ∼N(µ, ∑))[63]:
p(xi|{xj : j ̸= i}) = p(xi|{xj : j ∈ni})
(4.5)
where ni is the neighbours to a point (si) with the neighbouring points being close to si
(sj, j ∈ni).
The meaning of equation 4.5 is that the conditional probability of a point, xi, can be written
as conditionally dependent on the neighboring points (in ni). This, therefore, simplifies the
computational demands. Remember that this mindset is the same that was used in the
HMM and MRF implementations.
As for the MRF implementation, the MAP solution is found. In the GMRF, two likelihood
functions are considered: the field likelihood and the observation likelihood. The obser­
vation likelihood is stated in equation 4.6 [63].
z ∼N(ω, σ2
obs)
(4.6)
where σ2
obs is the variance of the observed water depths (z), and ω is the spatial field of
the bathymetry which is the field of interest. Note that the field estimates, ω, are set up in
a grid.
The likelihood of the bathymetry field, ω, is stated in equation 4.7 [63].
ω ∼N(µ, σ2
field · Q−1)
(4.7)
where Q is called the precision matrix, which is scaled with the variance of the bathymetry
field estimates, σ2

of the bathymetry field. The benefit of the Q­matrix is that it is a sparse matrix, which
describes the number of neighbors for each grid cell in the spatial field.

The Q­matrices are scaled with a correlation parameter, ϕ. Hence, the more neighbors the

and observation likelihood.

The GMRF model is expanded by including the image segmentation described in section

6
Discussion
This chapter discusses the steps of the implemented workflow (visualized in figure 4.1)
for bathymetric mapping of the Wadden Sea. Throughout this chapter, potential improve­
ments are suggested. First, the seafloor extraction methods from the raw full waveforms
are compared. Next, the bottom echo extraction from the point clouds is discussed, along
with an assessment of the quality and impact of image segmentation in the extraction.
Finally, the implemented spatial models are compared with additional recommendations
for future LiDAR surveys.
6.1
Seafloor extractions from the raw full waveforms
The first part of this thesis focused on developing alternative methods for processing the
raw full waveforms. It was evident that the Full­Waveform (FW) processing from RIEGL
outperformed the implemented alternative methods (comparing table 5.1 with table 5.2).
The potential reasons for the performance differences are discussed in this section.
6.1.1
Stacking technique
All full waveform decomposition methods were based on a stacked waveform. The idea
behind the stacking technique was to enhance the bottom echoes. However, as seen
in the examples from the low and high tide dataset in the Wadden Sea (figure 5.5a and
5.5c), this was often not the case. A reason for this issue could be found in the addi­
tion of the waveforms. The bottom echoes and surface peaks are added together in the
waveform summations. The summation can make the two reflections even more distant
since the surface echoes are proportionally greater than the bottom echoes. A potential
improvement could, therefore, be to only add the bottom echoes together.
This approach could be inspired by the article by Mader et al. [7], where they only search
for the seafloor reflections within a search corridor. The size of the search corridor could be
learned through inspection of typical characteristics for seafloor reflections in the stacked
waveform and then adapted to the individual waveform (See a visualization of the search
corridor technique in figure 6.1 from the article). This could be a way to limit the noise
affecting the seafloor extraction.
Figure 6.1: Visualization of search corridor technique from the article by Mader et al.
Source: [7]
However, it should be noted that the bottom echoes were significantly weakened in the
Wadden Sea surveys compared to the Northern Coast of Sealand survey. When zooming
in on the multi­beam recordings (in figure 5.9), it was observed that the seafloor reflections
Bathymetric Mapping of the Wadden Sea using Green LiDAR and Auxiliary Images
61
==PAGE:76==
were not aligned with a peak in the waveforms. The stacking technique cannot enhance
any bottom echoes with these conditions. Therefore, the technique is more suited for
working with waveforms where bottom echoes are near peaks, as in the Northern Coast
of Sealand dataset.
6.1.2
Smoothing of waveforms
The original waveforms in the Wadden Sea were relatively sparse, with only 120 points in
each. A spline interpolation with 1000 points was applied to address this, which greatly
smoothed the curves. This may have reduced the distinctiveness of the bottom echoes.
Although 3 criteria were set to prevent the reduction of the bottom echoes when stacking
the waveforms, the bottom echo in the Northern Coast of Sealand example (figure 5.1a)
appeared slightly attenuated in the stacked waveform. It is important to note that the
three criteria were based on the Wadden Sea dataset, not the Northern Coast of Sealand
dataset. Optimally, the criteria must be adapted to the conditions of the Northern Coast
of Sealand to avoid attenuation. In particular, the maximum spatial distance between the
waveforms in the first criterion (”Maximum spatial distance of 2 meters”) should be smaller
since the Northern Coast water area is not as flat as the Wadden Sea [33].

==PAGE:77==
Both the GMM and HMM rely on the assumption that every part of the full waveform be­
longs to a Gaussian distribution. However, when outliers infect the dataset, the simplicity
of the two methods can skew the decomposition. A possible solution is to remove the
outliers before using the unsupervised techniques. This could be done by learning the
characteristics of a typical Gaussian distributed bottom echo (shape, width, neighboring
structure, etc.) and filtering the full waveform based on the deviation from these charac­
teristics.
Furthermore, both methods assume that all three mediums (seafloor, water column, and
sea surface) are Gaussian distributed. However, as noted in the article by Richter et
al. [64], the water column is not necessarily Gaussian distributed due to the turbidity
influence. Since the signal strength is exponentially decayed in water, the Gaussian as­
sumption could lead to a bias in estimating the water column reflection. For future work, it
could be interesting to model the water column with the exponential decay function from
equation 2.1. It should, however, be noted that the water is very shallow in the surveyed
part of the Wadden Sea. Therefore, as stated in section 2.2, the water column does not
influence the waveform as much. Hence, this ”improvement” could be more important to
incorporate in areas with deeper water levels, such as near the Northern Coast of Sealand.
6.1.4
Potential of full waveform decomposition methods
In table 5.1, it was seen that the Peak Detection method was the best algorithm to de­
compose the waveforms from the Northern Coast of Sealand. Note that the surface and
bottom reflections are very prominent in these waveforms, whereas this is not the case
in the Wadden Sea, where it is challenging to detect any bottom echo peaks. Therefore,
the observation is that the Peak Detection method is sufficient for determining the bottom
echoes when working with areas with low turbidity but not in highly turbid areas. Since
no satisfactory alternatives have been developed to RIEGL’s FW­processing, it is recom­
mended to continue using the FW­processed point clouds in areas with high turbidity.
6.2
Point cloud dataset
Even though RIEGL’s FW­processing of the raw full waveforms outperformed the alter­
natives implemented in this thesis, the dataset is not without errors. A few things should
be noted concerning the dataset and the comparison to the multi­beam recordings.
The exact algorithm RIEGL uses to decompose the raw full waveforms has not been
provided. Still, presumably, RIEGL has a great deal of training data, which gives an un­
derstanding of how to decompose the waveforms. Based on the zoomed­in images in
figure 5.9, it appears that their algorithm does not only look at the peak of the seafloor
reflection but also the waveform pattern surrounding it. It is very difficult, even by visual
inspection, to understand how the bottom echo was located since the appearance of the
waveform varies. However, in the zoomed­in examples, RIEGL’s point cloud estimates
are similar to the multi­beam recordings when using the low tide survey.
It is important to understand that there is an uncertainty in the comparison between the
multi­beam recordings and point cloud estimates, as they are not necessarily perfectly
aligned (we only know they are within 30 cm of each other). This means there could be a
possible offset between the visualized multi­beam recordings and where the actual multi­
beam estimate would be placed in that given waveform (see visualization in figure 6.3).
However, the uncertainty is assumed to be small in the Wadden Sea since it is known to
be relatively flat [33].

6.2.1
Processing method
The issue in the high tide dataset was observed for both processing methods, but espe­
cially when using the Online­Waveform Processed (OWP) point clouds in figure 5.22a. In
this figure, the point cloud captured nearly no reflections from the seafloor.
When using the OWP, you need to be sure that RIEGL’s backlog has some waveforms
that were recorded according to the circumstances of your survey. Especially for a chal­
lenging area such as the Wadden Sea at high tide, the sediment in the water is excessive.
Since RIEGL’s waveform backlog is not known, there could potentially be no waveforms
matching this turbidity level. Although the Full­Waveform (FW) processing is typically
more noisy, it also contains more data than the OWP. And, as was viewed for the high
tide dataset, the seafloor could potentially be uncovered in figure 5.20b with the FW­
processing but not in figure 5.22 with the OWP.
If working with a low­turbid area such as the Northern Coast of Sealand, it could be rec­
ommended to use RIEGL’s OWP processing to save memory on board the airborne vehi­
cle. However, when working with high­turbid areas with more attenuated bottom echoes,
the OWP loses too much information. Instead, it is recommended that RIEGL’s FW­
processing be used under these conditions.

6.3
Point cloud filtering
The second part of this thesis involved extracting the bottom echoes from the FW­processed
point clouds.
Due to the high turbidity, as noted from figure 5.13a, the FW­processed point clouds were
typically infected with outliers. This thesis used DBSCAN to filter outliers with K­means
clustering and median filtering to extract the seafloor. The median filtering approach is
based on the assumption that the reflections from the seafloor will appear as one dense
layer in the point cloud. However, as previously shown in figure 6.2, a shadow effect
is noticed in the point clouds. If the shadow layer is not filtered out with DBSCAN, the
median filtering takes the median of the shadow and the top layer. Hence, there is a risk
of a slight bias in the seafloor estimation.
The shadow effect from the point clouds is also visible in the GMRF models. In the original
GMRF, some vertical lines are seen in the estimations over the banks in the channel test
area (see the outlines in figure 6.4). Due to the unnatural shape of these lines and through
comparison with the point cloud, these are classified as artificial features caused by the
shadow layer in the point cloud.
Figure 6.4: Example of shadow effects in the spatial models, seen as vertical lines in
bathymetry estimation using the GMRF (Model 1). The black dotted line shows where the
shadow effects occur in the labeled point cloud.
Noticeably, the shadow effects mainly appear over land. This could indicate that the DB­
SCAN hyperparameters are not tuned to land areas.
The DBSCAN’s performance highly depends on the choice of hyperparameters, partic­
ularly the ”Minimum samples.” In this thesis, the ”Minimum samples” were chosen as 5
points through trial and error. In comparison, in the article by Congshuang et al. [65], an
adaptive method was used to choose the ”Minimum samples” for creating a bathymetry
profile from a combination of Sentinel­2 and ICESat­2 data. Specifically, an Adaptive
Ellipse (AE) DBSCAN method is implemented in their article, where they make the ”Min­
imum samples” dependent on the point cloud’s noise level and point density. For future
work, it could be interesting to see if the AE­DBSCAN would remove the shadow effects
on land.

6.4
Point cloud labeling
Another factor that affects the bottom echo extraction is the inclusion of image segmen­
tation to label the point cloud. Naturally, it is only necessary to include point labeling in
areas with multiple surface types ­ as there typically will be in shallow coastal regions.
If investigating open water areas, e.g., figure 5.21, it is unnecessary to use the labeling
since all points are assumed to be water. Note also that open water areas typically lack
a georeferenced orthophoto (as seen in the orthophotos in figure 3.5).
Through tests, it was seen that it was necessary to modify the point cloud extraction
based on the surface types. If treating the dry areas the same as the water areas, it was
observed that the model selected the sparse part of the point clouds instead of the dense
(a visualization of this test can be seen in appendix, figure A.8). Labeling the point cloud
and automating the processing based on the surface type minimizes the need for manual
inspection.
Furthermore, an advantage of including the image segmentation is that it assigns a phys­
ical meaning to each part of the point cloud (as shown in the channel example in figure
5.13b). This can make it easier to visually verify that the extracted depths from the point
cloud correspond to the expected feature.
6.4.1
Markov Random Field (MRF)
Including image segmentation also puts high demands on the accuracy of the image seg­
mentation and the quality of the orthophotos. By visual inspection, the chosen image
segmentation method (Markov Random Field, MRF) appears to segment the images into
areas we would expect, e.g., near the channel in figure 5.12a. However, the MRF has lim­
itations in that it relies solely on the pixel intensity of the neighborhood, making it unable
to differentiate between artificial and real features. As a consequence, the shadows in the
optical images caused by processing errors or natural light (see example in figure 6.5) are
interpreted as ”real” features. Furthermore, the image segmentation fails to distinguish
the dry and wet areas on each side of the ice boundary in figure 5.12d due to overlapping
pixel intensities.

The image segmentation could be improved by including textures of each surface type
in the MRF. Often, horizontal wave textures are seen in open water areas, while organic
textures, as seen in figure 3.7c, occur on land. Inserting some pre­knowledge of these
textures could be beneficial to guide the segmentation.
An example of this was seen in the article by Abdollahi et al. [66], where road features
are extracted from orthophotos by including spectral­, geometric­, and texture information
in a Connected Component Analysis (CCA). The CCA could be advantageous in areas
with overlapping spectral characteristics and potentially eliminate the sensitivity to artificial
features in the orthophotos.
The MRF also depends on initializing mean pixel intensities for each feature type. The
initialization demands some pre­knowledge and effort from the model user, which could be
an issue if working with an area where the feature/surface types are unknown. However,
with the pre­knowledge of what to distinguish (as in this project), the initialization serves
as a way to guide the model. Even though the initialization is slightly automated with the
PCA and K­means scheme, manual inspection is still required to match the clusters to
the feature types. Note also that since the MRF is based on optical images, the pixel
intensities will vary with daylight. Therefore, ideally, the initialization should be updated
throughout the day.

6.5
Spatial modeling
The final part of this thesis involved creating spatial models of the bathymetry.
Due to the large performance differences, only the seafloor extractions from the FW­
processed point clouds were used in the spatial modeling. Table 5.2 showed that the
seafloor extractions from the point clouds agreed fairly with the multi­beam recordings,
varying within ± 5 cm. Gaussian Markov Random Field (GMRF) models were imple­
mented to fill the gaps between the seafloor extractions.
Overall, good agreement was found between the estimated bathymetry profiles using the
original GMRF, the DTMs, and the multi­beam recordings. However, some differences
were noted. This section discusses the uncertainties in the original GMRF model and the
impact of including image segmentation in the GMRF.

adaptations affected the spatial models in different ways.

Based on this test, it could be interesting to add an adaptive grid size dependent on the
surface type for future work. A minimal change in the bathymetry is expected in open­
water areas, so there is no need to adjust the grid size. However, more drastic changes
in the bathymetry could occur near coastal boundaries within a 1x1 meter grid cell, where
it could be beneficial to minimize the grid size.
The adaptive grid size could also be tuned to the point density. As stated previously, the
standard deviation highly depends on the point density. Therefore, a suggestion could
be to expand the grid sizes in areas with low density to include more information and
decrease the size in areas with higher point density. An example of an adaptive grid
size was investigated in the article by Mader et al. [7] where the grid size was changed
empirically depending on the point density and the topography characteristics. This could
potentially also be done with the Finite Element Method (FEM) [67] or another adaptive
mesh technique.
6.5.2
Using the GMRF to determine LiDAR depth limitations
One of the highlights of the original GMRF model is the provided standard deviation and
its coupling to the point density of the seafloor extractions. As stated, the higher standard
deviation was often correlated with a lower point density (for example, in figure 5.20b).
Therefore, the standard deviation could be used as a guideline for LiDAR depth limitations.
However, as indicated in the analysis of the OWP­processed point cloud (section 5.5),
only the surface reflections were recorded for the open water area, which, of course, is
not the bathymetry. However, the corresponding standard deviations were low since the
model had a dense point cloud to estimate the bathymetry. This stems from the model not
being adapted to LiDAR point clouds, which only include reflections from the sea surface.
Therefore, before using the standard deviation as a guide for LiDAR depth limitations, the
model must be adjusted to avoid expecting reflections from the seafloor.
6.5.3
Inclusion of image segmentation in the GMRF
As an expansion to the GMRF, it was investigated if including image segmentation of
the orthophotos could improve the bathymetry estimates. As expected, the three GMRF
models did not vary in areas with the same segmentation. Instead, the largest differences
between the models were found near the transitions between different surface types, for
example, near the boundary of the sandy island in the multi­beam test area (see figure
5.24).
Noticeably, the differences between the models were on the same scale as the stan­
dard deviations of the bathymetry estimates (for example, in figure 5.24). This indicates
that including the image segmentation is not significant with respect to the uncertainty
in the bathymetry estimates. However, with an adaptive grid size and improved image
segmentation, the impact of incorporating image segmentation in the GMRF could be en­
hanced. Note also that the image segmentation was already included in extracting the
bottom echoes. Therefore, if the image segmentation was not included in the point cloud
extraction, its inclusion in the spatial models might have had a larger impact.
It is important to note that the more variables the GMRF needs to estimate, the longer the
processing time. Especially Model 3 includes a lot of parameters with separate correlation
parameters for each Q matrix. At some points during the processing, it was observed that
this could result in poorer convergence of the parameters. Specifically, in areas with all 3
surface types present, it could lead to a poorer convergence if the quantity of two of the
surface types dominated the investigated area. Then, the correlation parameter, ϕ, for
the low­density surface type was typically more poorly estimated.

6.7
LiDAR surveying conditions
As has been continuously shown, the Wadden Sea is difficult to survey with Green LiDAR
due to the high turbidity. This final section discusses how some elements in the surveying
conditions affect the returned signal of the LiDAR and makes recommendations for future
LiDAR surveys in highly turbid water areas.
6.7.1
Altitude
In the Wadden Sea surveys, cf. table 3.2, the average altitude for the LiDAR flights was
144/148 meters for the low and high tide surveys. It was recommended by RIEGL 1 that
the altitude is 75 meters above mean sea level (m.s.l). Specifically for this survey, the
reason for the altitude change is the risk of the LiDAR flying too close to the water, which
could damage the instruments, and the LiDAR flying over urban areas with regulations
for the flying altitude. As noted from section 2.3.4, the point density is dependent on the
altitude of the aircraft (the range, R, in equation 2.4) and decreases with higher altitudes if
the PRR or angular speed of the laser beam is not adjusted accordingly [1]. Furthermore,
the beam divergence increases with higher altitudes, resulting in a lower SNR. In an area
with already attenuated seafloor reflections, such as the Wadden Sea, the decreased
point density and lower SNR could affect the likelihood of locating the seafloor reflections.
Furthermore, with larger footprint sizes, multiple returns can occur within the footprint.
Ideally, the altitude would be adjusted to the recommended to minimize the risk of atten­
uating the seafloor reflections even further. However, note that this would also result in a
slower surveying of the areas, which is often undesirable.
6.7.2
Time of year
The LiDAR dataset was recorded in November of 2023, which is not necessarily com­
plimentary to the LiDAR requirements. As seen in the image segmentation, some areas
were covered with ice during the surveys. As explained in the article by Kastdallen et al.
[23], white features, such as wave crashes and ice, are typically surface types that cause
higher signal losses. This was detected in the standard deviation plot with higher standard
deviations near the ice boundary ­ indicating a lower point density as seen in figure 5.18c.
Hence, the icier the water, the more uncertain the bathymetry estimates will be. Further­
more, it could also be assumed that the turbidity of the water increases in the winter period
with more powerful storms, mixing the ocean sediments even further. Therefore, for the
circumstances to be more complimentary to the LiDAR, it could be beneficial to survey
the Wadden Sea in spring instead of fall/winter.
With more tumultuous water in the fall/winter, it is assumed that more wave crashes ap­
pear, causing signal loss. Wave crashes are difficult to include in the GMRF through
image segmentation since the images are recorded at a different temporal scale than the
laser scan. Furthermore, the images have been down­sampled to a resolution of 1x1
meter; therefore, the wave crashes will not necessarily be apparent in the downsampling.
This is shown in figure 6.9 with a comparison between the image segmentation with and
without downsampling, where the wave textures to the right of the ice boundary are mini­
mized with downsampling. However, in the figure, the ice boundary is still captured with
downsampling. Therefore, the implemented image segmentation could uncover more
stationary ”white” areas, e.g., white foam near the coastal regions, that could also cause
signal loss. In this way, the image segmentation could be used as a tool to explain lower
point densities in future bathymetric surveys.

Time of day
As written in section 2.4, some tidal flats in the Wadden Sea area change morphology with

the low and high tide surveys are expected to create similar bathymetry profiles.
However, this was not observed when comparing the low and high tide surveys, tested in
section 5.4. As shown in the point clouds for the open water example 5.20a and 5.20b, the
density is much higher when extracting the seafloor from the low tide survey than the high
tide survey. This was also reflected in the standard deviation plots, where the standard
deviation was larger for the high tide GMRF.
In some tests, e.g., in the high tide GMRF in figure 5.21c, the scanning pattern was visible
in the bathymetry profile. Compared to the low tide estimate in figure 5.21a, this scan­
ning pattern could not be seen in the model. As noted from the corresponding standard
deviation in figure 5.21d, the areas with the visible scanning pattern also had the highest
standard deviation. This could indicate that with fewer points to estimate the bathymetry
profile, the model is only certain about the points right on the scan lines and cannot make
a certain estimation of the points between them.
Based on the analysis in this project, surveying the Wadden Sea during low tide is rec­
ommended for more certain bathymetry estimates. However, it should be noted that the
benefit of measuring at high tide is that you do not risk the areas having dried out with
water depths shallower than 30 cm (where the LiDAR cannot distinguish reflection lay­
ers). Therefore, high tide surveys could be a way to remove the shadow effects from the
bathymetry estimates (shown in figure 6.4).

6.8
Recommendations and future work
Throughout this discussion, some ideas for future work have been mentioned. The lists
below summarize all ideas and recommendations for the implemented workflow and future
LiDAR surveys in highly turbid water areas.
Recommendations for the workflow:
• Use RIEGL’s FW­processed point clouds when working in highly turbid areas
• Use RIEGL’s OWP­processed point clouds when working in low turbid areas or the
Peak Detection method on the raw full waveforms implemented in this thesis
• Include image segmentation of orthophotos to label the point clouds and modify the
bottom echo extractions accordingly
• Use DBSCAN and median filtering to remove outliers. This is especially critical in
areas with higher turbidity.
• Use the standard deviation of the GMRF as a guideline for the depth limitations of
LiDAR surveys
Future improvements of the workflow:
• Investigate if the stacked waveform could enhance more information about the bot­
tom echoes if looking in a search corridor around the seafloor reflections
• Test if excluding outliers in the raw full waveforms would improve the unsupervised
ML decomposition methods
• Make the ”Minimum samples” in the DBSCAN algorithm depend on the point density
and noise in the point clouds
• Include texture characteristics in the Markov Random Field (MRF) image segmen­
tations to distinguish surface types better
• Use an adaptive grid size in the GMRF that depends both on the point density and
the topography
Recommendations for surveying highly turbid water areas (focusing on the Wadden Sea):
• Survey the Wadden Sea during the spring to avoid icy areas and wave crashes
• If possible, survey the Wadden Sea at lower altitudes to increase the point density
and avoid additional attenuation of the seafloor reflections
• Survey during low tide in the Wadden Sea if possible. If not possible, be sure to use
the FW­processed point clouds during the high tide surveying instead of the OWP
• Record more overlaps between the LiDAR flights to ensure the reproducibility of the
LiDAR measurements
• Record another independent bathymetry dataset for better validation

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to develop a workflow for spatial modeling of the bathymetry in the
Wadden Sea using green LiDAR, incorporating image segmentation of orthophotos. The
implemented workflow was tested in five areas from a green LiDAR survey conducted in
November 2023 by NIRAS. The survey provided raw full waveforms, point clouds based
on RIEGL’s processing of the waveforms, and image recordings.
The developed workflow consists of two steps:
1. Extracting the bottom echoes
2. Creating a spatial model of the bathymetry
In the bottom echo extraction step, an investigation was conducted to determine if un­
supervised machine­learning techniques could improve RIEGL’s processing of the raw
full waveforms. The alternatives considered were a Peak Detection method, a Gaussian
Mixture Model (GMM), and a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) with Gaussian emissions.
However, after validating against a multi­beam recording by the Danish Coastal Author­
ity, these alternatives performed unsatisfactorily. The bottom echoes were often strongly
attenuated in the raw full waveforms due to the high turbidity in the Wadden Sea. Tests
indicated that training of the models is needed to capture the attenuated bottom echoes
in the raw full waveforms.
Instead, extracting the bottom echoes from RIEGL’s processed point clouds is recom­
mended when surveying highly turbid areas. It was found that it was necessary to modify
the bottom echo extraction to the surface type. This was done by labeling the point clouds
with an image segmentation of orthophotos. Due to the high turbidity in the Wadden Sea,
the point clouds often had sparse seafloor reflections and were infected by outliers. The
seafloor extraction was adapted to these circumstances with an outlier filtering using DB­
SCAN with a K­means clustering/median filtering step to isolate the seafloor in the point
clouds. The point cloud extraction significantly outperformed the raw full waveform ex­
tractions, yielding a 5 cm error margin to the multi­beam recording.
The Gaussian Markov Random Field (GMRF) is recommended for creating spatial models
of the bathymetry. The advantage of the implemented GMRF is that it provides a stan­
dard deviation of the estimates, which increases with sparser bottom echo extractions.
Hence, this standard deviation could be a guideline for the depth limitations of future Li­
DAR bathymetric surveys. It was tested to include the segmentation of orthophotos in the
GMRF. However, this did not prove significant with respect to the standard deviations of
the bathymetry estimates.
In conclusion, this thesis implemented a workflow for extracting bottom echoes from Li­
DAR point clouds and creating a spatial bathymetry model with a GMRF. The workflow
was adapted to the turbidity conditions of the Wadden Sea and demonstrated the advan­
tage of incorporating image segmentation of orthophotos when extracting bottom echoes
in shallow coastal regions.
For future work, it is suggested to include texture information in the image segmentation
to reduce sensitivity to artificial features in the orthophotos. Additionally, it is suggested
to use an adaptive outlier filtering dependent on point density, and to use an adaptive grid
size in the GMRF based on the point density and surface types.

A.7
Correlation lengths
The Q matrix can be used to find the correlation lengths (remembering that the Q matrix
is the inverse of the covariance matrix). This answers the question: What is the distance
between grid cells before they are no longer correlated?
The correlation matrix follows an AR(1) structure[63]. Based on the AR(1) structure, the
correlation lengths can be found using the correlation parameters, ϕ. It is assumed that
the grid cells will no longer be correlated if their internal correlation goes below 1
e [70].
In figure A.9, the correlation lengths for the horizontal and vertical neighbors (ϕ1 and ϕ2)
are seen for two tiles in the channel example using the GMRF Model 1. Note how, in both
examples, the correlation length decreases slightly over the 40­meter spatial distance.
This indicates a high correlation between the bathymetry estimates and large­scale fea­
tures.
Figure A.9: Examples of correlation lengths within grid cells from the channel example.
Note how the correlation only slightly decreases over the 40­meter spatial distance.

estimates. Note how the parameters are relatively similar for the horizontal and vertical
