Abstract
The scope of this thesis is to develop a method to verify wind (power) forecasts not only
differentiating on the time scale but also being able to distinguish between different fre-
quency intervals. It should be possible to evaluate a forecasting model for different
sites or different models for the same site.
This is achieved by using the Hilbert-Huang-Transform (HHT) with an Empirical Mode
Decomposition method (EMD). In the beginning, the signal is decomposed into its com-
ponents (modes). Those modes can e.g. represent the diurnal cycle. Several problems
arouse due to the short period a forecast is established for. Decreasing these problems
is quite difﬁcult, but after intensive testing (with both artiﬁcial and real data) the CEEM-
DAN approach seems to be the most suitable approach, though still not ﬂawlessly work-
ing. The decomposition of power forecasts is not working reliably enough due to its
linear behaviour in the region of nominal production disturbing the obtained modes
and is therefore not analysed further. In the next step the obtained trend is removed
and the Hilbert-Transform is applied to the data in order to get the Hilbert-Spectrum.
This spectrum is divided into several time and frequency intervals (bins). Afterwards,
the error between each bin of the spectra calculated for the measurements and the fore-
casts is calculated. In the end the method is applied to wind speed forecasts with a
lead time of 60 h for Risø, Østerild and Høvsøre (DK) obtained from a Weather and Re-
search Forecasting (WRF) model and the corresponding measurements from met masts.
The analysed time period stretches approximately over three months in the end of 2015.
It demonstrates, that the developed method is suitable to verify forecasts for different
sites and compare the skill of the forecasting method over longer periods while getting
a more sophisticated form of error evaluation.
As it is shown that the veriﬁcation process using HHT reveals additional information in
the error distribution, in further studies different forecast models should be tested for
their skill of predicting wind speeds at sites with unlike wind climates. Efforts should
be made to ﬁnd patterns in the error distributions over the year, time of day or for
depending on certain (meteorological) parameters. The analysis of the obtained infor-
mation might then lead to an improvement of the forecasting method itself or indicate
improvements or worsening in certain frequency intervals of the evaluated forecast
method against a deﬁned base model in general or for certain times and situations.

1 Introduction and Motivation
It is commonly agreed on, that CO2 emissions are a big driver for climate change and
have to be reduced drastically better sooner than later. With the power production be-
ing one of the big contributors of CO2 emissions it is reasonable to replace conventional
power production (high emissions) by renewable energies (very low emissions). For
example the average CO2 emissions per generated kWh in Germany were 560g/kWh
in 2014 of which 10.1% were produced by wind turbines [Eur14]. For shares of other
countries see: Figure A.1. The total GWP in CO2 emissions over the lifetime of a tur-
bine (including production, erection, operation, dismantling, etc.) is calculated to be
7 gram/kWh for a V-112 at medium wind speed sites [DGDS11] whereas an older re-
port for a V-90 stated only 4.6 gram/kWh [Ves06] which is negligible compared to the
average emissions from the energy sector. Simpliﬁed said: for each kWh of the mix
being replaced by a kWh produced by a wind turbine nearly the same amount of CO2
as the current average can be saved. In the whole EU the share of wind energy in the
power production reached 11.4 % in 2015 [Ewe16]; for the same year Denmark was
able to generate wind energy equivalent to covering 42 % of its electricity consump-
tion [NV16]. Furthermore, wind energy (onshore) becomes economically competitive
against conventional power plants [KMT+13] which together with subsidies leads to a
steady growth of wind power capacity in the EU (Figure A.2).
The penetration of high shares of wind energy (and other renewable energies) into the
existing grid system is not without problems. Wind is a ﬂuctuating resource and usu-
ally does not correlate with the demand in the system. Especially before the break
through of wind energy a certain limit of renewables in the system was assumed to
be impossible to exceed – mainly due to ﬂuctuations. Those limits have been proven
wrong now e.g. in Denmark. But ﬂuctuations in the wind power production are still
a problem – storages are not big enough (yet) so most of the power has to be used
when it is produced and conventional power plants have to be hold available and pro-
duce power immediately if wind power is not sufﬁcient to cover the demand, as de-
mand/load has to be always equal to the production.
In the ‘traditional’ system consisting solely of conventional power plants the produc-
tion can be planned ahead following the daily/weekly pattern of demand. Smaller
corrections have to be made during the day taking action against unforeseen events
(e.g. an emergency shut-down of a plant or similar).
Higher shares of renewables in the system – in this case wind energy – introduce ﬂuctu-
ations on the production side as well. This means that even while knowing the probable
demand for the next day, the planning of the production side gets more difﬁcult to pre-
dict. But planning for the day ahead is essential due to ramp-up and ramp-down rates
of conventional power plants and the way the market works. Each day at 12:00 a bid
has to be placed so that the market for the next 24h can be cleared. Intra-day prices

can reach more extreme values and even negative prices are possible mostly during
periods of high wind penetration: if the production exceeds demand or the the wind
power production has been underestimated a lot so that more power is generated than
can be consumed or traded with surrounding countries in these cases a turbine shut

This makes it also cost-inefﬁcient if planning ahead is not possible – with consequences
for nearly all stakeholders, that are e.g. conventional power producer (low market

well) and the society (indirectly paying for down-regulation).
The focus lays on decision-making problems in the power market (as the deﬁnition
of reserve requirements, market clearing, trading strategies or the coordination of re-
newables with storage systems). Nevertheless, one should keep in mind that accurate
forecasts are demanded for the planning of maintenance work on the turbines, espe-
cially offshore, as well as for non-wind power generation related problems related to
e.g. sailing, ship routeing and air trafﬁc control.
Additionally, wind farms (especially offshore) concentrate in certain regions and are
built in a limited area in which the weather system is similar, therefore intensifying the
problem of ﬂuctuations as the absolute variation is probably changing.
All in all this means that precise forecasts of wind speed are getting more and more
important for the system as the share of ﬂuctuating renewables increase. [BN11] Due to
the wind speed being inﬂuenced by so many factors it is one of the most difﬁcult me-
teorological parameters to predict. Furthermore, wind speed goes cubic in the power
production, therefore small improvements in the wind forecasts have quite an impact
in the power output predictions – the actual main goal.
Wind speed and therefore also the wind power output of the turbines ﬂuctuates with
different frequencies starting from minutes going up to days [Ped15]. To plan the power
generation especially by conventional power plants and to optimise bidding strategies
in liberalized markets, forecasts of a time span of 12 h are most valuable (due to the
Nord Pool market and the ‘bidding times’). Predictions in a range of milliseconds/sec-
onds can be used for the active control of the turbine decreasing loads and a achieving
a more steady power output, and long-term forecasts can be used to schedule mainte-
nance work, especially offshore. [HG10,WL12,GC12,GBK+11]
New approaches claiming to improve the forecasting precision are developed continu-
ously. To ﬁnd the best suited method it is necessary to evaluate the skill of new fore-
casting methods to (dis-)prove their superiority over others. Evaluating forecasts with
simple methods scores like the root mean square error (RMSE) may not reveal enough
information in order to rate a forecast properly depending on its application and does
not help to identify the insufﬁciencies of the model creating certain errors.
This is where the Hilbert-Huang-Transform comes in, it uses empirical mode decompo-
sition (EMD) to decompose a signal into its corresponding elements (modes) of different
frequencies. Different, from than other frequency analysis tools based on e.g. Fourier

Transform no stationary and linear data has to be assumed, what wind certainly does
not fulﬁll. Afterwards the Hilbert Transform is used to calculate the Hilbert spectrum,

The scope of the thesis is therefore to develop a method to verify wind, and if possible,
wind power forecasts, using the HHT. The veriﬁcation method is supposed to give a
more sophisticated insight on the error distribution depending on the time, as well as
the frequency scale. It should be possible to evaluate a forecasting model for different

itself. The results are then exemplarily shown for single forecasts at different sites, as
well as a combination of several forecasts over a longer time period.

2 Background
2.1 Wind
The origin of wind on large scales is roughly said to be found in the imbalance of solar
radiation reaching the Earth’s surface between the equator and the poles. The air is be-
ing heated up from the ground and warmer air is less dense than colder air (i.e. warm
air rises to higher altitudes). Combining this with the energy gradient and the fact that
the system tries to reach a balanced state results in a poleward transport of excess heat
from around the equator in both the ocean and the atmosphere.
So far this would result in a steady wind poleward at higher altitudes and towards the
equator at lower altitudes, but leaves out the Coriolis force responsible for the sidewise
deﬂection of the wind, causing the wind in the northern hemisphere to deﬂect to the
right. Due to the Coriolis force becoming very strong at around 30◦latitude the north-
ward motion almost stops there and the cooled air sinks down (creating a high pres-
sure zone, the subtropical high). A part of this ‘airstream’ is ﬂowing north and together
with the Coriolis force results in the dominant western wind direction in the latitudes
between 35◦and 65◦called the westerlies. This means the exemplarily analysed areas
are dominated by the westerlies as well. Other forces such as the Centrifugal force de-
termine the counter-clockwise cyclonic motion around a low pressure and clockwise
anti-cyclonic motion around a high pressure ﬁeld; at the same time it limits the maxi-
mum wind speed in a high pressure system.

The next constraint is the operating range of the turbine (see Figure 2.4). Today’s tur-
bines have a cut-in wind speed of ca. 3-4 m/s and a cut-out of up to 25 m/s - no power
will be produced at wind speeds lower or higher than these boundaries. For wind
speeds higher than cut-in but lower than nominal the turbines are run with the optimal
aero-dynamic cp resulting in less rpm and lower noise emissions than during nominal
operation. During the phase of nominal production (ca. 12-25 m/s; limits are design-
dependent) the power output is reduced by pitching the blades out of the wind. This
has mainly structural reasons as this even results in decreasing loads with increased
wind speeds, therefore being economical beneﬁcial as the structure can be designed for
smaller loads. Older turbines might still use an old technique using the stall effect, but
the result is the same. By limiting the power aerodynamically it is also possible to re-
duce the generator size to the nominal power output. In respect to the problem of the
thesis this means on the other hand, that an increase in wind speed has no inﬂuence on
the power output while the turbine is operated in the nominal power zone.

2.3 Forecasts
As mentioned in the introduction wind forecasts exist for a lot of purposes. Beginning
from citizens being worried of cycling home from work against the wind or planning a
picnic outside, people doing sports needing wind like sailing, kitesurﬁng or windsurf-
ing, up to companies trying to plan construction or maintenance work for which high
wind speeds can imply too high safety risks and therefore can not be performed, they
all demand wind forecasts of different length, accuracy and focus points.
However, wind forecasts are probably most important for stakeholders involved in the
operation of wind turbines. Direct marketing companies are hugely relying on good
forecasts as their strategy of bidding power production in the market is based on them.
With increasing wind power production the integration of it in the power system rises
to a challenge – the ﬂuctuations and uncertainties in the production are added to the
uncertainties in the production of conventional power and demand side uncertainties.
Societal seen a higher uncertainty is a reduction in social welfare. One might say a
reduction in uncertainties of wind power forecasts are a win for almost everyone.
2.3.1 Diﬀerent Kinds of Forecasts
There exist several different kinds of predicting the weather and wind in the future.
Probably based on long time observations some of the most simple kind of predictions
– weather proverbs – can actually be explained by meteorological phenomena. An ex-
ample is the seven sleepers day (Siebenschl¨afertag) with the pendant of for example
Swithun’s day in Great Britain in the beginning of July. The proverb claims that the
weather will stay the same for the following seven weeks. Assuming to take not only

the speciﬁc day (8.July) but the period between end of June and beginning of July into
account and adding tolerance to the predicted period especially towards shorter peri-
ods, the trend towards a cool and wet or a warm and dry summer is met in approx-
imately 80 % of the cases but regionally restricted to the area in south Germany. For
northern Germany the hit rate is only in the range of 55 % to 60 %.
The weather system tends to be quite constant during this time and the position of the
jet stream determines if the weather will be mostly warm and dry (jet stream north of
Germany) with a high pressure ﬁeld above Germany or colder and wet (jet stream south
of Germany or directly above) with low pressure ﬁelds above Scandinavia and/or Great
Britain. [KLF13,Kra15]
Luckily today no one has to rely on weather proverbs any more, forecasts for different
parameters are easily – free or commercially – available (and interpretable) for every-
one and the forecasts got way more precise while increasing the hit rate.
But today’s forecasts also have a down side, the compilation and calculation of weather
models (Numerical Weather Predictions, NWP) is highly complex and therefore compu-
tationally intensive. Figure 2.7 shows the complexity of the coupled physical processes
that need to be taken into account and accurately simulated in order to reproduce sur-
face measurements of atmospheric variables. This also implies, that the initial state has
to be known as close as possible before starting the simulation. In the following some
approaches are described. At this point it has to be remembered, that for this thesis the
wind forecast is the important parameter. Other parameters obtained at the same time
will be treated like a by-product but might still lead to improvements if a correlation
can be detected and the model accordingly adjusted. For example, a strong dependency
between the wind direction and the ﬂuctuations in 10-min power observations was ob-
served for the Horns Rev wind farm. [Akh07]
The type of output distinguishes different categories of forecasts. They can be divided
into point/deterministic forecasts, and in the more advanced/detailed probabilistic
forecasts.
Probabilistic forecasts can be in the form of quantiles, intervals and pre-
dictive distributions, as well as in trajectories/scenarios also called ensembles. [Pin15,
MCM+14] Furthermore, forecasts can be set to ﬁnd pre-deﬁned events such as ramps,
[GCCTLG15] high-variability periods, or maximum wind speeds [SPG15] – therefore
being called event-based forecasts. [MCM+14]
Different types of forecasts have to be evaluated in different ways, e.g. ensemble fore-
casts have to be treated in another way than point forecasts. [BHTP15,PG12] Point fore-
casting is the most classic approach to wind (power) forecasting, as it is only providing
single-valued estimates of the expected future wind speed or power output. But such
predictions limit the use of tools for decision-making under uncertainty. [MCM+14]
Further details about scenarios, event based forecasts, etc. and their evaluation can
be found in [GBK+11]. The thesis is based on point forecast data, hence a closer de-
scription of the more detailed output approaches seems not constructive. It might be
interesting though for further studies to adapt and compare the obtained method of
veriﬁcation to some of the probabilistic ones.

2.3.3 Origin of Forecast Uncertainty/Error
With each forecast a certain uncertainty comes along. That means there is a probability
that the forecasted value or range is going to happen or not. This uncertainty is not
necessarily the same all the time. In case of a point forecast this speciﬁc uncertainty is
not represented in the results and therefore can not be taken into account in the evalua-
tion even though it might be essential to make decisions. As the thesis deals with point
forecasts uncertainty does not play a big role directly, on the other hand the sources
of uncertainties are also contributing to the errors why it is important to identify to
possibly lower or handle them. Potential sources of uncertainties and errors are:
• mis- or imprecise understanding of physical dependencies
• inaccurate description of physical laws implemented in the model
• measurements, especially those used for the initial state
• imprecise formulation of the initial state and other model settings
• numerical aspects such as rounding mistakes or ﬁltering

2.4 Hilbert Huang Transformation
As the overall goal is to rate a forecast in comparing spectral bands of both the forecast
and the obtained data the ﬁrst step is to decompose the signal in the corresponding
frequencies. Thus the composition of signals of different frequency and size scales of
the data can be obtained. Though often used frequency analysis tools based e.g. on
the well-known Fourier Transformation (FT) are reconstructing the signal with a sum
of sinusoidal functions. This can be an advantage but require to assume the data to be
stationary and linear. But as Barnhart put it:
”Imagine standing in a grass ﬁeld for an hour feeling the intermittent puffs
of wind on your face, and it becomes clear that nature is not stationary.”
[Bar11]
Lots of data is from natural origin (unmistakable wind as well) and new methods were
and are developed. In this context the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) should be
named, adopting the traditional Fourier Transform for non-stationary signals in ﬁrst
breaking the signal into all stationary (overlapping) sections before applying it. Even
though it is now possible to examine non-stationary data a problem evolves from the
splitting of the signal in smaller pieces. The shorter the time series the less information
can be obtained for low frequencies (keyword: Nyquist) while longer time series aver-
age high frequencies more. [BMKN15]
Various other methods were designed for either non-linear but stationary or linear but
non-stationary data; for at the same time non-stationary and non-linear data no avail-
able methods could be found. [Hua05]
Not having the problem of the frequency-time resolution trade-off, the Wavelet Trans-
form ”family” (using speciﬁc mother wavelets instead of sinusoidal functions) found
its way into a lot of applications formerly using FT.
This is where the Hilbert-Huang transform (HHT) comes in, which is able to extract the
frequency components from a possibly both non-linear and non-stationary signal (wind
data). Instead of the globally deﬁned set of harmonics ﬁtted to the whole time series as
used in the FT, HHT has a local basis. And other than with the wavelet transform no
a priori decision about a suitable (wavelet) shape is required. On the other hand HHT
requires 4 ∆t to resolve variability instead of 2 ∆t with a FT. [VGPM10] A comparison
from Huang and Wu between the analysis methods of Fourier, Wavelet and HHT can
be seen in Table A.1. Nevertheless, besides this disadvantage this method has other
drawbacks and weaknesses (depending on the application) as well, of which those be-
ing relevant for the the application at hand will be described and possible solutions or
improvements suggested (subsection 4.1.1). But ﬁrst a description of the original/base
HHT concept is given.
The HHT consists of two parts, Empirical Mode Decomposition (EMD) decomposing
the signal and the Hilbert spectral analysis to obtain the instantaneous frequencies and
amplitudes to describe the signal more locally.

3.3 Forecast Data
The forecast data is obtained from Weather Research and Forecasting model (WRF), a
NWP model (subsection 2.3.1) output ﬁles (NetCDF). The WRF model output provides
forecasts not for a single site but for an area, the covered area is marked in Figure 3.1.
The grid of the available data consists of 201 nodes in the west-east dimension and 177
nodes in the south-north dimension. The distance between the grid nodes is 2 km and
is calculated for 13 different heights (layers). The map is projected to the polar stereo-
graphic coordinate system. Wind speed in the u, v and w component is not the only
calculated parameter, parameters such as (potential) temperature, pressure, precipita-
tion, indicators of the soil conditions, humidity and so on are included in the output
NetCDF ﬁle for almost every node in the three dimensional grid as well as not chang-
ing ones like e.g. the terrain height, the indicator for land or water and the land use
index.
Therefore, the ﬁrst step is to extract the most relevant data, which in this case are the
horizontal wind speeds (u, v) from the position(s) of the measured data (i.e. latitude,
longitude and height); the vertical component of the wind speed is neglected as it is
not linearly included in the measurements of a cup anemometer [Ped15] used in most
of the measurements and the vertical component is small compared to the horizontal
wind movements.

4.1 HHT implemented
4.1.1 Problems and Possible Solutions of EMD
Even though it is used or at least its usage is being analysed and NASA provides a
commercial product based on HHT [NAS], the method is not working ﬂawlessly in all
cases. Difﬁculties are connected to the way the EMD is implemented and the data be-
ing analysed with it. Therefore solutions or workarounds depend on the application as
well and can be useful in one case while worsening the situation in others.
Sample Resolution
The ﬁrst problem evolves directly from the ﬁrst step in the EMD process – the ﬁnding of
the envelopes. If the extrema fall in between sampling instants they will not be located
in a correct way. This results in the envelopes being generated for wrong positions. Er-
rors in the maxima are not the same as the errors in the minima and are not cancelling
each other out. This factor has a higher signiﬁcance for higher frequencies as the toler-
ance for not directly ﬁnding the extrema decreases.
The former used chirp signal (Equation 2.7) can be used to visualise this effect tellingly.
In Figure 4.1 the chirp signal with a sampling rate of 1 is plotted with its envelopes as
well as the mean of them. Due to the relatively small sampling rate the resolution at
higher frequencies is not sufﬁcient enough to place the extrema at the expected places.
The expected envelopes would be a horizontal line at -1 and 1 respectively. But with
the mean of the envelopes not being zero all the time artefacts are introduced to the
next step of EMD with the subtraction of it from the original signal. The only solution
to solve or at least minimize this problem is to use a higher sampling rate or to try to
up-sample the data [DS04, RFGL03]. As the latter involves some kind of interpolation
adding synthetic data, the outcome has to be questioned as well, especially as one get
more pleased with the more conﬁdent looking obtained results.
Using the same chirp signal again but this time with a twice as high sampling rate the
errors are getting signiﬁcantly lower at the same frequencies (Figure 4.2).
In Figure 4.3 the same example is used with a sample rate of 10, resulting in even
smoother envelopes. Deviations from the optimal envelopes can not be spotted with

‘real’ extrema otherwise lost due to a too low sampling rate. Each extrema is calculated
with three points, the one that seems to be the extrema and the closest neighbour to the
right and left respectively. Having located the extrema at a more precise position en-
tails it often being at a non-integer time-step though. The method seems promising to

help decomposing an examined signal with the aim to understand physical processes
or similar. However, even though the interpolation seems legit/logical without inter-

End Eﬀects
For the short time-series at hand the biggest concerns are connected to the end-effects.
During the EMD process the spline has to be determined as described in section 2.4.
However, at the ends of it the ﬁrst and second derivatives required for the spline ﬁt-
ting are unavailable which can cause the spline to ﬂuctuate wildly. This problem is
described e.g. in [QHL10, HW09] Carrying out tests on artiﬁcial data it could be seen,
that the deviations between the ‘real’ EMDs and the EMDs obtained from an short ex-
cerpt of the data are worst in the ends and stretch a bit (no speciﬁc length detected) into
the data. The magnitude of deviations are depending on the phase the data starts with,
i.e. a shift by one time step can cause a different effect on the envelopes.
Sometimes though, it could also result in mode mixing (one IMF more or less). A de-
tailed description of mode mixing is given in section 4.1.1. Depending on the artiﬁcial
phase shift the ﬂuctuation of the spline in the ends is higher or lower, with the cor-
respondent impact on the EMD. Though, for this to happen the time-series has to be
relatively short.
Due to the long time-series of several days with a resolution of 10-minute means the end
effects are neglected e.g. in [VGPM10]. With a resolution of 1h and a forecast length of
48 - 60 h the available data is signiﬁcantly shorter and the end effects can cause severe
difﬁculties.
Though not directly applicable to short-term forecast evaluation for comparison rea-
sons and to reveal the potential of the used method higher resolution wind data of a
longer time period was analysed and the results seem to be not effected by the end ef-
fects. For most applications another method being described in section 4.1.2 should be
preferred.
Different approaches exist to reduce the end effects. They differ in the way of provid-
ing additional points beyond the ends for the spline calculation. Probably the simplest
one is to mirror the data at the ends suggested e.g. by Barnhart [Bar11]. But there are
different ways of mirroring the data: one has to set the number of points mirrored and
decide if the ﬁrst/last point is repeated. Another option is to repeat/replicate only the
last minima and maxima. Another attempt is to deﬁne the ﬁrst/last point as minima
and maxima at the same time, with the disadvantage of forcing all IMFs to start and
end with zero.
Qingjie et al. are introducing additionally another method to mitigate the end effects to
some degree in extending the signal by mirroring it in high frequencies and using least
square polynominal extending in low frequencies. But sometimes the tested results are
worse than with the traditional EMD and as most others this seems to be only working
35 of 117
==PAGE:54==

with long time series. For example, they present the effect of their method with a signal
consisting of more than 5.000 data points. [QHL10]
Instead of mirroring parts of the signal Rato et al. [ROB08] suggest to only extrapolate
the ﬁrst/last minimum and maximum: After ﬁnding the min and the max (m1, M1) and
their locations (t1, T1, with T1 > t1), a new maximum M0 is added in front of the old
one located at -t1 while the new minimum m0 is added at -T. Another study [DS04]
recommends a similar method but with the new M0 added ∆tmax before M1, ∆tmax is
deﬁned as the distance between the ﬁrst and second maximum. The same method is
used to add a point for the lower spline and to extend both the splines at the end as
well (see Figure A.10). Taking slopes into account a more sophisticated method (see
Figure A.11) is introduced as well, but as the time of optimising the method is limited
and not the main goal in this thesis it is not analysed further. It might be interesting
though for further studies.
Testing these attempts on a part of randomly chosen data from the Risø measurements
with 1 h time steps and with different artiﬁcial signals the results are mixed. Repeating
the same method on the same data several times might give a meaningfully appearing
result but when moving the test sample to achieve a phase shift the results could differ
a lot, depending on the trend close to the ends.
As the overall aim is to compare the signals of forecasted and measured data, this prob-
lem adds to the complexity. During the process it is quite certain, that one of the sig-
nals is prone for this error while the other is not (or it is not corrupting the result so
much), causing problems with different amounts of IMFs. An evaluation on the IMFs
alone therefore does not seem to be a good idea if the mode mixing problem can not be
solved. Comparing different amounts of IMFs with each other is simply impossible.
In case of analysing only a part of a time-series it is recommended to apply the method
on the whole or a at least on a longer part of the data and cut later. However, merg-
ing different data ‘sets’ such as daily forecasts to create longer series, is probably (in
the long run) creating a higher disadvantage due to the prevalent jumps at the joining
points.
Spline crosses data
In the process of ﬁtting the quadratic spline through the extrema it occurs, that one
of the splines crosses the data and consequentially the mean of the envelopes is not
correctly detected, resulting in a wrong subtraction of it from the original signal. An
example can be seen in Figure 4.4. Note, that this section was picked for showing as
many problems as possible on average there are not so many crossings.
This problem can be addressed by an iteratively subtraction of the mean, i.e. the mean
of the so calculated upper and lower envelope is multiplied with a step size factor α
(1 < α < 1) before removing it from the signal, thus increasing the amount of steps
needed to get an IMF but reducing the chance of creating artefacts during the process.
The factor α can also be deﬁned to be equal to the correlation coefﬁcient between the
signal and the mean of the envelopes. [ROB08]

The EMD in Matlab works only with continuous data sets, i.e. that data points deﬁned

mirroring technique (see Figure A.12).
For the analysis carried out in this thesis the described method was not used though.

Mode Mixing
Mode Mixing is deﬁned as a ‘single IMF either consisting of signals of widely dis-
parate scales, or a similar scale residing in different IMF components’ [HW09]. The
EMD method can only extract IMFs differing by more than a factor of two in frequency.
[Bar11] Applied to real world data this can lead to IMF loosing it’s physical meaning,
e.g. at some times one IMF can have data of one cycle while representing another cycle
period at other times. If some modes are mixed it is also possible that the amount of
detected modes differ as one mode that should represent a cycle on its own is blended
completely into another one (or more) modes.
The mode mixing problem is a severe problem in respect of the thesis as it prohibits
the direct comparison between IMFs obtained form the forecasts and IMFs obtained
from the measured data. There are three problems related to this application. The
meaning of decomposing the data is to be able to check the different occurring cycles
37 of 117
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 4. METHOD
(each of them represented in a mode) of the forecasts against (hopefully) the same cy-
cles in the measurements, but if there is a different number of IMfs it is not clear which
IMF should be compared with which. Even though the number of IMfs being the same
with mode mixing occurring, the process of evaluation due to comparing the IMFs is
not very meaningful as one would rate the similarity of two cycles. This gives almost
necessarily a worse rating than it should. Furthermore, mode mixing is not an effect ap-
pearing all the time and would introduce an uncertainty and an unequally distributed,
nearly random bias in the data. Then the resulting ratings for different forecasts are
not based on consistent conditions and are not reasonably comparable. Similar con-
sequences arise if the intention is to verify the forecast for one single effect, e.g. the
accuracy of representing the diurnal cycle but the corresponding IMF is a mixture of
additional signals.
A lot of methods exist claiming to improve the mode decomposition and prevent the
modes from mixing or at least reduce the effect of it. Some of them, which have been
tested in this context are being explained in the following.
4.1.2 Mode Mixing - any possibility to get rid of it?
EMD - EEMD - CEEMDAN - improved CEEMDAN
As mentioned in the above section the method has room for improvement at least when
using it with the purpose of evaluation, for which accurate and constant results are
(kind of) fundamental but cannot be obtained with the original EMD method. This
might not be that essential for the decomposition of longer time series to spot different
factors inﬂuencing the data at hand but is increasing the accuracy of the process and
might lead to new ﬁndings.
EEMD
A ‘substantial improvement’ [HW09] over the original EMD method was de-
veloped in 2009 called Ensemble Empirical Mode Decomposition (EEMD).
It tries to take advantage of white noise added to the same signal over and over again
is being cancelled out again after taken the mean of the ensemble. Taking advantage of
this properties in a ﬁrst step white noise with an pre-deﬁned ﬁnite amplitude is added
to the data. The amplitude of the noise has to be ﬁnite so that the ensemble will cover
‘all’ possible possibilities in this range. At the same time it should assure the correspond-
ing signals to stay in the same IMF. Otherwise it will lead to mode mixing again. The
traditional EMD method is then used to decompose the signal into the IMFs. These two
steps are repeated a certain amount of times, each time with different randomly created
white noise. In the end the mean of all versions of each IMF is calculated. The effects of
the added white noise is supposed to cancel each other out if a sufﬁcient number of tri-
als is used. The only part that is not being averaged out is supposed to be the physical
meaningful signal. This procedure resembles physical testing of the same experiment
over and over again.
Intensive testing of the new method with a Matlab code from Huang and others [HO09]
both with real and artiﬁcially created data was performed to determine the usage of it
for the available data. Furthermore, the inﬂuence of the two parameters, the number of

CEEMDAN
Artiﬁcially created data reveals though that the EEMD still suffers from
mode mixing. Even though the ‘real world’ signals appear to be pretty certain it seems

it will have an effect on all the modes. In Figure 4.13 the decomposed signal of mea-
sured data in Risø at 94 m height in April is plotted. The data set includes one invalid
value that is set to 55 m/s in the beginning for better visibility and is not removed. The
impact on the decomposition of the signal (here: CEEMDAN, but would be the same
or similar for the others). Even though the biggest part of the peak’s amplitude is rep-
resented in the ﬁrst mode the steep slope combined with the cubic spline ﬁtting during
the process induces more oscillations on both sides of the outlier. The impact reminds
of the propagation of ‘ripples on a pond’ going from lower to higher modes.
At the same time the variations in the ‘original’ wind speed are – in relation to the peak
the outlier causes – too small to be visually present in the modes. It is self-evident that
a signal being decomposed like this is not usable to be compared later on against the
processed signal of a forecast and can corrupt the whole evaluation. This emphasises
again the importance of visual inspection of the processed data. Especially when work-
ing with data from several sources this is a necessity as invalid data is not marked in
the same way: two of the often used indicators are 99.99 or -999. Sometimes NaN is
used as well, but the method is not able to handle NaNs, a conversion is therefore nec-
essary. As said before forecasts are not taken into account during the evaluation process
when more than one value is missing or invalid (normally in the measured data). In the
example at hand only one point is invalid and would be therefore substituted with a
value between its predecessor and successor during the process what seems to be justi-

invalid data point is not marked as invalid but is just not listed. Those gaps have to be
taken care of, as otherwise the measurements are not connected to their corresponding
times. Here gaps are ﬁlled with invalid values in order to achieve easy to plot samples
equidistant over time.

4.2.1 New Method using CEEMDAN
All the evaluations methods shown so far are based in one or the other way on the dif-
ference between the forecasted and the measured value per time step e = ¯x −¯xp. The
idea of evaluating a forecast by ﬁrst decomposing the signal into its modes is to get ac-
cess to a more differentiated conclusion. As there are different ways to get to a forecast
some physical processes might be better represented in one provided forecast than in
another and vice versa. The aforementioned scores cannot show the needed details as
they always take the ‘end result’ – the sum of all processes – into account. As said be-
fore users of forecasts focus on different parts of a forecast, some are more interested in
fast ﬂuctuations of less than an hour (not possible with the available data) while some
are more interested in the hourly change in power production and others are only in-
terested in the general trend. This can be interpreted to some extend as an interest in
different frequencies being correctly represented in the data. Furthermore, a more de-
tailed evaluation could reveal that e.g. one mode is off in phase or amplitude. The next
step would be to identify the physical process the mode is representing and hopefully
being able to adjust the forecast model’s inaccuracy for this factor/process. Analysing
the speciﬁc error for different situations e.g. stable/unstable conditions, sunny days,
or west winds might reveal more in depth understanding of the error occurrence and
therefore help to reduce it.
A method using the improved CEEMDAN (explained in section 4.1.2) and HHT (sec-
tion 2.4) to gain some supplementary information in an evaluation process is described
in the following.
First Option
The ﬁrst attempt of evaluating each forecast is probably the most obvious
one: comparing each of the obtained modes from the forecast with the corresponding
ones from the measurements. In theory it seems straightforward but of course in prac-
tice two main problems turn up.
The ﬁrst and most severe is a consequence of strong mode-mixing – different amounts
of modes. Most of the times (during testing) the number of modes differed only by one
but seldom also by two. This entails a big problem of assigning the correct modes with
each other. Even if it is possible to ﬁnd the corresponding ones for the remaining ones
this implies that on one side there is one mode containing two while on the other side
there are two without a counterpart. An idea is to sum the two correctly decomposed
modes in order to get a corresponding counterpart for the evaluation. The new prob-
lem is then to ﬁnd the two ﬁtting modes. There might be a chance of ﬁnding them in
comparing the sum of two different modes with one mode on the other side and choos-
ing the one coming closest. But even when in some way it is possible to ﬁnd the two
modes that added up resemble a (probably mixed mode) of the other part, the problem
is still not being solved yet. As can be deduced from the deﬁnition of mode mixing
(section 4.1.1) the mode is not necessarily mixed over the full length of the period with
55 of 117
==PAGE:74==
CHAPTER 4. METHOD
another one, actually it is unlikely to happen in this way. The (vanished) mode is prob-
ably blended into several ones instead. If so, the whole comparison of modes does not
make sense any more – one is not comparing like with like. The part being mixed into
one mode results in an error there and additionally the missing part is creating an error
in its original mode. Still, the forecast and the measurements could be identical, only
decomposed in another way – completely distorting the evaluation and therefore not
suitable.
The second one is a direct result of the end-effects (section 4.1.1). With the ends of the
modes potentially going ‘wild’, i.e. taking unrealistic very high or low values, the error
between the compared modes is possibly high as well. The share of the ﬁrst/last few
values effected the most by the end effects differs a lot though and can exceed the error
of the remaining points easily. Especially the unpredictable magnitude of it makes an
evaluation including those points impossible. The solution here can be to exclude a
certain amount of data at the ends for the calculation of the error. Reducing the period
for the evaluation process is for sure the better option over getting a possibly wrong
evaluation in the ﬁrst place. But one still has to be cautious as the end effects are not
completely prohibited then. As described before the effects stretch further into the mid-
dle of the data and only avoiding them from the beginning really helps.
It is important here to mention that the EMD method used for this approach was still
the EEMD but due to its insufﬁcient result the presented method was no longer pur-
sued, also when changing to the CEEMDAN implementation of the EMD. This is due
to the fact that even though mode mixing appears less often with the improved CEEM-
DAN it still does from time to time.
With a further improved mode decomposition or an algorithm reliably sorting out the
cases of mode mixing it can be a good idea to test this approach again.
But the problem of a different amount of modes can occur for correctly decomposed
signals as well: there are simply less modes present in one or the other data set. This
can be e.g. due to periods of pretty steady wind speeds. The short time period of the
forecasts combined with a low sample rate probably eases the emergence of it though.
Second Option
A different amount of modes is no problem for an evaluation method
using the instantaneous frequency as the modes are combined into one ‘signal’. But the
ends can show a similar behaviour as shown for the modes suffering from end effects
if the ﬁrst/last minimum or maximum is far from the corresponding end. Furthermore,
and most importantly the calculation of it is very sensitive to changes in the mode de-
composition. Decomposing the same signal with EEMD and subsequently calculating
the inst. freq. mostly results in a different inst. freq. over time (sometimes even chang-
ing the trend) of it. It is therefore unreliable for the purpose of evaluation and is not
followed further. With the recently implemented CEEMDAN the variations are less but
still undeniable. With a further optimised decomposition method or a more stable way
of calculating the inst. freq. this approach is still very interesting. Different modes can
be combined to get an inst. freq. for certain frequency bands, e.g. leaving out fast ﬂuc-
tuations. In this way it might be possible to detect ramps as well (indicated as a steep
and rather long slope).

Even though a loss of information comes along with taking an
average over a certain area it seems reasonable to bin the spectrum in the time and the
frequency scale. It reduces the information to one averaged value per bin indicating
how much ‘activity’ can be found in it. The bins do not have to be equidistant and
the number can be chosen freely in both directions as well. The difference between the
so calculated averages for each bin between the measurement and forecast data (Fig-
ure 4.18a and 4.18b respectively) is calculated and can be plotted again as a heat map
(Figure 4.18c). In this example the spectra are divided into 6 frequency and 7 time bins;
the 6th frequency bin covers half the frequency range plotted (0.25 to 0.5 1/h) while the
remaining ﬁve bins of equal width cover the frequencies from 0 to 0.25 1/h. The borders
of the time scale binning are set to 0, 3, 7, 12, 18, 24, 36, and 48. The error for each anal-
ysed forecast and each bin can than be plotted to possibly ﬁnd some forecast dependant
trends. For the evaluation of a forecast method over a certain period the average per
bin can be taken indicating the average error per time and frequency bin. If a single-
valued rating is the wished outcome one option is to calculate the average of all bins
or the bins of interest. But the information for this case is massively reduced again and
will depend on the deﬁnition of the bin widths.
It is decided to use this method of a binned average with Gaussian smoothed spectra
for the following calculations as the sharp limit of the bins can otherwise create un-
wanted problems. In some cases the testing of a reasonable setting for the bin limits
revealed values of the measurement and forecast data being pretty close in frequency
for the same time period but with the bin boundary crossing directly in between. Those
cases induce a high error for both involved bins even though the ‘real’ error is not that
high. In using the Gaussian smoothing for the same case, shares of the high values
‘cross’ the border reducing the error on both sides. The method can be justiﬁed as the
goal is to ﬁnd the error of the forecast or an approximate time-frequency range and not
the error in a certain sharply deﬁned bin, especially as there are still uncertainties about

5.2 Comparison of Several Forecasts
So far, forecasts are compared side by side, which gives access to further information
about the error distribution over time and frequency. But in order to evaluate several
forecasting methods for one or more sites over longer periods, it is not really an option
to analyse each forecast with a lead time of 60 (or less hours) – the effort would be too
high. The rating would also be more subjective. Still, a side by side comparison (espe-
cially for special events) helps to understand the approach and can locate the source
of bad or good ratings. For the following analysis of several forecasts data from Risø
at a height of 94 m for the time period between 9.10.2015 and 2.01.2016 (approximately
3 months) are used as input. This relates to 81 valid forecasts starting at 12:00. In Fig-
ure A.30 the obtained spectra for each forecast are stringed together, the corresponding
wind data is plotted directly below.
One approach is to keep the binning over time and frequency and collect the error val-
ues for each bin. Each bin of the spectrum is represented with a plot containing all the
errors for the bin for each forecast. The positions of the plots are arranged in the same
way as the spectrum plots in one ﬁgure. This means that the bottommost plot to the
right presents the error values for the lowest frequency bin for the last time bin. The
x-axes of each plot show the forecast number while the y-axes are deﬁning the error
value. The scale for the error is only shown for the ﬁrst time bin but is the same for all
plots of one frequency range. In Figure 5.8 the obtained data for the 81 valid forecasts
for Risø is presented in the explained way. Due to the same scales per frequency bin
the spread and the occurrence of bigger errors can be detected at a glance. Using even
longer periods as input the plot can reveal patterns for certain bins. On the other hand
the clarity of the ﬁgure suffers a lot when either using more frequency/time bins (plots
get smaller) or analyse more forecasts (squeezing the x-axes or generating s wider ﬁg-
ure due to wider plots).
Combining all values in each plot of Figure 5.8 by calculating the mean one gets a single
value per bin. These values can be plotted as a heatmap with the same dimensions for
each bin as in the spectrum plots again. In this way it is easy to identify error ‘hotspots’,
i.e. frequency and lead-time intervals with an high corresponding error. The result can
be seen in Figure 5.9.
In order to create a more simple to understand evaluation of the forecasts without loos-
ing the information regarding different frequency intervals/bins per forecast, an aver-
age per frequency bin is calculated for each forecast over the lead time. This means that
in our case of six frequency bins six values are obtained indicating the corresponding
averaged error. In plotting the errors for one frequency bin for all forecasts one can get
a trend that should be further analysed afterwards. Figure 5.10 shows the calculated
results for the six frequency bins used in the example at hand.

6 Discussion
Mode Decomposition and Error Calculation
As exemplarily shown in chapter 5 the
developed method using the HHT-spectra obtained with the CEEMDAN method gen-
erates more information on the error distribution over the time and frequency scale. But
the large amount of information has to be condensed in a way to be easier to handle.
With the presented methods the information is reduced but can still have a scientiﬁc
value over the results available with traditional methods.
Nevertheless, these methods do not exploit all the information that can be gained from
the mode decomposition and the built spectra, e.g. so far neither variability nor stan-
dard deviation of the modes or bins of the spectra have been examined.
In this thesis the method was solely applied to wind forecasts and their corresponding
measurements, but it has become clear that the method is also usable to verify the pre-
diction of other parameters such as e.g. wind direction, temperature and pressure. In
case of wind direction one has to edit the values in order to avoid a jump from 0◦to
360◦every time the direction changes between East and West over North – results ob-
tained from the raw direction data is completely meaningless.
The ability to verify forecasts depending on the frequency entails the biggest weakness
of the method. With the need of equidistant time steps without data gaps the evalua-
tion of errors for speciﬁc conditions such as a deﬁned direction sector or temperatures
is much more complicated than for traditional scores. With this method both whole
data sets have to be processed ﬁrst to be able to decompose it correctly. The correspond-
ing errors for the predeﬁned conditions can only be selected afterwards. Additionally
a partial inspection has to be seen critically, as time-steps before and after the time step
of interest, inﬂuence each other during the decomposition. If used for this purpose (at
all) Gaussian smoothing should not be used. A promising approach might evolve from
trying to cluster the results (maybe mode wise) to gain more information about the er-
ror dependencies.
Based on frequency, it is difﬁcult to deﬁne a base to evaluate other forecasts against. The
new method is not able to evaluate the improvements over the common base model of
persistence or climatology prediction but only between methods being more advanced,
meaning in this case non-linear (section 4.2).
The problem of the method in general is its application on short-time forecasts mostly
delivered in an hourly resolution combined with the related insufﬁciencies of decom-
posing the modes (section 4.1.1). The CEEMDAN method showed a big improvement
77

but is still not working ﬂawlessly in this situation. This adds some uncertainties to the
obtained result of the comparison process. As the EMD or rather its improved imple-
mentations are used for different applications (subsection 2.4.2) further improvements
of the quite new method can be expected, as can be seen also by numerous papers
suggesting improvements for different steps of the EMD and trying to implement it
in different ways the possibilities of the method are not completely revealed yet. In
respect to the inaccuracies found in the EMD method during the process of ﬁnding a
suitable mode decomposition it attracted attention that in some reports the ‘original’
EMD or EEMD were used to decompose a signal (subsection 2.4.2), the question comes
up if using an improved mode decomposition would give different results. The better
the decomposition works the more validity can be related to the results. Furthermore,
it can open up to the approaches that were shown to be prone for errors when applied
to inaccurate mode decompositions section 4.2.
But there is a large amount of new methods and approaches to optimise wind (power)
forecasts being developed. One of the most recent ones is decomposing the wind speed
data with wavelet decomposition into selected frequency components before applying
the forecasting technique. They claim a dramatically improvement of accuracy and
longevity of prediction. The model is supposed to be capable of prediction lead times of
one week with an RMSE below 3 m/s [KAK16]. With the lead time not being restricted
to short periods but extended to up to a week the new evaluation method should not
suffer much from problems like the end-effects and give helpful additional information
to the single valued RMSE.
The inability so far to work directly with data containing gaps and it being prone to out-
liers demands high quality data (with high resolution). Another constraint for broader
use might be the complexity of the process and its computational intensiveness for long
time series. As more information is gained, the handling is more complex as well.
On the other hand the approach of Barnhart seems to be promising to be able to de-
compose data containing gaps and still keeping the trend for each mode (Figure A.12).
Another way might be to combine measurements from a wind park rather than us-
ing a single measurement (met mast or turbine), the probability that all measurement
systems will fail at the same time is pretty low. As a bigger area will be covered the
measurements should match the prediction settings of the forecasts as well. [Ros15]
Power Curve
If the forecasts are for power output instead of wind it is important to
normalize the power to the technical available nominal power. This removes the effect
of a reduced maximum power output due to maintenance or a technical shut-down (fail-
ure or grid management) that is normally not part of the power forecasts and would
otherwise distort the evaluation of them. Of course the normalization process has to be
omitted if the inﬂuence of those effects should be included in the error calculation as
well.

The problem of the decomposition of the power curve (section 4.3) might be possible
to avoid in using power output data of a whole wind farm or even better a cluster of
wind farms. As this includes shadow effects and other inﬂuences on the turbines it
should be less likely to reach the nominal power of all turbines for longer time spans
and therefore steady outputs should at least decrease in time.
Adding a predeﬁned continuous (high-frequency) signal (with the properties of an
IMF) to the data would make it possible for the improved EMD method (CEEMDAN)
to ﬁnd enough points and get a proper decomposition. After the process it should be
removed from the obtained modes and the other modes should be set to zero for those
times. Hopefully each mode has a zero crossing at the beginning and end of the time
of linearity. On the other hand high frequencies are removed from the signal before the
lower ones, therefore the effect of the added signal would only work for frequencies
with an even higher frequency. At the same time the frequency has to be high enough
to create several maxima and minima during the period of linear data, making it really
difﬁcult to deﬁne a suitable frequency, especially as the periods are not of the same
length.
Another idea that can be tested is to use the approach of Barnhard [Bar11] described
in section 4.1.1 to ﬁll data gaps in treating ‘nominal’ power output as gaps in order to
decompose the residual data correctly while still having the chance of detecting low
frequencies. To be able to evaluate the forecast the modes have to be set to zero again
for the corresponding periods before the creation of the spectrum.
A completely different approach would be to evaluate the wind forecasts but deﬁne
some kind of weighting/penalizing factor for different wind speeds. That means that it
is does not matter in the rating if the forecasted wind speed is 14 or 18 m/s when it was
measured to be 16 as the resulting power output would be the same. It would count the
same for wind speeds below cut-in. In the steepest part of the power curve the penalty
would be the highest having the highest (marginal) impact on the power output. The
method would be similar to an adaptation of the conversion between uncertainty in
wind and the uncertainty in power output resulting out of it (section 2.2 and especially
Figure 2.5).
Using some kind of weighting conversion would also give the advantage of being able
to set different individual weighs to the errors, depending on the application the fore-
cast is used for. In this way over- or under-estimation can get a higher weighting.
Later on, different amplitudes might be weighted differently or some frequency bands
(most probably the extreme high and low ones) can be completely ignored. Especially
this application would gain from the IMFs being decomposed in a way that they can be
compared directly with each other (no mode mixing present).
Outlook
The next step is to compare this method with other frequency based methods
like Fourier harmonics and wavelet analysis. As these are more widely known they
could act as a benchmark to evaluate the developed method against as well, given the
new method outperforms them.

7 Conclusion
The development of a scale dependant veriﬁcation method for short-term wind (power)
forecasts using the HHT gives rise to some problems in the very beginning of the pro-
cess of decomposing the signal into its modes. With the requirement of little uncertainty
in an evaluation method itself, inﬂuences like the end-effects and mode-mixing are not
acceptable in the magnitude they can appear using the original EMD method. Dur-
ing the course of this thesis the weaknesses of the original methods as well as their
improvements, are intensively tested on samples from measurements and artiﬁcially
created functions with different resolutions to ﬁnd the best suited. Even though still
having its ﬂaws and not working perfectly the improved CEEMDAN is chosen as it
seems to be the most reliable of the examined.
Some of the problems originate form the short time series that generally come along
when analysing short-term forecasts. In this case the forecasts have lead times of up to
60 h with hourly resolution. For a better performance of the method a higher resolution
is deﬁnitively recommended.
A meaningful analysis of power forecasts could not be established due to the HHT’s
behaviour on linear parts occurring when the turbine or the wind park is running at
nominal capacity. Nevertheless, some ideas on how to reduce or bypass the distorting
effects of it are discussed in section 6.
Despite the problems HHT is facing when analysing short time series, the results ob-
tained with CEEMDAN seem to be sufﬁcient as a base for the following steps.
As shown in the results the developed method can be used to compare forecasts with
measurements in different ways giving access to much more information about the
error distribution over lead time and frequency than single valued scores like the com-
monly used RMSE. Furthermore, it enables to combine forecast series and therefore
compare the skill of predicting WS (and other meteorological parameters) at different
sites or heights. The rating of different forecast models on their ability of prediction
could not be tested but is supposed to work exactly like the examples shown for the
comparison of the sites.
Evaluating a forecast for certain conditions only is however, a bit more complicated
as for other scores. It is e.g. not possible to preselect only wind speeds coming from
a sector of 30-60◦before applying the method; the sample would loose the required
equidistant time steps between the data points. Therefore, the samples have to be pro-
cessed over the full length ﬁrst and the results for the speciﬁc times can be sorted out in
post-processing. Of course, only the forecasts with data fulﬁlling the constraints should
be used when applying the method in order to decrease the calculation time.
Comparing the decomposition of different parameters might also give some informa-
tions about the dependencies resulting in errors, e.g. two parameters can be in phase

In total one can conclude that HHT, especially when using the improved version of
the CEEMDAN for decomposition, can deﬁnitely help to understand the problems of
a forecast not ﬁtting the measured data and give a more sophisticated view of the er-
ror. But more work has to be done to optimise the method for shorter time series as
generally used in most needed forecasts.

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
