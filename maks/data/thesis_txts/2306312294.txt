1 Abstract 
In order to reduce the fuel price and the emission of greenhouse gasses and pollutants, refused derived fuels 
(RDF) are increasingly used in cement calciners to replace fossil fuels. The RDF changes the combustion and the 
formation and reduction of NOx during RDF conversion in cement calciners. This limits the design and operation 
of RDF-fired cement calciners to achieve a low NOx emission. Thus, it is desirable to achieve a thorough 
understanding of the release and transformation of the fuel nitrogen during RDF conversion under calciner 
conditions. 
In combustion processes NOX are usually formed either from fixation of nitrogen in the combustion air at high 
temperatures or from chemically bound nitrogen in the fuel. Almost all combustion processes lead to the 
formation of NOX, mostly formed as NO with smaller amounts of NO2. NO can be oxidized afterwards to NO2 in 
the atmosphere. 
The experiments performed in this report are Single Particle Combustion (SPC), Elemental Analysis (EA), ash 
and moisture content and Thermo gravimetric Analysis-Fourier Transform Infrared spectroscopy (TG-FTIR). The 
SPC experiments were to determine the volatile/char content, EA for nitrogen content, and TG-FTIR for 
formation of NOx species during pyrolysis. The samples used in the experiments were Fiberboard, Spruce Bark, 
Bituminous Coal and SRF. 
The SPC experiments showed that the volatile content of the samples were in average Fiberboard 87.2 %, Bark 
77.2 %, Coal 31.4 % and SRF 81.4 %, all on dry basis. Fiberboard and SRF results did not change much in yield at 
the different temperatures, whereas Bark had some variations. The Coal changed a lot in both the volatile yield 
and the standard deviation, this means that the Coal sample was inhomogeneous. The char content of the 
samples were in average Fiberboard 10.48 %, Bark 7.63 %, Coal 49.27 % and SRF 11.96 %, all on dry basis. 
The nitrogen distribution was calculated from the SPC and EA results. The distribution for Fiberboard was quite 
stable with an average distribution of 89.4/10.6 % (volatile/char). The Bark and SRF had more variations, and 
Bark 1300 and SRF LHR had no nitrogen content in the char. This could be due to inhomogeneous samples, but 
it is more likely that it is because of the analyzing tools, which is not that accurate. The average distribution for 
Bark were 86.4/13.6 % and for SRF 84.5/15.5 %. 
The FTIR curves showed that methane, ammonia, carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide and either hydrogen 
cyanide or carbon dioxide were identified for Fiberboard, Coal and SRF. For Fiberboard and SRF isocyanic acid 
was also identified. It was not possible to be sure if the sample contained hydrogen cyanide or carbon dioxide. 
In Fiberboard the isocyanic acid was released from 199-250 °C and ammonia was released from 199-501 °C. In 
the coal no isocyanic acid was released and ammonia was released from 450-501 °C. For SRF the isocyanic acid 
was released from 370-476 °C and ammonia was released from 349-476 °C.

Background 
In order to reduce the fuel price and the CO2 emission, refused derived fuels (RDF) are increasingly used in 
cement calciners to replace fossil fuels. The RDF changes the combustion, and the formation and reduction of 
NOx during RDF conversion in cement calciners, and this have not been systematically studied. This limits the 
design and operation of RDF-fired cement calciners to achieve a low NOx emission. Thus, it is desirable to 
achieve a thorough understanding of the release and transformation of the fuel nitrogen during RDF 
conversion under calciner conditions. In addition, the possible interactions between the NOx species and 
volatiles/char formed from RDF need to be explored. 
Objectives 
 
Investigate the release of nitrogen during RDF pyrolysis 
 
Investigate the formation of nitrogen species during RDF pyrolysis 
Content 
 
Literature review of the cement production process, with a focus on calciners 
 
Literature review on the release and transformation of fuel nitrogen during combustion of RDF 
components  
 
Experimental study of the fraction of fuel nitrogen released during pyrolysis of different RDF 
components in a Single Particle Combustor (SPC) and a Thermogravimetric - Fourier transform 
infrared spectroscopy (TG-FTIR)

7 Literature Study 
7.1 Introduction 
The production of cement requires a high amount of energy. It is estimated that 10-15 % of the total industrial 
energy consumption comes from the cement industry [1][2]. Thus, the cement plants are intensive consumers 
of natural raw materials and fossil fuels, which leads to the emission of greenhouse gasses and pollutants such 
as CO2 and NOx [1]. Presently, the cement industry are the third major pollution source of nitrogen oxide, 
following electric power and automobile industry [3]. 
Furthermore, it is estimated that 30-40 % of the manufacturing cost of Portland cement represent the energy 
cost [4]. This means that the cement plants have a high focus of the fuel, and is always looking for cheaper 
materials to be used as fuel. The use of cheap alternative fuels is therefore one of the best ways to lower the 
cost of cement production. In addition to the energy use there is an increasing focus of reducing the emissions 
of gasses from the cement plants [5].  
Pollution of the atmosphere is long and well documented of its unwanted effects [6]. It ranges from local 
problems like odor nuisance, to regional problems like acidification and finally global effects which could 
change conditions for the human race and nature on the entire globe [6]. Cement plants have both health and 
environmental effects from their emissions. This is characteristically carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide and 
nitrogen oxides [7]. 
Nitrogen oxides (NOx) are air pollutants emitted from incineration and combustion processes.  NOX are toxic 
gaseous species, which cause respiratory diseases by damaging respiratory cells in both humans and animals, 
as well as causing visibility impairment, photochemical smog, rain and eutrophication. Other nitrogen species 
like N2O and NH3 can lead to increased greenhouse effect and eutrophication respectively [6][8]. 
In combustion processes NOX are usually formed either from fixation of nitrogen in the combustion air at high 
temperatures or from chemically bound nitrogen in the fuel. Almost all combustion processes lead to the 
formation of NOX, mostly formed as NO with smaller amounts of NO2. NO can be oxidized afterwards to NO2 in 
the atmosphere [9].   
The NOX emissions from stationary combustion plants have dropped by 73 % since 1990 (see Figure 1). From 
the figure, it can be seen that the main reason to the drop is the Public electricity and heat production. A 
secondary contribution to the reduction comes from manufacturing industries and construction. NOX is mainly 
reduced because of installation of low NOX burners, selective catalytic reduction (SCR) units and selective non-
catalytic reduction (SNCR) units [10]. Cement plants have multiple ways to reduce NOX emissions, these are: 
burn ability improvements, low NOX kiln burner, low NOX calciner or selective non-catalysis reduction (SNCR) 
[11].

There are multiple types of alternative fuels that can be used in the cement industry, like sewage sludge, refuse 
derived fuel, waste wood, tire derived fuel and many more. The fuels can be divided into a range of categories, 
from hazardous to non-hazardous and to solid, liquid and gaseous state [12]. 
Coal is the most used fuel in power plants, but biomass fuels as well as household and industrial waste are 
having an increased significance [9]. This is either for new plants running purely on biomass or waste, or for 
older plants, which is using mixtures of coal and biomass or waste.  
Alternative fuels are the next step in the incineration in cement production, but alternative fuels are a broad 
expression and is not a well-defined object yet. Some of the advantages from the alternative fuels are listed 
below [12]:

 
The alternative fuels are cheaper than regular fuels 
 
Fossil fuel resources are retained 
 
The ash produced from burning alternative fuels can be used to produce the clinker, so there is not any

7.2 Cement production 
7.2.1 Cement 
Burnt limestone (mortar) has been used as a binder material for construction since ancient times.  However, 
mortar is not durable and burning of limestone with impurities was discovered to result in a more durable 
paste. The main type of cement used today, is termed Portland   cement and is based on a mixture of 
limestone, sand and clay [12]. 
There has been a large development of the cement production in the last 150 years. The production processes 
used to produce Portland cement, have moved from shaft kiln units with a small production capacity of around 
7 tons cement clinker per day to modern rotary kilns, which have a production capacity of up to 12000 tons 
cement clinker per day. The main objective for upscaling the capacity has been to attain lower specific energy 
consumption per mass cement clinker produced. The modern kiln systems consist of separate processes, which 
include raw material pre-heating, a pre-calcination unit for calcination of limestone and lastly a rotary kiln for 
the final cement clinker burning.  The early energy consumption was about 1400-1500 kcal/kg clinker and the 
modern process is around 700 kcal/kg clinker [12]. 
7.2.2 Overall process 
The basic chemistry of cement manufacturing process starts with decomposition of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) 
in the calciner at about 900°C. It is decomposed to calcium oxide (CaO, lime) and carbon dioxide (CO2). This is 
followed by the clinkering process, where the calcium oxide reacts at a high temperature (usually around 1400-
1500°C) with silica, alumina and ferrous oxide to form silicates, aluminates and ferrites of calcium which the 
clinker comprises of. The clinkers are then milled together with gypsum and other additives to produce the 
final cement [13].  
In Figure 2 a typical production scheme of the cement is shown. First the calcium carbonate is extracted in a 
quarry and driven to a crushing plant where it is crushed to smaller parts, which is fit for transporting. The 
calcium carbonate is then preblended. Hereafter the raw materials are added to the process, where it starts 
with a grinding of the materials. The grinded material is then added to raw meal storage. The raw meal is 
subsequently added to a counter flow cyclone preheater, where air from the kiln and calciner comes in the 
opposite direction of the raw meal. After the preheating the raw meal enters the calciner where the calcium 
carbonate is calcined. The calcined materials are then added to the rotary kiln, where the cement clinker is 
produced. The clinker is hereafter cooled in a counter flow with air. Then the clinker is cooled even more, and 
is dedusted. The clinker could be sold directly or silica, alumina and ferrous oxide could be added and grinded 
to cement [13].

In cement production there are four main process routes: dry, semi-dry, semi-wet and wet [13]. In most 
processes, the dry process is used. 
- 
The dry process: Here, the raw material is ground and dried to a form of flowable powder. The raw 
meal is then fed to the preheater, precalciner or more rarely to the kiln. 
- 
The semi-dry process: Here the dry raw meal is pelletized with water and fed into a grate preheater 
before the kiln. 
- 
The semi-wet process: The raw meal comes in a form of slurry from the extraction. The slurry is 
dewatered in a filter press. The resulting filter cake is then extruded to pellets and fed to a grate 
preheater or directly to a filter cake dryer for raw meal production. 
- 
The wet process: Here the raw materials are ground in water to form pumpable slurry. The slurry is 
then fed either directly into the kiln or to a slurry dryer. 
The process route is usually determined by the state of the raw materials (dry or wet). A large part of today’s 
production is still based on wet processed. These plants usually only have access to moist raw materials, as is 
situated in Denmark and Belgium, and to some extent in the UK [13].

Raw materials and their winning 
The raw material used for raw meal comes from naturally occurring calcareous deposits, such as limestone, 
marl and chalk. They provide the source of calcium carbonate. Silica, alumina and iron oxide are found in 
various ores and minerals. The winning of nearly all of the raw materials involves mining and quarrying 
operations, mostly open surface quarries. In most cases the cement plants are built near a quarry, to limit the 
transportation cost of the raw material. After the raw material is recovered, is only a primary crushing remains 
before the raw material is sent to the plant [13].

7.2.3 Calcination process 
The IUPAC [15] definition of calcination is: “Heating to high temperatures in air or oxygen”. However, 
calcination is also used for a thermal treatment process in the absence or limited supply of oxygen, which is the 
definition of calcination in this report. The calcination technique has been used in the cement industry from 
around 1970 [14]. The calciner system have three different scopes: to convert the calcium carbonate into 
calcium oxide and carbon dioxide as shown in Reaction (R. 1) [16], to convert the fuel sufficiently and to reduce 
emissions [12].  
The calcination process is the first major chemical step in the thermal treatment of the raw materials in the 
cement production. The reaction is endothermic and consumes approximately 60 % of the total fuel energy 
required for the production with the rotary kiln accounting for the rest (40 %). The decomposition of CaCO3 
takes place above 700°C until all CO2 is expelled [16].

The ILC system is characterized by the flue gas coming from the rotary kiln and is led through the calciner (see

into the calciner. The kilns size can then be reduced since there is less gas flow through the kiln [12]. In Figure 6 
the modern design of an ILC system is presented and it shows where the calciner is positioned compared to the

The combustion characteristics of solid fuels in the ILC are considerably different than the one in the kiln, since 
the combustion happens in the suspension. This suspension type of combustion ensures good heat and 
external mass transfer. The utilization of gaseous fuels in the ILC needs mixing of fuel and oxidizer and is 
hereby mainly influenced by turbulence and chemical kinetics [12]. 
7.2.3.1.2 Separate-Line-Calciner system 
In the SLC system the flue gas from the kiln bypasses the calciner. In the SLC system (see Figure 7), the calciner 
design and thereby the combustion characteristics are similar to the ILC system, except for the fact that the 
calcination process takes place in a region with higher oxygen concentration than in the ILC.

In the SLC-Downdraught system (see Figure 8), the fuel is added in the top of the calciner and the tertiary air 
suspended with raw material particles are introduced tangentially.  Therefore, the fuel burns downward as a 
directed diffusion flame and the raw material particles are transported to the wall and downward by 
centrifugal and gravitational force. The distribution of the particles along the wall ensures a high flame 
temperature and cooling of the refractory wall. Therefore the combustion takes place in a region with a higher 
oxygen concentration than in the ILC system, since the flue gas from the kiln is not mixed until after the 
calciner. The downward direction of the flame may result in a lower solid fuel residence time. In general the 
high temperature and the high oxidation level provide good conditions for combustion [12].

When burning regular fuels like coal, oil and gas an ILC system is most commonly used. For difficult to burn 
fuels like petroleum, coke and anthracite a SLC system is usually preferred, since a high oxygen atmosphere 
ensures improved fuel burnout. However, ILC systems have generally lower specific NOX emissions than SPC 
systems. For this reason the ILC systems may also be used for difficult to burn fuels, but with an increased 
minimum residence time to ensure adequate fuel burnout [11]. 
In 2002 both ILC and SLC systems were capable of meeting the CO and NOX emission standards for almost any 
combinations of fuel and feed. The success of these calciners depends on their strong reduction and oxidation 
zones [11].

In the lower part of the ICL calciner the oxygen level is low since the fuel is admitted without addition of 
combustion air. The tertiary air is injected higher in the calciner (see Figure 5). The oxygen level in the calciner

Challenges of alternative fuel firing in cement plants 
Alternative fuels may vary from traditional fossil fuels in both physical and chemical aspects. The significant 
alterations of alternatives fuels compared to transactional fuels are listed below [23]: 
- 
Particle size: most solid alternative fuels have a larger particle size than the traditional fossil fuel.  
- 
Moisture content: The moisture content is significantly higher. Alternative fuels, such as biomass or 
sewage sludge, can have moisture content of up to 50 wt. %. 
- 
Volatile content: The volatile content is almost always higher in alternative fuels.  
- 
Heating value: The heating rate is usually lower since the particle size is higher. 
- 
Combustion behavior: Alternative fuels such as biomass usually have a faster char oxidation than solid 
fossil fuels, since it has a less ordered structure and higher content of hydrogen and oxygen. 
- 
Inhomogeneous physical and chemical properties. 
- 
Physical properties e.g. stick or low density fuels, make handling difficult 
The list above depends on the type of fuel and do not necessarily apply for all fuels [23].  
Wang et al. [24] tested if co-firing the cement process with biomass would cause the cement clinker not to 
follow the Chinese standard GB/T 1596-2005 for cement. They found that the cement was able to stay inside 
the limits for the standard, but the there was a small increase in the expansion and a decrease in the activity 
index compared to the cement produced from pure coal firing. They further found that when co-firing with 
biomass the flame was stable but the outlet temperatures of the furnace was decreased and the furnace 
efficiency was reduced from 94.67 % to 94.15 %.

7.3 Refuse Derived Fuel (RDF) 
Refuse-Derived Fuel (RDF) is a waste fuel used by industrialized countries as supplement for coal and in some 
cases used for firing in dedicated boilers. RDF is used in the energy and cement industries. 
In 2001 the European Climate Change Programme (ECCP) promoted the ECCP II [25] which states that the 
usage of renewable energy, should increase by 20 % by 2020. In this sense the cement industry is motivated to 
use RDF to reduce fuel cost, reduce GHG emissions, and to recover waste materials [1]. 
RDF is created from the high calorific fractions (HCF) of non-hazardous mixed municipal solid waste (MSW) or 
industrial waste, making it an interesting feedstock, since it is available all over the world. As shown in Figure 
10 the composition of RDF is typically a mixture of paper, cardboard, textiles, different kinds of plastics and a 
small part of unburnable items like glass. The first two components and some of the textiles are regarded as 
biomass owing to their biogenic origin. Because of that, a part of the RDF is considered renewable and CO2 
neutral [26].  This is one of the reasons why RDF is used in the energy industry. Another important reason is, 
that the price of the RDF is significantly lower than coal or oil. The price for RDF is difficult to estimate since it 
varies from place to place. Wzorek et al. [27] describes that produced alternative fuel cost around 23.8 EUR/t, 
while  Illinois Biomass Briquette Systems [28] sells RDF for 6 $/t. 
A third reason to use RDF is that the waste does not have to go to landfills and stock up [1]. 
RDF contains larger amount of combustible materials (such as biomass and plastics) than the MSW usually 
does. Compared to MSW, RDF typically has higher heating value, better quality and more homogeneous 
characteristics. Furthermore, the moisture and ash content of RDF is lower. It also has some disadvantages, 
such as the cost to process the MSW to create RDF. Another disadvantage is, that the total yield of energy per 
kg MSW is lower, than if the MSW is burned directly, since some combustible materials are discarded.

7.3.1 Production of RDF 
The general principle to produce RDF is to accept solid waste directly from the collection vehicle and then 
separate the solid waste into two different fractions, combustible and non-combustible [30]. 
RDF is made with a pre-processing step, where the solid waste is passed through a few unit operations to 
remove unwanted materials. The unit operations are usually a combination of density separation, magnetic 
separation, screening and size reduction, but other operations may be used dependent on the MSW content 
[30]. 
Generally, there is not a single solution on processing MSW. The composition of the MSW and  the purpose of 
the RDF determines the unit operations to produce it [30]. 
A typical RDF production system is shown in Figure 11. First the MSW enters the process, where it gets in a pre-
trommel. The dust particles (mostly inert materials) are then separated by a cyclone. The remaining materials 
enter a shredder to yield smaller particle sizes. Hereafter, the material enters a secondary trommel. After 
another separation of dust particles in a cyclone the rest of materials enters a magnetic separator to remove 
the metals. The rest of the material is finished as RDF. 
 
Figure 11 – A typical RDF production system [30] 
7.3.2 RDF properties 
Generally, RDF consists of paper and plastics, but it also contains materials, that have a relatively high content 
of ash, which can harm burners and boilers. The combustible part of the RDF can be classified in four main 
kinds of materials: cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin and plastics [31]. The first three categories have ash content, 
where it is only a few kinds of plastics which have significant ash content. 
Hirunpraditkoon et al. [32] have tested some RDF and about 95% of the material passes through 50mm square 
screens, which is required by ASTM E828 standard for RDF-3 specification.

7.4 Formation and reduction of NOX 
This section focuses on the formation and reduction of NOX in general and specifically in cement plants. 
Furthermore, general combustion and nitrogen content in different species will also be presented. 
7.4.1 General combustion of solid fuels 
The solid particles combustion can be described as a sequence of processes. When a particle is exposed to heat 
it will increase in size due to heat transport by conduction, convection and radiation, where the ratio depends 
on the local environment. The rate the particle is heated with is essential in the combustion process and varies 
with the industrial application [35]. 
The effect of temperature has been studied both experimentally and through modeling. The largest impact of 
temperature is the change in char yield. The yield decreases as a function of increasing temperature. However 
at around 1173 K the char yield stabilizes and any small decrements are believed to be caused by increased 
auto-gasification processes [35]. Dall’Ora et al. [36] showed that fast heating rate experiments resulted in 
melted chars, while the fibrous structure of the parent fuel was retained if it was exposed to low heating rates. 
The combustion starts with the particle is heated and the moisture content will evaporate and yield a dry 
particle. Then the organic matrix will start to decompose at around 500 K, and the devolatilization occurs 
rapidly. The released volatiles are then oxidized in gaseous homogeneous reactions. The left overs in the 
particle are char and ash. After the pyrolysis the char is oxidized in surface reactions and all that is left is the 
ash [35]. 
In modern kiln based cement plants there are mainly two places where NOX is formed, which is where the fuel 
is fired, i.e. in the kiln and in the calciner [17]. Thermal NOX forms at temperatures above 1300 °C and is formed 
from the oxidation of molecular nitrogen in the kilns during combustion. The higher the flame temperature of 
the kiln the more thermal NOX will be generated. The fuel NOX form at temperatures below 1300 °C and is 
formed from the nitrogen compounds in the calciners fuel to NOX. The nitrogen compounds pathway to form 
fuel  
The nitrogen compounds pathways to form fuel NOX, are to react with another nitrogen atom to form N2 and 
then to react with oxygen to form NOX or react with a NOX molecule to form N2 and O2 [11]. Thermally formed 
NOX is dominating in the kiln and the fuel formed NOX is dominating in the calciner. NOX can also be formed in 
higher temperature conditions from organically bound nitrogen of raw meal NOX [17]. In in-line systems the 
preheater should be able to reduce the NOX concentration with reaction with organic constituents of the raw 
meal. However, experience indicates that the contribution of this mechanism is insignificant [17]. In Table 4 the 
industrial raw meal composition can be seen and indicates that there is no nitrogen in the raw meal, hereby 
not contributing to the NOX outlet.

Furthermore the volatile content affects the NOX formation by four mechanisms [17]: 
- 
In sub-stoichiometric zones, hydrocarbon radicals, from the volatile, can react more freely with NOX

- 
The nitrogen in the volatiles can react with NOX to form N2 
-

-

stoichiometry during the combustion

Highly reactive chars reacts faster with NOX than low reactivity char to form N2  
- 
Highly reactive chars consumes oxygen faster than chars with low reactivity and hereby rapidly

7.4.2 Fundamental NOX formation mechanisms  
Nitrogen oxides can be formed in combustion processes by fixation of molecular nitrogen in the combustion air 
or from chemically bound nitrogen in the fuel. Formation of NO from N2 is generally from thermal NO

Formation of NO from fuel-N by oxidation is termed fuel-NO and the overall reactions for fuel-NO are quite 
well established. When the particle combustion begins the fuel-N is distributed between the volatiles and the 
solid char matrix. This distribution depends mainly on the temperature and the fuel structure [9]. For 
bituminous coals the volatile-N consists mostly of tarry compounds that rapidly decay at high temperatures to 
hydrogen cyanide (HCN) or soot-nitrogen. For low rank coals and biomass, light nitrogen species are released 
directly from the solid matrix. HCN is oxidized to NO from amines or converted to N2 by recombination of NO 
with other nitrogen containing species. If the temperature is low (e.g. fluid bed combustion) the remaining 
char-N and soot-N undergoes heterogeneous oxidation to yield NO and N2. If the temperature is too high the 
components evolve to light components as HCN. NO may be recycled by hydrocarbon radicals to cyanide or 
reduced to N2 by surface reactions [9]. 
In solid fuel systems, oxidation of fuel-N is generally the major source of NO, only with a small contribution 
from thermal NO, where other NO formation mechanisms are usually negligible [9].

7.4.3 NOX reduction 
There is more than 400 different reactions known to be involved in the NOX reduction and combustion process 
[11]. Thus to understand what is happening in a low NOX calciner more knowledge about the combustion 
process is required.  
Reburning is in the furnace with and injection of a second fuel downstream of the primary combustion where 
NO reduction occurs. The reburning then creates local fuel rich zones where the NO can be reduced with 
hydrocarbon radicals and with char [45]. 
In the calciner reburning takes place, caused by a series of reactions that involves gases, soot and char from the 
pyrolysis, char and raw meal. The reactions reduce the NOX concentration and can be seen in pathways (R. 8)-
(R. 12) [11]. 
 
 
Reaction (R. 8) and (R. 9) are catalyzed by raw meal and it is inhibited by oxygen. When introducing fuel and 
raw meal in the absence of tertiary air, the thermal NOX entering the calciner from the kiln is destroyed and the 
fuel NOX is suppressed by the absence of oxygen. But when there is a lack of oxygen, carbon monoxide will be 
produced, and therefore the tertiary air needs to be added above, which again promotes the formation of NOX. 
Therefore, it is a balance between having as little air possible to destroy the NOX and having enough air to 
inhibit the formation of carbon monoxide [11]. 
 
 
 
Reaction (R. 10) is reducing the NO with a hydrocarbon radical, which is formed from the pyrolysis of the 
calciner fuel. This reaction is favored by high temperatures, so the reduction zone with a temperature of 1150-
1200°C destroys thermal NOX and suppress fuel NOX [11]. In Figure 14 this effect is shown.

7.4.4 NOX control 
Biomass is considered CO2 neutral, so the emission of compounds such as SO2 and NO from biomass utilization 
may cause concern because of acid rain and photochemical smog. It is known that the reaction between NO 
and char may contribute to lowering the emission of NO both in CFB and pulverized fuel combustion and so, 
this reaction may be utilized to in situ reduction of NO [51]. 
Staged combustion is a method for creating reducing condition in the calciner and leading to partial oxidation 
of the fuel [11]. There are two methods of staged combustion, the fuel staging and air staging. In both cases 
reduction zones are created from incomplete combustion of the fuel. The best low NOX calciners are those that 
employ both strong reduction and strong oxidation zones, separately [11].  
The SCR and SNCR technologies can be used in the cement industry as a safety backup if the NOX emission 
otherwise would have been too high after the heating cyclones. 
 
Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) DeNOx technology  
The SCR technology has a NOX reduction efficiency of 70-90 % and is the most commonly used technology for 
DeNOx. In the SCR reaction the NOX in flue gas selectively reacts with the reagent from the catalyst to produce 
N2 and H2O. In reactions (R. 15) and (R. 16) the SCR chemical reactions are presented [65]. 
 
Most exhaust gasses in the SCR is reduced by ammonia or urea, and is usually selected based on handling, 
safety and economics [65].

very significant (2 fold for NH3 and 3 fold for HCN). This is due to the different fuel properties [49].  
Hansson et al. [58] describe how the nitrogen content is contained in some biomasses. Proteins account for 
most of the nitrogen in stem wood in Scots Pine with a content in the range of 66-87 %, but the fraction of 
nitrogen in protein form decreases over time, when the tree stops growing. Other forms of nitrogen could be 
contained in the form of DNA, RNA or similar structures [58].

Emission of NOX is the consequence of competition between NOX formation and NOX reduction, which are 
dependent on the chemical and physical conditions of the fuel and the operating conditions [56].  
When using alternative fuels in the kiln it usually reduces NOX formation when comparing to coal firing, except 
for sewage sludge, which is reported by Greco [63] to increase the NOX formation in the kiln flame. Lang [64] 
reported that the NOX reduction was a consequence of a lower flame temperature associated with the 
alternative fuels, and hereby a lower thermal NOX formation. Furthermore alternative fuels have a higher 
volatile content and is more likely to induce reburning reactions to reduce NOX.  
Because there are limited possibilities for reducing NOX concentrations in the kiln, there is a higher focus on 
creating the optimal calciner design to reduce the NOX emissions.  
7.4.9 Impact of alternative fuels on NOX emission in cement calciners 
Larsen [12] reports that in two out of three cases, meat and bone meal fired into a calciner would decrease 
NOX emissions with up to 60 %  compared to a calciner without alternative fuels. This is despite the fact that 
the meat and bone meal has high nitrogen contents (7.5-10.5 %). The reduction is caused by the relatively high 
volatile content of the meat and bone meal. 
Giugliano [67] reported that tire chips decrease the NOx emission by up to 40 % when admitted in a calciner 
below the tertiary air. When firing whole tires into the HOTDISC as a calciner fuel substitute of 40 energy % the 
NOx are reported to decrease at 15 % compared to a pure pet coke firing in the calciner [12]. RDF is reported to 
decrease the NOX emission when fired directly in the calciner  [12].

7.5 Literature study conclusion 
High amounts of the total industrial energy consumption comes from the cement industry and are hereby large 
consumers of raw materials and fossil fuels leading to emission of greenhouse gasses and pollutants. 
Furthermore, it is estimated that the energy cost, of the production of cement, are 30-40 %. These reasons lead 
the cement industry to find new and cheaper fuels, due to both the environment and economy. Refused 
Derived Fuels (RDF) is a rather new type of fuel that can be extracted from municipal waste or other waste 
streams. A cement plant in Norway reduced their fuel cost by 75 % through changing their fuels to alternative 
fuels. 
Other forms of alternative fuels could be: Sewage sludge, waste wood tire derived fuel. There are some 
challenges in the alternative fuel firing. Larger particle size, higher moisture content, higher volatile content 
(lower char content) and lower heating value. The heating value of RDF is 12-16 MJ/kg and for coal 21-32 
MJ/kg. All these factors make the combustion process different, and these processes are less known. 
In the pyrolysis of particles higher temperatures equals lower char yields (higher volatile contents). 
Furthermore, the decrease stops at around 1173 K and any small decrements are believed to be caused by 
increased auto-gasification processes. Higher volatile content in the fuel causes more formation of NO2. The 
nitrogen species released from the pyrolysis are HCN, NH3 and HNCO,  
Nitrogen is released from combustion processes and some are formed as harmful species, and it are important 
that the combustion processes lead to as much of the nitrogen to come out as N2 as possible. Nitrogen is 
released as either the oxidation of molecular nitrogen present in combustion air (thermal NOX) or as the 
oxidation of nitrogen compounds in fuel (fuel NOX). 
The NOX emissions from stationary combustion plants have dropped by 73 % (from 1990-2013) because of 
installation of low NOX burners, selective catalytic reduction units and selective non-catalytic reduction. 
Cement plans have multiple possibilities to reduce NOX emissions. These possibilities are burn ability 
improvements, low NOX kiln burner, low NOX calciner and selective non-catalytic reduction.  
One of the most important tasks of the cement calciner is to reduce emissions, and in the calcination step 
around 60 % of the total required energy for the plant, is used. A basic calciner consists of a high temperature 
NOX reduction zone (low oxygen content 3 %), high temperature oxidation zone and a calcination zone. The 
higher the exit temperature from the reduction zone the higher NO reduction is. 
Generally, the volatile content in the materials versus the nitrogen content could be put in different groups and 
matched together. These groups are organic waste materials, different RDFs, typically fossil fuels and 
tire/biomass. 
Fiberboard has a volatile content of approximately 82 % and a nitrogen content of 3.5 %. Bark has a volatile 
content of 86 % and a nitrogen content of 0.35 %. Bituminous coal has a volatile content of 25-38 % and 
nitrogen content of 1.3-1.8 %. SRF has a volatile content of 77 % and nitrogen content of 0.9 %.

9.1 Single Particle Combustion (SPC) 
The Single Particle Combustion is a device used to simulate furnace conditions between 900-1450 °C. The SPC 
that is used is custom built by DTU and is placed in building 228. It can be seen in Figure 23 and Figure 24. 
 
Figure 23 – Picture of DTU’s SPC device placed in building 228 
The SPC setup is built like a tube with isolation and a ceramic tube inside (see Figure 24). In the bottom of the 
tube there is a burner built (see Figure 25) for heating the device. Just above the burner, sparkers for ignition 
can be inserted into the SPC under startup, and after the device is running the sparkers are removed again. 
Furthermore, in the bottom there is a flame detector to make sure that only if there is a flame, there is a flow 
of fuel. In the middle of the tube there are 4 holes. One for the camera so it can capture the combustion of the 
sample, one for a flash light so it is easier to see at the camera, one for a protection tube, if it is the wire setup 
that are used and last a hole for the sample. The hole with the sample also contains a nitrogen purge, so when 
the particle is withdrawn from pyrolysis, the char combustion do not start.

In Figure 25 the metal burner can be seen. The burner has three different inlets which are used to mix the 
hydrogen, oxygen and nitrogen to make the atmosphere wanted to burn in the SPC. The inlets flow depends on

9.1.1 Procedure 
A general scheme of the SPC is presented in Figure 24. 
The SPC is rather slow at reaching a specific temperature (up to three hours) so it needs to be started a few 
hours before it should be used. Furthermore, the suction pyrometer needs to be started 10 minutes before 
used, since it needs to cool to below 8 degrees, so it will not damage the gas analyzers. The SPC is started with 
two sparkers, which is removed after the flame detectors have detected a flame. The suction pyrometer is used 
to measure the temperature, and when the wanted temperature is reached, the pyrometer is removed. 
Basically when the equipment is used to burn samples, the exact temperature is unknown but is assumed that 
the temperature is the same as when the pyrometer is pulled out. 
When the SPC is started and waiting for the temperature to stabilize, the samples could be prepared. The 
different samples were weighed off in an Eppendorf tube so no sample would go missing both before and after 
the pyrolysis. The samples were then inserted into a platinum basket which is connected to a ceramic stick. The 
stick was then inserted into the SPC and monitored and captured by a camera so it was possible to know when 
the pyrolysis was done (when the flame went out), and then pulled out into a water cooled chamber with a N2 
purge, to make sure that the char oxidation did not happen. After the cooling the sample was collected in the 
Eppendorf tube and weighed. All samples are done 5 times to achieve a good standard deviation, but some 
samples were lost in the combustion process, so all samples are produced between 3-5 times. 
Some of the SPC experiments were carried out with multiple particles, even though it is single particle 
combustion. This is because that one particle was not large enough (this was the case for Fiberboard, Bark and 
SRF) to supply the TGA device with enough mass of sample.

collected in small Eppendorf tubes (at around 1g), which had been weighted beforehand, so it could be 
extracted after. Five replicates of each sample were devolatized to make sure of consistency.

9.2 Elemental analyzer (CHNS-O)

9.3 Coupled TG-FTIR system 
The TG-FTIR system is used to gain knowledge about which gaseous species are released from the sample. The 
analyzer consist of two systems, the thermogravimetric analyzer (TG or TGA) which is determining the change 
in a samples mass at a giving temperature(in this case 20-1000°C), and the surrounding gas atmosphere and a 
Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR). This is a method to gain an infrared spectrum of a gas by using 
an interferometer and performing a Fourier transform on the resulting interferogram, which then calculates 
the spectrum. The TG-FTIR can be seen on Figure 36. 
 
Figure 36 - NETZSCH Perseus STA 449 F1 Jupiter® with the Bruker FTIR spectrometer coupled directly to a thermogravimetric 
analyzer.

9.3.1 TGA 
The thermogravimetric analyzer (TGA) consists of a high accuracy balance, which records the mass at a 
specified temperature and gas atmosphere. The sample inserted into the furnace is in contact with the carrier 
gas flowing through the furnace and all gases exit the TGA into the FTIR. A cross sectional view of the TGA can 
be seen in Figure 37. 
The settings for the system used in this project are. Temperature from 25-1000°C with a heating rate of 
10°C/min, and a pure nitrogen atmosphere with a flowrate of 50 ml/min. The weights of the samples were 60-
78 mg.

9.3.2 FTIR 
The gas from the TGA enters the gas cell in the Fourier Transform Infrared spectrometer (FTIR) from a

on a detector. The signal that is detected is called an interferogram. Software from the device then performs a 
Fourier transformation which then calculates the interferogram to a spectrum. These spectra are then used to 
identify the leaving gasses from the FTIR. The spectra used to identify spectra from the FTIR are taken from 
Gasmet Ansyco [72] homepage and can be seen in Appendix G – FTIR spectra. Schematics of the FTIR part can

9.4 Moisture and ash content 
The procedure for getting the moisture and ash content is described below: 
- 
First the crucibles were dried overnight in a 105 °C oven 
- 
Then the crucibles were cooled down in an desiccator 
- 
Then 1-3 g sample was weighed into the crucible with 0.01 mg accuracy 
- 
The samples were then put in a 105 °C oven for 3 hours 
- 
The samples were then cooled in an desiccator and weighed  
- 
The dried sample was then put into an oven with a ramp from 20-600°C in one hour, and the 
temperature was held for one more hour (except for the coal which was from 20-750 in 2 hours and 
held there for 3h). Then the sample was cooled down in the exicator and weighed. 
References are from ASTM Compass [75]–[77]. 
The moisture and ash content is calculated from (Eq. 1) and (Eq. 2).

11 Conclusion 
The objective for this report was to investigate the distribution between volatile and char nitrogen content in 
combustion in the calciner and furthermore to investigate the formation of gaseous species from pyrolysis of 
different fuels. This was obtained by conducting a literature study with a focus on calciners and NOx, and 
performing different experiments. The experiments were carried out in Single Particle Combustion (SPC), 
Elemental Analyzer (EA) and Thermo gravimetric Analysis-Fourier Transform Infrared spectroscopy (TG-FTIR). 
The samples used in the experiments were Fiberboard, Spruce Bark, Bituminous Coal and SRF. 
A test of different holding methods for the SPC device was performed with a wire and a basket. The ignition 
time was very different for the holding methods, but the devolatilization time was rather close to each other. 
This is due to the mass of platinum should be heated first before ignition can happen. After the ignition the 
heating rate is similar for both the wire and the basket up until the devolatilization is done. 
SPC experiments showed that the volatile content of the samples were in average Fiberboard 87.2 %, Bark 77.2 
%, Coal 31.4 % and SRF 81.4 %, all on dry basis. The experiments were conducted at 920, 1030, 1300 and 
1420°C. The Fiberboards and SRF results were rather stable and the different temperatures did not affect the 
volatile yield much. The Bark had more variations to the yield but without an obvious a trend. The Coals volatile 
yield changed a lot and had a very high standard deviation, which means that the Coal sample is 
inhomogeneous. The char content of the samples were in average Fiberboard 10.48 %, Bark 7.63 %, Coal 49.27 
% and SRF 11.96 %, all on dry basis. 
The carbon contents of the char samples should be more of less the same after the devolatilization, but some 
of the samples are outliers. It can be seen that all the char samples that was produced at 920 °C are lower than 
the other char samples. The bark and SRF sample at 1030 were also both lower than the other results. This is 
because the new SPC basket was used instead of the old one, and maybe because the platinum basket had to 
high mass the basket was cooled slower than the old basket and hereby the char oxidation could have started. 
The EA experiments showed that the fiberboard has an average nitrogen concentration of 2.74 % and has a 
high standard deviation. Comparing with literature, the result is a bit below the literature 3.5 % and 3.6 %. For 
bark the average nitrogen concentration of 0.23 % and when comparing the measured data with literature, the 
result is around the same as the literature 0.23 % and 0.34 %. The coal sample has nitrogen concentration of 
1.1 % and when comparing the measured data with literature, the result is below the literature 1.8 %. The SRF 
sample had a nitrogen concentration of 1.56 % and comparing the measured data with literature, the result is 
almost twice as much as the literature 0.86 % 
The nitrogen content distribution for Fiberboard was rather stable and the nitrogen distribution was 89.4/10.6 
% (volatile/char). For Bark and SRF it changed a bit more for the different temperature. The Bark 1300 and SRF 
LHR nitrogen contents in the char part are zero. This could be due to inhomogeneous samples, but it is more 
likely that it is because of the analyzing tools, which is not that accurate. The average nitrogen distribution for 
Bark were 86.4/13.6 % and for SRF 84.5/15.5 %. 
 
 
==PAGE:92==
 
Page 79 of 101 
 
For Coal the volatile distribution was below zero for 3 of the temperature. The average distribution for the 
negative results was -36.2/136.2 %. An explanation could be that the Coal sample was inhomogeneous, the 
pure sample which was analyzed in the elemental analyzer was the small sieve fraction, whereas the char 
fraction was produced from the large sieve fraction. The Coal LHR and Coal 1420 had an average distribution of 
75.6/24.4 %. 
From the FTIR curves it can be seen that methane, ammonia, carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide and either 
hydrogen cyanide or carbon dioxide were identified for Fiberboard, Coal and SRF. For Fiberboard and SRF 
isocyanic acid was also identified. It was not possible to be sure if the sample contained hydrogen cyanide or 
carbon dioxide. In Fiberboard the isocyanic acid was released from 199-250 °C and ammonia was released from 
199-501 °C. In the coal no isocyanic acid was released and ammonia was released from 450-501 °C. For SRF the 
isocyanic acid was released from 370-476 °C and ammonia was released from 349-476 °C. 
It can be concluded from this work that Fiberboard, Bark and SRF releases most of its nitrogen in the 
devolatilization while Coal releases most nitrogen in the char combustion.

12 Further work 
The further work from this project is to obtain more results from different kinds of fuel samples that could be 
components in RDF or simply RDF samples. This could be wheat straw, rape, cotton, different kinds of plastics 
or more wood samples. A new and more homogenous coal sample would be nice to compare with, instead of 
the inhomogeneous sample used in this project. The coal sample in this project could also be grinded and 
mixed into a more homogeneous mixture. 
Furthermore, more accurate results would be better, since that there are high standard deviations on many of 
the results, especially the results from the elemental analyzer. A new or better analyzer should be used for 
further work, to be sure that the results are consistent and there are not problems with analyzing the results. 
In further perspective the results could be used for modelling the nitrogen release in a calciner. The nitrogen 
distribution of volatile and char could be used to enhance a model for more accurate results rather than the 
model is based on assumptions of the release pattern. 
There could be looked more into the SPC holding methods. The two basket used for this project has not been 
compared, and the effect of the mass of the platinum could have an effect on the cooling time for the sample.
