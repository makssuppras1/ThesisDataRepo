Abstract
Raman spectroscopy is a method used to identify, detect and measure concen-
trations of individual chemicals in a mixture of chemicals. To do this, a large
number of Raman spectra (vectors of real numbers) are measured and signal
processing is used to identify the peaks in the spectra. In this thesis we investi-
gate the use of a neural network autoencoder to detect peaks in Raman spectra.
We ﬁnd that the autoencoder model is less computationally expensive than a
comparable existing method, but also ﬁnd that the autoencoder model achieves
a worse accuracy. Suggestions are given for how the model can be developed
further.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis is about Raman spectroscopy, the problem of source separation and
an autoencoder model used to solve the problem of source separation in the
context of Raman spectroscopy. Raman spectroscopy, described in section 1.2,
is a process used to identify and measure the amount of chemicals in a mixture.
Source separation, described in section 1.1, is the problem of separating a given
signal into its constituent parts. Our autoencoder model is described in chapter 2
and its performance is assessed in chapter 3.
1.1
Source separation
Within digital signal processing, blind source separation problems are problems
in which a set of source signals {sk}K
k=1 have been combined into a set of mix-
ture signals {xn}N
n=1 by some transformation fφ with parameters φ. Usually
the signal is also contaminated with a noise vector ϵ. Mathematically this can
be written as X = fφ(S) + ϵ. In this thesis, the signals are always real-valued
vectors. The goal in blind source separation is to recover the source signals S
and the parameters φ from the mixture signals X. If K ≪N, as is often the
case in this thesis, then the problem generally cannot be solved exactly and the
goal is instead to obtain estimates bS and bφ of source signals and the transfor-
mation. This is done by minimizing ||X −ˆf(bS)|| where ||·|| is an appropriate
distance measure.
Very often additional constraints are placed on bX and ˆf.
These constraints should be chosen based on knowledge about the signals one is
working with. For example, if X and S are audio signals one may assume that
f is a linear transformation with positive weights φ. The choice of appropriate
constraints is important for the success of the method.
A variant of blind source separation is parametric blind source separation,
in which the source signals S have a parametric representation θ. Thus the
goal is to obtain estimates bθ and bφ of the parameters for the source signal and

A related problem is nonblind source separation, in which both X and S are
given and the goal is to estimate the transformation parameters φ. In this thesis
we mainly focus on blind source separation, but nonblind source separation also
has many applications and will be encountered in the literature review.
Let us relate Raman spectroscopy and source separation. As described in

estimate φ based on X and S. Knowing the parameters φ can be very useful. For
example, if fφ is a linear transformation, consistently high values for the weights
associated with a particular chemical indicates that the chemical is present in
high concentrations relative to the other chemicals in the mixture.
A more
sophisticated analysis can lead to precise estimates of relative concentration of
each chemical in the mixture.
Blind source separation is similar, except that the pure spectra S are not
known. The goal is then to infer both the parameters φ and the source spectra
S based on X. The number of source spectra K is also unknown and must be
chosen. The spectra S can then be matched against a library of pure Raman
spectra to determine if certain chemicals are present in the mixture. Note that
usually the peaks of the source signals in S are matched against a Raman library,
while other aspects of the source signals may not be used.
In theory, blind source separation can be used for all the same applications as
nonblind source separation. However, blind source separation is a harder prob-
lem leading to greater uncertainty about the estimates of φ and S. Therefore
blind source separation is more often used for the simpler problem of detecting
chemicals rather than the problem of estimating concentrations.
1.2
Raman spectroscopy theory
Raman spectroscopy is a method used to identify, detect and measure concen-
trations of individual chemicals in a mixture of chemicals.
This is done by
illuminating the mixture with a laser and measuring the scattered light. The

fraction of the scattered light having a higher or lower frequency than the in-

the diﬀerent chemicals. These changes can be visualized by plotting a Raman
spectrum, which is a plot of intensity (usually measured in photon counts or
arbitrary units) vs. Raman shift (usually measured in cm−1). The Raman shift
denotes the change in wavenumber, where the wavenumber w, the frequency ν

1.2.1
Surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy
One popular physical change is surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS)
[7, 13]. It involves creating a surface of metallic nano-pillars and depositing
the sample on top of this surface.
An image of such nano-pillars is shown
in Figure 1.2a. Electromagnetic signals emitted from locations between nano-
pillars are strongly enhanced [19]. The signal enhancement even make single
molecule detection possible [20].
Using a method such as SERS has an eﬀect on the choice of algorithm used to
process the data. For example, diﬀerent statistical assumptions are reasonable
for Raman data collected from SERS. Most measurements in a SERS data
set are simply the background signal from the metal substrate, while a small
number of them are measurements of hot spots. This roughly means that the
intensities of the signals will have a heavy-tailed distribution. Most other Raman
techniques do not follow this pattern and thus the intensities will have e.g. a
normal distribution.
1.3
Problem description and scope
So far we have given a quite general description of source separation and Raman
spectroscopy. In this section we specify what form the problem will take in this
thesis.
==PAGE:15==
1.4 Limitations
5
The problem at hand is blind parametric source separation, i.e. the problem
of identifying the parameters θ of the K source signals S given only the mixture
signals X. We will solve this problem using an autoencoder. That is, a model
which takes X as input, transforms the input to an approximation bθ of the
parameters via a neural network and then transform back to an approximation
bX of the input data.
Since the focus of this thesis is on developing and testing a new method for
solving the problem, we make the following simplifying assumptions about the
data (using the notation from section 1.1):
1. There is only a single source signal, i.e. K = 1.
2. The transformation fφ is the identity function.
3. The noise vector ϵ consists of i.i.d Gaussian noise.
Furthermore, in this thesis we let S be a Gaussian curve as deﬁned in Equa-
tion 2.1, but the model can be extended to other parametric representations.
1.4
Limitations
In this section we explain the limitations of our model, stemming from its as-
sumptions. The model we have created is not immediately applicable to most
Raman data. However, the model is still of interest since it can be aided by
preprocessing data and could be extended to better handle Raman data.
• Number of peaks: The most prominent limitation of our model is that it
assumes that the signal of interest consists of a single peak with Gaussian
noise added. For most Raman data this is not a reasonable assumption,
with the signals containing many peaks. However, the model can still be
tested on some Raman data with appropriate preprocessing, e.g. cropping
the data such that only one peaks remains.
• Baseline: Our model assumes that the signal does not contain a "base-
line" signal. In practice most models working with Raman data try to
model such a baseline, e.g. as a constant value, a straight line or a polyno-
mial. However, one can also preprocess the data by attempting to remove
the baseline from the signal.

1.6
Literature review
In this section we ﬁrst brieﬂy review the literature on latent variable models
for the purpose of source separation. Afterwards, we review in more detail the
literature on the problem of source separation of Raman spectra.
1.6.1
Latent variable models and their applications
Latent variable models are a wide class of models. Given a set of observations
{xn}N
n=1, the goal is to infer a set of latent variables {zm}M
m=1. The idea is that
a "simpler" signal underlies the observation x, where simpler can mean various
things depending on the particular method.
One sense in which the variable z can be simpler is that z has a lower
dimensionality than x. An autoencoder neural network, a type of latent variable
model, is a neural network which transform each xn to a lower-dimensional
variable zn and then tries to transform zn to an approximation ˆxn of xn. This
forces the network to condense the essential information about x into a lower-
dimensional space. After the autoencoder has been trained, using the learned
latent variables rather than the original variables can give better results both for
unsupervised tasks (e.g. clustering) and supervised tasks (e.g. categorization)
[16].
Another way for z to be simpler is to assume that they act as "building
blocks" for the observations x. These are the kinds of latent variable models we
will mainly work with in this report. For example, one could assume that each
x is a linear combination of all the latent variables {zm}M
m=1. Examples of this
are principal component analysis and non-negative matrix factorization.
Latent variable models have many applications in digital signal processing.
They can be used e.g. for the problem of separating audio signals [27, 12] and
creating mathematical models of natural language [4, Section 7][35]. We now
turn to the use of latent variable models and their use for source separation of
Raman spectra.
1.6.2
Simple methods for Raman source separation
We ﬁrst review some of the simplest mathematical methods used for nonblind
and blind source separation of Raman spectra. These methods have, to some
extent, been superseded by more advanced methods or methods with more re-
alistic statistical assumptions, but they are a good starting point.
==PAGE:17==
1.6 Literature review
7
The simplest option for nonblind source separation is classical least squares
(CLS), which models the mixture spectra as a linear combination of the pure
spectra, i.e. X = AS + B [25]. The estimate ˆA is chosen such that the squared
Euclidean distance ||X −ˆAS|| is minimized. Partial least squares (PLS) is an-
other option when the pure spectra are known [6]. However, modeling the mix-
ture spectrum as a linear combination of pure spectra is not always realistic,
since many chemical mixtures behave nonlinearly due to molecular interaction.
[18]
For blind source separation, principal component analysis (PCA) [9] and
independent component analysis (ICA) [36] have been used. However, these
methods have the same problem as CLS, namely the assumption that the mix-
ture spectra can be expressed as a linear combination of a small number of
ﬁxed constituent spectra. Another statistical assumption that may be violated
is that the constituent spectra are independent [22, Section 1]. This assumption
is made by ICA.
1.6.3
Non-negative matrix factorization
Non-negative matrix factorisation (NMF) [28, 21] is a factor analysis method.
PCA and ICA also belong to this family of methods. The three methods diﬀer
by their assumptions about the data. The assumptions of PCA and ICA (or-
thogonal source signals and statistically independent source signals) may not be
realistic for Raman spectra. NMF assumes that X (after baseline correction) is
the product of two non-negative matrices, i.e. X = AS, where A, S ≥0. This
is a natural assumption for Raman spectra, since the source spectra S represent
light intensity (a positive quantity) and the weights A should be positive since
it does not make sense that light intensity could be "subtracted". Because the
assumptions of NMF ﬁt Raman data well, the method is often used for blind
source separation of Raman spectra and we devote a subsection to it. Note that
NMF is sometimes called multivariate curve resolution in the context of Raman
spectroscopy.
The estimates ˆA, ˆS are chosen to minimize ||X −ˆAˆS||There are many dif-
ferent choices for the distance measure || · ||, the optimization algorithm and
the constraints on the estimates ˆA and ˆS. This leads to many variations of the
method.
An example is the article [31] which chooses ||·|| as the usual Euclidean
distance.
Gradient descent is used to update ˆA and ˆS.
After each update,
values in the updated matrix ˆA or ˆS smaller than some threshold ϵ are forced
to approximately 0. This is a type of sparsity constraint and is meant to ensure
that the values in the matrices do not come from noise, but from a meaningful
signal. This relatively simple NMF algorithm is tested on an artiﬁcial mixture
of Raman spectral data where Gaussian noise has been added. As shown in [31,
Fig. 2], the reconstructed spectra ˆS are almost identical to the originals ˆS.
NMF is a non-convex problem in the variables A, S which means that a

globally optimal solution is not guaranteed to be found. Therefore the choice of
optimization algorithm is important. Lee and Seung used a simple-to-implement
update rule to update A and S simultaneously [21, Fig 2]. Another algorithm
often applied to Raman data is alternating least squares, which alternates be-
tween updating A and S [34]. This approach is known as multivariate curve
resolution - alternating least squares (MCR-ALS). The advantage is that op-
timizing A while S is ﬁxed (and vice versa) is a convex problem. However, a
global optimum is still not guaranteed. A third option is using the active set
method as shown in [15].
Two important variations of NMF are:
• Using sparsity constraints. The constraint are modiﬁed in [11] and can be
applied either to the matrix A, S or both, depending on the application.
The loss function is modiﬁed in [29], using ||X −AZS||, where Z is a
smoothing matrix. [29] claims better results than [11].
• Using a bayesian approach to NMF as shown in [26], [32] and [3]. This
allows one to set priors on the elements of A and S to match the character-
istics of the data. Furthermore, it allows one to get conﬁdence intervals for
the ﬁtted values, not just point estimates. A disadvantage of the Bayesian
approach is that it’s computationally expensive since some type of Monte
Carlo sampling has to be performed.
1.6.4
Hard models (parametric models)
Hard models, also known as parametric models, are useful in Raman spec-
troscopy. In these models it is assumed that the source signals in S belong to
a parametric family of functions, e.g. normal distributions or Voigt functions.
Instead of estimating a large vector for each source signal, one estimates a small
number of parameters. This has two main advantages:
• Forcing the source signals to have a certain shape may be more physically
realistic. For example [33, Section 1] argues that Voigt functions should
be used to model the source signals in Raman spectroscopy.
• The peak lokation (which is the most important information about a source
signal) is directly estimated as a parameter. In contrast, postprocessing
is required to ﬁnd the peak of a source function estimated as a vector.
Several articles describe a parametric modelling approach to nonblind source
separation [23, 30, 17, 1]. These articles all deal with the predicting the concen-
trations of chemicals in the mixture and achieve state of the art results on real
data sets.
A parametric modelling approach to blind source separation, called hard
modeling factor analysis, is described in [18]. It shows good results in recon-
structing the source signals and is even used to predict concentrations of chem-
icals in the mixture with good results, albeit not as good as in the nonblind

1.6.5
Neural networks and autoencoders
In this section we look at previous uses of autoencoders for Raman spectroscopy.
Neural networks are also of interest to us since they are part of the deep autoen-
coder model that we will use to perform source separation in this thesis. These
two tools have been used in connection with Raman spectroscopy before, but, to
our knowledge, have not been used to perform explicit blind source separation of
Raman spectra. [24] uses deep convolutional neural networks to classify chemi-
cals based on Raman spectra, but does not perform source separation explicitly
in the process. [5] used a deep autoencoder for classiﬁcation, but similarly let it
act as a black box which did not explicitly perform source separation. Finally,
[10] develops a sparse, deep autoencoder with a non-negativity constraint and
tests its performance on 4 standard data sets, but not on any spectral data.
Sparsity and non-negativity are constraints that make sense for Raman data, so
the model may be well suited for Raman data, but it does not perform source
separation as deﬁned in section 1.1. Rather than optimizing a set of basis vec-
tors or basis functions S which relate to the entire data set, they ﬁnd a latent
variable z for each observation x. Therefore we cannot directly build on this
article either.
In summary, neural networks and deep autoencoders have shown good per-
formance for classifying Raman spectra, but due to our focus on source separa-
tion, we cannot directly build on them.

true peak shape. The result will tend to have its highest value at the location
of the peak unless the signal-to-noise ratio is very low. This is illustrated in

2.2
General description of our model
Our model is an autoencoder neural network which takes a set of Raman spectra
as input and outputs a reconstruction of the Raman spectra. A diagram of the
model is shown in Figure 2.3. The model assumes that each Raman spectrum has
a single peak and can be modelled as a Gaussian curve with noise added. Thus
each spectrum is reconstructed as a Gaussian curve with appropriate location,
width and amplitude parameter.
Let us describe the model in more detail by describing
1. The latent variables of the model
2. The generative model which transforms the latent variables to the recon-
struction of the input
3. The inference model which transforms the input to the latent variables
The latent variables of the model are the location, width and amplitude of the
Gaussian curve. As is usually the case with autoencoders there is a large reduc-
tion in dimensionality when going from a Raman spectrum, usually consisting
of hundreds of real numbers, to the latent variables, which consist of just three
real numbers.
The generative model is more simple than the inference model. Given the
latent variables l, w and a it constructs outputs the Gaussian curve with those
three parameters, as deﬁned in Equation 2.1, and evaluates it in a number of
points such that it matches up with the input spectrum. Thus the generative
model is not a neural network and it does not even require any kind of training.
Due to the complexity of the inference model, we devote the next section to
describing it.

2.3
The inference model
The inference model is the most complicated part of the model. As shown in
Figure 2.3 it consists of a convolutional neural network (convnet), a softmax
operation, two mean operations to compute l and w and a division operation
to compute a. Brieﬂy, the idea behind the structure is as follows:
1. The convolution operation will output high values corresponding to the
ﬁlter which best matches the location and width of the true peak.
2. The softmax operation will emphasise the global maximum of the convo-
lution, corresponding to the best ﬁlter.
3. To infer the location l of the Gaussian curve we take the mean of the
softmaxed convolution over a set of candidate locations λ. Similarly, to
infer the width w of the Gaussian curve we take the mean of the softmaxed
convolution over a set of candidate widths ω.

rameters and perform elementwise division of the Raman spectrum with
the Gaussian curve. This gives us some ratios between the peak from the
data and our constructed Gaussian peak. Taking the mean of these ratios,
we get an estimate of the amplitude parameter a.

2.3.4
Division operation
Finally, having ﬁtted the location l and width parameter w to a Raman spec-
trum, the amplitude parameter can be determined by elementwise division of
the spectrum data and the Gaussian curve with parameters l and w (and am-
plitude parameter 1). It would not be appropriate to divide every element of
the spectrum and Gaussian curve: Values of the Gaussian curve far from the
peak are very small and values of the spectrum are noisy. Using the ratio of
such values would lead to a highly variable estimate. Instead the idea is to only
use information close to the peak. Speciﬁcally, we use only data points which
are at most w distance away from the peak location l. This is often between
10 and 20 data points. The resulting collection of ratios are averaged giving an
estimate of the amplitude parameter. Having obtained l, w and a, the task of
the inference model is complete.

The autoencoder model has been implemented and trained in Tensorﬂow. Source

The ﬁlters F in the convolutional network are trained via back-propagation

the training process, indicating that the initial values of the ﬁlters are close to

2.5
Loss function
The network is trained by minimising the the MSE between the training data X
and the reconstruction bX. Note that the thing we really care about is minimising
the error between the true and estimated peak location (and to an extent the
peak width and amplitude as well). However, we are constructing our model
for blind source separation and therefore it makes sense to train the network
without using knowledge of the true peak parameters.
2.6
Iterative method used for comparison
We need a method to use for comparison with our autoencoder method.
A
natural ﬁrst choice might be a method such as non-negative matrix factorization
(NMF), which is often used to analyze Raman data. However, we have made the
simplifying assumption K = 1, i.e. each spectrum contains only one peak. There
is no straightforward way to incorporate this constraint into NMF. Therefore
there is no straightforward way to compare our autoencoder and NMF on even
ground.
The same problem applies to most other methods used to analyze
Raman data.
We use an iterative method to optimize the parameters l, w and a of a Gaus-
sian curve such that the MSE loss function is minimized. In particular, we use
the L-BFGS-B method as implemented in the package scipy.optimize.minimize
from SciPy version 1.1.0 [14]. The L-BFGS-B method is primarily chosen since
it is able to incorporate upper and lower bounds for the optimization variables.
Since our autoencoder implicitly has bounds for the variables in the form of the
candidate locations and widths, it is important that the iterative method also
does such that the methods are on even ground.
The iterative method may be susceptible to getting trapped in local minima,
especially if the initial guess supplied to the method is far from the global
optimum.
For this reason we run the method many times with a randomly
selected initial guess each time and only use the output from the run with the
best MSE.

2.7
Advantages and disadvantages of our model
A neural network requires a relatively large amount of computing power to train.
However, once the training has been done, using the neural network on new data
requires much less computing power. This is because using a neural network
on new data usually only involves matrix multiplications and convolutions. In
comparison, other state of the art methods, e.g. [2], are iterative and/or use
sampling making them more computationally expensive.
A disadvantage of our method is that, unlike the iterative method used
for comparison, there is less possibility for "ﬁne-tuning" the parameters of the
Gaussian curve. This is because the autoencoder depends on emphasizing the
maximum of the convolution to such a degree that the inﬂuence from other
values of the convolution are negligible. This is turn means that one of the many
candidate parameters is emphasized to such a degree that other candidates are
basically ignored. With a ﬁnite number of candidate locations, this does not
the parameter estimate to get arbitrarily close to the optimal parameter value.
However, this weakness can be alleviated to some degree by having a large
enough number of candidate parameters.

Chapter 4
Discussion and conclusion
In this chapter we discuss the results of our experiments and conclude on our
hypotheses.
4.1
Dicussion
The ﬁrst experiment involves measuring the MSE for the two diﬀerent methods
and the results are shown in Figure 3.3. As mentioned, this experiment is sim-
ply done to conﬁrm that the methods are able to minimize their loss functions
reasonably well. We see that both are in fact able to achieve an MSE value
which is as good as the value achieved by using the noise-less input data as the
reconstruction. However, this does not allow us to conclude that the methods
are performing well, since we are actually interested in how well they can esti-
mate the location parameter. Having established that both methods are able
to minimize their loss functions, we can safely move on to looking at the other
results.
We start with the results for the timing experiments, which are the most
clear cut. Figure 3.6 shows that the wall-clock time for each method is not
signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the PSNR level of the data.
It also shows that the
autoencoder is around two orders of magnitude faster than the iterative method
when working with N = 1000 spectra. This is to be expected, since the cost of
inference for the autoencoder has been amortised by training the neural network.
With the weights and biases already computed, the most expensive operation
for the autoencoder is matrix multiplication.
On the other hand, the itera-
tive method must perform matrix inversions, a more expensive operation. One
may question whether is it reasonable to exclude the neural network’s training
time from the comparison, but the training only has to be performed once. Af-
terwards, an unlimited number of spectra can be processed without having to
==PAGE:42==
32
Discussion and conclusion
retrain the network. Therefore it would not be reasonable to include training
time for the neural network in the comparison.
The number of initial guesses for the iterative method has a strong eﬀect on
the wall-clock time. We have chosen two diﬀerent numbers of initial guesses, 5
and 20, which are the same numbers that are chosen in the experiments related
to hypothesis 1. These experiments show that 5 initial guesses results in worse
performance with respect to the location parameter. Thus we can safely say that
the wall-clock time for the iterative method has not been unfairly overestimated,
since choosing a lower number of initial guesses would harm the performance of
the iterative method far too much.
Next are the results for experiments pertaining to the accuracy of the meth-
ods when estimating the location parameter l. The top plot in Figure 3.4 shows
that for the synthetic data, the iterative method with 5 initial guesses mostly
performs worse than the autoencoder method. Raising the number of initial
guesses to 20 makes the iterative method slightly outperform the autoencoder
for all noise levels. However, the diﬀerence in performance is quite small and it
is not clear whether the diﬀerence is signiﬁcant.
The two bottom plots in Figure 3.4 show that the iterative method outper-
forms the autoencoder in estimating location and amplitude parameters, both
for 5 and 20 initial guesses. The focus of this report is on estimating the location
parameter, since this is what determines the "footprint" of diﬀerent molecules
in Raman spectroscopy. However, all else being equal, it is of course a strength
for the iterative method to estimate the width and amplitude parameter better.
Table 3.2 and Figure 3.5 show the performance of the autoencoder and the
iterative method with 20 initial guesses when applied to the two data sets from
[8]. Here we see a bigger diﬀerence in performance. The table shows that the
mean location error for the autoencoder is 42% higher than for the iterative
method when applied to the data set for concentration 1000 µM MHC. The his-
togram also shows that a location error between 0 and 2 is most common for the
iterative method, while a location error between 4 and 6 is most common for the
autoencoder. For the data set with concentration 2000 µMMHC, the diﬀerence
between the methods is smaller, but the iterative method still performs better.
It is not obvious what statistical distribution the location errors in Figure 3.5
follow and therefore there is no straightforward way of performing a test for
signiﬁcance. However, the overall picture is that the iterative method with 20
initial guesses consistently outperforms the autoencoder, sometimes by a large
margin.
4.2
Conclusion
Hypothesis 1 is rejected. It is clear from our experiments that the autoencoder is
not able to infer the location parameter better than the iterative method, since

the iterative method always performed best, although sometimes only by a small
margin. We also reject the hypothesis that the autoencoder has performance
equal to the iterative method based on the experiments with the data from [8].
It is especially noteworthy that the autoencoder was at its weakest when applied
to the real data from [8] as opposed to the synthetic data. This indicates that
the method may not be so robust when the statistical assumptions about the
data are not fulﬁlled.
Hypothesis 2 is accepted, since our experiments show that the wall-clock
time for our autoencoder method is around 2 orders of magnitude lower than
the wall-clock time for the iterative method when processing N = 1000 spectra.
4.3
Suggestions for further work
There are several promising ways to further develop the autoencoder method.
The most prominent one is adding the ability for the model to ﬁt multiple peaks.
It is not immediately clear how to do this, since the current model depends on
emphasizing a single peak via softmax. A simple idea is to apply the current
model repeatedly. That is, locate one peak using the current model and subtract
the ﬁtted peak from the data. Feed this modiﬁed to the model to ﬁnd the second
peak, subtract the ﬁtted peak second and so on.
Another possible extension is the ability to ﬁt a baseline rather than having
to preprocess the data.
One way of doing this is to incorporate ideas from
the article Peak detection and baseline correction using a convolutional neural
network by Schmidt et. al. (currently under review). In brief, if we choose
convolution ﬁlters that have zero-mean and are symmetric, the convolution will
in theory remove any locally smooth baseline from the signal, eliminating or
reducing the need for pre-processing.
