Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the influence of additively manufactured direct
hard tools on the surface roughness, gloss, and dimension of extruded plastic profiles.
A comparison of the quality of the extrudates produced by conventional and additively
manufactured inserts was performed through a series of experimental runs including post­
experiment measurements and data analysis. The smearing effect observed on the extru­
date surface was investigated. It was found that there is a big difference in surface quality
and dimension between the additive­manufactured tool and the traditionally­manufactured
tool. However, profiles extruded with these hard tools do not differ greatly in roughness
and dimensions. The work presented in this thesis indicates that the additively manu­
factured direct hard tool has great development potential in the field of polymer profile
extrusion.
Keywords: Polymer Extrusion, Laser Powder bed fusion, Fused Deposition Modeling,
Smearing effect, Extrusion simulation, DOE

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
In the last two decades, the plastics industry has been a global success story. Plastics
production ramped up from 180 million tons in 2000 to 380 million tons in 2020. Mean­
while, the current plastics industry shows a need for a combination of high complexity
and flexibility, it cannot be solved by simply adopting the old methods in an accelerated
manner [1]. Industry 4.0 or the fourth industrial revolution allows manufacturers to imple­
ment flexible manufacturing systems that can meet this need, and increase productivity
and cutting costs.
Among the all components of flexible manufacturing technologies, one of particular in­
terest is Additive Manufacturing (AM). This manufacturing process can help to reduce
production lead­time, manufacturing costs, and risks. Currently, there are two main appli­
cation purposes of AM technology: The improvement of a part’s performance and a sim­
plified manufacturing process. AM has a profound impact on the product design, process
flow, production line, factory model, and industrial chain combination due to its ability to
quickly produce complex shapes, and has become one of the most concerned disruptive
technologies in the manufacturing industry. With promising benefits for the development
and manufacturing of hard tools, the use of this manufacturing process in this field is
expected to increase constantly in the future [2].
This project attempts to compare the surface condition and dimension of profiles ex­
truded with three kinds of extrusion hard tools manufactured by different manufacturing
processes.
The novelty of this research project resides in three key aspects:
• The investigation of the influence of polymers, direct hard tool manufacturing meth­
ods, and cooling methods on the quality of extrudates. As far as the author knows,
it has not been studied by the scientific community using scientific methods.
• A special focus on the smearing effect observed on the extrudate surface.
• Using DOE and main effect plot to analyze and visually display the degree of in­
fluence of the three factors mentioned above on extrudates dimension and surface
roughness.

Chapter 2
State of the Art ­ Polymer Extrusion
2.1
Introduction
Extrusion is a widely used manufacturing technology in the field of thermoplastic polymers
manufacturing, which accounts for a large proportion in global polymer consumption. It
is a process used to manufacture products of a fixed cross­sectional profile by pushing
material through a die of the desired cross­section. Products of extrusion are generally
called extrudates.
Polymer extrudates include film, sheet, profile, pipe, lamination, coating of paper, wire
coating and cable covering, etc. They are widely used in many fields such as food, cloth­
ing, construction, and even aerospace[3].
A polymer extrusion system is usually composed of an extruder, a die, a calibration sys­
tem, and an extrudate collection system.
2.2
Extruder
Extruder shown in Figure 2.1 is widely used to melt polymer chips or pellets and provide
molten polymers for the extrusion process. The extruder is a complex system in which
the screws, barrel, and die are the main components to accomplish specific polymer pro­
cessing tasks.
Figure 2.1: Picture of extruder. [4].
The extrusion process can be divided into two types: continuous and semi­continuous.
The extruder used in the former are usually screw extruders, and the latter are usually
plunger extruders. Screw extruders can also be divided into single­screw and multi­screw
extruders. A diagram of components of a typical extruder can be seen in figure 2.2.

When extruding with a screw extruder, the pelletized polymer loaded into the hopper en­
ters the heated barrel by the rotating screw. Due to the external heating of the barrel and
the shear friction heat between the polymer itself and the polymer and the equipment, the
polymer melts and flows. At the same time, the polymer is evenly dispersed by the stirring
and mixing of the screw, and moves forward and compress. Finally, the polymer melt is
pushed out of the extruder and passes the die to form a continuum, which is cooled and
solidified to become a product.
The geometry of a conventional extruder single­screw is shown in figure 2.3. The screw
is the most significant part of an extruder. The performance and energy consumption of
an extruder heavily depends on the design of the screw. By optimizing the screw design,
the production rate of an extrusion line can be significantly improved [5] [6].

2.3
Die Design and Fabrication
2.3.1
Die Design
Die design is one of the most challenging aspects of extrusion engineering, The reason is
that it is difficult to predict the behavior of the melt in an extrusion die, so as to determine
the optimum flow channel geometry from engineering calculations. Nowadays, the profile
extrusion industry is still based on empirical knowledge and experience. Some general
rules that are useful in die design are [6] [7]:
• No dead spots in the flow channel;
• Steady increase in velocity along the flow channel;
• Assembly and disassembly should be easy;
• Land length about 10X land clearance;
• Avoid abrupt changes in flow channel geometry;
• Use small approach angles. generous internal and external radii on all corners; the
smallest possible radius is about 0.5 mm
• Maintain uniform wall thickness
• Avoid very thick walls
• Make interior walls thinner than exterior walls for cooling
• Minimize the use of hollow sections
Figure 2.4 shows applications of these rules to different profiles and flow channel geome­
tries.
Figure 2.4: Examples of application of die design guidelines. [6].

2.4
Calibrators
When the polymer melt is pushed out of the extruder and passes the die to form a con­
tinuum, the forming process has finished. However, if the melt strength of the extrudate
extruded from the die is not sufficient to maintain the desired shape, a calibrator is re­
quired. The calibrator is in close contact with the polymer melt and cools the extrudate.
When the extrudate leaves the calibrator, it has enough strength to be pulled by a haul­
off device, such as caterpillar take­Offs, and the extruded product will not be significantly
deformed. [6].
As discussed by Michaeli[8], there are five main types of calibrators:
• Slide calibrators
• External calibrators with internal air pressure
• External vacuum calibrators (Figure2.5)
• Internal calibrators
• Precision profile pultrusion (Technoform process)
A simpler calibration device is used in the laboratory for extrudates with simple geome­
tries. It is composed of a plate, the middle part of which is designed with the shape of
the final extrudate, a water tank, and rollers. The extrudate first passes through the plate,
where the actual dimensions of the pipe are primarily determined. Then it is cooled in the
water bath [7]. A picture of the plate and the tank is shown in Figure 2.6.
Figure 2.5: Example of the dry vacuum calibration system. [6].
2.5
Extrudate Quality Control
The quality of the extrudate is determined by its final dimensions and surface conditions.
There are a variety of methods that can be used to measure the thickness of the extrudate.
These methods can be roughly divided into contact and non­contact technologies. In con­
tact measurement technology, the micrometer is a commonly used instrument. However,
8

Figure 2.6: Calibration setup for simple geometries extrudates
the micrometer can only be used for point measurement, and it needs to be done manu­
ally. If the thickness changes are small, the spring­loaded dial gauge can be moved on the
extrudate. Contact can cause surface damage, and when the requirements for surface
quality are very high, these methods cannot be used to monitor continuous thickness.
Therefore, in order to avoid surface problems, non­contact methods are becoming more
and more common in automated extrusion production lines. Using radiation sensors, the
non­contact method utilizes common radiation such as α ­rays, β ­rays, γ ­rays, X­rays,
and infrared radiation. All of them have the same working principle, emitting a continuous
stream of radiation from the source in the direction of the material. When the radiation
passes through the extrudate, part of it is absorbed by the material, so the intensity of the
radiation hitting the sensor is lower. The amount of absorption depends on the density
and thickness of the extrudate. If the density is constant, the radiation absorption is a
direct measure of thickness [6].
The surface condition is also a determinant of the final quality of the extrudate. For some
products, visual inspections can be sufficient to evaluate the accuracy of quality. However,
for parts with large widths, fast line speeds, and small tolerance ranges, an automatic
inspection system is required to efficiently evaluate the final surface quality.
Several commercial automatic inspection systems using laser scanning systems have
been developed. The extrudate is scanned with a moving beam from a laser flying spot
scanner. The reflected or transmitted light beam has been changed by the characteristics
of the extrudate and is received by a light­sensitive detector. This design allows very high
scan rates (over 5000 scans per second) and allows 100% coverage of extrudates up to
4 m wide and moving at speed of 1 m/s or higher [6].

Chapter 3
State of the Art ­ Additive Manufactur­
ing
Additive Manufacturing is a process of joining materials to make parts from 3D model
data, usually layer upon layer, as opposed to subtractive manufacturing and formative
manufacturing methodologies [9].
Additive manufacturing technology does not require traditional tools, fixtures, and multi­
ple processing procedures. Using three­dimensional design data, parts of any complex
shape can be quickly and accurately manufactured on one device, thereby achieving ”free
manufacturing”. Additive manufacturing solves the manufacturing problems of many parts
with complex structures, greatly reducing the processing procedures and shortening the
processing cycle. And the more complex the structure of the product, the greater the
advantage of its manufacturing speed.
In the past 20 years, additive manufacturing technology has developed rapidly. The prin­
ciple of additive manufacturing combined with different materials and processes has de­
veloped many additive manufacturing equipment, which has reached more than 20 types.
This technology has been widely used in various fields, such as electronic products, au­
tomobiles, aerospace, medical, military, geographic information, art design, etc.
The development of any technology is accompanied by challenges. For additive manufac­
turing technology, although the overall application scale of it has shown an upward trend
in recent years, the growth rate has gradually slowed down. How to make enterprises
from tentatively trying 3D printing technology to be able to maturely apply the technology
to create value is still a major problem facing the [10].

3.2
Additive Metal Manufacturing
Metal additive manufacturing is a very promising additive manufacturing technology, and
it is a research hotspot in advanced manufacturing technology. Metal additive manufac­
turing technology uses high­energy beams (laser beams/electron beams/arc, etc.) as a
heat source to melt powder or wire materials to form metal parts layer by layer. According
to different energy sources and forming materials, typical metal additive manufacturing
includes Selective Laser Melting, Electron Beam Melting, Laser Engineered Net Shap­
ing, Electron Beam Freeform Fabrication, and Wire and arc additive manufacturing, etc.
Figure 3.1 shows a schematic diagram of these technologies.
Figure 3.1: Additive Manufacturing For Metal.[12]
3.2.1
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)
From a classification point of view, FDM is a kind of material extrusion process, which is
a manufacturing technology of heating and melting various filaments and then stacking
them. The working principle of FDM is: under the control of the computer, the heating
nozzle moves in the XY plane according to the cross­sectional profile information of the
product parts. The thermoplastic filamentary material is sent to the hot melt nozzle by the
wire feeding mechanism, and the nozzle is heated and melted into a semi­liquid state. It
is then squeezed out, selectively coated on the workbench, and quickly cooled to form a
layer of flakes. After the cross­section of one layer is formed, the worktable is lowered
to a certain height, and then the next layer is cladding as if the cross­section outline is
”drawn” layer by layer, and finally, a three­dimensional product part is formed.
The FDM process is very similar to one of the hot glue guns in its physical principle and
is relatively simple. The filaments of polymer material are softened and melted with the
help of heating, and then extruded, that is, pushed and forced through a nozzle with
reduced diameter, and then deposited layer by layer on a building platform or bed [14].
The standard diameter of the filament is 1.75 mm or 3 mm, provided by the spool. The
most common FDM machine has a standard Cartesian structure and extrusion head.
The filament is unrolled from the spool by a stepping motor and a set of gears, knurled
pulleys, or screws. These gears, knurled pulleys, or screws squeeze and squeeze the
filaments and push them into the extrusion head through the guiding PTFE tube. In some
configurations, the filament feeding mechanism is not fixed on the chassis, but is located
directly above the extrusion head and moves with it. In the latter case, the filament is

pulled along the PTFE guide tube by the feeding mechanism and then pushed into the
extrusion head (Figure 3.2) [13] [14].
Figure 3.2: Schematic illustration of the FDM process. [13].
FDM can also be used in the manufacture of metal parts. Figure 3.3 shows the FDM
process. The printed part is defined as ’green part’. The debinded part is defined as
’brown part’. and the sintered part is defined as ’FDMS (Fused Deposition Modeling and
Sintering) part’. When create metal parts by FDMS, the filament used consists of two
parts: metal powder and binder system [15][16]. The composite filament is printed to
produce a green part, during which the polymer is melted and extruded as a binder, but
the metal particles in it are still solid. In the next step, the green part is subjected to a
degreasing process to remove most of the polymer binder to obtain a brown part. Finally,
the brown parts are placed in a furnace and sintered to fuse the metal particles together
to form a dense FDMS part [17]. The FDM tools used in this project is manufactured in
this way.
Figure 3.3: Scheme of metal FDM. [17].
FDM has the common advantages of AM. For example, FDM supports a variety of ma­
terials, does not require supervision, and has a high buy­to­fly ratio. However, FDM also
has many drawbacks. One of the most frequently mentioned shortcomings of FDM is
part quality and detail. Since the material must be extruded in layers and has a certain

3.2.2
Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF)
Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF), also known as Selective Laser Melting (SLM), is a
widely used metal AM technology. The advantages of this process include high dimen­
sional accuracy and good surface roughness[18]. Figure 3.4 shows the principle of the
LPBF process. First, metal powder is sprayed on top of the base (built platform). These
powders are then selectively melted by a laser and then solidified to form a cross­section.
After that, the platform is moved down by one layer of thickness, and another layer of
powder is sprayed on top of the printed part by a powder recoating arm. The powder
is again melted and solidified to form a new cross­section. The continuous melting and
solidification of the multilayer powder lead to the forming of the final part.
Figure 3.4: Laser Powder Bed Fusion principle. [13].
LPBF process parameters can be roughly divided into powder­related, laser­related, and
powder­bed­related variables according to their properties, as shown in Figure 3.5. The
parameters related to the laser system that affect the LPBF process include laser type,
laser power, and spot size. Scanning parameters (such as scanning strategy, pattern
spacing, and scanning speed) significantly affect the component characteristics estab­
lished by LPBF [19]. The third category of LPBF process parameters is powder bed char­
acteristics. In most powder bed processes, the powder is added to the building platform
through a rake mechanism, which is also called recoating. The efficiency of the powder
feeding system is affected by many parameters, including the type of recoater, the num­
ber of powder feedings for recoat, the amount of powder recovered during each feeding
process, and the crucial powder properties. The thickness of the heavy coating is one of
the important process parameters to control the performance of the component. Layer

a different appearance was formed.

[36]. In order to analyze the different gloss of the two areas, surface roughness and

Chapter 8
Simulation
Simulation is a key technology for exploring models to optimize designs. It can also help
manufacturers assess risks, costs, and potential defects. Simulations create increased
process transparency, even process and quality parameters that cannot be directly mea­
sured can be visualized and optimized using simulations.
The combination of 3D modeling and simulation provides a cost and time­efficient ap­
proach for testing and optimizing an extrusion system before manufacturing. According
to the current state of the art, computer simulations offer the possibility of optimizing al­
most the entire extrusion process with the aid of simulations.
8.1
Extrusion Die Simulation
The simulation of extrusion die is an important part of extrusion simulation. With the de­
velopment of extrusion technology, the shape of extrudate has become more and more
complex. Complex extruded shapes with varying cross­sections are usually accompanied
by unbalanced flow, result in non­uniform velocity across the profile, resulting in unbal­
anced orientation and extrudate deformation that is very difficult to eliminate. It used to
be solved by the “cut­and­try” method, but it takes a lot of time and cost. now the problem
is mostly solved by computer simulation along with practical experience.
The 3D polymer extrusion die simulation software provides the ability to simulate and
calculate the flow pattern, pressure, temperature, and shear rate in the mold, and can
make the flow balanced by optimizing the die design, temperature, and pressure. Figure
8.1 shows unbalanced and balanced cross­sections before and after flow balancing.
Figure 8.1: Comparison of the balanced and unbalanced cross­sections. [38]

8.4
Discussion
In general, VEL is a fully functional and user­friendly extrusion simulation software, which
can help in optimizing mold design and manufacturability, predicting extrusion results, and
avoiding unbalanced flow, etc. However, it also has many limitations:
• Use cross­sections instead of 3D design of dies as the simulation object, grids are
generated on cross­sections;
• Temperatures cannot be defined for each section;
• The process of setting material parameters is very complicated.
For more accurate simulation results, 3D simulation software such as ANSYS and ABAQUS
can be used to simulate the extrusion process.

Chapter 9
Discussion
9.1
Polymer Extrusion
Due to its capability of producing parts with complex geometries and high manufacturing
productivity, polymer profile extrusion is widely used. However, the current practice and
industrial manufacturing still heavily rely on empirical knowledge and on­site decisions
during production, such as die design and haul­off speed selection. Many mechanisms in
the polymer extrusion process are remaining unknown, including the behavior of the melt
in the molding die and the calibration system, the smearing effect of the calibration system,
and its influence on the extrudate surface finish and gloss. They can be investigated in
future work.
The unknown of many mechanisms leads to the lack of precise and user­friendly simula­
tion software, which can lead to difficulties in the design and production process. Simu­
lation tools that are more accurate and include the full workflow will make digital testing
possible, enabling faster analysis and design optimization, thereby reducing costs.
9.2
Additive Manufacturing
AM technology is considered a ground­breaking technology, and many companies and
scholars are studying it. A large number of research outputs have promoted the wide
commercial use of this technology and it has bright industrial prospects.
However, due to the complexity of the process, many mechanisms have not been fully un­
derstood, and the predictive model and simulation software are not mature yet. Therefore,
it is necessary to be cautious when using AM technology to manufacture tools, because
tool may have a great impact on the subsequent process in industrial production. Hence
the research on AM technology in extrusion die production is very meaningful

Chapter 10
Conclusion
This thesis has been investigating the influence of additively manufactured direct hard
tools on the final quality of extruded plastic profiles. In order to fully explore the influence
of the direct hard tool, a series of research questions were raised, and the smearing
phenomenon observed during the experiment was also investigated and analyzed.
In chapters 2 and 3, the state of the art of plastic extrusion processes and AM technology
have been elaborated. Special focus was given to plastic extrusion calibration, extrudate
quality control, and metal AM techniques.
Chapter 4 illustrated the measurement strategy and result of three direct hard tools used
in this project. Dimension and roughness were measured to compare the accuracy and
precision of three manufacturing methods. Measurement results also provided significant
information when comparing the quality of extrudates extruded with different tools.
Chapter 5 provided a detailed description of the experimental set up and a list of all 18
experiment runs. The experimental conditions including the polymer heating tempera­
ture, haul­off speed, cooling method, and the rules followed during the experiment are all
explained in detail, and the variables control ensure the accuracy of the following com­
parison.
Dimension, cross­section shape, and roughness of extrudates have been shown and
compared in chapter 6 and further discussed in chapter 9. In particular, as far as the ex­
trudate thicknesses are concerned, cooling methods and the use of calibration die have
been demonstrated to be the most influential factors.
The smearing effect found on the surface of the PP extrudate using calibration water­
cooled in the experiment was illustrated in Chapter 7. Surface roughness and texture
direction index were used to measure the difference in gloss between the smearing zone
and non­smearing zone. However, it turns out that these parameters cannot be used to
reflect gloss difference. A new systematic method needs to be proposed to analyze the
difference in the gloss of the smearing zone.
Chapter 8 showed the simulation process of extrusion using VEL simulation software. The
calculation and setting of related parameters were also illustrated. Afterward, the results
were analyzed and compared with data measured during extrusion. The source of error
and the limitations of the VEL simulation software are discussed.
To summarize, the main benefit related to the implementation of additive manufacturing of
hard tools in the extrusion production process is the capability of manufacturing shaping
die with complex profiles. However, there are two main challenges in achieving this goal.
The first is that post­processing for additively manufactured shaping dies with a complex
profile is difficult and costly. Secondly, if post­processing is not applied to as printed tools,
it is necessary to have high additive manufacturing accuracy and precision. Finally and
most importantly, it must be ensured that the direct hard tool can extrude the extrudate
that meets the final quality requirements.

requirements. The surface roughness of the hard tool does not have a decisive influence

cooling method and a suitable design of the calibration die can effectively ensure the
finish of the final plastic profile. Besides, the hard tool manufacturing method or even the

die, the contact between extrudate and calibration die will significantly affect the final size

Chapter 11
Future work
Although this thesis investigated and discussed multiple experimental questions and phe­
nomena observed in the experiment, more questions and future research topics remain
to be explored. This chapter elaborates on the areas of focus for future work on this topic.
11.1
Hard tool measurement
Since the remaining FDM tools were not received before the end of the project, it was not
possible to measure multiple FDM tools to verify the manufacturing precision of FDM. After
receiving the remaining FDM tool, measurement can be performed. If the dimensions of
the remaining FDM tools are also far from the nominal dimension, then the 3D file needs
to be optimized during the design or printing preparation stage to compensate for the print
error.
In this project, due to limited time and resources, the tested hard tools were not cut for
roughness testing. Instead, replicas were used to reproduce the surface of hard tools.
Although replicas are capable of accurately reproducing the appearance of the surface
texture, and the average roughness of the replica is quite close to that of the master.
However, in future work, the tools should still be cut for measurement to ensure higher
measurement accuracy.
11.2
Experimental setup
Due to time constraints, the influence of the calibration die on the profiles extruded by
the additively manufactured direct hard tool has not been investigated. However, the final
results show that The surface roughness and dimension results of extrudates show that
the use of calibration die can greatly affect the final quality of extrudates. In future work,
calibration dies with different dimensions and surface roughness can be tested to explore
the effect of the calibration die on the molten polymer after it leaves the shaping die.
11.3
Extruded profiles measurement
In this project, the dimension of the profile was characterized by its thickness, but based
on the description of the profile shape in Figure 6.14 ­ 6.16, it can be seen that the cross­
sectional shape of the profile is not a uniform rectangle with rounded corners as expected.
Therefore, using thickness to characterize dimensions is not accurate enough. In future
work, CT can be used to scan the profile to obtain accurate cross­sectional shape and
cross­sectional area.
11.4
Study of. Smearing Effect
The smearing effect observed on the profile and the difference in gloss brought about by
it is a complex interdisciplinary subject in optical, materials science, and mathematics.
The current research has not yet been able to establish a universal model for calculating
gloss through surface roughness or texture. In future work, the first thing to do is to use
the glossmeter to measure the difference in gloss between the smearing zone and the

non­smearing zone, because although the difference in gloss is obvious to the naked
eye, the measurement difference could be very small. This is because gloss is affected
by multiple factors such as roughness, particle size, and particle morphology.
11.5
Simulation
In this project, VEL is used to simulate die flow. Its limitations have been discussed in
Chapter 8. In future work, 3D simulation software can be used to simulate die flow, and
it can even go further and build a full­device 3D model to simulate the whole extrusion
