Abstract 
Peeling is a reaction that occurs on polysaccharides under alkaline conditions and leads to their 
degradation. It can significantly affect their molecular weight, color and purity. This reaction is 
taking place in a step of the production process of an API at Leo-Pharma and causes part of it to 
degrade. In this step, pH adjustment with base is carried out and due to inadequate mixing local 
zones of high pH are generated, which result in peeling of the API. 
The purpose of this project was to investigate if better mixing of the API could be achieved by using 
a RJH system instead of the unbaffled off-center configuration currently used, in order to reduce 
the peeling phenomenon. Thus, mixing time measurements were performed with a RJH and a 
configuration as close as possible to that of Leo-Pharma in a pilot plant, in order to determine their 
performance. The performance of other configurations, such as the standard used for stirred 
tanks, was also examined for comparison purposes. The mixing time was measured by the 
decolorization technique with methyl red as pH indicator in water, which was found to be a 
suitable model liquid in terms of flow behavior to represent the API.  
Among the configurations that used an impeller for stirring with H/T=1 (i.e. the standard, an 
unbaffled with an off-center impeller and an unbaffled with a center-placed impeller), the one 
with baffles and the impeller placed in the center exhibited the best mixing performance. It was 
found that by placing the impeller in an eccentric position faster mixing can be achieved, since the 
configuration with the off-center impeller attained shorter mixing times at same power input 
compared to the similar system with the center-placed impeller. By comparing the mixing 
performance of the RJH with that of two unbaffled off-center mixing configurations, one with 
H/T=1 and another with H/T=0.59, it was found that the off-center configuration with H/T=0.59 
had the best mixing performance and that the RJH was the least efficient. It attained same mixing 
times at considerably larger values of P/V. Nevertheless, it was concluded that a RJH with a larger 
nozzle diameter should be used, which would lead to better results. In this way, more concrete 
conclusions on whether the RJH could lead to better mixing and therefore possibly to peeling 
reduction could be made. In addition, small scale mixing experiments were performed with a 
geometrically similar vessel to the off-center configuration of the pilot plant, for both liquid heights 
(i.e. H/T=1 and H/T=0.59). It was thereby made possible to obtain information about the 
dimensionless mixing time that can be used for design purposes. The power draw in the small-
scale vessel with H/T=0.59 was measured by calorimetry, whereby it was made possible to 
generate a graph of the Po in relation to Re that can be used for scale up/down. 
Peeling experiments were also performed with alginate solutions of two different concentrations 
and API solutions. It was found that no local change of color could be observed in the samples 
when adding base. After the base addition and stirring the color of the solutions still remained the 
same. It was concluded that in the case of the API samples this was probably because they were 
already out of specification. However, in another cycle of peeling experiments the initial solutions 
contained also NaCl except for API, since it was later notified that this was the case in the 
==PAGE:4==
 
procedure applied at Leo-Pharma. It was found that after adding base in these solutions a slight 
color change could be obtained, which was considered to be due to the addition of NaCl that 
increased the rate of the reaction. After further applying heating a color change could be clearly 
seen in some solutions of API and alginate. The absorbance of the solutions was measured before 
and after adding base, and after also applying heating. It was found that it increased after adding 
base and even more after heating, whereby it was deduced that this is an easy method to use in 
order to determine the extent of the reaction taking place after the base addition.

1. Literature survey 
1.1. Introduction 
Mixing is an operation commonly used in current industries, such as the food, chemical and 
pharmaceutical, with the purpose of improving mass and heat transfer and attaining homogeneity 
in the final product. If inadequate it can lead to lower yield and poorer quality of the product, 
which is translated to a significant loss of money for the industry. In order to achieve process 
optimization, it is therefore of crucial importance to acquire better understanding of the mixing 
process and improve the mixing performance. 
 
For this purpose, experiments have to be conducted to obtain insight into the mechanisms 
controlling the mixing process by e.g. using a visualization technique and primarily to measure the 
mixing time to evaluate the efficiency of the process. The mixing time is the time required to attain 
a specific degree of homogeneity in miscible liquids and combined with the power consumption is 
used to quantitatively determine the performance of any mixing configuration. However, it should 
be noted that it depends on the stirrer type, the vessel geometry, the liquid addition method and 
the physicochemical properties of the liquid and is therefore expected to vary for different mixing 
systems.  
 
In the context of this project, it was desired to investigate if better mixing of the API produced at 
Leo Pharma could be achieved by employing a different mixing configuration than the one 
currently used, in order to reduce the “peeling” phenomenon that occurs on pH adjustment with 
base and renders part of the API out of specification. In order to achieve that, mixing time 
measurements were carried out to compare the mixing performance of the different 
configurations. As follows, basic theory regarding mixing is introduced.  
 
1.2. Mixing mechanisms 
1.2.1. Laminar mixing 
Laminar flow is normally encountered when highly viscous liquids are used. For the flow to be 
indeed laminar at common rates of energy consumption, viscosities higher than 10 Pa·s are 
needed. Under these flow conditions, linear viscous forces dominate and cause the rapid 
elimination of nonlinear inertial forces (Nienow, et al., 1992). Fluid particles subjected to the 
strong viscous forces, flow in layers, which results in poor mixing (Cullen, et al., 2015). The rate of 
molecular diffusion in highly viscous liquids is also very slow, as can be seen from the Stokes-

It is, however, possible to improve the mixing performance in the laminar flow regime, by choosing 
the appropriate mixing equipment and using it with the right design and operational parameters. 
Such equipment induces periodic disturbances on the fluid, to disrupt continuous trajectories of 
particles and generate chaotic motion. Under the influence of a chaotic flow field, the fluid 
elements are stretched and, in addition, reoriented due to continuous changes in the flow 
direction. Reorientation brings about the folding of the fluid entities. Hence, the recurrence of 
stretching and folding leads to an exponential increase in the contact area between the liquids and 
an analogous reduction in the segregation scale (see figure 1). As the contact area between the 
liquids becomes larger, molecular diffusion becomes again significant, leading to better mixing 
(Szalai, et al., 2004). 
The process of stretching and folding of the fluid elements is also known as “Baker’s 
Transformation”, since it resembles the stretching and subsequent folding of dough carried out by 
a baker (Cullen, et al., 2015).

1.2.2. Turbulent mixing 
The flow developed in mixing configurations with rotating impellers is turbulent, when the 
viscosity of the fluid is less than 10 mPa·s. In this case, inertial forces are exerted on the fluid by 
the impeller’s rotation and induce its circulation throughout the mixing tank. During the passage 
of the fluid, diffusion of eddies, that is groups of molecules of many different sizes, takes place and 
leads to much more rapid mixing than that encountered in laminar flow. In addition, molecular 
diffusion, which is necessary for ultimate homogenization to be achieved, is considerably faster 
than in the highly viscous liquids associated with laminar flow. Hence, the mixing process in 
turbulent flow is more rapid than in laminar flow (Nienow, et al., 1992). 
As was already mentioned, this is greatly attributed to diffusion of eddies, which is the mixing 
mechanism in turbulent flow and will be elucidated as follows. An eddy, while travelling through 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
the bulk flow, rotates and exchanges material with its proximate environment, which causes 
alterations in its shape and size (Tiwari & Cullen, 2009). The large eddies have a size comparable 
with the impeller diameter and possess an important part of the kinetic energy. Interaction of 
these eddies with slowly-moving streams generates smaller eddies, which break into even smaller 
eddies. During this process, kinetic energy is imparted from larger to smaller eddies in a sequence 
of steps referred to as the turbulent energy cascade (Nienow, et al., 1992). 
1.3. Rheology 
The mixing process is carried out by applying shear forces on the liquid, in order to induce its 
movement. During the application of shear stress, the flow behavior of each sort of liquid is 
different and depends on its properties. In this respect the most important property is the liquid’s 
viscosity, which for some liquids might change when these are subjected to shear stress. Thus, it 
is imperative to be aware of the type of liquid used, in order to appropriately design the mixing 
process and thereby achieve the desired mixing performance. An overview of the different types 
of liquids based on viscosity will be provided as follows. 
1.3.1. Newtonian fluids 
Newtonian liquids have constant viscosity, independent of changes in shear rate. For this kind of 
liquids, the correlation between the shear rate (𝛾̇) and the shear stress (τ) is formed as follows:

Newtonian liquids is no longer constant when changes in pressure or temperature take place, since 
it depends on both of these factors. Examples of liquids that exhibit Newtonian behavior are oil, 
water, sugar solutions and glycerol (Brown, et al., 2004).

Time-dependent fluids 
Two types of fluids belong in this category, the rheopectic and the thixotropic fluids. The viscosity 
of thixotropic fluids gradually decreases with time, when a constant shear stress is exerted on 
them. When the shear stress is no longer applied, a progressive recovery of their original 
properties is observed. On the other hand, rheopectic fluids exhibit the opposite behavior, since 
their viscosity gradually increases during the application of shear stress (Brown, et al., 2004).

Viscoelastic fluids 
These fluids exhibit at the same time viscous and elastic properties. Polymer solutions show this 
kind of flow behavior. The mixing of such fluids by rotating impellers gives rise to the Weissenberg 
effect, where due to the development of normal forces the fluid climbs onto the shaft. This 
phenomenon might be so intense that the fluid reaches parts of the mixing configuration that 
function by electricity, such as the motor. It is obvious that this should be prevented for the 
operation of the mixing system to be conducted without problems (Nienow, et al., 1992). 
1.4. Experimental methods for determination of mixing time 
Mixing time, which is the time required to attain a specific degree of homogeneity in miscible 
liquids, is usually determined by introducing a pulse of a tracer with different properties into the 
bulk liquid and measuring the response (Villadsen, et al., 2011). It is the main parameter used to 
evaluate the performance of any mixing configuration and thus, various experimental techniques 
have been generated for its determination. Some of these techniques will be described in the 
following section. 
1.4.1. Colorimetric Methods 
Colorimetry is a simple, non-intrusive method employed not only to measure the mixing time, but 
also to visually observe the flow patterns developed in a transparent stirred vessel and detect the 
presence of any dead zones (Ascanio, 2015). It is implemented by injecting a colored tracer in the 
bulk liquid contained in the stirred vessel and observing its dispersion. However, the addition of a 
colored tracer to the bulk liquid makes it impossible to detect the last region of mixing because 
this region will be masked by any dye that surrounds it. The de-colorization technique is, therefore, 
to be preferred, whereby it is possible to observe the last area to be mixed. This is because this 
area is the last one to remain colored, whereas the rest of the liquid within the tank has cleared 
(Brown, et al., 2004). 
The de-colorization technique is carried out by coloring the entire liquid content of the stirred 
vessel using a chemical and then adding another chemical, which reacts and removes the color 
(Brown, et al., 2004). The mixing time is then determined as the time period from addition of the 
chemical that removes the liquid’s color until the last wisps of color disappear (Käppel, 1979). Two 
different reactions can be used in the decolorization method: a reaction between an acid and a 
base with an appropriate pH indicator present or the redox reaction between thiosulphate and 
iodine with starch present (Brown, et al., 2004). Good pH indicators are methyl red that turns from 
red to yellow, phenolphthalein that changes from pink to colorless and bromo-phenol blue that 
turns from blue to yellow, whereas the redox indicator thiosulphate-iodine changes color from 
dark blue to colorless (Villadsen, et al., 2011).  
Even though the de-colorization method presents many advantages, such as that it reveals the 
flow patterns and it is simple to implement, there is also a disadvantage associated with it. The 
determination of the end of mixing that takes place at the time point the liquid becomes colorless, 
is not objective, since it is based on the opinion of the person observing (Ascanio, 2015; Nere, et

1.4.2. Tracer Methods 
 
The conductivity method 
The conductivity method is carried out by adding a pulse of concentrated salt to the liquid 
contained in the mixing tank and measuring the conductivity over time by means of a probe 
(Villadsen, et al., 2011). The mixing time is then determined as the time period from addition of 
the concentrated salt pulse until the fluctuations in conductivity have decreased to a specific 
extent.   
This method has the advantages of being cheap and easy to implement. The conductivity probes 
have also very fast response times and the method can, therefore, be applied in systems that have 
short mixing times (Brown, et al., 2004). Nevertheless, it cannot be used in non-conducting 
systems or in systems that contain gas. With regard to the latter case, this is due to the ingress of 
non-conducting gas bubbles into the area between the electrodes that distorts the conductivity 
trace. According to Pinelli et al. (2001), the method can be applied in gas containing systems, if the 
probe is inserted in a cage to protect it from the gas bubbles, which results in a significant reduction 
of the noise level. Another disadvantage of the method is that a limited number of experiments 
can be carried out using the same liquid, since the accretion of salt decreases the probe’s 
sensitivity (Villadsen, et al., 2011).

The pH method 
The pH method is based on adding a basic or acidic tracer to the liquid of the mixing tank and 
monitoring the pH response by means of a probe.  
A drawback of this method is that the response times of pH probes are long. It should, therefore, 
not be applied for mixing time measurement of laboratory scale systems, since these have very 
short mixing times. On the positive side is that pH probes can be used in systems that contain a 
gas phase, since their measurement is not affected by the presence of gas bubbles (Villadsen, et 
al., 2011; Nordkvist, 2005)  
At this point, it should be pointed out that some issues regarding the use of probes, encountered 
both in the conductivity and pH method, should be taken into consideration. The presence of 
probes in a mixing vessel disturbs the bulk flow. In order to minimize this undesirable effect, the 
number of probes should be as limited as possible. Furthermore, it is important to choose the right 
location for the probes in the mixing tank, in order to obtain accurate measurements of the mixing 
time. The probes should be placed in the regions of the vessel that are less fiercely agitated, since 
these are going to be the last to become mixed. Thus, de-colorization experiments should initially 
be performed to detect where these poorly mixed regions are located (Brown, et al., 2004). 
The thermal method 
This method is implemented by adding a liquid tracer pulse with different temperature from the 
bulk liquid and measuring the temperature by thermocouples. 
A drawback of the method is that it is not appropriate for liquids with a viscosity susceptible to 
changes in temperature (Villadsen, et al., 2011). 
The fluorophore method 
The use of fluorophores as tracers is another possible method to perform mixing time 
measurements. This is attested by the study of Einsele et al. (1978), who added quinine as a tracer 
to a culture of S. cerevisiae and then measured fluorescence to determine mixing times.  
Αn advantage of the method is the fast response time. Nevertheless, as it is also true for the 
conductivity method, the existence of gas bubbles distorts the measurement of the sensors, and 
it is necessary to change the liquid, because after few experiments the sensitivity decreases 
considerably. In poor sensitivity results also the high level of inherent fluorescence of some 
fermentation media and thus, the method is inappropriate to be used on such media (Villadsen, 
et al., 2011). 
 
The radiotracer method  
In this method a radioactive tracer is used and by means of scintillation detectors the radioactivity 
is monitored. The scintillation detectors are installed outside the wall of the mixing vessel, which 
means that the method is non-invasive (Villadsen, et al., 2011; Nere, et al., 2003). This technique 
can also be used to measure the mixing time in systems that contain gas.

On the negative side is that the liquid volume measured by the scintillation detector is quite large 
when compared to the working volume of the vessel, leading to averaging effects (Villadsen, et al., 
2011). In addition, many safety issues should be taken into consideration before implementing the 
method (van't Riet & Tramper, 1991). 
 
1.4.3. Model liquids 
Mixing of various kinds of liquids is encountered in the chemical, pharmaceutical, food and other 
process industries. However, when mixing experiments have to be conducted, it is not always 
possible to use the actual liquids. For this reason, liquids with suitable properties to represent the 
actual liquids are being used, called model liquids. The properties of some of these liquids will be 
described as follows. 
 
Water 
Water can be used in mixing experiments to simulate the rheological behavior of Newtonian 
liquids in the turbulent regime. As a model liquid it presents many advantages, since it is readily 
accessible, cheap and safe to use. Furthermore, its disposal is inexpensive and easy. 
 
Glucose syrup 
Glucose syrup is a transparent liquid that exhibits also Newtonian behavior and has very high 
viscosity. It is indicative that at room temperature its viscosity is approximately 150 Pa·s. Due to 
its high density and viscosity, it cannot be easily diluted to generate solutions of the desired 
viscosity. One possible method to achieve this is by slowly adding the glucose to a mixing tank 
containing the amount of water needed. An alternative is to use a mixer with high mixing capacity 
to vigorously stir the liquid. Once dilutions of the syrup are obtained, it is important to add 
fungicide and biocide, because otherwise fermentation will occur and render the liquid unusable. 
 
Glycerol 
Glycerol is a viscous liquid that has Newtonian behavior. At room temperature the viscosity of 
99.9% glycerol is approximately 1.6 Pa·s. It is a more transparent liquid compared to glucose syrup 
and it can, therefore, be used for LDA experiments. Another property of glycerol that one should 
be aware of, is its high hygroscopicity. It will absorb water from the air, which should be hindered 
to preserve its viscosity. 
 
CMC 
Sodium carboxymethylcellulose (CMC) is available in various grades, which can be used to 
generate solutions either with newtonian or pseudoplastic properties. CMC solutions are 
inexpensive and have a viscosity relatively insensitive to slight changes in dilution and

ensure that they can be used for the required period of time, their degradation should be 
prevented by adding fungicides. On the other hand, CMC solutions are inappropriate for

Sodium alginate 
Sodium alginate is a polysaccharide usually extracted from seaweed. More specifically, it is a 
copolymer composed of α-L-guluronic acid and β-D-mannuronic acid linked by 1-4 glycosidic 
bonds. It is widely used in biomedical, food and pharmaceutical industries due to its stabilizing 
properties and gelling ability. Aqueous solutions of sodium alginate have high viscosity and exhibit 
shear-thinning non-Newtonian behavior. They can be used as model liquid to investigate the 
peeling reaction that occurs on polysaccharides under alkaline conditions and leads to their 
degradation, as was done in the experiments of this project (Ma, et al., 2014; Belalia & Djelali, 
2014). 
 
1.5. Mixing in mechanically stirred vessels 
In industry mixing of fluids is commonly performed in mechanically stirred vessels to meet various 
objectives. However, there are many different geometries and types of mixing equipment that can 
be employed to design a mixing system. Their choice is crucial to construct an effective mixing 
system and should be based on the properties of the liquid to be mixed and the specific process 
requirements. The following section presents the geometry of a conventional stirred vessel and 
the types of agitators that can be used in a mixing configuration. 
 
1.5.1. Mixing equipment 
Mixing is usually performed in stirred tanks of cylindrical shape, such as the one shown in Figure

tank walls (Doran, 2013). Baffles are used in mixing of low viscosity liquids to prevent swirling of 
the liquid, since it can result in poor mixing (Hemrajani & Tatterson, 2004). An impeller is used to 
carry out the mixing duty and is mounted on the centrally located shaft, which is driven by the 
motor. Τhe rotation of the impeller has the effect of pumping the liquid and creating a consistent 
flow pattern. Τhe liquid is forced to move away from the impeller’s vicinity, circulates through the

1.5.4. Eccentric position of impeller in mixing configurations without baffles 
The most commonly used geometry of stirred tanks is the one with baffles and the impeller 
placed in the center of the vessel. However, there are some applications for which vessels 
without baffles are more suitable, such as for mixing of high viscosity liquids, in crystallization or 
in the pharmaceutical and food industry where cleanliness is a priority. In such stirred vessels the 
lack of baffles, which results in inefficient mixing due to the rotation of the liquid as a whole mass, 
is counterbalanced by placing the impeller in an off-center position. Results of research carried 
out by using unbaffled stirred vessels with an off-center impeller will be summarized as follows. 
 
Karcz et al. (2005) conducted mixing experiments using the thermal method in an unbaffled 
stirred tank with an off-center propeller to study the effect of eccentricity on the mixing time. 
They used a tank with a diameter of 0.7 m, a working volume of 0.27 m3 and a H/T ratio equal to 
one. The eccentricity was varied in a range between 0 and 0.57 and it was found that by 
increasing the eccentricity the mixing time decreases, whereas the power consumption 
increases. Galletti et al. (2009) used various techniques (i.e. flow decolorization and visualization, 
laser Doppler anemometry) to examine the effect of eccentricity on the features of flow 
developed in an unbaffled vessel that was agitated by an off-center Rushton turbine. The tank 
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
had a diameter of 290 mm and a H/T ratio equal to one. It was found that two vortices are 
formed, one below and one above the impeller. The upper vortex had a dominant influence on 
the flow field and presented an inclination that increased with increasing eccentricity. In addition, 
it oscillated inducing flow instabilities that are of great importance for macromixing. The 
frequency of the instabilities was found to decline with increasing eccentricity. 
The same finding regarding the presence of two vortices and the generation of flow instabilities 
by the upper vortex was also made by Ameur (2016). In his study a numerical method was 
performed to investigate mixing of shear thinning liquids by an off-center six-curved blade 
impeller inside a cylindrical unbaffled vessel. It was found that placing the impeller in an eccentric 
position led to a reduction of the segregated areas in the entire vessel. Also, the best mixing 
features could be achieved by placing the impeller at one third of the height of the vessel and an 
eccentricity between 24 and 48 % of the diameter of the vessel. 
 
1.6. Scale-up of stirred tanks 
For the design of an industrial mixing process, research is often carried out to determine the 
optimal mixing configuration and operating conditions by performing laboratory scale 
experiments, which are easier and less costly compared to full-scale experiments. The results are 
used to scale up from laboratory to industrial size. Scale up, is, however, not straightforward and 
many issues have to be considered, in order to design an industrial mixing system with the same 
quality of mixing as in the laboratory scale system.  
 
An issue of great importance concerns the choice of the scale-up method to be used. Every 
method is normally based on geometric similarity between the two systems. This means that 
geometrical ratios such as D/T, C/T, W/D and H/T must be held constant. Nevertheless, complete 
geometric similarity cannot always be achieved (Hemrajani & Tatterson, 2004).  
 
The scale-up criterion applied is what differentiates the method and should be chosen according 
to the sort of mixing and the process objective (Chhabra & Richardson, 2008). A simple criterion 
that can be used is constant tip speed (πND), which results in a lower impeller speed and a longer 
mixing time in the large vessel. Constant power per unit volume (P/V) is a criterion commonly 
applied in industry  (Chhabra & Richardson, 2008), which leads to a decrease in the impeller 
speed and an increase in the mixing time. Another criterion that can be used is constant mixing 
time, which is equal to constant impeller speed. This criterion is necessary for fast reactions and 
results in a considerable increase in the power. Constant torque per unit volume (Tq/V) can also 
be applied (Hemrajani & Tatterson, 2004). Thus, there are various scale-up methods and in order 
to choose the appropriate one for the given conditions, one should consider the changes in the 
process parameters and how these will affect the process result.

1.7. Mixing by RJH 
1.7.1. Introduction to RJH 
Rotary jet heads were originally exploited for tank cleaning purposes. The walls of an empty tank 
were sprinkled with jets of cleaning solutions, which due to rotation could reach all parts of the 
tank. They are also commonly used in the chemical and food industry for effective cleaning in 
place (CIP) of vessels (Nordkvist, 2005). In the period 2001-2003 the rotary jet head was 
characterized for submerged mixing and after its modification by the company Iso-Mix, it has 
been used as a mixing system  (Nordkvist, et al., 2003). A rotary jet head is shown in Figure 10. 
 
 
Figure 10: Illustration of a rotary jet head: 1. guide, 2. turbine, 3. distributor arms, 4. nozzles (Nordkvist, et al., 
2003) 
 
The liquid is transferred by means of a pump from the bottom of the tank to the rotary jet head. 
It enters the rotary jet head through a guide (1) and reaches the turbine (2), which starts to 
rotate. The rotation of the turbine is, through a gearbox, transformed into vertical rotation of the 
distributor arms (3) and concurrently horizontal rotation of the RJH body. The liquid passes then 
through the distributor arms and is released from the nozzles to sweep all tank internals. The 
sweeping pattern of the liquid jets is repeated after 45 vertical rotations and 43 horizontal 
rotations. The time needed for the pattern to be completed depends on the liquid flow rate, 
which can be adjusted by changing the applied pressure. If the applied pressure is increased, the 
flow rate increases and thereby the time is reduced  (Nordkvist, et al., 2003).

1.7.4. Previous work with RJH 
This section provides an overview of some of the conclusions drawn by Vognsen (2007), who 
conducted mixing experiments with rotary jet heads and changed various parameters to 
investigate their influence on the system. For the experiments two different types of RJH were 
used, the IM15 with a nozzle diameter of 7 mm and the IM10 with a nozzle diameter of 3.9 mm. 
The decolorization technique was employed for determination of mixing time. 
 
Experiments were carried out using the IM10 with its standard turbine and with a modified one 
that resulted in lower rotational speed of the RJH. It was found that the slower rotation increased 
the distance that the jets traveled and thereby led to a significant reduction of the mixing time. 
Thus, when designing a RJH mixing system it is important to take into account the effect of the 
rotational speed on the distance that the jets travel, since this affects the mixing time. 
 
The mixing time depends also on the feed position of the tracer. This was investigated by 
performing experiments, where the tracer was added either to the surface of the liquid or 
through the recirculation loop. It was found that when adding from the top the mixing time was 
2.6 times longer than when adding through the loop. The fact that faster mixing is achieved by 
adding through the loop was considered reasonable, since in this way the tracer is distributed 
throughout the entire liquid volume and thus, more efficiently than adding it in one specific area, 
as the liquid’s surface. 
 
In addition, it was of interest to examine the effect of the position of the RJH on the mixing time 
attained. The RJH was, therefore, moved from its standard position in the middle of the liquid, to 
the bottom of the vessel and larger mixing times were obtained. The increase in mixing time by 
placing the RJH lower into the liquid was attributed to zoning and to poorer tracer distribution, 
since an excess of tracer was added to the bottom of the vessel, where only a small volume of 
the whole liquid was located. 
 
Another parameter that was varied to understand its influence on mixing time was the liquid 
height. The geometry was changed from H/T=1 to H/T=2.5 and the mixing time obtained was 
approximately 2 times longer at low flow rates and 2.6 longer at high flow rates. The larger 
difference in mixing time at high flow rates was because mixing at H/T=1 became faster and faster 
with increasing flow rate compared to mixing at H/T=2.5. The reason for the slower mixing at 
H/T=2.5 was found to be the difficulty of the RJH to reach the higher part of the vessel. By using 
a modified turbine at H/T=2.5 to cause slower rotation, the reach of the RJH improved and 
thereby the mixing times obtained were not much longer than those for H/T=1.

1.8. The peeling reaction 
Under alkaline conditions polysaccharides are degraded through a reaction known as peeling or 
endwise depolymerization. During this reaction, which occurs in bleaching and alkaline pulping 
processes, monosaccharide units are cut off from the reducing end groups (Roberts, 2007). 
 
The mechanism of the reaction is shown in Figure 11. The reaction is initiated by isomerization 
of the reducing end to a ketose. The glucosidic linkage in the 4-position of the ketose is cleaved, 
resulting in detachment of the end group and tautomerization to a 4-deoxy-2,3-glucodiulose. The 
2,3-diulose structure is then subjected to benzylic acid rearrangement and forms isosaccharinic 
acids. Several other acids are also generated by competing mechanisms. (Ek, et al., 2009; Hon & 
Shiraishi, 2001; Sjöström, 1993)  
 
 
Figure 11: Mechanism of peeling reaction (Biermann, 1996)  
 
The peeling reaction proceeds until a stopping reaction occurs or it is physically impeded, for 
instance by a crystalline region (Franzon & Samuelson, 1957). In the stopping reaction, shown in 
Figure 12, beta-hydroxy elimination takes place on the C-3 position. The end group undergoes a 
benzylic acid rearrangement and thereby forms a metasaccharinic acid, which is stable under 
alkaline conditions (Sjöström, 1993). In the case of cellulose, 65 glucose units are removed before 
the end group becomes stable (Franzon & Samuelson, 1957).

2. Experimental part 
The purpose of the experiments was to investigate if better mixing of the API produced at Leo-
Pharma could be achieved by using a RJH instead of an agitator in a mixing configuration without 
baffles, in order to reduce the peeling phenomenon that occurs on pH adjustment with base and 
renders part of the API out of specification. Thus, as a first stage mixing experiments were 
performed in a pilot plant with a mixing configuration as close to that of Leo-Pharma as possible 
and with a RJH mixing configuration, in order to compare the mixing performance of the two 
systems and conclude on whether the RJH could lead to more efficient mixing and thus, possibly 
to reduction of peeling. Mixing time measurements were also carried out with other 
configurations that used an agitator for stirring, such as the most common with baffles and a 
center-placed stirrer, for comparison purposes. 
As a second stage, peeling experiments were conducted in laboratory scale using the API and 
alginate that was considered to be a good model liquid to be used instead of the API for larger 
scale peeling experiments, with the purpose of: 
• inducing peeling to both of them by adding base and observe if any color change occurred, 
• finding a method to determine the degree of peeling provoked 
• and preparing for mixing experiments in the pilot plant using alginate instead of the 
expensive API, where the degree of peeling after base addition could be determined and 
correlated with the performance of the mixing configuration and the operational 
parameters used. 
 
In addition, peeling experiments were performed according to the procedure followed at Leo-
Pharma to investigate how peeling took place and if any specific process parameters accounted 
for the peeling phenomenon. Small-scale mixing experiments with a configuration close to that 
of Leo-Pharma were also carried out to obtain data for design purposes. The procedure and the 
equipment used for the aforementioned experiments will be described in further detail in the 
following sections.

2.2. Model liquid for mixing experiments 
Rheological measurements of four API solutions with different concentrations were performed, 
in order to conclude on a model liquid that could best fit the rheological behavior of the API 
and would, thus, be used for the mixing experiments. The concentrations of the four API 
solutions were 5, 7, 9 and 11 wt. %. These values were thereby chosen, so that one of the 
solutions would have exactly the same concentration as that of the API solution used at Leo-
Pharma, while the other solutions would have a concentration slightly higher or lower than that. 
The experimental configuration used for the rheological characterization of the API solutions 
was an AR2000 Rheometer from TA Instruments (see Figure 16). This is a rotational viscometer 
that is comprised of two recessed end concentric cylinders, -a geometry suitable for rheological 
measurements of low viscosity liquids-, with the inner cylinder rotating and the external one 
remaining stationary (Searle method). The inner cylinder (rotor) is usually referred as bob, 
whereas the external cylinder (stator) is referred as cup (Liptak, et al., 2016).  
 
Figure 16: The AR2000 Rheometer used for the rheological characterization of the API solutions 
In the specific rheometer used, the bob had an outer radius of 14 mm and the cup had an inner 
radius of 15 mm, so that the gap formed between them was 1 mm. The viscosity measurement 
of each API solution was conducted, by initially calibrating the rheometer and then inserting 
about 7 ml of the solution into the gap, where due to the rotation of the inner cylinder the liquid 
was sheared. More specifically, controlled shear stress experiments (approximate shear stress 
range: 0 - 4.5 Pa) were carried out at a temperature of 25oC, where a specific torque was applied 
and the resulting rotational speed was measured (as in Figure 17). The torque is correlated with 
the shear stress and the rotational speed with the shear rate. In this way, exact values of the 
shear stress and the corresponding shear rate were obtained. Based on the knowledge of these 
two values, the dynamic viscosity of each solution was determined.

2.3. Method for measurement of mixing time 
For the determination of mixing time, various methods were considered. Among them were the 
conductivity and the pH method, since the mixing tank was equipped with a conductivity sensor 
and a pH electrode. However, none of these methods was chosen for different reasons. It was 
decided not to proceed with the conductivity method, because it can be used for a limited 
amount of experiments and its sensitivity gradually decreases. The pH method could not be 
used, since the pH electrode available had very long response time and this would lead to 
overestimated mixing time measurements. In addition, both of these methods use probes, 
which should be placed in the last unmixed zones to obtain correct mixing time measurements. 
The last unmixed zones of the mixing systems used were in different areas and it was not 
feasible to install probes in some of these areas. For all the above reasons, it was decided to 
use the decolorization technique, which could be applied since the mixing vessel was

2.4. Experimental setup for small-scale mixing experiments 
Mixing time measurements were carried out in a small cylindrical vessel that was geometrically 
similar to the unbaffled off-center configuration of the pilot plant, in order to obtain data for 
design purposes. The vessel had a diameter of 16 cm and was mounted with a 45o pitched blade 
turbine, which had a diameter of 5 cm and an eccentricity similar to that of the larger scale 
configuration (e/R=0.275). The base of the vessel was, however, torispherical and not conical as 
that of the configuration in the pilot plant. Two water volumes were used to simulate the liquid 
heights applied in the larger scale mixing experiments, namely 2.2 L and 3.5 L. The volume of the 
base added as tracer was 0.4 ml for the small bulk volume and 0.6 ml for the large one. The 
additions were made by means of a pipette from the top of the vessel and the mixing time was 
measured for impeller speeds in a range between 100 and 500 rpm. For each speed four to nine 
measurements were taken consecutively, using again the decolorization technique as described 
for the mixing experiments carried out in the pilot plant.

2.6. Experimental procedure for peeling experiments 
Peeling experiments were carried out in laboratory scale using the API and alginate. Two cycles 
of experiments were conducted. The purpose of the first cycle was to induce peeling to both the 
alginate and the API and see if it was possible to visually observe it, find a method to determine 
the degree of peeling, and prepare for larger scale peeling experiments. In the second cycle of 
experiments, the procedure of Leo-Pharma that resulted in peeling was imitated, in order to 
investigate if any specific parameters applied during the process could account for the peeling 
phenomenon.  
For all experiments solutions of alginate and/or API of various concentrations were prepared in 
triplicates. Their absorbance was measured using the spectrophotometer Shimadzu UV-1280 and 
quartz cuvettes in a wavelength range between 200 and 500 nm, after the solutions have been 
diluted 20 times. Photos were also taken of the initial solutions and after making any kind of 
addition. 
==PAGE:39==

To carry out the first cycle of experiments, API solutions of 9 wt. % concentration and alginate 
solutions of 0.5 wt. % and 4 wt. % concentration were prepared. Their volume was 40 ml. The 
base used was 1M and 5M sodium hydroxide. Two separate sets of experiments were conducted, 
one for each concentration of base, where 0.5 ml of base was added to all the concentrations of 
alginate and API. The addition was made in a specific area without stirring, to create conditions 
of high pH locally to observe peeling. The absorbance of the solutions was measured before 
adding base and after adding it and applying stirring. The solutions were also heated to 80o and 
their absorbance was measured to investigate the influence of an increase in temperature on the 
reaction taking place. 
To imitate the procedure followed at Leo-Pharma, solutions with 9 % w/v API and 10 % w/v NaCl 
of 200 ml were prepared. The NaCl used was common kitchen salt. Initially, 0.5 ml of 30 % w/v 
NaOH were added locally to the solutions without stirring. Their absorbance was measured 
before the base addition and after the base addition and stirring. Then 15 ml of 2 % w/v H2O2 
were added and the absorbance of the solutions was again measured. In the last stage, 0.25 ml 
of 30 % w/v NaOH were added without stirring and the absorbance was measured after stirring. 
It should be noted that the absorbance was measured after each addition to determine in which 
step peeling took place.

3. Results & Discussion 
In order to fulfill the objectives of this project, mixing and peeling experiments were carried out. 
The results of these experiments are presented and elaborated on in this chapter. 
In the first part of this chapter the results of experiments that were carried out as a preparatory 
step before the mixing experiments are presented. The purpose of most of these was to verify 
that the equipment necessary for the mixing experiments provided correct measurements, 
whereas other experiments were performed to determine the time needed to add the tracer 
through the loop in the RJH mixing system. 
3.1. Time needed for tracer addition through the loop 
In the RJH mixing system the tracer is added through the loop and has, therefore, to travel from 
the plastic containers until the nozzles of the RJH to be injected in the liquid. The time point at 
which the tracer is added in the liquid must be known for the measurement of mixing time. For 
this reason, experiments were performed to estimate the time needed for the tracer to reach 
the nozzles of the RJH, so that it was possible to determine the time point of tracer addition. In 
these experiments water was used and methyl red as pH indicator. The water had an intense 
yellow color due to the initial basic pH. This was desired so that it could be easily observed when 
the acid used as tracer was injected in the liquid, since the acid would have red color. In this way, 
the time needed for tracer addition was estimated as the time from the start of pump P2 (used 
to add the tracer), which was known, until the first spouts of red color could be observed. An 
example of the time point of tracer addition where the first red-colored jets can be seen is 
presented in Figure 22. 
 
Figure 22: Example of the tracer addition time, where the first red-colored jets of acid can be seen 
This experiment was carried out for various flow rates, since the time needed for tracer addition 
would be different for a high flow rate compared to a low flow rate, and two quantities of acid:

4. Conclusions 
Mixing, which is a unit operation widely used in current industries, can have a significant effect on 
the outcome of the whole process. If inadequate it can lead to lower productivity and poorer 
quality of the final product.  
Such an issue has to be confronted in the process applied by Leo-Pharma to produce an API, where 
due to inadequate mixing of the API a phenomenon known as peeling occurs on pH adjustment 
with base that renders part of it out of specification. This results in loss of product and therefore 
loss of money. 
In this project it was desired to examine the mixing performance of the RJH system versus that of 
the unbaffled off-center mixing configuration currently used at Leo-Pharma, in order to conclude 
on whether the RJH could lead to better mixing and thereby possibly to reduction of peeling. 
Therefore, the mixing time had to be measured with both configurations to determine their 
performance. Among various methods that were tried for determination of mixing time, the 
decolorization technique with methyl red as pH indicator proved to be the most appropriate given 
the available equipment. To implement this method, the time point of tracer addition had to be 
known, as is the case for most similar techniques. For the RJH this was an issue, since the base 
used as tracer was added through the loop and it led to decolorization, which made it impossible 
to observe when it was added. However, by conducting experiments the time it takes for the tracer 
to travel from the containers to the nozzles of the RJH was measured and thereby the time point 
of tracer addition was determined. After succeeding in developing a robust method, experiments 
with both mixing systems were carried out in water, which was found to be a suitable model liquid 
in terms of rheological behavior to represent the API. In addition, mixing time measurements were 
conducted with other configurations, such as the standard with baffles and a center-placed stirrer, 
for comparison purposes. 
The results of the experiments showed that among the mixing configurations with H/T=1 that 
employed a PBT for stirring, the system without baffles and the impeller placed in the center had 
the poorest mixing performance. It attained the longest mixing times at same power input 
compared to the other configurations, which is reasonable since the lack of baffles results in 
swirling of the liquid. The unbaffled system with the off-center impeller proved to be considerably 
more efficient, from which it can be concluded that by placing the impeller in an eccentric position 
faster mixing can be achieved. The configuration with baffles and the stirrer placed in the center 
was found to attain the best mixing performance, which was, however, close and became even 
equal to that of the off-center configuration as the power input increased. Taking this into account, 
it can be deduced that when unbaffled systems have to be used placing the impeller eccentrically 
can compensate to a great extent for the lack of baffles. 
The RJH was found to be less efficient compared to both unbaffled off-center mixing 
configurations. These were different only in the liquid height, the one with H/T=0.59 was used to 
imitate as closely as possible the configuration of Leo-Pharma and the other with H/T=1 for a more 
==PAGE:78==
70 
 
fair comparison with the RJH. From the results it was made obvious that the RJH attained same 
mixing times at much higher values of P/V. It was concluded that this was probably due to the 
characteristics of the specific RJH model used. If a RJH with a larger nozzle diameter was used the 
power input to achieve a specific mixing time would significantly decrease, which would lead to 
much better results for the RJH. On the other hand, among the two off-center mixing 
configurations the one with the lower liquid height achieved the fastest mixing, which was 
probably due to the smaller volume and the fact that mixing in the top could be attained faster, 
since the impeller was closer to the surface due to the lower liquid height. 
Small-scale mixing experiments were also conducted with a configuration that was geometrically 
similar to the unbaffled off-center configuration of the pilot plant, for the two different liquid 
heights used in the larger scale experiments (H/T=1 and H/T=0.59). In this way, it was made 
possible to obtain a graph, where the Ntm for the different sized configurations and both 
geometries was plotted against the Re. From the results obtained with the small-scale 
configuration for the two different geometries it was concluded that for approximately half the 
volume, the number of impeller revolutions needed for homogenization was about half, which 
indicated that the pumping rate of the impeller was the same in the two volumes. It was also 
observed that under turbulent flow conditions the Ntm values of the different sized systems were 
relatively constant. Furthermore, the power draw in the small-scale configuration with H/T=0.59 
was determined by calorimetry. It was thereby made possible to determine power numbers and 
examine whether these are scalable, since values of the power numbers of the corresponding off-
center configuration in the pilot plant were available. It was observed that the Po values for the 
small-scale configuration and Re>104 followed a descending trend, which is contrary to what is 
known for the Po in baffled vessels under turbulent flow conditions (i.e. constant Po for Re>104).  
It was concluded that this could be because the mixing configuration studied had no baffles and 
that in such systems the power numbers are expected to become constant at higher Re.  
In addition, two cycles of peeling experiments were performed. In the first cycle, where solutions 
of 0.5 and 4 wt. % alginate and 9 wt. % API were used, the purpose was to induce peeling to both 
types of polysaccharide, see if it was possible to visually observe it and find a method to determine 
the degree of peeling. Base was, therefore, added to each of the solutions and their absorbance 
was measured before and after adding base, and after further applying heating. Two separate sets 
of experiments were conducted, in order to use two different concentrations of base, namely 1M 
and 5M. It was found that no local change of color could be observed in the samples when adding 
base, which was not expected bearing in mind that in the process applied at Leo-Pharma a local 
change of color in the API is clearly seen. After adding base and stirring it was again not possible 
to see a color change in the samples. It was concluded that in the case of the API samples this was 
probably because the API was already out of specification. Thus, the sites on the molecule prone 
to peeling were already removed. After further heating the samples it was made possible to 
observe a color change in some, which cannot be regarded as an indication of peeling since it is 
likely that other reactions were also stimulated by heating. 
==PAGE:79==
71 
 
In the second cycle, the experiments were conducted according to the procedure followed at Leo-
Pharma and therefore, a solution with API and NaCI was used. Initially, base was added to this 
solution, and a color change was obtained. It was concluded that this was probably due to the 
addition of NaCl that increased the rate of the reaction. In the next step, hydrogen peroxide was 
added that removed the color of the sample. Any conclusion about its effect on the reaction could, 
however, not be made by looking at the absorbance curve. 
With regard to the results of the absorbance measurements, these showed that the absorbance 
of the samples increased after adding base and even further after also applying heating, especially 
at a wavelength area between 250 and 300 nm. Taking this into account, it can be concluded that 
after the base addition a chemical reaction is taking place that is intensified by heating, leading to 
even higher absorbance. Measuring the absorbance can, therefore, be considered as a fast and 
easy method to determine the extent of the reaction taking place and should be combined with 
chemical analysis to ascertain whether or not this reaction is indeed peeling. 
On the whole, it can be concluded that the model of the RJH used was found to be less efficient 
compared to the unbaffled off-center configuration used at Leo-Pharma. This does not mean that 
it is unlikely that the RJH system would result in better mixing and thereby probably to reduction 
of peeling. If a RJH with a larger nozzle diameter was used, a significantly better mixing 
performance would be attained. However, apart from using a more efficient mixing configuration, 
another way to reduce peeling in the current mixing system could be to change the method of 
base addition. The base could be added from the top using a funnel, in order to prolong the 
addition time and thereby decrease the occurrence of local zones of high pH during pH adjustment 
that result in peeling.  
With respect to future work that can be carried out, mixing experiments could be performed with 
a RJH of a larger nozzle diameter and the off-center configuration used to imitate that of Leo 
Pharma in water, in order to determine which of the two systems is more efficient. If the RJH is 
found to have a better mixing performance, further mixing experiments could be carried out with 
the two systems in a polysaccharide solution with high enough concentration to induce peeling 
and same flow behavior as that of the API. In this way, it could be examined if the RJH could also 
lead to reduced peeling by measuring the absorbance of the polysaccharide before and after the 
experiments. In addition, peeling experiments with various combinations of alginate and base 
concentrations could be performed to measure the absorbance of the alginate solutions after base 
addition over time and thereby obtain data for the rate of the peeling reaction (which has already 
been attempted in the current thesis). In this way, a kinetic model for the reaction could be 
constructed and used to predict the absorbance development in mixing systems with different 
concentrations of alginate and base by compartment modelling, so that it can be determined 
which system results in the lowest increase in absorbance and therefore the lowest degree of 
peeling.
