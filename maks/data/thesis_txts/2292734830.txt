Summary 
Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from waste and wastewater are of concern as global amounts are 
increasing. Wastewater treatment leads to the production of sludge that must be processed further. This 
process is often energy demanding or requires space as in the case of sludge treatment reed beds (STRBs). 
The application of STRBs has become common in Denmark and approximately 20% of the sludge is 
currently treated in such facilities. The technology makes use of gravitation and evaporation for dewatering 
as well as the reeds’ capability of evapotranspiration and mineralization. Among the environmental 
advantages of STRBs are the low energy consumption and the absence of chemicals.  
So far, the research on the environmental impact of STRBs with regard to GHG emissions is limited and 
therefore, the aim of this project is to contribute to the collection of data on this topic. 
The static chamber method was used to measure emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4) and 
nitrous oxide (N2O) over resting periods during July and August 2015. Seven chambers were installed in 
basin four at the STRB of Helsinge wastewater treatment plant (WWTP). Mean fluxes and standard 
deviations were calculated and visualized. Weighted mean emissions were determined to take noticeable 
spatial and temporal variations into account.  
The weighted mean emissions of CO2ranged from 2885 (day 17 after loading) to 18962 mg m-2 h-1 (day 5). 
For CH4 the values were between 145 and 647 mg m-2 h-1, and for N2O the recorded mean emissions were in 
the interval 2.77-56.1 mg m-2 h-1. For all gases, a decrease was observed throughout the resting period. 
However, CH4 revealed a rather diffuse pattern, while N2O had a drastic peak on day 11 despite the overall 
decreasing trend. Mean emissions during summer were found to be 7463, 272 and 15 mg m-2 h-1 for CO2, 
CH4 and N2O, respectively. 
The values are relatively high compared to emissions found in other studies. Wet conditions can have 
contributed to anaerobic environments in the sludge residue with subsequent methane production. 
Furthermore, a longer measuring period could have beneficial.  
As expected, the emission during summer was higher than during other seasons. Compared to emissions 
measured during fall and spring the reported values are at least a factor of two higher except for N2O 
during fall.  
The yearly emission of GHG (excluding CO2 as it is biogenic) from the reed bed was estimated to be 
62 t CO2 eq. In comparable units, this corresponded to 592 t CO2 eq ha-1 yr.-1 which was between values found 
in other studies. Considering only the emissions during summer, the sum of CO2 equivalents was high 
compared to literature. 
Methane and N2O contributed roughly 60/40 to the total emission during spring and summer, while the 
contribution of N2O was almost 80% during fall. When CO2 was considered as well, its contribution to the 
total emission was stable for the three investigated seasons (approximately 40%).

1. Introduction 
Sludge generated through wastewater treatment is a waste-product requiring further processing. As the 
global production of wastewater is increasing, wastewater treatment will remain an ongoing issue [1]. 
Within the waste category, wastewater is one of the major contributors to the emission of CH4 (methane) 
and N2O (nitrous oxide) [2]. Moreover, CH4 and N2O are potent greenhouse gases making even relatively 
small emissions a considerable issue [1]. Energy use and the emission of greenhouse gases (GHG) are 
inextricably linked with the much debated topics of global warming and climate change. Therefore, it is 
desirable to evaluate the environmental performance of currently applied wastewater treatment 
technologies which includes the evaluation of GHG emissions. In fact, legislation even requires wastewater 
treatment plants (WWTPs) to increase their efficiency [3]. 
To reduce the economic, environmental and health burden of sludge disposal, the sludge is treated prior to 
disposal. Several technologies for volume reduction, stabilization and sanitation exist. The sludge treatment 
reed bed (STRB) is one technique, whose application is common in Denmark. As knowledge about the GHG 
emissions from STRB is limited, the purpose of this thesis is to contribute to the elucidation of this issue. 
More in detail, the purpose is to determine the gas emissions from an STRB at Helsinge WWTP by taking 
measurements directly in the field. The aim is thus to evaluate the emissions with regard to spatial and 
temporal variability, as well as differences between seasons. The annual estimation of GHG emissions from 
the basin is also pursued. Furthermore, the estimated values shall be compared to values found in other 
studies of similar treatment facilities.  
The estimation of a dynamic quantity such as a wetland’s gas emissions is challenging as the discharge 
fluctuates significantly in time and space and depends on many different factors [4]. So far, the amount of 
data on this research topic is limited [5, 6, 7]. Moreover, Ugetti et al. (2012a) argued that more precise 
results regarding GHG emissions from STRBs could be achieved when measuring over a longer period [8].  
It is expected that the GHG emissions increase immediately after the loading with fresh sludge followed by 
a drop during the rest period [9]. Moreover, emissions during summer are expected to be higher than 
during other seasons due to increased microbial activity at elevated temperatures [7, 10, 11, 12, 13].

2. Theory 
2.1 Wastewater 
Water which cannot be released to natural waters due to environmental, health or economic reasons is 
considered wastewater. Both domestic and industrial wastewater requires treatment before discharge to 
the environment [14].  
2.2 Treatment of wastewater 
There are around 1558 wastewater treatment plants in Denmark with a total load of about 7.7 million 
person equivalent (PE) of wastewater annually [15, 16]. In 2011, 995 WWTPs with a capacity larger than 30 
PE were registered in Denmark [16]. One PE defines the contribution per person and can be assessed with 
respect to e.g. BOD, COD, N or P [17, 18]. In Denmark one PE corresponds to a daily water consumption of 
200 liters [18].  
The purpose of wastewater treatment is to eliminate or neutralize contaminants i.e. reducing the organic 
and inorganic matter to prevent microbial growth and removing toxic substances and pathogens [14]. A 
biological oxygen demand (BOD) of less than 5 BOD units in the effluent is an indication of efficient 
treatment [14]. The treatment is a multistep operation that makes use of physical, chemical and biological 
processes that occur in successive steps. There is a wide range of different treatment methods to remove 
unwanted substances such as dissolved and undissolved organic matter and nutrients from the 
wastewater. Below, the methods that are typically applied at Danish WWTPs are described.  
2.2.1 Mechanical treatment 
In the first stage, the wastewater is lead through grates or screens in order to remove larger solid items. 
The removed waste is usually combusted at incineration plants [18]. Other ways of disposal are storage and 
landfilling or grinding of the debris in a disintegrator and subsequent recycling [19]. The grates can have 
different shapes and coarseness depending on the design of the WWTP. The application of disintegrators or 
comminutors is an alternative to get rid of larger items in the wastewater [19].  
Sand and other material that has passed through the grate settles in the next stage, the grit chamber. The 
purpose is to remove matter that does not decompose [18]. Larger particles such as sand and gravel have a 
high settling velocity and can form solid sediments on the bottom of the basin [19]. It is very important to 
eliminate those particles early during the treatment process to avoid abrasion of the pumping system and 
clogging of the valves [18]. The grit chamber can be combined with a grease trap, where some of the fatty 
substances such as oil and gasoline can be removed through short aeration [19, 18]. However, a substantial 
part of the fatty substances is bound to the precipitable sludge and will thus move on through the WWTP 
[19].  
In the last stage of the mechanical treatment, undissolved organic material is removed in the primary 
sedimentation basin. The wastewater moves slowly through the funnel-shaped clarifier and the material 
subsides due to gravity [18, 19]. There are different types and shapes of sedimentation basins (e.g. Imhoff 
tanks, circular or rectangular). Fat and deposited material from the sedimentation basin is lead to digestion 
tanks [18].

2.2.2 Biological treatment 
There are many different ways to treat the wastewater biologically. The method that is applied will depend 
on the type of sewage, as there are significant differences between, for instance, domestic and industrial 
sewage. Below, one of the most commonly applied methods, the activated sludge process [18], is 
described, which is also used at the WWTP of Helsinge [20]. The method is suitable for wastewater with 
low organic matter content such as domestic sewage. Anaerobe methods may be utilized for treatment of 
wastewater with higher content of (insoluble) organic material [14].  
The purpose of biological treatment with activated sludge is the removal of organic matter as well as 
ammonia and nitrate [18]. The wastewater is lead to the first tank of the biological treatment, the aeration 
tank, where it is mixed with recirculated sludge from the activated sludge tank [14]. The sludge contains 
large amounts of microorganisms that are specialized in degrading the organic matter in the particular type 
of wastewater as specific microorganisms prefer certain conditions and will grow quickly in their favored 
environment [19]. Thus, the addition of microorganisms for the treatment of normal wastewater (not 
containing specific chemicals) is not necessary, although biomass must be continuously removed [19]. The 
water in the tank is aerated and stirred to ensure appropriate oxygen levels. Besides oxygen and 
degradable organic matter, bacteria also need trace metals and nutrients, especially nitrogen and 
phosphorous, in order to grow and reproduce [18]. The bacteria form flocs which other microorganisms 
attach to [14, 19]. The sludge’s capacity to precipitate is a prerequisite for a successful treatment process. 
Furthermore, the process depends on a range of factors such as oxygen supply, pH and temperature, 
concentration of organic matter, trace metals and toxins as well as sludge age, sludge load and degree of 
recirculation [18]. 
The typical retention time for an activated sludge tank is five to ten hours. The organic matter is thus not 
oxidized completely, but the majority is adsorbed by the flocs [14]. Subsequently, particles in the 
wastewater deposit on the bottom of the secondary sedimentation basin to form the activated sludge. A 
large part of the sludge is reused, while the surplus is taken to the sludge treatment [19, 18].  
2.2.3 Tertiary treatment  
As phosphorous and nitrogen often are not fully removed by the biological treatment, chemical treatment 
is commonly applied. During chemical wastewater treatment, precipitants are used to remove 
phosphorous mainly originating from the use of detergents [18]. Hence, small particles (colloids) are 
transformed into precipitable flocs by addition of chemicals. Iron compounds, aluminum salts or calcium 
hydroxide are mostly used as precipitants. The compounds that form, e.g. iron phosphate, are poorly 
soluble and thus, settle to the bottom of the tank from where they are removed [18]. Chemical treatment is 
strongly pH dependent and can be applied prior to biological treatment (pre-precipitation), simultaneously 
or afterwards [18]. The content of nutrients such as phosphate, nitrite and nitrate is decreased significantly 
through tertiary treatment [14]. Eventually, the water is led to the final settling tank where sludge particles 
are allowed to deposit at steady flow velocity [18]. Other tertiary treatment methods are the use of e.g. 
disinfection with UV radiation, ozone, or chlorine as well as bioreactors or filtration [14].  
Samples from every stage in the treatment until discharge into the environment are analyzed. Moreover, 
the operation of a WWTP is monitored by computers which collect data and record errors [21]. Finally, the 
water is released to natural waters like streams or lakes [14].

2.3 Sludge 
Sludge originates from mechanical, biological or chemical treatment in a WWTP. It consists of a mixture of 
water and microorganisms as well as organic and inorganic substances that are formed or released during 
the wastewater treatment [19]. The substances derive from the discharge of households, industry or the 
runoff from solid surfaces. Chemical residues from wastewater treatment or organic compounds produced 
during biological treatment appear as well. A large part of the compounds in the sludge, for instance 
nitrogen and carbon, is converted in the draining system and in the WWTP. The water content in sludge 
from WWTP depends on the treatment method and is usually 95-99% corresponding to a dry solids (DS) 
content of 1-5%. Moreover, the water content increases with increasing organic matter content and 
decreasing particle size as it is a matter of bonding forces between dry solid particles and surrounding fluid. 
In other words, external energy is required to break the bonds to dewater the sludge [19].  
2.3.1 Sludge treatment  
It is essential to reduce the volume of sludge as much as possible to decrease costs and energy spent on 
transportation and final disposal. Hence, the purpose of sludge treatment is to reduce the volume and 
ensure a sludge quality that is acceptable for the final disposal [19]. According to The Danish Society of 
Water and Wastewater Treatment, 133,927 t DS of sludge originating from wastewater treatment are 
handled in Denmark every year [22]. About half of the sludge volume is spread on agricultural soils, while 
the rest is either incinerated or treated in sludge mineralization plants, landfilled etc. [22, 5]. Sludge 
treatment can be of chemical, physical or biological kind and normally involves volume reduction, 
stabilization and sanitation.  
Sludge from the activated sludge tanks is mainly led back, while the excess sludge is removed and 
processed [19, 14]. After being treated, the sludge can be used as e.g. isolation material, sandblasting 
material or as fertilizer in agriculture [21, 23]. 
Concentration 
The first step in sludge treatment is the concentration of raw sludge. During sludge concentration the freely 
available water is released through gravity. The maximum dry matter content that can be achieved is 
10-15% which is also the technical limit for common pumping systems. Two methods are commonly 
applied: flotation and sedimentation [19]. Primary sludge precipitates well in sedimentation tanks, while 
secondary (activated) sludge often has to be treated in centrifuges, which calls for addition of polymer [24].  
Dewatering 
Dewatering occurs through centrifugation, filtration or spreading of the sludge onto sludge beds. The 
methods are either static (vacuum filters, pressure filters, belt filter press and filter press) or dynamic 
(centrifuge, rotating screening unit). The sludge volume is reduced as water is removed from the sludge 
[19]. The reject water is returned to the WWTP, while the dewatered sludge receives further treatment or 
disposed of. Often chemicals are added to change the conditions, so the sludge becomes more suitable for 
dewatering. Water from rural sewage can achieve a maximum of 30-40% DS and becomes semisolid at 
35-40% DS.

Drying 
The drying process separates the part of the water with the strongest bonds. Drying can be partly applied 
instead of the dewatering stage, but it requires a high amount of energy and thus high costs. Therefore, it is 
only applicable if excess heat is available. The sludge volume is reduced significantly (DS up to 95%) and 
sanitation is achieved simultaneously [19, 24].  
Stabilization 
The purpose of stabilization is to reduce the risk of infection, odors and to improve the dewatering capacity 
of the sludge. Easily degradable organic compounds are transformed by chemical, biological or thermal 
processes. Stabilization occurs either biologically by aerobe or anaerobe digestion, chemically through 
stabilization with lime, or thermally by composting or combustion [19].  
Sanitation  
The removal of pathogens such as bacteria, viruses and parasites is the purpose of sanitation. Usually 
heating to 70-80 °C (pasteurization) is involved, but also the increase of pH to above 12 by means of lime 
can lead to partial sanitation [24]. The method is only applied when necessary before disposal, as it is 
energy intensive. The content of water, organic matter and nutrients in the sludge remains fairly the same 
during sanitation. An alternative to pasteurization is the treatment in a biogas reactor followed by 
sanitation tank or radiation exposure [19].

The sludge has to be analyzed before disposal as there can be residues of heavy metals, xenobiotics or 
pathogens in the sludge. Sludge can be used for soil improvement because it contains nutrients. Thus, 
application on agricultural land is a common option if limit values for harmful substances are not exceeded 
and the limits for addition of fertilizer obeyed. Other procedures for final disposal can be incineration, 
controlled landfilling or encapsulation [19].  
2.4 Sludge treatment reed beds  
As this study concerns GHG emissions from STRBs, this sludge dewatering technology is described more 
detailed below.  
In Denmark, sludge treatment reed beds have been operated since 1988 [23]. There are currently more 
than 1000 of such facilities in use in Europe [25] and more than 120 in Denmark [26]. About 19-20% of the 
sludge from WWTPs in Denmark is treated in STRBs corresponding to 30,000 t DS of sludge [25, 27]. Earlier, 
it was very common to spread sewage sludge directly onto agricultural lands in Denmark, but stricter 
legislation has been implemented as problems with hazardous compounds became more and more obvious 
[23]. However, sludge that has been dewatered in a sludge mineralization plant can be spread on 
agricultural land as soil improver [23] or used on forest land or as landfill cover [27] if limit values for the 
content of heavy metals and hazardous organic compounds are respected.  
The technique is environmentally friendly and cost-efficient as only little energy is needed for the pumping 
system of the sludge and reject water [28, 29]. Furthermore, no chemicals have to be added which can be 
the case when other dewatering procedures are utilized [28, 19]. The final product has soil improving 
abilities and a large part of potentially harmful substances has been mineralized; both of which contribute 
further to the method’s eco friendliness [30, 31]. Hence, a sterile product that does not require further 
treatment is the outcome. Moreover, it is possible to treat different types of sludge with DS contents 
between 0.5 and 5% [27]. Other advantages are the easiness of operation and maintenance (e.g. the 
performance is not affected by heavy rain fall or freezing conditions) as well as the simple integration into 
the landscape.  
2.4.1 Construction 
An STRB consists of several basins to which excess sludge from a WWTP is distributed through vertical 
loading pipes [32] (Figure 1 and Figure 2). The basin is lined with a waterproof membrane and equipped 
with an over-dimensioned aeration system for reject water as well as drainage pipes to return the reject 
water to the WWTP [32]. Common reed (Phragmites australis) is planted in a growth layer which rests on 
top of a filter layer consisting of gravel and sand. The total height of gravel, filter sand and growth medium 
is 0.55-0.6 m [28]. In the basin, the sludge accumulates over time to form a solid residue [28]. To measure 
the height of the sludge residue indicator poles are placed on the brink of the basin [27]. 
Dry solids content and sludge flow data is registered for individual basins before loading. Operation and 
monitoring of the system occurs automatically which optimizes dewatering and mineralization [32].

The system design and dimensioning depend on the production rate and the dewatering characteristics of 
the sludge and as well as climatic conditions. A facility should have a capacity that is 10-20 % above the 
excess sludge production [33, 28]. The area loading rate depends on the sludge type and the climate as well 
as emptying prognosis. In Denmark the rates typically applied are 50-60 kg DS m-2 yr.-1 [23, 28, 27]. Most 
Danish facilities have a capacity of 200 t DS yr.-1 and several can treat 300-1000 t DS yr.-1 The largest plants 
even have a capacity of more than 2000 t DS yr.-1 [27]. 
2.4.2 Processes 
The volume of sludge in an STRB is decreased in several ways. Due to gravity a large fraction of the sludge 
water runs vertically through the filter layer and is taken away as reject water (draining). More water is 
removed by evapotranspiration (sum of transpiration and evaporation). The volume is further reduced due 
to the microbial decomposition of the organic matter in the sludge residue (mineralization) [32, 28]. The 
dewatering efficiency is significantly affected by the construction of the filter and growth layer. There has 
to be a capillary connection between the filter layers in order to avoid ‘hanging water’ [33]. The mechanical 
activity of the reeds assists in dewatering the sludge as the reeds’ movements make the upper layer of the 
sludge residue cracked and porous during the rest period. This improves the aeration of the sludge residue 
along with shoots and rhizomes that grow within the sludge and filter layer and thus provide oxygen to the 
microorganisms in the sludge layer [33, 28]. Moreover, the reject water helps to aerate the sludge and the 
filter, while oxygen also diffuses from the surface of the sludge layer. The density of the vegetation is 
important as well as sufficient time for the vegetation to establish [33]. The reeds are planted with a 
density of 1 or 4 pots per m2 with 1.5 or 0.5 L, respectively. Other functions such as contribution to the 
mineralization as well as the increase of evapotranspiration and run off capacity are also fulfilled by the

reeds [27]. Sanitation can be achieved in sludge beds due to long retention times (> one year) and regular 
aeration [19]. Furthermore, the sludge is stabilized during treatment in an STRB [23, 34, 35].

2.4.3 Operation 
A sludge treatment facility’s lifetime can exceed 20 or even 30 years if well-operated [27, 23]. Normally, an 
operation cycle lasts about 10-12 years including a two-year period for commissioning and about four-five 
years for emptying. After the start-up phase, during which loading is gradually increased, the facility runs at 
full capacity which corresponds to the excess sludge production of the respective WWTP [28]. During 
emptying, one or a few basins are taken out of operation while the same overall loading rate is maintained 
in the remaining basins. The sludge residue is excavated, usually during spring, and stored until disposal in 
the fall [9]. Subsequently, the re-establishment phase takes place. The upper 20 cm of the growth layer are 
left, so it is possible for the vegetation to regrow before a new operation cycle commences [33].  
An STRB is loaded with sludge from activated sludge tanks, final settling tanks, concentration tanks or 
digesters or a mixture [33, 28]. Before being led to the sludge treatment facility, the sludge is stirred in a 
buffer tank to become homogenous and, if necessary, it is aerated and mixed with reject water [28]. The 
dry solids content of the inflow sludge is typically between 0.5 and 5%. At the end of an operation cycle, the 
sludge residue can have up to 40% DS if the system is designed, constructed and operated correctly leading 
to effective dewatering and mineralization of the organic material [33, 28].  
A loading cycle of an STRB includes short periods of loading (days) followed by longer rest periods (weeks to 
months) during which the dewatering takes place [32]. At least eight basins are required to ensure efficient 
dewatering, an operational period of at least ten years and proper mineralization. Moreover, an 
appropriate number of basins is important to keep a balance between loading and resting, also during 
emptying periods [32]. 
Typical problems of STRBs are inefficient dewatering, poor vegetation, odors, low mineralization or rapid 
growth of the sludge layer.  Reduced dewatering can result from poor vegetation, while in some cases the

vegetation seems fine, but the dewatering is still poor [33]. For the vegetation growth to be unaffected, the 
sludge and filter should not dry out until the sludge residue is approximately 0.3 m [32]. Operational 
problems can occur due to improper construction or design of the plant (e.g. incorrect dimensioning) and 
operational errors such as overloading or inappropriate balance between loading and resting periods. 
Nowadays, loading rates of 50-60 kg DS m-2 yr.-1 are applied in Denmark, while the plants were overloaded 
earlier [33]. The type of sludge can also play a role as it has been observed that primary sludge lay like a 
membrane acting as a wet seal that prevented oxygen from passing through the sludge residue. The results 
are anaerobic conditions and CH4 production which can inhibit plant growth together with the physical 
membrane-like layer [33].  
2.5 Biological processes  
The decomposition (or mineralization) of organic matter involves a range of chemical reactions depending 
on different enzymes and electron donors [14]. Emissions of CO2, CH4 and N2O occur by loss through plants 
and via interfaces of soil and water [36].  
Aerobic oxidation of organic matter produces CO2 and water: 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2𝑂𝑂+ 𝑂𝑂2  𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 + 𝐻𝐻2𝑂𝑂 [36] and ammonia 
(NH3) if the organic matter contains nitrogen: 𝐶𝐶5𝐻𝐻7𝑂𝑂2𝑁𝑁 + 5𝑂𝑂2 5 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 + 𝐻𝐻2𝑂𝑂+ 𝑁𝑁𝑁𝑁3 [19].  
Respiration in the root system of plants and microbial processes in soils and sediments contribute to CO2 
production, while plants use CO2 for photosynthesis. The overall reaction for aerobic respiration is: 
𝐶𝐶6𝐻𝐻12𝑂𝑂6 +  6𝐻𝐻2𝑂𝑂 +  6𝑂𝑂2 +  38 𝐴𝐴𝐴𝐴𝐴𝐴+ 38 𝑃𝑃= 6 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 +  12𝐻𝐻2𝑂𝑂+ 38 𝐴𝐴𝐴𝐴𝐴𝐴 [36]. Oxygen serves as electron acceptor in 
aerobic respiration. While O2/H2O is the strongest redox couple, other electron acceptors become 
important in anaerobic environments, for example NO3-, Fe3+, SO42-, CO32- [14].  
During anaerobic degradation of organic matter CH4 is formed by fermentation and subsequent 
methanogenesis [36]. The process of anaerobic digestion can be divided into four steps which run parallel: 
hydrolysis, acidogenesis (or fermentation), acetogenesis and methanogenesis [37]. Several pathways with 
different substrates can thus lead to CH4 formation [14]. Methane formation is common in sediments of 
treatment wetlands. The generated CH4 is either converted into CO2 via oxidation or discharged through 
the airways of plants or from water or soil surfaces [36]. The amount of degradable organic matter and 
moisture as well as the temperature are crucial. Production of CH4 increases with temperature, while it is 
improbable at temperatures below 15 °C [2]. 
Nitrous oxide originates from the degradation of urea, proteins and nitrate in the wastewater by the 
processes of autotrophic nitrification and heterotrophic denitrification [2]. The gas is stable in the 
atmosphere for more than 100 years [36] and most of it is produced by denitrification [2].  
Through nitrification processes, ammonia (NH3) and organic nitrogen are converted into nitrate (NO3-) 
under the use of oxygen [2]. In activated sludge plants this process normally occurs in parallel with organic

3. Methodology 
3.1 Description of study site 
This project concerns greenhouse gas emissions from an STRB at the WWTP of Helsinge. Helsinge belongs 
to the Municipality of Gribskov which is located in North Zealand and has 41,078 inhabitants or 17,559 
households [39]. The municipality has established the corporation GribVand Inc. to manage the water 
supply and wastewater treatment [40]. The corporation includes ten smaller WWTPs, a sewage network of 
around 600-700 km as well as approximately 250 pumping stations [40, 41]. In the renovated parts of the 
network, rainwater and sewage are separated which means that the WWTP receives less effluent than 
earlier. However, 323 out of 651 hectares of the supply area still have joint sewers [20]. The wastewater 
treatment plants have a debited volume of water of 1,788,657 m3 yr.-1 and headwaters of 5,454,985 m3 yr.-1 
corresponding to a total annual organic load of 34,918 PE [41].  
The WWTP of Helsinge is a mechanical-biological activated sludge plant with chemical phosphorous 
removal and nitrification and finally a sand filter. Ammendrup stream receives the treated water [20]. 
Preliminary water removal at the local WWTPs is followed by either mechanical drainage at Højelt stockpile 
area or treatment at the sludge mineralization plant of Helsinge [21]. 
About 35% of the sludge in the Municipality of Gribskov is treated in sludge mineralization plants. The 
remaining sludge is processed otherwise (apart from biogas production). In 2013, about 70% of the 
expenses were spent on sewage treatment and 30% on sludge treatment [41]. 
The STRB of Helsinge is one of the biggest plants in Europe and includes 14 reed beds and a greenhouse as 
stockpile area for the dewatered sludge. The sludge treatment facility of Helsinge has been in operation 
since 1996 and has a capacity of 630 t DS yr.-1. The WWTP in Helsinge treats 42,000 PE and produces 500-
600 t DS of sludge annually [23]. 66% of the inflow is made up of the excess sludge from the activated 
sludge plant (SAS) and the final settling tanks. The remaining 33% is concentrated anaerobic SAS that 
originates from four to seven smaller plants located nearby Helsinge. The two types of sludge are mixed 
before being pumped to the STRB [23]. The basins of the sludge treatment facility have a total surface area 
of about 14,700 m2. The plant is designed for an area loading rate of 60 kg DS m-2 yr.-1 while, in reality, it is 
operated with a rate between 46 and 83 kg DS m-2 yr.-1 [23]. The basins are planted with common reed 
(Phragmites australis (Cav.) Trin ex steud). Loading occurs through vertical pipes distributed in each reed 
bed. During a loading cycle the reed bed receives sludge for four to seven days and subsequently rests for 
40-60 days [23]. The Helsinge STRB was emptied for the first time from 2005 till 2008 and in 2011 after it 
had been operated for nine years. The second emptying period is in progress, running from 2013 till 2016. 
As the total capacity is maintained during emptying periods, the maximum area loading rate is temporarily 
exceeded (> 80 kg DS m-2 yr.-1) [23].  
This study was conducted in basin number four of Helsinge STRB. As the other basins, it has a surface area 
of approximately 1050 m2. The operation of this reed bed is supervised by the staff of Orbicon; a private 
company specialized in among others STRBs.

Through multiplication with the chamber’s volume, the gas’ mass per chamber was calculated and then 
converted into milligrams. This value was further divided by the area of the chamber to obtain the mass per 
area. A scatter plot of those values was created to check if they were lying on a straight line. Often, the first 
measuring point was not on a straight line with the remaining values, and thus, it was usually ignored [9].  
The flux from the chamber area was determined from the increase of the gas concentration inside the 
static chamber over time applying linear regression [4, 6]. The slope was calculated as the difference in 
mass per area between measurement point two and six which was then divided by the time interval [6, 9].  
As the increase in gas concentration with time sometimes could not be fitted with linear regression, other 
measuring points than the second and the sixth were tried. If the relation was not linear (correlation 
coefficients, R2 lower than 0.9) the data were generally ignored [42]. 
After gas fluxes for all chambers and gases had been determined for each measuring day, mean values and 
standard deviations were calculated and the results were visualized in plots. Moreover, the values were 
converted into t ha-1 yr.-1 and further into CO2 equivalents by multiplying with a global warming potential 
(GWP) (28 and 265 for CH4 and N2O, respectively) [44].  
To estimate the total annual emission from the basin, the mean for each season was obtained from the 
sum of CO2 equivalents divided by four. As data from the winter season are lacking, the emissions were 
estimated by taking the average of emissions during spring and fall. Finally, the total emission stated in CO2 
equivalents was calculated as the sum of the four seasons’ mean values.  
3.4 Weighting 
For practical reasons, it is not possible to measure the emissions at every spot in the basin at all times. It is 
therefore necessary to model the total discharge of the three gases as a mean value of the emissions 
measured inside the seven installed chambers. Hence, to take the spatial and temporal variations into 
account the calculated mean values were weighted. For spatial variations, the Kriging method was used. 
Kriging is an interpolation technique whose weighting is based on the spatial distribution of and the 
distance between the recorded points as well as the location of the points to be predicted. The regionalized 
variable theory, which Kriging rests upon, implies a homogenous variation of the modeled data across the 
surface (more information can be found in e.g. Deutsch, 1996 [45] ; Li & Heap, 2014 [46]; Nielsen, 2004 
[47]). 
The software “Surfer 13” (Golden Software, LCC.) was applied for the purpose. An Excel file containing a 
32 x 32 basis grid for each of the gases had been created beforehand and the calculated emission rates 
were inserted at the chambers’ positions. The grid file was then used with the software to create a grid 
report for each gas and every measuring day. Among the many variables stated in the grid report, the 
Z-value of the harmonic mean was used [48, 9]. 
The Kriging-weighted mean values were subsequently weighted with respect to time [49]. The function 
SUMPRODUCT is used in Excel to multiply each mean value with a weighting factor based on the number of 
days the measurement represents (for the first measuring that is days since last loading event, and for the 
remaining measurements it is the days since the previous measurement) [9].

5. Discussion 
Spatial variability 
The emissions from the different chambers varied strongly and hence, high standard deviations from the 
mean emission rates were observed (standard deviations are shown in Appendix I). This suggests distinct 
variations in the discharge of gases at different locations throughout the basin. With regard to the three 
zones, it is difficult to observe a clear trend in the chambers’ emissions. Chamber four and five, for 
instance, were located quite close to each other and both were surrounded by plenty of reeds. Chamber 
four has presented some rather high emission rates, while fluxes in chamber five were lower than in most 
chambers, especially for N2O and CH4.  
On the other hand, chamber one and two were located at spots with poor vegetation and CO2 emissions 
were relatively low compared to the other chambers, while the CH4 emission was high. This is in agreement 
with the fact that plants provide suitable conditions for microbes that mineralize the organic matter 
increasing the emission of CO2 (e.g. Olsson, et al., 2014 [7]). On the other contrary, plants also take up 
carbon due to photosynthesis, especially during summer [36]. This contradicts with the relatively high CO2 
emissions in chamber four and seven compared to the remaining chambers, as those two chambers were 
surrounded by plenty of reeds.  
Chamber one and two (poor vegetation cover) revealed high CH4 emissions along with chamber six and 
seven. Both Inamori et al. (2007) and Grünfeld & Brix (1999) state that methanotrophic bacteria in the 
roots of vegetation in constructed wetlands enhance CH4 oxidation hence decreasing the emissions [50, 13]. 
On the contrary, Cui et al. (2015) reported higher CH4 emissions from planted than unplanted beds during 
summer [10]. It is suggested that the reeds contribute to CH4 emission due to their aerenchyma system 
from where the CH4 is released to the atmosphere [51].  
Uggetti et al. (2012b) also found values varying in space without a clear pattern [6]. Overall, they 
considered the distribution to be uniform related to the rapid spreading of the influent. The same is 
assumed to be in evidence at Helsinge’s STRB despite its larger dimensions and the fact that reeds can 
impede the sludge spreading [6].    
The large variability among the emissions from the chambers is probably due to different biological 
conditions in the sludge residue throughout the basin. To take these variations into account Kriging was 
applied.  
Temporal variability 
Looking at the Kriging-weighted mean values, CO2 emission rates showed an increase during the first week 
after loading, and subsequently a considerable drop from day 15. A similar trend was observed by Cui et al. 
(2015) [10]. Olsson et al. (2014) also reported increasing CO2 emissions after partial dewatering during the 
first week [7].  
With regard to CH4, the emission dropped after a high initial value (day 2) and then increased on day 17 and 
19. From the log book (shown in Appendix IV) it can be seen that at this time, an unusual amount of flies as 
well as snails and worms were observed, while the weather had changed towards more rain. Even a 
cloudburst was recorded before the measurement on August 5 2015 (day 24 of the rest period). 
23 
 
==PAGE:34==
Furthermore, it was noted that the surface of the sludge residue seemed more wet than otherwise. 
Methane production is influenced by moisture [6] as well as temperature, pH and substrate availability [11, 
52]. There was also observed some bad odor from the sludge residue during this period.  The sum of these 
signals might point to more anaerobic conditions and thus increased CH4 production compared to other 
days during the rest period.  
According to Olsson et al. (2014), which found much lower emission rates for CO2 and CH4 than this study, 
the high emissions after a loading event can be partly explained by the fact that the added sludge acts as 
fresh substrate for microbial degradation [7]. On the other hand, the layer of fresh sludge can function as a 
wet seal which prevents gas exchange [33]. This could account for low emission rates immediately after 
loading, which are followed by an increase in emissions when the sludge residue is partially dewatered. 
Hence, air exchange and degradation again occur aided by the higher oxygen availability. The degradation 
of organic matter under aerobic conditions leads first and foremost to the emission of CO2, while high the 
presence of CH4 indicates methanogenesis that happens under anaerobic conditions [14]. 
Cui et al. (2015) and Olsson et al. (2014) also found CH4 emissions rates that were decreasing with time [10, 
7]. A possible explanation is the inhibition of methanogenesis in aerobic conditions favoring CH4 oxidation 
[53]. Uggetti et al. (2012b) report different trends for three loading events: while CH4 increased rapidly 
after the first loading and decreased during the following days, there was observed a peak in emissions on 
the second and third day after subsequent loadings [6]. An initial increase can be partly explained by the 
fact that organic matter in the fresh sludge acts as substrate for anaerobic decomposition by microbes 
(methanogenesis) [6].  
The pattern for N2O was rather diffuse, but overall the emission decreased noticeably after the evident 
peak on day 11. Nitrous oxide is formed during the removal of nitrogen by nitrification and denitrification 
which depends on the hydraulic loading rate as well as oxygen, carbon and nitrogen availability [6]. Uggetti 
et al. (2012b) found that N2O decreased after loading [6]. Denitrification, which is considered the main 
source of N2O production in constructed wetlands, is an anaerobic process, though depending on nitrate 
supply from aerobic nitrification [54]. When conditions are strongly anaerobic, N2O may be reduced to 
gaseous nitrogen prior to discharge to the atmosphere [6].  
Seasonal variations 
The emissions during summer are considerably higher than during the two other seasons. This was 
expected because microbial processes occur at slower rates at lower temperatures [7]. 
Substantial variations in emission during different seasons were also reported by Cui et al. (2015) [10]. They 
found the highest CO2 emissions at the end of June. Søvik et al. (2006) measured N2O and CH4 emissions 
from constructed wetlands and also concluded that they were higher during summer than winter [11]. 
Constructed wetlands are used for wastewater treatment and hence not totally the same technique. 
Nevertheless, there are some similar mechanisms as sludge originates from wastewater. 
Furthermore, Inamori et al. (2008) stated that N2O emissions from constructed wetlands peaked during the 
growth season in summer, while Grünfeld & Brix (1999) found a correlation between elevated 
temperatures, solar radiation and increased CH4 emissions [12, 13].

Comparison with other studies 
In general, the values for all gases seem very high compared to most emission rates found in the literature 
(Table 9). Ugetti et al. (2012a and b) found N2O emissions in the same range as this study [8, 6]. Olsson et 
al. (2014) suggested that CH4 was mainly present in the deeper layers and that some of it was oxidized 
before it reached the atmosphere [7]. As much higher values of CH4 emission are found in this study, it is 
presumed that in this case, methanogenesis also occurs in the upper layers of the sludge residue. Grünfeld 
& Brix (1999) found that high CH4 emission during the day was caused by the plants’ release of CH4 
produced during night and captured in the sediment [13]. This phenomenon could help explaining the high 
CH4 emissions found in this study.

During the measuring campaign the sludge residue seemed very wet and cracks were observed rarely. This 
could be caused by a frequent addition of moisture from above due to periods of heavy rain and even 
cloudbursts - or it could point to reduced dewatering.  Inefficient dewatering and consequent anaerobic 
conditions can be caused among others by operational errors such as overloading [33]. This is unlikely, as 
the facility is designed with an appropriate number of basins and the operation is supervised by 
experienced staff. The reeds seem healthy, short periods of loading are followed by rest periods and, 
according to verbal information, the facility is doing well [9]. Moreover, the dry solids content of the sludge 
residue is 15-20% in the upper one meter according to own measurements (results not included) which is 
quite in the normal range [7, 31]. This fact further confirms the theory that the facility is doing well in 
dewatering the sludge.

bit short (~40 days compared to two to two and a half months as suggested by Nielsen). Sufficient rest 
periods are important to ensure efficient dewatering with the desired degree of mineralization and quality 
of sludge [33].

Another study by Ugetti et al. (2012a) found emissions of 0.09 kg CO2 eq m-2 d-1 and 0.25 kg CO2 eq m-2 d-1 for 
a Danish and a Spanish STRB, respectively, corresponding to 329 and 913 t CO2 eq ha-1 yr.-1 [8]. Compared to 
the total annual emission found in this study, the emission at the Spanish STRB is high, but again, 
measurements took place during summer which could lead to an overestimation. Compared to emission 
during summer found in this study, the values reported by Ugetti et al. (2012a) are lower. It should be 
noted that the dimension of the Spanish STRB was smaller and the sludge loading rate was lower than at 
Helsinge STRB, while the Danish facility was fairly in the same range. The difference between the two 
Danish plants could be due to performance or maybe the weather during that summer was drier yielding 
less anaerobic conditions.  
It should be mentioned that other authors did not use the same values for the GWP of CH4 and N2O as this 
study (25 and 298 vs. 28 and 265, respectively). 
Despite the large quantities, the STRB has, altogether, a much lower environmental impact than alternative 
technologies for dewatering or disposal (centrifuges and transportation) [55, 30, 10]. 
Assumptions and weighting  
In order to obtain correct emission rates from the sludge residue in the basin, measurements should be 
taken all the time and at every spot in the basin. As this is too time-demanding and technically difficult to 
realize, it is necessary to make assumptions. In this case, it is assumed that the emissions from the basin 
can be represented by the emissions measured inside the seven chambers on the selected measuring days. 
The measurements were taken on specific days without constant intervals. Many measurements directly 
after two consequent loading events were planned, as it was expected to be the most interesting period 
[9].  
Weighting with respect to time was applied. It was assumed that measurements taken on a specific day 
represent the emissions that occur during the time since the last loading or measuring event. It is difficult 
26 
 
==PAGE:37==
to know whether this assumption proves correct, as the measured values could vary substantially from day 
to day. Moreover, the intervals between the measuring were random. This means that a value from a day 
with a longer pause before the next measurement outweighed other measurements with regard to the 
mean emission. Since the underlying biological conditions probably change rather slowly with time, a 
measurement taken on a specific day might rather represent the emissions during the period immediately 
before and after. However, assuming that emissions follow a statistical normal distribution, it is probable 
that days without measurements would have shown emissions in the range of the recorded values. For 
future measuring campaigns, a schedule with e.g. the same number of days between every measurement 
could be created.  
Due to technical issues with plant, the last measurement of the first rest period was taken 24 days after 
load, out of a 32 days long resting period. However, more measurements had been planned. During fall and 
spring measurements were taken over period of 37 and 43 days, respectively. Maybe this fact contributed 
to lower mean emissions during fall and spring, as the sludge has had more time to become dewatered. A 
longer measuring period might have been beneficial in order to more exactly investigate the trend in 
emissions during summer.  
Regarding the total annual emissions from the basin stated in CO2 equivalents, it was assumed that 
emissions during winter can be estimated as the average of emissions during spring and fall, as the winter 
season lies between those two. This might be an overestimation, as the mean temperature during winter is 
lower than in spring or fall suggesting less microbial activity. It also should be mentioned that total GHG 
emission includes only the flux of gases directly emitted from the sludge bed. Energy for the pumping 
system, excavation and transportation of the sludge after the treatment etc. are not included in the 
calculation. If a more holistic analysis of the environmental impact (e.g. an LCA) of the sludge treatment 
plant was to be made, those numbers should be included.  
Finally, to conduct this study it was necessary to access the sludge bed, place boards to walk on the sludge 
residue and cut some of the reeds. Hence, a certain disruption of the processes in the basin can have 
occurred. Especially CH4 emission is sensitive as CH4 has a tendency to accumulate in bubbles that are being 
released abruptly [6].

6. Conclusion 
Emissions of CO2, CH4 and N2O from basin four at Helsinge STRB have been measured with the static 
chamber method throughout a rest period in July-August and the corresponding fluxes were calculated.  
High variability in emission rates among the seven chambers was observed indicating some spatial 
variation. However, no obvious pattern could be observed, and hence the distribution is considered fairly 
uniform.  
With regard to temporal variation, the discharge of all three gases had obviously decreased towards the 
end of the rest period, though with a rather diffuse trend especially for CH4 and a high peak in N2O emission 
in the middle of the period.  
Compared to values from other studies, the emission rates found in this study seem relatively or even 
extremely high. Only few authors have reported values in the same range.  
Comparison across seasons showed that the emissions during summer were higher than during fall or 
spring, which was also expected. Regarding the relative contribution of the different gases to the total 
emission in CO2 equivalents, it was observed that the contributions were quite the same during spring and 
summer (40% for CO2 and CH4, 20% for N2O), while N2O contributed much more during fall (almost 50% and 
when CO2 is excluded almost 80%). 
The total emission from the basin based on the emissions measured during three seasons was estimated to 
be 592 t CO2 eq ha-1 yr.-1.
