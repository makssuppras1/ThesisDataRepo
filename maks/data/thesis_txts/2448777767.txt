Abstract 
The thermal indoor climate and the heating system efficiency plays a major role in 
the energy consumption of existing buildings. Heating systems are generally de-
signed to provide thermal comfort at all times. Thus, designed for outdoor tempera-
tures that rarely occurs in reality and the heating systems are therefore mainly op-
erated under part load. Part load operation can lead to unanticipated heat losses and 
non-optimal system operation. Additionally, district heating (DH) powered space 
heating systems are very exposed to system imbalances, which also leads to non-
optimal operation. A non-optimal building operation compromises the thermal indoor 
climate and/or the energy consumption. 
The project is a proof of concept, dealing with a case study school building from the 
1920’s. It is set out to investigate potentials for optimization of building operation 
with the use of various sensor data, building information and environmental meas-
urements. The focus of the study is on the thermal indoor climate, the DH consump-
tion and prospects for implementation of low-temperature district heating (LTDH). 
Findings show the thermal climate can be improved to acceptable levels by imple-
menting operational changes with a 11% DH consumption increase. The total DH 
costs are on the other hand decreased by 10% compared to the initial costs, as a 24% 
discount is achieved with reductions in the DH return temperature, by elimination of 
system imbalances. The lowered return temperature can pay off necessary invest-
ments on the heating system with a simple payback time of 1.09 years or 4.71 years 
if LTDH is to be fully implemented. 
It is concluded that viable operational optimizations can be performed on an exist-
ing school building, by eliminating system imbalances and reducing the DH return 
temperature. The performed optimizations are considered economically favorable for 
both the customer and the overall energy system, and they result in an improved 
thermal climate for the occupants. The main barriers of implementing LTDH is cur-
rently a range of critical heating appliances, the domestic hot water system design 
and the user influences on the space heating operation.

1 Introduction 
Today, almost 40% of Denmark’s total energy consumption is related to building 
operation. The operational energy is primarily consumed by heating, ventilation and 
lighting. Optimization of the building operation is therefore decisive for reaching the 
current political goals of being free of fossil fuels by 2050 [1]. 
 
 
1.1 
Building energy 
The Danish building code standards has especially influenced new developments 
towards greatly reduced energy consumptions. A significant potential for reducing 
energy consumption does though lie in the existing building mass, due to the major 
share of the building stock, the long lifetime of the buildings and the lack of a future-
proofed design at the construction time. New developments does generally have a 
markedly lower energy consumption, than the average existing building mass [2].  
 
1.1.1 Energy savings in existing buildings 
Energy savings in heat consumptions has the largest return of investment, when 
dealing with the existing building stock. The Danish building code is dealing with this, 
by demanding certain insulation levels when larger alterations and renovations are 
carried out on existing buildings.  
A well-insulated building is generally first priority, but not always feasible- or ade-
quate in order to reduce the energy consumption to an acceptable level. The effi-
ciency of the heating system plays a major role in the total energy consumption of 
buildings in colder climates and heating systems are generally designed to provide 
thermal comfort at all times. Thus, the heating systems are designed for full load, 
which rarely occurs in reality. Heating systems does most often operate under part 
load, which leads to unanticipated heat losses and a non-optimal system operation. 
Another reason to a large heat consumption and/or an unacceptable thermal climate 
is due to that the heating system is very exposed to system imbalances (e.g. due to 
rogue heating appliances, malfunctions and wrong settings). 
Analyses shows, that heat demand for existing buildings in Denmark should be less 
than 80 kWh/m2 by 2050, in order to achieve the most rentable reduction level for 
the society. This is a heat demand reduction of 31-40%, compared to today’s demand 
for existing buildings. The total heat consumption for existing buildings (constructed 
before 2000) should be decreased by 0.7 percentage points per year in actual 
==PAGE:14==
2 |  
 
savings, in order to achieve the most rentable reductions towards the 2050 goal. 
Statistics does though show that the current trends are reductions by just 0.25 per-
centage points per year (2000-2018), which is considerably less reductions than 
what has to be done. If Denmark achieves significantly less or significantly more sav-
ings in the existing building stock, the total costs of the energy system will increase, 
either due to more production units or due to saving measures with higher costs [3], 
[4]. 
 
1.1.2 District heating 
The overall European heat market for buildings is currently dominated by fossil-
fuels in onsite boilers, which account for two-thirds of the heat supply, while the 
market share for district heating in buildings just reaches 13% [5]. District heating 
(DH) does though have a coverage of approx. 50% of the total heat demand from 
buildings in Denmark and the coverage is expected to reach approx. 63% by 2040 
[6]. District heating does provide an efficient way to utilize renewable thermal en-
ergy sources and ‘waste’ heat sources, such as surplus heat from industry and power 
production based on biomass/waste. District heating can also increase the flexibility 
of the energy system, e.g. by using heat pumps and large thermal storages [7].  
Large-scale implementation of district heating should even be approx. 15% lower 
in total costs, compared to the strategies proposed by the European Commission in 
the ‘Energy roadmap 2050’ report [5], [8]. Consequently, district heating has the ca-
pability to play a major role in bringing down the greenhouse gas emissions.  
 
Low-temperature district heating 
An inexpensive transition to a more sustainable DH energy system, could be based 
on lower supply- and return temperatures, than the current temperatures of approx. 
80°C and 40°C for supply and return respectively. Simple estimations show  DH tem-
peratures reduced from 80/40°C to 60/30°C, lessens the costs by up to 22%, when 
delivering district heating based on a mix of solar heating and heat pumps [7].  
Low-temperature district heating (LTDH) does though have limitations, due to cus-
tomer demands and installations. Domestic hot water (DHW) installations are usually 
supplied with water at temperatures above 50°C, and Danish standards does actually 
even recommend water temperatures above 60°C, in order to minimize the risk of 
legionella bacteria growth in the hot water supply [9]. A further requirement for suc-
cessful implementation of LTDH is that the return temperature decrease corresponds 
to the decrease in the supply temperature, in order to avoid mass flow increases in 
the system. An increased mass flow will result in less cooling of the water and an 
increased return temperature. A low return temperature, on the other hand, reduces 
heat losses in the DH network and increases efficiency in cogeneration systems. 
As there is a strong correlation, between the flow through a heating appliance and 
the temperature of the return water, the resulting flow in a space heating (SH) sys-
tem is strongly affected. Even if just a small fraction of the heating appliances in a 
building are not correctly controlled, it can result in an unnecessary high return tem-
perature. Detailed monitoring and adjustments of heating systems can be crucial, in 
terms of achieving an optimum return temperature with the given dimensioning of 
the system.

1.1.3 Smart buildings 
Actual potentials for energy savings and reductions in the DH temperatures, due to 
heating system alterations in existing buildings, can be difficult to estimate in detail. 
The existing building stock is rarely equipped with sufficient information, data and 
controls to implement detailed and precise optimizations on the building service sys-
tems. Larger buildings are though often already equipped with some sort of a build-
ing management system (BMS). The BMS systems include a certain level of remote 
monitoring with the purpose of delivering comfortable light, indoor air quality and 
thermal climate with the lowest possible energy consumption. By combining detailed 
and precise consumption metering with monitoring of the indoor environment, it is 
possible to provide a transparency in between energy efficiency and occupant com-
fort [10].  
Most of the current BMS systems uses a predefined control, but a truly smart build-
ing is intelligently reacting on datapoints from several sensor inputs and are capable 
of predicting the needs of the building and its occupants. The building service sys-
tems are greatly influenced by the presence- and activities of the building residents. 
Thus, sensor based extraction of building data provides insights to the operation of 
buildings, that can be used to create continuous diagnostics- and optimizations of 
the operation. Optimum comfort and significant energy savings can be achieved by 
intelligently operating the building, using visualizations and extrapolations of pat-
terns from ‘big data’ sets, consisting of building information, energy consumptions, 
environmental measurements and occupancy information. 
 
 
1.2 
Aim and scope 
The purpose of the project is to investigate the potential for optimization of build-
ing operation with the use of various sensor data, building information and environ-
mental measurements. The project is a proof of concept, determining whether it is 
possible to collect-, visualize- and analyze data about the building services and 
thereby improve- and optimize the operation of the building.  
The project will primarily deal with a case study, wherein the building installations 
and indoor climate of an existing school building from the 1920’s will be investigated. 
The case study will have a special focus on the heating system and the investigation 
will be performed by the use of high-resolution IoT sensors and building simulations. 
The possibility-/feasibility of heating the building with low-temperature district 
heating will be evaluated as a part of the study.  
Based on detailed monitoring of a heating system in a public school building, this 
project seeks to optimize water temperatures and mass flow without compromising 
the user comfort. The collected data will be visualized in a compact and comprehen-
sive way, in order to make administrators and facility managers efficiently control 
the building systems and benefit from it. 
It is expected that the results will lead to significant improvements in the building 
performance of the given school building. The detailed sensor data will give an ex-
tensive knowledge on the energy use and indoor climate, and it will be the backbone 
in the investigations of possibilities to reduce the district heating return tempera-
tures and the evaluation of low-temperature district heating feasibility.

1.3 
Hypothesis 
The following main hypothesis and sub-hypotheses will be investigated, in order to 
deal with the given aim and scope: 
 
It is possible to optimize the building operation of an existing school building – from 
the 1920’s – by eliminating malfunctions and reducing the district heating return 
temperature. 
 
a) The current heating system capacity is sufficient (/over-dimensioned). 
b) Errors and malfunctions can be detected and -eliminated by simple proven meth-
ods, such as corrections of; hydraulic unbalances, dimensioning faults, wrong 
settings, etc. 
c) It is energy- and cost optimal to skip the domestic hot water circulation in favor 
of decentral electrical heating.  
d) Energy savings and/or -bonuses, due to building operation optimizations, are 
sufficient to pay off the investments of necessary changes on the heating sys-
tem. 
e) It is possible to maintain compliance with the indoor climate regulations, when 
reducing the district heating return temperature. 
 
If all sub-hypotheses are true, it should be feasible to optimize the building opera-
tion of an existing school building, by elimination of malfunctions and reductions in 
the district heating return temperature, and the main hypothesis is therefore true.

2 Building operation 
Optimum comfort at the lowest possible energy consumption is what a sustainable 
operation of a building intends to achieve. Correct control of building service systems 
makes this correlation possible but will result in a performance gap if incorrectly con-
trolled. A typical problem is a lack of monitoring and a lack of feedback from the oc-
cupants, meaning that control problems would not get identified and corrected. An-
other issue is that design assumptions often over-dimension the installations to ac-
count for worst case scenarios, which does not reflect the actual demands. If these 
over-dimensioned installations are operated at full load when the demands are lower, 
they will compromise the occupant comfort and the energy use.  
It is therefore very important to have a detailed overview of the building, the ser-
vice systems and the use of the building, in order to operate the building correctly. 
The following sections will introduce the major aspects within building operation of 
existing buildings.  
 
 
2.1 
Building service systems 
Building service systems does generally ensure that the building has comfortable 
temperatures, good ventilation and proper lighting, but can also refer to a large range 
of additional technical installations within a building. A good indoor climate has doc-
umented impacts on the health, performance and wellbeing of the occupants [16]–
[18]. Thus, the main focus of the following sections will be on the thermal building 
services and the indoor climate during the heating season. 
 
2.1.1 Indoor air quality and ventilation 
Ventilation is necessary in terms of removing pollutants from the indoor air and is 
crucial in terms of maintaining a good indoor climate. High CO2 concentrations is ex-
perienced to be a problem in school buildings in Denmark [16], and the CO2 levels is 
a very good indicator for the general indoor air quality. Ventilation systems can be 
used to remove air pollutants, such as CO2, and can be used for heating and cooling 
as well.  
A mechanical ventilation system consists of an air handling unit (AHU) connected 
to a duct distribution system, to an outdoor air supply and an outdoor air exhaust. 
The AHU is typically equipped with air filters, one or two fans, a heat exchanger and 
possibly a heating/cooling coil and a humidifier/dehumidifier. The air distribution duct 
==PAGE:20==
8 |  
 
system is equipped with a range of dampers and possibly a range of sensors in the 
inlets/exhausts towards the ventilated zones. 
Mechanical ventilation systems can generally be divided in CAV systems and VAV 
systems. CAV systems operate under a constant air volume flow and the system can 
be controlled towards a single design IAQ parameter (e.g. a pollutant level or a heat 
load). VAV systems operate under variable air volumes and the system can be con-
trolled corresponding to the actual demands. VAV systems can be controlled by sev-
eral different IAQ parameter set-points simultaneously.  
Natural ventilation is ensured through infiltration and opening of windows and 
doors. Some mechanical ventilation systems are operated as hybrid systems and are 
relying on natural ventilation at zones without mechanical air supply. The natural 
ventilation systems can be controlled by sensors and automatic openings as well as 
the mechanical systems. The controls of ventilation systems are described in section 
2.4.1. 
 
2.1.2 Domestic hot water 
Hot water used in sinks and showers are delivered by a domestic hot water (DHW) 
system. District heating supplied DHW systems are indirectly connected to the sub-
station supply, where the DH supply is physically separated from the DHW supply. 
DHW systems can be made with- or without a hot water tank and must be able to 
deliver temperatures above 50°C, in order to avoid legionella bacteria growths [9].  
A DHW system with a hot water tank is designed to release hot water from the top 
of the tank, while cold water enters the bottom of the tank, ensuring that the tank 
is always full. A water based heating coil inside the tank or a mantle around the tank 
functions as a heat exchanger in between the DH- and the DHW water. 
Indirectly and instantaneous DHW heating through a heat exchanger, without a hot 
water tank, does in most cases require a circulation loop with a circulation pump on 
the secondary side of the heat exchanger (as shown on Figure 3). The circulation of 
the DHW ensures sufficient temperatures and satisfactory waiting times throughout 
the distribution system. The controls of DHW systems are described in section 2.4.2. 
 
2.1.3 Thermal comfort and space heating 
Space heating (SH) systems are necessary to ensure a satisfactory thermal environ-
ment, which is comprised of air temperature, surrounding surface temperatures, air 
speeds and air humidity. A person’s perception of thermal comfort depends on their 
metabolic rate, clothing, and personal preference. Thus, the perception of the thermal 
environment can be very individual [18].  
Typical Danish installations for space heating consist of a DH substation, which is 
either directly- or indirectly connected to the SH distribution system.  
A directly connected DH supply is simple, inexpensive and capable of reaching lower 
return temperatures than an indirectly connected supply. The direct system is 
though exposed to severe damage risks if any leaks are experienced, because of the 
huge mass flow from the DH plant and the supply water temperature which will not 
be adjustable within the building.  
==PAGE:21==
| 9 
 
The DH water is physically separated from the SH water by a heat exchanger in 
indirect systems. This separation demands a circulation pump and an expansion sys-
tem on the secondary side of the heat exchanger. The pressure in the SH system will 
typically be lower than in the direct systems and the control of the water cooling 
would be made easier, as the SH supply temperature can be regulated as well. 
The typical indirect installations are connected through a plate heat exchanger, as 
shown on Figure 3, where a motorized valve on the supply of the secondary side will 
be able to control the supply temperature to the heating appliances within the dis-
tribution system. The AHU is sometimes equipped with a water based heating coil (in 
order to ensure acceptable supply air temperatures) and this would typically be con-
nected with an indirectly connected distribution loop as well [19]. The controls of 
heating systems are described in section 2.4.3.

district heating substation.

2.2.1 Danish building regulations 
BR18 requires that the thermal indoor climate must be sufficiently healthy and 
comfortable in consideration of the use. The regulations are further elaborated by 
that the planning, construction, operation and maintenance of heating systems must 
be carried out in a way which ensures that there are no health risks for people or 
comfort-related nuisance and that no unnecessary use of energy will occur. In rooms 
where people stay for longer periods of time, it must be ensured that a satisfactory 
thermal indoor climate in terms of health and comfort can be maintained during the 
intended use and activities of the room [20]. 
Theoretical documentation of the thermal indoor climate must be obtained via cal-
culations based on the conditions in the critical rooms and based on the design ref-
erence year (DRY 2013 climate data) for the calendar year 2010 [21]. Functional 
testing is required on ventilation-, heating and cooling, and lighting systems [20]. 
 
2.2.2 Danish standards 
The building regulations is referring to a range of standards in relation to calcula-
tions and functional testing.  
BR18 requires heating systems to be planned and established as specified in DS469 
‘Heating and cooling systems in buildings’ [22]. The installations must be insulated 
against heat loss and condensation in accordance with DS452 ‘Thermal insulation of 
technical installations’, the domestic hot water systems must be operated according 
to DS439 ‘Code of practice for domestic water supply installations’ [9], and energy 
consumption calculations has to be made according to DS418 ‘Calculation of heat 
loss from buildings’ [23]. 
Methods to specify, verify and control the indoor climate is found in DS474 ‘Code 
for indoor thermal climate’ [24] and DS/EN15251 ‘Indoor environmental input pa-
rameters for design and assessment of energy performance of buildings addressing 
indoor air quality, thermal environment, lighting and acoustics’ [25].

2.3 
Service system monitoring 
Existing buildings does often have a performance gap in between the theoretical- 
and the actual building operation. This can be due to a large range of dissimilarities 
in between the assumptions related to the theoretical calculations and the actual 
conditions. Additionally, over time the operational requirements change, and the ser-
vice system efficiencies tend to degrade. Existing buildings are therefore often per-
forming well below their potential, consuming more energy than necessary and cost-
ing more to operate than they should [26]. Thus, it is important to measure and check 
whether the operation and performance of a building and its building service systems 
meet the design assumptions and -requirements.  
Typical symptoms on lacking- or wrong control of heating and/or ventilation sys-
tems are; cold air temperatures, draughts, overheating, bad air quality, simultaneous 
heating and cooling, too high/low SH supply temperatures and/or supply air or large 
heat/water/power consumptions [27]. 
Detailed monitoring and analysis of building data is key to ensure optimal operation 
of building services [10]. Existing building commissioning is a systematic process for 
investigating, analyzing, and optimizing the performance of building systems 
through the identification and implementation of low/no cost and capital intensive 
facility improvement measures and thereby ensuring their continued performance 
[26]. The term existing building commissioning covers processes that deals with the 
occupied operational phase of the building life, with a specific purpose of optimizing 
the building system performances and to ensure optimal correlation in between IEQ 
and energy consumption. 
 
2.3.1 Indoor air quality 
Indoor air quality measurements are generally based on an indirect approach of 
measuring the ventilation rates, e.g. derived by the use of CO2 measurements. CO2 
concentration levels are measured directly, and especially measured in buildings 
where people are the main pollution sources.

CO2 measurements shall generally be made where occupants are known to spend 
most of their time, and preferably at head level during typical high load conditions. 
High load conditions are generally during the heating season due to limited use of 
windows during the period with colder outdoor air temperatures [25]. 
According to BR18, teaching rooms in schools must be ventilated via a ventilation 
system which includes injection, evacuation and heat recovery for heating of the in-
jection air. The supply of outdoor air must be minimum 5.0 l/s per person and 0.35 l/s

gated from, if the ventilation system is a demand controlled VAV system. The venti-

hours [20].

2.3.3 Energy consumption 
Typical energy consumptions in buildings are power, for lighting, equipment and 
mechanical ventilation, and district heating, for space heating and heating of domes-
tic hot water. 
 
Power consumption 
Power consumptions due to lighting and equipment is mainly during occupied hours, 
but a ventilation system is typically running 24/7, either with a decreased load or full 
load during unoccupied hours. A VAV system can reduce the air supply during inoc-
cupation, while CAV systems can be forced to run at full load, if the system cannot 
be turned off (due to regulations or the physical design of the system). 
 
District heating consumption 
Buildings does mainly consume district heating for space heating and domestic hot 
water, but also heating coils for ventilation systems can be utilizing DH. The DH re-
turn pipe on the primary side of the substation is equipped with a meter to settle 
accounts for the heat consumption. This meter is either measuring the consumed 
energy [kWh], calculated by the water volume and the cooling hereof, or simply just 
the water volume [m3] independently of the return temperature.  
DH suppliers are working on reducing the supply- and return temperatures and sim-
ultaneously ensuring the highest possible temperature difference in the between 
the supply- and return pipes. This is done in order to minimize the heat loss from the 
pipe system and to optimize the heat production. The difference in between the sup-
ply- and return temperatures has an impact on the necessary mass flow, needed to 
deliver according to the heat demand and also the power consumption of the circu-
lation pumps. 
An increase in the supply temperature, as a solution to increase the temperature 
difference, would result in larger temperature fluctuations within the pipe system 
and larger heat losses to the surroundings. Thus, a lowering of the return tempera-
ture is preferable, if possible. The return temperature is very dependent on the heat-
ing systems and controls hereof at the consumer side. Bad cooling of the DH water 
can have a large range of reasons, but is mainly due to wrong dimensioning, bad sys-
tem design, use of bypasses and DHW circulation [19].  
In order to give consumers an incitement to save on the heat, the prices often com-
bine the energy- and volume measurements, and some DH suppliers has tariff incite-
ments on the return temperature.

Service system controls 
Service systems are usually controlled by a combined control system, called a build-
ing management system (BMS). BMS systems monitors and controls the technical 
building installations and services within a building. BMS systems links the function-
ality of the service systems and operates them as a single integrated system. The 
controls of the ventilation, DHW and SH system (described in the following sections) 
are all implementable in a combined BMS system.

2.4.1 Ventilation system controls 
Ventilation systems can be controlled from a range of different requirements to the 
indoor climate. The control can be adjusted to meet user demands on parameters 
such as CO2 concentration, air temperature, air humidity and air pressure. Control of 
the system in regard to the indoor climate parameters requires sensors that can reg-
ulate e.g. fans, dampers and heating/cooling coils.  
CAV systems can be controlled by a single IAQ parameter set-point and would there-
fore have compromises on either energy use or IEQ. VAV control systems enables 
precise- and demand corresponding air supply and ensures a comfortable indoor cli-
mate the occupants. The VAV control system can furthermore save energy, by low-
ering the air supply when there is less demand [27]. Hybrid systems would typically 
consist of either a CAV- or a VAV controlled system in combination with manually 
operated windows. Natural ventilation systems can though be controlled automati-
cally by IAQ set-points as well as the mechanical ventilation systems. 
 
2.4.2 DHW system controls 
Domestic hot water systems are usually operated on a fixed 24/7 schedule with a 
constant temperature setpoint and the distribution systems in larger buildings does 
typically have a circulation loop with a circulation pump on the secondary side of the 
heat exchanger. The circulation pump can be of either a fixed- or a variable mass 
flow.  
 
2.4.3 SH system controls 
An indirect space heating system control does typically consist of a weather com-
pensation system, where the supply temperature on the secondary side of the heat 
exchanger (as shown on Figure 3 in section 2.1) is controlled in relation to the out-
door temperature. This control is generally performed on a motorized valve on the 
SH supply pipe and a circulation pump is then ensuring satisfactory pressure levels 
to keep the water circulating in the distribution system. The weather compensation 
is typically controlled by a heating curve, determining a fixed relation in between the 
outside air temperature and the SH supply temperature on the secondary side of the 
heat exchanger. The heating curve control has an upper limit in regard to the deliv-
ered temperatures by the DH supply.  
Many control systems have a possibility to implement operational schedules, which 
can be put on the motorized valve or the circulation pump. Schedules can e.g. be set 
to limit the water mass flow and temperatures during night, weekends or other un-
occupied hours.  
 
Thermostats 
Individual thermostatic radiator valves (TRVs) are typically installed on all heating 
appliances, in order to control the water mass flow according to the required indoor 
air temperature and the corresponding heat output of the heating appliance. The 
TRVs are operating autonomously and do not require any auxiliary energy.  
The most common TRVs has a build-in temperature sensor, while other types have 
a separate sensor connected with a capillary tube. Figure 4 shows an example of a 
==PAGE:27==
| 15 
 
typical TRV with a build-in sensor. The TRV consists of a valve body and a thermo-
static head, functioning as the actuator and control unit. If the sensor detects tem-
peratures below the setpoint it will expand. The corrugated tube will then be com-
pressed and open up the valve seat by putting less pressure on the valve spindle. 
Thus, the water mass flow will increase until the ambient air temperature reaches 
the setpoint. The TRVs does generally have a proportional band of approx. 2°C and 
they are regulated towards the temperature setpoint, which is set manually by the 
thermostatic head. The valve is regulating the mass flow in relation to the tempera-
ture difference in between the thermostat setpoint and the temperature detected 
by the sensor [28].  
Programmable electronic TRVs does exist for residential use. These smart TRVs can 
be controlled wirelessly with operational schedules and in some cases be pro-
grammed to detect open windows, by dramatic air temperature reductions, and then 
temporary reduce the mass flow according to the window openings. The digital TRV 
types are though yet to be programmed for larger systems and they are typically 
little resistant to vandalism and user interference, which can be problematic in e.g. 
institutional buildings.

3.7 
Building management system 
The building management system on Borgerskolen, and in the investigated building 
Vinkelbygningen, is a CTS system (Central Tilstandskontrol og Styring). The BMS sys-
tem controls the air handling unit, the domestic hot water and the space heating 
system. The school is divided into several buildings/zones controlled individually. The 
investigated building, Vinkelbygningen, has an individual BMS control for both the 
AHU, the DHW and the space heating.  
The building management system is controlled by the municipality of Høje-Taastrup 
at the facility management department (Center for Ejendomme og Intern Service), 
and the system controls can only be accessed by a physical presence at the control 
computer at the school, or by a remote access from Høje-Taastrup town hall [12]. 
 
3.7.1 AHU control 
The BMS settings for the mechanical VAV ventilation system has a supply air tem-
perature setpoint of 21°C, a supply pressure setpoint of 115 Pa and a return pressure 
setpoint of 160 Pa [12]. The fan is running 24/7 [13], and the fan speed is controlled 
indirectly by the CO2 level, as the CO2 sensors controls the exhaust dampers in the 
class rooms. The CO2 sensors are thereby influencing the air pressure in the system, 
of which the BMS system is maintaining at the given pressure setpoints.  
The minimum air supply during occupied hours is assumed to be 0.35 l/s/m2 floor 
area, as demanded in classrooms by the current Danish building code [20]. The max-
imum CO2 concentration setpoint is assumed to be approx. 1600 ppm based on the 
actual concentrations measured (see section 5.1).  
 
Duct system distribution losses are assumed to be 1.16 W/m2 floor area and the 
assumption include both thermal conduction and air leakage losses [34]. 
 
3.7.2 DHW control 
The DHW circulation pump is constantly running and cannot be controlled by the 
BMS system. The temperature meters on the supply- and return at the secondary 
side of the hot water exchanger can be monitored in the BMS system, as well as the 
return temperature on the primary side of the exchanger can be monitored. The ac-
tual control of the DHW circulation loop is controlled automatically by the tempera-
ture inputs to the valves, shown on Figure 11. The automatic control is set to comply 
with a supply temperature of 55ºC from the hot water exchanger.  
The total DHW consumption are assumed to be 20 m3 per year, based on historic 
data from the last 3 years. The total monthly DHW consumptions are shown in Table 
7, in section 5.2. The total volume is distributed evenly out on the occupancy sched-
ule.  
 
Distribution losses from the DHW system are assumed to be 0.5 W/m2 floor area and 
the losses are specified to account for pipes that pass through the building without 
having to describe their exact path and insulation properties [34].

4 Method 
The main research of this master’s thesis is done as a case study on the operation 
of the existing school building, Vinkelbygningen (see chapter 3). The aim is to im-
prove the indoor climate and reduce the energy consumption, due to optimized op-
eration. The primary methods applied to address the objective has been; a literature 
review, field registrations, IoT measurements, simulations and field testing. 
The following sections are presenting the more specific tools and methods, which 
has been applied during the project. 
 
 
4.1 
Field registrations 
All of the descriptions of the case study (in chapter 3) are based on physical regis-
trations-, measurements- and observations of the building physics and -systems. Cer-
tain diagrams build on historical drawings, which has been further developed and 
substantially updated to show the current state of the building.  
The physical registrations did e.g. include identification-, categorization- and meas-
urements of windows, heating appliances and hot water distribution systems. User 
behavior- and influences was monitored and evaluated alongside every visit to the 
building, as well. 
 
4.1.1 Thermographic pictures 
Research on existing data from thermographic pictures, of the heating appliances 
in the building, were performed, in order to get an overview of the issues with the 
current operation of the heating system. The observations made has a major influ-
ence on the chosen locations for the installation of the IoT temperature sensors on 
the DH system (se section 4.2). 
The thermographic pictures of the heating appliances and -pipes were taken in con-
nection with a correlation analysis of the heating system in Vinkelbygningen [11]. 
Figure 19 is showing an example of how two identical convectors has a different 
heat output, even though they are situated in the same room and therefore should 
be supplying similar heat outputs in order to have an optimal operation. These imbal-
ances in the system are not rarely seen, as the registered temperatures in Appendix 
M also shows. 
==PAGE:48==
36 |  
 
The maximum registered supply temperature to a heating appliance was 46.1ºC, 
which seems rather low for a day in the end of January (29th of January 2018). The 
maximum return temperature was 42.0ºC and 15 of the 58 registered appliances had 
a return temperature above 30ºC, with a poor utilization of the supplied heat. The 
poor heat utilizations registered are most likely due to high water flows in the appli-
ances, as individual appliances are making up for the appliances with a lower ther-
mostat setting, when located in the same zone. The poor cooling is most likely also 
due to the low supply temperatures of the water, which demands a higher mass flow 
than higher supply temperatures, in order to maintain the temperature setpoint of 
the thermostat.

4.1.2 Occupant influences 
The use of the building and the user behavior can have large impacts on the general 
indoor climate and energy consumption. The major sources to an increase in the en-
ergy consumption has therefore been registered and implemented in the simulations 
with the assumptions stated in chapter 3. 
A range of windows has been registered fully open in unoccupied rooms and hall-
ways several times. Figure 20 shows an example of how the windows above the 
heating appliances can be left wide open during the heating season. Registrations 
made on the 30th of November counted 7 open windows during the afternoon, even 
though the building is equipped with a mechanical ventilation system in all of the 
classrooms. The major sources to an increased energy use are when the heat losses 
through the open windows makes the thermostats open up the thermostatic valves 
to maximum capacity, in order to compensate for the decrease in the indoor air tem-
perature. A similar problem occurs in relation to the automatic entrance doors, where 
the heating appliances located close by are running at very high capacity levels, due 
==PAGE:49==
| 37 
 
to the large infiltrations through the door openings. Figure 49 (in section 4.4.2) 
shows an example of how a radiator has been placed right next to an automatic en-
trance door. 
User influence on individual thermostats within the same heating zone are a main 
source to an unbalanced heating system. This is especially a problem in institutional- 
and public buildings, where the users are having less direct economic consequences 
by an unbalanced and less efficient heating system. Many variable settings were reg-
istered within the same rooms, which has been due to adjustments of individual ther-
mostats, probably by the occupants located right next to the given thermostat (see 
Appendix L for TRV setting registrations).

the operation of the building systems, including physical modifications on the sys-
tems in the building.  
The EQUA software IDA ICE has been used for the dynamic building simulations in 
this project. IDA ICE is accurately modelling the building, its systems and its control-
lers [34].

a setpoint of 19ºC, the entrances 17ºC and the unoccupied 2nd floor of the northern

4.4.2 System balancing 
An uneven distribution of heat between single heating appliances or entire heating 
zones is a result of a heating system in imbalance. A heating system is, on the other 
hand, balanced when an even distribution of hot water is ensured. This will result in 
minimum operational costs and ensure optimum conditions to provide a comfortable 
indoor climate.  
A range of simple- and proven methods has been used to optimize the heat distri-
bution and to reduce the mass flow through the heating system. Bleeding of the 
heating appliances was essential, in order to have an even heat distribution inside 
the individual appliances and the appliances in between each other. The thermostatic 
valves mounted on each heating appliance compensates for load variations, when 
adjusted correctly. The thermostats were therefore checked for malfunctions and 
adjusted to ensure an even heat distribution. Rogue appliances and -networks were 
identified and dealt with, as a part of the balancing as well.  
 
Bleeding of heating appliances 
The entire space heating system was emptied for air on the 30th of November and 
a few of the appliances was emptied again on the 19th of December 2018. The air-
filled appliances were mainly found on the 2nd floor of the building, but some of the 
appliances located at the top of a riser loop was registered containing air as well.  
When the heating appliances contains air, they would struggle to get sufficiently 
warm, as the upper half of the appliance would contain the less warm air, on behalf 
of the hot water that should have been running through instead. An air filled heating 
appliance would therefore never be able to reach the nominal output. 
Figure 46 is showing how a bleed valve key is used to bleed a convector. The key 
is used to open the valve for air to pass through the little black hole seen above 
where the key is inserted. The valve is kept open until water starts trickling out. 
 
Thermostat replacements 
New thermostatic heads have been installed at valves where the thermostat was 
either missing or rogue. Figure 47 is showing an example of a vandalized thermo-
static head, which has been broken off the valve socket. When the valve is missing a 
thermostatic head, the heating appliance is running at maximum load and most likely 
sending large amounts of warm water back in the return pipe system.  
A rogue/defect TRV can worst case scenario result in a fully open valve or a fully 
closed valve. It can also result in a wrong and unwanted setpoint, which is not match-
ing the setting of the thermostat. The rogue thermostatic heads found in the build-
ing, was due to missing/broken setting limitations at the setting endpoints. The ther-
mostats could therefore exceed the manufacturers minimum- and/or maximum pre-
settings.  
Two thermostatic heads were added, due to vandalism, on the 30th of November 
2018. One additional TRV was missing on the 14th of December, and three other 
thermostatic heads were found defect and replaced as well. Another TRV were found 
vandalized on the 17th of January 2019 and was replaced with a new one.

within the rooms. When the settings within a room are different from one another, 
the appliances with the highest setpoint is compensating for the turned off appli-
ances with a lower setpoint. This is resulting in a bad heat distribution within the 
room and in the heating system in general. A single heating appliance would most

were registered within the same rooms, which has been due to adjustments of indi-

setting of 3 is according to Table 5 (in section 3.6.5) approx. 20ºC, while a setting of 
2 is approx. 16ºC. The lower setting of the hallways and toilets were mainly due to 
observations of the many open windows during occupied hours, which results in huge

air temperatures. All of the hallway TRVs and a range of the classroom TRVs were 
readjusted back to a setting of 2 and 3 for the hallways and classrooms respectively, 
as too high air temperatures and/or too little cooling of the SH water were experi-

5.3 
Recommendations 
A range of recommendations to improve the building operation further are given in 
the following sections. Some of the recommendations includes estimated invest-
ment costs and simple payback times. The advices will primarily be dealing with the 
return temperatures, the thermal climate, the implementation of LTDH and how to 
improve the current sensor setup. 
 
If any of the heating appliances in the building are operated at too high mass flows 
(e.g. at full output) the return temperature will be increased. Thus, the recommended 
first priority implementation is to install institutional thermostats on all of the heat-
ing appliances (see section 5.3.1) in order to maintain the current low return temper-
atures in the system.  
The second recommendation would be to add heating capacity in critical classrooms 
(see section 5.3.2) and to add heating capacity in the hallways and restrooms (see 
section 5.3.3). An increased heat output in the critical classrooms would most likely 
improve the thermal climate, the return temperatures and open up for further reduc-
tions in the supply temperature. The hallways and restrooms are currently under-
dimensioned for maintaining acceptable occupied zone air temperatures and would 
therefore require additional heat output if the zones should be heated as occupied 
zones, without compromising the return temperature. 
A third recommendation is to evaluate an installation of flow limiting valves in the 
hallways (see section 5.3.4) and to reduce the air changes from- and to the building 
entrances (see section 5.3.6).

pacity improvements in the most critical classrooms has to be applied. If to maintain 
the same heat output from a heating appliance, it can either be operated at a higher

erate with a lower supply temperature and higher flow rate, and thereby sacrificing

the lower return temperature [50]. A compromise on the return temperature is unde-

The remaining parameter to increase is the installed capacity in the most critical 
zones of the building. This section suggests dealing with the critical classrooms 
alone, while the following section will deal with the hallways and restrooms as well. 
The most critical classrooms are defined by having too little heating capacity, when 
compared to the increased demand by a lowered supply temperature. Figure 58, Fig-

either two- or three older column radiators, which is recommended to be exchanged 
by new appliances with a larger output.

Application of automatic flow limiting valves on exposed heating appliances has 
therefore been considered and evaluated throughout the project. The heating appli-

of the heating appliances in the hallways are located on the main distribution pipe in

air around the given thermostats, which would make the valves start closing.

Thermostatic radiator valves with automatic flow limitation has an integrated flow 
limiter that prevents unwanted overflows. The maximum mass flow can be adjusted 
directly at the valve and the preset maximum flow will then not be exceeded at any 
time. The valve controls the mass flow independently from the differential pressure 
and overrules the thermostatic control [51]. 
 
Table 14 shows the estimated mass flows at full power of the heating appliances 
in the hallways and entrances. The mass flows range in between 14 l/h and 44 l/h. A 
thermostatic radiator valve with a differential pressure controller are though recom-
mended to be limited to no less than 25 l/h or 10 l/h for Danfoss and IMI Hydronic 
valves respectively [51], [52]. A limitation of 25 l/h would be insufficient for the ma-
jority of the radiators in the hallways, while 10 l/h might be sufficient for the majority 
of the radiators.  
Implementation and installation of the flow limiting valves would require further 
investigations (in terms of dimensioning and saving potentials), and the time frame 
of this project did not allow for physical testing of this. It is therefore recommended 
to look further into a solution with application of flow limiting valves. 
 
5.3.5 Consider redesigning the DHW system 
The DHW system is currently a limiting factor for a low return temperature and it is 
especially resulting in high return temperatures during months with little- or no heat 
demand. One of the major barriers of implementing LTDH is the increased legionella 
risk with the lower supply temperatures [50]. The current heating setpoint of the 
DHW supply is 55ºC, in order to reduce the legionella risks. The supply temperature 
can e.g. be lowered with a system heated through a combination of DH and electricity 
(or even electricity alone). Such a hybrid system would typically heat the water to a 
temperature of approx. 35ºC, and then boost the temperature to approx. 45ºC by a 
small heat pump or an instantaneous electric heater [53].

The DHW distribution and circulation network of Vinkelbygningen is rather large for 
the very little consumption (see Table 8 in section 5.2.2). Thus, there could be some 
potential savings on the heat losses and a potential for a lower return temperature 
with a different system setup. It is therefore recommended to evaluate the current 
design of the DHW system and to look into the possibility of boosting- or heating 
entirely by electricity.  
 
A part of the DHW supply pipe is currently uninsulated, as Figure 75 shows. The 
uninsulated part of the pipe stretches over approx. 4 meters and is causing unnec-
essary heat losses. Thus, it is recommended to insulate this part of the supply pipe. 
 
5.3.6 Reduce the entrance heat losses 
The automatic entrance doors are causing large air changes and large temperature 
fluctuations in the entrance areas. The current design of the entrances is therefore 
evaluated to be non-heatable with the currently installed heating appliances (see 
section 4.4.2). 
An easy implementable recommendation is to ensure all of the doors, connected to 
the indoor zones from the entrances, are closing automatically. Entrance C2 does 
furthermore need a door at the top of the stairs to the 2nd floor, in order to minimize 
the heat losses from the 2nd floor hallway. It is very recommended to install a door 
here. 
 
A second recommendation would be to evaluate if the automatic opening of the 
entrance doors is necessary. Automatic closings alone would cause less heat losses 
to the surroundings. The C2 entrance door is especially a problem, as it opens when 
occupants move through the entrance area (e.g. when walking from the teacher’s 
room to the hallway).  
The TRVs in the entrances are currently on anti-freeze mode, but does still reach 
rather high temperatures due to the location of the thermostats (see Figure 49 in 
section 4.4.2 for an example of an exposed TRV). The thermostats located right next 
to openings towards the outside air would have to be relocated or fitted with flow 
limiting valves (see section 5.3.4) in order to reduce unnecessary heat losses. 
 
5.3.7 Improve the sensor setup 
It is recommended to create a combined data platform for all of the data collected 
in the building, in order to control efficiently and to make fault detection easier for 
the building operation managers. Prediction and forecasting of the user behavior and 
desires is another feature that could be implemented to an intelligent setup with a 
combined sensor- and control system, with plausible benefits for both the indoor cli-
mate and the energy consumption.  
 
It is advised to look into electronic ‘smart’ thermostatic heads as an alternative for 
the regular thermostatic heads and eventually also for the installed IoT air tempera-
ture sensors. The smart thermostatic heads can be controlled by time schedules with 
different setpoint temperatures, depending on the time of the day. Additionally, they

can be programmed to detect open windows, by registering large and rapid temper-
ature drops.  
Smart TRVs are also capable of interacting with each other and to be controlled 
wirelessly from a single platform. The smart thermostatic heads, which is currently 
on the market, are though intended for residential use and not yet implementable in 
larger systems. There is also a lack of protection for institutional uses, where a lim-
ited user influence and protection against vandalism is desired [54].  
 
Another recommendation is to avoid battery powered sensors, where it is possible, 
in order to eliminate data gaps from down-time and recharging. It is recommended to 
have sensors with a constant power supply, and ideally with a battery as a backup if 
the power supply has breakdowns.

6 Discussion 
The main task of this project was to optimize the heating system performance of 
the case study building and to increase the thermal comfort for the occupants. The 
study was performed through physical- and sensor based field registrations and the 
proposed alterations was investigated with dynamic building simulations prior to ac-
tual implementations. 
 
 
6.1 
Assumptions 
The field registrations were making the basis for the simulations and documenting 
the impacts of the alterations made on the systems. Even though the project includes 
a highly detailed sensor setup, it has been necessary to make a range of assumptions 
and simplifications, which will be discussed briefly in the following sections. Assump-
tions regarding the estimated investment costs and simple payback times will be de-
scribed as well. 
 
6.1.1 Registrations and measurements 
Assumptions and simplifications of the building physics, service system designs and 
user interference were mainly registered physically on the site, while the energy 
consumption, system temperatures and indoor climate where accompanied by sensor 
based measurements. 
 
The physical registrations did e.g. include identification-, categorization- and meas-
urements of constructions and service systems. The sizing and assumptions related 
to the physical characteristics of the building elements can therefore be varying 
slightly from the actual characteristics.  
User behavior and the influences hereof were monitored and evaluated alongside 
every on-site visit, as random spot tests, and is therefore not necessarily a proof for 
the actual normal conditions. The main user influences on the thermal climate and 
the heating system included infiltration due to window- and door openings, unbal-
anced TRV settings and vandalism on TRVs and heating appliances.  
 
==PAGE:98==
86 |  
 
The building operation data has been collected with a range of different IoT sensors 
with different purposes. The IC-Meters measures the indoor climate conditions and 
the NorthQ sensors measures heat-, water- and electricity consumptions, while the 
ReMoni sensors measures the temperatures on the heating system and the sur-
rounding air temperatures. 
Most of the battery powered IC-Meters where down due to lack of battery during 
larger periods of the study. Thus, the thermal climate only was measured in a few 
selected zones during these periods. Additionally, very few IC-Meters were located 
in the hallways and the IC-Meters were therefore supplemented by air temperature 
measurements from the ReMoni sensors located in the hallways. A smaller range of 
the ReMoni sensors does also have several data gaps due to the location of the sen-
sors and the limited connection range of the gateway. The thermal environment 
might therefore mistakenly have been considered acceptable due to an absence of 
data (either due to battery break down or a lack of sensors in the given zones). Addi-
tionally, the location of the air temperature sensor influences the measured temper-
atures. The IC-Meters are all wall-mounted and not necessarily representing the ther-
mal climate within the occupied zone of the given rooms.  
However, the extent of the uncertainty that follows all of these assumptions and 
due to the many installed sensors at various locations, it is not considered to be a 
significantly large uncertainty. 
 
The district heating energy consumption are measured with a NorthQ sensor on the 
primary side of the DH substation, while all of the adjustments has been made on the 
space heating system, on the secondary side of the SH exchanger. The change in the 
energy consumption on the SH system alone is therefore not possible to isolate from 
the data, as the primary side consumption includes the DHW- and AHU consumption 
as well. It would have been an advantage to be able to isolate each of the DH con-
suming sources, in order to extract the specific SH consumption changes due to the 
adjustments performed during the project.  
 
6.1.2 Dynamic building simulations 
Several building model calibrations have been performed, in order to create a real-
istic base for the proposed changes to the building operation. The simulated condi-
tions are based on the physical on-site registrations and the simulations does gener-
ally match the on-site sensor measurements very well, when considering the dissim-
ilarities due to the outdoor temperature.  
The many simplifications and assumptions related to the building model might 
though make the simulated outputs deviate from the actual measurements, when 
further parameter adjustments are added to the simulation model. The outputs from 
the initial simulation model and the adjusted models does however appear to corre-
late with the reality, when the impacts of the adjustments have been measured on-
site. Hence, the probability of the assumptions related to the building simulations 
being correct is fairly high. 
 
==PAGE:99==
| 87 
 
6.1.3 Investment costs and -benefits 
The estimated investment costs and simple payback times of this thesis is a simple 
indication of the expected total costs and benefits of the alterations. The recom-
mended investments are aimed at a reduced return temperature and finally to pre-
pare the case study building for low-temperature district heating. 
 
The estimations include a great deal of uncertainty and is based on very simple 
calculations. The unit costs are based on assumed average prices or corresponding 
to prices estimated by D. S. Østergaard [7]. Working hour costs related to the 
installation and calculations of simple payback times are based on the studies by D. 
S. Østergaard [7] as well. The calculations does not consider interest rates nor 
currency inflation.  
Regarding the savings, it is assumed that the consumption is unchanged and that 
only the difference in the return temperature incitement tariff discount is calculated 
as the savings. The increased consumption of the new settings is neglected in the 
calculations, as it is assumed to be a necessary change, in order to achieve an ac-
ceptable indoor thermal climate. Hence, the increased comfort of occupants is an ad-
ditional benefit which is difficult to quantify but has been included indirectly by ne-
glecting the increased energy consumption (due to increased occupant comfort) in 
the simple payback time calculations.  
All of the listed uncertainties and assumptions influences the total costs of the 
changes to the heating system and the costs may therefore be higher than estimated 
in this study. However, it seems probable that the current analysis provides a rea-
sonable cost estimate for the implementation of a reduced return temperature and a 
feasibility for low-temperature district heating in the case study building. 
 
 
6.2 
Final reflections 
The findings of this study do show a considerable potential for implementing strat-
egies and methods to lower the return temperature and thereby prepare existing 
buildings for low-temperature district heating.  
Nevertheless, the findings need to be further strengthened through additional re-
search and studies in a larger scale with a larger range of case studies. 
 
6.2.1 Method scalability 
The methods applied in this study includes a large range of IoT sensors and a huge 
amount of physical registrations, which most likely will be too extensive to replicate 
directly on similar existing buildings in a larger scale. Hence, a simplified and less 
extensive approach would be preferable and necessary in terms of scaling up the 
methods and corresponding outcomes to other existing buildings. 
 
Consistent commissioning with elimination of malfunctions and wrong settings can 
be performed in many ways and does not necessarily require a large sensor setup, in 
order to locate heating system malfunctions.  
6 Discussion 
==PAGE:100==
88 |  
 
Radiator bleeding is e.g. an easy method to ensure the heating system is capable 
of reaching nominal outputs and the air filled radiators can actually be detected eas-
ier by physical inspection than by interpreting sensor data.  
Another solution that would make a potential for large improvements, without hav-
ing a direct need for a large sensor setup, would be to install protective institutional 
TRVs. Manual TRVs in institutional buildings are exposed to many users with differ-
ent demands and the heating systems in these types of buildings can easily experi-
ence imbalances. An easy implementable solution to this would be to install protec-
tive TRVs to limit the user influences and thereby possibly achieve similar outcomes 
as found during this study.  
 
The physical registrations and the simulations were made in order to detect missing 
heating capacity and did require an extensive preliminary work. The physical regis-
trations would be difficult to do without, unless sufficient service system- and con-
structional information exists from beforehand. The simulations could probably be 
replaced by simple heat loss calculations. The calculations would however still have 
to be made individually on each of the heated zones, in order to locate critical zones 
with under-dimensioned heating output.   
 
A less extensive method approach does though come with a risk of larger uncer-
tainties and incorrect assumptions. The less extensive approach would therefore re-
quire a more consistent system commissioning, in order to minimize faulty settings 
due to the modest setup and preliminary work.  
 
6.2.2 Further work 
The findings of this study need to be further strengthened through additional re-
search on a larger scale. Future research needs to be carried out on more buildings 
and several other types of buildings, in order to verify the hypothesis on a more gen-
eral level.  
In terms of strengthening the findings and recommendations for this specific case 
study, the suggested necessary changes on the heating system has to be applied 
and the outcomes has to be documented. The findings and recommendations of the 
case study show potential for LTDH implementation but does fully realize and docu-
ment a LTDH implementation, without these implementations being made.

7 Conclusion 
As demonstrated through the findings of this thesis, it is possible to optimize the 
building operation of an existing school building – from the 1920’s – by eliminating 
malfunctions and reducing the district heating return temperature. 
The optimizations depend on detailed fault detection and correction. The fault de-
tection has been carried out with both physical- and sensor based registrations on a 
very detailed level. The detailed data from the case study building has made it pos-
sible to use simple and proven methods to correct the detected malfunctions, and 
thereby reduce the overall return temperature of the district heating system substa-
tion.   
 
The current heating system capacity of the case study building has been proven to 
be sufficient at 70/40/20ºC design temperatures. The simulated design heat loss of 
the building is 73.7 kW (see section 4.3.2) and the total nominal output of the heating 
appliances is 72.9 kW (see section 3.6.4). Thus, the overall system capacity is suffi-
cient (0.8 kW short and actually slightly under-dimensioned) for the design outdoor 
temperature of -12ºC, but not necessarily sufficient for every zone, when evaluated 
independently (see section 5.1.4).  
The system is though over-dimensioned in terms of being sufficient for the design 
outdoor temperature, which rarely occurs in reality. Thus, it is possible to operate the 
heating system with lower temperatures during most of the year without having to 
run at full load. 
 
Errors and malfunctions can be detected and eliminated by simple- and proven 
methods as demonstrated by the fault detections and corrections performed on the 
case study building (see section 4.4.2) and has been demonstrated to be very effec-
tive to reduce the space heating return temperatures and improve the thermal indoor 
climate (see chapter 5). 
 
It has been proven possible to ensure an acceptable thermal indoor climate while 
reducing the district heating return temperature (see section 5.1). The indoor air tem-
peratures were initially too cold (in certain zones), according to the indoor climate 
regulations, but is now acceptable. There are though still room for improvements, in 
terms of fine-tuning the desired temperature setpoints according to the occupant 
needs. The hallways are currently functioning as groupwork areas, but the heating 
system capacity in these zones are insufficient to ensure temperatures acceptable 
==PAGE:102==
90 |  
 
for an occupied zone, without compromising the return temperatures. Additionally, 
some of the classrooms has to be equipped with more heating capacity if the supply 
temperature is desired to be lowered further, without compromising the return tem-
perature.  
 
Energy savings due to a lowered return temperature and the corresponding incite-
ment tariff discounts can pay off the necessary investments on the heating system 
(see section 5.3.1, 5.3.2 and 5.3.3). The analysis shows the estimated necessary in-
vestments on the heating system have payback times in the range of 1.09-4.71 
years, when looking at the return temperature discounts independently. Increases in 
operational hours and total heat consumptions has been neglected in these calcula-
tions, as it is evaluated that a thermal indoor climate has to be ensured at all occupied 
hours, and this was not sufficiently ensured with the initial system settings.  
 
Concerning the DHW system, it is plausible that it is energy- and cost optimal to skip 
the domestic hot water circulation in favor of decentral electrical heating, but it has 
not been verified through this study. It has not been possible to demonstrate a good 
example of how to redesign the DHW system, due to the extends of the project, and 
it is therefore not possible to verify this part of the hypothesis. Possible redesigns 
and -modifications were described along with a recommendation to evaluate the cur-
rent system and the potential benefits of a system redesign. 
 
 
Based on the findings and prerequisites of this project, it can be concluded that it 
is feasible to optimize the building operation of an existing school building, by elimi-
nation of malfunctions and reductions in the district heating return temperature. 
Thus, the main hypothesis is considered to be true.  
The performed optimizations can be considered economically favorable for both the 
customer and for the overall energy system. Additionally, the optimizations result in 
an improved thermal climate, favorable for the building occupants.  
The main barriers of achieving low-temperature district heating in this specific case 
study is currently a range of critical heating appliances and the large user influences 
on the heating system operation.

R 
Site visit note 
 
Note from site visit at Borgerskolen, Høje-Taastrup, 22nd of January 2019: 
 
 
 
Notat efter besøg på Borgerskolen, Høje Taastrup, d. 22. januar 2019 
 
Generelt fint termisk indeklima i L-bygningen (udskoling). Der blev ændret på nogle termostater 
i enkelte lokaler, der havde radiatorer med utilfredsstillende afkøling. 
Der var højere temperatur end ønsket på flere gangarealer. Termostater blev låst i lavere interval. 
Flere vinduer var ikke lukket tæt til. Oplukkelig lodpost til brandflugt var ikke lukket korrekt, 
hvilket blokerede for at den gående ramme ved siden af kunne lukke ordentligt til. Kold ind-
trængende luft fra utæthederne er lige over termostaterne, og kan potentielt give termostaten en 
misvisende måling af rumtemperaturen. 
Radiatoren ved toiletter i stueplan var afspærret, og gav dermed ingen varme. Radiatoren på 
toiletter på 1. sal var meget varm med ringe afkøling, og det ønskede termiske indeklima var ikke 
nået. Denne radiator er for lille (kan ikke levere ønsket effekt ift. rummets varmetab). 
I det store uudnyttede rum i tagetagen var to af tre radiatorer helt slukket (og muligvis også 
afbrudt), mens den tredje radiator stod på 4 og var meget varm (meget ringe afkøling). 
Der blev fundet ca. 4 meter rør til varmt brugsvand uden isolering. Rørene løber oppe under 
loftet i fællesarealer i stueplan, omkring hjørnet hvor de to bygninger er bygget sammen. 
 
Pumper: 
*Centralvarme: Ingen ændring 
*Brugsvand: Fra trin III (44W) til trin I (5W) 
*Til ventilation fra varmecentral: Fra trin III (22W) til AutoAdapt (pumpen justerede selv til 5W) 
*Til ventilation i tagetage: Fra trin III (22W) til AutoAdapt (pumpen justerede selv til 22W)
