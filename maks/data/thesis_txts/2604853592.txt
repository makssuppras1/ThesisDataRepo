Abstract
A parallel framework for 3D topology optimization of Acoustic-Structure Interaction(ASI)
problems is implemented based on a simple method for ASI. The parallel framework is
written in C++ and uses the PETSc library for parallelization. The method for ASI is based
on extended FEM where the structural, acoustic, and coupling matrices are assembled in
the entire domain. Though the computational complexity is increased, the advantage of
the method is the use of regular structural and acoustic FEM. Moreover, the method does
not require tracking of the acoustic-structure interface during optimization. The frame-
work is deemed efficient owing to the parallelization and simplicity of the method when
compared to other FEM based methods such as mixed formulation. In the developed
framework, the simplicity of the method is preserved while the associated increased com-
putational complexity is tackled by speedup through parallelization.
The accuracy of the ASI method can be controlled by a contrast parameter that is used to
set the artificial material parameters. In this work, it is shown that the simple methodology
closely matches the results of the conventional ASI methodology. Furthermore, the ap-
plication to topology optimization has been demonstrated with examples. Through which,
some key aspects of the optimization framework are illustrated and verified. Additionally,
the scalability of the framework is shown.

Introduction
Density-based topology optimization has been in use for decades since the seminal work
of Martin Philip Bendsøe and Kikuchi 1988. Though topology optimization was originally
formulated for structural problems its use has extended to other physics such as acoustics
and heat transfer. The recent advancements, deal with applying topology optimization to
solve coupled multi-physics problems. The challenge in these works, being to solve these
problems accurately and efficiently. The multi-physics problems are inherently complex
from a topology optimization perspective due to the increased computational complexity
and implementation of the coupling of involved physics.
Topology optimization is already being applied to a popular class of multi-physics prob-
lems, Fluid-Structure Interaction(FSI) in works such as Andreasen and Ole Sigmund 2013.
A sub-class of FSI, Acoustic-Structure Interaction(ASI) has become more relevant than
ever. The growing relevance is mainly associated with the increase in miniaturization of
devices and usage of MEMS. In all such applications, the coupling between the acoustic
and structural physics must be considered while designing. Furthermore, topology opti-
mization is well suited in designing such devices due to their small size and advanced
manufacturing capabilities, which allow for easy and rapid realization of the generated
design. For instance, the optimization of Hearing instruments is actively being studied
(Creixell-Mediante et al. 2018;Søndergaard and Pedersen 2014). The target being re-
ducing the feedback at the receiver of the hearing instrument. From a simplified model of
the Hearing instrument shown in Figure 1.1, the relatively small dimension and presence
of acoustic cavities can be observed. In such applications, the strong coupling i.e. two-
way coupling effect between the vibration of structure and sound pressure in the acoustic
medium cannot be ignored. Moreover, it is essential to perform optimization in 3D to have
a provision for acoustic cavities. Therefore it is of interest to develop topology optimization
method for acoustic-structure interaction in 3D.
1.1
Topology Optimization for Acoustic Structure Interaction
Two approaches are widely used in the topology optimization of ASI problems: 1. Level-
set methods and 2. Density-based methods. The former allows for the definition of a
crisp acoustic-structure interface by level-set curve, which is updated at every iteration.
Whereas in density-based methods such an interface is not defined. Instead, a continuous
variable called density ρ P [0
1] is assigned to every voxel. Where ρ = 0 and ρ = 1
indicate the acoustic fluid and the structural solid respectively. Mixed FE formulation is
an approach under the density based method where the acoustic structure interface need
not be tracked(Dilgen et al. 2019a). Moreover, it overcomes the limitation that level-set
methods are very sensitive to the initial guess.
ASI problem has been successfully solved using a mixed finite element approach in 2D by
Gil Yoon, Jensen, and Ole Sigmund 2007. A more simple method has been proposed by
Jensen 2018 which is based on an extended FEM approach. In this approach, the acous-
tic and structural matrices are assembled throughout the computational domain without
the need for acoustic-structure interface tracking. The advantage is that it uses standard
structural and acoustic FEM unlike the mixed element approach in Gil Yoon, Jensen, and
Ole Sigmund 2007.
The downside of the mixed element as well as Jensen’s approach(Jensen 2018) is the

1.2
Objective and Scope of the work
The main goal of this work was to implement the methodology proposed by Jensen 2018
for topology optimization of 3D Acoustic-Structure Interaction(ASI) problems in a parallel
framework. The implementation is deemed efficient, primarily because the methodology is
simple and standard FEM for acoustic and structural physics is used. Thereby requiring
no special treatment as in the case of mixed formulation as in Gil Yoon, Jensen, and
Ole Sigmund 2007. Secondly, the implementation is parallelized to gain computational
efficiency.
1.2.1
Developed Framework
A parallel framework has been built based on the static structural optimization parallel
framework provided by Aage, Andreassen, and Boyan Lazarov 2014, which is an open-
source, fully parallel optimization program in C++. The ASI optimization framework, in this
work, has been built with an intention that it can be easily used for several optimization
problems and extended.
The structure of the developed framework is shown in Figure 1.2, where distinction can
be made between parts that were modified to a great extent or built from scratch, required
minor modifications and unchanged. Herein, major modifications imply parts where sev-
eral new methods were implemented. Whereas minor modifications are those where the
existing routines simply had to be extended to handle complex numbers. Note that the
open source implementation can only handle real field solution thereby modifications are
required in the methods to handle complex field solutions that may arise in the dynamic
problem.

1.3
Parallel Computing and PETSc environment
With the increasing computational complexity of the problems encountered in topology
optimization, the prime feasible option remains to achieve speed up by parallelization of
the solution process(Aage and Boyan Lazarov 2013). In parallel computing, the speed
up is achieved by breaking down the larger problem into smaller parts that can be solved
independently and simultaneously by multiple processors.
An important consideration in parallel codes is scalability, that is, increasing the number
of processors should result in faster execution. A detailed discussion on scalability in
the context of topology optimization framework can be found in Aage and Boyan Lazarov
2013. Where, the authors emphasize the importance of full parallelization of the topology
optimization framework. Furthermore, the authors justify the decision to use C++ rather
than conventionally faster programming languages such as FORTRAN or C. The lost
efficiency in C++ can be compensated by means of using readily available multitude of
optimized math libraries in C++. Whereas this may not be the case in FORTRAN/C, where
the libraries are limited.
1.3.1

PETSc(Portable Extendable Toolkit for Scientific Computing) allows for implementing scal-
able solution of algebraic systems. Thereby completely eliminating or minimizing the need
to explicitly use MPI(Message Passing Interface). PETSC is particularly useful for its nu-
merous linear solvers and built in parallel vector and matrix operations. Note that PETSc is
not a standalone software but rather a library. The programming model while using PETSc

The mathematical model describing the physics forms the fundament of FEM. Typically for
FEM, a mathematical model consisting of the partial differential equation describing the
domain behavior and boundary conditions must be developed. To formulate the model,
assumptions are made which impose certain limitations on physics. One must be aware
of these limitations to avoid solving a problem with a FEM that does not accurately repre-
sent the underlying physics. The governing equations of the acoustic-structure interaction
physics and the assumptions made to arrive at them are discussed in this chapter. The
presented equations are based on S.W. Rienstra 2019, Jakob Jensen 2019 and Larson
and Bengzon 2013.

Finite Element Model

Validation of Acoustic Structure
Interaction Physics
To verify the implementation of the Acoustic-Structure interaction physics, the solution ob-
tained from the PETSC implementation was bench-marked against the solution of COM-
SOL Multiphysics. It is to be noted that problems were solved with identical mesh and
the same order of interpolation functions in COMSOL. Thus the validation is a good in-
dicator of the accuracy of the extended FEM approach used in PETSC implementation.
Moreover, this forms a check for bugs, if any, in the implemented framework.
The validation has been carried out for the two problems: Problem 1 and Problem 2
for various cases representing different boundary conditions and loading to check the
accuracy of critical aspects of the implementation.
4.1
Solver Details
The solver used in PETSC framework is MUMPS(Multi-frontal Massively Parallel sparse
direct Solver), released for public use by Amestoy, Duff, and L’Excellent 2000, based on
LU factorization as a preconditioner. MUMPS is useful for solving large systems of linear
equations with a matrix that can be either unsymmetric, symmetric positive definite, or
general symmetric. The MUMPS solver and other popular direct solvers are reviewed in
Puzyrev, Koric, and Wilkin 2016. Moreover, all problems solved in this work are solved
using MUMPS.
While the COMSOL solutions have been obtained using the default solver settings in
COMSOL 5.4, which also uses MUMPS.

5
Topology Optimization
The typical computational flow in gradient-based topology optimization entails starting
from an initial guess and then iteratively solving the physics, computing sensitivities, and
updating the design by MMA(Method of Moving Asymptotes) algorithm. Additionally, fil-
tering and projection may be used to counter issues such as mesh dependency and non-
discreteness. The typical flow-chart of topology optimization can be found in p.14 of Martin

5.3
Filtering
In the material distribution based topology optimization, two commonly encountered com-
plications are mesh dependency and checkerboard pattern in the result [Martin P. Bend-
søe and O. Sigmund 2004]. Such designs are impractical to realize and hence a regular-
ization scheme is required. Sigmund [Ole Sigmund 2007] has reviewed the widely used
regularization schemes and lists the criteria for such schemes. Some of the requirements
being: ability to produce mesh-independent and checkerboard free solutions, simple im-
plementation, stable convergence, not too many constraints and tuning parameters, low
CPU time and easy to manufacture(minimum radius of features, minimum hole size etc)
designs.
In this work two regularization schemes: 1) Sensitivity filtering and 2) Density filtering are
available. In both density and sensitivity filter the element density and sensitivity respec-
tively are modified based on the elements in its neighbourhood. The neighbourhood of
an element e, denoted by N[e] consists of all those elements whose center lies within a
distance rmin from element e’s centre. This is formulated as
N[e] = ti| }c[i] ´ c[e]} ď rminu

a concern on how to treat elements at mesh boundaries. This is addressed as a pitfall
by Sigmund [Ole Sigmund 2007] as in principle one has to expand filter to include ghost
elements outside the mesh. However to simplify the implementation most works, including
this work, do not perform this special treatment.

5.4
Projection
Even though filtering overcomes most of the complexities described in the previous sec-
tion, the issue with grey transition still persists. Grey transition refers to having regions
of intermediate densities between black(upper density bound) and white(lower density
bound) regions. This could pose a problem in certain multi physics problems which are
very sensitive to the interface boundary. For instance this has been reported to be a
problem in Gil Ho Yoon and Ole Sigmund 2008, where the authors optimize electrostatic
actuators. Therefore the filtered design variable eχ has to be projected to a 0/1 space by
means of projection.
A simple projection function without the need for conditional statements has been pro-
posed in F. Wang, Boyan Stefanov Lazarov, and Ole Sigmund 2011. The projection func-
tion that maps filtered density eχ to projected density χ is given by

two parameters is illustrated in Figure 5.2. Where it can be observed that η controls the
threshold value and β controls the smoothness of the curve. For β Ñ 8, the Equation 5.20
delivers the same output as Heaviside function H(ex ´ η).

Physical variable
It must be noted that the design variable of optimization topology indicator χ is not the
same as filtered and projected topology indicator χ. The latter is referred to as physical
variable since it has a physical meaning and used in calculating the element matrices. A
consequence of this is that the correct sensitivities with respect to χ must be computed
using chain rule.

8.3
Optimization with Volume Constraint
It has been shown in Goo, Kook, and S. Wang 2020, that the optimization of the specific
problem, starting from a completely solid domain, when subjected to a volume constraint
V ˚ = 0.7 results in a design that resembles that of the 2D case produced in the works of
Gil Ho Yoon and Ole Sigmund 2008 and Dilgen et al. 2019b. In both the works, problems
with convergence at local minima has been reported. It has been recommended in Gil
Ho Yoon and Ole Sigmund 2008 to start the optimization starting with a fully solid domain

have been tuned to arrive at the expected design.

Performance of body fitted designs
For verification of the optimization solutions, the body-fitted designs, Design I and Design

or discretization was set to linear. Thus the analysis physics is identical to that used
in optimization framework. However the mesh used in COMSOL analysis is different.
Tetrahedral elements are required for meshing over the complex structural profile.
The results of the analysis in COMSOL are shown in Figure 8.8 and Figure 8.9; The
displacement field |u| of the structure and sound pressure level at the output surface are
shown.
From the displacement field, it may be observed that Design I’s response is marked by
maximum displacement at its center and minimal variation over its thickness(x ´ axis).
That is, displacement magnitude is large at the center of both, input and output, surfaces.
On the other hand, Design II has the highest displacement amplitude at the center of
the surface facing the input acoustic domain. But the surface facing the output domain
is isolated sufficiently from these large displacements. Lastly, one may observe that the
displacement field of Design I is lower compared to that of Design II.
Therefore the exciting force to output acoustic domain is smaller for Design I. Conse-
quently Design I has a much smaller pressure amplitude at the output surface. This is
evident in the sound pressure level fields shown in Figure 8.9.
The COMSOL results are following the behavior expected based on the results of the
optimization framework. Even though the optimization result Design I is very different
from the results of previous works such as Gil Yoon, Jensen, and Ole Sigmund 2007 Goo,
Kook, and S. Wang 2020, it is a better solution compared to Design II, which is qualitatively
similar to that of the previous works. To get more concrete conclusions, it is recommended
to run the same problem in the mixed formulation of Gil Yoon, Jensen, and Ole Sigmund
2007. Thereafter, compare the results of the optimization in COMSOL. Alternatively, one
could study the performance of Design I obtained by simple ASI methodology, in mixed
formulation for comparison.
For the specific problem, it was observed that achieving a design similar to that of Goo,
Kook, and S. Wang 2020 was not possible based on mesh and optimization conditions
stated in Goo, Kook, and S. Wang 2020. Furthermore, it appears that coupling interpola-
tion can influence which local minimum solution is achieved.

9
Conclusions
9.1
Summary and Conclusions
In this work, the simple methodology proposed in Jensen 2018 for 2D problems has been
extended to 3D Acoustic-Structure Interaction with application to density-based topol-
ogy optimization. The computational scheme has been presented in detail in chapter 3.
Where, especially, the details on the construction of the coupling matrices are elaborated.
A parallel framework has been built based on the static structural optimization parallel
framework provided by Aage, Andreassen, and Boyan Lazarov 2014. The developed
framework is written in C++ and uses the PETSc library for parallelization. The developed
ASI optimization framework can be easily used for several optimization problems and ex-
tended. The framework allows for passive elements and multiple-frequency optimization.
Two kinds of objective functions, Objective I and Objective II(as per the nomenclature
used in this work), have been implemented.
In the report, the accuracy of the proposed method based on extended FEM models is
demonstrated using two example problems for various boundary conditions. The imple-
mentation has been verified by comparing the extended FEM results with the results of
COMSOL. Moreover, it has been shown that it is possible to achieve sufficient accuracy
by choosing the appropriate material contrast parameter βc.
Furthermore, the application to topology optimization has been shown for two-sample
problems corresponding to the two implemented objective functions. In the first prob-
lem, aspects of the program such as projection filtering and multi-frequency optimization
were verified. The occurrence of highly non-discrete design and associated limitations in
achieving a physically meaningful design was highlighted. Thereby the use of projection
filtering is recommended. Moreover, the effect of the acoustic-structure interaction on
the optimized design was shown. Thus emphasizing the necessity of using ASI physics
for such problems. Lastly, the parallel scalability of the framework was demonstrated.
However, further study on scalability has been recommended.
As the second example, the optimization was performed on the acoustic transmission
problem which has been solved in works such as Gil Yoon, Jensen, and Ole Sigmund
2007 and Goo, Kook, and S. Wang 2020. It has been shown that the optimization frame-
work results in a design that is similar to the ones found in the relevant works. However,
the specific design was arrived at by changing the coupling interpolation functions. Fur-
ther investigation is required to study the effect of the coupling interpolation functions.
Nevertheless, the solutions were verified in COMSOL. Thus the optimization framework
appears to be accurate in the implementation of the physics and sensitivities.
9.2
Future work
Since the work has been the first of a kind implementation in 3D. There is a broad scope
for future work. A few promising areas of work are described below.
The efforts during this work were mainly utilized in creating a framework and validating
it. Therefore it is of interest to use the framework to solve larger and more complicated
problems with practical relevance. Such problems may involve optimization over a range
of target frequencies. Therefore, the feasibility of applying model order reduction such as
SOAR(Second Order Arnoldi Reduction) could be considered.

Another area for improvement is enhancing the developed code. For instance, some
routines in the developed framework are catered to running uniform mesh. This could be
modified to allow for finer mesh refinement in some areas of the domain. Moreover, for
problems with a large number of passive elements, it is worth customizing the framework
to have regular FEM elements over the passive domains.
Lastly, as stated in Jensen 2018 further work on the choice of material interpolation func-
tions is recommended. Moreover, one could investigate the effects of coupling interpola-
tion functions suggested in this work.
