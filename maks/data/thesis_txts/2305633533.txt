Abstract
In the world of yacht racing, competing on even ground is of great impor-
tance. Only sailing in class boats is not a real option, as the number of
boats in each class is usually too small to warrant cups specific to each
class. Competing under a handicap system is thus the only option, and it
must be a fair and objective system to ensure even grounds.
The Danish Handicap (DH) made by the technical committee of the
Danish Sailing Association (DS) is described and used to calculate the the-
oretical driving and heeling forces from the sails of an X40 sail yacht.
A computational fluid dynamics (CFD) set-up is made in Star-CCM+
and validated against wind tunnel tests of the sails of a model scale AC33.
A number of settings are tested to find the cheapest way of obtaining reliable
results. The influence of simulating the X40 sails together or separately is
found to be significant at 29 % decrease in driving force when separating
the sails, as is the influence of the mast on the driving force from the main
sail with a 40.7 % increase when omitting the mast.
The resulting driving and heeling forces of the X40 sails at true wind
speeds of 4 m/s, 6 m/s and 8 m/s from DH and Star-CCM+ are listed and
compared, and found corresponding poorly especially at heavy wind. Three
changes of the sail force prediction model of DH are suggested, in order
to improve the model. A flattening factor is already implemented in the
model, and the influence of this factor is investigated and specific numbers
are suggested. A reef factor from the model is suggested changed, in order
to better describe the physical meaning of reefing, and a twist factor is
introduced to account for twisting sails in heavier winds.
Keywords: CFD, Star-CCM+, sail boat, handicap system, sail force pre-
diction model, Danish Sailing Association, Danish Handicap.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the world of sail yacht racing, knowing who wins the race is important. If
all boats at the start line are identical, whoever crosses the finish line first,
is the winner. But in many races, this is not the case. Some people race
with the same boat as they use for pleasure sailing, while some build their
own boat optimized for racing and different from any and every other boat.
In order to avoid the boring consequence of one boat in the club always
winning because it is simply faster than the rest, a handicap system is
necessary. One system punishes the winner, giving the rest a better chance
in the next race; one system predicts the top speed of each boat based on
the hull length and the winner is the boat with the highest average sailed
speed to theoretical top speed ratio; but most modern systems use a whole
range of parameters and measurements of the boat, its rigging, equipment
and sails to predict precisely how fast the boat should be sailing in a wide
variety of conditions. The conditions on the race course are then inserted
together with the actual sailing times of each boat, and a winner can be
found. For this method to be precise, it is of the utmost importance to
predict the boat speed of quite different boats in varying conditions very
accurately.
1.1
Literature
Sail aerodynamics can be investigated using full-scale or model scale mea-
surements, or by numerical simulation. Full-scale driving force and heel-
ing force and moments are difficult to measure, and require a very special
boat, e.g. as the Berlin Sail–Force–Dynamometer [12], the MIT dynamome-
ter [11], or the Fujin [15]. Full-scale pressure measurements are easier to
make, since those can be made without altering the hull, but by mount-
ing pressure gauges on the sails [7]. Model scale forces and moments are
commonly measured in wind tunnel tests, but according to [8], [9] and [6],
measuring pressure distribution across the sails will give a better base for

1.2
Scope of thesis
In this report, the method for prediction of sail forces in the handicap system
used by The Danish Sailing Association (DS), Danish Handicap (DH), will
be tested against Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) calculations. This
will be done by simulating air flow over the sails of an X40 sail yacht using
the CFD program Star-CCM+. The results will then be used to suggest
improvements to the sail force prediction model.
Other ways of testing the quality of the sail force prediction model in-
clude full scale and wind tunnel tests. Full scale tests are quite difficult and
expensive to make, since they require either a very special boat or installing
pressure gauges on the sails. Wind tunnel tests require a very precise model
to be built, and are also quite expensive. Compared to these two methods,
CFD is relatively cheap and it is easy to test many different set-ups in a
reasonable amount of time.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter, the theory of how upwind sails work will be examined,
and the necessity of using a handicap system for racing will be illuminated.
Furthermore, a specific handicap system is described, and the sail force
prediction model of this system is examined.
2.1
Sail forces
A sail boat cannot sail directly into the wind, but by sailing at a suitable
angle to the wind and changing side every now and then, it is possible to
arrive at a location that was earlier directly into the wind. This is called
tacking, and is illustrated in figure 2.1 with the true wind angle (TWA )
drawn. When there is air flow around a trimmed sail, the air moving at the
leeward side will be bend towards the sail, and sped up, while the air on the
windward side will be slowed down. From Bernoulli, this results in a lower
pressure on the leeward side and a higher pressure on the windward side,
as shown in figure 2.2(left) (red colours are high pressure, blue colours are
low pressure). The pressure difference over the sail results in a force locally
perpendicular to the fabric. This force will be different in both direction
and strength for every location on the sail, but adding all contributions
together results in a force vector global for the sail. This force is usually
split into two components: lift, which is perpendicular to the wind direction
and drag, which is in line with the wind. It is also relevant to split it into
two other components, namely driving force along the boats centreline, and
heeling force perpendicular to the boats centreline. In figure 2.2(right), the
resultant force and the two common ways of splitting it up, is shown.

2.2
Handicap systems in general
When many different boats race against each other, it is usually clear what
boats will be the first across the finish line, and what will be the last. This
is not only due to the crew, but mainly the differences between the boats.
In order to race on a more even ground, each boat is given a handicap,
effectively removing the advantage of having the fastest boat. When the
handicap rule works perfectly, only the crews make a difference in who wins.
Determining the size of handicap to apply to each boat is no easy task.
Simple handicap rules rely on very few parameters, and are easy to optimize
the boat to, in order to gain advantage in spite of the applied handicap. One
such rule predicts the boat speed solely on the water line length, and even
though it would seem like an unfair rule, it works reasonably well when all
participating boats are built for cruising. With boats optimised for racing,
though, this rule becomes insufficient, and more advanced calculations and
measurements are required in order to obtain a fair evaluation.
2.3
Danish Handicap
The Danish Sailing Association (DS) has made a handicap rule named Dan-
ish Handicap (DH) [2]. This rule has been under development since its
origin, taking into account more and more variables and boat design pa-
rameters. The rule includes a hydrodynamic model taking into account
the hull shape, keel(s) and rudder(s), and an aerodynamic model dealing
primarily with the sails, but also the superstructure, appendages, rigging
and expected crew position. All these inputs are put together into a veloc-
ity prediction program (VPP), which then predicts the boat performance.
The result is the expected boat pace in seconds per nautical mile in light,
medium or heavy wind, either on a classical up/down course or on a mixed
course. It also includes a mixed condition, mixed course pace used for long
distance racing.
When used by a club planning and executing a race, the participating
boats are split into groups of similar boats, to minimize the effect of factors
outside the reach of the handicap system, like changes in wind and current,
and to allow the smaller boats to sail a shorter course than the larger,
usually faster, boats. Before each race starts, the race committee reads the
wind condition and displays the light, medium or heavy wind signal. After

3.2
Turbulence models
Three of the most commonly used models are k −ϵ, k −ω and Spalart −
Allmaras (SpA). SpA is a one-equation model, in that it only adds one
extra unknown and thus one extra equation to solve the flow. It does not
use a wall function to simplify the boundary layer close to a wall, and thus it
is good for simulating semi-turbulent flows or calculating lift and drag. The
k −ϵ and k −ω models add two extra unknowns, kinetic energy k and rate
of dissipation of kinetic energy or specific rate of dissipation respectively.
Both models use wall functions, and while k −ϵ is good for external flows,
k −ω is better at internal flows. k −ϵ is good at converging, while k −ω is
more sensitive to the initial guess, which can be obtained using k −ϵ. Both
have relatively low memory requirements. In section 4.1, the three models
are used to solve the same simulation, and the results are compared.
3.3
Settings in Star-CCM+
To simulate air flow over sails, the commercial CFD code Star-CCM+ [1] is
used. This program covers everything from importing geometry over pre-
processing and simulation to post-processing and presenting results. Most
results are exported, however, for treatment in either Microsoft Excel or
MATLAB [10]. In this section, the set-up is described and commented, and
examples of output from post-processing is given.
3.3.1
Domain and mesh
The domain is shown in figure 3.1(left). It extends 2 Lengths of Water Line
(LWL) aft of the mast, and 1 LWL in front; 2 LWL to leeward and 1 LWL
to windward; 2.5 LWL above the water and 0.5 LWL below the water. This
size is chosen as a compromise between having a large enough domain to
avoid boundary conditions influencing the flow close to the sails, and a small
enough domain to keep the number of cells low for a shorter simulation run
time.
A close up of the mesh is shown in figure 3.1(right), with both a hori-
zontal and vertical plane inserted. The cylindrical refinement zones at the
leading edge of the jib and around the mast are clearly seen on the horizon-
tal plane. 12 refinement zones where made in total, 6 blocks for refining

3.3.2
Physics
Figure 3.2 shows the physics models chosen in Star-CCM+. Constant den-
sity results in an incompressible flow, as discussed in section 3.1. With
segregated flow (as opposed to coupled flow), the solver calculates the flow
velocities and pressure separately instead of coupled. This reduces the com-
putation time, but may lead to problems in case of high density fluctuations
like supersonic flows. Since this problem only features relatively low veloc-
ities, a segregated flow solver should be able to converge. In section 4.4.1,
it is tested whether a steady state solution can be used in place of a more
expensive transient solution.

3.3.2.1
Boundary conditions
The boundaries of the domain are split into three groups. Below is a slip
condition wall; forward, windward and above is a velocity inlet; behind and
leeward is either flow split or pressure outlet. In section 4.4.3 it is tested if
a flow split and pressure outlet yields the same resulting forces on the sails.
The sails are modelled as baffle interfaces, with separate surfaces on each
side, effectively making them infinitely thin, impenetrable walls.
3.3.3
Plots
To output the resulting forces on the sails, a report is made for the total
forces on each sail in every direction. Each report is then monitored, and
the monitors are plotted. The plots are set to automatically be saved to
.cvs-files for treatment in MATLAB. Selected plots are automatically saved as
.png-images for a quick visual interpretation of the quality of the simulation.
This is done with the residuals, and one plot of forces in each direction, see
figure 3.3, in this case with a true wind speed (TWS ) of 4 m/s.

3.3.5
Tools
A number of tools are available to assist in setting up either the simulation
or the representation of results. For this project, especially three tools were
important.
3.3.5.1
Coordinate systems
The simulation has a global coordinate system in which the sail model is
imported into. This system has x negative in the sailing direction and y
positive upwards. But since the boat is heeling, it is advantageous to create
a coordinate system rotated around x, to keep y in the mast direction. Ad-
ditionally, the boat is sailing at an angle to the wind, and thus a coordinate
system is defined with x positive in the wind direction, making it easier
to apply the wind correctly. Figure 3.5 shows the heeling angle and true
wind angle, along with their respective coordinate systems together with
the global system and the sails.
3.3.5.2
Motion
Since the boat is not stationary while sailing, the wind experienced on
board is different from when observed globally. This can be simulated by
calculating the apparent wind speed and angle from the true wind speed
and angle added to the boat speed and direction. Another, more true, way
of doing it is by assigning a velocity to the entire domain, simulating the
boat actually moving through the wind. A number of motions are available,
but for this problem, a simple translation will do. This translation is in the
negative x-direction of the global coordinate system, with the magnitude
of the boat speed.
3.3.5.3
Field functions
A large number of field functions, which is a post-processing calculation used
for data treatment, can be tweaked and used, and the user can create his
or her own functions. Some functions, like the pressure coefficient, require
reference values to be set in order to show the correct results.

4.2
Mesh convergence
In figure 4.2, the driving and heeling forces are shown based on different
refinement methods and different base sizes. The red curve is with coarse
refinements, green and blue are finer, while the cyan curve has the finest
refinement zones. It can be seen that for a sufficiently high number of cells,
the forces are independent of the number of cells. This convergence occurs
at approximately 106 for the refinement method illustrated in cyan. This
method uses 9 refinement zones, as opposed to 6 zones used by the method
represented in green. Figure 4.3(left) shows the cyan method with a base
size of 0.4 m and approximately 2.3 · 105 cells, while figure 4.3(right) shows
the green method with a base size of 0.25 m and approximately 1.2 · 105
cells. Even though the base size is almost twice as large in figure 4.3(left),
the refinement zones increase the number of cells, and the flow close to
the sails is resolved more finely. This is important, since this is where the
largest gradients in the flow are. Throughout the simulations for this report,
attention has been paid to include a sufficient number of refinement zones
for resolving the flow close to surfaces and especially edges. It was not
possible to mesh more than 107 cells due to lack of computer memory, but
most simulations are run with 106 to 107 cells.

4.3
Validating against a known solution
To validate the set up of a simulation in Star-CCM+, a problem with a
known solution is preferable. For this purpose [5] is a good source, since
this paper is made specifically for validating numerical code against wind
tunnel test data on the sails from an AC33 (33 foot America’s Cup class
catamaran). The wind tunnel test data from [5] includes pressure coefficient
measurements along four horizontal lines on each of the two sails. According
to [8], [9], and [6], it is better to validate using pressure distributions than
global forces on the sail, since different pressure distributions might yield
the same forces, while the same pressure distribution cannot yield different
forces. Numerical calculation results for the AC33 are also included in [5].
A simulation was set up in Star-CCM+ and the pressure coefficient along
the same horizontal lines was output to .cvs-files. Figure 4.4 shows the
pressure coefficients from wind tunnel tests, from numerical data obtained
in [5] (named VLM) and from numerical data obtained by the author of
this report in Star-CCM+. The figure shows very good correspondence be-
tween the data obtained in Star-CCM+ and the numerical data from [5], and
reasonably good correspondence to the experimental data. To quantify this
correspondence, the coefficient of determination (R2) is calculated for each
section of both sails using R2 = 1 −SSE /SST , where SSE is the sum of
squared errors (difference between model data and test data) and SST is
the sum of squared totals (test data). This is done in MATLAB using the
norm-function on test data and model data interpolated to the measuring
positions of the test data. Table 4.5 shows the resulting coefficients of de-
termination and the improvement in the results from Star-CCM+ compared
to VLM. Most sections show a worsening of 2 % to 9 %, but the top of the
main sail shows an improvement of 19 %. The numbers shown in table 4.5
support the visual interpretation of figure 4.4 that the simulation set up in
Star-CCM+ is satisfactory.

4.4.1
Transient or steady
The flow past a sail may include time variations and thus require a tran-
sient solver. This is possible in Star-CCM+, but requires a large amount of
iterations, increasing the simulation run time significantly, compared to a
steady state solution. A test was made to assess the consequence of simpli-
fying the simulation. Figure 4.6(left) shows the residuals at the last eight
time steps, each covering 20 iterations. It can be seen that the residuals
decrease to less than 2 · 10−4 at the end of each time step, implying that
the solution has converged. The Courant number was well below 7 in the
entire domain during the entire run, indicating that the time step was suf-
ficiently small compared to the cell size. Figure 4.6(right) shows the lift
forces over time, with reasonably steady values after the first few seconds.

forces from the corresponding steady state solution. The simulation was

Improvement of sail
force prediction model
In Star-CCM+, the sails are optimally trimmed including both flattening
and twist, but without reefing. By including these factors in the sail force
prediction model, it will be possible to describe the physics better, and
obtain results closer to the ones obtained by CFD. Thus the improvement
of the prediction model consists of
1. Using the flattening factor (Fl ) already implemented in the sail force
prediction model.
2. Improving the way the reef factor (Reef ) is implemented in the sail
force prediction model.
3. Adding a twist factor (Tw ) for de-powering the sails and lowering the
centre of effort, equations (2.12-2.16).
6.1
Flattening factor
The flattening factor is multiplied directly on the combined lift force (equa-
tion (2.3)) and multiplied squared on the induced and quadratic drag forces
(equations (2.6) and (2.7)). A flattening factor of Fl = 1 means an unflat-
tened sail, while Fl = 0 is a sail flattened so extreme that it does not produce
any lift. Trimming a real sail, one would apply very little to no flattening
in calm wind, and gradually increase flattening as the wind speed increases.
Flattening should be applied to the jib and main sail simultaneously. For
the jib, it is done by moving the sheet point outwards while tightening the
sheet and adjusting the lead block to avoid introducing twist. For the main
sail, it is done by easing the traveller while tightening the outhaul and sheet
correspondingly. The results are found by trial and error, and are illustrated

6.4

The main sail is assumed to be full until around 10 m/s, and thus ReefM = 1
for TWS of 4 m/s, 6 m/s and 8 m/s. The jib is assumed to be a code 1 at

only with more durable fabric, and thus ReefJ = 1 for TWS of 4 m/s and
6 m/s. At 8 m/s a code 2 or heavier is expected, and thus ReefJ = [0; 1].
Flattening and twist is expected to occur at TWS > 4 m/s, and will be left
open for optimisation.

CHAPTER 7
Future work
While this report has suggested some modifications to the sail force predic-
tion model of DH, the suggestions are not simply implemented solely on
the information found here. In order to fully gain the advantages of imple-
menting the factors, values of high quality are necessary. For future work,
it is thus suggested to improve the quality of these factors, and also to have
a look at the lift and drag coefficients used.
7.1
Find flattening, reef and twist factors
To find the correct values of flattening, reef and twist factors, it is necessary
to have solid results to test against. In this report, only one set of upwind
sails is used for each of the three true wind speeds to find driving and
heeling forces. No hull resistance or righting moment has been calculated
or simulated. This makes the resulting factors difficult to validate, and thus
decreases the potential quality of the factors found.
With known resistance and righting moment (from CFD, wind tunnel,
towing tank and/or full-scale tests), a brute force method for optimising
the factors can be used by simply varying the factors and checking which
combination results in the smallest difference between the calculated and
the known driving forces and heeling moments.
7.2
Revision of lift and drag coefficients
The lift and drag coefficients provided by the technical committee of the
Danish Sailing Association are used by other handicap systems as well, but
they are not well documented, and may need to be revised [14]. Currently,
the coefficients only depend on the type of sail and the apparent wind angle,
but ideally they should be individual to the sail and its set-up, meaning the
==PAGE:64==

sail combination, wind speed, apparent wind angle, and flattening, twist
and reef factors.
By running a lot of simulations on sails from different boats in varying
set-ups (including sail combination, wind speed and angle, flattening, reef
and twist factors), it might be possible to find coefficients universal to
each sail type, dependent only on apparent wind speed and angle. This
might also remove the factors from force calculations (but probably not for
centre of effort), and the same simulations could be the base for finding the
influence of those factors on the centre of effort.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The use of Star-CCM+ was described, and a number of tests were run to
ensure reliable results. With model scale sails of the AC33 from [5], three
common turbulence models were tested to yield similar results. A mesh
convergence study found that with proper refinement, 106 cells was sufficient
to get mesh independent solutions. Pressure coefficients at four horizontal
sections of both jib and main sail of the AC33 model were compared to those
found by wind tunnel tests in [5], and good correspondence was found. For
the X40 sails, it was tested whether a steady state solution was sufficient in
place of a more expensive transient one, and little difference in results was
found. The effect of y+ was tested, specifically when unable to create prism
layers on the surfaces, and it was found to be of little consequence, even up
to y+ = 216. The effect of using flow split or pressure outlet as boundary
condition was tested, and this was found to influence convergence rather
than actual results.
A difference of 29 % was found when comparing the driving force of
sails simulated together, with adding the forces of the two sails simulated
separately. Since sails are flown together the vast majority of time during
racing, it is important to have coefficients and formulations reflect that in
the handicap system. The influence of the mast was found to be significant,
especially at 40.7 % on the driving force from the main sail, and mast shapes
deviating significantly from the norm (including rotating masts) may need
special treatment in the handicap system. The resulting driving and heeling
forces were listed at true wind speeds of 4 m/s, 6 m/s and 8 m/s.
The aerodynamic model of the handicap system DH was described and
used to find driving and heeling forces at the same conditions.
These
were found to deviate significantly from the forces obtained in Star-CCM+,
and three improvements were suggested. A flattening factor already imple-
mented in the model was used to improve the forces. It was suggested to
split the reef factor implemented in the code into two factors separate for
the jib and main sail, respectively. The factors were inserted in the calcu-
==PAGE:66==
52
8 Conclusion
lation of sail area directly, rather than the resulting forces. Furthermore,
implementation into the calculation of centre of effort for the main sail was
suggested. A twist factor was suggested similar to the flattening factor, but
with the side effect of lowering the centre of effort. Changing the reef factor
and adding a twist factor were suggested based on the physical properties
and conditions of upwind sails, and the actual methods of optimising the
sail shape available to modern race yachts while racing.
For future work, it was suggested to find values of the factors mentioned
above, by first creating a known solution of driving force and heeling mo-
ment, and then testing which combination of values would give the best
fit.
This should be done separately for each wind speed, and optimally
for a large number of different boats to find a reasonable universal set of
values. It was also suggested to improve the quality of the lift and drag
coefficients used by DH and other handicap systems, as these are not well
documented. Ideally, this should be done with flattening, reef and twist in
mind, thus allowing those factors to be a part of the coefficients instead of
separate values. Furthermore, the centre of effort should depend on wind
speed, based on the same simulations and the notion that sails are always
ideally trimmed while racing.
In summary, the goal of suggesting improvements to the sail force predic-
tion model of DH was successful, although more tests and simulations are
necessary to ensure the best possible values for factors universal to all sail
boat types covered by DH. The use of CFD to find driving and heeling forces
to compare with the prediction model was useful, and the poor correspon-
dence between results at higher wind speeds prompted the implementation
of more factors to the prediction model to better mimic the physic of sail
trim. Expanding the amount of CFD simulations to include many different
boats will give a much more stable foundation for the prediction model, and
improve the quality of the handicap system as a whole.
