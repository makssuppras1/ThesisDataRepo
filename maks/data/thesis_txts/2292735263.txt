Abstract
Aquaculture production of sh has become an important industry, since a growing
population on earth needs more food.
At the same time there are contaminants like
mercury (Hg) which is able to accumulate in the marine food-chain.
Also there is an
increase of microplastics in the oceans, and the eects of microplastics are not fully
understood. Knowledge about how microplastics may aect uptake and elimination of
contaminants or nutrients is needed. Toxicokinetic modelling is the study of uptake and
elimination of a toxic compound in an organism, and is useful when it comes to assessing
the safety of a compound. The aim of this project was to determine concentration of the
contaminants Hg, cadmium (Cd) and lead (Pb), in addition of the essential nutrients
selenium (Se), zinc (Zn) and copper (Cu) in llets from Atlantic salmon. The salmon
were fed with ve dierent diets with and without microplastics and MeHg. A carry-over
assessment from sh to feed for the elements was performed in addition of toxicokinetic
modelling of Hg.
Determination of total content in feed and llets for salmon was done by the use of
inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometer (ICPMS). While speciation of MeHg was
done by isotope dilution GC-ICPMS. Despite high concentrations of Cd in feed, only ten
samples were above LOQ (of 74). The average concentration was 0.85 ± 0.04 µg · kg-1w.w
(¯x ± SD, n = 10). Pb concentrations were low, with only two samples above LOQ. These
had concentrations of 28.5 ± 2.8 and 45.5 ± 4.6 µg · kg-1w.w.
Se had an average
concentration of 210 ± 17 µg · kg-1w.w (¯x ± SD, n = 75). Zinc did not accumulate and
the average concentration were 4.96 ± 0.35 mg · kg-1w.w (¯x ± SD, n = 74). Cu showed a
decreasing trend in concentration by time,
with concentration ranging from 702
µg · kg-1w.w at the highest to 279 µg · kg-1w.w at the lowest.
The toxicokinetic model gave a large uncertainty for the diets because the sh suered
from stress during the phase where they were weighed several times. But the assimilation
eciency was calculated to be 118 % ± 20 %, 101 % ± 44 % and 116 % ± 92 % for diet 3,
4 and 5 respectively. The latter two have an uncertainty which is enormous. The half-life
calculations also suers from a high uncertainty as they are based on a common parameter,
kel. The concentration of Hg ranged between 22 - 45 µg·kg-1w.w. A statistical test showed
that there were a dierence in the concentrations between the diets, with diet 5 (Plastic
and MeHg contaminated) as the one with the highest. That could be due to a dierence
in feed concentration or micoplastics do have an aect.

Introduction
As resources are diminishing terrestrial, humans are turning towards the ocean to meet
the growing population's need for more food. In the time period from 1990 - 2012 the
global numbers for wild caught sh were more or less constant at around 80 million
tonnes per year as is depicted in light blue in gure 4.1 [1]. Aquaculture production (dark
blue) of sh on the other hand is increasing every year and reached a record of 66 million
tonnes in 2012. It is expected to increase in the years to come.
Figure 4.1: Overview of wild caught sh and aquaculture sh worldwide [1].
The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United States (FAO) states that sh is
one of the main sources of proteins for about 3 billion people [1]. Fish is an important
source of more than just proteins. Some nutrients worth to mention are selenium, iodine,
fat soluble vitamins such as vitamin D and essential fatty acids like omega 3 and 6 [1].
Aquatic organisms are therefore a very important part of the human diet. Consequently
the sh should be as safe as possible, and not contain contaminants at harmful levels.
Many contaminants end up in the ocean and enters the marine food-chain, and there
following also in sh. Both arsenic, lead, mercury, cadmium, polychlorinated biphenyls
(PCB's) and pesticides are contaminants that can be found in seafood [2]. Since many
people are dependent on sh this raises a huge concern.
Because the seafood and other types of food might pose a risk to humans if contaminants
are present at high concentrations, the authorities within a country need to have some
limits for what is allowed.
EU's authority for assessing the safety of food is called
European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), and they provide risk assessments and set
maximum limits for contaminants in foodstu.

the Arctic ocean [3]. The huge plastic chunks oating around in the ocean have been a

5.1.1
Chemical properties and forms
The toxicity of a compound is in many cases a result of the chemical composition of the
element. Hg is not an exception. Metallic Hg (Hg0) has a melting point of −38.8◦C, and
is therefore liquid at room temperature. Furthermore the elemental Hg (Hg0) is volatile,
which is special compared to other metals. The reason why Hg has been used for various
applications throughout the history is because it has a low ability of expanding at
varying temperatures [5].
In physical terms it means that Hg has a low thermal
expansion coecient.
Hg is a soft acid, which is characterised by a low charge density and the ability to be
polarised [6]. Soft acids bind strongly to soft ligands. Soft ligands could be nucleobases,
thiols and sulphur containing molecules amongst others [7].
Other good examples of
sulfur containing molecules in animal bodies are the amino acids (AA) cystein and
methionin which have one sulfur atom in the structure. They are both depicted in gure
5.1. The oxidation states of Hg can be 0 (as in metallic or vapour), +1 (Hg 2+
2
) and +2
(Hg2+). The most encountered is oxidation state 0 and +2. Hg2+ can bind to inorganic
and organic ligands.

5.1.2
Sources
Hg exists in the environment naturally, but during the last 500 years there has been an
increase of Hg in the environment [9]. The amount of Hg released to the air in 2010 was
estimated to 1960 tonnes per year [10], and as much as 30 % of these are thought to
originate from anthropogenic activity. The Hg cycle can be viewed in gure 5.2 where
natural and anthropogenic sources are shown.
Coal red power plants and small gold
mines are the main emission sources to air. A huge part of the emission comes from Asia,
which is responsible for 48 % of the total mercury (tHg) release to air [10]. Deposition
from the atmosphere occurs when Hg is oxidised by ozone or radicals. This way Hg is
distributed to locations far away from the contamination source. Inorganic mercury (iHg)
released directly to the water is estimated to 1000 tonnes per year [10].
Burning of fossil fuels, industrial synthesis (as catalyst), renement of metals and
incineration of waste is just a couple of examples of how anthropogenic sources increase
available Hg in the environment.
Some areas where Hg has been used in smaller
quantities is as cure for syphilis (calomel, Hg2Cl2), oor polishing, wood perservative
(organic mercury), fungicide (Ethyl mercury), teething powder (calomel), disinfectant at
hospitals (HgCl2),
soaps (HgI), vaccine preservative (Thimerasol,
ethyl mercury),
batteries, thermostats and in the synthesis of chlorine and sodium hydroxide. The list
can go on.
Even though most of the products are not in use today there is still an
exposure route to humans through food (seafood in special) and air.
Bacteria in sediments are able to convert sedimented unreactive Hg to the toxic MeHg.
Vitamin B12 in the coenzyme form, methylcobalamin is able to add methyl groups to
both Hg2+ and the Hg0. The methylated ion CH3Hg+ is more lipophilic than Hg2+ and
is bioaccumulated better than the other [11]. Plankton and other organisms will take up

5.1.3
Mercury in humans
There are two kinds of poisoning in the literature, acute and toxic. Chronic poisoning is
a result of regular exposure to small doses, which exceeds the excretion rate of the
compound in the body. Acute, on the other hand, is the intake of a dose that in worst
case is lethal. The three forms of Hg have dierent pathways and toxic routes in humans
and will be described individually here.
Elemental mercury
Hg0 is lipid soluble, therefore it can cross the membrane to the blood [13]. In the red blood
cells it is oxidised to Hg2+ [13]. The Hg0 vapor can aect the entire respiratory system,
but the most common eect is related to the central nervous system (CNS), as it easily
goes through the blood-brain barrier and enters the CNS. Chronic symptoms of elemental
Hg poisoning could be uncontrolled muscle shivering, personality changes, hallucinations
and gingivitis. It can also cross through the placenta to fetus in pregnant women. If Hg0
is ingested orally the absorption is poor [13].
Inorganic mercury
iHg usually exist as salts. There are both mercurous salts (Hg22+) and mercuric salts
(Hg+2), with the latter being more toxic than the former. The reason for that is thought
to be due to a higher solubility. The inorganic form of the element (Hg2+) is normally
not a lethal as MeHg or Hg0, which is explained by the fact that only 7 % is absorbed in
the gastrointestinal tract [13]. The 7 % of iHg which is absorbed will accumulate in the
kidney [13].
iHg has the ability to bind to AA's transporters which is described in gure 5.3. Through
"masking" like cystine (two cysteine AA's joined together) iHg can be transported by
AA's transporters throughout the body. By doing so it travel easily in the blood, and is
transported across the blood-brain barrier and placenta. Once inside the brain, Hg can

5.9.1
ICPMS
The ICP is very useful for analysis at low concentrations due to the high temperature it
can reach (6,000-10,000 K) due to the plasma which is more stable than other ame
techniques. Also the inert conditions with argon gas eliminates many of the interferences
a ame instrument would encounter. The ICP is started by a spark from a Tesla coil that
ionises the argon gas, and free electrons are released. The electrons are then accelerated
in a radio-frequency eld giving them higher energy.
When the electrons collide with
atoms, energy is transferred to the entire gas, providing a more or less constant
temperature of 6,000-10,000 K. A constant energy (temperature) is crucial to ensure that
the analyte is properly ionised.
One of the main advantages of the ICP coupled to a mass spectrometer (MS) is the
ability to detect several elements at the same time and the fact that it is able to detect
analytes from ppm to ppt [54]. The reason why it is possible to measure several elements
at the same time is due to the separation of m/z ratios, and the fact that every element
has a given m/z ratio. In the ow diagram in gure 5.11 it is shown how the analyte
changes throughout the instrument. The sample is introduced into a spray chamber by a
nebuliser that creates a ne aerosol of the liquid sample. In the spray chamber larger
droplets of aerosol are removed, and the smaller ones are passed further to the plasma. It
is important to maintain a constant temperature in the spray chamber to avoid drift in
the instrument as well as solvent entering the plasma is lowered.
Hence the plasma
temperature can be higher and oxide interference is reduced. In the plasma the aerosol is
quickly dried and one are left with dry particles. The particles are decomposed, vaporised
and atomised before the atoms are ionised (removal of one electron).
Figure 5.11: States the analyte goes through during ICMPS measurements. Modied from [55]
To be able to get a concentration of the analyte from a sample a calibration curve is
needed.
A calibration curve is made by measuring standard solutions at specic
concentration. The known concentrations will give a signal or response that equals that
specic concentration. The signal is a response measured by the MS and has the unit of
counts. With a higher number of ions reaching the detector, the higher the counts will

Interferences in the ICPMS
In general there are three types of interferences a chemist most likely will stumble upon
when working with atomic or molecular spectroscopy. These are ionisation interference,
spectral interference and chemical interference. Ionisation interference is a problem if the
ionisation energy of a compound is high. All analyte atoms might not be ionised, and
hereby lowering the concentration of analyte.
Chemical interference is the result of
reactions in the solution leading to less analyte available for analysis.
Even though
chemical and ionisation interference is more or less removed in ICP, there is a major
contribution from the spectral interference. Spectral interference is when the signal of the
analyte has an overlap with other combination of atoms. When an ICPMS is operated in
standard mode there is a high possibility that polyatomic species or other ions that has
the same m/z ratio will interfere with the analyte. One way to maneuver around this
problem is by installing a collision/reaction cell (CRC) or operating the ICPMS with a
triple quadrupole (ICP-QQQ-MS) [56]. In gure 5.12 the hardware of an ICP-QQQ-MS
is shown.
Figure 5.12: Hardware of an ICP-QQQ-MS system (cite agilent manual pdf. [56]
The three quadrupoles are placed in the vacuum region, and the rst and third are xed
on a specic m/z ratio, while the second is the CRC. The instrument can be operated
using the CRC both as collision chamber or reaction chamber. If it is used as a reaction
chamber the spectral interference is removed by adding a reactive gas that chemically
transform the species to something else. When it is used as collision cell an inert gas like
helium is added, and will collide with polyatomic species or doubly charged ions like rare

Gas chromatography (GC) takes advantage of the dierent properties such as polarity

By chemical interactions or adsorption to the

The carrier gas, or also mobile gas phase, is required to be chemically inert.

When it comes to the sample injection, it is important that a suitable amount of sample

Conclusion
This experiment has determined concentration and carry-over of the elements Hg, Pb,
Cd, Se, Zn and Cu in the llets of Atlantic salmon. The growth of the ve dierent diets
did not dier from each other. Hence microplastics did not seem to aect the growth.
Hg showed to bioaccumulate over the time period tested. The concentrations were below
the maximum limit in sh muscle and feed.
There was a signicant dierence in the
concentrations of Hg of the contaminated diets, but it is not possible to determine from
the experimental dataset of this report if it is due to a real dierence in the uptake and
elimination of the feed, or if it is due to a signicant dierence in the concentration of the
feed. The diet based on clean microplastics with contaminants added through oil was the
diet with highest Hg concentration.
The main form of Hg present in feed and sh is
MeHg, which is of concern as this is more toxic than the other forms of Hg.
Pb and Cd concentrations were almost not detectable in sh, and the carry-over from
feed to llet was low, even though the concentration of Cd in feed were just below the
maximum limit set by EU. This is because they do not accumulate in the muscle part of
sh, but other parts like kidney, liver and brain. The exposure of these two contaminants
from sh is low as long as it is the llet that is eaten.
Se concentration accumulated in the sh until a certain time point (12 weeks) before it
decreased, and the reason for that could be coupled to the increase in elimination of Hg
that has been seen in other experiments. Zn and Cu did not accumulate, even though
concentrations of Zn were close to the maximum limit in feed.
A decrease in Cu
concentration for all diets was observed.
But more data points are needed to prove
statistically if the concentration is lower.

Perspectives
The eect of microplastics in sh is a eld of research which is developing, and there are
still many questions to answer. In the future other sizes of microplastics could be studied,
as well as other types. Also the impact on other sh species and marine organisms should
be a target of research. Furthermore a more thorough study on microplastics alone could
give an indication if it can lead to toxic eects by itself in marine species. Humans and
how they react to plastic particles is another aspect that could be studied.
The toxicokinetic modelling in marine species might change as the physical parameters in
the oceans change. Temperature, pH and salinity aect the solubility of contaminants. If
the pH is lowered elements that were previously precipitated into the sediments could be
released into the environment.
The same can be said for an increase in temperature,
compounds are usually more soluble at higher temperatures.
The fact that Cu showed a decreasing tendency should be tested to see if it is the
contaminants that are responsible for the decreasing absorption,
or if it is the
microplastics.
The Hg:Se relation is interesting. As the form of Se aect the assimilation and excretion
of Hg, more extensive studies where dierent forms of Se is added to feed is needed.
