Abstract 
In the field of medical device development, startups face the two-sided challenge of creating 
innovative solutions while complying with strict regulatory requirements. This thesis aims to 
bridge this gap by extending the KTH Innovation Readiness Level framework, by introducing a 
Compliance Readiness Level (CpRL) that specifically addresses the regulatory challenges in the 
medical device sector. Through an extensive thematic analysis consisting of interviews with 
industry experts and startups, combined with research in regulatory frameworks like the EU 
Medical Device Regulation, EU AI Act, and others, this research offers an understanding of 
compliance in the relation to innovation. The CpRL dimension proposed in this thesis is a tool 
designed to guide startups through the complex process of regulatory compliance, from early 
development stages to post-market activities. This research provides practical insights for 
startups eager to innovate while ensuring regulatory compliance.

I. Introduction 
Medical device startups face the dual challenge of innovating while navigating complex regulatory 
compliance. This thesis addresses this critical challenge. 
The integration of advanced technologies like Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning 
(ML) in medical devices has expanded the potential for innovation but also introduced regulatory 
complexities. This thesis suggests extending the KTH Innovation Readiness Level (IRL) 
framework with a Compliance Readiness Level (CpRL), specifically designed for medical device 
startups. This addition aims to guide startups from early development to successful market entry, 
ensuring compliance. 
Authored by Petros Evangelos Maravelakis, the thesis is founded on empirical research, expert 
interviews, and a thorough analysis of regulatory frameworks such as the EU Medical Device 
Regulation and the EU AI Act. The goal is to provide a extensive guide for startups to balance 
innovation and compliance. 
This work is more than an academic contribution; it is meant to be a practical resource for 
startups in the medical device sector, offering a framework to integrate regulatory compliance 
into the innovation process.

II.1. Compliance in the health tech sector 
In health tech products and services, compliance helps to ensure that they are safe, work well, 
and are secure, especially when dealing with health data and patient care. 
Compliance is the external alignment with regulatory requirements, which is done in practice by 
following a fixed set of rules, standards, and guidelines issued by the authorities.   
Complying with regulations is important to operate and for earning public credibility and keeping 
patients safe. Regulatory incompliance can affect many stakeholders, resulting in the loss of 
customers and credibility, and may cause unsafe products or services. Internally this could result 
in reduced revenue, fines, and increased risk for future operations and company existence. 
Achieving regulatory compliance is the result of having awareness about what the requirements 
are, secondly knowing how to fulfill those requirements, and lastly, having or obtaining the skills 
to do so.

II.1.a. EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR) 
The EU Medical Device Regulation dictates rules on medical devices in the EU. It was revised in 
May of 2021 and now represents a set of stricter and more detailed rules, to make sure medical 
devices are safe and high-quality. This means even more products, including some kinds of 
software, now must follow these safety standards.   
One of the main changes of the EU MDR is that it demands stricter supervision on devices with 
higher patient risks making sure safety and reliability of operation is maintained. Manufacturers 
must provide solid evidence of their product claims. 
Manufacturers must monitor their products more closely after they are sold and quickly report 
any problems. The law also states that medical devices need to have clear labels containing full 
information, so people know exactly what and how they are using the medical device, including 
the risks associated. This is where the newly stated database comes into play, the European 
Database on Medical Devices (EUDaMeD), which strictly tracks medical devices from the moment 
they are produced until they reach the market, including post-market surveillance. 
The EUDaMed offers a direct supportive role to the EU MDR ensuring a central accessible 
database with all the critical information about the medical devices on the market. 
If software is categorized as a medical device, there are specific rules to make sure it's safe and 
that it works as intended, including checks during its development and testing phases.   
Having medical devices in the form of software does not necessarily dictate any increase or 
decrease in regulatory restrictions compared to physical medical devices. What dictates the level 
of restrictions is directly correlated to the risk level that the patient is introduced to.  
The EU MDR is an important step towards more transparent and safer rules in the EU for medical 
devices. Meanwhile, it also introduces a challenge for manufacturers to meet these new 
standards. The overall purpose of the EU MDR is to make sure patients are safe and the devices 
they use are of good quality.  
According to a book describing a regulatory guide for medical device start-ups (Zajki-
Zechmeister, 2023), it is critical to manage pre-market activities well to avoid issues with the 
Notified Bodies and authorities. This is to avoid pre-market claims, which are not met when the 
product is launched. 
==PAGE:10==
 
10 
It also describes a risk of noncompliance because of neglected post-market surveillance. It argues 
to stay in close contact with customers to get valuable feedback and refine the product. Zajki-
Zechmeister states that the post-market activities to stay compliant include clinical reviews, 
which could then be combined with customer feedback. Doing these interviews also helps the 
company understand target groups and customers better.  
Zajki-Zechmeister points out that even though diseases can often be addressed globally, it is 
important to learn about the individual markets. Countries usually have different ways of 
operating their healthcare system, reimbursement schemes, and other arrangements. Also, CE 
certification is mainly relevant in the EU because of the EU MDR, thus it will not have the same 
gravity outside the EU with a CE certification.  
In the book, Zajki-Zechmeister also discusses the complexities of market entry and scaling for 
MedTech startups. While diseases and medical indications can often be addressed globally, the 
reimbursement of costs by health insurance companies varies by market, limiting global 
scalability. It also cautions that CE marking, while recognized internationally, is only valid within 
the European Economic Area, and different requirements apply in other countries. 
 
II.1.b. Notified Body 
A notified body is an organization chosen by the EU to verify if certain products, like medical 
devices, comply with the related regulations. Before medical devices are sold, they must be CE-
certified to ensure patient safety. This certification by the notified bodies ensures that the product 
meets certain performance and safety standards set by the EU. One of these standards is the MDR. 
The notified body provides different necessary services such as auditing, evaluating, and 
certifying products. 
 
II.1.c. EU AI Act 
On the 8th of December 2023, the European Parliament agreed on the EU AI Act, which was 
designed to protect people from harm, considering the fast development in recent years and AI 
being applied to more and more products, including medical device software solutions. The law 
is expected to be effective in 2025 and will introduce significant challenges for companies 
working with AI. The EU AI Act regulates AI systems based on how risky they are. The ones with 
higher risks must follow stricter rules. Companies using AI in medical devices are placed in the 
high-risk category, with a stricter set of rules and obligations.  
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
This includes being clear and transparent about how the AI makes decisions, making sure the AI’s 
decisions are correct, as well including a thorough consideration of potential ethical dilemmas.  
According to a paper discussing applied AI in medical devices in the EU (Zanca et al. 2022), not 
only is it required to have compliance with MDR but also to ensure the safety and performance of 
the medical device software AI-system. It emphasizes how this is achieved by ensuring 
compliance with the EU AI Act. 
The paper also describes the importance of proper device performance risk management and 
clinical trials to ensure patient safety. 
 
II.1.d. NIS and NIS2 
In 2016 the EU effectuated the NIS (National Information Security Directive), a directive whose 
main purpose is to increase cyber safety and security and to improve cooperation between 
private and public sectors. Cyber safety means the focus on the safety of the people using a certain 
digital product/service, and cyber security focuses on protecting the actual product/service. 
By ensuring the use of risk management practices and the reporting of major cyber threat 
incidents to the authorities, the NIS would help protect critical sectors such as energy, transport, 
health, and banking, as well as digital providers such as online marketplaces or social media. 
An extension to the NIS was later added. This was the NIS2 which was effectuated in January 
2023. The main additions include broadening the scope to include industries such as social media 
platforms and telecommunication, as well as introducing a cap size, which means that entities 
from the relevant sectors with more than 50 employees and an annual turnover of more than 10 
million EUR, will be subject to the NIS/NIS2 as well. 
While this regulation is not restricted to companies working with medical devices only, it still 
represents a significant challenge to EU-based companies working with medical device software. 
 
II.1.e. KTH Innovation Readiness Levels  
The KTH Innovation Readiness Level (KTH IRL) is a modern innovation framework originally 
published in a 2018 thesis by Lunner and Wormann at the Royal Insitute of Technology (KTH) in 
Stockholm, Sweden (Lunner & Worrmann, 2018). 
The KTH IRL builds on top of the Technology Readiness Level (TRL), initially developed in-house 
at NASA in the mid-70s and later officially published in 1989 (Sadin et al., 1989). 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
While the TRL from NASA focuses on technology alone, the scope of the KTH IRL is to create a 
more holistic tool, with a spectrum of different key dimensions, critical for the success and 
progression of an innovation process – from the very beginning of basic principles to the end goal 
of being flight-proven (framed in the NASA terminology). 
6 Dimension 
The KTH IRL consists of these 6 aspects: technology, team, business, funding, customer, and IP. 
This multidimensional approach helps create this holistic view and a tool that appeals to 
industries more broadly.

Brief elaboration of the 6 dimensions: 
Customer Readiness Level (CRL): The focus of this dimension is to validate the needs of potential 
customers. This includes market research, identifying the pains of the customers, and validating 
their needs. Doing so helps align the development of the product with the actual demands in the 
market increasing the chances of success. 
Technology Readiness Level (TRL): The scope of this dimension is aligned with the original one 
of NASA, to evaluate the status of the technology and make sure it moves through processes such 
as research, prototyping, testing, and maturation of the technology. The result will be a 
technology that has been thoroughly tested and is well-performing for the application intended. 
Business Readiness Level (BRL): This dimension focuses on maturing the business aspect, 
building a viable business model, financial planning, ensuring a market fit, and in the end 
successful big-scale sales. This ensures a sophisticated business model that offers a sustainable 
and growing business. 
IP Readiness Level (IPRL): This dimension focuses on protecting the company's intellectual 
properties including trademarks, trade secrets, and patents. Key points of this dimension are the 
initial development of an IP strategy and later the execution and implementation of future tools 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
and procedures to maintain the protection. The goal is to secure the company while minimizing 
the legal risks. 
Team Readiness Level (TmRL): This dimension is essentially about identifying and securing the 
team members needed to successfully move the innovation to the different end goals of the other 
dimensions. This dimension is the executive part of this multidimensional ecosystem 
(framework). It helps team development from the level of team incompetence and deficiency to 
a high-performing self-sufficient team. 
Funding Readiness Level (FRL): This dimension focuses on the financial resources. Ensuring that 
the funding needs are met is a critical element of an innovation strategy. This dimension explores 
the different funding sources such as angel investors, soft money(grants) and others, aswell as 
preparing for selling your project through effective pitches. The end goal of this dimension is to 
ensure funding needs are met, and future funding needs are planned and continuously 
considered. 
9 Levels 
Another key component of the framework is the fragmentation of each dimension into 9 levels. 
This creates a “latter” that helps users identify the status of the progress as well as identify the 
next steps that need to be executed. 
Important to notice is that Level 1 is the entry-level learning about basic principles, after which 
you keep progressing up towards Level 9 which is the end goal of being flight-proven. This analogy 
would apply to all 6 of the dimensions.

II.2. Innovation in startups 
II.2.a. Innovation metrics 
Being able to measure innovation is key to understanding progress.  
The following are usefull metrics to track innovation in a medical device company (Nilsson & 
Ritzén, 2014): 
1. Innovation Time Allocation: Here it is registered how much time is dedicated to 
innovation-related activities outside standard projects. 
2. Cross-functional Team Metrics: Here the number and the progress of cross-functional 
teams and workshops are evaluated. 
3. Idea Conceptualization Projects: Specifically, projects working with idea 
conceptualization are considered particularly interesting. Here it is assessed what 
status the idea conceptualization projects have. 
4. External Collaboration Tracking: Here it is evaluated how many new external 
collaborations have been made, and how effective they have been. 
5. Idea Submission Analysis: Here the quantity and quality of new product-related ideas 
are registered. 
6. Idea Diversity Metrics: This metric investigates the diversity of the idea sources, 
including external contributions. 
7. Patent Filing Count: Here the number of new patents filed are registered. 
8. Process Improvement Initiatives: In this metric, the version, implementation, and 
impact, of the organizational process improvement ideas, are evaluated.

II.2.b. Challenges of innovation 
When navigating between multiple countries, regulations can vary a lot thus creating challenges 
regarding compliance (Bayon et al. 2016). Bringing new products to the market may introduce 
legal complexity. Regulation and product market launch increases the time and cost of this 
process.   
Bayon argues the importance of new solutions representing not only new technology but also 
addressing real-world needs.  
Especially awareness around the company type is key to understanding its weak or strong points: 
While SMEs are more agile and can focus better on niche areas, they are challenged when scaling 
and navigating this complex regulatory area. Larger companies on the other hand are more 
resourceful and can better handle these challenges, however lacking the same agility as an SME.   
Bayon also states that Innovation in medical device companies must be in a format where 
technological advancement and regulatory compliance are in balance. This is to ensure that new 
products are both innovative yet safe to use. Balancing these two parameters is a permanent 
challenge, because companies need to both innovate while staying compliant.

III.2. Thematical Analysis 
To analyze the qualitative data a thematic analysis was chosen. Thematic analysis suits the 
handling of diverse qualitative data input. This specific form of analysis works in a 5-phase flow, 
it uses inductive reasoning and provides strong results because of the processes of revisiting data, 
as well as the ability to identify both explicit and implicit aspects of data, e.g. the obvious and 
underlying meaning of the qualitative data.  
The thematic analysis will follow these 5 steps: 
1. Data Familiarization: Investigate data from interviews, published work, case studies, and 
regulations, to better understand what the data means.  
2. Data Coding: Organizing data by labeling different parts that are important and have 
relevant features.  
3. Identifying Themes: The labeled data is reviewed and grouped into themes.  
4. Reviewing and Defining Themes: The themes and organizing decisions are reviewed to 
make sure they align with the intended purpose of the analysis. This step synthesizes and 
finetunes the results.  
5. Write-up: In this step, the results from the analysis relate to the research question, and a 
generalization is attempted.

V. Discussion & Future Work 
Expansion of the KTH IRL framework 
The Compliance Readiness Level (CpRL) framework, suggested in this thesis, offers a structured 
approach for medical device startups to navigate the very complex area of regulatory 
compliance.  
However, the scope of this research has been primarily focused on the European context, 
primarily focusing on the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR) and the EU AI Act.  
Future research could investigate the possibilities of the CpRL framework in other regulatory 
areas, such as the US, where the FDA's regulatory compliance requirements present a different 
set of challenges and opportunities for medical device startups. 
Another area for future investigation could be the use of modern Quality Management Systems 
(QMS) software implementing the CpRL framework directly. These systems are developing fast 
and it would benefit users if they would adopt such a framework. 
Applying Startup Experience in Larger Corporations 
The agility and innovative properties of startups provide a unique perspective on regulatory 
compliance. An interesting area of future research could be to examine how the experiences and 
strategies of startups in achieving regulatory compliance can be translated into larger, more 
established organizations.  
This could include studying how the principles of the CpRL framework can be scaled and 
adapted to fit the operational models of larger corporations, particularly in the field of AI-driven 
medical technology, which is evolving rapidly. 
Validation of CpRL 
It is clear that having only 2 case studies performed as interviews, certainly yields poor 
validation of the CpRL framework. 
Future research could feature validation where multiple startups could try out the framework 
for an extended period.

VI. Conclusion 
This thesis has critically examined and extended the KTH Innovation Readiness Level 
framework through the introduction of a Compliance Readiness Level (CpRL).  
The key point of this research was to investigate how medical device startups can achieve 
regulatory compliance in Europe while achieving innovation and bringing new technology to 
the market. 
The findings show that extending the KTH Innovation Readiness Level framework with the 
CpRL creates a great overview for startups to navigate the complexities of regulatory 
compliance. 
Early-stage compliance elements have emerged as fundamental for startups, requiring a 
proactive approach to regulatory challenges. This thesis highlights the importance of 
understanding and adapting to regulatory frameworks, particularly the EU Medical Device 
Regulation and the EU AI Act, from the early phases of product development and beyond market 
entry. 
The research also highlights the important role of external collaborations and internal resource 
planning, to maintain a balance between innovation and compliance, especially under the 
regulations such as the MDR and the EU AI Act. 
Lastly this thesis provides an extensive guide for medical device startups to integrate regulatory 
compliance into their innovation processes effectively.
