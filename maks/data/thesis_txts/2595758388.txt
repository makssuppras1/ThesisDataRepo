Abstract
i
Abstract
The distribution grid is facing a major challenge from the future load increase from
the charging of electric vehicles. In order for the distribution system operators to plan
appropriately, they have to know the magnitude of the load increase and the critical grid
areas. Previous studies have examined the impact on the low voltage distribution grid, but
none have investigated how the medium voltage grid is affected. This thesis determines the
probable impact of additional EVs and charger to a model representing a real Danish grid.
To examine the most probable impact of the charger increase, a probabilistic approach is
used to determine the placement and activation of chargers. By simulating a worst case
scenario day 500 times, the average and 95 % fractile loading of the system components
are found. Based on the 95 % fractile maximum loading of the system components it is
found that 27 lines and 26 transformers are overloaded in the examined grid. This amounts
to up to 3 % of the lines and 54 % of the transformers being overloaded. When examining
the busses with critical voltage deviations it is found that the voltage deviations of 5 %
of the system busses are critical. Most of the critical components are in low residential
area. Costs of reinforcing the grid is determined to be between 1650 and 2300 DKK per
customer, with possibilities for reductions by reusing overloaded transformers. Finally, it
was found that implementing smart charging can help to reduce the number of overloaded
components by up to 31% and the busses with critical voltage deviations up to 18 %. The
results of this thesis can be used to give a prediction of the impact of home, fast, and
superchargers on the Danish distribution system planning.

1. Introduction
1
1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
The world is facing one of the biggest challenges in newer times, the climate crisis. The
debate on how to combat the climate crisis has become increasingly prevalent in the last
decades, with its political popularity increasing substantially within the last couple of years.
The Danish Parliament has agreed upon a goal of reducing the Danish CO2 emissions by
70% compared to 1990 levels before 2030 [1]. One of the propositions for reaching this
goal is to transition a large share of the Danish personal car ﬂeet from internal combustion
engine (ICE) vehicles to electric vehicles (EVs)[2].
Traditional road transport makes up 71.7 % [3] of the total emissions seen in the transport
sector, which represents 22 % [4] of the total Danish emissions. Due to the signiﬁcant
share of the emission caused by the transport sector, it is an area where a large part of the
emission reduction can be obtained. By replacing ICE vehicles with EVs and charging
with green energy from wind and solar farms, EVs can reduce the CO2 emissions for road
transport signiﬁcantly [5].
EVs have been developing rapidly in recent years [6], and buying an EV is becoming more
attractive due to economic and technical barriers eroding [7]. This includes more affordable
price ranges, increased availability of different models, and increased ranges fulﬁlling most
travelling requirements [8, 9]. Public charging availability has also increased signiﬁcantly
but is still one of the major barriers as well as the mindset of people, being that EVs
cannot fulﬁl their requirements [10]. With increased political and public awareness about
the climate and willingness and commitment to act, it is expected that the EV share will
increase over the next decade following the ambitions and recommendations from various
organisations [11, 2].
Following the increasing interest in EVs many of the traditional ICE vehicle producers
have started to produce EVs as well [12]. There were about 24 different EV models in the
Danish car ﬂeet in 2019 and more are coming every year [13, 12]. New models bring new
technology and increased ranges. The newest EV models have battery capacities of up to
100 kWh (Tesla model S) [14]. Furthermore, concept cars have been announced, like the
Tesla Cybertruck which reaches battery capacities of up to 200 kWh [15].
While increased battery capacities bring increased ranges, larger capacity also means longer

charging times. Improving public fast and supercharging availability and power level are
crucial factors to counteract long charging times. It is expected that the availability of charg-
ers will be increased by more fast and superchargers being installed at convenient places,
like workplaces, supermarkets and alongside heavily trafﬁcked roads [16]. In regards to
the power level, high power superchargers are already being installed, where the largest
superchargers in Denmark currently being able to supply EVs with up to 350 kW [17]
and the new CHAdeMO 3.0 charger plug announced for 2021 supporting up to 500kW
charging [18].
The increased availability and power level however, leads to another issue regarding how
the distribution grid will be able to handle load increase caused by the charging of EVs [19].
This enhanced load development must be taken into account when planning future load
development. Based on expectations from the Danish Energy Agency, and Danish En-
ergy (DE) projects that by the start of 2030, the total amount of EVs in Denmark will reach
885,000 and there will be around 3,600 fast and 2,300 superchargers [20]. It is expected that
especially the medium voltage (MV) and low voltage (LV) grid will be affected by this [19].
As of 2020, most of the EV charging is occurring at home [21, 22, 20]. In the future, it is
expected that a larger share of the EV charging will be at public charging points. To ensure
a stable distribution grid in the future, the distribution system operators (DSOs) must be
prepared for problematic grid areas and what to expect in regards to system load increases.
Previous studies investigating the impact of EV charging on the distribution grid have
focused on the home charging impact to the LV part of the grid [23]. Investigating the MV
part of the distribution grid is however just as relevant as it is expected that a large share of
the new fast and superchargers will be connected directly to this part of the grid due to the
high power. More than this, increased loading in the LV grid will affect the MV grid as
well. The DSOs are responsible for ensuring stable operation of the distribution grid. To
account for increased EV loading in their planning, they need to know about the probable
magnitude of the problems. This will give them the tools needed to prepare for the future
and maintain the high voltage quality, high security of supply, and low amount of outages,
which Denmark is known for.
1.2
Objective and approach
The purpose of this master thesis is to investigate the impact of home, fast, and supercharg-
ers on the distribution grid planning. The motivation for this being to provide the DSOs
with indications of the magnitude of increased loading and critical locations in the grid,
both crucial for the planning of the future MV distribution grid.
==PAGE:15==
1.3
Thesis Outline
3
This master thesis will take the uncertainty connected with future charging of EVs into
account by following a probabilistic approach to the future distribution of EVs, chargers
and when which chargers are activated. This will be accomplished by generating charging
load proﬁles for EVs using different chargers and allocating the load proﬁles to randomly
generated set points of a MV grid model representing a real Danish MV distribution grid.

2.1
Dimensioning criterion
One of the DSOs’ key tasks as a grid operator is to maintain a high security of supply.
When assessing the operation of distribution grids, two main criteria need to be fulﬁlled:
1. Voltage criteria
2. Current criteria
2.1.1
Voltage criteria
The voltage criteria state that a certain level of voltage quality has to be maintained during
operation of the grid. To maintain a good voltage quality, it is required that the voltage
does not have excessive ﬂuctuations, and the voltage is within the required voltage limits.
Following the recommendation 16 [24] made by DE Forskning og Udvikling (DEFU), fast
changes of the voltage should normally not exceed 5 % of the nominal voltage, Un, but
changes up to 10 % of Un is however accepted for a limited amount of times each day.
From this it can be read that the voltage should, for the most part, lie within the following:
0.95·Un < Unode < 1.05·Un
However, this 10 % band is for both production and consumption, therefore, when looking
at mainly consumption loads, the whole 10 % span should not be allowed. The danish
DSOs are normally looking at allocating a conservative span of 5 % to consumption which
results in the span seen in Equation 2.1.
0.975·Un < Unode < 1.025·Un
(2.1)
Keeping the voltage within boundaries is both an important legal requirement and a techni-
cal requirement. Many electrical appliances are designed for a speciﬁc rated voltage, Un,
and will not work properly otherwise [25]. Fault detection is also a key reason as faults are
harder to detect if the voltage has large variations under normal operation.
Increased electromobility through more EVs leads to load increases, especially at the grid
edges [19, 23]. This will potentially cause voltage problems on the radials. This can be
explained by a line with a load connected as seen in Figure 2.1 [26].

2.1.2
Current criteria
From the current criteria, it follows that the current ﬂowing through the components should
not exceed the current ratings of the components [25]. The current is limited due to thermal
limitations of the component [25]. The thermal limits during normal operation are usually
calculated by the original equipment manufacturer (OEM) and listed in the component
datasheet. From the thermal limit of the component, the maximum current during normal
operation can be calculated. The maximum (rated) power ﬂow through the component
can then be calculated from the maximum current limit. Current and power limits are
sometimes given by OEMs.
The power ﬂowing through the component divided with the rated power limit gives a
percentage loading of the component, see Equation 2.7. 100 % loading would be the
maximum allowable loading of the component during normal operation.

Designing the system so that all components are loaded close to the maximum loading
might however cause some additional problems if an adjacent component fails. Following
the basic criteria for the design and operation of power systems, a power system should be
able to maintain full operation during an n-1 scenario [27].
In a n-1 scenario, one system component is lost and the loading of the component is
transferred to other components of the system. Depending on the system topology and the
location of the faulty component, the loading of the component is normally transferred to
a couple of other components, depending on the topology of the grid, and therefore the
system components need to have extra loading capacity available [27].

Charging of EVs
The EVs seen today widely use lithium-ion batteries for the accumulator. There are
different methods for charging a lithium-ion battery. One of the most widely used is the
constant current, constant voltage method. With this charging method, it is possible to
charge the EV efﬁciently and without damaging the battery excessively.
Constants current, constant voltage charging is typically done in four steps:
1. Pre-charge
2. Charging with constant current
3. Charging with constant voltage
4. Recharging
Initially the battery is pre-charged at a low current if the battery is not already pre-charged.
Then it is switched to charging at a constant current higher than the pre-charge current. It
charges with this current until it reaches a threshold, either set by the state of charge (SoC)
or the battery voltage. When the threshold is reached the battery is charged with constant
voltage and decreasing current until the SoC reaches 100 %[29].

2.4.1
Degradation of batteries
One of the main limitations for the charging power is the degradation of the battery caused
by the charging itself. It is in the best interest of the EV user to keep the capacity of
the battery at a maximum for a long time to prolong the lifetime of the EV battery. The
producers of both EVs and chargers conduct extensive research into the degradation and
how to maintain a long battery life while still being able to charge the battery in a short
amount of time.
The primary type of degradation considered for charging of EVs is the cycle degradation.
The cycle degradation is normally given by the battery producer, where an amount of
==PAGE:23==

cycles is stated at a certain depth of discharge (DoD). An example could be 1000 cycles at
DoD 60 % which means that after 1000 cycles where the battery cell is discharged from
100 % to 40 % and charged again, the battery would reach a state of health (SoH) at around
80 % where the battery normally is considered at the end of its lifetime [31].
The degradation of the battery keeps dropping as the battery technology keeps improving
which makes it possible to increase the charging power and maintain the same life time
of the batteries. This means that charging power will increase over time and should be
considered for the future charging power of the EVs.

Geographical diversity and DEF-codes
2.6.1
Area division of the Danish distribution grid
DE has divided the Danish distribution system into ﬁve different zones, describing differ-
ent geographical area types, called GIS-zones (Geographical Information System). Each
GIS-zone has a different composition of building types and thus there are different concen-
trations of EVs and chargers.
The ﬁve areas are as follows [36]:
• Tall residential
• Low residential
• Hinterland
• Vacation Homes
• Industry
Tall residential [36]:
The area tall residential is the area seen mostly in larger cities,
where buildings are three ﬂoors or taller and the spacing between buildings is small.
The tall residential area accounts for 0.4 % of the total Danish area and is characterized by
high energy density and short feeders.
As the buildings are closely situated to one another, the residents of the area normally do
not have access to a garage where they can charge their electric vehicle. Most of the EV
owners of the area are therefore expected to charge their EV using roadside charging or
charging at their workspace.
Small parts of the tall residential area will be commercial buildings. The heating of the
area mostly consists of district heating, and solar panels are not widely used.
Low residential [36]:
The low residential area consists of lower building with one or
two ﬂoors but still closely situated to one another. The area accounts for 6.4 % of the total
Danish area. A suburb is an example of a low residential area.
The low residential area is especially challenging as it is expected to see a large increase
in EVs and heat pumps (HP). As a large share of the residents of the area has its own
parking, it is expected that the main way of charging will be home charging. This will
cause increased stress to the local distribution grid and 10kV/0.4kV feeder.
It is assumed that a larger part of the low residential area will be commercial buildings and
shops.
==PAGE:28==
2.6
Geographical diversity and DEF-codes
16
Hinterland [36]:
Hinterland is the largest of the ﬁve areas and accounts for 90 % of
the total Danish area. It is characterised as a low energy density area with house types
similar to the low residential area but also includes farmhouses. The spacing between the
housing is much larger than the low residential area, which results in longer radials. As the
radials are longer it results in a weaker grid. The EV concentration is assumed to be less
than the country average. Today a lot of the houses and farms in the area Hinterland are
heated using oil and wood pellet furnaces, however, it expected to transition to HP due to
economic feasibility.
The Hinterland area also consists of some commercial buildings.
Vacation Homes [36]:
Vacation homes areas account for 1.1 % of the total Danish area.
The electricity consumption differs from a normal consumption as the consumption is
larger in the summer and vacation times. Due to this, the radial is normally longer than
usual, leading to a weaker grid, which might result in complications regarding the voltage
quality. Within the vacation home areas, individual house consumption can vary quite a lot
since some vacation homes are rented out, and some are for private use only.
This results in very different needs for heating by HPs and charging of EVs. It is assumed
that the users of the vacation home bring one EV and that half of the vacation homes with
an EV has access to a home charger.
When people arrive at their vacation home they have normally driven a longer distance,
and therefore need more charging within the ﬁrst day and afterward the EV is not used as
much as usual.
Industry [36]:
The last area is the industrial area which accounts for 2 % of the total
Danish area. It is characterised by a few users but with high energy consumption. It differs
from the other areas as it is mostly connected straight to the 10kV feeder. It is assumed to
be the least homogeneous area as within 24 hours the consumption varies signiﬁcantly.
The EV charging in the area will mostly be workers charging during work hours, as well as
ﬂeet charging and commercial vehicle charging. The heating is expected to mostly consist
of HPs. The growth of EVs and HPs differs from the other areas [37].

Methodology
This section will describe the methods used for determining the impact of home, fast,
and superchargers on the distribution grid planning. First, the examined grid model and
the assumptions introduced. Next is the load proﬁle generation described along with the
method for assessing the results, and ﬁnally the uncertainties connected with the study are
described and how these are minimised.
3.1
Distribution grid model
DE has provided a MV distribution grid which the study will be based upon.
The grid is based on a real Danish grid, represented in a PowerFactory-model. Due to
conﬁdentiality the names of components are changed for the study and are simply named
following the format ”Component type and number” for example the ﬁrst transformer will
have the name ”Transformer 1”.
Information on the grid can be seen below:
• 1,279 line components
• 56 transformer components
• 1,316 bus components
• 710 base loads
• 710 HP loads
• 710 photovoltaic (PV) generation units
• 710 EV loads
• 1 external grid
• 7 synchronous machine generation units
• 84,255 customers

The wealth of Denmark is increasing, meaning more and more families can afford or
prioritises a personal vehicle or a second one [39][40]. This leads to an increase in the
number of personal vehicles in Denmark which is projected to reach 3.3 million in 2030.
No growth in the number of personal vehicles is expected between 2030 and 2040 [20].

increases.

3.2.6
Losses and charging times
Charger losses and charging times are correlated as described in subsection 2.4. Higher
losses result in longer charging times. The losses are different depending on the component
and the charger type.
Grid component losses:
The components of the PowerFactory grid model have losses
deﬁned which are following the data sheets for the speciﬁc components. The losses are
outputted from PowerFactory as active power losses.
AC charger losses:
The AC chargers are only used for home charging as seen from the
charger types and connections assumptions. The AC charger losses are assumed to be 26 %
of the charging power which is the average loss of the chargers between 2.3 and 11 kW,
see subsection 2.4 [32].
The charging times for the AC chargers are calculated using Equation 2.12 and the time
varies from 1 to 3 hours depending on the charger.

charging times for the fast and superchargers are calculated in the same way as for the AC
chargers, it is assumed that the larger the charger, the longer distance the user will have
driven since the last charging event. Therefore fast chargers are assumed to charge the

Coincidence factor for EV charging
Different CF proﬁles are assumed for different kinds of EV charging.
The coincidence factor varies for the different kinds of charging, home, fast and super, as
well as temporally; weekday/weekend and summer/winter. the shape of the CF is deter-

Figure 3.1a shows the CF for general home charging and fast and supercharging in major
city areas. Figure 3.1b and Figure 3.1c show the general home, fast, and supercharging
CF for rural areas. Figure 3.1d shows the CF for charging in vacation home areas.
The CF for home charging is based on DEs estimations for arrival time at home with
a peak around 16-17.00 with a spread of a couple of hours [47]. The home charger CF
curve is based on DEs assumed home charger CF curve, and the magnitude is adjusted
according to account for the amount of EVs in the grid. CF curve for fast charging weekday
and weekend is based on an in-depth Norwegian study of fast charging in Norway from
2019 [21]. The superchargers are assumed to have the same shape as fast chargers, but
with a lower peak. The peak CF for fast and supercharging is based on the utilisation ratio
of the chargers. The peak CF for supercharger are assumed to be 2/3 of the fast charger
peak, due to a higher power level.
As can be seen in Figure 3.1a and Figure 3.1b, the CF varies for different areas. The

different CFs will be utilised for different areas. The study found that areas with major
cities were consistent on it being either a weekday or weekend, therefore only 2 different
proﬁles. For areas without major cities more going through travel and weekend travel is
experienced, therefore 4 different proﬁles are used, Monday-Thursday, Friday, Saturday
and Sunday.

3.2.8
Smart charging
By implementing a timer that activates the home chargers at a certain time, it is possible
to move the load demand of home chargers from normal peak hours to less loaded hours.
Often people do not need to use their car after returning home from daily activities
before the next morning. Therefore they do not need their EV to be charged immediately.
Nonetheless will the average EV user plug in their car and start the charging right away.
That means that the EV will be charging in the peak hours of normal consumption. For
normal daily use, an EV can be fully recharged in three hours or less. Due to the short
charging period, it is possible to push the charging to non-peak hours and still be fully
charged by the morning. The effect of smart charging will be tested as a solution to
lowering the total amount of overloaded components of the MV distribution grid. Smart
charging is implemented by decreasing the coincidence factor for home chargers in peak
hours between 14.00 and 19.00 and increasing the coincidence factor with the decreased
amount between 20.00 and 03.00. Two smart charging scenarios are tested, smart charging
scenario 1 where the peak is decreased with 30 % and smart charging scenario 2 where
the peak is decreased with 60 %. The result of the smart charging scenarios on the home
coincidence factor can be seen in Figure 3.2.

3.3
Load proﬁles and generation units
EV load proﬁles are used to simulate the impact of the home, fast and superchargers. In
this section, the generation of the EV load proﬁles and the implementation of the baseload
proﬁles and generation units will be explained.
The base house loads, HP loads, PV generations, and synchronous machine generations
have been generated by DE.
3.3.1
Baseload and generation proﬁles
DE has generated the baseloads and base generation of the system. The loads and gen-
eration are year speciﬁc and contain baseload and generation proﬁles for buildings, HPs,
PVs, and synchronous machines. The proﬁles describe the loading and generation for
two weeks, one week during summer and one week during winter. The loading described
in the proﬁle represents the 95 % fractile consumption and generation at the nodes. The
resolution of the proﬁles is hourly and it is, therefore, necessary to change the resolution
of the load and generation proﬁles if the simulation is done with a resolution higher than
hourly.
To achieve proﬁles with a higher resolution than hourly, modiﬁcations have to be done.
New proﬁles are made with time steps of the desired resolution. Each time step within the
hours of the original proﬁles will be granted the value of the hour, resulting in the average
load of the hour remaining the same. This means that for an example if the load proﬁles
are desired with a resolution of 15 minutes, the hour describing the average load between
15 and 16 is split into four parts each describing 15 minutes which has the same average
load as the initial hour step. This is done for all hourly steps of the initial proﬁles in order
to get load and generation proﬁles with a resolution of 15 minutes.

3.3.2
Generation of EV load proﬁles
Generation of the EV load proﬁles is a key element for examining the impact of home, fast,
and superchargers on the planning of medium voltage distribution grid. This part will go
through how the load proﬁles are generated.
To generate the charger proﬁles, it is necessary to know the number of chargers and EVs in
the system, and on which system nodes they are located.
Determining the amount of EVs and home, fast, and superchargers on the nodes:
For calculating the amount of EVs, home, fast, and superchargers a 37x20 matrix is used.
This 37x20 matrix contains information on how many EVs, home, fast, and superchargers

3.4
Result assessment
To determine the impact of load increases due to EVs and chargers in the future, it has to
be compared to a reference scenario for 2020 and 2030 without EVs. These base years are
only using the baseload proﬁles from DE, this includes baseload, HP consumption, and
PV generation. The two base years are compared to determine grid status in 2030 before
adding EV impact.
The grid status will be determined based on the voltage and the current criteria for both
base years and the EV scenario and compared. Furthermore, an approximate cost of
reinforcing the grid is determined for the EV scenario, based on the current criteria.
3.4.1
Voltage deviation determination
For assessing the voltage deviation of the nodes, the maximum voltage deviation is
calculated for all the simulated days. Then, the 95 % fractile of the voltage deviations for
the busses are found. If the 95% fractile voltage deviation of a bus exceeds 2.5 %, the bus
voltage is deemed critical following subsection 2.1.
3.4.2
Overload determination following the current criteria
Whether a component is overloaded or not is based on the assumption for the overloading
of components, subsection 3.2. Components are included as overloaded if the maximum
of the 95 % fractile loading on the investigated day, exceeds the overload threshold of
66.67 % at some point.
3.4.3
Line power loss determination
Losses of lines are calculated in PowerFactory following subsection 2.2. The losses will
be compared to the system without EVs to give an approximation of the expected loss
increase due to the charging of additional EVs.

3.5
Uncertainties
With assumptions follows uncertainties, this part will describe the uncertainties connected
with the assumptions of subsection 3.2. The impact of the uncertainties will be tested in
the sensitivity analysis, section 5.
3.5.1
Amount of EVs
The primary variable which determines the increase in energy consumption and the load
increase is the actual amount of EVs in Denmark. For the normal 2030 simulation, this
number is determined to be 885,383 [45]. The amount of EVs is based on an expected
energy usage for EVs, the efﬁciency of EVs, and an average driving distance per year [20,
42]. The energy amount is based on expected development following political goals and
==PAGE:48==
3.5
Uncertainties
36
could have large variations since it depends on the Danish people to adopt EVs to a large
extent.
3.5.2
Average charging power
The different classes of EVs have very different power utilisation of the different charger
types. Furthermore, the higher power level chargers are underutilised to a higher degree. It
is difﬁcult to predict what the actual power utilisation degree for the corresponding chargers
will be for the different EV classes. Therefore the system consequences of variations in
the power utilisation degree will also be investigated.
3.5.3
Maximum loading threshold
The maximum loading threshold is based on a general rule of all components having two
alternative paths for the power ﬂow during a fault. This is of course very general, and this
criterion will change depending on the topology of the grid and common practice for the
DSOs. In some places there will be only one alternative path, other places there will be
more. The DSO could also use a different maximum loading thresholds for planning.
3.5.4
Coincidence factor
Following that, only a few studies have been conducted on the CF of systems with a large
share of EVs, and large ranges in the results of the studies, the CF is connected with large
uncertainty. It is therefore vital to investigate the impact of the CFs on the results.
3.5.5
Charger composition
As a predicted composition of chargers is used for the simulations, it is connected with
uncertainties. A composition with generally higher power chargers can lead to higher
peaks and therefore the impact of variations of the charger composition has to be tested as
well.
3.5.6
AC charger losses
The losses in the home chargers are assumed to be at around 26 %. This is based on a
study from DTU [32], but other sources suggest lower values. Therefore, this is tested to
see whether the impact of lower losses, which results in shorter charging times for home
charging, has any signiﬁcant impact on the results.

6. Conclusion
68
6
Conclusion
The focus on additional EVs as part of reducing the Danish CO2 emissions leads to an
increase in the number of chargers. The additional EVs and chargers increases the energy
demand and stress on the distribution grid. Therefore, the effect of the additional loading
caused by home, fast, and superchargers should be determined to ﬁnd the impact on the
necessary planning of the MV distribution grid of the future.
Load proﬁles describing the charger loading are generated based on projections for the
number of EVs, home, fast, and superchargers. A probabilistic approach is taken to de-
termine the components which are most likely to be overloaded. This is done by random
allocation and activation of the chargers, to give the most probable future scenario. The
load proﬁles are then incorporated into a model of a MV distribution grid which represents
a real Danish grid and load ﬂow analysis is performed. Based on this, it is found that
future load increases due to increased number of home, fast, and superchargers will cause
a signiﬁcant number of components to be overloaded in the MV distribution grid model.
It is found that the enlarged component loading leads to 2.1 % of the lines and 46.4 % of
the transformers to be overloaded. Compared to the average peak hour line losses found
in 2020, the average peak hour line losses in the EV scenario 2030 has increased from
1.1 to up to 3.2 kW per km, an increase of 190 %. Increased system loading is found to
cause up to 5 % of the system busses to experience critical voltage deviations. The impact
of the home, fast, and superchargers are most signiﬁcant on the low residential and high
residential areas of the grid. From the number of overloaded components and busses with
critical voltage deviations in the system during the EV scenario, it can be concluded that
the Danish MV distribution grid will require reinforcement. Reinforcement costs are found
to be between 1650 to 2300 DKK per customer in the grid, depending on the loading
fractile examined.
Smart charging has been found to help mitigate the number of overloaded components and
busses with critical voltage deviations, by reducing cost of required reinforcement by up
to 29 %. Furthermore, it was found that by replacing overloaded transformers with other
overloaded transformers of larger nominal power, costs could be reduced by additional
25 %.
Finally, it is concluded that the Danish distribution system is facing sizeable challenges
from the impact of home, fast, and superchargers. By incorporating the ﬁndings in this

thesis, planning done in advance can minimise the economic impact of home, fast, and
superchargers on the MV distribution grid.

7. Future work
70
7
Future work
As more home, fast, and superchargers are implemented to the Danish distribution system,
more accurate projections for the future development will be possible. By having more
accurate projections, better estimations of the number of EVs and chargers in the MV
distribution can be made. Further research into coincidence factors and charger composition
will help in giving more accurate projections of the impact of home, fast, and superchargers
on the MV distribution grid. By extending the model to cover a larger geographical area
could help in determining the validity of the results found in this thesis. If charging of
electriﬁed heavy transport is incorporated into the normal charging infrastructure, this
could also affect the ﬁndings in this thesis. The same is true for normal low power road
side charging which is assumed included in home charging in this thesis. A Norwegian
study found differences in the coincidence factor between summer and winter due to longer
charging times in colder weather.
