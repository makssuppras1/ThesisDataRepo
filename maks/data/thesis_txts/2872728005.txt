Abstract—Hearing aids need to reduce background noise while
also compensating for hearing loss, so that speech becomes clearer
and easier to understand for people with hearing impairments.
Recent research has shown that deep learning models can
be trained to handle both tasks at the same time by using
auditory models that simulate how sound is processed in the
ear. However, many existing systems still struggle when the
environment changes or when the input becomes more complex.
In this thesis, we build on a baseline joint system for noise
reduction and hearing loss compensation, and propose several
improvements. We first introduce two changes to the auditory
front-end: a temporal averaging method that smooths the input
features over time, and an adaptation module that mimics
how the ear reacts to changes in sound level. We then add
a filtering method that splits the signal into slow and fast
modulations, which are related to speech and background noise,
and design new loss functions that make use of this separation.
Seven training strategies are tested, each focusing on different
combinations of these modulation features.
We evaluate the system on a dataset that combines clean
and noisy speech with simulated hearing loss. The results are
measured using standard perceptual metrics such as HASQI,
HASPI, PESQ, ESTOI, and SNR. Although the proposed front-
end modules and loss strategies were designed with perceptual
motivation, they showed little to no improvement over the
baseline in objective evaluation. The baseline system remained
the best performance model under test.
Index Terms—hearing loss compensation, noise reduction,
differentiable auditory model, deep learning
I. INTRODUCTION
Hearing loss affects a large and growing part of the global
population, especially older adults. In everyday life, noisy
environments often make it difficult for hearing-impaired
individuals to understand speech, even when using hearing
aids. This challenge has motivated the development of signal
processing algorithms that improve speech clarity by reducing
background noise and restoring frequency-dependent hearing
deficits.
Traditional approaches typically address noise reduction and
hearing loss compensation as separate problems [1]. However,
noise suppression alone may distort important speech cues,
while hearing compensation without considering noise can

amplify unwanted background sounds. Therefore, there is a
growing interest in combining both objectives in a single
framework to achieve better overall speech intelligibility and
listening comfort.
A recent framework called Controllable Noise Reduction
and Hearing Loss Compensation (C-NR-HLC) has shown that
it is possible to jointly optimize noise reduction and hearing
loss compensation using a deep neural network guided by a
multi-task loss [2]. While this approach is effective, it still
relies on a simplified auditory front-end and does not fully
reflect how the human auditory system processes temporal and
spectral information.
Moreover, many existing systems assume that reducing
background noise is always beneficial. However, for people
with hearing loss, this may not always be the case [3].
Environmental sounds and even background noise can carry
important contextual cues that support orientation, safety, and
naturalness. Over-suppressing these signals may lead to a
artificial listening experience that lacks richness or situational
awareness. Therefore, the need for a more refined and percep-
tually informed strategy that does not treat noise as a purely
negative element.
This thesis aims to improve the perceptual quality and
robustness of the joint NR and HLC system by enhancing
the auditory front-end and supervision strategies. Specifically,
we introduce a temporal smoothing module to align the model
with the slower modulations typically accessible to hearing-
impaired listeners, who often struggle to perceive fast temporal
fluctuations due to reduced temporal resolution in the auditory
system. Inspired by the adaptation dynamics observed in
human auditory nerve models [10], we replace the static log-
compression with a dual time-constant adaptation mechanism
to better reflect how the auditory periphery responds to
changes in sound level. Furthermore, we design modulation-
domain filtering and frequency-specific loss functions to guide
the network toward perceptually relevant structures, based on
the observation that different modulation frequencies encode
complementary cues—such as slow envelope modulations for
speech rhythm and faster components for fine acoustic detail.

II. RELATED WORK
In recent years, deep learning has significantly advanced
the field of speech enhancement, particularly in noisy envi-
ronments. Many studies have focused on learning mappings
from noisy to clean speech using time-domain or frequency-
domain representations [4] [5], often with convolutional or
recurrent neural network architectures. These approaches have
shown impressive results in improving signal-to-noise ratio
and speech intelligibility for normal-hearing listeners. How-
ever, most of them are not designed with hearing-impaired
users in mind and do not account for the altered auditory
perception caused by hearing loss.
Efforts have also been made to incorporate hearing loss
compensation into neural models. Some studies simulate the
impaired auditory periphery and attempt to restore speech per-
ception through dynamic range compression or gain shaping
[7]. These systems are typically optimized using fixed audio-
grams and may rely on handcrafted features or filterbanks.
Although effective to some extent, they are rarely trained
jointly with noise reduction modules and often overlook the
complex interactions between noise suppression and hearing
compensation.
More recent approaches aim to address both tasks simulta-
neously. One proposed framework [2] that jointly trains noise
reduction (NR) and hearing loss compensation (HLC) using a
differentiable auditory model. Their system allows controllable
task balancing and shows promising improvements in per-
ceptual quality for both normal-hearing and hearing-impaired
conditions. We use this model as the baseline in this study.
Fig. 1.
Joint optimization framework [2], where NR and HLC outputs are
supervised through differentiable auditory models for NH and HI conditions.
As illustrated in Figure 1, the framework takes as input a
noisy speech signal x, a clean reference y, and a representative
audiogram a. A shared neural speech processor Fθ outputs two
signals: a denoised version ˆyNR and a compensated version
ˆyHLC. These outputs are passed through two separate auditory
models: ANH for normal hearing, and AHI for impaired
hearing based on the audiogram.
The denoised signal is evaluated using the NH auditory
model, while the compensated signal is assessed using the HI
model [8]. Both evaluations produce feature representations
that are compared to those of the clean target y through a loss
function ℓ. The resulting losses provide perceptually grounded
gradients to train Fθ.
This dual-path supervision approach offers a principled
solution to the challenge of optimizing for both NR and
HLC. Importantly, it avoids training solely on waveform
reconstruction, instead using perceptually motivated feature
comparisons as the learning objective. However, one limitation
of this framework is that it applies the loss functions uniformly
across the entire auditory feature space. It does not account
for the varying perceptual importance of different modulation
frequencies, which are known to carry complementary infor-
mation for speech perception. Addressing this limitation forms
a key motivation for the present thesis.
III. SYSTEM MODEL
This chapter details the architectural enhancements intro-
duced to the baseline Controllable Noise Reduction and Hear-
ing Loss Compensation (C-NR-HLC) framework. The original
framework employs a Band-split Recurrent Neural Network
(BSRNN) [9] to jointly predict a denoised output (ˆyNR) and
a hearing loss compensated signal (ˆyHLC), guided by a multi-
task loss that dynamically weights the objectives based on their
respective predictive uncertainties.
A key component of this framework is the differentiable
auditory model, which simulates the perceptual process-
ing of both normal-hearing and hearing-impaired listeners.
This model is a simplified and differentiable version of the
Computational Auditory Signal Processing and Perception
(CASP) model [8]. To reduce computational complexity, base-
line model removed the outer ear stage, uses a static log-
compression instead of dynamic adaptation loops, and removes
the modulation filterbank. Despite these simplifications, the
model retains the essential elements of auditory processing,
such as the dual-resonance non-linear (DRNL) filterbank and
inner hair cell transduction, and has been shown to effectively
predict behavioral outcomes in psychoacoustic tasks. Hearing
loss is modeled by applying frequency-specific gain reductions
derived from audiograms to the outer and inner hair cell stages.
While the baseline provides a strong starting point, its
auditory front-end is based on relatively simplified transfor-
mations. By mimicking key properties of the human auditory
system, such as modulation filtering, and frequency-dependent
adaptation, we aim to enable the model to learn richer and
more noise-robust internal representations.

The remainder of this chapter presents the three specific
architectural interventions investigated in this work: (i) a
baseline temporal smoothing module using frame-averaging
integration, (ii) a bio-inspired dual time-constant adaptation
mechanism to replace the static compression stage, and (iii)
a complementary approach combining modulation-domain fil-
tering with a series of frequency-selective loss designs.
A. Frame-Averaging Integration
The inclusion of a temporal integration module was not
solely intended for baseline comparison. Instead, it was driven
by two hypotheses rooted in both auditory perception and
model learning dynamics. Specifically, we applied temporal
smoothing to test whether it could enhance the model’s
robustness and better align its input representations with the
perceptual characteristics of hearing-impaired listeners.
First, we hypothesized that smoothing the temporal features
would encourage the model to ignore perceptually irrelevant,
detailed fluctuations. The raw output from the auditory front-
end often contains rapid variations, which may not be critical
for speech perception but could act as noise during training.
By applying controlled averaging across frames, the model
is guided to focus on more stable and perceptually dominant
structures.
Second, temporal smoothing was designed to simulate cer-
tain perceptual limitations experienced by individuals with
sensorineural hearing loss. These listeners typically exhibit
reduced frequency selectivity and a diminished capacity to
resolve rapid temporal changes. Smoothing the envelope was
therefore expected to encourage the network to rely on slower
modulations, which are more accessible to hearing-impaired
users. Thus, this module is not only a signal processing en-
hancement, but also a perceptually grounded modeling choice.

the optimal temporal resolution for input smoothing and to
better understand how envelope versus fine structure informa-
tion affects performance in joint noise reduction and hearing
loss compensation.
B. Bio-inspired Dual Time-Constant Adaptation
One of the main challenges in modeling auditory perception
is to capture how the auditory system adapts to sounds
over time. The baseline system use a static log-compression
function as a simple and efficient way to model this behavior.
However, this type of compression does not reflect the actual
time-varying responses observed in auditory nerve (AN) fibers.
In physiological studies, AN fibers are known to respond
strongly at the beginning of a sound and then quickly reduce
their firing rate to a steady level—a process known as short-
term adaptation. This kind of adaptation helps emphasize
sound onsets, such as the beginning of speech sounds, while
still allowing the system to stay sensitive to continuous input.
To better reflect short-term auditory adaptation, we intro-
duce a bio-inspired Dual Time-Constant adaptation module.
This design is grounded in established auditory models that
simulate the connection between inner hair cells and auditory
nerve fibers. In particular, the model by [30] introduced
an exponential adaptation mechanism with two distinct time
constants, 2 ms and 60 ms, used to capture both rapid onset
responses and slower decay toward a steady state.
The work in [10] extended this structure by introducing
more detailed modeling of synaptic adaptation, including a
power-law mechanism to capture long-term effects such as for-
ward masking. Although our implementation does not include
this power-law component, we adopt the dual exponential
framework as a biologically motivated alternative to static
compression. This approach allows us to better approximate
the temporal dynamics observed in auditory responses, while
keeping the design simple and efficient for integration into
BSRNN systems.
==PAGE:4==
The Dual Time-Constant adaptation module in our imple-
mentation consists of two main stages:
• Non-linear transduction: The input signal x first passes
through a softplus function, defined as
Softplus(x) = ln (1 + ex)
(3)
This operation acts as a smooth approximation to half-
wave rectification and non-linear compression in the inner
hair cells. It ensures a non-negative output, aligning with
the nature of neural firing rates.
• Adaptive filtering with dual exponential decay: The
transformed signal is then convolved with a kernel de-
signed to mimic neural adaptation. The impulse response
of this kernel is composed of two exponentially decaying
components:
h(t) = e−t/τr + e−t/τst
(4)
Here, τr denotes the fast time constant, set to 2.0 ms,
and captures the immediate response to sudden stimuli.
The slower time constant, τst, set to 60.0 ms, accounts
for mid-term adaptation to sustained input.
This adaptive mechanism smooths the input over time,
capturing both short and mid-term changes in the signal.
The resulting representation reflects auditory adaptation and
helps the BSRNN better track meaningful temporal patterns
in speech.
C. Modulation-Domain Filtering and Multi-Task Loss designs
This section presents a key innovation of the thesis: the
complementary design of modulation-domain feature decom-
position and frequency-selective loss functions. Motivated by
the hypothesis that distinct modulation bands encode com-
plementary perceptual cues, we propose a parallel filtering
module that explicitly disentangles the modulation spectrum.
Building on this, we design a series of targeted loss strategies
to improve task-specific learning for both Noise Reduction
(NR) and Hearing Loss Compensation (HLC).
1) Modulation-Domain Feature Decomposition: The base-
line auditory model, after its adaptation stage, provides a
single feature stream per auditory channel. While effective,
this representation combines multiple aspects of the signal’s
modulation spectrum. We hypothesize that explicitly decom-
posing this spectrum is beneficial, as different modulation
frequencies carry distinct perceptual roles:
• Low-frequency modulations (e.g., below 12 Hz) pri-
marily encode the speech envelope, syllabic rhythm, and
prosodic structure. These cues are strongly correlated
with speech intelligibility and are therefore particularly
relevant for HLC.
• High-frequency modulations capture rapid temporal dy-
namics such as consonant bursts, fine phonetic transitions,
and background noise fluctuations. These features are
important for both NR, by distinguishing noise from
speech, and for enhancing speech naturalness and clarity,
particularly in conveying detail and texture in speech.

The filtering is realized using Butterworth Infinite Impulse

identify the optimal cutoff frequency, we tested values in-

isolate the syllabic rhythm more precisely, while higher cutoffs
preserve more phonetic detail, transients, and background
fluctuations features that contribute to both intelligibility and
the perception of naturalness or noise type.
2) Frequency-Selective Multi-Task Loss Design: Building
on this decomposition, we developed a series of loss designs
that supervise specific modulation bands. These extend the
baseline loss function proposed by [2], which defines separate
losses for NR and HLC.

outputs: a denoised signal, ˆyNR, and a compensated signal,
ˆyHLC. The corresponding loss terms are both computed in the
auditory domain:
• Noise Reduction Loss (LNR): This loss aims to minimize

(5)

balance between NR and HLC objectives during training.

A key feature of this framework is the ability to provide con-

the model to be retrained. Once the speech processor Fθ is

Intuitively, this objective function allows the network to
learn to down weight a task’s loss term if its related uncertainty
is high. For example, if the model is very uncertain about its
HLC prediction for a particular sample, it can increase the
value of µHLC , which reduces the contribution of LHLC to
the total loss. At the same time, because the log-uncertainty
terms µNR and µHLC are themselves added to the final
objective, they act as regularizers, preventing the model from
simply learning to be infinitely uncertain about both tasks. This
mechanism allows the model to automatically balance the two
objectives as training progresses.

filters decompose the feature space into distinct modulation
bands, allowing the network to receive targeted feedback on
specific temporal dynamics. In particular, this design enables
the training process to differentiate between slow-varying
envelope cues and fast-changing transient structures, both of
which contribute uniquely to speech intelligibility and quality.

IV. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
This chapter details the experimental setup used to train and
evaluate the proposed architectural enhancements described
in Chapter III. To ensure a fair and direct comparison, the
core components of our setup, including the speech processor
architecture, datasets, and primary training procedure, are
consistent with the baseline framework established by [2]. All
experiments were conducted using a sampling rate of 16 kHz.
A. Datasets and Scene Generation
To ensure robust and generalizable models, training was
performed on a large and diverse set of dynamically generated
acoustic scenes, following the procedure described in.
• Training Data: Clean speech utterances were drawn
from a comprehensive collection of publicly available
corpora, including DNS5 [12], LibriSpeech [13], MLS
[14], VCTK [15], and EARS [16], providing a total
of 1713 hours of speech data. The noise sources were
selected from DNS5 [12], WHAM! [17], FSD50K [18],
and FMA [19], amounting to 541 hours of noise data.
• Scene Simulation: During training, scenes were gen-
erated on-the-fly. Each scene was created by placing
one speech source and up to three noise sources within
a simulated room. Room dimensions were randomly
selected from a range of [3x3x2.5] to [10x10x4] cubic
meters. Room Impulse Responses (RIRs) were simulated
as in [20], with the reverberation time (T60) randomly
chosen between 0.1 and 0.7 seconds. The Signal-to-Noise
Ratio (SNR) for each noise source was randomly selected
between -10 and 20 dB.
• Test Data: For evaluation, a fixed set of 1000 scenes was
used. These scenes were generated with speech utterances
from the Clarity dataset [21], noise segments from the
TUT database [22], and RIRs from the DNS5 dataset,

ensuring a consistent and reproducible evaluation across
all models.
• Audiograms:The hearing loss profiles, represented by
audiograms, are a critical input to the system. To expose
the model to a wide variety of hearing loss configurations
during training, an audiogram, a, was generated for each
scene by first randomly selecting one of the standard
audiograms from [23]. To increase diversity, a random
jitter within the range of [-10, 10] dB was then applied to
each threshold of the selected audiogram, after which the
thresholds were clipped to a realistic range of [0, 105] dB
HL. Conversely, during testing, each scene was processed
for every one of the standard audiograms from [23]
without any jitter to ensure a consistent and comparable
evaluation.
B. Model Architecture and Training Parameters
• Speech Processor: The core speech processor for all
experiments is the Band-Split Recurrent Neural Network
(BSRNN) [9] architecture described in. The Short-Time
Fourier Transform (STFT) uses a frame length of 32
ms and a hop size of 16 ms with a Hann window. The
network architecture consists of N=64 channels and L=6
recurrent layers.
• Differentiable Auditory Model: This is the primary
component modified in our work. The baseline auditory
model is a simplified, differentiable version of the CASP
model [8], which includes a DRNL filterbank, an inner
hair cell (IHC) stage with half-wave rectification, and an
instantaneous log-compression adaptation stage. Our pro-
posed model variants, as detailed in Chapter III, replace
or supplement specific stages of this baseline model. For
instance, the dual time-constant model replaces the log-
compression stage, while the frame-averaging and mod-
ulation filtering modules are inserted after the adaptation
stage. All other aspects of the auditory model remain
consistent with the baseline.
• Training
Procedure: Models were trained for 200
epochs, where one epoch is defined as 10,000 scenes.
We use the Adam optimizer [24] with an initial learning
rate of 1e−3, which is reduced by a factor of 0.99 after
each epoch. Gradients are clipped with a maximum L2
norm of 5.
C. Evaluation Metrics
To provide a comprehensive assessment of model perfor-
mance, a suite of objective metrics was employed, consistent
with. These metrics were chosen to evaluate different aspects
of the processed signals, from basic signal fidelity to more
complex perceptual attributes relevant to hearing aid users.
• Hearing Loss Compensation Performance:
– HASPI (Hearing-Aid Speech Perception Index) [25]:
This intelligibility metric is specifically designed for
hearing-impaired listeners, comparing the temporal
envelope of the processed signal with that of the
clean reference through an auditory model. Scores
range from 0 to 1.
– HASQI (Hearing-Aid Speech Quality Index) [26]:
This metric predicts speech quality for hearing-
impaired listeners, combining features related to
signal distortion and noise reduction into a single
quality score from 0 to 1.
• Noise Reduction Performance:
– PESQ (Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality)
[27]: This metric predicts the subjective quality of
the speech signal, with scores typically ranging from
-0.5 to 4.5.
– ESTOI (Extended Short-Time Objective Intelligibil-
ity) [28]: This metric predicts speech intelligibility,
with scores ranging from 0 to 1, and is known to
correlate well with human listener performance in
noisy conditions.
– SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio): We also report the
standard segmental Signal-to-Noise Ratio to measure
the level of noise reduction.

differentiate between desirable, slow-moving speech envelopes
and undesirable smearing of crucial, fast-moving speech cues.
The auditory system’s own temporal processing is highly
dynamic and adaptive, a quality this simple module cannot
replicate.
B. Dual Time-Constant Adaptation
To further explore biologically motivated pre-processing,
we implemented a dual time-constant adaptation mechanism
inspired by the exponential adaptation dynamics observed
in human auditory nerve models. This design incorporates
both short-term and long-term integration to capture rapid
fluctuations and slower trends in the input signal, mimicking
the behavior of outer hair cell adaptation loops. The primary
objective of this experiment was to determine whether replac-
ing the static log-compression stage of the baseline model with
a more bio-inspired, dynamic adaptation mechanism would
yield a significant performance improvement.

The BSRNN architecture used in the baseline model was

representation. The long impulse response of the double-
exponential kernel introduces significant temporal dependen-

hundreds of milliseconds. From a machine learning perspec-
tive, the BSRNN may have failed to adapt to the new long-
memory features, treating their temporal correlations as noise
and thus degrading performance.
Therefore, the failure of this module strongly suggests
that simply inserting a more complex, biologically motivated
component is not a guaranteed path to success. It highlights
that a successful enhancement likely requires a complemen-
tary co-design of the feature extraction process, the network
architecture, and the learning objective itself.
C. Modulation-domain supplementary loss
This section details the results of our core experimental
investigation, which was designed to explore the complemen-
tary effects between the proposed modulation-domain filtering
module and a series of multi-task loss designs. The central
innovation of this approach is that the low-pass (speech
envelope) and high-pass (fine structure) signals produced by
the filtering module are not merely used as enhanced features.
Instead, they form the basis for four new, independent loss
terms: one for each signal component (low-pass, high-pass)
within each of the two main tasks (NR, HLC). Each of these
new loss terms is governed by its own learnable uncertainty
parameter.
The investigation was therefore conducted in two sequential
stages. First, we performed an ablation study to determine the
optimal decomposition boundary (i.e., the cutoff frequency)
for the filtering module. Second, using this optimal frequency,
we conducted a comprehensive evaluation of the different loss
function strategies, which leverage these new decomposed
loss terms in various combinations, to identify the most
effective training objective. All models in this section were
trained by incorporating the filtering module into the baseline
architecture.

at representative alpha values.

When α is set to 0.0, the system operates purely in a hearing
loss compensation capacity. The primary goal is to improve
speech perception for an impaired listener without explicit
noise reduction. Therefore, our analysis in this mode focuses
on the two most relevant perceptual metrics for this task: the
Hearing-Aid Speech Quality Index (HASQI) and the Hearing-
Aid Speech Perception Index (HASPI). The results for all
loss function strategies across the different hearing profiles
are presented in Figure 5 (HASQI) and Figure 6 (HASPI).
A main observation from these results is that none of the
proposed complex loss strategies overall outperform the origi-
nal baseline across all hearing profiles. As seen in both figures,
the baseline model (black line) remains highly competitive.
However, a deeper analysis shows that low-pass and high-pass
modulations affect each profile differently.
Focussing on the HASQI results in Figure 5, we observe a
subtle but consistent advantage for strategies C2 (LNR+LHLC
+ Lhp,HLC supervision) and C7 (LHLC + Llp,NR + Lhp,NR)
on the milder, high-frequency hearing loss profiles (N1, N2,
N3) and the flatter profiles (S1, S2). The high-pass component
of the modulation spectrum contains fine temporal details that
contribute to the perceived naturalness of speech. For listeners
with milder impairments who can still process these details,
the explicit supervision on this component provided by the
C2 and C7 strategies appears to yield a small but measurable
improvement in perceived quality.
Conversely, an analysis of the HASPI results in Figure 6
tells a different story, especially for the more severe hearing
loss profiles. For profiles N5 and N7, the baseline itself
struggles, and the C2 and C7 strategies perform even more
poorly. In these challenging conditions, it is the strategies that
explicitly supervise the low-pass component (e.g., C1(LNR +
LHLC + Llp,HLC), C3(LNR + LHLC + Llp,HLC + Lhp,HLC),
and C6(LNR + Llp,HLC + Lhp,HLC)) that show a relative
advantage, achieving HASPI scores 0.1-0.2 higher than the
baseline. This aligns with the principle that for listeners with
severe hearing loss, the slow-varying temporal envelope (the
low-pass component) is the single most important cue for
speech intelligibility. In these cases, forcing the model to focus
on correctly restoring this robust envelope information, as
strategies C1, C3, and C6 do, is more critical for intelligibility
than preserving fine structure, which these listeners may no
longer be able to perceive anyway.
The optimal loss strategy in the HLC-only mode is highly
dependent on both the listener’s hearing loss profile and the
desired perceptual outcome (quality vs. intelligibility). Our re-
sults suggest that for milder hearing loss, a focus on preserving
the high-frequency modulation details can enhance perceived
quality (HASQI). However, for more severe hearing loss, the
priority must shift to robustly restoring the low-frequency
speech envelope to maximize intelligibility (HASPI).

in a pure noise reduction (NR-only) mode. The HLC signal
path is effectively disabled, and the model’s sole objective is
to estimate the clean speech signal from the noisy input. The
performance in this mode is therefore best evaluated using
traditional speech enhancement metrics that measure signal
fidelity and objective quality, namely PESQ, ESTOI, and SNR.

encourage the model to remove noise too aggressively across
the board. However, the defining feature of the C7 strategy, is
that it replaces this single, holistic objective with two separate
loss terms: one for the low-pass speech envelope (Llp,NR) and
one for the high-pass fine structure (Lhp,NR). This change
allows the model to learn a more balanced and intelligent
noise reduction approach. It can be more gentle when cleaning
the crucial low-pass envelope, focusing on preserving its
shape to avoid distortion, while potentially applying stronger
noise reduction to the high-pass component where noise is
often more prominent. This more differentiated strategy likely
results in fewer artifacts, leading to a better PESQ score. The
effect appears to be most critical for the severe N7 hearing
profile, where preserving the fundamental structure of speech
is important.

VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
A. Conculsion
This thesis investigated several architectural and method-
ological enhancements to a baseline framework for joint
noise reduction (NR) and hearing loss compensation (HLC).
Building on the observation that the baseline system relies
on a simplified auditory front-end, we explored three dis-
tinct avenues for improvement: a simple temporal integration
Fig. 14.
Averaged SNR scores across all hearing profiles for baseline and
each training strategy (C1–C7) at α=0.6 (Hybrid).
module, a bio-inspired dynamic adaptation mechanism, and
a complementary approach combining feature decomposition
with advanced, frequency-selective loss functions.
Through a range of experiments, we uncovered insights into
the challenges and trade-offs inherent in jointly optimizing
NR and HLC. The frame-averaging module, failed to improve
upon the baseline, with aggressive smoothing shown to be
detrimental to performance. Similarly, replacing the static
log-compression with a more bio-inspired dual time-constant
adaptation module also resulted in a performance degradation.
Therefore, modifying the auditory front-end in isolation is
ineffective; any such enhancement must also account for how
the speech processor (BSRNN) will interpret these new and
unfamiliar features.
This led to our core investigation into the complementary
design of feature decomposition and targeted loss functions.
The results from this final, extensive set of experiments repre-
sent the primary contribution of this work. We found that by
decomposing the auditory signal into low-pass and high-pass
components, we could influence the model’s behavior under
some conditions. However, our analysis showed there was
no single ”best” loss strategy, but rather a trade-off between
competing perceptual goals.
B. Future work
This work relied exclusively on algorithmic metrics (e.g.,
HASPI, HASQI, ESTOI, PESQ, SNR) to evaluate intelli-
gibility and quality. Although these metrics offer practical
approximations, they do not fully capture how real users
perceive the output. Future work should therefore include
perceptual listening tests with hearing-impaired participants to
validate the actual benefits of different enhancement strategies.
While some loss configurations yielded comparable results
to the baseline in a few cases, these instances were limited
==PAGE:14==
and not consistent across evaluation metrics or hearing loss
profiles. Future work could explore personalized optimization
strategies that adapt to individual listener profiles, aiming to
improve perceptual relevance and effectiveness.
