Abstract
In this thesis it is examined in which order the welding residual stress influence on the de-
sign of supersize monopiles. This is done both experimentally and by use of finite element
modelling.
The experiment is performed on a 500 mm x 500 mm x 40 mm welded steel plate of grade
355 with quality ML. The welding process is sub arc welding and the method used in the
experiment is the contour method. By the use of the contour method it is possible to obtain
the residual stress all the way through the welded plate.
The finite element modelling is performed as a 3D model in Abaqus, which is created to
be as realistic as possible and this type of finite element software also make it possible to
involve the sequential coupled approach. This means that the model is created as at cou-
pled model, which takes the temperature dependent material properties and the welding
process into consideration, which is used to compute the stress distribution. This model is
adjusting to the results found experimentally.
In this thesis the maximum welding residual stress is found at a level of 612 MPa, which
is above the tensile and yield strength for the base material. Furthermore, it is concluded
that the contour method is a good destructive method, since more cuts can be performed
in one specimen. The welding residual stress is also investigated by numerical modelling.
Here it is found that the numerically predicted stress level exceeds the tensile and yield
strength for the base material, and furthermore is higher at some point than the stress
determined by experiment. Finally it is shown that the peak temperatures are quite close
to the experimental result with just approximately 6 % of deviation. But the heat output
is still an open chapter since there is a significant deviation from the experimental result
to the numerical model. Since the maximum tensile stress for both the experiment and
numerical model exceed the yield and tensile strength, it may be necessary to make a stress
relieve in order to improve the fatigue life of the welded structures.

Resumé
I dettespeciale undersøges residualspændingerfra svejsning og hvilketomfang de vil påvirke
designet af ekstra store monopæle. Dette gøres både eksperimentelt og ved brug af finite
element modellering.
Forsøget er udført på en 500 mm x 500 mm x 40 mm svejst stålplade af klasse 355 med
kvalitet ML. Svejseproceduren er pulversvejsning, og metoden anvendt i eksperimentet er
konturmetoden. Ved brug af konturmetoden er det muligt at måle residualspændinger
igennem den svejste plade.
Finite element modelleringen er udført som en 3D-model i Abaqus, som er skabt så realis-
tisk som muligt. Denne type af finite element-software gør det også muligt at involvere den
sekventielle koblede tilgang, hvilket betyder, at modellen er oprettet som en koblet model,
der tager hensyn til temperaturafhængige materialegenskaber og til svejseprocessen, som
bruges til at beregne spændingsfordelingen. Denne model er justeret i forhold til resul-
taterne fundet eksperimentelt.
Det er fundet i dette speciale, at den maksimale svejsespænding ligger på 612 MPa, hvilket
er over træk- og udbyttestyrken for basismaterialet. Endvidere er det konkluderet, at kon-
turmetoden er en god destruktiv metode, da flere udskæringer kan udføres af prøven.
Derefter konstateres det, at spændingerne for den numeriske model i nogen områder er
højere end de eksperimentelte fundne spændinger og ligeledes overstiger trækstyrken og
fyldespændingen for basismaterialet. Endelig er det vist, at temperaturtoppene ligger ret
tæt på eksperimentet med cirka 6 % af afvigelse. Varmeeffekten er stadig et åbent kapi-
tel, da der er en signifikant afvigelse i forhold til varmeffekten fundet eksperimentelte og
numerisk. Da spændingerne for både eksperimentet og den numeriske model overstiger
udbyttet og trækstyrken, kan det være nødvendigt at lave en spændings aflastning for at
forbedre de svejsede strukturers træthedsliv.

Introduction
Wind energy’s contribution to the total amount of produced energy has increased signifi-
cantly over the years. One of the main sources of wind energy is offshore wind farms. The
offshore wind industry is constantly growing due to the increasing demands from the re-
newable electricity generation targets [1, 2]. In the offshore wind turbine industry different
types of constructions substructures are available, which are illustrated in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Different types of offshore wind turbine substructures (foundation) [3]
The offshore foundation which is most successful and widely used is the monopile so-
lution and approximatly 70% of the offshore wind farms worldwide are installed using
the monopile solution [4]. The monopile structure is a relatively simple design consisting
of a cylindrical steel tube. The wind turbine tower is usually connected to the monopile
with the use of a transition piece, however in some cases the tower and monopile are di-
rectly connected. The monopile is embedded into the seabed as illustrated in Figure 1.1.
An advantages of using monopiles is that the penetration depth is adjustable to suit the
seabed and environmental conditions [5]. Another important factor to consider in re-
lation to monopile foundations is that the size has increased during the years, and it is
==PAGE:20==

expected that the size keeps increasing. The development of the pile size is shown in Fig-
ure 1.2. Monopiles is a steel structure, which is composed by steel plates welded together
[6]. Monopiles are exposed to cyclic loads which results of fatigue of the structure. One
main reason for fatigue concern is due to welding residual stresses introduced by the weld-
ing process [7]. However, due to the complexity of the welding process and the structural
response, the distribution and magnitude of residual stresses are unknown in large struc-
tures such as monopiles. Hence, if the distribution and magnitude of the welding residual
stresses can be documented to be lower than the yield stress magnitude, this provide the
opportunity to optimize the design [8].
Figure 1.2: The development of the monopile size [9]
Experiments have been performed in order to investigate the welding residual stresses in
offshore wind turbine substructures where a wide number of assumptions have been made.
Additionally numerical modelling is a well implemented tool used to investigate the weld-
ing process of this problem. Most literature and previous studies consider smaller spec-
imens or else the experimental investigation is performed using hole drilling or neutron
diffraction. This thesis considers the contour method which is a destructive method, which
use is limited.
==PAGE:21==
1.1 Objective
3
1.1
Objective
The objective of the thesis is to investigate welding residual stresses in a welded plate with
the thickness of 40 mm with use of the contour method. The overall objective is formulated
as follows:
• Investigate andinterpretationof literatureandthe performanceofthecontourmethod
• Determine weldingresidual stresses of a specified welded joint in a super-size monopile
foundation experimentally with use of the contour method
• Demonstrate the welding residual stresses from the experiment with use of compu-
tational welding mechanics
• Investigate means for optimization of the manufacturing process of the monopile by
reducing the level of welding residual stresses
1.2
Scope of Work
Due to the lacking knowledge of the residual stress in welded joints, which are exposed to
cyclic loads, the main purpose of this project is to investigate the residual stresses in welded
steel plates with a thickness of 40 mm. The investigation of the residual stress will be per-
formed by an experiment with the use of the contour method. This is done to access how
the welding residual stresses will be presented in supersize monopiles. Since the residual
stress is caused by the welding process and fatigue, the influence of these factors on the
residual stresses will initially be described together with the definition of residual stress.
The problem will be examined by using finite element modelling, where a numerical model

Fatigue
Fatigue is important in relation to welds and residual stresses, especially for constructions
located in open water, where wind and wave loads are dominating. Fatigue is one of the
main causes of damage and therefore a main concern in design of offshore steel construc-
tions.
In this chapter the fundamental fatigue mechanisms is described in relation to offshore
wind substructures. First a definition of fatigue in general will be described along with
the fatigue cracking modes and how the governing factors affects the fatigue crack and
propagation. Then fatigue will be described in relation to welds, with focus on the influence
from residual stresses caused by the welding process.
2.1
Deﬁnition of Fatigue
Fatigue often occur due to cracks and is a progressive damage. The damage normally ap-
pears in the exposed areas of the structure, such as near the weld, near the joints or con-
struction details from machining. These areas are in general stronger than the surrounding
structure, but are also more exposed to damage and is therefore also more exposed to fa-
tigue. Fatigue occur as cracks in structures and can therefore be defined as a failure mode,
which occur when structures are exposed to cyclic load conditions repeatedly this results
in change in the stress condition and leads to growth of cracks in the structure. The failure
of the structure occurs when the size of the cracks becomes so large, that the cross sectional
area is reduced to an extent where it fails to carry the exposed load [7].
There are three types of crack modes in relation to fatigue, the modes can either be tension
mode or shear mode, the three modes are as following [7]:
• Mode 1 is the opening/tension mode
• Mode 2 and 3 are shear modes
In cases of cyclic loaded structures the fatigue mode would typically occur as the tensile
mode, mode 1, because the cyclic appearance by tensile load will lead to crack propaga-
tion [7]. This means structures like monopiles mostly fail where the tensile stresses are
dominating or near welds[6]. In Appendix A.1 the modes are described in more details.

Fatigue in Relation to Welded Joints
Fatigue in relation to welded joints behaves differently compared to unwelded steel mem-

structures. This is a result of the effect of residual stresses on the stress range, weld defects
and stress concentrations due to geometric considerations of the weld, which all is present
in welded structures [12]. Even though all three parameters have a great influence on the
fatigue life, it is the effect of the residual stresses which is interesting for this project.

The Welding Process
The heat generated by the welding process is considered as one of the most dominating
loads influencing the structure. The understanding of the welding process according to
the welding procedure specifications (WPS), is a crucial condition in order to establish a
numerical welding model. This chapter will describe the WPS, welding processes, heat
properties and weld geometry.
3.1
Welding Procedure Speciﬁcation
To ensure that the weld quality and the requirements of the weld are fulfilled, the joint
specific Welding Procedure Specification (WPS) should be followed during fabrication of
the joint. The WPS is a document with guidelines about how the weld has to be produced.
The WPS layout varies, some are defined either brief or long and detailed. If the joint is
complex and critical to the intergrity of the structure the procedure specifications should
be more detailed [14]. The specifications in the WPS is detailed information of the welding
process, filler material, joint design, granular flux powder, base material and welding pa-
rameters. Examples of these are the welding speed, wire feeding and voltage. The WPS are
specific for each welding process in combination with the specific joint and material used
and the requirements are therefore also individual. An example of these specifications can
be seen in Appendix B.1.
The WPS is based on experiments and is used to ensure a sensible weld with good me-
chanical properties. The qualification process is often performed in form of mechanical
testing of the preliminary weld. Furthermore, the weld is also examinted visually and by
non-destructed testing. If all tests shows satisfying results and there is no influential de-
fects at the weld, the prelimninary welding procedure remain the qualified WPS [13]. If
significant changes in the WPS qualification variables occur, it may affect the properties of
the welded joint, and it is required to make a requalification [14].

Welding processes
The joined processing of the weld is called fusion welding. Fusion welding is a process
where two or more base materials are melted together. Since the materials have to reach at
least the melting point, the welding process requires temperatures of minimum 1500 ◦C.
Fusion welding has three main types: Gas welding, Arc welding and High-energy beam
welding. These three types of welding all contain different types of welding processes, with
different properties [15]. Presented in this work is arc welding in form of submerged arc
welding (SAW).
In a welding process the power density and the penetration depth have a great influence
on the welds visibility. Arc welding is a surface welding process, which means the power
density and penetration depth is limited to the region near the surface. Some different

3.2.1
Submerged Arc Welding
Submerged arc welding(SAW) is an arc welding process, which means the heat source is
produced by an electrical arc, which is used to melt and join metals. An electrical arc is
made of a high amount of current, which is maintained by a column with thermally ionized
gas called a plasma [14]. The total output in arc welding is produced from amperage and
voltage in the arc in situations of direct current [17].
The arc welding process use an electrode wire, which has three different polarities which
can be applied through the welding. The polarities are; (a) Direct-Current Electrode Neg-
ative, (b) Direct-Current Electrode Positive and (c) Alternating Current. Each polarity has
different affect on the penetration and weld geometry. The most common polarty used is
the Direct-Current Electrode Positive[15]. The different polarities are shown in Figure 3.2.

Theory
The contour method is a relaxation method, which can be used to determine residual
stresses by measuring the deformations on the material surface. This method is based
on Bueckner’s superposition principle, which assumes the material behaves elastic during
the relaxation of residual stress and the removal process of the material does not induce
stresses of sufficient magnitude to affect the measured displacements [30, 31]. The super-
position principle is illustrated in Figure 5.1.
(a) Step A
(b) Step B
(c) Step C
Figure 5.1: Superposition principle for calculation of the residual stress by using the con-
tour method [30]
The superposition principle is shown in Figure 5.1 and is divided in three parts. The first
part A, illustrates the material in the original shape and with the original residual stresses,
which are the ones to be determined. Part B is one side of the material after the cut, and
here the surface is relaxed, and the displacements can be measured on the surface. The
reason for the deformation of the surface is because of the residual stresses released during

the cut. Finally part C is where the material surface is forced back to the original shape
and the original residual stresses is troughout the part as [30, 31]:
σA(x, y, z) = σB(x, y, z) + σC(x, y, z)
(5.1)
The stresses after the cut are unknown, but according to the superposition principle the
stresses normal to the relaxed surface in B, has to be zero, since the surface is free. This
means the stresses in C along the plan of the cut is correct. Taken this into account Equa-
tion 5.1 is given as [30]:
σA(x, y, z) = σC(x, y, z)
(5.2)
Furthermore the original σx and τxy residual stress distribution on the cut plan can be de-
termined using the principle. This makes it possible to specify the boundary condition of
the body: on the cut plan the displacements are specified and the rest of the boundaries
are stress free. The forces through the body are zero as well. [31]
5.1.1
Assumptions and Approximations
There are some assumptions and approximations in order to use the superposition prin-
ciple. The assumption that the relaxation of residual stresses occurs elastically and the cut
does not induce stresses which is common for the relaxation methods. But in relation to
this assumption, the contour method have one unfamiliar assumption. This is according
to the cut of the body, which will deform slightly based on the stresses, which is released
and therefore there is an uncertainty on the assumption even though the cut is perfectly
straight. Experiments and finite element simulations have been conducted to document
that the deformations on the surface has no significant effect, which makes the assumption
about a flat cut valid for the contour method [31].
Another approximation for the contour method is about the measurement of the stresses.
It is only the normal stresses measured used this method, which means the shear stresses
are not taken into account. This influences the displacements, which means it is only
the surface in x-directions there are going back to the original form and the ones in the
y-direction are unconstrained. If the approximation has to be exact, the shear residual
stresses have to be zero along the plane of the cut. The deformations as a result from the
released residual stress can be assessed when the surface tractions on the cut plane are
considered [31]. This principle are shown in Figure 5.2.

5.2
The Cut
Cutting is the first step of the contour method, which makes it of great influence of the rest
of the process and on the results. But in order to use the contour method some assumptions
and approximation is necessary to be considered as mentioned in the section above. Along
with this, there are also some more specific assumptions in relation to the cut of the surface,
which treats the cutting errors. There are two forms of errors in relation to cutting, anti-
symmetric errors and symmetric errors. Anti-symmetric errors are typical averaged away,
which is done by taking the average of the two cut surfaces. The symmetric errors are
not possible to average away, but these are the ones there has to be considered doing the
cutting. The anti-symmetric and symmetric errors are described in the following sections.
5.2.1

Anti-symmetric errors are often examined when shear stresses are considered. The cut
along the plane when both the normal and shear stresses are present makes the to halves
with a different contour. In the case related to the one described above the surface traction
are as in Equation 5.3. Since there are shear stresses is represented, which has an effect on
the contour, the errors can be averaged away [32]. The main cause of anti-symmetrical er-
rors is when the cut is not straight, but by measuring the two surfaces the error is averaging
away in the data process[30], this is shown in Figure 5.3.

When averaging anti-symmetric errors away, an assumption has to be taken into account.
The contour part caused by the normal stresses has to be symmetrical, which has a require-
ment to the stiffness in the two sides of the cut and this stiffness has to be the same. When
this assumptions is considered in practice, the contour only has to be symmetrical along

has to be averaged away. Another example can be if the specimen is only clamped on one
side of the cut, the cut path will be straight, but as the stresses relaxes the parts will move.

parts are moving, and the two contours are therefore very different from each other and
can not be averaged away, and it is therefore recommended to clamp the specimen on both
side of the cut. [32]
5.2.2
Symmetric Errors
The symmetrical errors cannot be averaged away but has to be considered when the cut is
made. Since this method is quite new [31, 33, 34], only one method has been successful
when making the cut, and this is the wire Electric Discharge Machining (EDM). Using the
EDM the cutting of the specimen has to be considered. Some condition has to be consid-
ered to avoid symmetrical errors in relation to the EDM. Many of the symmetrical errors
are straightforward. This could be: local cutting irregularities, the wire breaks or some
unfamiliar particles overburns. Another error could occur if the width of the cut changes
or if the cut is bowed. Errors may appear if stresses are induced by the cutting. These sym-
metrical errors are handled either by the settings of the EDM or by data smoothing. Some
of the assumptions above are less accurate near the edges of the cut, this are primarily in
proportion to the cut width. Using the EDM for the cutting some uncertainty near the
edges may occur, which will influence on the contour results and should therefore not be
considered in the data. One of the main reason to use the EDM in relation to the contour
method, is that the contour cut cannot recreate a cut in the previously cut surface. When
cutting the specimen in the region where the stresses are in compression, the surface may
have to be recut, since the surface cut might pinch behind the wire [32], as shown in Figure

the original width, but the material removed is reduced in relation to the measurement of
the original state of body. Since the Bueckner’s superposition assumes the original stresses
returns the step C, this assumption also assumes that the material points return to their

about the cut, but both approximations cause almost similar effect. The effect also depends
on the stress state of the cut tips as in the ”bulge” error. So if the ”bulge” error is reduced
so is the plasticity error [32].
If the ideal cut should be performed it should have zero width, introduce no stresses and
allow no cut tip plasticity [32].

The Wire Electric Discharge Machine (EDM)
The process of the wire EDM cutting is induced by electrical breakdown between the spec-
imen and the continuously travelling wire electrode and is a non-contact technique. The
wire EDM is using deionised water, which has a low viscosity, a small mechanical deviation
of the cut, high thermal conductivity and provide effective cooling. Normally the cutting
done by the wire EDM is performed in two step, first a roughing cut where the shape of the
cut is made and afterwards a skim cut is used on the surface to produce a higher quality.
This double cutting will cause some problems with the purpose of the contour method.
First when the roughing cut are done on the surface, there will be induce stresses and the
surface might get reshaped. Then second cut the skim cut would remove the surface pro-
file from the first cut and the surface will give no useful measurements. For this reason
the EDM settings has to be change when it is used to perform a cut related to the contour
method. The EDM should therefor only make a single cut, which should be the skim cut.
This will slow down the procedure, because the cutting rate is much reduced using a skim
cut [36].

5.3
Measuring the Surface
The second step in the contour method is measuring of the contour surface. The measure-
ment of the surface has three categories: tactile, optical and electromagnetic. To measure
the contour surface a grid has be be created which makes it possible to measure the contour
of the surface and the deformations. In this project the touch probe method will be used.
The contour method measurements makes the assumption that the material measured is
isotropic and homogeneous [30].
The contour of the surface will in general have a peak-to-valley magnitude on the order of
10 μm to 100 μm. The method used to measure the surfaces height field of this level has
to be accurate. To make the measurements of the contour surface a Coordinate Measuring
Machine (CMM) is used. Some errors may occure as a result of the cut, which can be
averaged away, with this in consideration, both surface contours have to be measured. The
measurement is performed at one side of the specimen at the time. The specimen are
placed in the CMM such as the cut surface are exposed, which means the surface where
the residual stress appear will be measured. Doing the measurement the same location of
the measuring point should be used, when it is possible. If this is possible, the coordinate
direction will be reversed and should be considered when the two sides has to be compared.
Using the CMM it is not possible to get the residual stresses directly, but the deformation
of the contour in the cut surface. Using the CMM the measuring can take several hours,
because a measurement of this type occur at about one-per-second frequency. Because the
measuring is time-consuming the temperature stability is essential and the CMM has to be
isolated from thermal fluctuations [30]. When the measurement are performed it has to
be exact in both transverse locations and the height. The dataset of the measurement are
represented discrete, which is important because the measurements may have been done
in a continuous or analog fashion, this means the dataset will be processed and stored in
series of discrete points [36]. In order to measure the surface contour different techniques
exist. The different techniques and their qualification are shown in Appendix D.1.

6 Numerical Analysis of Welding
The purpose of using Computational Welding Mechanics (CWM)is to create methods and
models which are able to control and design the welding processes in order to obtain suit-
able mechanical performance of the welded structure or component [41].
In this chapter the fundamentals of the numerical analysis are described in relation to weld-
ing. First the concept of the numerical modelling is described, then the coupled problem
and at last the use of finite element method and the software used to model the welding
process.
6.1
Concept of Numerical Modelling
A numerical model is typically used in order to give an approximation of a physical prob-
lem, in this case the welding process. It also provides insight into the physical problem
without any need of experimental work, which typical result in high cost. In order to val-
idate the model, a meticulous description of the physical problem is required along with
proper choices in relation to the mathematical model. The process from where the tech-
nological problem start to the final solution is illustrated in Figure 6.1.

6.3
Finite Element Method
Using a Finite Element Method (FEM) to analysis the welding problem (welding residual
stresses and welding deformations), which simulates the thermal and mechanical material
and components behaviour during the weld, is the understanding of the implementation
needed. To model the problem as real as possible, the following aspects have to be under-
stood [17]:
• A three-dimensionally model should be designed, at least in the area of the weld, in
order to allow different cooling conditions on the surface and in the interior.
• The modelled process is of a high degree transient, with particularly different field
gradients depending on time and position as a cause of the rapid heating-up and
cooling-down events.
• The modelled process is non-linear and temperature dependent in a high degree
regarding to the behaviour of the thermomechanical material.
• The momentary and local behaviour of the material is depending on thermal and
mechanical stress and strain prehistory.
• During welding is the material molten and in some cases add in the component, as
the material solidifies the connectivity of the component changes.
• Changes in state and microstructure should be simulated.
• Cracks and defects, which could occur at critical points, make the continuum con-
cept doubtful.
There are several software packages available to handle the computational welding me-
chanics by numerical solution. In this project a finite element analysis (FEA) program is
used, the Abaqus FEA together with the plug in Abaqus welding interface (AWI).

rotation analyses, such as non-linaer problems with large plastic strains, is it preferred to
use the linear shape function compared to the higher order shape function, since is per-
formed by simple element formulations, which results in more robust numerical model
and analysis [51].

In order to choose full or reduced integration it should be taken into consideration that
reduced integration are less prone to locking effect compared to full integrated simple el-
ements. Another aspect of the reduced integration elements is these may produce zero
energy modes (hourglass) and therefore requires hourglass control [51]. The hourglass
mode also means instability could occur due to the stiffness matrix begin zero. Basically
the reduced integration means the analysis will take less time to run but can effect the ac-
curacy of the element for some problems. In non-linear problems such as plasticity, creep
or incompressible materials it is recommended to use the reduced integration instead of

Selection of Mesh Density
The mesh density makes it possible to model the problem more accurate, but a more dense

analysis also depends on the mesh density, it is of interest to make the best fit of the mesh
density. According to this the mesh density near the weld and HAZ is increase and then
decreased along the distance from the weld. The reason to make the model more accurate
in these regions is that large temperatures and stress gradients are expected.

8.3.3
Calibration
The purpose of calibrating the heat model is to obtain the heat input as accurate as possible.
This can be done in more than one way. A simple way to calibrate the model is to adjust
the chunk length. Thus if the chunk length is increased the heat input is overestimated,
where by decreasing the chunk length the heat input is underestimated. It is therefore
important to calibrate the chunk length to obtain the experimental heat model. Another
factor related to the chunk length is the computational time range, for a model with a lot
of chunks the time range will increase significant, so decreasing the chunk length may be
quite time-consuming. Other opportunities to calibrate the heat model is to adjust the
torch temperature or the torch time.

8.4.2
Addition of Welding Passes
Simulation of the addition of filler material is important when a multi-pass weld has to be
produced. The simulation are performed in several steps, first the geometry of the weld
passes have to be performed, which is done by solid chunks to fill out the weld. Afterwards
is a deactivation or removal procedure of the weld pass geometry is performed, this pro-
cedure is different from the thermal to mechanical model. In the thermal model all the
chunks are removed before the welding procedure is generated and then the chunks are
added one by one in the same direction as the prescribed temperature is applied. When
the chunks are applied, the weld chunks temperature is the same as the solidification tem-
perature at 1500 ◦C. When this process are performed in the mechanical model there are
two approaches of the deactivation or removal procedure of the weld passes, which are the
quiet- and inactive- element approach. These approaches are a part of AWI.
The quiet element approach are known as the pass-level bead removal in AWI. In this re-
moval method the chunks are removed one at the time and not all at once and the applied
in the model shorty after, this approach are shown in Figure 8.3. The removal of the weld
chunks occurs in a short model change step and when it is applied in the model again
is the weld chunk strain free. The weld chunks that have not been through this process
have soft properties in the beginning of the analysis. This results in minimal effect on the
surrounding, since such properties give almost anonymous behaviour.
Figure 8.3: Sketch of quiet element approach [11]
The inactive element approach are known as the model-level bead removal in AWI. This
removal method removes all the weld chunks in the initial stage and then the weld chunks
are applied again in as the torch passes, this is the same approach as used for the thermal
model. This process of the addition filler material are illustrated in Figure 8.4.

Selection of Welding Pass Procedure
Choosing an approach in relation to the mechanical model some pros and cons for both
types should be discussed. In relation to the quite approach is the benefit that the mesh are
updated continuous, which results in the weld chunk elements have dynamic conditions
until they are reinserted strain free. This can also be a disadvantage since the weld chunk
elements still have not been strain free, can affect the surrounding with incorrect strains.
But taking the inactive element approach into account there will not occur a problem with
the surroundings, since the weld chunk elements are placed one at the time. This process
can be a disadvantage in relation to the elements, since they may be severely distorted. This
distortion may cause the negative element volume errors and the solution fails.
Taken all this in to consideration is the chosen approach for this project the quite element
approach, since large strains are expected in the mechanical model.
8.4.3
Modelling of The Weld Pass Geometry
In addition to knowing more precisely how the filler material is placed in the weld pool, the
geometry of the weld passes are essential. To ensure the correct weld pass area are modelled
in the numerical model, the specification in WPS has to be followed, since the weld pass
area indicates the filler material added over time. This are of significant interest when a
multi-pass weld is modelled. In the WPS three parameters are given, which indicates the
area of the weld pass:
• Area of filler material/electrode per pass
• The total weld area
• The disposition area
By knowing all of these parameters it is possible to obtain the weld pass geometry. The
area of the filler material are given in diameter, and if this area are divided by the total weld
area the number of weld passes needed is found. This may seem enough to get the weld
geometry, but the disposition area are needed as well, since the weld speed also have an
influence. Without the disposition rate a large welding speed will cause the weld pass area
to be less than the area of the filler material. But when the disposition rate are known, it
is possible to obtain the correct weld pass area, since the disposition rate are an expression
of the amount of weld material placed by a given size electrode and is proportional to the
wire feed speed.
To obtain a realistic numerical model, the weld shape should be considerate as well. Mod-
elling the shape of the weld pass will be an approximation, since each weld is performed
different. Another approach is to model the weld shape as a weld already perform. But
however the weld shape is modelled, the numerical shape are considered negligible ac-
cording to [25, 41]. To get the most realistic and correct weld, the most important matter
is to add the correct amount of filler material over the correct time range.

FEM Analysis
The final step of the contour method is to determine the residual stresses with the use of
a FE model and the determined displacements. In this case it is the longitudinal residual
stresses, which are examined.
According to the superposition principle explained in section 5.1 the geometry of the
model should be created as the deformed shape, and then the surface should be forced
back and give the residual stress as in step C. But this is not how the model is created, in-
stead is the model geometry created with a flat surface and then the displacements are used
at displacement boundaries, which will give the residual stresses. The approach are used,
since it should give the same results and it is easier to model a straight surface compared
to a deformed one.
The FE model are modelled with the dimensions of one of the halves of the specimen and
then partitions are made to make it possible to apply the mesh with variating density near
the edge, where the boundary conditions is placed, and then the mesh density can be more
coarse along the width. Another important factor is the boundary conditions, which pre-
vent body rotation, these are placed in each corner of the plate where the displacement
boundaries are applied. The created model is shown in Figure 9.14.

Discussion
In this chapter the obtained results will be discussed along with reasonable sources in rela-
tion to the appertaining results. First the experiment will be discussed, followed by numer-
ical model, first the heat source model followed by the stress model, and lastly a comparison
of experiment and numerical model will be made.
11.1
Experimental Results
The results for the experiment is presented in section 10.2.4 and the largest residual stresses
are shown in Table 10.2. Here the largest stress is found in the bottom of the plate, which is
assumed to be an effect of the bottom pass, which is the final pass of the welding. Since the
bottom is applied last the heat and cooling phase at this point, which is the reason for the
stresses to occur, influence the bottom of the plate and the stress at bottom of the plate will
be the largest. The largest stress occur 10 mm from the bottom, where the largest stress over
the middle of the plate occur 28 mm from the bottom, which is 8 mm over the middle. It
is in earlier studies [56, 57] found that the same trend occur, that the maximal stress occur
near the top and bottom of the weld. The reason for a high stress in this area could be a
result of the heat from the closing passes. Furthermore is it known fatigue appear near the
weld toe, and as the largest tensile residual stress is found near the bottom of the weld, it
seem this is the most exposed area. Then a large stress appear at the top area 0 mm from
the top, this stress may not be true, since the displacement boundary conditions applied
in the numerical model at this area is extrapolated with the used of mirror the x, y and z
matrix.
The largest residual stress for 10 mm and 28 mm from the bottom is compared with the
tensile and yield strength for the base material. The results are presented in in Table 10.3,
where the deviation for 10 mm is respectively 22.25 % and 46.07 % where for the 28 mm
the deviation is 8.05 % and 29.10 %. This means the residual stress exceeds both the tensile
and yield strength, where according to the theory the residual stresses should be lower.
But since this is only performed at one cut and at one specimen, it is hard to discuss the
reason the residual stresses occur higher, since it may only be in this case. But one reason
for the maximum tensile residual stress occur higher than the ultimate stress level, may
be as a cause of the cross plates, which was welded underneath the main steel plate. Since
the welded plate will in some order deformed during the welding, the cross plates will
==PAGE:132==
114
Discussion
somehow support the welded plate against this deformation, which will lead to further
tensile stresses will occur in the plate.
Another approach made in section 4.2.2 is that for plates with welds over 457 mm the
longitudinal residual stress is uniformly distributed and it is illustrated in Figure 4.7 that the
magnitude is just around 300 MPa [14]. Comparing this appoach with the experiment, the
magnitude of the residual stress is again exceeded. Another observation made in relation
to the experimental result is that the residual stresses decrease along the z-axis, which was
expected, but already 90 mm in the plate is the maxsimum residual stresses decreased with
almost 80 %. So if a new cut should be performed is it according to Prime [32] stated
”The length of this region is about 1.5 times the Saint Venant’s characteristic distance. The
characteristic distance is often as the part thickness, but is more conservatively taken as the
maximum cross-sectional dimension unless specific information about the stress variation
is known”, which in this case would mean at new cut could be perform 60 mm into the plate,
and at this point is the stress decreased with about 70 %, which is of significant magnitude.
One last observation is made, which is in relation to the stress distribution. The stress dis-
tribution is studied it is shown that the stress distribution near the edge is quite rough along
the distance from the weld as shown in Figure 10.18, but as the distribution is investigated
through the plate, it is found that the distribution becomes more uniform along the z-axis,
which is shown in Figure 10.20. Shape of the stress distribution gets less rough already 10
mm into the plate and actually reach an almost perfect distribution 15 mm into the plate.
The roughness of the distribution seem to be caused by a too rough cut and not by the weld
process. This means the size of the wire used in the wire EDM actually has more influence
of the measuring and then the residual stress and the sizes recommended should be taken
more into account when the cut is performed.
Comparing the extrapolated data with the data which is not extrapolated has to been pre-
sented in the results, but at briefly commandant on the difference is presented here. For
the two models the maximum stress is not at the same place and of same magnitude, for
the extrapolated data is the maximum stresses located 10 mm and 28 mm from the bot-
tom as earlier stated, but for the other case is the maximum stress placed 5 mm and 34
mm from the bottom. This is why no comparison has been made in section 10.2.4, since
the maximum stress is not at the same position, this may be a cause of the displacement
boundaries do not affecting the whole surface when the data is not extrapolated.

is observed it is clear the input parameters in the numerical model need some adjustment,
since large deviations occur, especially around the top bead and at the beads placed after
number four. Because of time restrictions and not enough computer capacity the heat input
is not investigated further, but solely mentioned to emphasize that adjustment is needed
to obtain a more realistic model. Another factor that should be mentioned regarding to
the difference in the heat output, is that it is assumed using the SAW process that all heat
is absorbed in the weld and no heat loss to the surroundings, but as the heat output is this
much high at bead in the weld in the numerical model , it could also be as a result of some
heat loss when the specimen is welded.
11.3
Results from the Mechanical Analysis
The result presented in section 10.3.8 is discussed, along with some reasons for the occu-
rance of the deviations. First the stress distribution will be discussed, and the main obser-
vation is that the distribution do not go towards zero at the edges, which may be a result
of the mechanical boundary conditions. Since these boundary conditions do not include
the precise stiffness of the cross plates applied on the experiment specimen and the small
plates to obtain the outlet from the welding process is not included.
Another study is performed in the mechanical analysis to check the equilibrium of the
stress distribution which shows the equilibrium for almost all cases is under 5 % which
is assumed acceptable, but since the model with 4 chunks is a little above in 10 mm from
the bottom of the plate, the 8 chunk model seem to be a better use in relation to the stress
distribution.
Last is the maximum longitudinal stresses for both the model with 4 and 8 chunks com-
pared with the tensile strength and the yield strength. Here it is shown for both cases the
maximum longitudinal stress is above the determined ultimate stress levels, this may be as
result of the heat source model. Since it is found the heat output for the heat source model
==PAGE:134==

Discussion
also is above the experimental heat output and the mechanical analysis rely on the thermal
analysis, this seem to be one reason for the deviation, which needs further investigation.
11.3.1
Experiment Compared with Numerical Stress Results
Last is the stresses for the numerical model and the experiment compared and discussed.
Since the bottom pass is not included in the numerical model, some deviations may occur
just due to this. Especially in relation to where the largest longitudinal stress is found. The
largest longitudinal stress for the examined areas, was found 10 mm from the bottom in the
experiment and 28 mm from the bottom in the numerical model. The longitudinal stress
in the numerical model was actually larger than the largest stress found in the experiment.
But where the largest stress is found at the plate is definitely depending on the bottom pass.
In regards to the bottom pass, is it also shown that the numerical model deviate about 40
% 10 mm from the bottom and only around 17 % 28 mm from the bottom, this is also a
clear sign of the importance of the bottom pass.
Another observation is the stress distribution 28 mm from the bottom is higher for the nu-
merical model than the experiment. This may be due to the plasticity, since it is assumed
in relation to hardening the plastic strain will be less than the ones in the numerical sim-
ulation, which also results in lower stresses. Another reason which is mentioned above
may be this deviation is a result of the thermal analysis, which is not performed accurate
enough in relation to the welding process.
Last is the shape of the two distributions observed, where it is shown 50 mm from the
weld the distribution shape start to deviate. As for the experiment, the shape is almost
as wanted, where the stresses becomes zero at the edges, but this is not the case for the
numerical results, here the stress distribution continues to be lower and do not turn and
goes towards zero. This is also discussed above, since the theory assumes the distribution
to go towards zero at the edges, which is also the instance for the experiment in some order.
The boundary conditions of the numerical model should therefore be investigated further,
to obtain the right distribution for the numerical model.

12
Conclusion
In this thesis the purpose has been to investigate the welding residual stress using the con-
tour method and by numerical modelling. It can be concluded that the welding residual
stress found by use of the contour method is of large magnitude near the bottom, as a cause
of the bottom pass. Another conclusion regarding the experiment is that the welding resid-
ual stresses are about 22 % higher than the tensile strength and about 46 % higher than the
yield strength, this is for the maximum tensile stress near the bottom. The second largest
residual stress is 8 mm above the middle, which also is above the tensile and yield strength
but only by 8 % and 29 %. Furthermore it is concluded that the magnitude of the welding
residual stress also exceeds the theory. It is concluded that the statement concerning Saint
Ventant principle is acceptable [32] , as the magnitude of the residual stresses are decreased
approximately 70 % at the location. Finally it is concluded that this method is of good use
if stresses is needed at any depth, but to conclude whether or not the method is accurate
enough more experiments have to be performed. This thesis shows the welding residual
stress are too high compared with the theory and the results obtained in [54].
Regarding the numerical model can it be concluded that the heat input in the heat source
model has a pivotal influence on the result both for the thermal and mechanical. As a cost
of the heat source model not begin fully similar to the experiment it can be concluded the
results for the stresses are too high compared with the experimental stresses. This can be
concluded as a result of the plasticity and the boundary conditions. Furthermore can it be
concluded that the mechanical boundary conditions do have a significant influence of the
stress distribution, and it is therefore important to know the precise set up and conditions
the plate is exposed to and then with some more knowledge of the Abaqus simulation
should it be possible to obtain the right stress distribution. It is also concluded that the
stresses found in the numerical model also exceed the determined ultimate stress levels
found in [54]. Lastly it can be concluded that a numerical model creates realistic demon-
strations of an issue of this kind, and can be used in the analysis.
Finally an overall conclusion for the thesis is the contour method using the touch probe
CMM is a quite slow and time-consuming method, which also means it is a quite expensive
method in labour force. But the method itself makes it possible to obtain the welding
residual stress in thick specimens, which is needed since the trend is increasing monopile
sizes and hence thicker steel plates. And with the method even though it is a destructive
method it is possible to perform more than one experiment of a specimen, since only a
symmetrical cut is needed around the part where the stress magnitude is of no significant

order. The results in the thesis from both the experimental and numerical part show that
the stress exceeds both the yield and tensile strength, and it is concluded that it is necessary
to relieve the stress to improve the fatigue life in the welded structures.

the material parameters. This would also lead to a more accurate Abaqus model.

A.1
Fatigue Modes
In Figure A.1 is the three fatigue modes shown. The three crack modes are defined as:
Mode 1 is the opening mode, where the crack extend perpendicular to the main principle
stress, this means the tensile force pulls the crack surface apart. Mode 2 and 3 is shearing
modes, mode 2 is in-plane forward and mode 3 is anti-plane. Both modes are where the
cracks are small in the beginning but expeditiously grows and reach the transition to the
tensile mode, mode 1 [7].
Figure A.1: Different fatigue crack opening modes [7].
A.2
Initiation Period and Crack Growth Period
The periods initiation and crack growth is consequence of the cyclic slip. The slip involves
cyclic plastic deformation, this means dislocation activities happens.
In the initiation period encompass crack propagation, where the crack is limited to the
microstructure level. In these period a low stress level occur which means the plastic de-
formations are limited to a small amount of grains in the material. Because of the low
constraint on slip this microplasticity preferably occurs in the grains at the surface in the
material. The plastic deformation in the surface are less limited by the neighbouring grains
compared to subsurface grains, which is a consequence from the low stress level.
==PAGE:154==
136
Fatigue
The crack growth period covers the phase where the cracks no longer is restricted to the
surface but the cracks penetrations in the grain layers. This means the crack growth is not
limited by the surface boundaries but by the grains boundaries in the underlying layers.
The crack growth is typically perpendicular to the loading direction [7]. In Figure A.2 it is
illustraded how the crack grows in the grain layers.
Figure A.2: Cross section of the crack growing in the grain layers [7].
When the structure is exposed to cyclic stresses beyond the initiation period, the crack
propagation areexpressed byfatigue life, which isused to measurethe anticipatelife period.
The fatigue life is typically illustrated using S-N curves, and shows the amount of stress
cycles at a specific stress level before the structure fails. There are four main parameters,
which influence the fatigue strength [12]:
• The stress range, which is the stress difference
• The detail geometry of the structure
• The material characteristics
• The environment
Fatigue life dependence on the stress range, which is decisive on whether or not fatigue
will occur. Beside that the stress range indicates if the stress is in tensile or compression,
and this is in specific interest, specially when the residual stresses has a major influence.

A.3
Global Design
In the design phase of the monopile the global increase of the thickness is an aspect since
the size of the wind tube are growing. The challenge is that the thickness has an influence
the the fatigue strength, which will decrease as the thickness increase. This is relevant since
the reason to this challenge are the residual stresses and how the stress distribution changes
when the plate thickness increases. [58] The change of stress distribution are shown in
Figure A.3.
Figure A.3: Stress distribution in relation to the plate thickness [58]
In Figure A.3 the stress concentration near the weld toe are shown in consideration on how
the geometric effects the stress gradient. The stress distribution depends on the geometrical
size of the weld joint, this results in the stress gradient near a thin joint will appear steeper
than near a thick joint. This means in an instance where the same size of crack appears in
both the thin and thick plate, the thick plate will be exposed to bigger stresses, which results
in the initial crack growth rate will be larger. Considering the fatigue crack propagation lift
is mostly located in the initiation period, the increased stress near the surface will overrule
the long crack propagation distance, this means the fatigue life is reduced in thicker joint
compared to thinner joints [58].
A.4
Weld Geometry
The shape of the weld is a dominating factor on the stress concentration near the weld toe
[28]. In a result of this the non-welded structures does often have fatigue strength which
is higher than welded structures, which means the resistance against crack initiation and
crack propagation are lower in welded joints. Consider the weld as perfect without any
weld defects present, the two main factors are residual stresses and the stress concentration
in the weld toe [13]. In this section the stress concentration in the weld toe will be described
and later on the residual stresses will be described.
In the weld geometry there is some controlling parameters in relation to the size of stress
concentration. Two of the parameters are more dominating, which are the notch root ra-
dius of the weld toe ρw and the angle of the weld toe θ, which defines the welds convexity.
==PAGE:156==
138
Fatigue
These parameters are dominating since they are effecting the degree of rearranged stress-
streamlines [58]. The governing parameters effect the weld are shown in Figure A.4.
Figure A.4: The governing parameters at the weld [58]
To attain as small stress concentration as possible the transition between the plate and weld
material should be smooth. This will appear when the angle at the weld toe approaches to
180◦and the height of the weld reinforcement s goes towards zero. Likewise would the
stress concentration be large if the transition is very conspicuous. It is possible to reduce
potential stress concentration by grinding the weld, which decreases the angle of the weld
toe [7].
A.5
Weld Defects
Welds is the most possible loctation for generation of defects. Weld defecrs mostly oc-
curs because of inferior execution and poor metallurgical knowledge, which results in steel
cracking [10]. In all types of material weld defects are classified in six main groups [28].
1. Lack of fusion
2. Lack of penetration
3. Voids
4. Inclusions
5. Cracks
6. Imperfect shape
The first five groups are internal defects, since they exist in the material and the sixth groups
’Imperfect shape’ are an external defect, which include poor shape, undercutting and over-
lap [28]. Some of the most typical defects in a butt weld are illustrated in Figure A.5.

B.3
Fusion Zones
In the fusion zone the microstructure is a function of composition and solidification con-
ditions. Compostion has a great influence on the microstructure and properties, since a
small difference will result in large variations in the microstructure and properties. The mi-
crostructure can also change dramatically in some systems if the solidification and cooling
rates changes. There are defined three types of fusion zone; autogenous, homogenous, and
heterogeneous. The three types are classified by wether or not there is used filler metal and
the composition of the filler metal in relation to the base meterial [15]
Autogenous: is a welding process, which do not use filler metal, and the base metal is
melted and resolidified and forms the fusion zone. For some materials weldability issues
can make it impossible to perform autogenous welding [15].
Homogenous: is welding where filler metal is used, which matches closely to the base metal
composition. In this fusion zone requirements for the filler and base metal properties has
been set, which is that the properties has to be match closely, this could be in properties
such as heat treatment response or corrosion resistance [15].
Heterogeneous: is welding made with filler metal, where the composition and the base
metal is different from each other. To obtain acceptable properties or service performance
dissimilar filler metals are required, since many base metal compositions may possess poor
==PAGE:161==

weld ability. Dissimilar compositions are required in considerations as: include strength,
weld defect formation (e.g., porosity), weld ability/solidification cracking resistance, heat
treatment response, corrosion resistance, filler metal cost, and operating characteristics of
the consumable [15].
B.4
Weld Sequence
The weld sequence is performed on basis of a specified welding sequence schedule for
welded structures with high-quality, since the weld sequence has a high influence on the
residual stresses and distortion [17]. The quality of the weld must be a least as high quality
of the base material. To ensure the weld has sufficient strenght the top surface must have
a surge, which means the top surface must be higher than the base materiale. The weld
can be perform by a single pass or multi pass. A single weld pass is used for thin plate
and no shaping of the plate is necessary, where for thick plates over 4-5 mm is it crucial to
perform a shaping and the weld are made by a multi pass. The shapping is performed to
actived what is call a full welded welding. A full welded welding is performed for one side
of the plate in such way that a weld bead is formed on the root side of the plate, which is
described as the bottom pass [59]. The two weld sequences are illustrated in Figure B.2.

Residual stresses are divided in three classifications [17]:

• The second and third order residual stresses, σII and σIII, extends over microscopic

The first order residual stresses, are averaged stresses over several crystallites. Second order
residual stresses are averaged within the area of crystallites subregions in a range of 1 mm
- 10 μm. The third order residual stresses are acting in the range of 10 μm - 1nm, which is
the atomic areas [17].

E.3.4
Plasticity
The plastic property of the metal is crucial in relation to the shape of the metal and that
the structure deforms plastic in case of overload, which may relieve the pressure before
the structure breaks. Plastic deformation are defined as deformation spread on the elas-
tic zone. By unloading the structure the elastic deformation are removed but the plastic
deformations remains and becomes the permanent deformations [49].
In materials like steel, the dislocation moves at different values than the applied stress.
Hence the yield strength of the material increase as a result of the loading and unloading
of the steel structure. This is the foundation of strain hardening and give understanding on
the yield strength increase during cold working or in connection of welding. The residual
stress due to non uniform thermal expansion are developed as an effect of this [25].
In non-hardening materials is the stress point which is where the material start to yield
which is always the same for a given temperature. This is call a constant yield locus and the
strains are indeterminate. In such case the material is following a perfect plasticity model.
Only few materials shows this plastic behaviour. Hence the perfect plasticity model is used
conservative, since hardening will occur in reality. As a cost of the material is hardening
the plastic strains will occur less than those forecast by the simulations. Strain or work
hardening result in the material gets stronger as it deforms. In case the material reach the
limit for a tension load in one direction, then a reversed loading may cause lowering of the
yield stress [25].
In cases of a cyclic alternating loading condition such as multipass welding the Bauschinger
effect should be considered. The Bauschinger effect is where the yield stress reduce in one
direction as a result from inelastic loading in the opposite direction [25]. This means if the
