Abstract
3D Reconstruction of Environments from Full Waveform LiDAR Data
This work is a contribution to the processing of full waveform LiDAR data applied to three
dimensional reconstruction of environments.
We are concerned with two speciﬁc stages of processing: Peak Detection and Interpolation. The
former is addressed with Wavelet Theory, and the latter with the concept of Homotopy, and our
main aim is assessing their feasibility as tools for dealing with the mentioned stages.
To make this study viable its scope was restricted, and we focused on pulse-based airborne small
footpring LiDAR systems for Topography, reconstruction based on strongest returns, a small
data set, and the PulseWaves data exchange format.
We conclude that the use of both wavelets and homotopies is useful in full waveform LiDAR
data processing, but some further work is required before implementing a scalable solution to
massive data sets for real-life applications.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Full waveform LiDAR is an active remote sensing system and technique that produces range
measurements between the sensor and its target as well as data to derive reﬂectance and perhaps
geometric properties of the sensed objects, since all returned energy is recorded [24].
According to [15], the ﬁrst full-waveform systems were designed in the 1980s for bathymetric
purposes. For topography, the ﬁrst of such systems (SLICER, LVIS and GLAS [5, 6, 54]) were
developed by NASA about two decades ago. The ﬁrst commercial system (Litemapper [18] based
on the RIEGL LMS-Q560 laser scanner) was developed in 2004. All mentioned systems are
airborne large-footprint systems, except GLAS which is spaceborne, and the RIEGL scanner
which is small-footprint. Many applications have been found in atmospheric studies, glaciology,
land topography, hidrology, metrology, forestry, target or power line detection, corridor and
coastal mapping, among other [6, 54, ?, 24]. Our focus is on airborne small-footprint systems for
topographic applications.
LiDAR Data Processing
From a general standpoint, data processing is the [process] of deriving information about a sensed
object with the purpose of gaining understanding in a qualitative, quantitative, visual or any
other way. Based on our review of the literature, especially [40, 24] and references therein, as
well as our own considerations, we propose the following categorization for the processing of full
waveform LiDAR data for general purposes:
Waveform Analysis This stage consists of determining peak attributes in a waveform with
the purpose of characterizing scatterers. It could be accomplished with several methods
yielding various results [40, 24, 16]: Peak Detection (Thresholding, Leading-Edge Detection,
Constant Fraction Detection, Center-of-Gravity Detection), Decomposition based on Model
Fitting (Levenberg-Marquardt, Expectation Maximization, Reversible Jump Monte Carlo
Markov Chain, Bayesian Inference), and Deconvolution. In our work, we deal with this
stage using Peak Detection based on Wavelet Theory (Ch.2 sec.2.3 and Ch.5).
Waveform Interpolation This stage consists of interpolation of waveforms as functions (of
time) with the objective of obtaining a denser 3D waveform space, and subsequently a
denser 3D point cloud, that allows to produce any proﬁle from the region of interest. The
production of proﬁles is an aim borrowed from 3D Reconstruction techniques in Computer
Graphics and explains the use of the expression “3D reconstruction of environments” in
the title of our work. We observe that increasing the number of 3D points is of interest
for forestry applications [24], however at the same time, it could be a drawback in the

Contribution
This work is a contribution to the processing of full waveform LiDAR data applied to the three
dimensional reconstruction of topographic environments. We assess whether Wavelet Theory
and Homotopy are suitable tools for data peak detection and interpolation, respectively. Thus,
the character of the study is exploratory. However, due to time limitations the results here
obtained are restricted to our focus on pulse-based airborne small-footprint LiDAR systems for
topography, a small data set, the PulseWaves data exchange format, and reconstruction based
on strongest returns.
Organization
The thesis is organized as follows: Chapter 2 gives the necessary theoretical and technical
background for the remainder of this study; Chapter 3 describes the data set used in the
forthcoming data processing stages; Chapter 4 deals with the removal of defective data; Chapter
5 presents our work on wavelet-based peak detection; Chapter 6 presents our work on homotopy-
based waveform interpolation; and Chapter 7 provides some conclusions.

2.1
LiDAR Technology
LiDAR is an active sensing system and technique, whereby a laser beam is used as the sensing
carrier to measure the distance (range) between the sensor and the illuminated area in the region
of interest. LiDAR stands for Light Detection And Ranging.
There are two ranging methods in LiDAR [51]. In the pulse ranging method the system measures
the travel time of a short light-pulse from emission until reception. In this case, range R can be
determined because the travel time t is deﬁned by
t = 2R

where c is the speed of light in vacuum. In the second method, continuous wave ranging, the
system measures the phase diﬀerence between the emitted and received signal, that is continuously
transmitted light. The range can be determined, after resolving the integer ambiguity, because
the phase diﬀerence φ is deﬁned by
φ = 4πf

where f is the frequency of the signal.
This work is focused on pulse-based range measurement and henceforth we will implicitly
assume such method. As explained in [51], the advantage of lasers is that high energy pulses
can be realized in short intervals and the laser’s short wavelength can be highly collimated.
As a consequence, LiDAR systems allow for very long ranges, high range resolution, eﬃcient
suppression of background radiation, and high accuracy.

System Components and Scanning Attributes
A LiDAR system can be carried on a spaceborne or an airborne (including unmanned aircraft
systems) platform. We are concerned with the latter. Such a system consists of a laser transmitter
and a receiver, a mechanical scanner, a position and orientation system (which in turn consists
of a diﬀerential GPS and an inertial measurement unit), a storage medium, and a system for
signal digitization [24].
A LiDAR system must be fully speciﬁed in order to determine costs of data acquisition and
the feasibility of projects that the acquired data are expected to support [13]. The scanning
attributes are a type of speciﬁcations concerning the scanning operation, and next we refer to
a few of them that are of our interest with respect to the PulseWaves data exchange format
(Section 2.2).
An anchor point is a point in real-world coordinates that is the origin of a laser beam emitted
by the LiDAR instrument. The beam axis is the line around which the laser beam propagates
forming a narrow cone. The footprint is the locus that is the intersection of a beam and a plane
positioned perpendicularly to the beam axis at a distance from the instrument equal to the
nominal ﬂight height. These attributes are depicted in Figure 2.1a.

2.2
Data Formats
The acquisition of massive LiDAR data entails the requirement for eﬃcient storage, exchange,
and access of data. For several years now the LAS format has been the de facto data exchange
format among LiDAR communities. Its speciﬁcation is owned by the American Society for
Photogrammetry & Remote Sensing (ASPRS) and is an open format intended to allow LiDAR
hardware and software tools to output data in a common format.
The LAS speciﬁcation has been upgraded for a span of about thirteen years since it was ﬁrst
released in 2003. The current version is LAS 1.4-R13 [3], approved on 14 November 2011 and
last revised on 15 July 2013. The speciﬁcation was developed primarily for exchange of LiDAR
point cloud data and it ﬁrst added support for storing waveform data in version 1.3 [4]. It has
evolved to support the exchange of any three dimensional geo-referenced cartesian tuple.
An alternative LiDAR data exchange format that has been gaining recognition is the PulseWaves
format. It was created as an open-source eﬀort and vendor-neutral format with the purpose of
storing not only discrete returns but geo-referenced laser pulses and those parts of the outgoing
and returning waveforms that were digitized. The speciﬁcation was ﬁrst drafted on 23 December
2011, and its current version is PulseWaves 03-R11 [21] from 8 November 2015, released under
the LGPL-2.1 license [14].

2.3
Wavelets
Extracting information from a signal is a two-stage process. A ﬁrst analytic stage whereby the
signal is mapped to a transformed signal that can be expressed as a combination of functions that
are eﬃciently computable and well-understood. The second stage is synthetic, and it guarantees
that whatever information is extracted in terms of the transformed signal in the ﬁrst stage can
indeed be translated in terms of the original signal.
Mathematically, what is involved is a functional between the function space where the signal
resides and the function space where the transformation resides. Both spaces are generated by
corresponding bases. The two-stage process can thus be seen as a computation of direct and
inverse coeﬃcients, and this is the reason why it is common in practice to refer to the coeﬃcients
as the transform (direct or inverse). Observe that when the signal and the transformation reside
in the same function space there is a single set of coeﬃcients to be computed.
Besides many traditional bases, wavelets have appeared as an alternative that suits well for
studies where time and frequency information is necessary. The underlying motivation behind
wavelet analysis is that time and frequency are in an inverse resolution relation, so that the
higher the resolution is in time the lower it is in frequency and viceversa. This means that, given
a time-frequency space, to extract high-frequency information about a signal one best does it
in the frequency dimension, whereas to extract low-frequency information one is best placed in
the time dimension. Wavelet analysis allows us to extract information in such a time-frequency
space. For convenience, a notion of scale is deﬁned as the inverse of frequency and it is [put
forward] to be in direct relation with time.

Discrete Wavelet Transform
The Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) is the next step to make wavelet analysis computationally
feasible. It is a procedure to calculate wavelet coeﬃcients in an algorithmically eﬃcient manner.
Since a CWT coeﬃcient is a measure of similarity between a wavelet and the signal at a given
scale, it could then be possible to calculate such similarity for speciﬁc frequency bands. In the
DWT, the signal is passed through a series of low-pass ﬁlters and high-pass ﬁlters to analyze
respectively the low and high frequencies of the signal. Figure 2.2 is a graphical representation
of the actual process.
(a)
(b)
Figure 2.2: Discrete Wavelet Transform [27]: (a) multilevel decomposition; (b) ﬁltering and
downsampling.
The process is a multi-level decomposition of the signal [43]. It starts by splitting the signal
spectrum into two equal parts.
The cA1 coeﬃcients contain the low-frequency component
of the signal, while the cD1 coeﬃcients contain the high-frequency component, and they are
called the level one approximation and detail coeﬃcients, respectively. These coeﬃcients are
obtained, as seen in Figure 2.2b, by applying corresponding low-pass and high-pass ﬁlters and
then downsampling by two. In this manner, the frequency resolution of the signal is changed by
the ﬁltering operations, and the scale is changed by the downsampling operation. The procedure
is then repeated on the cA1 coeﬃcients as input, until a level (scale) that depends on the nature
of the signal or suits a given criterion of entropy, but which, in any case, is bound by a maximum
that is determined by the sampling size of the signal. The discrete wavelet transform can be
seen as an eﬃcient procedure for sampling CWT coeﬃcients on a dyadic grid, based on powers
of two for both time and scale.
The synthesis process or reconstruction of the signal is the inverse DWT and it essentially
consists of the reverse operations of analysis, that is, ﬁrst upsampling and then ﬁltering. The
reconstruction ﬁlters are not identical in general, and perfect reconstruction is possible only if the

2.4
Homotopy
Homotopy is a concept of Topology that has been successfully used as a tool in Numerical Analysis
to solve systems of nonlinear equations. In that context, it acquires the name of Homotopy
Continuation [1].
A homotopy formalizes the notion of continuous deformability between functions [2]. Given two
continuous functions f, g : X →Y mapping a topological space X into a topological space Y , we
say that they are homotopic if there exists a continuous function H : X × [0, 1] →Y such that
H(x, 0) = f and H(x, 1) = g for all x ∈X. The mapping H is called a homotopy between the two
functions and the second component in its domain, that we denote by λ, is called the homotopy
parameter. Homotopy is an equivalence relation among the set of all continuous functions from
X to Y .
Figure 2.4 shows a graphical representation of a homotopy between functions f (the blue-color
one on the front) and g (the red-color one on the back). In Figure 2.4a, function f is continuously
deformed as the homotopy parameter λ takes values from 0 to 1, until reaching g. We refer to f
as the initial map and to g as the terminal map, and we say that for each ﬁxed value of λ we
obtain a continuation.

3.1
Data Attributes
Pulses are uniquely tagged by GPS time in seconds-of-week up to microseconds. There are
three types of pulses according to the descriptors in the pulse structure. Whereas all outgoing
pulses have ﬁxed sampling (24 sample units at 1 ns sampling interval) and no segmentation, the
incoming waveforms have a variable structure:
200.002 One low-power incoming waveform with one segment with 60 sample units at 1 ns
sampling interval. There are 2344 such waveforms which amounts to 98.9% of the total of
the data set.
200.003 Two incoming waveforms, one low-power and one high-power, each with one segment
with 60 sample units at 1 ns sampling interval. There are 14 waveforms of this type. In
this case, we used only the high-power waves.
200.004 Two low-power incoming waveforms each with two segments with 60 sample units at 1
ns sampling interval. There are 10 waveforms of this type. In this case, we used only the
ﬁrst wave.
In the PulseWaves format there is no explicit data about the altitude nor speed of ﬂight, thus,
attributes such as pulse density and return density cannot be calculated accurately.
Return intensity is recorded in 8 bits and so its value ranges between 0 and 255. Pulse information
such as edge-of-scan-line seems to be missing; indeed, all pulses have a bit-value equal to zero in
this record, implying that none of the pulses are at the end of a scanning line.

4.2
Dull Waveforms
The removal of dull waveforms is a process that requires the distinction between which waveforms
may carry information and which ones do not. This process is conceptually akin to that of
discrimination of measurements relative to the noise ﬂoor of the system at the level of analog
signal processing. In the case of LiDAR waveforms, signal information is primarily, but not
exclusively, deﬁned by the existence of peaks in the signal.
It should be observed, being rigorous, the subtle diﬀerence between the process of determining
whether there might be peaks in a signal and the process of detecting the peaks of a signal.
The ﬁrst process is concerned with information discrimination whereas the second process is
concerned with information extraction.
Yet, in spite of the previous clariﬁcation both processes could be combined for the sake of
eﬃciency as long as the characteristics of the data so allow it, which is precisely the situation for
LiDAR waveforms. Below, we discuss, via examples, the procedure concerned with information
discrimination and we defer the discussion on peak detection until Chapter 5.

Chapter 5
Peak Detection
As a ﬁrst approximation, a waveform is a sum of variations of its originating laser pulse that are
the responses caused to the pulse by illuminated objects along the beam axis. The number of
responses is related to the number of objects and the variations are due to several factors such as
the laser wavelength, the refraction index of the medium, the pulse incidence angle, roughness
and scattering coeﬃcients of the illuminated objects, and geometric properties of the objects.
For an example in the case of the geometric eﬀect on laser pulses, consider Figure 5.1. On
the left it is shown the widening of the footprint on a steep slope, causing a wider peak in the
returned waveform relative to the width of the pulse, and consequently an uncertainty in range
measurement. Figure 5.1b, on the right, shows the split of the footprint on a discontinuous
surface, causing the generation of two peaks in the returned waveform and consequently an
uncertainty in position of the wall’s edge.
In order to determine characteristics of the region of interest under investigation and reconstruct
it, it is natural to process LiDAR waveforms by matching each pulse to its corresponding returned
wave with the purpose to extract relevant information. As a minimum, for each peak in each
waveform, this information should consist of three peak attributes: location, amplitude and
width (full width at half maximum). In the sequel, we focus on attribute extraction for highest
peaks only (strongest returns; see Chapter 1).
We must warn that at this point, we are referring to the notion pattern matching in a somewhat
broad sense. In particular, it should not be confused with the established technique in Computer
Science with the same name, or the template-matching technique in Digital Image Processing.
Nevertheless, it shares the underlying principle of investigating an object (e.g. a string, an image,
a waveform) for the existence of a predetermined constituent (e.g. a token, a template-image, a
pulse-shape).
Under such broad viewpoint, we allow ourselves to include within the pattern matching approach
virtually every method aimed at attribute extraction of peaks in LiDAR signals, even though
they are not framed in such terms: peak detection, leading edge detection, constant fraction
detection, center of gravity detection, Gaussian decomposition and deconvolution. We refer the
reader to [40] for an overview of these methods and references.
In this chapter, we aim at exploring the use of wavelets for peak detection as a more typical
example of the pattern matching approach. We start in Section 5.1 describing the process of
ﬁnding the peaks on the digitized waveforms as a basis for further discussion. In Section 5.2, we
brieﬂy examine the use of simple Gaussian modeling for peak detection. Finally, Section 5.3 is
dedicated to wavelet-based peak detection.

5.3
Detection using Wavelets
The idea of using wavelets for peak detection is in accordance with the pattern matching approach.
Indeed, it is expected that the coeﬃcients produced by a wavelet analysis of a given signal
provide suﬃcient information regarding attributes of peaks as a consequence of matching the
signal against a chosen wavelet at diﬀerent locations and scales.
The use of wavelets for LiDAR data processing has found many applications, such as noise
reduction [11], image segmentation [52], feature-extraction of LiDAR-derived canopy height
model [10], point-cloud classiﬁcation [45], or vertical object detection [29]. Fewer applications
have been found on peak detection [47, 48, 28].
What follows in the next sections is a description of our eﬀort in using wavelet analysis as an
alternative methodology for extracting peak location in LiDAR signals.
5.3.1
Pulses as Adapted Wavelets
The construction of special wavelets used as patterns for detection of features in signals is not
a novelty [26, 55, 9], however in the context of peak detection in full waveform LiDAR for
topography there are no precursors, to the extent of the author’s knowledge. Indeed, whereas
there exist studies on wavelet-based peak detection of LiDAR signals [47, 48], they have been
conducted using a Gaussian wavelet, which is a “general-purpose” wavelet from the literature.
No satisfactory justiﬁcation is given as for the choice of such a wavelet (cf. Section 5.3.2).
In this section, we use LiDAR pulses as initial forms to construct admissible wavelets and
subsequently perform wavelet analysis on waveforms intended for peak detection. Henceforth,
we will refer to the new wavelets so constructed as pulselets. The construction is made using
MATLAB’s pat2cwav function, which uses least-squares optimization to approximate the pattern.
In Figure 5.4 we illustrate the process of calculating a pulselet and using it in a wavelet analysis
for a speciﬁc case. Figure 5.4a shows waveform 1926 (black) and its generating pulse (blue) that
has been translated to align with the location of the waveform’s peak at sample unit 20. It can
be seen how the return almost replicates the pulse’s features.
Figure 5.4b shows several pulselets that are diﬀerentiable at the boundaries and diﬀer in degrees
of accuracy. All adapted wavelets have integral near zero and l2-norm equal to one. Our choice,
the wavelet in black, has degree 0 and is the best approximation to the pulse.
The result of the CWT is shown in Figure 5.4c. We note that an event is detected in the vicinity
of the waveform’s peak location at sample unit 20. We perform the analysis for all scales up to
twice the number of samples of the pulse in order to gain better information.

5.3.2
Continuous Model
The analysis described in the previous section shows the potential in utilizing adapted wavelets
as a tool for peak detection. From a computational viewpoint we notice that such an approach
demands additional resources in order to compute the pulselets previous to the CWT analysis.
Our implementation was additionally severely constrained by the fact that the MATLAB toolbox
requires that new wavelets are registered in the toolbox previous to the analysis through disk
storage. It is known that disk input-output operations increase the time-complexity of programs
and thus we desisted in our enterprise of performing a full analysis with adapted wavelets.
Thus, we turned into performing wavelet analysis using some well-known wavelets in the literature
which are built-in MATLAB functions in the Wavelet Toolbox and would improve eﬃciency. In
order to choose a wavelet that is adequate to subsequent processing of waveforms, we applied
a wavelet decomposition and peak detection of all waveforms with all possible wavelets in
MATLAB’s toolbox that are valid in the continuous model (65 wavelets), with the purpose to
select one wavelet that best represents the data.
Given a ﬁxed wavelet, the preservation criterion consisted of comparing the locations of the
highest peaks as produced by the wavelet analysis with the corresponding locations on the
original data (i.e., the locations of the peaks on the digitized waveforms, cf. 5.1). By counting
the diﬀerences it was then possible to obtain a measure of ﬁdelity to the data set.
Observe that our approach is in contrast to previous research on CWT for peak detection of
full waveform LiDAR signals [47, 48], where the wavelet of choice is the Gaussian, based on a
statement about the “closeness” of this wavelet to the echo shape being detected in comparison
to other wavelets in the literature.

peak information contained in the digitization, having a ﬁdelity rate of 95%, with mistakenly
detected highest peaks in 126 waveforms out of the total 2368 in the dataset.

Waveform Interpolation
Interpolation is an ancient problem that is still actively researched [25]. It can be stated as the
estimation of sound values of an implied function given a discrete sample set of the function.
The qualiﬁcation of soundness is there to emphasize the fact that the new values should conform
to a (tacit) speciﬁcation of the function.
Somewhat formally, interpolation consists of the computation of an interpolant Pf of an unknown
function f, given a sample {(xi, f(xi))}n
i=1 with x1 < . . . < xn, such that Pf coincides with f in
all domain points of the sample, and Pf is at least continuous.
Besides continuity other algebraic or functional properties of the interpolant may be imposed,
but following Hyman [20], a good interpolant must maintain geometric properties of the data
that are considered essential, such as monotonicity and convexity. There are, too, practical
aspects such as computational time and space complexity that should be assessed.
The piecewise polynomial cubic Hermit interpolant has been proposed in the literature [20, 34, 19]
as an interpolant that satisﬁes all of the abovementioned concerns under certain conditions. A
few deﬁnitions are in place.
For each i = 1, . . . , n, let fi denote the given value of f on xi, and {fi} be called the sample
data. Let ∆i→= xi+1 −xi be the forward local-mesh-spacing at xi, and Si→= fi+1 −fi/∆i→be
the slope of the straight line segment between the mesh bounds. Similar deﬁnitions are assumed
for the backward local-mesh-spacing at xi and its slope, denoted ∆←i and S←i respectively.
The sample data are locally monotone at xi, if S←iSi→> 0. The sample data are locally convex
at xi if S←i > 0 while Si→< 0, and concave if the contrary holds.
Consequently, the interpolant is piecewise monotone if (Pf)(x) is monotone between fi and
fi+1 for x between xi and xi+1. A piecewise monotone interpolant preserves the convexity (or
concavity) of the sampling data. Finally, the interpolant is class Ck if it is continuous and has
continuous derivatives of all orders up to k.

of f on xi and is assumed to be given.

6.1
Types of Interpolation
There are two diﬀerent types of waveform interpolation that we wish to consider. When two
waveforms belong to the same scan line, then we refer to along-scan interpolation, whereas if
the two waveforms belong to diﬀerent but adjacent scan lines, then we refer to across-scan
interpolation.
An example is depicted in Figure 6.1. In 6.1a, three laser beams are displayed originating at
their anchor points (black), passing through the target surface (blue) and hitting corresponding
strongest returns (yellow) of their waveforms. In 6.1b beams 1926 (grey-left) and 1927 (grey-right)
lie on the same scan line, as indicated by the short dotted line. Instead, beams 1926 (grey-left)
and 1967 (purple) lie on two diﬀerent but adjacent scan lines as indicated by the long dotted line.
Along-scan interpolation should conceptually be simple to perform since, provided that two
consecutive beams originate at the same anchor point, all what is required in the homotopy is to
let the homotopy parameter represent the scanning-angle between the beams.
However, the assumption that along-scan beams share the same anchor point does not actually
hold (see Figure 6.2 for an example). Attempting to calculate a virtual anchor point as the
geometric intersection of every two consecutive beams may not be helpful or even possible, since
beams may be segments of intersecting lines whose intersection is too far away for computations
to be eﬃcient, or worse, segments of skew lines (in three-dimensional space). A workaround to
this situation is to start with an interpolation between anchor points of every two consecutive
beams, before waveform interpolation.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
This research work was concerned with the processing of full waveform LiDAR data in the context
of pulse-based airborne small-footprint LiDAR systems applied for topography. We showed
how wavelet theory and the concept of homotopy could be applied in data processing for peak
detection of strongest returns and waveform interpolation, respectively.
There remain several issues of interest that we would like to consider and will be discussed next.
Data Screening
By the end of section 4.1 it was mentioned that the procedure applied to the removal of odd
waveforms was graphical rather than programmatic. Clearly, the latter is preferred in a more
automated solution to LiDAR data processing.
A second observation regarding this stage is that even though the result of ﬁrst removing odd
waveforms and then dull waveforms (as we did in this work) is the same as the result of the
reversed operation, the former order is preferable in terms of computational eﬃciency. This is
due to the fact that some odd waveforms may also be dull waveforms, whose removal processing
time is greater.
Thresholds for Finding Peaks
The method used to establish the thresholds that determine whether or not waveforms may
have peaks, namely the maximum curvature of the curve implied by the decreasing sequence
of samples of a waveform, could be appropriate for waveforms originated from topographic
applications although experiments with more data sets should be performed. We must warn that
our implementation in terms of slopes of normals was convenient for the purpose of this study
but as it stands, it is not advisable in general, since it is too sensitive to local characteristics of
the sampling.
As an example, Figure 7.1 shows the result of establishing the thresholds for waveform number
1646 and a synthetic waveform (i.e., a waveform that has been artiﬁcially built and is not part of
the data set) that is a slightly modiﬁed version of it. The diﬀerence occurs since there is a very
small variation in slopes of normals among the ﬁrst three samples. In case 7.1a, the variation is
too small that it is found by the algorithm as the ﬁrst point of slow oscillation. In case 7.1b, the
variation disappears by adjusting the third sampling value, and the ﬁrst point of slow oscillation
seems more realistic.

Peak Detection
Admittedly, the graphical results of wavelet analysis with DWT are less spectacular than those
with CWT, but wavelet analyses in the discrete model are more eﬃcient. Our case analyses
show that the discrete model also achieves better data representation. Nonetheless, it is likely
that the peak detection methods in both models can be improved. In the continuous model, for
instance, a natural approach is to consider the matrix of CWT coeﬃcients as a two-manifold in
three dimensional euclidean space, and apply notions of diﬀerential geometry for the location
of waveform peaks. Regarding the discrete model we observe that the use of pulselets would
be desirable, but at the moment there is no procedure to compute them as admissible wavelets
for DWT. In this line, a plausible approach is the work of Swelden [41, 42] known as the lifting
method.
Finally, it must be said that our focus all along was in peak detection of strongest returns, and
that a full-ﬂedged solution to detect all peaks in a waveform will require more work. In terms of
DWT analysis, this means to reconsider the number of levels of decomposition and establish an
appropriate criterion based on approximation and detail coeﬃcients to determine the location of
peaks.
Homotopy for Compression
In this study, we used the concept of homotopy as a method of interpolation of waveforms as
functions of time. It is natural to ask whether the concept can also be used as a method of data
compression. This is case. Indeed, an entire scanning fan could be interpolated by providing full
waveforms of only the edge beams and peak information of the peaks for intermediate waveforms.
Using homotopy one could then reconstruct the intermediate waveforms using the intermediate
peak locations as path constrains. Clearly, experiments should be performed to assess the quality
of the compression.

This work is a contribution to the processing of full waveform LiDAR data applied to three dimensional reconstruction 
of environments.
We are concerned with two speciic stages of processing: Peak Detection and Interpolation. The former is addressed 
with Wavelet Theory, and the latter with the concept of Homotopy, and our main aim was assessing their feasibility as 
tools for dealing with the mentioned stages.
To make this study viable its scope was restricted, and we focused on pulse-based airborne LiDAR systems, recon-
struction based on strongest returns, a small data set, and the PulseWaves data exchange format.
We conclude that the use of both wavelets and homotopies is useful in full waveform LiDAR data processing, but 
some further work is required before implementing a scalable solution to massive data sets for real-life applications.
