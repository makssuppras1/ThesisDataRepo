Summary
This thesis focuses on deep learning methods applicable to graph structured data.
In particular, we work with graph neural networks, a novel approach to learning
on graphs in which each node combines messages from its neighbors using neural
networks. We evaluate their performance on node classiﬁcation task and compare it
with other graph-based methods as well as well established classiﬁers from machine
learning ﬁeld that do not exploit graph structure. The models are tested on three
datasets.
Furthermore, we attempt to explain predictions made by graph neural networks
on the example of citation network of court cases from the European Court. For this
end a method measuring sensitivity of an output to input features is used as well as
a method assigning importance scores to every neighbor of a given node.
This study indicates that graph neural networks consistently outperform classi-
ﬁers that do not consider graph structure and often perform better than shallow
graph-based methods on the three datasets considered. We also conduct extensive
experiments with the two explainability methods and gain useful insights into how
the predictions are generated.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this chapter we give a brief introduction to graphs and an overview of their in-
teresting applications in diﬀerent research areas. We familiarize the reader with a
concept of machine learning and the challenges faced by practitioners when applying
machine learning on graphs. Then we introduce a range of methods that can ad-
dress these challenges, pointing out their limitations and advantages, and we discuss
the importance of explainability of machine learning models. Finally, we outline the
scope and structure of this report.
1.1
Graphs
Graph are a data structure that, due to a rapidly progressing globalization in recent
decades, can be used to describe more and more phenomena surrounding us. Math-
ematically, they can be formulated as G = (V, E), where V represents a set of nodes
(also called vertices), and E represents a set of edges (also called arcs) that connect
pairs of nodes that are in some sense related to each other. These connected nodes
are then said to be adjacent. A neighborhood of a node v is induced by a set of all
nodes adjacent to v. Furthermore, the pairwise relationship can be either undirected,
in which case the edges connect unordered pairs of nodes symmetrically, or directed,
where edges have direction and connect ordered pairs of nodes. Sometimes graphs
are allowed to contain loops, which are edges that connect a node to itself. We also
deﬁne a notion of a path, which is a sequence of distinct edges that joins two vertices
together.
Ultimately, graphs are used to study relationships, that is, anything that can
be abstracted or represented by a mathematical notion of relation between entities
of interest, which is what makes them special. Instead of looking at an entity in
isolation, we can consider it in the context of its neighborhood and the connections
with the rest of the graph, which makes the analysis much more interesting.
Because of their high level of expressiveness, many systems and interactions in
the real world are naturally represented by the graphs, which in turn leads to their
wide applications.
For instance, in social sciences, graphs can be used to denote
relationships between individuals.
This has been leveraged by many giant social
media services like Facebook or LinkedIn to suggest friends one may know in reality.
Neuroscientists leverage graph-based methods to analyze connectivity patterns in a

human brain - one of the most complex network in the world [BB09]. Google maps
the transportation system as a graph, where the intersection of two (or more) roads
is considered as a node and the road connecting two nodes is an edge. The algorithm
behind the navigation system in Google Maps service is based on ﬁnding the shortest

also be represented as a directed graph, with pages corresponding to graphs and links

Use of graphs has even led to an emergence of new science ﬁelds, like network

the latter is to identify drugs that may interact with each other when taken together
and therefore cause negative side eﬀects [Hop07].
Therefore, graphs are a universal data structure, a shared vocabulary, for describ-
ing a great variety of complex systems. However, they can not only serve us as a
useful, structured knowledge repository but they have also started playing a key role
in modern machine learning applications.
1.2
Machine Learning
It is said that the term "machine learning" was ﬁrst introduced by Arthur Samuel
in 1959 [Sam00] and it refers to a concept of learning without being explicitly pro-
grammed. He argued that it is possible to program computers so that they learn from
experience and the programming eﬀort can be eliminated. Indeed, machine learning
is about automatically improving computer algorithms through the use of data, which
can be compared to the way that humans learn from experience.
Initially, around 1950s - 1980s, machine learning was heavily driven by domain
knowledge, which was incorporated into the mathematical model, that could capture
the physics of the set-up under study [Sim18]. Eventually, by 1990s, this approach has
became more data-driven. This happened by replacing the step of acquiring domain
knowledge and studying the problem of interest in detail by collecting suﬃciently large
amounts of data (training examples) fed into a learning algorithm, that can recognize
patterns and extract conclusions from it. It was possible due to the unprecedented
availability of data that we experience nowadays, and the constantly increasing power
of computational resources.
Machine learning can be roughly divided into three main classes of techniques:
supervised, unsupervised and reinforcement learning. In this report we mainly focus
on the ﬁrst category, where the algorithm is provided with not only the input data
but also the desired output, also known as a supervisory signal. The task then is
to learn a function that could predict the output associated with a new input. This
is achieved through an iterative optimisation of an objective function. For example,
we can train an algorithm to recognize faces or detect certain objects in an image,

translate speech to written text or even to predict the weather. Most of the current
machine learning applications fall in this category, and while it may be potentially
easy to acquire enough input data, output data is not always available and labelling
the training examples (collecting outputs) may require extensive eﬀort.
Unsupervised methods, on the other hand, learn solely from input data, which is
not labeled, meaning that none of the training examples is assigned to any output. The
goal is to identify hidden structures in a given dataset by ﬁnding patterns, similarities
or diﬀerences between observations. One of the main applications of unsupervised
learning is grouping and clustering of data points so that the ones in the same cluster
are similar to each other according to some speciﬁed criteria while diﬀerent from the
observations in other clusters.
Reinforcement learning lies somewhere in between supervised and unsupervised
techniques. Here, an algorithm is exposed to an environment where it makes decisions
and receives feedback (also called a reward) for selecting an output given an input
and the current state of the environment. This feedback indicates to what extent
the action performed fulﬁlls the desired goals of a learner. Therefore, there is some
form of a supervision, but it is based solely on the actions performed on a trial
and error basis by the algorithm itself and not provided as a speciﬁcation of a desired
output for every input data. Interesting applications of unsupervised learning include
autonomous vehicles or a bot learning to play a game against a human.
At this point it is also essential to mention deep learning algorithms which have
been showing superior performance in computer vision, natural language preprocess-
ing and speech recognition. They are a subset of machine learning methods that
attempt to mimic human brain and are based on artiﬁcial neural networks. The term
"deep" refers to a concept of stacking up multiple layers of such networks, each build-
ing upon the previous one. This allows them to progressively extract higher-level
features from the raw input, which even further removes the dependency on human
experts when it comes to feature engineering. The process of signals travelling from
the input layer (point of ingestion the data for processing), through the hidden layers
(where all the transformations of the input take place), to the output layer (where
the ﬁnal prediction is made) is called forward propagation. After calculating errors in
the predictions, the signal is sent back through the neurons to adjust network’s pa-
rameters using algorithms like gradient descent in an eﬀort to train the network. This
process is called backpropagation and is something that revolutionized the learning
capabilities of neural networks.
It is important to notice that all of the above methods are designed and work very
well with certain kinds of regular data structures, deﬁned on a discrete Euclidean
domain. For example, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) work well over grid-
structured data such as images, and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) can capture
information from sequential data such as text, audio or videos.

1.3
Machine Learning on Graphs
Since the topic of this thesis is an application of deep learning on graphs, it raises
the question of how can we leverage these methods on graphs? Among the various
computational tasks that we can perform on them, the three most popular ones
are node-level, edge-level and graph-level tasks. The most common node-level task
is node classiﬁcation or regression where the model tries to predict some property
associated with a node based on the characteristics of its neighbors. Edge-level tasks
may include prediction of existence of a link between two entities and in graph-level
tasks the prediction characterizes an entire graph instead of a single node or edge.
What additionally empowers these tasks is the fact that very often we can also use
feature information associated with a node or an edge in a graph. Sometimes even
the whole graph can be further described by a real-valued attribute vector. These
features might provide useful information about the graph. For instance, a user in
a social network on Facebook can be further described by not only the connections
it has to other users but also the information about age, gender, proﬁle picture or
hobbies. Existence of edge features is less common and most methods treat the rela-
tionships between nodes as a binary variable, aiming to predict the existence of edge
between two nodes [Tan+15]. However, considering the example of a social network,
relationships here can be very complex, with users being connected to their family
members, co-workers or colleagues. This implies that edges can be of diﬀerent types,
being one-dimensional vectors of integer values, but they can also be multidimen-
sional [Gil+17], indicating for instance when the two people met or whether they
live together. If the node or edge features are used as an input to the embedding
algorithm, the learned representation will capture not only the topological but also
the semantic graph information [Cha+20].
A lot of research has been conducted to apply machine learning on graphs to
perform the above mentioned tasks, however, it is a non-trivial problem due to a
variety of reasons [Wu+22].
First of all, classical machine learning methods were designed for regular data
structures, while graphs cannot be expressed as a grid- or sequence-like objects, which
makes it hard to generalize some of the basic mathematical operations on them. In
other words, there is no ﬁxed ordering of nodes in a graph or any notion of a reference
point, which is very diﬀerent from a grid data like images, where their orientation
is clear. For example, because graphs may have a variable size of unordered nodes,
where each of them may have a diﬀerent number of neighbors, it is diﬃcult to deﬁne
meaningful convolutions in a graph domain.
Transforming graphs to a Euclidean
space, as required by traditional machine learning methods, yields inevitable loss
of relationship information along with its strength or a direction of this connection
between nodes. Secondly, in an increasingly digital information age, observed graphs
can have billions of nodes and edges and designing scalable models whose complexity
would grow linearly with respect to the graph size remains a key challenge. Next, the
diversity and heterogeneity of properties that a graph can contain requires diﬀerent

5
types of architectures than the ones that are already used in traditional deep learning.
Lastly, as mentioned above, because graphs are a universal data structure, utilized in
many diﬀerent ﬁelds, it may be necessary to incorporate domain knowledge to solve a
given task, but at the same time integrating expert knowledge may complicate model
design.
Another reason for why the oﬀ-the-shelf machine learning methods are not applica-
ble to graphs is the assumption that data points must be independent and identically
distributed (i.i.d.). When observations are dependent on each other, we need to ac-
count for that and use methods that model dependencies between all input points.
Whereas if the observations are not identically distributed, we cannot guarantee that
the model will generalize to data not used in the training.
Nodes in graphs are
inherently connected which automatically breaks the i.i.d. assumption [Ham20].
Machine learning tasks for node classiﬁcation may be based on the notion of ho-
mophily, which was found to be an organising principle, especially in social networks.
It is a tendency of nodes to connect with other nodes that are similar [MSC01]. Based
on that we can build a classiﬁer that aims to predict similar labels for neighboring
nodes [Zho+03]. There is also a concept of structural equivalence, where nodes with
a similar neighborhood structure will have similar labels [Don+17]. Therefore, when
dealing with graph structures, we want to exploit these concepts and model relation-
ships between nodes rather than treat them as independent observations.
Because of this it is essential to ﬁnd eﬀective graph representations that would
help us eﬃciently apply the above-mentioned tasks while retaining the information
stored both in the graph structure and in the features associated with every node.
Therefore, all of the computational tasks mentioned at the beginning of this section
are often called downstream tasks, as their performance heavily relies on the quality
of the graph representations calculated prior to executing the prediction algorithm.
1.4
Representation Learning
A desired way of constructing such a representation is to encode each node to a low-
dimensional vector representation (node embedding), that preserves their position in
a graph, the structure of their local neighborhood and the content of nodes [Ham20].
This embedding is a point in a latent space, where geometric relations in this space
correspond to interactions in the original graph [HYL17b].
In the rich literature
we can ﬁnd two main ways of achieving this goal: feature engineering and feature
learning.
Traditional feature engineering on graphs focuses on preparing hand-crafted fea-
tures that rely on summary statistics calculated on diﬀerent levels of the graph. These
attributes are well-deﬁned and describe fundamental properties of a graph, however,
features obtained this way can be suboptimal for a given downstream task, as often
we do not have prior knowledge about what features are essential [MT21]. Addition-
==PAGE:16==
6
1 Introduction
ally, obtaining them requires signiﬁcant human eﬀort and can be computationally
expensive for large graphs.
On the other hand, feature learning aims at creating the features automatically.
The fact that this process can be guided by the downstream task (like node classiﬁ-
cation or link prediction) results in a better performance. Broadly speaking, feature
learning can be divided into feature selection, which is about removing redundant or
irrelevant node features, and representation learning that focuses on generating new
node features [MT21] that are not only easier to extract but are more useful and
powerful in distinguishing between diﬀerent types of nodes.
Among the various ways of learning representations, we can distinguish shallow
and deep methods. Shallow methods, which are discussed in more details in Sec-
tion 2.3, encompass more traditional techniques aimed at learning embeddings that
can eﬀectively reconstruct the pairwise node relationships [Ham20]. In other words,
given a pair of nodes that are similar to each other in the original graph, we want to
generate node embeddings that are close in the embedding space.
However, shallow methods have certain limitations. They are rather computa-
tionally ineﬃcient and not scalable since there is no parameter sharing. The reason is
that they generate a unique embedding for each node and the number of parameters
needed grows as O(|V |), where |V | is a number of nodes in a graph. Moreover, they
can also be statistically ineﬃcient as parameter sharing can act as a powerful form of
regularization [HYL17b]. Furthermore, these algorithms are transductive, meaning
that they generate embeddings for a single ﬁxed-size graph and thus they cannot
encode nodes that were not present in a training set. We would need to recompute
embeddings for all nodes in a graph to include a new vertex. They are also unable
to utilize node or edge features and do not consider diﬀerent types of nodes, which
in many scenarios can be highly informative about the node’s position in a graph.
Deep methods can overcome all of the aforementioned weaknesses of shallow meth-
ods. They are known as Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and are intended to apply
deep learning methods to graphs.
It is a more generalized approach that usually
leverages both the input node features and the local neighborhood structure, rather
than the entire graph. GNNs iteratively aggregate feature information from neighbors
to update the current representation of each node. To achieve that, a neural message
passing is implemented, where the goal is to update an embedding of one node with
the combined messages from its neighbors using neural networks. This framework
can be generalized to many kinds of tasks, including graph-, edge- or node-level pre-
diction tasks. GNNs have attracted a signiﬁcant amount of research in recent years
and are thought to be a critical block for modelling relational data.
1.5
Explainability methods
As mentioned above, deep learning have revolutionized the world of artiﬁcial intel-
ligence and have enabled unprecedented breakthroughs in a variety of applications.
==PAGE:17==

7
However, while most of the research has been focusing on enabling their superior
performance, not enough focus has been devoted to reasoning about their predictions
and to eﬀorts to explain their decisions in human terms. Therefore, there is a growing
need to build a conﬁdence regarding their behaviour in real-world applications, espe-
cially the ones pertaining to fairness, privacy and safety [Yua+20; Sel+17; Ola+18].
Explanations can be of great help to practitioners who can more eﬀectively debug
their model or detect unintended biases learned by the model [Ade+18],
Several methods have been proposed to explain predictions of deep neural networks
for image and text data [SVZ13; TG20; Sel+17; Du+18; DLH19]. The most straight-
forward approaches are input-dependent and include studying gradients or weights
to analyze the sensitivity of output to input features.
Meanwhile, interpretability techniques for graph data have been much less ex-
plored, which is critical for understanding GNNs. There have been some eﬀorts to
apply the methods used in conventional deep learning to graph domain, however,
this remains a non-trivial task. Graphs are much less intuitive than grid-like objects,
such as images. In graphs there is no notion of locality and each node has a variable
number of neighbors. What is most interesting in them, is the structure, topology
information that they carry. But because this is represented as a discrete, adjacency
matrix, it is not possible to apply e.g. methods that use input optimization [SVZ13]
to explain the prediction, as adjacency matrix cannot be optimized the same way as
pixel values in an image [Yua+20].
Furthermore, to fully understand model predictions, domain knowledge is a neces-
sity. While images and text are easy to grasp, graphs model complex systems coming
from social science, chemistry and biology. These are interdisciplinary studies, that
are not yet fully explored, and where the expert knowledge is still lacking.
Hence, obtaining human-intelligible explanations for deep neural networks and
for GNNs in particular is a challenging task, that requires more attention from the
community.
1.6
Scope
The fundamental goal of this report is to explore the capabilities of graph neural net-
works and verify their advantages over traditional machine and deep learning models
in terms of performance. Through our numerical experiments we wish to demon-
strate that for relational data, models that exploit inherent relationships between the

As one of the goals of this report is to explore methods for learning graph represen-
tations, in this chapter we motivate the use of Graph Representation Learning tech-
niques in machine learning on graphs, deﬁne notation and diﬀerent concepts related
to graphs, and give an introduction to the most prominent methods for generating
shallow embeddings, using a generalized encoder-decoder framework.
As pointed out in Chapter 1, graphs are a complex and diverse data structure from
non-Euclidean domain, that explicitly represent dependencies between the nodes.
Given the widespread applications of graphs in diﬀerent domains, the availability of
huge amounts of data, and the recent advancements of machine learning models, there
has been a surge of interest in applying them on graph-structured data. However,
the central problem in machine learning on graphs remains: how can we incorporate
the rich information about relationships between the nodes along with their features
into a machine learning model?
The answer is Graph Representation Learning (GRL), which aims at learning a
d-dimensional vector - called embedding - that summarizes the position of a node in a
discrete graph domain and the structure of its local neighborhood [HYL17b]. There-
fore, the goal is to map every node in a graph into an embedding space that reﬂects
the original structure of the graph. Ideally, distances between the nodes in the learned
representation space should capture the relationships represented by edges between
nodes or reﬂect their neighborhood structure in an original graph.
Consequently,
nodes that have similar connections in a graph, will also be characterized by close
vector representations in this space. Such an intuition is well visualised in Figure 2.1,
which presents 2-dimensional embeddings for a small social network [Zac77]. It can
be easily seen how the distances between nodes in an embedding space reﬂect the
topology of the original graph.
Because these vector representations are dense and continuous, they can be easily
exploited by standard statistical or machine learning models. The performance of

GRL is also a way to omit a tedious and manual task of feature engineering as a

Notation and assumptions
We assume that an input to an embedding algorithm is a graph G = (V, E), repre-

GRL algorithms can be roughly divided into two classes of problems: supervised
and unsupervised. In an unsupervised setting a mapping is learned using only the
information stored in A and possibly also in X. Therefore, often graph structure is
the only available information. The goal is to learn embeddings that can well approx-
imate the original graph and this is usually achieved by minimizing a reconstruction
loss.
Some methods utilize contrastive objective [Cha+20], that is, they compare
the embeddings of close-by pairs of nodes with distant ones [PAS14] or contrast real
graphs versus fake ones [Vel+18]. In a supervised scenario learning the embeddings is
guided by a downstream task, optimized with a use of classiﬁcation or regression la-
bels that the downstream machine learning task tries to predict [HYL17b]. Therefore,
the mapping is learned for some speciﬁc purpose.
Additionally, we can categorize GRL algorithms based on whether they operate in
a transductive or an inductive setting. It is assumed that in a transductive learning,
the underlying graph domain is ﬁxed - all nodes are seen during the training phase,

2.2
Encoder-decoder perspective
An encoder-decoder framework was proposed by [HYL17b] to introduce structure and
a uniﬁed notation into a very diverse ﬁeld of representation learning approaches. It
involves two key components: an encoder, which maps every node in a graph to a
d-dimensional space (based on its position in a graph, local neighborhood structure,
and/or its attributes) and a decoder, which then takes the learned embeddings and
uses them to decode structural information about each node’s neighborhood from the
original graph. This decoded information might be a set of neighbors of node u (its
corresponding row in an adjacency matrix, A[u]) or a classiﬁcation label associated
with it (e.g., a community that a node belongs to in a graph).
The general intuition behind this idea is that if we can learn embeddings that
encode as much variance present in the data as possible, then we should be able
to accurately decode high-dimensional information about the graph - such as global
roles of the nodes or the structure of their neighborhoods. It would mean that they
contain all the relevant information to be suitable as an input for a machine learning
model [HYL17b].
We can mathematically formulate the encoder as a function

2.3
Shallow Embeddings
The main characteristic of shallow methods is a fact that the encoder is simply an
embedding lookup function to an embedding matrix Z, where each node is assigned a
unique embedding:
ENC(v) = Z[v],
(2.8)
The methods described in this chapter yield diﬀerent answers to questions about
what information to preserve in the node embeddings and how to preserve it. For
example, one might assume that nodes that are similar should be close to each other
in a graph domain, i.e. they might share neighboring nodes or the path between
them is relatively short. In this case methods that attempt to randomly traverse the
graph, measuring node co-occurrence or neighborhood overlap, to discover localized
structures, might be helpful [PAS14], [GL16]. Next important network feature that
is desirable to preserve are communities within a network, i.e. groups of nodes that
are densely connected within one community, while sparsely connected with others.
Preserving them into embedding space can reveal organizational and functional com-
ponents of a network [Wan+16]. The diﬀerence from the previous approach is that
this one preserves not only the microscopic structure but also the community struc-
ture. The resulting representations of nodes within one community should be closer
then those from other community [Wan+16]. These kinds of methods exploit the
notion of homophily, mentioned in Section 1.3.
Another criteria that can be used to deﬁne similar nodes is their role in a graph
- we might want to diﬀerentiate hubs, i.e. nodes with a signiﬁcantly higher number
of connections than the majority of them, from nodes with less connections. For this
purpose methods that exploit the notion of structural equivalence have been developed
[FRS17]. What is important to note is that structural equivalence does not imply
connectivity [GL16], that is, node with the same structural role can be located far
from each other in a graph, which is why the methods preserving co-occurrence might
not be suitable in this case.

2.3.1.1
DeepWalk
DeepWalk [PAS14] is an algorithm that generalizes deep learning methods for natural
language preprocessing to graph domain since its implementation relies heavily on
the word2vec language model [Mik+13b], which transforms words into numerical
representations - word embeddings. It embeds words that are often used together in
a sentence (or in a similar context) close to each other in the embedding space. This
is done with the help of the Skip-gram model [Mik+13b], which, given a target word,
tries to predict surrounding words. It is a shallow neural network with one hidden
layer, which essentially tries to predict the context of a target word - words that could

be located within a certain distance before and after it. It does so by predicting the
probabilities of a given word being present given the input word. The center word is
then said to be co-occurred with the context words.
DeepWalk is an extension of this technique to graphs and aims to extract co-
occurrence relations between nodes. It treats nodes in a graph as words and gener-
ates sentences using random walks, where V is a vocabulary, and the set of generated
random walks is a corpus. The motivation behind this analogy is that if the degree
distribution in a graph follows a power law, the distribution of nodes appearing in
random walks is also a power law distribution [PAS14]. It was observed that word fre-
quency distribution in natural language is also very similar to power law distribution
[PAS14].
Random walks on graphs are generated in a very straightforward way - starting
the walk from a node v(0) in graph G, we randomly select one of its neighbors and
add it to the path. We repeat the process until a speciﬁed length of the walk has
been reached.
Here, random walks act as a similarity function. In a nutshell, nodes with similar
neighbors should have similar node representations and random walks allow us to
capture this. It is advantageous to use them as such because of their expressiveness.
They encapsulate both local and higher-order neighborhood information which results
in rich embeddings. They are also eﬃcient since we limit the node-pairs considered
during training to the ones generated by random walks and do not include all possible
pairs. It is also easy to parallelize this process and simultaneously explore diﬀerent
parts of the graph using several walkers [PAS14].

Preserving structural roles
The methods described above are likely to generate similar embeddings for nodes
close to each other in the graph domain, having similar neighborhoods. However, in
some real-world applications it is desirable to generate similar embeddings for nodes
that are characterized by the same structural role in a graph. These nodes are not

2.3.2.1
Struc2Vec
Struc2Vec explicitly extracts structural similarity between nodes independently of
node and edge attributes as well as their position in the network [FRS17].
One
advantage of this approach is that it does not require the graph to be fully connected
and still identiﬁes structurally similar nodes in diﬀerent connected components.
Authors of this method state two important properties that a successful approach
to learning structural similarity should exhibit [FRS17]:
1. Distance between latent representations of nodes should be correlated with their
structural similarity.
2. Latent representations should be generated independently of the edge or node
attributes. The reason is that node or edge features strongly exhibit homophily,
that is, nodes in some proximity to each other are more likely to share features,
which may not be the case for more distant nodes. Thus, incorporating node
or edge features (including node labels) can introduce noise to the embeddings
generated with the aim of assessing structural similarities, instead of homophily.
In struc2vec, a hierarchical structural similarity measure is proposed, which is
based on the intuition that if two nodes have the same degree, they should be struc-
turally similar. Furthermore, if their neighbors also have similar degree, than the
nodes should be even more related in terms of their structural role.

2.4 Limitations of shallow embedding methods

2.4
Limitations of shallow embedding methods
While shallow embedding algorithms can generate meaningful and rich embeddings,
these methods come with certain drawbacks.
First of all, the fact that they generate a unique embedding for each and every
node in a graph means that they scale badly as there is no parameter sharing. There-
fore, the number of parameters needed grows as O(|V |). Secondly, these methods are
transductive - they can only generate embedding for nodes from a single ﬁxed-size
graph, and embedding new nodes joining the network means the necessity of per-
forming additional rounds of optimization and recomputing representations for all
nodes in a graph to include new vertices. Lastly, they can capture only the structural
information of the graph, not being able to utilize any of the possibly available node
or edge features.
The focus of the next section is on more advanced encoders, based on deep neural
network architectures that can alleviate all of the above limitations. The basic idea
behind these approaches is to build a representation for a given node by iteratively
aggregating its local neighborhood information and leveraging node or edge features
to further inform the embeddings.

The previous chapter discussed methods that were optimized to generate a unique

Section 1.3, so there is a need for building a new kind of neural network architectures.
Problem deﬁnition. We start by formally deﬁning the problem of learning node
embeddings with graph neural networks. Let G = (V, E) denote a graph with a set

and node features. These embeddings can then be utilized for diﬀerent downstream
tasks, among which the most popular ones are node or graph classiﬁcation and link
prediction.
In this report we will focus on representative architectures aimed at
supervised node classiﬁcation.
One naive approach to deﬁning a neural network over a graph is to simply use its
adjacency matrix. We could concatenate its rows into a single vector and input it
into a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to generate embedding of an entire graph. This
could be formulated as follows:

3.1
The general framework
Before graph neural networks were standardized into a uniﬁed framework, diﬀerent
approaches for learning on graphs had been published by independent researchers.
Therefore, GNNs can be theoretically motivated in a variety of ways and the same
fundamental model has been derived in three main distinct areas [Ham20]. First,
we can look at them from the perspective of graph signal processing, where they
are a generalization of convolutions to non-Euclidean domain [Bru+13]. Simultane-
ously, neural message-passing approaches were developed, which were an analogy to
message-passing algorithms for probabilistic inference in graphical models [DDS16].
In fact, this particular approach forms the basis of most modern GNN architectures.
Finally, they were also conceptually inspired by an analogy to the Weisfeiler-Lehman
graph isomorphism test [HYL17a; Xu+18a].
Regardless of the theoretical foundations, the key aspect of GNNs is that they use
a message-passing mechanism to exchange information between nodes and update
them using neural networks [Ham20] and in this section we detail the foundations of
it and discuss diﬀerent variants that can be formed under this framework.

3.1 The general framework

The basic intuition is that, at each iteration (also called layer) in a GNN, we
update the hidden embedding hu of every node u ∈V by aggregating a set of messages
coming from its neighbors. In other words, we collect representations of the nodes in
the neighborhood calculated in the previous layer as well as the representation of the
target node itself, transform it, aggregate into a single message, and pass it on. The
concept of a graph neural network was ﬁrst proposed at the beginning of 21ˆst century
by Scarselli et al. [Sca+05; Sca+09] and Merkwirth and Lengauer [ML05]. It was
later reintroduced and reﬁned by Gori et al. [Gil+17] to derive the general message-
passing formulation that most of the present GNN architectures can be expressed
with.
This is also why these methods are often called convolutional, since representing
a node as a function of its neighborhood is similar to how a receptive ﬁeld of a
convolutional kernel works in computer vision [HYL17b].

Up to this point, our discussion of the building blocks of GNNs assumed that there
is only one type of relation between nodes. However, there are many applications,

relational. The edge notation now extends to triplets (u, r, v) ∈E, where r ∈R is a
relation type between node u and v, and we deﬁne a separate adjacency matrix per
relation type r Ar. To give an example, consider a Twitter social network, in which

3.3
Layer connectivity
So far, we introduced diﬀerent formulations and architectures of a single GNN layer.
A standard way to construct a full graph neural network is to stack GNN layers
sequentially, such that the input to layer 0 are the raw node features X, that are
then being transformed layer by layer until some predeﬁned number of iterations K
is reached.
Deeper GNNs can help us leverage long-term dependencies in the graph [Ham20],
however, there arises an important issue that prevents stacking too many GNN layers
together, since the notion of a layer in a GNN is a bit diﬀerent than the notion of
a layer in classical convolutional neural networks. In GNNs, the depth of a neural
network tells us what is the distance of the farthest nodes that we collect information
from while calculating representation for the target node.
It does not mean how
expressive, complex or powerful a model is, since this depends on the design of each
individual layer itself.
Therefore, GNNs tend to suﬀer from an over-smoothing problem, or a curse of
depth [Xu+18b], when we stack many layers together.
We talk about the over-
smoothing problem, when node embeddings converge to the same or very similar
values, which is not desired.
To discuss it further, let us deﬁne the notion of a receptive ﬁeld.
A receptive
ﬁeld is a set of nodes that determine the embedding of the node of interest. If our
network is built of K layers, then the receptive ﬁeld of node v consists of nodes in the
K-hop neighborhood around v. Consequently, if the receptive ﬁelds of many nodes
overlap, then they are going to collect information from the same set of nodes, which
in turn can result in a similar representations for all of them. Figure 3.2 shows the
intersection of receptive ﬁelds between two nodes of interest (marked in yellow), based

an almost-uniform distribution [Ham20].
Therefore, to deal with this problem, we need to enhance the expressive power of
the GNN layers, instead of building deeper models. This can be achieved in many
ways.
First, let us get back to the message transformation and aggregation operators.
One straightforward solution to adding more expressiveness to a GNN layer is to
design these operators as a two- or three-layer perceptrons, instead of just one linear
transformation, as they were originally deﬁned in section Section 3.1.2 [YYL20].
Another very eﬀective option is to add layers that do not necessarily pass messages,
and usually they are deﬁned as MLPs [YYL20]. They are added before the ﬁrst, and
after the last GNN layer, and we can think of them as a pre- and post-processing steps.
Pre-processing layers are particularly useful when working with features represented
as images or text, and where the encoding of input features is necessary.
Post-
processing layers can add more expressiveness in cases where further reasoning over
the node embeddings is needed.
Lastly, the notion of self-loops, (also called skip connections or residual connec-
tions), introduced in Section 3.2.2, can also aid in solving the problem of over-
smoothing.
One observation from the above discussion about the over-smoothing
is that the embeddings from "earlier" layers (in this context understood as lower lay-
ers in the GNN, that collect information from the close-by neighbors) can in some
==PAGE:49==

cases better diﬀerentiate between the nodes. Therefore, the motivation behind skip
connections is that these "shortcuts" increase the impact of the earlier layers on the
ﬁnal node embedding.
In practice, they tend to work best for node classiﬁcation
tasks, where the network structure exhibits strong homophily [Ham20].
The intuition behind the skip connections and the explanation for why are they
so eﬀective, is that they can be perceived as a mixture of models [VWB16], i.e., the
model is now a weighted combination of a previous and the current layer message
and in that sense it is a mix of two diﬀerent models.
3.3.1
Jumping Knowledge Networks
A very interesting solution to the curse of depth in GNNs are Jumping Knowledge
Networks [Xu+18b]. In their paper, the authors propose an adaptive approach to
learning the order of aggregation for diﬀerent graph structures. They make an obser-
vation that the nodes that are part of subgraphs characterized by diﬀerent properties
(especially in terms of the level of connectivity) will need diﬀerent number of aggrega-
tion rounds (layers in GNN) to calculate informative embeddings, which makes their
approach structure-aware.
In particular, they diﬀerentiate between expander and bounded treewidth sub-
graphs, representing strongly connected and smaller communities respectively. These
are the main subgraph structures that social and web networks usually consist of.
Expanders are deﬁned as dense, highly connected graphs, where the information dif-
fuses very quickly. Consequently, a lot of nodes will be part of a receptive ﬁeld of a
node in the expander graph. On the other hand, bounded treewidth subgraphs do
not exhibit highly connected structures, and are easy to cut. A random walker would
rapidly expand and cover most of the nodes in the expander-like graphs, while for
tree-like structures, it will move much slower, with high chances of getting "trapped"
inside the tree.
As a result, on expander, a deeper model will collect too much information, mixing
features from almost the entire graph, generating a ﬁnal embedding that is almost
equally inﬂuenced by all of the nodes. On tree-like subgraphs, a too shallow model
will not be able to collect enough information and thus, it would be desirable to jump
by one or two hops away to learn more about the community.
The main idea behind their proposal is, independently for each node, at the ﬁnal
layer, "look" at the intermediate representations from each individual layer.
This
way, the embeddings from all the previous layers "jump" to the last one, and can be
potentially combined, resulting in the desired adaptivity. This way we enable the
model to determine whether the most important information comes from the close-by
nodes or is it necessary to aggregate more distant parts of the graph. We can look
at this mechanism as another form of implementing skip connections.

3.4
Input Graph Augmentation
The computation graph does not need to be the same as the raw input graph structure
and there are a couple of reasons for why it is not always an optimal solution. First
of all, the input graph might be too sparse, in which case the message-passing oper-
ation is ineﬃcient, as a deeper neural network would be necessary to collect enough
information. Secondly, when the graph is too dense, or the message-passing is to be
performed on a high-degree node (for example very popular social media user) and
millions of nodes constitute the computation graph, then this operation will be very
expensive. In such situation, it may be enough to select a subset of nodes, instead
of aggregating over all of them. Lastly, the graph itself can be too large. Even if
it is manageable to load the entire graph together with the node features into the
memory, because of the recursive structure of GNNs, additional resources would need
to be allocated for computing embedding at layers k −1 and k.
A solution to the ﬁrst case - sparse graphs - is to add virtual edges [Gil+17].
This is particularly useful when working with bipartite graphs, which consist of two
disjoint sets, such that the edges exist only between nodes from diﬀerent sets (there
==PAGE:51==
3.4 Input Graph Augmentation
41
is no connection between nodes from the same set). For example, when dealing with
a citation network of authors and papers, we might want to add an edge between
two authors who wrote the same paper, or between two papers, that were written
by the same person. Another idea for a sparse graph with too few edges is to add a
virtual node [Gil+17; KBG18] that connects to all or several nodes. This way, the
aggregation distances between the nodes become greatly shortened, which makes it
no longer necessary to build deep GNNs, because the new node enables much more
communication.
When it comes to very dense or large graphs, we can resort to neighborhood sam-
pling. This is a very important strategy that enables scaling GNNs to massive graphs,
which is crucial in industrial applications like recommender systems, where the size of
the graph can reach tens of millions of nodes connected by billions of edges [Wan+20;
Yin+18]. One strategy that can allow us to learn over this kind of systems is random
neighborhood sampling.
3.4.1

3.4.2
Mini-batching - GNN perspective
The procedure described above is not directly applicable to graph neural networks
operating on massive graphs. Because the number of independently sampled nodes M
is much less than N (M ≪N), the nodes would be isolated from each other, and ma-
jority of their neighbors will not be included in the mini-batch. This is a fundamental
issue, given that GNNs operate by aggregating information from the neighborhood.
This means that no message-passing can be performed with this naive approach, and
gradients computed over isolated nodes will not be in any way representative of the
full gradient.

The neighbor sampling procedure, which is a key idea of GraphSAGE [HYL17a],
is the ﬁrst implementation of mini-batching for GNNs. Recall from the deﬁnition
of a computation graph from Section 3.1.1, that to calculate the embedding of a
node with a K-layer GNN, a K-hop neighborhood of the target node is needed, and
the rest of the graph structure can be ignored. Therefore, the key insight behind
the neighborhood sampling algorithm, is that given the M sampled nodes, we can
generate their embeddings using their respective M K-hop neighborhoods. In other
words, rather than using mini-batches composed of nodes only, mini-batches of K-hop
neighborhoods are constructed. With this, with have all the necessary information
to generate node embeddings, calculate the loss over the mini-batches, perform SGD,
and update the model parameters accordingly.
However, the issue with a computational and memory ineﬃciency still remains, as
for each node we need to get the entire K-hop neighborhood and pass it through the
computation graph. This is approach is prone to a neighborhood explosion problem,
where the number of nodes taking part in the message-passing grows exponentially
with the depth of a GNN [Zen+19], which is also visualised in Figure 3.2.
This
problem is even more apparent when a hub node is present in the neighborhood.
To alleviate this, the computation graphs are pruned and constructed by uniformly
sampling H neighbors of every node in the K-hop neighborhood, which eﬀectively
means that every node will aggregate messages from at most H other nodes, trans-
lating to QH
k=1 Hk nodes involved. This means that the computation graph will still
grow exponentially, but their fan-out will be upper-bounded by H. Choosing H is
therefore a trade-oﬀbetween computational eﬃciency and higher variance of gradient
estimates, which leads to more unstable training.
One can also choose other strategies for neighborhood sampling. The problem
with the uniform node sampling is that every node has the same probability of be-
ing chosen. Because many graphs tend to have highly skewed degree distributions
[CSN09], with most of the nodes being single or very low-degree ones, there is, in
fact, higher probability of sampling nodes that are perhaps not the most important
ones, but rather noisy and not informative (e.g. users that are not very engaged
or other entities that do not carry signals that could be useful for the prediction).
Therefore, another strategy, that works very well in practice [Zen+19], is to use ran-
dom walk based samplers [LF06; RT10; Yin+18]. First, random walk with restarts
are computed, starting the random walks at the target node. Then the H neighbors
with the highest scores are selected. The resulting graph will include nodes that most
frequently occur in the random walk and should be more representative of the full
neighborhood than the ones sampled uniformly.
The problem of deﬁning proper sampling strategies to construct the computation
graph and in which kind of cases they should be used is still an open research question
and such an investigation would certainly beneﬁt the ﬁeld of machine learning on
graphs.
Lastly, it is worth mentioning an algorithm, that, instead of sampling nodes, per-

passing procedure in a full-batch implementation is fast. In each layer only 2 × |E|

3.5
Applications
As mentioned in the Introduction (Chapter 1), graphs are a highly universal data
structure that can be employed to model relational data in various research domains.
Having said that, although the ﬁeld of machine learning on graphs is still in its
early stages, Graph Neural Networks have already proven to achieve state-of-the-art
performance in many diﬀerent applications [GMP21].
They were shown to be particularly eﬀective in the area of recommender sys-
tems, where they are used to predict links between users and products. One of the
largest applications of deep graph embeddings for a web-scale recommender system
is PinSAGE [Yin+18]. This model was designed by Pinterest1, a content discovery
application, where users can save and organize Pins, which are visual bookmarks to
online content (recipes, clothes, products, etc.), onto boards. Authors modelled this
system as a bipartite graph, in which Pins and boards are two disjoint sets of nodes.
The goal is to generate embeddings for Pins, whose features are combined visual
and textual information transformed into embeddings (images associated with a Pin
were converted into a vector representation using VGG16 model [SZ14] and text was
transformed by word2vec [Mik+13b]).
They key innovation of this model is its high scalability - it operates on three
billion nodes and 18 billion edges. PinSAGE can handle such graphs by sampling the
neighborhood around the node, similarly to GraphSAGE (Section 3.2.4), however,
here the set of neighbors to convolve over is based on short random walks which in
turn assess the importance of speciﬁc nodes using visit counts. Moreover, to further
speed up the training, diﬀerent parallelization patterns were proposed.
Another example of a recommender systems powered by GNNs is the one created
by Uber Eats2.

drugs under investigation. As a result, the athors discovered halicin - a molecule
previously tested for anti-diabetic properties - now repurposed to be used as a potent
antibiotic.
More recently, GNNs were used for proﬁling attacks on interaction data [Cre+22],
where the goal is to identify individuals based solely on the pseudonimyzed data of
interactions between them from one time period in another non-overlapping time pe-
riod. The intention was to highlight how sensitive to such attacks location data can
be. In this study mobile phone dataset of more than 40 000 users was used, which
contained information on the type of the interaction, its direction, when it happened
and how long it lasted. With the help of Graph Attention Network (Section 3.2.5),
representations of individuals were learned based on their weekly k-hop interaction
network. Using 2-hop neighborhood, the model could correctly identify 52% individ-
uals with a delay of one week and rank the right person among the top 10 candidates
with a probability of 77%. Furthermore, it was also shown that even with the de-
lay of 20 weeks, the model was correct 24% of the time, posing a real risk to the
identiﬁcation of an individual.

4.1.1
Open Graph Benchmark datasets
Open Graph Benchmark (OGB) is a project that was created to facilitate scalable,
robust, and reproducible graph machine learning research [Hu+20] by providing di-
verse, challenging and realistic benchmark datasets. Its authors were motivated by
a range of issues that they identiﬁed in the methodology and a use of benchmarks
in the existing literature. They point out that most of the frequently used datasets
were unrealistically small when compared to graphs in real-world systems, which ex-
ceed billions of edges and connections [Wan+20; Yin+18]. This resulted in developing
models that are hard to scale to larger graphs. Moreover, they argue that there was no
uniﬁed experimental and evaluation protocol. Even though the same datasets were
==PAGE:56==
46
4 Results
used in multiple studies, the researchers employed diﬀerent data splits, evaluation
metrics and cross validation schemes, making it hard to meaningfully and rigorously
compare the performance of diﬀerent model designs, deterring the advancement of
graph machine learning research.
In OGB a particular focus is put on data splitting. The authors argue that due
to relatively large sizes of the training sets, the performance on the most popular
datasets quickly saturated and the generalization gap between validation and test
sets was very small.
This is related to the fact, that a common practice was to
randomly split the data. This means that there was no distribution shift between
the splits, which did not resemble a real-life scenario, which makes the evaluation of
model performance too optimistic. Therefore, they proposed much more challenging
and realistic data splits that allow for improving the out-of-distribution generalization
performance.
In this study two social networks from the OGB project were used: ogbn-arxiv
(referred to as "arXiv" in the following) and ogbn-products (referred to as "Amazon
products" in the following) datasets. The letter "n" in "ogbn" refers to node prop-
erty prediction task in the OGB nomenclature. The public leaderboard presenting
the recent advancements in performance on these datasets can be accessed with the
following url: https://ogb.stanford.edu/docs/leader_nodeprop/.
4.1.1.1
ogbn-arxiv
ArXiv dataset is a network of citations between arXiv papers, extracted from Mi-
crosoft Academic Graph (MAG) [Wan+20], coming from Computer Science category.
Each paper is associated with a 128-dimensional feature vector, obtained by aver-
aging embeddings of words from its title and abstract. The word embeddings were
computed by word2vec model [Mik+13b] over the MAG corpus.
The task is to predict one of the 40 subject areas1, that were manually determined
for each paper by its authors and arXiv moderators.
The distribution of subject
areas in this dataset can be seen in Appendix A.1.
In contrast to other popular
citation networks that are randomly split [YCS16], arXiv dataset is split based on
a publication date: training set includes all papers published until 2017, validation
set consists of papers published in 2018, and papers published in 2019 are in the
test set.
This resembles a realistic scenario, in which the model is trained on all
existing papers in order to classify the newly-published ones. Although this network
is inherently directed, the GNN models were trained on an undirected one for the sake
of simplicity. In addition, performance scores presented in the OGB leaderboard were
also achieved on the undirected graph. The statistics in Table 4.1 were calculated for
the undirected graph.

4.1.1.2
ogbn-products
Ogbn-products is an undirected and unweighted co-purchasing network [Bha+16].
It is the largest dataset among the ones considered in this report.
Nodes repre-
sent products sold in Amazon, while edges between them indicate that the products
were bought together. 100-dimensional node features were generated as in [Chi+19],
by running PCA [FRS01] on a bag-of-words representation of product’s description.
What is characteristic about this dataset, is that it contains isolated nodes. This is
not problematic for GNNs, since in such case they will aggregate only the features of
the isolated nodes.
The task is to predict one of 47 top-level product categories. The distribution
of products categories in this dataset can be seen in Appendix A.1. In contrast to
randomly assigning 90% of the nodes to the training set and the rest to the testing set,
as in [Chi+19], OGB presents a much more challenging and realistic split. Speciﬁcally,
the nodes are split based on a sales ranking (their popularity). Consequently, 8% of
the most frequently purchased products comprise the training set, next top 2% are
included in the validation set, leaving the remaining 90% of nodes for the testing set.
This strategy matches more closely a realistic approach, where the most important
nodes are prioritized during manual labelling.
4.1.2
European Court judgements dataset
This dataset (referred to as "EU judgements" in the following) is a paragraph-to-
paragraph citation structure of a series of court cases from the European Court. It
means that the citation relationship is captured at the level of a paragraph and not
at the level of a case. This is advantageous since in law one does not refer to an
entire case but to a speciﬁc paragraph which was deemed relevant to support one’s
argument [RRR16; Mon+21]. It is thus important to keep in mind the diﬀerence
between these two notions.
The dataset was extracted from EUR-Lex2, a European Union database of legal
acts. It was narrowed down to include only the judgements from the case-law sector.
It was kindly provided to us by Henrik Palmer Olsen3, a Professor from a Faculty of
Law on University of Copenhagen.
The task on this dataset is formulated as a multi-class classiﬁcation problem,
where the goal is to predict one of the 24 subjects of case a given paragraph belongs
to.
Consequently, the prediction is made on a paragraph-level, even though the
property itself is inherently related to the whole case. In this case we resorted to
randomly splitting the data into training, validation and test sets.

To handle this, we ﬁrst calculated the number of occurrences of each individual cate-

XGBoost classifier
Among many machine learning methods, XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting)
[CG16] has been empirically proven to be highly eﬀective in many classiﬁcation and
regression problems, and has been consistently a top contender in most Kaggle7 com-
petitions [CG16; BCM21]. It was also used by every top 10 team in KDDCup 2015
[CG16].
This algorithm is based on a tree boosting system. Boosting refers to the concept
of an ensemble constructed of weak predictors, usually decision tree models. They
are added sequentially such that subsequent models improve the performance (that
is, correct prediction errors) of prior ones. The word gradient is there because models
are ﬁt using diﬀerentiable loss function, which is optimized using gradient descent
algorithm. The eXtreme component in its name refers to the fact that the speciﬁc
implementation developed by its authors oﬀers software and hardware optimizations,
as well as more regularized model formalization. It can be parallelized, trained on
GPU, and applied to problems with billions of examples. As a result, XGBoost is a
highly eﬃcient and scalable algorithm, an open-source project, and a Python library.
There is a number of parameters that can inﬂuence performance of XGBoost.
Below is a description of six parameters that we decided to tune:
• max_depth - maximum tree depth for base learners. This parameter controls
how specialized each learner is to the training data and can allow the model
to learn patterns that are speciﬁc only to it. It is therefore important when
ﬁghting overﬁtting.
• learning_rate - boosting learning rate. It controls the contribution of each
learner on the ensemble prediction to make the boosting process more conser-
vative. Another important parameter to prevent the overﬁtting.
• gamma - minimum loss reduction required to make a further split on a leaf node
of the learner. The larger the value, the shallower the trees.
• min_child_weight - minimum sum of instance weight needed in a child. It
can prevent the model from insisting to split the node until purity is reached
and therefore learning patterns speciﬁc to particular sample selected for the
tree. The larger the value, the more conservative the model is, but too large
values lead to underﬁtting.
• subsample - fraction of observations randomly sampled for each learner. Usu-
ally in a range between 0.5 and 1.
• colsample_bytree - fraction of features randomly sampled for each learner.

MLP
The third baseline model is a neural network with three fully connected hidden layers.
It is a very powerful and versatile yet simple model to get a challenging benchmark on
the chosen datasets. It is often used as a baseline in graph machine learning research
[Vel+17; Xu+18b; Xu+18a; Mor+19].
The MLP was trained in a mini-batch manner, and the size of the batch was a
hyperparameter. We also included dropout [Sri+14] and batch normalization [IS15] in
the architecture. Dropout is a technique that allows for randomly dropping neurons
along with their incoming and outgoing connections. Each unit is dropped temporar-
ily, with a certain probability p. Dropout oﬀers a very computationally cheap and
exceptionally eﬀective regularization method that signiﬁcantly improves generaliza-
tion capabilities and works for neural network architectures of all kinds. In our case
it quickly became apparent that not applying dropout results in consistently lower
performance, therefore, p equal to 0 is not even included in the range of considered
values in Table 4.4.
With batch normalization [IS15] we ensure that the input to every layer is nor-
malized (that is, not only to the input layer but also to every hidden one). This
means that the input distribution does not change, which results in much faster and
better convergence. For a d-dimensional input x = (x1, ...xd), each dimension k is
normalized as follows:

We decided to include node2vec [GL16] (Section 2.3.1.2) as a representative method of
shallow embedding algorithms. It is an intermediate step between the aforementioned
baselines and GNNs. The ﬁrst operate solely on node features, not looking at the
relationships between the observations. Node2vec, on the other hand, considers only
the graph structure, ignoring node features.
We used a PyTorch Geometric [FL19] implementation of this algorithm, as it
also supports GPU computations. The following set of parameters was tuned, whose
optimal values can be seen in Table 4.5:
• embedding_dim - dimension of the generated embedding.
• walk_length - length of the random walk.
• q - in-out parameter, controlling the probability of choosing inward versus out-
ward nodes during random walk. Higher values (q > 1) approximate a breadth-
ﬁrst search, while lower approximate depth-ﬁrst search (Section 2.3.1.2).
• walks_per_node - number of random walks sampled per each node.
• context_size - context size considered for positive samples. It is the max-
imum distance between the target node and the context node in the random
walk.
• num_negative_samples - the number of negative samples (co-occurrence
tuples) to use for each positive sample. This parameter is used for negative sam-
pling [Mik+13a], an optimization technique used to speed up node2vec training.
Recall that node2vec oﬀers a ﬂexible approach of exploring the neighborhood via
random walks. This is because the probability of choosing the next node is not the
same for all neighbors (uniform), but instead it is controlled by two parameters -
q and p (Eq. (2.13)). We set the value of p to 1.5 for all graphs with the aim of
encouraging graph exploration, since p > 1 means higher chances of avoiding 2-hop
redundancy by getting back to the previous node in a random walk. Because citation
and social networks usually exhibit strong homophily, we only consider values of
the parameter q lower than 1.
This way we wish to encourage depth-ﬁrst search
(DFS) and exploration of larger parts of the graph since visiting more distant nodes
is advantageous for encapsulating homophily in graphs [GL16].

very expensive. Such learning helps achieving better performance, which is a natural
consequence of having access to more data. As such, when we are given a full graph,
there is no reason to not to fully use it. We can thus build a better learner using both
labelled and unlabelled data and we observed that this approach has been the most
widely used in the literature. Consider a scenario in which a new user joins a social
network and the task is to compute its representation. It seems obvious that such a
user will connect to the nodes that were already used to train the model. Therefore,
to generate the embedding for a new node we will use the representations of nodes
that were already seen by the model.
A more pragmatic reason for choosing it is the fact that the performance of diﬀer-

4.3.4
Conclusions
To summarize, our numerical investigations indicate that for node classiﬁcation tasks
GNNs outperform machine learning models that do not consider graph structure.
They also often perform better than shallow network-based methods that on the
other hand do not incorporate node features in the calculations (in this study shallow
methods are represented by node2vec). Furthermore, we show that performance of
GNN models is dependent on the level of homophily present in the graph datasets.
Lastly, we demonstrate that the procedure applied to splitting the nodes into training,
validation and test sets yields a great impact on the ﬁnal performance. Speciﬁcally,
using random splits leads to results that are overly optimistic and may not be repre-
sentative of the realistic scenario.

Number of influential nodes
Another potentially interesting use-case of this method is to analyze the number of
inﬂuential nodes per degree. Is this relationship purely linear? How does it change
depending on the number of layers in a model? Can we use such analysis to extract
backbone of the network [SBV09], by removing irrelevant nodes with lower inﬂuence?
One way we can measure this is through thresholding - by setting an arbitrary
threshold ϵ we deem every node with an inﬂuence higher than ϵ as inﬂuential.
Figure 5.13 presents the number of inﬂuential nodes per degree, for six diﬀerent
values of ϵ. Colour indicates size of a 2-hop neighborhood.
By observing the range of y-axis it can be seen that the number of inﬂuential
nodes drastically drops as we increase the value of ϵ. For the lowest ϵ considered, the
number of inﬂuential nodes does not drop below the degree and for nodes with more
than 60 neighbors, this relationship is indeed linear. When it comes to nodes with
a lower degree, i.e. between 1 and 30, it can be seen that the number of inﬂuential
nodes often strongly exceeds the degree and can be even four or ﬁve times larger than
the degree.
For higher values of ϵ, we do not observe any inﬂuential nodes for hubs. The plot
for ϵ = 0.005 also shows how the inﬂuence spreads out and gets smoothened across
the neighborhood, as the nodes with larger neighborhoods (marked with a warmer
colour in the ﬁgure) have fewer inﬂuential nodes than nodes with a similar degree but
a smaller neighborhood.
This observation convinces us that using arbitrarily deﬁned values to threshold
inﬂuence scores is not a suitable approach to extracting backbone of the network. Its
magnitude is highly dependent on node degree and using the same ϵ for all nodes can
lead to disregarding all neighbors of hub nodes, while at the same time still keeping
all neighbors of low-degree nodes.
To work around this, dynamic thresholds could be considered, deﬁned for each
node individually. Another approach could involve quantiles and only keeping the
nodes with an inﬂuence in the top 1% of all inﬂuence scores. Unfortunately, due to
time constraints we were not able to test these alternative methods.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
In this chapter we reﬂect over the results presented in this report and propose several
ideas for continuing this line of work.
6.1
Discussion
We conducted multiple experiments to demonstrate the eﬀectiveness of graph neural
networks for node classiﬁcation tasks. Our investigations indicate that GNNs out-
perform every baseline that do not consider graph structure and often outperform
node2vec - a graph-based shallow model that ignore node features.
6.1.1
Model training
We performed extensive hyperparameter tuning for the seven models trained. In this
short section we wish to discuss optimal hyperparameters for GNNs that we ﬁnd
particularly interesting.
From Table 4.6, Table 4.7 and Table 4.8 we can see that only the EU judge-
ments dataset beneﬁted from adding a linear layer at the input and output of the
GNN model. One possible explanation for this is that the input features of the OGB
datasets were crafted well enough so that any additional transformations only con-
fused the model, whereas running a one-layer MLP on the paragraph embeddings
before feeding them into the model actually increased its expressiveness.
Furthermore, what is surprising, is that the mean aggregator turned out to be the
best performing one across all datasets for the GraphSAGE model. As pointed out in
[Xu+18a], sum is a more expressive aggregation because it is injective. An injective
function ensures that every possible output has at most one associated input. In
other words, it produces a unique output for every possible input. In the context
of GNNs it means that an injective aggregator function is more expressive and can
diﬀerentiate between two non-identical graph structures by generating two diﬀerent
representations for them. [Xu+18a] showed that because mean is not injective, it fails
to discriminate between certain non-identical graph structures and this aggregator
output the same value for them, while sum could successfully tell them apart. One
explanation for why mean performed best in our case is that the injectivity of a
GNN aggregation function is not the determining factor of its performance [ASC20].

86

Perhaps such level of expressiveness is not necessary for node classiﬁcation problems,
and, in fact, in some cases it might be desirable that two very similar but still diﬀerent
subgraphs are given the same embedding.
6.1.2
Data splitting procedure
We evaluated all models on the two OGB datasets using two data splitting procedures
- a random split and a split proposed by OGB authors which is supposed to be more
challenging by mimicking a more realistic approach. Our experiments clearly indicate
that random splits signiﬁcantly simplify the problem and lead to overly optimistic
results. All GNNs are similarly robust as the gaps between the performance on OGB
and random splits do not diﬀer signiﬁcantly between the models. This highlights the
importance of developing GNN architectures that will be capable of handling out-of-
distribution test data as in real-world applications the testing distribution is likely to
suﬀer from uncontrolled shifts. Building GNNs that could achieve stable performance
under distribution shifts and be robust to more types of spurious correlations has also
become a new research direction in the community [Fan+21; Li+21].
6.1.3
Explainability
In an eﬀort to explain GNN predictions, two methods were employed. We reused
attention weights learned by GAT and calculated gradients of last-layer embeddings
with respect to the input features. We learned that the importance of target nodes’
features is particularly high for low-degree nodes due to limited or mixed information
coming from small neighborhoods.
In overall, attention weights gave us more useful and intuitive insights as to how
the network behaves on the EU judgements dataset. We believe that one of the reasons
for this is that attention weights describe the importance of the immediate neighbors
while inﬂuence distributions are calculated over all nodes in a network. On one hand
it may potentially reveal some long-range dependencies in a graph. On the other,
usually there is too much noise coming from more distant parts of the neighborhood
and any important messages sent from outside of the 1-hop neighborhood are hard
to decipher.
Furthermore, the attentions are calculated between pairs of embeddings from the
same layer, while gradients describe a linear relationship between input and fully
processed output embeddings - with multiple layers in between. Therefore, perhaps
it might be more sensible to calculate gradients of output embeddings with respect
to previous-layer embeddings since the latter would already contain aggregated in-
formation about the neighborhoods. This strategy may match more closely the type
of information that is exchanged in the attention mechanism, which would allow for
discovering patterns in inﬂuence distributions that could be similar to those hidden
behind attention weights, and, therefore, be more intuitive. A possible downside of
this approach is a loss of interpretability power since raw input features are usually
==PAGE:97==

more easily understandable by humans than node representations from intermediate
layers of a neural network.
6.1.4
EU judgements dataset
GNNs achieved an accuracy of 95%, which outperforms other baselines (excluding
node2vec) by around 40%. This example shows how the access to graph topology
information and the position of individual nodes in a graph are a critical factor for
node-classiﬁcation tasks. We can conclude that the addition of new edges between
paragraphs from the same case greatly contributed to the overall performance by
increasing the level of homophily in the graph. This is well illustrated in Figure 5.6,
which presents examples of nodes with mixed neighborhoods, where almost all original
neighbors are of a diﬀerent class. These nodes would most likely be misclassiﬁed if
it was not for the new small cliques attached to them. Nevertheless, we would like
to emphasize that the new clique did not dominate these neighborhoods (it did not
became the mostly connected part of it, that is, the remaining neighbors were also
well connected), which further suggests that the network learned non-trivial patterns.
In addition, we implemented the simplest version of a label propagation algo-
rithm. If the homophily is that strong, than perhaps randomly drawing a class from
a distribution induced by the neighborhood can lead to a similar performance. By
performing such simulation on test nodes, the resulting average accuracy over 10 runs
is 86%, which, in overall, is already a good result and gives an indication that the
problem is relatively simple (it is worthwhile to note that the level of homophily in
a graph is also 86% (Section 4.3.1)). GNNs achieved a result higher by around 10
percentage points. Now the question remains whether such performance gain is sat-
isfactory considering that hundreds of thousands of parameters need to be optimized
for this purpose.
6.2
Further work
There is a number of further steps that could be undertaken to deepen the analysis and
to extend the diﬀerent topics discussed in Chapter 3 and Chapter 4. Unfortunately,
time constraints prohibited us from exploring some of the very interesting topics that
we listed below.
First of all, it would be highly beneﬁcial to conduct analysis of GNNs sensitivity
to diﬀerent hyperparameters. We expect that the number of layers and the embed-
ding dimension have the biggest inﬂuence, although the eﬀect of existence or lack of
dropout and batch normalization could also be studied in more detail.
Along the same vein, it would be interesting to analyze inﬂuence distributions
calculated for diﬀerent number of layers in a model. One might want to study the
eﬀect that the number of layers has on the size of the neighborhood that is truly

inﬂuential. In addition, inﬂuence distributions are an interesting way to study the
eﬀect of skip connections and the importance of target node’s features.
When analyzing the distribution of attention weights, entropy was used to measure
the level of concentration. However, entropy is directly dependent on the node degree
and it is not trivial to decouple this. Therefore, it might be advantageous to explore
other measures, such as Kullback-Leibler divergence, which is used to quantify the
discrepancy between two distributions. In this case the other distribution that the
attention weights should be compared to is a uniform distribution.
There is also a number of actions that can be performed in connection to the
EU judgements dataset. One of them is applying a relational graph neural network
(RGCN) [Sch+18] that recognizes diﬀerent types of edges in a graph by specifying
diﬀerent weight matrices for each edge type (Section 3.2.6). It would be beneﬁcial to
verify whether this approach can result in an even higher performance. Furthermore,
building a recommendation system would be an interesting task. For this purpose one
might reuse the already generated embeddings to recommend paragraphs based on
the similarity between them. However, the embeddings generated by GCN, GAT and
GraphSAGE were optimized for node classiﬁcation. It might be advantageous to use
other architectures that can adaptively balance the information about graph struc-
ture and similarity between node features to generate node representations [Jin+21].
Another way to tackle this problem would be to turn it into a link prediction task,
where the goal is to infer whether an edge exists between two paragraphs.
Lastly, in our report two diﬀerent methods were used in an attempt to interpret
predictions made by GNNs but this topic undoubtedly needs further exploration.
Therefore, as a further course of action we recommend exploring GNNExplainer model
[Yin+19], which is another very promising technique in this ﬁeld of research. GN-
NExplainer generates counterfactual explanations by maintaining only a subset of the
original neighborhood that minimizes the diﬀerence in prediction probabilities. This
way the nodes that have the highest inﬂuence on the prediction are revealed and the
irrelevant or redundant entities are removed. It is a truly versatile approach as it can
generate explanations at a level of a single node, a community, or a full graph.

We analyze the weighted instead of a macro F1-score on purpose. We do not ﬁnd

the same to the overall score as the metric for a class with 172199 instances. We
acknowledge that in many applications (such as fault diagnosis, medical diagnosis or

much more important than the majority class, and failing to do so in not acceptable.
However, in our scenario, most frequent classes are usually the most important ones.
As an example, in the Amazon products dataset we want the classiﬁer to have par-
ticularly good performance on products that are most popular on the platform, and
the least popular categories are usually of lower importance.
