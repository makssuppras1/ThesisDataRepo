Abstract (EN)
The demand for safe and clean drinking water is high. It was reported from WHO that in 2020, 2 billion people
worldwide did not have access to clean drinking water. Unsafe drinking water as well as poor sanitation are linked to
transmission of an array of diseases, herein diarrhoea. It is estimated that approximately 830 thousand people annually
die from diarrhoea as a result of unsafe drinking water, poor sanitation and hand hygiene.
Solar water disinfection (SODIS) is a commonly used household water treatment technique, where PET plastic containers
are ﬁlled with contaminated water and placed in direct sunlight for at least 6 hours, after which time it is safe to drink.
The SODIS technique utilizes the combination of solar UV-radiation and thermal energy to eliminate potential pathogenic
microorganisms that are present in the water.
4Life Solutions (formerly known as SolarSack) is a Danish based impact start-up company, whose mission is to bring
safe and affordable drinking water to the 2 billion people worldwide who currently do not have access to clean water.
4Life Solutions has created the SaWa bag, a product that is based on SODIS technology and functions as a water puriﬁer,
safe storage container, and water dispenser. The multi-layer plastic composition of the SaWa bag offers a high UV pen-
etration and the back of the bag increases the water temperature to above 45◦C thereby signiﬁcantly decreasing the time
to decontaminate the water in the sun. The SaWa bag is able to eliminate 99.99% of dangerous pathogens after exposure
to 4 hours of sunlight. Although the SaWa bag has been tested to perform well in both simulated and real-life conditions,
a few cases have been found where bioﬁlm has formed on the inside of a used SaWa bag. This formed the bases for the
present study in this master thesis project.
The present thesis investigates the bioﬁlm formation resistance of the SaWa bag, through the development and ap-
plication of an assay to quantify the bioﬁlm forming on the surface of the SaWa materials. The objectives were reached
through experiments in which the physiology and bioﬁlm formation properties of E. coli as a model microorganism were
evaluated. The results obtained from these experiments were used as a basis for developing an assay in which the bioﬁlm
formation resistance of SaWa materials were investigated. The assay was based on a crystal violet bioﬁlm assay, in which
modiﬁcations were included in order to ﬁt the assay to the SaWa material. These modiﬁcations involved using lake wa-
ter as a substrate to simulate surface water which is typically used as a drinking water source when using SaWa. As a
comparison, 10-fold diluted LB medium was also used as a substrate to simulate a more nutrient-rich environment. The
bioﬁlm formed on the SaWa materials was quantiﬁed by staining and extraction of crystal violet. The results from the
assay showed that bioﬁlm formation had occurred on the back side material of SaWa when incubated in 10-fold diluted
LB medium compared to the front where no bioﬁlm formation occurred.
The present study provides valuable insight into the complex procedure behind the principle of assay development.
It is concluded that the development and application of a crystal violet bioﬁlm formation assay using SaWa materials
showed that bioﬁlm was able to form on the surface of the back side SaWa material. However, the level of bioﬁlm for-
mation varied with different substrates. These ﬁnding gives rise to the assay having the potential to become the basis of a
standard assay to test the bioﬁlm formation resistance of SaWa. However, additional research, optimization and multiple
applications using different materials and simulations included in the assay is required, in order for the assay to become a
part of a test protocol for future evaluation of the bioﬁlm formation resistance of SaWa.

1.1
The demand for clean drinking water
Safe and readily available water is essential for public health, regardless of whether it is used for drinking, domestic
use, food production or recreational purposes. By improving the water supply, sanitation, and management of water re-
sources, it can greatly contribute to reduction in poverty [1]. In a 2017 report from the World Health Organization (WHO)
and the United Nations Children’s Fund (UNICEF), it was stated that in 2015, 5.2 billion people (71% of the global
70
population) had access to safe drinking water by using a safely managed drinking water service; that is, one located on
premises, available when needed and free of contamination. In contrast, 884 million people still lacked a basic drinking
water service, 263 million people spent over 30 minutes per round trip to collect water from an improved source (con-
stituting a limited drinking water service), while 159 million people still collected drinking water directly from surface
water sources (i.e. contaminated water sources) [2].
75
Subsequently in a 2021 report from WHO and UNICEF, a 5-year progress of the Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs) from 2015 to 2020 was described, herein the progress regarding safe drinking water. In the report, it was stated
that from 2015 to 2020, the proportion of the global population using safely managed drinking water services had in-
creased from 70% to 74% and the number of people without safely managed services decreased by 193 million. However
80
in 2020, 2 billion people still lacked safely managed services, including 1.2 billion people with basic services, 282 mil-
lion people with limited services, 367 million people using unimproved sources, and 122 million people collecting and
drinking surface water. Thus, the number of people without safely managed drinking water had increased from 884
million (in 2015) to 2 billion (in 2020). However, the number of people collecting drinking water directly from surface

1.3
Safe drinking water by solar water disinfection
Solar water disinfection (SODIS) is a commonly used household water treatment technique, in which transparent plas-
tic containers (usually polyethylene terephthalate - PET) are ﬁlled with contaminated water and placed in direct sunlight
for at least 6 hours, after which time it is safe to drink. The SODIS technique utilizes the combination of solar ultra-violet
(UV) radiation and thermal energy to eliminate the potential pathogenic microorganisms that are present in the water. The
135
wavelength ranges of UV are: 400-315 nm for UV-A; 315-280 nm for UV-B; and 280-100 nm for UV-C. UV-C radiation
is the highest in energy, but is retained by the ozone layer, and is therefore not present in the atmosphere. UV-A and UV-B
radiation are lower in energy and reach the earth’s surface and thus, play a role in SODIS. These types of radiation is
considered mutagenic physical agents as they cause damage in living organisms [12]. UV-A radiation plays an important
role in the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in water, such as singlet oxygen, superoxide, hydrogen peroxide,
140
and hydroxyl radical [13].
Of the ROS, superoxide is the largest oxidant. By the addition of a proton, it leads to the formation of hydrogen superox-
ide, which is a very active oxidizing agent. These sorts of transformations lead to the formation of an array of free radicals,
which initiate diverse reactions in the metabolism of the cell, which in turn leads to cell damage. Such reactions include;
lipid peroxidation in the polyunsaturated lipids of membranes; Protein denaturation and thus, inactivation; modiﬁcation
145
of bases in nucleic acids, thereby generating mutagenesis and carcinogenesis. In contrast, UV-B radiation directly targets
cellular DNA, by being absorbed and causing adverse effects on living systems such as bacteria and other microorganisms
as well as humans. Although UV-B radiation constitutes less than 1% of the total solar energy, it is still a highly active
component of solar radiation that causes chemical modiﬁcation in DNA and changes its molecular structure by formation
of dimers between adjacently located pyrimidine bases [14, 15].

Background Theory
3.1
Microbial bioﬁlms
A bioﬁlm is deﬁned as an assemblage of microorganisms that is irreversibly (i.e.: not removed by gentle rinsing)
associated with a biotic or abiotic surface and enclosed in a matrix of primarily polysaccharide material. The bioﬁlm may
be composed of one single or multiple species of microorganisms, and can form on a wide array of surfaces, including
210
living tissues, indwelling medical devices, industrial water system piping, or natural aquatic systems, as well as on plastic
surfaces [16–18].
Formation of bioﬁlms can occur in various types of environments and constitutes a major problem in the industry, in
the medical setting, in nature as well as in various other contexts. In the case of bioﬁlm formation in an industrial
setting, bioﬁlm formation is a major health concern in the food industry, as there are a multitude of locations in which
215
bioﬁlms can be formed. Bioﬁlms can be formed on any surface in every aspect of the food production pipeline. Examples
of such surfaces include liquid pipelines, pasteurizer plates, employee gloves, animal carcasses, and packing material.
Microbial strains often involved in bioﬁlm formation in the food industry involves Bacillus cereus, Escherichia coli,
Listeria monocytogenes, Salmonella enterica, and Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus), all of which through production
of various toxins contribute to a major health hazards, that may give rise to fatal consequences with infants, pregnant
220
women, and elderly individuals at particular risk [19]. In the case of bioﬁlm formation in a medical context, bacterial
bioﬁlms are involved in both the development of serious chronic infections as well as on the surface of medical devices,
such as catheters and cardiac pacemakers. Thus, bioﬁlms are of great importance in infection control and healthcare-
associated infections, due to their tolerance and ”resistance” to antimicrobial agents, and therefore pose a challenge in
treating healthcare-associated infections, thereby putting patients at risk [20]. An example of bioﬁlm occurring nature is
225
the formation of bioﬁlm on microplastic (< 5 mm in diameter). Due to plastic pollution microplastic is becoming a more
prominent concern due to its persistence and potential adverse effect on the environment and its biota. It has been recently
reported that microplastics in nature may promote the formation of bioﬁlm on its surfaces as well as increased expression
of antibiotic resistance genes. A previous microplastic bioﬁlm formation study has revealed that the bioﬁlm formed
on microplastics had a distinctive structure compared to natural substrates (rock and leaf). Additionally, metagenomic
230
analyses revealed a wide-spectrum and distinctive resistome in the microplastic bioﬁlm, and the opportunistic human
pathogens Pseudomonas monteilii, Pseudomonas mendocina, as well as the plant pathogen Pseudomonas syringae were
detected merely in the microplastic bioﬁlm and not in the bioﬁlms formed on natural substrates [21, 22].
3.1.1
Components of bioﬁlm
In the majority of bioﬁlms, the microorganisms account for less than 10% of the dry mass, while the matrix accounts
235
for the remaining 90%. The matrix is the extracellular material, in which the bioﬁlm cells are embedded, and is mostly
produced by the microorganisms themselves. The extracellular matrix consists of different types of biopolymers, which
are known as extracellular polymeric substances (EPS). The structure and components of bioﬁlm is illustrated in ﬁgure
3.1. EPS forms the basis of the three-dimensional structure of the bioﬁlm, and is responsible for adhesion to surfaces
and cohesion in the bioﬁlm. Additionally, EPS immobilize the bioﬁlm cells and keep them in close proximity, thereby
240
allowing strong interactions, including cell-cell communication, and formation of synergistic microconsortia [23]. These
microconsortia, also known as microcolonies, are the basic structural units of a bioﬁlm. The microcolonies are in most
cases mushroom-shaped or rodlike and can consist of a single or multiple types of microorganisms. EPS constitutes the
major fraction of the microcolonies, and protects the bioﬁlm cells from a wide array of environmental stresses, such as
UV radiation and abrupt changes in pH levels [24]. The different components that constitutes the bioﬁlm and EPS will be
245
described more detail in this section.

Water channels
Water channels are channels that are formed between the microcolonies through which water ﬂows. These water channels
function as a simple circulatory system, distributing nutrients to the microcolonies, while receiving harmful metabolites
250
[24].
Exopolysaccharides
Exopolysaccharides are polysaccharides which are synthesized by the microorganisms and released into the external en-
vironment. The ability to synthesize exopolysaccharides is widely common among microorganisms, and the exopolysac-
255
charides play an important role in bioﬁlm formation. Exopolysaccharides are therefore considered to be an important
component of the bioﬁlm matrix, and they constitute about 50-90% of the total organic matter in a bioﬁlm. The ex-
opolysaccharides are ascribed to a wide array of different roles in bioﬁlms, which includes adhesion, cell aggregation,
water retention, and cohesion of bioﬁlms. Exopolysaccharides act as a protective barrier against infection and antimicro-
bial agents, and it mediates accumulation of organic and inorganic compounds from the environment. Furthermore, the
260
exopolysaccharides serves as a nutrient source, providing carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorous containing compounds for
the microorganisms in the bioﬁlm to utilize [18, 25].
Extracellular proteins
The bioﬁlm matrix may contain signiﬁcant amounts of proteins that may exceed the content of polysaccharide on a mass
265
basis. Various extracellular enzymes have been detected in bioﬁlms, wherein many of them are involved in the degrada-
tion of biopolymers. The substrates of the extracellular enzymes include water-soluble polymers (such as polysaccharides,
proteins, and nucleic acids), as well as water-insoluble compounds (such as cellulose, chitin, and lipids). The presence
of enzymes which degrade the components of EPS, makes the matrix an external digestive system, that breaks down
biopolymers to low-molecular-mass products that can in turn be taken up and used as carbon sources and other sources of
270
energy. Some enzymes may be involved in the degradation of EPS to promote the detachment of bacteria from bioﬁlms
[23].
Extracellular DNA
The genetics and the environment in which the microorganisms grow are determining factors for the composition of a
275
bioﬁlm. Extracellular DNA (eDNA) is an important component of bioﬁlm that was recently discovered. The presence
of eDNA was initially observed in bioﬁlms of Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa), Streptococcus intermedius, and
Streptococcus mutans. Afterwards, eDNA was discovered in Enterococcus faecalis and Staphylococcus. The most com-
mon mechanism by which eDNA is released is by autolysis, a process in which cells are destroyed by their self-produced

enzymes. Release of eDNA plays a crucial role in the development and stabilization of the bioﬁlm, as previous studies

have shown that the addition of DNAse inhibits the formation of bioﬁlm. Furthermore, eDNA represents an important
mechanism for horizontal gene transfer between bacteria [26, 27].
3.1.2
The process of bioﬁlm formation
Bioﬁlms are often formed in response to changes in the surrounding environment. Such changes involve nutrient
285
availability, oxygen levels, pH, and temperature. These external stress factors inﬂuence the behavior of the cells as they
give rise to intracelullar responses. The intracellular responses include genetic and metabolic regulation which in turn
changes the physiological behaviour of the cell. The regulation makes the cell able to attach onto and move across a sur-
face, detect cell density, and ultimately form a 3-dimensional mesh of cells enclosed by exopolysaccharides. Additionally,
the presence of membrane proteins, extracellular polysaccharides, and signaling molecules also plays an important role in
290
the formation of bioﬁlm [18, 28]. The formation of bioﬁlms is divided into ﬁve major steps.
1. Attachment: During the attachment process, a conditioning layer is formed, which consists of a loose collection of
carbohydrates and proteins, which along with minerals get unite in hard water. This conditioning layer attracts the
microbial cells to get attached to the surface.
295
2. Irreversible attachment: As soon as the conditioning layer has been formed, electrical charges accumulate on
the surface of the layer, which attract microorganisms having an opposite charge. This results in the irreversible
attachment of the microorganisms. These charges er sufﬁciently weak that the microorganisms could be easily
removed by mild cleansers and sanitizers.
3. Proliferation: During this phase, the microorganisms get attached to the surface as well as with each other through
300
secretion of EPS, that encloses the cells within a glue-like matrix. The cells will begin to proliferate, thereby
forming microcolonies.
4. Maturation: In the maturation phase, the bioﬁlm matures by supporting the rapid growth of microorganisms. Com-
plex diffusion channels are present in the mature bioﬁlm to transport nutrients, oxygen as well as other components
important for microbial growth. Waste products and dead cells are simultaneously removed.
305
5. Dispersion: The dispersion process involves the dispersal of bioﬁlm in which actively growing cells gradually shed
daughter cells. As long as fresh nutrients are provided, the bioﬁlm will continue to grow. When the bioﬁlm is
deprived of nutrients, the microorganisms return to their planktonic state by detaching themselves from the surface.
By detaching from the surface, it allows the microorganisms to obtain sufﬁcient nutrients. These specialized cells
are able to colonize new surfaces and restart the bioﬁlm formation cycle [18, 28–30].

3.1.3
Factors inﬂuencing bioﬁlm formation
The solid-liquid interface between a surface and a medium gives rise to an ideal environment for the attachment and
growth of microorganisms. However, certain factors must be taken into consideration before a clear picture of microbial
attachment can be obtained. Such factors include the effects of the substratum, conditioning ﬁlms, characteristics of the
315
medium, environmental factors, and the properties of the microorganisms.
Substratum effects
The solid surface may have an array of different characteristics, that play an important role in the attachment process.
It has previously been reported that the extent of microbial colonization appears to increase as the surface roughness
320
increases. This is due to the fact that the shear forces are reduced and that the surface area is higher on rougher surfaces.
For example, previous studies have suggested that E. coli O157:H7 strain a higher degree of bioﬁlm formation on the
surface of both stainless steel and glass, compared to plastic (polypropylene) surfaces. The difference in the results may
have been due to the hydrophobic nature of the polypropylene material [32]. Alternatively, the substratum may be ma-
nipulated in order to obtain a smoother surface. Other previous bioﬁlm formation studies using S. aureus suggested that
325
by patterning laboratory-coated paper with polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS)-based ink, the level of bioﬁlm formation on
the PDMS-coated surface was reduced in comparison to the paper substrates coated with latex, Kaolin, and precipitated
calcium carbonate. This was due to the smooth and hydrophobic nature of the PDMS-based ink [33].
Conditioning ﬁlms
330
The initial attachment of microorganisms to a surface is the preliminary step in bioﬁlm formation, and is dependent on the
formation of conditioning ﬁlms. Conditioning ﬁlms are the result of adsorption of naturally occurring dissolved organic
matter (DOM) or extracellular polymeric substances secreted by microorganisms to a solid surface. Conditioning ﬁlms
may be composed of proteins, lipids, polysaccharides, aromatic amino acids as well as many other unspeciﬁed macro-
16 of 61
==PAGE:18==
January 23rd, 2022
molecules. The presence of conditioning ﬁlms may have a signiﬁcant impact on the initial adhesion of bacteria by altering
335
the physico-chemical properties of the solid surfaces, such as surface tension, charge density and roughness [34, 35]. The
formation of conditioning ﬁlms is dependent, among other things, on the properties of the material on which the bioﬁlm is
formed. Previous studies investigating the formation of conditioning ﬁlms on polylactic acid plastic and PET, suggested
that polylactic acid plastic was more prone to accumulation of biomolecules from the surrounding environment, due to
the hydrophilic nature of the plastic material. This lead to a higher surface roughness, and thereby favoring attachment
340
of microorganisms [36]. In addition to substrate properties, the content of DOM in the aqueous medium, as well as the
microorganisms present in the medium may also have an inﬂuence on the formation of conditioning ﬁlms [37].
Properties of the microorganisms
During the bioﬁlm formation process, once irreversible attachment has occurred, the cells will begin to proliferate and
345
generate microcolonies. Within the microcolonies, the cells communicate with one another through quorum sensing (QS)
systems, a communication system based on chemical signaling. The QS system plays an important role in modulation of
cellular functions, population density-based pathogenesis, nutrient acquisition, transfer of genetic material among cells,
motility, and synthesis of secondary metabolites [38]. QS is dependent on the production, release, detection, and response
to extracellular signaling molecules, which are known as autoinducers. The response to the autoinducers is usually initia-
350
tion of gene expression for coordinated activities. All bacteria possess a QS system depending on the particular strain. QS
systems in bacteria have in general been divided into three classes: (1) is the LuxI/LuxR QS system in Gram-negative bac-
teria that uses acyl-homoserine lactones as autoinducers, (2) is the oligopeptide-two-component-type QS system found
in Gram-positive bacteria, which use small peptides as autoinducers, and lastly (3) is the LuxS-encoded autoinducer 2
(AI-2) QS system that is found in both Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria. Each type of autoinducer is detected
355
and responded to by a precise sensing system and regulatory mechanism [39, 40]. QS systems play an essential role in
bioﬁlm formation. It has previously been reported that chemical synthesis of QS speciﬁc inhibitors in P. aeruginosa, will
give rise to covalent binding to LasR, an important transcriptional activator in the QS system of the microorganism. This
resulted in inhibition of QS-regulated gene expression and subsequent reduction in virulence factor secretion and bioﬁlm
formation [41].
360
E. coli is one of the strains in which the LuxS-encoded autoinducer 2 (AI-2) QS-system is found. Here, the AI-2
acts as a signaling molecule for interspecies communication which directly initiates bioﬁlm formation through a motil-
ity QS regulator (MqsR). Studies have shown that a mutation of the LuxS gene (encoding the AI-2 synthetase) not only
inhibited the production of AI-2, but also signiﬁcantly reduced the bioﬁlm biomass in E. coli. The study also suggested
365
that mutation in the lrsK gene (encoding the AI-2 kinase) resulted in failure of expressing the lsr transporter apparatus.
This in turn, gave rise to accumulation of AI-2, which negatively affected the percentage of bioﬁlm biomass [42]. Recent
studies have shown that there is a wide array of non-clinical E. coli strains which have a high potential of forming bioﬁlm,
one of which is periphytic E. coli. Periphytic E. coli are found in fresh-water aquatic environments. Previous studies in
which periphytic E. coli was isolated and investigated regarding their bioﬁlm formation competencies suggested that their
370
bioﬁlm formation capacities were signiﬁcantly higher in comparison to the isolates from the other groupings. The other
groupings included human E. coli isolates, bovine E. coli isolates and STEC (Shiga Toxin-producing E. coli) isolates [43,
44].
Although most bioﬁlm research have been focusing on single-species bioﬁlm, the most predominant form of bioﬁlms
375
occurring in nature consists of multiple species of microorganisms. The combination of multiple species of microorgan-
isms may have a negative impact on the individual species which are present in the bioﬁlm. One study which involved
Vibrio parahaemolyticus and Listeria monocytogenes showed that the formation of mono-species bioﬁlm of the individ-
ual strains were higher, compared to the mix-species bioﬁlm in which both species were involved. Further experimental
analysis revealed that the gene expression of certain genes involved in bioﬁlm-regulation was down-regulated in the
380
mixed-species bioﬁlm as well as loss of of metabolic activity [45]. Another study which involved Delftia tsuruhatensis
SJ01 and clinical isolates wild-type P. aeruginosa PAO1 and P. aeruginosa PAH, showed that the bacterial extract from
Delftia tsuruhatensis had anti-QS properties by inhibiting the production of violacein, an indicator of QS activity. Fur-

Environmental factors
Bioﬁlm formation is largely affected by the environment. Depending on the environment, the cells will respond by altering
genetic expression. Environmental factors such as temperature, oxygen levels, nutrient levels, as well as accumulation of
metabolites will determine whether the cells form or leave the bioﬁlm, implying that bioﬁlms are constructed to adapt to
local conditions. The underlying second messengers responsible for linking environmental factors to genetic regulation is
395
cyclic adenosine 3’,5’-monophosphate (cAMP) and cyclic dimeric guanosine 3’,5’ monophosphate (c-di-GMP) [48]. In
he case of the former, cAMP binds to the cAMP receptor protein (CRP), thereby generating a cAMP-CRP complex. Pre-
vious studies investigating the function of cAMP-CRP in E. coli, have shown that cAMP-CRP mediates the synthesis of
curli ﬁbers, which are an important components of the extracellular matrix in bioﬁlm as they are involved in the adhesion
and attachment of bacteria to a surface. Additionally, cAMP-CRP regulates ﬂagellum biosynthesis, which is essential for
400
bacterial motility and chemotaxis. Lastly, cAMP-CRP directly inhibits the transcription of the rpoS gene, which inhibits
the initiation bioﬁlm formation. Thus, cAMP-CRP directly promotes bioﬁlm formation by repressing transcription of
rpoS [49].
In the case of c-di-GMP, the level of this metabolite is modulated in response to different environmental changes,
405
herein oxygen, nitrogen and carbon limitations as well as environmental cues such as nitric oxide and temperature and
light. Depending on the level of c-di-GMP, it will result in bioﬁlm formation (if the level of c-di-GMP is high) or viru-
lence and dispersal (if the level of c-di-GMP is low). The microorganisms in which c-di-GMP is modulated includes Xan-
thomonas camprestis, Legionella pneumophila, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Pseudomonas putida, and Shewanella onei-
densis [50].
410
3.1.4
Methods for studying bioﬁlms
As bioﬁlm formation constitutes a major challenge to public health, different methods have been employed to analyze
bioﬁlm formation and development, both quantitatively and qualitatively. Some assays are suitable for quantiﬁcation of
the bioﬁlm matrix, while other assays are capable of evaluating both living and dead cells or exclusively quantifying vi-
able cells [51]. Additionally, different types of assays are available for studying different stages of bioﬁlm formation. An
415
example of this is by the use of a Robbins Device, which is a device that is suitable for investigation of early-stage as well
as late-stage bioﬁlm formation. By using this device, adhesion, maturation, and detachment can be analyzed. Moreover,
this apparatus makes it possible to reproduce real environments, and to test multiple materials simultaneously. However,
some of the limitations involved in this apparatus is the fact that it is not possible to test multiple microorganisms simul-
taneously, and that the reproducibility of the experiments is poor.
420
An example of a device to study merely early-stage bioﬁlm formation is the Calgary bioﬁlm device, as this device can
be used to study adhesion, detachment as well as susceptibility to anti-microbial materials. An assay using this device
also makes it possible to test multiple materials and conditions simultaneously, and there is a high experimental repro-
ducibility. However, one of the limitations of this device is that the reproducibility of real environments is poor[52].
425
A common and simple assay for investigating early stage bioﬁlm formation is to use a 96-well microtiter plate to
incubate the bioﬁlm and staining the bioﬁlm with a crystal violet (CV) solution. CV is a basic dye which binds indiffer-
ently to negatively charged bacteria and polysaccharides of the EPS matrix. After staining, acetic acid or ethanol is used
to extract the CV and the absorbance is quantiﬁed using a plate reader. The absorbance is directly proportional to the
430
18 of 61
==PAGE:20==
January 23rd, 2022
bioﬁlm biomass. This assay may be modiﬁed in order to investigate the anti-bioﬁlm properties of an agent or a material
by coating the surface of the wells in the microtiter plate, or alternatively, inserting a novel material into the wells. After
a speciﬁc time period in which bioﬁlm formation has occurred, the wells are emptied or the material previously inserted
in the well is carefully removed. The surfaces are then washed and the number of adhering microorganisms is assessed
[52]. Although this type of assay is fairly simple, there are some limitations. First, CV stains both viable and dead cells,
435
as well as any material adhering to the surface of the plate. Therefore, staining with CV may overestimate the number
of adherent bacteria. Other limitations are related the low reproducibility of the method, i.e.: experimental condition of
bioﬁlm growth, the speciﬁc nature and concentration of the solvent and the elution time of the CV are crucial steps [51,
53, 54].
440
Another type of assay is the LIVE/DEAD BacLight Assay. This type of assay is based on the use of two nucleic acid-
binding stains: Syto9, which stains all the cells green and propidium iodide, which penetrates cells whose membranes has
been damaged, thereby staining them red. Thus, viable cells will ﬂuoresce green while dead cells will ﬂuoresce red/orange
and viable and total counts can be obtained in one staining step. Additionally, the ﬂuorescence is high with a strong con-
trast between the red and green ﬂuorescing cells and the background ﬂuorescence is minimal. The main drawback of this
445
assay is the need of observing a statically relevant portion of the sample representing the entire bacterial population. As
is it not possible to obtain a total count of bacterial cells, this method only provides a semi-quantitative result [51, 55].
Another limitation involved in the LIVE/DEAD assay is the fact that it is used to detect early-stage bioﬁlm formation, and
is not suited for late-stage bioﬁlm formation. Previous studies have shown that LIVE/DEAD assays using BacLight as
well as various other stains will provide adequate visualization of viable and dead cells, particularly in the early stages of
450
bioﬁlm formation. Other studies have reported that the LIVE/DEAD stain coverage may be reduced in mature bioﬁlms,
due to the accumulation of EPS compared to early-stage bioﬁlms. The excess of EPS may prevent the stain from diffusing
through to the center of the bioﬁlm, thereby resulting in a reduced coverage [56, 57].
Aside from the described methods for studying bioﬁlms, advanced microscopic techniques may also be employed to
455
visually investigate bioﬁlm formation, such as electron microscopy and confocal laser scanning microscopy. Additionally,
genetic methods such as PCR and ﬂuorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) may be an approach for bioﬁlm formation
studies. Thus, a standard method for bioﬁlm formation has yet to be determined [51].
For this master thesis project, bioﬁlm formation on SaWa material will be evaluated. Therefore, a relevant method
460
for assessing this matter should fulﬁll a number of criteria. Such criteria should involve affordability and accessibility
regarding both the consumables to be used in the assay, the machinery for maintenance of microorganisms, as well as the
apparatus used for quantiﬁcation and analysis of the bioﬁlm formation. The environmental parameters to be considered
in this method should be modiﬁable so that natural environmental conditions can be simulated in the lab. Moreover, the
method should be sufﬁciently simple to be reproduced by others, and should be applicable to other microorganisms.

Selection of model microorganisms
The proper choice of microorganisms to be used in any kind of experimental work is crucial. Microorganisms which
are well-characterized, easy to grow, cultivate, and maintain in the lab are the optimal microorganisms to be used as models
for experimental work [52, 58]. When it comes to the choice of microorganism to be used in bioﬁlm formation assays,
the most determining factor of whether or not to use a certain microorganism is if the microorganism phenotypically
470
exhibit strong bioﬁlm formation properties, with certain strains being more prominent in their bioﬁlm formation abilities
compared to others [59]. For this master thesis project, the model microorganisms used should be relevant in the contexts
of SaWa, i.e., it should be microorganisms which would be present when SaWa is used for disinfecting contaminated
surface water.

3.2.1
Escherichia coli
475
Escherichia coli (E. coli) is a rod-shaped, gram-negative proteobacterium that belongs to the family of Enterobac-
tericeae, and is a member of the fecal coliform group of bacteria. The primary habitat of E. coli is thought to be inside the
lower intestine of warm-blooded animals, including humans. It is estimated that approximately 90-99% of all coliforms
in fecal material are E. coli bacteria, and because the organism is present in high concentration in the gut ﬂora of healthy
animals, E. coli has been identiﬁed as an ideal fecal indicator. Although most E. coli are considered harmless to humans,
480
certain pathogenic strains of E. coli can invade and infect the gastrointestinal system, thereby causing severe illness. In-
fection with pathogenic E. coli usually gives rise to severe diarrhea. Diarrhea is the result of the reversal of the normal
net absorptive status of water and electrolyte absorption to secretion. The pathogenic E. coli can occur in soil and water,
and this occurrence is a result of these cells being released into the environment (their secondary habitat) through fecal
deposition [60–62].
485
E. coli is the most obvious model microorganism to use in a bioﬁlm formation study due to the fact that E. coli is well-
characterized, has a short generation time and it is easy to cultivate in the lab [63]. Secondly, E. coli has extensively been
used as a model microorganism for bioﬁlm studies [64–66]. In addition, E. coli is used as the indicator microorganism in
performance studies of the SaWa bag, and occurs in the naturally contaminated water which is puriﬁed by SaWa in the
490
contexts in which SaWa is implemented.
3.2.2
Oscillatoria sancta
Cyanobacteria (commonly known as blue-green algae) are photosynthetic microorganisms, that play a key role in the
oxygenation of the earth’s atmosphere, and can occupy any terrestrial ecosystem including freshwater, marine and soil
habitats. Massive proliferation of cyanobacteria, dominated toxic cyanobacteria constitutes a serious concern regarding
495
public health, as certain species of cyanobacteria are able to synthesize secondary metabolites, including neurotoxins [67].
Seasonal changes in light, water temperature, ﬂow, nutrient availability (primarily nitrogen and phosphorous), and salin-
ity are all factors that may contribute to cyanobacterial growth and ampliﬁcation of cyanotoxin production. High-density
growths (cyanobacterial blooms) often appear as bright green scum or mats on water surfaces and are a known health
hazard because of the increased concentration of bacteria and their resultant toxins. Cyanotoxins are classiﬁed by the type
500
of cell or tissue they affect: cytotoxins (cell toxicity), hepatotoxins (liver), dermatotoxins (skin), and neurotoxins (nervous
system) [68].
Oscillatoria sancta (O. sancta) belongs to the genus of Oscillatoria and consists of cyanobacteria that produce ﬁl-
aments of cells known as trichomes. Oscillatoria are able to ﬁx molecular nitrogen from the atmosphere through the
505
synthesis of the enzyme nitrogenase, and is usually non-heterocystous. However, Oscillatoria may differentiate into het-
erocysts, if the concentration of dissolved inorganic nitrogen is absent. Heterocysts are non-dividing cells that contain
the nitrogenase enzyme, but are unable to perform oxygenic photosynthesis. Additionally, it has previously been re-
ported that nitrogenase is sensitive towards molecular oxygen and therefore, several species of ﬁlamentous cyanobacteria
has developed heterocysts [69]. O. sancta is one of the many genera of cyanobacteria that produces a wide variety of
510
different cyanotoxins, all of which fall under the categories of cyclic peptides, alkaloids and non-protein amino acids.
Cyanotoxins negatively affect both the liver, the skin, and the nervous system. Aside from Oscillatoria, examples of other
cyanotoxin-producing genera of cyanobacteria include Microcystis, Nostoc, Lyngbya, Microcoleus, and Synechococcus
[70]. Although cyanobacteria are ubiquitous, they dominate the bioﬁlms in tropical environments. This is due to the fact
that they are better at tolerating the higher temperatures compared to other species of microorganisms [71, 72]. Since
515
the SaWa bag has been primarily implemented in countries located in tropical regions of the world, it would therefore be
highly relevant to include cyanobacteria in the assay development for assessing bioﬁlm formation on SaWa. Additionally,
in one of the cases in which bioﬁlm formation had been observed on the inside of the SaWa bag, a pre-liminary meta-
genomics study was performed. The study showed that the bioﬁlm inside the bag was a multi-species bioﬁlm, in which
cyanobacteria was the dominant species, constituting 46% of the bacteria. Of the cyanobacteria, Oscillatoria was one of
520
the main genera.

3.3
Assay Development for assessing bioﬁlm formation on SaWa
As mentioned in the previous section (section 3.1.4), there is no standard experimental method for investigating
bioﬁlm formation, as it is dependent on which aspect of bioﬁlm formation it is desired to investigate. As commonly used
approaches for bioﬁlm studies does not apply to the plastic material of the SaWa bag, it is therefore necessary to develop
525
an assay that is especially catered to test the bioﬁlm formation resistance of the SaWa material. The process of developing
such an assay will consist of two stages. The ﬁrst stage will involve the characterization of the growth kinetics and bioﬁlm
formation abilities of the selected model microorganisms and the second stage will be the development of a potential
assay, including optimization and possible application to other microorganisms as well as alternative plastic materials.
Once the baseline of the assay set-up has been proposed, optimization of different factors that may inﬂuence the results
530
of the assay may be taken into consideration. Firstly, factors such as temperature, pH, and nutrient levels (which would
include different simulations of nutrient levels) may be considered as they are the primary factors inﬂuencing bioﬁlm
formation. Afterwards, more speciﬁc parameters may be taken into consideration. Such parameters include simulation
of different contamination levels using E. coli, potential use of water gathered from different sources (as different levels
of DOM may inﬂuence bioﬁlm formation), as well as possible addition of other microorganisms, thereby assessing the
535
bioﬁlm formation resistance of the SaWa material using a multi-species bioﬁlm. By slowly taking each parameter in to
consideration, the possibility of simulating natural environments in the lab will increase.

it would not be expected that the OD600 in the wells containing cells in lake water would be higher than those containing
1010
cells in 10-fold diluted LB medium, as LB medium is a very nutrient-rich.
In regards to bioﬁlm formation in the implementation of this assay, there was a higher degree of bioﬁlm formation
on both the front and the back of the SaWa plastic material which had been incubating on top of surface lake water
compared to those which had been incubated on top of 10-fold diluted LB medium (ﬁgure 5.9c and ﬁgure 5.9d). The
1015
ﬁndings in this ﬁgure highly deviates from the theory in that it would be expected that there would be a higher degree of
bioﬁlm formation on the plastic pieces which had been incubating in 10-fold diluted LB medium due to the high levels of
nutrients. Furthermore, the OD570 of the front side plastic material of the SaWa increased from after 48 hours to 72 hours
of incubation (ﬁgure 5.9c), while the OD570 of the back side material of the SaWa bag decreased from 48 hours to 72 hours

1020
the assay. The results deviated from the expected behaviour of bioﬁlm formation. Bacteria incubated in 10-fold diluted
LB medium would be expected to generate a higher level of bioﬁlm due to the higher content of nutrients in the medium
compared to lake water which has a scarce nutrient level [88]. However, what needs to be taken into consideration is the
fact that no optimizations of the assay had been included when the assay was implemented for the ﬁrst trial.
Therefore, the following optimizations were included in order to eliminate the factors which could have given rise to the
1025
large variations in the results:
• Uniform sizes of SaWa plastic samples: During the ﬁrst implementation of the assay, the SaWa plastic samples
were roughly cut to an appropriate size so they would ﬁt in the wells of a 6-well tissue culture test plate. The
slight variations in size gave rise to differences in degrees of bioﬁlm formation on the inward surfaces of the plastic
samples. In order to minimize this variation, a circular piece of paper with a diameter of 2.5 cm was used as a
1030
stencil to cut out the SaWa plastic samples, thereby ensuring the samples having a uniform shape and size.
• Measurement of OD600 of overnight cultures before the washing process: In the ﬁrst trial of the assay, the
washing step of the overnight cultures was initiated without measuring the OD600, i.e.: the optical density of the
overnight cultures was not controlled, and therefore there was a certain degree of uncertainty regarding whether the
cells had reached stationary phase before being washed. Additionally, during the washing steps, some cell biomass
1035
was also lost, which was not taken into consideration. To ensure that the cells have reached stationary phase prior
to washing the OD600 was thus measured before initiating the washing step.
• Increasing the working volume when incubating the SaWa plastic samples in the 6-well test plates: During
the washing step of the overnight cultures, a 2 mL aliquot of the overnight culture was taken and washed in either
surface lake water or 10-fold diluted LB medium, after which the washed sample was loaded into the well of the
1040
6-well culture plate. Thus, the working volume of each well was 2 mL, and the cells in the washed samples merely
utilized the nutrients present in the substrate they had been washed in. The working volume of the wells is 3 mL,
so by adding an extra mL of either surface lake water or 10-fold diluted LB medium, the presence of additional
nutrients may promote bioﬁlm formation, thereby optimizing the assay.

1135

Another alternative to this assay could be the measurement of anti-bacterial activities on plastic surfaces as it is de-
scribed in ISO Standard 22196:2007: Plastics - Measurement of antibacterial activity on plastics surfaces [93].
1140
The procedure described in this standard is initiated by preparation of the test samples in the dimensions of 50± 2 mm
× 50± 2 mm of the material of interest (the test specimen). An overnight culture of the microorganism of interest is
prepared and is diluted to a bacterial concentration between 2.5 × 105 cells per mL and 10 × 105 cells per mL with a
target concentration of 6 × 105 cells per mL (the test inoculum). The test specimen is then laid in a sterile Petri dish
with the surface to be tested facing up. 400 µL of the test inoculum is then pipetted on top of the test specimen. The test
1145
inoculum is covered with a piece of ﬁlm cut in the dimensions of 40± 2 mm × 40± 2mm. The ﬁlm is gently pressed
against the test inoculum so the inoculum spreads to the edges of the ﬁlm, making sure to not let the inoculum leak beyond
the edges of the ﬁlm. The plates are then incubated at the number of attached cells per cm2 of test specimen is assessed.

the SaWa plastic materials. Therefore, if this method is to be used to investigate the bioﬁlm formation resistance of the
SaWa materials, some adaptations may be taken into consideration. As mentioned, the SaWa plastic materials are not
1155
anti-microbial, so preparing a test inoculum with a target concentration of 6 × 105 cells per mL may be too high of a
concentration, and the number of adhering bacterial cells will be difﬁcult to estimate. Therefore, it may be necessary to
further dilute the solution, thus making it more plausible to estimate the number of adhering cells per cm2 of material.
These adaptations may be accessible. However, when additional factors need to be taken into consideration, the challenges
arise. One challenge is the fact that this method needs to be adapted in a way so that simulations of real-life implementation
1160
of the SaWa bag can be possible. One approach is to make smaller sachets of the SaWa bag, ﬁll the sachet with a certain
concentration of bacteria to simulate a speciﬁc contamination level and incubate it under UV light. The sachet is then
opened, emptied of its contents and a 50 mm × 50 mm piece of the sachet may be cut out, rinsed and incubated on top of
an agar plate. The plastic material is then removed and the agar plate is then incubated at 37◦C overnight. The number of
CFU per cm2 is then assessed. However, some of the challenges in this approach may affect the ﬁnal result. One of the
1165
challenges is when the sachet is cut open, some of the SaWa material (and adhering bacterial cells) may be lost. Therefore,
it is very important to consider how the plastic sachets will be constructed. With the desired dimensions of the material
being 50 mm × 50 mm, the dimensions of the sachet need to be sufﬁciently larger. With the dimensions of the plastic
materials being larger, it will also be more difﬁcult to estimate the number of CFU per cm2 of material, as there may not
be a consistent number of adherent cells on the entire surface of the material.

Future Perspectives
Throughout the experimental work of this master thesis project, bioﬁlm formation of E. coli ATCC 15597 to the surface
of the front and the backside material of the SaWa bag has been investigated and conﬁrmed. However, the ﬁndings in this
report is merely the beginning of a long and complex process in investigating the bioﬁlm formation resistance of SaWa. As
mentioned in section 6.3.4, there are possible alternatives for studying the bioﬁlm formation resistance of SaWa which can
1175
be used, herein a method described in the ISO Standard 22196:2007. However, what needs to be taken into account when
considering the method is the fact that the estimated number of adherent cells on a surface does not necessarily directly
correlate with bioﬁlm biomass, as bioﬁlm biomass consists of additional molecular components aside from cells [20].
Therefore, it may be necessary to perform the assay proposed in this study (ﬁgure5.8) in combination with inspiration
taken from the method described in ISO Standard 22196:2007. Through this combination, both the number of adherent
1180
cells per cm2 of the SaWa material surface as well as the bioﬁlm biomass can be estimated. This modiﬁcation of the
assay needs to be performed under simulated natural conditions, i.e., using lake water as a substrate. Moreover, a speciﬁc
concentration of E. coli ATCC 15597 needs to be determined before initiating the assay, in order to simulate a certain
level of fecal contamination using E. coli as an indicator microorganism. The different fecal contamination levels that can
occur in unsafe natural drinking water sources on low-income communities in Africa are deﬁned in the table below [94].

7.1
Further modiﬁcations and simulations to be included in the assay
The proposal of the optimized assay shown in ﬁgure 7.3 is merely the basis for further modiﬁcations and consid-
erations. In this assay, simulations of natural conditions (i.e., the use of lake water) and fecal contamination levels are
included. Additional environmental simulated conditions may be taken into consideration in the future application of this
assay. Such conditions include:
1225
• The Xenon Test Chamber: The Xenon Test Chamber is an apparatus which is able to reproduce full-spectrum
sunlight. Moreover, an example of an additional feature with the Xenon Test Chamber is adjustable temperatures,
which can be set at any point between 35◦C and 103◦C [95]. Thus, by incubating the plates inside the chamber,
it will be possible to properly investigate the bioﬁlm formation resistance of the SaWa materials by simulating
the environmental conditions (solar radiation and temperature levels) under which the SaWa is used under natural
1230
conditions.
• Turbidity: Turbidity is deﬁned as the measure of the relative clarity of a liquid. Turbidity is an optical measurement
of the amount of light which is scattered by the material in the water when light is shined through a water sample.
The higher the intensity of scattered light, the higher the turbidity and the turbidity is expressed in nephelometric
turbidity units (NTU) [96]. Because the drinking water sources in low-income communities can have varying
1235
turbidities, different levels of turbidity may be included with the upper limit of turbidity being < 30 NTU for proper
use of the SaWa bag. For simulating the turbidity of a water sample, 4Life Solutions use ISO 12103-1, A2 ﬁne
test dust, which is also used in one of the WHO general testing protocols to evaluate household water treatment
technologies [97].
• Different types of water sources as substrates or addition of nutrients to the substrate: As discussed previously,
1240
the presence of dissolved organic matter may have an inﬂuence on the formation of conditioning ﬁlms formed on
the surface of the SaWa material, which will in turn, affect the bioﬁlm formation [34]. Thus, it may be necessary to
use other water samples as substrate for the assay, as the differences in the content of DOM may inﬂuence bioﬁlm
formation. Alternatively, nutrients such as phosphorous and nitrogen may be added to the bacterial cultures.
• Application of the assay to combinations of microorganisms: Application of the assay to E. coli ATCC 15597
1245
will only be the beginning. Bioﬁlms in nature rarely consist of a single species, but is usually a combination
of multiple species of microorganisms. Thus, it will be ideal to apply this assay to combinations of different
microorganisms. Such organisms can be the ﬁlamentous cyanobacterium O. sancta, or other cyanobacteria such as
Mycrocystis. Other relevant microorganisms, which can be found in natural drinking water sources in low-income
communities in Africa may also be used. Such microorganisms may include fungi [98].
1250
The factors mentioned above can initially be taken into consideration one by one to investigate how the factors individually
affect the bioﬁlm formation resistance of the SaWa materials. Once this stage has been completed, different combinations
of the factors may be taken into consideration. By gradually applying the considerations in different combinations, it will
be possible to investigate whether these factors will have a positive or negative impact on the bioﬁlm formation resistance
of the SaWa plastic materials.

Application of the assay and modiﬁcations to investigate the bioﬁlm formation resistance
of used SaWa plastic materials and anti-microbial plastic materials
The assay along with its modiﬁcations may initially be applied to a new SaWa bag which has not been used, but may
also be applied to investigate the bioﬁlm formation resistance of a used SaWa bag, as well as alternative plastic materials.
In terms of applying the assay to investigate the bioﬁlm formation resistance of a used bag, the bioﬁlm formation resistance
1260
may differ depending on how long the bag has been used. In terms of alternative plastic materials, two antimicrobial plastic
materials are currently being considered. The ﬁrst material is from BioCote. The plastic material is constructed so that
the surface of the material has been coated with silver ions, which possess anti-microbial properties. Both case studies
performed by BioCote as well as other previous studies revealed that adhesion of the bacteria to a BioCote-treated surface
greatly reduced the number of viable cells compared to surfaces which were not treated with BioCote [99, 100]. The
1265
second material is from parx materials. The plastic material from parx materials has been produced to have a built-in
resistance against bacterial adhesion and proliferation through incorporation of a biocide called ”Saniconcentrate” during
the plastic manufacturing process. Saniconcentrate merely constitutes 3% of the plastic material, while the remaining
97% consist of the plastic material of interest [101–103].

8
Conclusion
1270
The aim of the present thesis was to develop a test protocol which could be used to evaluate the bioﬁlm formation
resistance of the SaWa bag. The experimental work conducted in the present study constituted the basis for this protocol,
and it involved the development of a bioﬁlm formation assay which was modiﬁed to ﬁt the SaWa bag material using
different E. coli strains.
1275
Throughout the present study, it was found that there was a prominent difference in both the colony morphology and
bioﬁlm formation properties between the two E. coli strains, with E. coli ATCC 15597 being better at forming bioﬁlm
compared to E. coli NCTC 9001. The assay which was developed to evaluate the bioﬁlm formation resistance of SaWa
material was based on a bioﬁlm assay using crystal violet. However, the assay was modiﬁed in order to investigate the
formation of bioﬁlm on surfaces, thereby ﬁtting to the SaWa bag material. Through ﬁrst trials and optimizations of the
1280
assay, it was conﬁrmed that by conducting this assay, the bioﬁlm occurring on the surface of the SaWa materials could be
quantiﬁed. Through this assay, it was found that E. coli ATCC 15597 was able to form bioﬁlm on the back side material
of the SaWa bag with the levels of bioﬁlm formation varying with different substrates.
The experimental journey throughout this master thesis project has resulted in an assay that may potentially help 4Life
1285
Solutions in evaluating the bioﬁlm resistance of different plastic materials and choose the best material for the SaWa
product in their goal of helping the 2 billion people worldwide get access to clean drinking water.
