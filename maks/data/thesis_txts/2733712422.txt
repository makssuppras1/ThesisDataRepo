Abstract
Bioengineering plays a crucial role in today’s society. From developing simple everyday
compounds to developing highly advanced drugs. Despite all the advancements in the
field of bioengineering, there are still problems that have yet to be solved.
The realm of fully automated solutions is predominantly accessible to large commercial
organizations capable of substantial investments, driven by expected returns. However,
for smaller companies and academic institutions, creating a feasible business case proves
challenging.
The costs associated with acquiring, implementing and maintaining the equipment mean
that the accessibility to automated equipment is limited. and they have to rely on manual
labor for many of the procedures. Consequently, these facilities end up heavily relying
on manual labor for various procedures. This not only results in reduced efficiency and
hinders the transformation of facilities into high­throughput systems but also introduces
human factors such as fatigue and errors.
This paper tackles the problem of developing a cost­effective robotic arm that can be
integrated with the existing affordable OT­2 liquid handler. In doing so, a low­cost and
accessible system will be developed that relies on automation rather than manual labor.
Throughout the project, efforts were made to minimize the drawbacks associated with
low­cost components while validating the overall performance of the robot. Existing tech­
nologies from various technical sectors, such as FDM manufactured parts, affordable
actuators, and open­source software, were adapted and integrated to suit this particular
application.
The outcomes of this study demonstrate the feasibility of creating an affordable open­
source robot designed for laboratory use. This achievement has the potential to signifi­
cantly broaden the reach of automation, making it accessible to a wider audience. Further­
more, the developed device offers great flexibility and potential for adaptation, opening
up numerous possibilities for future projects and improvements.

Introduction
1.1
History of automation
The journey of robotics from its inception to its current state is something remarkable.
Initially, robots were primarily associated with heavy industrial manufacturing, where au­
tomation revolutionized assembly lines. In the early stages of automation, robots were
responsible for taking over repetitive and physically demanding tasks, which resulted in
an increase of production rates and improved precision. This phase of robotics evolu­
tion, represented by early industrial robots, created the foundation for what was to come.
Automation has significantly increased production rates, efficiency, and product quality.
Robots, equipped with precision and speed, have become integral to assembly lines,
leading to higher output and fewer defects. The achieved level of automation has not
only improved product quality but also reduced costs, making products more accessible
to consumers [1][2]. Nevertheless, the robots utilized in industrial settings lack intelli­
gence, primarily because they are designed for specific tasks and do not possess the
adaptability and problem­solving capabilities associated with human intelligence. How­
ever, they remain an integral part of manufacturing and it is hard to imagine the industry
without them [3].
As robotics continued to advance, it shifted from its initial industrial roots to applications in
everyday life and various industries. This marked the moment where robots were starting
to be used outside of the industrial setting. This expansion of robotics beyond the factory
walls was a testament to the versatility and adaptability of these intelligent machines.
However, the real shift occurred with the implementation of information technology in
robots. The integration of various technologies such as sensors, artificial intelligence,
and machine learning took the robots to a new level.
The ability of robots to perceive and adapt to their surroundings has not only made them
more versatile and safer when working alongside humans but has also changed how
society views and utilizes their potential. No longer limited to repetitive and physically
demanding tasks within the controlled environment of manufacturing plants, robots began
to infiltrate our daily lives, bringing with them a wide range of benefits and possibilities
[3][4].
The implementation of automation and robots in the field of life sciences serves as a
prime example of emerging robot applications, promising almost unlimited potential that
is currently starting to be recognized. This integration can significantly improved a wide
range of applications within the life sciences, advancing fields like medical surgery, patient
support, rehabilitation, and numerous other healthcare­related activities.[2].

Automation in the Bioengineering Industry
In the domain of scientific exploration and experimentation, the state of automation in
research laboratories has significantly advanced, with breakthroughs and innovations in
technology contributing to faster discoveries. Amid these advancements, the need for
precision, efficiency, and reliability in laboratory processes has become increasingly more
relevant.
The emergence of bioengineering laboratories is an example of these advancements.
These advanced facilities integrate robotics and automation. They are equipped with

1.2.1
High­throughput testing
High­throughput testing is a sophisticated methodology employed across various scien­
tific and industrial domains. It represents a pivotal innovation that addresses the chal­
lenges posed by the need to efficiently analyze a substantial volume of samples or data
points. This imperative task, if attempted manually, would be not only impractical but also
extremely time­consuming.
At its essence, high­throughput testing relies on the foundational principles of automation
and parallelization. By capitalizing on these principles, it empowers the simultaneous ex­
ecution of multiple tests or analyses, ensuring the rapid processing of data. This transfor­
mative approach capitalizes on advanced technologies and robotics to seamlessly facili­
tate the collection and processing of data, thereby establishing itself as an indispensable
tool in modern research and industry.
High­throughput experimentation is particularly important in the life sciences domain due
to the nature of the procedures. Here, research and development often necessitate the
precise delivery and dispensing of biological samples and solutions, frequently in vast
quantities, each containing minuscule volumes. The applications within this field are di­
verse and encompass genomics, proteomics, drug discovery, and beyond [7].
Without the intervention of robotics, these tasks would prove to be not only labour in­
tensive and time consuming but also error­prone, potentially leading to inaccuracies with
far­reaching consequences. Robotics ensures the required accuracy, efficiency, and pre­
cision required to overcome the complexities of high­throughput testing in the life sciences
[5].
1.2.2
Industry 4.0
The arsenal of robotics for biological applications consists of an array of tools, devices,
and systems, combining both general­purpose and specialized technologies. Robot ma­
nipulators, computer vision systems, and micro­actuators have become essential com­
ponents for automating various tasks. Specialized devices, such as high­throughput bio­
solution preparation systems, micro­force sensors, and non­traditional tools designed for
handling bio­solutions and manipulating cells, reflect the diversity and innovation in this
field [3].
As the demand for intersection of biotechnology and cutting­edge technologies grows, it
becomes evident that Industry 4.0 plays a pivotal role in transforming the landscape. The
integration of biotechnology with Industry 4.0 involves the integration of digital systems
and advanced technologies, including big data, IoT, robotics and advanced automation,
AI and virtual environments, and 3D printing, into the realm of biotechnology.
Achieving Industry 4.0 in the domain of Life Sciences, requires the implementation of dig­
ital systems and cutting­edge technologies into the domain of biotechnology. This would
facilitate the seamless merging of physical and virtual systems, resulting in the creation
of what is known as cyber­physical systems. While this integration has already been
present within prominent biotechnology companies and academic research laboratories
(to a limited extent), the biotechnology sector still isn’t on the same level as manufactur­
ing industries in terms of automating research facilities which is a critical requirement for

1.3
Challenges of establishing a laboratory
Establishing a research laboratory is a significant and ongoing financial commitment. Be­
fore embarking on such a project, it’s crucial to have a well­defined rationale and a solid
business case in place. Building a bioengineering laboratory goes beyond physical infras­
tructure. In today’s scientific sector, characterized by advanced technologies and high­
throughput methods, creating an effective research environment requires careful con­
sideration. The development of a biofoundry also demands substantial and continuous
financial investment, emphasizing the need for a clear justification and a strong business
foundation before proceeding [5]. Besides financial investments, these facilities demand
investments in terms of both time and human capital to set up and operate effectively.
The amount of funding required depends on the intended scale and scope of the facil­
ity. It doesn’t just include the acquisition of high­throughput equipment, encompassing
expenses related to consumables, software, and the recruitment and support of skilled
personnel essential for the setup and operation of the laboratory [3].
When seeking investment for projects of this magnitude, it becomes important to account
for all these elements carefully. Provisions must be made to secure not only the initial
funding but also the ongoing financial support necessary for maintaining the facility. Long­
term commitment and support are vital, as it determines the magnitude, sustainability and
success of the investment[5].

1.3.1
Commercial versus academic facilities
By delving into the analysis of the business case, distinct differences become apparent
between these two types of entities.
Commercial bio research facilities are driven by profit motives, focusing on research
projects that can lead to marketable products or services, such as pharmaceuticals or
biotechnological solutions. They often secure funding from private investors, pharmaceu­
tical companies, or venture capital, with an expectation of financial returns. Research
in commercial facilities is tightly aligned with commercial interests, emphasizing applied
research and the protection of intellectual property rights. Their personnel includes sci­
entists with industry experience, and their primary goal is product development and com­
mercialization. While collaborations with academic institutions may occur, these are of­
ten characterized by contractual agreements addressing intellectual property and profit­
sharing [6].
In contrast, academic research facilities prioritize advancing scientific knowledge and ed­
ucation. Education is a central mission for academic facilities, offering academic pro­
grams and degrees while adhering to principles such as open research, peer review, and
academic integrity. They secure funding from universities, government grants, and phil­
anthropic organizations, focusing on contributions to the scientific community rather than
profitability. Research in academic facilities is diverse, including both fundamental and
applied research, with an emphasis on spreading the gathered knowledge. These facili­
ties employ faculty, graduate students, and postdoctoral researchers rather than relying
solely on industry specialists. It’s worth noting that research pace in academic settings
may be slower due to rigorous peer review and academic publication requirements, which
differ significantly from the competitive environment and budget constraints typically as­
sociated with externally sourced grants in research programs [5][6].
1.3.2
Cost
The fact remains that the biggest challenge is still the expensive nature of the laboratory
equipment. The high cost and complexity of advanced robotic systems pose economic
barriers, limiting their accessibility, especially in academic settings. Therefore the design
of the lab must be carefully planned in order to assess what type of equipment and what
level of automation is required. A typical liquid­handling station can be considered as an
example. The prices for this type of equipment from verified manufacturers often fits in
the range of $9,000–$250,000. It’s crucial to recognize that most laboratories utilize mul­
tiple liquid handling devices, requiring additional equipment and modules. This cascading
effect results in project costs that often soar into the millions of dollars [10].
Such an investment may not pose significant challenges for specialized companies aim­
ing to develop commercial solutions that promise eventual profitability. However, for aca­
demic and research institutions, dealing with the substantial upfront costs remains a chal­
lenging task, especially when the resources must be allocated very carefully due to the
lack of investors willing to sponsor these projects.
Therefore, the only feasible option is to employ automated solutions for essential tasks,
while other responsibilities continue to be carried out manually. The equipment can be
categorized into different levels of automation to gain insights into varying degrees of
sophistication and their associated costs. Level 7 represents the most advanced and
expensive equipment on the market which can be bought and implemented in a highly
developed laboratory. Meanwhile, tier 5 and 6 would be a typical liquid handling equipment
which can perform multiple tasks and offers flexibility that allows some sort of workspace
reconfiguration [11].

1.3.3
Manual Labor Challenges
The absence of big investments which in turn forces the facilities to compromise on au­
tomation, exposes these environments to issues arising from the reliance on manual labor.
In the academical environment, a typical university research laboratory often relies on a
number of tasks performed manually by researchers or students. While this hands­on
approach is driven by necessity, it introduces many disadvantages [6].
In contrast to semi­automated approaches, fully automated processes in industrial envi­
ronments, despite requiring substantial investments, allow for seamless multitasking. This
integration of robotics and computer programming across various workflow steps, such as
reagent transfer, sample preparation, result detection, and sample storage, streamlines
operations. These fully automated solutions, including liquid handling workstations, utilize
single­tasking automation tools like pumps, shakers, plate readers, centrifuges, heating
blocks, and thermocycling devices [10]. The integration of automation into laboratory op­
erations results in a multitude of valuable benefits, including [6]:
• Reduction in errors
Human error remains a considerable concern. The precision required in these pro­
cesses is prone to inconsistencies stemming from factors such as fatigue, distrac­
tion, or even slight variations in technique. These inaccuracies can compromise
experimental results, leading to potential setbacks in research outcomes and delay­
ing scientific advancements .

• Greater experimental rates
Automation’s ability to accelerate experimental processes revolutionizes the pace
of research and development. Laboratories equipped with automated systems can
conduct experiments at an incredible rate. This not only expedites the discovery of
new insights and solutions but also allows for a more comprehensive exploration of
various hypotheses and experimental conditions.
• Improved safety
By automating hazardous or repetitive tasks, it minimizes the exposure of personnel
to potential risks. Moreover, automated systems are equipped with safety features
and fail­safes to prevent accidents. This fosters a safer work environment and miti­
gates the potential for workplace injuries or incidents.
• Faster clinical translation
In fields such as biotechnology and pharmaceuticals, the swift translation of re­
search findings into clinical applications is crucial. Automation expedites this pro­
cess by streamlining the development and testing of new therapies, diagnostic tools,
or pharmaceuticals. This acceleration not only benefits patients by delivering timely
healthcare solutions but also enhances a company’s competitive edge in the market.
• Researcher efficiency
A significant drawback of performing tasks manually is their inherent inefficiency.
The time consuming nature of these tasks not only decreases the throughput of
experiments but also limits the amount of work that can be accomplished within
a given time frame. By incorporating automated equipment, the time allocated to
routine procedures can be minimized, allowing researchers to concentrate on more
intellectually demanding tasks.
• Enhanced data generation
Automation in laboratories generates a substantial increase in data volume. This
data can be harnessed to enhance AI­driven hypothesis generation. By leveraging
artificial intelligence, automated labs can uncover valuable insights and patterns
within the extensive dataset, accelerating scientific discoveries.
Deciding to go with partially automated and still relying on manual labor to a higher extent
means that these benefits cannot be achieved to the fullest extent possible.
1.4
Democratizing lab automation
To address these challenges, one viable approach is the creation of cost­effective automa­
tion devices. These devices aim to bridge the gap between manual and fully automated
solutions. Existing semi­automated systems seek to capitalize on the advantages of both
approaches and position themselves as intermediaries along the automation spectrum.
These problems gave rise to new solutions of cost effective automation such as affordable
liquid handlers. They tackle the issue of the top down pressure in the current market [6].
Top­down pressure refers to manufacturers exerting control over the market by compelling
them to purchase specific equipment. This often results in equipment from one company
being incompatible with equipment from another company. Consequently, manufacturers
can demand higher prices since customers have limited alternative choices.

flexibility of the OT­2 is a significant advantage. It can be adapted for a wide variety of

and the ability to modify protocols through its Python API, allowing it to meet the specific

Design methodology
The design phase commences with a detailed assessment of the fundamental prerequi­
sites for the robotic system. This process enables the identification of essential features
and attributes that must be incorporated into the forthcoming robot design. The following
requirements, as articulated by the DTU Bioengineering department, serve as a compre­
hensive framework for the design and development of the robot:
• Complete OT2 Accessibility
The robot should have the capability to access all positions within the OT2 without
the need for internal placement.
• Precision within Tolerance
The replication precision should meet or exceed a 1mm standard.
• Raspberry Pi based control system
The system’s central control should be facilitated by a Raspberry Pi.
• Control Flexibility
The robot arms should be controllable either by the OT2 or autonomously, possibly
through the use of serial commands.
• Plate Handling Proficiency
The robot should be able to relocate plates that with no lids, enabling the move­
ment of plates between any deck position and the plate hotel, both forwards and in
reverse.
• Modular Construction
The robot should be designed in a modular fashion to simplify reconstruction by
individuals without specialized expertise. This entails making components plug­and­
play, reducing soldering requirements, and minimizing custom calibration between
different arms.
• Grid Integration for Replication
The robot arm should be compatible with a grid structure, simplifying replication
across different setups.
• Payload Capacity
The robot should have the capacity to manipulate payloads weighing up to 350
grams.
• Random Access Plate Hotel Development of a random access plate hotel is es­
sential to facilitate organized plate placement within the system.

Affordable 3D printed robots
Modular robotic arms, particularly those crafted using 3D printing, represent a significant
advancement in robotic technology. They are designed with the dual goals of easy repli­
cation and assembly in mind. The modular design approach tackles the challenge of
creating cost­effective robotic solutions, allowing for significant flexibility throughout the
project, especially when rapid prototyping is involved. 3D printing serves as the ideal
method for constructing these modular structures, not only due to its cost­effectiveness
but also because of its adaptability to various project needs.
In comparison to conventional industry solutions, where replacement parts are often ex­
pensive and require specialized expertise for installation, 3D printed components offer
a more user­friendly and economical alternative. Utilizing 3D printed parts for building
robotic arms ensures that these devices are not only affordable but also easily modifiable
for future improvements or adaptation to different applications. This approach also makes
these robots accessible to a wider range of users, as they can be printed using most stan­
dard 3D printers, lowering the barrier to entry in robotics and making these innovations
more widely available. The field of 3D printed modular robotic arms is rich with innovation
and practical applications. These robotic arms have vast potential in various sectors, from
education and research to industrial manufacturing and beyond.
The advantage becomes most apparent when a cost analysis of the components is con­
ducted. In the context of prototype development, smaller product quantities can be ex­
pected. Specialized or custom­made parts can entail a considerable investment, signifi­
cantly impacting the overall project cost. Given that the cost per unit tends to decrease as
production volume increases, opting for a cost­effective approach becomes even more
compelling. It’s worth noting that the possibility of the final iteration of this developed
robot featuring a structure composed of specially manufactured parts, such as aluminum,
remains a viable option.
Additionally, it can be expected that many components may need to be manufactured
multiple times throughout the project’s lifecycle. This can arise due to project adjustments,
design revisions, and the pursuit of an optimal balance between weight and strength, as
well as precision tolerances.
A notable limitation of 3D­printed structures is their constraint in terms of rigidity and
durability. In comparison to alternative materials, 3D­printed components typically exhibit
lower density and significantly reduced stiffness. Consequently, the design of these parts
must prioritize optimization, striking a balance between achieving the necessary rigidity
while minimizing weight, volume, and material usage.
Within the realm of 3D printing, three primary materials emerge as favored choices for
constructing the main body of the robot. These materials include:
• PLA, also known as Polylactic Acid, it is commonly used in 3D printing because of
its stiffness, ease of use, low melting temperature, and price. It’s a versatile material
known for its compatibility with various 3D printers and ability to produce detailed
and precise prints.
• ABS, known as Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene. It is a common thermoplastic poly­
mer known for its durability, impact resistance, and versatile range of applications.
ABS is widely used in manufacturing and engineering for items such as plastic car
parts, toys, electronic housings, and consumer goods. One notable characteristic
of ABS is its ability to withstand a wide temperature range, making it suitable for
both high and low­temperature environments.

Actuators
In this section, the critical aspect of selecting actuators, specifically motors, which sig­
nificantly influence the overall performance of the robot will be discussed. The choice of
motors is a pivotal decision, and a careful analysis of available options is essential. Among
the various motor types commonly used in robot arms, their characteristics, advantages,
and limitations will be explored in order to justify the selection of the most suitable motor
type for this project.
BLDC motors ­ These motors offer high power and speed in a relatively small package.
They are more efficient and have longer lifetimes than brushed DC motors because they
do not have brushes that wear out. Their high torque­to­weight ratio makes them suitable
for robotic arms needing high precision and reliability. The reason they will not be used
in this project is the cost aspect – not only are they more expensive than simple stepper
motors, but also other components they require, such as motor drivers, are also more
expensive.
AC motors ­ In some industrial robotic applications, AC motors are used, particularly
where high power is necessary. They are robust and have high endurance for continuous
usage. They will not be used in this application due to their size, functionality, cost, etc.
Servo motors ­ used for precise control of angular or linear position, velocity, and ac­
celeration. They consist of a suitable motor coupled to a sensor for position feedback.
Servo motors are commonly used in robotic arms for their precise control and are ideal
for smaller, lighter robotic applications. For this project, finding a servo that is powerful
enough, would mean a more expensive choice. Additionally, servos have a limited range
of operation.
Stepper motors ­ These are popular in robotics for their precise control of movement and
position. Stepper motors move in discrete steps, allowing for exact open­loop positioning
and easy control. They are often used in applications where precise control over motion
is more important than high­speed operation.
Brushed DC motors ­ Less common in modern robotic arms, these motors are simple
and cost­effective for basic movement requirements. They are easy to control but have
less precision and shorter lifetimes compared to brushless motors.
The selection of stepper motors in the project is justified due to their precision, cost­
efficiency, simplicity of control, availability, robustness, lower maintenance, and customiza­
tion capabilities. Stepper motors provide the required level of accuracy for the project’s
tasks while keeping costs in check and simplifying the control system, making them a

Gear reduction systems
Gear reduction systems in robotics extend the lifetime of motors primarily by allowing
them to operate within their most efficient speed range. Motors are more efficient at higher
speeds where they consume less power and generate less heat for a comparable amount
of work. Gear reduction converts this high­speed, low­torque output to a more usable
lower­speed, higher­torque output for robotic applications. This means the motor can work
at an optimal speed, reducing the overall strain and wear, which in turn leads to a longer
operational life. Additionally, efficient operation at higher speeds helps with better heat
management, a crucial factor in prolonging motor life. By reducing the risks of overheating
and excessive wear, gear reduction systems make a significant contribution to enhancing
the reliability and durability of robotic systems.
When designing the gear reduction system, the expected joint performance has to be
considered. Since the actuators are the driving force behind the joints, they also determine
the joints performance and accuracy. Therefore, it is required to not only do analysis of the
mechanical structure of the robot in terms of stiffness and rigidity but also the performance.
Going back to the initial requirements for the robot, the desired repeatability <1mm can
be used as a reference point for the minimum expected performance.
Attaining the desired levels of precision and repeatability demands a validation process,
through rigorous testing procedures. In the context of a 4­degree­of­freedom (4 DOF)
robot, precision is determined by critical factors including backlash, positioning accuracy,
and the structural rigidity of the robotic arm. In this specific configuration, the accuracy of
end effector positioning can be attributed to the influence of both joint 1 and joint 2.
More precise analysis of this problem will be performed during the mechanical design
when choosing components, designing torque reduction mechanisms. The maximum
achievable precision of these joints inversely correlates with the level of backlash present
within them. Backlash, in the realm of robotics, refers to the clearance or lost motion
within a mechanical system due to the presence of gaps between its components. It
can be defined as the maximum angular motion of the output gear when the input gear
remains fixed [13]. It is also worth noting that completely reducing backlash to zero Is
practically impossible. Not only would that mean that the gears working together have the
perfect dimensions, which is impossible when considering that 3D printers have a limited
resolution and accuracy when printing, resolution of the converted 3D models but also
the fact that this is a manufacturing method that deforms due to a significant temperature
change of the used material while the part is created.
To further enhance our understanding of the robot’s precision capabilities, it is essential
to consider specific data on precision metrics and associated parameters. This data can
include factors such as encoder resolution, servo motor performance, and control algo­
rithms. However, during the design phase, joints 1 and 3 and responsible for achieving
this position in the XY cartesian coordinate space.

2.10.1
Motor Drivers
StepStick drivers are compact and versatile modules used for controlling stepper motors
in various applications, including robotics. They offer precise control over motor move­
ments, thanks to features like microstepping, adjustable current limits, and broad voltage
compatibility. These drivers are commonly used in robotics for tasks requiring accurate
positioning and motion control, making them a popular choice among DIY enthusiasts and
hobbyists.
Despite their advantages, StepStick drivers have some limitations to consider to make a
correct assessment. These include:
• Current Limitations: StepStick drivers may not support high­current applications,
limiting their use in projects that require powerful stepper motors.
• Heat Generation: When operating at higher currents, these drivers can generate
significant heat. Adequate heat dissipation measures are necessary to prevent over­
heating.
• Limited Advanced Features: Compared to more advanced stepper drivers, Step­
Stick modules may lack features like advanced microstepping options or additional
protection mechanisms.
• Reduced Precision: While StepStick drivers offer microstepping capability, their
precision may not match that of more specialized or high­end stepper drivers, af­
fecting the accuracy of fine movements.
Since stepper motors are widely used in 3D printers, a technology that has been devel­
oped specifically for them as a way of addressing these problems will be used. An add­on
has been developed by company Makerbase. This add­on utilized a hall sensor and a
magnet placed on the read side of the motor’s shaft. This way, a cheap magnetic encoder
can be used for a closed­loop system. .
The specific model that was chosen is called MKS Servo42C. It is important to empha­
size that this device is not a textbook definition of a servo motor but rather a closed­loop
stepper. However, for the sake of simplicity, we will keep referring to it as Servo42C. This
device can use signals generated by a separate control board as well as generate signals
for the motor on its own.

Simulations
After establishing the robot’s configuration and creating a 3D model, the next crucial step
is defining the kinematic model. A kinematic representation is crucial to achieve accurate
and precise control over the robot’s movements. This model will serve as the founda­
tion for understanding how the robot’s various joints and links interact to achieve desired
positions and orientations in three­dimensional space.
In the field of robotics, two primary kinematic representations are widely used to describe
the relationship between a robot’s joint motions and the resulting end­effector position and
orientation:
Forward Kinematics
This is the simpler and more straightforward representation. It involves calculating the
position and orientation of the robot’s end­effector based on the known joint angles or
joint values. This representation is valuable for tasks like trajectory planning, where there
is a need to determine the end­effector’s path as the robot moves through different joint
angles.
The forward kinematic model typically consists of a series of homogeneous transformation
matrices that describe the relative transformations between consecutive links and joints in
the robot’s kinematic chain. By multiplying these matrices together, a final transformation
from the robot’s base to its end­effector, effectively determining its position and orientation.
Inverse Kinematics
Is a more complex and computationally challenging representation. It deals with the oppo­
site problem, seeking to find the joint angles or joint values required to achieve a specific
desired end­effector position and orientation.
Inverse kinematics is particularly useful for tasks such as robot programming, where there
is a need to specify the end­effector’s target position and orientation. Solving inverse kine­
matics problems often involves using mathematical techniques like trigonometry, geome­
try, and numerical optimization methods. For robots with multiple degrees of freedom or
redundant joints, finding a suitable set of joint values that satisfies the desired end­effector
pose can be a complex task.
3.1

robot’s configuration. These reference coordinates define specific positions and orienta­
tions in space and adhere to the general assumptions for coordinate definitions. Each
coordinate is characterized by its respective x, y, and z axes at each joint.
These frames can be used to describe the transformation of each coordinate system in
relation to another coordinate system.
Having an established robot configuration which describes the forward kinematics can be
solved using the Denavit­Hartenberg convention.

Software
In this project, the need arises to control both low­level and high­level components of
the SCARA robot. To fulfill this requirement, the decision was made to employ a Rasp­
berry Pi for high­level control and a microcontroller­based MKS Gen L2.1 motherboard
for low­level tasks. The rationale for this choice has been detailed in the previous section
regarding component selection.
These two boards feature distinct software setups due to their differing roles. They can
be treated as separate entities requiring effective communication. The MKS motherboard
transmits data to the Raspberry Pi while retaining the capability to autonomously man­
age the robot’s components. For instance, it can halt the motors upon the triggering of
any endstops on joints J2 or J3. These endstop activations signify maximum or minimum
position attainment, and the motherboard can promptly respond by ceasing motor opera­
tion without waiting for Raspberry Pi instructions. This enhances safety by preventing the
robot from exceeding its bounds and potentially causing motor stalls.
An additional benefit of using two boards with distinct software setups is the flexibility it
affords to users. Users can modify high­level functions without concerns of interfering
with low­level control. This separation streamlines the user experience, as the low­level
code is intricate and necessitates a higher level of expertise compared to the simplified
Python code running on the Raspberry Pi.

Configuration of Marlin for a SCARA robot involves the specification of the kinematic
model, motor parameters, and endstop settings. Kinematic configuration includes the
definition of parameters such as arm link lengths, angles, and motion directions. Motor
parameters need to be configured through the declaration of steps per millimeter, ac­

changes related to the endstops configuration are also required to dictate the mother­

Marlin’s adaptability is a convenient feature, allowing for customization of the firmware to

needs.
Marlin operates by interpreting G­code commands sent from the Raspberry Pi or another
controller. These commands provide instructions for various robot operations, including
movement and positioning. Marlin’s built­in commands handle these operations, enabling
precise control of the robot’s kinematics and motors.
G­code, an abbreviation for ”Geometric Code,” serves as a common language within com­
puter numerical control (CNC) and 3D printing. It functions as a set of directives that guide
automated machines, including 3D printers and CNC routers, in executing precise oper­
ations and movements. G­code essentially contains the information about the motions
(type of motion, positions, path, etc) In the context of a SCARA robot, the versatility of G­
code extends beyond its traditional association with 3D printing. While originally designed
for computer­aided manufacturing, G­code can be repurposed to control the movements
and functions of robot for a wide range of tasks, not only related to motion control.

4.1.1
Path planner
The Marlin path planner, specifically its ”look­ahead” function, plays a critical role in op­
timizing the movement of a 3D printer’s print head or, in the case of a SCARA robot, its
end­effector. This feature allows the printer or robot to anticipate and plan its path more
effectively, enhancing both print quality and efficiency.
56 Advancing Laboratory Automation in Life Sciences through Robotic System Design and Implementation
==PAGE:65==
The look­ahead function in Marlin examines the G­code commands in advance, analyzing
the path the print head or end­effector will follow. By considering upcoming instructions,
it can make real­time adjustments to the movement, ensuring smoother transitions be­
tween segments and minimizing abrupt directional changes. This results in reduced print
artifacts, such as overshoots or ringing, and improved overall print quality.
Additionally, the look­ahead function contributes to faster printing or robot operation speeds.
It optimizes travel paths by minimizing unnecessary decelerations and accelerations, re­
ducing print or task completion times.
4.1.2
Motion profiles
Marlin includes functions for more optimized motion control using motion profiles. Based
on the given motion parameters, the software controls the steppers accordingly. 3D print­
ers typically have very high acceleration and do not use gear reduction rations since the
require torque is very minimal. In this project, the challenge revolves around the trans­
portation of plates with liquids. This is why aiming for a smooth motion with relatively low
acceleration, velocity and jerk is a must. This is quite different when compared to the
acceleration used in 3D printers. 3D printers while using very low velocity when printing,
still use high acceleration (and high velocity during simple travel movements). However,
the same tools can be used to achieve the desired effect.
This simplification significantly streamlines the implementation, as there is no longer a
need to consider real­time stepper pulse generation, thanks to the availability of built­in
functions.
Figure 4.3: S­curve motion profile resulting for a smooth motion by the help of custom
motion parameters
In this project, an S­curve motion profile will be used. It requires the values for jerk,
acceleration and velocity and then creates the correct motion profile. The graph above
showcases a scenario, where the joint achieves the specified maximum velocity. There
are cases however, where the joint does not achieve the desired specified maximum
velocity during the travelled distance. This code also accounts for that and essentially
gets rid of the part where the joint travels at max velocity. In this scenario, the joint only
accelerates and then immediately decelerates in order to have a zero velocity when it
arrives at the desired end position.

Independent control
In the current project setup, a Raspberry Pi is employed for high­level control, transmitting
instructions to the MKS Gen L2.1 motherboard, which, notably, possesses the inherent
capability to independently manage the entire control process, including intricate tasks
such as kinematic calculations.
Equipped with the Marlin firmware, the MKS Gen L2.1 can autonomously interpret G­code

robot movements without external dependencies. In essence, the MKS Gen L2.1 func­

4.1.4
Software endstops
In Marlin firmware, software endstops are an optional feature for the controlled devices.
They define virtual limits to ensure safe operation and prevent collisions or mechanical
damage. These boundaries can be precisely calibrated, enhancing the robot’s accuracy,
making them valuable for precision tasks like laboratory automation.
Software endstops in Marlin offer adaptability. Users can configure these limits to match
specific applications or adjust operational boundaries as needed, providing versatility for
various tasks.
Importantly, software endstops are integrated seamlessly with the firmware’s kinematic
calculations. While the robot moves, the firmware continuously monitors its position on
each axis, ensuring strict adherence to predefined limits. If the robot approaches or ex­
ceeds these boundaries, the firmware intervenes promptly, halting the motion for safety
and controlled operation.
The two board can be easily integrated via a simple USB connection. Once this connec­
tion has been established, it becomes possible to send individual signals directly to the
MKS board or even entire Gcode files.

Marlin also includes homing functions which offer some built­in algorithms, however, in

are directly tied with the higher­level functionality of the robot which will be handled by the

4.2.3
Homing algorithms
Homing algorithms are especially important for open­loop based robot applications. When
considering an open­loop system, the joint positions are predicted based on the kinematic
model, control commands and other additional features. In practical terms, this means
initiating the motion from a predefined starting point and then calculating the real­time po­
sition as time advances. Reverting to the original starting position enables the verification
of the robot’s position as it approaches a designated point in the physical world. However,
this control method is prone to errors arising from the impact of external factors on the
system. This can result both in deviations from the predicted position, collisions and also
fatal errors.
Figure 4.5: Homing algorithm (on the left) and Near Mode Homing (on the right)
There are two different homing modes. The normal homing algorithm relies on having data
on the current robot’s position and then generating motions that move the joint to the home
position (while accounting for a small error margin) in a specified order. If a joint does not
reach the home position throughout its movement, this indicates that either the data on
the robot’s position was incorrect or something prevented the robot from homing its joint.
This results in the homing procedure failing and a different homing method (NearMode)
is required.
The NearMode homing method essentially takes advantage of the high backdrivability.
This algorithm requires the user to position the joints close to the home position and then
the robot would go slightly back and forth trying to find its home position.

Repeatability

and repeatable pressure on the measuring instrument’s contact point.

5.2
Lift capacity
The subsequent step involved an examination of the impact of specific loads on the robot’s
arm. As detailed in Chapter II, Joint 2 is driven by a lead screw rotated by a 0.59Nm
Nema 17 motor. Initial tests revealed that this motor possesses a significantly higher
load­bearing capacity than required for the intended application.
In the initial configuration, a lead screw with a 2mm lead was selected, which provided
Advancing Laboratory Automation in Life Sciences through Robotic System Design and Implementation 63
==PAGE:72==
substantial torque but at the expense of speed. The linear actuator’s maximum speed in
this configuration was approximately 3.2mm/s, falling short of the desired performance.
To overcome the speed limitation, further tests were conducted, utilizing a lead screw with
4mm and 8mm leads. While the latter choice resulted in a 4x increase of speed, it also
introduced a critical issue that needed to be addressed.
The requirement for all joints was to maintain load­bearing capability even in the absence
of motor power. In the 8mm lead configuration, it was observed that the holding torque fell
short, resulting in a gradual arm collapse when the motor was deactivated. Consequently,
the decision was made to use the 4mm lead screw configuration as it provided sufficiently
high speeds while maintaining high torque, ensuring the robot’s stability and reliability.
The biggest challenge is of course the stiffness of the arm. It directly correlates to the
maximum load the arm can handle. When determining the load range that can be safely
transported by the robot, two crucial things must be considered: the strength of the ma­
terial and the stiffness. While 3D­printed elements can be designed to optimize strength,
they inherently retain a degree of flexibility, particularly when compared to more rigid ma­
terials like metal. This inherent flexibility implies that they can begin to deform under load,
even before reaching their critical, irreversible breaking point.
Therefore, it is crucial to measure the vertical displacement of the end­effector as a func­
tion of the applied load. This function can then be used to account for the displacement
by including an offset in the positioning algorithm.
The test has been carried out with a fully extended arm configuration while additional load
was incrementally applied to joint 4 during the test process.
Figure 5.6: Vertical displacement for weights ranging from 50g to 2000g
The tests have been carried out for a range of weights ­ from 0g to 2000g. Although it is
not expected for the robot to carry more than 500g, this test was supposed to visualize
the flexibility of the arm and the ability to maintain position under load. The outcome data
of the tests has been visualized in fig. 5.6. It can be noticed that the displacement for no
applied load is not equal to zero. This is of course caused by all the components mounted
on the arm and the weight of the arm itself resulting in a displacement of 3mm at the arm’s
64 Advancing Laboratory Automation in Life Sciences through Robotic System Design and Implementation
==PAGE:73==
end position. It can be observed that the displacement matches an exponential function
which shows that applying more load does not deform the arm at the same rate.
As the arm is inherently flexible, it exhibits a progressive increase in resistance force
as displacement grows. This phenomenon continues until it reaches a critical juncture
referred to as the ’yield point,’ where the arm’s material undergoes permanent deformation
while still retaining its structural integrity. Beyond this yield point, the arm no longer reverts
to its original shape after the load is removed.
It is worth noting that during the tests, the yield point was not surpassed, given that the
applied weight was insufficient to reach it. Within the arm’s structure, the links connecting
joint 3 and joint 4 were identified as the weakest components. To mitigate excessive
flexibility, these links were constructed from PLA material. Had these elements been
manufactured from PETG, a considerably steeper displacement function would have been
observed due to the material’s differing properties.
In conclusion, while the arm has demonstrated the capability to handle weights of up to
2kg, it’s important to acknowledge that this may not be optimal for long­term durability.
Additionally, it’s crucial to account for the end effector’s displacement by incorporating an
offset into the positioning algorithm. The real limiting factor in the maximum weight that
can be lifted will therefore most likely not be the stiffness of the arm or the motor’s torque
but it will rather be determined by the type and strength of the used gripper.

Gripper
The gripper’s reliability required extensive testing in order to assess whether changes
need to be made. Despite the fact that the gripper has been designed to be rigid and has

5.6
Stall protection
One of the notable features of the robot involves its safety measures, including collision
detection. Although there was insufficient time in this project to directly implement collision
detection within the robot’s system, a simple alternative is available.
The motor driver boards come equipped with an integrated stall protection function. Uti­
lizing the magnetic encoder on the drivers, it becomes possible to identify motor stalls.
When a stall is detected, power to the motor is instantly cut off, and the motor is disen­
gaged, resulting in no applied torque. This is made feasible by the high backdrivability
of the actuator. As a result, this setup guarantees that the robot will not pose any dan­
ger to individuals who may find themselves on its path and ensures the protection of any
equipment integrated with the device.

heavily limits the performance and control capabilities. The biggest issue is that the motor
and driver still act as an external closed­loop and in order to retrieve the encoder data, a
query has to be made to the driver. In UART mode, there are two main motion commands:
• Move motor to an angle
• Continuously rotate motor until interrupted
As it turned out, querying for the encoder data while the motor is performing a motion to
a defined angle, interrupts the current motion.
The other, continuous rotation mode, allows to access the encoder data without interrupt­
ing the motion, however the motion would then have to be stopped through the Raspberry
Pi that is making decisions based on the encoder data in real time. The approach being
employed is less than ideal, given that a Raspberry Pi is not intended for real­time control.
Moreover, it results in the loss of one of the major benefits of stepper motors, which is a
dependable open­loop control system. This is because in the latter motion mode, there
is no data available on how many pulses have been generated for the motor.
The fact remains that this control method (when improved through future firmware up­
dates) has a lot of potential as it would allow to use a Raspberry Pi as the only control
board since the low­level control would be achieved through the motor drivers (eliminat­
ing the problem of a Raspberry Pi’s lack of a real time clock and not being designed for
controlling low­level components in real time).
6.5.5
Collision detection
An important functionality that has not been fully explored during the project’s duration
is a collision detection functionality by measuring the vibrations of the robot. By using
a MPU6050 module containing a gyroscope and an accelerometer, a collision detection
algorithm can be created. This is the same module that was used for the vibration tests
in Chapter V. This algorithm would determine whether an obstacle has been hit or if there
is motor stall by comparing the measured values with a threshold. Of course, having a
static threshold might pose a problem since vibrations will also be present during move­
ments. Therefore, a better solution would be a dynamic threshold whose value depends

and acceleration.
