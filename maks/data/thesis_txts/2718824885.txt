Abstract
UVCBs covers complex chemical mixtures where some of the constituents may be
unknown and are challenging to test due to (semi)volatile and hydrofobic properties
which may require different testing strategies. This study performs three closed sys-
tem biodegradation tests on the essential oil, Cedarwood Virginia Oil; two Whole
Substance combined with Constituent Specific analytics (WSCS) tests to determine
primary biodegradation kinetics of the main constituents using environmental rel-
evant concentrations and a standardized OECD 301F Readily Biodegradability test
to determine complete mineralization. All tests were conducted using AS from a
WWTP where the WSCS tests differentiated between a diluted and undiluted TSS
concentration of 0.036 and 3.63 g/L with an incubation time of 28 and 4 days,
respectively. All main constituents in cedarwood oil were fully degraded (<SDL)
with DT50 ranging from 4.65-7.43 days for the diluted WSCS test whereas only
Widrol and Cedrol were fully degraded (<SDL) with DT50 of 0.43 and 0.88 days
respectively for the undiluted test. The remaining constituents in the undiluted
test were degraded between 52-75% with DT50 ranging from 2-3.76 days. It was
strongly indicated that the constituents were sorbed to the sludge matrix in the
undiluted test impacting the bioavailability why the remaining constituents were
not fully degraded. In addition, it was found that the biodegradation rate constant
in the diluted test were slower than in the undiluted test but not in the magnitude
as expected. The OECD 301F test using a TSS concentration of 0.036 g/L showed
a degradation of 34.3% why cedarwood oil did not meet the requirements for ready
biodegradability.
In parallel, an abiotic control was set up revealing significant
abiotic losses of 69.4-99.0% of the constituents to oxitop instruments thus making
the test unsuitable for UVCBs and the results unreliable. It was found that the
WSCS test and a modification of the OECD 301F test are complementary meth-
ods providing data on both primary degradation of the individual constituents and
mineralization of the whole substance. The findings presented in this study reveals
the need for a multipronged approach integrating whole substance and constituent
based information when assessing the fate and effects of UVCBs in the environment.

Introduction
Substances of unknown or variable composition, complex reaction products, or
biological materials are shorten to UVCBs and covers complex chemical mixtures,
where some of the constituents are unknown and thus difficult to identify. Many
different compounds are categorized as UVCBs such as essential oils and petroleum
fractions, which contain hundreds of (semi)volatile and hydrophobic constituents.
These constituents may originate from natural sources making the composition
vary with the seasons or geographic location with different physical-chemical and
toxicological properties (Hughes et al. 2022; Lai et al. 2022).
Essential oils are used in a wide range of consumer products, like household- and
personal care products. These products are expected to end up in the local waste
water treatment plant (WWTP), why they are known as ”down the drain” products
(Bickers et al. 2003; Møller et al. 2021). Essential oils are produced at significant
levels worldwide and future sales of essential oils are expected to increase. Since
essential oils are very complex mixtures the characterization and assessment of
hazards associated with these substances are very challenging (Dimitrov et al. 2015;
Lai et al. 2022).
Biodegradation is important for chemical hazard assessment as it is a key parame-
ter for predicting transformations and losses of a substance, and thus its fate in the
environment. Biodegradation depends on many factors, such as the properties of
the specific compound, the microbial community and the environmental conditions
(Davis and Madsen 1996). When assessing biodegradation of a compound, it is
important to consider the degree of bioavailability of the compound in the current
matrix. Processes like sorption is known to have an impact on the bioavailabil-
ity of compounds which might influence the outcome of the data retrieved from
biodegradation tests (Joutey et al. 2013).
Biodegradation testing is commonly performed to meet product marketing claims
and environmental regulatory requirements. A compound must demonstrate eco-
toxicological information, including ready biodegradability, to meet the require-
Page 1 of 78
==PAGE:25==
1

ments put forth by REACH (Registration, Evaluation, Authorization and Restric-
tion of Chemicals) (Situ Biosciences 2022).
REACH has adopted standardized
chemical testing strategies developed by the OECD (Organisation for Economic
Co-operation and Development). Testing UVCBs using standardized biodegrada-
tion tests such as the OECD 301F Ready Biodegradability test defined by mineral-
ization of the whole substance can be problematic as it is not designed for mixtures
including (semi)volatile and/or hydrophobic constituents (Brown et al. 2018).
A novel testing approach of UVCBs is to assess the primary biodegradation kinet-
ics of the individual constituents in a closed system using activated sludge from a
WWTP. The purpose of conducting the test as a closed system is to prevent losses
of (semi)volatile compounds however it can be a challenge to uphold aerobic condi-
tions, especially for experiments using activated sludge where the consumption of
oxygen is high due to high microbial activity.
In this study, two experimental setups consisting of three closed system biodegra-
dation tests will be conducted.
Two tests are Whole Substance test combined
with Constituent Specific analytics (WSCS), and a third test follows OECD 301F
test. The two WSCS tests using co-solvent spiking will be performed to determine
biodegradation kinetics of cedarwood oil in activated sludge under aerobic condi-
tions. The WSCS tests will be performed using diluted and undiluted activated
sludge. For the analysis of the WSCS tests, automated solid phase microextrac-
tion (SPME) coupled to gas chromatography-mass spectrometer/mass spectrometer
(GC-MS/MS) will be applied on the test systems for measuring primary biodegra-
dation relative to abiotic controls. The biodegradation results are compared against
the assessment of the regulatory OECD 301F Ready Biodegradability test, which
follows a standardized process to screen whether a compound pose a risk to the envi-
ronment (OECD 1992). The OECD 301F test is highly conservative and determines
whether a chemical or substance as a whole can be classified as ready biodegrad-
able, however it has proven to be problematic for hydrophobic and (semi)volatile
compounds (Brown et al. 2018) which will be addressed in this study.

Background
2.1
Biodegradation
When chemicals enter the environment, one main pathway of removal is through
degradation. Degradation processes can either be abiotic, where the primary pro-
cesses are hydrolysis, photolysis, volatilisation and adsorption to particulate matter
or as biotic degradation. This thesis will focus on biotic degradation (abbreviated to
biodegradation) under aerobic conditions. Through biodegradation, substances are
transformed into simpler compounds by microorganisms through metabolic or enzy-
matic processes (Battersby 1990; Hammershøj 2020; Knowledgebank 2022; OECD
2022).
There are different types of biodegradation substances can be subjected to. The
following section will focus on two biodegradation types; mineralization and pri-
mary degradation. Mineralization is defined as complete degradation of a substance
resulting in a complete removal from the environment. When a substance under-
goes mineralization, organic compounds are converted into its mineral components
e.g CO2, H2O, inorganic salts, for aerobic conditions, and methane for anaerobic
conditions (Battersby 1990; Knapp and Bromley-Challoner 2003; Schwarzenbach
et al. 2003). The term primary biodegradation is applied when the parent substance
is subjected to a small degree of degradation, resulting in losses of active surface
properties. There is no major transformation of the whole molecule, but rather a
removal of a head group in the structure, leading to changes in the physical chem-
ical properties of the substance (Chemlink Specialities - Harke group 2017; Knapp
and Bromley-Challoner 2003).
2.1.1
Biodegradation kinetics
Biodegradation kinetic is an expression for the degradation of compounds over-
time. When determining biodegradation kinetics, different types of biodegradation
models can be applied. A variety of mathematical models have been developed to
Page 4 of 78
==PAGE:28==
2
BACKGROUND
accommodate the numerous variables which can affect biodegradation of substance
in the environment (Battersby 1990). Multiple non-linear models have been formu-
lated based on the Monod equation which takes the variability of substrate and cell
density into account. Non-linear models which incorporates variation of substance
and cell density is of high importance when analysis microbial kinetics (Simkins
and Alexander 1984). Some of the most commonly used models are the first-order
reactions, Monod growth and second-order kinetics (Battersby 1990). The model
used in this study is the first-order kinetics which is chosen based on the high cell
density of the activated sludge and the low concentration of cedarwood.
By applying a first-order degradation model to experimental data, degradation rate
constants can be derived from non-linear regression analysis. Obtaining degrada-
tion rate constants it is possible to gain insight into the rate of degradation per
day appose to a removal percentage at a fixed time. Degradation rates are known
to be influenced by varies parameters e.g temperature and bioavailability, by either
reducing or enhancing the biodegradation rates (Martin Alexander 2000; Birch et
al. 2018). Applying models describing degradation rates is an important tool used
in predicting and assessing the fate of compounds in the environment (Battersby
1990).
2.1.2
Regulatory biodegradability testing
A key information in regards to evaluating chemicals and their fate and effect in the
environment, is to know whether the substance is persistent or readily biodegraded
(Cousins et al. 2019). Because the persistence of a chemical is governed by the
rates at which they are removed by biological and chemical processes, biodegrada-
tion tests are used to asses the persistence of a chemical in a regulatory context
(Boethling et al. 2009). In order to obtain this data, chemicals are subjected to a
test strategy derived from OECD’s chemical testing strategy (Baun et al. 2017).
Assessment of biodegradability follows a standardized OECD screening or higher
tiered tests. OECD divide the biodegradation tests into three tiers of testing: i)
Ready biodegradability, ii) Inherent biodegradability and iii) Simulation (OECD
Page 5 of 78
==PAGE:29==
2
BACKGROUND
2006). When stepping down the test hierarchy, the unproblematic substances are
removed at screening level (1. tier test), resulting in only problematic substances
are subjected to further testing. When moving from one tier to another down the
hierarchy, the complexity of the test and the economic consequences increase (Baun
et al. 2017).
Ready biodegradability tests (OECD 301 A to F) are highly conservative using
high concentrations of the test substance and the inoculum consist of unadapted
microorganisms. To be classified as readily biodegradable, a substance has to meet
the requirements specified by the method. The pass levels of 70% removal of DOC
or 60% of ThOD must be reached in a window of 10-days within the 28-d period of
the test for a positive outcome (OECD 1992). Inherent degradation tests (OECD
302 A to C) are used to determine whether a substance is inherently biodegrad-
able. If the results indicate that inherent biodegradability occur, it indicates that
the substance has a potential for degradation under favourable conditions. Simula-
tion tests (OECD 303, 304, 307, 308 and 309) are used to determine biodegradation
at environmentally relevant concentrations of the chemical in specific environments
e.g. soils, WWTP, seawater and surface water (OECD 2006).
In this study, cedarwood oil will be tested for readily biodegradability following
the OECD 301F test. OECD (1992) describes the test as being the most suitable
method for chemicals which are poorly soluble in water and volatile or adsorbing,
however in a study by Brown et al. (2018) it was demonstrated that the test was
problematic for such chemicals.

UVCBs are a group of mixed chemicals and are found in many products; deter-
gents, fragrances and personal care products which are all categorized as ”down the
drain” products, why it is primarily expected to be found in WWTP. Due to the ap-

plications of UVCBs, 20%-40% of registered chemicals in the European Union and
in the United States consists of UVCBs, making them highly relevant for further
investigation of fate and effect in the environment (Lai et al. 2022). Fluctuations
in seasonal and temporal exposure to the source material entails variation in the
composition, which impacts the UVCBs fate and effects in the environmental. In
addition, manufacturing processes or transformation products resulted from spon-

2.2.1
Challenges of characterization and testing of UVCBs
International regulatory agencies have highlighted the difficulties in characterizing
UVCBs and assessing their environmental fate (Salvito et al. 2020). Since UVCBs
constitutes of unknown or variable constituents, there are many challenges when
facing characterizations of UVCBs and predicting the fate and effect (ECHA 2022c;
Lai et al. 2022). For all constituents at concentrations ≥10% and constituents
which are risk drivers, independently of concentration, must be specified using a
IUPAC name and a CAS number (ECHA 2017). Different approaches can be used
for characterization and risk assessment of UVCB. Three different test approaches
have been suggested; whole substance, group and/or block of constituents, or based
on individual constituents. However, as any approach, there are different advan-
tages and limitations. The limitations of whole substance biodegradation test, is
that the test results may not be representative of all the constituents in the test,
due to their differences in physical-chemical properties. For the group/block and
individual constituent assessment, the lack of assessment of all the constituents as
one whole mixture is missing, why it is difficult to make a complete risk assess-
ment. In addition, an assessment of individual constituents made on those which
contributes to the composition of the UVCB above a threshold of 10% may lead
to an exclusion of constituents which may pose a risk to the environment, but are
below the threshold (Salvito et al. 2020, Møller et al. 2021, S¨uhring et al. 2022).
Page 7 of 78
==PAGE:31==
2
BACKGROUND
Currently, no definitive methodology to characterize UVCBs are established, but
are mostly based on the generic properties of the identified constituents (ECHA
2022b).
In general, biodegradation studies are very well documented for single chemical
compounds, however, there is a rather large deficiency when it comes to biodegra-
dation studies of mixtures. The reason may be explained by the fact that regulatory
tests regarding biodegradation, is constructed for single compounds. Additionally,
when the main constituents undergo biodegradation, risk assessment becomes more
complex, as the degradation products of the main constituents changes intrinsic
properties (Møller et al. 2021; Salvito et al. 2020; S¨uhring et al. 2022).
When chemicals enters the environment, they are mixed and their biodegrada-
tion may be affected and altered based on the interactions of the other chemicals
present. As these complex mixtures are formed in the environment, it is a regula-
tory challenge, that biodegradation tests and data are lacking for complex mixtures
(Hammershøj 2020). In addition, it may be a technical challenge testing the whole
substance and interpret the resulting data because the laboratory conditions may
differentiate from the conditions occurring in the environment (Salvito et al. 2020).
Regulators, academia and industry have together called for the development of
chemical risk assessment methods specifically for UVCBs (Salvito et al. 2020).
2.2.2
Current legislation
The chemical legislation in EU is led by REACH which aims to ensure a high
level of protection of human health and minimize environmental risks. REACH
have pointed the responsibility on the industry to provide the data and documen-
tation of possible environmental impacts, such as risk and safety information on
the substances they produce, following the saying ”no data, no market” (European
Commission 2020). REACH makes the registration and assessment based on the
information regarding the physical-chemical characteristics, environmental fate and
potential hazards of substances to clarify initial concerns for human health or for
the environment (ECHA 2016; ECHA 2022a). Substances are divided into two

main groups: i) well defined substances with a defined quantitative and qualitative
composition and ii) UVCB substances. The registration of UVCBs are challenging
(cf. Section 2.2.1) as the variability and the number of constituents are relatively

imported above 10 tonnes per year, a complete Chemical Safety Assessment (CSA)
is required, including an evaluation of whether substances are persistent, bioac-

2.3
Activated sludge in wastewater treatment plant
Activated sludge (AS) is used as a treatment process in WWTP, which uses mi-
croorganisms to remove dissolved organic matter from the wastewater. As 95% of
the microorganisms are aerobic species it is vital to uphold aerobic conditions dur-
ing treatment. During the treatment, oxygen is blown into the raw sewage where
the wastewater is mixed and organic matter is oxidized. The microbial commu-
nity in the AS, consist of broad blend of microorganisms e.g fungi, protozoans and
metazoans, which also contributes to the treatment process, in their own manner.
A common protozoa is the Ciliates, which contributes to the formation of regular-
shaped flocs, which makes the flocs settle better. In addition to the Ciliates, the
Metazoa Rotifer also contributes to the stabilization of flocs in the settling process
(Zika Rech 2009). Another key microorganism is the bacteria, which contributes to
the biological degradation process of the organic matter in the wastewater. As the
Page 12 of 78
==PAGE:36==
2
BACKGROUND
bacteria consumes the dissolved organic matter, they develop a biofilm around the
cell wall, which enables the cells to stick together which results in flocs of bio-soilds,
or sludge (Solutions 2018; Water technology 2014; Zika Rech 2009).
Figure 1 is a simplified illustration of a general WWTP. The plant is made up by
preliminary treatment, primary clarifier where primary sludge is formed, areation
tank where the sludge is aerated to allow aerobic biodegradation, secondary clarifier
where the solids and liquids are separated, and last the effluent. As presented on
Figure 1, part of the AS from the secondary claifier is recycled back to the areation
tank, whereas primary sludge and waste sludge are categorized as waste products
and will be disposed (Artola-Garicano et al. 2003). The sampling site of the AS
used in this study was the aeration tank.

for cedarwood oil show that for all main constituents but Widrol and Cedrol, the

is supported by the adsorption percentage to sludge which is in the range of 46 to

bioavailability of substances in regards to biodegradation.
Sorption is a physical and chemical process by which a substance accumulate at
the interface between two phases usually a solid and a fluid (Nazaroff and Alvarez-
Cohen 2001; Schwarzenbach et al. 2003). Sorption reduces the availability of a
compound for biological or chemical degradation reactions, affecting the bioavail-
ability of a given compound and thus its rate of biotransformation by organisms
(Schwarzenbach et al. 2003).
It is important to note, that is is only the freely
dissolved concentration of a compound which is available to collide with interfaces
and be removed from the water. A WWTP varies on a daily basis in both mi-
crobial activity and communities, organic matter content and the concentration of
organic contaminants, which all influences the sorption processes and the bioavail-
ability (Artola-Garicano et al. 2003). Additionally, the dissolved compounds prop-
erties strongly influences the compounds tendency to sorb. Sorption may also be
driven by a chemicals hydrofobicity, which sorb in order to avoid the aqueous phase
(Thompson and Goyne 2012).
2.4
Chemical analysis
2.4.1
Automated Solid Phase Microextraction
Automated Solid Phase Microextraction (SPME) is a non-exhaustive technique
based on the partition equilibrium of the analytes between the sample matrix and
the extraction phase. The extraction of the sorbent is done by a fully automated
auto sampler with a fused silica fiber coated with a stationary phase e.g poly-
dimethylsiloxan (PDMS), which is highly suitable for non-polar compounds. The
PDMS is produced in various thicknesses where a thin stationary phase is most suit-
able for large molecules and a thick stationary phase is most suitable for volatile
Page 14 of 78
==PAGE:38==
2
BACKGROUND
compounds. More so, the detection limit is also dependent on the thickness of
the stationary phase.
The amount of adsorbed analytes are proportional with
the thickness of the fiber and the time until equilibrium is reached (Prosen and
Zupanˇciˇc-Kralj 1999). The extraction is done either by placing the fiber in the
headspace or directly into the sample matrix. The analytes in the sample will then
partition from the sample onto the fiber until equilibrium is reached. Throughout
the extraction phase the vial is agitated by movement and stirring to achieve a
faster equilibrium. As the sample is agitated the temperature of the sample can be
increase which will shorten the extraction time as the diffusion coefficients in the
water will increase, however, this will also decrease the partitioning thus heating
of the sample is primary recommended for extraction from the headspace (Prosen
and Zupanˇciˇc-Kralj 1999). After the extraction process, the fiber is inserted in to a
Gas chromotography (GC) column where the compounds are desorped by heating
and the SPME is conditioned for to prevent carry over (Møller et al. 2021).
2.4.2
Gas Chromatography
As the compounds are desorbed from the PDMS fiber, they will diffuse into the GC
column where they will volatilize and mix with a carrier gas (e.g helium or nitrogen).
For low concentration samples the compounds are transported by the carrier gas in
the GC colum in the split or splitless mode (Turner 2021). The split mode is most
suitable for high concentrations, whereas the splitless mode is most suitable for
lower concentration as all the vaporized analytes goes into the column To prevent
carry over from a previous sample clean carrier gas is send pass the bottom of the
septum and out to waste (Chasteen 2000). As the carrier gas passes through the
column, the compounds present in the sample will separate by interactions with the
stationary phase on the inside of the column. The stationary phase is chosen based
on the properties of the analytes e.g for non-polar compounds a stationary phase
made of polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) is preferable (Turner 2021).
Interaction
with the stationary phase causes the compounds to slow down according to their
molecular size and weight. Because the compounds are slowed down and separated
Page 15 of 78
==PAGE:39==
2
BACKGROUND
through the GC column, they have different retention times thus they are detected
at different times (Møller et al. 2021; UKEssay 2015). Following the separation of
the compound in the GC column, the analytes molecules are detected.
2.4.3
Mass spectrometry/Mass spectrometry
The compounds from the GC column are detected using Mass spectrometry/Mass
spectrometry (MS/MS). MS/MS is also known as tandem mass spectrometry. There
are multiple methods of detection e.g triple quadrupole (QqQ) which is used in this
study, and is made up of three quadrupoles, Q1, Q2 and Q3. The MS/MS involves
three distinct steps of selection-fragmentation-detection. The first quadrupole (Q1)
the precursor (or parent) ion, with a specific mass to ratio (m/z), is selected by
ionization of the analytes coming from the GC column by blasting them with elec-
trons. The precursor ion then goes into the second quadrupole, Q2 (the collision
cell) where the ion is fragmented. After fragmentation, a product (or daughter) ion
with a specific m/z is produced in the third quadropole, Q3 (Møller et al. 2021;
Stone et al. 2009). The multiple reaction monitoring method (MRM) is applied
to the MS/MS which enables quantification of analytes within a complex mixture.
Detection of the product ions are done by counts of signal from product ions with
the same m/z which then provides a spectrum as output. The result of the anal-
ysis is then given as a chromatography where the m/z rations are presented as a
function of retention time (National High Magnetic Field Laboratory 2022).
2.4.3.1
Multiple reaction monitoring (MRM)
Specific ions are predetermined to be selected in the MRM method based on
the specific analytes which is to be analyzed. As mentioned, the predetermined
ions are called the precursor ions (parent ions) and these are passed through the
first quadrupole into fragmentation (in the second quadrupole) and onto the third
quadrupole where product ions (daughter ions) are formed in. The transition of go-
ing from precursor ion to the product ion is called ion transition (LabCE 2023; Pitt
2009). By adding multiple stages of mass selectivity increases the specificity mean-

2
BACKGROUND
ing that background noise is greatly reduced as the signal to noise is increased. By
combining QqQ with multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) it is possible to target
specific analytes in a complex mixtures (Pitt 2009; Stone et al. 2009). The MRM
method used for analysis have previously been developed by DTU Environment,
thus the MRM method is no further described.

Tested concentration
A test concentration of 474 µg/L was chosen for the WSCS test systems based on
the principle to keep the concentration at low environmental relevant levels, but at
the same time, high enough to ensure that the signals of the added cedarwood oil
were above detection limit for the GC-MS/MS. By keeping the spiked volume to
the WSCS tests to a minimum (1 µL) reduced the amount of co-solvent. This was
desired, as the co-solvent, in this case ethanol, contributes to the carbon content,
which increase the oxygen demand in the test systems. Reducing the co-solvent to
a minimum it was more likely to uphold oxic conditions in the test system during
the incubation period.

3.6
OECD 301F test
The OECD 301F is a manometric respirometry regulatory test which screens chemi-
cals for ready biodegradability in an aerobic aqueous mineral medium, following the
OECD 301 guideline. The purpose of the test is to investigate whether the test com-
pound is fully mineralized. The method is based on a closed bottle system, achieved
by using OxiTop® instrumentation. An automatic respirometer implanted in the
OxiTop® was used to track the oxygen uptake throughout the inoculation period
by measuring the decreasing partial pressure in the bottles. A sodium hydroxide
(NaOH) pellet was added to a rubber sleeve to capture the evolved carbon dioxide
Page 33 of 78
==PAGE:57==
3
MATERIALS AND METHODS
in order for the pressure to drop in the flasks and to regulate the pH. The bio-
chemical oxygen demand (BOD) from the biodegradation of cedarwood oil can be
calculated by subtracting the oxygen consumption of the inoculum blank from the
total oxygen uptake for cedarwood oil. The ThOD for cedarwood oil was calculated
as a weighted average based on the known constituents fractions using the chemical
equation of complete mineralization (without nitrification) (see Appendix C Sec-
tion C.5 Equation C8). The biodegradability of cedarwood oil was subsequently
calculated as follow:
% degradation = BOD
ThOD · 100
(8)
The pass levels for ready biodegradability for the manometric respirometry method
are 60% of ThOD production. This pass level has to be reached in a 10 day window
within the 28 day period of the test.
3.6.1
Tested concentration
For the test, pure Cedarwood Virginia Oil (described in Section 3.2.1) was used.
The concentration of cedarwood oil in the test systems was chosen based on the
ThOD of the cedarwood oil. To detect a decrease in the partial pressure in the
flasks, the oxygen uptake had to be at least 50-100 mg ThOD/L (OECD 1992).
In Appendix C Section C.5 it has been estimated that 1 mg cedarwood oil has
a ThOD of 3.21 mg O2. A test concentration of 30 mg cedarwood oil/L which
corresponds to a ThOD of 96.3 mg O2/L was chosen for the test to ensure a change
in pressure above detection level in the flasks. Using a concentration of 30 mg

a test concentration complied with the minimum oxygen uptake (50 mg ThOD/L)

activity within the test systems. This threshold is marked by a red line on both
graphs on Figure 11.

measured at time 0 and then at every time step. The oxygen concentrations for the
diluted and undiluted tests can be seen in Figure 11 and in Appendix D, Section

4.3.2

Stated in the OECD 1992 guideline, the oxygen uptake of the inoculum blank
should be in the range of 20 to 30 mg O2/L and no greater than 60 mg O2/L in
28 days. The incoculum blank, containing only diluted AS, met the requirements,
as the oxygen uptake of the triplicates were in the range of 26 to 27.1 mg O2/L
in 28 days. Additionally, the oxygen uptake of the abiotic tests, containing only
diluted ACS and cedarwood oil, were in the range of 7.9 to 10.2 mg O2/L in 28
days, which can be explained by a background pressure drop from the oxitop test
setup. The O2 uptake of the inoculum blanks and abiotic tests are displayed to-
gether with the O2 uptake of cedarwood oil and NaOAc in Appendix D, Section D.5.
The procedure control test using NaOAc was run in parallel to check the operation
of the procedure and experimental technique. After 28 days, the average BOD of
NaOAc was 0.86 mg O2/mg NaOAc (see Appendix D Section D.4). The proce-
dure control meets the requirement of 60% degradation in a 10 day window and
showed complete mineralization of NaOAc in 21 days, validating the procedure.
The biodegradability of NaOAc is displayed in Appendix D, Section D.6.
Generally, the results obtained from the test do not indicate inhibition due to
toxicity of either the reference compound nor the test substance, as the O2 uptake
and degradation curves did not reached a plateau.

5.1
WSCS biodegradation test
In this study, a first-order model was fitted to the test results, as the test used
activated sludge and low test concentrations, which are in compliance with the
theory presented in Simkins and Alexander (1984). From the WSCS tests it was
found that all of the main constituents in cedarwood oil were fully degraded (<SDL)
for the diluted test (Figure 8) whereas only Widrol and Cedrol were fully degraded
(<SDL) for the undiluted test (Figure 9).
Furthermore, it was found that the
biodegradation rate constants in the diluted WSCS test was up to 10.41 times
slower (ranging from 1.28 to 10.41) than the undiluted WSCS test. More so, all
constituents in the diluted test showed to have lag-phases ranging from 0 to 2 days
and for the undiluted test lag-phases were in the range of 0 to 0.47. Based on
the short lag-phases in the two WSCS tests it is hypothesized that a broad range
of bacteria is present in the activated sludge (Bertrand 2019; Leahy and Colwell
1990).
As individual organisms are limited to only degrading a small range of
hydrocarbons, it is necessary that an assemblage of mixed microorganism is present
in activated sludge (Leahy and Colwell 1990). In general, it is found that activated
sludge consist of a highly diverse microbial composition (Peces et al. 2022; Z.-B.
Wang et al. 2016). Furthermore, it is assumed that the microorganism present in
the activated sludge have been subjected to pre-exposure, why they did not need an
adaptation period. This assumption is also in compliance with the characterization
of cedarwood oil being a ’down the drain’ compound ending up in the wastewater
stream (S¨uhring et al. 2022).
The lack of further degradation of the constituents in the undiluted test can ei-
ther be explained by the constituents being unavailable for the microorganisms
based on their hydrophobicity and/or due to the high oxygen consumption hav-
ing an inhibiting effect on the microorganisms. The hydrophobicity of the main
constituents drives the partition towards the headspace in the diluted test and to-
wards the sludge matrix in the undiluted test, as presented in Table 9. Based on

it was found that the majority of constituents will reside either in the headspace
or sludge matrix making them unavailable to microbial degradation. A study by
S¨uhring et al. (2022) found that through SimpleTreat modeling of cedarwood oil in
a WWPT, the constituents would predominantly be removed by partition to the
sludge and second through volatilization, which corresponds to our findings of the

Since the density of AS present in the diluted test system was 100 times lower
compared to the density of AS in the undiluted test, it was hypothesized that the
diluted test would show a markedly slower biodegradation kinetics compared to the
undiluted test. This was also proven to be the case, however not in the magnitude
as expected. Since the concentration of TSS in the undiluted test was higher com-

sludge. The high sorbent loading together with the Kaw and Kow coefficients of the
constituents reduced the freely dissolved concentration of cedarwood oil, making
the constituents unavailable for the microorganism,thus impacting the degradation
rates seen in the undiluted test. It has previously been assumed that the total con-

surface comprises of bacteria, which are the primary agents for biodegradation (Lee
et al. 1998). In Lee et al. (1998) the question of the influence of sorption in terms of
bioavalabiliy of compounds is touched upon. In the model formulations presented

that the sorbed compounds to AS are in fact available for the microorganism and
thus influence the degradation positively. Similar results were found in Hickman
and Novak (1989) which showed a direct positive relationship between degrada-
tion rate and bacteria density. However a study by Martin Alexander (2000) have
found that when a matrix of e.g sludge is present, the bioavailability is affected
influencing the degradation rate, why the net biodegradation rate is limited by
sorption of compounds. The theory on bioavailability by Martin Alexander (2000)

Initial assessments of cedarwood oil using the standard OxiTop® system following
the OECD 301F Manometric Respirometry test indicated a degradation of 34.3%

it did not meet the requirements. The regulatory OECD 301F test method require
high test concentrations which may inhibit microbial activity. OECD (1992) rec-

microbial activity if the biodegradability of the chemical did not reach the pass
level and that it may be re-tested at low, non-inhibitory concentrations in a rel-
evant simulation test. Even though the biodegradation of cedarwood oil did not
meet the pass levels, it was not indicted to be caused by microbial inhibition as

5.3
Discussion of methods
The method employed in the WSCS tests and OECD 301F test asses the biodegra-
dation using two different approaches where primary degradation and mineraliza-
tion are examined respectively. For UVCBs these two approaches have some ad-
vantages and disadvantages. The WSCS test uses SPME coupled to GC-MS/MS
to separate and identify constituents and to determine the primary biodegrada-
tion kinetics of the main constituents in cedarwood oil. Using this method also
allows to investigate how biodegradation of the constituents can affect each other
within the mixture, which are also the case in the environment where environmen-
tal pollutants exist as mixtures (Ramakrishnan et al. 2011). As the test conditions
are the same for all chemicals present in the mixture, the biodegradation kinetics
are directly comparable. In addition, the test allows to investigate UVCBs as a
whole substance at environmentally relevant low concentrations, which are more
likely to occur in the environment compared to the conservative test OECD 301F
Page 60 of 78
==PAGE:84==

which uses high concentrations. The disadvantages is that the method does not
include the degradation products following primary degradation, thus not know-
ing whether the metabolites posses persistent properties. Additionally, the minor
unknown fractions are neither included in the assessment, nor their degradation
products.
However, applying a SCAN method can be use full to help identify
problematic degradation products. A study by Møller (2020) performed a SCAN
method in parallel to a study similar to the WSCS test using Cedarwood Virginia
Oil to investigate whether any degradation products of the main constituents would
occur. Møller (2020) concluded that no degradation products were observed but
also emphasizes that the governing reason was that the used SPME-GC chemical
analysis technique is focusing mainly on the non-polar, lipophilic and volatile com-
pounds and elaborates ”Typically, metabolites are more polar making them more
available to the microorganisms in water. Therefore, polar metabolites are often
more easily degraded, but there are exceptions which will not be discovered using
the SPME-GC technique” (Møller et al. 2021). A general conception is that the
transformation of organic compounds reduces the hydrophobicity, persistence and
toxicity of the chemical compound, though with exceptions (Møller et al. 2021;
Smitha et al. 2017). Following the OECD 301F test, on the other hand, targets
the whole substance and its complete mineralization based on the ThOD. However,
calculating the ThOD of a UVCB can only be an estimation of the whole substance
as the unknown fractions can be arduous accounting for. Since the method targets
the whole substance it is not possible to identify which components of the mix-
ture that constitutes persistent properties if the substance is not mineralized. This
is problematic in regards to a regulatory and commercial perspective, and aggra-
vates the development of new products or the improvement of existing products
on the market. A study by K. J. Jenner et al. (2011) found that Thujupsene was
only degraded by 51% following the OECD 301F test with a test concentration of
100 mg/L. Subsequently an additional primary degradation analysis of Thujopsene
showed that it was degraded, thus disappeared from the test medium, which could
indicate an exception where the metabolites are more persistent than its parent

predicted, it is pertinent to check the PBT-characteristics of expected metabolites”.
More research of the fate of metabolites and possible degradation products of cedar-
wood oil is needed, as exclusion of the full degradation of all major- and minor
constituents and their respective degradation products may lead to a insufficient
biodegradation assessment of cedarwood oil as a whole subastance.
Currently, there is a knowledge gap on how the presence of multiple constituents
in a mixture affect biodegradation kinetics of individual chemicals (Hammershøj
2020). Especially when assessing full mineralization of UVCBs, owing to the fact
that it is not even possible to distinguish and identify all of the constituents. Assess-
ing primary degradation kinetics of chemicals in a mixture using a whole substance
approach, like the WSCS tests employed in this study, makes it possible to inves-
tigate how the biodegradation of the known constituents may affect each other at
low concentrations which are also the case in the environment. Using this method
comes closer to an understanding of mixture effects, as it can investigate the pri-
mary biodegradation of known constituents for any UVCB, making it possible to
examine correlations of internal constituent behavior across mixtures. A mixture
is also known to affect the individual solubility of chemicals in a substance, ”The
solubility of a constituent within a multi-component mixture may be orders of mag-
nitude lower than the aqueous solubility of the pure constituent in water” (Z. Wang
et al. 1964). For the known constituents of cedarwood oil most solubility’s are
below 1 mg/L (except Widrol and Cedrol). For mixture components, the solubility
can be calculated using Raoult’s law for ideal liquid mixtures (Banerjee 1984; Birch
et al. 2019) (see Appendix E.1), which showed a decrease in the solubility for each
of the constituents, thus the whole mixture. The solubility is important to consider
in research and in the regulatory sector e.g. for hazard assessment of chemicals, as
the dissolved concentration is limited by the water solubility, and thus reduce the
exposure and uptake of chemicals by organisms (Birch et al. 2019).
Additionally, when constituents in a mixture undergo biodegradation, risk assess-

5.4
Future work - testing and assessment of UVCBs
Based on the aforementioned limitations regarding the WSCS test and the OECD
301F test some modifications and improvements of the two test methods are pro-
posed in the following section. Additionally, a recommendation for a more compre-
hensive assessment of UVCBs is presented.
The following is proposed to overcome the limitations presented regarding the
WSCS test method. For the undiluted test none of the constituents (but Widrol
and Cedrol) were fully degraded within the testing period of 4 days. In order to
obtain more insight into the primary biodegradation kinetics of the constituents, it
is necessary to prolong the undiluted test. As the test duration was limited by the
oxygen content in the system, a larger fraction of headspace should be considered
to introduce more oxygen. By up-scaling the headspace it allows to maintain the
relevant density of AS in the system which represents the conditions in a WWTPs.
Further more, in order to track the potential changes to the pH in the WSCS test
system, an initial pH measurement of the activated sludge at the collection site

and continuous measurements over the duration time should be done. By doing
so, it would become evident whether changes in pH is a limiting factor and thus
contributing to the lack of further degradation. More so, an additional test setup
investigating the sorption potential to AS for constituents in UVCBs at environ-
mentally relevant concentrations would help interpret the results of the undiluted
test and provide a deeper insight of whether sorption is a minor or major limiting
factor for biodegradation.
As previously described, the OECD 301F test using regular oxitop system is not
suitable for (semi)volatile and hydrophobic chemicals as a significant amount of
the test concentration will be lost to the oxitop instruments. Therefore testing of
UVCBs for readily biodegradability calls for new ways to asses complete mineral-
ization or introducing modifications to improve the excising methods (e.g. modi-
fications made by Brown et al. (2018)). Brown et al. (2018) found that the losses
of constituents to the system were reduced by 90% by substituting the regular
oxitop instrument with a OxySenor. Since losses of constituents are shown to be
significantly reduced when using a OxySensor, it is proposed to perform the read-
ily biodegradability test OECD 301F using a OxySensor or similar oxygen sensor
instruments with none or minimal amount of adsorbing phases like plastic. Doing
so would provide a more reliable result of the mineralization of UVCBs thus pro-
vide a more accurate assessment. Alternately, a OECD 310 ’CO2 in sealed vessels
(Headspace test)’ could be conducted, which is also stated to be suitable for volatile
and non-soluble substances (OECD 2014).
It is an advantage to conduct the WSCS test as it is possible to identify the prob-
lematic constituents in a mixture. This identification may help manufactures ease
the development of new products or improvements to already existing products.
Another advantage of the WSCS test is that it is possible to test UVCBs at envi-
ronmental relevant concentration appose to the OECD 301 standards which require
rather high concentrations as the test aims to be conservative. However, an ad-

This study reveals a need for a multipronged approach integrating whole sub-
stance and constituent based data. It is proposed to combine both WSCS test
and OECD test (with the aforementioned modifications) to obtain greater insight
into biodegradability of mixtures including (semi)volatile and hydrophobic chemi-
cals. By conducting the two biodegradation studies concurrently it is possible to
generate larger data sets on primary degradation kinetics and relating it to miner-
alization (determining readily biodegradability), thus obtaining more insight into
assessment of UVCBs. Depending in the wanted outcome in regards to biodegra-
dation assessment the two methods could also be conducted in a sequence. If the
tested substance classifies as ’not readily biodegradable’ in adherence to the OECD
301 Readily biodegradability standards, then a WSCS test can be conducted for
more information on the individual constituents.

of understanding UVCBs fate and effect.

6
Conclusion
In this study, biodegradation kinetics and half-times for the main constituents in
cedarwood oil were successfully determined by conducting two Whole Substance
biodegradation tests combined with Constituent Specific analytics (WSCS test) in
a closed system at an environmentally relevant concentration. Diluted and undi-
luted activated sludge from a WWTP was used as inoculum and primary biodegra-
dation kinetics was found using an SPME GC-MS/MS analytical method. All main
constituents were found to be degraded (<SDL) within 28 days for the diluted test,
whereas only Widrol and Cedrol were found to be degraded (<SDL) within 4 days
for the undiluted test. It was strongly indicated that α-Cedrene, β-Cedrene, Thu-
jupsene, β-Chamgrene, β-Himachalene and Cuparene were all sorbed to the sludge
matrix impacting the bioavailability why they were not fully degraded within 4
days. The diluted test system showed to have slower biodegradation kinetics com-
pared to the undiluted test, however not in the magnitude as expected. Despite
the high oxygen demand of the activated sludge it was still possible with proper
dimensioning to uphold aerobic conditions during both WSCS tests. However, as
the constituents in the undiluted test was not fully degraded within 4 days, it
was proposed to prolong the test. By doing so, it was necessary to increase the
headspace in test to prevent oxygen deficiency which could inhibit the activity of
the microorganism.
An additional test following the standardized OECD 301F method Manometric
Respiratory Readily Biodegradability test using OxiTop® was conducted concur-
rently with the two WSCS tests. Initial assessments of cedarwood oil indicated a
degradation of 34.3%, thus did not meet the requirements for readily biodegrad-
ability (minimum 60% mineralization in 28 days). In parallel to the OECD 301F
test an abiotic control test was set up which revealed significant losses of 69.4 to
99.0% of the constituents to the oxitop instruments, thus making the OECD 301F
test unsuitable for UVCBs.

CONCLUSION
Since the OECD 301F test showed to be unsuitable for testing biodegradability
of UVCBs, the results obtained were unreliable, why a comparison to the WSCs
test results were not possible. However the two methods were comprehensively
discussed and evaluated upon biodegradability testing and assessment of UVCBs.
It was found that the WSCS test and a modification of the OECD 301F test were
complementary methods as a combination of the two methods enables a more com-
plete assessment of the biodegradation of cedarwood oil by providing data on both
primary degradation of the individual constituents and mineralization of the whole
substance.
The findings presented in this study are of interest to people within the field of
environmental evaluation of chemicals in mixtures, regulators and industry, and
reveals the need for a multipronged approach integrating whole substance and con-
stituent based information when assessing the fate and effects of UVCBs in the
environment.

APPENDIX D
D.6
Biodegrability of NaOAc
The biodegrabaility of the procedure control test substance, NaOAc, are shown
in Figure D3. It can be seen that the SEM for time stamp increases over time.
This can be explained by the BOD calculation, where the O2 uptake of NaOAc are
subtracted by the average O2 uptake of the inoculum blank, making the difference
across triplicates larger. Especially, one of the triplicates were to notice as it showed
a degradation of NaOAc of 166% in 28 days. There a several reasons why this
could happen. First, the amount of NaOAc added to the flasks were chosen based
on the amount of cedarwood oil added to the system, 30 mg/L, corresponding to
a ThOD of 23.4 mg NaOAc O2/L. This might cause problems for precise detection
of pressure drop in the flask. To overcome this, the amount of NaOAC added to
the flasks should have been in the range of 50-100 ThOD, as it is recommended in
the OECD 1992 guideline. Second, due to the small amount of NaOAc, weighing
off 11 mg on a weight were very difficult, increasing human error and uncertainties
of the measuring method. Due to the uncertainties on the exact amount added to
the system, the calculated ThOD might be over- or underestimated, impacting the
calculation of % NaOAc degraded in the system.

E.1
Raoult’s solubility of test substance
For ideal liquid mixture the dissolved concentrations of mixture components are
presented in Equation D4 (Raoult’s Law), where Ci is the water equilibrium mole
concentration of the i’th component, xi is the mole fraction of the i’th component in
the mixture and Si is the solubility of the pure form of the i’th component (Banerjee
1984; Birch et al. 2019).
Ci = xi · Si
(D4)
A calculation example is presented for α-Cedrene in cedarwood oil: mole fraction
[-] = xi = 0.31 and solubility of the pure form [mg/L] = Si = 0.15, the equilibrium
mole concentration of α-Cedrene is then found to be:
Ci = xi · Si = 0.31 · 0.15mg/L = 0.047mg/L
(D5)
